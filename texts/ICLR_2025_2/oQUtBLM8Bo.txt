009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Most existing safe multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) algorithms con-
012            sider the constrained Markov decision process (CMDP) problem, which targets
013            bringing the mean of constraint violation below a user-defined threshold. How-
014            ever, as observed by existing works albeit for the single-agent case, CMDP al-
015            gorithms suffer from unstable training when the constraint threshold is zero. This
016            paper proposes EFMARL, a novel MARL algorithm that improves upon the prob-
017            lems faced in the zero constraint threshold setting by extending the epigraph form,
018            a technique to perform constrained optimization, to the centralized training and
019
               distributed execution (CTDE) paradigm. We validate our approach in different
               Multi-Particle Environments and Safe Multi-agent MuJoCo environments with
020
               varying numbers of agents. Simulation results show that our algorithm achieves
021
               stable training and the best performance while satisfying constraints: it is as safe
022            as the safest baseline that has significant performance loss, and achieves similar
023            performance as baselines that prioritize performance but violate safety constraints.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      Multi-agent systems (MAS) play an integral role in our aspirations for a more convenient future with
029
      examples such as autonomous warehouse operations (Kattepur et al., 2018), large-scale autonomous
030
      package delivery (Ma et al., 2017), traffic routing (Wu et al., 2020), and power systems (Biagioni
031   et al., 2022). For MAS, distributed policies are desirable due to their ability to scale to a large
032   number of agents compared to centralized policies (Pereira et al., 2022; Saravanos et al., 2023). To
033   construct such policies, multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) (Zhang et al., 2021; Garg et al.,
034   2024) has emerged as an attractive method. While the learned policies must be safe for real-world
035   deployment, many MARL algorithms do not explicitly consider safety constraints (Sunehag et al.,
036   2017; Rashid et al., 2020b; Yang et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2020; Peng et al., 2021; Rashid et al.,
037   2020a), but instead optimizing for a single objective that must be designed to incorporate safety.
038   Although safe MARL methods have been developed in recent years (Gu et al., 2023; Liu et al.,
039
      2021; Ding et al., 2023; Lu et al., 2021; Geng et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2024), most of these methods
      target the constrained Markov decision process (CMDP) (Altman, 2004) setting, which only asks for
040
      the mean constraint violation to stay below a user-defined threshold. This is unacceptable for safety-
041
      critical applications such as autonomous vehicles or human-robot interactions, where any constraint
042   violation can be fatal. While this can be addressed by setting the constraint violation threshold to
043   zero in the CMDP, in this setting the popular Lagrangian methods experience training instabilities
044   which result in sharp drops in performance during training, and non-convergence or convergence to
045   poor policies (So & Fan, 2023; He et al., 2023; Ganai et al., 2024; Huang et al., 2024).
046
      These concerns have been identified recently, resulting in a series of works that enforce hard con-
047
      straints (Zanon & Gros, 2020; Zhao et al., 2021; So & Fan, 2023; Ganai et al., 2024) using tech-
048   niques inspired by Hamilton-Jacobi reachability (Tomlin et al., 2000; Lygeros, 2004; Mitchell et al.,
049   2005; Margellos & Lygeros, 2011; Bansal et al., 2017) in deep reinforcement learning (RL) for the
050   single-agent case and have been shown to significantly improve safety compared to other safe RL
051   approaches. However, to the best of our knowledge, theories, and algorithms for safe RL are still
052   lacking for the multi-agent scenario, especially when policies are executed in a distributed manner.
053   While single-agent RL methods can be directly applied to the MARL setting by treating the MAS
      as a centralized single agent, the joint action space grows exponentially with the number of agents,


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      preventing these algorithms from scaling to scenarios with a large number of agents (Guestrin et al.,
055   2002; Sunehag et al., 2017; Foerster et al., 2018).
056
057   To tackle the problem of zero constraint violation in multi-agent scenarios with distributed policies1
058
      while achieving high collaborative performance, we propose Epigraph Form MARL (EFMARL).
      EFMARL directly tackles the multi-agent constrained optimal control problem (MACOCP), whose
059
      solution satisfies zero constraint violation. To solve the MACOCP, EFMARL uses the epigraph
060
      form technique (Boyd & Vandenberghe, 2004), which has previously been shown to result in better
061   policies compared to Lagrangian-based methods (So & Fan, 2023) in the single-agent setting. Con-
062   sidering the multi-agent setting, we propose an extension of the epigraph form that falls under the
063   centralized training distributed execution (CTDE) paradigm.
064
      We validate EFMARL using various tasks from multi-particle environments (MPE) (Lowe et al.,
065
      2017) and Safe Multi-agent MuJoCo (Gu et al., 2023) with varying numbers of agents and compare
066
      its performance with existing safe MARL algorithms using the penalty and Lagrangian methods.
067   The results suggest that EFMARL achieves the best performance while satisfying constraints: it is
068   as safe as conservative baselines that achieve high safety but sacrifice performance, while matching
069   the performance of unsafe baselines that sacrifice safety for high performance. In addition, while the
070   baseline methods require different choices of hyperparameters to perform well in each environment
071   and suffer from unstable training because of zero constraint violation threshold, EFMARL is stable
072   in training using the same hyperparameters across all environments, indicative of the algorithm’s
073   robustness to environmental changes. To summarize, our contributions are presented below:
074
          • Drawing on prior work that addresses the training instability of Lagrangian methods in the
075
            zero-constraint violation setting, we extend the epigraph form method from single-agent RL to
076
            MARL, improving upon the training instability of existing MARL algorithms.
077
078
          • We present theoretical results showing that the outer problem of the epigraph form can be
            decomposed and solved in a distributed manner during online execution. This allows EFMARL
079
            to fall under the CTDE paradigm.
080
081       • We illustrate through extensive simulations that, without any hyperparameter tuning, EFMARL
082
            achieves stable training and is as safe as the most conservative baseline while simultaneously
            being as performant as the most aggressive baseline across all environments.

085   2    R ELATED WORK
086
087   Shielding for Safe MARL One popular method of providing safety to learning-based methods
088   is using shielding or a safety filter (Garg et al., 2024). Here, an unconstrained learning method is
089   paired with a shield or safety filter using techniques such as predictive safety filters (Zhang et al.,
090   2019; Muntwiler et al., 2020), control barrier functions (Cai et al., 2021; Pereira et al., 2022) or
091
      automata (ElSayed-Aly et al., 2021a; Xiao et al., 2023; Melcer et al., 2022). Such shields are often
      constructed before learning begins and are used to modify either the feasible actions or the output of
092
      the learned policy to maintain safety. One benefit is that safety can be guaranteed during both train-
093
      ing and deployment since the shield is constructed before training. However, they require domain
094   expertise to build a valid shield, which can be challenging in the single-agent setting and even more
095   difficult for MAS (Garg et al., 2024). Other methods can automatically synthesize shields but face
096   scalability challenges (Melcer et al., 2022; ElSayed-Aly et al., 2021b). Another drawback is that the
097   policy after shielding might not consider the same objective as the original policy and may result in
098   noncollaborative behaviors or deadlocks (Qin et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2023; 2024).
099
100   Unconstrained MARL Early works that approached the problem of safety for MARL focused
101   on navigation problems and collision avoidance (Chen et al., 2017b;a; Everett et al., 2018; Semnani
102   et al., 2020), where safety is achieved by a sparse collision penalty (Long et al., 2018), or a shaped
103   reward penalizing getting close to obstacles and neighboring agents (Chen et al., 2017b;a; Everett
104
      et al., 2018; Semnani et al., 2020). However, adding a penalty to the reward function changes the
      original objective, so the resulting policy may not be optimal for the original constraint optimization
105

107   and information received via message passing with other agents (Garg et al., 2024), although this setting is

      problem. In addition, the satisfaction of collision avoidance constraints is not necessarily guaranteed
109   by even the optimal policy (Massiani et al., 2023; Everett et al., 2018; Long et al., 2018).
110
111   Constrained MARL In contrast to unconstrained MARL methods, which change the constraint
112   optimization problem to an unconstrained problem, constrained MARL methods explicitly solve the
113   CMDP problem. For the single-agent case, prominent methods for solving CMDPs include primal
114   methods (Xu et al., 2021), primal-dual methods using Lagrange multipliers (Borkar, 2005; Tessler
115   et al., 2019; He et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2024), and via trust-region-based approaches (Achiam
116   et al., 2017; He et al., 2023). These methods provide guarantees either in the form of asymptotic
117   convergence guarantees to the optimal (safe) solution (Borkar, 2005; Tessler et al., 2019) using
118   stochastic approximation theory (Robbins & Monro, 1951; Borkar, 2009), or recursive feasibility
119
      of intermediate policies (Achiam et al., 2017; Satija et al., 2020) using ideas from trust region op-
      timization (Schulman et al., 2015a). The survey (Gu et al., 2022) provides an overview of the
120
      different methods of solving safety-constrained single-agent RL. In multi-agent cases, however, the
121
      problem becomes more difficult because of the non-stationary behavior of other agents, and similar
122   approaches have been presented only recently (Gu et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2021; Ding et al., 2023;
123   Lu et al., 2021; Geng et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024). However, the CMDP setting
124   they handle makes it difficult for them to handle hard constraints, and results in poor performance
125   when the constraint violation threshold is zero (Ganai et al., 2024).

295   4.2       S OLVING THE INNER PROBLEM USING MARL
296
297   Following So & Fan (2023), we solve the inner problem using centralized training with proximal
298   policy optimization (PPO) (Schulman et al., 2017). We use a graph neural network (GNN) back-
299   bone for the z-conditioned policy πθ (oi , z), cost-value function Vϕl (x, z), and the constraint-value
300   function Vψh (oi , z) with parameters θ, ϕ, and ψ, respectively. Note that other NN structures can be
301   used as well. The implementation details are introduced in Appendix E.
302   Policy and value function updates During centralized training, the NNs are trained to solve the
303   inner problem (13b), i.e., for a randomly sampled z, find policy π(·, z) that minimizes the total
304   value function V (x0 , z; π). We follow MAPPO (Yu et al., 2022a) to train the NNs with advantage
305   decomposition (Gu et al., 2023). Specifically, when calculating the advantage for i-th agent, Ai
306   (Schulman et al., 2017), with the generated advantage estimation (GAE) (Schulman et al., 2015b)
307   for each agent, instead of using the cost function V l (Yu et al., 2022a), we apply the decomposed
308   total value function max{Vψh (oi , z), Vϕl (x, z) − z}. We perform trajectory rollouts following the
309   dynamics for x (1) and z (14) using the learned policy πθ , starting from random sampled x0 and
310   z 0 . After collecting the trajectories, we train the cost-value function Vϕl and the constraint-value
311   function Vψh via regression and use the PPO policy loss to update the z-conditioned policy πθ .

516    Varying ξ in the outer problem To robustify our approach against estimation errors in V h , we solve
517    for a zi that is slightly more conservative by modifying (15b) to Vψh (oi , zi ) ≤ −ξ (Section 4.3). We

519    higher values of ξ result in higher safety rates and slightly higher costs, while the reverse is true for

521    when the learned V h has estimation errors. We thus recommend choosing ξ close to ν.

524    6              C ONCLUSION
525
526
       This paper introduces EFMARL for the multi-agent constrained optimal control problem. EFMARL
527
       extends the epigraph form method from single-agent RL to MARL and addresses the training insta-
528    bility of Lagrangian methods in the zero-constraint violation setting. We decompose the epigraph
529    form problem into a centralized inner problem solved in centralized training, and a distributed outer
530    problem solved during online execution. Experimental results on MPE and the safe Multi-agent
531    MuJoCo suggest that, unlike baseline methods, EFMARL uses a constant set of hyperparameters
532    across all environments, and achieves a safety rate similar to the most conservative baseline and
533    similar performance to the baselines that prioritize performance but violate safety constraints.
534    Limitations The theoretical analysis in Section 4.3 suggests that the connected agents must com-
535    municate z and reach a consensus. If the communication on z is disabled, although our experiments
536    show that the agents still perform similarly, the theoretical optimality guarantee may not be valid. In
537    addition, the framework does not consider noise, disturbances in the dynamics, or communication
538    delays between agents. Finally, as a safe RL method, although safety can be theoretically guaranteed
539    under the optimal value function and policy, this does not hold under inexact minimization of the
       losses. We leave tackling these issues as future work.

929   C     D ISCUSSION ON I MPORTANCE OF P ROPOSITION 1
930
931   Establishing Proposition 1 is key to EFMARL. Namely,
932
933          1. Satisfying dynamic programming implies that the value function is Markovian. In other
934             words, for a given z 0 , the value at the kth timestep is only a function of z k and xk instead
935             of the z 0 and the entire trajectory up to the kth timestep.
936          2. Consequently, this implies that the optimal policy will also be Markovian and is only a
937             function of z k and xk .
938          3. Rephrased differently, since the value function is Markovian, this implies that, for a given
939             z 0 and x0 , the value at the kth timestep is equal to the value (at the initial timestep) of a
940             new problem where we start with z̃ 0 = z k and x̃0 = xk .
941          4. Since we relate the value function of consecutive timesteps, given a value function estima-
942             tor, we can now control the bias-variance tradeoff of the value function estimate by using
943             k-step estimates instead of the Monte Carlo estimates.
944
             5. Instead of only using the k-step estimates for a single choice of k, we can compute a
945             weighted average of the k-step estimates as in GAE to further control the bias-variance
946             tradeoff.

987    E.2.1    M ULTI - PARTICAL ENVIRONMENTS (MPE)
988
       We use directed graphs G = (V, E) to represent MPE, where V is the set of nodes containing the
989    objects in the multi-agent environment (e.g., agents Va , goals Vg , landmarks Vl , and obstacles Vo ).
990    E ⊆ {(i, j) | i ∈ Va , j ∈ V} is the set of edges, denoting the information flow from a sender node
991    j to a receiver agent i. An edge (i, j) exists only if the communication between node i and j can
992    happen, which means the distance between node i and j should be within the communication radius
993    R in partially observable environments. We define the neighborhood of agent i as Ni := {j | (i, j) ∈
994    E}. The node feature vi includes the states of the node xi and a one-hot encoding of the type of
995    the node i (e.g., agent, goal, landmark, or obstacle), e.g., [0, 0, 1]⊤ for agent nodes, [0, 1, 0]⊤ for
996    goal nodes, and [1, 0, 0]⊤ for obstacle nodes. The edge feature eij includes the information passed
997
       between the sender node j and the receiver node i (e.g., relative positions and velocities).
998    We consider 6 MPE: Target, Spread, Formation, Line, Corridor, and ConnectSpread. In each envi-
999    ronment, the agents need to work collaboratively to finish some tasks:
1000
       Target (Nayak et al., 2023): Each agent tries to reach its preassigned goal.
1001
1002   Spread (Dames et al., 2017): The agents are given a set of (not preassigned) goals to cover.
1003   Formation (Agarwal et al., 2020): Given a landmark, the agents should spread evenly on a circle
1004   with the landmark as the center and a given radius.
1005
       Line (Agarwal et al., 2020): Given two landmarks, the agents should spread evenly on the line
1006
       between the landmarks.
1007
1008   Corridor: A set of agents and goals are separated by a narrow corridor, whose width is smaller than
1009   4r where r is the radius of agents. The agents should go through the corridor and cover the goals.
1010   ConnectSpread: A set of agents and goals are separated by a large obstacle with a diameter larger
1011   than the communication radius R. The agents should cover the goals without colliding with obstacles
1012   or each other while also maintaining the connectivity of all agents.
1013
