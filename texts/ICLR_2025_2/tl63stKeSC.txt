008                                                  A BSTRACT
009
               Partial differential Equations (PDEs) are an essential tool across science and en-
010
               gineering. Recent work has shown how contemporary developments in machine
011            learning models can directly help in improving methods for solution discovery of
012            PDEs. This line of work falls under the umbrella of Physics-Informed Machine
013            Learning. A key step in solving a PDE is to determine a set of points in the do-
014            main where the current iterate of the PDE’s solution will be evaluated. The most
015            prevalent strategy here is to use Monte Carlo sampling, but it is widely known
016            to be sub-optimal in lower dimensions. We leverage recent advances in asymp-
017            totic expansions of quadrature nodes and weights (for weight functions belonging
018            to the modified Gauss-Jacobi family) together with suitable adjustments for pa-
019
               rameterization towards a data-driven framework for learnable quadrature rules. A
               direct benefit is a performance improvement in solving PDEs via neural networks,
020
               relative to existing alternatives, on a set of problems commonly studied in the lit-
021
               erature. Beyond finding a standard solution for an instance of a single PDE, our
022            construction enables learning rules to predict solutions for a given family of PDEs
023            via a simple use of hyper-networks, a broadly useful capability.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Differential equations are an indispensable tool across science, providing a framework for mod-
029
      eling/analyzing diverse physical dynamics. Most real-world settings lead to differential equations
      where analytical solutions are not possible, but research over decades has led to a mature set of
030
      numerical methods which can provide an approximate solution in many cases (Ames, 2014; Tre-
031
      fethen & Bau, 2022). Advances in our understanding of deep neural networks as universal function
032   approximators has led to nice results that span both these topics (Chen et al., 2018; Kidger, 2022).
033   Specifically, a growing body of work in the last five years or so has identified novel architectures,
034   by marrying differential equation solvers with deep learning and these formulations offer surprising
035   new capabilities. For example, one now has access to completely data-driven approaches (Li et al.,
036   2020b;a; Kovachki et al., 2021) which use observational data to estimate the operator for a PDE.
037   For small-sample sizes, we have means of obtaining new class of differential equation solvers that
038   exploit neural networks to encode physical laws (Raissi et al., 2019; Kharazmi et al., 2019).
039   Roughly speaking, the aforementioned line of work (Karniadakis et al., 2021), discussed in more
040   detail later in §7 can be broadly classified under three main threads: (a) PDE solvers based on
041   neural networks (PINNs) (Raissi et al., 2019); (b) PDE discovery (e.g., symbolic regression) (Holt
042   et al., 2023; d’Ascoli et al., 2023) and (c) operator learning (e.g., Fourier Neural Operator). Li et al.
043   (2020b). This classification is loosely based on the amount of data or physics used to solve/inform
044   the forward/inverse problem, (Boullé & Townsend, 2023). Our paper falls under the first category,
045   where we wish to solve a set (or family) of PDEs which share the same differential operator but
046
      vary in other ways. Here, we seek to identify how a learning mechanism can deliver efficiency gains
      solely based on the shared structure and knowledge of physics; without the use of any labeled data.
047

       Such a reformulation enables the use of numerical methods which build-up sums that converge
055   to the integral’s true value. This has the added benefit of easing regularity conditions on u and
056   solve the original problem in a weighted sense. A quadrature method (Golub & Welsch, 1969) will
057   choose evaluation points (nodes) and corresponding weights to minimize the approximation error.
058   The evaluation points may be constant step/uniform or adaptive: implying either fixed or adaptive
059   quadrature rules for estimating the integral.
060
      Challenges in computing an integral. In many applications from fluid dynamics (turbulent flow)
061   (Kutz, 2017) to radiation treatment planning (fluence calculation at tissue interfaces) (Lou et al.,
062   2021; Beckham et al., 2002) to materials science (fracture mechanics) (Aliabadi & Rooke, 1991;
063   Rice & Tracey, 1973), the associated data involves irregular behavior including singularities. In
064   continuous monitoring devices, sensors may malfunction. The most rudimentary form of uniformly
065   splitting the domain of integration into equal sub-domains, in many cases, is insufficient owing to
066   the singularity associated with the integrand. Further, even a sophisticated scheme of partitioning,
067   runs into difficulties in the multi-dimensional case. A common solution is to use some variant of
068   Monte Carlo sampling. In higher dimensions, we have no choice but to sample at large and expect
069
      the estimated solution to converge to the true solution, given enough runtime. In lower dimensions,
      Monte Carlo sampling is sub-optimal (Rivera et al., 2022) and several strategies to improve the
070
      speed and accuracy of the integral computation are known, the prominent ones being some variant
071
      of adaptive quadrature scheme, essentially choosing an adaptive grid dependent on the integrand.

      Contributions: We start with a given PDE and propose a method to learn its solution. The learn-
089   ing is achieved via two separate learnable components. First is the actual solution function for the
090   PDE which is parameterized using a neural network. The second is a parameterized weight func-
091   tion which in turn induces a family of orthogonal polynomials. Our parameterization of the weight
092   function together with asymptotic expansions when implemented carefully on modern GPUs, can
093   take advantage of parallel compute to generate a very large number (millions) of quadrature nodes
094   and weights in constant time. This provides an alternative to Monte Carlo sampling of points for
095   low-dimensional problems. We deploy our formulation to solve several commonly used PDEs and
096   achieve better performance that existing adaptive and non-adaptive sampling schemes. Our param-
097
      eterization of weight function enables learning a quadrature predictor for a family of PDEs (with
      shared structure), a very useful capability.

132   We will use u to denote the solution function for a given PDE. Since, u is parameterized/learned,
133   it is commonly called the trial function. L denotes the differential operator acting on u. We will
134   mostly deal with non-homogeneous problems and use the function f to denote the non-homogeneity.
135   Furthermore, g, g1 , . . . will be used to denote functions corresponding to the initial and/or boundary
136   conditions as needed for the PDE at hand. As is standard, we will use Ω to denote the domain of
137   definition of the PDE and ∂Ω to denote its boundary.
138   In its most generic form, an operator L operating on a function u, with a non-homogeneous term f
139   along with boundary and/or initial conditions is

143   along the normal direction, i.e., Neumann boundary conditions or both. The number of boundary
144   and/or initial conditions needed to completely determine a solution depends on the dimensionality

155   Before presenting our approach, we briefly summarize two approaches to solving the PDE above:
156   via the strong and weak form of the PDE respectively. This will help emphasize how certain choices
157   (such as quadrature rule and collocation points) will be key to our parameterization and thereby,
158
      learning.
159   Strong Form. A generic PDE shown in (7) is in its strong form. Our example in (8) is also in the
160   strong form with a specific choice for the operator. Solving the PDE in its strong form is equivalent
161   to asking that the equations in (8) are satisfied exactly at several points along the domain Ω and
      boundary ∂Ω. This can be done by sampling a large number of points distributed uniformly (Monte

163   component, a uniform sampling approach can result in poor approximation of the solution, and has
164   been studied extensively (Rivera et al., 2022).

      to hold in a “weighted” sense for the entire function. This yields the weak form, which involves
167
      integration with a test function. For the 2D-Poisson equation, using a test function v(x, y) and

172   Solving the PDE in its weak form moves the previous choice of points to a choice of test functions.
173
      The idea is to use a family of test functions based on the problem at hand and different methods
      emerge from these choices. These methods are called Galerkin methods. One common strategy at
174
      this point is to use integration by parts and make use of the boundary conditions to gradually reduce
175
      the order of derivatives from the solution u over to the test function v, making them symmetric
176   and at the same time easing regularity requirements over the desired solution u. If one decides that
177   the test functions in the weak form are Dirac-delta functions, then the Galerkin method reduces to
178   collocation and the weak form becomes the strong form involving differential equations, which then
179   need to be satisfied at the collocation points. In our discussion of the strong form, we will refer to
180   the choice of collocation points interchangeably with the choice of test functions.
181   Remark 3.1 (Minimum Principle:). A third route for solving PDEs is the minimization of an energy
182   associated with the PDE. This method is often referred to as the Rayleigh-Ritz method. But the
183   Galerkin method is general and does not require the problem to be symmetric.

185   4   H OW TO LEARN Q UADRATURE RULES ?
186
187   The above discussion helps underscore the importance of choice of test functions in the weak form
188   or the choice of collocation points for solving the PDE in its strong form. It is natural to ask: can
189   the underlying physics play a role in informing these choices? This section describes such a model
190   by leveraging the rich theory of orthogonal polynomials.
191   Learning the weight function: We consider weight functions to be continuous and positive func-
192   tions defined in some interval I. Each such weight function w(x) induces a family of orthogonal
193   polynomial (OP) given by (3). Our goal of learning the weight function corresponding to a set of
194   OP is to enable a learnable (or adaptive) quadrature. In order for the method to be practical, we want
195   to compute these efficiently. Our method exploits asymptotic expansions of quadrature nodes for
196   efficiency which are most complete for modified Gauss-Jacobi type weight function. Hence we con-
197   sider the modified Gauss-Jacobi form (4) and parameterize the modifier h(x) in (4) using a neural
198   network with parameters θ. This keeps the construction simple but offers other interesting benefits
199
      we will see shortly. So, our learnable weight function has the form:
200                                wθ (x) = (1 − x)α (1 + x)β hθ (x);              x∈I                  (10)
201
      In (10), α and β can also be parameterized/learnt but in our experiments, we find that only learning
202
      hθ (x) suffices. Next, we will see how this learnable weight function nicely ties to the choice of test
203   functions for the weak form and the collocation points in the strong form.
204   Remark 4.1. We will consider the interval of the weight function I to be the same as Ω, the domain
205   of the PDE. Extensions to higher dimensions can simply be done considering each dimension as
206   independent and stacking the sampled values, this approach is sufficient as we demonstrate in our
207   experiments. More efficient extensions can involve the use of tensor-product or sparse grids (Garcke
208   et al., 2006). The dimensionality of wθ will be implicitly determined by the PDE.

242   5     L EARNING Q UADRATURE RULES E FFICIENTLY
243
244   Numerically solving PDEs can be involved (Sewell, 2005), depending on the granularity of dis-
245   cretization used in the algorithm. For the strong form, this means efficiently identifying the colloca-
246   tion points and then evaluating the PDE at these points. For the weak form, the compute requirement
247   stems from numerically approximating the integral via a quadrature rule. The above discussion laid
248
      out a mechanism to learn the weight function. It is not obvious yet whether this can be done effi-
      ciently. With our learnable weight functions, (a) in the strong form (7),(8), this means finding the
249
      roots of corresponding orthogonal polynomial which will serve as the collocation points. (b) In
250
      the weak form, in using our learnable weight function as the test function, we must determine the
251   quadrature rule and evaluate the integral in the weak form (11) efficiently. Determining the quadra-
252   ture rule means finding the roots of an orthogonal polynomial and the corresponding quadrature
253   weights. To do this, we exploit recent advances in asymptotic expansions of orthogonal polynomi-
254   als and their roots.

336   Benefits of simplifications/parameterization. The simplifications above offer multiple benefits.
337   First, we avoid computing contour integrals within a differentiable learning framework. Second, we
338   are able to compute all nodes and weights in parallel thereby making the process very efficient even
339   for a very large number (millions) of nodes. It is worth noting that the exact procedure to compute
340   the nodes (beyond the leading order term) as outlined in Section 4.2 of (Opsomer & Huybrechs,

342   cients. Empirically, we verify that the distribution of nodes and weights from the simplifications

      and parameterization choices does not harm the distribution of quadrature which converges to the

      7   R ELATED W ORK
514
515
      Beyond the literature described in §1, a large body of work focuses on discovering solutions to PDEs
516
      using neural networks. We mention some ideas and how our framework is different. In contrast to
517   Physics Informed Neural Networks (PINN)s Raissi et al. (2019), we use data-dependent sampling of
518   collocation points. While Variational-PINN (Kharazmi et al., 2019) and hp-VPINN (Kharazmi et al.,
519   2021) solve PDEs in weak form, they use a careful choice of test functions, which is learnable in
520   our case. Finally, compared to Deep-Ritz (Yu et al., 2018), our method does not need the minimum
521   energy principle to be applicable. In fact, as noted earlier, we provide a novel way to learn solving
522   PDEs which is complementary to existing works. We also acknowledge recent ideas focused on
523   or adjacent to adaptive quadrature (Rivera et al., 2022; Omella & Pardo, 2024; Lau et al., 2024),
524   which either directly try to optimize node locations thereby resulting in a much larger optimization
525
      problem or fall-back to problem-specific regularizer(s) which may limit their applicability.

527   8   C ONCLUSIONS
528
529   We present a data-driven approach to solve PDEs, by exploiting new results of fast quadrature com-
530   putation using asymptotic expansions and recent capabilities of implicit function differentiation. We
531   demonstrate the incorporating our learnable quadrature scheme, LearnQuad while solving a PDE
532   can lead to performance improvement over exisiting adaptive and non-adaptive sampling schemes
533   across a diverse set of PDEs. Additionally, we show that incorporation of LearnQuad is extremely
534   beneficial when solving a family of PDEs – where the alternative would be to deploy a Monte Carlo
535   based scheme for each instance individually. Our proposed hyper-network based approach generates
536
      the solution to a PDE instance from a given family in just a single forward pass.
537   While our proposed framework is independent of the dimensionality of the problem, incorporation of
538   techniques such as sparse grids, can potentially yield better performance by exploiting the structure
539   better. It would be interesting to combine learnable quadratures with quasi-Monte Carlo technique
      for potential benefits.

1243   classical methods are indeed effective, one motivation behind the sizable PINN literature is an alter-
1244   native which is advantageous in many scenarios: (a) PINN based solutions are mesh-independent;

1249   where the PDE solution is not given in a closed form, which is true for most scenarios.

1251   C.2   PINN LOSS OVER PDE SOLVER
1252
1253
       Our object of interest in this work is PINN. PINNs provide a mesh independent solution, are more
       amenable to non-linearities and are easier to scale and implement. Hence, PINNs offer many benefits
1254
       in several cases and for this reason, are being studied extensively. Next, we justify our choice of
1255
       PINN loss instead of a PDE solver.
1256
1257   While the learnable quadrature rule is amenable for classical solvers, there are several issues. Sup-
1258   pose we use a classical solver instead of a PINN loss. This means that for each update of parameters
1259
       θ in leanrable weight function wθ (which induces the quadrature) we will need to (i) generate quadra-
       ture points using current wθ , (ii) solve the system of equations (either implicitly using a solver or
1260
       iteratively), (iii) compute some loss/quality and (iv) update θ to improve this metric. This poses sev-
1261
       eral challenges. Explicit (iterative) solvers are memory-intensive when unrolling across time steps,
1262   sensitive to numerical instabilities, thereby requiring fine time steps and increased computational
1263   cost. When differentiating through a numerically unstable solver, the gradients can become inaccu-
1264   rate or blow up. Implicit solvers demand solving linear or nonlinear systems. Computing Jacobians
1265   for implicit differentiation requires significant computational resources. Furthermore, matrix in-
1266   version or solving linear systems as part of implicit differentiation introduces high computational
1267   overhead. Hence, we can agree that integrating PDE solvers into neural network modules presents
1268   challenges for both explicit and implicit solvers due to the above mentioned issues in computing
1269   gradients which are necessary to update the models via back-propagation. Therefore, in order to
1270
       make learnable quadrature feasible – the main goal of this work – we leverage the PINN loss which
       is more suited for the end-to-end learning framework.
1271
1272   Another aspect worth mentioning is regarding the setup for a family of PDEs. Without a scheme to
1273   learn the common structure shared between different instances of the PDE, it would require solving
1274   each instance separately at each desired resolution. To conclude, the choice of the loss is important
1275   not for solving individual PDEs, but for permitting the learning of quadrature rules that can then be
1276
       used across multiple problems/solution schemes.
1277
1278   C.3   C ONTRAST WITH OTHER ADAPTIVE METHODS FOR PINN S
1279
       Our main contribution is not just solving PDEs, but learning how to optimally sample points based
1280
       on the PDE’s structure. We emphasize that advantages stem directly from our core theoretical con-
1281   tribution: the learnable weight function that induces problem-specific quadrature rules. This is fun-
1282   damentally different from both classical adaptive methods and other existing ML approaches like
1283   R3Daw et al. (2023), RARLu et al. (2021), RADWu et al. (2023); all of whom invariably rely on
1284   computing error estimates through residual-based estimators or gradient thresh-holding which are
1285   problem-specific, need to be chosen carefully, and sometimes may need to solve additional local
1286   problems. Instead we adaptively learn where refinement may be needed in an end-to-end fashion in
1287   conjunction with the PINN loss and no additional explicit error estimation is required.
