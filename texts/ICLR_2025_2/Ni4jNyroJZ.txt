011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Recent studies have shown that regularization techniques using soft labels, e.g.,
014            label smoothing, Mixup, and CutMix, not only enhance image classification ac-
015            curacy but also improve model calibration and robustness against adversarial at-
016
               tacks. However, the underlying mechanisms of such improvements remain under-
               explored. In this paper, we offer a novel explanation from the perspective of the
017
               representation space (i.e., the space of the features obtained at the penultimate
018
               layer). Our investigation first reveals that the decision regions in the representa-
019            tion space form cone-like shapes around the origin after training regardless of the
020            presence of regularization. However, applying regularization causes changes in
021            the distribution of features (or representation vectors). The magnitudes of the rep-
022            resentation vectors are reduced and subsequently the cosine similarities between
023            the representation vectors and the class centers (minimal loss points for each class)
024            become higher, which acts as a central mechanism inducing improved calibration
025            and robustness. Our findings provide new insights into the characteristics of the
026            high-dimensional representation space in relation to training and regularization
027
               using soft labels.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   The drive to improve the performance of classification models has led to the development of various
033
      regularization methods that use soft labels instead of one-hot encoded hard labels for classification
      targets. The regularization techniques such as label smoothing (Szegedy et al., 2016), Mixup (Zhang
034
      et al., 2018), and CutMix (Yun et al., 2019) have demonstrated significant success in improving
035
      classification accuracy across various benchmarks.
036
037   However, their impact goes beyond accuracy improvement. Studies have shown that these tech-
038   niques contribute to better-calibrated models, aligning predicted probabilities more closely to actual
039
      accuracy (Guo et al., 2017; Müller et al., 2019). Furthermore, they have been shown to strengthen
      model robustness against gradient-based adversarial attacks, where subtle, imperceptible noise is
040
      added to input data to intentionally mislead models (Goodfellow et al., 2014; Yun et al., 2019; Fu
041
      et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2021).
042
043   While the benefits of soft labels are evident, the underlying mechanisms by which they achieve these
044   improvements remain largely unexplained. This is where our study comes in. In this paper, we offer
045
      a deeper understanding of how soft labels enhance model calibration and adversarial robustness by
      examining the model’s representation space. Intuitively, data points that are correctly classified with
046
      lower confidence are located near decision boundaries, making them more vulnerable to small per-
047
      turbations and reducing robustness (Hein et al., 2019; Kim et al., 2024). Therefore, when calibration
048   and robustness are investigated, it is crucial to explore how decision boundaries are formed and how
049   features, i.e., the outputs of the penultimate layer are distributed within the decision regions. To un-
050   derstand the characteristics of decision regions and boundaries, we begin by analyzing their shapes
051   that can be observed in low-dimensional (2D and 3D) visualizable representation spaces. We then
052   examine whether these characteristics persist in the original high-dimensional representation space.
053   Based on the results, we study how the feature distribution in the representation space changes de-
      pending on the use of soft labels and how such changes can improve calibration and robustness.

054
      Our work can be summarized as follows:
055
056   1. We show that the decision regions form cone-like structures around the origin of the rep-
057      resentation space, and explain that this is because logits are calculated as the dot product
058      between features (representation vectors) and the weight vectors of the classification layer.
059      This structure is consistent in both low and high-dimensional representation spaces across various
060      models and different training recipes (e.g., regularizations, weight initializations).
061   2. We describe the distribution of features in the representation space using two metrics: (1) the
062      magnitude of the features, and (2) the cosine similarity of the features to the class center, the point
063      in the representation space where the classification loss is minimal. Our findings demonstrate
064      that training models with regularization significantly reduces the magnitude of the features,
065      leading to tighter clustering around the class center.
066   3. Using the findings above, we explain why regularization using soft labels leads to improved cali-
067      bration and robustness. We show that feature vectors with smaller magnitudes improve model
068      calibration, as reducing the feature magnitude acts similarly to temperature scaling, a common
069      post-hoc calibration method. Furthermore, by analyzing gradient directions in the representation
070      space, we show that smaller features tend to be distributed in robust regions, which align
071      better with the class center vector.

073   2    R ELATED W ORK
074
075   Calibration and robustness. Calibration refers to the alignment between a model’s confidence and
076   its actual accuracy. Guo et al. (2017) found that modern neural networks often exhibit overconfi-
077   dence, leading to miscalibrated predictions. To address this, various techniques have been proposed,
078   including temperature scaling (Guo et al., 2017), which is a single-parameter variant of Platt scal-
079   ing (Platt et al., 1999). Concurrently, the vulnerability of neural networks to adversarial attacks has
080   received significant attention since it was demonstrated that imperceptible input perturbations could
081
      lead to misclassifications (Szegedy et al., 2013). Moreover, the introduction of the Fast Gradient
      Sign Method (FGSM) (Goodfellow et al., 2014) has spurred the development of numerous attack
082
      and defense strategies (Carlini & Wagner, 2017; Madry et al., 2018; Croce & Hein, 2020; Deng &
083
      Mu, 2024).
084
085   Regularization techniques. Data augmentation and regularization techniques such as label smooth-
086   ing, Mixup, and CutMix have gained significant attention for their ability to improve model general-
087
      ization. Label smoothing distributes a small portion of probability mass uniformly across all labels,
      softening the one-hot encoded targets (Szegedy et al., 2016). Mixup linearly interpolates both inputs
088
      and labels, generating virtual training examples (Zhang et al., 2018). CutMix extends this idea by
089
      replacing rectangular regions in one image with patches from another, adjusting labels proportion-
090   ally (Yun et al., 2019). These methods have demonstrated promising results not only in enhancing
091   model generalization but also in improving model calibration and robustness to adversarial attacks
092   (Yun et al., 2019; Fu et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2021).
093
      There are studies investigating why these regularization techniques enhance model performance.
094
      Regarding calibration, Thulasidasan et al. (2019) examined whether the improvement in calibration
095
      is due to augmented data preventing memorization and overfitting. They trained models with convex
096   combinations of images but used hard labels. The results indicated that simply mixing features does
097   not improve calibration, emphasizing that smooth labels are crucial for achieving well-calibrated
098   models. Recently, visualizations have shown that Mixup-generated training data tend to cluster near
099   decision boundaries, leading the models to make less confident predictions and reducing miscali-
100   bration caused by overconfidence (Fisher et al., 2024). In terms of robustness to adversarial attacks,
101   Zhang et al. (2021) demonstrated that minimizing the Mixup loss is approximately equivalent to
102   minimizing an upper bound of the adversarial loss, thereby improving robustness. However, there is
103   still a lack of a comprehensive explanation for why soft labels enhance calibration and robustness
104
      from the perspective of representation space, which is the focus of this paper.
105   Representation space. Several studies have explored the dynamics of the representation space in
106   deep learning models. Wang et al. (2017) visualized the representation space using 2-dimensional
107   features and found that features are distributed in a radial pattern. Another notable concept is Neu-
      ral Collapse, which shows that both weight vectors and feature vectors converge to an Equiangular

146   3     D ECISION R EGIONS IN THE R EPRESENTATION S PACE
147
148   As mentioned in the introduction, when evaluating calibration and robustness, it is necessary to
149   examine how decision boundaries are formed and how features are distributed within the decision
150   regions. In this section, we visualize the representation space to show how the decision regions are
151   shaped and explain the reasons behind these shapes.
152
153
      3.1   2D AND 3D R EPRESENTATION S PACES
154
155
      A classification model typically consists of a feature extractor that maps inputs into features and a
156   classification layer that uses those features to make decisions. The representation space of the model
157   refers to the space where the output of the feature extractor, or more specifically, the output of the
158   penultimate layer of the model resides. It usually has high dimensionality (e.g., 2048 for ResNet50
159   and 768 for Swin-T), making it challenging to visually analyze its characteristics. To address this,
160   we insert a linear layer between the feature extractor and the classification layer, mapping the output
161   features into 2D (or 3D) vectors, and adjust the input dimension of the classification layer accord-
      ingly. As a result, the new representation space is 2D (or 3D), which facilitates visual examination.

246   3.3   W HY ARE D ECISION R EGIONS C ONE - SHAPED ?
247
248   To understand the shape of the decision regions, we investigate what happens when a feature passes
249   through the classification layer. In other words, we examine the factors that influence the calculation
250   of logits. A feature is assigned to the class showing the largest logit value by the classification layer.
251   Given a feature f for an input image, the logit yc for class c can be expressed as follows.
252
253
254
                                    yc = wcT f + bc = ||wc || · ||f || cos θ + bc ,                              (1)
255
256
257
      where wc and bc are the weight vector and the bias of the classification layer for class c, respectively,
258   and θ is the angle between f and wc . Therefore, the elements that can affect the prediction result
259   for an image are ||wc ||, cos θ, and bc .
260
261
      Note that ||wc || and bc are commonly applied to all data, so they are trained not to vary much across
      different classes (Papyan et al., 2020). In addition, we find that most bias values are very close to zero
262
      at the end of training and thus have minimal impact on the ranking of logit values (further details are
263
      provided in Appendix C). The only remaining factor that can play a decisive role in the classification
264   result is the cosine similarity between the feature vector f and the weight vector wc for class c. In
265   other words, the classification result depends on the alignment between the class’s weight vector
266   and the feature vector. Thus, decision boundaries are formed based on the degree of alignment, and
267   consequently, the decision regions take the shape of cones centered at the origin, aligned with the
268   weight vectors. This explains why different model structures (differing only in feature extractors,
269   while using the same classification layers as in Equation 1) and varying weight initializations do not
      alter the characteristic cone-shaped decision regions forming around the origin.

      4     E FFECT OF R EGULARIZATION
286
287   In Section 3, we demonstrated that decision regions form cone-like shapes around the origin in the
288   representation space. In this section, we investigate the distribution of features within this space.
289
      Specifically, we describe our methods for determining how far a feature is located from the decision
      boundary and explain how features are distributed based on these methods. We then discuss how the
290
      feature distribution changes due to regularization and how these changes are related to improvements
291
      in calibration and robustness performance.
292
293
294   4.1   A NALYSIS M ETHODS
295
296
      In a low-dimensional space, it is easy to identify the approximate distribution of features, as shown
      in Figure 1. In a high-dimensional space, however, visualization becomes challenging, making it dif-
297
      ficult to understand how features are distributed. To overcome this, we establish criteria for analysis
298
      of feature distributions. In particular, we quantify how close a feature is to the decision boundary
299   based on its confidence value.
300
301   In Figure 4(a), we present the confidence contours of decision regions in the 2D representation space.
302
      Suppose that we want to move a correctly classified feature with high confidence (red dot) to reduce
      its confidence. This can occur in two ways: 1) by moving radially toward the origin (blue dot) or 2) by
303
      moving toward the nearest decision boundary (green dot). Therefore, we determine how far a specific
304
      feature is from the decision boundary using two criteria: 1) the root mean square (RMS) of the
305   feature (we use RMS as the feature magnitude to compensate for different dimensionalities across
306   models) and 2) the cosine similarity of the feature with the class center (crosses in Figure 4(b)),
307   where the classification loss is minimal for that class. The class center for a certain class is found by
308   optimizing an arbitrary feature vector in the representation space to achieve the lowest loss for that
309   class using the gradient descent method. Figure 4(c) shows the loss and gradient directions in the
310   2D representation space, where the point from which the gradients flow out corresponds to the class
311   center. Discussions on different candidates for the class center can be found in Appendix E, where
312   we verify that the minimum loss point is the best option for measuring proximity to the decision
313
      boundary based on confidence.
314   We show the relationship of the confidence vs. the RMS of features and the cosine similarity of
315   features with the class center in the top row of Figure 5. As expected, the smaller the RMS of
316   features or the lower the cosine similarity is, the lower the confidence is, indicating proximity to the
317   decision boundary.

443   4.4   I MPACT ON A DVERSARIAL ROBUSTNESS
444
      How does the use of regularization lead to better robustness against gradient-based adversarial at-
445
      tacks? To explain this, we examine the gradient directions in the 2D space of ResNet50 on CIFAR-10
446
      in Figure 7(a). Note that the gradients shown in the figure is used to perturb the data in FGSM (and
447   iterative FGSM), which is the most basic gradient-based attack. In areas with high cosine similarity
448   to the class center (marked with a cross), the gradient directions point toward the origin. However,
449   in areas with low cosine similarity, the gradients are nearly orthogonal to the class center vector,
450   heading toward the decision boundary. Specifically, we show two samples in Figure 7(a): circles
451   represent the features of correctly classified clean images, and triangles represent features after per-
452   turbation by an amount of ϵ = 8/255. The feature vector well-aligned with the class center vector

454   toward the origin. On the other hand, the feature vector poorly aligned with the class center vector
455
      (orange sample) moves toward the nearby decision boundary and easily becomes misclassified.
456   To verify this in the original high-dimensional representation space, we apply the same attack to
457   ResNet50 on ImageNet. The results are shown in the middle row of Figure 5, where the blue bars
458   represent the histogram of cosine similarity between features and their class centers, and the orange
459   line shows the attack success rate for each confidence bin. For regularized models, the number of
460   features with high cosine similarity to the class center increases, and these features exhibit lower
461
      attack success rates, which is consistent with the results observed in the 2D representation space.
      Further discussions on adversarial robustness are provided in Appendix H.

      6     C ONCLUSION
519
520
      In this paper, we have explored the underlying mechanisms through which regularization techniques
521
      using soft labels, such as label smoothing, Mixup, and CutMix, enhance both model calibration and
522   robustness to gradient-based adversarial attacks. Our investigation focused on how decision regions
523   are formed and how regularization influences feature distributions in the representation space.
524
525
      Our analysis revealed that decision regions form a cone-like structure around the origin, with features
      distributed radially within these boundaries. Additionally, we showed that regularization reduces the
526
      RMS of representation vectors, leading to tighter clustering of them. We further explained that the
527
      formation of tighter clusters in regions with small RMS not only improves calibration by mimicking
528   the effect of temperature scaling but also increases resilience to adversarial perturbations.
529
530   We believe that these findings provide a new perspective on the dynamics induced by regularization
531
      in the representation space. Nevertheless, our study also calls for follow-up studies in several direc-
      tions. In particular, we plan to extend our analysis to investigate dependence on model components
532
      and regularization hyperparameters.

886   It is natural to measure the proximity of a feature to the decision boundary based on its confidence.

889   from the decision boundary). In this section, we examine the following candidates for the class
890   center: 1) the mean of correctly classified features within a class (class mean), 2) the weight vector

       H    F EATURE RMS VS . P ERTURBATION RMS
1081
1082
       One may argue that regularized models could be expected to be also vulnerable to adversarial attacks
1083   because their features are close to the decision boundary near the origin. However, we find that the
1084   distance to the decision boundary near the origin is farther than that to the decision boundary at
1085   the side of the cone-shaped decision region when the distance is measured in the input domain. In
1086   Figure 15, we compare the feature RMS and perturbation RMS (i.e., the RMS of the difference
1087   between the features of clean and perturbed input images) for various models trained on ImageNet
1088   when the same amount of input perturbation (ϵ=8/255) is applied. The positive correlation shown in
1089   the plot suggests that the features close to the origin change less than the features located far from
1090   the origin under the same amount of input perturbation. Therefore, robustness is determined mostly
1091
       by the direction that a feature moves due to perturbation instead of the proximity of the feature to
       the decision boundary near the origin.
