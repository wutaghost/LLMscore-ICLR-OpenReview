009                                                    A BSTRACT
010
011            Developing a foundation model for time series forecasting across diverse domains has
012            attracted significant attention in recent years. Existing works typically assume regularly
013            sampled, well-structured data, limiting their applicability to more generalized scenarios
014            where time series often contain missing values, unequal sequence lengths, and irregular
015            time intervals between measurements. To cover diverse domains and handle variable
016            regularities, we propose FlexTSF, a universal time series forecasting model that possesses
017            better generalization and natively support both regular and irregular time series. FlexTSF
018            produces forecasts in an autoregressive manner and incorporates three novel designs: VT-
019
               Norm, a normalization strategy to ablate data domain barriers, IVP Patcher, a patching
               module to learn representations from flexibly structured time series, and LED attention,
020
               an attention mechanism seamlessly integrating these two and propagate forecasts with
021
               awareness of domain and time information, enabling effective time series forecasting across
022            varying regularities. Experiments on 12 datasets show that FlexTSF outperforms state-
023            of-the-art forecasting models respectively designed for regular and irregular time series.
024            Furthermore, after self-supervised pre-training, FlexTSF shows exceptional performance in
025            both zero-shot and few-show settings for time series forecasting.

      In this paper, we propose FlexTSF 1 , a highly flexible time series forecasting model that not only can be
055   broadly applicable but also performs well across time series with variable regularities in terms of various
056   measurement types, completeness, lengths, or time intervals. The model employs a decoder-only architecture,
057   where point-level time series input data is organized into patches, with each newly generated patch serving as
058   the prediction for a bunch of future values (Woo et al., 2024; Das et al., 2024). Built on this backbone, the
059   model incorporates several novel designs to address the diversity challenges of time series data.
060
      To address the challenge of domain diversity, we propose VT-Norm, a value and time normalization strategy
061   that unifies data with different characteristics by decoupling static domain information and dynamic temporal
062   patterns. This strategy allows time series, regardless of scale or granularity, to be standardized, enabling the
063   model to focus on learning temporal patterns and dependencies in a unified manner. Additionally, domain-
064   specific features are extracted and structured into a "Leader node", which serves as a holistic reference and
065   provides guidance for subsequent processing stages. This strategy enables the model’s self-adaptation to
066   different domains.
067   To address the challenge of structural diversity, we propose to enhance the patching module, which is
068   originally designed to organize data points within a fixed window as a representation unit for model input (Nie
069   et al., 2023). This approach implicitly assumes a uniform receptive field along the temporal dimension for
070   each patch, a condition that only holds in regular and well-structured time series data. In light of this, we
071   propose IVP Patcher, a continuous-time patching module capable of learning representations for time series
072   patches that exhibit various structural diversity, such as missing values, arbitrary lengths, and various time
073   intervals. The IVP Patcher is based on neural Initial Value Problem (IVP) solvers (Xiao et al., 2024), which
074   model the temporal evolution process across arbitrary time intervals rather than the value patterns in fixed
075
      temporal window, providing effective support for both regular and irregular time series data.
076   We implement the forecasting process by proposing LED Attention, a causal self-attention mechanism that
077   incorporates a Leader node, layerwise time Embeddings, and a Dummy patch to iteratively process the
078   time series for autoregressive prediction. To evaluate our designs, we deploy FlexTSF across 12 datasets
079   according to train-validation-test split scheme to demonstrate its capability in tackling different time series
080   characteristics from diverse domains and regularities. Furthermore, we pre-train FlexTSF on a large collection
081
      of datasets in an self-supervised manner, and evaluate its performance in zero-shot and few-shot settings,
      demonstrating its capability and potential to serve as a foundation model for time series forecasting.
082
083   We summarize our main contributions as follows:
084
               • We introduce FlexTSF, a universal forecasting model for time series, offering a flexible solution to
085
                 tackle time series with domain diversity and structural diversity.
086
087            • We propose novel designs: VT-Norm for domain self-adaptation, IVP Patcher for handling time series
088              with variable regularities, and LED attention, which seamlessly integrates both to autoregressively
089
                 propagate forecasts with domain and time awareness.
090            • We conduct extensive experiments showing FlexTSF’s excellence in classic, zero-shot, and few-shot
091              scenarios. Ablation studies confirm the positive impact of its three key components.

      3.2     P ROPOSED M ODEL : F LEX TSF
162
163   A dataflow overview of FlexTSF is illustrated in Figure 2. The process begins with VT-Norm and then
164   segmenting the time series into patches and appending a dummy patch at the end. For each patch, an IVP
165   solver evolves the latent states derived from all data points in the patch window backward in time to obtain a
166   patch representation. These representations are subsequently processed by a stack of LED Attentions, with a
167   Leader node encompassing statistical domain features extracted during normalization. After a forward pass,
168   the representation of the final node, which corresponds to the dummy patch in the input, is passed to another
169   IVP solver that evolves forward in time to generate the future time series values at specified time points.
170   These predicted values replace the previous dummy patch, allowing the the autoregressive process to continue
171
      until all values within the forecasting horizon are produced. Next, we describe the details of each component.

      3.2.1    VT-N ORM
174   To address the challenges faced by foundation models due to diverse time series data characteristics, such
175   as varying measurement types, scales, and sampling intervals, we propose VT-Norm to standardize both
176   values and timestamps. Given the time series observation X = {xi }M   i=1 from dataset D, we apply a two-step
177   normalization scheme: global normalization and instance normalization. First, we calculate the mean and

182
      standard deviation σi . Let Gg and Gi denote the normalization operations using global and instance statistics,

      3.2.3   LED ATTENTION
248
249   IVP Patcher primarily focuses on modeling the internal patterns within each patch. However, inter-patch
250   correlations must also be effectively captured for accurate predictions. While the Transformer’s self-attention
251   mechanism is usually adopted for this purpose, this mechanism has been found to be less effective in capturing
252   temporal order for time series data due to the limitations of their positional encoding functions (Zeng et al.,
253   2023). To better accommodate time series with variable regularities, we adapt the Transformer self-attention
254   layer into the LED Attention, incorporating the following novel design elements.
255   First, we enhance the temporal encoding technique in the LED Attention to model complex inter-patch
256   dependencies with irregular time intervals. Inspired by rotary position embedding (RoPE) (Su et al., 2024)
257   which offers strong theoretical properties and inductive bias for continuous values aligned with temporal
258   information, we adopt this method and calculate Query and Key of the self-attention module by
259
260                                            fQ/K (r, τ ) = (W r) eiτ θ ,                                        (3)
261
262   where W denotes a learnable transformation matrix, i is the imaginary unit i, α is a non-zero constant, and τ
263   denotes the patch time indicator. Since IVP Patcher is designed to model the evolution of patches with respect
264   to their initial timestamps, the first timestamp within each patch is designated as the time indicator τ . It is
265   noteworthy that the patch time indicators {τk }Kk=0 may exhibit variable intervals, reflecting the irregular and
266
      continuous nature of the time series, making them well-suited to the RoPE technique.
267   In addition, we utilize the static domain information L, extracted from VT-Norm, by transforming this feature
268   and appending it to the beginning of the input. This allows patches from any positions in the sequence to
269   attend to domain-specific information during the autoregressive process. Furthermore, considering the LED
270   Attention operates on the patch-level representations, we append a dummy patch at the end of the sequence,
271   associated with the patch time indicator of the prediction horizon. This dummy patch experiences multiple

      to late fusion of time information after LED Attentions when no dummy patch is used (4.2.4).

310
      We evaluate FlexTSF’s forecasting performance for both regular and irregular time series across different
      domains and training settings. We conduct three stages of experiments using two non-overlapping groups
311
      of datasets: the pre-training dataset Dp and the held-out dataset Dh . In the first stage, we perform classic
312
      training-validation-testing experiments on Dh to demonstrate the effectiveness of FlexTSF. Next, we pre-train
313   FlexTSF on Dp, yielding a pre-trained model with 61 million parameters. This model is initially used to
314   perform zero-shot forecasts on the test partition of Dh , evaluating its potential as a foundation model, and then
315   fine-tuned on Dh for time series forecasting, assessing its adaptability to new domains in few-shot scenarios.
316
      Each dataset in Dh is split into training, validation, and testing sets, following their original splits if known or
317
      a split ratio of 8:1:1, otherwise. As in prior works ((Ansari et al., 2024; Nie et al., 2023; Shukla & Marlin,
318
      2021)), we repeat each experiment three times with different random seeds for dataset splitting and model
319   parameter initialization. To ensure fair comparisons of model performance across datasets, we uniformly
320   use the first 80% of each time series as input and the remaining 20% as the prediction target. The model
321   performance is assessed using mean squared error (MSE). All models are tested in the same computing
322   environment with NVIDIA Tesla V100 GPUs.

461   5      C ONCLUSION
462
      We introduced FlexTSF, a universal time series forecasting model designed to address the challenges posed
463
      by the domain and structural diversity of time series. FlexTSF comes with three novel designs, namely
464
      VT-Norm, IVP Patcher, and LED Attention, which together enable the model to generalize across diverse
465   domains and handle irregularities in time series data. Our experiments across 12 datasets demonstrate that
466   FlexTSF consistently outperforms state-of-the-art models specifically designed for either regular or irregular
467   time series. FlexTSF’s ability to excel in both few-shot and zero-shot settings highlights its versatility as a
468   foundation model. In future work, the impact of pre-training on datasets with larger scale could be further
469   explored to push the boundaries of foundation models in time series forecasting.

      Then the question follows, can we maximize log pθ (X ∗ | Z0 ) by maximizing log pθ (X + | Z0 )? The answer
      is Yes, with some presumption. The fundamental assumption of all time series forecasting models is that
651
      future values X + can be predicted based on past values X . To achieve this, researchers often further
652
      assume that X and X + follow the same distribution. This distribution can be explicit, such as a Gaussian
653   distribution, which leads to the concept of stationarity, where statistical properties—such as mean, variance,
654   and autocovariance—remain constant over time. Alternatively, the distribution can be implicit or unknown,
655   in which case it can be learned by neural networks. In this work, we adopt the same assumption. From this
656   perspective, if a neural network is good at modeling X + , then it is good at modeling X ∗ . Modeling either can
657   be used to train a neural network. We chose to model X + because it is more efficient.

721
722   A.3.1    T RANSFORMERS FOR T IME S ERIES F ORECASTING
723
724
      Time series forecasting has been a longstanding research area, evolving from traditional statistical approaches
      such as ARIMA (Box & Jenkins, 1970) and GARCH (Bollerslev, 1986) to modern deep learning models
725
      based on RNNs (Salinas et al., 2020), CNNs (Borovykh et al., 2018), and Transformers (Zhou et al., 2021).
726
      In recent years, the powerful sequential modeling capabilities of Transformers have led to the development
727   of numerous Transformer-based models (Li et al., 2019; Zhou et al., 2021; Wu et al., 2021; Zhou et al.,
728   2022; Liu et al., 2021), with an emphasis on reducing the computational complexity of the original attention
729   mechanism to enhance the feasibility of long-term time series forecasting. Notably, PatchTST (Nie et al.,
730   2023) represents a significant advancement by employing patch-level representations instead of processing
731   individual records at each timestamp. This strategy has become a cornerstone, which has proven effective in
732   capturing complex temporal patterns and improving forecasting accuracy.
733
734   A.3.2    F OUNDATION M ODELS FOR T IME S ERIES F ORECASTING
735
      Building on the remarkable successes of large language models (LLMs) across various domains, several
736
      studies have attempted to adapt LLMs to the domain of time series forecasting. PromptCast (Xue & Salim,
737   2023) transforms numerical input into prompts, and frames the forecasting task in a conversational manner.
738   GPT4TS (Zhou et al., 2023) and Chronos (Ansari et al., 2024) finetune GPT-2 (Radford et al., 2019) and
739   T5 (Raffel et al., 2020) by directly training them on time series data for respective dataset or applying time
740   series tokenization. Time-LLM (Jin et al., 2024) is a reprogramming method that aligns input time series data
741   with text prototypes, and transforms them into prefix prompts to augment LLM’s ability to reason with time
742   series data. TEMPO (Cao et al., 2024) decomposes time series into trend, seasonal, and residual components
743   and designs prompts for distribution adaptation. TEST (Sun et al., 2024) employs contrastive learning to
744   align time series representations with the language space of LLM. More related to our model is ISTS-PLM
745
      (Zhang et al., 2024), which adapts LLM to handle irregularly sampled time series.
746
747

750   practices (Weerakody et al., 2021). In recent years, substantial progress has been made in developing models
751   to handle irregular time series, as demonstrated by works such as GRU-D (Che et al., 2018), Latent-ODE

769   A.4      I LLUSTRATION OF T HE T RAINING P ROCESS
770
771   As illustrated in Figure 4, to simplify the description, let’s assume we have one variable in the time series,
772   the patch length is 1, and observations are collected at irregular timestamps. In the supervised training or
773   inference phase, each forward pass generates the next patch, which is then used as input to predict subsequent
774   values until the entire forecast horizon is populated. During unsupervised pre-training, instead of using the
775   full sequence, a sub-sequence is randomly selected at each iteration. First, a starting position is randomly
776
      determined, followed by the random selection of the sub-sequence length. The goal of pre-training is to
      predict the observations that follow the sub-sequence.
