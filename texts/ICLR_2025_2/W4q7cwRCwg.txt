009                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011               The effectiveness of most graph neural networks is largely attributed to the message-
012               passing mechanism. Despite the significant success in homophilic graphs (i.e.,
013               similar nodes are connected by edges), message-passing mechanism in heterophilic
014               graphs (i.e., dissimilar nodes are connected by edges) is still challenging. Due
015               to the existence of low-order but dissimilar neighbor nodes in a path, messages
016               from similar but high-order neighbor nodes are often weakened. In this paper,
017
                  firstly, we conduct both theoretical and empirical analysis of the layer-by-layer
                  local nature of the message-passing mechanism. Then, we propose a novel GloMP-
018
                  GNN for heterophilic graphs by comprehensively introducing global insights into
019
                  the message-passing mechanism. 1) During the message propagation phase, the
020               global insight is introduced from the perspective of graph structure. We design
021               a structure-based global propagation strategy, where messages can be effectively
022               propagated with the bridge of virtual edges between a global virtual node and
023               graph nodes. Moreover, a global edge adaption approach is included to aggregate
024               messages with adaptive edge weight adjustment. 2) During the feature updating
025               phase, the global insight is introduced with a feature-augmented compensatory
026               updating method. Through a multi-view feature updating mechanism, the node
027               feature representation can be effectively augmented by compensating the weak-
028
                  ened message from different views. Finally, we conduct extensive experimental
                  evaluations on eight datasets, which demonstrate the superiority of our proposed
029
                  GloMP-GNN. As broader impacts, GloMP-GNN consistently performs well across
030
                  multiple layers and also effectively prevents the over-smoothing problem. Codes
031               are available on Github1 .

034   1       I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Graph Neural Network (GNN) has emerged as an important method for graph representation learning,
037   which have been widely used across various fields, such as social network analysis Huang et al.
038   (2024); Yang et al. (2022), bioinformatics Zhang et al. (2024); Liu et al. (2024), and financial risk
039   assessment Wang et al. (2023); Qian et al. (2024). The effectiveness of most GNNs is largely
040   attributed to the message-passing mechanism Gilmer et al. (2017a), a prevalent paradigm that
041   aggregates information from neighbor nodes to update the representation of nodes. Despite the
042
      significant success in homophilic graphs (i.e., similar nodes are connected by edges), message-
      passing mechanism in heterophilic graphs (i.e., dissimilar nodes are connected by edges) is still
043
      challenging. Due to the existence of low-order but dissimilar neighbor nodes in a path, messages
044
      from similar but high-order neighbor nodes are often weakened.
045
046   To tackle the challenges posed by heterophilic graphs, several advanced methods have been developed
047   to enhance the message-passing mechanism Zheng et al. (2022); Luan et al. (2024). Approaches
048
      like blending high-order neighbors Zhu et al. (2020); Song et al. (2023); Wang & Derr (2021) and
      identifying potential neighbor nodes Pei et al. (2019); Suresh et al. (2021) aim to expand the effective
049
      neighborhood, but they may also amplify intermediate layers, introducing noise and over-reliance on
050
      irrelevant information. Other strategies focus on optimizing message aggregation, such as adaptive
051   message aggregation Yan et al. (2022), layer-specific weight learning Chien et al. (2020), and diverse
052   aggregation schemes Luan et al. (2022); Maurya et al. (2022); Du et al. (2022). Additionally,

064   spectral methods differentiate between distinct-class neighbors using signed messages to capture high-
065   frequency signals Yang et al. (2021); Bo et al. (2021). With continuous efforts, previous methods have
066   alleviated the problem of the message-passing mechanism from different perspectives and achieved
067   remarkable progress on heterophilic graphs.
068   However, few studies have fundamentally pointed out and solved the underlying problem, which
069   is largely caused by the layer-by-layer localized nature of the current message-passing framework.
070   Unlike these methods, we first theoretically and empirically analyze the localized layer-by-layer
071   nature of the message-passing mechanism. Along this line, we propose to address the problem by
072   introducing global insights into the message-passing mechanism. As illustrated in Figure 1, on
073   heterophilic graphs, the message from similar but high-order neighbor nodes is often weakened by
074   low-order dissimilar neighbor nodes in conventional GNN. Graph Transformer Shi et al. (2021)
075   alleviates the issue by establishing global dependencies between nodes with fully-connected edges,
076
      but this will also greatly increase the extra quadratic computational complexity. Moreover, recent
      studies also reveal the over-globalizing problem Xing et al. (2024) in Graph Transformers with
077
      fully-connected edges. Unlike Graph Transformer, inspired by the concept of virtual node Gilmer
078
      et al. (2017b), we introduce global insights on heterophilic graphs by establishing virtual edges
079   through a global virtual node, with only linear extra complexity.
080
081   To tackle the localized layer-by-layer nature of the message-passing mechanism, in this paper, we
082
      comprehensively introduce global insights into conventional message-passing mechanism and propose
      a novel Global Message-Passing Graph Neural Network (GloMP-GNN) for heterophilic graphs. To
083
      be concrete, the global insights of GloMP-GNN are reflected in two aspects. 1) During the message
084
      propagation phase, the global insight is introduced with a structure-based global propagation (SGP)
085   strategy from the perspective of graph structure. By adding a global virtual node, messages between
086   similar but high-order neighbor nodes can be effectively propagated with the bridge of virtual edges
087   between the virtual node and graph nodes. Moreover, for redundant and noisy edges, a global
088   edge adaption approach is included in SGP to adaptively aggregate messages by adjusting related
089   edge weights; 2) During the feature updating phase, the global insight is introduced with a feature-
090   augmented compensatory updating (FCU) method from the perspective of node feature. Through a
091   multi-view feature updating mechanism, the node feature representation can be effectively augmented
092   by compensating the weakened message from different views. The main contributions of our work
093
      are summarized as follows:
094
              • We theoretically and empirically analyze the localized layer-by-layer nature of message-
095
                passing mechanisms. By comprehensively introducing global insights from both structure
096             and feature perspectives, we propose GloMP-GNN with a global message-passing mecha-
097             nism for heterophilic graphs.
098
099
              • We propose a structure-based global propagation strategy by establishing virtual edges
                between the global virtual node and graph nodes. Moreover, a global edge adaption approach
100
                is included to aggregate messages with adaptive edge weight adjustment. In this way,
101
                messages between similar high-order neighbor nodes can be effectively propagated.
102
103           • We propose a feature-augmented compensatory updating method with multi-view feature up-
104
                dating mechanism. In this way, the node feature representation can be effectively augmented
                by compensating the weakened message from different views.
105
106           • Extensive experimental results on eight datasets demonstrate the superiority of our proposed
107             GloMP-GNN. As broader impacts, GloMP-GNN consistently performs well across multiple
                layers and alleviates the over-smoothing issue.

199
      In such graphs, nodes that are close yet dissimilar might receive lower influence weights, potentially
      weakening the contribution of distant but similar nodes in the same path. Although recent models like
200
      JKNet Xu et al. (2018), GPRGNN Chien et al. (2020) and GCNII Chen et al. (2020) have introduced
201
      residual connections to preserve self-information and jumping links or learning weights separately for
202   each layer, the influence intensities of distant nodes can hardly benefit from this. Since an increase in
203   the weight of a distant layer can affect the weights of intermediate layers, this may introduce noise
204   and an overreliance on intermediate layers that may contain irrelevant or even harmful data. As a
205   result, the influence intensities from high-order neighbors are very small, presenting a challenge in
206   capturing valuable information from distant nodes for heterophilic graphs.

      Inspired by the concept of virtual node Gilmer et al. (2017b); Cai et al. (2023), we add a Global Virtual
238
      Node (GVN) to connect every node in the graph. Then, virtual-connected edges between the GVN
239
      and graph nodes can be established. In this way, messages between similar but high-order neighbor
240   nodes can be effectively propagated with the bridge of virtual edges. Therefore, the following theorem
241   can be formulated:
242
243   Theorem 2 In graph G, if a global virtual node is added to connect every node on the graph, then
244   for any node on the graph, its maximum neighbor order is 2.
245
246   Based on Theorem 2, messages can be effectively and efficiently propagated between similar but
247   high-order neighbor nodes in heterophilic graphs.

499   5   R ELATED W ORK
500
501
      In heterophilic graph neural networks, various strategies have been proposed to enhance effective-
502   ness Zheng et al. (2022); Zhu et al. (2023); Khoshraftar & An (2024). A central approach is ego-
503   and neighbor-embedding separation, effectively employed by models such as H2 GCN Zhu et al.
504   (2020) and FSGNN Maurya et al. (2022). Building on this, H2 GCN and TDGNN Wang & Derr
505   (2021) aggregate higher-order neighborhood information across layers. Other methods focus on
506   aggregation strategies, like OrderedGNN Song et al. (2023), which aligns the hierarchy of rooted
507   trees with neuron order, adaptive channel mixing in ACM-GNN Luan et al. (2022), and gated kernels
508   in GBK-GNN Du et al. (2022). While these methods enhance the internal structures of GNNs, models
509   like Geom-GCN Pei et al. (2019) and WRGNN Suresh et al. (2021) refine the message-passing
510
      mechanisms for heterophilic graphs Qiu et al. (2024); Pan & Kang (2023), though they often involve
      complex, dataset-specific designs, limiting their generalizability. Additionally, spectral methods
511
      like GPR-GNN Chien et al. (2020), BernNet He et al. (2021), JocabiConv Wang & Zhang (2022),
512
      ALT Xu et al. (2023), and FAGCN Bo et al. (2021) use signed messages to capture high-frequency
513   signals. However, these methods may suffer from the “negative times negative equals positive” effect,
514   which is problematic for multi-class heterophilic graphs Liang et al. (2024). More recently, global
515   information has been incorporated into models, including message-passing-based methods Li et al.
516   (2022); Liang et al. (2024), and Graph Transformer-based methods Shi et al. (2021); Rampášek et al.
517   (2022); Chen et al. (2023); Fu et al. (2024). However, these approaches incorporate global context
518   through external mechanisms, such as global coefficient matrices or self-attention, without addressing
519   the inherent limitations of the layer-by-layer structure in the message-passing mechanism.
520   Recent analyses have uncovered issues with several widely-used heterophilic graph datasets Platonov
521   et al. (2023). For instance, datasets like Squirrel and Chameleon suffer from data leakage due to
522   duplicate nodes, while smaller datasets such as Cornell, Texas, and Wisconsin face class imbalance
523   and limited size (fewer than 1K nodes). Additionally, evaluations of heterophilic GNN models on
524   new, larger datasets (10K-50K nodes) revealed that these advanced models often underperform, even
525   lagging behind traditional GNNs like GCN Kipf & Welling (2016), GAT Veličković et al. (2018), and
526   GraphSAGE Hamilton et al. (2017). Thus, there is a need for more profound analysis to understand
527
      the underlying causes and to develop solutions that are more robust and adaptable for real-world
      graph scenarios.

530   6   C ONCLUSION
531
532
      In this paper, we first conducted both theoretical and empirical analysis of the localized layer-by-layer
533
      nature of the message-passing mechanism. Then, we introduced a Global Message-passing Graph
534   Neural Network (GloMP-GNN) for heterophilic graphs. By innovatively integrating a structure-based
535   global propagation and a feature-augmented compensatory update module into the message-passing
536   framework, GloMP-GNN effectively addresses the issue where messages from high-order but similar
537   neighbor nodes are often weakened during propagation. We hope that our work will inspire further
538   research in this direction. In future work, we plan to extend GloMP-GNN to other graph mining
539   tasks, such as graph classification and downstream tasks like anomaly detection, as well as explore
      its potential in handling various types of graph data, such as heterogeneous graphs and hypergraphs.

               graph structure.

      (2) Classic GNN methods:
834
835          • GCN Kipf & Welling (2016) is a semi-supervised graph convolutional network model that
836            learns node representations by aggregating information from neighbors.
837          • GraphSAGE Hamilton et al. (2017) is a framework for inductive representation learning on
838            large graphs based on sampling algorithms.
839
             • GAT Veličković et al. (2018) uses attention mechanisms to weigh neighbor contributions,
840            allowing different neighbors to contribute differently to the node’s new representation.
841
             • GATv2 Brody et al. (2022) improves upon GAT by introducing a more expressive and
842
               flexible dynamic attention mechanism.
843
844   (3) Selective information propagation methods:
845
846          • H2 GCN Zhu et al. (2020) integrates ego and neighbor-embedding separation, and higher-
847            order neighborhoods, showing enhanced performance on heterophilic graphs.
848          • GBK-GNN Du et al. (2022) introduces a bi-kernel feature transformation, capturing both
849            homophily and heterophily properties.
850          • GCNII Chen et al. (2020) is an extension of graph convolutional network with initial
851            residual and identity mapping which can relieve the problem of over-smoothing.
852
             • FSGNN Maurya et al. (2022) is a GNN model that uses “Soft-Selector” for adaptive feature
853            choice and “Hop-Normalization” for improved node classification performance.
854
             • OrderedGNN Song et al. (2023) is a GNN model that aligns the hierarchy of the rooted-tree
855
               of a central node with the ordered neurons in its node representation.
856
857   (4) Graph signal-based methods:
858
859          • GPR-GNN Chien et al. (2020) is a novel GNN architecture that adaptively learns General-
860            ized PageRank (GPR) weights. It can effectively handle both homophily and heterophily
861            and prevents feature over-smoothing.
862          • FAGCN Bo et al. (2021) utilizes a self-gating mechanism to adaptively integrate different
863            signals in message passing, enhancing the adaptability of the model and addressing over-
               smoothing problems in various networks.
