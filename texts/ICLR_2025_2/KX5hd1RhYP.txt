009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Randomized smoothing is a popular approach for providing certified robustness
012            guarantees against adversarial attacks, and has become a very active area of re-
013            search. Over the past years, the average certified radius (ACR) has emerged as
014            the single most important metric for comparing methods and tracking progress in
015            the field. However, in this work, we show that ACR is an exceptionally poor met-
016
               ric for evaluating robustness guarantees provided by randomized smoothing. We
               theoretically show not only that a trivial classifier can have arbitrarily large ACR,
017
               but also that ACR is much more sensitive to improvements on easy samples than
018
               on hard ones. Empirically, we confirm that existing training strategies that im-
019            prove ACR reduce the model’s robustness on hard samples. Further, we show that
020            by focusing on easy samples, we can effectively replicate the increase in ACR.
021            We develop strategies, including explicitly discarding hard samples, reweighing
022            the dataset with certified radius, and extreme optimization for easy samples, to
023            achieve state-of-the-art ACR, although these strategies ignore robustness for the
024            general data distribution. Overall, our results suggest that ACR has introduced a
025            strong undesired bias to the field, and better metrics are required to holistically
026            evaluate randomized smoothing.

      Adversarial robustness, namely the ability of a model to resist arbitrary small perturbations to its in-
031   put, is a critical property for deploying machine learning models in security-sensitive applications.
032   Due to the incompleteness of adversarial attacks which try to construct a perturbation that manipu-
033   lates the model (Athalye et al., 2018), certified defenses have been proposed to provide robustness
034   guarantees. While deterministic certified defenses (Gowal et al., 2018; Mirman et al., 2018; Shi
035   et al., 2021; Müller et al., 2023; Mao et al., 2023; 2024a; Palma et al., 2023; Balauca et al., 2024) in-

038   tified defense is Randomized Smoothing (RS) (Lécuyer et al., 2019; Cohen et al., 2019), which
039
      computes the maximum certified radius for every input given the accuracy of a base model on noisy
      inputs pA .

      Main Contributions Our key contributions are:
055
056       • We theoretically prove that with a large enough certification budget, ACR of a trivial classifier
057         can be arbitrarily large, and that with the certification budget commonly used in practice, an
058         improvement on easy inputs contributes much more to ACR than on hard inputs, more than
059         1000x in the extreme case (§4.1 and §4.2).
060       • We empirically compare RS training strategies to Gaussian training and show that all current
061         RS training strategies are actually reducing the accuracy on hard inputs where pA is relatively
062         small, and only focus on easy inputs where pA is very close to 1 to increase ACR (§4.3).
063         Figure 1 conceptually visualizes this effect.

067         push pA extremely close to 1 for easy inputs via adversarial noise selection. With these simple
068         modifications to Gaussian training, which do not optimize robustness for the general data

076   2    R ELATED W ORK
077
078   Randomized Smoothing (RS) is a defense against adversarial attacks that provides certified robust-
079   ness guarantees (Lécuyer et al., 2019; Cohen et al., 2019). However, to achieve strong certified
080   robustness, special training strategies tailored to RS are essential. Gaussian training, which adds
081   Gaussian noise to the original input, is the most common strategy, as it naturally aligns with RS
082
      (Cohen et al., 2019). Salman et al. (2019) propose to add adversarial attacks to Gaussian training,
      and Li et al. (2019) propose a regularization to control the stability of the output. Salman et al.
083
      (2020) further shows that it is possible to exploit a pretrained non-robust classifier to achieve strong
084
      RS certified robustness with input denoising. Afterwards, Average Certified Radius (ACR), the
085   average of RS certified radius over the dataset, is commonly used to evaluate RS training: Zhai
086   et al. (2020) propose an attack-free mechanism that directly maximizes certified radii; Jeong & Shin
087   (2020) propose a regularization to improve the prediction consistency; Jeong et al. (2021) propose
088   to calibrate the confidence of smoothed classifier; Horváth et al. (2022) propose to use ensembles
089   as the base classifier to reduce output variance; Vaishnavi et al. (2022) apply knowledge transfer on
090   the base classifier; Jeong et al. (2023) distinguishes hard and easy inputs and apply different loss
091   for each class. While they all improve ACR, this work shows that ACR is a poor metric for robust-
092   ness, and that these training algorithms all introduce undesired side effects. This work is the first to
093
      question the development of RS training strategies evaluated with ACR and suggests new metrics as
      alternatives.

096   3    BACKGROUND
097
098   In this section, we briefly introduce the background required for this work.
099
      Adversarial Robustness is the ability of a model to resist arbitrary small perturbations to its input.
100
      Formally, given an input set S(x) and a model f , f is adversarially robust within S(x) iff for any
101   x1 , x2 ∈ S(x), f (x1 ) = f (x2 ). In this work, we focus on the L2 neighborhood of an input, i.e.,
102   S(x) := Bϵ (x) = {x′ | ∥x − x′ ∥2 ≤ ϵ} for a given ϵ ≥ 0. For a given (x, y) from the dataset
103   (X , Y), f is robustly correct iff ∀x′ ∈ S(x), f (x′ ) = y.
104
105   Randomized Smoothing constructs a smooth classifier fˆ(x) given a base classifier f , defined
106   as follows: fˆ(x) := arg maxc∈Y Pδ∼N (0,σ2 I) (f (x + δ) = c). Intuitively, the smooth clas-
107   sifier assigns the label with maximum probability in the neighborhood of the input. With this
      formulation, Cohen et al. (2019) proves that fˆ is adversarially robust within BR(x,pA ) (x) when

109   and pA := maxc∈Y Pδ∼N (0,σ2 I) (f (x + δ) = c) is the probability of the most likely class. Aver-
110

      4     W EAKNESS OF ACR AND THE C ONSEQUENCE
126
127
      We now first theoretically show that ACR can be arbitrarily large for a trivial classifier, assuming
      enough budget for certification (§4.1). We then demonstrate that with a realistic certification bud-
128
      get, an improvement on easy samples could contribute more than 1000 times to ACR than on hard
129
      samples (§4.2). Finally, we empirically show that due to the above weakness of ACR, all current RS
130   training strategies evaluated with ACR reduce the accuracy on hard samples and only focus on easy
131   samples (§4.3), improving ACR at the cost of performance on hard samples.

193   4.3   RS T RAINING T RADES OFF H ARD S AMPLES FOR ACR
194
195
      We have shown that ACR strongly prefers easy samples in §4.2. However, since ACR is not differ-
      entiable with respect to the model parameters because it is based on counting, RS training strategies
196
      usually do not directly apply ACR as the training loss. Instead, they optimize various surrogate ob-
197
      jectives, and finally evaluate the model with ACR. Thus, it is unclear whether and to what extent the
198   design of training algorithms is affected by the ACR metric. We now empirically quantify the effect,
199   confirming the theoretical analysis. Specifically, we show that SOTA training strategies reduce pA
200   of hard samples and put more weight (measured by gradient norm) on easy samples compared to
201   Gaussian training.
202
      Figure 3 shows the empirical cumulative distribution of pA for models trained with SOTA algorithms
203
      and Gaussian training. Clearly, for various σ, SOTA algorithms have higher density than Gaussian
204   training at pA close to zero and one. While they gain more ACR due to the improvement on easy
205   samples, hard samples are consistently underrepresented in the final model compared to Gaussian
206   training. As a result, Gaussian training has higher P(pA ≥ 0.5) (clean accuracy), and SOTA algo-
207   rithms exceed Gaussian training only when pA passes a certain threshold, i.e., when the certified
208   radius is relatively large. This is problematic in practice, indicating that ACR does not properly
209   measure the model’s ability. For example, a face recognition model could have a high ACR but
210   consistently refuse to learn some difficult faces, which is not acceptable in real-world applications.

214   larger gradients contribute more to training and thus are more important for the final model. As

      7    D ISCUSSION
455
      We have shown that ACR does not uniformly represent the robustness of a model for different data.
456
      Therefore, the field has to seek better alternative metrics to evaluate the robustness of models under
457
      RS. We suggest to use certified accuracy at various radii as a more informative metric, which can be
458   easily computed with the same certification budget as ACR. In addition, since achieving maximum
459   certified accuracy at all radii is a challenging task, we should allow algorithms to customize models
460   for different radii, including the certification hyperparameter σ. This is similar to the practice in
461   deterministic certified training (Gowal et al., 2018; Mirman et al., 2018; Shi et al., 2021; Müller
462   et al., 2023; Mao et al., 2023; 2024a;b; Palma et al., 2023; Balauca et al., 2024).
463
      While our modifications presented in §5 are not designed to improve robustness for the general
464   data distribution, they show effectiveness in increasing certified accuracy at large radius. Other
465   existing algorithms show similar effects. Therefore, it is important to note that the field has indeed
466   made progress over the years. However, new metrics which considers robustness more uniformly
467   should be developed to evaluate RS, and algorithms that outperform generally at various radii are
468   encouraged. We hope this work can inspire future research in this direction.

      8    C ONCLUSION
471
472
      This work rigorously demonstrates that Average Certified Radius (ACR) is a poor metric for Ran-
473   domized Smoothing (RS). Theoretically, we prove that ACR of a trivial classifier can be arbitrarily
474   large, and that an improvement on easy inputs contributes much more to ACR than on hard inputs.
475   Empirically, we show that all state-of-the-art (SOTA) strategies reduce the accuracy on hard inputs
476   and only focus on easy inputs to increase ACR. Based on these novel insights, we develop strategies
477   to amplify Gaussian training by reweighing the training data to focus only on easy inputs. Specif-
478   ically, we discard hard inputs during training, weight the dataset with their contribution to ACR,
479   and apply extreme optimization for easy inputs via adversarial noise selection. With these intuitive
480   modifications to the simple Gaussian training, we replicate the effect of SOTA training algorithms
481
      and achieve a new SOTA ACR. Overall, our results suggest the need for evaluating RS training with
      better metrics.

628   lies in how the noise is updated based on the gradient. For l2 PGD, the noise is updated as follows:

630                          δ ∗ = δ + ϵ · ∇δ L(f (x + δ), y)/∥∇δ L(f (x + δ), y)∥2 ,
