                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
               OpenAI’s Sora highlights the potential of video generation for developing world
010            models that adhere to fundamental physical laws. However, the ability of video
011            generation models to discover such laws purely from visual data without human
012            priors can be questioned. A world model learning the true law should give pre-
013            dictions robust to nuances and correctly extrapolate on unseen scenarios. In this
014            work, we evaluate across three key scenarios: in-distribution, out-of-distribution,
015            and combinatorial generalization. We developed a 2D simulation testbed for object
016            movement and collisions to generate videos deterministically governed by one or
017            more classical mechanics laws. This provides an unlimited supply of data for large-
018
               scale experimentation and enables quantitative evaluation of whether the generated
               videos adhere to physical laws. We trained diffusion-based video generation mod-
019
               els to predict object movements based on initial frames. Our scaling experiments
020
               show perfect generalization within the distribution, measurable scaling behavior for
021            combinatorial generalization, but failure in out-of-distribution scenarios. Further
022            experiments reveal two key insights about the generalization mechanisms of these
023            models: (1) the models fail to abstract general physical rules and instead exhibit
024            “case-based” generalization behavior, i.e., mimicking the closest training example;
025            (2) when generalizing to new cases, models are observed to prioritize different
026            factors when referencing training data: color > size > velocity > shape. Our study
027            suggests that scaling alone is insufficient for video generation models to uncover
028            fundamental physical laws, despite its role in Sora’s broader success.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Foundation models (Bommasani et al., 2021) have emerged remarkable capabilities by scaling the
033   model and data to an unprecedented scale (Brown, 2020; Kaplan et al., 2020). As an example,
034   OpenAI’s Sora (Brooks et al., 2024) not only generates high-fidelity and surreal videos, but also has
035   sparked a new surge of interest in studying world models (Yang et al., 2023).
036            “Scaling video generation models is a promising path towards building general
037            purpose simulators of the physical world.” — Sora Report (Brooks et al., 2024)
038
039
      World simulators are receiving broad attention from robotics (Yang et al., 2023) and autonomous
      driving (Hu et al., 2023) for the ability to generate realistic data and accurate simulations. These
040
      models are required to comprehend fundamental physical laws to produce data that extends beyond
041
      the training corpus and to guarantee precise simulation. However, it remains an open question whether
042   video generation can discover such rules merely by observing videos, as Sora does. We aim to provide
043   a systematic study to understand the critical role and limitation of scaling in physical law discovery.
044
045
      It is challenging to determine whether a video model has learned a law instead of merely memorizing
      the data. Since the model’s internal knowledge is inaccessible, we can only infer the model’s
046
      understanding by examining its predictions on unseen scenarios, i.e., its generalization ability. We
047
      propose a categorization (Figure 1) for comprehensive evaluation based on the relationship between
048   training and testing data in this paper. In-distribution (ID) generalization assumes that training
049   and testing data are independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.). Out-of-distribution (OOD)
050   generalization, on the other hand, refers to the model’s performance on testing data that come
051   from a different distribution than the training data, particularly when latent parameters fall outside
052   the range seen during training. Human-level physical reasoning can easily extrapolate OOD and
053   predict physical processes without encountering the exact same scenario before. Additionally, we
      also examine a special OOD capacity called combinatorial generalization, which assesses whether

      Moreover, real-world videos typically contain complex, non-rigid objects and motions, which present
065
      significant challenges for quantitative evaluation and even human validation. The rich textures and
066
      appearances in such videos can act as confounding factors, distracting the model from focusing on
067   the underlying physics. To mitigate these issues, we specifically focus on classical mechanics and
068   develop a 2D simulator with objects represented by simple geometric shapes. Each video depicts
069   the motion or collision of these 2D objects, governed entirely by one or two fundamental physical
070   laws, given the initial frames. This simulator allows us to generate large-scale datasets to support the
071   scaling of video generation models. Additionally, we have developed a tool to infer internal states
072   (e.g., the position and size) of each object in the generated video from pixels. This enables us to
073   establish quantitative evaluation metrics for physical law discovery.
074   We begin by investigating how scaling video generation models affects ID and OOD generalization.
075   We select three fundamental physical laws for simulation: uniform linear motion of a ball, perfectly
076   elastic collision between two balls, and parabolic motion of a ball. We scale the dataset from 30K
077   to 3 million examples and increase the video diffusion model’s parameters from 22M to 310M.
078   Consistently, we observe that the model achieves near-perfect ID generalization across all tasks.
079   However, the OOD generalization error does not improve with increased data and model size,
080   revealing the limitations of scaling video generation models in handling OOD data. For combinatorial
081
      generalization, we design an environment that involves multiple objects undergoing free fall and
      collisions to study their interactions. Every time, four objects from eight are selected to create a video.
082
      In total, 70 combinations (C84 ) are possible. We use 60 of them for training and 10 for testing. We
083
      train models by varying the number of training data from 600K to 6M. We manually evaluate the
084   generated test samples by labeling them as “abnormal” if the video looks physically implausible. The
085   results demonstrate that scaling the data substantially reduces the percentage of abnormal cases, from
086   67% to 10%. This suggests that scaling is critical for improving combinatorial generalization.
087
      Our empirical analysis reveals two intriguing properties of the generalization mechanism in video
088
      generation models. First, these models can be easily biased by "deceptive" examples from the training
089
      set, leading them to generalize in a "case-based" manner under certain conditions. This phenomenon,
090   also observed in large language models (Hu et al., 2024), describes a model’s tendency to reference
091   similar training cases when solving new tasks. For instance, consider a video model trained on data of
092   a high-speed ball moving in uniform linear motion. If data augmentation is performed by horizontally
093   flipping the videos, thereby introducing reverse-direction motion, the model may generate a scenario
094   where a low-speed ball reverses direction after the initial frames, even though this behavior is not
095   physically correct. Second, we explore how different data attributes compete during the generalization
096   process. For example, if the training data for uniform motion consists of red balls and blue squares,
097   the model may transform a red square into a ball immediately after the conditioning frames. This
098
      behavior suggests that the model prioritizes color over shape. Our pairwise analysis reveals the
      following prioritization hierarchy: color > size > velocity > shape. This ranking could explain why
099
      current video generation models often struggle with maintaining object consistency. We hope these
100
      findings provide valuable insights for future research in video generation and world models.

      2.2    V IDEO G ENERATION M ODEL
128
      In this paper, we focus exclusively on video generation models, leaving discussions on other modeling
129   methods for future work. Following Sora (Brooks et al., 2024), we adopt the Variational Auto-
130   Encoder (VAE) and DiT architectures for video generation. The VAE compresses videos into latent
131   representations both spatially and temporally, while the DiT models the denoising process. This
132   approach shows strong scalability and achieves promising results in generating high-quality videos.
133
      VAE Model. We employ a (2+1)D-VAE to project videos into a latent space. Starting with the
134
      SD1.5-VAE structure, we extend it into a spatiotemporal autoencoder using 3D blocks (Yu et al.,
135   2023b). All parameters of the (2+1)D-VAE are pretrained on high-quality image and video data to
136   maintain strong appearance modeling while enabling motion modeling. More details are provided
137   in Appendix A.3.1. In this paper, we fix the pretrained VAE encoder and use it as a video compressor.
138   Results in Appendix A.3.2 confirm the VAE’s ability to accurately encode and decode the physical
139   event videos. This allows us to focus solely on training the diffusion model to learn the physical laws.
140
      Diffusion model. Given the compressed latent representation from the VAE model, we flatten it
141   into a sequence of spacetime patches, as transformer tokens. Notably, self-attention is applied to the
142   entire spatio-temporal sequence of video tokens, without distinguishing between spatial and temporal
143   dimensions. For positional embedding, a 3D variant of RoPE (Su et al., 2024) is adopted. As stated
144   in Sec.2.1, our video model is conditioned on the first c frames. The c-frame video is zero-padded to
145   the same length as the full physical video. We also introduce a binary mask “video” by setting the
146   value of the first c frames to 1, indicating those frames are the condition inputs. The noise, condition
147   and mask videos are concatenated along the channel dimension to form the final input to the model.

149   2.3    O N THE V ERIFICATION OF L EARNED L AWS
150
151   Suppose we have a video generation model learned based on the above formulation. How do we
152   determine if the underlying physical law has been discovered? A well-established law describes
153
      the behavior of the natural world, e.g., how objects move and interact. Therefore, a video model
      incorporating true physical laws should be able to withstand experimental verification, producing
154
      reasonable predictions under any circumstances, which demonstrates the model’s generalization
155
      ability. To comprehensively evaluate this, we consider the following categorization of generalization
156   (see Figure 1) within the scope of this paper: 1) In-distribution (ID) generalization describes the
157   setting where training data and testing data are from the same distribution. In our case, both training
158   and testing data follow the same law and are located in the same domain. 2) A human who has
159   learned a physical law can easily extrapolate to scenarios that have never been observed before. This
160   ability is referred to as out-of-distribution (OOD) generalization. Although it sounds challenging,
161   this evaluation is necessary as it indicates whether a model can learn principled rules from data.
      3) Moreover, there is a situation between ID and OOD, which has more practical value. We call


                                                               3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      this combinatorial generalization, representing scenarios where every "concept" or object has been
163   observed during training, but not their every combination. It examines a model’s ability to effectively
164   combine relevant information from past experiences in novel ways. A similar concept has been
165   explored in LLMs (Riveland & Pouget, 2024), which demonstrated that models can excel at linguistic
166   instructing tasks by recombining previously learned components, without task-specific experience.

250   4                  C OMBINATORIAL G ENERALIZATION
251
252   In Section 3, video generation models failed to reason in OOD scenarios. This is understand-
253   able—deriving precise physical laws from data is difficult for both humans and models. For example,
254   it took scientists centuries to formulate Newton’s three laws of motion. However, even a child can in-
255   tuitively predict outcomes in everyday situations by combining elements from past experiences. This
256   ability to combine known information to predict new scenarios is called combinatorial generalization.
257   In this section, we evaluate the combinatorial abilities of diffusion-based video models.
258

      5     D EEPER A NALYSIS
325
326   In this section, we aim to investigate the generalization mechanism of a video generation model,
327   through systemic experimental designs. Based on the findings, we try to identify certain patterns in
328   combinatorial generalization that might be helpful in harnessing or prompting the models.
329
330   5.1       U NDERSTANDING G ENERALIZATION FROM I NTERPOLATION AND E XTRAPOLATION
331
332   The generalization ability of a model roots from its interpolation and extrapolation capability (Xu
333   et al., 2020; Balestriero et al., 2021). In this section, we design experiments to explore the limits
334
      of these abilities for a video generation model. We design datasets which delibrately leave out
      some latent values, i.e. velocity. After training, we test model’s prediction on both seen and unseen
335
      scenarios. We mainly focus on uniform motion and collision processes.
336
337   Uniform Motion. We create a series of training sets, where a certain range of velocity is absent. Each
338   set contains 200K videos to ensure fairness. As shown in Figure 5 (1)-(2), with a large gap in the
339   training set, the model tends to generate videos where the velocity is either high or low to resemble
340
      training data when initial frames show middle-range velocities. We find that the video generation
      model’s OOD accuracy is closely related to the size of the gap, as seen in Figure 5 (3), when the gap
341
      is reduced, the model correctly interpolates for most of OOD data. Moreover, as shown in Figure 5
342
      (4) and (5), when a subset of the missing range is reintroduced (without increasing data amount), the
343   model exhibits stronger interpolation abilities.
344
345   Collision. It involves multiple variables, and is more challenging since the model has to learn a
346
      two-dimensional non-linear function. Specifically, we exclude one or more square regions from the
      training set of initial velocities for two balls and then assess the velocity prediction error after the
347
      collision. For each velocity point, we sample a grid of radius parameters to generate multiple video
348
      cases and compute the average error. As shown in Figure 6 (1)-(2), an interesting phenomenon occurs.
349   The video generation model’s extrapolation error demonstate an intringuing discrepancy among OOD

404   5.3   H OW D OES D IFFUSION M ODEL R ETRIEVE DATA ?
405
406   We aim to investigate the ways a video model performs case matching—identifying close training
407   examples for a given test input. We use uniform linear motion for this study. Specifically, we compare
408   four attributes, i.e., color, shape, size, and velocity, in a pairwise manner. Through comparisons, we
409
      seek to determine the model’s preference for relying on specific attributes in case matching.
410   Every attribute has two disjoint sets of values. For each pair of attributes, there are four types of
411   combinations. We use two combinations for training and the remaining two for testing. For example,
412   we compare color and shape in Figure 8 (1). Videos of red balls and blue squares with the same
413   range of size and velocity are used for training. At test time, a blue ball changes shape into a square
414   immediately after the condition frames, while a red square transforms into a ball. We observed
415
      no exceptions on 1,400 test cases, showing that the model prioritizes color over shape for case
      matching. A similar trend is observed in the comparisons of size vs. shape and velocity vs. shape, as
416
      illustrated in Figure 8 (2)-(3), indicating that shape is the least prioritized attribute. This suggests that
417
      diffusion-based video models inherently favor other attributes over shape, which may explain why
418   current open-set video generation models usually struggle with shape preservation.
419
420   The other three attribute pairs are presented in Figure 9. For the continuous attributes of velocity and
421
      size, we selected two distinct values at the extreme boundaries to create binary comparisons: 1.0 and
      4.0 for velocity, and 0.7 and 1.4 for size. For the comparison of color vs. size, the training set consists
422
      of: (1) red, small balls and (2) blue, large balls. During evaluation, across 202 evaluation cases, size
423
      consistently transformed while color remained fixed, clearly demonstrating that color > size. Similar
424   observations are evident in Figure 9 (2) and (3), leading to the conclusion that color > velocity and
425   size > velocity. Based on the above analysis, we establish the prioritization order as color > size >
426   velocity > shape.

      Attribute composition. As shown in Figure 14 (1)-(2), certain attribute pairs—such as velocity and
471
      size, or color and size—exhibit some degree of combinatorial generalization.
472
473   Spatial composition. As given by Figure 11 (left side) in the appendix, the training data contains two
474   distinct types of physical events. One type involves a blue square moving horizontally with a constant
475   velocity while a red ball remains stationary. In contrast, the other type depicts a red ball moving
476
      toward and then bouncing off a wall while the blue square remains stationary. At test time, when the
      red ball and the blue square are moving simultaneously, the learned model is able to generate the
477
      scenario where the red ball bounces off the wall while the blue square continues its uniform motion.
478
479   Temporal combination. As illustrated on the right side of Figure 11, when the training data includes
480   distinct physical events—half featuring two balls colliding without bouncing and the other half
481   showing a red ball bouncing off a wall—the model learns to combine these events temporally.
482
      Consequently, during evaluation, when the balls collide near the wall, the model accurately predicts
      the collision and then determines that the blue ball will rebound off the wall with unchanged velocity.
483
484   With these attribute, spatial, and temporal combinatorial patterns, the video generation model can
485   identify basic physical events in the training set and combine them across attributes, time, and space
      to generate videos featuring complex chains of physical events.

      5.5   I S V IDEO S UFFICIENT FOR C OMPLETE P HYSICS M ODELING ?
487
488   For a video generation model to function as a world model, the visual representation must provide
489   sufficient information for complete physics modeling. In our experiments, we found that visual ambi-
490   guity leads to significant inaccuracies in fine-grained physics modeling. For example, in Figure 10, it
491   is difficult to determine if a ball can pass through a gap based on vision alone when the size difference
492   is at the pixel level, leading to visually plausible but incorrect results. Similarly, visual ambiguity in a
493   ball’s horizontal position relative to a block can result in different outcomes. These findings suggest
494
      that relying solely on visual representations, may be inadequate for accurate physics modeling.

505   6     R ELATED W ORKS
506   Video generation. Open-set video generation is mostly based on diffusion models (Rombach et al.,
507   2022; Ho et al., 2022b;a; He et al., 2024) or auto-regressive models (Yu et al., 2023a;b; Kondratyuk
508   et al., 2023). These models often require a pretrained image or video VAE (Kingma, 2013; Van
509   Den Oord et al., 2017) for data compression to improve computational efficiency. Some approaches
510   leverage pretrained Text-to-Image (T2I) models for zero-shot (Khachatryan et al., 2023; Zhang
511   et al., 2023b) or few-shot (Wu et al., 2023) video generation. Additionally, Image-to-Video(I2V)
512   generation (Zeng et al., 2024; Girdhar et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023a) shows that video quality
513
      improves substantially when conditioned on an image. The Diffusion Transformer (DiT) (Peebles
      & Xie, 2023) demonstrates better scaling behavior than U-Net (Ronneberger et al., 2015) for T2I
514
      generation. Sora (Brooks et al., 2024) leverages the DiT architecture to directly operate on spacetime
515
      patches of video and image latent codes. Our model follows Sora’s architecture and conceptually
516   aligns with I2V generation, relying on image(s) for conditioning instead of text prompts.
517
518   World model. World models (Ha & Schmidhuber, 2018) aim to learn models that can accurately
519
      predict how an environment evolves after some actions are taken. Previously, they often operated in
      an abstracted space and were used in reinforcement learning (Sutton, 2018) to enable planning (Silver
520
      et al., 2016; Schrittwieser et al., 2020) or facilitate policy learning through virtual interactions (Hafner
521
      et al., 2019; 2020). With the advancement of generative models, world models can now directly
522   work with visual observations by employing a general framework of conditioned video generation.
523   For example, in autonomous driving (Hu et al., 2023; Jia et al., 2023; Gao et al., 2024; Zheng et al.,
524   2024), the condition is the driver’s operations, while in robot world models (Yang et al., 2023; Black
525   et al., 2023; 1xw, 2024), the condition is often the control signals. Genie (Bruce et al., 2024) instead
526   recovers the conditions from video games in an unsupervised learning manner. In our physical law
527   discovery setting, it does not require a per-step action/condition since the physical event is determined
528   by the underlying laws once an initial state is specified. See more related works in Appendix A.1.
529
530   7     C ONCLUSION
531
532   Video generation is believed as a promising way towards scalable world models. However, its
533
      capability to learn physical laws from visual observations has not yet been verified. We conducted the
      first systematic study in this area by examining its generalization performance across three typical
534
      scenarios: in-distribution, out-of-distribution (OOD), and combinatorial generalization. The findings
535
      indicate that scaling alone cannot address the OOD problem, although it does enhance performance
536   in other scenarios. Our in-depth analysis suggests that video model generalization relies more on
537   referencing similar training examples rather than learning universal rules. We observed a prioritization
538   order of color > size > velocity > shape in this "case-based" behavior. In conclusion, our study
539   suggests that naively scaling is insufficient for video generation models to discover physical laws.

      A.1    M ORE RELATED WORKS
838   Physical reasoning. It refers to the ability to understand and predict the way objects will interact
839   under certain conditions according to physical laws. Melnik et al. (2023) categorize physical reasoning
840   tasks into passive and interactive tasks. Passive tasks often require the AI to predict certain properties
841   of objects, e.g., materials (Bell et al., 2015; Bouman et al., 2013), physical parameters (Wu et al.,
842   2016), stability of a physical system (Groth et al., 2018), or involve question-answering for the
843   agent to recognize conceptual differences (Weitnauer et al., 2023; Yi et al., 2019), or describe a
844   physical scenario (Ates et al., 2020). For interactive tasks, AI is required to control some objects in
845   the environment to complete certain tasks based on physical commonsense, e.g., solving classical
846
      mechanics puzzles (Bakhtin et al., 2019), flying a bird to reach a target position (Xue et al., 2023),
      and using a tool (Allen et al., 2020). Two works closely related to ours are Girdhar et al. (2020) and
847
      de Silva et al. (2020). Girdhar et al. (2020) introduce a forward prediction model to aid physical
848
      reasoning but do not address what is learned in the prediction model. de Silva et al. (2020) attempt
849   to discover universal physical laws from data with abstracted internal states and human expertise
850   introduced in the dynamic model design. In contrast, we focus on recovering physical laws from raw
851   observation without any human priors, akin to a newborn baby.

      A.3.1     VAE A RCHITECTURE AND P RETRAIN
874
875   We commence with the structure of the SD1.5-VAE, retaining the majority of the original 2D
876   convolution, group normalization, and attention mechanisms on the spatial dimensions. To inflate
877   this structure into a spatial-temporal auto-encoder, we convert the final few 2D downsample blocks of
878   the encoder and the initial few 2D upsample blocks of the decoder into 3D ones, and employ multiple
879   extra 1D layers to enhance temporal modeling. For all the downsample blocks where temporal
880   downsampling is required, we replace all the original 2D downsample layers with re-initialized causal
881   3D downsample layers by adding causal paddings to the head of the frame sequence (Yu et al., 2023b)
882
      and introduce an additional causal 1D convolution layer after the original ResNetBlock. As for the
      decoder part, all the 2D Nearest-Interpolation operations are substituted by a 2D convolution layer
883
      and a channel-to-space transformation, which are specifically initialized to behave precisely the same
884
      as Nearest-Interpolation operations before the first training step. For the discriminator part, we inherit
885   the structure of the original 2D PatchGAN (Isola et al., 2016) discriminator used by SD1.5-VAE, and
886   design a 3D PatchGAN discriminator based on the 2D version. Different from the generator module,
887   we train the group of discriminators from scratch for the consideration of stability. Subsequently, all
888   parameters of the (2+1)D-VAE are jointly trained with high-quality image and video data to preserve
889   the capability of appearance modeling and to enable motion modeling. For the image dataset, we
890   filter data samples from LAION-Aesthetics (Beaumont & Schuhmann, 2022), COYO (Byeon et al.,
891   2022) and DataComp (Gadre et al., 2023) with high aesthetics and clarity to form a high-quality
892   subset. As for the video dataset, we collect a high-quality subset from Vimeo-90K (Xue et al., 2017),
893
      Panda-70M (Wang et al., 2020) and HDVG Wang et al. (2023). In the training process, we train the
      entire structure for 1M steps and only the random resized crop and random horizontal flip are applied
894
      in the data augmentation process.

935

945
946   To evaluate the generated videos, we conducted human evaluations to determine the abnormal ratio,
947   defined as the proportion of videos that violate physical laws as assessed by human intuition. We
948   utilized 60 templates for training and reserved 10 unused templates for evaluation. For the in-template
949   evaluation set, we sampled 2 videos from each of the 60 training templates, forming a total of 120

951   each of the 10 reserved templates, forming a total of 100 videos. We recruited 10 human evaluators
952

953
      evaluators and report the result. Each evaluator received the following instruction: “For each video
954
      shown to you, all objects start with free fall. You should judge whether the physical processes in the
955   video obey physical laws based on your intuition. Select ‘Yes’ if the process adheres to physical laws
956   and ‘No’ otherwise. Please make your judgment based on your first impression”.

       A.5.2    C AN PRETRAINED MODELS LEARN PHYSICAL LAWS ?
1001
1002
       The primary goal of this paper is to answer the fundamental question: “Can a video generation
1003   model learn physical laws?” Existing pretrained models are built on large-scale datasets sourced
1004   from publicly available internet content. However, the extent to which these datasets contain videos
1005   about physical laws or structured physical interactions remains unknown. This lack of transparency
1006   leads to critical concern - contamination of evaluation data: There is a risk that pretrained models
1007   may have been exposed to data similar to our evaluation experiments during training. This potential
1008   overlap undermines the validity of assessing their ability to learn physical reasoning. To address
1009   these concerns, we chose to train a model from scratch, which provides complete control over the
1010   training data. This approach allows us to precisely ensure that the results stem from the model’s actual
1011
       learning process rather than potential pretraining data overlap or contamination. This methodology
       is supported by recent research into the mechanisms of language models, where training relatively
1012
       small models from scratch has proven effective in isolating and analyzing reasoning processes more
1013
       rigorously.
1014
1015   To assess whether the inability to learn physical laws is specific to our setup or extends to open-
1016   source pretrained models, we conducted experiments using the Stable Video Diffusion (SVD) model
1017
       on a representative uniform motion task. We fine-tuned SVD for 300k steps, following the same
       training setup as DiT-B in our experiments. The results, summarized in Table 4, reveal several key
1018
       observations. First, SVD’s VAE demonstrates a very small reconstruction error, indicating that its
1019
       VAE component effectively preserves kinematic information. However, the diffusion component
1020   of SVD, despite pretraining, performs worse than DiT-B in both in-distribution (ID) and out-of-
1021   distribution (OOD) predictions. We attribute this to the considerable gap between the pretraining
1022   dataset (internet videos) and the simplified kinematic test scenarios used in this evaluation. Notably,
1023   the OOD error for SVD is an order of magnitude larger than its ID error, a trend consistent with DiT-B.
1024   This finding reinforces the robustness of our conclusion that pretraining alone does not address the
1025   inability of video generation models to generalize to OOD scenarios, a limitation that persists across
       architectures, including open-source pretrained models like SVD.

       A.6.3             P RINCIPLE B EHIND DATA R ETRIEVAL IN THE D IFFUSION M ODEL
1124
1125   As discussed in Section 5.3, the diffusion model appears to rely more on memorization and case-
1126   based imitation, rather than abstracting universal rules for OOD generalization. The model exhibits a
1127   preference for specific attributes during case matching, with a prioritization order of color > size >
1128   velocity > shape. In this section, we aim to explore the reasoning behind this prioritization.
1129   Since the diffusion model is trained by minimizing the loss associated with predicting VAE latent, we
1130   hypothesize that the prioritization may be related to the distance in VAE latent space (though we use
1131   pixel space here for clearer illustration) between the test conditions and the training set. Intuitively,
1132   when comparing color and shape as in Figure 14 (1), a shape change from a ball to a rectangle results
1133   in minor pixel variation, primarily at the corners. In contrast, a color change from blue to red causes
       a more significant pixel difference. Thus, the model tends to preserve color while allowing shape to


                                                                                                                          21
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1134
       vary. From the perspective of pixel variation, the prioritization of color > size > velocity > shape can
1135   be explained by the extent of pixel change associated with each attribute. Changes in color typically
1136   result in large pixel variations because it affects nearly every pixel across its surface. In contrast,
1137   changes in size modify the number of pixels but do not drastically alter the individual pixels’ values.
1138   Velocity affects pixel positions over time, leading to moderate variation as the object shifts, while
1139   shape changes often involve only localized pixel adjustments, such as at edges or corners. Therefore,
1140   the model prioritizes color because it causes the most significant pixel changes, while shape changes
1141   are less impactful in terms of pixel variation.
1142   To further validate this hypothesis, we designed a variant experiment comparing color and shape, as
1143   shown in Figure 16. In this case, we use a blue ball and a red ring. For the ring to transform into
1144   the ball without changing color, it would need to remove the ring’s external color, turning it into
1145   blank space, and then fill the internal blank space with the ball’s color, resulting in significant pixel
1146   variation. Interestingly, in this scenario, unlike the previous experiments shown in Figure 14 (1), the
1147   prioritization of color > shape does not hold. The red ring can transform into either a red ball or a
1148   blue ring, as demonstrated by the examples. This observation suggests that the model’s prioritization
1149
       may indeed depend on the complexity of the pixel transformations required for each attribute change.
       Future work could explore more precise measurements of these variations in pixel or VAE latent
1150
       space to better understand the model’s training data retrieval process.

1167   A.6.4    FAILURE C ASES IN C OMBINATORIAL G ENERALIZATION
1168
       In Figure 11, we present successful cases of spatial and temporal combinatorial generalization, where
1169   the trained model can combine these two types of events across spatial and temporal dimensions to
1170   generate videos under unseen conditions. However, the model does not always succeed in performing
1171   such compositions, and here we illustrate some failure cases. As shown in Figure 17, when the
1172   training set lacks a red ball in a bounce event, the model sometimes struggles. In the model’s generated
1173   video, the red ball sometimes disappears after a collision. This likely stems from the model’s data
1174   retrieval mechanism: since the training set does not include a red ball in a collision scenario, when
1175   collision happens, the model retrieves similar training cases without the red ball, causing it to vanish
1176   post-collision. In summary, while combinatorial generalization allows the diffusion model to generate
1177
       novel videos by composing spatial and temporal segments from the training set, its reliance on
       data retrieval limits its effectiveness. As a result, the model may produce unrealistic outcomes by
1178
       retrieving and combining segments without understanding the underlying rules.
