009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               Hierarchical Imitation Learning (HIL) is a promising approach for tackling long-
012
               horizon decision-making tasks. While it is a challenging task due to the lack
013
               of detailed supervisory labels for sub-goal learning, and reliance on hundreds
014            to thousands of expert demonstrations. In this work, we introduce SEAL, a
015            novel framework that leverages Large Language Models (LLMs)’s powerful se-
016            mantic and world knowledge for both specifying sub-goal space and pre-labeling
017            states to semantically meaningful sub-goal representations without prior knowl-
018            edge of task hierarchies. SEAL employs a dual-encoder structure, combining
019            supervised LLM-guided sub-goal learning with unsupervised Vector Quantization
020            (VQ) for more robust sub-goal representations. Additionally, SEAL incorporates a
021            transition-augmented low-level planner for improved adaptation to sub-goal tran-
022
               sitions. Our experiments demonstrate that SEAL outperforms state-of-the-art HIL
               methods and LLM-based planning approaches, particularly in settings with small
023
               expert datasets and complex long-horizon tasks.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      The advancement of LLMs brings transformative change to how agents learn to interact and make
030
      decisions (Brohan et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023). LLMs like GPT-4 (Achiam et al., 2023) possess
031   remarkable semantic understanding ability (Liu et al., 2023), human-like reasoning capability (Wei
032   et al., 2022), and rich common sense knowledge (Bubeck et al., 2023), enabling them extracting
033   insights from language instructions to support decision-making agents (Eigner & Händler, 2024).
034
      A promising path towards LLM-assisted decision-making is to improve Deep Reinforcement Learn-
035
      ing (DRL) agents through reward design (Kwon et al., 2023; Ma et al., 2023). Yet DRL often suffers
036
      from sample inefficiency and still requires extensive interactions with the environment particularly
037   for tasks with long planning horizons and sparse rewards, where agents are rewarded only upon task
038   completion (Zhang et al.). Alternatively, Imitation Learning (IL) can be trained to learn generaliz-
039   able policies from expert demonstrations (Schaal, 1996). By learning from successful state-action
040   pairs, IL avoids the sample-expensive exploration and exploitation required in DRL. While IL per-
041   formance can still be limited in long-horizon tasks due to compounding errors with accumulated
042   errors leading to significant deviations from desired trajectories (Nair & Finn, 2019). Hierarchical
043   Imitation Learning (HIL) (Le et al., 2018a) leverages the sub-goal decomposition of long-horizon
044   tasks into a multi-level hierarchy and reduces the relevant state-action space for each sub-goal such
045
      as goal-states (Ding et al., 2019) and Task IDs (Kalashnikov et al., 2021). To address this, lan-
      guage instruction has come as an aid for sub-goal specification, as such instruction can be both
046
      informative and flexible (Stepputtis et al., 2020). Language-conditioned HIL approaches either
047
      train the high-level sub-goal encoder and low-level policy agent separately (Prakash et al., 2021)
048   or jointly (Hejna et al., 2023). Though both methods have achieved impressive results, learning
049   language-based sub-goals remains challenging, as it requires a large number of expensive sub-goal
050   labels (Chevalier-Boisvert et al., 2018a). To overcome this, various methods have been proposed to
051   infer both sub-goal boundaries and supervision-free representations (Garg et al., 2022; Jiang et al.,
052   2022; Kipf et al., 2019; Simeonov et al., 2021). Despite these efforts, these language instructions are
053   unstructured, and often fall short of generalization to newer tasks while not seamlessly integrated
      into IL policy training (Wang et al., 2019a; Mees et al., 2022).

054
      The capabilities brought by Large Language Models (LLMs) spark new promises for tackling such
055   instruction challenges in IL and especially HIL settings. It has shown leveraging LLM’s strong
056   reasoning and semantic abilities help break down complex, ambiguous language instructions into
057   manageable steps for a high-level plan (Huang et al., 2022; Ahn et al., 2022; Huang et al., 2023).
058   LLM excels at emulating human-like task decomposition, (Huang et al., 2022; Wei et al., 2022) This
059   ability has already been harnessed by researchers to produce high-level plans based on textual task
060   instructions (Ahn et al., 2022; Prakash et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2023). However, while these plans
061   reflect some task hierarchy, they are not directly executable due to the reliance on pre-trained low-
062   level policy agents for primitive actions (Prakash et al., 2023). Moreover, most high-level plans are
063
      static and require frequent LLM calls to update as states change, which is highly costly (Song et al.,
      2023). These challenges limit applicability of LLM-based approaches in assisting HIL. Motivated by
064
      these promises and challenges, we want to address the following question: “Can pre-trained LLMs
065
      serves as a prior to define task’s hierarchical structure, establish sub-goal library autonomously,
066   and use them to closely guide both high-level sub-goal learning and low-level agent?”
067
068   In this paper, we explore the possibility of using LLM-generated high-level plans to assist both
069
      sub-goal learning and policy training in Hierarchical Imitation Learning. We introduce SEmantic-
      Augmented Imitation Learning (SEAL), a novel hierarchical imitation learning framework that uti-
070
      lizes pretrained LLMs to generate semantically meaningful sub-goals. Give the fact that sub-goal
071
      are also oftenwise labeled by human and understandable by LLMs, SEAL represents each sub-
072   goal with one-hot latent vectors, and employs these representations to convert expert demonstration
073   states into supervisory labels for high-level latent variable learning. To enhance the learning process,
074   SEAL features a dual-encoder structure for sub-goal representation. One encoder learns sub-goal
075   vectors in a supervised manner using LLM-provided labels, while the other leverages unsupervised
076   Vector Quantization (VQ) (Wang et al., 2019b) to map expert demonstration states to latent sub-
077   goal representations. The effectiveness of the learned latent variables is evaluated within the task
078   environment by comparing their contribution to selecting optimal actions, with success rate as the
079   confidence metric. We design a weighted combination of the two encoders’ outputs as the final sub-
080
      goal representation, thereby reducing over-reliance on a weaker encoder, mitigating over-fitting, and
      improving overall robustness. Additionally, we present a transition-augmented lower-level policy
081
      agent that prioritizes intermediate states corresponding to sub-goal transitions by assigning higher
082
      weights, reflecting the hierarchical structure of long-horizon tasks. Extensive experiments on two
083   tasks KeyDoor and Grid-World and show that SEAL can outperform several state-of-the-art super-
084   vised and unsupervised HIL approaches. To summarize, our main contributions include:
085
086          • We propose SEAL, a novel hierarchical imitation learning framework that leverages Lan-
087            guage Models to generate high-level plans and provide semantically meaningful sub-goal
088            representations without any prior knowledge of task hierarchical structure.
089          • To enhance SEAL’s effectiveness, we introduce a dual-encoder structure that combines su-
090            pervised LLM-based sub-goal learning with unsupervised VQ-based representations, en-
091            suring reliability and robustness in sub-goal learning. Additionally, SEAL incorporates a
092            transition-augmented low-level planner for better adaptation to challenging intermediate
093            states where sub-goal transitions occur.
094          • We demonstrate that SEAL outperforms several state-of-the-art HIL approaches, particu-
095            larly on small expert datasets, and shows superior adaptability to longer-range composition
096            tasks and task variations.

      2   R ELATED W ORKS
099
100
      Imitation Learning. Imitation Learning encompasses two primary approaches: Behavioral Cloning
101
      (BC) (Bain & Sammut, 1995) and Inverse Reinforcement Learning (IRL) (Ng et al., 2000). BC re-
102   lies on a pre-collected expert dataset of demonstrations, where the agent learns to mimic the actions
103   in an offline manner. While BC is simple to implement, it is prone to compounding errors, partic-
104   ularly when the agent encounters states not present in the expert’s demonstrations (Zhang, 2021).
105   In contrast, IRL methods (Ho & Ermon, 2016; Reddy et al., 2019; Brantley et al., 2019) involve
106   interacting with the environment to collect additional demonstrations, aiming to infer the underly-
107   ing reward function that the expert is optimizing. The agent then learns by optimizing this inferred
      reward. However, IRL approaches are more challenging to implement (Kurach et al., 2018), typ-


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      ically requiring more computational resources and data. In this work, we primarily adopt the BC
109   architecture in a hierarchical setting, while incorporating insights from IRL by using environment
110   interactions to validate the reliability of learned latent sub-goal variables.
111
112
      Bi-Level Planning and Execution. Hierarchical Imitation Learning (HIL) enhances the ability of
      imitation learning agents to tackle complex, long-horizon tasks by breaking them down into smaller
113
      sub-goals and conditioning the agent’s behavior on those sub-goals. The high-level agent chooses
114
      the sub-goals, while the low-level agent learns to accomplish specific controls under selected sub-
115   goals (Jing et al., 2021). Many HIL approaches, such as Hierarchical Behavior Cloning (Le et al.,
116   2018a) and Thought Cloning (Hu & Clune, 2024), rely on supervisory labels for sub-goal learning,
117   but such annotations are often difficult to obtain. To address this limitation, unsupervised methods
118   like Option-GAIL (Jing et al., 2021), LOVE (Jiang et al., 2022), SDIL (Zhao et al., 2023), and
119   CompILE (Kipf et al., 2019) have been developed to infer sub-goals directly from expert trajectories.
120   However, the lack of labeled guidance in these approaches makes meaningful sub-goal discovery
121   more challenging and hence reduces the reliability of the learned policies.
122   LLMs for Planning. Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated significant potential in
123   decision-making processes. Direct generation of action sequences usually do not lead to accurate
124   plans (Silver et al., 2022; Valmeekam et al., 2023; Kambhampati et al., 2024). Recent studies have
125   successfully utilized LLMs to decompose natural language task instructions into executable high-
126   level plans, represented as a sequence of intermediate sub-goals (Ahn et al., 2022; Prakash et al.,
127   2023; Huang et al., 2023). While LLMs can be also applied to translate user-given language in-
128   structions to symbolic goals (Mavrogiannis et al., 2024; Xie et al., 2023) Additionally, LLMs can
129
      function as encoders, identifying current sub-goals based on both observations (sometimes images)
      and language task descriptions to facilitate high-level plan execution (Fu et al., 2024; Malato et al.,
130
      2024; Du et al., 2023). However, these approaches typically still depend on pre-trained low-level
131
      planners for generating executable primitive actions. In this work, we leverage LLM-generated
132   high-level plans to assist in learning both sub-goals and low-level actions simultaneously.

      3   P RELIMINARY
136
137
138   In this paper, we look into the long-horizon, compositional decision-making problem as as a
139   discrete-time, finite-step Markov Decision Process (MDP). MDP can be represented by a tuple
140
      (S, A, T , r, γ), where S, A denotes the state and action space, T (st+1 |st , at ) : S × A → S denotes
      the transition function, r : S × A → R is the reward function and γ ∈ [0, 1] is the discount factor.
141
142   In standard settings of Hierarchical Imitation Learning (HIL), instead of having access to the reward
143   function r, the agent has access to a dataset of expert demonstrations D = {τ1e , τ2e , ..., τN
                                                                                                    e
                                                                                                      }, which
144   contains N expert trajectory sequences consisting of state-action pairs {(st , at )}, where st ∈ S,
145   at ∈ A, T is the time horizon for planning, 0 ≤ t ≤ T . In this paper, the expert trajectories are not
146
      labeled with any rewards nor subhorizon segments. We assume HIL agents operate in a two-level
      hierarchy though our method can also be applied to problems with more levels:
147
148
149
             • High-level Sub-goal Encoder πH (gt |st ): Selects a sub-goal gt ∈ G based on the current
               states st , where G is the space of sub-goals.
150
151
             • Low-level Policy Agent πL (at |gt , st ): Executes actions conditioned on both the current
152
               state st and sub-goal gt .
153
154
      In this work, we focus on settings where agents lack access to the sub-goal space G, relying in-
155
      stead on an oracle full task instruction M in natural language. While well-defined G aids efficient
156   HIL agent learning (Hauskrecht et al., 2013), its acquisition is difficult due to missing task-specific
157   knowledge (Nachum et al., 2018; Kim et al., 2021). Natural language task instructions, though eas-
158   ier to obtain as they are common-used commands from human (Stepputtis et al., 2020), are hard to
159   map to hierarchical structures due to their complex and ambiguous nature (Zhang & Chai, 2021;
160   Ahuja et al., 2023; Ju et al., 2024). In this work, we investigate leveraging LLMs to parameterize G
161   from language instructions with its powerful semantic and world knowledge, and pre-label states in
      D to guide effective learning of πH and πL in hierarchical imitation learning.

      4     SEAL FOR H IERARCHICAL I MITATION L EARNING
183
184
      The key idea of SEAL is to learn high-level sub-goal representations using supervisory labels gen-
185
      erated by LLMs. In previous works, such labels were typically provided by human experts via
186
      instructions (Pan et al., 2018; Le et al., 2018a), making them expensive to obtain. However, with
187   the assistance of LLMs, we introduce an efficient and reliable method to automatically generate
188   labels that map states to sub-goals. Specifically, LLMs are used to semantically extract a high-
189   level plan from the full-task language instruction M and map states in expert demonstrations to
190   sub-goals within this plan. Using these learned sub-goal representations, the model then learns the
191   corresponding low-level actions. An overview of our SEAL framework is illustrated in Fig. 1.
192

197

198   Derive Sub-goal Space of Task Prior works have demonstrated that LLMs can establish a mean-
199   ingful chain of sub-tasks from task instruction as high-level plan (Huang et al. (2022); Prakash et al.

221   Naturally, we consider using these LLM-generated labels for sub-goal representations to train a

224   the guidance provided by the labels. However, it is prone to over-fitting on the training dataset.

227
      supervised learning encoder and an unsupervised learning encoder, producing a weighted-average
      sub-goal representation. The weighted combination allows for flexibility, prioritizing the encoder
228
      that performs better for a particular task or dataset, ultimately enhancing robustness and improving
229
      generalization.

348
349   In this section, we evaluate the performance of SEAL on two long-horizon compositional tasks:
350
      KeyDoor and Grid-World. We compare SEAL’s performance with various baselines, including non-
      hierarchical, unsupervised, and supervised hierarchical IL methods, in both large and small expert
351
      dataset scenarios. Following this, we analyze how SEAL enhances task completion performance.

354   5.1    S IMULATION E NVIRONMENTAL S ETUP
355
356
      KeyDoor The MiniGrid Dataset (Chevalier-Boisvert et al., 2018b) is a collection of grid-based envi-
      ronments designed for evaluating reinforcement learning and imitation learning algorithms in tasks
357
      requiring navigation, exploration, and planning. Among these environments, we start with KeyDoor,
358
      an easy-level compositional task that requires the player to move to the key and pick up it to unlock
359   the door. To add complexity, we enlarge the original 3 × 3 grid environment to 10 × 10 size, and
360   randomly initialize the locations of player, key and door for each episode. To facilitate understand-
361   ing by LLMs, we convert the environment into a vector-based state, with elements including the
362   coordinates of the player, key, and door, as well as the different statuses of the key (picked or not)
363   and door (locked or not). The maximum time-steps T of one episode is set to 100. We evaluate our
364   SEAL on expert datasets with 30, 100, 150, 200 demonstrations generated by an expert bot.
365   Grid-World The environment is a 10x10 grid world with a single player and multiple objects ran-
366   domly distributed at various locations. The player’s objective is to visit and pick up these objects in
367   a specific order. This task is more challenging than KeyDoor due to its longer-range compositional
368   nature, involving more sub-goals. In this work, we set the number of objects in the grid world to
369   range from 3 to 5, to test SEAL’s effectiveness in solving longer-range tasks. Similar to KeyDoor,
370   the fully observed environment is converted into a vector-based state, with elements representing
371   the coordinates of the player and objects, as well as their statuses (picked or not). The maximum
372   time-steps per episode is set to 100. We evaluate SEAL on expert datasets with 200, 300, and 400
373
      demonstrations generated by an expert bot.

      6     C ONCLUSION
531
532
      In this work, we introduce SEAL, a novel HIL framework that leverages LLMs’ semantic and world
533
      knowledge to define sub-goal spaces and pre-label states as meaningful sub-goal representations
534   without prior task hierarchy knowledge. SEAL outperforms baselines like BC, LISA, SDIL, and TC
535   across various environments, particularly in low-sample and complex longer-range compositional
536   tasks. Our approach achieves higher success and sub-goal completion rates, with assistance of
537   the dual-encoder proving more robust than the pure LLM encoder and the transition-augmented
538   low-level policy. SEAL also adapts well to varying task complexities and latent dimensions. Our
539   current design still observes training instability, and we are interested in making more efficient SEAL
      approaches while under partially observed states.

827
828   KeyDoor The environment is based on the DoorKey setting from the MiniGrid Dataset, but with
829   modifications to make the state compatible with LLM input for sub-goal mapping. Instead of us-
830   ing image states, we convert the state into an 8-dimensional vector that captures crucial object in-
831
      formation: {x-coordinate of key, y-coordinate of key, x-coordinate of door, y-coordinate of door,
      x-coordinate of player, y-coordinate of player, key status (picked: 1, not picked: 0), and door status
832
      (unlocked: 1, locked: 0) }. Wall obstacles are removed to avoid interference. The action space
833
      consists of 6 primitive actions: move up, move down, move right, move left, pick up, and unlock.
834   The key can be picked up only when the player reaches the key’s coordinates, and the door can
835   be unlocked only if the player reaches the door’s coordinates with the key already picked up. The
836   language task instruction M is defined as: ”Pick up the key, and then unlock the door.” The episode
837   ends when the door is successfully unlocked or the maximum time steps T = 100 are reached.
838
      Grid-World The environment is based on the grid world used in (Kipf et al., 2019; Jiang et al.,
839
      2022). Similar to KeyDoor, the image-based states are converted into a vector format for LLM input,
840   capturing crucial information about objects: x and y coordinates of Object 1, x and y coordinates of
841   Object 2, ..., x and y coordinates of the player, status of Object 1 (picked: 1, not picked: 0), status
842   of Object 2, .... For Grid-World with 3, 4, or 5 objects, the state vector has dimensions 11, 14, and
843   17, respectively. Wall obstacles and irrelevant objects are removed to avoid interference. The action
844   space consists of 5 primitive actions: move up, move down, move right, move left, and pick up. An
845   object can be picked up only when the player reaches its coordinates. The language task instruction
846   M is defined as: ”Pick up Object 1, then pick up Object 2, then...” The episode ends when the
847   player picks up all objects in the correct order or after the maximum time step T = 100. At the start
848
      of each episode, the coordinates of all objects and the player are randomly reset.
849   Sub-goal Spaces Identified by LLMs We use GPT-4o to decompose the language task instructions
850   for both the KeyDoor and Grid-World environments into their respective sub-goal spaces. In the
851   KeyDoor environment, there are K = 4 sub-goals: {move to the key, pick up the key, move to the
852   door, unlock the door}. In the Grid-World environment, with 3, 4, and 5 objects, the number of
853   sub-goals is K = 6, K = 8, and K = 10, respectively, including: {move to object 1, pick up object
854
      1, move to object 2, pick up object 2, ...}. For both sub-goal spaces, we parameterize each language
      sub-goal in it by a K-dim one hot vector.
