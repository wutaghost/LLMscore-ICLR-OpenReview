                                                 A BSTRACT
008
009           Geometry is a ubiquitous tool in computer graphics, design, and engineering.
010           However, the lack of large shape datasets limits the application of state-of-the-art
011           supervised learning methods and motivates the exploration of alternative learning
012           strategies. To this end, we introduce geometry-informed neural networks (GINNs)
013           – a framework for training shape-generative neural fields without data by leveraging
014           user-specified design requirements in the form of objectives and constraints. By
015           adding diversity as an explicit constraint, GINNs avoid mode-collapse and can gen-
016           erate multiple diverse solutions, often required in geometry tasks. Experimentally,
017
              we apply GINNs to several validation problems and a realistic 3D engineering
              design problem, showing control over geometrical and topological properties, such
018
              as surface smoothness or the number of holes. These results demonstrate the
019
              potential of training shape-generative models without data, paving the way for new
020           generative design approaches without large datasets.

041   1   I NTRODUCTION
042
043   Recent advances in deep learning have revolutionized fields with abundant data, such as computer
044
      vision and natural language processing. However, the scarcity of large datasets in many other domains,
      including 3D computer graphics, design, engineering, and physics, restricts the use of advanced
045
      supervised learning techniques, necessitating the exploration of alternative learning strategies.
046
047   Fortunately, these disciplines are often equipped with formal problem descriptions, such as objectives
048   and constraints. Previous works for PDEs (Raissi et al., 2019), molecular science (Noé et al.,
049   2019), and combinatorial optimization (Bengio et al., 2021) demonstrate these can suffice to train
050
      models even in the absence of any data. The success of these data-free approaches motivates an
      analogous attempt in geometry, raising the question: Is it possible to train a shape-generative model
051
      on objectives and constraints alone, without relying on any data?
052
053   We address this question by introducing geometry-informed neural networks or GINNs. GINNs are
      trained to satisfy specified design constraints and to produce feasible shapes without any training

054
      samples. A GINN solves a topology optimization problem using neural fields, which offer detailed,
055   smooth, and topologically flexible geometry representations, while being compact to store. This
056   setup is analogous to physics-informed neural networks but with a high number of varied constraints:
057   differential, integral, geometrical, and topological.
058
      In contrast to both physics-informed neural networks and classical topology optimization, GINNs
059
      allow to generate multiple solutions by enforcing solution diversity as an explicit constraint. This
060
      is of high interest when applied to problems with solution multiplicity, e.g., induced by under-
061   determinedness or near-optimality common in geometry problems. To connect back to our research
062   question: with GINNs we can train neural fields that satisfy user-specified design constraints, and by
063   adding diversity as an explicit constraint, we can generate a multiplicity of solutions. GINNs can
064   thus be used as shape-generative models trained purely on constraints and without data.
065
      We take several steps to demonstrate GINNs experimentally. We formulate a tractable learning
066
      problem using constrained optimization and by converting constraints into differentiable losses. After
067   solving several introductory problems, we proceed to a realistic 3D engineering design problem.
068   Figure 1 illustrates this task of designing a jet-engine lifting bracket, or geometrically – connecting
069   cylindrical interfaces within the given design region. We show different GINNs trained with various
070   additional smoothness and topology requirements. Figure 6 shows a GINN trained on the same task
071   but with an additional diversity constraint. Surprisingly, this induces a structured latent space, with
072   generalization capacity and interpretable directions.
073   We show that training shape-generative networks using constraints and objectives without data is a
074   feasible learning strategy, paving the way for new generative design approaches without large datasets.
075   Our main contributions are summarized as follows:
076
077            1. We introduce GINN - a framework for training shape-generative neural fields without data
078               by leveraging design constraints and avoiding mode-collapse using a diversity constraint.
079            2. We apply GINNs to several validation problems and a realistic 3D engineering design
080               problem, showing control over geometrical and topological properties. 1

128   2.2   N EURAL FIELDS
129
130
131   A neural field (NF) (also coordinate-based NN, implicit neural representation (INR)) is a NN (typically
132   a multilayer-perceptron) representing a function f : x 7→ y that maps a spatial and/or temporal
133   coordinate x to a quantity y. Compared to discrete representations, NFs are significantly more
134   memory-efficient while providing higher fidelity, continuity, and access to automatic differentials.
135
      They have seen widespread success in representing and generating a variety of signals, including
      shapes (Park et al., 2019; Chen & Zhang, 2019; Mescheder et al., 2019), scenes (Mildenhall et al.,
136
      2021), images (Karras et al., 2021), audio, video (Sitzmann et al., 2020), and physical quantities
137
      (Raissi et al., 2019). For a more comprehensive overview, we refer to the survey by Xie et al. (2022).
138
139   Implicit neural shapes (INSs) represent geometries through scalar fields, such as occupancy
140   (Mescheder et al., 2019; Chen & Zhang, 2019) or signed-distance (Park et al., 2019; Atzmon &
141
      Lipman, 2020). In addition to the properties of NFs, INSs also enjoy topological flexibility supporting
      shape reconstruction and generation. We point out the difference between these two training regimes.
142
      In the generative setting, the training is supervised on the ground truth scalar field of every shape
143
      (Park et al., 2019; Chen & Zhang, 2019; Mescheder et al., 2019). However, in surface reconstruction,
144   i.e., finding a smooth surface from a set of points measured from a single shape, no ground truth is
145   available and the problem is ill-defined (Atzmon & Lipman, 2020; Berger et al., 2016).
146
147
      Regularization methods have been proposed to counter the ill-posedness in geometry problems.
      These include leveraging ground-truth normals (Atzmon & Lipman, 2021) and curvatures (Novello
148
      et al., 2022), minimal surface property (Atzmon & Lipman, 2021), and off-surface penalization
149
      (Sitzmann et al., 2020). A central effort is to achieve the distance field property of the scalar field for
150   which many regularization terms have been proposed: eikonal loss (Gropp et al., 2020), divergence
151   loss (Ben-Shabat et al., 2022), directional divergence loss (Yang et al., 2023), level-set alignment
152   (Ma et al., 2023), or closest point energy (Marschner et al., 2023). The distance field property can
153   be expressed as a PDE constraint called eikonal equation |∇f (x)| = 1, establishing a relation of
154   regularized INSs to PINNs (Gropp et al., 2020).
155
      Inductive bias. In addition to explicit loss terms, the architecture, initialization, and optimizer can
156   also limit or bias the learned shapes. For example, typical INSs are limited to watertight surfaces
157   without boundaries or self-intersections (Chibane et al., 2020; Palmer et al., 2022). ReLU networks
158   are limited to piece-wise linear surfaces and together with gradient descent are biased toward low
159   frequencies (Tancik et al., 2020). Fourier-feature encoding (Tancik et al., 2020), sine activations
160   (Sitzmann et al., 2020), and wavelet activations (Saragadam et al., 2023) allow to control this
161   frequency bias. Similarly, initialization techniques are important to converge toward desirable optima
      (Sitzmann et al., 2020; Atzmon & Lipman, 2020; Ben-Shabat et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023).

      2.3   (DATA - FREE ) G ENERATIVE MODELING
163
164   Generative modeling (Kingma & Welling, 2013; Goodfellow et al., 2014; Rezende & Mohamed,
165   2015; Tomczak, 2021) is almost exclusively performed in a data-driven (i.e., supervised) setting to
166   capture and sampling from the underlying data-distribution. However, notable exceptions exist.
167
      Boltzmann generators (Noé et al., 2019) are a prominent example of data-free generative models.
168   They are trained to capture the Boltzmann distribution associated with an energy landscape. In the
169   generative setting, GINNs also learn a distribution minimizing an energy as an implicit combination
170   of constraint violations and objectives. However, Boltzmann generators avoid mode-collapse using
171   an entropy-regularizing term which presupposes invertibility making them not directly applicable to
172   function spaces. Instead, GINNs use a more general diversity term to hinder mode-collapse over the
173   function space of shapes.
174   Conditional neural fields allow for generative modeling of functions. By conditioning a base
175   network F on a modulation (i.e, latent) variable z, a conditional NF encodes multiple fields simul-
176   taneously: f (x) = F (x; z). The different choices of the conditioning mechanism lead to a zoo of
177   architectures, including input concatenation (Park et al., 2019), hypernetworks (Ha et al., 2017),
178   modulation (Mehta et al., 2021), and attention (Rebain et al., 2022). These can be classified into
179   global and local mechanisms, which also establishes a connection between conditional NFs and
180   operator learning (Wang et al., 2024). For more detail we refer to Xie et al. (2022); Rebain et al.
181   (2022); Wang et al. (2024).
182   Generative design refers to computational methods that automatically conduct design exploration
183   under constraints set by designers (Jang et al., 2022). It holds the potential of streamlining innovative
184   solutions, e.g., in material design, architecture, or engineering. In particular, GINNs can be seen as
185   solving the general task of topology optimization – finding the material distribution that minimizes
186   a specified objective subject to constraints. However, while classical methods optimize a single
187   shape directly, we optimize a GINN that generates diverse feasible shapes. This encourages design
188
      space exploration and supports downstream tasks, while allowing to incorporate even sparse data
      samples, if available. While generative design datasets are not abundant, deep learning has previously
189
      shown promise in material design and topology optimization. For more detail, we refer to surveys on
190
      generative models in engineering design (Regenwetter et al., 2022) and topology optimization via
191   machine learning (Shin et al., 2023).

342   Topology describes properties of a shape that are invariant under deformations, such as the number of
343   connected components or holes. Certain materials and objects display specific topological properties
344   (Moore, 2010; Caplan et al., 2018; Bendsoe & Sigmund, 2011), e.g., connectedness, which is a basic
345   requirement for the propagation of forces and by extension manufacturability and structural function.
346   Despite topological properties being discrete-valued, persistent homology (PH) is a tool that allows to
347
      formulate a differentiable loss. In brief, it identifies topological features (e.g., connected components)
      and quantifies their persistence w.r.t. some filtration function. For our implicit shapes, this is the
348
      implicit function f itself. Consequentially, the birth and death of each feature can be matched to a
349
      pair of critical points of f . Their values can then be adjusted to achieve the desired topology.
350   In practice, we follow the standard procedure of first discretizing the continuous function onto a
351   cubical complex. We additionally filter cells outside the given design space to prevent undesirable
352   connections. Throughout this section, we use a constraint that encourages connected shapes with a
353   minimal number of holes. We detail PH and our approach in Appendix E.

391   4.2   VALIDATION PROBLEMS
392
393   Generative PINN solving an under-determined reaction-diffusion problem. As a primer to
394   geometry tasks, we begin by illustrating solution multiplicity on an under-determined physics system.
395   While most familiar problems in physics are well-defined, cases exist where, e.g., the initial conditions
396
      are irrelevant and general PDE solutions are sought, such as in chaotic systems or animations. We
      first demonstrate how a PINN can also be extended to provide diverse stationary solutions using a
397
      system of reaction-diffusion with no initial condition. Figure 4 illustrates the resulting Turing patterns
398
      that continuously morph during latent space traversal. For more detail, we refer to Appendix B.
399
400   Plateau’s problem to demonstrate GINNs on a well-posed problem. Plateau’s problem is
401   to find the surface M with the minimal area given a prescribed boundary Γ (a closed curve in
402
      X ⊂ R3 ). A minimal surface is known to have zero mean curvature κH everywhere. Minimal
      surfaces have boundaries and may contain intersections and branch points (Douglas, 1931) which
403
      cannot be represented implicitly. For simplicity, we select a suitable problem instance, noting
404
      that more appropriate geometric representations exist (Wang & Chern, 2021; Palmer et al., 2022).
405   Altogether, we represent the surface as M = ∂Ω ∩ X and the two constraints are: Γ ⊂ M and
406   κH (x) = 0 ∀x ∈ M . The result in Figure 5(a) qualitatively agrees with the known solution.
407
408
      Parabolic mirror to demonstrate a different geometry representation. Although we mainly
      focus on the implicit representation, the GINN framework extends to other representations, such as
409
      explicit, parametric, or discrete shapes. Here, the GINN learns the height function f : [−1, 1] 7→ R
410
      of a mirror with the interface constraint f (0) = 0 and that all the reflected rays should intersect at the
411   single point (0, 1). The result in Figure 5(b) approximates the known solution: a parabolic mirror.
412   This is a very basic example of caustics, an inverse problem in optics, which we hope inspires future
413   work combining GINNs and the recent developments in neural rendering techniques.
414
      Obstacle to introduce diversity and connectedness. Consider a 2D rectangular design region E
415
      with a circular obstacle in the middle. The interface I consists of two vertical line segments and
416
      has prescribed outward-facing normals n̄. We seek shapes that connect these two interfaces while
417   avoiding the obstacle. Despite this problem admitting infinitely many solutions, the naive application
418   of the generative softplus-MLP leads to mode-collapse. This is mitigated by employing the additional
419   diversity constraint as illustrated in Figure 5(c).

423   4.3   E NGINEERING DESIGN CASE STUDY
424
425
      The problem specification is based on an engineering design competition hosted by General Electric
426   and GrabCAD (Kiis et al., 2013). The challenge was to design the lightest possible lifting bracket of a
427   jet engine subject to both physical and geometrical constraints. Here, we focus only on the geometric
428   requirements: the shape must fit in a provided design region E and attach to five cylindrical interfaces
429   I: a horizontal loading pin and four vertical fixing bolts. Instead of minimizing the volume subject to
430   a linear elasticity PDE constraint, we minimize the surface smoothness E subject to a topological
431   connectedness constraint. Conceptually, this formulation is similar but avoids the need for a PDE
      solver in the training loop. These requirements are detailed in Table 1 and illustrated in Figs. 1 and 3.

469   Optimization. The convergence behavior of the aforementioned runs with and without diversity is
470   shown in Figures 10 and 11. Despite having up to seven loss terms, adaptive ALM automatically
471
      balances these and minimizes each constraint violation. However, the variance in several losses
      remains high. This is largely due to the diversity and smoothness terms, which are hard to optimize
472
      and increase the necessary number of iterations by roughly a factor of two and five, respectively. In
473
      addition, the necessary surface point sampling accounts for roughly 50% of the runtime, motivating
474   improved strategies. Overall, training a single shape takes roughly 10K iterations and 1 hour.
475   Similarly, the discussed diverse model takes 50K iterations and 72 hours.

      Surface smoothness. As illustrated in Figure 3, the smoothness term significantly improves the
      visual quality of the shapes. However, it is a difficult objective. First, the values of the surface-
478
      strain E span several orders of magnitude and small deviations in the surface sampling cause large
479
      variance. Hence, the strategies described in Section 4.1 are critical to stabilize training. Employing
480   Elog improves convergence further, but also leads to a different surface quality due to the concavity
481   property of log (cf. top and bottom rows in Figure 1). In previous works optimizing integrals over
482   fixed domains, adaptive ALM deals with the range issue by adjusting location-specific penalties.
483   However, this strategy is not directly applicable to our moving surfaces, which presents a unique
484   future challenge. In addition, curvature is a second-order differential operator and is expected to be
485   ill-conditioned, motivating the use of second-order optimizers to further refine the results (Ryck et al.,
      2024; Rathore et al., 2024).

515   5   C ONCLUSION
516
517   We have introduced geometry-informed neural networks demonstrating shape-generative modeling
518   driven solely by geometric constraints and objectives. After formulating the learning problem and
519   discussing key theoretical and practical aspects, we applied GINNs to several validation problems
520
      and a realistic engineering task.
521   Limitations and future work. GINNs combine several known and novel components, each of
522   which warrants an in-depth study of theoretical and practical aspects, including alternative shape
523   distances and their aggregation into a diversity, conditioning mechanisms, and a broader range of
524   constraints.
525   In this work, we focused on building the conceptual framework of GINNs and validating it experimen-
526
      tally. This included a realistic generative design task. However, we considered a modified version of
      the original task and did not compare to established topology-optimization methods as this required
527
      an integration of a PDE solver – a task that future work should address.
528
      Even though ALM is a significant improvement over the naive approach of manually weighted loss
529   terms, the recent literature on multi-objective and second-order optimizers suggest further possible
530   improvements.
531   Finally, we investigated GINNs in the limit of no data. However, GINNs can integrate partial obser-
532   vations of a single or multiple shapes. This combination of classical and machine learning methods
533   suggests a new approach to generative design in data-sparse settings, which are of high relevance in
534   practical engineering settings.

1396   Persistent Homology is one of the primary tools which has emerged from topological data analysis
1397   (TDA) to extract topological features from data. Data modalities such as point clouds, time series,

       The dimension of Hn (X) counts the number of n-dimensional features and defines the Betti number
       bn : for n = 0 the number of connected components, for n = 1 the number of holes, for n = 2 the
1518
       number of voids.
1519
1520   Filtrations on the space X are defined using a filter function f : X → R. Using a sequence of
1521   increasing parameters αn with αk < αn for k < n we can define a sequence of nested subspaces of
1522   X as sub-level sets Xn = f −1 ([−∞, αn ]). We then have
1523
1524
                                             ∅ ⊆ X1 ⊆ · · · ⊆ XN = X                                         (23)
1525
1526
       The homology of each of these nested complexes Cn (Xi ) can be computed.
1527
1528   Persistent Homology encodes how the homology of an increasing sequence of complexes changes
1529   under a given filtration. Topological features appear and and vanish as the filter function sweeps
1530   over X. The birth time b of a feature defined as the value αn at which the homology of Cn (Xn )
1531   changes to include this feature. The death time d of a feature is analogously defined as the value
1532
       αn at which it is removed from Cn (Xn ). The persistence of a feature is defined as the length of its
       lifetime l = d − b.
1533
1534   For each Betti number bn (for each homology class Hn ) the information about the persistent homology
1535   of a given filtration is encoded in a persistence diagram containing the points (b, d) of the birth
1536   and death pairs of all n-dimensional topological features (changes in the dimension of Hn ). The
1537   persistence diagrams contain the entire topological information about underlying the space or shape
1538
       for a sufficiently fine filtration.
1539   To compute the persistent homology of a neural field, we evaluate the network on a cubical complex
1540   on the domain of the field, i.e. a grid in RN . The output is simply a gray scale image (we are only
1541   dealing with scalar fields in this work) and the PH can computed with existing algorithms. The
1542   current SotA algorithm for PH computation on cubical complexes is CRipser Kaji et al. (2020).
1543   Given a grayscale image and a filtration value a, the sublevel set at a is the binary image resulting
1544   from thresholding the image for values smaller or equal to a. For every such binary image, which
1545   defines a weighted cubical complex with coefficients in Z/2Z, the homology can be computed. The
1546   persistence homology is then obtained by sweeping the thresholding value a through R.
1547
       In general, we are interested in computing the PH within a given envelope, which is not necessarily
1548   a rectangular region. We achieve this by sampling the field in a rectangular domain containing the
1549   envelope and setting the value of points not in the envelope to ∞. Applying the PH computation to
1550   this altered image then correctly returns the evolution of persistence features within the envelope.
1551   The only drawback of this method is the additional computational cost of having to include the grid
1552   points outside the envelope in the PH computation, which is why the bounding domain should be
1553   chosen tightly around the envelope.
1554   The PH computation itself does not have to be differentiable (and the CRipser library we use is not)
1555   because the cells, i.e. the grid point of the image, at which a given persistence feature is born or killed
1556   are stored. Hence we can simply use the network output at this grid coordinate to compute the loss
1557   and there are no issues concerning differentiability or having to re-implement the PH computation
1558   into PyTorch.
