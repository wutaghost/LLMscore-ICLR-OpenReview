009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Self-supervised learning (SSL) is a data-driven learning approach that utilizes the
012            innate structure of the data to guide the learning process. In contrast to supervised
013            learning, which depends on external labels, SSL utilizes the inherent characteristics
014            of the data to produce its own supervisory signal. However, one frequent issue with
015            SSL methods is representation collapse, where the model outputs a constant input-
016
               invariant feature representation. This issue hinders the potential application of SSL
               methods to new data modalities, as trying to avoid representation collapse wastes
017
               researchers’ time and effort. This paper introduces a novel SSL algorithm for time-
018
               series data called Prediction of Functionals from Masked Latents (PFML). Instead
019            of predicting masked input signals or their latent representations directly, PFML
020            operates by predicting statistical functionals of the input signal corresponding to
021            masked embeddings, given a sequence of unmasked embeddings. The algorithm is
022            designed to avoid representation collapse, rendering it straightforwardly applicable
023            to different time-series data domains, such as novel sensor modalities in clinical
024            data. We demonstrate the effectiveness of PFML through complex, real-life classi-
025            fication tasks across three different data modalities: infant posture and movement
026            classification from multi-sensor inertial measurement unit data, emotion recogni-
027
               tion from speech data, and sleep stage classification from EEG data. The results
               show that PFML is superior to a conceptually similar pre-existing SSL method
028
               and competitive against the current state-of-the-art SSL method, while also being
029
               conceptually simpler and without suffering from representation collapse.

032   1    I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Self-supervised learning (SSL) can be described as a data-driven learning paradigm where the training
035   process is guided by the inherent structure of the data itself. Unlike supervised learning that relies
036   on externally provided labels, SSL exploits the intrinsic properties of the data to generate its own
037   supervisory signal (Balestriero et al., 2023). SSL enables the model to learn rich feature representa-
038   tions from large amounts of unlabeled data that can be used as a starting point for downstream tasks,
039
      either as such or by fine-tuning the feature extractor to be better suited for solving some specific
      task (Erhan et al., 2010). Since typically there is an abundance of unlabeled data but a scarcity of
040
      labeled data, the use of SSL has been shown to reduce the need for large, manually annotated datasets
041
      (van den Oord et al., 2018; Baevski et al., 2020; Chen et al., 2020). In addition to SSL algorithms
042   that have been developed for a single data modality, SSL algorithms that can be applied to multiple
043   different data modalities have gained popularity in recent years (van den Oord et al., 2018; Akbari
044   et al., 2021; Baevski et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023). These methods and their extensions have shown
045   great success in e.g. audio, image, and text data (van den Oord et al., 2018; Hénaff et al., 2020;
046   Akbari et al., 2021; Baevski et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023; Baevski et al., 2023; Yoon et al., 2023;
047   Zhu et al., 2023; Lian et al., 2023).
048   However, many SSL algorithms suffer from two issues: First, SSL algorithms are usually complex,
049   with a plethora of hyperparameters that need careful tuning for the algorithm to work properly. This
050   hinders the ability of SSL algorithms to be applied to new data domains, where the selection of these
051   hyperparameters is not self-evident. For example, in contrastive learning-based SSL, the selection
052   of positive and negative samples during training is essential for the algorithm to work properly.
053   However, deciding which samples should be assigned to positive and negative categories is not always
      apparent (Kalantidis et al., 2020; Robinson et al., 2021; Balestriero et al., 2023). As another example,


                                                         1

054
      determining the number of clusters for clustering-based SSL algorithms (such as Caron et al. (2020)
055   and Hsu et al. (2021)) in a new data domain or task can be difficult. Examples of such domains
056   could include, for instance, different types of medical time-series data (e.g. EEG, ECG, or EMG
057   recordings) that come in various dataset sizes and from various recording configurations. Second, a
058   common failure mode during SSL pre-training is representation collapse, where the model ends up
059   outputting a constant, time-invariant feature representation. Representation collapse is very common
060   in SSL pre-training (Hua et al., 2021; Jing et al., 2022; Balestriero et al., 2023; Garrido et al., 2023),
061   and many SSL methods apply different countermeasures to tackle the problem (see Section 3.1).
062   In the present study, we propose a new SSL algorithm for time-series data called Prediction of
063   Functionals from Masked Latents (PFML). In PFML, the aim is to predict statistical functionals of the
064   input signal corresponding to masked embeddings, given a sequence of unmasked embeddings. The
065   overall methodological aim of our method is to have an SSL algorithm that would be as straightforward
066   as possible to apply to various time-series data domains with minimal hyperparameter optimization,
067   and without the risk of representation collapse. The contributions of the present study are as follows:
068
            1. We propose a novel SSL algorithm for time-series data, PFML, that does not suffer from
069
               representation collapse, rendering the method straightforward to apply to new time-series
070            data domains. To the best of our knowledge, PFML is the first work within the field of SSL
071            for time-series data where the central idea of reconstructing statistical functionals is utilized.
072
            2. We demonstrate the effectiveness of PFML using three different data modalities with
073            complex, real-life classification tasks: infant posture and movement classification from
074            multi-sensor inertial measurement unit (IMU) data, emotion recognition from speech data,
075            and sleep stage classification from EEG data.
076         3. We show that PFML obtains both superior results against a conceptually similar pre-existing
077            SSL method, and competitive results against the current state-of-the-art data modality
078            agnostic SSL method, while also being conceptually simpler and without suffering from
079            representation collapse.

      2    R ELATED W ORK
082
083
      Most of the advances in SSL have focused on developing new, better-performing algorithms with
084   some specific data modality in mind. For speech data, Baevski et al. (2020) presented an SSL
085   algorithm where the basic idea is to mask speech embeddings and then solve a contrastive task
086   that is defined over a quantization of the embeddings which are simultaneously learned during the
087   pre-training task. Hsu et al. (2021) proposed that instead of solving a contrastive task, they predict
088   cluster targets of masked embeddings. Furthermore, the SSL method by Chen et al. (2022) also uses
089   masking of embeddings, but the authors simulate noisy speech inputs and predict pseudo-labels of
090   the original speech from the masked embeddings.
091   Similar to the advances in SSL for audio data, there have been significant developments in SSL
092   for image data as well (Lee et al., 2017; Gidaris et al., 2018; Caron et al., 2018; Grill et al., 2020;
093   Chen et al., 2020; Radford et al., 2021; He et al., 2022; Bao et al., 2022; Oquab et al., 2024). Grill
094   et al. (2020) presented an SSL method that uses two neural networks that learn from each other’s
095   representations of differently augmented views of the same image. He et al. (2022) proposed masked
096   autoencoders (MAE) that try to reconstruct masked patches of input images using an asymmetric
097   encoder-decoder architecture. The SSL algorithm by Bao et al. (2022) tokenizes images into visual
098
      tokens, followed by masking some image patches and then trying to recover the original tokens from
      the masked patches.
099
100   SSL has also excelled in natural language processing (Devlin et al., 2019; Brown et al., 2020; Tay et al.,
101   2023; OpenAI, 2023). Devlin et al. (2019) introduced an SSL method which obtains bidirectional
102   feature representations from unlabeled text by conditioning on both the left and right textual context.
103   The method by Brown et al. (2020) uses an autoregressive model which alternates dense and locally
104
      banded sparse attention patterns in their Transformer model. OpenAI (2023) proposed an expanded
      version of Brown et al. (2020) by making the model not only larger, but also capable of handling
105
      image inputs in addition to text inputs.
106
107   More recently, SSL literature has seen a growing number of work towards SSL algorithms capable of
      running the pre-training task on multiple different data modalities. The authors of van den Oord et al.


                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      (2018) developed an SSL approach that predicts future embeddings based on previous context using
109   contrastive learning. They showed that their method was able to learn useful feature representations
110   for audio, image, text, and reinforcement learning in 3D environments. The SSL method by Akbari
111   et al. (2021) also uses contrastive learning, but their method simultaneously takes audio, video, and
112   text data as input and creates multimodal feature representations. These features were shown to work
113   well with multiple different downstream tasks, i.e. video action recognition, audio event classification,
114   image classification, and text-to-video retrieval. Wang et al. (2023) proposed an SSL method that
115   performs prediction of masked tokens in a unified manner on images, texts, and image-text pairs.
116   Their experiments showed that their method achieves state-of-the-art performance on various vision
117
      and vision-language tasks. Baevski et al. (2022) proposed data2vec, an SSL method for audio, image,
      and text data. In their approach, the model tries to predict masked latent features of an older version
118
      of itself that are both normalized and averaged over multiple Transformer layers. Their results in
119
      downstream tasks demonstrate the effectiveness of the method in all three data modalities.
120
121   For modality agnostic SSL algorithms, objective functions play a crucial role in guiding the learning
122   process. These functions can be broadly categorized into three types: instance discrimination,
123
      clustering, and masked prediction. Instance discrimination aims to distinguish between different
      instances of data, thereby encouraging the model to learn unique features for each instance and
124
      enhancing the discriminative power of the learned representations. Contrastive learning methods,
125
      such as van den Oord et al. (2018); Baevski et al. (2020); He et al. (2020); Akbari et al. (2021);
126   Pizzi et al. (2022), are an example of instance discrimination-based SSL methods. Clustering, on
127   the other hand, groups similar instances together in the feature space, fostering the model to learn
128   common features among instances belonging to the same group. Methods like Caron et al. (2020);
129   YM. et al. (2020); Hsu et al. (2021) are examples of clustering-based SSL methods. Lastly, masked
130   prediction involves the task of predicting masked parts of the input data based on the unmasked parts,
131   thereby encouraging the model to learn contextual relationships within the data. Examples of such
132   SSL methods include Devlin et al. (2019); Wang et al. (2020); Baevski et al. (2022); He et al. (2022);
133   Xie et al. (2022).

137   3.1   M OTIVATION
138
139   One key issue with many SSL methods is the problem of representation collapse, where the model out-
140   puts a constant, input-invariant feature representation, leading to a trivial solution of the pre-training
141
      task (Jing et al., 2022; Balestriero et al., 2023). This considerably slows down the development
      process for novel data domains and/or tasks due to the necessity of operating in uncertainty, when it
142
      is not clear whether the representation collapse is caused by an ill-posed task or by the SSL algorithm.
143
      To avoid this, SSL methods have taken several different countermeasures: Baevski et al. (2020) use the
144   same target representations in their contrastive learning task in a dual manner, i.e. both as a positive
145   and a negative example. Grill et al. (2020) both add an additional predictor to their training regime
146   and use a moving average of their so-called online neural network to avoid representation collapse.
147   Bardes et al. (2022) add a regularization term to their loss function that both maintains variance of the
148   embeddings and decorrelates each pair of variables. In data2vec (Baevski et al., 2022), the authors
149   tackle representation collapse by carefully selecting their model hyperparameters and promoting
150   target representation variance through feature normalization. Also, in the code implementation of
151   data2vec1 , pre-training is stopped if the variance of either model predictions or training targets falls
152
      below a predefined threshold.
153   Intuitively, given a trivial task, the model does not learn useful feature representations during pre-
154   training. In contrast, if the learning objective is too complicated, the model fails to converge to
155   a useful solution. For time-series data, i.e. a waveform (e.g. audio) or a set of waveforms (e.g.
156   multi-channel EEG), trying to reconstruct masked parts of the input signal given the unmasked parts
157   of the signal (as in e.g. MAE (He et al., 2022)) is a very complex task. This is due to the fact that
158
      a time-series signal can have large temporal variation even between short periods of time. While
      joint learning of a priori unspecified latent representations and their prediction allows discarding of
159
      this irrelevant variation (as in, e.g., van den Oord et al. (2018) or Baevski et al. (2020)), the problem

195   Regarding point (1), by making the model predict statistical functionals of masked latent features
196   instead of predicting the input signal x itself, we relieve the model from the complex task of modelling
197   the high-dimensional distribution of x in detail. We validate this argument of generating better features
198   for downstream tasks by reducing the computational complexity of the pre-training task in Section 4,
199   where we compare our proposed method against MAE. In theory, the set of statistical functionals
200   can be chosen so that the desired and deterministically calculated statistical properties of the data,
201   and thereby their variance, are preserved in the target features. Furthermore, regarding point (2), we
202   show in our experiments in Section 4 that it is more beneficial during pre-training to mask the latent
203
      features instead of masking the input directly. This further alleviates the complexity of the learning
      task in particular for the encoder module.

206   3.2   P REDICTION OF F UNCTIONALS FROM M ASKED L ATENTS
207
      Figure 1 depicts an overview of the PFML pre-training pipeline. First, a single- or multi-channel
208
      signal x is framed into a sequence of short-term frames {x0 , x1 , ...}, xn = {xt , xt+1 , ..., xt+N −1 },
209
      of N samples each. Then, a set of m functionals, F = {F0 , F1 , ..., Fm−1 }, is computed for each
210   frame xn to produce corresponding functional values fn = {F0 (xn ), F1 (xn ), ..., Fm−1 (xn )}. Here,
211   functionals are defined as mathematical operations which map a time series of arbitrary length into a
212   single value, such as the mean or variance of the signal. The frames xn are also fed to an encoder
213   model, which converts the framed signals into embeddings zn . Some of these embeddings are masked
214   randomly at time steps M (for example, M ∈ {1, 2} in Figure 1), after which all zn are used as an
215   input for a Transformer-based model to obtain outputs yn . Finally, a prediction loss is computed
      between the outputs of masked time steps yM and their functional counterparts fM . As a result,


                                                          4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      PFML pre-training optimizes the prediction of functionals of input signal frames corresponding to
217   the masked embeddings, given the unmasked embeddings from the temporal context of these frames.
218
219   In PFML, predicting only one or a few functionals of a framed signal can be a trivial task, and will
220
      most probably lead to learning feature representations that are not very useful for downstream tasks.
      However, as the number of functionals that each describe some property of the framed signal grows,
221
      a more accurate description of the signal can be obtained (see e.g. McDermott & Simoncelli (2011)
222
      for representing perceptual properties of sound textures with functionals). Therefore, as the number
223   of different functionals grows, the PFML algorithm is getting closer to predicting all of the nuances
224   of the input signal.
225
226
      Let us assume the following in PFML pre-training:
227         • Assumption 1: There is temporal variability across the frames xn . This assumption is
228           reasonable as real-world data typically exhibits temporal variability.
229         • Assumption 2: Given Assumption 1, a set of non-trivial functionals F computed from
230           xn also contains variance across the frames. This follows naturally since non-constant
231           functionals derived from variable data also exhibit variability.
232
233   Under these assumptions, as the model is trying to predict the computed functionals fn given the
234
      embeddings zn , good model predictions yn that lead to low prediction loss values also inherently
      contain variance. On the contrary, if yn were to contain zero variance across the frames while fn
235
      contains variance, the prediction loss would be high. Consequently, PFML pre-training does not
236
      converge to collapsed feature representations, as long as Assumptions 1 and 2 hold true. For a more
237   detailed formulation, see Appendix A. Empirical results (see Section 4.4) support this theoretical
238   claim, showing that PFML maintains variance in predictions across various datasets.
239

253   For masking the embeddings, in each training and validation minibatch we randomly select frames

264   bidirectional recurrent neural networks (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997; Cho et al., 2014), could
265

267   4   E XPERIMENTS
268
269   We evaluate our PFML method using three different datasets of time-series data with complex clas-
      sification tasks: infant posture and movement classification from multi-sensor IMU data, emotion


                                                         5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      recognition from speech data, and sleep stage classification from EEG data. For each dataset, we first
271   run SSL pre-training with unlabeled data using PFML, after which we fine-tune our models for down-
272   stream classification tasks using labeled data. We compare PFML against three different baselines:
273   MAE (He et al., 2022), data2vec (Baevski et al., 2022), and not using pre-training at all. We selected
274   MAE for our experiments since it is conceptually very similar to PFML, and we chose data2vec since
275   it is the current state-of-the-art data modality agnostic SSL method. In order to make the prediction
276   of functionals directly comparable with predicting the input signal, we use a slightly modified version
277   of MAE where we mask embeddings instead of masking inputs.
278   For PFML pre-training, our models consist of a modality-specific frame-level encoder
279   (detailed in Sections 4.1, 4.2, and 4.3 for IMU, speech, and EEG data, respec-
280   tively) and a Transformer network consisting of T Transformer encoder blocks.                       Be-
281   tween the encoder and Transformer networks there is a CNN-based relative posi-
282   tional encoder followed by a GeLU (Hendrycks & Gimpel, 2016) activation and layer
283   normalization (Ba et al., 2016). We frame our input signals before feeding the data into an en-
284   coder model, and we compute functionals from these frames as our training targets. For multi-channel
285
      data, we compute functionals separately for each channel. The functionals are then z-score normalized
      across the entire pre-training dataset. For computational efficiency, we pre-compute the functionals
286
      of each signal frame before the pre-training process. After the Transformer encoder blocks, we add
287
      a linear projection to convert the Transformer outputs into predicted functionals. Pre-training is
288   run until validation loss convergence, and we use the model with the lowest validation loss as our
289   pre-trained model. Starting from an initial learning rate, we gradually reduce the learning rate during
290   model training with a reduction factor of 0.5 based on the plateauing of the validation loss.
291
      We pre-train our models using MAE and data2vec in a similar manner as for PFML, and we use the
292
      same model architecture for all three pre-training algorithms. MAE pre-training is run in a similar
293
      manner as PFML pre-training, with the only exception of predicting the input signal frames instead
294   of functionals. For data2vec pre-training, we used the instance-normalized (Ulyanov et al., 2016) and
295   averaged outputs of each feed-forward part of all Transformer encoder blocks as our training targets.
296   If we observed that a representation collapse occurred during data2vec pre-training, we restarted the
297   pre-training process. For further details on the data2vec algorithm, see Baevski et al. (2022). We
298   used mean squared error loss for all pre-training processes except for PFML with speech data, where
299   we found L1 loss to work better.
300   We fine-tune our pre-trained models in two stages. In the first stage, two randomly initialized fully-
301   connected GeLU layers followed by a softmax function are added after the Transformer model. Then,
302   these layers are fine-tuned separately as the weights of the encoder and Transformer are frozen. In the
303   second stage, the entire model is fine-tuned with the same hyperparameters as in the first fine-tuning
304   stage with one exception: The learning rate LR is linearly increased from 0.001 · LR to LR during a
305   warm-up period of 20 training epochs, followed by reduction by a factor of 0.5 based on validation
306   loss plateauing. We use weighted categorical cross-entropy loss by weighting the loss of each sample
307   by its class’ inverse frequency.
308   We also test the linear separability of the features learned by our pre-trained models. In this case,
309   we only add one linear layer after the Transformer model, and we fine-tune this single layer while
310   the weights of the encoder and Transformer are frozen. As a baseline, we perform the same linear
311   evaluation for a randomly initialized model without any pre-training.
312   For pre-training and fine-tuning, we use the RAdam (Liu et al., 2020) and Adam (Kingma & Ba,
313   2015) optimizers, respectively. For the “no pre-training” condition, we simply omit pre-training,
314   the first fine-tuning stage, and the learning rate warm-up period of the second fine-tuning stage. We
315   used an NVIDIA Tesla V100 GPU to train our models, and we implemented the code using PyTorch
316   version 1.13.1. Our implementation is publicly available on GitHub.2
317
      In order to demonstrate the superiority of PFML against the state-of-the-art SSL method for multiple
318   data modalities, data2vec, in terms of representation collapse, we ran PFML, MAE, and data2vec
319   pre-training 10 times using the best hyperparameter combinations for each SSL method and for
320   each data modality. We defined representation collapse to have occurred if the variance of either the
321   embeddings or model outputs fell below 0.01 for 10 consecutive pre-training epochs, during which
322   the validation loss was decreasing. In our preliminary experiments, we found that this condition was

      4.1   I NFANT P OSTURE AND M OVEMENT C LASSIFICATION
329
330   For infant posture and movement classification, we use the multi-sensor IMU data from Airaksinen
331   et al. (2022). The data contains 24-channel signals from infants (three gyroscope and three accelerom-
332   eter channels, four limbs) with a sampling rate of 52 Hz. We window the signals into 120-sample
333   frames (approx. 2.3 seconds) with 50% overlap. For further details about the dataset, see Airaksinen
334   et al. (2022).
335   For model pre-training, we use a 387-hour set of unlabeled IMU data from infant free-form play that
336   has been automatically screened for signal quality (Vaaras et al., 2023b). This subset contains 4669
337   sequences of 260 consecutive frames, each corresponding to five minutes of data. As the encoder,
338   we use the same four-layer CNN-based encoder architecture as in Airaksinen et al. (2022) with
339   three minor modifications that were found to improve training efficiency and system performance
340   when replicating the experiments of Airaksinen et al. (2022): We added layer normalization after
341   the last two convolutions to make the pre-training process more stable, the kernel size of the second
342
      convolutional layer of the CNN encoder was changed from [4,5] to [3,5], and the originally temporally
      asymmetrical padding was set to [1,2] to make it symmetric. The pre-training data is randomly split
343
      into a training and validation set in a ratio of 80:20 sequences, and we input 260-frame sequences
344
      into the model.
345
346   For fine-tuning our pre-trained models, we use a 29-hour (91,449 frames) labeled dataset of IMU data
347   (41 recordings and distinct participants) for two separate tasks: posture classification and movement
348
      classification. The data contains nine annotated movement categories (still, roll left/right, pivot
      left/right, proto/elementary/fluent movement, transition) and seven annotated posture categories
349
      (prone, supine, left/right side, crawl posture, sitting, standing) for each 2.3-second frame. For model
350
      training, we use all annotated data, but we only use the frames in which all annotators agreed on
351   the label for model testing. We train our models separately for both classification tasks using the
352   so-called iterative annotation refinement labels from Airaksinen et al. (2020).

458   4.5   A DDITIONAL H YPERPARAMETER E XPERIMENTS
459
      In order to demonstrate that it is more beneficial during pre-training to mask the latent features
460
      instead of masking the input directly, we ran PFML pre-training for all three datasets twice: either by
461
      masking the inputs or by masking the embeddings. Subsequently, we fine-tuned our models for all
462   five classification tasks, and the results are shown in Table 6 of Appendix C. As can be observed from
463   the results, it is more beneficial for downstream tasks if we alleviate the complexity of the pre-training
464   task for the encoder by masking the embeddings instead of masking the inputs. The only exception
465   was with EEG data, where it did not make a difference whether inputs or embeddings were masked.
466
      For each data modality, we also experimented with different configurations of masking probability
467
      pm and the length of the masks ml . We ran PFML pre-training using different configurations of
468   pm and ml , and then we fine-tuned the pre-trained models. For IMU and speech data, we only
469   experimented with one classification task each, namely classification of movement from IMU data
470   and classification of valence from speech data. The results for different configurations of pm and ml
471   for IMU, speech, and EEG data are shown in Appendix C in Tables 7, 8, and 9, respectively. For IMU
472   data, the differences between different masking strategies are rather small, whereas for speech and
473   EEG data the selection of masking hyperparameters has a notable effect on fine-tuning performance.
474   We also experimented with the effect of discarding some of the functionals in PFML pre-training
475   for IMU data. After pre-training, we fine-tuned our model for movement classification, and the
476   results are presented in Table 10 of Appendix C. The results indicate that using the full set of 11
477   functionals during PFML pre-training provides the best outcome. As the number of discarded
478   functionals increases, the prediction task becomes simpler and the training targets are able to capture
479   less information of the input signal frames, leading to worse fine-tuning performance.
480   Finally, we tested different mask types for PFML pre-training using IMU data. We either replaced
481   the masked embeddings with a fixed vector of zeros, ones, random Gaussian noise (as in e.g. Baevski
482   et al. (2023)), or a learnable mask token (as in e.g. Baevski et al. (2022)). After PFML pre-training
483   using the four different mask types, we fine-tuned the pre-trained models for movement classification.
484   Table 11 of Appendix C presents the comparison results for different mask types. As can be observed,
485   the choice between a mask of ones or random Gaussian noise does not have a notable impact on the
      performance. However, using a learnable mask token yielded slightly worse results than a vector


                                                                     9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      of ones or random Gaussian noise, and a vector of zeros yielded the worst results. We observed
487   that either using a vector of ones, random Gaussian noise, or learnable mask tokens for masking the
488   embeddings promoted embedding variance, whereas using a vector of zeros provided a smaller level
489   of variance for the embedding representations during pre-training. This lower level of variance for
490   embeddings might potentially hinder the fine-tuning process, resulting into a lower performance in
491   downstream tasks.

      5   C ONCLUSION
494
495
      In this paper, we presented PFML, a novel SSL algorithm for time-series data that avoids the common
496   SSL issue of representation collapse. PFML operates by predicting statistical functionals of the
497   input signal corresponding to masked embeddings, given a sequence of unmasked embeddings. We
498   demonstrated the effectiveness of PFML using five different classification tasks across three different
499   data modalities: infant posture and movement classification from multi-sensor IMU data, emotion
500   recognition from speech data, and sleep stage classification from EEG data. Our results show that
501   PFML is superior to a conceptually similar SSL method, MAE. Our results also show that PFML is
502   competitive against the current state-of-the-art data modality agnostic SSL method, data2vec, while
503   being conceptually simpler and without suffering from representation collapse. The fact that PFML
504
      matches the performance of data2vec while also avoiding the issue of representation collapse renders
      PFML more straightforward to apply to new time-series data domains, such as in the case of clinical
505
      time-series data. The present work may also be extended to other domains than time-series data, such
506
      as images where functionals could be computed of, e.g., image patches.
507
508
      Limitations We selected the present set of 11 functionals for their effectiveness across the three
509   data modalities used in the present study, aiming for potential generalizability and a robust start-
510   ing point to other data domains and downstream tasks. However, carefully selecting the number
511   and type of functionals specifically for different modalities may lead to better results than pre-
512   sented here. Also, we did not include data augmentation in our pre-training processes to save
513   computational time for PFML pre-training, as we wanted to pre-compute the functionals before
514   the model training. As shown in e.g. Chen et al. (2020); Grill et al. (2020); He et al. (2022);
515   Balestriero et al. (2023), data augmentation during pre-training may lead to improved performance
516   on downstream tasks. Nonetheless, performing masking for randomly sampled frames is already
517
      a form of data augmentation in itself. Furthermore, other model architectures besides CNN-based
      encoders or Transformer encoder blocks could also be used, and this may improve PFML pre-
518
      training performance. Lastly, we acknowledge that typically SSL pre-training is run with very large
519
      minibatch sizes using multiple GPUs, and the results of the present experiments might improve
520   with larger minibatch sizes. However, to promote reproducibility and encourage other researchers
521   to try PFML, we deliberately pre-trained our models using relatively small minibatches so that
522   the pre-training processes could be run on a single GPU with 16 GB of VRAM. As detailed in
523   Appendix D, our method used only a moderate amount of computational resources.
524
525   Broader Impacts Since the main goal of PFML is to make the algorithm straightforwardly applica-
526   ble to different time-series data domains, our method makes it easier to apply SSL pre-training for
527   time-series data without complex tuning of hyperparameters or the need to profoundly understand
528   the target data domain. As an example, properties of different medical time-series data, such as
529   those obtained with EEG, ECG, or EMG, can be dependent on the clinical environment, the specific
530   measurement equipment and setup, or clinical population being measured (Watson et al., 2019). This
531
      limits the applicability of ’universal’ pre-trained models predominant in computer vision and speech
      technology. In a similar manner, various industrial sensor setups, such as those for system monitoring
532
      and predictive maintenance (accelerometers, magnetometers etc.), can result in data unique to a
533
      particular environment or machine type. In these cases, the use of PFML pre-training can be practical,
534   since applying modality-specific SSL algorithms or fine-tuning pre-trained models from other data
535   modalities might not generalize well to novel time-series data domains. Hence, PFML may promote
536   the use of machine learning as an assisting tool in e.g. clinical healthcare or other limited-data
537   domains. However, as with all classifiers, machine-learning models trained using PFML might make
538   errors. Incorrect model-based decisions, such as incorrect diagnoses, may be detrimental in some
539   cases. Lastly, any bias, private information, or harmful content in the pre-training data can, in theory,
      be reflected to the feature representations that are learned by PFML.

      A    P ROOF OF N ON -C OLLAPSED F EATURE R EPRESENTATIONS IN PFML
757
758
           P RE -T RAINING
759
      This section provides a more detailed mathematical formulation for the proof that PFML pre-training
760
      does not converge to collapsed feature representations.
761
762   Let x be a single- or multi-channel time-series signal, framed into a sequence of short-term frames
763   {x0 , x1 , ...} of N samples each, where xn = {xt , xt+1 , ..., xt+N −1 }. We define a set of m func-
764   tionals, F = {F0 , F1 , ..., Fm−1 }, to be computed for each frame xn to produce a set of computed
765
      functionals fn . Here, we refer to functionals as mathematical operations which map a time series of
      arbitrary length into a single value, such as the mean or variance of the signal. Also, let zn be the
766
      output embeddings of an encoder model given the input xn , and let yn denote the output predictions
767
      of a Transformer-based model given the input zn .
768
769   To formalize the relationships between inputs and outputs, let us define the following functions:
770
             • Let F be the set of functionals that maps the input frames xn to the computed functionals
771
               fn , i.e., fn = F(xn ) = {F0 (xn ), F1 (xn ), ..., Fm−1 (xn )}.
772
773          • Let g be the function that maps the embeddings zn to the predictions yn , i.e., yn = g(zn ).
774
      Let us assume the following in PFML pre-training:
775
776          • Assumption 1: There is temporal variability across the frames xn . Formally, let σ 2 (xn )
777            denote the variance of xn across the frames, and σ 2 (xn ) > 0.
778
             • Assumption 2: Given Assumption 1, the set of non-trivial functionals F computed from
779            xn also contains variance across the frames. Formally, let σ 2 (fn ) denote the variance of fn
780            across the frames, and σ 2 (fn ) > 0.
781
782   Under these assumptions, we aim to show that the predictions yn also contain variance across the
783   frames, i.e., σ 2 (yn ) > 0.
784   In PFML pre-training, the model learns to predict the computed functionals fn given the embeddings
785   zn . The prediction loss L is defined as
786
787                                                      N
788
                                                  1 X              2
                                               L=       (fn − yn )                                            (3)
789                                               N n=1
790
791   for the MSE loss and
792
793                                                       N
                                                     1 X
794                                             L=         |fn − yn |                                         (4)
                                                     N n=1
795
796   for the L1 loss.
797
798   To minimize either the MSE or L1 loss functions (Equations 3 and 4, respectively), the predictions
799
      yn must closely match the computed functionals fn . If yn were to contain zero variance across the
      frames, i.e., σ 2 (yn ) = 0, while fn contains variance, i.e., σ 2 (fn ) > 0, the prediction loss L would be
800
      high. This is because the constant predictions yn would not be able to capture the temporal variability
801
      in fn .
802
803   Therefore, to achieve low prediction loss values, the predictions yn must also contain variance across
804   the frames, i.e., σ 2 (yn ) > 0. Consequently, PFML pre-training does not converge to collapsed
805
      feature representations, as long as Assumptions 1 and 2 hold true. □
806   Given that real-world time-series data generally shows temporal variability, and computed functionals
807   derived from such data are expected to reflect this variability, Assumptions 1 and 2 are valid for most
808   real-world datasets.
