009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            One of the most fundamental, and yet relatively less explored, goals in transfer
012            learning is the efficient means of selecting top candidates from a large number of
013            previously trained models (optimized for various “source” tasks) that would per-
014            form the best for a new “target” task with a limited amount of data. In this paper,
015            we undertake this goal by developing a novel task-similarity metric (BeST) and an
016
               associated method that consistently performs well in identifying the most trans-
               ferrable source(s) for a given task. In particular, our design employs an innovative
017
               quantization-level optimization procedure in the context of classification tasks that
018
               yields a measure of similarity between a source model and the given target data.
019            The procedure uses a concept similar to early stopping (usually implemented to
020            train deep neural networks (DNNs) to ensure generalization) to derive a function
021            that approximates the transfer learning mapping without training. The advantage
022            of our metric is that it can be quickly computed to identify the top candidate(s) for
023            a given target task before a computationally intensive transfer operation (typically
024            using DNNs) can be implemented between the selected source and the target task.
025            As such, our metric can provide significant computational savings for transfer
026            learning from a selection of a large number of possible source models. Through
027
               extensive experimental evaluations, we establish that our metric performs well
               over different datasets and varying numbers of data samples.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Transfer Learning Pan and Yang (2010) Weiss et al. (2016) is a method to increase the efficacy of
033   learning a target task by transferring the knowledge contained in a different but related source task.
034   It is known that the effectiveness of supervised learning depends on the amount of labeled data.
035   However, for various practical problems (e.g., medical imaging), collecting large quantities of la-
036   beled data might not be easy, as data collection and labeling is a tedious, expensive, and sometimes
037   infeasible task (data is scarce, e.g., rare medical diseases). By employing transfer learning, we can
038   enhance performance even with limited labeled data. Talking specifically of image classification,
039
      research in Oquab et al. (2014) showed how image representations learned with convolutional neu-
      ral networks (CNNs) on large-scale annotated datasets can be efficiently transferred to other visual
040
      recognition tasks with limited data. The work in Yosinski et al. (2014) studied the impact of trans-
041
      ferring features learned in different CNN layers and Long et al. (2015) describes how deeper layers
042   can be more effectively transferred to a target CNN. The core idea of transfer learning is that differ-
043   ent models trained for different sets of classes might learn some common features about the image
044   in the initial layers that are not too task-specific. Recent studies show how transfer learning can be
045   performed for CNNs by initializing the target neural network using feature-learned source CNN and
046   adding a few dense layers to map the source model output to target labels.
047
      From the literature Yosinski et al. (2014), it is evident that the choice of source model affects the
048   target task performance as not every source shares similarities with the target, with some sources
049   resulting in a phenomenon called negative transfer Wang et al. (2019). In transfer learning research
050   the usual question is - given a source and target task, how to transfer? In contrast, in this work,
051   we are trying to answer - given a target task and many different source tasks, which source to
052   choose for best transfer? With the increasing availability of pre-trained learning models for a variety
053   of classification tasks, it is now more important to assess the similarity between many existing
      source models S1 , S2 , ...Sn and a new target task T to find the best matching source task. The

      straightforward approach for source selection is to train the target model using each source to find
055   its accuracy. However, this is generally time-consuming given the complexity of large-scale neural
056   networks. This calls for a quick task similarity metric that will help us rank a group of candidate
057   source tasks without computationally intense neural network training for each of them. Specifically,
058   this metric must measure the potential of a source model to be favorably utilizable by a given target
059   task. We do not aim to propose the metric as an alternative to training but rather as a pre-processing
060   step before training with the best source. Moreover, for it to be useful, we would like the metric to
061   have the following properties –
062
             • Reliable in identifying good pairs: The metric provides an accurate and reliable ranking
063
               for source models with high transfer learning performance (e.g., > 90% accuracy).
064
065
             • Time efficient: Metric calculation should provide significant computational savings result-
               ing in less time taken as compared to training a transfer learning model.
066
067          • Architecture indifferent: The metric does not use any knowledge of the architecture of
068
               the layers added on top of the pre-trained model. Hence, it should perform equally well if
               compared against different architectures if they have near-optimal accuracy performance.
069
070   In this work, we undertake this challenge for the scenario where source and target tasks are image
071   classifiers. Our contributions can be summarized as — We propose a novel quantization-based
072   approach to measure relatedness between a given source-target task pair to evaluate transfer learning
073   performance. Our results show that our method can accurately rank source tasks for source models
074   with a high transfer learning accuracy. We have a novel way to use the concept of generalization and
075   early stopping, typically used in neural network training, to a problem outside the typical use case.

077   2    R ELATED W ORK
078
079   Quantization as a technique is not new to machine learning as it has been used to reduce the com-
080   putational and memory costs of running inference by representing the weights and activations with
081   low-precision data types like 8-bit integers instead of the usual 32-bit floating point. Several works
082   Courbariaux et al. (2015); Gupta et al. (2015); Micikevicius et al. (2018), Gholami et al. (2021),
083   talk about breakthroughs of half-precision and mixed-precision training. However, to the best of
084   our knowledge, our approach to using quantization to transform model softmax output to evalu-
085
      ate task-transferability is novel. Authors in Dwivedi and Roig (2019) propose an approach to use
      Representation Similarity Analysis (RSA) to obtain a similarity score among tasks by computing
086
      correlations between models trained on different tasks. A study for automated source selection for
087
      transfer learning in CNNs using an entropy-based transferability measure is presented in Afridi et al.
088   (2018). Methods like NCE Tran et al. (2019) and LEEP Nguyen et al. (2020) use the source and
089   target labels to estimate transfer performance. In reality, we often do not have access to the source
090   data but only to the source model as considered in our setup. Other methods like LogME You et al.
091   (2021), GBC Pándy et al. (2022) and H-score Bao et al. (2019) use the source model embeddings
092   and target data to estimate transferability. The work in Dai et al. (2019) evaluates transferability for
093   Named Entity Recognition (NER) tasks in NLP. However, the method used domain-specific knowl-
094   edge of NLP tasks, and hence cannot be generalized for other tasks (e.g., image classification). A
095   similarity measure based on a restricted Boltzmann machine is proposed in Bou Ammar et al. (2014)
096
      to automatically select the best source task for transfer in the context of reinforcement learning.

146   Problem Statement: Given a set of p pre-trained source models S = {fs1 , fs2 , ..., fsp } and a target
147   dataset D, say Ti = T (fsi , fc , D) represents the ground truth of transferability for ith source. We
148   want to define a transferability measure that takes the source model and target dataset as input, such
149   that if Mi represents the score given by the measure for ith source, the transferability ranks of the
150   source models according to {Mi }pi=1 are very close to the ranks calculated using {Ti }pi=1 .
151   To avoid using traditional neural network training to get the transferability of a source model fs to
152   a target dataset D, we need to derive an analytical function corresponding to the custom model fc ,
153   that maximizes the prediction accuracy on target data. To ensure that its architecture-indifferent, it
154   should only using the distribution of softmax output2 and target labels. Given that we have limited
155   target data samples, the estimation of this continuous distribution is imprecise.
156
      To understand the need for quantization, assume that the source and target models are binary clas-
157   sifiers, and consider a ‘hardmax’ case where the softmax output [p1 , p2 ] is transformed to a 2 × 1
158   one-hot discrete vector. In this case, there are only 4 choices for the custom model mapping (binary
159   input to binary output) and it is easy to formulate the accuracy of each choice based on the estimation

163   tion to one-hot vectors, affecting the accuracy of the mapping. The quantization approach helps us
164   use the best of both worlds, where instead of mapping the softmax to a one-hot ‘hard’ vector, we can
165   perform a custom transformation to a desired precision. It helps reduce our setup from a real-valued
166   mapping (softmax to finite label) to a quantized mapping (one-hot vector to finite label) resulting in

      Our algorithm (BeST) for best pre-trained source model selection calculates the optimal quantization
169   level and corresponding metric for each model using target train and validation data. The source
170   model with the highest metric is the model most suitable for the target task. The key part of this
171   algorithm is how the metric is calculated and how well it can represent the performance of transfer
172   learning. In Subsection 4.1, we define the quantization function for any m-ary source to n-ary target
173   and Subsection 4.2 talks about the necessary notations and definitions. Subsection 4.3 talks about the
174   variation of train and validation accuracies as the quantization level increases. Finally, in Subsection
175   4.4 we present the definition of our transferability metric and the algorithm to compute it.

      6    C ONCLUSION AND FUTURE WORK
529
530
      In this paper, we studied the problem of selecting best pre-trained source model for transfer learn-
      ing for a given target task with limited data. We propose BeST, a novel quantization-based task-
531
      similarity metric to measure transferability without needing a classical training process. The exper-
532
      imental results on different datasets show that the metric can accurately rank and predict the best
533   pre-trained source model from a given group of models. It is shown that the metric is indifferent
534   to the architecture of the custom model used until all of them have near-optimal performance. The
535   performance of our metric increases with an increase in the number of data samples. We also show
536   that our metric provides significant time savings over training a neural network for transfer learning
537   implementation. There are certain limitations when scaling to multiclass cases where the compu-
538   tation time increases non-linearly with an increase in the number of data samples for transfer from
539   source classifying a large number of classes. A possible future direction is to refine the method to
      overcome scalability challenges.
