009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Distribution shifts between training and test graphs typically lead to the decreased
012            performance of well-trained graph neural network (GNN) models, negatively af-
013            fecting their ability to generalize in real-world applications. Although there have
014            been advances in addressing graph distribution shifts through various model archi-
015            tectures and training strategies, implementing existing solutions in practical GNN
016
               deployment and serving at test time can be challenging, as they often necessitate
               significant modifications or retraining of the GNNs. To address such challenges,
017
               in this work, we propose a novel method, i.e., Test-Time Graph REBirth, dubbed
018
               TT-GREB, to effectively generalize the well-trained GNN models to the test-time
019            graphs under distribution shifts by directly manipulating the test graph data. Con-
020            cretely, we develop an overall framework designed by two principles, corresponding
021            to two sub-modules: (1) prototype extractor for re-extracting the environment-
022            invariant features of the test-time graph; and (2) environment refiner for re-fining
023            the environment-varying features to explore the potential shifts. Furthermore, we
024            propose a dual test-time graph contrastive learning objective with an effective
025            iterative optimization strategy to obtain optimal prototype components and envi-
026            ronmental components of the test graph. By reassembling these two components,
027
               we obtain a newly reborn test graph, which is better suited for generalization on
               the well-trained GNN model with shifts in graph distribution. Extensive experi-
028
               ments on real-world graphs under diverse test-time distribution shifts verify the
029
               effectiveness of the proposed method, showcasing its superior ability to manipulate
030            test-time graphs for better GNN generalization ability.

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Recent advances in graph neural networks (GNNs) have achieved great success with promising
036   learning abilities for various graph structural data in numerous real-world applications (Zhang et al.,
037   2022; Zheng et al., 2022a;b; 2023c;a; Jin et al., 2022; Zheng et al., 2022c). Well-designed GNN
038   models are ultimately intended for practical deployment and serving on various graph learning
039
      tasks (Zheng et al., 2023b; Liu et al., 2023b; Yu et al., 2023). However, these expertly trained GNNs
      generally experience significant performance degradation due to the graph distribution shift issue
040
      between the training and the test graphs (Wu et al., 2022b; Liu et al., 2023a; Chen et al., 2023b; Yu
041
      et al., 2023). The main reason behind such distribution mismatch lies in the underlying environmental
042   variations, with time-related attribute changes, agnostic corruptions, and inconsistent graph data
043   collection procedures (Sui et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023b; Jin et al., 2023). These factors would lead
044   to considerable differences in node contexts, graph structures, and the overall scale of graphs during
045   the test-time stage.
046
      To overcome the model generalization challenge caused by graph distribution shifts, there is a
047
      growing focus on research into learning with distribution shifts on graphs, from the model-centric
048   perspective (Wu et al., 2022b; You et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023c; Wu et al., 2020). Typically,
049   these existing methods incorporate invariant representation learning into GNN development through
050   customizing model architectures and training strategies (Xu et al., 2019; Zhu et al., 2021; Liu et al.,
051   2022; Wu et al., 2022a). However, in real-world GNN deployment and serving, it may not be
052   always practical to re-design GNN model architectures or re-train well-trained GNN models by test-
053   time fine-tuning. This would be more challenging when these well-trained GNNs are continuously
      in service online, as accessing and modifying their parameters becomes more difficult. Given

068   all these circumstances including complex training-test graph distribution shifts and inaccessible
069   online-serving GNN model fine-tuning, an intriguing problem emerges:
070
071    Question: Is it possible to give the rebirth of the test-time graph data from the data-centric perspective, to
072    serve various well-trained GNN models for better generalization under distribution shifts?
073
074   In this work, we provide a feasible graph data-centric solution to answer this question by enhancing
075   the test-time graph data quality, without accessing the training graph data or modifying the well-
076   trained GNNs, as shown in Fig. 1. Specifically, the original test graph would undergo a rebirth
077   process to emerge as a new test graph, which is then fed into the well-trained GNN model for direct
078   inference without retraining or fine-tuning. In the test-time graph rebirth process, we identify two
079   essential components that decomposed a graph under distribution shifts: (a) the environment-invariant
080   component retains consistent informative features (such as node class label prototypes) across various
081
      training and test graph distributions; (b) the environment-varying component dictates the extent of
      shifts in graph data distribution between training and test.
082
083   In light of this, we propose a novel test-time graph rebirth method for serving good GNN generaliza-
084   tion under graph distribution shifts with two principles: (1) re-extracting the environment-invariant
085   features of the test-time graph for identifying the predictive pattern of node class label prototypes with
086   a prototype extractor, and (2) re-fining the environment-varying features to explore the potential
087
      shifts in the training-test distribution with a environment refiner, leading to the alignment of the
      training-test environment latent space with improved generalization capabilities for online GNN
088
      deployment. Furthermore, due to the unknown test-time graph node labels, we propose a dual
089
      test-time graph contrastive learning objective with self-supervision signals, along with an effective
090   iterative optimization strategy to obtain expressive prototype features and environmental features
091   of the test graph. In this way, we preserve prototype features to determine the node class label’s
092   predictive patterns, meanwhile, we adjust the environmental features of the original test graph to
093   match those of the training graph. Then, these components are reassembled into a newly reborn test
094   graph, which better suits the well-trained GNNs’ generalization under distribution shifts.
095
      In summary, the contributions of this work are as follows:
096
097                • Graph Data-centric Paradigm. We introduce a novel graph data-centric paradigm, Test-
098                  Time Graph REBirth (TT-GREB), designed to enhance the generalization ability of well-
099                  trained GNNs to real-world test graphs experiencing distribution shifts at test time.
100                • Innovative Solution. We develop a comprehensive framework with two core components:
101                  a prototype extractor that identifies invariant features within test-time graphs, and an envi-
102                  ronment refiner that adjusts varying features to align the latent spaces of training and test
103                  environments. These components are effectively optimized through a dual test-time graph
104                  contrastive learning objective and an iterative optimization strategy.
105                • Comprehensive Evaluation. We evaluate the proposed method on real-world test graphs
106                  under diverse graph distribution shifts. Extensive experimental results reveal consistently
107                  strong generalization ability on various well-trained GNN models, providing compelling
                     evidence for the efficacy of the proposed method.

      2    R ELATED W ORK
109
110   Test-time Adaption. Our work is related to test-time adaption methods, which aim to enable dynamic
111   tuning of the pre-trained model to generalize and adapt well to the test samples (Sun et al., 2020;
112   Wang et al., 2020; Chen et al., 2023a; Liang et al., 2020; Jang et al., 2022). The pioneering work is
113   Test-Time Training (TTT) (Sun et al., 2020), which re-trains model weights at test time on a single
114   test image sample through a self-supervised learning objective with an auxiliary task. Moreover,
115   TENT (Wang et al., 2020) proposes a fully test-time adaption problem that only accesses model
116   parameters and the test data. Considering most existing methods are model-centric and serve the
117   computer vision domain, which might not fit GNN models and graph data, GTRANS (Jin et al., 2023)
118
      first proposes to adapt test graph data without accessing the training procedure and GNN architectures.
      However, GTRANS employs a fully parameterized matrix to represent modified test-time graph node
119
      features and engages in graph structure learning within the dynamic node representation learning
120
      process. For one thing, the fully parameterized approach to node feature learning merely adds to
121   the original node features, which narrows the scope for learning updated test graphs. For another
122   thing, GTRANS uses a binary-space projected gradient descent method, limiting the flexibility in
123   handling diverse graph structures. In this work, we conduct the parameterized decomposition of the
124   test graph to give it a rebirth by re-extracting the invariant features and re-fining test-time varying
125   features of test graphs. This allows for more adaptable modifications to test graphs with an expended
126   optimization space of our proposed TT-GREB.
127
128   Graph Learning Under Distribution Shifts. Our work is also relevant to the research topic of graph
129   learning under distribution shifts, whose goal is to develop a GNN model for better generalization
130   ability on test graphs under graph distribution shifts (Wu et al., 2020; Chen et al., 2023b; Sui et al.,
131   2023; Guo et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2022a; Wang et al., 2022). Typically, UDAGCN (Wu et al.,
132   2020) conducts unsupervised graph domain adaption with a domain adversarial method to learn
133
      domain-invariant embeddings across the source domain and the target domain. AIA (Sui et al.,
      2023) proposes graph data augmentation with effective GNN learning to handle the covariate shift on
134
      graphs for the graph classification task. Different from these graph model-centric methods, in this
135
      work, we mainly focus on modifying the test graph data with self-supervision signals to deal with
136   the distribution-shifted test graph learning problem. Therefore, the critical distinction between our
137   work and existing methods is that our work is primarily concerned with a graph data-centric learning
138   paradigm to directly manipulate test graph data for serving better the GNN generalization ability
139   under distribution shifts.

252   3.2        M ETHODOLOGY
253
      According to the two principles, in this work, we propose a novel method, named TT-GREB, to
254
      address the test-time graph rebirth problem for serving good GNN generalization under distribution
255   shifts at the test time.
256
257   The overall framework is illustrated in Fig. 3. Concretely, the proposed TT-GREB consists of
258
      two components: (1) a prototype extractor identifies features that remain unchanged in different
      environments, mainly determined by node class labels, where these features can be consistent in both
259
      training and test graphs and reflect the predictive pattern of GNN models; and (2) an environment
260
      refiner adjusts the environment-varying features of the test-time graph, to match the latent distribution
261   of the training graph’s environment. This alignment ensures that the GNN, which is well-trained
262   on the training graph, demonstrates strong generalization capability on the rebirth test graph. More
263   details of the modular design of our proposed TT-GREB are presented below.

      3.2.2   O PTIMIZATION O BJECTIVE .
313
314   During the test time, to improve the well-trained GNN model generalization under graph distribution
315   shifts, the significant challenge faced with graph data-centric transformation through test-time graph
316   rebirth is the scarcity of test ground-truth labels. Consequently, this makes it more challenging to
317   conduct supervised learning by minimizing the cross entropy loss, which is the most readily and
318   straightforward solution. Therefore, with (1) the insufficient node class labels of the test graph, (2) the
319   inaccessible training graph for online GNN deployment, and (3) the unknown graph distribution shifts,
320
      it is imperative to develop an effective self-supervised learning objective along with an appropriate
      optimization strategy.
321
322   In this work, we propose a dual test-time graph contrastive learning objective with an effective
323   iterative optimization strategy. For one thing, we use self-supervise signals from the well-trained
      GNN’s output node representations to guide the learning of prototype extractor with the graph


                                                          6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      contrastive learning loss LPro , following the parameter-free principle (Jin et al., 2023). Through the
325   lens of the general graph contrastive learning scheme, the core idea is to maximize the similarity
326   between two consistent views of the same graph, and to minimize the similarity when the views are
327   not in agreement. For another thing, we perform a decomposition of environment-varying features on
328   the test graph, by ensuring the environmental discrepancy under the graph distribution shifts during
329   the test time. Considering the inaccessible training graph, we encourage the discrepancy between the
330   environmental characteristics of the reborn test-time graph and the original test-time graph, leading
331   to the graph environment refinement loss LEnv to optimize the proposed environment refiner. As
332   illustrated in the lower section of Fig.3, these two optimization objectives are iteratively refined using

530
531
      In this work, we proposed a new graph data-centric method, test-time graph rebirth (TT-GREB),
      aimed at enhancing the generalization ability of GNN models to test-time graphs affected by distri-
532
      bution shifts through direct manipulation of the test graph data. The overall framework includes a
533
      prototype extractor for learning environment-invariant features and an environment refiner for adjust-
534   ing environment-sensitive features, followed by a dual test-time graph contrastive learning objective
535   and an efficient iterative optimization strategy, facilitating the extraction of optimal prototype and
536   environmental components of the reborn test graph. Our extensive experiments on real-world graph
537   datasets under various test-time distribution shifts confirm the superiority of our method, underscoring
538   its innovative capacity to modify test-time graphs for enhanced GNN generalization. A potential
539   limitation of this work is its current focus on node-level tasks, but future extensions are expected to
      adapt it for broader applications in graph-level and edge-level tasks.
