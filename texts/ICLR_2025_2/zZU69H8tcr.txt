009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               Large Language Models (LLMs) have achieved significant success in the field of
012
               Natural Language Processing (NLP). However, due to their large model size and
013
               high inference costs, the application of LLMs is restricted. Pruning is regarded as
014            an effective method to reduce the size of LLMs. Mainstream pruning methods for
015            LLMs typically apply a uniform ratio to prune all the layers or determine layerwise
016            sparsity based on simple criteria. Such manually or semi-manually designed prun-
017            ing strategies often lead to suboptimal results, which makes reinforcement learn-
018            ing a feasible solution. However, current reinforcement learning-based pruning
019            methods usually have redundant environment designs or multiple agents, render-
020            ing them ill-suited to massive LLMs. Hence, we propose SparsitySolver, which
021            first incorporates reinforcement learning into the pruning of LLMs, supporting
022
               various pruning granularity. SparsitySolver employs an improved reinforcement
               learning environment, allowing for a rapid pruning strategy search with a small-
023
               scale agent. Moreover, to lessen the performance decline caused by structured
024
               pruning, we propose a compensation method capable of restoring performance
025            without introducing additional parameters to the model. We evaluate our approach
026            on LLaMA-V1/V2, Mistral, and the OPT families across multiple pruning granu-
027            larities, achieving performances surpassing the state-of-the-art methods.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated outstanding performance in a wide range of lan-
034   guage tasks Zhang et al. (2022); Brown et al. (2020); Bubeck et al. (2023); Touvron et al. (2023a;b).
035   However, LLMs come with a substantial model size and high inference costs, meaning deploying
036   pre-trained models demands expensive computational resources. Hence, techniques aiming at re-
037   ducing the size and computational demands of LLMs, commonly known as model compression, are
038   gaining increasing attention. Numerous compression methods for LLMs have been introduced, en-
039
      compassing distillation, quantization, and pruning Hu et al. (2021); Frantar et al. (2022); Xiao et al.
      (2023); Lin et al. (2023); Lee et al. (2023); Frantar & Alistarh (2023); Ashkboos et al. (2024).
040
041   Pruning is an effective method to reduce the quantity of model parameters and computations. Con-
042   sidering the substantial cost of fine-tuning for LLMs, mainstream research concentrates on post-
043   training pruning of LLMs without fine-tuning Frantar & Alistarh (2023); Ashkboos et al. (2024);
044   An et al. (2024); Wang et al. (2024); Sun et al. (2023). In this area, several pruning methods for
045
      LLMs, including Wanda Sun et al. (2023), SparseGPT Frantar & Alistarh (2023) and SliceGPT
      Ashkboos et al. (2024), opt to prune LLMs using uniform sparsity ratios per layer. Compared to
046
      the costly global pruning An et al. (2024), such a uniform strategy is simpler and more suitable for
047
      large-scale LLMs. In addition, certain approaches propose layerwise sparsity ratios that are non-
048   uniform, an example being OWL Yin et al. (2023) that determines the layerwise sparsity ratio based
049   on the proportion of outliers in each layer, while BESA Xu et al. proposes searching for the optimal
050   pruning rate for each layer in a differentiable manner. However, based on previous studies Wang
051   & Tu (2020); Fang et al. (2023), the differences and inter-dependencies between various layers of
052   the model constitute a complex issue. Factors such as the type of each layer, its location within
053   the network, and the associated operators, all influence the appropriate sparsity ratio for that layer.
      Thereby, a question arises: what is the most suitable sparsity strategy for large language models?

      Given that the massive structure of LLMs usually includes dozens of decoder layers, each containing
079   numerous parameters, the search space for pruning strategy is immense. Thus, manually designing
080   or brute-force exploring sparsity strategy becomes nearly impossible. Reinforcement learning poses
081   a solution to this challenge, with various methods exploring the use of reinforcement learning for
082   pruning strategy search He et al. (2018); Alwani et al. (2022); Yu et al. (2021; 2022). However,
083   the environments constructed by these methods are overly redundant, making them unsuitable for
084   massive LLMs. Also, their environment design is skewed. In the environment designed by AMC
085   He et al. (2018), the rewards for intermediate layers pruning are all zero. Only once the pruning of
086   the final layer is done, can a valid reward be assessed on the test set. This type of environment is
087
      abnormal as reinforcement learning finds it difficult to manage sparse reward scenarios. Therefore,
      we suggest simplifying the pruning environment as a solution to correct the sparse reward and avoid
088
      additional computations arising from dealing with the environment.
089
090   To efficiently and accurately explore the suitable pruning strategy for LLMs, we propose Sparsi-
091   tySolver, a reinforcement learning-based post-training pruning method for LLMs, supporting both
092   structured and unstructured pruning. Furthermore, to address the model damage after structured
093
      pruning, we introduce a compensation method to recover the model’s performance. Fig. 1 illustrates
      an overview of our approach. The contributions are summarized as follows:
094
095
096          • We propose a simple and efficient reinforcement learning environment, improving the
097
               sparse reward environment in existing RL pruning methods without the need for additional
               computation. Within our developed environment, a small-scale RL agent is enough to attain
098
               quick convergence, thereby making it apt for searching pruning strategies for LLMs.
099
100
             • To mitigate the performance loss after structured pruning, we propose reconstruction com-
101            pensation, which requires no additional parameters for recovery.
102
103          • We conduct multiple experiments on LLMs including OPT Zhang et al. (2022), Mistral
104            Jiang et al. (2023), LLaMA-V1 Touvron et al. (2023a), and LLaMA-V2 Touvron et al.
105            (2023b) families, verifying that SparsitySolver can explore more suitable sparsity strate-
106            gies in both structured and unstructured pruning, demonstrating better perplexity than cor-
107            responding state-of-the-art methods.

122   2     R ELATED W ORKS
123
124   LLM Pruning Many studies Frantar & Alistarh (2023); Sun et al. (2023); Yin et al. (2023); Ashk-
125   boos et al. (2024); Ma et al. (2023); An et al. (2024) indicate that pruning is a practical approach
126   for reducing the scale of LLMs. SparseGPT, according to Frantar & Alistarh (2023), utilizes the
127   inverse of the Hessian matrix to prune and subsequently update weights. In a different study, Wanda
128   Sun et al. (2023) brings a new pruning criterion for LLMs, combining the weight magnitude and
129   input activations to retain outliers. Carrying this forward, OWL Yin et al. (2023) introduces a non-
130
      uniform layerwise sparsity ratio, decided based on the proportion of outliers in each layer. BESA
      Xu et al. proposes searching for the optimal pruning rate for each layer in a differentiable manner.
131
      However, the above methods either only support or mainly support unstructured pruning. Since
132
      unstructured pruning leads to irregular sparse patterns and requires specialized hardware support,
133   other approaches are centered around the exploration of structured pruning. LLM-Pruner Ma et al.
134   (2023) performs structured pruning based on gradient information, and then conducts fine-tuning
135   using LoRA Hu et al. (2021). SliceGPT Ashkboos et al. (2024) converts the LayerNorm into RM-
136   SNorm, performs a transformation on every block of the model through computational invariance,
137   and then carries out the corresponding pruning. FLAP An et al. (2024) proposes a framework that in-
138   cludes global structure search and baseline bias compensation. However, the above methods either
139   require fine-tuning or introduce additional parameters. For example, the baseline bias compensa-
140   tion in FLAP introduces bias to linear layers that are initially without bias. The transformation in
141
      SliceGPT goes even further by adding a new linear layer to each skip connection. These compensa-
      tion methods, which require the introduction of additional parameters, contradict the original intent
142
      of pruning and may impact subsequent deployment and inference.
143
144
      Reinforcement Learning-based Pruning Several methods He et al. (2018); Alwani et al. (2022);
145
      Chen et al. (2020); Yu et al. (2021; 2022) propose utilizing RL agents to search for pruning strate-
146
      gies. AMC He et al. (2018) first suggests using reinforcement learning agents to explore pruning
147   strategies. Chen et al. (2020) propose a deep reinforcement learning-based runtime pruning method,
148   where a runtime agent and a static agent jointly make sparsities. DECORE Alwani et al. (2022)
149   utilizes multi-agent reinforcement learning to determine whether each channel should be pruned.
150   GNN-RL Yu et al. (2022) and AGMC Yu et al. (2021) employ Graph Neural Networks to capture
151   the features of the pruned network, and then use reinforcement learning to search for effective prun-
152   ing strategies. However, the aforementioned methods either require complex environment handling,
153   such as GNN-RL and AGMC, need cooperation among multiple agents, like in the case of DECORE
154   and DRL-based methods, or involve abnormal environments like AMC. Such complex methods are
155
      unsuitable for exploring pruning strategies in large-scale LLMs.

210   Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) Multiple reinforcement learning algorithms aim to search
211   within continuous action spaces, examples include Deep Deterministic Policy Gradient (DDPG)

215   tiable mapping from pruning strategy to rewards. Given the simplicity of our designed environment
      state, we can further reduce the size of the critic network.

525   5   C ONCLUSION
526
527
      We propose SparsitySolver, a reinforcement learning-based method for large language model prun-
528   ing that allows for various levels of granularity. In order to more effectively explore suitable pruning
529   strategies for LLMs, we introduce reinforcement learning search into LLM pruning for the first time.
530   Through our enhanced reinforcement learning environment, the agent can converge in a short pe-
531   riod and quickly derive a pruning strategy. Furthermore, we propose a reconstruction compensation
532   method for structured pruning to recover the model performance without introducing additional pa-
533   rameters. Our experimental results have confirmed the efficacy of our method. We hope our work
534   can provide assistance in the design of future LLM pruning strategies.
