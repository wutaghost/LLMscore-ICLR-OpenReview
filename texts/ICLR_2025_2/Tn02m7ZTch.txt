009                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011            The Dense Associative Memory generalizes the Hopfield network by allowing for sharper
012            interaction functions. This increases the capacity of the network as an autoassociative
013            memory as nearby learned attractors will not interfere with one another. However, the
014            implementation of the network relies on applying large exponents to the dot product of
015            memory vectors and probe vectors. If the dimension of the data is large the calculation can
016            be very large and result in imprecisions and overflow when using floating point numbers
017            in a practical implementation. We describe the computational issues in detail, modify the
018
               original network description to mitigate the problem, and show the modification will not
               alter the networks’ dynamics during update or training. We also show our modification
019
               greatly improves hyperparameter selection for the Dense Associative Memory, removing
020
               dependence on the interaction vertex and resulting in an optimal region of hyperparameters
021            that does not significantly change with the interaction vertex as it does in the original
022            network. Our modifications also allow us to train a Dense Associative Memory with larger
023            interaction vertices than have been used in any previous literature.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Autoassociative memories are a class of neural networks that learn to remember states, typically also allow-
029   ing nearby states to iterate towards similar learned states. These networks act as memories for the learned
030   states, reconstructing lost information and correcting errors in probe states. The Hopfield network (Hopfield,
031   1982; 1984) is perhaps the most studied model in the class. However, as with all autoassociative memories,
032   the Hopfield network suffers from capacity issues – the number of states that can be stored in a network
033
      without error is limited. In the Hopfield network with Hebbian learning, this has been shown to be roughly
      0.14N for a network of dimension N (McEliece et al., 1987; Hertz, 1991). The Dense Associative Memory,
034
      also known as the modern Hopfield network, generalizes the classical Hopfield network by introducing an
035
      interaction function parameterized by an interaction vertex (Krotov & Hopfield, 2016; 2018). This func-
036   tion controls the range of the influence for learned states, allowing control of the sizes of the attractors
037   and increasing the network capacity. Krotov & Hopfield (2016) also introduce several other generalizations
038   which are parameterized by additional hyperparameters relating to learning, including the initial learning
039   rate, learning rate decay, momentum, learning temperature and the exponent on the error term. Additional
040   hyperparameters were introduced such as the form of the interaction function, the number of memory vec-
041   tors, and more. In effect, the Dense Associative Memory is a potentially more powerful autoassociative
042   memory, but at the cost of increased complexity and reliance on hyperparameter tuning.
043   We focus on the implementation details of the Dense Associative Memory. In particular, we show the exact
044   form given by Krotov and Hopfield suffers from issues relating to computation and numerical stability. This
045   form calculates the dot product between two vectors of length N then immediately applies a potentially large
046   exponentiation based on the interaction function. This can cause inaccuracies in the floating point numbers

      used for computation, or even completely overflow them. In Section 4 we show a modification to the original
048
      form – a normalization and shifting of scaling factors – that prevents the computational problems, and
049   prove that the modifications do not change the network behavior for a specific class of interaction functions:
050   homogenous functions. Fortunately, the typical interaction functions – the polynomial interaction function
051   (Equation 7) and rectified polynomial interaction function (Equation 8) – are in this class. We show our
052   modifications do not alter the properties of the autoassociative memory, such as the capacity, but do appear
053   to have desirable effects on the network over the course of training. In Section 5 we provide experimental
054   results that show our modified network has a stable region of optimal hyperparameters across a wide range
055   of interaction vertices. This is in comparison to the original network which had the optimal hyperparameters
056   shift dramatically as the interaction vertex changed even for the same dataset. We also show that the optimal
057
      region of hyperparameters is no longer heavily dependent on the size of the data vectors or the interaction
      vertex, meaning applying the Dense Associative Memory to a new task will not require massively retuning
058
      the hyperparameter selections. A comprehensive list of our results, shown in Appendix A and B, demonstrate
059
      successful training of a Dense Associative Memory with interaction vertices up to n = 100, an arbitrary
060   stopping point with indications of higher interaction vertices being possible and stable. Current literature
061   has not discussed using an interaction vertex this large.

      2   L ITERATURE R EVIEW
080
081
      Our proposed method of shifting the scaling factors within the interaction function does not appear to have
082
      been suggested previously, and other implementations of the Dense Associative Memory do not seem to
083   have included it. However, many implementations of the Dense Associative Memory use the feed-forward
084   equivalence set forth by Krotov & Hopfield (2016). This equivalence allows the Dense Associative Memory
085   to be expressed with some approximations as a feed-forward densely connected neural network with a single
086   hidden layer. This architecture is much easier to implement using traditional deep learning software libraries.
087   The feed-forward equivalent model implicitly implements our proposed changes by selecting values of the
088   scaling factor that negate terms arising from a Taylor expansion. This may help explain why the feed-forward
089   version of the model is more stable and popular than the auto-associative version.
090   Normalization is a typical operation in neural networks. In autoassociative memories specifically, we may
091   apply a normalization term to provide a constant power throughout network calculations, which ensures
092   calculations are proportional only to the magnitudes of the learned weights rather than the magnitude of
093   the probe vector. Even more specifically, in the Hopfield network this is typically achieved by using binary

                                                           2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


094
      valued vectors. It has been shown networks using these vectors have the same behavior as networks using
095
      graded (continuous value) neurons (Hopfield, 1984). Normalization may also be applied in the learning
096   rule, such as in the Hebbian learning rule (Hebb, 1949). Normalization in learning may be used to simply
097   scale the weights into something more interpretable, as in the Hebbian, or to achieve a different behavior
098   during training. For example, batch normalization aims to improve training by normalizing the inputs to a
099   layer across a batch – allowing the network to focus only on the variations in training data rather than the
100   potentially overwhelming average signal (Ioffe & Szegedy, 2015). Layer normalization is a technique used
101   in training recurrent neural networks and removes the dependence on batch size (Ba et al., 2016). These
102   normalizations techniques are more complex than what we suggest. Our modifications are not aiming to
103   supersede these techniques in the Dense Associative Memory but simply improve network stability and
104
      practicality on an implementation level. Moreover, our suggestions do not exclude the possibility of using
      these other normalization techniques.
105
106   Networks related to the Dense Associative Memory have employed some normalization techniques in a sim-
107   ilar manner to our work. Perhaps most closely related is the continuous, attention-like Hopfield network
108   (Ramsauer et al., 2021) which has shown promising results in the realm of transformer architectures. Ram-
109   sauer et al. operate over a slightly different domain; spherical vectors rather than bipolar vectors. While
110
      the vector magnitude is still constant, the network has changed rather significantly from the one introduced
      by Krotov and Hopfield, which may slightly change the arguments we make below. However, we note that
111
      no analysis of the network stability in relation to floating point accuracy is made, and the remainder of our
112
      modifications are not applied (e.g. shifting scaling factors inside the interaction function), which our work
113   expands on considerably. Further works have applied a similar normalization, albeit without noting why it is
114   useful for network stability (Millidge et al., 2022; Liang et al., 2022; Alonso & Krichmar, 2024). Literature
115   on Dense Associative Memory applications and derivatives discuss normalization either in a separate context
116   or only tangentially. Extensive work has been done on contrastive normalization (a biologically plausible ex-
117   planation of network behavior) in the Dense Associative Memory and its relation to the restricted Boltzmann
118   machine (Krotov & Hopfield, 2021). Other research employs advanced normalization techniques, including
119   some we discuss above such as layer normalization, by treating the Dense Associative Memory as a deep
120   recurrent network (Seidl et al., 2021). Again, these works do not consider shifting the scaling factors within
121
      the interaction function.

      5   H YPERPARAMETER T UNING
343
344
      The original Dense Associative Memory suffers from very strict hyperparameter requirements. Changing
      the value of the interaction vertex significantly changes the optimal hyperparameters for training. We find
345
      that our modifications — particularly, normalizing the similarity scores in the learning rule — remove the
346
      dependence on the interaction vertex.
347
348   We focus on the most important hyperparameters for learning: the initial learning rate and temperature.
349   Other hyperparameters were tuned but did not display behavior as dramatic as we present here. We use a
350
      learning rate decay of 0.999 per epoch, a momentum of 0.0, and an error exponent of m = 1.0. We found
      similar results using a decay rate of 1.0 and higher values for momentum. We also found we did not require
351
      changing the error exponent m, which Krotov & Hopfield (2016) found to be useful in learning higher
352
      interaction vertices. This perhaps indicates we can remove this hyperparameter and simplify the network.
353
354   The network is trained on 20 randomly generated bipolar vectors of dimension 100. Even for the lowest
355   interaction vertex n = 2 this task is perfectly learnable. Larger dimensions and other dataset sizes were
356
      tested with similar results. After training, we probe the network with the learned states; if the probes move
      only a small distance from the learned states, the network operates as an acceptable associative memory.
357
      We measure the average distance from the final, stable states to the learned states, for which a lower value
358
      is better. We repeat the experiment five times for each combination of hyperparameters, of which select
359   interaction vertices are shown in Figure 1. All of our results can be found in Appendix A. In particular,
360   Appendix A.3 shows our results for interaction vertices up to n = 100; far above any interaction vertices
361   documented in other literature. Note that for the unmodified network have avoided floating point overflow
362   by engineering our experiments to remain within the bounds of a double. The performance degradation seen
363   at larger interaction vertices is not due to floating point overflow.
364
      In the original network, the optimal hyperparameter region shifts considerably with the interaction vertex.
365   At even modest interaction vertices we find the optimal region is fleeting enough to not appear in our grid
366   search. It is tempting to claim that a finer grid search may reveal the region to persist. However, inspection
367   of Figure 1e shows that not only has the optimal region vanished at this granularity, but the same region now
368   has high distance measure. Even if the optimal region exists and is very small, it is apparently surrounded
369   by an increasingly suboptimal region. This is troublesome and makes working with the network difficult,
370   especially when altering the interaction vertex even slightly.
371   For our modified implementation of the Dense Associative Memory, we have shifted the scale of the inverse
372   temperature as discussed in Section 4. We find the optimal region shifts slightly for small interaction vertices,
373   but unlike the original network we find the region stabilizes and remains substantial for large interaction
374   vertices. Most notably, we find that the optimal region’s position remains stable and size remains large
375   across many values of the interaction vertex for the same network dimension.

      5.1   H YPERPARAMETER S ELECTION IN C LASSIFICATION TASKS
424
425   We have focused on the Dense Associative Memory as an autoassociative memory, where all neurons are
426   updated at each step and may be updated numerous times until the state reaches stability. Another, perhaps
427   more popular use case of the network in current literature is as a classifier. By splitting the memory vectors
428   into two parts – a section for input data and a section for classes as logits – the network can be run as
429   a classifier by only updating the classification neurons, and only updating those neurons once (Krotov &
430   Hopfield, 2016).
431   We conduct a similar hyperparameter search over both the original and modified implementation of the
432   Dense Associative Memory for a classification as we did for the autoassociative tasks in Section 5. Specifi-
433   cally, we trained a Dense Associative Memory to classify the MNIST dataset and measured the validation F1
434   score for a validation split of 20%. We found that the optimal hyperparameter region in classification tasks
435   was of roughly the same shape, size, and relative location between both the original and modified imple-
436   mentations for each respective interaction vertex. This indicates that our modifications have preserved the
437   hyperparameter stability in classification based tasks, but not improved as seen in Section 5. However, we
438   also observe that the optimal inverse temperature in our modified implementation to be consistently around
439
      β ≈ 1, meaning we have a better idea of where to search for optimal hyperparameters. While not as sig-
      nificant a result as in autoassociative tasks, this result is still useful in working with the Dense Associative
440
      Memory as the location of the optimal hyperparameter region is consistent across different datasets and
441
      tasks. Our full results of these experiments can be found in Appendix B.
442

444   6     C ONCLUSION
445
446   In this work, we have investigated the technical details of the Dense Associative Memory and its implemen-
447   tation. We note that the original network specification leads to floating point imprecision and overflow when
448   calculating intermediate values for both update and learning. We provide details on when this imprecision
449   occurs and show the conditions are more likely when the interaction vertex is large based on the feature-
450   to-prototype transition of the memory vectors (Krotov & Hopfield, 2016). We propose a modification to
451   the network implementation that prevents the floating point issues. We prove our modifications do not alter
452
      the network properties, such as the capacity and autoassociative nature. Our proof relies on the interaction
      function being homogenous, however this property is stronger than is required, and we find empirically that
453
      some nonhomogenous functions also give well-behaved Dense Associative Memories. We then show our
454
      modified network has optimal hyperparameter regions that do not shift based on the choice of interaction
455   vertex for purely autoassociative tasks. For classification like tasks, such as MNIST classification, our mod-
456   ifications do not appear to radically improve the optimal hyperparameter region but rather shift the region to
457   a common location that makes tuning the network easier. Our modifications greatly simplify working with
458   the Dense Associative Memory, as experiments on a dataset do not need to search across a potentially large
459   hyperparameter space for each change in the interaction vertex. We also find several hyperparameters do not
460   need tuning in our experiments, hinting at a potentially simpler network that is easier to tune and interpret.
