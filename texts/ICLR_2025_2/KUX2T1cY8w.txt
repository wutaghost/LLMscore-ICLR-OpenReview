009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               Backpropagation (BP) has long been the cornerstone of deep neural network train-
012            ing. While neural networks trained with backpropagation typically have high ac-
013            curacy and precision, they suffer from limitations in their robustness to adver-
014            sarial perturbation. Biologically plausible (bio-plausible) learning rules, on the
015            other hand, are more robust. Yet, they typically underperform in terms of accu-
016            racy and precision, which has limited their widespread adoption. In this work,
017            we aim to bridge this gap. We propose a novel approach where neural networks
018            are pre-trained using backpropagation and fine-tuned using bio-plausible learn-
019            ing rules. We use several types of Sign-Symmetry learning methods to fine-tune
020
               models pre-trained using backpropagation. We explore the effectiveness of this
               approach in two tasks, image classification and image retrieval, then demonstrate
021
               that it improves robustness against gradient-based adversarial attacks while of-
022
               fering comparable accuracy and precision compared to the use of backpropaga-
023            tion alone. These findings show the benefit of mixing backpropagation and bio-
024            plausible learning rules, suggesting the need for further research by the commu-
025            nity to evaluate this approach on other tasks.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   The human cerebral cortex learns through updating synapses based on input signals that activate
032   multiple regions involved in the learning process Hebb (2005); Markram et al. (1997); Bliss &
033
      Lømo (1973). The complex multilayered structure of our brain’s neural network makes it challeng-
      ing to determine the exact mechanism responsible for learning in the brain Lillicrap et al. (2020).
034
      A common way to address this problem in Deep Artificial Neural Networks is through a credit as-
035
      signment algorithm responsible for updating synaptic connections based on feedback signals. These
036   algorithms typically differ in the way the feedback signal is backpropagated Lansdell et al. (2019).
037   The most widely used credit assignment algorithm is Backpropagation (BP) Rumelhart et al. (1986).
038
039
      While BP has been an indispensable tool for building effective neural networks, studies have shown
      that it is not robust to perturbation attacks Szegedy (2013). Adversarial perturbation is a phe-
040
      nomenon commonly observed in neural networks, wherein carefully crafted inputs can deceive
041
      models into making incorrect predictions. An attacker can exploit the weaknesses of a trained neu-
042   ral network by introducing subtle perturbations to an input image that are not perceivable to the
043   human eye but significantly alter the model’s output. This alteration could be as simple as adding
044   a small amount of noise to the image (e.g., slightly changing pixel values). Such modifications
045   can result in the model’s accuracy dropping dramatically, even though the altered inputs are nearly
046   indistinguishable from legitimate inputs Zhang et al. (2019b).
047
      Unlike backpropagation, bio-plausible learning rules are more robust to adversarial attacks. These
048   learning rules generally refer to training methods for artificial neural networks that closely mimic the
049   mechanisms observed in biological systems. In this approach, properties found in natural learning
050   mechanisms are applied to artificial neural networks, aligning them with their natural counterparts.
051   Examples of these learning rules include Feedback Alignment (FA) Lillicrap et al. (2014) and Sign-
052   Symmetry rules, such as Fixed Random-magnitude Sign-concordant Feedback (frSF), Batchwise
053   Random Magnitude Sign-concordant Feedbacks (brSF), and Uniform Sign-concordant Feedback
      (uSF) Liao et al. (2016).


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      While these learning rules are more robust to adversarial attacks, they typically underperform in
055   terms of accuracy and precision when compared to backpropagation. Multiple studies have demon-
056   strated that the performance disparity between BP and FA can be substantial. For example, Bar-
057   tunov et al. (2018) showed that, on the task of image classification, the BP achieves a top-1 error rate
058   reaching 71.43% which is much better than the 93.03% achieved by FA (on ImageNet). Despite the
059   robustness of bio-plausible learning rules, their inferior performance compared to backpropagation
060   has significantly limited their widespread adoption. In this work, we aim to bridge the gap between
061   BP and bio-plausible methods. We propose to reconsider the integration of bio-plausible methods
062   when training deep neural networks through backpropagation.
063   We suggest a novel approach where neural networks are pre-trained using backpropagation and
064   fine-tuned using bio-plausible learning rules. In particular, we use the Sign-Symmetry learning
065   rules to fine-tune models that were pre-trained using backpropagation. We explore the effectiveness
066   of this approach in two tasks, image classification and hashing-based image retrieval, and show that
067   neural networks trained using this method have significantly better robustness against gradient-based
068   adversarial attacks while being close to backpropagation in terms of accuracy. The main intuition
069
      behind our contribution is the following: the bio-plausible learning algorithms that we consider
      do not rely on computing precise gradients to update weights; making them more resilient against
070
      gradient-based adversarial attacks.
071
072   We assess the effectiveness of our approach in different scenarios: 1) For different backbones pre-
073   trained using backpropagation (Alexnet, VGG16 and ResNet-18); 2) For different bio-plausible
074   learning rules (uSF, frSF, and brSF); 3) For different gradient-based adversarial attacks (2 attacks
075
      for each task); and 4) For different datasets (at least 3 datasets for each task). Our robustness results
      demonstrate that models fine-tuned using our method were more robust to gradient-based adversarial
076
      attacks in all the cases while still having performance that is close to classical backpropagation in
077
      terms of accuracy. These findings show the benefit of mixing backpropagation and bio-plausible
078   learning rules, suggesting the need for further research by the community to evaluate this approach
079   on other tasks.
080
081
      One of the main motivations behind this work is to explore the effectiveness of bio-plausible learning
      methods in the task of fine-tuning (i.e., mapping high-quality embeddings to an output). Our findings
082
      suggest that sign-symmetry bio-plausible learning methods (which we study in this paper) can map
083
      high-quality embeddings to an output, assuming such embeddings are effectively created (in this
084   work we create them using backpropagation). This suggests the need for more research on the
085   development of bio-plausible learning methods that can effectively learn high-quality embeddings,
086   which would enable the use of bio-plausible learning rules end-to-end for the effective training of
087   neural networks.
088
      It also highlights the need to evaluate bio-plausible learning methods using more metrics, such as the
089
      robustness against gradient-based attacks, in addition to the classical metrics used in the literature.
090
091   The contributions of this paper are as follow:
092
             • We propose a novel method for training deep neural networks for the tasks of image classi-
093
               fication and hash-based image retrieval: pre-train backbone models using backpropagation,
094
               and fine-tuning them using Sign-Symmetry learning rules.
095
096
             • We demonstrate that the proposed approach leads to significantly better robustness to
               gradient-based adversarial attacks while still providing close accuracy compared to back-
097
               propagation alone.
098
099          • We open-source all of our code to the research community, opening the door for further
100            research in this area.

104   Backpropagation and Bio-plausible Learning Rules While known to be the most efficient learn-
105   ing algorithm, backpropagation has been criticized for being biologically implausible in many as-
106   pects, particularly the weight transport problem Grossberg (1987); Crick (1989); Schwartz (1993).
107   It is believed that the brain uses asymmetrical feedback signals, whereas BP uses the same weights
      for both the forward and backward passes Lillicrap et al. (2020). Most promising work on bio-


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      logically plausible credit assignment methods builds upon backpropagation and attempts to mini-
109   mize weight transport. Figure 1 illustrates learning methods relevant to this work and the degree
110   to which weights are used to conduct the backward pass. The backpropagation algorithm uses a
111   symmetrical feedback structure, representing the most extreme example of weight transport. Sign-
112   Symmetry Liao et al. (2016) uses the sign matrix of the weights to conduct the feedback pass, with
113   three common forms: Uniform Sign-Concordant Feedback (uSF), which uses the exact sign of the
114   weight matrix, representing a less extreme example of the weight transport problem, Fixed Random
115   Magnitude Sign-Concordant Feedback (frSF), and Batchwise Random Magnitude Sign-concordant
116   Feedbacks (brSF), which are a type of approximate gradient that has been shown to be sufficient for
117
      learning and does not require temporally locked gradients Czarnecki et al. (2017). The raw form
      of the approximate gradient is known as Feedback Alignment (FA) Lillicrap et al. (2014), where a
118
      fixed random feedback matrix is used for learning, entirely avoiding weight transport. This class of
119
      methods, which uses a synthetic backward matrix, was supported by research Lillicrap et al. (2016)
120   that claims symmetry is not necessary to train neural networks. Counter-intuitively, fixed random
121   feedback matrices could force the feedforward synaptic connections to align with their weights by
122   making the error derivative, calculated by the forward weight matrix, close to the synthetic one that
123   is calculated by the synthetic backward matrix Lillicrap et al. (2020), hence the name ‘feedback
124   alignment’. Moreover, biologically plausible alternatives could potentially address BP’s sequential
125   nature that renders it computationally inefficient. Methods relying on local information to update
126   synaptic connections, such as Target Propagation, have been proposed Le Cun (1986); Hinton et al.
127   (2007); Bengio (2014); Lee et al. (2015), but they do not scale in performance compared to back-
128
      propagation Bartunov et al. (2018), discouraging further research on them.

132   The Robustness of Bio-plausible Learning Rules When it comes to assessing the robustness
133   of bio-plausible learning rules, relatively little work has been conducted in this area. In the study
134
      by Sanfiz & Akrout (2021), the authors evaluated the robustness of bio-plausible credit assign-
      ment methods, including FA, uSF, frSF, and others. Their findings demonstrated that these methods
135
      exhibit enhanced performance under certain conditions. The study employed various attack meth-
136
      ods, including white-box (gradient-based) attacks such as FGSM Goodfellow (2014), PGD Madry
137   (2017), APGD Zimmermann (2019), and TPGD Zhang et al. (2019a). For black-box attacks, the
138   researchers utilized the Few-pixel attack Su et al. (2019) and Square attack Andriushchenko et al.
139   (2020). Results from the white-box attacks were particularly useful in highlighting the differences
140   in robustness between backpropagation (BP) and bio-plausible learning rules. However, black-box
141   attacks yielded similar results across the methods and did not provide sufficient evidence to claim
142   any general tendencies in robustness.

194   Sign-Symmetry The Sign-Symmetry algorithm Liao et al. (2016) shares similarities with Feed-

196   symmetrical sign-sharing in feedback (instead of a random matrix, it uses the sign matrix of the

201

202

204

205            M is defined as a matrix of uniform random numbers ∈ [0, 1] and is initialized once and
206            fixed through the training.
207
            3. Batchwise Random Magnitude Sign-concordant Feedbacks (brSF): This is a variation of
208            frSF where the magnitude matrix M is redrawn after each parameter update.

      Hash Adversary Generation HAG Yang et al. (2018) is a technique for generating adversarial
      examples to deceive deep hashing retrieval systems. It employs an iterative optimization process
231
      to modify query images, maximizing the Hamming distance between the original and adversarial
232
      hash codes. To overcome the challenge of discrete binary hash codes, HAG utilizes a surrogate
233   gradient method. The technique primarily focuses on untargeted attacks, which do not aim at specific
234   incorrect images. Instead, it seeks to confuse retrieval systems by producing semantically irrelevant
235   results.
236
237   Smart Deep Hashing Attack SDHA Lu et al. (2021) is an advanced adversarial attack method
238   that targets deep hashing models. Unlike HAG, SDHA adopts a more refined approach by using a
239   dimension-wise Hamming distance strategy to generate adversarial examples. SDHA improves upon
240   previous attacks by incorporating a ranking-sensitive approach. Instead of treating all dimensions
241   of the hash code equally, SDHA prioritizes dimensions that are more vulnerable to perturbation.
242
      This strategy minimizes unnecessary changes to the image while maximizing the attack’s impact on
      the retrieval results. SDHA also factors in the role of relevant images during adversarial example
243
      generation. By considering how similar images influence the average precision (AP) of the retrieval
244
      system, SDHA more effectively degrades the system’s performance. An AP-oriented weight func-
245   tion is used to assign different importance to retrieved images based on their rank, enabling SDHA
246   to target those contributing the most to the system’s overall retrieval performance.

      4   M ETHOD
250
      Tasks We apply our proposed training method to two tasks: image classification, and hashing-
251
      based image retrieval. Since the first is well known, we will provide more details about the second.
252   The task of hashing-based image retrieval aims to map high-dimensional image data into compact
253   binary codes while preserving semantic similarity between images Hussain et al. (2022); Xia et al.
254   (2014). The core idea is to transform images into low-dimensional binary hash codes that can be
255   efficiently stored and quickly compared. The hashing-based image retrieval task involves two main
256   steps. In the first, a hash function is learned. The goal of the hash function is to map input images
257   to continuous encoding while ensuring that the assigned codes are semantically relevant Luo et al.
258   (2022). The following step is the binary code generation: the hidden representation of the input
259   image is binarized using a sign function Yang et al. (2022). During the retrieval process, the binary
260
      codes of query images are compared with those of database images using Hamming distance. This
      allows for rapid identification of potential matches Fang & Liu (2021).
261
262
      Learning Method and Model Architectures We propose to use backpropagation for pre-training
263
      followed by the use of a Sign-Symmetry credit assignment method for fine-tuning. We use a CNN-
264   based backbone model that was pre-trained on ImageNet (IMAGENET1K V1) using backpropaga-
265   tion (e.g., RestNet-18). We then append a task-specific fully connected layer to adapt the network
266   to the particular task, either a hash layer or a classification layer, with its parameters trained from
267   scratch. Subsequently, we fine-tune all network parameters using a Sign-Symmetry method (e.g.,
268   uSF). We do not freeze the weights of the backbone during fine-tuning. We use the hashing loss
269   HyP2 Xu et al. (2022) to fine-tune the task of image retrieval, setting the hashing size k to 32, while
      we use the Cross-Entropy loss function for image classification.

      Datasets We evaluate our approach on datasets of varying complexity, ranging from simple to highly
277

      In hashing-based image retrieval, we adhere to the protocols established in the deep hashing litera-

286   (2024). We use four datasets: CIFAR-10, NUS-WIDE, MS-COCO, and ImageNet100. CIFAR-
287   10 is partitioned into 500 images per class for training, with 100 each for testing and validation.
288   NUS-WIDE Chua et al. (2009), originally containing 269,648 images with 5,018 tags, is filtered to
289   148,332 images based on 21 prevalent concepts, with 10,500 for training and 2,100 each for testing
290   and validation. MS-COCO Lin et al. (2014)’s 2017 release allocates 10,000 images for training and
291   5,000 each for testing and validation. ImageNet-100, a subset of ImageNet, comprises 100 classes
292   with 13,000 training images and 5,000 evenly split between testing and validation. In all cases, the
293
      remaining images serve as the database for querying.
294
      Training Pre-trained backbones, initially trained on ImageNet, were loaded through torchvision.

308   Adverserial Robustness Evaluation Setup Following the literature on adversarial robust-
309
      ness Athalye et al. (2018); Carlini et al. (2019); Uesato et al. (2018); Yuan et al. (2023) and studies
      on the robustness of bio-inspired methods Vuyyuru et al. (2020); Sanfiz & Akrout (2021), we use
310
      the same experimental setup with a few adjustments, which we highlight. Our implementation of
311
      adversarial attacks is based on the Foolbox Python package Rauber et al. (2017) for the classification
312   task and on the work of Yuan et al. (2023) for the hashing-based retrieval task. Following the work
313   by Vuyyuru et al. (2020), we employ the L∞ variants of FGSM and PGD to assess the robustness
314   of classification models. FGSM is a single-iteration method that does not require any hyperparame-
315   ters. For PGD, we set the step size α to ε/3 and executed the algorithm for 5 iterations. We vary the
316   perturbation magnitude ε from 0 to 0.5 and measure the robust accuracy.
317
      We assess the robustness of the hashing-based image retrieval models against two non-targeted ad-
318   versarial attacks: HAG and SDHA. These are iterative methods, for which we set the number of
319   iterations to 5. We vary the perturbation magnitude ε from 0.001 to 0.5. Through this configura-
320   tion setup, we aim to compare the robustness behavior of each learning method as we increase the
321   perturbation magnitude. Our primary focus is not on the attacks’ effectiveness but rather on the
322   relative performance of the hashing models under varying degrees of adversarial perturbation. This
323   explains our decision to set the number of iterations to a relatively low value (5). This choice offers
      advantageous computational efficiency, although it may not converge to optimal results.

524    8                  C ONCLUSION
525
526
527   In conclusion, our proposed approach narrows the gap between bio-plausible learning methods and
528   backpropagation. By integrating backpropagation with Sign-Symmetry methods, we have demon-
529   strated the potential of achieving high robustness while maintaining performance comparable to BP.
530   Improvements in robustness against adversarial attacks were significant and this was achieved on
531
      both tasks, image classification and hashing-based image retrieval. These results hold important
      implications for the field of deep learning, suggesting that biologically inspired learning rules can
532
      address some of the limitations of backpropagation, particularly in terms of adversarial robustness,
533
      without a significant degradation in performance. The observed improvements across various archi-
534   tectures, including AlexNet, VGG-16, and ResNet-18, and datasets of increasing complexity, such as
535   CIFAR-10, ImageNet-100, ImageNet-1000, MS-COCO, and NUS-WIDE, further support the broad
536   applicability and efficacy of this approach. Future research could investigate the application of this
537   hybrid learning approach to a wider range of neural network architectures and tasks. Moreover,
538   understanding the underlying mechanisms that contribute to the increased robustness observed in
539   Sign-Symmetry methods could lead to the development of new biologically inspired learning rules
      that further enhance both performance and robustness.
