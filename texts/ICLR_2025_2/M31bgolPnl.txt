011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) has proven highly ef-
014            fective in aligning Large Language Models (LLMs) with human preferences.
015
               However, the original RLHF typically optimizes under an overall reward, which
               can lead to a suboptimal learning process. This limitation stems from RLHF’s
016
               lack of awareness regarding which specific tokens should be reinforced or sup-
017
               pressed. Moreover, conflicts in supervision can arise, for instance, when a chosen
018            response includes erroneous tokens, while a rejected response contains accurate
019            elements. To rectify these shortcomings, increasing dense reward methods, such
020            as step-wise and token-wise RLHF, have been proposed. However, these exist-
021            ing methods are limited to specific tasks (like mathematics). In this paper, we
022            propose the ”Adaptive Message-wise RLHF” method, which robustly applies to
023            various tasks. By defining pivot tokens as key indicators, our approach adaptively
024            identifies essential information and converts sequence-level supervision into fine-
025            grained, subsequence-level supervision. This aligns the density of rewards and
026
               action spaces more closely with the information density of the input. Experi-
               ments demonstrate that our method can be integrated into various training meth-
027
               ods, significantly mitigating hallucinations and catastrophic forgetting problems,
028
               while outperforming other methods on multiple evaluation metrics. Our method
029            improves the success rate on adversarial samples by 10% compared to the sample-
030            wise approach, and achieves a 1.3% improvement on evaluation benchmarks such
031            as MMLU, GSM8K, HumanEval, etc.

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   In recent years, generative AI models have made significant achievements, with preference align-
037   ment by reinforcement learning playing an essential role in this progress (Ouyang et al., 2022; Tou-
038   vron et al., 2023; Rafailov et al., 2024; Dubey et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2024a; OpenAI et al., 2024).
039
      These methods, such as reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF), mostly involve sam-
      pling from the policy, obtaining rewards, enhancing the logits of positive samples according to
040
      rewards, and reducing the logits of negative samples. However, dealing with sparse rewards is chal-
041
      lenging in reinforcement learning: can large language models effectively learn whether each token
042   is correct through sparse reward signals, like scoring on a whole sample? This paper presents a the-
043   oretical analysis and experimental investigation into this issue, aiming to identify the most effective
044   strategy of reward signals for guiding the utilization of reward models in preference alignment by
045   reinforcement learning.
046
      The work of Rafailov et al. (2024) represents the autoregressive reward models using a contextual
047
      bandit framework. Studies by Radford et al. (2019) and Zhong et al. (2024) conceptualize the reward
048   model as a token-level Markov Decision Process (MDP). These approaches illustrate that the reward
049   model possesses fine-grained reward capabilities, but they do not provide the error equation between
050   the reward signal generated by the model and the actual reward scores. Building on this foundation,
051   our paper further quantifies the error in the reward signal. Our work indicates that this error mainly
052   arises from the coarse-grained nature of the reward signal compared to the actual rewards and the
053   inherent stochastic errors within the reward model itself. The coarse-grained reward signal can be
      optimized by reducing its granularity into a finer granularity, while the inherent stochastic errors can


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      be mitigated by using the overall reward of a longer sequence to represent the rewards of individual
055   tokens or subsequences. This indicates that when assigning a reward signal to a sequence, it is
056   important to use a fine-grained approach to provide different scores for its various parts. However,
057   overly granular segmentation can increase the reward error. This demonstrates the need to align the
058   density gap of the information within the sequence with the reward signal to reduce the total reward
059   error, thereby improving the accuracy of the rewards (figure 1).
060
      Leveraging the above theoretical framework, we further propose a method: “Adaptive Message-wise
061   RLHF” shown in figure 3. This approach identifies key signals through rewards and generalized
062   advantages during the generation process, allowing the model to adaptively partition samples. The
063   resulting sub-sequences offer flexible control over gradient propagation through various methods.
064   This adaptability in gradient management enhances the model’s learning capabilities and reduces
065   model hallucinations.

173   In the context of preference alignment, various methods such as Reinforcement Learning from Hu-
174   man Feedback (RLHF), Direct Preference Optimization (DPO), and Rejection Sampling can be
175   unified under a common optimization framework. This framework involves a strategy πθ (x) and a
176   preference feedback function F (x), which incorporates reward signals, preference differences, or
177   rejection conditions. The generalized optimization objective can be formulated as:
178
179

180                                               θ
181
182   Here, F (x) is defined based on the specific method employed: For RLHF, F (x) = R(x), repre-
183   senting the reward function.For DPO, F (x) can be a function of pairwise preference comparison,
184   such as log σ(fθ (x+ ) − fθ (x− )).For Rejection Sampling, F (x) can be a conditional function like
185
      F (x) = R(x) · I(R(x) ≥ threshold), used to exclude samples that do not meet the criteria.
186   This unified framework abstractly describes how preference-aligned strategies aim to align the
187   model’s output with human preferences by maximizing an expected value. Each method achieves
188   its specific optimization goal by defining F (x) in different forms.

      3.2     M ETHODS
271
272
273   As mentioned in Section 2.1, the Bradley-Terry model and its augmented variant, the Plackett-
274
      Luce model, can be represented as a Markov Decision Process (MDP) due to their autoregressive
      generative Transformer structure. This representation enables fine-grained reward acquisition.
275
276   Leveraging this feature, researchers have explored step-wise and token-wise alignment methods.
277   However, these approaches still face some unresolved issues. Step-wise methods, which rely on
278   artificially defined step divisions, struggle to generalize across diverse tasks, particularly in writing
279   and RAG tasks with lower information density. This leads to difficulties in obtaining meaningful
280
      steps and significantly reduces reward accuracy. Token-wise methods, which directly supervise
      using token-level rewards, are confined to online on-policy frameworks. This limitation makes
281
      effective training challenging in other scenarios and hinders generalization to domains where models
282
      are less robust, such as telling humorous jokes or solving complex mathematical problems.
283
284   Figure 1 shows token-wise methods, on the other hand, suffer from excessive supervisory signal
285   density, resulting in noisy rewards with high variance, and fail to fully utilize the model’s inher-
286
      ent knowledge. As shown in figure 2, step-wise methods face a mismatch between the density of
      supervisory signals and the sampled supervised sequence in the action space.
287
288   To address these issues, we identify key tokens in sampled examples using signals of significant
289   difference. We then divide the samples based on this critical information, thereby leveraging the
290   model’s inherent knowledge more effectively. Practical evidence shows that this approach often
291   outperforms human annotators in identifying key information and dividing steps.

294   3.2.1    A DAPTIVE L OSS M ASK .
295
296
      In our method, we propose an innovative approach that segments sequences adaptively at the mes-
297
      sage level into subsequences, rather than at the step or token level. This adaptive segmentation is
298
      paired with a masking strategy instead of training on a step-by-step basis. The rationale behind
299   adopting a masking approach includes the following advantages:
300
301   Reduced Computational Complexity: By using a loss mask for subsequent segmentation, only a
302
      single forward and backward pass is required to train a sample. In contrast, the step-wise approach
      requires K forward and backward passes, where K is the number of steps.
303
304   Enhanced Flexibility: This method does not alter PPO, DPO, or other methods themselves; it
305   simply employs a loss mask during backpropagation, making it applicable across various methods.
306   Simplified Implementation: This approach is widely adopted by frameworks like TRL (von Werra
307   et al., 2020) and OpenRLHF (Hu et al., 2024) and has gained community recognition, making it
308   very suitable as a baseline.
309
      By leveraging the masking method, we achieve more efficient and adaptable training processes,
310
      improving the overall performance and scalability of the models.

      5.1     M ETHODS OF ALIGNMENT AND RLHF
463
464   Preference alignment is to guide AI systems to achieve predetermined objectives, preferences, and
465   moral principles of individuals or groups (Gabriel, 2020). We primarily guide or fine-tune mod-
466   els through reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF): we reinforce behaviors that are
467   highly evaluated by human and penalize those that receive lower evaluations (Christiano et al., 2023;
468   Stiennon et al., 2022; Ouyang et al., 2022; Bai et al., 2022). Representation alignment is another
469   issue explored in this paper, which gives us another perspective on viewing preference alignment.
470   We divide preference alignment into reward model alignment and policy alignment. This approach
471
      allows us to model the alignment of representations between rewards and actions. Research on
      representation alignment has been conducted in both recommendation systems (Wang et al., 2022)
472
      and representation learning (Wang & Isola, 2022). However, there is still little work available to
473
      reference on the alignment between reward signals and the action space in RLHF.
474
475   Self-supervised large language models (LLM) of increasing scale have demonstrated remarkable ca-
476   pabilities in handling zero-shot(Radford et al., 2019) or few-shot prompts(Brown, 2020; Narayanan
477
      et al., 2021; Chowdhery et al., 2023) across a wide range of generation tasks. By fine-tuning the lan-
      guage model using human-generated demonstrations and subsequent output rankings, researchers
478
      developed InstructGPT (Ouyang et al., 2022).This model is notably preferred when larger models
479
      are assessed in human evaluations (Mishra et al., 2021; Sanh et al., 2021; Thoppilan et al., 2022).
480   Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) is one of the core methods behind the suc-
481   cess of InstructGPT and has received widespread attention. RLHF is a fusion of two research areas.
482   First, the reward model is optimized based on human preferences, ensuring that the model, such
483   as the Bradley-Terry model (Bradley & Terry, 1952), aligns closely with the preferences exhibited
484   in human-preferred datasets. Subsequently, reinforcement learning algorithms, proximal policy op-
485   timization (Schulman et al., 2017) are employed to fine-tune the language model to maximize the
      given reward.

      5.2   R ESEARCH ON FINE - GRAINED REWARD SIGNALS
487
488   Some recent studies have suggested that step-wise rewards yield better results than sequence-wise
489   on mathematical problems (Lightman et al., 2023b; Uesato et al., 2022; Lai et al., 2024; Wang et al.,
490   2024). Other research has shown that token-wise reward signals are more effective than sequence-
491   level supervision signals in specific tasks like summarization (Zhong et al., 2024; Feng et al., 2024;
492   Zeng et al., 2024b).These methods all suggest that the reward signal at the sequence level can be
493   further refined, and we also observed the same phenomenon in our experiments.
494   Several studies have explored selective token methods to improve efficiency and performance in lan-
495   guage model training and optimization. Selective Preference Optimization (SePO) was introduced,
496   which uses DPO to estimate a token-level reward function, thereby enabling efficient selection and
497   optimization of key tokens (Yang et al., 2024b). Selective Language Modeling (SLM) was proposed
498   as a novel approach that focuses on training language models using only high-value tokens identi-
499   fied by a reference model, thereby achieving state-of-the-art results with significantly fewer tokens
500
      (Lin et al., 2024). Token-level Direct Preference Optimization (TDPO) was developed to optimize
      policy at the token level for better alignment with human preferences, incorporating forward KL di-
501
      vergence constraints for each token and utilizing the Bradley-Terry model for token-based rewards
502
      (Zeng et al., 2024a). These selective token methods demonstrate the potential of improving effi-
503   ciency and performance in language model training and alignment by focusing on the most relevant
504   or informative tokens.

      6     C ONCLUSION
508
509
      In this paper, we propose a new RLHF method that partitions sequences into different sub-
      subsequences, which we call adaptive message-wise RLHF. Experiments demonstrate that this
510
      method can be adapted for various approaches, including PPO, DPO, and rejection sampling, and
511
      can also be applied to a wide range of downstream tasks. Furthermore, it improves win rates by
512   10% on adversarial test datasets compared to previous methods, achieving an average increase of
513   1.6% across multiple objective evaluation datasets. This indicates that we have successfully unified
514   various fine-grained supervision methods within a single framework.
515
      In the future, we plan to make two improvements based on this work. First, we will explore more
516
      methods and experiments for handling sub-sequences, aiming to leverage the intrinsic information
517
      of sub-sequences while maintaining stability akin to that of mask tokens. Second, we will integrate
518   more control theory methods into dense supervision approaches, further merging and unifying the
519   fields of control theory and NLP.

928   In NLP tasks, it is often necessary to ignore specific tokens, such as padding, during training. Here
929   is a detailed explanation of how masking works with cross-entropy loss:
930   Cross-Entropy Loss Definition:                 X
931                                           L=−            yi log(pi )
932                                                      i
933   Here, yi is the ground-truth distribution (typically one-hot encoded), and pi is the predicted proba-
934   bility from the model.
935
936
      Introducing Mask for Ignoring Tokens: Define a mask mi , where mi = 0 if the token at position
      i is to be ignored, and mi = 1 if it should be included in the loss.
937
938   Applying Mask to the Loss: To ignore tokens, the masked loss is calculated as:
939                                            X
940                                     L=−         mi yi log(pi )
                                                     i
941
942   This ensures that positions where mi = 0 contribute zero to the loss, effectively ignoring those
943   tokens.
944   Effect on Gradients: By applying the mask, during backpropagation, the gradient will not flow
945   through positions where mi = 0, as the contribution to the loss from these positions is zero:
946
947                                     mi yi log(pi ) = 0 if mi = 0
948
949   This approach allows for selective backpropagation, ensuring that only relevant tokens influence the
950
      model’s parameter updates.
