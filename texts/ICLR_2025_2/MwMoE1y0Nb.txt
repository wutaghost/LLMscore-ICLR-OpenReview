                                                   A BSTRACT
012
               We introduce a novel distribution shift, called the VI-Shift, that mimics the trade-
013
               off between object instances and viewpoints in the visual experience of infants.
014            Motivated by findings in infant learning literature, we study this problem through
015            the lens of domain adaptation, but without ImageNet pretraining. We show that
016            the performances of two classic domain adaptation methods, Joint Adaptation Net-
017            work (JAN) and Domain Adversarial Neural Networks (DANN), deteriorate with-
018            out ImageNet pretraining. We hypothesize that the separability of source and tar-
019            get category clusters in the feature space plays a crucial role in the effectiveness of
020            JAN. So, we propose 3 metrics to measure category separability and demonstrate
021            that target separability in the pretrained network is strongly correlated with down-
022
               stream JAN and DANN accuracy. Further, we propose two novel loss functions
               that increase target separability during pretraining by aligning the distribution of
023
               within-domain pairwise distances between the source and target distributions. Our
024
               experiments show that the application of these loss functions modestly improves
025            downstream accuracy on unseen images from the target dataset.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Imagine an infant engaged in play with their mother. As the infant uses their budding grasping
031   abilities to interact with their toy cars, their mother repeats the word ‘car’ to them. Over time, the
032   infant learns to associate the word ‘car’ with the physical toy cars before them (Pereira et al., 2014).
033   Infants’ visual experiences are characterized by extended bouts of experience with a small num-
034   ber of familiar objects (e.g., toy ducks at home), with a large number of rarer exposures to less
035   familiar objects (e.g., real ducks at the park). This pattern of exposure to instances of a particular
036   category yields a long-tailed distribution, where some instances (e.g. their toys/household objects)
037   are seen very frequently, while most instances (e.g. objects they see outdoors) are seen more rarely:
038   (1) The head of the distribution is rich in the distribution of viewpoints, i.e. viewpoint-dominated
039   (VD), while (2) the tail of the distribution is rich in the number of different category instances, i.e.
040
      instance-dominated (ID).
041   Infants learn about categories by linking heard words to the objects they see. However, in natural
042
      interactions with parents, the share of object words tends to be quite low (Stärk et al., 2022). Ad-
      ditionally, visual scenes for infants are often cluttered (Clerkin et al., 2017); there is no clear object
043
      that a heard word refers to. Learning with such ambiguity and noisy signals can be difficult. In
044
      contrast, joint play experiences with parents offers more straightforward opportunities for category
045   learning. When playing with an object, an infant’s visual scene is dominated by the object they are
046   holding (Smith et al., 2011). Armed with clear visual targets, infants use heard nouns more effi-
047   ciently to learn object names (Suanda et al., 2019; Pereira et al., 2014). Further, these sessions often
048   generate more verbal inputs from parents (Tamis-LeMonda et al., 2017), making these experiences
049   potentially better learning experiences for infants.
050   This work is motivated by the contrasts between the VD experience during object play and ID
051   experience otherwise; both in distributions of visual experience and in the presence of clear learning
052   signals in the form of heard words. Specifically, we ask the following question: To what extent is
053   it possible to successfully classify unlabeled ID images by learning directly from labeled VD and
      unlabeled ID images?

054
      This question is similar to the domain adaptation (DA) framework (Ben-David et al., 2010) in ML;
055   we use the VD dataset as the source and the ID dataset as the target. Existing DA methods (Long
056   et al., 2015; French et al., 2017) rely on ImageNet (Deng et al., 2009) pretraining; they leverage
057   high-quality features that comes with supervised training on massive, labeled datasets. This is not
058   developmentally plausible, as infants do not learn from massive, labeled datasets. Instead, we in-
059   vestigate learning features directly from VD and ID datasets. We argue that: in learning directly
060   from VD and ID distributions, learners can leverage cross-distribution learning signals that enforce
061   consistency between the two distributions. As existing methods rely on ImageNet pretraining, there
062   is a dearth of models that use cross-distribution signals to learn features directly from the task data.
063   Our contributions are:
064    • We introduce a novel distribution shift, called the VI-Shift, and investigate learning under this
065      distribution shift using the domain adaptation framework. Consistent with our developmental
066      motivation, we evaluate two classic yet effective DA methods, Domain Adversarial Neural Net-
067      work (DANN) (Ganin et al., 2016) and Joint Adaptation Network (JAN) (Long et al., 2017) on
068
         the VI-Shift without ImageNet pretraining and show that this causes degradation in performance.
       • We investigate how separability of category clusters in the pretrained network affects downstream
069
         DA evaluation. To this end, we propose three metrics to measure the separability of category clus-
070
         ters. Using these metrics, we show that DA accuracies using both DANN and JAN on the target
071      dataset are strongly correlated with the separability of target clusters in the pretrained network.
072    • We propose two Maximum Mean Discrepancy (Gretton et al., 2012) based loss functions for
073      improving the separability of target categories during pretraining. These losses align the distribu-
074      tions of pairwise image distances across the two datasets. We apply these losses in conjunction
075      with contrastive learning signals. Our results show that the application of these losses leads to
076      both improved performance and increased category separability in the feature space.

080   2.1   VI-S HIFT
081   Visual experience during object play is viewpoint-dominated with a relatively small number of ob-
082   jects. Developmental psychologists have observed that such experiences drive visual learning in
083   infants (Yurovsky et al., 2013; Clerkin et al., 2017). Further, parents often provide object labels
084   (Suanda et al., 2019), which can provide straightforward opportunities for category learning. In
085   contrast, most other object experiences are instance-dominated, where available labels can be sparse
086   and noisy (Clerkin et al., 2017; Stärk et al., 2022). We call this the VI-Shift. We instantiate VI-Shift
087
      using the Toybox (Wang et al., 2018) dataset and by curating a category-matched ID dataset from
      ImageNet (Deng et al., 2009) and MS-COCO (Lin et al., 2014).

123   2.2   C OMPARISON WITH OTHER DISTRIBUTION - SHIFT DATASETS
124
125
      Handling distribution shifts is an important problem; otherwise, models fail to generalize when
      the test distribution differs from the training distribution (Torralba & Efros, 2011). Several tasks
126
      have been proposed considering different kinds of distribution shift: for image classification, tasks
127
      like domain adaptation (Ben-David et al., 2010) and domain generalization (Blanchard et al., 2011)
128   provide different frameworks for handling the distribution shift problem. Different datasets have
129   been proposed that to handle distribution shift: Office-31 (Saenko et al., 2010), Office-Caltech (Gong
130   et al., 2012), PACS (Li et al., 2017), Office-Home (Venkateswara et al., 2017), DomainNet (Peng
131   et al., 2019) and ImageNet-R (Hendrycks et al., 2021) are some popular datasets used for distribution
132   shift problems. However, the VI-Shift is different from these existing distribution shifts. While these
133   datasets handle a variety of distribution shifts such as changes in camera source (Office-31, Office-
134   CalTech) and image rendition styles (PACS, DomainNet, ImageNet-R), the different domains within
135   these datasets are still Instance-Dominated. These datasets do not have a data distribution that would
136
      be called Viewpoint-Dominated.
137   The VisDA-2017c (Peng et al., 2017) shift is similar to the VI-Shift problem; however, the
138   Viewpoint-Dominated dataset in the VisDA-2017c dataset consists of 2d renderings of simple 3d
139
      models. These images do not capture texture and color of real-world objects.

      2.3   E XPERIMENTAL S ETTING : D OMAIN A DAPTATION
141
142   Domain Adaptation is a popular framework for addressing distribution shifts between training and
143   test data. The theory of domain adaptation suggests that good performance on the test domain
144   can be achieved by jointly optimizing a source domain error and the divergence between the two
145   domains (Ben-David et al., 2010). In this work, we use two classic DA methods, Joint Adaptation
146   Network (JAN) (Long et al., 2017) and Domain Adversarial Neural Network (DANN) (Ganin et al.,
147
      2016). These methods take a complementary approach towards reducing the divergence loss: while
      JAN directly minimizes an explicit alignment loss, DANN takes an adversarial approach that makes
148
      determining the domain label for datapoints difficult.
149
150   Joint Adaptation Network (JAN) JAN jointly optimizes a classification loss on the source dataset
151   and alignment loss on the distribution of target features with the source features. The JAN alignment
152   loss, called the JMMD loss is based on the Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) loss (Gretton et al.,
153   2012) which measures the distance between two distributions as the distance between their mean
154   embeddings in a RKHS. Given two datasets Xs and Xt with ns = |Xs | and nt = |Xt |, the MMD
155   loss is defined as:

159
160   Domain Adversarial Neural Network (DANN) DANN adopts an adversarial signal to minimize
161   the domain divergence. Specifically, they augment the source classification loss with an additional
      loss for predicting the domain label for datapoints. Ganin et al. (2016) use a Gradient Reversal Layer

      2.4.1       P RETRAINING S CHEMES
168   We use the following pretraining schemes and evaluate how JAN performs under these varying
169   settings:
170   1. Random Initialization: We initialize the network with random weights and apply JAN directly.
171      This is expected to be the most difficult condition.
172   2. Toybox Supervised: We pretrain the network using supervised learning on the Toybox dataset.
173      This condition is also likely to be difficult for JAN because the pretrained network has not been
174      exposed to any instance-dominated dataset.
175
      3. IN-12 SSL: We use self-supervised learning (SSL) to pretrain the network using IN-12 images.
         Specifically, we use the Decoupled Contrastive Loss (DCL) (Yeh et al., 2022) for this purpose.
176
      4. IN-12 Supervised: In this condition, we pretrain the network directly on the target IN-12 dataset
177
         using supervised learning. We expect JAN to perform well with this initialization.
178   5. Joint Supervised: Here, we pretrain the network by training it jointly on both Toybox and IN-12
179      dataset using supervised learning. In this setting too, we expect JAN to do well.
180   6. Joint Supervised-24: This is a variant of the previous setting; we distinguish between the two
181      datasets so the network is presented with Toybox cars and IN-12 cars as two separate categories.
182   7. ILSVRC pretraining: This is the default experimental setting for domain adaptation experiments
183      and we expect JAN to perform well under this condition.

243   3.2     C ATEGORY D ISTRIBUTION M ODELING
244   We model the group of images in category as a probabilistic distribution. To do this, we utilize 2d
245   embeddings obtained from running UMAP on the dataset.
246
247   3.2.1    UMAP EMBEDDINGS
248   We use UMAP (McInnes et al., 2018) to obtain 2D features for each datapoint. UMAP is a popular
249   dimensionality reduction tool; it uses a manifold learning approach to find low-dimensional embed-
250   dings that are faithful to local structure in high dimensions. Two details are important here: (i) We
251   run UMAP jointly using both datasets; this enables UMAP to learn a reducer that takes into account
252   the joint structure of the two datasets. (ii) We do not use image labels during UMAP. This ensures
253   that the low-dimensional embeddings are built only from the geometric structure of the datapoints.
254
      3.2.2    O UTLIER R EMOVAL
255
256   To reduce the impact of outliers during probabilistic modeling, we remove them from the 2D dat-
257   apoints for each category by adopting a non-parametric approach (Wilkinson et al., 2005) based
258   on minimum spanning trees (MSTs). Specifically, we construct an MST for every category and
259
      remove some of the longer edges leading to disconnected components. Then, we discard the
      components which have size less than 5. To remove edges, we apply an adaptive threshold,
260
      τ = q97.5 + 1.5 ∗ (q97.5 − q2.5 ), where q97.5 and q2.5 are the 97.50-th and 2.50-th quantile edge
261
      lengths. Any edge longer than τ is dropped from the MST. This ensures that less than 5% of the
262   edges are dropped; in practice, we find that very few are dropped.
263

428   We use a joint contrastive learning framework for learning representations. In addition to being a
429   powerful learning signal, contrastive loss functions are flexible and can be adapted to the presence
430   of category labels. For IN-12, we use the Decoupled Contrastive Loss (DCL) (Yeh et al., 2022)
431   signal to learn representations. DCL has been shown to outperform other contrastive methods with
      smaller batch sizes. For Toybox, we incorporate the category labels into the loss function. This is

432
      done through two modifications to DCL: (1) All images within the same minibatch belonging to a
433   particular category are considered as positive pairings for each other, and (2) We remove images

436   4.2     L EARNING FEATURES BY ALIGNING INTRA - DOMAIN DISTANCE DISTRIBUTION
437
      Under the joint contrastive learning setting, we expect strong category separability to emerge for
438   Toybox. How can we leverage Toybox category separability to increase category separability in
439   IN-12?
440
441   Pairwise-MMD We propose aligning the distribution of intra-domain pairwise image distances
442   between the two domains. This requires two additional steps: (1) We calculate the intra-domain
443   pairwise feature distance for each minibatch during training. (2) We use the MMD loss (Gretton
444
      et al., 2012) to minimize the distance between these two distributions. We call this the Pairwise-
      MMD loss.
445
446   Neighbors-And-Strangers MMD (NAS-MMD) Within a minibatch, the number of across-class
447   image distances largely outnumbers the number of within-class image distances. This might cause
448   the MMD distance to weigh the across-class distances more strongly. To avoid this, we propose a
449   variant, which we call Neighbors-And-Strangers MMD loss. For every image within a minibatch, we
450   find its 3 nearest neighbors and 3 furthest strangers within the minibatch. We then apply the MMD
451   loss separately on the neighbors distribution and on the strangers distribution between Toybox and
452
      IN-12. Fig 5 shows a schematic of the NAS-MMD method.
453   Empirically, we found that direct application of the MMD loss causes the Toybox distribution to
454   shift to adapt to the IN-12 distribution. To prevent this, we apply the MMD loss and the NAS-MMD
455
      loss in an asymmetric manner: we restrict gradient flow through the source branch. This encourages
      the IN-12 features to shift to match the Toybox distribution.

      6   C ONCLUSION
524
525   In this work, we have introduced a novel distribution shift, called VI-Shift, over distributions of
526   viewpoints and instances; this distribution is motivated by the visual experience of infants that drives
527   category learning. We looked at this problem through the lens of domain adaptation in a develop-
528   mentally plausible setting, i.e. without large-scale pretraining. We showed that two classic domain
529   adaptation methods, JAN and DANN, underperform on this challenging task. Further, we seeked to
530   understand how separability of categories in the pretrained feature space affects downstream domain
531
      adaptation performance. To do this, we proposed 3 metrics for measuring category separability and
      showed that downstream JAN accuracy is strongly correlated with target cluster separability. To
532
      learn pretraining models more suited to the VI-Shift, we proposed two MMD-based methods for
533
      promoting category separability in the target dataset by matching its distribution of intra-domain
534   pairwise image distances to that of the source domain. Our experiments show that this approach
535   yields an improvement in the downstream accuracy with both JAN and DANN.
