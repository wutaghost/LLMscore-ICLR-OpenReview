011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Spiking neural networks (SNNs) hold significant promise as energy-efficient alter-
014            natives to conventional artificial neural networks (ANNs). However, SNNs require
015
               computations across multiple timesteps, resulting in increased latency, heightened
               energy consumption, and additional memory access overhead. Techniques to re-
016
               duce SNN latency down to a unit timestep have emerged to realize true superior
017
               energy efficiency over ANNs. Nonetheless, this latency reduction often comes at
018            the expense of noticeable accuracy degradation. Therefore, achieving an optimal
019            balance in the tradeoff between accuracy and energy consumption by adjusting
020            the latency of multiple timesteps remains a significant challenge. In this paper, we
021            introduce a new dimension to the accuracy-energy tradeoff space using a novel
022            one-hot multi-level leaky integrate-and-fire (M-LIF) neuron model. The proposed
023            M-LIF model represents the inputs and outputs of hidden layers as a set of one-hot
024            binary-weighted spike lanes to find better tradeoff points while still being able to
025            model conventional SNNs. For image classification on static datasets, we demon-
026
               strate M-LIF SNNs outperform iso-architecture conventional LIF SNNs in terms
               of accuracy (2% higher than VGG16 SNN on ImageNet) while still being energy-
027
               efficient (20× lower energy than VGG16 ANN on ImageNet). For dynamic vision
028
               datasets, we demonstrate the ability of M-LIF SNNs to reduce latency by 3× com-
029            pared to conventional LIF SNNs while limiting accuracy degradation (< 1%).

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Neural networks have become a fundamental technique for solving many important problems such as
035   image classification, object detection, and face recognition (Krizhevsky et al., 2012; Redmon et al.,
036   2016). As neural network accuracy improves, models become increasingly complex, making their
037   energy-efficient deployment on the edge a significant challenge. In order to reduce the computational
038   complexity of these tasks, spiking neural networks (SNNs) (Maass, 1997) were proposed as an
039
      alternative to traditional artificial neural networks (ANNs) (Krizhevsky et al., 2012; Simonyan &
      Zisserman, 2014). SNNs infer inputs across multiple timesteps while ANNs perform a one-shot
040
      inference, essentially over a single timestep. Neurons in SNNs differ from those in ANNs as they
041
      operate on sparse binary spike trains as opposed to non-binary ‘analog’ activations, resulting in the
042   substitution of multiplications with energy-efficient additions (Han et al., 2016).
043
044   To model spikes over time, SNNs employ various techniques, most notably the leaky integrate-and-
045
      fire (LIF) neuron model (Hunsberger & Eliasmith, 2015; Burkitt, 2006). Each neuron is charac-
      terized by two parameters: firing threshold and membrane leakage. During a timestep, a neuron
046
      either remains silent or produces a spike if the membrane potential exceeds its firing threshold. The
047
      membrane potential can shrink over time depending on the membrane leakage and is reset if a spike
048   is produced. Using such models, many training methods have emerged and can be categorized into
049   two main approaches: ANN-SNN conversion and direct training. ANN-SNN conversion methods
050   (Rueckauer et al., 2017; Diehl et al., 2015; Bu et al., 2023) convert the weights of a pre-trained ANN
051   to an iso-architecture SNN. However, these methods can require a large number of timesteps (on the
052   order of 1000 in some cases (Sengupta et al., 2019)) to achieve comparable or better accuracy than
053   ANNs. Note that multi-timestep inference results in both a higher number of operations and more
      memory storage/accesses which can dominate the compute cost (Horowitz, 2014). Therefore, the


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      need for multi-timestep processing from ANN-SNN conversion has been the primary factor making
055   widespread deployment of SNNs impractical for energy-constrained edge scenarios.
056
057   Direct training using surrogate gradient-based optimization and back-propagation through time
058
      (BPTT) (Rathi & Roy, 2023; Deng et al., 2022; Yao et al., 2023) has enabled training SNNs with sig-
      nificantly fewer timesteps, occasionally reducing them to just a single timestep (Chowdhury et al.,
059
      2022). However, these SNNs still lag ANNs in terms of accuracy. For image classification on static
060
      datasets (Rawat & Wang, 2017; Krizhevsky et al., 2012), SNNs cannot bridge this accuracy gap with
061   ANNs without increasing the number of timesteps, which in turn reduces energy efficiency. More-
062   over, single-timestep SNNs require iterative temporal pruning (Chowdhury et al., 2022) to converge,
063   rendering training more time-consuming. For image classification, multi-timestep SNNs tradition-
064   ally outperform ANNs on dynamic vision sensor (Leñero-Bardallo et al., 2011) data but suffer from
065   a sharp accuracy degradation (Li et al., 2024) with a reduced number of timesteps.
066
      As conventional LIF models employ binary-valued neuron spike outputs, traditional SNNs are re-
067   stricted to solely scaling the temporal dimension T (i.e., the number of timesteps) to achieve different
068   accuracy-energy efficiency tradeoffs. To address this limitation, we extend the range of neuron spike
069   outputs to include more values by introducing a new dimension S to the accuracy-energy tradeoff
070   space using a novel one-hot multi-level leaky integrate-and-fire (M-LIF) neuron model as illustrated
071   in Figure 1. The proposed one-hot M-LIF model has the following key properties: 1) it uses the S
072   dimension to represent hidden layer outputs (inputs) as a set of So (Si ) binary-weighted spike lanes,
073   and 2) it limits the simultaneous firing behavior of those So (Si ) spike lanes to only a single lane per
074   timestep. These two properties of our one-hot M-LIF model enable new accuracy-energy tradeoff
075
      points for SNNs while still only requiring additions (without multiplications) like the conventional
      LIF model. Furthermore, the proposed one-hot M-LIF model can be easily integrated into prior
076
      existing training frameworks. For static datasets such as CIFAR and ImageNet, we demonstrate
077
      that one-hot M-LIF SNNs outperform conventional LIF SNNs in terms of accuracy while achiev-
078   ing better or comparable energy efficiency on various architectures including the high-performance
079   spike-driven transformer (Yao et al., 2023). For dynamic vision datasets such as DVS-CIFAR10 (Li
080   et al., 2017; Orchard et al., 2015), we show that M-LIF SNNs using multi-level input layer encod-
081   ing can achieve reduced timesteps (energy consumption) compared to conventional LIF SNNs for
082   comparable or better accuracy. To summarize, the main contributions of this paper are:
083
084          • We propose a new direction to balance SNN energy efficiency and accuracy by expanding
085            the neuron inputs and outputs using a novel one-hot multi-level leaky-integrate-and-fire
086
               (M-LIF) neuron model.
087          • We enable a new tradeoff and show that one-hot M-LIF SNNs are more accurate than iso-
088            architecture LIF SNNs while consuming comparable or lower energy with fewer timesteps.
089          • We demonstrate the benefit of one-hot M-LIF SNNs with dynamic vision sensor-based
090            input compared to conventional SNNs. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first SNN
091            work to achieve a top-1 accuracy of 82.5% on DVS-CIFAR10 using only 3 timesteps.

127   2.2     ANN-SNN C ONVERSION
128
129
      ANN-SNN conversion methods (Sengupta et al., 2019; Rueckauer et al., 2017; Diehl et al., 2015;
      Bu et al., 2023; Deng & Gu, 2021; Li et al., 2021a) convert the weights of a pre-trained ANN to an
130
      iso-architecture SNN. Specifically, these methods convert the output of a rectified linear unit neu-
131
      ron in an ANN into a sequence of binary spikes in the SNN over multiple timesteps. The primary
132   challenge in this technique is determining the firing threshold in such a way that it balances the
133   accuracy-latency tradeoff. In these methods, the firing thresholds are generally determined by pro-
134   filing the pre-trained ANN and recording a certain percentile of layers’ input activation distributions.
135   However, these heuristic techniques can lead to a sub-optimal choice of firing threshold and can also
136   require a large number of timesteps (up to 1000 timesteps) to achieve comparable or better accuracy
137   than ANNs, thus further aggravating the accuracy-latency tradeoff. This multi-timestep processing
138   requirement is a challenge for widespread SNN deployment as it primarily introduces more memory
139   storage and accesses which can be significantly higher than compute cost (Horowitz, 2014).

178   2.4   Q UANTIZED -ACTIVATION ANN S
179
180   As the number of timesteps converges to one, conventional SNNs become closely related to binary
181
      activated artificial neural networks (BNNs) (Wang et al., 2020; Rastegari et al., 2016) as both use
      binary activations to perform an inference over a single timestep. Chowdhury et al. (2022) discuss
182
      that they are in fact distinct. While SNNs quantize outputs to spikes (i.e. {0, 1}), BNNs quantize
183
      outputs to be ±1. Unlike BNNs which use non-linear activation functions where the firing threshold
184   is zero, the firing threshold is learnable in SNNs. Chowdhury et al. (2022) observe that this allows
185   SNNs to outperform BNNs in terms of accuracy and scale better to larger datasets such as ImageNet.
186   Moreover, LIF enables SNNs to extend the same network for sequential processing unlike BNNs.
187   Similarly, our one-hot M-LIF SNNs become closely related to log-quantized-activation ANNs (LQ-
188   ANNs) (Zhou et al., 2016; Yin et al., 2019) as timesteps converge to one, but remain distinct for
189   analogous reasons. A discussion regarding similarities and differences is provided in Section 3.2.
190
191   2.5   Q UANTIZED -ACTIVATION SNN S
192
193   Prior SNN works have explored the interaction between activation bit-width and timesteps (Xiao
194   et al., 2019; Miao et al., 2018; Wang & Zhang, 2023; Feng et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023; Li & Zeng,
195   2022). Multi-spike (Xiao et al., 2019; Miao et al., 2018) and multi-threshold (Wang & Zhang, 2023;
196
      Feng et al., 2022) neurons increase activation precision via uniform quantization but raise energy
      costs, disrupting the multiplication-free nature of traditional SNNs. Xiao et al. (2019) propose
197
      parallel and cascade multi-threshold (MT) models to enhance activation precision per timestep. The
198
      parallel-MT model uses multiple threshold values, summing spikes from all firing lanes at each
199   timestep, while the cascade-MT model processes them sequentially. In contrast, our one-hot M-LIF
200   neuron employs a single threshold and power-of-two multiples to fire one spike lane per timestep, a
201   learned constraint distinct from prior methods. This enables efficient FP32 weight exponent updates
202   via a single INT8 addition as discussed in Section 4, unlike the multiplication demands of uniformly
203   quantized outputs. Miao et al. (2018) utilize non-multiplicative thresholds and focuses on residual
204   networks without results on larger datasets like ImageNet or advanced architectures like spike-driven
205   transformers, while also lacking energy analyses. Burst-spike models (Wang et al., 2023; Li & Zeng,
206   2022) enhance precision by increasing spike rates per timestep but struggle to scale down to single-
207
      timestep processing ( T = 8 timesteps on ImageNet). Our one-hot M-LIF neuron reduces timestep
      requirements to T = 1, significantly cutting memory access overhead.

310   4   E NERGY C ONSUMPTION E STIMATION
311
312   We evaluate the inference energy of our approach based on the approach in Chowdhury et al. (2022);
313   Yao et al. (2023). In conventional SNNs, 32-bit floating-point (FP32) additions replace the FP32
314   multiply-and-accumulates (MACs) of ANNs except in the first layer which uses direct encoded
315   inputs. For one-hot M-LIF SNNs (ω = 1), inputs (outputs) are restricted to powers of 2, and
316   multiplying by a power of 2 corresponds to adjusting the 8-bit integer (INT8) exponent of the FP32
317   multiplicand (see Appendix). Therefore, scaling the intermediate FP32 membrane potential by 2s
318   during integration in Equation 6 corresponds to increasing or decreasing its exponent in the INT8
319   format. According to Horowitz (2014), an INT8 addition consumes 30× less energy than a FP32
320
      addition, hence the overhead of scaling in M-LIF SNNs is negligible and FP32 additions dominate
      the energy consumption of one-hot M-LIF SNNs.

392   5     E XPERIMENTS & R ESULTS
393
394   We validate our one-hot M-LIF model and compare the performance and inference energy of our
395   one-hot M-LIF SNNs with existing SNN works on both static and dynamic image classification
396   tasks. Our proposed neuron model can be integrated into existing SNN training methodologies. We
397   compare against the hybrid training methods (Chowdhury et al., 2022; Rathi & Roy, 2023) for static
398   tasks (Section 5.1) and the temporal efficient training method Deng et al. (2022) for dynamic vision
399   tasks (Section 5.2). We also evaluate the impact of one-hot M-LIF on more complex SNN-based
400
      high performance models such as the spike-driven transformer (Yao et al., 2023). As in prior works,
      we employ direct input encoding for static tasks such that the input layer is fed with full-precision
401
      pixels. We also fix all layers to use the same number of (input) output spike lanes, S, as this reduces
402
      the number of hyperparameters. The source code is available at: to be released upon acceptance.

406   5.1.1    I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
407
408   We apply hybrid direct training as described in Rathi & Roy (2023); Chowdhury et al. (2022) to

411   batch-norm parameters with the weights of the corresponding layer. We then copy the weights of the
412
      pre-trained ANN to an iso-architecture one-hot M-LIF SNN and use the 90-th percentile of the input
      activation distribution as each layer’s threshold θl . The SNN is then trained using BPTT but without

414
      and ImageNet using Transformer-2-512 and Transformer-8-512 by replacing all LIF neurons with
415   one-hot M-LIF neurons while using the same training methodology as in Yao et al. (2023). Note that
416   Transformer-L-D represents a model with L encoder blocks and D channels. These networks are
417   trained from scratch using BPTT without any pre-trained ANN initialization or batch norm fusion.

526   6   C ONCLUSION
527
528
529   SNNs hold promise as an energy-efficient alternative to traditional ANNs. However, achieving an
530   optimal balance in the accuracy-energy tradeoff by adjusting latency remains a significant challenge
531
      for widespread deployment. To that end, we introduce the dimension of spike lanes to conventional
      SNNs using a novel M-LIF neuron model without latency and computational complexity overhead.
532
      The proposed model represents the inputs and outputs of hidden layers as a set of one-hot binary-
533
      weighted spike lanes. Using our one-hot M-LIF neuron model, we are able to find new and better
534   tradeoff points for both static and dynamic vision tasks. In particular, our one-hot M-LIF-based
535   SNNs achieve a top-1 accuracy of 71.05% on ImageNet using VGG16 and enhance the computa-
536   tional efficiency by 20×. One-hot M-LIF neurons also improve the accuracy-latency tradeoff for
537   advanced network architectures such as spike-driven transformers (> 3% higher accuracy with 4×
538   fewer timesteps on ImageNet). For dynamic vision tasks, such as image classification using dynamic
539   vision sensor data, our one-hot M-LIF SNNs retain higher accuracy (82.5%) when scaling down to
      fewer timesteps (3) on CIFAR10-DVS thus providing better energy efficiency.

783   A.2     FP32 P OWER OF T WO M ULTIPLICATION
784
      As discussed in the main text, both M-LIF SNNs and LIF SNNs perform FP32 additions during in-
785
      tegration to calculate the membrane potential at each timestep. For M-LIF SNNs, FP32 weights are
786   scaled by a power of 2 prior to integration. As illustrated in Figure 4 which provides details regard-
787   ing the FP32 number format, power of two multiplication corresponds to adjusting the 8-bit integer
788   exponent which requires 30× less energy than the FP32 addition (Horowitz, 2014). Hence, the over-
789   head is negligible and M-LIF SNNs are considered to leverage additions instead of multiplications
790   like their LIF counterparts.
791
      Our proposed approach is designed to be adaptable to various hardware architectures, including
792   custom ASICs or FPGAs, where the operation depicted in Figure 4 can indeed be implemented effi-
793   ciently. It is also worth noting that modern commercial instruction set architectures, such as x86, in-
794   clude specific support for efficiently scaling floating-point numbers. For example, the x87 floating-
795   point unit (FPU) provides specialized instructions like fscale (Intel Corporation, 2024) designed
796   to scale floating-point numbers by powers of two, rather than performing a general floating-point
797   multiplication. This indeed suggests that the operation described in Figure 4 aligns with existing
798   commercial hardware capabilities.

      We conducted an ablation study where we varied the number of spike lanes S while keeping the
946   number of timesteps T fixed at 3 for VGGSNN on DVS-CIFAR10. We provide the results below
