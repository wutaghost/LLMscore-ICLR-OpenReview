009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            We present VColRL, a reinforcement learning framework designed to solve the
012            vertex coloring problem (VCP), where the objective is to assign colors to the ver-
013            tices of a graph with the minimum number of colors, such that no two adjacent
014            vertices share the same color. The framework is built on a novel Markov Deci-
015            sion Process (MDP) configuration to effectively capture the dynamics of the VCP,
016            developed after evaluating various MDP configurations. Our experimental results
017            demonstrate that VColRL achieves competitive performance in terms of using
018
               fewer colors as compared to advanced mathematical solvers and other metaheuris-
               tic approaches while being significantly faster. Additionally, our results show that
019
               VColRL generalizes well across different types of graphs.

022   1   I NTRODUCTION
023
024   Combinatorial optimization is a branch of mathematical optimization that focuses on identifying
025   the best solution from a finite collection of possibilities. Common examples of combinatorial op-
026   timization problems include the traveling salesman problem (TSP, (Voigt, 1831)), the maximum
027   independent set problem (MIS, (Miller & Muller, 1960)), and the vertex coloring problem (VCP,
028   (Birkhoff, 1912)). However, most combinatorial optimization problems are NP-hard, making find-
029   ing exact solutions in many real-world scenarios impractical.
030   Over the years, researchers have developed efficient heuristics (e.g. (Knuth, 1997)) to address these
031   problems. However, these heuristics are often problem-specific and require effective application of
032   domain knowledge. In addition to heuristic approaches, researchers have developed advanced op-
033   timization solvers such as the Gurobi Optimization Studio (Gurobi Optimization, 2023) and IBM
034   ILOG CPLEX Optimization Studio (IBM, 2024), which excel at finding exact solutions for com-
035   plex problems. These solvers utilize sophisticated mathematical techniques and algorithms, such
036   as branch-and-bound (Morrison et al., 2016) and cutting-plane methods (Lee et al., 2015), (Goffin
037   & Vial, 2002), to handle large-scale integer, and mixed-integer programming problems with high
038
      precision. Despite their powerful capabilities, the compute and time required for these solvers to
      find optimal solutions increase substantially as the problem size grows (Luppold et al., 2018).
039
040   An alternative approach to tackling complex optimization problems is through data-driven meth-
041   ods. These methods provide a simpler structure and do not require extensive domain expertise. By
042   leveraging Machine Learning, data-driven approaches focus on learning from data and performing
043   straightforward computations, such as matrix multiplications (Burkov, 2019). This simplicity often
044
      results in significant time savings, as these techniques can process large datasets and yield solutions
      more rapidly compared to traditional solvers for bigger problem instances. Over the past decades,
045
      the rise of neural networks and advanced ML methods has further enhanced these data-driven tech-
046
      niques, making them more powerful and versatile.
047
048   While supervised learning offers one path, obtaining labeled datasets can be challenging. As a
049   result, alternative methods are needed. Reinforcement learning presents a promising option as it
050
      does not rely on labeled data. Instead, it frames the problem as a Markov Decision Process (MDP),
      allowing the system to learn optimal strategies through iterative interactions with the environment
051
      and feedback (Sutton & Barto, 2018), (Ernst & Louette, 2024).
052
053   Deep reinforcement learning (DRL), a subset of reinforcement learning, has gained prominence due
      to its ability to handle high-dimensional state and action spaces using deep neural networks. DRL

      combines reinforcement learning with deep learning to model complex environments and discover
055   intricate patterns that are difficult to capture with traditional methods (Lapan, 2020). By leveraging
056   deep neural networks, DRL can learn sophisticated policies and make decisions based on large and
057   complex datasets, further enhancing its effectiveness in solving complex optimization problems.
058   This approach has proven particularly useful in areas such as robotics (Morales et al., 2021), au-

061   For graph-related problems, Graph Neural Networks (GNNs, (Scarselli et al., 2008)) provide a sig-

065   method allows GNNs to effectively model complex graph structures. Such capabilities are partic-

067   crucial for achieving optimal color assignments. Furthermore, integrating GNNs with DRL can en-
068   hance their effectiveness by combining learned graph representations with reinforcement learning
069
      strategies, thereby improving performance.
070   Our Contribution: This paper introduces VColRL, a novel reinforcement learning framework tai-
071   lored for solving the VCP using the PPO (Schulman et al., 2017) algorithm in conjunction with the
072   GraphSAGE (Hamilton et al., 2017) architecture. VColRL leverages an episodic MDP that models
073   the dynamics of graph coloring by defining states, actions, transitions, and rewards. Central to our
074   approach is a novel reward strategy that treats the color set as ordered, penalizing non-contiguous
075
      assignments to ensure unique and efficient solutions in terms of color usage. By focusing on min-
      imizing the highest-numbered color used, this strategy improves learning stability and reduces am-
076
      biguities in the solution space.
077
078   In addition, we incorporate a detailed study of rollback mechanisms for conflict resolution. Unlike
079   prior methods (Ahn et al., 2020) that rely solely on hard rollback, we systematically evaluate both
080   hard and soft rollback methods to experimentally demonstrate that hard rollback provides superior
081
      performance. Details about these rollbacks are discussed in section 3.1. Our framework also extends
      the deferral action strategy, originally proposed by Ahn et al. (2020) for the Maximum Independent
082
      Set (MIS) problem, to address the unique challenges of VCP. By deferring decisions for certain
083
      vertices, the framework simplifies subproblems and enhances the agent’s ability to minimize color
084   usage. To the best of our knowledge, VColRL is the first framework to incorporate the deferral
085   action in the context of VCP.
086
087
      Through an exhaustive evaluation of configurations combining different rollback strategies, reward
      mechanisms, and action models, we identify the optimal design for the VCP. Extensive experiments
088
      on synthetic graphs and benchmark datasets showcase that VColRL achieves competitive results
089
      compared to state-of-the-art solvers and metaheuristic algorithms while maintaining significantly
090   lower computational costs. Furthermore, the framework demonstrates strong generalization across
091   graph types and scalability to larger problem instances, making it a robust solution for the VCP.

094   2   R ELATED W ORK
095
096   Vertex coloring problem is a well-known NP-hard combinatorial optimization problem (Garey &
097   Johnson, 1976). Exact solvers for this problem require exploring an exponentially large solution
098
      space to guarantee optimality, which becomes computationally infeasible as the graph size grows
      (de Lima & Carmo, 2018). Solutions to the vertex coloring problem can be categorized into two
099
      main approaches: conventional methods and machine learning-based approaches.
100
101   Conventional Methods: This category includes approximation algorithms such as DSatur, Welsh-
102   Powell (Aslan & Baykan, 2016), as well as mathematical solvers like Gurobi and CPLEX, which
103   provide exact solutions but at a high computational cost, especially for large graphs. Additionally,
104
      metaheuristic approaches such as Simulated Annealing, Genetic Algorithms, and Tabu Search are
      commonly used for solving the VCP (Mostafaie et al., 2020), (Dokeroglu & Sevinc, 2021). They
105
      are capable of generating high-quality and near-optimal results.
106
107   Machine Learning-Based Approaches: Several studies have used supervised learning for solving
      the vertex coloring problem. Das et al. (2019) have introduced a supervised learning approach where


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      a deep learning model, using Long Short-Term Memory layers followed by a correction phase, is
109   trained on optimal coloring of graphs to handle invalid color assignments. Over time, researchers
110   have recognized the need for graph-specific architectures to better address the complexities inherent
111   in vertex coloring problems. Lemos et al. (2019) has employed Graph Neural Networks (GNN)
112   (Scarselli et al., 2008) to predict the chromatic number of graphs, leveraging GNNs’ ability to cap-
113   ture graph structures. Similarly, Ijaz et al. (2022) has utilized GNNs to solve the vertex coloring
114   problem, focusing on efficiently finding the chromatic number of large graphs.
115   However, as graph sizes increase, obtaining ground truth solutions becomes impractical, leading to
116   the emergence of reinforcement learning as a promising solution. Huang et al. (2019) has adapted
117   AlphaGo Zero (Silver et al., 2017) with graph embeddings, introducing a novel deep neural network
118   architecture known as FastColorNet for the vertex coloring problem. Cummins & Veras (2024)
119   have highlighted the potential of RL to tackle the vertex coloring problem but also pointed out its
120   limitations without label-invariant representations. They have emphasized the importance of inte-
121   grating GNNs to enhance RL performance by providing essential structural insights. Gianinazzi
122   et al. (2021) has proposed a greedy combined probabilistic heuristic for the vertex coloring prob-
123
      lem that integrates reinforcement learning and an attention mechanism (Vaswani, 2017) for vertex
      selection. In their framework, they only used a terminal step reward based solely on color count.
124
      Similarly, Watkins et al. (2023) introduced ReLCol, a method that combines Q-learning with GNN
125
      for feature extraction.
126
127   The most closely related work is by Ahn et al. (2020), which applies reinforcement learning to the
128   MIS problem using the deferral action strategy. While effective for binary-state problems, their
129
      framework does not naturally extend to the VCP, where permutations of color assignments result
      in multiple equivalent solutions with the same number of colors, creating a non-unique solution
130
      space that complicates learning and slows convergence. VColRL addresses these challenges with
131
      a novel reward strategy that minimizes the highest-numbered color used, ensuring solution unique-
132   ness, improving learning stability, and accelerating convergence time. Although both works utilize
133   GraphSAGE (Hamilton et al., 2017) architecture, it is not central to VColRL’s approach. Graph-
134   SAGE serves as one of several possible architectural choices for capturing graph structure and can
135   be replaced with other graph neural network architectures without affecting the core contributions
136   of VColRL, which lie in its MDP design, reward strategies, and exhaustive evaluation of MDP con-
137   figurations. These elements, combined with the adaptation of the deferral action strategy proposed
138   by Ahn et al. (2020) to the multi-state complexities of VCP, set VColRL apart as a scalable and
139   effective solution for graph coloring.

      We now describe our framework for the VCP. Given a graph G = (V, W) with vertex set V and
      edge set W, the objective is to assign colors to the vertices of a graph with the minimum number of
145
      colors, so that no two adjacent vertices share the same color. In our approach to the VCP, we begin by
146
      attempting to color the vertices using as few colors as possible from a set C = {1, 2, . . . , n}, where
147   n is initially assumed to be large enough to cover all vertices. If any vertices cannot be colored due
148   to constraints, they are temporarily left uncolored and addressed separately to complete the solution.
149   The resulting solution can be represented as a vector x = [xi : i ∈ V] ∈ ({0} ∪ C)V , where each
150   element xi either indicates the color assigned to vertex i from the set C or xi = 0 denotes that vertex
151   i has not been assigned any color.
152
153   3.1   M ARKOV D ECISION P ROCESS FOR THE VCP
154
155   We model the VCP as a finite MDP, which terminates when either all vertices are colored or the time
156   limit (episode length) is reached.
157
      The key components of the MDP are:
158
159   States: States represent the current configuration of the system. A state is represented by a vertex-
160   state vector s = [si : i ∈ V] ∈ ({∗, 0} ∪ C)|V| , where si ∈ C denotes the color assigned to vertex
161   i, and si = ∗ indicates that the vertex is undecided, meaning it is yet to be colored. Initially, all
      vertices are undecided (si = ∗ for all i ∈ V). The process ends when no undecided vertices remain

      or the time limit is reached. After termination, any vertex i that remains undecided will be referred
163   to as uncolored and will be assigned si = 0.
164
165   Actions: Actions represent the decisions the agent makes for the undecided vertices. Let V∗ denote
166
      the set of undecided vertices. We consider two models for action:
167
             • Model with Deferral: In this model, the agent can either defer the decision for an undecided
168
               vertex or assign it a color. This is represented by a∗ = [ai : i ∈ V∗ ] ∈ ({∗} ∪ C)|V∗ | ,
169
               allowing the agent to delay coloring certain vertices and focus on smaller parts of the graph
170            first.
171
172
             • Model without Deferral: In this model, the agent must assign a color to each undecided
173
               vertex without the option to defer. This is represented by a∗ = [ai : i ∈ V∗ ] ∈ C|V∗ | .
174
      Transitions: Transitions define how the system moves from one state to another after an action is
175
      taken. The transition from state s → s′ for action a∗ occurs in two phases: the update phase and
176   the clean-up phase.
177
178   Update Phase: The action a∗ , determined by the policy for the undecided vertices V∗ , is applied to
179
      create an intermediate state ŝ. Specifically, sˆi = ai if i ∈ V∗ , and sˆi = si otherwise.
180   Clean-up Phase: The clean-up phase ensures the resulting state s′ is conflict-free. For each pair of
181   conflicting vertices (i.e., vertices assigned the same color), we consider two rollback models:
182
183          • Hard Rollback Model: Both conflicting vertices are reset to the undecided state, providing
184
               greater flexibility to revisit and resolve conflicts, though this may require more steps.
185          • Soft Rollback Model: Only the vertices involved in the latest action are reset to the un-
186            decided state, leaving previous assignments unchanged. This approach resolves conflicts
187            more quickly but offers fewer opportunities to adjust earlier assignments.
188

288   3.3   A DDRESSING I NCOMPLETE S OLUTIONS IN THE VC OL RL F RAMEWORK
289
290   The model is designed to output a color assignment for each vertex at each state, requiring the
291   learning of a probability distribution over a fixed set of colors. We set this color set length to 15.
292   VColRL begins with an initial set of 15 colors. An incomplete solution may occur either when the
293   time limit is reached before all vertices are colored, resulting in fewer than 15 colors being used, or
294   when all 15 colors are utilized but some vertices remain uncolored. In either case, the subgraph of
295   uncolored vertices is extracted, and the coloring process is restarted on the subgraph. This iterative
296
      process continues until all vertices are successfully colored. The final color count is obtained by
      summing the total number of colors used across all iterations.

387   by postponing some decisions to later timesteps. This mirrors human problem-solving, where cer-
388   tain vertices are addressed first to simplify the decision process in subsequent stages. Conversely, in
389   the without deferral action strategy, the agent is forced to make decisions immediately, regardless
390   of its confidence, which hinders its performance. Lastly, the max-color reward strategy treats the
391   color set as ordered, encouraging the agent to assign colors sequentially from the first color up to
392   k, ensuring the solution is unique in terms of the colors used. This contrasts with the color-count
393
      strategy, where multiple optimal solutions can exist. For instance, if three colors are needed to color
      a graph, under the color count strategy, both color assignments ‘1, 2, 3’ and ‘1, 2, 4’ will yield a
394
      color usage penalty of 3. However, under the max-color reward strategy, the former will yield a
395
      color usage penalty of 3, while the latter will incur a penalty of 4, ensuring a unique and contiguous
396   solution in terms of used colors in the trained model thereby reducing randomness in the learning
397   process by providing an organized and effective approach for the VCP.
398
399
      In summary, HDM is an MDP configuration developed by combining our novel max-color reward
      strategy with the deferral action strategy proposed by Ahn et al. (2020). This configuration outper-
400
      forms others in terms of training time and graph coloring efficiency, making it the optimal choice.
401
      Figure 2d supports this conclusion, as the critic loss trajectory for HDM demonstrates the highest
402   stability among all.

519
520   In this paper, we propose VColRL, a deep reinforcement learning framework for solving the VCP.
521   Our method is built on a novel MDP framework, which introduces a reward mechanism designed to
522   minimize the highest-numbered color used from an ordered set. Through extensive experimentation,
523
      we show that VColRL outperforms or performs equivalently to the baselines like VColMIS and
      Greedy in terms of color usage for most of the graph types, and performs competitively with Gurobi
524
      and TabucolMin with a significant reduction in execution time. Although VColRL faces challenges
525
      with the DSJC family of graphs, its generalization across other graph types demonstrates its potential
526   for graph coloring tasks.

684   Feature Aggregation and Concatenation, and Transformation.
685   In the Neighborhood Sampling module, each vertex samples a few of its neighboring vertices for
686   aggregating their features to be able to capture the information about its neighborhood, but before
687   sampling, the vertex features are normalized by multiplying each feature vector by the inverse square
688   root of the vertices’ degree. This normalization helps in stabilizing the learning process. Graph-
689   SAGE typically allows for sampling a subset of neighboring vertices; however, in our approach, we
690   sample the entire neighborhood. This ensures that all relevant vertices are considered for efficient
691   processing, which is essential for accurate feature aggregation.
692   In the Feature Aggregation and Concatenation module, the features of the sampled vertices are ag-
693   gregated and normalized again. The aggregated features are then concatenated with the original
694   vertex features, resulting in a feature vector that is twice the length of the input vector. This helps to
695   retain the information about the vertex and its neighborhood, providing a comprehensive represen-
696   tation for further processing.
697   In the Transformation module, the concatenated and aggregated features (represented as a matrix
698   with the number of rows equals the number of vertices in the graph) are multiplied by a weight
699   matrix, followed by the addition of a bias matrix. Both these matrices are learnable components of
700   our model. An activation function is then applied to produce the final output, enabling the model to
701   capture complex relationships in the data. The final output feature vectors then become the input for
      the next layer, allowing the model to propagate information through multiple layers.
