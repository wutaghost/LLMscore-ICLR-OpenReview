011                                                  A BSTRACT
012
013                Large language models (LLMs) have shown promise in various domains but face
014                challenges in chemistry due to limited domain knowledge and computational ca-
015                pabilities. To address these issues, tool-augmented language agents like Chem-
016                Crow and Coscientist have been developed. However, their evaluations remain
017
                   narrow in scope, leaving an unclear understanding of how these tool-augmented
                   agents perform across various real-world applications. In this study, we conduct
018
                   a comprehensive evaluation to bridge this gap. Specifically, we develop ChemA-
019
                   gent, the most capable chemistry agent to date, equipped with 29 tools capable
020                of handling a wide spectrum of tasks. We then conduct a comprehensive assess-
021                ment across three datasets, namely SMolInstruct, MMLU-chemistry, and GPQA-
022                chemistry, which can be categorized into specialized chemistry tasks and general
023                chemistry questions. Surprisingly, tool-augmented agents do not consistently out-
024                perform the base LLM without tools, and the impact of tool augmentation is highly
025                task-dependent: It provides substantial gains in specialized chemistry tasks but po-
026                tentially hinders performance in general chemistry questions. We further engage
027                domain experts and conduct error analysis, revealing that errors in general chem-
028
                   istry questions primarily occur due to minor inaccuracies at intermediate stages of
                   the problem-solving process, highlighting the need for further research into bal-
029
                   ancing tool use with intrinsic reasoning abilities, to maximize the effectiveness of
030
                   language agents in chemistry. 1

      1       I NTRODUCTION
034
035
      Large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated remarkable capabilities across multiple domains,
036   showcasing their potential as versatile problem-solving tools. However, when it comes to chemistry,
037   these models face significant challenges, such as incorrect calculation, lack of domain knowledge,
038   or their inability to perform certain tasks like reaction prediction (Ramos et al., 2024; Mirza et al.,
039   2024). To address this limitation, researchers have proposed LLM-based agents integrated with
040   specialized tools to tackle a wide range of chemistry-related problems. For example, ChemCrow
041   (M. Bran et al., 2024) incorporates 18 tools, ranging from web search to chemical reaction pre-
042   diction, significantly expanding LLMs’ capabilities in chemistry. Another notable example is Co-
043   scientist (Boiko et al., 2023), which integrates control of a cloud lab, enabling LLMs to automate
044
      chemical experiments.
045   Despite the promise of these tool-augmented LLMs, existing evaluations have been largely qualita-
046   tive and very limited in scope. For instance, ChemCrow (M. Bran et al., 2024) was assessed using
047   only 14 self-created specific tasks, and they primarily focuse on synthesis of compounds. Similarly,
048   Coscientist’s evaluation (Boiko et al., 2023) involved merely six tasks. These narrow assessments
049   leave a significant gap in our understanding of how these tool-augmented agents perform across
050
      diverse chemistry tasks in real-world applications.
051   In this work, we aim to conduct a comprehensive evaluation of tool-augmented agents across di-
052   verse chemistry tasks to grasp a deep understanding of the potential and limitations of existing

055   ble chemistry agent to date. It leverages the ReAct framework (Yao et al., 2023), and integrates 29
056   tools including PubChem and molecular property predictors, as well as many present in ChemCrow
057   (M. Bran et al., 2024). (2) We compile three datasets that cover different types of chemistry prob-
058   lems in real world for comprehensive evaluation: SMolInstruct (Yu et al., 2024), which contains 14
059   types of specialized molecule-centric tasks; MMLU-chemistry , a subset of the MMLU benchmark
060   (Hendrycks et al., 2021) that contains high school and college exam-like questions; and GPQA-
061   chemistry, a subset of the GPQA benchamrk (Rein et al., 2023) that contains difficult graduate-level
062   questions. (3) In order to conduct a meaningful error analysis with actionable insights, we propose
063
      a reasoning-grounding abstraction framework for existing chemistry agents, where reasoning means
      to refelct current status and plan for next step, and grounding means to ground the plan into doable
064
      actions in environment. (4) We engage with chemistry experts to conduct error analysis, where we
065
      analyze the error in each sample, so as to understand the places where agents make mistakes.
066
067   Through comprehensive experiments, we demonstrate that ChemAgent substantially outperforms
068   ChemCrow on all chemistry problems. However, contrary to expectation, ChemAgent does not
069
      consistently outperform the base LLM without tools, and the impact of tool augmentation is
      highly dependent on task characteristics. Specifically, for specialized chemistry tasks involving
070
      professional molecular representations (e.g., SMILES (Weininger, 1988)) and specialized chemical
071
      operations (e.g., compound synthesis, property prediction), augmenting LLMs with task-specific
072   tools can yield substantial performance gains. Conversely, for general chemistry questions that re-
073   quire more extensive internal knowledge and reasoning, there often lacks specific tools to address
074   these needs adequately. In such cases, tool augmentation may potentially impair LLMs’ intrinsic
075   reasoning abilities and lead to diminished performance. Further manual analysis with domain ex-
076   perts shows that errors on general chemistry questions primarily occur due to minor inaccuracies
077   at intermediate stages of the problem-solving process, suggesting the need to improve the intrinsic
078   reasoning abilities of tool-augmented LLMs.

      3.1.2   MMLU-C HEMISTRY
204   To effectively and efficiently evaluate the models, we build MMLU-Chemistry, a subset of 70
205   chemistry question samples derived from the widely-used MMLU dataset (Hendrycks et al., 2021).
206   Specifically, to increase the difficulty and differentiation of the questions, while avoiding erroneous
207   samples presented in the original MMLU, we select samples that appear in both MMLU-Pro (Wang
208   et al., 2024) and MMLU-Redux (Gema et al., 2024). These two datasets are verified versions of
209   MMLU, and MMLU-Pro has extended the answer options from 4 to 10 to introduce more chal-
210   lenges. When the gold standard answers from both sources match, we utilize the 10 options from
211   MMLU-Pro. In cases of discrepancies, we manually review and correct any potential issues. To
212
      reduce the cost of evaluation, we eliminated samples where all models performed correctly in our
      preliminary experiments. This results in a final set of 70 questions, divided evenly between 35 high
213
      school-level and 35 college-level questions.
214

      From the results presented in Table 4, we can draw several key observations:
237
238   Contrary to expectations, the ChemAgent models frequently underperforms their base LLM
239   counterparts across multiple configurations. Specifically, while ChemAgent achieves the highest
240
      overall performance in one specific configuration (Claude, 5-shot), it demonstrates inferior perfor-
      mance compared to the base LLMs in all other configurations. Notably, there exists a substantial
241
      performance gap (11.9%) between GPT-4o and the GPT-based ChemAgent in the 0-shot condition.
242
      This trend persists across both high school and college subsets, and is also observed with ChemCrow,
243   suggesting a consistent pattern rather than an isolated occurrence. This observation challenges the
244   intuitive assumption that tool augmentation would invariably enhance the capabilities of base LLMs
245   by providing additional valuable information. It also contradicts the expectation that an agent system
246   could default to raw LLM capabilities when tools offer no advantage. Our empirical evidence indi-
247   cates that this is not uniformly the case, highlighting the requirement of more attention on building
248   tool-augmented agents on certain applications.
249   Comparing 0-shot and 5-shot performance, the addition of examples (5-shot) yields minimal
250   improvement for base LLMs but results in significant enhancement for ChemAgent. This
251   disparity may be attributed to the extensive pre-training of base LLMs on general chemistry ques-
252   tions, potentially rendering additional examples redundant for task comprehension. Conversely, for
253   ChemAgent, the step-wise demonstration examples appear to effectively guide the LLMs in reason-
254   ing and tool utilization, thereby optimizing the problem-solving process. This finding suggests that
255   incorporating examples can be a valuable strategy for enhancing the performance of agent systems.

350   failed to provide correct answers. The chemistry expert was invited to meticulously examine the
351   discrepancies between ChemAgent’s responses and those of GPT-4o and list the errors. The results
352   of this analysis are presented in Figure 4, from which we can draw several significant observations:
353   Unlike the SMoIInstruct dataset where tool errors predominate, the MMLU-Chemistry
354   dataset reveals a higher proportion of reasoning errors, accounting for nearly 70%. This shift
355   can be attributed to the nature of MMLU tasks, which typically demand broader knowledge and
356   more intricate chemical reasoning while relying less on external tools.
357
      The observed reasoning errors tend to manifest as minor inaccuracies at various interme-
358   diate stages of the problem-solving process. Among the 7 reasoning errors in Figure 4, none
359   resulted from an incorrect overall method. Instead, they arose from small mistakes during execu-
360   tion. For instance, ”faulty assumptions” occurred when the model applied inapplicable conditions,
361   while ”wrong domain reasoning” resulted from incorrect logical reasoning steps. This behavior re-
362   sembles that of a student who understands the overall concept but makes careless mistakes under
363   exam conditions. Compared to raw LLMs, the tool-augmented agent seems to be more prone to
364   such errors.
365   Although less prevalent than in the SMoIInstruct dataset, tool errors remain a significant
366   portion of the observed errors. This persistence underscores the ongoing need for refinement and
367   enhancement of the tools integrated into the ChemAgent system.

      Recent advancements in large language models (LLMs) have led to the development of sophisti-

376   consistently outperforming GPT-4 in accuracy. Similarly, Coscientist exemplifies the integration of
377   semi-autonomous robots in planning and executing chemical reactions with minimal human inter-
      vention. Other notable agents include Chemist-X (Chen et al., 2024), which focuses on designing

      5   C ONCLUSION
398
399
      In this paper, we conducted a comprehensive evaluation of tool-augmented language agents for
400   chemistry problem-solving. Our study introduced ChemAgent, an advanced agent leveraging 29
401   specialized tools, and assessed its performance across diverse chemistry tasks using three datasets:
402   SMolInstruct, MMLU-Chemistry, and GPQA-Chemistry.
403
      Our findings reveal that while ChemAgent substantially outperforms ChemCrow and demonstrates
404
      significant improvements on specialized tasks, it does not consistently surpass the base LLMs with-
405
      out tools. The impact of tool augmentation is highly dependent on task characteristics. For tasks
406   requiring specialized molecular operations, tool integration yields notable performance gains. How-
407   ever, for general chemistry questions necessitating extensive reasoning and domain knowledge, tool
408   augmentation may hinder performance.
409
      The error analysis highlights that tool errors predominate in specialized tasks, whereas reasoning
410
      errors are more frequent in general chemistry questions. This suggests the need for robust tool
411
      implementations and enhanced reasoning capabilities.
412
413   Overall, our research underscores the potential and limitations of tool-augmented LLMs in chem-
414   istry, emphasizing the importance of task-specific tool selection and integration strategies. Future
415
      work should focus on improving tool accuracy and developing mechanisms to balance tool use with
      intrinsic reasoning abilities to maximize the effectiveness of language agents in chemistry.
