011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            This paper introduces HYCOMB, a cascading Hybrid model that innovatively in-
014            tegrates Collaborative Filtering (CF), Content-Based Filtering (CB), and Context-
015
               Aware (CA) methods to address the challenge of data sparsity in tag recommenda-
               tion systems. Unlike traditional models that rely heavily on user-item interactions,
016
               HYCOMB enhances recommendation diversity and interpretability by utilizing
017
               semantic clustering in CF to extract and analyze user sentiment from tags, adding
018            a layer of nuanced understanding often missing in conventional systems. The CB
019            component advances this by applying sophisticated NLP techniques to refine these
020            recommendations based on item attributes, while the CA component incorporates
021            movie synopses for deeper contextual understanding. Developed and tested using
022            the MovieLens 20M dataset, our model demonstrates significant outperformance
023            over baseline methods in terms of precision and recall, achieving scores of 0.813
024            and 0.364 respectively. Further, a newly introduced Overall Total Similarity met-
025            ric that underscores its ability to deliver relevant and diverse recommendations.
026
               HYCOMB’s strategic amalgamation of CF, CB, and CA not only mitigates the
               effects of sparse data but also improves the precision and diversity of tag recom-
027
               mendations, reflecting a more accurate alignment with user preferences.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Tag recommendation systems are pivotal in organizing and personalizing content on various online
033   platforms, especially within social media and content management systems. These systems leverage
034   user-enabled tagging, where individuals freely assign tags or labels to describe content in a person-
035   alized manner, thereby enhancing user interaction. Tags serve as a critical form of User Generated
036   Content, improving search accuracy, content management, and user engagement, which are essen-
037   tial for platform dynamics (Iferroudjene et al., 2023; Najafabadi et al., 2019b). Recommendation
038   algorithms harness these tags to understand user preferences and generate personalized recommen-
039
      dations, thus guiding users toward content that resonates with their interests and enhancing content
      discoverability. The primary methodologies employed are collaborative filtering (CF), content-based
040
      (CB) filtering, and context-aware (CA) strategies, each presenting unique benefits and inherent lim-
041
      itations that could impact the efficacy of recommendations (Casillo et al., 2023; Najafabadi et al.,
042   2017; Najafabadi & Mahrin, 2016) .
043
044   CF algorithms analyze user behavior data to identify patterns and suggest tags based on shared
045
      preferences. For example, users tagging ”outdoor activities” might receive recommendations for
      ”hiking” or ”camping.” Despite its utility, CF faces challenges with data sparsity, reducing the qual-
046
      ity of recommendations when user interactions are sparse (Casillo et al., 2023; Lonjarret et al.,
047
      2021; Najafabadi et al., 2017; Najafabadi & Mahrin, 2016) . CB approaches analyze the features
048   of items—textual, visual, etc.—to suggest relevant tags. For instance, a recipe might be tagged as
049   ”vegetarian” or ”pasta.” While CB methods are effective, they can result in narrow recommenda-
050   tions that may not fully reflect broader user interests or ensure diversity due to limited item context
051   (Karabila et al., 2023; Najafabadi et al., 2019a; Sun et al., 2015) . CA methods enhance recommen-
052   dations by incorporating contextual elements such as the user’s location, time of day, or device type.
053   For example, a context-aware system might recommend workout-themed playlists when a user is
      jogging in the morning (Casillo et al., 2023; Najafabadi et al., 2017).

098   2.1   OVERVIEW OF TAG R ECOMMENDATION A PPROACHES
099
100   CF is a widely used method in tag recommendation systems, often chosen to tackle the data sparsity
101   issue. This challenge arises from the inherent structure of the user-item matrix, which records inter-
102   actions between users and items based on previous ratings or tags. Typically, this matrix is sparse,
103   with few non-null entries, as the number of items significantly exceeds the number of users.
104   User-based CF predicts ratings by identifying users with similar rating behaviors. These similar
105   users form a basis for predictions, making CF particularly effective in the presence of sparse data.
106   Furthermore, clustering has been successfully applied to enhance CF systems by grouping items
107   based on semantic similarity. This approach creates a denser representation of preferences, improv-
      ing the accuracy of similarity calculations within a sparse matrix. Hybrid models have demonstrated


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      promising results in addressing the data sparsity problem in recommendation systems. These models
109   often merge matrix factorization techniques with neighbor-based methods, leveraging the strengths
110   of one approach to offset the weaknesses of the other. For example, hybrid collaborative recom-
111   mendation approaches combine collaborative filtering with matrix factorization and implicit user
112   feedback (Xu et al., 2023; Xin et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2020) .
113
      Review-Based Matrix Factorization (RMF) is highlighted as a method that integrates review-based
114
      hybrid collaborative filtering with matrix factorization, specifically addressing data sparsity issues.
115   RMF utilizes sentiment analysis to extract detailed product features and consumer opinions from re-
116   views, constructing an item-topic rating matrix for unknown user rating predictions. This approach
117   not only mitigates overfitting problems in sparse rating datasets but also harnesses textual reviews to
118   enhance recommendation accuracy, demonstrating significant applicative value in product promo-
119   tion (Duan et al., 2022).
120
      Another effective hybrid method involves co-embedding item attributes and user ratings (Onan,
121   2023; Najafabadi et al., 2021; Yang et al., 2018), this approach integrates a rich set of item features
122   to understand the semantic similarity between items, which is crucial when user-item interactions are
123   sparse. By blending item attributes with user ratings, the model gains insights into user preferences
124   and identifies which attributes are most influential in recommendations.
125
      Previous techniques of fusion models used for recommending tags has attracted attention, the at-
126
      tempts have been proposed to improve the performance. The method hybrid CF with tags and time
127   (Zhang et al., 2018) novelly combines user-based and item-based CF with utilizing tag information
128   to calculate item or user similarity to address data sparsity. However, its genre tag may not capture
129   nuances of movie content granularity and the cosine similarity for tag calculation may not show the
130   weighted importance of tags. Another attempt hybrid CF with convolutional neural network model
131   (Khan et al., 2021) combines traditional CF and deep learning methods to improve tag recommenda-
132   tion, it analyzes how CNN and CF model complement each other but doesn’t analyze the correlation
133   between tags for recommendation, although outperforms state-of-the-art methods in terms of recall,
134   it does not perform well on other metrics especially when training data is sparse. Deep-learning
135
      methods are also used to build recommendation models, label-wise attention and adversarial train-
      ing for tag recommendation (Wang et al., 2021) adopts BERT-based label-wise attention using ad-
136
      versarial training to fine-tune model performance but increases the chance of overfitting and poor
137
      generalization to unseen data. Graph-based model for knowledge enhanced tag-aware recommen-
138   dation (Wang et al., 2022) is proposed with the use of auxiliary user tag knowledge to enhance
139   user preference modeling, it can naturally address the sparsity issue with an additional attention-
140   based graph that adaptively learns from heterogenous neighbours. The complexity of collaborative
141   recommendation graph reduces transparency in prediction and require external sources for data to
142   construct the auxiliary graph which may not be carried out in dataset in small scale.
143
      The work presented in our paper is designed to address the challenges of data sparsity and en-
144   hance the personalization of tag recommendations. Unlike existing methods, which typically focus
145   on singular aspects of recommendation systems, our approach synergistically leverages multiple di-
146   mensions of user and item data. We enrich traditional CF and CB models by incorporating sentiment
147   analysis, adding an emotional context that captures more nuanced user preferences. Our context-
148   aware component further tailors recommendations to users’ environmental and temporal contexts,
149   ensuring a dynamic and responsive recommendation system. This comprehensive model not only
150   addresses the limitations of traditional hybrid models in handling sparse data but also significantly
151   enhances the diversity and accuracy of recommendations, marking a pioneering advancement in the
152
      field of recommendation systems.

211   3.1   C OLLABORATIVE F ILTERING BASED ON S ENTIMENT A NALYSIS
212
213   In order to better generate candidate tags with utilizing advanced natural language processing, we
214   propose collaborative filtering based on sentiment analysis. Initially, we process pre-processed tags
215   along with a subset of user data. This involves conducting semantic analysis to generate clusters
      of similar tags, utilizing their GloVe vector representations. Subsequently, sentiment analysis is


                                                       4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      performed on these clusters to evaluate the emotional context of the tags. Following this, we apply
217   traditional CF to these sentiment-enriched tags to generate an initial set of recommendations. This
218   set serves as the input for further refinement in the CB model. Here’s how the process unfolds:
219
220
      Step 1: Semantic analysis is the initial step where we convert tags into numeric representations
      using pre-trained GloVe vectors. This process enables the model to understand and interpret the
221
      meanings of tags based on their usage in specific contexts. Utilizing GloVe embeddings pre-trained
222
      on extensive datasets like Wikipedia allows us to capture complex semantic relationships without the
223   overhead of training from scratch, ensuring our model quickly adapts to the nuances of movie-related
224   content. Step 2: Following semantic analysis, tags are clustered to enhance the semantic precision
225   in sentiment analysis. We employ a Hybrid Hierarchical-K-means++ Clustering approach, which
226   optimizes centroid selection to improve cluster quality. This method effectively groups semantically
227   similar tags, facilitating the aggregation of sentiments that are expected to be similar. The cluster-
228   ing process ensures that the tags within each cluster reflect a uniform sentiment, which simplifies
229   the subsequent sentiment analysis steps. Step 3: After clustering, sentiment analysis is conducted
230   using a fine-tuned pre-trained classification model. The model is initially fine-tuned on a subset of
231
      manually labeled tags, identified through active learning to maximize efficiency. Once fine-tuned,
      the model’s accuracy is validated and then used to assign sentiment values to the remaining tags, en-
232
      suring comprehensive and precise sentiment mapping across the dataset. Step 4:In the final step, we
233
      construct a user-movie matrix where each entry reflects a sentiment value derived from user-tagged
234   interactions. This matrix forms the basis of our collaborative filtering process, where sentiment sim-
235   ilarity between users guides the recommendation system. By employing a K-Nearest Neighbours
236   algorithm, we identify users with similar sentiment tendencies, allowing us to recommend movies
237   that align with their expressed preferences.
238
      Through this process, recommendations are tailored not only by suggesting movies but also by
239
      predicting tags that these users are likely to use based on their sentiment profile. The list of recom-
240   mendations for each user is passed to the content-based filtering model. This provides a set of initial
241   recommendations that is further enhanced and refined.

      3.2   C ONTENT-BASED R EFINEMENT IN C OLLABORATIVE F ILTERING
244
245   The content-based (CB) filtering model enhances the recommendations from the CF approach by
246   addressing data sparsity issues and leveraging tag-based features to improve recommendation qual-
247   ity. In this process, we utilize the same subset of users and tags processed in the CF model to ensure
248   consistency and stability in the foundation of our recommendations. This alignment helps maintain
249   coherence between the models. Five components contained in Content-Based Analysis: Named En-
250   tity Recognition (NER), Part of Speech (POS) Tagging, Semantic Clusters, Sentiment Features, and
251   Unsupervised Word Sense Disambiguation (WSD).
252   • Based on WNUT 2017 taxonomy, we fine-tune the NER pre-trained model using MoDAL to fit
253   our data. By assigning NER labels to tags, we can identify and classify named entities within tags
254   to better understand their context and relevance.
255
      • Employing POS tagging from spaCy model, we analyze the grammatical roles of tags, aiding in
256
      the precise interpretation of their semantics.
257
258   • Semantic Clusters and Sentiment Features are extracted from the previous CF model, which respec-
259   tively provide group similar meanings and sentiments and deepen understanding of user preferences
260
      expressed through tags, thus enhancing the specificity of the recommendations.
261   • Critically, the model resolves ambiguities in tag meanings by utilizing a context-aware model. Un-
262   like typical WSD applied at sentence or document levels, our model tackles single-word tags which
263   lack surrounding lexical information. To effectively disambiguate these tags, we utilize context vec-
264   tors created in the Context-Aware Model for each movie. For instance, the tag ‘bat’ could ambigu-
265   ously refer to both an animal and sports equipment. By applying the context vector, ’bat’ associated
266
      with a documentary about animals is accurately identified as the mammal, while in a baseball film,
      it is recognized as sporting equipment. This nuanced approach ensures that each tag is interpreted
267
      in its correct context, significantly enhancing the accuracy of our tag-based recommendations.
268
269   After the tags are fully processed and analyzed, the model refines the initial CF recommendations by
      comparing the semantic and sentiment-related features of user tags with those of the recommended

      tags. This comparison employs kernel density estimation between context vectors and sentiment
271   scores to assess similarity. The model outputs a list of tag recommendations that best match the
272   user’s preferences based on semantic similarity and sentiment alignment. These recommendations

274   ences.

      3.3   I NTEGRATION OF C ONTEXT IN THE H YBRID M ODEL
280
281   In our hybrid recommendation model, context is critical for interpreting user-item interactions effec-
282   tively, particularly within the social/entertainment domains characteristic of the MovieLens dataset.
283   This model views the item, specifically movies, as essential contextual features. Tags, when used
284   in isolation, can introduce ambiguity due to their varied meanings across different contexts. For
285   instance, the same tag may convey different meanings when applied to different movies, especially
286   since there are no restrictions on tag repetition. Three steps ensure movie-related data is integrated
287   to establish a cohesive contextual framework for effective tag disambiguation and refined recom-
288
      mendation delivery.
289   Step 1. Identification of Contextual Features: Movies are designated as key contextual features due
290   to their critical role in clarifying the meanings and relevance of tags. This strategy acknowledges
291   that tags, devoid of contextual support, are prone to misinterpretations since their significances can
292   shift dramatically across different films. Step 2. Creation of Context Vectors for Movies: We extract
293   synopses from trusted sources to capture the thematic and narrative elements of each movie. Addi-
294
      tionally, we construct weighted context vectors utilizing the retrieved synopses and movie genres.
      We employ GloVe for semantic richness and TF-IDF to emphasize contextually significant terms
295
      within the synopses, ensuring the vectors are both rich in semantic detail and contextually relevant.
296
      Step 3. Integration with Content-Based Model: Application in Word Sense Disambiguation (WSD):
297   Within the content-based filtering model, these context vectors are pivotal for the WSD task. They
298   furnish vital insights into the broader narrative and thematic contexts of the films, facilitating more
299   accurate disambiguation of tag meanings relative to the movie genre and synopsis.
300
      This approach not only boosts the specificity of our recommendations by ensuring tags are aligned
301
      with their corresponding movie contexts but also elevates the overall accuracy of the content-based
302
      model’s outputs.

482   in each method by synergistically leveraging their strengths. Our model addresses the prevalent
483   issue of data sparsity in CF through the implementation of semantic clustering using GloVe vectors,
484   enhancing both the diversity and the quality of recommendations. Simultaneously, the CB model
