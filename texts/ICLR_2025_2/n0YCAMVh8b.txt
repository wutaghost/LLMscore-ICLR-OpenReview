009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) are the backbone of many deep learning
012            methods, but optimizing them remains computationally expensive. To address
013            this, we explore multiscale training frameworks and mathematically identify key
014            challenges, particularly when dealing with noisy inputs. Our analysis reveals that
015            in the presence of noise, the gradient of standard CNNs in multiscale training may
016            fail to converge as the mesh-size approaches to 0, undermining the optimization
017
               process. This insight drives the development of Mesh-Free Convolutions (MFCs),
               which are independent of input scale and avoid the pitfalls of traditional convo-
018
               lution kernels. We demonstrate that MFCs, with their robust gradient behavior,
019
               ensure convergence even with noisy inputs, enabling more efficient neural network
020            optimization in multiscale settings. To validate the generality and effectiveness of
021            our multiscale training approach, we show that (i) MFCs can theoretically deliver
022            substantial computational speedups without sacrificing performancein practice,
023            and (ii) standard convolutions benefit from our multiscale training framework in
024            practice.

      In deep learning, multiscale or pyramidal approaches have been used in image processing tasks such
      as object detection, segmentation, and recognition, where analyzing multiple resolutions is key (Scott
067
      & Mjolsness, 2019; Chada et al., 2022), reviewed in Elizar et al. (2022). Recent methods improve
068
      standard CNNs for multiscale computations by introducing specialized architectures and training
069   methods. For instance, He & Xu (2019) uses multigrid methods in CNNs to boost efficiency and
070   capture multiscale features, while Eliasof et al. (2023b) focuses on multiscale channel space learning,
071   and van Betteray et al. (2023) unifies both. Li et al. (2020b) introduced the Fourier Neural Operator,
072   enabling mesh-independent computations, and Wavelet-NNs were explored to capture multiscale
073   features via wavelets (Fujieda et al., 2018; Finder et al., 2022; Eliasof et al., 2023a).
074
      While often overlooked, it is important to note that these approaches, can be divided into two families
075   of approaches that leverage multiscale concepts. The first is to learn parameters for each scale, and a
076   separate set of parameters that mix scales, as in UNet (Ronneberger et al., 2015). The second, called
077   multiscale training, enables the approximation of fine-scale parameters using coarse-scale samples
078   (Haber et al., 2017; Wang et al., 2020; Ding et al., 2020; Ganapathy & Liew, 2008). The second
079   approach aims to gain computational efficiency, as it approximates fine mesh parameters using coarse
080   meshes, and it can be coupled with the first approach, and in particular with UNets.
081   Our approach. This work falls into the second category of multiscale training. We study multiscale
082   algorithms that use coarse meshes to approximate high-resolution quantities, particularly the gradients
083   of network parameters. Computing gradients on coarse grids is significantly cheaper than on fine
084   grids, as noted in Shi & Cornish (2021). However, for efficient multiscale training, parameters on
085   coarse and fine meshes must have "similar meaning," implying that both the loss and gradient on
086   coarse meshes should approximate those on fine meshes. Specifically, the loss and gradient with
087   respect to parameters should converge to a finite value as h ‚Üí 0. In this work, we show that standard
088   CNN gradients may not converge as the mesh size h approaches 0, suggesting CNNs under-utilize
089
      multiscale training. This motivates our development of mesh-free convolution kernels, whose values
      and gradients converge as h ‚Üí 0. Our approach builds on Differential-Operator theory (Wong, 2014)
090
      to create a family of learnable, mesh-independent convolutions for multiscale learning, resembling
091
      Fourier Neural Operators (FNO) (Li et al., 2020a) but with further expressiblity.
092
093   Our main contributions are: 1. Propose a new multiscale training algorithm, Multiscale SGD.
094   2. Analyze the limitations of standard CNNs within a multiscale framework. 3. Introduce a family of
095
      mesh-independent CNNs inspired by differential operators. 4. Validate our approach on benchmark
      tasks, showcasing enhanced efficiency and scalability.

527   6     C ONCLUSIONS
528
529   In this paper, we introduced a novel approach to multiscale training for deep neural networks,
530   addressing the limitations of standard mesh-based convolutions. We showed that theoretically,
531
      standard convolutions may not converge as h ‚Üí 0 for noisy inputs, hindering performance. To solve
      this, we proposed mesh-free convolutions inspired by parabolic PDEs with trainable parameters.
532
      Our experiments demonstrated that mesh-free convolutions ensure consistent, mesh-independent
533
      convergence, also in the presence of noisy inputs. Moreover, our empirical findings suggest that
534   our multiscale training approach can also be coupled with standard convolutions ‚Äď positioning our
535   multiscale training approach as an alternative to standard SGD. This approach is well-suited for high-
536   resolution and 3D learning tasks where scalability and precision are crucial. Although the current
537   implementation of FFT-based convolutions like our MFCs is slower than standard convolutions, their
538   theoretical FLOPs count is competitive. We hope that the theoretical merits discussed in this paper
539   will inspire the development of efficient implementations of such methods.

1074   image on the finest resolution, 2 images on the next resolution, 4 on the third resolution, and 8 on the
1075   coarsest resolution. Thus, we compare its runtimes with the time it takes to process a batch of 15

       MFC-ResNet architectures.
