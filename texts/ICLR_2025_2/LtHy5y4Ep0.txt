011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Black-box prompt learning has proven to be an effective approach for customiz-
014            ing large language models (LLMs) offered as services to address various down-
015            stream tasks. Within this domain, policy gradient-based methods have garnered
016            substantial attention as a prominent approach for learning discrete prompts. How-
017            ever, the highly imbalanced data distribution in the real world limits the appli-
018            cability of such approaches by influencing LLMsÔÇÖ tendency to favor certain cat-
019            egories. To tackle the challenge posed by imbalanced data, this paper pioneers
020
               the integration of pairwise AUC loss into the policy gradient optimization of dis-
               crete text prompts and proposes learning discrete prompts with doubly policy
021
               gradient. Unfortunately, the doubly policy gradient estimation suffers from two
022
               variance components, resulting in unstable optimization. As a further improve-
023            ment, we propose (1) a novel unbiased variance-reduced doubly policy gradient
024            estimator and (2) incorporating the STORM variance reduction technique. Ulti-
025            mately, we introduce a novel momentum-based discrete prompt learning method
026            with doubly policy gradient (mDP-DPG). Crucially, we provide theoretical con-
027            vergence guarantees for mDP-DPG within standard frameworks. The experimen-
028            tal results show that mDP-DPG surpasses baseline approaches across diverse im-
029            balanced text classification datasets, emphasizing the advantages of our proposed
030            approach for tackling data imbalance. Our code is available at the following URL:
031
               https://anonymous.4open.science/r/DPDPG-1ECB.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036
037   Large language models (LLMs) have achieved milestone accomplishments on a wide range of nat-
038   ural language processing (NLP) tasks (Brown et al., 2020; Devlin et al., 2018; Raffel et al., 2020).
039
      However, the increasing parameters pose challenges for tuning. Prompting has emerged as the
      parameter-efficient paradigm for adapting LLMs to specific NLP tasks (Li & Liang, 2021; Lester
040
      et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2023). Well-crafted prompts can effectively enhance the performance of
041
      LLMs on various downstream tasks instead of retraining. Recently, considering that most exist-
042   ing LLMs, such as GPT-4, only provide cloud-based API services, researchers have introduced the
043   Language-Model-as-a-Service scenario, where users are limited to interacting with LLMs solely
044   through APIs, creating a black-box setting (Sun et al., 2022b;a). Black-box prompt learning has
045   been an effective strategy for adapting LLMs to downstream tasks due to the opacity of black-box
046   LLMs (Deng et al., 2022; Prasad et al., 2022; Diao et al., 2022).
047
      Currently, much attention has been focused on black-box discrete prompt learning, with policy
048   gradient-based methods becoming highly influential (Diao et al., 2022; Lin et al., 2023). Within
049   these investigations, discrete prompt learning is treated as a distribution optimization problem, using
050   policy gradient to update the prompt distribution and overcome the issue of inaccessible gradients in
051   black-box LLMs. However, these efforts focus solely on vanilla text classification without any addi-
052   tional handling of imbalanced data. These imply that adapting them to address the class imbalance
053   problem to bridge the gap between LLM services and downstream tasks while providing theoretical
      convergence guarantees remains a significant challenge.


                                                        1
054
      Departing from idealized situations, real-world data usually features severe class distribution imbal-
055   ances, where certain minority classes are markedly less prevalent than majority classes in classifi-
056   cation problems (Henning et al., 2022). For example, non-hateful tweets are more prevalent than
057   hateful ones on Twitter (Waseem & Hovy, 2016), and the positive and negative reviews on Amazon
058   are also imbalanced. Specifically, the ratio of negative to positive movie reviews is approximately
059   6.24, while it is as high as 9.75 for book reviews (Gao et al., 2021).
060
      Simultaneously, imbalanced class distributions hinder prompt learning by making LLMs more likely
061   to prioritize well-represented classes, which in turn lowers prompt performance. Dong et al. (2022)
062   initially identified this phenomenon within the computer vision (CV) domain, noting that prompts
063   could benefit models on long-tail data, but their performance still lagged far behind the state-of-
064   the-art. Similarly, class imbalance issues also impact the NLP black-box prompt learning. When
065   the downstream task is a text classification problem, cross-entropy is typically chosen to build the
066   objective function. However, this becomes unreasonable in the presence of imbalanced data since
067   the minority class data have little influence, leading to the aforementioned issues (Liu et al., 2020).
068   A common approach in previous research for addressing imbalanced class distributions is to use
069   AUC (Area Under the ROC Curve) maximization as the optimization objective. The AUC is de-
070   fined as the probability that the prediction score for a positive example exceeds that for a negative
071   example in statistical terms (Hanley & McNeil, 1982; 1983), and AUC maximization is proposed
072   to address the imbalanced data problem (Zhao et al., 2011; Yuan et al., 2020). However, high
073   variance emerges as a major challenge in the learning process of prompts in two respects. Firstly,
074   the sampling of prompts from the prompt distribution introduces inherent randomness, making the
075
      optimization process unstable. Secondly, sampling both positive and negative examples for AUC
      maximization will also introduce high variance. In scenarios with imbalanced classes, the sampling
076
      of data for training not only amplifies the disparity between majority and minority classes but also
077
      increases the variance of gradient estimation during optimization. This duality of variance poses sig-
078   nificant difficulties in effectively learning prompts that generalize well across all classes, particularly
079   in settings with highly imbalanced data.
080
081
      In order to tackle the above problems, we propose a novel momentum-based discrete prompt learn-
      ing method with doubly policy gradient (mDP-DPG) that utilizes AUC maximization to adapt
082
      LLMs to downstream tasks with imbalanced data. By minimizing AUCÔÇÖs pairwise surrogate loss us-
083
      ing policy gradient, mDP-DPG prevents prompts from being degraded by the majority class, thereby
084   preserving performance. Moreover, due to the doubly sampling of examples during gradient estima-
085   tion, we refer to the policy gradient in mDP-DPG as the doubly sampled policy gradient, abbreviated
086   as doubly policy gradient. We further propose an unbiased, variance-reduced doubly policy gradi-
087   ent estimator (VR-DPGE) to improve convergence in practice. While the estimator suffers from
088   variance, stochastic sampling of mini-batches from the dataset also introduces variance into gradi-
089   ent estimation. To reduce the dual variance, we introduce the momentum-based variance reduction
090   strategy STORM (Cutkosky & Orabona, 2019; Huang et al., 2020). STORM does not rely on con-
091   structing variance-reduced gradients through giant batch sizes, as SVRG does (Johnson & Zhang,
092
      2013; Xiao & Zhang, 2014). Instead, it employs a variant of momentum, making it can be seam-
      lessly integrated into the optimization of pairwise AUC loss for variance reduction. Additionally,
093
      unlike other policy gradient-based methods for black-box discrete prompt learning, mDP-DPG has
094
      rigorous theoretical convergence guarantees.
095
096   The main contributions of this work are summarized as follows:
097
098
            1. We propose a novel momentum-based discrete prompt learning method named mDP-DPG,
               which introduces VR-DPGE and STROM strategy to address challenges posed by dual
099
               variance. Using pairwise AUC loss as objective function, mDP-DPG preserves promptsÔÇÖ
100
               performance in downstream tasks with class imbalance. As far as we know, we are the first
101            to discuss how to address imbalanced data in, NLP prompt learning.
102
103         2. We establish rigorous theoretical analysis of the mDP-DPG. Specifically, we provide proof
104
               that mDP-DPG achieves an oracle complexity of O(1/¤Á3 ), validating its effectiveness in
               optimizing the pairwise AUC loss for black-box prompt learning in the context of imbal-
105
               anced data.
106
107         3. Numerous experimental results on RoBERTa-large, GPT2-XL, and Llmma3 show that our
               method achieve state-of-the-art across various class imbalance datasets, demonstrating the

                effectiveness of prompts learned through mDP-DPG on imbalanced data. Furthermore,

113   2    R ELATED W ORKS
114
115   Black-Box Prompt Learning. There is a significant amount of research focusing on black-box
116   prompt learning, which has achieved promising results in NLP tasks (Brown et al., 2020; Prasad
117   et al., 2022; Han et al., 2023; Hou et al., 2023). Prompts come in two formats: continuous and
118
      discrete. The continuous prompt is a series of vectors, which concatenates with token embeddings.
      Sun et al. (2022b) propose the black-box tuning (BBT) framework, which optimizes prompts in
119
      low-dimensional subspace and obtains continuous prompts in the original space through a random
120
      matrix. Sun et al. (2022a) present an improved version of BBT that adds continuous prompt prefixes
121   to each hidden layer of LLM. Zheng et al. (2023) point out the inappropriateness of random matric
122   in Sun et al. (2022b) and leverage meta-learning to identify the optimal subspace. On the other hand,
123   the discrete prompt is a sequence of tokens, which is more appropriate for real applications. Deng
124   et al. (2022) formulate discrete prompt learning as a reinforcement learning problem and generate
125   discrete prompts using policy network. Diao et al. (2022) utilize policy gradients to optimize the
126   distribution of the discrete prompt. Zhao et al. (2023) introduce a genetic algorithm to search for
127   discrete prompts guided by LLMs predictive probabilities.
128   AUC Maximization. AUC maximization is a machine learning paradigm aimed at maximizing the
129   AUC score of models. A substantial amount of research has been dedicated to this topic, integrating
130   it with various contexts such as supervised learning (Joachims, 2005), semi-supervised learning
131   (Iwata et al., 2020), online learning (Gao et al., 2016), and federated learning (Yuan et al., 2021).
132   Many algorithms frequently minimize the pairwise surrogate loss for AUC maximization. Zhao
133   et al. (2011) propose two online AUC maximization algorithms, which utilize the reservoir sampling
134   technique to avoid memorizing all training samples. Gao et al. (2016) focus on the challenge of
135
      optimizing with only a single pass of training samples and propose a regression-based algorithm
      using square surrogate loss. The issue of the pairwise surrogate loss lies in the necessity to construct
136
      sample pairs from opposite classes. To overcome this challenge, Ying et al. (2016) demonstrate the
137
      equivalence between AUC optimization and the saddle point problem. Liu et al. (2020) extend the
138   aforementioned equivalence to the case of deep neural network models.
139
140   Variance Reduction. In optimization problems, variance reduction is a frequently utilized improve-
141
      ment method (Cutkosky & Orabona, 2019). Numerous variance reduction techniques necessitate
      setting gradient checkpoints and calculating gradients at these checkpoints with giant batch sizes
142
      Johnson & Zhang (2013); Fang et al. (2018); Zhou et al. (2018). Cutkosky & Orabona (2019) pro-
143
      pose a variance reduction technique based on the momentum variant, termed STORM, which facil-
144   itates variance reduction in non-convex optimization without giant batch sizes. Huang et al. (2020)
145   propose a similar variance reduction technique for zero-order optimization to accelerate black-box
146   minimization and minimax optimization problems.

380   Datasets. To evaluate the effectiveness of our methods, we conduct experiments on 4 datasets
381   including 2 widely used datasets from the GLUE benchmark (Wang et al., 2018), CoLA (Warstadt
382   et al., 2018), MRPC (Dolan & Brockett, 2005),and 2 real-world class imbalanced datasets: Amazon
383   books and electronics reviews (McAuley et al., 2015). For GLUE benchmark, we perform down-
384   sampling on datasets with a given imbalanced ratio (negative to positive samples ratio) ¤ä = 20, 50
385   to construct the imbalanced scenarios. For 2 real-world datasets, we down-sample datasets with
386
      ¤ä = 10, which facilitates experiments and closely approximates the original imbalance ratio. We
      use AUC to measure the performance of handling imbalanced data.
387
388   Backbone Models. We select RoBERTa-large (Liu et al., 2019), GPT2-XL (Radford et al., 2019),
389   Llama3 (AI@Meta, 2024) as our backbone models and conduct experiments separately. These
390   models have approximately 355M, 1.5B, and 8B parameters, respectively.
391   Baselines. We compare our proposed methods with the following black-box prompt learning meth-
392   ods under the same experimental settings: Manual Prompt performs the zero-shot evaluation on
393   the LLMs with human-written templates, and the results can serve as initial points. BBT optimizes
394   the continuous prompts in a random low-dimensional subspace through covariance matrix adapta-
395   tion evolution strategy (Sun et al., 2022b). GAP3 introduces a genetic algorithm that considers the
396   prompt as individual and employs auxiliary LLM to generate discrete prompts from the empty (Zhao
397   et al., 2023). BDPL utilizes policy gradients to optimize discrete prompt distribution as mentioned
398
      in Section 3.1 (Diao et al., 2022).
399   Implementation Details. The proposed methods and all baselines are implemented using PyTorch
400   and experimented on NVIDIA A40 GPUs with 48 GB memory. For all backbone models, we initial-
401   ize them with checkpoints provided by the HuggingFace. The details of the input template, output
402   label words, and hyperparameters can be found in the Appendix C.

527   6     C ONCLUSION
528
529   In this paper, we propose a momentum-based imbalanced black-box discrete prompt learning frame-
530   work mDP-DPG to handle imbalanced data in downstream tasks. Within this framework, we propose
531
      VR-DPGE and introduce the STORM technique for variance reduction to achieve more stable op-
      timization. We demonstrate the effectiveness mDP-DPG on constructed imbalanced scenarios and
532
      real-world imbalanced datasets, showing performance improvements in class imbalance problems.
533
      Although the AUC loss in our framework is specifically tailored for binary classification, we discuss
534   in Appendix D.2 how to overcome the limitations of binary classification and provide additional
535   experimental results. In addition, minimizing pairwise AUC loss in our framework suffers from the
536   challenge of constructing sample pairs from opposite classes. Formulating AUC maximization as
537   an equivalent saddle point problem has become dominant in addressing this challenge. However,
538   this technique cannot be directly applied to our problem, as our objective function requires taking
539   the expectation over prompt T, which would invalidate the existing theoretical derivations. In future
      work, we will investigate how to introduce it to our framework.

1382   D.6   R EAL -W ORLD A PPLIACTION
1383
1384   To show performance of our method in real-world applications, we add three additional represen-
1385   tative imbalance datasets: BB (Burfoot and Baldwin) Burfoot & Baldwin (2009), originally de-
1386   veloped for satire news detection; Job Scams and SMS datasets Boumber et al. (2024), derived
1387   for fraudulent job postings and spam message detection, respectively. The BB dataset consists of
1388   4,000 true news articles and 233 satire articles. Its challenge lies in satire articles mimicking the
1389
       tone and style of true news while incorporating exaggerated or absurd content, requiring semantic
       understanding and background knowledge for accurate classification. The Job Scams dataset, de-
1390
       rived from the Employment Scam Aegean Dataset, includes 14,295 cleaned job advertisements, of
1391
       which 599 are fraudulent postings, presenting a significant class imbalance. Fraudulent postings
1392   often use fake job positions to deceive applicants, typically featuring short and structured texts. The
1393   SMS dataset contains 6,574 messages, of which 1,274 are spam or phishing. These deceptive mes-
1394   sages are typically brief and generic promotional content, whereas genuine messages reflect more
1395   personalized communication. The diversity of these datasets ensures the broad applicability of the
1396   experiments.
