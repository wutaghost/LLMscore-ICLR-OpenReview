009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               Data imbalance and label noise are common factors hindering classifier’s perfor-
012
               mance. Data-level approaches to addressing imbalanced learning usually involve
013
               resampling by adding or removing samples, which often results in information
014            loss or generative errors. Building upon theoretical studies of the impact of im-
015            balance ratio on decision boundaries across various evaluation metrics in binary
016            classification, it is uncovered that introducing appropriate label noise can alter
017            the biased decision boundaries and thus enhance the performance of classifiers
018            in imbalanced learning. In this paper, we introduce the Label-Noise-based Re-
019            balancing (LNR) approach to solve both binary and multi-class imbalanced clas-
020            sifications by employing a novel design of asymmetric label noise model. In con-
021            trast to other data-level methods, our approach is easy to implement and alleviates
022
               the issues of informative loss and generative errors. We validated the superiority
               of this method on synthetic and real-world datasets. More importantly, our LNR
023
               approach can integrate seamlessly with any classifiers and other algorithm-level
024
               methods for imbalanced learning. Overall, our work opens up a new avenue for
025            addressing imbalanced learning, highlighting the potential advantages of balanc-
026            ing data through beneficial label noise.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Imbalanced data is pervasive in many real-world problems, including fraud transactions (Phua et al.,
033   2004), disease diagnoses (Fotouhi et al., 2019), and behavioral analysis (Azaria et al., 2014). These
034   datasets are often characterized by a significant disparity in the number of samples between classes,
035   with some classes possessing a large number of samples while others containing very few samples.
036   This imbalance poses a challenge for standard classification algorithms, which typically require
037   either a similar sample size across classes or a sufficient number of minority class samples to achieve
038   a desired level of efficacy (He & Garcia, 2009; Krawczyk, 2016).
039   In practice, the rarity of samples (often corresponding to abnormal cases) makes the minority class
040   the primary focus, rendering accuracy an inadequate metric for classification, especially in the bi-
041   nary case. Commonly used metrics for imbalanced learning, such as the F1 score, G-mean, and
042   Area Under the Curve (AUC) for binary classification (He & Garcia, 2009; Krawczyk, 2016), or
043   Recall, Precision and Many/Medium/Few-shots for evaluating step-wise or long-tailed multi-class
044   data, better reflect performance in classifying minority classes samples (Cao et al., 2019; Wang et al.,
045
      2021a). In binary classification, this paper theoretically demonstrates that the decision boundary op-
      timized for accuracy deviates from one that maximizes other metrics, such as the F1 score, impairing
046
      classifier performance. When optimizing for accuracy, classifiers tend to classify all samples into
047
      the majority class as the class imbalance becomes extreme, thus limiting the F1 score. Extensive
048   research has attempted imbalanced learning through resampling techniques, which aim to create a
049   more balanced dataset by either removing or adding samples (Kaur et al., 2019; Napierala & Ste-
050   fanowski, 2016; Wang et al., 2020; 2021a). These data-level methods often come with drawbacks
051   such as potential information loss, over-fitting, and the introduction of generative errors. Another
052   line of research focuses on algorithm-level methods (Chawla et al., 2004; Cao et al., 2019; Wang
053   et al., 2021b; Li et al., 2022a), which adjust the learning algorithm itself. However, these methods
      are typically tailored to specific classifiers or problem setups, limiting their general applicability.

054
      Presentation or introduction of label noises, which solely flips the label of instances to another
055   class, significantly impacts the decision boundary of classifiers. This paper theoretically investi-
056   gates how the imbalance ratio affects the decision boundaries under different metrics like accuracy
057   and F1 score in binary classification. Inspired by recent studies (Ahfock & McLachlan, 2021; Can-
058   nings et al., 2020) on harmless label noises that do not degrade classifier performance under certain
059   conditions, we propose a novel Label-Noise-Re-balancing (LNR) approach that introduces artificial
060   label noise to reallocate imbalanced data labels and thus modify the decision boundary to improve
061   classifier performance. In addition, we extend the LNR approach to address multi-class classifica-
062   tion problems where class imbalance is present. Our proposed LNR method operates at the data
063
      level, making it flexible and adaptable for use with any classifier or algorithm-level method. This
      seamless integration allows for enhanced performance in both binary and multi-class settings, with-
064
      out being restricted to specific classifiers or requiring significant algorithmic modifications, thereby
065
      offering a robust solution to imbalanced learning challenges.

      2      R ELATED WORKS AND CONTRIBUTIONS
093
      Imbalance learning. In imbalanced binary classification, resampling is a one-time data-level ap-
094
      proach that balances sample sizes through under-sampling or over-sampling. Over the past decades,
095
      extensive approaches have been proposed to address the issues of information loss from under-
096   sampling and over-fitting from over-sampling. Solutions include methods for selectively removing
097   samples based on distribution information, such as Tomek-links (Tomek, 1976), ENN (Wilson &
098   Martinez, 2000), NCL (Li et al., 2022a), OSS (Kubat & Matwin, 2000), and ClusterCentroids (Lin
099   et al., 2017), as well as techniques for synthesizing new minority class samples based on minor-
100   ity class distribution information, such as SMOTE (Chawla et al., 2002), Borderline-SMOTE (Han
101   et al., 2005), and ADASYN (He et al., 2008). These methods are often combined with conventional
102   machine learning algorithms.
103   In multi-class classification, imbalance scenarios such as long-tailed or step-wise sample size dis-
104   tributions become more complex due to multiple minority classes and varying imbalance ratios.
105   Algorithm-level methods can be divided into two main types: multi-expert and multi-stage ap-
106   proaches. Multi-expert methods, such as RIDE (Wang et al., 2021b) and NCL (Li et al., 2022a),
107   reduce uncertainty in minority (tail) classes by employing multiple experts, enhancing representa-
      tion through knowledge aggregation. Multi-stage methods, like LDAM-DWR (Cao et al., 2019)


                                                                                   2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      and GCL (Li et al., 2022b), improve tail class accuracy by splitting training into feature learn-
109   ing and classifier learning phases, using techniques like logit margin adjustments and reweighting.
110   Data-level methods enrich minority classes by generating new samples using generative models or
111   auto-encoders (Wang et al., 2020; Schwartz et al., 2018; Wang et al., 2018; 2021a). These genera-
112   tive solutions typically rely on high-quality pre-trained models or encoders to minimize generative
113   errors, which can increase model complexity and uncertainty. In recent state-of-the-art (SOTA)
114   approaches, a combination of data-level and algorithm-level techniques is common, as they comple-
115   ment each other to enhance performance in imbalanced learning.
116   Harmless Label Noise. Instance-dependent noise (Frénay & Verleysen, 2013; Manwani & Sastry,
117   2013), where the label flip-rate (or mislabel-rate) depends on its true class and instance features, is
118   the most general case of label noise. It is commonly assumed that samples with similar features are
119   more likely mislabeled (Cannings et al., 2020; Goldberger & Ben-Reuven, 2022; Song et al., 2022),
120   particularly in the class-overlapping region. While label noise is typically harmful and most research
121   (Goldberger & Ben-Reuven, 2022; Liu & Tao, 2015; Patrini et al., 2017; Xiao et al., 2015; Zhu et al.,
122   2003) has mainly focused on mitigating the impact of label noise on the decision boundary, recent
123
      studies (Ahfock & McLachlan, 2021; Cannings et al., 2020) have demonstrated that under certain
      conditions, label noise can be harmless or even beneficial. In Ahfock & McLachlan (2021), the
124
      label noise model, proportional to classification difficulty, exhibits symmetry around the decision
125
      boundary, preserving the decision boundary of logistic regression unchanged in a balanced dataset.
126   Contrarily, in Cannings et al. (2020), the noise model is proportional to the posterior probability
127   of the opposite class, making the regret ratio less or equal to 1, thereby slightly improving the
128   classifier performance. However, both works primarily focus on balanced data and do not explore
129   how observed phenomena can be leveraged to develop methods for improving classifier performance
130   in imbalanced real-world data by introducing harmless label noise.
131
      Motivation and Contributions. Previous works (Ahfock & McLachlan, 2021; Cannings et al.,
132   2020) on harmless label noise inspire the development of a methodology to reshape decision bound-
133   aries through label noise under general models, extending from harmless to beneficial categories
134   when addressing real-world imbalanced learning problems. It is worth mentioning that the label
135   noise employed in this paper is asymmetric such that only the samples from the majority class have
136   a certain probability of being flipped to the minority class, aligning with imbalanced learning. This
137   contrasts with the symmetric label noise considered in Ahfock & McLachlan (2021); Cannings et al.
138   (2020), where the minority samples may also be flipped to the majority class. This paper revisits
139   fundamental questions in imbalanced learning: A) How does data imbalance bias the classifier’s
140
      decision boundary? B) How can biased decision boundaries be corrected through label flipping to
      improve classification performance? C) How to introduce artificial label noises to improve classifier
141
      performance in both binary and multi-class settings?
142
143   Addressing these questions, the main contributions of this paper are: a) Theoretical justification
144   of how imbalance ratio biases the decision boundary and quantification of its deviation under dif-
145   ferent evaluation metrics in binary classification. b) Definition of a beneficial label noise model
146
      that corrects the biased classifier’s decision boundary by harmlessly reallocating imbalanced data
      labels. c) Development of a novel methodology (LNR) to introduce carefully designed beneficial
147
      label noise to imbalanced data, improving the performance of classifiers on predicting minority class
148
      samples in both binary and multi-class classification. Experiment results on synthetic and real-world
149   data validated the superiority of our proposed method and its versatility in integrating with existing
150   algorithm-level methods.

      In this section, we analyze the impact of the imbalance ratio on decision boundaries across various
156   evaluation metrics in binary classification, which motivates the development of our Label-Noise-
157   based Re-balancing (LNR) approach. Assume that two imbalanced classes have prior probabilities
158   π1 = Pr(Y = 1) and π0 = Pr(Y = 0) = 1 − π1 , respectively, where Y = 1 refers to the minority
159   (positive) class while Y = 0 represents the majority (negative) class. In the imbalanced scenario,
160   the sample class priors satisfy π1 ≪ π0 and the imbalance ratio is π0 /π1 . For class c ∈ {0, 1} and
161   the sample space X ⊂ Rd , we denote the class-conditional distribution as X | Y = c ∼ Pc and the
      marginal distribution of X as PX (x) = π1 P1 (x) + π0 P0 (x).

163
164   Define η(x) = Pr(Y = 1 | X = x). The Bayesian decision boundary of the optimal classifier that
165   maximizes the accuracy in the sample space X is defined as S = {x ∈ X : η(x) = 0.5}.
166   Lemma 1. The optimal Bayesian decision boundary is S = {x ∈ X : P1 (x)/P0 (x) = π0 /π1 }.
167
168   If the data is balanced with π0 = π1 , Lemma 1 implies that the Bayesian decision boundary corre-

171

172
      samples, which is crucial in practical applications, is compromised. In extremely imbalanced cases,
173   the accuracy remains high even if all samples are classified into the majority class.

      Motivated by the distortion of decision boundaries caused by the imbalance ratio, as observed in
261
      the last section, we propose a novel Label-Noise-based Re-balancing (LNR) approach. This method
262   introduces beneficial asymmetric label noise to enhance the performance of classification algorithms
263   in imbalanced learning in both binary and multi-class settings.
264
265   4.1        M ETHODOLOGY
266
267   The proposed LNR method consists of two steps: (1) introduce artificial label noises to the original
268   data, and (2) train a classifier on the noisy data. We first focus on binary classification to motivate and
269   justify the introduction of label noise, which plays a crucial role in correctly adjusting the decision
      boundary on the relabeled data. Extensions to the multi-class setting are explored in Section 4.3.

      Suppose the original data (X, Y ) ∼ P. Define ρ(x) = Pr(Y ∗ = 1 | X = x, Y = 0) as the
271   probability of flipping a sample from the true label Y = 0 to the noisy label Y ∗ = 1, similarly,
272   define γ(x) = Pr(Y ∗ = 0 | X = x, Y = 1). With the flipped labels, we obtain the noisy data pairs
273   (X, Y ∗ ) ∼ P∗ . Consequently, η ∗ (x) = Pr(Y ∗ = 1 | X = x) = η(x)[1 − γ(x)] + [1 − η(x)]ρ(x).
274
      The label noises discussed in Ahfock & McLachlan (2021); Cannings et al. (2020) are symmet-
275
      ric and proportional to classification difficulty η(x), where both positive and negative samples are
276
      equally likely to be flipped on the decision boundary. The classifier remains consistent on such sym-
277   metric label noises, counterbalancing the distortion and leaving the decision boundary unchanged.
278   For example, with ρ(x) = η(x), ρ(x) + γ(x) = 1, we have η ∗ (x) = η(x), making the noise model
279   harmless and preserving the decision boundary.
280
      However, in imbalanced data, the overwhelming number of majority class samples invading the
281
      minority class region often results in significant misclassification of minority class samples. To
282
      achieve a higher F1 score by adjusting the decision boundary and reducing the imbalance ratio near
283   the decision boundary, we consider an asymmetric label noise model that only flips majority class
284   samples to minority class samples. This approach shifts the biased decision boundary toward the
285   majority class region rather than neutralizing the shifts with symmetric noise. Since deeply invaded
286   majority class samples cause greater harm, we aim for a noise model directly proportional to the
287   extent of this invasion. Therefore, we propose the flipped rates ρ(x) and γ(x) satisfy:
288
                                                 ρ(x) ∝ η(x),   γ(x) = 0.                                 (2)
289
290   In this model, the probability of majority class samples being flipped to minority class is proportional
291   to their probability of being classified as minority class, which is intuitive. Setting γ(x) = 0 means
292   that only majority class samples with Y = 0 are flipped at the rate ρ(x). Using this label noise
293   model, we can generate the noisy data, as outlined in Algorithm 1. As an example shown in Figure
294
      3, the asymmetric label noise model pushes the biased decision boundary (left vertical dashed line)
      on imbalanced data distribution (solid curves) toward the majority region at the corrected decision
295
      boundary (right vertical dashed line) on the data distribution with label noises (dashed curves).

      reducing the number of the majority class. The Bayesian decision boundary that maximizes accuracy

329   4.2   C ONSTRUCTING LABEL NOISE MODEL IN BINARY CLASSIFICATION
330
331   In practice, estimating the flip-rate ρ(x), which is proportional to η(x) in binary classification, be-
332   comes essential. We propose utilizing the estimated posterior probabilities η̂(x) obtained from an
333   arbitrary classifier trained on imbalanced data to approximate η(x), thereby constructing the label
334   noise model ρ(x). As discussed in Section 3.1, the decision boundary of the classifier trained with
335
      imbalanced data is biased from the optimal decision boundary, often underestimating η(x). How-
      ever, we can still leverage these estimated probabilities to capture the similarities to the minority
336
      class and construct an appropriate noise model using Algorithm 1.
337
338   Step 1. Train a classifier Cf on the original imbalanced data and obtain the corresponding posterior
339           probabilities η̂[i] = Cf (X[i]). Although η̂[i] may be biased, the majority class samples
340           with features similar to those of minority class samples still tend to exhibit relatively higher
341           posterior probabilities of being classified into minority classes. As a result, these samples
342           are expected to have relatively higher probabilities of being relabeled as the minority class.
343   Step 2. Employ z-score standardization on η̂[IndMA ] to get Z[IndMA ], where IndMA is the index of
344           majority class samples. This is used to magnify the differentiation in the posterior proba-
345           bilities of the majority class samples. The deviation level, measured by Z[IndMA ], ranks all
346           majority class samples, proportional to the similarity with minority class samples.
347   Step 3. Rescale Z[IndMA ] to [0, 1] by calculating ρ[IndMA ] = max(tanh(Z[IndMA ] − tf lip ), 0),
348           where the threshold tf lip is a tunable parameter for selecting appropriate samples with
349           high similarities to minority class features. Notably, tanh(Z[IndMA ] − tf lip ) > 0 when
350           Z[IndMA ] > tf lip , which zeros the flip rates of majority class samples below tf lip .
351   Step 4. Flip majority class samples into minority class according to their flip rate ρ[IndMA ].
352
353   Empirically, optimal tf lip can be selected by cross-validation. See Section 5 and Appendix for more
354   discussions on practical implementation.

375   4.3   E XTEND TO MULTI - CLASS CLASSIFICATION WITH ONLINE LABEL NOISE UPDATING
376
377   Unlike binary classification tasks that contain only one minority class, multi-class data may in-
      clude multiple minority class samples. To make LNR adopt the various imbalance ratios between


                                                        7
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


378
      classes, we weigh the flip rate of being flipped into different classes by the class prior weight vector
379   θC , where C is the class vector. These weights are calculated based on the sample sizes NC , and
380   max(θC − θC [y], 0) guarantees label flipping is only proceeded from majority class to minority class
381   and preventing label flipping between classes with the same class prior. The label-flipping procedure
382   for multi-class scenarios is detailed in Algorithm 2.
383
      Due to that more complex neural networks are typically used in multi-class image classification
384
      tasks, training a separate flipping rate estimator is costly, unlike in binary classification scenarios.
385   As our method allows for using any classifier as the flipping rate estimator, we directly utilize the
386   model itself to calculate the posterior probabilities with softmax(f c(wt ; X, Y )) during training,
387   where f c(wt ; X, Y ) is the output of the last fully-connected layer of model wt at epoch t. Similar
388   to the Deferred Re-Weighting (DRW) approach (Cao et al., 2019), we postpone the introduction
389   of label noise until the model has converged (after TD epochs) in the fine-tuning stage leveraging
390   possible influence on the feature extraction process.
391
392   Algorithm 2: Constructing label noises during training at round t
393
      Input: Feature X and labels Y , and the model wt at current epoch t;
394   Parameters: Flipping threshold tf lip , Deferred epochs TD ;
395   Q ← softmax(f c(wt ; X, Y )) where f c(wt ; ·, ·) is the last fully-connected layer output of
396   model wt ;
397   θC ← 1 − minMax(NC ) where NC counts the samples of each class;
398   µ ← mean(Q), γ ← std(Q);
399   for (x, y t ) ∈ (X, Y ) do
400       Calculate Z-score for sample x: Zx ← Q[x]−µ γ   ;
401       Get the flipping strength for class c ∈ C: θc=y ← max(θC − θC [y], 0) ;
402       Calculate the class-wize flip-rate for sample x: Fx ← max(tanh(Zx − tf lip ), 0) × θc=y ;
403       U ← uniform(Fx );
404       if U contains 1 and t > TD then
405           y t ← C[indexOf(max(Fx [U == 1]))] where C is the class vector. ;
406       end
407       Update the model wt with (x, y t )
408   end
409
410
      It is important to note that the posterior probabilities produced by a multi-class model form a C × 1
411   vector where C is the number of classes. A majority class sample has C − 1 posterior probabilities
412   corresponding to being classified as other classes, resulting in C − 1 distinct flipping rates. In the
413   case of majority class samples exhibiting similar features to multiple minority classes, resulting in
414   multiple high flip rates, we flip such majority samples into the minority class having the highest flip
415   rate. Random flipping is employed in the event of a tie on flip rates.

      5.2      S ENSITIVITY A NALYSIS
524
      With an appropriate label noise model, LNR requires an adequate tf lip to introduce label noises.
525   When the label flip threshold tf lip is too low, we observe poorer performance on MLP than the
526   baseline, indicating that a low tf lip leads to excessive noise being introduced. When tf lip is too
527   high, only a few samples are flipped until the performance gain approaches zero. The full sensitivity
528   analysis and figures are detailed in Appendix C.3.

      6     C ONCLUSION
531
532
      In this paper, we theoretically analyze the impact mechanisms of imbalanced data on classifiers.
533
      By investigating the influence of label noise on the data, we propose a novel method of artificially
534   introducing label noise to enhance the performance of imbalanced learning. Traditional resampling
535   algorithms suffer from information loss and the introduction of generative noise. In contrast, our
536   LNR method balances classes by simply flipping the labels of majority class samples that avoid
537   these issues. Through testing on simulated and real-world data, our method not only demonstrates
538   superiority in the recognition performance of minority class samples but also shows comprehensive
539   improvements across multiple metrics. In addition, our LNR approach can integrate seamlessly with
      any classifiers and other algorithm-level methods for imbalanced learning.

1123

1124

1129   while maintaining a low error rate for negative samples will have a higher AUC. Unlike F1 and
1130   G-mean, which are more sensitive to the prediction performance of minority class samples, AUC is
1131   a more conservative metric for comprehensively reflecting the performance of a classifier. Methods
1132   that significantly increase the number of minority class samples to improve F1 score or G-mean
1133   often come at the cost of higher errors in negative samples (FP). Therefore, we desire a re-balancing

1302   D.2    I NFORMED U NDER - SAMPLING
1303
1304   Tomek-links (Tomek, 1976) first finds the nearest neighbor (1-Nearest Neighbour) for each sample
1305   as links. If the nearest neighbor and the sample come from different classes, the majority class
1306   sample in this link is removed from the data. This simple and efficient cleaning rule prioritizes the
1307
       removal of samples in class-overlapping regions, thereby reducing the impact of imbalanced data on
       the classification boundary.
1308
1309   Instead of using 1-NN, the ENN (Edited Nearest Neighbours) method (Wilson & Martinez, 2000)
1310   calculates k-nearest neighboring samples for each majority class sample to obtain smoother guid-
1311   ance from the data distribution and removes samples that are inconsistent with their nearest neigh-
1312   bors. ENN exclusively removes the noisy and ambiguous instances along the decision boundary.
1313
       However, it’s insufficient to balance the data with large imbalance ratio by merely removing the
       majority class samples around the borderline. Therefore, the CNNs (Condensed Nearest Neighbors)
1314
       (Hart, 1968) was originally proposed to reduce the memory requirements for the KNN algorithm to
1315
       mitigate the gap. CNNs retained all minority class samples and only the majority class samples that
1316   can not be classified correctly with the rest of the data. It aims to find the smallest subset of the most
1317   informative data and remove the redundant majority class samples far from the decision boundary.
1318   The CNNs algorithm can be run iteratively to achieve the desired size of under-sampled data.
1319
       However, CNNs is extremely sensitive to noise, as noisy samples are more likely to be misclassified
1320
       and retained by CNNs. In advance, the OSS (One-Sided Selection) (Kubat & Matwin, 2000) method
1321
       first removes redundant examples from the majority class that are far from the decision boundary
1322   using CNNs and then cleans the noisy samples retained by CNNs using the Tomek-links. (Li et al.,
1323   2022a) has argued that data cleaning should be done before the reduction of the majority class.
1324   Therefore, NCR (Li et al., 2022a) first applies ENN to obtain data with less noise before reducing
1325   the redundant majority class samples.
1326
       However, the methods mentioned above involve reducing class imbalance by removing samples,
1327
       which often leads to under-sampled majority class samples no longer being representative of the
1328   original ones. Instead of discarding majority class samples, the CC (ClusterCentroids) (Lin et al.,
1329   2017) approach reduces class imbalance by replacing the original majority class dataset with cen-
1330   troids of the K-means. By adjusting the number of centroids, CC can effectively reduce the majority
1331   class samples while ensuring that the synthetic samples remain representative of the original data,
1332   achieving higher AUC performance.

1336   SMOTE. For each instance xi in the minority class, SMOTE searches its k-nearest neighbors in
1337
       the same class. One neighbor is randomly selected, denoted as x′i , and the seed samples are xi and
       x′i . The new sample xnew is then generated at:
1338
1339                                          Xnew = xi + (x′i − xi )δ
1340
       where δ is a random number between [0,1]. Figure (a) demonstrates the idea of SMOTE.
1341
1342   The drawback of SMOTE is obvious. The synthetic samples do not remain in the same distribution
1343   as the seed samples, which introduces generative errors to the original distribution (Karystinos &
1344   Pados, 2000). SMOTE also uses outliers/noise as the seed samples, which increases the generative
1345   errors undesirably. For instance, three outliers on the right of Figure 10 generate unwanted new
1346
       samples.
1347   Borderline-SMOTE. Borderline-SMOTE is proposed in (Han et al., 2005) to address this prob-
1348   lem, in which only the minority samples near the borderline are over-sampled. Borderline-SMOTE
1349   divides all minority samples into 3 groups: SAFE, DANGER, and NOISE. For each instance xi in
       the minority class, borderline-SMOTE searches its m-nearest neighbors from the entire data. The


                                                          25
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1350
       number of minority samples among the m-nearest neighbors is denoted by m′ . If all neighbors of
1351   xi are from the majority class, in this case, m = m′ , then xi is considered as NOISE and eliminated
1352   from the generating steps. In the case of m > m′ > m      2 , namely, the number of majority nearest
1353   neighbors is larger than the number of its minority ones, xi is added to the DANGER set. Only
1354   the minority samples in the DANGER set are used as seed samples. The generating mechanism is
1355   similar to SMOTE. Figure 10(b) shows the synthetic samples generated by borderline-SMOTE.
1356
       The borderline-SMOTE naturally focuses on the difficult-to-learn samples, namely the borderline
1357   minority samples mixture with the majority samples. However, the threshold provides a rigid bound-
1358   ary when defining the DANGER set. The generating process is still blinked without further guid-
1359   ance.

1361
       the samples lying on the borderline but with a much softer boundary. ADASYN finds the impurity
1362
       of the neighborhood by normalizing ri = δi /k, where P  the δi is the number of majority samples in

1364   1. The number of synthetic data examples that need to be generated for each minority example xi is

1366   and minority classes, respectively, β is a number from [0,1] that specifies the desired balance level
1367   after generating the synthetic data. The generating procedure is the same as SMOTE. ADASYN
1368   provides impurity as guidance while generating the synthetic data. However, the noise samples are
