009                                                    A BSTRACT
010
011                While various preferred features determine human preferences, current preference
012                learning frameworks for large language models (LLMs) simplify them with binary
013                pairwise comparisons and scalar rewards. This simplification could make LLMs’
014                responses biased to mostly preferred features such as longer responses which
015                would be exacerbated in online learning scenarios as the biases can be accumulate
016
                   continuously throughout the iterations. To address these challenges, we propose a
                   novel framework called P F P (Preference Feature Preservation). The key idea of
017
                   P F P is maintaining the distribution of human preference features throughout the
018
                   online preference learning process. Specifically, P F P first trains a feature classifier
019                using the existing offline pairwise human preference data. Then, using this classifier
020                and the distribution preserving optimization, P F P maps appropriate preference
021                features for each input instruction during online learning. Lastly, P F P trains LLM
022                using the existing preference learning framework, by incorporating the preference
023                feature of each data into system prompts and enabling LLM to explicitly handle
024                various human preferences. Our experiments demonstrate that P F P successfully
025                mitigates the bias in preference features that arise during online learning, and
026                achieves superior performance compared to previous preference learning methods
027
                   on general benchmarks including AlpacaEval 2.0 and MT-Bench. We also observe
                   that P F P almost resolves a length bias issue, a long-standing problem of online
028
                   preference learning, even though it was not specifically designed to tackle this.1

031   1       I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Aligning large language models (LLMs) using human feedback, particularly by learning from human
034   preferences, yields remarkable successes in various NLP tasks and real-world applications such
035   as coding assistants and chatbots (Anthropic, 2024; Dubey et al., 2024; OpenAI, 2024b; Team
036   et al., 2023). To improve the alignment of LLMs, various preference learning algorithms, such as
037
      Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) (Ouyang et al., 2022) and Direct Preference
      Optimization (DPO) (Rafailov et al., 2023), have been explored. A common assumption across
038
      these works is that human preference is provided in a binary pair-wise comparison (Ziegler et al.,
039
      2019; Hong et al., 2024). This approach enables easy modeling of human preference using the scalar
040   reward such as the Bradley-Terry (BT) model (Bradley & Terry, 1952); however, it also has critical
041   limitations from over-simplification and fails to capture the complexity of human preferences.
042
043
      One typical mis-aligned behavior of LLMs trained under current preference learning methods is
      a length bias; as shown in Fig. 1(a), LLMs tend to produce and prefer longer responses after the
044
      alignment procedure (Park et al., 2024; Dubois et al., 2024; Singhal et al., 2023). Despite various
045
      attempts to mitigate this issue, such as heuristically penalizing response length within reward models
046   (Chen et al., 2024), length bias remains a persistent and challenging problem. In addition, beyond the
047   bias toward the specific preference feature, another key challenge is a bias toward the preferences
048   of the majority (Santurkar et al., 2023). As the reward model likely assigns higher rewards to the
049   responses preferred by the majority, aligned LLMs with this reward model could be also biased. It
050   makes LLMs suffer to generate the proper responses for diverse users with various preferences.
051
      This issue becomes even more problematic in online preference learning scenarios, which progres-
052   sively improves the alignment of LLMs by iterating the generation of preference data and learning

078   preference features remains consistent throughout the online preference learning process. Here, the
079   key idea is to explicitly extract preference features of each input instruction and handle them using
080   system prompts of LLMs (see Fig. 1(c)); it enables LLMs to generate and learn preference data with
081   intent. Specifically, P F P first estimates the initial distribution of preference features of the given
082   human preference dataset, by inferring which features mainly determine binary human preferences.
083   We then train a preference feature classifier, which maps each input instruction to appropriate
084
      preference features with additional optimization for the distribution preservation, during the online
      learning process. Finally, P F P trains LLM using the existing preference learning framework, by
085
      converting the mapped preference features of each generated data into the system prompts of LLMs.

087   We demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed P F P by applying it to align recent open-sourced
088   LLMs, e.g., Mistral (Jiang et al., 2023a), with the commonly used preference dataset (UltraFeedback
089   Cui et al. (2023)) and evaluation benchmarks (AlpacaEval 2.0 (Dubois et al., 2024) and MT-bench
090

      as an online preference learning algorithm, which enables Iterative DPO (Xu et al., 2023; Xiong
091
      et al., 2024) without using an external reward model. Our experimental results demonstrate that
092
      P F P successfully eliminates the bias in preference features during online learning. As shown in
093   Fig. 1, responses generated by the model trained with P F P mitigate bias in preference features, unlike
094   the model trained with Iterative DPO. Additionally, P F P achieves superior performance compared
095   to previous online preference learning methods. For example, our framework achieves 7.58% →
096   15.24% increase in AlpacaEval 2.0 length-controlled win rate compared to the SFT model, while
097   Iterative DPO achieves 7.58% → 13.13% increase. More interestingly, P F P effectively reduces the
098   occurrence of length bias during online preference learning, despite not being specifically designed to
099   address this issue. These results demonstrate that our framework is highly competitive and practical
100   for real-world applications, underscoring the robustness and versatility of the proposed framework.

102   2    R ELATED W ORKS
103
104   LLM alignment with human preference. Aligning LLMs with human intentions and values using
105   human feedback data now becomes a defacto standard to obtain well-performing LLMs (Ziegler
106   et al., 2019; Ouyang et al., 2022). Typically, this feedback is collected by asking human annotators
107   to compare two responses generated from the same input prompt and assign a binary preference
      label based on their judgment. One of the most widely adopted approaches is RLHF (Christiano


                                                                            2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      et al., 2017; Stiennon et al., 2020), where a reward model is trained to capture human preferences
109   (Bradley & Terry, 1952), and the LLM is then fine-tuned to optimize for this learned reward. To
110   prevent issues such as reward over-optimization and model collapse, KL divergence regularization is
111   commonly employed during this fine-tuning process. However, RLHF presents several challenges
112   such as computational overheads from training reward models, as well as the instability associated
113   with online reinforcement learning algorithms. To address these issues, alternative approaches have
114   been extensively proposed (Rafailov et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2023; Meng et al., 2024; Hong et al.,
115   2024); for instance, Rafailov et al. (2023) propose DPO, which eliminates the need for a separate
116   reward model by deriving a training objective that is mathematically equivalent to RLHF.
117   Online preference learning. Existing preference learning methods can generally be categorized
118   into two approaches depending on whether they use the fixed human preference dataset (offline
119   preference learning, e.g., DPO) or progressively enlarge dataset from the iterations of sampling and
120   labeling (online preference learning, e.g., RLHF). While online methods typically achieve superior
121   performance due to train with more data, they also demand significantly more computational costs
122   from sampling responses and labeling preferences. To address this challenge, recent work has focused
123
      on developing efficient batch-online preference learning techniques, such as Iterative DPO (Xu et al.,
      2023; Xiong et al., 2024; Rosset et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2024; Calandriello et al., 2024). Iterative DPO
124
      generates thousands of responses in each iteration (batch) and constructs labeled preference datasets
125
      by judging the preference using the reward model (Jiang et al., 2023b). This dataset is then used to
126   train LLMs with offline methods like DPO, and the iteration repeats, resulting in more efficient and
127   stable alignment.
128
129
      Bias of LLMs after alignment. One prominent issue observed in LLMs after alignment with
      existing preference learning methods (RLHF and DPO) and binary preference labels is the emergence
130
      of a length bias, where LLMs tend to generate and favor the longer responses (Park et al., 2024;
131
      Singhal et al., 2023). Not only for the trained LLM policy, automated evaluation methods, including
132   reward models and LLM-as-a-judge frameworks, also often exhibit a bias toward longer outputs,
133   complicating the accurate assessment of LLM performance (Dubois et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2023).
134   Although various strategies have been proposed to mitigate length bias, such as incorporating length
135   penalties into the reward function (Park et al., 2024) or adjusting the objective function (Chen
136   et al., 2024), the issue remains difficult to fully resolve. Another key challenge is a bias toward the
137   preferences of the majority (Santurkar et al., 2023) which can yield other unexpected and hidden
138   biases, as the reward model will likely assign higher rewards to the responses preferred by the
139   majority. This issue becomes more problematic in the online preference learning setup, as the bias of
140
      LLMs accumulates with more iterations. In this paper, we propose a new approach to mitigate this
      problem by explicitly extracting the preference features and handling them via system prompt.

223   4.1        E XTRACTING PREFERENCE FEATURE FROM BINARY HUMAN PREFERENCE DATA
224
225   We first assume that some features affect the judgment of human preference between the responses
226   for the given input prompt; we call them preference features. Following Lee et al. (2024), we
227   predefined these preference features and organized them into five different classes (e.g., tone, style,
228   informativeness, etc.), denoted as P = [C1 , C2 , C3 , C4 , C5 ], as shown in Table 6. Each class Ck
229   contains up to five sub-features, represented as Ck ∈ {c1k , c2k , c3k , c4k , c5k }; for example, in style, one
230   of the classes, consists of following five sub-features: clarity, conciseness, format, vividness, and
231
      consistency. Under this definition„ we extract the preference features of the pairwise offline human
      preference data D using the feature extractor. We implement the feature extractor by prompting LLM
232
      such as GPT-4o (OpenAI, 2024b), to infer the likely preference features that led the annotators to
233
      provide specific feedback. Specifically, for the input instruction x and the two responses yw and yl , the
234   feature extractor is defined as p = LLMFE (x, yl , yw ) where p = [p1 , ..., p5 ], where each pi represents

286   4.3    L EARNING PREFERENCE FEATURES THROUGH SYSTEM PROMPT
287
288   Synthesizing system prompt from preference feature. We need to generate responses and judge
289   the preference using the LLM policy πθ conditioned on the given preference feature. However, it can
290
      be difficult as the preference features have the form of short words that are not suitable for LLM, for
      example, the feature set is represented as follows: [Conciseness, Formal, Accuracy, Intermediate,
291
      Efficiency]. To address this, we convert these discretized preference features into the system prompt,
292
      which is a natural language description about the preference feature, and add it in front of the instruct
293   x as the usual system prompt (see Sec. 3). Specifically, the system prompt s is created through the
294   system prompt synthesizer, which is realized by prompting LLM that receives features as input and
295   generates a system prompt, i.e., s ∼ LLMSS (p). Then, we augment the online learning dataset X     et by
296
      incorporating the generated system prompt, i.e., St = {(s, x)|x, p ∈ X    et }. We created the prompt
297   for LLMSS by modifying the prompt used in Lee et al. (2024) (see Appendix C). Using St , one can
298   perform the existing online preference learning method, such as iterative DPO.
299
300
      Double system prompt sampling and scheduling. While incorporating preference features into
      LLM using the system prompt enables LLM to understand and handle them better, we observe
301
      that conditioning specific system prompts could reduce the diversity between sampled responses.
302
      This reduced diversity makes preference judgment between them difficult and consequently leads to
303   decreased performance (see Table 3). To prevent this, we propose to augment the online learning data
304   set Xt by sampling two system prompts, i.e., St = {(s1 , s2 , x)|x ∈ Xt } and s1 , s2 ∼ LLMSS (p).
305   Then, during the dataset construction process, each system prompt is used to sample the different
306   response, i.e., yi ∼ πt−1 (si , x) where i = 1, 2. Finally, using Eq. 5 and 6, we judge the preference
307   between y1 and y2 with randomly chosen s between s1 and s2 , and construct the labeled dataset
308   Dt = {(s, x, yl , yw )|x ∈ Xt } for t-th iteration which is used to learn the t-th policy πt .
309   In addition, to improve the effectiveness of online preference learning, we propose progressively
310   increasing the training examples’ difficulty. To this end, we simply reduce the temperature used for
311   system prompt sampling as the iteration increases, which reduces the diversity between two system
312   prompts. We expect that it also reduces the distance between two responses y1 and y2 from online
313   response sampling with πt−1 and St , i.e., more difficult to learn; therefore, this approach improves
314   the effectiveness of online preference learning by continuously increasing the difficulty of the task.

518   5.5   L ENGTH B IAS
519
520   The way P F P reduces length bias is fundamentally different from the traditional length control
521   methods. In general, length bias has been handled using heuristic methods. The lengt penalty method
522
      works by heuristically subtracting a bias based on the length in the reward term from the reward
      model (Dong et al., 2024). Alternatively, as seen in the R-DPO approach (Park et al., 2024), length
523
      bias can be mitigated by the adding length regularization into the DPO loss. The common point is
524
      that the difference in length between two sentences is simply processed heuristically. However, we
525   point out that the method tends to be sensitive to hyper-parameters and often fails to work effectively
526   in practice. To evaluate how well P F P manages length control, we compare P F P with the length
527   penalty method and R-DPO applied to Iterative DPO. We have tried both methods, and found that
528   the R-DPO method with α = 0.01 was best applied method. Details about the experiment are in
529   Appendix B. As shown in Table 4, the overall reduction in length remained limited. This shows that
530   P F P is more effective in controlling length compared to traditional methods.
531
532   6     C ONCLUSION
533
534   In this paper, we propose P F P, a novel framework that explicitly preserves preference features during
535   the online preference learning process to reduce bias. We demonstrate that incorporating preference
536   features from human feedback into system prompts and preserving the feature distribution over each
537   iteration of online learning effective in preventing bias. This not only aligns human preferences
538   better than the existing Iterative DPO method but also succeeds in almost eliminating length bias and
539   preference features that occur in the learning process. These findings are further supported by various
      benchmarks and additional analyses.
