                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011
               The current era has witnessed the success of the transformer in the field of classical
012
               deep neural networks (DNNs) and the potential of quantum computing. One
013
               naturally expects that quantum computing can offer significant speedup for the
014            transformer. Recent developments of quantum transformer models are faced with
015            challenges including the expensive cost of non-linear operations and the information
016            loss problem caused by measurements. To address this issue, this paper proposes a
017            scheme called measurement information multiple-reuse (MIMR). MIMR enables
018            the repeated utilization of intermediate measurement data from former layers, thus
019            enhancing information-transferring efficiency. This scheme facilitates our quantum
020            vision transformer (QViT) capable of achieving exponential speedup compared to
021            classical counterparts, with the support of many parameters and large depth. Our
022
               QViT model is further examined with an instance of 86 million parameters, which
               halves the requirements for tomography error compared to the one without MIMR.
023
               This demonstrates the superior performance of MIMR over existing schemes. Our
024
               findings underscore the importance of exploiting the value of information from
025            each measurement, offering a key strategy towards scalable quantum deep neural
026            networks.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032   The transformative era of deep learning has witnessed the rise of varieties of large-scale models,
033
      wherein the transformer Vaswani et al. (2017) emerges as a cornerstone in this evolution. At the heart
      of the transformer’s success lies its attention mechanism, a paradigm-shifting approach that allows
034
      for the effective management of billions of parameters, maintaining trainability and adaptability
035
      across diverse applications. However, the computing resource of the transformer scales quadratically
036   with the sequence length. This limitation has emerged as a bottleneck in the continued scaling of
037   transformer models, necessitating innovative approaches to extend their capabilities.
038
039
      Quantum computing is a promising solution to the computational limitations of classical machine
      learning, offering exponential speedup and enhanced computing capabilities. In the field of quantum
040
      deep neural networks (QDNNs), plenty of works have been proposed Beer et al. (2020); Liu et al.
041
      (2024); Li et al. (2020); Kerenidis et al. (2020a). Their potential applications include fields of image
042   recognition Li et al. (2020), quantum physics Liu et al. (2022), data classification Hur et al. (2022),
043   and so forth. Despite the relatively mature development of QDNNs, the advancement of quantum
044   algorithms for transformers has lagged behind.
045
      Early attempts at quantum transformer have been made, based on either variational quantum circuits
046
      (VQCs) Cerezo et al. (2021), or quantum linear algebra (QLA) Childs et al. (2017); Liu et al. (2021);
047
      Krovi (2023). The VQC-based quantum transformers Cherrat et al. (2022); Evans et al. (2024) lack
048   provable quantum advantage and also suffer from trainability problems like barren plateaus Wang et al.
049   (2021); McClean et al. (2018) and local minima Anschuetz & Kiani (2022); Bittel & Kliesch (2021).
050   The QLA-based quantum transformers Guo et al. (2024); Liao & Ferrie (2024) have theoretical
051   speedup while lacking full end-to-end implementation. Neither multi-layer implementation nor
052   backpropagation has been realized yet. Nikhil et al. presented a quantum transformer model which
053   utilizes the Linear Combination of Unitaries and Quantum Singular Value Transform primitives as
      building blocks, this model provides a quantum attention layer, the implementation of other layers,


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      the complexity of quantum-classical data conversion between different layers, and the end-to-end
055   implementation needs further investigation.
056
057   In this work, we propose a full implementation of a multi-layer quantum transformer based on
058
      QLA, including the realization of forward pass and backpropagation. Our quantum transformer has
      exponential speedup on the sequence length on both forward pass and backpropagation compared
059
      to the classical counterpart. To enable the stacking of layers, a major improvement of our work
060
      is the utilization strategy of classical information reuse. While the classical information that can
061   be extracted from the quantum state in every single measurement is limited, we argue that the
062   measured information has not been carefully utilized in previous works, inducing an unexpected
063   information loss and forbidding the deepening of a quantum neural network. This phenomenon has
064   been observed in the similar QLA-based quantum deep neural network Kerenidis et al. (2020a) and
065   is further explored in this work. To address this issue, we propose the Measurement Information
066   Multiple-Reuse (MIMR) scheme to mitigate the information loss across layers by making full use
067   of the measured information. To showcase the utility of MIMR, we construct a quantum vision
068   transformer (QViT) with 86 million parameters, demonstrating improvement in accuracy with image
069
      classification tasks of real-world datasets as well as strong robustness against information loss.

      2.1   M OTIVATION : NECESSITY OF INFORMATION REUSE IN CONSTRUCTING QUANTUM DEEP
076
            NEURAL NETWORK
077
078
079   In this section, we discuss the pivotal role of reusing intermediate measurement information to
080   accelerate classical deep neural networks (DNNs) via quantum computing. A multi-layer DNN,
081
      involving nonlinear operations at each layer, can be viewed as a discrete nonlinear system where each
      layer symbolizes a step in its evolution. Current research indicates that quantum computing struggles
082
      to effectively accelerate the evolution of strongly nonlinear systems, with complexity potentially
083
      increasing exponentially with the number of evolution steps Liu et al. (2021). This exponential
084   increase in complexity directly conflicts with the expectations for quantum speedup and presents a
085   significant challenge for QDNNs. This discussion extends to limitations in efficiently implementing
086   quantum backpropagation, necessitated not only by the non-linear operations of gradients but also by
087   the need for information on intermediate quantum states.
088
      One approach to addressing the challenges mentioned above involves incorporating measurement
089
      operations at intermediate steps of QDNNs. Specifically, we can introduce intermediate measurements
090   after each layer, using measurement outcomes to reconstruct the output before feeding it into the
091   next layer. Consequently, the complexity increases linearly with the number of layers. Similar
092   ideas are employed in many recent works, including the quantum convolutional neural networks
093   (QCNNs) Kerenidis et al. (2020a) and quantum algorithms for solving nonlinear systems Xue et al.
094   (2021); Krovi (2023); Chen et al. (2022).
095
      However, the cost associated with intermediate measurements can be substantial, potentially under-
096   mining the quantum advantage offered by QDNNs. Thus, a cost-effective scheme for intermediate
097   measurements becomes essential. Intermediate measurement, a form of quantum tomography, in-
098   volves several efficient tomography techniques such as l∞ tomography Kerenidis et al. (2020b),
099   shadow tomography Aaronson (2018); Huang et al. (2020), and neural network-based methods
100   Carrasquilla et al. (2019); Torlai et al. (2018).
101
      Given Holevo’s bound Holevo (1973), each measurement on an n-dimensional quantum state can
102   extract only log n bits of information, rendering the process of extracting classical information from
103   quantum states highly inefficient. Efficient tomography algorithms aim to reduce the number of
104   measurements, maximizing the utility of the classical data derived from each measurement. Despite
105   these advancements, existing quantum acceleration methods for DNNs, such as QCNNs, do not fully
106   exploit the potential of intermediate measurements. Typically, each layer in these networks blocks
107   the output obtained from previous layers and only conveys the most recent measurement information
      to the next layer.

123   2.2    M EASUREMENT INFORMATION MULTIPLE - REUSE
124
125   To address this inefficiency, we propose a multiple-reuse strategy for intermediate measurement data,
126   developing techniques to repeatedly leverage this information throughout the network. This strategy
127   ensures more efficient use of quantum resources and enhances the practical scalability of QDNNs.
128   The multiple-reuse strategy is derived from residual connection, a basic building block in DNNs.
129   Residual connection is written as y = x + f (x), where f (x) is the output of a layer with input x.
130   The idea of implementing a multiple-reuse strategy is to add intermediate measurements to a branch
131   of the residual connection layer. Then the previous information can be transferred from the other
132   branch and perform Quantum Add, as shown in Figure 1.
133   As a comparison, previous works adopted single-reuse strategy Kerenidis et al. (2024); Guo et al.
134   (2024), of which the intermediate measurements are executed after each quantum layer, and only
135   the most recent measurement results are passed to the next layer. Quantum residual connections
136   have been explored in the context of quantum neural networks based on variational quantum circuits.
137   Wen et al. introduced a residual connection framework utilizing the linear combination of unitary
138   operations to enhance the expressivity of quantum neural networks on NISQ devices Wen et al.
139   (2024). Similarly, Muhammad et al. proposed a residual approach, termed ResQNets, to mitigate
140   the barren plateaus problem in quantum neural networks Kashif & Al-Kuwari (2024). While these
141
      residual connection strategies are well-suited for variational quantum circuits operating on NISQ
      devices, their direct application to quantum transformers presents significant challenges. Notably,
142
      the success rate decreases exponentially with the number of residual layers, and the computational
143
      complexity grows polynomially with the context length, posing substantial scalability limitations.
144
145   In our multiple-reuse strategy, each layer contains a residual connection that allows all previous
146   intermediate measurement results to be passed through an independent branch of the residual con-
147
      nection without additional measurement. As shown in the bottom part of Figure 1, the input x and
      the measurement results m1 , m2 , · · · , ml are all passed to the final quantum layer. Compared to the
148
      single-reuse strategy, the data flow in the multiple-reuse strategy is more active. Wen et al. realized
149
      residual connections by the frame of a linear combination of unitary to enhance the expressivity of
150   quantum neural networks in NISQ devices Wen et al. (2024), the complexity of their scheme increases
151   exponentially with the residual connection layers, and their scheme cannot solve the “exponential
152   increase” problem caused by nonlinear layers. Muhammad et al. also proposed a residual approach
153   for mitigating barren plateaus in quantum neural networksKashif & Al-Kuwari (2024).
154
      We can make full use of the information obtained from all intermediate measurements to build
155
      more efficient QDNNs based on the multiple-reuse strategy. To study the practical influences, in
156   Appendix B we compare the extent of information loss using single- and multiple-reuse strategies,
157   by observing the cosine similarity between input and output through two processes. It shows that
158   it mitigates information loss for state reconstruction. In Figure fig-tomography-error-cub-forward,
159   numerical evidence shows that it achieves less information loss during the forward pass of the
160   quantum transformer, thus likely improving the effectiveness of the training process. The multiple-
161   reuse strategy can alleviate the problem of information loss with the number of layers and therefore
      help build up a deeper quantum neural network.

      3     Q UANTUM VISION TRANSFORMER
163
164
      In this section, we introduce our developed QViT. Traditional transformer architectures consist of
165
      nonlinear layers and non-unitary matrix computations, presenting substantial challenges in leveraging
166   quantum computing for acceleration. Current research faces the following challenges: (1) The
167   complexity increases exponentially with the number of layers; (2) The success probability of imple-
168   menting non-unitary matrix operations in quantum linear algebra is less than 1. Our QViT effectively
169   addresses the challenge of exponential resource growth associated with an increase in layers and
170   significantly reduces the probabilistic steps during the execution process. This enhancement is derived
171   from two major innovations in our implementation of QViT: (1) We employ the MIMR scheme
172   within the transformer encoder layer to prevent the issue of complexity from increasing exponentially
173   with the number of layers. (2) Apart from the Attention layer, the computational processes in other
174
      layers of the transformer are independent for different tokens. We implement these layers using
      quantum arithmetic operations, thereby circumventing the probabilistic issues typically encountered
175
      with quantum linear algebra.
176
177   Based on the above ideas, we build the complete forward pass and backpropagation process for
178   the QViT. Both the forward pass and backpropagation processes achieve exponential speedup with
179   respect to the sequence length n, while the complexity increases linearly with the number of layers l,
180
      as detailed in Theorem 3.1.

210
211   Quantum layers are the layers that provide quantum speedup, including QPos, QNorm, QAttn, QAdd,
212   and QFFN. One method to implement these operations involves quantum linear algebra, inherently
213   producing probabilistic outcomes at each step. We notice that, apart from the QAttn layer, other
214   layers can be regarded as n independent d-dimensional operations. Consequently, we can implement
215   these layers using d-dimensional quantum arithmetic operations, which are not affected by success
      probability. This approach significantly reduces the number of probabilistic steps involved.

      3.1.2    Q UANTUM - CLASSICAL DATA TRANSFER LAYERS
271
272   As stated in the previous section, the QViT construction process also requires intermediate measure-
273   ment steps, which are realized in quantum-classical data transfer layers. The quantum-classical data
274   transfer layers contain QSave and QLoad processes. QSave is a quantum tomography process, we
275   use l∞ tomography to realize QSave procedure. QLoad procedure is used to encode the tomography
276   results into the following quantum operations, the QLoad is realized by querying QRAM followed
277   by QDAC. See Appendix C.2, C.5 and C.3 for relevant knowledge of l∞ tomography, qRAM and
278   QDAC.
279   In the QViT, we add the QSave and QLoad procedures after the quantum multi-attention layer. There
280   are two reasons: (1) The QAdd layer is behind the quantum multi-attention layer, which means we
281   add the QSave and QLoad procedures before the QAdd layer. Then, based on the multiple-reuse
282   strategy introduced in Section 2.2, the previous measurement information can also be reused from the
283   other branch of the QAdd (The red line of the Figure 2(c)). (2) The quantum multi-attention layer is
284   different from other layers, Other layers can be regarded as n independent d-dimensional operations,
285   but the quantum multi-attention layer cannot. So other layers can be realized by implementing
286
      d-dimensional quantum arithmetic operations in parallel, and the implementation process does not
      require measurement, the implementation details are introduced in Appendix D. Therefore, we add
287
      the QSave and QLoad procedures after QAttn.
288
289   In the QViT architecture, we integrate the QSave and QLoad procedures subsequent to the quantum
290   multi-attention layer for two primary reasons: (1) The QSave and QLoad procedures are prior to
291   the QAdd layer, which allows for the reuse of previous measurement information from the alternate
292
      branch of the QAdd layer, as delineated in the multiple-reuse strategy discussed in Section 2.2 and
      illustrated by the red line in Figure 2(c). (2) The QAttn layer differs fundamentally from other
293
      layers, which typically consist of n independent d-dimensional operations. Unlike these layers, the
294
      quantum multi-attention layer contains n × n-dimensional operations. Consequently, while other
295   layers can execute d-dimensional quantum arithmetic operations in parallel without intermediate
296   measurement, the implementation of the QAttn layer is more complex, the details are shown in
297   Appendix D. Therefore, we add the QSave and QLoad procedures after the QAttn layer.

309   3.3     BACKPROPAGATION
310
311
      Next, we detail the backpropagation process, which mirrors the structure of the forward pass. The
312   implementation is depicted in Figure 3. There are key differences between the backpropagation
313   and forward propagation processes: (1) We incorporate layer tomography prior to the QAttn layer,
314   performing tomography subsequent to the backpropagation through the QAttn layer. (2) The back-
315   propagation process involves computing gradients for parameters across the MLP, Attention, and
316   Position Embedding layers. We prepare the amplitude encoding of these parameter gradients by
317   querying the intermediate data from the propagation process and then apply l∞ tomography to
318   capture the sampled parameter gradients. Comprehensive implementation details for each phase of
319   the backpropagation are provided in Appendix D.
320   The backpropagation also contains QSave and QLoad procedures, which are performed after the
321   backpropagation of the QAttn layer. Alike the forward pass, the previous measurement information
322   during the backpropagation process can be reused from the alternate branch of the QAdd layer,
323   thereby implementing the multiple-reuse strategy. This approach ensures that information measured
      during the backpropagation is efficiently utilized.

824   C.5.1     I NTRODUCTION
825
      In this section, we introduce quantum random access memory (qRAM) Giovannetti et al. (2008), a
826
      quantum architecture fundamental to our framework. QRAM serves as a generalization of classical
827   RAM, leveraging quantum mechanical properties to enhance computational efficiency.
828
829   In classical RAM, a discrete address i is provided as input, retrieving the memory element xi stored
830
      at that location. Conversely, in qRAM, a quantum superposition of different addresses |ψin ⟩ is input,
      and qRAM returns an entangled state |ψout ⟩ where each address is correlated with the corresponding
831
      memory element:
832
833                                   N −1                                   N −1
                                      X                   qRAM               X
834                        |ψin ⟩ =          αi |i⟩A |0⟩D −−−→ |ψout ⟩ =            αi |i⟩A |xi ⟩D ,    (18)
835                                   i=0                                    i=0
836
      where N is the size of the data vector x, and the superscripts A and D denote "address" and "data"
837
      respectively.
838
839   While we have characterized our QViT as the quantum deep learning framework in the fault-tolerant
840   era, it is still imperative to incorporate the simulation of noisy qRAM. It is frequently used in
841
      QSave and QLoad in QViT. Within this framework, the primary role of qRAM is to retrieve pixel
      information stored in a massive matrix of size 220 by 210 . Each pixel can hold either 32 or 64 bits,
842
      necessitating a (30, 64) or (30, 32)-qRAM configuration. Our numerical simulations demonstrate
843
      promising results. For a (30, 64)-qRAM configuration, we observe an average state fidelity of 87%.
844   This fidelity increases to 91% for the (30, 32)-qRAM configuration.
845
846
      C.5.2     P RACTICALITY OF Q RAM USED IN QV I T
847
848   The practicality of qRAM has been investigated on such a scale under our numerical experiments.
849   Prior research indicates that qRAM infidelity scales as O(n(n + k)), where n represents the address
850   size and k denotes the word length. This implies that infidelity exhibits quadratic growth with respect
851   to address size for a fixed k and increases with word length for a fixed address size n. Based on these
852   established relationships, our experiments employed data with a fixed word length k to maintain
853   consistency with the established infidelity relation. Subsequently, we extrapolated the findings to the
854
      case of n = 30. Using these extrapolated data F (30, k), we employed a linear function to predict the
      infidelity value when k = 64.
855
856   All simulations were conducted under a controlled environment with 10−5 damping noise. We suc-
857   cessfully simulated qRAM configurations ranging from (20, 20) and below. The observed infidelities
858   agree to the O(n(n + k)) relationship, demonstrating a quadratic dependence on address size n for a
859   fixed word length k, as shown in Figure 7 and Figure 8.
860   We leveraged the data obtained from these simulations to derive polynomial expressions that accu-
861   rately capture the relationship between infidelity and address size. Subsequently, these quadratic
862   expressions were utilized to extrapolate and predict the infidelities of (30, k)-QRAM for a range
863   of k values from 1 to 20. As a result, we have obtained a comprehensive set of predicted QRAM
      infidelities for (30, k) configurations, where k ranges from 1 to 64, as shown in Figure 9.
