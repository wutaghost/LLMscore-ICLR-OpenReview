011                                                             A BSTRACT
012
013
                Recent advancements in generic 3D content generation from text prompts have
                been remarkable by fine-tuning text-to-image diffusion (T2I) models or employing
014
                these T2I models as priors to learn a general text-to-3D model. While fine-tuning-
015
                based methods ensure great alignment between text and generated views, i.e., se-
016             mantic consistency, their ability to achieve multi-view consistency is hampered
017             by the absence of 3D constraints, even in limited view. In contrast, prior-based
018             methods focus on regressing 3D shapes with any view that maintains uniformity
019             and coherence across views, i.e., multi-view consistency, but such approaches
020             inevitably compromise visual-textual alignment, leading to a loss of semantic de-
021             tails in the generated objects. To achieve semantic and multi-view consistency
022             simultaneously, we propose SeMv-3D, a novel framework for general text-to-3d
023             generation. Specifically, we propose a Triplane Prior Learner (TPL) that learns
024
                triplane priors with 3D spatial features to maintain consistency among differ-
                ent views at the 3D level, e.g., geometry and texture. Moreover, we design a
025
                Semantic-aligned View Synthesizer (SVS) that preserves the alignment between
026
                3D spatial features and textual semantics in latent space. In SVS, we devise a
027             simple yet effective batch sampling and rendering strategy that can generate ar-
028             bitrary views in a single feed-forward inference. Extensive experiments present
029             our SeMv-3D’s superiority over state-of-the-art performances with semantic and
030             multi-view consistency in any view. Our code and more visual results are available
031             at https://anonymous.4open.science/r/SeMv-3D-6425.

051   1    I NTRODUCTION
052
053   Text-to-3D generation (T23D) aims to generate corresponding 3D content based on text prompts
      with a broad range of applications, including games, movies, virtual/augmented reality, and robotics.


                                                                           1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      The previous works mainly focus on a per-scene optimization problem (Poole et al., 2023; Lin et al.,
055   2023; Wang et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023), which yields fine texture and geometric details. How-
056   ever, these methods incur substantial time and computational overhead, as each object generated
057   requires multiple optimizations to approximate the textual semantics. To overcome this issue, the
058   general text-to-3D has been proposed by learning a generic model capable of synthesizing various
059   objects in a feed-forward manner, which is a flexible and promising way. Without optimized re-
060   finement for pre-scene, general text-to-3D faces two core challenges: a) Multi-view Consistency,
061   which maintains coherence across multiple 3D views, and b) Semantic Consistency, which requires
062   semantic alignment of the generated 3D context with the text.
063   Benefiting from the great breakthroughs in the text-to-image diffusion (T2I) models, two research
064   lines of rationalization have recently emerged in general text-to-3D, including fine-tuning T2I mod-
065   els and utilizing these models as priors to train 3D generation models. Specifically, fine-tuning-based
066   methods seek to transfer the strong single-view generation capabilities of pretrained T2I models
067   (e.g., great semantic alignment between text and vision) directly to generate multiple views with
068   consistent relationships, such as MVDream (Shi et al., 2023) and DreamView (Yan et al., 2024). Yet
069
      these methods are inherently ambiguous without explicit 3D constraints, leading to notorious multi-
      view inconsistency (e.g., multi-face Janus problem, shown as Figure 1a) and limited-view. Con-
070
      versely, prior-based methods primarily leverage the T2I models as semantic-visual initialization and
071
      subsequently train on large-scale 3D datasets. They solely focus on regressing the corresponding 3D
072   shapes, which naturally ensures consistency across multiple views, such as Shap-E (Jun & Nichol,
073   2023) and VolumeDiffusion (Tang et al., 2023). However, it sacrifices portions of the well-learned
074   semantic alignment information of the original T2I model, inevitably resulting in inconsistency be-
075   tween the generated visuals and their corresponding semantics, presented in Figure 1b. Thus, how to
076   effectively and simultaneously achieve semantic and multi-view consistency remains to be explored
077   for the general text-to-3D task.
078   Toward the above goal, we propose a novel framework, named SeMv-3D, which learns an efficient
079   triplane prior to ensure uniformity across all views of an object and align its semantics with the text.
080   Empirically, the triplane has been validated as an efficient and compact 3D representation for object
081   modeling (Chan et al., 2022). Unlike existing methods that directly learn the entire triplane fea-
082   tures, we emphasize spatial correspondence within the triplane to capture the underlying 3D details.
083   Specifically, we propose a Triplane Prior Learner (TPL) that integrates 3D spatial features into a
084   triplane prior. In practice, TPL first eliminates irrelevant backgrounds or components to preserve
085   essential 3D information by our object retention module and then captures spatial correspondence
086
      within triplane space to enhance its visual coherence by triplane orthogonalization module, a new
      task-specific attention component. Moreover, we design Semantic-aligned View Synthesizer (SVS)
087
      that deeply interacts between textual and visual features within triplane priors through a triplane
088
      latents transformation module, significantly improving semantic consistency. Additionally, in SVS,
089   we incorporate a simple yet effective batch sampling and rendering strategy (by fitting multiple
090   views at once), enabling the generation of any view in one single step. From Figure 1c, we can
091   see that our method performs better in multi-view and semantic consistency than other compared
092   methods.
093
      To summarize, our main contributions are threefold:
094
095   1) We devise a SeMv-3D, a novel general text-to-3D framework, which simultaneously ensures
096   semantic and multi-view consistency.
097   2) We propose a TPL, which learns a triplane prior to effectively capture consistent 3D features
098   across generated views. Moreover, we devise a SVS that deeply explores the alignment between
099   textual and 3D visual information, substantially improving semantic consistency.
100
      3) Extensive experiments show the superiority of our SeMv-3D in both qualitative and quantitative
101
      terms of multi-view and semantic consistency. Besides, our method presents a new property, i.e.,
102   the generation of any view in one feed-forward inference.

105   2   R ELATED WORKS
106
107   Text-to-3D (T23D) aims to synthesize 3D representations (3D voxels, point clouds, multi-view im-
      ages, and meshes) from textual descriptions. Early works of T23D directly train generation models


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      on small-scale 3D datasets, which restricted the semantic diversity and geometry fidelity of the
109   3D outputs. With the emergence of pretrained Text-to-Image (T2I) diffusion models, recent works
110   utilize semantic-visual prior knowledge of these T2I models for fine-grained and diverse 3D genera-
111   tion. Existing works can be grouped into two categories based on generalization ability: 1) Per-scene
112   Text-to-3D and 2) General Text-to-3D.
113
      Per-scene Text-to-3D. Per-scene Text-to-3D requires per-scene optimization when generating a new
114
      scene. The mainstream idea is using knowledge from pre-trained T2I models to guide the optimiza-
115   tion of 3D representations. DreamFusion (Poole et al., 2023) employs a technique known as Score
116   Distillation Sampling (SDS). This approach utilizes large-scale image diffusion models (Rombach
117   et al., 2022; Saharia et al., 2022) to iteratively refine 3D models to match specific prompts or im-
118   ages. Similarly, ProlificDreamer (Wang et al., 2023) develops Variational Score Distillation (VSD),
119   a structured variational framework that effectively reduces the over-saturation problems found in
120   SDS while also increasing diversity. Further enhancements are offered by several studies (Qian
121   et al., 2023; Qiu et al., 2023; Wang & Shi, 2023), which address the challenges of multiple faces by
122   using diffusion models fine-tuningd on 3D data. The strategy of amortized score distillation is exam-
123
      ined in other references (Lorraine et al., 2023; Qian et al., 2024). Numerous additional works (Chen
      et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2023; Tsalicoglou et al., 2023; Zhu & Zhuang, 2023) have substantially im-
124
      proved both the speed and quality of these approaches. Despite fine-grained texture details through
125
      optimization, these methods usually require a lengthy period, ranging from minutes to hours, to gen-
126   erate only a single object. Contrastly, our approach employs a feed-forward method that requires no
127   per-scene optimization.
128
129
      General Text-to-3D. Methods in General Text-to-3D achieve open-domain T23D without needing
      additional optimization for each new scene. These methods can be divided into two categories based
130
      on their implementation process: fine-tuning-based and prior-based approaches. Prior work SDFu-
131
      sion (Cheng et al., 2023) takes dense SDF grids as the 3D representation, which is computational
132   cost and unable to render textures. Point-E (Nichol et al., 2022) and Shap-E (Jun & Nichol, 2023),
133   trained on millions of 3D assets, generate point clouds and meshes respectively. 3DGen (Gupta
134   et al., 2023) combines a triplane VAE for learning latent representations of textured meshes with
135   a conditional diffusion model for generating the triplane features. VolumeDiffusion (Tang et al.,
136   2023) trains an efficient volumetric encoder to produce training data for the diffusion model. With
137   insufficient 3D data to learn, recent works tend to utilize 2D priors to help the training. Inspired by
138   image-to-3D models (Liu et al., 2023b;a), image diffusion models are adopted for 3D generation.
139   MVDream (Shi et al., 2023) and DreamView (Yan et al., 2024) attempt to jointly train the image
140
      generation model with high-quality normal images and limited multi-view object images to produce
      various object images. Recently, SPAD (Kant et al., 2024) builds upon MVDream to achieve arbi-
141
      trary view generation. Despite these advancements, current methods still struggle to generate both
142
      semantic and multi-view consistent views. In contrast, our approach learns a complete 3D prior,
143   enabling arbitrary view generation while maintaining consistent results across different views.

148   3.1   OVERVIEW
149
      To simultaneously maintain semantic and multi-view consistency, we propose a novel framework
150
      called SeMv-3D for general Text-to-3D, which is illustrated in Figure 2. Our SeMv-3D gener-
151

154   SVS in Sec 3.3 then transforms it into latent space while aligning it with semantics information and
155   finally renders arbitrary views by incorporating a simple yet effective strategy in one single step.

      3.3     S EMANTIC - ALIGNED V IEW S YNTHESIZER
254
255   Given the learned consistent triplane prior through our TPL, we aim to utilize it to synthesize multi-
256   views. While current prior-based methods suffer from the sacrifices of well-learned textual-visual
257   alignment in regressing 3D. To this end, we introduce a Semantic-aligned View Synthesizer (SVS)
258   composed of a Triplane Latents Transformation module, in Sec 3.3.1, aiming to facilitate the deep
259   interaction between textual and visual features to improve semantic consistency. While existing
260   methods can only generate limited views or multi-view with multiple inference steps, we adopt a
261
      simple yet effective training strategy to generate arbitrary views in a single step, illustrated in Sec
      3.3.2. More illustration information is presented in Appendix. A.3.
262
263
      3.3.1    T RIPLANE L ATENTS T RANSFORMATION
264
265   The Triplane Latents Transformation (TLT) module plays a crucial role in SVS, learning the ac-
266   tual implicit triplane representation and further aligning semantics with orthogonalized 3D features.
267   Unlike prior-based methods, which do not incorporate semantic alignment during the formation of
268   implicit fields, our approach introduces semantic alignment during the construction of the triplane
269   implicit field. Given the spatial orthogonality of the triplane, we do not simply incorporate text
      embeddings but instead align semantic features with the orthogonalized 3D triplane features. This

      approach enables precise semantic matching across different 3D visual feature regions. To raise
271   an example, “blonde hair” features could align with their visual features within orthogonalized

285

287   ray sampling, given a batch of camera positions o, for a ray path r(t) = oi + td in the direction

328   4.1      E XPERIMENT S ETUP
329
330   Evaluation Metrics. Following previous work (Hong et al., 2024; Shi et al., 2023), we conduct a
331   comprehensive evaluation incorporating both objective and subjective assessments. More details are
332   provided in Appendix A.4.2. For objective evaluation, we select three commonly used evaluation
333   metrics, including 1) Clip Score (Zhengwentai, 2023): which measure the consistency of the seman-
334   tic alignment between the input text and generated object; 2) Aesthetic Score1 : which represents the
335
      aesthetic performance of the generated object; and 3) Views / One-step: which indicates the upper
      limit on the number of views that the model can generate in one feed-forward step. For subjective
336
      evaluation, we conduct a user study in which users evaluate the results from three perspectives -
337
      1) Users Prefer: similar to Aesthetics Scores, which indicates the user’s liking for the generated
338   views; 2) Semantic Consistency: which measures how well the generated objects match the text
339   like Clip Scores; and 3) Multi-view Consistency: which assess the consistency of objects between
340   each view.
341
      Baselines. To showcase the outstanding performance of our SeMv-3D in both semantic and multi-
342
      view consistency, we also compare it with many state-of-the-art methods, which can be categorized
343
      into two types: 1) Fine-tuning-based methods - MVDream (Shi et al., 2023) and DreamView (Yan
344   et al., 2024) that generate high-quality but limited multi-views while SPAD (Kant et al., 2024) can
345   generate any multi-view but with low consistency. 2) Prior-based methods - (i) Point-E (Nichol
346   et al., 2022) that employs DALLE (Ramesh et al., 2021) as priors and converts it into vivid point
347   clouds. (ii) Shap-E (Jun & Nichol, 2023) that generates higher quality mesh representations based
348   on the Point-E. (iii) VolumeDiffusion (Tang et al., 2023) that designs a volumetric encoder to pro-

456   4.5   A BLATION S TUDY
457
458   In this section, we conduct comprehensive ablation studies to validate the effectiveness of each
459
      component in SeMv-3D, including Triplane Prior Learning (TPL) and Semantic-aligned View Syn-
      thesizer (SVS). More results are presented in Appendix A.2.3.
460
461   Ablation Study of TPL. Figure 4 evaluates the effectiveness of each component in TPL by tak-
462   ing successively our proposed Object Retention (OR) modules, Triplane Orthogonalization (TO)
463   modules, and Orthogonalization Attention (OA) into the base model. Here, we select SD2.1 as our
464   base model. Overall, all the proposed components contribute significantly to the total quality of
465
      generation. Specifically, the base model first performs the worst with accompanied by much irrele-
      vant information. By integrating the OR into the baseline, extraneous backgrounds can be removed
466
      while retaining the subject well. It reveals the importance of OR, which avoids the influence of
467
      irrelevant information on the quality of generation. Then, the TO with adopting temporal attention
468   is added to the above model, which aims to learn the orthogonal spatial relationships of the triplane.
469   Unfortunately, temporal attention can only capture the general spatial correspondences, failing to
470   preserve and align the finer details of the object itself. Finally, fusing the three modules into the
471   base model strictly ensures the correct spatial relationships between the generated triplanes and ef-
472   fectively learns high-fidelity visual information with consistent multi-view alignment. The results
473   indicate OA is more effective in grasping spatial correspondences than temporal attention.
474   Ablation Study of SVS. Figure 5a investigates the efficacy of SVS, which has core components, in-
475   cluding Orthogonalization Attention (OA) and Cross-Attention (CA). Firstly, we find that removing
476   OA drastically decreases quality in geometry and texture (e.g., decline of red cubes and unshaped
477   pillows), which indicates that OA of SVS plays an important role in grasping high-precision de-
478   tailed visual features from triplane prior. Then, both CA and OA are deleted, and in the absence of
479   semantic guidance, the features extracted by the model from the triplane are somewhat biased, and
480   useless features may be extracted to generate 3D. For example, the large out-of-shape artifacts at the
481   end/head of the bed, suggesting that CA indeed serves as a semantic guidance. Finally, the final SVS
482
      can reconstruct multi-view outputs with realistic geometric details and consistent alignment across
      different views, demonstrating its efficacy.

485   experiment by reconstructing text input to generate different 3D content via SVS while maintaining

873   A.3     I LLUSTRATION OF S E M V-3D
874
875   Illustration of TPL. The core idea of TPL is to fully leverage the knowledge of existing pre-trained
876   models, such as Stable Diffusion (SD), to integrate a 3D-feature-based prior. This approach aims
877   to mitigate the limitations of prior-based methods, including the incompleteness of 2D priors and
878
      the potential loss of information during dimensional upscaling. To achieve this, we design two key
      steps, OR and TO, to transform existing pre-trained models into 3D Triplane prior learners.
879
880   Constructing a high-quality and comprehensive Triplane prior requires learning the correspondence
881   among the features of three planes representing the same object in the pre-trained model. However,
882   outputs from pre-trained models often contain complex backgrounds and components unrelated to
883   the prompt, which severely disrupt the learning of spatial correspondences between planes. To
884
      address this, we propose the OR module, which removes irrelevant background and focuses on
      generating the primary content of the prompt.
885
886   Building on this, the TO module further learns the spatial correspondences among the three planes.
887   By leveraging the inherent spatial relationships of the Triplane representation, the TO module en-
888   ables the learning of a comprehensive and integrated 3D prior. This approach significantly improves
889   the quality and consistency of the 3D features, providing a robust foundation for subsequent SVS.
890   Illustration of SVS. The core concept of SVS is to introduce fine-grained semantic matching in the
891   construction of implicit 3D representations. This process fundamentally involves aligning semantic
892   features with orthogonalized 3D features. Unlike traditional prior-based methods, our approach
893   integrates semantic matching into the construction of the triplane implicit field, aiming to achieve
894   precise alignment between semantic features and orthogonalized triplane features.
895   In practice, instead of merely combining text embeddings with 3D features, our goal is to establish
896   accurate alignment between semantic features and the triplane’s orthogonal visual features. How-
897   ever, due to the inherent invisibility of the correspondence between 3D features and specific visual
898   regions, manual alignment of semantic features to visual regions is impractical. To address this
899   challenge, we introduce an orthogonal attention mechanism.
900   Specifically, we integrate semantic and visual features in the same feature space, allowing them to
901   adaptively align through attention during the implicit triplane reconstruction based on the spatial or-
902   thogonal relationships of the triplane. This enables semantic features to automatically align with the
903   most likely visual feature regions, ultimately achieving precise semantic alignment across different
904   3D visual regions. This approach effectively resolves the challenges of aligning semantics with 3D
905   features and significantly enhances both model performance and generation quality.

910   A.4.1    I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS .
911
912   We train our framework on a subset (∼ 500k objects) of Objaverse dataset (Deitke et al., 2023).
913   We use Stable Diffusion 2.1 to initialize the triplane prior learner (TPL), and train it in the object
914   retention stage for 150k steps with the learning rate 5 × 10−4 , and in the triplane orthogonalization
915   stage for 60k steps with the learning rate 5 × 10−5 . The semantic-aligned view synthesizer(SVS) is
916   trained for 100k steps with a learning rate of 5 × 10−4 . All experiments and training are conducted
917   on eight NVIDIA A6000 GPUs, adopting the AdamW (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2019) optimizer for
      all stages with β1 = 0.9, β2 = 0.95, and weight decay 0.03.

929   A.4.2     M ETRICS D ETAILS .
930
931   Selection of Metrics. In open-domain generation tasks, the absence of corresponding ground truth
932   (GT) makes it impractical to use reconstruction metrics such as SSIM (Wang et al., 2004) for eval-
933
      uation. In image and video generation tasks, specific metrics like FID (Heusel et al., 2017) and
      FVD (Unterthiner et al., 2018) are commonly designed to comprehensively assess generation qual-
934
      ity. However, in the context of 3D generation tasks, no analogous metric (something like F3D) has
935
      yet been established.
936
937   Therefore, we follow the evaluation protocol used in prior works. Objectively, we assess generation
938   quality and semantic alignment using the CLIP score. Subjectively, we conduct a user study to
939
      evaluate multi-view consistency and generation quality comprehensively. Additionally, to further
      demonstrate the high quality of the generated results from multiple perspectives, we incorporate the
940
      aesthetic score as a supplementary evaluation metric.
941
942   User Study Setting. Due to the lack of diverse objective evaluation metrics for general text-to-3D
943   methods, user studies are commonly employed to further validate the effectiveness of these ap-
944   proaches. In this experiment, we invited 40 highly educated individuals with undergraduate degrees
945
      or higher to participate in the evaluation. Among them, approximately 20 have experience in AI-
      related research or work, 10 are engaged in artistic professions, and the remaining 10 are involved
946
      in fields such as civil engineering, architecture, and sports.
947
948   In practice, each user is first given 9 groups of generated four views (e.g., 0◦ , 90◦ , 180◦ , 270◦ ) and
949   the corresponding prompts. Then, they are asked to select their preferred method from three levels,
950   including Users Prefer, Semantic Consistency, and Multi-View Consistency. They first evaluate the
951
      overall quality and selected their preferred option (Users Prefer). Then, based on consistency, they
      separately identify the method with the highest semantic alignment (Semantic Consistency) and
952
      the method with the greatest consistency across different views (Multi-View Consistency).

      A.5     L IMITATION .
955
956   Lack of High-Quality Dataset.The goal of the general text-to-3D task is to learn a generic model
957   that can generate various objects in a feed-forward manner. However, this field lacks high-quality
958   large-scale text-3d pairing data, influencing the quality of generation.
959
      Graininess Issues. We observe that fragmented white graininess occasionally appears in certain
960
      thin, sheet-like objects and along the edges of some objects. Upon analysis, we identify two po-
961
      tential factors: First, the dataset contains broken objects that are not fully filtered out, and their
962   characteristics (as illustrated in Fig. 10) are learned by the model, resulting in discrete noise artifacts
963   during generation. Second, due to computational resource limitations, the model is trained with a
964   relatively small batch size, which may have impacted its robustness, particularly in handling thin
965   objects.
