009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Achieving high-resolution, identity consistency, and accurate lip-speech synchro-
012            nization in face visual dubbing presents significant challenges, particularly for
013            real-time applications like live video streaming. We propose MuseTalk, which
014            generates lip-sync targets in a latent space encoded by a Variational Autoen-
015            coder, enabling high-fidelity talking face video generation with efficient infer-
016
               ence. Specifically, we project the occluded lower half of the face image and itself
               as an reference into a low-dimensional latent space and use a multi-scale U-Net
017
               to fuse audio and visual features at various levels. We further propose a novel
018
               sampling strategy during training, which selects reference images with head poses
019            closely matching the target, allowing the model to focus on precise lip movement
020            by filtering out redundant information. Additionally, we analyze the mechanism
021            of lip-sync loss and reveal its relationship with input information volume. Ex-
022            tensive experiments show that MuseTalk consistently outperforms recent state-of-
023            the-art methods in visual fidelity and achieves comparable lip-sync accuracy. As
024            MuseTalk supports the online generation of face at 256x256 at more than 30 FPS
025            with negligible starting latency, it paves the way for real-time applications. The
026            codes and models will be made publicly available upon acceptance.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030
      Generating realistic talking face animations from audio has gained significant attention in recent
031   years, with applications spanning visual dubbing, the film industry, digital assistants, and be-
032   yond (Kim et al., 2018; Pataranutaporn et al., 2021; Song et al., 2019; Gu et al., 2019). The primary
033   goal of talking face generation is to synchronize two distinct modalities—audio and visual—so that
034   the lip’s movements align with the input speech content, producing lip-synced, high-fidelity videos.
035
      Existing talking face generation approaches can be broadly classified into three main types based
036
      on their training paradigms and data requirements: person-specific talking face (Song et al., 2020;
037   Lahiri et al., 2021; Guo et al., 2021), one-shot talking head (Stypu kowski et al., 2024; Chen et al.,
038   2024; Xu et al., 2024a;b), and few-shot face visual dubbing (Cheng et al., 2022; Prajwal et al., 2020a;
039   Zhong et al., 2023; Park et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023). For person-specific talking face methods,
040   the training process typically incorporates the subject’s specific identity. While these methods can
041   generate highly photorealistic talking face videos, the need for re-training or fine-tuning for each
042   new speaker limits their practicality in real-world applications. Meanwhile, with the rapid advance-
043   ments in GANs and diffusion models for video synthesis, one-shot talking head techniques have
044   emerged, which can drive a single reference facial image to produce synchronized lip movements,
045
      realistic facial expressions, and natural portrait animations. Although these methods can generate
      vivid talking head videos, they require vast amounts of training data, significant computational re-
046
      sources, and time-consuming inference processes (Guo et al., 2024), making them unsuitable for
047
      real-time interactions, such as digital human live streaming. As a result, few-shot face visual dub-
048   bing techniques, which focus on reconstructing the mouth region of the source face based on driving
049   audio, have gained significant attention in applications such as video translation, film dubbing, and
050   real-time interactions like virtual digital humans, where speed and efficiency are crucial. But in
051   fact, because humans are particularly sensitive to even slight misalignments between speech and
052   facial movements, the key criteria for high-fidelity face visual dubbing are high resolution, identity
053   consistency, and lip-speech synchronization. However, due to the weak correlation between audio
      and visual inputs, the primary challenge remains how to align these two heterogeneous modalities

      sufficiently. Then, from an information-theoretic perspective, the central issue is to effectively min-
055   imize the uncertainty (entropy) of the sources while mitigating the impact of noise and irrelevant
056   information (Lahat et al., 2015).
057
058
      To achieve this purpose, some approaches typically leverage an encoder-decoder structure to align
      audio and visual representations. They use convolutional networks with several up-sampling layers
059
      to synthesize mouth region pixels directly from latent embeddings (Prajwal et al., 2020b; Xie et al.,
060
      2021; Cheng et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023). Nevertheless, within such a straightforward fusion
061   mechanism, the information screening process often fails to adequately preserve the facial texture
062   details from the reference image. This can lead to a loss of texture quality and result in blurry,
063   identity-inconsistent visuals, even with high-resolution training data (Zhang et al., 2023). Beyond
064   visual quality, lip-speech synchronization is a more crucial metric for visual dubbing, as it measures
065   the temporal correspondence between the input audio and the synthesized video streams. A straight-
066   forward yet effective approach to this issue is to incorporate an auxiliary pre-trained network, such
067   as a lip-sync discriminator (Prajwal et al., 2020b; Chung & Zisserman, 2017) or a lip-sync loss (Park
068   et al., 2022) at the end of the generator to assess audio-visual coherence. This can be viewed as a
069
      form of Mutual Information Estimator (Zhu et al., 2020), but there is limited research analyzing its
      mutual information regulation mechanism, particularly regarding how it selects the most relevant
070
      features from redundant source information. Therefore, this paper addresses the three key chal-
071
      lenges of few-shot face visual dubbing by focusing on controlling the complexity and information
072   volume of the input source, along with implementing an efficient information fusion mechanism.
073   We also attempt to answer two fundamental questions: (1) How to achieve high-resolution and
074   Identity-Consistency visual content? and (2) How to obtain lip-synced mouth movement?
075
      In this paper, we introduce MuseTalk, a real-time framework for face visual dubbing that employs
076
      mouth inpainting technology. MuseTalk takes as input a occluded lower half of a face image, a
077
      reference face from the same individual, and an audio track, and outputs a face image with lips that
078   are seamlessly synchronized with the audio. Specifically,
079
080         1. To generate high resolution face images (256×256), while ensuring real-time inference ca-
081            pabilities, we introduce a method to produce lip-sync targets within a latent space. This
082            space is encoded by a pre-trained Variational Autoencoder (VAE) Kingma & Welling
083            (2013), which is instrumental in maintaining the quality and speed of our framework.
084         2. Within the latent space, we employ a U-Net structure for the generative model. This struc-
085            ture integrates visual and audio embeddings across different scales through cross-attention.
086            This approach allows MuseTalk to manage the flow of information efficiently, preserving
087            essential visual features and enhancing the synchronization.
088         3. We observe that, during testing, the closer the pose of the occluded face is to that of the
089            reference face during testing, the better the generation results, due to reduced variation.
090            Typically, both the occluded and reference faces are derived from the same frame, akin to
091            the approaches in Cheng et al. (2022); Prajwal et al. (2020b). However, during training,
092            they adopt a random sampling strategy that introduces significant pose variations between
093            the occluded and reference faces. To bridge this gap, we propose Selective Information
094
               Sampling (SIS), which selects reference images with head poses closely aligned to the
               target while ensuring distinct lip movements. This strategy sharpens the model’s focus on
095
               the intricate textures of the mouth region.
096
097         4. We also delve into the mechanism of the widely utilized lipsync loss by using our proposed
098
               Adaptive Audio Modulation(AAM) strategy to understand its role in enhancing lip-sync
               accuracy. By modulating the amount of input information, the lip-sync loss facilitates
099
               the model’s ability to extract more pertinent information from the two modalities, thereby
100
               optimizing the mutual information.
101
102   We conduct qualitative and quantitative experiments to evaluate our MuseTalk, and experimental
103   results show that our method significantly outperforms existing techniques in terms of both visual
104   quality and lip-sync accuracy, paving the way for more advanced applications in audio-driven visu-
105   ally dubbing and beyond.

113   2   R ELATED W ORK
114
115
      Existing audio-driven talking face generation methods can be broadly categorized into three types:
116
      person-specific, one-shot talking head and few-shot face visual dubbing methods. The key difference
117
      among them is how much information do they need from reference visual content to generation of
118   photo-realistic talking face animations.
119
120   Person-specific talking face methods, which incorporate target characteristics during training, can
121
      generate high-fidelity, identity-preserving results. However, these approaches typically rely on 3D
      models (Song et al., 2020; Thies et al., 2020; Guo et al., 2021) or Neural Radiance Fields (NeRF)
122
      (Park et al., 2022) as intermediate representations and require several minutes of footage to learn the
123
      audio-lip mapping, making them time-consuming and limiting their real-world applicability.
124
125   One-shot talking head methods have recently gained significant attention due to their ability to
126   generate lifelike facial expressions and manage head motion dynamics using a single reference im-
127
      age. A common approach is to use intermediate representations like facial landmarks or 3D meshes.
      For instance, (Chen et al., 2019; Zhou et al., 2020) propose a two-stage pipeline where an audio-to-
128
      landmark module is followed by a landmark-to-video generation, effectively disentangling speaker
129
      identity from speech content. Other works, such as (Chen et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2021a), uti-
130   lize 3D coefficients to drive facial motion based on audio-predicted expression, pose, and geometry.
131   However, these methods often struggle with fine-grained details like teeth and mouth textures, lim-
132   iting the overall fidelity of the generated videos due to the coarse control granularity of 3D models.
133   With the recent advancements in diffusion models for video generation, audio-driven talking head
134   synthesis has seen progress with single-image setups. Works such as (Tian et al., 2024; Xu et al.,
135   2024a; Chen et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2024b) demonstrate how diffusion models can
136   generate realistic portrait videos driven by audio inputs. While diffusion methods excel in enhancing
137   generation diversity, they can introduce uncertainty and loss of identity-related details, leading to ar-
138
      tifacts such as overly beautified faces or loss of specific textures. Furthermore, these methods require
      large amounts of training data, substantial computational resources, and a time-intensive multi-step
139
      inference process, which limits their practicality for real-time or resource-constrained applications.
140
141   Few-shot face visual dubbing focuses on replacing the mouth region of a source face based on driv-
142   ing audio. The most common approach utilizes an encoder-decoder architecture. For instance, works
143   like (Prajwal et al., 2020a; Park et al., 2022; Xie et al., 2021; Cheng et al., 2022) employ separate
144
      image and audio encoders to extract features, which are then fused using a single decoder to gener-
      ate the mouth region pixels directly on the source face. While these methods achieve lip movements
145
      that correspond well to speech content, the simple fusion mechanism often fails to preserve the facial
146
      texture details from the reference image. This results in a loss of texture fidelity and produces blurry,
147   identity-inconsistent visual outputs, even when trained on high-resolution data (Zhang et al., 2023).
148   To address these limitations, (Zhang et al., 2023) propose a Deformation Inpainting Network, where
149   a deformation operation shifts pixels into their correct positions, preserving high-frequency texture
150   details. However, this method can still introduce local blur due to redundant information from the
151   reference images and restrict natural lip movement. Additionally, the inpainting module is prone to
152   overfitting, leading to noticeable color differences when applied to unseen images.

289   3.3       A DAPTIVE AUDIO M ODULATION (AAM)
290
291   The driving audio is the key factor influencing the patterns of lip movements, so the information
292
      in both modalities (audio and visual) must be complete and highly coherent, capturing cues like
      pronunciation and tonality. The conventional approach is to manually synchronize audio and video
293
      before training. For example, (Wang et al., 2023) uses a 0.2-second audio segment centered on the
294
      pose reference as input. However, achieving precise synchronization is challenging due to different
295   sampling rates between the two modalities. While our multi-scale fusion architecture addresses this
296   issue to some extent, it does not entirely resolve it.
297
298
      Building on previous research, we found that incorporating lip-sync loss (Prajwal et al., 2020a;
      Cheng et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2023) significantly enhances lip synchronization. However, we
299
      sought to understand its underlying mechanism. Through experiments, we discover that lip-sync
300
      loss acts as an information modulator, regulating the input data and enabling the model to extract
301   more relevant information from both modalities, thus optimizing mutual information. As illustrated
302   in Fig. 2 (b), we assume the information contained in the synchronized audio input corresponding
303   to the target lip movement follows a normal distribution. Lip synchronization-related information
304   is concentrated in the middle range, while other information may pertain to finer details, such as
305   pronunciation habits and temporal relations. To evaluate the quality of the generated results, we
306   vary the length of the input audio segment (T = 1, 3, 5, 7) to control the information volume.
307   This not only enhances lip-speech synchronization but also improves the image quality (see Frechet
308   Inception Distance (FID) in Table 4).

328   Dataset Preparation. We utilize two widely recognized high-resolution talking face datasets in
329   our experiments: HDTF (Zhang et al., 2021b) and MEAD (Wang et al., 2020). The HDTF dataset
330   consists of approximately 410 in-the-wild videos at 720P or 1080P resolution. We randomly select
331   20 videos for testing and use the remaining videos for training. All videos are trimmed to thirty-
332   second clips for both the training and testing phases. We compile 1836 videos featuring neutral
333   expressions and frontal views to create the MEAD-Neutral dataset, in line with the methodology
334   in (Zhang et al., 2023). From this dataset, we randomly choose 240 videos across 6 identities for
335   testing. At testing stage, we employ a protocol that mirrors real-world usage, where the video and
336
      audio are from different sources, and the reference image is taken from the current frame. This
      is consistent with the unpaired evaluation protocol used by Wav2Lip (Prajwal et al., 2020b) and
337
      VideoRetalking (Cheng et al., 2022), ensuring a fair comparison.
338
339
340
      Evaluation Metrics. The experiments are designed to assess the method’s visual fidelity, identity
      preservation, and lip synchronization capabilities. Frechet Inception Distance (FID) (Heusel et al.,
341
      2017) is employed for visual quality assessment. We choose FID because this task lacks pixel-level
342
      ground truth; the input mouth regions are altered, making pixel-level metrics like PSNR, SSIM,
343   and LPIPS less suitable. FID measures the similarity between the distribution of generated images
344   and real images, providing a robust metric for visual fidelity in the absence of ground-truth talking
345   videos. Identity preservation is measured by calculating the cosine similarity (CSIM) between the
346   identity embeddings of the source and generated images. Lip synchronization is evaluated using
347   lip-sync-error confidence (LSE-C) (Prajwal et al., 2020b).
348
349   Compared Baselines. The proposed method is benchmarked against several state-of-the-art real-
350   time video dubbing techniques: 1) Wav2Lip (Prajwal et al., 2020b), which is renowned for generat-
351   ing realistic lip synchronization in videos by utilizing a robust pre-trained lip-sync discriminator; 2)
352   VideoRetalking (Cheng et al., 2022), which delivers high-quality audio-driven lip synchronization
353   for talking head video editing through a process of expression neutralization, lip-sync generation,
354
      and identity-aware enhancement; 3) DI-Net (Zhang et al., 2023), which is noted for creating photo-
      realistic and emotionally consistent talking face videos by employing a dual-encoder framework
355
      coupled with a facial action unit system; 4) TalkLip (Wang et al., 2023), which introduces an innova-
356
      tive contrastive learning approach to improve lip-speech synchronization and utilizes a transformer
357   to encode audio in sync with video, taking into account the global temporal dependencies of audio.

361   Table 1 presents the results of our quantitative analysis on the HDTF and MEAD-Neutral datasets.
362   MuseTalk outperforms the competition, achieving the highest scores in FID and CSIM, and compa-

369
      method of randomly sampling a list of reference images introduces redundant information to the

370
      as shown in the LSE-C score. Furthermore, the DI-Net model’s performance on the MEAD dataset

      is unsatisfactory, with the generated results exhibiting noticeable color differences, likely due to its

      4.4   ABLATION STUDIES
462
      To evaluate the impact of each main component used in MuseTalk, we make an comprehensive
463   ablation study experiment on HDTF dataset.
464
465   Selective Information Sampling. We analyze the impact of different sampling methods. As
466   shown in Table 2, random sampling approaches, previously used in studies such as (Zhang et al.,
467   2023; Cheng et al., 2022; Prajwal et al., 2020b), result in lower-quality images, reflected in higher
468   FID scores. In contrast, we propose two alternative sampling methods: Distinct-Mouth Sampling
469   and Pose-Aligned Sampling, to demonstrate the effectiveness of our Selective Information Sampling
470   (SIS) strategy in filtering out redundant information. Distinct-Mouth Sampling, which emphasizes
471   capturing detailed lip movements, improves the lip synchronization score (LSE-C), but compro-
472   mises visual fidelity. On the other hand, Pose-Aligned Sampling focuses on matching head poses,
473
      achieving the highest image quality with the lowest FID and highest CSIM scores. However, this
      approach sacrifices lip sync accuracy, as the model reproduces more reference information instead
474
      of generating authentic lip movements, leading to an information leakage issue. Our Selective Infor-
475
      mation Sampling method strikes a balance by aligning head poses with the target while preserving
476   distinct lip movements. This dual focus allows the model to capture intricate mouth details, resulting
477   in superior performance across all metrics, balancing both image quality and lip sync accuracy.
478
479   Multi-scale Fusion. Leveraging the multi-scale data fusion mechanism within the UNet architec-
480   ture, MuseTalk achieves effective audio-visual integration for visual dubbing. As shown in Table 3,
481   shallow feature fusion proves insufficient, particularly in enhancing lip synchronization (LSE-C). In
482   contrast, the full multi-scale fusion significantly improves both audio-visual coherence and image
483   quality, underscoring its importance in achieving high-quality results.
484
485   Adaptive Audio Modulation. As shown in Table 4, the addition of Lsync leads to a significant
      average improvement across the three evaluation metrics (FID, CSIM, LSE-C) for varying audio

526   4.5      C ONCLUSION
527
528
529   We propose MuseTalk, a groundbreaking framework designed to address the significant challenges
530   in generating high-quality, real-time talking faces. Inspired by Stable Diffusion, we bypass the
531
      time-consuming diffusion process and directly model the correlation between audio and visual data,
      utilizing a novel combination of a Variational Autoencoder (VAE) and Whisper models for feature
532
      extraction, alongside a U-Net architecture for generating targets in latent space. This innovative ap-
533
      proach not only ensures high resolution and identity consistency but also achieves precise lip-speech
534   synchronization. To be specific, MuseTalk integrates audio features and latent visual representations
535   through multi-scale feature fusion within the U-Net, optimizing the output results through the pro-
536   posed SIS and AAM strategies. The experiments conducted on the HDTF and MEAD-Neutral
537   datasets show the superior performance of MuseTalk compared to existing state-of-the-art methods,
538   underscoring its potential to set new standards in digital communication and multimedia. Besides,
539   MuseTalk can achieve a generation speed of 30 frames per second on an NVIDIA Tesla V100,
      confirming its capability for real-time applications.
