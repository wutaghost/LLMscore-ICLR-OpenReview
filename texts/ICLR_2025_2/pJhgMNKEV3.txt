009                                                      A BSTRACT
010
011                There is a growing trend of teaching large language models (LLMs) to solve math-
012                ematical problems through coding. Existing studies primarily focus on prompting
013                powerful, closed-source models to generate seed training data followed by in-
014                domain data augmentation, equipping LLMs with considerable capabilities for
015                code-assisted mathematical reasoning. However, continually training these mod-
016                els on augmented data derived from a few datasets such as GSM8K may impair
017
                   their generalization abilities and restrict their effectiveness to limited question
                   types. Conversely, the potential of improving such LLMs by leveraging large-scale,
018
                   expert-written, diverse math question-answer pairs remains unexplored. To utilize
019
                   these resources and tackle unique challenges such as code response assessment,
020                we propose a novel paradigm that uses a code-based critic model to guide steps
021                including question-code data construction, quality control, and complementary
022                evaluation. We also explore different alignment algorithms with self-generated
023                instruction/preference data to foster continuous self-improvement. Experiments
024                across both in-distribution (up to +5.7%) and out-of-distribution (+4.4%) bench-
025                marks in English and Chinese show the effectiveness of the proposed paradigm.

028   1       I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Though large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated strong performance on mathematical
031
      benchmarks, they still face challenges in achieving accurate computation and reasoning, especially in
      out-of-distribution scenarios. For example, even the recent closed-source LLM o1-mini struggles with
032
      multiplication beyond eight digits (Deng, 2024) using step-by-step reasoning (or Chain-of-Thought,
033
      CoT) (Wei et al., 2022). To alleviate the computational burden on LLMs, particularly those of
034   smaller sizes, there is a growing trend of utilizing code and code interpreters to enhance precise
035   computation and reasoning of LLMs in solving mathematical problems (Chen et al., 2022; Gao
036   et al., 2023b; Zhou et al., 2023). An effective method involves prompting closed-source LLMs to
037   generate code-based solutions for given questions. However, previous studies demonstrated that
038   closed-source models, without extra test-time compute, still struggle with real-world high school and
039   college-level math exams (Liu et al., 2024). Solving advanced problems through coding demands not
040   only mathematical expertise but also interdisciplinary knowledge and skills, including programming
041   and natural language, making it a more formidable challenge. Previous code-assisted studies primarily
042
      focus on using closed-source LLMs such as GPT-4 to label a few small-scale, representative datasets
      such as GSM8K (Cobbe et al., 2021) and MATH (Hendrycks et al., 2021), verifying the correctness of
043
      the solutions via pattern-based answer matching, and training models on the verified data for further
044
      in-distribution data augmentation through sampling, code execution, and answer validation (Wang
045   et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023; Gou et al., 2024; Lu et al., 2024). However, continually learning from
046   these datasets or their augmented versions, regardless of the use of code, is evidently less effective
047   for improving the generalization of LLMs due to the limited diversity.
048
      On the other hand, large-scale, expert-written, mathematical question-answer (QA) pairs from
049
      educational web resources remain under-studied to improve code-assisted math reasoning abilities
050
      of LLMs. These resources span educational levels from primary school to college and include
051   various question types and answer formats, such as multiple-choice, application, proof, and cloze.
052   To use these resources to self-improve code-assisted1 LLMs, instead of further extensively distilling

068   closed-source models, one natural solution is to use a fine-tuned model to generate code samples for
069   each problem and use the valid data to (iteratively) improve this LLM, similar to self-improved CoT
070   reasoners (Zelikman et al., 2022; Yuan et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2024; Hosseini et al., 2024) over data
071   with reference answers. However, the key challenge is to determine whether the self-generated
072   code responses align with reference answers in diverse formats. Fortunately, with the aid of an
073   external code interpreter, we are less concerned about potential computation errors in intermediate
074   CoT reasoning steps. We assume a code solution is more likely to be correct if its execution result
075
      matches the reference answers, thus shifting the focus from the step-by-step comparison to comparing
      the reference answers with the code execution results. Based on our analysis (Section 3.1), we
076
      observe that most cases primarily require format conversion between plain text and code syntax (e.g.,
077
      “(x-5)(xˆ2-4x+7)” vs. “(x-5)*(x**2-4*x+7)” and “(1, -2, 2, -3)” vs. “{A:1, B:-2, C:2, D:-3}”) and
078   relatively simple numerical calculations, which do not require advanced logical reasoning abilities or
079   in-depth language-specific knowledge (Section 3.5).
080
081
      These observations and task simplification motivate us to design a critic model to evaluate the
      correctness of the code execution result against the reference answer by simply predicting Y ES or N O
082
      (see examples in Table 1). As illustrated in Figure 1, this critic model is used to guide multiple steps
083
      during self-improvement. We first train a model with seed question-code data following previous
084   code-assisted studies and consider it as the initial policy model. In each iteration, we use the current
085   policy model to generate code samples for new questions and keep the highest-scoring valid code
086   responses rated by the critic model for supervised fine-tuning (SFT) in the subsequent iteration. To
087   foster continuous improvement, we also explore different preference learning algorithms such as
088   DPO (Rafailov et al., 2024) and ORPO (Hong et al., 2024) with self-generated preference data, where
089   the preference labels are also provided by the critic model.
090   We perform experiments on various model families, such as Llama3-8B (AI@Meta, 2024) and
091   DeepSeek-Coder-7B (Daya Guo, 2024), and Qwen2-7B (Yang et al., 2024). Experimental results
092   across both in-distribution (up to +5.7%) and out-of-distribution (OOD) (+4.4%) benchmarks in
093   English and Chinese show the effectiveness of self-improving LLMs using our proposed paradigm
094   with large-scale mathematical QA pairs. The resulting 7-8B models can outperform state-of-the-art
095   70B code-assisted math LLMs (Gou et al., 2024) by 11.9% in OOD scenarios. Notably, we observe
096   a strong correlation between the traditional heuristic-based evaluation method and the critic model
097   (Section 3.5), with the latter reducing the additional human effort needed to design rules for new
098
      mathematical benchmarks. Additionally, introducing SFT loss into the DPO training is surprisingly
      effective in controlling the code response length. To summarize the contributions of this work:
099
100            • To the best of our knowledge, this is the first attempt to leverage large-scale web QA pairs
101              to improve the code-assisted mathematical reasoning abilities of LLMs.
102            • To better leverage these large-scale, diverse QA pairs, we propose a novel iterative self-
103              improving paradigm that employs a new critic model to guide various steps such as data
104              construction and filtering. This critic model can also serve as a complementary evaluation
105              scorer, reducing the reliance on heuristic design for new evaluation tasks.
106            • Extensive experiments on both English and Chinese tasks demonstrate the effectiveness of
107              our paradigm, and our comprehensive analysis of the key factors in achieving continuous
                 improvement at different stages may shed light on future studies.

133   2.1   T RAINING AN I NITIAL M ODEL
134
135   One key factor for an iterative paradigm is to have a reasonably strong initial model. To train such a
136   model, we first use high-quality seed data to fine-tune an LLM, resulting in model Mseed . We use
137   Mseed to generate code samples and keep up to four predictions per question wherein the execution
138   result of the code matches the reference answer and combines the seed data and the self-distilled data
139   to train M0 , which is further used as the initial model for later stages. We will introduce more details
140
      about the seed data construction in the experiment section.
141
142   2.2   B UILDING A M ULTI -U SE C ODE -BASED C RITIC M ODEL
143
144
      To improve LLMs with large-scale, diverse-format math QA data without code annotations, several
      challenges arise in data utilization, filtering, and evaluation. First, previous studies primarily use
145
      pattern-based methods to compare predictions and ground truth answers during validation and
146
      evaluation. This works well for GSM-style datasets, where answers are single numbers and well-
147   formatted (e.g., “72” in “...72 clips altogether in April and May.\n #### 72”). However, pattern-based
148   methods face inherent challenges in handling diverse answer types and formats and bridging the
149   gap between natural language and programming language. For example, with the MATH dataset,
150   comparing CoT predictions with reference answers in LaTeX-like format already requires human-
151   written patterns and answer conversion (Yue et al., 2023). This complexity is compounded when
152   predictions are presented in code syntax, even when the critic task is already simplified to compare
153   the reference answer with the code execution result.
154   To address the above challenges, we propose building a code-based critic model optimized by the
155   following objective:
156
157
                                                 L(rϕ ) = − log rϕ (y | q, a, c, e),                                                     (1)
158
159   where q denotes a question, a is the reference answer to q, c represents the code response to q, and e
160   is the execution result of code c. To simplify the task, we let y be either “Y ES ” or “N O ”. Examples
161   are shown in Table 1. We leave other formulations, such as training a scalar critic model (Ouyang
      et al., 2022), to future work.

      2.3   C ODE DATA G ENERATION
163
164   As mentioned previously, our goal is to leverage web math QA data to continuously self-improve
165   the code-assisted mathematical reasoning ability of LLMs. For well-formatted, web-collected math
166   data such as APE (Zhao et al., 2020) and CM (Qin et al., 2021), where most answers are one or two
167   numerical values (see examples in Table 16), it is efficient and effective to compare the reference
168   answer and the execution result of the code using scripts released by previous studies (Section 3.2).
169   For real-world math data involving various types of questions, such as multiple-choice, multi-part
170
      questions, fill-in-the-blank, application, and proof, using a critic model introduced in the previous
      section is more flexible and saves the need for the labor-intensive and time-consuming process of
171
      creating task-specific patterns. Note that for all questions, we only use their reference answers to
172
      verify the correctness of code execution results, rather than directly using these answers – often short
173   and inconsistent in style – for training. Additionally, we only use benchmarks’ training sets.
174
175   In the k + 1-th iteration, for each new question, we use the current policy model πθk to generate
176
      five code samples and execute them to obtain the results. For questions in the diverse-format web
      data, the critic model is then used to predict Y ES or N O for each response (ai , cij , eij ) given qi .
177
      We use the probability of Y ES or N O as the confidence value for the critic model’s judgment. A
178
      higher probability score indicates a greater confidence in the code response, either agreeing with or
179   disagreeing with the reference answer.

288   3.2     I MPLEMENTATION
289
290   We use L LLAMA FACTORY (Zheng et al., 2024) for efficient fine-tuning built upon DeepSpeed
291   (ZeRO-3). Our experiments are conducted using 8XA100 40GB GPUs. We train LLMs with BF16
292   mixed-precision. The training for the self-improving paradigm takes approximately 96 hours. With
293
      80 workers in multi-processing mode on a CPU machine, we can execute about 9,003 code samples
      per minute. Each model at each stage is trained for two epochs with a learning rate of 1e-5 for SFT
294
      and 1e-6 for preference learning. We set the SFT loss coefficient (λ in Equation 7) to 1.0. The
295
      maximum sequence length is set to 1024, and the batch size is set to 64. We set λ1 to 0.8 and λ2 to 3.
296
297   We experiment with various LLMs to select backbone models such as CodeLlama-7B-Python (Roziere
298   et al., 2023), Llama3instruct (AI@Meta, 2024), CodeQwen1.5-7B-Chat (Team, 2024), QWEN2(Yang
299
      et al., 2024), and Deepseek-Coder-7B-instruct-v1.5 (Daya Guo, 2024), which demonstrate strong
      coding capabilities on code-related benchmarks. Due to limited computational resources, we use
300
      their 7-8B versions with their default templates and leave the model scaling up for future work.
301
      We primarily follow the evaluation scripts from previous studies (Liang et al., 2024) for Chinese
302   benchmarks and FastEval2 for English benchmarks GSM8K and MATH, which often use Python for
303   numerical comparison. We also make adjustments to these scripts, as our predicted answers are in
304   code syntax. CodeLlama-7B-Python is used as the backbone model to train the code-based critic
305   model for three epochs with the maximum sequence length 4096.

503
504
      Evaluation: For automatic math evaluation on well-formatted benchmarks, previous studies mostly
      use heuristics and external tools (e.g., the Python EVAL () function) to compare answers and predic-
505
      tions (Fourrier et al., 2023; Gao et al., 2023a), which works quite well for single numerical value
506
      answers, as seen in datasets such as GSM8K (Cobbe et al., 2021), ASDiv (Miao et al., 2020), and
507   SVAMP (Patel et al., 2021). However, since answers from web resources are diverse in formats and
508   language-code syntactic differences, using carefully designed task-specific heuristics becomes less
509   feasible for comparing answers and code execution results. For datasets beyond value-style answers
510   such as MATH (Hendrycks et al., 2021), closed source LLMs are also used for evaluation such as
511   OpenAI-Evals. However, this approach is not cost-effective for assessing large-scale code samples.
512
      Self-Improvement: Several approaches (Li et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2023b; Lu et al., 2023; Yuan
513   et al., 2024; Hu et al., 2024) use the LLM itself or a separate critic model (Ouyang et al., 2022) for
514   scoring or validating natural-language responses. This work focuses on tool-assisted assessment
515   of code responses to math questions. Similar to previous self-improvement CoT studies (Zelikman
516   et al., 2022; Hosseini et al., 2024; Yuan et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2024), we use ground truth answers
517   to assist training data validation and filtering, as it is still challenging to train a good critic/reward
518   model for math reasoning without reference answers, even for solution-level assessment (Lightman
519   et al., 2023; Daheim et al., 2024). In our paradigm, a single iteration of DPO can already enhance
520   performance, and additional iterations on unseen data might further improve results, as suggested by
521
      previous online studies with CoT reasoning (Dong et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024). However, it has
      been shown that using a general-purpose reward model yields fewer improvements in mathematical
522
      reasoning compared to the gains observed in other tasks.
523
524   Data Augmentation and Knowledge Distillation: Though recent studies have shown that CoT or
525   code-assisted in-distribution data augmentation will lead LLMs to achieve strong performance on
526   in-distribution math datasets (Luo et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2023a; An et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024),
527
      we leave data augmentation on web data (either CoT or code-assisted reasoning) for future work.
      We only use GPT-4 to annotate seed/critic training data, and using closed-source LLMs to annotate
528
      the code responses of large-scale web questions is not explored. Unfortunately, SOTA code-aided
529
      models (Gou et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2023), trained on English data, have limited capability in
530   labeling diverse-format questions written in Chinese.

533   5    C ONCLUSIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
534
535   We introduce a novel paradigm for improving LLMs, which employs a code-based critic model to
536   guide stages such as the creation and filtering of question-code data as well as complementary evalua-
537   tion. We also investigate various alignment algorithms using self-generated instruction/preference
538   data for further improvement. Results show the effectiveness of self-improving LLMs with this
539   proposed paradigm. Future research includes studying post-training on code-only data to enhance the
      computational capabilities of LLMs and improvement of the critic model.

789
      We compare with SOTA code-assisted models trained on augmented MATH and GSM8K datasets
      ToRA and MathCoder. Before detailed comparisons, we first review the background of the use of code
790
      for mathematical reasoning. Code can be used either directly (Chen et al., 2022; Gao et al., 2023b)
791
      (code-only) or interactively (Wang et al., 2023) during problem-solving. The latter approaches such as
792   ToRA and MathCoder jointly solve problems using CoT explanation and code. One advantage of these
793   interactive methods over code-only methods is that the final step of their solution is usually written
794   in CoT, allowing the easy use of existing scripts designed for CoT-style benchmarks for evaluation.
795   However, this does not allow for robust comparisons for unseen diverse-format comparisons. In
796   addition, the role of using tools multiple times to address a single math problem is unclear based
797   on the performance difference of interactive methods (Table 3). For example, ToRA needs 1.02
798   tool interaction rounds per question while MathCoder requires 2.05 for MATH and GSM8K. This
799   work focuses on the direct usage of code as a case study to avoid multi-step inference and leave the
800
      interactive setting for future studies.
801   For ToRA 7B3 and 70B4 models, we use their official inference scripts.5 On MathBench, ToRA needs
802   an average of 1.00 and 1.01 tool interaction rounds per question. It seems its final CoT reasoning
803   primarily focuses on adjusting formatting answers to fully leverage existing CoT evaluation scripts.
804   We use ToRA’s generated code and execution result, keeping the rest of the inputs for the critic model
805   the same. We also experiment with replacing the execution results with the CoT outputs, but this does
806
      not result in significant changes. Our self-improved 8B model outperforms one SOTA code-assisted
      model, ToRA-70B, across all subcategories on this OOD dataset (Table 12).
807
808   For MathCoder, we evaluate its best-performing 34B model6 and 7B model7 , which needs 1.53 and
809   2.13 tool interaction rounds per question, respectively. We also use their released inference scripts8
      and follow the data format.

956   A.7     OTHER D ISCUSSIONS
957
958   Language Diversity of Resources: in this paper, we focus on large-scale question-answer pairs from
959
      educational websites for Chinese, and accordingly, our critic model used for guiding self-improvement
      is trained on Chinese data. While considering resources in other languages such as English could
960
      enhance the the generalizability of LLMs, it would require extensive human efforts for data collection
961
      and cleaning, which is beyond the scope of this work. On the other hand, since the backbone LLMs
962   are pre-trained and aligned on multi-lingual data and our seed data includes English instruction
963   data, the initial policy already exhibits reasonable performance on in-distribution (Section 3.3) and
964   out-of-distribution benchmarks (Section 3.6). Self-improving this initial policy model on Chinese
