009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011
               We investigate the look-ahead capabilities of chess-playing neural networks, specif-
012
               ically focusing on the Leela Chess Zero policy network. We build on the work of
013            Jenner et al. (2024) by analyzing the model’s ability to consider future moves and
014            alternative sequences beyond the immediate next move. Our findings reveal that the
015            network’s look-ahead behavior is highly context-dependent, varying significantly
016            based on the specific chess position. We demonstrate that the model can process
017            information about board states up to seven moves ahead, utilizing similar internal
018            mechanisms across different future time steps. Additionally, we provide evidence
019            that the network considers multiple possible move sequences rather than focus-
020            ing on a single line of play. These results offer new insights into the emergence
021
               of sophisticated look-ahead capabilities in neural networks trained on strategic
               tasks, contributing to our understanding of AI reasoning in complex domains. Our
022
               work also showcases the effectiveness of interpretability techniques in uncovering
023
               cognitive-like processes in artificial intelligence systems.

027   1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      Recent advances in artificial intelligence have produced systems capable of superhuman performance
030   in complex domains like chess and Go (Silver et al., 2018). However, the mechanisms underlying
031   these systems’ decision-making processes remain poorly understood. A key question is whether
032   neural networks trained on such tasks learn to implement sophisticated planning algorithms, or if
033   they rely primarily on pattern matching and heuristics.
034
      This paper builds on recent work by Jenner et al. (2024) that found evidence of learned look-ahead
035
      behavior in a chess-playing neural network. We extend their analysis to examine longer-term planning
036   capabilities and the consideration of alternative moves. Specifically, we investigate whether the
037   network encodes information about future board states and potential move sequences beyond just the
038   next move.
039
      Understanding the internal reasoning processes of these models is important for several reasons.
040
      First, it provides insights into the nature of intelligence that emerges from neural network training,
041
      potentially informing our understanding of both artificial and biological cognition (McGrath et al.,
042   2022). Second, it has practical implications for improving AI systems in strategic domains, as a deeper
043   understanding of their planning mechanisms could lead to more efficient and robust architectures
044   (Czech et al., 2024). Finally, it contributes to the broader field of AI interpretability, which is essential
045   for building trustworthy and controllable AI systems (Chattopadhyay et al., 2019).
046
      In this context, understanding the depth and sophistication of learned look-ahead behavior is particu-
047
      larly relevant. While Jenner et al. (2024) has demonstrated the existence of look-ahead behavior in
048   chess models, understanding how this capability scales to longer sequences is important for several
049   reasons. First, it helps us understand the limits of learned look-ahead behavior - whether models
050   can truly chain together long sequences of moves or if they rely primarily on short-term patterns.
051   Second, analyzing how the model processes moves at different time horizons can reveal whether it
052   uses similar or different mechanisms for near-term versus long-term planning. Finally, understanding
053   these capabilities in chess provides insights that may generalize to other domains where long-term
      planning is essential, such as robotics or strategic decision-making.

054
      Recent work in mechanistic interpretability has made significant strides in understanding the internal
055   workings of language models (Geva et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023) and game-playing models (Li
056   et al., 2023a; Nanda et al., 2023). However, most of these studies have focused on relatively simple
057   tasks or isolated components of larger systems. Our work aims to bridge this gap by analyzing
058   sophisticated planning behavior in a state-of-the-art chess engine.
059
      Chess provides an ideal testbed for this investigation due to its well-defined rules, clear strategic
060
      elements, and the availability of strong neural network-based models (Ruoss et al., 2024). Unlike
061   language models, where the notion of "correct" behavior is often ambiguous, chess allows for
062   precise evaluation of model performance and decision-making. Additionally, the game’s complexity
063   necessitates long-term planning and consideration of multiple possible futures, making it a rich
064   domain for studying advanced cognitive processes in AI systems.
065
      Our key contributions are:
066
067          • Demonstrating that the model’s look-ahead behavior is highly dependent on the specific
068            type of chess position, with different piece capture and checkmate scenarios being stored
069            differently in the residual stream, and processed differently by the multiple attention heads;
070
             • Extending the analysis of Jenner et al. (2024) of look-ahead behavior to the 5th and 7th
071
               future moves in chess positions. Specific attention heads seem strongly responsive to longer
072            term future moves, and the model appears to process some future moves using similar
073            concrete internal mechanisms;
074
             • Showing that the model considers multiple move sequences, not just a single line of play.
075
               Moreover, corrupting the board squares relevant to the alternative moves often improves
076            the model’s prediction accuracy in choosing the optimal move, as expected for look-ahead
077            behavior.
078
079   These findings provide new insights into the look-ahead capabilities that can emerge in neural
080   networks trained on strategic planning tasks. They also demonstrate how interpretability techniques
081   can uncover sophisticated cognitive processes in AI systems. Our work contributes to the growing
082   body of research on AI planning and reasoning (Chen et al., 2021; Hao et al., 2023; Ivanitskiy et al.,
083   2023; Garriga-Alonso et al., 2024), offering a detailed look at how these capabilities manifest in a
084
      complex, real-world domain.
085   To obtain these results, we construct novel approaches to analyze the model’s look-ahead behavior,
086   extending the techniques used in Jenner et al. (2024). We introduce a puzzle set notation that
087   disentangles the model’s behavior for different types of chess positions, and enables a clearer analysis
088   of the model’s look-ahead behavior for higher move counts. We use activation patching to measure
089   the causal importance of different board squares in the model’s decision-making process, probing
090
      to test the prediction accuracy of the model’s future moves, and ablation to identify the attention
      heads that are responsible for the model’s look-ahead behavior. By showcasing how these techniques
091
      can be used in a complementary manner, we expect their usefulness to extend to future mechanistic
092
      interpretability studies of other models with potential look-ahead or planning capabilities.
093
094   We also adapt the board corruption technique used in Jenner et al. (2024) to work for multiple move
095   sequences, and apply it to analyze the model’s consideration of alternative moves. This analysis
096
      should be suitable for future studies of planning behavior in other domains, by making it easier to
      produce contrastive pairs for activation patching, thereby enabling a more fine-grained analysis of the
097
      model’s behavior for different look-ahead strategies.

      2.2   DATASET
141
142   We use Lichess’ 4 million puzzle database as a starting point. Each puzzle in our dataset has a starting
143   state with a single winning move for the player whose turn it is, along with an annotated principal
144   variation (the optimal sequence of moves for both players from the starting state).
145   In our analysis, we refer to moves in the principal variation as follows: The 1st move is the initial
146   move made by the player in the starting position. The 2nd move is the opponent’s response to the 1st
147   move. The 3rd move is the player’s follow-up move after the opponent’s response. We extend this
148   notation to refer to subsequent moves (e.g., 5th move, 7th move) when analyzing longer sequences.
149
      The puzzles were curated into three datasets: a 22k puzzle dataset used in Jenner et al. (2024), solvable
150
      for the Leela model but difficult for weaker models to solve, and used for the 3 and 5-move analysis; a
151   2.2k dataset of 7-move puzzles; and 609 puzzles for the alternative move analysis. Additional details
152   on the dataset generation can be found in Appendix H.

      2.3   A NALYSIS T ECHNIQUES
155
156   We employ three main techniques to analyze the internal representations of the model:
157
158   Activation Patching. This technique, also known as causal tracing (Meng et al., 2023), allows us
159   to measure the causal importance of specific model components. For a given board state and model
160   component (such as a particular square in a particular layer), we replace the clean activations of
161   that component with those from a different “corrupted” board state. If this intervention significantly
      changes the model’s output, it suggests that the patched component contained necessary information


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      about the clean state that differed in the corrupted state. In our chess setup, we employ the approach
163   of Jenner et al. (2024), where the corrupted board state is a minimally modified version of the original
164   board state, where the optimal next move is different but still non-trivial (see Appendix H for more
165   details). Patching then consists of replacing the clean activations (from a particular layer or attention
166   head) associated with a particular board square by their corrupted counterparts, generated using the
167   corrupted board as input to the model.
168
169   Probing. We use linear probes to decode information from the model’s internal representations. A
170   probe is a small classifier trained to predict certain information (e.g., the position of a piece or a future
171   move) from the model’s hidden states. High probe accuracy suggests that the probed information is
172   explicitly encoded in the model’s representations. In our setup, we use probes to test the prediction
173
      accuracy for the puzzles’ future moves, based on the model’s internal states when given the current
      board state as input (see Fig. 3).
174
175
      Ablation. We employ zero ablation, particularly when analyzing attention heads. In this technique,
176
      we selectively set certain weights or activations to zero, effectively removing their contribution to the
177
      model’s output. By comparing the model’s performance before and after ablation, we can assess the
178   importance of specific components (such as individual attention heads or attention patterns) to the
179   model’s decision-making process. This method is particularly useful for identifying key mechanisms
180   involved in look-ahead behavior. In our setup, we apply zero ablation to individual weights in
181   specific attention heads, in order to determine which board squares certain attention heads are mainly
182   attending to.
183
      These techniques allow us to investigate how the model represents and processes information about
184   current and future board states, providing complementary insights into its look-ahead capabilities.
185   While activation patching reveals what information is causally necessary for the model’s decisions,
186   probing can identify information that is encoded but not necessarily used for the final move choice.
187   For example, our probing results show that the model encodes information about opponent moves,
188   even when patching does not provide conclusive causal evidence. Similarly, while patching provides
189   a broad causal view applied across the entire model, ablation provides a fine-grained view of which
190   board squares the model’s attention heads are attending to, giving us a better qualitative understanding
191   of the model’s behavior.

193   2.4   P UZZLE SET NOTATION
194
195
      In (Jenner et al., 2024), it was observed that the Leela model internally treats cases where the player’s
      moved piece is immediately captured by the opponent differently from cases where the opponent
196
      piece moves to an unrelated square (see Fig. 1). When considering more complex future move
197
      sequences, the increasing number of different scenarios treated distinctly by the model makes its
198   analysis challenging. To combat this problem, and disentangle the model’s behavior for different
199   cases, we introduce a new labelling approach for each chess puzzle that we analyze.
200
201
      We start by separating the data into different puzzle sets depending on the similarity between the
      board squares involved. In particular, for each player and opponent move, we label the move based
202
      on the square the piece moves to. For the analysis, we do not consider the squares the pieces start in,
203
      as we verify that this additional complexity does not play a significant role in the model’s internal
204   behavior (see Appendix A), as previously observed in Jenner et al. (2024).
205
206   Since there are far more combinations when considering a larger number of moves, we use the
207
      notation s1 s2 · · · sn to refer to a sequence of squares, where si = sj iff the i-th and j-th move squares
      are the same. Since we are considering up to 7 moves, all si are one single digit. We start with s1 = 1
208
      and raise the digit used whenever a new square is different from the ones in previous moves. As
209
      shorthand, we may use uppercase letters to represent arbitrary digits. Starting alphabet letters (like A,
210   B, and C) are used to represent distinct digits, while ending alphabet letters (like X, Y, and Z) are used
211   to represent any digit combination. For instance, while the notation 111XY = {11111, 11112, 11122,
212   11123} would represent any puzzle set starting with 111, the notation 111AB = {11112, 11123}
213   represents the 2 puzzle sets starting with 111 where the final two squares are distinct. The set {11111,
214   11122} could be represented by both approaches, using either 111AA or 111XX. Additionally, we
215   occasionally may prefix the sequence with the letter M (resp. N), to denote the subset of puzzles
      where the optimal move sequence results (resp. does not result) in a checkmate.

      3              R ESULTS
257
258
      In this section, we verify that the Leela chess model looks ahead into the fifth and seventh moves
259
      when solving chess puzzles, and later shown evidence that the model is able to consider multiple
260   future branches when choosing the best move to play.
261
262
      The starting move squares do not play a significant direct role. While the starting move squares
263
      (i.e. the squares the pieces start in before they are moved) are generally critical for assessing the right
264   next moves for each player, the results obtained in our analysis are consistent with the conclusion in
265   Jenner et al. (2024) that the starting move squares do not seem to play a significant direct role in the
266   look-ahead behavior of the model. Instead, the model seems to process the board state by directly
267   encoding the moves of interest in the associated squares the pieces move to. Consequently, the model
268   responds strongly to corruptions on the destination squares of moves, while showing negligible effects
269   for the starting squares (see results in Appendix A). Therefore, we only focus our analysis on the
      squares the pieces move to during each move, and ignore the squares the pieces start in.

351
352   We note from Fig. 4 (and Figs. 24 to 26 in Appendix E) that some heads matter a lot for the fifth and
353   seventh move analysis.
354
355   L12H12 is also important for 5th and 7th moves. In Jenner et al. (2024), it was shown that
356   attention head L12H12 moves information “backward in time” from the third to the first move square,
357   for some 3-move puzzles.
358   In Fig. 5, we show that L12H12 is also important for the 5th and 7th moves. The results are shown in
359   Figs. 16 to 18 in Appendix D. We note that, for puzzles with five moves, L12H12 may be responsible
360   not only for moving information backward in time from the third to the first move square, but also
361   from the fifth move square.
362   We hypothesize that the attention head moves information backward in time from square C to A (or
363   the 1st move square) when the puzzle set has the form (· · · )AAC(· · · ), and to a lesser extent when it
364   has the form (· · · )ABC(· · · ), while not responding to the form (· · · )ACC(· · · ). When the set matches
365   the pattern at multiple turns, the later turn often takes precedence (for instance, we would expect
366   11223, which matches both AAC(· · · ) and (· · · )AAC, to mainly move information from the 5th, and

464   Mechanistic Interpretability: Recent works have studied mechanistic interpretability in various
465   contexts, including factual associations, in-context learning, and arithmetic reasoning tasks (Geva
466   et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023). Key techniques, used in our work, include activation patching (Vig
467   et al., 2020; Meng et al., 2023; Geiger et al., 2021; Li et al., 2023b), probing (Hewitt & Liang, 2019;
468   Gurnee et al., 2023; Dai et al., 2022), and ablation (McGrath et al., 2023). Researchers have applied
469   these techniques to game-playing models, including Othello (Li et al., 2023a; Nanda et al., 2023),
470   chess (Karvonen, 2024), and Blocksworld (Wang et al., 2024). Our work extends these approaches
471   to understand look-ahead planning in chess, providing a comprehensive view of how chess-playing
472
      transformers represent and manipulate information about future game states.
473
474   Chess-playing neural networks: Following the success of AlphaZero (Silver et al., 2018), there
475
      has been increased interest in neural network approaches to chess. Recent work has explored more
      efficient architectures (Czech et al., 2024) and training procedures (Ruoss et al., 2024) for chess
476
      networks, and studied their representations (Karvonen, 2024). Our study focuses on understanding
477
      the reasoning capabilities of these models, particularly in how they process and utilize information
478   about future game states.
479
480   Look-ahead and planning: Several recent papers have investigated whether neural models encode
481   information about future states. In the language domain, studies have examined if models can
482   anticipate upcoming tokens (Pal et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2024). In the chess domain, Jenner et al.
483   (2024) provided evidence of learned look-ahead in a policy network. Our work extends this analysis
484   to longer-term planning and alternative move considerations. There is ongoing research into the
485   planning and reasoning capabilities of large models (Men et al., 2024; Yao et al., 2024; Hao et al.,
      2023; Ivanitskiy et al., 2023; Garriga-Alonso et al., 2024). Some work has found evidence of multi-

486
      step planning (Chen et al., 2021), while other studies suggest limitations in systematic reasoning. Our
487   work in chess provides a controlled environment to study these questions, offering insights that may
488   be applicable to language models and other domains.

      5    C ONCLUSION
493
494   In this study, we have explored the look-ahead behavior of the Leela chess model when solving chess
495   puzzles, with a particular focus on understanding how the model processes and utilizes information
496   about future moves.
497   First, we demonstrate that the model can process information about board states up to seven moves
498   ahead, though this capability becomes progressively weaker for more distant moves. The model’s
499   look-ahead behavior is highly context-dependent, varying significantly based on the specific puzzle
500   set and whether the sequence leads to checkmate.
501
      Second, we find evidence that the model processes some future moves using similar concrete internal
502
      mechanisms, particularly through specialized attention heads like L12H12. These mechanisms appear
503   to be pattern-sensitive rather than timing-specific, suggesting the model has learned some general
504   strategies for processing look-ahead information rather than just heuristic rules.
505
506
      Third, our analysis reveals that the model considers multiple move sequences simultaneously, with
      different attention heads specializing in processing different types of positions. For instance, L12H12
507
      shows stronger responses in checkmate scenarios, while L12H17 is more active in non-checkmate
508
      positions. This specialization suggests the model has learned to handle different tactical situations
509   using distinct mechanisms.
510
511   Our methodological approach, combining activation patching, probing, and ablation techniques,
512
      provides complementary insights into the model’s behavior. While patching reveals causally necessary
      information, probing shows that the model encodes additional information (such as opponent moves)
513
      that may be used more subtly. This multi-faceted analysis approach could prove valuable for future
514
      studies of planning behavior in other domains.
515
516   These findings have broader implications for our understanding of how neural networks can develop
517   sophisticated planning capabilities through training. The emergence of specialized components and
518
      general pattern-matching mechanisms, without explicit programming, suggests potential approaches
      for developing AI systems capable of strategic planning in other domains.
519
520   Future work could explore how these look-ahead capabilities generalize to other chess positions not
521   present in the training data, or to modified versions of chess with slightly different rules. Additionally,
522   investigating whether similar mechanisms emerge in neural networks trained on other strategic games
523   or real-world planning tasks could provide valuable insights into the generality of these findings.
524
525
526
      Broader Impacts: Understanding how models develop look-ahead capabilities and handle complex
      decision trees could inform the development of AI systems for other strategic tasks, and may help
527
      improve our understanding of how these capabilities may generalize, or fail to do so, in novel
528
      scenarios.

      Our probing analysis provides additional insights into the model’s ability to encode and utilize
921   information about future moves. We conducted probing experiments on various puzzle sets to
922   complement our activation patching results and gain a more comprehensive understanding of the
923   model’s internal representations.
924
      Figure 3 presents the probing results for the puzzle set 1123456. The probe’s accuracy shows a clear
925
      decreasing trend as the move square becomes increasingly distant from the present state. This decline
926
      in accuracy is particularly pronounced for the 7th move square, suggesting that while the model does
927   encode some information about very distant future moves, this information becomes increasingly
928   uncertain or difficult to extract.
929
930
      Figure 15 shows the probing results for the puzzle set 12345, offering insights into how the model
      encodes information about both player and opponent moves. Several key observations can be made:
931
932          • The probe can find both player and opponent move squares with high accuracy, generally
933            peaking at layer 13. This suggests that the model encodes information about opponent
934            moves, even though activation patching does not show a strong direct response for these
935            squares.
936          • The probe’s accuracy decreases as the predicted move becomes more distant from the present
937            state, consistent with our observations from activation patching.
938
             • Interestingly, the 4th move (an opponent move) seems more difficult to predict than the
939
               player’s 5th move. This could indicate that the model’s representation of opponent moves is
940            less direct or more uncertain than its representation of the player’s own future moves.
941
             • The probe’s accuracy for the random chess model is notably higher for the first and sec-
942
               ond move squares, possibly reflecting some inherent biases or common patterns in chess
943
               openings.
944
945   These probing results complement our activation patching findings by revealing that the model does
946   encode information about future moves, including opponent moves, even when this information
947   does not have a strong direct effect on the model’s output. This suggests that the model’s internal
948   representations are rich and multifaceted, capturing various aspects of potential future game states.
949   The discrepancy between probing and activation patching results, particularly for opponent moves,
950   highlights the complexity of the model’s decision-making process. It suggests that while information
951   about opponent moves is present in the model’s representations, it may be utilized in more subtle or
952   indirect ways than information about the player’s own moves.
953
      These findings underscore the importance of using multiple analysis techniques to gain a comprehen-
954
      sive understanding of the model’s internal workings and decision-making processes.

1042
1043   The L12H12 head behavior (Figs. 16 to 18) is consistent with the following observations:
1044
1045

1046

1047
1048

1049

1050
                                       extent for 11234XY.
1051
1052
                                     • The head moves information from the 5th to the 3rd move square for the puzzle sets 11223
                                       and 12223 (pattern (· · · )AAC).
1053
1054                                 • The head moves information from the 7th to the 1st move square for the puzzle sets 1123334,
1055

1056                                 • The head does not directly move information from the 3rd move square for puzzle sets
1057                                   122 or 122XY. It also does not move information from the 5th move square for puzzle sets
1058                                   (· · · )ACC (such as 11222, 12344).
1059   Nonetheless, the hypothesis is not compatible with the set 12334, since we would expect to observe
1060   behavior in between sets 12223 and 11223, and to mainly move information from the 5th to the 3rd
1061   or 1st move square, instead of from the 3rd to the 1st move square. We would also expect some effect
1062   from the 3rd to 1st move square for 12344. For 7-move puzzles, we observe no 3rd to 1st effect for
1063   123VWXY sets.

1065   residual stream patching analysis, where we observed that the model tends to move information
1066   from future move squares to earlier move squares. Specifically, this head seems to prioritize moving
1067   information from the 3rd to the 1st move square for patterns like AAB and ABC, from the 5th to the
1068   1st or 3rd move square for patterns like AAC, and from the 7th to the 1st move square for patterns
1069   like AAC and ABC.

1073                                   form AABCD, where C is different from D, and D is preferably equal to A. In sets of the
1074                                   form AABCA, the model relies more heavily on L12H17 than on L12H12.
1075                                 • L13H3 (Fig. 20) seems to move information "backward in time" for puzzle sets of the form
1076                                   AABCD, where either C=D or B=C.

1355   This section presents a comprehensive analysis of attention head patching results for various puzzle
1356   sets, providing insights into how different attention heads contribute to the model’s decision-making
1357   process.
1358
       For 5-move puzzles (Figures Fig. 24 and Fig. 25), we observe distinct patterns:
1359
1360
1361
1362          • Strong response to L12H12 and L13H3: Some puzzle sets (e.g., 11223, 11233, 11234) show
1363            a strong response to patching these heads, suggesting their crucial role in processing these
1364            positions.
1365
1366
1367
1368
1369
1370
              • Mixed responses: Puzzle set 11234 also responds strongly to L12H17, indicating a more
                complex interaction of attention heads for this set.
1371
1372
1373
1374
1375
1376          • Strong response to L12H17: Some sets (e.g., 11222, 12223) respond strongly to L12H17
1377            patching but hardly to L12H12, suggesting different mechanisms at play for these positions.
1378
1379
1380
1381
1382
              • Weak or inconsistent responses: Some puzzle sets (e.g., 12233, 12234) do not show strong
1383
                responses to any particular attention head, which may indicate more distributed processing
1384            or the involvement of other model components.
1385
1386
1387
1388
1389
1390          • Response to L11H10 and L11H13: Some sets (e.g., 11111, 11112) show responses to these
1391
                heads, but ablation results suggest their role may be more subtle or indirect.
1392
1393
1394
       For 7-move puzzles (Fig. 26), the patterns become more complex, potentially reflecting the increased
1395
       difficulty in processing longer move sequences.
1396
1397   The analysis of checkmate vs. non-checkmate scenarios (Figures Fig. 27 and Fig. 28) reveals
1398   significant differences in attention head responses between these two types of positions. This suggests
1399   that the model may employ distinct processing strategies for checkmate and non-checkmate positions,
1400
       potentially reflecting the different strategic considerations involved in each case.
1401   These results highlight the context-dependent nature of the model’s attention mechanisms and
1402   the complex interplay between different attention heads in processing chess positions. They also
1403   underscore the importance of considering factors like move sequence length and the presence of
       checkmate possibilities when analyzing the model’s behavior.

1706   F    A LTERNATIVE MOVE SETUP AND ADDITIONAL RESULTS
1707
1708
       This section details our approach to analyzing how the model considers alternative moves, focusing
       on puzzles with two distinct branches of play.
1709
1710   In order to study the alternative move analysis, we have to find puzzles that satisfy a significant
1711   number of constraints:
1712
1713
              • The puzzle’s principal variation (PV) must have length 3. This is in order to simplify the
                analysis, and remove higher future move squares from consideration.
1714
1715          • The puzzle’s PV must not be a checkmate. In practice, we observe that not only does the
1716            model treat different puzzle sets differently, but it also seems to have a different behavior
1717
                when a checkmate in 2 is a likely option, even if not the most likely.
1718          • The puzzle must have two distinct branches:
1719              – The model must be ambivalent between two first moves. Each move should have a
1720                 probability of around 1/2. In practice, we impose a lower bound of p = 0.3 for the
1721                 two moves.
1722              – Given a first move, the model must be confident in the second move. In practice,
1723                 we impose a lower bound of p = 0.7 for the second move.
1724              – Given the first two moves, the model must be confident in the third move. In
1725                 practice, we impose a lower bound of p = 0.7 for the third move.
1726              – One of the branches must correspond to the PV. Otherwise, the model cannot be said
1727                 to be close to solving the puzzle, and it would be unclear to what extent the model’s
                     attention is due to the alternative move setup.


                                                                32
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1728
                The latter two conditions are mainly to ensure that the model’s attention is not too spread
1729            out over relatively unlikely future moves. Essentially, we are interested in puzzles like the
1730            bottom example in Fig. 29 (but without the checkmate scenario).
1731
              • The two first and third move squares must all be distinct. Otherwise, it would be
1732
                impossible to distinguish the effects of the 4 squares in the analysis.
1733
1734
              • The puzzles should still be hard for the weaker model to solve. The hardness threshold is
                maintained at 0.05, as in Jenner et al. (2024).
1735
1736          • The weaker model should be confident in the second move. The forcing threshold is
1737            maintained at 0.7, as in Jenner et al. (2024).
1738          • The corrupted puzzle versions should be viable for both branches. Previously, we found
1739            the corrupted puzzles using only constraints with the PV moves. Here, we also require that
1740            the corrupted puzzles are viable for both branches. Otherwise, the corrupted puzzle may
1741            treat the branches differently, and lead to unclear results.
1742
       These constraints are highlighted in Fig. 29. Regrettably, starting with the whole Lichess’ puzzle
1743
       dataset, these constraints reduce the original 4062423 puzzles to around 600 puzzles. In practice,
1744   we observe that about half to two thirds of the puzzles have differences between the probabilities
1745   assigned to the two branches’ first moves that are non-negligible, and that may explain some of the
1746   limited log odds reductions observed in Figs. 30 and 31.
1747

       H    I MPLEMENTATION DETAILS
2053
2054
       Our implementation is heavily based on the implementation described in Jenner et al. (2024), and pre-
2055   viously made available at https://github.com/HumanCompatibleAI/leela-interp.
2056   For the activation patching, probing, and zero ablation results, modifications were made to account
2057   for the case of more than 3 moves. For the purposes of reproducing the results, the code may be
2058   found in [link omitted for review].
2059
2060   Analysis Techniques. Our analysis builds on techniques from Jenner et al. (2024), which we detail
2061   here for completeness. For activation patching, we first run the model on the original position to
2062   get the “clean” activations. We then create a corrupted position by replacing specific moves in the
2063   game history and run the model on this corrupted position. Next, we copy activations from specific
2064   attention heads in the corrupted run into the corresponding locations in the clean run. Let mc be the
2065
       correct move, sp be the patched model state, and sc be the clean model state. The log odds change
       ∆L of the target move is then defined as:
2066
2067
2068                               ∆L = log odds(mc | sp ) − log odds(mc | sc )                               (1)
2069
2070
       where log odds(mc | s) represents the logarithm of the odds that the model assigns to the correct
       move mc given state s. A negative ∆L indicates that patching reduces the model’s preference for the
2071
       correct move, while a positive ∆L indicates that patching increases it.
2072
2073   For linear probing, we extract activations from each attention head when running the model on chess
2074   positions. We then train a bilinear probe to predict the board square associated with the move of
2075   interest. The probe accuracy serves as a measure of what information is encoded by the model. The
2076
       trained probe’s accuracy is also compared against a random baseline.
2077
       Puzzle generation. Besides the dataset used by Jenner et al. (2024), we create two additional
2078
       datasets. The 7-move dataset is created by starting from the 4 million puzzle dataset by filtering for
2079
       puzzles with exactly 7 moves, and where the 7th move square is distinct from the other odd move
2080   squares. Additionally, as for the first dataset, we filter for puzzles that are solvable by the Leela model
2081   but not a weaker model. The generation of the alternative move dataset is described in Appendix F.
2082
2083   Generating corrupted puzzles. For the bulk of the puzzles, we rely on the implementation from
2084   Jenner et al. (2024). For the alternative move dataset, we ensure that the corrupted puzzles are viable
2085   for both branches, by applying the constraints described in Appendix D of Jenner et al. (2024) to
2086   both branches.
2087
2088   Data Filtering. To ensure reliable results, we apply several filtering criteria to the positions. For
2089   the alternative move analysis, we require the probability of each of the two first moves to be at least
2090   0.3, and the probability of the second and third moves to be at least 0.7. Additionally, as in Jenner
2091   et al. (2024), we maintain the hardness threshold of 0.05 and forcing threshold of 0.7.
