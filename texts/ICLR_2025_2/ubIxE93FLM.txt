009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               Despite significant advancements, current large multimodal models (LMMs) strug-
012
               gle to bridge the gap between low-level visual perception—focusing on shapes,
013            sizes and layouts—and high-level language reasoning involving semantics, events
014            and logic. This limitation becomes evident in tasks requiring precise visual per-
015            ception, such as comparing geometric properties or solving visual algorithmic
016            reasoning problems. To study this failure mode, we focus on an important visual
017            domain: vector graphics—images composed purely of 2D objects and shapes,
018            which are prevalent in various LMM-based agent tasks in web, visual design, and
019            OS environments. We identify two key research questions: how can we enable
020            precise visual perception, and how can we facilitate high-level reasoning based
021
               on such low-level perceptions? To accurately capture low-level visual details, we
               utilize Scalable Vector Graphics (SVG) for precise encoding of visual scenes. How-
022
               ever, SVGs are not readily interpretable by LLMs or LMMs in a zero-shot manner.
023
               To address this challenge, we propose the Visually Descriptive Language Model
024            (VDLM), which introduces an intermediate textual representation called Primal
025            Visual Description (PVD). PVD translates SVGs into a text-based abstraction
026            comprising primitive attributes (e.g., shape, position, measurement) along with
027            their corresponding values. PVD can be learned with task-agnostic synthesized data
028            and represents visual primitives that are universal across various vector graphics.
029            This abstraction is more structured, allowing for direct interpretation by founda-
030            tion models for zero-shot generalization to different reasoning tasks. Without
031            any human-annotated data, empirical results demonstrate that VDLM leads to
032
               significant improvements in state-of-the-art LMMs, such as GPT-4o, across various
               low-level multimodal perception and reasoning tasks on vector graphics. Addition-
033
               ally, we provide extensive analyses of VDLM’s performance, showing that our
034
               framework offers improved interpretability due to its disentangled perception and
035            reasoning processes. Finally, we demonstrate the promise of this representation by
036            showing a positive correlation between the quality of the PVD perception and the
037            end-task performance.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
042
043   In recent years, large multimodal models (LMMs) (OpenAI, 2023b; Anil et al., 2023; Liu et al.,
044   2023b; Chen et al., 2023b; Bai et al., 2023) have achieved impressive performance across a broad
045
      spectrum of general vision-language benchmarks (Goyal et al., 2017; Fu et al., 2023; Liu et al.,
      2023d; Yu et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023a). However, these monolithic LMMs still struggle with
046
      seemingly simple tasks that require precise perception of low-level visual details. In particular, we
047
      empirically observe that LMMs frequently exhibit this failure mode in vector graphics, which are
048   images composed purely of 2D objects and shapes, devoid of any camera viewpoint. For example, a
049   state-of-the-art LMM like GPT-4o (OpenAI, 2024) can still fail 43% of the time when comparing the
050   lengths of two line segments, and 54% of the time when solving a simple 2×2 maze. LMMs’ ability
051   to understand vector graphics is largely underexplored compared to natural images but is essential
052   for growing downstream applications, such as LMM-based agents in web, visual design, and OS
053   environments (Zhou et al., 2023b; Liu et al., 2024; Xie et al., 2024; Rawles et al., 2024; Zheng et al.,
      2024; Lù et al., 2024). To address this challenge, we identify two main research questions. First,

      For our initial question, we explore vectorizing a rasterized image using the Scalable Vector Graphics
      (SVG) representation, which describes a scene with paths (e.g., polygons and splines) and their
080
      corresponding measurements and positions. SVG representations, by nature, are unbiased towards
081
      high-level semantics and can capture low-level visual details in text. The vectorization process can be
082   faithfully accomplished with an off-the-shelf, rule-based raster-to-vector algorithm. However, despite
083   being text-based, SVG is insufficient for language reasoning. Our preliminary experiments (§A)
084   demonstrate that existing large language models (LLMs) are unable to interpret machine-generated
085   SVG codes in zero-shot settings. Moreover, finetuning a model to reason about raw SVG codes can
086   be inefficient and infeasible without corresponding task-specific annotations.
087
      To address the challenge posed in our latter question, we propose training a language model to
088   align the extracted SVG paths to an intermediate symbolic representation, which can directly be
089   leveraged by foundation models such as LLMs or LMMs for low-level visual reasoning. We introduce
090   Primal Visual Description (PVD), which bridges the low-level SVG codes and the high-level
091   language space for reasoning about vector graphics. Specifically, we train an LLM-based (Jiang et al.,
092   2023) SVG-to-PVD model, which transforms the raw SVG paths into a set of primitive attributes
093   (e.g., shape, position) with corresponding predicted values (e.g., rectangle, pixel coordinates of
094   the vertices). See Figure 1 in the blue box for an example. Notably, the PVD representation
095   contains primitive attributes that are universal across vector graphics, and thus can be learned with
096   procedurally generated ⟨SVG, PVD⟩ pairs without task-specific annotations. Since PVD is more
097   structured and closer to natural language, it allows for direct interpretation by pretrained foundation
098
      models.
099   Comprising SVG-based image perception and primitive-level abstractions, we present our method,
100   the Visually Descriptive Language Model (VDLM). VDLM has three components: a rule-based
101   visual encoder that converts images to SVG to capture precise visual features, a learned language
102   model that translates SVG to PVD, and an inference-only LLM or LMM reasoner that conducts
103   zero-shot reasoning about downstream tasks with the PVD representation. For VDLM with LMM
104
      reasoners, we keep the original visual features of the input image and add the PVD representation
      seamlessly into the text prompt as additional visual descriptions. An overview of VDLM is provided
105
      in Figure 1.
106

      the reasoner, can already achieve strong zero-shot performance in various visual reasoning tasks, out-

148
      of LMMs when reasoning about tasks that require precise, low-level perception in vector graphics.

      Second, we introduce VDLM, a visual reasoning framework that operates with intermediate text-

156   2      VDLM F RAMEWORK
157
158   We present the VDLM framework, which comprises three components. First, a rule-based perception
159   module transforms images into SVG format, accurately capturing low-level visual details (§ 2.1).
160   Second, a trained language model aligns SVGs with intermediate visual descriptions by mapping
161   SVG paths to primitive shapes (§ 2.2). Third, an inference-only LLM or LMM reasons about the
      downstream tasks with the text-based perception results (§ 2.3). See Figure 2 for an overview.

175   2.1   E NCODING I MAGES INTO SVG WITH RULE - BASED A LGORITHMS
176
177   Prior work (Krojer et al., 2022; Tong et al., 2024) has demonstrated that, although CLIP-based (Rad-
178   ford et al., 2021) vision encoders are effective at capturing high-level visual semantics, they can
179   fall short in preserving fine-grained visual details. As an alternative, we propose extracting an SVG
180   representation that more accurately captures the detailed measurements. Unlike raster graphics,
181   such as JPEG or PNG images, which represent images through a grid of pixels, SVG describes
182
      shapes, lines, and colors using mathematical expressions and paths with precise coordinates. We
      posit that these distinctions allow SVG representations to more faithfully describe visual scenes in
183
      vector graphics.
184
185   To empirically verify this, we conduct a suite of preliminary experiments (§ A) investigating the
186   potential of using SVG for representing visual inputs. We find that on vector graphics tasks, fine-
187   tuning the LLM backbone, Vicuna (Chiang et al., 2023), of an LLaVA-1.5 (Liu et al., 2023a), with
188
      SVG representations consistently outperforms fine-tuning the entire LLaVA model with CLIP-based
      features. Importantly, we can leverage a rule-based raster-to-SVG parsing algorithm (VTracer) for
189
      converting any image into SVG without learning. This enables us to obtain an unbiased initial
190
      description of the visual input. However, we observe two key challenges (§ A.3) when working with
191   raw SVG representation. First, off-the-shelf LLMs, e.g., GPT-4 (OpenAI, 2023a), have a limited
192   zero-shot reasoning ability on SVG representation. Even with fine-tuning, training an LLM to directly
193   understand raw SVG code can still be challenging. Second, fine-tuning on task-specific ⟨SVG,
194   question, answer⟩ pairs limits generalization to unseen tasks and domains. We discuss our approach
195   of extracting intermediate representations below.
196
197   2.2   L EARNING A LIGNMENT OF SVG TO PVD WITH L ANGUAGE M ODELS
198
199   Primal Visual Description (PVD). We propose Primal Visual Description, a higher-level abstrac-
200   tion that transforms low-level SVG paths to more structured primitives required for reasoning. PVD
201   is a text-based visual description that consists of a set of primitive geometry objects, e.g., circles and
202   line segments. Each PVD element contains the primitives’ attributes (e.g., color, shape, position, size)
203   with corresponding predicted values (e.g., blue, circle, pixel coordinates of the center, length of the
204
      radius). An example of the PVD representation is as follows (See Figure 13 for full definitions):
205
206          {"type":        "circle", "center": [252, 315], "radius":                                202,
207
             "color":        [175, 155, 98], "style": "filled shape"}
208
209   Notably, the PVD is a higher level of abstraction that can be extracted from SVG, from which we
210   can directly leverage the strong reasoning abilities of an off-the-shelf LLM or LMM to generalize
211   across various downstream tasks. Moreover, the PVD is general enough to serve as a unified visual
212
      description across different types of vector graphics, as most complex concepts can be composed of
      multiple primitive shapes. For example, a “cross” can be composed of two “rectangles.”
213
214   As shown in Figure 3, the ontology of the Primal Visual Description contains 9 primitive shape types
215   that can be composed to cover diverse vector graphics in the wild. The primitive shapes include
      circles, ellipses, rectangles, triangles, polygons, line segments, grids, paths, and graphs. A path

      in PVD is defined as a non-intersecting polyline. Graphs and grids are defined as a set of vertices
217   connected by a set of edges.

219   Learning alignment with a language model. We then train a language model to generate PVD
220   outputs from SVG inputs. The input is a single SVG path depicting a visual concept, and the output
221   is the predicted one or more primitives in the defined PVD ontology. During inference, given an
222
      arbitrary raster image, we first convert it into a raw SVG file, which may contain a large number
      of SVG paths, including unimportant noise and speckles. To denoise the raw SVG file and extract
223
      salient shapes, we propose an incremental decomposition algorithm. Specifically, we incrementally
224
      include SVG paths while checking the difference between the partially rendered image of currently
225   chosen paths and the fully rendered image of the original raw SVG file. We compute the summation
226   of the absolute pixel-by-pixel difference between the two images and set an empirical threshold.
227   If the difference after adding a new path is below this threshold, i.e., if the added path does not
228   bring much additional visual information to the scene, we will skip that path. For the ordering of
229   the path selection, we follow the default ordering from VTracer that heuristically places the paths
230   with a larger area at the front. The paths that come afterward will be stacked on top of previous
231   paths during rendering. Upon obtaining the decomposed single SVG paths, we first generate their
232   PVD representation individually. We then aggregate the individual PVD predictions into a holistic
233
      perception of the entire image using this JSON template: ["object 0": <PVD output for
      path 0>, "object 1": <PVD output for path 1>, ...].
234
235   Importantly, since PVD is task-agnostic, the data for training the SVG-to-PVD model can be procedu-
236   rally generated without human annotation. We develop a data generator leveraging PIL.ImageDraw*
237   and VTracer, which creates a large-scale ⟨SVG, PVD⟩ paired dataset containing randomly generated
238   primitives. In some real-world tasks, such as geometry problems, multiple primitive shapes with the
239
      same color can overlap. When converted to SVG, these shapes tend to be parsed into one merged
      SVG path. To enable the SVG-to-PVD model to learn to decode individual primitives from such
240
      compositional concepts, we additionally generate data instances with randomly overlapped shapes.
241
      The target PVD representation, in this context, is a list of primitive PVD JSON objects. We ensure
242   that each generated image contains only one unicolor object, single or composed, so that the con-
243   verted SVG contains a single SVG path. This facilitates a language model in effectively learning the
244   alignment between SVG and PVD.
245
      To improve the robustness to unseen inference images, we randomize the image sizes, the positions
246
      and rotations of the shapes, as well as the styles of the shapes (filled or outlined). We additionally use
247
      two data augmentation methods, Gaussian Blur and Pixel Noise, to add variance to the training SVG
248   paths. Our final dataset contains 160K ⟨SVG, PVD⟩ pairs. More details can be found in Appendix C.
249
250   We fine-tune a pretrained Mistral-7b (Jiang et al., 2023)† model on the synthesized PVD 160K dataset
251
      to perform SVG-to-PVD generation. We conduct full-parameter fine-tuning for 3 epochs with a
      learning rate of 1e-5. The training objective is a standard Language Modeling loss on the generated
252
      PVD tokens as follows:

      3.1      TASKS
301
302   Low-level visual reasoning tasks. Evaluating LMMs in tasks that require precise visual perception
303
      about vector graphics is a highly underexplored research area and has limited existing resources. To
      this end, we construct a new evaluation benchmark that comprises 9 tasks which cover important
304
      aspects of low-level visual perception and reasoning, including measurements, spatial relations,
305
      counting, logical reasoning, and complex reasoning problems such as maze solving. The description
306   of each task is as follows: (1) Angle Classification: Identify whether an angle is acute or obtuse. (2)
307   Length Comparison: Determine whether two line segments are of equal length. (3-4) Shapeworld
308   Spatial Reasoning: The Shapeworld (Kuhnle & Copestake, 2017) dataset on spatial relations
309   with images containing exactly two objects or multiple objects. (5) Shapeworld Superlative:
310   The Shapeworld dataset on superlative statements. (6) NLVR: The Natural Language for Visual
311   Reasoning dataset (Suhr et al., 2017) which contains diverse counting, spatial reasoning, and logical
312   reasoning queries. (7) Geoclidean 2-shot Learning: A repurposed Geoclidean (Hsu et al., 2022)
313   dataset requiring the model to understand a compositional geometric concept with only two reference
314
      examples. (8-9) Maze Solving: Solve a 2×2 or 3×3 maze, given the starting and ending positions.
      Among these tasks, Angle Classification, Length Comparison, and Maze Solving are newly created
315
      from scratch (See Appendix F for more details).
316
317   High-level visual reasoning tasks. Although the focus of this work is on low-level visual reasoning,
318   we additionally include a set of high-level tasks to investigate the impact of VDLM on knowledge

320
      of the primitives. We leverage VGBench (Zou et al., 2024), a benchmark originally proposed for
      evaluating LLMs in understanding and generating vector graphics codes. In this work, we evaluate
321
      LMMs and VDLM-mm for question-answering based on the rasterized VGBench SVG images.

      4.2                    D EEP D IVE INTO FAILURE M ODES : A T RANSPARENT E RROR A NALYSIS
467
      The improved interpretability, resulting from PVD’s disentangled perception and reasoning, allows
468   us to conduct an in-depth analysis of the failure modes of VDLM. We examine the errors made
469   by VDLM-txt in low-level reasoning tasks, where the PVD representation is the only perception
470   accessible to the LLM reasoner. We find that both the perception step (SVG-to-PVD) and the
471   reasoning step (PVD-to-answer) can contribute to errors. On tasks that require complex multistep
472   reasoning, such as Maze Solving, reasoning errors become more prevalent; otherwise, perception
473   errors most directly contribute to poor performance. Details and illustrative examples of these errors
474   are provided in Appendix B, along with a distribution of perception and reasoning errors from
475   human analyses. The prevalent error types for both perception and reasoning steps are summarized
476
      as follows.
477   Common perception errors include failures in faithfully perceiving novel shapes that are not covered
478   by or cannot be composed within the PVD ontology; failures in capturing intentional constraints
479   between primitives, such as a line exactly segmenting a circle, due to the random nature of the data
480   generation on the positioning of objects; and failures in capturing very small objects, due to the
481   heuristic thresholding in the incremental SVG decomposition algorithm. In Table 5, we show that the
482
      proposed augmentation during synthetic data generation improves PVD perception. However, we
      see that there is still a large room for improvement, by defining a more general visually descriptive
483
      representation, diversifying, and scaling up the data generation pipeline. We leave this to future work.
484
485   Common reasoning errors over the PVD perception include failures in discovering intentional
      constraints without being explicitly asked, such as automatically recognizing that a rhombus is not

486
      the same concept as a general quadrilateral; failure in handling ambiguous instructions; and failure in
487   complex multi-step reasoning tasks, such as solving mazes.

489   5    R ELATED W ORK
490
      Visual shortcomings in large multimodal models. While state-of-the-art LMMs achieve strong
491
      performance on existing multimodal benchmarks (Goyal et al., 2017; Fu et al., 2023; Liu et al.,
492
      2023b;d; Yu et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023a), which primarily focus on natural images, recent work (Lu
493   et al., 2023; Yue et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2023a; Hsu et al., 2022; Gao et al., 2023)
494   has shown that they struggle with charts, geometric diagrams, and abstract scenes. This observation
495   aligns with recent studies investigating visual shortcomings in LMMs. Tong et al. (2024) suggests
496   that current LMMs struggle with visual details because the image-text contrastive pretraining of the
497   CLIP visual backbone does not encourage the preservation of fine-grained visual features, such as
498   orientation and quantity. To address this issue, recent studies have either leveraged the mixture-of-
499   experts approach (Tong et al., 2024; Fan et al., 2024; Lu et al., 2024; Jain et al., 2023b), incorporating
500   various types of vision encoders, such as SAM (Kirillov et al., 2023), DINOv2 (Oquab et al., 2023),
501
      or introduced auxiliary losses that emphasize local details during multimodal pretraining McKinzie
      et al. (2024); Bica et al. (2024); Varma et al. (2023). In this work, we propose a novel perspective for
502
      addressing this visual deficiency in vector graphics with an intermediate perception representation.
503
504   Image vectorization and program synthesis. Generating vectorized or symbolic representations of
505   visual concepts has been a topic of interest in both the NLP and computer vision communities. Recent
506   work (Vinker et al., 2022; Lee et al., 2023; Ma et al., 2022; Rodriguez et al., 2023; Jain et al., 2023a;
507
      Tang et al., 2024; Xing et al., 2024; Hu et al., 2024) has investigated generating vector graphics codes
      from raster images or text prompts. In this work, we focus on the reverse problem of understanding
508
      and reasoning about vector graphics as visual inputs. We find that vector graphics reasoning serves
509
      as a challenging testbed to evaluate low-level visual reasoning abilities in large multimodal models
510   (LMMs). Although Bubeck et al. (2023); Cai et al. (2023); Zou et al. (2024); Qiu et al. (2024)
511   have shown initial promise in using large language models (LLMs) to understand the semantics of
512   vector graphics codes, as shown in § A.3, they still struggle with understanding precise low-level
513   details. Therefore, we propose the intermediate Primal Visual Description representation to further
514   enhance low-level perception and reasoning, without sacrificing the performance of semantic under-
515   standing. This work is also heavily inspired by related work in neural-symbolic models (Ritchie et al.,
516   2016; Wu et al., 2017; Yi et al., 2018; Mao et al., 2019; Hsu et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2023; Trinh
517   et al., 2024). This paradigm aims to de-render visual scenes into structured representations, retrieve
518
      programs from input text, and execute these programs on the image representations. Instead of defin-
      ing task-specific symbolic programs, we extend the idea to learning a task-agnostic visual description
519
      that can be directly reasoned about by off-the-shelf foundation models for task generalization.
520
521   Disentangling perception and reasoning in large multimodal models. Another closely related
522   line of work has investigated disentangling visual perception and reasoning with visual program-
523   ming (Gupta & Kembhavi, 2023; Surı́s et al., 2023; Ge et al., 2023; Wu & Xie, 2023) and tool-
524
      using (Wu et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023c). These models leverage the code generation capabilities
      of LLMs to compose and employ a set of vision-language or vision-only models, such as object
525
      detection and image caption models, as subroutines for solving visual reasoning tasks. Despite
526
      promising performance on natural images, as shown in § 3, we find that these models are still limited
527   by the existing vision-language models’ inability to process low-level primitives effectively.
528
529   6    C ONCLUSIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
530
531
      We present VDLM, a novel approach designed to address the limitations of large multimodal models
      in performing precise low-level perception and reasoning tasks in vector graphics. By leveraging SVG
532
      representations and introducing an intermediate symbolic abstraction, VDLM enables precise capture
533
      of low-level visual features as well as direct use of LLMs and LMMs for generalization. VDLM not
534   only outperforms existing state-of-the-art LMMs such as GPT-4o but also enhances interpretability
535   through its disentangled perception and reasoning process. The limitations of this work primarily
536   stem from the capability of the SVG-to-PVD perception module. Although the PVD has already
537   shown significant promise with a limited ontology and a fully synthesized training dataset, it is mainly
538   designed for handling 2D vector graphics with basic primitives. Future directions include building a
539   more general intermediate representation that has a broader coverage and can be extended from 2D
      vector graphics to 3D and natural images.

      A     P RELIMINARY E XPERIMENTS ON SVG R EPRESENTATIONS
931
932
933   We introduce a suite of probing tasks to evaluate current LMMs’ capabilities in performing tasks with
934   vector graphics. The results show that even state-of-the-art LMMs, such as GPT-4V, struggle with
935   tasks that require precise perception of low-level primitives, such as comparing the lengths of two
936   lines. We then investigate where this deficiency originates and propose an alternative representation,
937   Scalable Vector Graphics (SVG), for representing such precise low-level features. We find that,
938   compared to image-based representations, SVG representations can be more efficient for visual
939
      reasoning on vector graphics. However, they are not without their own limitations, which we will
      elaborate on in § A.3.
940
941
942
943   A.1    I MAGE AND SVG R EPRESENTATIONS
944
945   In the probing tasks, we include both discriminative and generative tasks, each with varying levels of
946   emphasis on low-level visual details. Illustrations of the input and output examples are available in
947   Figure 7. We additionally include a non-vector-graphics task, Clevr QA, which consists of realistic
948   3D rendered scenes. This is to test the limits of SVG representations in encoding 3D objects within
949
      realistic images. Detailed statistics of these tasks can be found in Table 6.
950   For each task, we consider two evaluation settings: zero-shot and fine-tuning. We explore two types
951   of representations for the input image: (1) direct use of the image pixels, encoding them into patch
952   embeddings with an image encoder, e.g., CLIP (Radford et al., 2021); (2) conversion of the image
953   into SVG code using a rule-based raster-to-SVG converter (VTracer).
954   For fine-tuning with the image input, we instruction-finetune Llava-v1.5-7b (including the LLM-
955   backbone and the projection layer) using Lora (Hu et al., 2022) on the training set for one epoch. For
956   fine-tuning with the SVG input, we only fine-tune the LLM backbone of Llava-v1.5, Vicuna (Chiang
957   et al., 2023), using Lora for one epoch, with the input image’s SVG code concatenated in the context.
958   The results are shown in Table 3. Key observations include:
959   (1) The SOTA open-source LMM, Llava-v1.5, struggles to achieve non-trivial performance on most
960   probing tasks even with dedicated fine-tuning. On tasks with binary choices, Llava tends to predict
961   homogeneous answers, disregarding differences in the input image.
962
      (2) The SOTA closed-source LMM, GPT-4V, excels on task Line or Angle, which focuses on querying
963
      the high-level semantics of the primitive concept (“what’s in the image”). However, its performance
964
      significantly decreases on tasks requiring more precise low-level perception, e.g., Angle Classification
965   and Length Comparison.
966
967   (3) Fine-tuning the LLM backbone, Vicuna, with SVG inputs consistently outperforms fine-tuning
968
      the entire Llava model with image inputs. This highlights the potential of using SVG as an alternative
      representation in vector graphics.
969
970   (4) We note that SVG may inherently be inefficient in representing rendered 3D scenes and realistic
971   images due to factors like camera perspectives, lighting, and shadows. While our focus in this work

1055   A.3    R EMAINING C HALLENGES OF U SING SVG R EPRESENTATIONS
1056
1057   Although we have demonstrated that SVG can serve as a promising alternative representation for
1058   reasoning about vector graphics, we identify several remaining challenges:
1059   (1) Pretrained LLMs, including the most capable ones such as GPT-4 (OpenAI, 2023a), possess
1060   limited out-of-the-box understanding of SVG code. This limitation is evidenced by the low zero-shot
1061   performance of GPT-4 with SVG input (see row 2 in Table 3).
1062
       (2) Even after finetuning, the SVG-based LLM may still underperform zero-shot GPT-4V on certain
1063
       tasks, particularly those involving complex scenes, such as Shapeworld Scene and Maze Scene. In
1064   these instances, the SVG code becomes excessively verbose. These findings suggest that learning a
1065   model to directly comprehend the raw SVG code of an entire image poses significant challenges.
1066
1067
       (3) A fundamental challenge, irrespective of the chosen representation for visual input, is the lack of
       generalization capability to unseen tasks and various vector graphics image domains. If we rely on
1068
       existing LMM training mixtures, even any image can be converted into SVG code, the tasks remain
1069
       biased towards high-level semantics. In addition, it is infeasible to directly manually construct and
1070   annotate ⟨SVG, question, answer⟩ pairs covering diverse tasks with vector graphics.
1071
1072   These challenges motivated us to propose another layer of abstraction, the Primal Visual Description,
1073
       aimed at bridging the gap between low-level perception and high-level language reasoning on
       downstream tasks.

1161
       Common perception errors. (1) Novel shapes not covered by the Primal Visual Description
1162
       (PVD): For example, as visualized in Figure 11, the Shapeworld dataset includes a “semicircle” shape
1163
       type which is not in the PVD ontology; we see that the learned SVG-to-PVD model tends to predict
1164   it as an ellipse. This perception error directly contributes to the inferior performance of VDLM-mm
1165   compared to GPT-4o on the Shapeworld tasks, as shown in Table 1.
1166
1167
       (2) Accurate constraints between primitives: Although the PVD accommodates scenarios where
       multiple objects of the same color overlap, the attributes, e.g., position, of each object are decided
1168
       independently and randomly. Thus, the SVG-to-PVD model often fails to capture intentional
1169
       constraints between objects; for example, a line that perfectly segments a circle. These constraints are
1170   particularly emphasized in the Geoclidean 2-shot Learning task (Figure 11), where VDLM struggles
1171   to outperform GPT-4V and GPT-4o.
1172
1173
       (3) Very small objects: During inference, the iterative decomposition process heuristically ignores
       SVG paths that only contribute only minor differences to the reconstructed image. This method
1174
       effectively reduces noise from the rule-based image-to-SVG converter but may omit very small
1175
       objects in some cases. Adjusting this threshold is necessary for specific scenarios.
1176
1177
1178   Common reasoning errors. (1) Discovering intentional constraints: Without specific queries, the
1179   reasoning model can fail to identify intentional constraints. For example, differentiating a rhombus
1180   from a general quadrilateral, as shown in Figure 12.
1181
       (2) Handling ambiguity: Visual inputs sometimes provide useful inductive biases that can help
1182   the model better understand the task or make reasonable assumptions when the instructions are
1183   ambiguous. For instance, when presenting an angle in an image and asking whether it is an acute
1184   or obtuse angle, as in Figure 4, it is visually straightforward to assume that the angle is defined
1185   by the middle point as the vertex with rays extending outwards. However, without such visual
1186   cues, reasoning over pure symbolic representations makes it challenging to infer which angle the
1187   question refers to among the detected undirected edges. To mitigate ambiguity, adding more precise
