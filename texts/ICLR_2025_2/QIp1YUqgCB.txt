010                                                    A BSTRACT
011
012                Extending the success of text-to-image (T2I) synthesis to text-to-video (T2V) syn-
013                thesis is a promising direction for visual generative AI. Popular training-free sam-
014                pling algorithms currently generate high-fidelity images within the Stable Diffu-
015
                   sion family. However, when applied to video diffusion models (VDMs), these
                   techniques result in limited diversity and quality due to the low-quality data in
016
                   video datasets. We focus on inference to mitigate this issue, and then we propose
017
                   a training-free paradigm that optimizes the initial Gaussian noise by introduc-
018                ing a targeted semantic prior bias into the sampling process from a smoothing
019                perspective. The paradigm significantly improves both the fidelity and semantic
020                faithfulness of the synthesized videos. Guided by theoretical analysis using ran-
021                dom smoothing and differential equations, our resulting method                   can
022                be understood as approximately incorporating third-order derivatives into gradient
023                descent, which contributes to be better convergence in learning semantic informa-
024                tion. A more efficient version,                      , is proposed to achieve better
025                experimental results by leveraging a momentum mechanism. Both S MOOTH I NIT
026                and FAST-S MOOTH I NIT demonstrate promising empirical results across various
027                benchmarks, including UCF-101/MSR-VTT-related FVD, Chronomagic-bench,
028
                   and T2V-Compbench, setting a new standard for noise initialization in VDMs.

      1       I NTRODUCTION
031
032
      Text-to-video (T2V) synthesis (Ho et al., 2022; Singer et al., 2022; Bao et al., 2024; Blattmann
033
      et al., 2023) is a promising topic in artificial intelligence-generated content (AIGC). In contrast to
034   text-to-image (T2I) synthesis (Rombach et al., 2022; Esser et al., 2024; Peebles & Xie, 2023), which
035   benefits from a large, well-curated dataset such as LAION-5B (Schuhmann et al., 2022) of image-
036   text pairs to train powerful diffusion models for generating diverse and high-quality images, T2V
037   generation methods often produce suboptimal results due to the cluttered, watermarked nature and
038   small scale of existing training datasets (Bain et al., 2021; Soomro, 2012). Compared to retraining a
039   desired video diffusion model (VDM), one of the most straightforward and inexpensive solutions is
040   to develop ideal plug-and-play algorithms to enhance the fidelity and diversity of composite videos.
041   Unfortunately, the training-free sampling algorithms (Song et al., 2023a; Lu et al., 2022c; Bao et al.,
042
      2022) that perform well in T2I synthesis often struggle to meet expectations in T2V generation1 .
      Given this, while recent approaches (Wu et al., 2023; Qiu et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024b; Jeong
043
      et al., 2024) leverage the sampling characteristic of VDMs to enhance the quality of video by en-
044
      suring temporal consistency and semantic faithfulness, there remains a lack of genuine attempts to
045   comprehend and explore the training-free sampling paradigm from the perspective of initial noise.
046
047   In this paper, we aim to answer the question: “To what extent can just optimizing the initial Gaussian
048
      noise improve the generation ability of VDMs?”. Sufficient empirical results (Qi et al., 2024; Ban
      et al., 2024; Mao et al., 2023; Shirakawa & Uchida, 2024) consistently demonstrate that the syn-
049
      thesized image is highly sensitive to the initial Gaussian noise, particularly when sampling through
050
      ordinary differential equation-based (ODE-based) sampling algorithms (Lu et al., 2022b; Song et al.,
051   2023a). Even injecting a slight perturbation into the initial noise can significantly alter the generated
052   object’s color, position, and morphology. These observations suggest that there may exist a special

078   Identifying smoothing as the primary tool for optimizing the initial noise is crucial, as VDMs are
079   built on stochastic processes (Song et al., 2023b), and the randomness inherent in smoothing (Cohen
080   et al., 2019) can be effectively combined with stochastic differential equations (SDEs). Thus, it is
081

083
      sion,                    . Specifically, S MOOTH I NIT first adds a perturbation to the initial Gaussian
084
      noise, then injects semantic information related to the perturbation through the combined action of
085   DDIM (Song et al., 2023a) and DDIM-Inversion (Mokady et al., 2023), and finally computes the
086   expectation of the noise with semantics, conditioned on the perturbation, as the optimized noise.
087   The Corollary 3.4 in this paper proves that S MOOTH I NIT is analogous to introducing an additional
088   third-order term on top of gradient descent, leading to more robust convergence. Further, FAST-
089   S MOOTH I NIT introduces a momentum mechanism on top of S MOOTH I NIT to dynamically update
090   the initial noise before injecting semantic information. This approach not only accelerates conver-
091   gence but also reduces truncation error with theoretical guarantees (i.e., Theorem 3.5).
092   We conduct extensive experiments in Sec. 4 on various VDMs, including A NIMATEDIFF (Guo et al.,
093   2023), M ODELSCOPE -T2V (Wang et al., 2023), and L ATTE (Ma et al., 2024), where the experi-
094   ments show a consistent and substantial improvement over the widely used baselines (e.g., DDIM)
095   under several popular benchmarks, including UCF-101/MSR-VTT-related FVD (Soomro, 2012),
096   Chronomagic-Bench-150 (Yuan et al., 2024), Chronomagic-Bench-1652 (Yuan et al., 2024), and
097   T2V-Compbench (Sun et al., 2024). Moreover, FAST-S MOOTH I NIT outperforms F REE I NIT (Wu
098   et al., 2023) and U NICTRL (Chen et al., 2024b) in almost all metrics under the premise of treating
099
      the backbone (Ho et al., 2020; Peebles & Xie, 2023) as a black box and only modifying the initial
      noise, effectively substantiating that SMOOTHNESS is compatible with VDMs.

      In this paper, we extensively explore initial noise optimization to improve the quality and fidelity of
      synthesized videos from a smoothing perspective. Through empirical investigations and theoretical
532
      analysis, we confirm the significant sensitivity of the final video to the initial noise and demonstrate
533
      the F ORWARD -I NVERSION operator’s ability to consistently optimize this noise by injecting seman-
534   tic information. Building on these insights, we propose a basic algorithm, S MOOTH I NIT, which
535   improves optimization by accounting for bias introduced by intra-closed ball perturbations, thereby
536   enhancing VDM generation quality. We further design an advanced version, FAST-S MOOTH I NIT,
537   which achieves better performance and faster convergence by dynamically updating the input to the
538   F ORWARD -I NVERSION operator during iterations. We hope that our exploration of smoothing in
539   VDMs inspires future research on more efficient noise initialization techniques, contributing to the
      AIGC community.

760   Here we present the relevant metrics and benchmarks used for comparison in the main paper.
761
762   UCF-101-related FVD. The UCF-101 dataset is an action recognition dataset consisting of 101
763   categories. All videos are sourced from YouTube, with a fixed frame rate of 25 frames per second
764   (FPS) and a resolution of 320×240. Several previous works (Blattmann et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2023;
765   Chen et al., 2024b) validate the generation performance of VDMs on the UCF-101 dataset using
766   FVD (Unterthiner et al., 2019). However, a comprehensive evaluation benchmark for UCF-101
767
      remains unavailable. Consequently, we follow F REE I NIT, using the prompts listed in Ge et al. (2023)
      to synthesize videos and evaluate inference performance with FVD. Specifically, we synthesize 5
768
      videos for each of the 101 prompts provided by Ge et al. (2023), resulting in 505 synthesized videos.
769
      We then compute the FVD between these 505 synthesized videos and 505 videos randomly sampled
770   from the UCF-101 dataset (5 per class) by Open-Sora-Plan’s2 built-in FVD evaluation code.
771
772   MSR-VTT-related FVD. The MSR-VTT dataset (Xu et al., 2016) is a large-scale dataset for
773   open-domain video captioning, consisting of 10,000 video clips from 20 categories. The standard
774   split includes 6,513 clips for training, 497 clips for validation, and 2,990 clips for testing. We used
775   all 497 validation videos for our measurement. First, we generate a total of 1,491 videos based on
776   the prompts from the 497 validation videos, with each prompt generating three different videos to
777   ensure evaluation stability. We finally use Open-Sora-Plan’s3 built-in FVD evaluation code to assess
778   the results.
779
780   Chronomagic-Bench-150. Chronomagic-Bench-150, proposed in (Yuan et al., 2024), is a com-
781
      prehensive benchmark with a primary focus on the metamorphic evaluation of timelapse T2V syn-
      thesis. This benchmark includes four major categories of time-lapse videos: biological, human-
782
      created, meteorological, and physical, and extends these into 75 subcategories. Each subcategory
783
      contains two hard prompts, resulting in a total of 150 prompts. Chronomagic-Bench-150 com-
784   prises four metrics: UMT-FVD (↓), MTS CORE (↑), UMTS CORE (↑), and GPT4 O -MTS CORE
785   (↑), each used to evaluate different aspects. More specifically, UMT-FVD (↓) (Liu et al., 2024b)
786   uses the UMT (Li et al., 2023) feature space to compute FVD and evaluate the visual quality of
787   the synthesized video. MTS CORE (↑) measures metamorphic amplitude, reflecting the degree of
788   change across frames. UMTS CORE (↑) employs the UMT (Li et al., 2023) feature space to com-
789   pute CLIPScore (Hessel et al., 2021), assessing the text relevance of the synthesized video. Finally,
790   GPT4 O -MTS CORE (↑) is a fine-grained metric that uses GPT-4o (Achiam et al., 2023) as an evalu-
791   ator, aligning with human perception to accurately reflect the metamorphic amplitude and temporal
792
      coherence of T2V models. In this paper, we use metrics UMT-FVD (↓), UMTS CORE (↑), and
      GPT4 O -MTS CORE (↑) because we find that MTS CORE (↑) exhibits a peculiar phenomenon: per-
793
      formance decreases as the number of sampling steps increases (from 30 to 50), which may introduce
794
      ambiguity.
795
796   Chronomagic-Bench-1649. Chronomagic-Bench-1649, proposed in (Yuan et al., 2024), is a com-
797   prehensive benchmark with a primary focus on the metamorphic evaluation of timelapse T2V syn-
798   thesis. This benchmark has 75 subcategories like Chronomagic-Bench-150 but has 1649 prompts,
799   which is more comprehensive compared to the lightweight benchmark Chronomagic-Bench-150.
800   Chronomagic-Bench-1649 comprises four metrics: UMT-FVD (↓), MTS CORE (↑), UMTS CORE
801   (↑), and CHS CORE (↑), each used to evaluate different aspects. More specifically, UMT-FVD
802   (↓) (Liu et al., 2024b) uses the UMT (Li et al., 2023) feature space to compute FVD and evaluate
803   the visual quality of the synthesized video. MTS CORE (↑) measures metamorphic amplitude, re-
804
      flecting the degree of change across frames. UMTS CORE (↑) employs the UMT (Li et al., 2023)
      feature space to compute CLIPScore (Hessel et al., 2021), assessing the text relevance of the syn-
805
      thesized video. Finally, CHS CORE (↑) evaluates temporal coherence, ensuring that the generated
806
      videos maintain logical progression and continuity. Similar to Chronomagic-Bench-150, we ignore
807   the metric MTScore (↑) in our experiments.

      T2V-Compbench. TV2-Compbench (Sun et al., 2024) is a benchmark specifically designed for

1270
1271   In this section, we discuss a series of plug-and-play algorithms focused on VDMs, including
1272   F REE I NIT (Wu et al., 2023), F REE N OISE (Qiu et al., 2024), U NICTRL (Chen et al., 2024b), and
1273   I4VG EN (Guo et al., 2024). F REE I NIT primarily uses DDIM (Song et al., 2023a) and the diffu-
1274   sion forward process to generate new noise and reinitialize the noise by blending low-frequency
1275   components with high-frequency noise using a spatio-temporal filter, ultimately synthesizing the
1276   “clean” video through DDIM sampling. F REE N OISE is a training-free approach for synthesizing
1277   longer videos, ensuring both high video quality and computational efficiency. U NICTRL maintains
1278   semantic consistency across frames using cross-frame self-attention control, while simultaneously
1279
       enhancing motion quality and spatiotemporal consistency through motion injection and spatiotem-
       poral synchronization. I4VG EN first generates high-quality images using a T2I diffusion model,
1280
       then transforms them into Gaussian noise with preserved semantic information through a standard
1281
       diffusion forward process. Next, it incorporates the VDM’s temporal information using the score
1282   distillation sampling (SDS) algorithm, and finally samples from the standard DDIM. Since the offi-
1283   cial implementation4 of I4VG EN lacked the necessary packages and code as of press time, it is not
1284   compared in this paper.
