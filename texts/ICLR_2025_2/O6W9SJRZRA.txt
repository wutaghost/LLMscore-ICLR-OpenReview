009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            The reliability of deep time series models is often compromised by their tendency
012            to rely on confounding factors, which may lead to incorrect outputs. Our newly
013            recorded, naturally confounded dataset named P2S from a real mechanical pro-
014            duction line emphasizes this. To avoid “Clever-Hans” moments in time series, i.e.,
015            to mitigate confounders, we introduce the method Right on Time (RioT). RioT
016            enables, for the first time interactions with model explanations across both the
017            time and frequency domain. Feedback on explanations in both domains is then
018
               used to constrain the model, steering it away from the annotated confounding
               factors. The dual-domain interaction strategy is crucial for effectively addressing
019
               confounders in time series datasets. We empirically demonstrate that RioT can
020
               effectively guide models away from the wrong reasons in P2S as well as popular
021            time series classification and forecasting datasets.

024   1    I NTRODUCTION
025
026   Time series data is ubiquitous in our world today. Everything that is measured over time generates
027   some form of time series, for example, energy load (Koprinska et al., 2018), sensor measurements in
028   industrial machinery (Mehdiyev et al., 2017) or recordings of traffic data (Ma et al., 2022). Various
029   neural models are often applied to complex time series data (Ruiz et al., 2021; Benidis et al., 2023).
030   As in other domains, these can be subject to confounding factors ranging from simple noise or
031   artifacts to complex shortcut confounders (Lapuschkin et al., 2019). Intuitively, a confounder, also
032   called “Clever-Hans” moment, can be a pattern in the data that is not relevant to the task but correlates
033
      with it during model training. A model can incorrectly pick up on this confounder and use it instead
      of the relevant features to e.g. make a classification. A confounded model does not generalize well to
034
      data without the confounder, which is a problem when employing models in practice (Geirhos et al.,
035
      2020). For time series, confounders and their mitigation have yet to receive attention, where existing
036   works make specific assumptions about settings and data (Bica et al., 2020).
037
038   In particular, it is essential to mitigate shortcut confounders, i.e., spurious patterns in the training data
039
      used for the prediction. If a model utilizes confounding factors in the training set, its decision relies
      on wrong reasons and fails to generalize to unconfounded data. Model explanations play a crucial role
040
      in uncovering confounding factors, but they are not enough on their own to address them (cf. Fig. 1 I).
041
      While an explanation can reveal that the model relies on incorrect factors, it does not alter the model’s
042   outcome. To change this, we introduce Right on Time (RioT), a new method following the core ideas
043   of the explanatory interactive learning (XIL) paradigm (Teso & Kersting, 2019), i.e., using feedback
044   on explanations to mitigate confounders (cf. Fig. 1 II). RioT uses traditional explanation methods
045   like Integrated Gradients (IG) (Sundararajan et al., 2017) to detect whether models focus on the right
046   or the wrong time steps and utilizes feedback on the latter to revise the model.
047
      However, confounding factors in time series data extend beyond the time domain. For example, a
048   consistent noise frequency in an audio signal can act as a confounder without being tied to a specific
049   point in time. RioT can handle these types of confounders by incorporating feedback in the frequency
050   domain. To highlight the importance of mitigating confounders in time series data, we introduce a
051   new real-world, confounded dataset called P RODUCTION P RESS S ENSOR DATA (P2S). The dataset
052   includes sensor measurements from an industrial high-speed press, essential to many manufacturing
053   processes in the sheet metal working industry. The sensor data for detecting faulty production is
      naturally confounded and thus causes incorrect predictions after training. Next to its immediate

087   2       R ELATED W ORK
088
089   Explanations for Time Series. Within the field of explainable artificial intelligence (XAI), various
090   techniques to explain machine learning models and their outcomes have been proposed. While many
091   techniques originate from image or text data, they were quickly adapted to time series Rojat et al.
092   (2021). While backpropagation- and perturbation-based attribution methods provide explanations
093   directly in the input space, other techniques like symbol aggregations (Lin et al., 2003) or shapelets
094   (Ye & Keogh, 2011) aim to provide higher-level explanations. For a more in-depth discussion of
095   explanations for time series, we refer to surveys by Rojat et al. (2021) or Schlegel et al. (2019). While
096
      it is essential to have explanation methods to detect confounding factors, they alone are insufficient to
      revise a model. Thus, explanations are the starting point of our method, as they enable users to detect
097
      confounders and provide feedback to overcome confounders in the model. In particular, we build
098
      upon Integrated Gradients (IG) (Sundararajan et al., 2017). This method computes attributions for
099   the input by utilizing model gradients. We selected it because of its several desirable properties like
100   completeness or implementation invariance and its wide use, also for time series data (Mercier et al.,
101   2022; Veerappa et al., 2022).
102
      Explanatory Interactive Learning (XIL). There is a growing field of research investigating con-
103
      founding factors and how to overcome them. However, these mainly focus on visual data domains.
104
      One paradigm to overcome these confounders is using explanatory interactive learning, which de-
105   scribes the general process of revising a model’s decision process based on human feedback(Teso &
106   Kersting, 2019; Schramowski et al., 2020). A key element of XIL is using the model’s explanations
107

      to incorporate human feedback, thus revising the model’s mistakes (Friedrich et al., 2023a). This is
109   primarily done to revise models that show Clever-Hans-like behavior (being affected by shortcuts in
110   the data) to prevent them from using these shortcuts Stammer et al. (2020). Several methods apply
111   the XIL paradigm to image data. For example, Right for the Right Reasons (RRR) (Ross et al., 2017)
112   and Right for Better Reasons (RBR) (Shao et al., 2021) use human feedback as a penalty mask on
113   model explanations. Instead of penalizing wrong reasons, HINT (Selvaraju et al., 2019) rewards the
114   model for focusing on the correct part of the input. Furthermore, Friedrich et al. (2023b) investigate
115   the use of multiple explanation methods simultaneously. Although these methods are often employed
116   to address confounders in image data, their application to time series data remains unexplored. To
117
      bridge this gap, we introduce RioT, a method that incorporates the core principles of XIL to the
      unique characteristics of time series data.
118
119   Unconfounding Time Series. Next to approaches from interactive learning, there is also some other
120   work on unconfounding time series models. This line of work is generally based on causal analysis of
121   the time series model and data (Flanders et al., 2011). Methods like Time Series Deconfounder (Bica
122   et al., 2020), SqeDec (Hatt & Feuerriegel, 2024) or LipCDE (Cao et al., 2023), perform estimations
123
      on the data while mitigating the effect of confounders in covariates of the target variable. They
      generally mitigate the effect of the confounders through causal analysis and specific assumptions
124
      about the data generation. On the other hand, in this work we tackle confounders within the target
125
      variate, and have no further assumption besides that the confounder is visible in the explanations of
126   the model, where these previous methods cannot easily be applied.

171   Attributions for Forecasting. In a classification setting, attributions are generated by propagating
172   gradients back from the model output (of its highest activated class) to the model inputs. However,
173   there is often no single model output in time series forecasting. Instead, the model generates one
174   output for each timestep of the forecasting window simultaneously. Naively, one could use these W
175   outputs and generate as many explanations e′1 (x), . . . e′W (x), where each e′i (x) is the IG explanation
176   using the i-th time step from the forecasting window as target instead of a classification label. This
177   number of explanations would, however, make it even harder for humans to interpret the results, as
178   the size of the explanation increases with W (Miller, 2019). Therefore, we propose to aggregate the
179
      individual explanations by averaging in Eq. 2. Averaging attributions over the forecasting window
      provides a simple yet robust aggregation of the explanations. Other means of combining them,
180
      potentially even weighted based on distance of the forecast in the future are also imaginable. Overall,
181
      this allows attributions for time series classification and forecasting to be generated similarly.

183   Attributions in the Frequency Domain. Time series data is often given in the frequency rep-
184   resentation. Sometimes, this format is more intuitive for humans to understand than the spatial
185
      representations. As a result, providing explanations in this domain is essential. Vielhaben et al. (2023)
      showed how to obtain frequency attributions of the method Layerwise Relevance Propagation (Bach
186
      et al., 2015), even if the model does not operate directly on the frequency domain. We transfer this
187
      idea to IG: for an input sample x, we generate attributions with IG, resulting in e(x) ∈ RT (Eq. 1 for
188   classification or Eq. 2 for forecasting). We then interpret the explanation as a time series, with the
189   attribution scores as values. To obtain the frequency explanation, we perform a Fourier transformation
190   of e(x), resulting in the frequency explanation ê(x) ∈ CT with Ê for the entire set.

193   3.2   O BTAIN
194
195   The next step of RioT is to obtain user feedback on confounding factors. For an input x, a user can
196   mark parts that are confounded, resulting in a feedback mask a(x) ∈ {0, 1}T . In this binary mask, a
197   1 signals a potential confounder at this time step. Thereby, it is not necessary to have feedback for
198
      every sample of the dataset, as a mask a(x) = (0, . . . , 0)T corresponds to no feedback. Feedback
      can also be given on the frequency explanation in a similar manner, marking which elements in the
199
      frequency domain are potential confounders. The resulting feedback mask â(x) = (â(x)re , â(x)im )
200
      can be different for the real â(x)re ∈ {0, 1}T and imaginary part â(x)im ∈ {0, 1}T . For the whole
201
      dataset, we then have spatial annotations A and frequency annotations Â.
202
203   As the annotated feedback masks come from human experts, obtaining them can become costly.
204   However, confounders often occur systematically, and it is thus possible to apply the same annotation
205   mask to many samples. This can drastically reduce the number of annotations required in practice.
206

      3.3   R EVISE
208
209
      The last step of RioT is integrating the feedback into the model. We apply the general idea of using
210   a loss-based model revision (Schramowski et al., 2020; Ross et al., 2017; Stammer et al., 2020)
211   based on the explanations and the annotation mask. Given the input data (X , Y), we define the
212   original task (or right-answer) loss as LRA (X , Y). This loss measures the model performance and
213   is the primary learning objective. To incorporate the feedback, we further use the right-reason loss
214   LRR (A, E). This loss aligns model explanations E = {e(x)|x ∈ X } and user feedback A by
215   penalizing the model for explanations in the annotated areas. To achieve model revision and a good
      task performance, both losses are combined, where λ is a hyperparameter to balance both parts

247   It is important to note that giving feedback in the frequency domain allows a new form of model
248   revision through XIL. Even if we effectively still apply masking in the frequency domain, the effect
249   in the original input domain is entirely different. Masking out a single frequency affects all time

252
      flexible than before. The Fourier transformation is a prominent example but not the only possible
      choice for this. Using other transformations like wavelets (Graps, 1995), is also possible.

293   Production Press Sensor Data (P2S). RioT aims to mitigate confounders in time series data. To
294   assess our method, we need datasets with annotated real-world confounders. So far, there are no
295

297

301   identified regions in the time series that vary with production speed, potentially distracting models
302   from relevant classification indicators, especially when no defect and normal runs of the same speed

304   beyond training. The default P2S setting includes normal and defect runs of different speeds, with an
305   unconfounded setting of runs at the same speed. Further details on the dataset are available in App. B.

      In summary, RioT effectively generalizes from small subsets of feedback and remains robust against

490
      Limitations. An important aspect of RioT is the human feedback provided in the Obtain step.
      Integrating human feedback into the model is a key advantage of RioT, but can also be a limitation.
491
      While we have shown that a small fraction of samples with annotations can be sufficient, and that
492
      annotations can be applied for many samples, they are still necessary for RioT. Additionally, like
493   many other (explanatory) interactive learning methods, RioT assumes correct human feedback.
494   Thus, considering possible repercussions of inaccurate feedback when applying RioT in practice
495   is important. Another potential drawback of RioT are increased training costs. RioT requires
496   computation of a mixed-partial derivative to optimize the model’s explanation when using gradient-
497   based attributions. While this affects training cost, the loss can be formulated as a Hessian-vector
498   product, which is fast to compute in practice, making the additional overhead easy to manage.

501   5       C ONCLUSION
502
503   In this work, we present Right on Time a method to mitigate confounding factors in time series data
504   with the help of human feedback. By revising the model, RioT significantly diminishes the influence
505   of these factors, steering the model to align with the correct reasons. Using popular time series models
506   on several manually confounded datasets and the newly introduced, naturally confounded, real-world
507   dataset P2S showcases that they are indeed subject to confounders. Our results, however, demonstrate
508   that applying RioT to these models can mitigate confounders in the data. Furthermore, we have
509   unveiled that addressing solely the time domain is insufficient for revising the model to focus on the
510   correct reasons, which is why we extended our method beyond it. Feedback in the frequency domain
511
      provides an additional way to steer the model away from confounding factors and towards the right
      reasons. Extending the application of RioT to multivariate time series represents a logical next step,
512
      and exploring the integration of various explainer types is another promising direction. To further
513
      reduce the required human annotations, exploring the use of semi-automated feeback techniques,
514   transfer learning or LLMs are potential next steps. Additionally, we aim to apply RioT, especially
515   RioTfreq , to other modalities as well, offering a more nuanced approach to confounder mitigation. It
516   should be noted that while our method shows potential in its current iteration, interpreting attributions
517   in time series data remains a general challenge.

706   Adaption of Integrated Gradients (IG). A part of IG is a multiplication of the model gradient
707   with the input itself, improving the explanation’s quality (Shrikumar et al., 2017). However, this
708   multiplication makes some implicit assumptions about the input format. In particular, it assumes
709   that there are no inputs with negative values. Otherwise, multiplying the attribution score with a
710   negative input would flip the attribution’s sign, which is not desired. For images, this is unproblematic
711
      because they are always equal to or larger than zero. In time series, negative values can occur and
      normalization to make them all positive is not always suitable. To avoid this problem, we use only
712
      the input magnitude and not the input sign to compute the IG attributions.
713
714   Computing Explanations. To compute explanations with Integrated Gradients, we fol-
715   lowed the common practice of using a baseline of zeros. The standard approach worked
716   well in our experiments, so we did not explore other baseline choices in this work. For
717
      the implementation, we utilized the widely-used Captum5 library, where we patched the
      captum._utils.gradient.compute_gradients function to allow for the propagation
718
      of the gradient with respect to the input to be propagated back into the parameters.
719
720   Model Training and Hyperparameters. To find suitable parameters for model training, we per-
721   formed a hyperparameter search over batch size, learning rate, and the number of training epochs.
722   We then used these parameters for all model trainings and evaluations, with and without RioT. In
723
      addition, we selected suitable λ values for RioT with a hyperparameter selection on the respective
      validation sets. The exact values for the model training parameters and the λ values can be found in
724
      the provided code6 .
725
726   To avoid model overfitting on the forecasting datasets, we performed shifted sampling with a window
727   size of half the lookback window.

778
      computations of additional gradients. Given a model fθ (x), parameterized by θ and input x, then

793   A.4    D ETAILS ON C ONFOUNDING FACTORS
794
      In the datasets which are not P2S, we added synthetic confounders to evaluate the effectiveness of
795   confounders. In the following, we provide details on the nature of these confounders in the four
796   settings:
797
798
      Classification Spatial. For classification datasets, spatial confounders are specific patterns for each
      class. The pattern is added to every sample of that class in the training data, resulting in a spurious
799
      correlation between the pattern and the class label. Specifically, we replace T time steps with a sine
800
      wave according to:
801                                     confounder := sin(t · (2 + j)π)
802
      while t ∈ {0, 1, . . . , T } and j represents the class index, simulating a spurious correlation between
803
      the confounder and class index (Fig. 8).
804
805   Classification Frequency. Similar to the spatial case, frequency confounders for classification are
806   specific patterns added to the entire series, altering all time steps by a small amount. The confounder
807   is represented as a sine wave and is applied additively to the full sequence (T = S):
808                                   confounder := sin(t · (2 + j)π) · A
809
      where A resembles the confounder amplitude.

840   Forecasting Spatial. For forecasting datasets, spatial confounders are shortcuts that act as the actual
841   solution to the forecasting problem. Periodically, data from the time series is copied back in time. This
842   “back-copy” is a shortcut for the forecast, as it resembles the time steps of the forecasting window.
843
      Due to the windowed sampling from the time series, this shortcut occurs at every second sample. The
      exact confounder formulation is outlined in the sketch below (Fig. 10), with an exemplary lookback
844
      length of 9, forecasting horizon of 3 and window stride of 6. This results in a shortcut confounder in
845
      samples 1 and 3 (marked red) and overlapping in sample 2 (marked orange). This can for example
846   occur in scenarios such as data transmission. Transmission glitches, manifesting as packet losses or
847   duplications, can subtly introduce irregularities into the time series data.
848
849
      Forecasting Frequency. This setting differs from the previous shortcut confounders. The frequency
      confounder for forecasting is a recurring Dirac impulse with a certain frequency, added every k
850
      time steps over the entire sequence (of length S), including the forecasting windows. This impulse
851
      is present throughout all of the training data, distracting the model from the real forecast. The
852   confounder is present at all time steps: i ∈ {n · k|n ∈ N ∧ n · k ≤ S} with a strength of A:
853
854
                                              confounder := A · ∆i
855
856
857   Such a confounder could, for example, occur when monitoring of water flow through a pipe in an
858   assembly line. Suppose that during the capturing, a defect in one of the rollers adjacent to the water
859   pipe induces a systematic stutter. This mechanical stutter, in turn, generates repeated impulses in the
860
      water flow sensor’s readings. These impulses act as a systematic frequency confounder, which can
      negatively influence the performance of the forecasting model.
861
862   In conclusion, confounders are only present in the training data, not validation or test data. We
863   generate an annotation mask based on the confounder area or frequency to simulate human feedback.
      This mask is applied to all confounded samples except in our feedback scaling experiment.

       applications. However, for the time domain, datasets with explicitly labeled confounders are not
       present, highlighting the challenge of assessing model performance against the complex nature of
1057
       practical confounding factors.

1059   To bridge this gap, we introduce P2S, a dataset that represents a significant step forward by featuring
1060   explicitly identified confounders. This dataset originates from experimental work on a production line
1061   for deep-drawn sheet metal parts, employing a progressive die on a high-speed press. The sections
1062
       below detail the experimental approach and the process of data collection.

1064   B.1   R EAL -W ORLD SETTING
1065
       The production of parts in multiple progressive forming stages using stamping, deep drawing and
1066
       bending operations with progressive dies is generally one of the most economically significant
1067
       manufacturing processes in the sheet metal working industry and enables the production of complex
1068   parts on short process routes with consistent quality. For the tests, a four-stage progressive die was
1069   used on a Bruderer BSTA 810-145 high-speed press with varied stroke speed. The strip material
1070   to be processed is fed into the progressive die by a BSV300 servo feed unit, linked to the cycle of
1071   the press, in the stroke movement while the tools are not engaged. The part to be produced remains
1072   permanently connected to the sheet strip throughout the entire production run. The stroke height
1073   of the tool is 63 mm and the material feed per stroke is 60 mm. The experimental setup with the
1074   progressive die set up on the high-speed press is shown in Fig. 12.
1075   The four stages include a pilot punching stage, a round stamping stage, deep drawing and a cut-out
1076   stage. In the first stage, a 3 mm hole is punched in the metal strip. This hole is used by guide pins in
1077   the subsequent stages to position the metal strip. During the stroke movement, the pilot pin always
1078   engages in the pilot hole first, thus ensuring the positioning accuracy of the components. In the
1079   subsequent stage, a circular blank is cut into the sheet metal strip. This is necessary so that the part
       can be deep-drawn directly from the sheet metal strip. This is a round geometry that forms small

       B.2   DATA COLLECTION
1117
1118   An indirect piezoelectric force sensor (Kistler 9240A) was integrated into the upper mould mounting
1119   plate of the deep-drawing stage for data acquisition. The sensor is located directly above the punch
1120   and records not only the indirect process force but also the blank holder forces which are applied by
1121   spring assemblies between the upper mounting plate and the blank holder plate. The data is recorded
1122   at a sampling frequency of 25 kHz. The material used is DC04 with a width of 50 mm and a thickness
1123   of 0.5 mm. The voltage signals from the sensors are digitised using a "CompactRIO" (NI cRIO 9047)
1124   with integrated NI 9215 measuring module (analogue voltage input ± 10 V). Data recording is started
1125
       via an inductive proximity switch when the press ram passes below a defined stroke height during the
       stroke movement and is stopped again as it passes the inductive proximity switch during the return
1126
       stroke movement. Due to the varying process speed caused by the stroke speeds that have been set,
1127
       the recorded time series have a different number of data points. Further, there are slight variations in
1128   the length of the time series withing one experiment. For this reason, all time series are interpolated
1129   to a length of 4096 data points and we discard any time series that deviate considerably from the
1130   mean length of a run (i.e., threshold of 3). A total of 12 series of experiments, shown in Tab. 11, were
1131   carried out with production rates from 80 to 225 spm. To simulate a defect, the spring hardness of
1132   the blank holder was manipulated in the test series that were marked as defect. The manipulated
1133   experiments result in the component bursting and tearing during production. In a real production
       environment, this would lead directly to the parts being rejected.

       B.3   DATA CHARACTERISTICS
1167
       Fig. 14 shows the progression of the time series recorded with the indirect force sensor. The force
1168
       curve characterises the process cycle during a press stroke. The measurement is started by the trigger
1169
       which is activated by the ram moving downwards. The downholer plates touch down at point A
1170   and press the strip material onto the die. Between point A and point B, the downholder springs are
1171   compressed, causing the applied force to increase linearly. The deep drawing process begins at point
1172   B. At point C, the press reaches its bottom dead centre and the reverse stroke begins so that the punch
1173   moves out of the material again. At point D, the deep-drawing punch is released from the material
1174   and now the hold-down springs relax linearly up to point E. At point E, the downholder plate lifts off
1175   again, the component is fed to the next process step and the process is complete.

       The presented dataset P2S is confounded by the speed with which the progressive tool is operated.
1180

1181

1184   frictional force is dependent on the frictional speed. This is particularly evident in the present case,
1185   as deep-drawing oils, which can optimize the friction condition, were not used in the experiments.

1187   to 2500 (end of deep drawing). In addition, the impulse of the downholder plate affecting the die
