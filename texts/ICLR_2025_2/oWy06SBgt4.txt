009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011
               Fully quantized training (FQT) accelerates the training of deep neural networks by
012
               quantizing the activations, weights, and gradients into lower precision. To explore
013            the ultimate limit of FQT (the lowest achievable precision), we make a first attempt
014            to 1-bit FQT. We provide a theoretical analysis of FQT based on Adam and SGD,
015            revealing that the gradient variance influences the convergence of FQT. Building on
016            these theoretical results, we introduce an Average 1-bit Quantization (AQ) strategy.
017            The strategy leverages the heterogeneity of gradients to mitigate gradient variance
018            by pruning less informative gradients and enhancing the numerical precision of
019            remaining gradients. Additionally, we propose Sample Channel joint Quantization
020            (SCQ), which utilizes different quantization strategies in the computation of weight
021
               gradients and activation gradients to ensure that the method is friendly to low-
               bitwidth hardware. Finally, we present a framework to deploy our algorithm.
022
               For fine-tuning VGGNet-16 and ResNet-18 on multiple datasets, our algorithm
023
               achieves an average accuracy improvement of approximately 6%, compared to
024            per-sample quantization. Moreover, our training speedup can reach a maximum of
025            5.13× compared to full precision training.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Training neural networks has a high computational cost and memory footprint. Training with low-
032   precision arithmetic (a.k.a., fully quantized training or FQT) can enhance computational and memory
033
      efficiency. FQT quantizes weights, activations, and gradients into low-bitwidth numerical formats,
      enabling a fast implementation of both forward and backward propagation on low-precision hardware.
034
035   The speedup potential of FQT depends on the numerical precision. Research aims to reduce the
036   training numerical precision, without compromising convergence speed or accuracy. The required
037   precision has been reduced from FP/INT16 (Micikevicius et al., 2017; Das et al., 2018) to FP/INT8
038   (Wang et al., 2018b; Banner et al., 2018; Zhu et al., 2020; Yang et al., 2020). As of now, some work
039
      (Sun et al., 2020; Chmiel et al., 2021; Xi et al., 2023) have successfully pushed precision down to 4
      bits.
040
041   As the training numerical precision continues to decrease, a natural question arises:
042
043               What is the ultimate limit of FQT (i.e., the minimum achievable bitwidth)?
044
045   Answering this question not only advances our understanding of FQT but also provides a crucial
046   direction for future hardware design strategies. Ideally, if we can push the bitwidth down to 1-bit,
047
      the training can be implemented with binary operations, such as XNOR and bitcounting operations
      (Courbariaux et al., 2016), and hardware design might be greatly simplified. Binary computation is
048
      already shown possible for inference acceleration, such as XNOR-Net (Rastegari et al., 2016), but
049
      1-bit training remains unexplored.
050
051   Reducing the bitwidth for FQT is challenging because of (1) the lack of theoretical understanding,
052   especially how gradient quantization affects the convergence; (2) the large quantization error of
053
      gradients, which causes a sharp performance drop or even divergence when reducing gradient bitwidth
      lower than 4-bit (Fig. 1). Due to these challenges, the current research frontier is still 4-bit FQT.

086

089   2    R ELATED W ORKS
090
091
092   Quantization Aware Training. QAT is a method designed to accelerate inference by quantizing the
093
      activations and weights. Various works (Zhou et al., 2017; Choi et al., 2018; Zhang et al., 2018; Jacob
      et al., 2018; Dong et al., 2019; Tang et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2023) have been developed to quantize
094
      weights and activations into lower bitwidth. Furthermore, some studies (Rastegari et al., 2016; Bulat
095
      & Tzimiropoulos, 2019; Wang et al., 2020; Bai et al., 2020; Wu et al., 2023; Qin et al., 2023) have
096   reduced the numerical precision of weights and activation values to 1 bit. However, QAT does not
097   quantize gradients, and as a result, the backward propagation cannot be accelerated.
098
099
      Fully Quantized Training. FQT further quantizes the gradients into lower precision, compared with
      QAT. Hence, FQT allows for efficient implementation of both forward and backward propagation on
100
      low-bitwidth computational units. FQT, unlike optimizer quantization (Lin et al., 2022a), involves
101
      quantizing weights, activations, and gradients altogether. Optimizer quantization only quantizes
102   weight update (weight gradients), thus reducing communication costs but not accelerating computation
103   (Saha et al., 2022). Early works on FQT use FP16 (Gupta et al., 2015; Micikevicius et al., 2017) or
104   INT16 (Das et al., 2018) values to constrain weights, activations, and gradients. After that, various
105   8-bit numerical formats (Wang et al., 2018b; Banner et al., 2018; Zhu et al., 2020; Yang et al., 2020;
106   Xi et al., 2024) have been proposed that further push the bitwidth of data to 8 bits. Subsequently,
107   Chen et al. (2020) provides theoretical bounds on how the quantization scheme (bitwidth, type of
      quantizer) affects the quality of the quantized gradient. Based on that, some works have successfully

      trained several networks with 4-bit activations/weights/gradients (Sun et al., 2020; Chmiel et al.,
109   2021; Xi et al., 2023). The current research frontier is 4-bit FQT, but it still is not the ultimate limit.

      We discuss the computational overhead of our method. Our implementation is not fully optimized, as
509
      the comprehensive hardware-algorithm co-design is beyond the scope of this paper. Our experiments

512   Training speedup. We compare the training time of the FP32 PyTorch and our 1-bit FQT for

      VGGNet16 and ResNet18. We vary the resolution of the input and summarize the speedup of our
      method in Table 4. For VGGNet16, our algorithm achieves an average speedup of 3.74× and 2.49×
514

515
      average speedup. Additionally, to assess the acceleration potential of 1-bit FQT, we compare their
516   speedup at the same optimization level (unoptimized). The results indicate that across multiple cases,
517   the speedup is above a hundredfold. On edge devices, our method achieves a speedup of over 50×.
518   This gap indicates significant acceleration potential for 1-bit FQT. Finally, we analyzed why the
519   speedup of ResNet18 is lower than that of VGG16, concluding from Fig. 10 in Appendix E that our
520   implementation is more favorable for layers with more filters, which leads to a higher speedup for
521   VGG16, as it has a higher average number of filters per layer.
522   Average 1-bit vs. 1-bit. We compared the runtime of average 1-bit matrix multiplication and 1-bit
523   matrix multiplication across different matrix sizes in Table 6. The results demonstrate that the
524   difference in runtime between these two methods is minimal, indicating similarity in the runtime

529   7     C ONCLUSION
530
531   We propose a hardware-friendly 1-bit FQT method in this work, which pushes the limit of FQT.
532   Through convergence analysis, we propose AGP to reduce the variance of the quantizer, thereby
533   enhancing the convergence of quantized training. Subsequently, to address the issue of unacceleratable
534   weight gradient computation, we present a SCQ strategy. Finally, we propose a framework that
535   practically accelerates training, achieving a speedup of up to 5.13× compared to full precision training.
536
      While our approach focuses solely on convolutional neural networks in this study, experiments indicate
      its potential applicability to other architectures.
537
538   Limitations: The primary limitation of this work lies in its ability to achieve 1-bit FQT in transfer
539   learning tasks but not in training from scratch. To the best of our knowledge, even the 3-bit FQT from
      scratch is still an open problem.

1120

1123   Binary multiplication is the crucial operation. In our approach, both forward and backward
1124   propagation are implemented through binary multiplication. For example, For two vectors, X1 and
1125   X2 , each of length 32, encode them into two unsigned 32-bit integers, x1 and x2 . The multiplication
1126
       of the two is implemented as follows:
1127                           SUM(X1 ⊙ X2 ) = bitcount(XNOR(x1, x2)) << 1 − 32
1128   where the dot product of two vectors, each of length 32, is efficiently replaced by a single bitcount and
1129   XNOR operation, effectively reducing energy consumption and time overhead. However, it is worth
1130   noting that an unbiased quantizer maps data to 0 or 1, rather than -1 or 1. Therefore, some conversion
                                     n
1131   is required. For X1 ∈ {1, −1} , X2 = ReLU(X1 ), it is clear that (S/2)X1 +Z +(S/2) = SX2 +Z.
1132   Therefore, some adjustments are needed: a straightforward modification of the scaling factor S and
1133   zero point Z is sufficient to achieve the transformation. This transformation requires only one
       multiplication and one addition for the scaling factor and zero point, thus incurring minimal overhead.
