009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Deploying large and complex deep neural networks on resource-constrained edge
012            devices poses significant challenges due to their computational demands and the
013            complexities of non-convex optimization. Traditional compression methods such
014
               as distillation and pruning often retain non-convexity that complicates fine-tuning
               in real-time on such devices. Moreover, these methods often necessitate extensive
015
               end-to-end network fine-tuning after compression to preserve model performance,
016
               which is not only time-consuming but also requires fully annotated datasets, thus
017            potentially negating the benefits of efficient network compression. In this paper, we
018            introduce a novel distillation technique that efficiently compresses the model via
019            convex optimization – eliminating intermediate non-convex activation functions
020            and using only intermediate activations from the original model. Our approach
021            enables distillation in a label-free data setting and achieves performance comparable
022            to the original model without requiring any post-compression fine-tuning. We
023            demonstrate the effectiveness of our method for image classification models on
024            multiple standard datasets, and further show that in the data limited regime, our
025
               method can outperform standard non-convex distillation approaches. Our method
               promises significant advantages for deploying high-efficiency, low-footprint models
026
               on edge devices, making it a practical choice for real-world applications. We show
027
               that convex neural networks, when provided with rich feature representations from
028            a large pre-trained non-convex model, can achieve performance comparable to their
029            non-convex counterparts, opening up avenues for future research at the intersection
030            of convex optimization and deep learning.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Deep Neural Networks (DNNs) have become the cornerstone for a broad range of applications such
035   as image classification, segmentation, natural language understanding and speech recognition (Rawat
036   & Wang, 2017; Torfi et al., 2021; Alam et al., 2020; Sultana et al., 2020). The performance of these
037
      models typically scale with their size, width and architectural complexity, leading to the development
      of increasingly large models to achieve state-of-the-art results. However, this also necessitates
038
      significant computational, memory and energy resources to attain reasonable performance (Bianco
039
      et al., 2018; Rosenfeld, 2021); making their deployment impractical for resource-constrained edge
040   devices – such as smartphones, microcontrollers, and wearable technology – which are of daily use
041   (Ignatov et al., 2019; Anwar & Raychowdhury, 2020; Seng et al., 2023; Chen & Liu, 2021). Edge
042   deep learning is crucial in these contexts to enable real-time processing, reduce latency, and ensure
043   reliable operation without constant network connectivity (Kusupati et al., 2018; Tan & Le, 2019;
044   Voghoei et al., 2018; Zaidi et al., 2022). Even in cases where such devices can make predictions by
045   offloading the the computations to large models on cloud servers, developing memory and compute
046   efficient models is still advantageous as it reduces network bandwidth usage, lowers operational costs,
047   and allows cloud services to serve more clients within the same resource constraints.
048   Non-convex optimization, which forms the bedrock of training DNNs, poses significant theoretical
049   and practical challenges. Finding the global minimum can be NP-hard in the worst case and the
050   optimization landscape is riddled with local minima (Yao, 1992; Bartlett & Ben-David, 1999) and
051   saddle points (Dauphin et al., 2014), making it difficult to guarantee convergence to a global optimum
052   (Choromanska et al., 2015). Moreover, even training these models in a distributed manner on multiple
053   GPUs can take days or weeks to complete. Convex optimization, on the other hand, is well-studied
      with strong theoretical guarantees and efficient algorithms (Boyd & Vandenberghe, 2004). While

054
      there are recent works that show one can reformulate the optimization of certain classes of non-convex
055   NNs as convex problems (Pilanci & Ergen, 2020b; Ergen et al., 2022a), convex models are generally
056   considered less expressive than their non-convex counterparts (Guss & Salakhutdinov, 2019; Scarselli
057   & Chung Tsoi, 1998) and have been overshadowed by the success of deep learning in recent years.
058
059
      Although large non-convex DNNs possess immense expressive power, they often contain redundant
      weights that contribute little to the overall performance in downstream tasks (Frankle & Carbin,
060
      2019; Han et al., 2015). This phenomenon called Implicit Bias has been well established in both
061
      theory and practice (Soudry et al., 2024; Chizat & Bach, 2020; Morwani et al., 2023; Shah et al.,
062   2020). Additionally, while DNNs have the capacity to memorize the training dataset, they often end
063   up learning basic solutions that generalize well to test datasets (Zhang et al., 2021). Both of these
064   observations motivate using smaller, compressed models that eliminate unnecessary parameters while
065   achieving comparable performance. Common techniques for model compression include network
066   pruning (Han et al., 2015), low-rank approximation (Denil et al., 2013), and quantization (Gong et al.,
067   2014). However, these methods often significantly degrade performance of the resulting model unless
068   supplemented by another round of extensive fine-tuning on the labeled training data post-compression,
069   which may not be feasible in many practical scenarios. Knowledge distillation (KD) is a promising
070
      alternative that enables a smaller student model to learn from a larger teacher model (Hinton et al.,
      2015) by training the student to mimic the outputs or intermediate representations of the teacher by
071
      matching certain statistical metrics between the two models. While KD can significantly enhance the
072
      performance of the student model, it often still requires fine-tuning the student model on labeled data
073   in conjunction with the activation matching objective, limiting its applicability in situations where
074   labeled data or compute is scarce or unavailable.
075
076   In this paper, we bridge the gap between the non-convex and convex regimes by demonstrating that
077   convex neural network architectures can achieve performance comparable to non-convex models for
078
      downstream tasks when leveraging rich feature representations. To demonstrate this, we focus on the
      model compression via KD framework and propose a novel approach that compresses non-convex
079
      DNNs via convex optimization. Our approach replaces the original complex non-convex layers in
080
      the teacher module with simpler layers having convex gating functions in the student module. By
081   matching the activation values between the teacher and student modules, we eliminate the need
082   for post-compression fine-tuning on labeled data, making our method suitable for deployment in
083   environments where labeled data is scarce. Our method not only maintains the performance of the
084   original large model, but also capitalizes on the favorable optimization landscape of convex models.
085   This enables users to employ a range of highly efficient and specialized convex solvers tailored to
086   their resource requirements, resulting in faster convergence and the potential for on-device learning
087   using online data—a significant advantage for edge devices with limited computational resources.
088   Summary of our Contributions:
089
090   Efficient Convex-based Distillation and Deployment: We introduce a novel knowledge distillation
091   approach that leverages convex neural network architectures to compress DNNs. By replacing non-
092   convex layers with convex gating functions in the student module, we exploit the well-understood
093
      theoretical foundations and efficient algorithms available in the convex optimization literature. Our
      method achieves better performance than non-convex distillation approaches even when using the
094
      same optimizer due to a more favorable optimization landscape. Moreover, the convexity of the
095
      student module allows us to employ faster and more specialized convex optimization algorithms,
096   leading to accelerated convergence and reduced computational overhead. This facilitates real-time
097   execution and on-device learning on resource-constrained edge devices.
098
099   Label-Free Compression Without Fine-Tuning: Our method focuses on activation matching
100   between a larger non-convex teacher module and a smaller convex student module on unlabeled
101
      data. We show that for many real-world tasks, convex distillation in itself sufficient for the resulting
      model to perform well at inference time, eliminating the need for any fine-tuning on labeled data after
102
      compression. This makes our approach applicable in scenarios where labeled data is scarce.
103
104   Bridging the Non-Convex and Convex Regimes: To the best of our knowledge, this is the first
105   work that marries the expressive representational power of non-convex DNNs with the theoretical
106   and computational advantages of convex architectures. Our empirical results on real-world datasets
107   demonstrate that convex student models can achieve high compression rates without sacrificing
      accuracy, often significantly outperforming non-convex compression methods in low-sample and

109   neural networks when leveraging rich features from pre-trained non-convex teacher models, opening

      2     OTHER R ELATED W ORK
113
114   Knowledge distillation (KD) has been a pivotal technique for compressing DNNs by transferring
115   knowledge from a large teacher model to a smaller student model. In this section, we discuss several
116   related methods, highlighting their advantages and the limitations that our approach addresses.
117
118   In their seminal work, Romero et al. (2015) introduced FitNets, where a thin and wide student
119
      model mimics the behavior of a larger teacher models by matching intermediate representations.
      By aligning the activations of a guided layer in the student with the outputs of the hint layer in the
120
      teacher, they prevent over-regularization and allow effective learning of the student on the training
121
      dataset. Since Fitnets were introduced, many methods have proposed modifications on top of them,
122   such as designing new types of features (Kim et al., 2020; Srinivas & Fleuret, 2018), changing the
123   teacher/student architectures (Lee et al., 2022) and distilling across multiple layers (Chen et al.,
124   2021). However, nearly all of them require access to labeled training data for fine-tuning, and the
125   optimization process remains non-convex, making convergence guarantees difficult.
126
127   Tung & Mori (2019) proposed a method inspired by contrastive learning: preserve the relative
128   representational structure of the data in the student model by ensuring that input pairs that produce
129
      similar (or dissimilar) activations in the teacher model produce similar (or dissimilar) activations
      in the student network. This approach improves generalization but is computationally expensive,
130
      especially with large batch, due to the quadratic complexity of producing pairwise comparisons.
131
      Further, training objective is a weighted sum of cross entropy loss between the pairwise similarity
132   activations and a fine-tuning loss on the training data, necessitating access to labeled training data.
133
134   Heo et al. (2018) introduced an activation transfer loss that minimizes the differences in boolean
135   activation patterns instead. This addresses the issue of the ℓ2 loss emphasizing samples with large
136   activation differences while underweighting those with small yet significant differences, which can
137   make it difficult to differentiate between weak and zero responses. However, their method involves
138
      optimizing a computationally intensive non-convex objective function.
139
      Recent advances have explored KD without access to the original training data. Yin et al. Raikwar
140   & Mishra (2022) proposed generating synthetic data by sampling from a Gaussian distribution and
141   adjusting the distributions within the teacher model’s hidden layers. While this allows for label-free
142   distillation, the synthetic data may not capture the complexity of real data distributions. Additionally,
143   their method relies on specific implementations of BatchNorm layers in the teacher model and focuses
144   on label-free distillation without addressing the challenges of non-convex optimization. Since our
145   method is only concerned with intermediate activations and not the input-output nature of samples, it
146   is also directly applicable on the synthetic data generated by their approach.

318   4.2 ACCELERATING D ISTILLATION USING C ONVEX S OLVERS
319   In the previous subsection, we discussed how one can compress a large model by distilling its large
320   non-convex modules into smaller convex modules. When the optimization problem is convex, saddle
321   points and non- global minima do not exist, facilitating faster convergence even with momentum-
322   based optimization methods (Assran & Rabbat, 2020). Therefore, convex formulations can accelerate
323   the test-time fine-tuning of the compressed model in presence of new data when compared to their
      non-convex counterparts. To push these benefits to the limit, we employ fast convex solvers.

      Given that the only constraint on S is matching the shape of the input-output activations with T , we
325   draw inspiration from the work of Mishkin et al. (2022) on 2-layer convex and non-convex MLP
326   models, and setup S as a 2-layer GReLU MLP along the lines of Theorems 2 and 3. While we
327   demonstrate the effects of convexity when using standard optimtizers like Adam (Paszke et al., 2019)
328   to train S, solving the resulting group lasso regression can be more efficiently handled by specialized
329   convex optimization algorithms. The SCNN Python library 1 provides fast and reliable algorithms for
330   convex optimization of two-layer neural networks with ReLU activation functions. Specifically, it
331   implements the R-FISTA algorithm, an improved version of the FISTA algorithm (Beck & Teboulle,
332   2009), which combines line search, careful step-size initialization, and data normalization to enhance
333
      convergence. We utilized R-FISTA as the convex solver in this paper.

529   6     C ONCLUSIONS
530
531
      In this paper, we have introduced a novel approach that bridges the non-convex and convex regimes
      by combining the representational power of large non-convex DNNs with the favorable optimization
532
      landscape of convex NNs. Our experiments show that the disillation via convex architectures performs
533
      at least as good as prevalent non-convex distillation methods. Furthermore, our approach successfully
534   distill models in a completely label-free setting without requiring any post-compression fine-tuning
535   on the training data. This work opens new avenues for deploying efficient, low-footprint models
536   on resource-constrained edge devices with on-device learning using online data. Future work could
537   focus on developing convex optimization methods to directly solve for optimal weights without
538   resorting to a one-vs-all setting for multi-dimensional outputs. Additionally, exploring applications
539   in other domains, such as natural language processing and generative models, could further validate
      and expand the applicability of our approach.

805   To improve upon the one-vs-all W2 returned by SCNN, we recompute it by using a group elastic
