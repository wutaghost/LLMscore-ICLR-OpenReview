               Current evaluation over large language model (LLM) generation is mostly focus-
012            ing on instruction following, which misses a critical aspect: even if a response is a
013            instruct-following generation does not guarantee its factual accuracy. This type of
014            following instruction but factually wrong hallucination phenomenon, as we called
015            Intent Hallucination problem, remains under-explored for current LLM evalua-
016            tion. To this end, we introduce FAITH QA, a novel benchmark for intent hallu-
017            cination that contains 18,068 problems, covering both query-only and retrieval-
018            augmented generation (RAG) setups with varying topics and difficulty. Further,
019            we propose that LLM’s intent hallucination problem can manifest in two granu-
020
               lated ways: minor fabrication, where the response introduces sentence-level fac-
               tually incorrect information or major fabrication, where the paragraph level of the
021
               response is entirely factually inaccurate or fabricated. We further evaluate vari-
022
               ous state-of-the-art LLMs on the proposed FAITH QA benchmark. Our analysis
023            on the results demonstrates that models exhibit varying degrees of omission and
024            misinterpretation, which leading to intent hallucination phenomenon. To facili-
025            tate future research, we further introduce an automatic LLM evaluation method
026            I NTENT D ECOMPOSE that (1) breaks the query into constraints, each assigned a
027            different importance label and (2) calculates an importance-weighted score based
028            on how well the response addresses the constraints. Our analysis shows that I N -
029            TENT D ECOMPOSE significantly outperforms the baseline.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      Large language models (LLMs)’s generation has been widely used for generation tasks (OpenAI
035
      et al., 2024; Dubey et al., 2024; Jiang et al., 2023). Nonetheless, evaluating their generation quality
036   accompanied with two major challenges. First, the generation could convey factually incorrect
037   statement; second, it could misalign with the query, meaning it may not fully or correctly address
038   the query. While there is extensive research addressing the second challenge, a instruct-following
039   generation does not guarantee its factual accuracy, leading to ”false-positive”, as shown in Fig 1. We
040   term this type of ”following instruction but factually wrong” phenomenon as Intent Hallucination,
041   which has been largely overlooked in current research (Ji et al., 2023; Balakrishnan et al., 2019).
042   The key challenge arises from the interplay between factual accuracy and query alignment. An ideal
043   response must not only fully align with the query but also be factually correct. Evaluating LLM’s
044   generation for intent hallucination is particularly challenging because (1) queries can be long and
045   complex due to task requirements(Liu et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2024), and (2) LLMs often provide
046   generation that appears to align with the query but contains factual inaccuracies. This can manifest
047   in two granulated ways hallucination: minor fabrication, where the response introduces sentence-
048   level factually incorrect information or faribation, and major fabrication, where the paragraph level
049   of the response is entirely factually inaccurate or fabricated.
050   Evaluating LLM generation’s factual accuracy while maintaining alignment with the query is cru-
051   cial. Most of today’s LLM applications, including reasoning, Retrieval Augmented Generation
052   (RAG), and Question Answering, depend on both precise alignment with the query and factual
053   correctness. However, instruction following (query alignment) alone is insufficient to guarantee
      the generation as an ideal response, as it may still contain factual inaccuracies. This phenomenon,

084   To address the first challenge, we propose that the two major scenarios of Intent Hallucination
085   lies in two types: non-paragraph level minor fabrication, and paragraph level major fabrication.
086   Essentially, when an LLM mostly addresses a query, it’s responses that either partially or signif-

088   FAITH QA, the first benchmark specifically designed to address the two key scenarios: minor fab-

091

092   a wide range of topics and difficulty levels, and has proven to be challenging even for state-of-the-
093   art models, also proving the prevalence of Intent Hallucination. We hope that FAITH QA will drive
094   further progress in improving query alignment solutions in the future.
095

096
      new evaluation method that focuses on assessing both a generation’s query alignment and factual
097
      accuracy. Our approach involves three major steps: (1) Decomposing the query by concepts and
098   actions, then converting it into a series of short statements, each representing a specific requirement
099   the generation must meet; (2) Assigning an importance-weighted binary label to each constraint,
100   allowing for a fine-grained evaluation of instruction following; and (3) Verifying the factual correct-
101   ness of the generation by self-consistency and Wikipedia check. Our analysis shows that I NTENT
102   D ECOMPOSEoffers a more comprehensive evaluation compared to pure LLM grading baselines,
103   effectively detecting both instruction misalignment and factual inaccuracies.

      2     P RELIMINARY
118
      As we introduced, detecting Intent Hallucination is challenging as it requires both factual check and
119
      instruction following. Here, we outline our two key insights for instruction following in this paper.
120
121
      2.1   I NTENT C ONSTRAINT: A F UNDAMENTAL U NIT
122
123   A query typically consists of multiple concepts and actions, each representing a distinct intent and
124   carrying specific meaning within the given context. Failure to address any concepts or actions can
125   lead to a hallucinated generation that deviates from query’s intention. Despite great efforts, most
126   previous and concurrent work either (1) focusing solely on factual precision or in-context recall,
127   neglecting the critical role of the query in generation(Li et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2023), or (2) con-
128   sidering the query as a whole, leading to coarse-grained evaluation of the generation, e.g., assigning
129
      equally low score to both generations in Fig 2.
130   To enable a fine-grained, query-centric evaluation, we introduce intent constraint – short statements
131   that each express a single requirement for generation to address (see examples in Fig 2). A query,
132   defined by the concepts and actions it contains within its context, can be broken down into these
133   intent constraints, with each one representing a distinct concept or action. Addressing each of these
134   constraints helps reduce the risk of hallucinated responses that misalign with the query’s intent.
135
      Meanwhile, since intent constraints are semantically derived from the original query, combining
      them ensures they collectively retain the original meaning of the query. Intent constraints, being
136
      more fundamental units compared to queries, provides a more fine-grained evaluation.
137
138   Definition. Let M represent a language model, q a query, and R = P (M | q) the model’s re-
139   sponse. We define the process of converting a query q into a series of I NTENT C ONSTRAINT C(q),
140   where C(q) = {c1 , c2 , c3 , . . . } represents the intent constraints derived from the query. Combining
141
      together, intent constraint set C(q) retains the original meaning of the query. Taking into account
      that the concepts and actions within a query can have varying levels of importance (e.g., subject and
142
      object), intent constraints are categorized into three subsets:
143
144          • Cm : Mandatory constraints that must be addressed in the first priority.
145
             • Ci : Important constraints that should be addressed after mandatory constraints.
146
147
             • Co : Optional constraints that are desirable but not essential.
148   Thus, we have C(q) = {Cm , Ci , Co }.
149
150
      2.2   I NSTRUCTION -F OLLOWING : OMISSION OR MISINTERPRETATION OF INTENT
151         CONSTRAINTS .
152
153   After establishing a fine-grained, query-centric perspective, we formally define Instruction-
154   Following as LLM’s failure on addressing word level concepts or actions, which expresses itself
155   as an omission or misinterpretation of intent constraints. When LLMs either omit parts of the query
156   (e.g., failing to address specific concepts/actions) or misinterpret it (e.g., responding to concept-
157   s/actions that is invented), it all reflect LLM’s failure on accurately capture the word level meanings.
158   Having intent hallucination as the fundamental evaluation metrics for Instruction-Following is par-
159   ticularly important when dealing with complex, multi-condition queries. Under such cases, a lan-
160   guage model might generate a response that only addresses most of the query while failing to address
161   the other parts. Evaluating the fulfillment of generation over intent constraint offers an approach to
      distinguish these nuance differences effectively.

193
194   I NTENT D ECOMPOSE consists three primary components: (1) Intent Constraint Generation, which
195   breaks the original query into a series of intent constraints, (2) Constraint Score, which assesses
196   LLM’s generation based on the fulfillment of the intent constraints, and (3) Fact Check, where we
197   perform self-consistency check for Fact and adopt Wikipedia as reliable source. We utilize LLMs
198
      for the both components.
199
200   3.1   I NTENT C ONSTRAINT G ENERATION
201
      In this section, we break the original query into a set of semantically equivalent constraints. Our
202
      method has high flexibility, accommodating different queries involving Retrieval-Augmented Gen-
203   eration (RAG). We introduce the process as following. Prompt Template can be found in Appendix
204   A.1.
205
206
      Step 0: Preliminary Assessment. In this step, the language model conducts an initial analysis
      of the given query to ensure the presence of all information to start generation. This step is cru-
207
      cial, particularly for RAG queries, as it mitigates external content influence (Liu et al., 2023; Wu
208
      et al., 2024) and identifies potential missing information. A failed Preliminary Assessment triggers
209   a request, indicating insufficient information within the query.
210
211   Step 1: Semantic Role Identification. Inspired by Semantic Role Labeling (Pradhan et al., 2005),
212
      the model identifies the fundamental components of the query from an action-oriented perspective:
      main subject, action, and context. This approach enables I NTENT D ECOMPOSE to flexibly accom-
213
      modate diverse query types and structures.
214
215   Step 2: Intent Constraint Decomposition. We first instruct the language model to analysis the
      context of given prompt over seven categories: location, time, subject, action, qualifiers, and quan-

      tity. Given the expanded analysis over context and the fundamental components, the model is then
217   asked to generate a series of intent constraints. Each Intent Constraint is a concise, explicit statement
218   specifying a requirement for the generation to address. Recognizing the varying degrees of signifi-
219   cance among the constraints, we further request the model to evaluate each constraint and assign it
220   to one of three hierarchical categories: mandatory, important, or optional. 1
221
      The final output is a series of intent constraints that captures the original query’s semantics, where
222
      each constraint is clearly labeled with importance.

248   3.3       FACT C HECK
249
250   Inspired by Min et al. (2023) and Wang et al. (2023), we adopt a two-step approach to ensure the
251   factual correctness of LLM’s generation.
252   Step 0: Self-Consistency Check. First, we instruct the language model to check if there is factual
253   incorrectness over the generation. We perform the check for 5 times individually, then select the
254   most consistent answer as the result. We performed manual evaluation before we decide to adopt
255   this strategy. Please refer to Appendix A.1.3 for more detail.
256
      Step 1: Wikipedia as reliable source. In this step, we perform knowledge retrieval for each gen-
257
      eration’s subject. In particular, we adopt the Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) framework
258   developed based on WikiPedia knowledge base (Semnani et al., 2023) to verify the fact check result
259   in the previous step.

      4.1     TASK
271
272   Here, we introduce the task design of FAITH QA Benchmark on minor fabrication and major
273   fabrication. We designed four tasks with varying complexity and topics.
274
      Minor Fabrication. This dataset focuses on the extent to which LLMs tend to generate a non-
275
      paragraph level intent hallucination. We choose open-ended multi-constraint FactQA setup here to
276   encourage LLMs generate longer output. An ideal response should generate a list of factual accurate
277   subjects, addressing all constraints properly.
278
279            • FactQA. LLM is provided with a FactQA question that consists with multiple constraints.
280              We control the problem difficulty by adjusting the number of constraints. The questions
281              are in Open Answer style, where the LLM is expected to generate a list of subjects that
282              satisfy the the query. We cover a range of topics across various domains, including culture,
283              technology, and history.
284
285
      Major Fabrications. This dataset evaluates at what extent do LLMs generate a paragraph level
      intent hallucination. We adopt Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) setup to better elicit hal-
286
      lucination. LLMs are given a query with multiple external contents, where the query could only
287
      be answered if all external contents are provided. For each case, we manually remove one piece
288   of external content, examining whether LLMs will fabricate the missing content. An ideal response
289   would detect the missing content and either ask for further clarification or refuse to answer the query.
290
291            • Response Evaluation. LLM’s task is to evaluate how well a user’s response to a given
292              query aligns with the external article. We treat the query, the user’s response, and the
293              external article as three distinct external contents; the task can only proceed if all three are
294              provided. When given the task, one of the three content sources is randomly removed. LLM
295              should not fabricate the missing content at any level and should refrain from generating a
296
                 response. The provided contents are from different topics: culture, technology, health and
                 history.
297
298            • Content Analysis. LLM’s task is to manipulate three provided external articles following
299              query’s instruction. There are two setups for the task: Relationship Analysis, where LLMs
300              are expected to analysis the relationships between the three articles; Content Summary,
301
                 where LLMs are expected to summarize the contents and compare their performance. The
                 task can only proceed if all three articles are provided. When given the task, one of the
302
                 three external articles is randomly removed. LLM should not fabricate the missing content
303
                 at any level and should refrain from generating a response. The provided contents are from
304              different topics: culture, technology, health and history.
305
306   For quality control, please refer to Appendix A.2.3.

      Hallucinations in LLMs. In the field of Large Language Models (LLMs), ”hallucination” generally
474   refers to instances where the models generate outputs that are nonfactual, irrelevant, or fabricated
475   outputs. Various tasks, including question answering Sellam et al. (2020), translation Lee et al.
476   (2018), summarizing Durmus et al. (2020), and dialogue Balakrishnan et al. (2019) have all observed
477   such phenomena, as noted in several studies Ji et al. (2023). Here, we defined and work on a
478   particular type of hallucination, intent hallucination, that has been widely overlooked by current
479   research.
480   Instruction Following Benchmarks. To tackle the challenge of enhancing models’ understand-
481   ing of complex instructions, researchers have developed several methods. For example, Sun et al.
482   (2023) and propose six strategies for creating complex instructions based on a small set of handwrit-
483   ten seed data. In addition, Zhou et al. (2023) utilize crowdsourcing to collect a limited number of
484   high-quality, complex user query-response pairs. Mukherjee et al. (2023) adopt a different strategy
485   by prompting GPT-4 to generate reasoning steps for simpler instructions, thereby adding complexity
      to the training data. Our benchmark is different by be the first complete open-ended benchmark that
