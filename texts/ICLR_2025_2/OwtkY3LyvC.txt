009                                                      A BSTRACT
010
011                Certified robustness provides a theoretical lower bound for adversarial robustness
012                and arouses widespread interest and discussions from the research community.
013                With theoretical support to improve the certified robustness on the training set,
014                practitioners endeavor to train a more certified robust model during inference on
015                the test set. However, the experimental neglect on the training set and the the-
016                oretical ignorance during inference on the test set induce a gap between train-
017                ing and testing for certified robustness. By establishing an equivalence between
018
                   the convergence of training loss and the improvement of certified robustness, we
                   recognize there is a trade-off between expressive power and generalization (as-
019
                   suming a well-conditioned optimization) for certified robustness, which is similar
020
                   to the underfitting and overfitting discussed in machine learning. To investigate
021                this trade-off, we design a new orthogonal convolution-Controllable Orthogonal
022                Convolution Kernel (COCK), which provides a broader range of expressive power
023                than existing orthogonal convolutions. Empirically, there is a power-driven shift
024                from vanilla classification accuracy to certified robustness in the sense of the op-
025                timal trade-off between expressive power and generalization. The experimental
026                results suggest that by carefully improving the expressive power from the optimal
027                trade-off for vanilla classification performance, the model will be more certified
028                robust.

031   1       I NTRODUCTION
032
033   The adversarial robustness (Szegedy et al., 2013) of Deep Neural Networks (DNNs) has attracted
034
      extensive attention from the research community. Theoretically, certified robustness serves as a
      lower bound for adversarial robustness against any adversary under certain constraints (Tsuzuku
035
      et al., 2018). Currently, though Multi-Layer Perception (MLP) trained on MNIST (LeCun, 1998)
036
      has achieved great success in certified robustness, neither MLP nor even Convolution Neural Net-
037   works (CNNs) perform satisfactorily on real-world datasets such as CIFAR (Krizhevsky, 2009) and
038   ImageNet (Russakovsky et al., 2015). In particular, the certified robustness degrades dramatically
039   during inference on the test set.
040
      At present, practitioners are devoted to improving the certified robustness of different network archi-
041
      tectures during inference on real-world datasets while most of the studies are establishing theoreti-
042
      cal guarantees on the training set. The experimental neglect on the training set and the theoretical
043   ignorance during inference on the test set induce a gap between training and testing for certified
044   robustness. Our paper aims to understand this gap and explore what actually affects the certified
045   robustness during inference rather than during training.
046
      Revisiting the fundamental problem in machine learning that there is a trade-off between expressive
047
      power and generalization (overfitting and underfitting) when assuming a well-conditioned optimiza-
048
      tion,1 we realize there is a similar trade-off for certified robustness as well by establishing an equiva-
049   lence between the convergence of classification loss and the improvement of certified robustness for
050   Lipschitz-constrained models. Upon understanding the trade-off for certified robustness, we define
051   the risk and accuracy for certified robustness following the corresponding descriptions in machine
052   learning.

      To explore this trade-off further, we focus on designing a tool capable of altering the expressive
055   power. At present, orthogonal constraints on convolutions (Singla & Feizi (2021); Li et al. (2019);
056   Trockman & Kolter (2021)) for the 1-Lipschitz property have achieved relatively competitive per-
057   formance in both vanilla classification accuracy and certified robustness on real-world datasets.
058   Nevertheless, owing to the strict orthogonal constraint on the parameter space, orthogonal con-
059   volutions lack enough expressive power to cover the training set2 and the flexibility to alter the
060   expressive power. Motivated by the Orthogonal Newton Iteration (ONI) algorithm (Huang et al.,
061   2020) that trades expressive power and orthogonality off, we design a new orthogonalization con-
062   volution kernel-Controllable Orthogonal Convolution Kernel (COCK) to provide a broader range of
063
      expressive power by altering the restriction to the parameter space.
064   Theoretically, COCK is proven to be more certified robust than other general orthogonal convolu-
065   tions owing to the improvement of expressive power. Empirically, the improvement of expressive
066   power enables COCK to cover the training set better and hence improves the certified robustness
067   on the training set. Both theoretical and empirical results align well with our understanding of the
068   equivalence between classification loss convergence and certified robustness improvement.
069   By altering the expressive power of COCK, we experimentally observe that the optimal trade-off
070   between expressive power and generalization for certified robustness is more powerful than the
071   optimal trade-off for vanilla classification accuracy during inference. Namely, there is a power-
072   driven shift from vanilla classification accuracy to certified robustness. By carefully improving the
073   expressive power from the optimal trade-off for vanilla classification accuracy, we can obtain a more
074   certified robust model.
075   We summarize our contributions as follows:
076
077          • We recognize there is a gap between training and testing for certified robustness. Further,
078            we identify that there is a trade-off between expressive power and generalization for certi-
079            fied robustness by establishing an equivalence between enough power to cover the training
080            set and the improvement of certified robustness. Similar to the fundamental framework in
081
               machine learning, we formulate the risk and accuracy in the setting of certified robustness.
082          • We design a new orthogonal convolution-Controllable Orthogonal Convolution Kernel
083            (COCK), serving as a tool to investigate the trade-off between expressive power and gen-
084            eralization for certified robustness. COCK alleviates the weakness of orthogonal convolu-
085            tions in expressive power and provides a broader range of expressive power.
086          • We empirically observe that the optimal trade-off between expressive power and gener-
087            alization for certified robustness is more powerful than the optimal trade-off for vanilla
088            classification accuracy. By carefully improving the expressive power from the optimal
089            trade-off for vanilla classification accuracy, we can obtain a more certified robust model.

      Certified Robustness for Lipschitz-constrained Models. Several studies have been conducted to
094
      understand and improve the certified robustness of Lipschitz-constrained models. Tsuzuku et al.
095
      (2018) established certified robustness for Lipschitz-constrained neural networks against l2 attack
096   and suggested that the Lipschitz norm and the output margin jointly determine the certified robust-
097   ness on the training set. Upon this framework, on the one hand, multiple studies (Cisse et al. (2017);
098   Gouk et al. (2020); Miyato et al. (2018); Qian & Wegman (2019)) intend to control the Lipschitz
099   norm of neural networks. On the other hand, optimization techniques (Lee et al. (2020); Ono et al.
100   (2018)) are developed to modify the training process by enlarging the output margin. All these
101   works are devoted to theoretically improving the certified robustness on the training set and empiri-
102   cally improving the certified robustness on the test set, leaving a gap between training and testing to
103   be explored.
104   Orthogonal Convolution. Orthogonalization methods regularizing for the 1-Lipschitz property re-
105   markably improve certified robustness. Generally, it is categorized into soft orthogonal constraint

109   niuk (2018); Balestriero & richard baraniuk (2018); Lu et al. (2018); Miyato et al. (2018); Yoshida
110   & Miyato (2017)) restricts the parameter space by directly adding an orthogonalization penalty to
111   the loss function during training. The hard orthogonal constraint (Huang et al. (2017); Singla &

122   Relation Between Vanilla Classification Accuracy and Adversarial Robustness. Adversarial
123
      training (Madry, 2017) and its certified variants (Wong & Kolter (2018); Huang et al. (2021);
      Zhang et al. (2021); Gowal et al. (2018)) which involved minimizing a worst-case loss (or its ap-
124
      proximation) using uniformly-bounded perturbations to the training data are the most well-known
125
      approaches in adversarial defenses. These approaches imply the existence of a discrepancy be-
126   tween vanilla classification accuracy and adversarial robustness. Upon this discrepancy, several
127   regularization-based methods (Raghunathan et al. (2020); Leino et al. (2021); Zhang et al. (2019);
128   Hoffman et al. (2019); Gouk et al. (2020); Zhang et al. (2019)) have been proposed to make a
129   trade-off between vanilla classification accuracy and adversarial robustness. Typically, Zhang et al.
130   (2019) decomposed robust error as the sum of classification error and boundary error and further
131   undertook the trade-off by introducing a weight factor. Our paper explores the discrepancy between
132   vanilla classification accuracy and certified robustness standing on the trade-off between expressive
133   power and generalization. We find an empirical relation between the discrepancy (between vanilla
134
      classification accuracy and certified robustness) and the trade-off (between expressive power and
      generalization).

476
477
      Our paper mainly connects certified robustness with the fundamental machine learning framework
      for Lipschitz-constrained models. The equivalence between enough power to cover the training set
478
      and the improvement of certified robustness for Lipschitz-constrained models exposes that there is
479
      a trade-off between expressive power and generalization (assuming a well-conditioned optimiza-
480   tion) for certified robustness. We provide key insight into understanding the gap between training
481   and testing for certified robustness. Empirically, we observe that there is a power-driven shift from
482   vanilla classification accuracy to certified robust accuracy in the sense of the optimal trade-off be-
483   tween expressive power and generalization. This phenomenon suggests that expressive power is
484   crucial for certified robustness both on the training set and on the test set. By carefully improving
485   the expressive power from the optimal trade-off for vanilla classification performance, we can obtain
      higher certified robustness.
