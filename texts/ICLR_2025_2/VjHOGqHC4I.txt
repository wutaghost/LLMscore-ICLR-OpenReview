011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            The Transformer architecture has revolutionized the Natural Language Processing
014            field and is the backbone of Large Language Models (LLMs). The Transformer
015            uses the attention mechanism that computes the pair-wise similarity between its
016            input tokens to produce latent vectors that are able to understand the semantic
017            meaning of the input text. One of the challenges in the Transformer architecture
018            is the quadratic complexity of the attention mechanism that prohibits the efficient
019            processing of long sequence lengths. While many recent research works have at-
020
               tempted to provide a reduction from O(n2 ) time complexity of attention to semi-
               linear complexity, it remains an unsolved problem in the sense of maintaining a
021
               high performance when such complexity is reduced. One of the important works
022
               in this respect is the Perceiver class of architectures that have demonstrated ex-
023            cellent performance while reducing the computation complexity. In this paper,
024            we use the PerceiverAR that was proposed for Auto-Regressive modeling as a
025            baseline, and provide three different architectural enhancements to it with varying
026            computation overhead tradeoffs. Inspired by the recently proposed efficient atten-
027            tion computation approach of Long-LoRA, we then present an equally efficient
028            Perceiver-based architecture (termed as Long LoRA Pereceiver - LLP) that can be
029            used as the base architecture in LLMs instead of just a fine-tuning add-on. Our
030            results on different benchmarks indicate impressive improvements compared to
031
               recent Transformer based models.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036
037   The Transformer architecture has revolutionized the field of artificial intelligence, especially in Nat-
038   ural Language Processing (NLP) Vaswani (2017). The recent success of Large Language models
039
      such as ChatGPT Achiam et al. (2023), Gemini Team et al. (2023), Llama Touvron et al. (2023);
      Dubey et al. (2024), etc. with their comprehension and reasoning capabilities, is a testament to the
040
      effectiveness of the Transformer architecture. Prior to Transformers, deep Convolutional Neural
041
      Networks (CNNs) had demonstrated amazingly well results in computer vision applications, how-
042   ever, their performance does not show the same effectiveness when applied to NLP. One of the
043   reasons CNNs are typically less effective in NLP is their limited receptive field, due in part to the
044   convolution operation. The Transformer on the other hand, uses the attention mechanism. This op-
045   eration measures the pairwise similarity between the words of the entire input sequence in order to
046   comprehend it.
047
      Consider the matrix Q, K and V containing rows representing the learnt position encoded (PE) em-
048   bedding of each token in d dimensions (i.e., 1×d). Then the attention A = softmax(QK T ) contains
049   the dot product similarity of each input token with every other token in the input sequence. For an
050   input sequence with n tokens, Q, K ∈ Rn×d and attention A ∈ Rn×n . The Transformer divides the
051   attention calculation into parallel heads, to be able to refine the learning and comprehend the contex-
052   tual meaning of the input sequence. Each head computes the attention on a portion of the embedding
053   dimension. The output in each head is computed by further multiplying the attention A with V . The
      canonical Transformer’s operation can be summarized by the following equations Vaswani (2017),

      For language models, if text generation is the goal, the model is trained in an autoregressive manner
      where it learns to predict the next token given an input sequence of tokens. In autoregressive gen-
072
      eration, the previously predicted token becomes part of the next input sequence. For autoregressive
073
      NLP models, the training process can be made highly effective by masking the attention matrix in
074   a triangular fashion so that future tokens are not visible. The triangular masking helps in creating
075   more (input, output) training pairs, as from an input training sequence of size n, n − 1 training pairs
076   can be created by simply hiding the next token one at a time. For NLP classification, the masking of
077   the attention is not needed, as the classification decision is made on the entire input sequence.
078
      Since the attention computation in each head measures the pairwise similarity in the input sequence,
079
      its time complexity is O(n2 ) if the sequence length is n. With larger NLP models being created
080   operating on longer sequence lengths, and with multiple heads in each layer and many layers, the
081   computational costs of Transformer training is becoming an important issue. Considerable research
082   effort is being put into making the attention mechanism more efficient since it is the dominant com-
083   putation. Numerous research papers have proposed ideas to reduce the quadratic time complexity of
084   attention to linear or sub quadratic complexity. Some of the important works in this respect include:
085   TransformerXL Dai et al. (2019), Linformer Wang et al. (2020), Longformer Beltagy et al. (2020),
086   Reformer Kitaev et al. (2020), Performer Choromanski et al. (2020), Long-Short Attention Zhu et al.
087   (2021), Perceiver Hawthorne et al. (2022); Jaegle et al. (2021); Jaegle et al., among others. Recently
088
      State Space Models Gu et al. (2021); Fu et al.; Dao & Gu have drawn considerable research at-
      tention and provided impressive results in some domains. It is yet to be seen if they can be better
089
      alternatives for NLP generative models. Thus we limit our comparisons to transformer-based archi-
090
      tectures in this work. We provide a brief background in the above related Transformer approaches
091   towards achieving lower attention complexity. Then we elaborate on the PerceiverAR Hawthorne
092   et al. (2022) architecture that we further enhance in this work.

094
095   2    R ELATED W ORK AND C ONTRIBUTIONS
096
097   One of the important works that reduces the quadratic time complexity of attention to O(n) is Lin-
098   former Wang et al. (2020). The authors of Linformer empirically observed that attention has low
099
      rank, and therefore can be approximated by a low rank matrix. To achieve this, the Q and V matrices
      ∈ Rn×d are projected to lower dimensions by using a learnable projection along the sequence dimen-
100
      sion. This results in Q, V ∈ Rk×d where k < n. Consequently the attention is A = QK T ∈ Rn×k .
101
      Since k is fixed the attention complexity is O(n). Note that the output A × V is still ∈ Rn×d ,
102   i.e., input and output dimensions match. Because Linformer compresses the information along the
103   input sequence dimension during the projection process, it cannot be used in effective autoregres-
104   sive training as the masking of attention for future tokens cannot be accomplished. However, for
105   classification problems where masking of attention is not needed, the Linformer architecture is ef-
106   fective in reducing attention complexity. Another notable work in the area of reducing the attention
107   complexity is Reformer Kitaev et al. (2020) which uses locality sensitive hashing. This reduces the
      attention complexity to O(nlog(n)).

      A popular important architecture that allows autoregressive modeling and handles long input con-
109   texts efficiently is termed as TransformerXL Dai et al. (2019). It follows a different approach than
110   trying to compress the input sequence. It divides the input sequence into segments and uses segment
111   level recurrence. After processing one segment, the model reuses the hidden states from the last
112   layer of that segment as the initial hidden state for the next segment. It also introduces relative posi-
113   tional encoding to allow it to handle longer contexts. TransformerXL presented excellent perplexity
114   results on benchmarks. Its slight drawback is the recurrent mechanism that may lose information
115   over long contexts. A mathematical approach to reducing the attention complexity is taken in Per-
116   former Choromanski et al. (2020) which uses random features and projections. The attention is
117
      decomposed into a product of non-linear functions of original query and key matrices referred to
      as random features. This allows the attention to be theoretically encoded more efficiently but the
118
      complexity of this encoding for long sequences may be higher.
119
120   In Longformer Beltagy et al. (2020) a different approach was employed which relies on sparse at-
121   tention in order reduce its complexity. The Longformer authors proposed different sparse attention
122   patterns such as sliding window, dilated sliding window attention and partially sparse global atten-
123
      tion. In sliding window, only the nearby tokens are attended to, whereas in dilated sliding window,
      a diagonal partially sparse attention pattern is used. One architecture that partially relies on sliding
124
      window attention in handling long sequences is the long-short Transformer Zhu et al. (2021). Here
125
      the short attention refers to the sliding window while the long attention divides the entire context
126   into small compressed segments. Both short and long attention are combined in the final attention.
127   Since the long attention is based on compression, it may lose some important contextual information
128   in an autoregressive generation.
129
      Another approach to efficient attention was proposed in PerceiverAR Hawthorne et al. (2022). It
130
      accomplishes efficient handling of long contexts by diving the input sequence in two components of
131
      history and latent. The query matrix is computed on the latent part, whereas the key and value are
132   computed on the entire context. This results in a cross attention in the first layer ∈ Rl×d where l is
133   the latent size. The output from the first layer ∈ Rl×d . The remaining layers operate on the l × d
134   which is smaller than the n × d size in a standard Transformer. For Transformers with many layers,
135   the PerceiverAR approach is quite efficient because of the smaller size being operated upon after
136   the first layer. Since the history component of the input is not used in autoregressive modeling, and
137   since it also gets compressed into the latent part after the first layer, we attempt to improve upon
138   these short coming via different architectural enhancements in this paper.
139   An important advancement in handling very long contexts has been recently proposed in Lon-
140   gLoRA Chen et al.. Even though it computes attention in a sliding window manner, it captures
141   the entire context via a division of the attention heads into two groups. It performs a shift in the at-
142   tention in the second group to propagate the attention information. We use this intriguing concept in
143   a different way in enhancing the PerceiverAR architecture to accomplish same goals as LongLoRA
144   in terms of attention efficiency.
145   1. We propose three enhancements to the PerceiverAR architecture to overcome the loss of informa-
146   tion that gets compressed into the latent part after the first layer. Each enhancement has efficiency
147   computation overhead tradeoffs.
148
      2. Inspired by the ideas of LongLoRA, we present a simple overlapping PerceiverAR segmented
149
      architecture that achieves the computational efficiency of sliding window but with the entire context
150   being available as the computation flows down the layers of the Transformer.
151
152   3. In addition to the efficient computation, the pairwise overlapping segment attention extracts more
153
      meaningful context resulting in a high performance architecture, which we empirically verify.
154   In the following section, we first elaborate on the PerceiverAR architecture before presenting en-
155   hancements to its design.

158   3   P ERCEIVER AR A RCHITECTURE
159   The fundamental concept behind PerceiverAR is to split the input sequence into two components
160   which we denote as the history and latent sequences. We denote the input as x with corresponding
161   sequence length n. After the tokenization and embedding is carried out, the input can be considered
      as composed of a history component and a latent component as: x ∈ Rn×d = xhistory ||xlatent ,

163   latent length as l. After the embedding operation, history ∈ Rh×d and latent ∈ Rl×d . The
164   PerceiverAR computes the query only on the latent part in the first layer of Transformer, while the
165   key and values are computed on the entire sequence length of size n. Thus the attention computation
166   in the first layer produces an output of dimension Rl×d :

      Since the output from first layer of Transformer ∈ Rl×d the remaining layers do a normal attention
174
      on inputs of size l, without splitting the input into two parts as done in the first layer. For auto-
175
      regressive training, an input of n tokens is used to create (n−1) training pairs, such that the expected
176   output of first token is the second token, and the expected token of inputs up to (m − 1) tokens is the
177   mth token. The PerceiverAR uses the history part as a fixed input, hence the autoregressive training
178   can only be done on the latent part of input. Thus, to hide the future tokens in the training of the first
179   layer, the upper triangular part of the attention matrix is set to −∞. The remaining layers operate
180   only on the input size of the latent length, so the triangularization of the attention matrix is done on
181   the square matrix corresponding to the latent part.
182   The attention architecture of the PerceiverAR is shown in Figure 1a. The additional details of layer
183   normalization, feed forward network and skip connections are omitted. The attention complexity
184   of PerceiverAR in the first layer of Transformer is O(l × n) while the remaining layers have a
185   complexity of O(l2 ). This provides a significant reduction in computation, especially when l < n
186   and many layers are used in the Transformer. While the PerceiverAR has been able to accomplish
187   impressive results on NLP benchmarks Hawthorne et al. (2022). It has two main drawbacks which
188   we address in this work:
189
            1. Latent Training Dependency - The training for AutoRegressive generation can only use
190
               the latent part of the input. Therefore, more training is required to accomplish the same
191
               learning as a normal Transformer (provided the model does not overfit).
192
193
            2. Lossy History - The history is implicitly compressed into the latent output of first layer
               and is not explicitly refined as in a normal Transformer via many attention layers.
194
195   We improve upon the above drawbacks and present three different enhancements for better utiliza-
196   tion of the history component in the PerceiverAR.

      4.4 L ONG L O RA I NSPIRED P ERCEIVER AR (LLP) A RCHITECTURE
360
      The complexity of computing attention is O(n2 ). One way to reduce this complexity is by dividing
361   the input sequence into disjoint segments and only computing the attention in each segment itself.
362   However, the disadvantage of such an approach is that there is information loss due to lack of in-
363   formation flow between segments. LongLoRA Chen et al. solves this problems via shifted sparse
364   attention (S 2 Attn). In S 2 Attn, the sequence length is split into different groups and each group
365   computes the attention individually. To support the information flow between different groups, the
366   attention heads are divided in two halves. In the second half of the groups, the tokens are shifted
367   by half the group size. This simple shift causes the information to be shared between neighboring
368   groups. The primary application of LongLoRA was demonstrated in applying LoRA (Low Rank
369
      Adaptation) to the self attention layers in extending the sequence or the context length of exist-
      ing LLMs. For example, LongLoRA can extend the context length of Llama-2 which is originally
370
      trained on a sequence length of 4K tokens by finetuning with data using 16K context length. This
371
      is accomplished by dividing the context length into 4K sequences. The S 2 -Attn in LongLoRA will
372   divide the attention heads in two groups, such that the first group conducts self-attention from 1st
373   to 4096th tokens. For the second groups, the tokens are shifted by 2048, such that the attention in
374   second group starts from 2049th token and ends at (4096+2028)=6144th token. This approach pro-
375   posed by LongLoRA does not increase computation complexity, but allows information exchange
376   between different groups. As the information flows through different layers of the Transformer, the
377   information exchange expands to all the segments causing the net attention to become close to the
      standard full attention.

      Inspired by the idea of LongLoRA’s overlapping attention, we apply this concept to enhance the
379   PerceiverAR design by dividing the input sequence into segments, such that the PerceiverAR allows
380   communication of information from previous segment to the current segment. This is accomplished
381   by first dividing the input sequence into disjoint segments. Next each segment is further divided into
382   two halves, a history component and a latent component. The standard PerceiverAR computation
383   is applied to consecutive pairs of half segments. Specifically, Q is computed on the current half
384   segment while computing K and V is done on the current and previous half segments. Thus the
385   PerceiverAR operates upon overlapping segments, with the overlap amount being half the size of a
386   segment. We term this approach as LongLoRA inspired Perceiver (LLP) architecture. It is depicted
387
      in Figure 3d. The ATTN block is a regular attention block which operates upon half of the first
      segment only. The attention equations governing the ATTN block with segment size s are:

408   and does a normal attention computation with triangular masking on the first half segment. This
409   is done so that even the first half segment can be used in autoregressive implementation, unlike a
410   normal PerceiverAR where the history part cannot be used in autoregressive training in terms of
411   masking this part. All blocks in a layer output data of 2s × d size. These are then catenated to form
412
      an output of size n × d. All layers in the LLP architecture are identical as shown in Figure 3d.
413   The main advantage of the proposed LLP architecture is that it provides identical efficiency in terms
414   of computations as the LongLoRA design. Figure 3a 3b 3c shows the sparse attention pattern as
415   it is computed in the first layer, and how each subsequent layer increases the attention receptive
416   field (because of overlap of half segments in the PerceiverAR blocks). As shown in Figure 3c,
417   after enough layers, the information from all previous segments is available to the PAR block as it
418
      calculates the attention on the two half segments. Thus we achieve a similar benefit in calculating
      the attention as Long LoRA. We also overcome the drawback of the PerceiverAR where the history
419
      component of the input sequence cannot be used in autoregressive training. In our LLP model, the
420
      entire input sequence can be used in autoregressive training, thus giving us the benefit of highly
421   efficient attention computation, full sequence length auto regressive training, and availability of
422   entire previous context as enough layers are used in the architecture.

742   7.3    V ISUAL E XPLANATION AND D ISCUSSION OF THE LLP A LGORITHM
743
744   In the LLP algorithm, the PerceiverAR operation is performed on a pair of overlapping half segments
745   Sij , where i is the half segment number and j is the layer number in each layer of Transformer.
746   This is visually shown in Figure 4. The effective receptive attention field increases as computation
747
      progresses down the layers of Transformer. For example, the calculation of the output in segment
      S4 in layer 3 is denoted as S43 and uses S42 and S32 from the previous layer (layer 2). Q is computed
748
      on S42 while K and V are computed on S32 and S42 . Note that S32 in turn uses S21 and S31 from the
749
      previous layer (layer 1), and S21 further uses S10 and S20 . The segment information accumulated
750   by S43 from previous segments is depicted with a yellow color in Figure 5. Thus, even though
751   the attention computation is local with size 2s × s if the segment size is s, the result is that the
752   propagation of information from all previous segments down the layers occurs. This is due to the
753   fact that PerceiverAR style attention between pair of consecutive overlapping segments is performed
754   in our LLP approach. The first half segment is treated as a special case (as it cannot form a pair with
755   a previous segment), and normal full attention is carried out on it with upper triangular masking, to
      aid in autoregressive modeling.

      Advantages of the LLP Approach - In the LLP approach, efficient attention computation without
      any loss of contextual information down the layers of the Transformer occurs. This is due to local
780
      pair wise segment attention, and overlapping segments in the LLP architecture. One reason, this
781
      approach performs better than full attention is that each layer in the early stages is learning to predict
782   the next token with less information. For example, in Figure 4 the predicted tokens in S31 (layer 1)
783   only use the information from S20 and S30 and do not use context from S00 and S10 . In layer 2, the
784   computation of S32 has information available from S01 , S02 and S03 (but not S00 ). The following layer
785   (layer 3) will have the information from all previous segments in computation of S33 . Thus, there is
786   no loss of information in the next token prediction of any given segment in later layers, but initial
787   layers are learning to predict the next tokens with less information. This is analogous to an implicit
788   dropout in the earlier layers. This may be a contributing factor as to why our LLP model outperforms
789   an equivalent full attention Transformer. In summary, the LLP approach combines efficiency and
790
      enhanced learning which results in a significantly improved Transformer architecture, as evidenced
      by the results on benchmarks.

               def LLPAttentionAlgorithm(x):
824                # b=batch, n = sequence length, h = number of heads
825                # compute qkv as in a regular transformer
                   # split heads
826                q, kv = map(lambda t: rearrange(t, 'b n (h d) -> (b h) n d', h = h), qkv)
                   # split q, kv into segments
827                qs = rearrange(q, 'c (s m) d ->c s m d', s = num_segments)
828                kvs = rearrange(kv,'c (s m) d ->c s m d', s = num_segments)
                   # catenate consecutive segments for kv
829                fcat = lambda t : torch.cat([kvs[:,t,:,:],kvs[:,t+1,:,:]], dim=1).unsqueeze(dim=1)
                   kvs_ccs = torch.cat([fcat(t) for t in range(0,self.num_segments-1)],dim=1)
830                qs_ccs = qs[:,1:self.num_segments,:,:]
831                lkv = self.norm(kvs_ccs)
                   # compute attention in all segments
832                attn = einsum('c s m d, c s n d -> c s m n', qs_ccs, kvs_ccs)
833                # masking for autoregressive modeling
834                m_size = attn.shape[-2]
                   causal_mask = torch.ones(m_size, m_size, device = device).triu_(1).bool()
835                attn[:,:,:,self.segment_len:].masked_fill_(causal_mask, mask_value)
836                # attention in first segment - normal self attention
837                attn0 = einsum('c s m d, c s n d->c s m n',qs[:,0:1,:,:],kvs[:,0:1,:,:])
                   attn0.masked_fill_(causal_mask, mask_value)
838                attn_0= attn0.softmax(dim=-1)
                   attn_1 = attn.softmax(dim = -1)
839                # apply dropout
840                attnd_0 = self.attn_dropout(attn_0)
                   attnd_1 = self.attn_dropout(attn_1)
841
842                # generate outputs by multiplying attention with kvs
                   out0 = einsum('c s i j, c s j d -> c s i d', attnd_0, kvs[:,0:1,:,:])
843                out1 = einsum('c s i j, c s j d -> c s i d', attnd_1, kvs_ccs)
844                out1 = rearrange(out1,'c s z d-> c (s z) d')
845                out_1 = rearrange(out1, '(b h) n d -> b (n) (h d)', h = h) # combine heads
                   out_1o = self.to_out(out_1) # project to compute output for the latent part
846                out0 = rearrange(out0,'c s z d-> c (s z) d') # merge segments
                   out_0 = rearrange(out0, '(b h) n d -> b (n) (h d)', h = h)
847                out_0o = self.to_out_0(out_0) # history component
848                out = torch.cat([out_0o, out_1o], dim = 1) # output both history and latent
                   return out
