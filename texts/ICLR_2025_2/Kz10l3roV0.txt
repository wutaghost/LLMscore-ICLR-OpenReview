                                                  A BSTRACT
013
014
               Due to the limited size of real-world time series data, current Transformer-based
015
               time series forecasting algorithms often struggle with overfitting. Common tech-
016
               niques used to mitigate overfitting include channel-independence and seasonal-
017            trend decomposition. However, channel-independent inevitably results in the loss
018            of inter-channel dependencies, and existing seasonal-trend decomposition meth-
019            ods are insufficient in effectively mitigating overfitting. In this study, we propose
020            DIMS, a time series forecasting model that uses multi-stage training to capture
021            inter-channel dependencies while ensuring the independence of seasonal and trend
022            components. The computation of channel dependency is postponed to the later
023            stage, following the channel-independent training, while the seasonal and trend
024            components remain fully independent during the early training phases. This ap-
025
               proach enables the model to effectively capture inter-channel dependencies while
               minimizing overfitting. Experiments show that our model outperforms the state-
026
               of-the-art Transformer-based models on several datasets.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Time series forecasting is widely utilized across multiple domains such as transportation, energy,
032   meteorology, retail, and finance. With the rapid development of deep learning, numerous studies
033   have applied deep learning algorithms to multivariate time series forecasting. These studies have
034   leveraged CNNs, RNNs, and various ensemble algorithms, which have achieved excellent results in
035   computer vision and natural language processing (NLP) tasks, for time series forecasting and have
036   seen some success. The Transformer architecture has recently demonstrated outstanding perfor-
037   mance in fields such as computer vision and NLP, as seen in models like BERT (Vaswani (2017)),
038   GPT (Achiam et al. (2023)), ViT (Dosovitskiy (2020)), and Swin-Transformer (Liu et al. (2021)).
039
      Consequently, many studies are now applying the Transformer architecture to time series forecasting
      tasks, such as in FEDformer (Zhou et al. (2022)) and Autoformer (Wu et al. (2021)).
040
041   In contrast to areas such as computer vision and natural language processing, which benefit from am-
042   ple datasets, real-world time series forecasting frequently encounters limitations in data availability.
043   Acquiring real-world time series data is inherently time-intensive, often requiring substantial peri-
044   ods to gather sufficient observations. For example, if electricity consumption is recorded hourly, it
045
      could take more than a year to compile 10,000 data points. This results in a significant temporal
      cost for acquiring time series data, and over time, the distribution of the data may change, indicat-
046
      ing that merely augmenting the dataset does not guarantee enhancements in predictive performance.
047
      Due to these inherent characteristics of time series data, certain counterintuitive phenomena have
048   been observed in time series forecasting experiments. For instance, many well-designed time series
049   forecasting models based on Transformer architectures perform worse than simpler linear models
050   (Zeng et al. (2023)). This is not surprising, as Transformer models typically require large datasets
051   to achieve optimal performance, which real-world time series data often cannot provide, leading to
052   rapid overfitting in Transformer models. Encouragingly, PatchTST (Nie et al. (2022)) has achieved
053   superior results to linear models by using a unique patching method and a channel-independent
      mechanism. The channel-independent mechanism ignores the relationships between channels (di-

      mensions) of the time series data, treating each channel as a homogeneous univariate time series.
055   This mitigates overfitting and improves prediction performance, further highlighting the importance
056   of mitigating overfitting to enhance the accuracy of time series forecasting.

058
      As demonstrated by the “simple” models such as DLinear and PatchTST, which achieve excellent
      performance, simpler methods and architectures often yield better results in time series forecasting
059
      tasks. However, “simple is difficult”, as it remains a challenge to simultaneously leverage the power-
060
      ful correlation-capturing capabilities of Transformers while avoiding the overfitting often induced by
061   time series data. In this study, we propose a channel-Dependent seasonal-trend-Independent model
062   using Multi-Stage training (DIMS) for time series forecasting. Our model harnesses the powerful
063   feature extraction capabilities of the Transformer architecture and accounts for the dependencies
064   between time series channels while avoiding premature overfitting.
065
      In summary, our main contributions are as follows:
066
067          • We propose a season-trend independent pattern representation and training method that
068            maintains the independence of season and trend components for most of the training pro-
069            cess. This method prevents interference between the seasonal and trend components during
070            training, thereby alleviating overfitting.
071          • The dependencies between time series channels are incorporated into the forecasting
072            model. We propose a method that delays the computation of inter-channel dependencies,
073
               integrating this calculation only after the model completes the channel-independent time
               series dimension operations. This ensures that the capture of time series features is not
074
               disturbed, ultimately allowing the integration of the inter-channel dependency module to
075
               improve prediction performance rather than prematurely causing overfitting. For datasets
076            that do not meet the assumption of channel independence, we modify the positional encod-
077            ing of the time series to further enhance prediction performance.
078          • We conduct experiments on seven widely used public real-world datasets, and the results
079            show that our model outperforms state-of-the-art Transformer-based models.

083
084   PatchTST (Nie et al. (2022)) divides time series data into several patches and introduces mechanisms
085   such as channel independence and parameter sharing. Crossformer (Zhang & Yan (2023)) employs
086   a patch division method similar to PatchTST, while also introducing cross-channel attention. Its
087
      proposed TSA model structure and attention routing algorithm between channels significantly re-
      duce the computational cost of cross-channel attention, which is crucial for high-dimensional time
088
      series data. iTransformer (Liu et al. (2023)) treats each channel of the time series as an individual
089
      patch, using feedforward neural networks to extract temporal features and employing a Transformer
090   architecture to capture cross-channel dependencies. In experiments with shorter input sequences,
091   iTransformer demonstrated notable improvements.
092
093
      It is worth noting that for time series forecasting, especially in long-term forecasting tasks, complex
      models do not necessarily lead to better prediction accuracy. Models like DLinear, NLinear (Zeng
094
      et al. (2023)), and RLinear (?) use simple linear layers to capture temporal features and adopt a
095
      channel-independent mechanism, which entirely ignores inter-channel relationships. These simple
096   models can achieve prediction accuracy comparable to, or even better than, more complex models.
097
098   At the same time, some studies have focused on the differing nature of the seasonal and trend com-
099
      ponents in time series data. Autoformer (Wu et al. (2021)) decomposes time series data layer by
      layer through a moving average method to extract periodic components. In its encoder, it applies
100
      self-correlation to the seasonal component while discarding the trend component, which is only
101
      reintroduced during the final prediction phase in the decoder. FEDformer (Zhou et al. (2022)) also
102   separates the trend component from the time series data. Its key frequency domain enhancement
103   module only processes the seasonal component while ignoring the trend, adding the trend compo-
104   nent back only in the final prediction output. The seasonal and trend components can be viewed
105   as the high-frequency and low-frequency parts of a time series, respectively. Consequently, some
106   studies have further transformed time series data from the time domain to the frequency domain for
107   analysis. FiTs (Xu et al. (2023)) uses the Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) to convert sequences into the
      frequency domain and applies a low-pass filter to extract the core features of the sequence. It then

109   performance using just 10k parameters. JTFT (Chen et al. (2024)) employs the Discrete Cosine

141   3.1     S EASONAL AND T REND C OMPONENTS
142
143   Existing methods for seasonal-trend decomposition typically input the separated seasonal and trend
144   components into two or more modules for synchronized training. While this approach alleviates
145   overfitting, it does not entirely prevent interference between the seasonal and trend components.
146   This is because when producing the final prediction, these methods often sum the predicted seasonal
147
      and trend components, causing the gradient of the prediction error to propagate back to both the
      seasonal and trend prediction modules simultaneously. Since the error reflects the overall time series
148
      and does not distinguish between the seasonal and trend components, it interferes with their accurate
149
      prediction, leading to faster overfitting.
150
151   To avoid the drawbacks of synchronously training the seasonal and trend components, we chose
152   to independently train the prediction models for the seasonal and trend components. As shown in
153
      Figure 1, the entire training dataset X is first decomposed into a seasonal component XS and a trend
      component XT . In the seasonal phase, the Transformer structure corresponding to the seasonal
154
      component is trained on XS. Similarly, in the trend phase, the Transformer structure corresponding
155
      to the trend component is trained on XT . After completing the training in both the seasonal and
156   trend phases, the two models are combined and jointly trained on the original dataset X.In the final
157   training stage, the data is first passed through a moving average module for seasonal and trend
158   decomposition. The decomposed data is then fed into the pre-trained seasonal and trend prediction
159   models, and the predictions from the two models are summed to obtain the final prediction result. It
160   is noted that while we emphasize Seasonal-Trend Independence during the early training phases, the
161   two components are not independent in the final training stage, which allows the model to capture
      the interdependencies between the seasonal and trend components.

      3.2   C HANNEL I NDEPENDENT OR D EPENDENT
163
164   To avoid overfitting, many recent studies have employed channel-independent methods for training
165   and forecasting multivariate time series. Although channel independence has brought significant
166   improvements, handling each dimension of multivariate time series independently while ignoring
167   cross-channel relationships can result in the loss of key information. This makes it unreasonable to
168   rely solely on channel independence for further improving prediction performance. However, intro-
169   ducing cross-channel relationships can indeed lead to overfitting more quickly. For instance, despite
170
      Crossformer employing several innovative techniques, it still cannot entirely prevent overfitting, ul-
      timately limiting its predictive performance. Nevertheless, Crossformer provides valuable insights
171
      and offers an efficient way to establish cross-channel relationships.
172
173   We believe that Crossformer’s introduction of cross-channel relationships exacerbates overfitting for
174   two reasons. First, the failure to decompose the seasonal and trend components leads to overfitting,
175   as trend and seasonal components often exhibit different characteristics. Without decomposition,
176
      the model struggles to distinguish between these features. In this case, introducing cross-channel
      attention not only fails to improve prediction accuracy but may also exacerbate overfitting. Second,
177
      introducing cross-channel relationships too early interferes with the model’s ability to learn temporal
178
      features. The most critical characteristic of time series data is the temporal feature within each
179   dimension. Therefore, if cross-channel dependencies are introduced prematurely, it hampers the
180   model’s learning of temporal features, negatively impacting the final prediction performance.
181
182
      To address the first issue, our proposed season-trend independence method effectively resolves the
      overfitting caused by the lack of decomposition. To tackle the second issue, we delay the calcula-
183
      tion of cross-channel dependencies until after the channel-independent computations are completed.
184
      The model first performs channel-independent attention calculations, corresponding to the tempo-
185   ral module, focusing exclusively on capturing temporal features within the data. Once the training
186   phase of the channel-independent module is complete, the cross-channel attention module is added
187   to the model, creating a structure similar to a sandwich. A second round of training is then con-
188   ducted, referred to as the “Cross Stage”. This approach allows the model to capture inter-channel
189   dependencies while avoiding interference with the learning of temporal features.

248
249
      In studies like PatchTST, each dimension of the time series data shares the same model parameters,
      which is reasonable for most datasets since the dimensions can be regarded as homogeneous. How-
250
      ever, this assumption is not appropriate for the weather dataset, as the data includes different types
251
      such as temperature and wind speed, which are not homogeneous. Therefore, in our model, we
252   modify the positional encoding for this dataset, replacing Edp with EdpC
                                                                               , which means that different
253   channels have distinct positional encodings. This approach allows us to map different types of data
254   into the same vector space.
