009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Decisions made using machine learning models can negatively impact individuals
012            in critical applications such as healthcare and finance by denying essential services
013            or access to opportunity. Algorithmic recourse supplements a negative AI decision
014            by providing rejected individuals with advice on the changes they can make to their
015            profiles, so that they may eventually achieve the desired outcome. Most existing
016            recourse methods provide single-step changes by using counterfactual explanations.
017            These counterfactual explanations are computed assuming a fixed (not learned)
018
               distance function. Further, few works consider providing more realistic multi-step
               changes in the form of recourse paths. However, such methods may fail to provide
019
               any recourse path for some individuals or provide paths that might not be feasible,
020
               since intermediate steps needed to reach the counterfactual explanation may not
021            be realizable. We introduce a framework for learning an optimal distance function
022            and threshold to compute multi-step recourse paths for all. First, we formalize the
023            problem of finding multi-step recourse paths. Given a set of feasible transitions, we
024            propose a data-driven framework for learning the optimal distance and threshold
025            for each step with PAC (Probably Approximately Correct) guarantees. Finally,
026            we provide a data augmentation algorithm to ensure that a solution exists for all
027            individuals. Experiments on several datasets show that the proposed method learns
028            feasible recourse paths for all individuals.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Machine learning (ML) models are increasingly being used for algorithmic decision-making in high
      stakes applications. Hence, when individuals are adversely affected by these decisions, the provision
034
      of transparent explanations for the negative decisions becomes paramount. For example, consider the
035
      scenario where credit line applications of bank customers get denied. The imperative for transparency
036   and explainability is further underscored by regulatory mandates such as the Equal Credit Opportunity
037   Act (ECOA), the Fair Credit Reporting Act (FCRA) (Ammermann, 2013), the ’Right to Explanation’
038   enshrined in the EU General Data Protection Regulation (EU-GDPR) (Goodman & Flaxman, 2017),
039   and the U.S. AI Bill of Rights (House, 2022).
040
      These explanations often take the form of sequential steps aimed at achieving desired or favorable
041
      outcomes for affected users. Such recommended steps represent algorithmic recourse, and provide
042   users with a pathway to address adverse decisions by gradually changing their profile to one that
043   most likely receives the positive decision. Single-step recourses frequently rely on counterfactual
044   explanations (CFEs), which propose changes to the input data that would lead to a different decision
045   outcome (Wachter et al., 2017). However, recent research has highlighted the limitations of single-
046   step recourses, advocating instead for multi-step recourse paths towards favorable outcomes (Verma
047   et al., 2020; Venkatasubramanian & Alfano, 2020). It is imperative that such recourse paths remain
048   realistic, meaning they should be both feasible and actionable (Poyiadzi et al., 2020b), in order to
049   effectively assist end-users. Furthermore, algorithms designed to provide realistic recourse paths
050
      should be able to provide recourse for every individual i.e., realism constraints should not come at
      the cost of no recourse for some individuals.

098   model-agnostic and only requires access to a classifier’s prediction probability output. Given a set of

100   approximate the true feasibility relationship for transitions. Using this learned feasibility relationship,
101   we provide a data augmentation technique that creates a recourse path for every individual that
102   initially received the negative outcome. This is shown in 1(c). The key characteristics of such a
103   path are 1) all intermediate transitions of it are more feasible and 2) the final point in it receives the

115   1.1   BACKGROUND AND R ELATED W ORK
116
117   For a literature overview, (Karimi et al., 2022) provide various definitions, formulations, and solutions
118   to recourse, and highlight connections to other challenges like security, privacy, and fairness. Here,
119   we discuss the methods most relevant to ours.
120   Counterfactual explanations as recourse: A common approach in literature is to treat counterfac-
121   tual explanations (also known as contrastive explanations) as recourse-by-example (Wachter et al.,
122   2017; Sokol et al., 2019; Ustun et al., 2019; Guidotti, 2022). Yet, recent work has highlighted the
123   limitations of counterfactual explanations for algorithmic recourse, emphasizing the need to consider
124   causal relationships between features (Liu et al., 2024; Bynum et al., 2024).
125
      The counterfactual-based approach when considered in its simplest form (identifying a minimally
126   distant counterfactual) relies on strong assumptions. Indeed, consider an individual who is subject to
127   an unfavorable AI prediction and who is given an example of another individual (real or synthetic)
128   as a ‘successful counterpart’ and a blueprint for future improvement. For such a recourse to be
129   helpful or reliable, one must assume that the suggested counterfactual data point is plausibly a “future
130   version” of the original rejected individual. This assumption is not generally true, as the algorithms
131   that identify a counterfactual typically only enforce proximity and sparsity between the counterfactual
132   and the original point. These methods neither specify the concrete actions needed to attain the
133   counterfactual state, nor do they consider causal process that governs how features change jointly
134
      over time. To assert that a counterfactual is likely or plausibly achievable for the original point, one
      solution is to use causal modeling, but as we detail later, this is difficult in real settings. Another
135
      approach is to rely on additional safeguards to ensure that a counterfactual might be realistically
136
      achievable. Path-based methods, which our solution is an example of, aim to provide such safeguards.
137
138   Causality-aware recourse: How can we make sure that a given positively classified point (i.e., a
139   counterfactual) represents an achievable state for a given initial point? The most principled approach
140
      is to deploy causal reasoning. Yet, methods that take this route have to grapple with prohibitive
      complexity of accurately modeling causal relationships on multivariate data.
141
142   In this line of work, experiments are typically limited to synthetic data on a few variables, suggesting
143   limited applicability of these methods in critical real-life settings (König et al., 2021; Dominguez-
144   Olmedo et al., 2022; Karimi et al., 2020; Dominguez-Olmedo et al., 2023). Furthermore, deploying
145   the machinery of do-calculus (Pearl, 2009) requires starting with a proposed intervention, and hence
146
      the methods for causally aware recourse must comb through large sets of possible changes that a
      person might enact, in order to then generate ‘feasible’ samples from each of the counterfactual
147
      distributions for each intervention. This is not only computationally expensive, but also non-intuitive,
148
      as ideally we would like to check feasibility of an identified target point, and not the other way
149   around.
150
151   A different approach focused on providing recourse for any differentiable machine learning-based
152
      decision-making system is in (Joshi et al., 2019). The method relies on modeling the underlying data
      distribution or manifold, but is not applicable to gradient-free models, which are commonly used in
153
      critical settings such as healthcare and personal finance.
154
155   Path-based recourse: In its minimal form, providing recourse might simply mean identifying a target
156   point corresponding to a more favorable AI decision. To find a better target, several methods rely on
157   building paths – sequences of point transitions between the initial point and one of its counterfactual
158
      points. This is known as path-based recourse. The existence of a path grants credibility to the end
      point of the path as a feasible target state. Indeed, it is more likely that a target state is achievable if
159
      we can describe a series of small (and hence arguably feasible) steps that lead to the target state.
160
161   Path-based approach to identifying recourse involves iteratively optimizing changes to an individual’s
      features, while controlling the radius to which each change is constrained to ensure that each


                                                           3
162
      transition is feasible. (Hamer et al., 2023) introduce Stepwise Explainable Paths (StEP), a data-driven
163   framework offering users interventions to alter outcomes, with privacy and robustness guarantees.
164   A prominent example of a path-based recourse algorithm is FACE (Poyiadzi et al., 2020a) – a
165   model-agnostic method for generating counterfactual explanations. Similar work includes (Small
166   et al., 2023; Nguyen et al., 2023). Despite its many advantages, existing path-based methods suffer
167   from two key shortcomings: a) if the distance threshold used to constrain the search for each ‘next
168   step’ is too large, recourse path may involve transitions that are either costly, unlikely, or infeasible
169   and b) if the distance threshold is small, the algorithm may fail to find a path for some individuals
170   The strength of our work is in addressing the shortcomings of existing path-based methods. Mitigating
171   failure to provide recourse is of particular importance, as such failures potentially trigger fairness
172   concerns. Consider data collected under racial or gender-sensitive selection bias. If members of a
173   certain demographic group are under-represented in either data class, it is likely that fewer group
174   members will receive a path to recourse, as FACE and similar algorithms rely on density to identify
175   suitable transitions. In this work, we take a position in asserting that everyone deserves recourse.

179   2.1   F ORMAL P ROBLEM D EFINITION
180
181   In our recourse setting, individuals are represented by feature vectors from some feature space I.
182   We are given a set of individuals V ⊆ I, and a trained classifier f : I 7→ [0, 1] together with
183   some threshold α ∈ (0, 1). An individual x ∈ I receives the positive outcome of the classifier iff
184
      f (x) ≥ α, and the negative outcome iff f (x) < α. We define Vp = {x ∈ V | f (x) ≥ α} and
      Vn = {x ∈ V | f (x) < α} to be the positive and negative individuals of the input set V respectively.
185
186   The goal of our problem is to provide actionable recourse to individuals in Vn . For an individual
187   x, we define actionable recourse as a path of feasible transitions x = x1 7→ x2 7→ x3 7→ . . . 7→ xk ,
188   where xi ∈ I for all i ∈ [k], the transition xi 7→ xi+1 is feasible and relatively “easy”, and xk is the
189   first positive profile (f (xk ) ≥ α) in the sequence. The recourse interpretation of the above path is
190
      that the individual can gradually change their profile, starting from x, and consecutively making the
      feasible change from xi 7→ xi+1 for all i ∈ [k − 1], they can reach a positive profile xk .
191
192   We assume that we are given a “distance” function d : I 2 7→ R≥0 and a threshold τ ≥ 0. Then,
193   x 7→ y is feasible iff d(x, y) ≤ τ , under the interpretation that the larger d is the more dissimilar
194   the two individuals that are compared. Note that we do not require d to be a metric. For example,
195   when d is not symmetric we capture directional feasibility; x 7→ y might be feasible while y 7→ x
196
      is not. To capture how easy a feasible transition is, we assume that we are given a weight function
      w : I 2 7→ R≥0 . For a feasible x 7→ y, the larger w(x, y) is the more difficult the transition. This
197
      work addresses two problems:
198
199   The Problem of Learning (d, τ ): Prior works assume explicit knowledge of d and τ to define
200   feasible transitions. In our work, we show how we can explicitly learn d, τ so that we approximate
201   the ground-truth transition feasibility function as optimally as possible.
202   The Augmentation Problem: Finding feasible recourse for x ∈ Vn corresponds to finding a path
203   Px = {x = x1 , x2 , x3 , . . . , xk } for some k ≥ 1, such that xi ∈ V for all i ∈ [k], d(xi , xi+1 ) ≤ τ for
204   all i ∈ [k − 1] and yk ∈ Vp . However, this might not always be possible. For that reason, we want
205   to augment V by adding a new set of individuals U ⊆ I, such that it is always possible to find a path
206   Px = {x = x1 , x2 , x3 , . . . , xk } for some k ≥ 1, with xi ∈ V ∪ U for all i ∈ [k], d(xi , xi+1 ) ≤ τ for
207   all i ∈ [k − 1] and yk ∈ Vp ∪ Up , where Up are the individuals of U receiving the positive classifier
208
      label. We also want the weights w(xi , xi+1 ) for consecutive profiles to be as small as possible.

      4    D ISCUSSION
505
506
      On the availability and construction of feasible transitions data and choice of constraints:
507   Ideally, every end-user subject to a models decision should be able to provide constraints under which
508   they can obtain recourse, and existing methods such as (Sharma et al., 2020; Ustun et al., 2019) can
509   include these in their optimization to find realistic recourse. However, having constraints for every
510   individual, especially in large-scale applications is challenging. While we show that our framework
511   is also applicable when provided a causal graph, often having access to complete or imperfect causal
512   knowledge (Karimi et al., 2020) is also not viable, especially for large scale datasets.
513   Instead, our paper takes a data-driven approach where constraints for the path (to learn the distance
514   function and threshold) can be provided based on domain knowledge by practitioners (eg., in finance
515   and healthcare). For example, individuals can only increase their income by ten percent of their current
516   income. While other methods that provide algorithmic recourse, several of which are implemented in
517   (Pawelczyk et al., 2021). can accommodate for these constraints, certain constraints can lead to all
518   these methods returning no recourse (eg., allowing income to only increase by ten percent might not
519   provide recourse to low income individuals). Instead, our method still allows for multi-step recourse
520   paths through augmentation, ensuring that a recourse path is provided.
521
522   On the choice of distance functions for algorithmic recourse: Seminal methods for counterfactual
523
      explanations (Wachter et al., 2017; Ustun et al., 2019; Sharma et al., 2020; Mothilal et al., 2020) and
      several others implemented in (Pawelczyk et al., 2021) use some lp norm to generate counterfactual
524
      explanations. Hence, we consider the L1 and L2 distance functions. (Chen et al., 2020) show
525
      that using the Mahalanonbis distance can capture feature interactions. Cosine and Jensen-Shannon
526   distances are not widely used in the recourse literature, however, they show that distance functions do
527   not need to be metric for our method to work. Other distance functions can also easily be incorporated
528   into our proposed method for distance function and threshold learning.

532
533
      We studied the problem of providing actionable recourse by suggesting multi-step transitions (recourse
      paths) to individuals. We presented an augmentation algorithm that empirically provides recourse for
534
      all. To strengthen the feasibility of recourse, we are the first to study the problem of PAC learning
535
      of the ground-truth transitions through a hypothesis class of distance functions and thresholds. We
536   see two main limitations in our work which are opportunities for future work: 1) for the hypothesis
537   class in (1), we can studying more expressive feasibility functions, e.g., feasibility measures using
538   casual constraints and 2) learning feasible transitions involved label generation ; future directions
539   will include using human annotators for labeling transitions.
