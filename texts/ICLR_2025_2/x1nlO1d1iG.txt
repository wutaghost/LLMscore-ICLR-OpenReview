009                                                    A BSTRACT
010
011                As large language models (LLMs) exhibit potential in solving complex mathemat-
012                ical tasks, increasing attention has been directed toward constructing benchmarks
013                to evaluate their mathematical capabilities. However, existing benchmarks are ei-
014                ther limited to specific task types (e.g., long-text problem understanding) or rely
015                solely on a coarse measure of answer accuracy, making them insufficient for as-
016                sessing a model’s authentic mathematical proficiency. In this paper, we propose
017
                   CogMath, which provides a comprehensive assessment of LLMs’ mathematical
                   abilities based on human cognitive processes. Specifically, inspired by cogni-
018
                   tive theories, CogMath formalizes the reasoning process into 3 stages that align
019
                   with human cognition: problem comprehension, problem solving, and solution
020                summarization, and encompasses 9 fine-grained evaluation dimensions from per-
021                spectives such as numerical calculation, knowledge, and counterfactuals. In each
022                dimension, to carry out a scientific evaluation, we develop an “Inquiry-Judge-
023                Reference” multi-agent system, where the Inquiry agent generates inquiries that
024                assess LLMs’ mastery from this dimension, the Judge agent ensures the inquiry
025                quality, and the Reference agent provides correct responses for comparison with
026                the LLMs’ actual performances. A LLM is considered to truly master a problem
027                only when excelling in all inquiries from the 9 dimensions. In experiments, we
028
                   evaluate 7 mainstream LLMs by applying CogMath to three benchmarks, which
                   cover the full K-12 mathematical curriculum. The results reveal that the authentic
029
                   mathematical capabilities of current LLMs are overestimated by 30-40%. More-
030
                   over, we locate their strengths and weaknesses across different stages/dimensions,
031                offering constructive insights to further enhance their reasoning abilities.

034   1       I NTRODUCTION
035
036   The rise of large language models (LLMs) has marked a pivotal moment in artificial intelligence.
037   Particularly within the realm of mathematical reasoning, these models have made breakthroughs
038   in solving complex mathematical problems. For example, GPT-4 has achieved over 75% accuracy
039   on the high school competition-level MATH dataset (Hendrycks et al., 2021). More recently, the
040   OpenAI-o1 model has surpassed 70% accuracy on the AIME math competition, placing it at a
041   level comparable to the top 500 US high school students 1 . This remarkable progress has not only
042
      redefined the potential of AI in mathematics but also spurred a growing body of research dedicated
      to evaluating and understanding the mathematical proficiencies of these models.
043
044   To systematically assess the mathematical ability of LLMs, numerous benchmarks have been intro-
045   duced. For instance, E-GSM (Xu et al., 2024) includes mathematical problems across four different
046   length ranges to assess LLMs’ generalization capabilities regarding problem text length. GSM-
047   Plus (Li et al., 2024) introduces eight variants of GSM8K dataset (Cobbe et al., 2021) to investigate
048
      the robustness of LLMs. MPA (Zhu et al., 2024) rewrites four existing datasets based on five prin-
      ciples, confirming that the mathematical abilities of LLMs may be affected by data contamination.
049
      However, on one hand, these benchmarks tend to be overly task-specific, requiring the construction
050
      of particular types of mathematical problems (e.g., long-text problems) to investigate one or some
051   specific aspects of a model’s capabilities (e.g., long-text understanding). On the other hand, they rely
052   on a coarse accuracy metric to evaluate the overall performance of models, without deeply assessing
053

      their internal mathematical reasoning processes. Consequently, they are unable to fully grasp the
055   entire spectrum of mathematical capabilities that LLMs possess.
056
057   In this paper, we propose CogMath, which offers a scientific and comprehensive evaluation of
058
      LLMs’ mathematical abilities by delving into the cognitive stages of reasoning processes employed
      by humans. Specifically, psychological research points out that humans undergo three stages when
059
      reasoning about a mathematical problem (Schoenfeld, 2014; Lesh & Doerr, 2003; Dehaene et al.,
060
      1999): problem comprehension, problem solving, and solution summarization, which can be formal-
061   ized as Eq (1). For a given problem P , humans first rely on a comprehension system fcomprehend
062   to grasp its semantics. Then, by combining the semantic information with the mathematical knowl-
063   edge K, the logical solving system fsolve in the human brain derives the answer. After obtaining
064   the answer, humans organize the solving process to form a complete logical chain and summarize
065   the solution into a coherent methodology, which is denoted as fsummarize .

068
069   Corresponding to the three cognitive stages, we design nine evaluation dimensions to ensure a scien-
070   tific and comprehensive assessment. Each dimension evaluates the LLM’s performance in one stage
071
      from perspectives such as computation, knowledge, and counterfactual reasoning. For example, in
      problem comprehension stage, we assess the model’s ability to handle different formulations of the
072
      same problem (e.g., paraphrasing or counterfactually removing conditions) to determine whether it
073
      truly understands the core meaning. In problem solving stage, we break down the solution into three
074   orthogonal aspects: problem-solving strategy, numerical computation, and knowledge application,
075   and evaluate LLMs in each aspect independently. In Solution summarization stage, we go beyond
076   traditional forward evaluation by introducing intermediate step questions and backward reasoning
077   tasks, testing whether the model can trace back through its reasoning pathway. Through these nine
078   dimensions, we can systematically gain insights into both the strengths and weaknesses of LLMs.
079
      Moreover, we design an “Inquiry-Judge-Reference” multi-agent system to carry out scientific eval-
080   uation in each dimension. The Inquiry agent is responsible for posing an inquiry about a problem
081   from this dimension. These inquiries either 1) ask about the original problem text or the problem-
082   solving steps, 2) rephrase the problem while maintaining the same difficulty and knowledge scope,
083   or 3) construct “pseudo problems” to test the model’s boundaries in counterfactual scenarios. To
084   ensure the quality of inquiries, we design a Judge agent for each Inquiry agent to evaluate and refine
085   its output. Besides, for each inquiry, we introduce a Reference agent to provide the correct answer,
086   serving as a standard to evaluate whether a LLM’s actual performance on that inquiry meets ex-
087   pectations. Compared with existing evaluations that rely solely on an answer accuracy, CogMath
088
      considers a LLM to truly master a problem only after excelling in all inquiries in 9 dimensions.
089   In experiments, we apply CogMath to the most representative mathematical benchmarks GSM8K
090   and MATH, along with an additional dataset we collected, MExam, which is composed of real exam
091   tests that cover the full K-12 curriculum. Then, we evaluate 7 mainstream LLMs including GPT-
092   4 (Achiam et al., 2023), GPT-3.5-Turbo (OpenAI, 2023), Gemini-1.5-Flash (Team et al., 2023),
093   Deepseek-v2.5 Liu et al. (2024a), Llama3-8B (Meta, 2024), Llama2-13B (Touvron et al., 2023),
094
      and Mixtral-8x7B-Instruct (MistralAITeam, 2023). Our key experimental findings are as follows 2 :
095
               • The authentic mathematical capabilities of current LLMs are overestimated by 30-40%. For
096
                 instance, GPT-4 has truly mastered only 39.7% and 67.1% of the problems in MATH and
097              GSM8K datasets, respectively. Moreover, this overestimation is not solely attributable to
098              data contamination, but rather to an excessive imitation of superficial patterns of reasoning.
099
               • We locate the deficiency stage of LLMs. Weaker models (e.g., Llama2-13B) still struggle
100
                 in problem comprehension stage, while stronger models (e.g., GPT-4, Deepseek-v2.5) face
101
                 challenges primarily in problem solving stage, particularly in their mastery of knowledge.
102
103            • Confronted with a counterfactual setting, current LLMs may exhibit an inherent “over-
104              correction” behavior, automatically aligning with patterns from the training data.
105            • Existing prompting techniques, such as CoT and ICL, may fail to consistently and reliably
106              improve the mathematical reasoning capabilities of LLMs.

      2     R ELATED W ORK

110   2.1   L ARGE L ANGUAGE M ODELS
111
112   Large language models (LLMs) have significantly advanced the field of natural language process-
113   ing (NLP). Models like OpenAI-o1, GPT-4 (Achiam et al., 2023), and GPT-3.5-Turbo (OpenAI,
114   2023) have set new performance milestones across numerous NLP tasks, such as sentiment classi-
115   fication (Zhang et al., 2024b), question answering (Hendrycks et al., 2021), and translation (Wang
116   et al., 2023a). To further enhance their reasoning and problem-solving abilities, several advanced
117
      techniques have been introduced. Among them, Chain-of-Thought (CoT) (Wei et al., 2022), Tree-
      of-Thought (ToT) (Yao et al., 2024), and Graph-of-Thought (GoT) (Besta et al., 2024) simulate
118
      structured and logical reasoning paths using chains, trees, and graphs, respectively, allowing models
119
      to handle multi-step problems more effectively. Program of Thought (PoT) (Chen et al., 2023a)
120   and PAL (Gao et al., 2023) introduce formal programming and have the LLMs generate executable
121   code, thereby performing more rigorous and deterministic computations. Another key development
122   in LLMs is In-Context Learning (ICL) (Dong et al., 2022), where the model can learn from a few
123   examples to generalize and solve unseen problems. In addition, there are many other key techniques,
124   such as self-consistency (Wang et al., 2023b) and Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) (Chen
125   et al., 2024). We refer the readers to a more detailed survey conducted by Zhao et al. (2023).
126
127   2.2   E VALUATION ON LLM S ’ M ATHEMATICAL A BILITY
128
129
      We categorize existing mathematical benchmarks from two perspectives: problem difficulty and
      problem types. In terms of difficulty, MATH (Hendrycks et al., 2021) and CHAMP (Mao et al.)
130
      are representative of high school competition-level datasets, while GSM8K (Cobbe et al., 2021)
131
      and MAWPS (Koncel-Kedziorski et al., 2016) are composed of elementary-level math word prob-
132   lems. From the perspective of problem types, E-GSM (Xu et al., 2024) includes four categories
133   of math problems of varying lengths to evaluate LLMs’ generalization on longer contexts, The-
134   oremQA (Chen et al., 2023b) and MathBench (Liu et al., 2024b) test LLMs’ ability to prove and
135   apply theorems, while MathVista (Lu et al., 2024) and GeoEval (Zhang et al., 2024a) focus on visual
136   reasoning and deeper geometric reasoning problems. To mitigate the impact of data contamination,
137   some studies introduce perturbations into existing benchmarks, such as GSM-Plus Li et al. (2024)
138   and MPA (Zhu et al., 2024) that consist of eight/five variations of GSM8K, respectively. However,
139   we argue that existing benchmarks are often task-specific, which rely on particular type of problems
140
      to test one or some specific capabilities (e.g., long-text understanding). Moreover, these benchmarks
      lack in-depth exploration of models’ reasoning processes, instead relying on coarse overall accuracy
141
      metrics, which makes it difficult to precisely identify at which cognitive stage the LLM encounters
142
      issues. Consequently, this limits the ability to provide interpretable guidance for improving LLMs.
143

145   3     O UR E VALUATION F RAMEWORK : C OG M ATH
146
147
      To achieve a comprehensive and scientific evaluation, we draw inspiration from how humans solve
      mathematical problems. Specifically, psychological theories indicate that human reasoning pro-
148
      cess consists of three stages: problem comprehension, problem solving, and solution summariza-
149
      tion (Schoenfeld, 2014; Lesh & Doerr, 2003; Dehaene et al., 1999). As formalized in Eq. (1), these
150   three stages build upon each other, with each stage taking the output of the previous one as in-
151   put. Problem comprehension involves analyzing problem P ’s information, such as word semantics,
152   text structure, and given conditions. Problem solving stage combines the problem information with
153   relevant knowledge K (e.g., the concept of word “half”, area formula of rectangle) to infer a so-
154   lution. Finally, in solution summarization stage, humans engage in self-summarization, reviewing
155   their thought processes, organizing clear logical steps, and forming a structured methodology.
156   Therefore, as illustrated in Figure 1, in our CogMath framework, we evaluate LLMs’ mathematical
157   abilities from the above three stages of problem-solving process. For each stage, we design multiple
158   dimensions to assess LLMs from various perspectives. For instance, to assess a model’s problem
159   comprehension stage, beyond investigating its accuracy after rephrasing the original problem, we
160   can explore its sensitivity to changes in problem conditions, such as adding irrelevant information or
161   disrupting problem sentences. Overall, for these three stages, we develop a total of nine dimensions
      that form a cohesive and comprehensive evaluation, with details presented in Table 1.

198   In each dimension i, to scientifically quantify a LLM’s performance, we design an “Inquiry-Judge-
199   Reference” multi-agent system. The Inquiry agent poses an inquiry qi related to the original problem
200   P that aligns with the given dimension. The Judge agent evaluates the quality of qi and repeatedly
201   invokes the Inquiry agent until a reasonable inquiry is obtained or the maximum number of iterations
202   δ is reached. The Reference agent generates an answer ai to qi , which is used to assess whether
203   a real LLM’s response to qi is correct. Depending on the dimension, the inquiry qi could be a
204
      question about the solution steps of problem P , a rewording of problem P that does not affect its
      difficulty or required knowledge, or a counterfactual question (e.g., removing a necessary condition)
205
      aimed at testing the robustness. Prompts for all agents are presented in Appendix A. For humans,
206
      truly mastering a mathematical problem requires a solid performance at each dimension. Hence, in
207   CogMath, only when a LLM passes all dimensions can we conclude that it has genuinely mastered
208   the problem P . Notably, these evaluation results also serve as a multifaceted analysis of the model,
209   revealing gaps between its performance in each dimension and human cognition.

211   3.1    S TAGE 1: Problem Comprehension
212
213   The problem comprehension stage serves as the foundation for the entire problem-solving pro-
214   cess. From fine-grained to coarse-grained understanding, it involves capturing the details of words,
215   phrases, and sentences in the problem, as well as translating the mathematical concepts, conditions,
      and definitions on a broader scale. To evaluate how well a LLM performs in this stage, we focus on


                                                                          4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      how it grasps the underlying implications of various perturbations made to the original problem and
217   responds appropriately. For different granularities, we design four dimensions:
218
219   • Dimension 1: Sentence Paraphrasing. If a human truly understands a mathematical problem,
220
      she will demonstrate a robust understanding of the problem’s meaning despite changes in wording
      or sentence structure. Inspired by this, this dimension evaluates the LLM’s ability to understand a
221
      problem that has been rephrased using different but synonymous expressions. Successful handling
222
      of this dimension indicates the model’s proficiency in grasping the core concepts and recognizing
223   that, despite linguistic variations, the underlying problem remains unchanged.
224
225   To achieve this, we ask the Inquiry agent to pose a paraphrased version of the original problem P
226
      as q1 , while preserving the mathematical essence (e.g., “Jacob had $21 ...” in Table 1). Since the
      answer to the rephrased problem remains the same as the original, the Reference agent can directly
227
      use the original answer as the reference a1 for evaluation. This ensures that the inquiry focuses on
228
      how well the model can interpret the reworded problem without needing to solve it differently.
229
230   • Dimension 2: Sentence Disruption. To prevent a LLM from simply memorizing the solution
231   based on the semantics of the original problem, we propose this dimension from a counterfactual
232
      perspective. To disentangle the impact of semantics on reasoning, the Inquiry agent randomly dis-
      rupts the word order within each clause of the original problem, creating a “pseudo problem” q2 ,
233
      where the words remain the same as in P , but from a human perspective, the entire problem is
234
      unreadable and unsolvable. In this case, the Reference agent does not need to generate an answer,
235   as the expected response is simply “unsolvable”, and the Judge agent is also no longer required to
236   make any judgments. If the LLM’s response to q2 is the same with the original answer, it indicates
237   that this model is likely recalling an answer based on certain keywords or patterns rather than truly
238   understanding the problem. Therefore, this dimension helps us assess whether the LLM is genuinely
239   solving the problem or relying on superficial clues (Sun et al., 2023).
240
      • Dimension 3: Missing Condition. For humans, understanding what the given conditions are in
241   a math problem is a critical step in the comprehension process. If essential conditions are missing,
242   we can recognize that the problem becomes unsolvable. Therefore, in this dimension, we still adopt
243   a counterfactual approach: if, after the removal of a necessary condition, the LLM is still able to
244   produce the original answer, it suggests that the model is relying on the semantic similarity to the
245   memorized problem to map out the solution, rather than genuinely solving it. As illustrated in
246   Appendix A.2.1, we ask the Inquiry agent to omit one key condition from the original problem,
247   presenting an underspecified version of P as inquiry q3 . The Judge agent needs to carefully assess
248   whether only one condition has been removed and whether the inquiry q3 does not alter any other
249
      parts of the original problem’s formulation. The Reference agent does not generate an answer, as the
      model should also recognize that q3 is unsolvable without the missing information.
250
251   • Dimension 4: Redundant Condition. In contrast to Missing Condition, we design this dimen-
252   sion that introduces irrelevant conditions into the problem. For example, an extra condition such
253   as “Before meeting ... make sure it was correct” might be added to the problem shown in Table 1.
254   A LLM that truly masters problem P should distinguish between essential and non-essential infor-
255
      mation, ensuring that unnecessary data does not interfere with the reasoning process. Therefore,
      the Inquiry agent presents a problem q4 with one redundant condition. The Judge agent evaluates
256
      whether the extraneous detail does not affect the solution to the original problem, and the Reference
257
      agent provides the original answer, as the added information should not affect the solution.
258
259
260   3.2   S TAGE 2: Problem Solving
261
262   This stage primarily involves three key components: solving strategy, numerical calculation, and
263   mathematical knowledge (Sweller, 1988; Jonassen, 2000). The solving strategy is an organization
264   of logical thinking specific to the problem, numerical calculation refers to arithmetic operations,
265   and mathematical knowledge reflects common principles that apply across problems. These three
266
      components are orthogonal to each other and together form the foundation of reasoning. To evaluate
      whether a LLM genuinely grasps these components, we design the following three dimensions:
267
268   • Dimension 5: Analogical Reasoning. The solving strategy serves as a commonality across differ-
269   ent problems, allowing a human to solve multiple similar problems using the same underlying logic.
      To this end, in this dimension, the Inquiry agent presents a problem that is conceptually consistent


                                                       5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      to, but not identical to, the original problem P , as q5 (e.g., “Tom had 21 comic books...” in Table 1).
271   This tests the LLM’s ability to generalize the solving strategy, demonstrating a deeper understanding
272   of the underlying reasoning thought. To be notice, q5 does not alter the problem-solving process. It
273   retains the same approach, difficulty level, and required knowledge, with the complexity and core
274   principles remain unchanged. Based on this, the Reference agent also does not need to generate a
275   completely new answer. Instead, it makes minor adjustments to the original solution, ensuring the
276   accuracy of the new answer a5 (Appendix A.4.3).
277   • Dimension 6: Numerical Transformation. Generally, the solving strategy represents the essen-
278   tial structure of solution, and the final solving step can be seen as plugging the numerical values from
279   the original problem into the strategy. Therefore, if a human has mastered the problem, changing
280   the numerical values will not affect the ability to solve it. Based on this idea, in this dimension, the
281   inquiry q6 is a variant of the original problem P that modifies its numerical values (e.g., replace the
282   numbers “21” and “100” with “30” and “120” in Table 1). Since q6 is a new problem, we instruct
283   the Reference agent to refer to the original answer and provide a corresponding new answer.
284   • Dimension 7: Knowledge Redefinition. Knowledge forms the foundation of human cognition,
285   guiding how abstract principles are applied during the solution process (Goldman, 1986; Habermas,
286   2015). For example, solving the problem in Figure 1 requires commonsense about the concept of
287   “half”. This understanding is flexible—if the problem redefines the calculation of “half”, a human
288   who truly grasps the concept will adapt her reasoning to fit the new definition. This process implies
289   that an authentic mastery does not simply rely on memorized facts but can adjust the thought process
290   based on the new knowledge. A model that merely relies on pattern recognition or memorization
291
      may fail when faced with new definitions, as it lacks the flexible understanding required to adapt.
292   To assess if a LLM can do this, the Inquiry agent adaptively modifies a key mathematical definition
293   within problem P by introducing a statement like “Assume ‘half’ means one-third of the given
294   amount” in inquiry q7 . This redefinition forces the LLM to adapt its solution based on the modified
295   concept. The Reference agent then generates a new solution based on the redefined knowledge, and
296   the Judge agent assesses whether q7 with the new definition is solvable.
297
298   3.3   S TAGE 3: Solution Summarization
299
300   After completing a problem-solving stage, humans often reflect on their reasoning process, sum-
301   marizing the steps they took and the methodology behind their approach (Cottrell, 2023; Dewey,
302   2022). This summarization helps consolidate the understanding of not just the solution, but also the
303   overall thought process, which can then be applied to similar problems in the future. In this stage, a
304   human that truly masters the problem can accurately recall intermediate reasoning steps and verify
305   the solution by working backward. To mimic these processes, we examine two critical dimensions:
306   • Dimension 8: Intermediate Step Questioning. In human reasoning, breaking down the problem-
307   solving process into smaller, manageable steps is essential for clarity and learning. Beyond evalu-
308   ating the final answer, assessing whether a LLM has precisely understood the intermediate steps is
309   an indispensable part of determining if it truly grasps a problem. Therefore, in this dimension, the
310   Inquiry agent presents an inquiry q8 that asks a LLM to explain one of the key intermediate steps
311   during the problem-solving process (e.g., step 2 in Appendix A.7.1). This ensures that the model
312   is not just arriving at a correct final answer by coincidence or pattern recognition, but is following
313   a clear, logical sequence throughout the entire solution. Then, the Judge agent checks whether q8
314
      corresponds to a specific step in the original reasoning process, and the Reference agent generates
      an explanation for this step based on the original solution.
315
316   • Dimension 9: Backward Reasoning. Inspired by Yu et al. (2024); Weng et al. (2023), backward
317   reasoning is a crucial and challenging mathematical reasoning ability. It refers to inferring missing
318   information by reasoning backward from the solution, mirroring how humans check their thought by
319   retracing their reasoning to ensure there are no mistakes (Rips, 1994). Therefore, it can be used to
320
      evaluate whether LLMs maintain consistency and logical coherence from both directions—forward
      and backward. If a model truly understands the problem-solving process, it should be able to perform
321
      this reverse reasoning without contradictions.
322
323   For this purpose, our Inquiry agent formulates inquiry q9 by masking a key numerical value from the
      original problem P and requiring the model to infer the missing value based on the original solution.

353
      To achieve comprehensive evaluation, we apply our CogMath to two of the most representative
354   mathematical benchmarks, GSM8K (Cobbe et al., 2021) and MATH (Hendrycks et al., 2021), along
355   with our constructed MExam dataset. GSM8K is an elementary-level math word problem dataset
356   that primarily involves numerical understanding and reasoning skills. MATH is a high school
357   competition-level dataset, consisting of 7 subcategories, such as algebra, geometry, and number
358   theory. MExam is composed of 6,353 questions manually collected from real exams, which covers
359   the full K-12 mathematics curriculum. For GSM8K and MATH, since their training sets may have
360   already been used in the training process of current LLMs, we apply CogMath on their public test
361   sets, which contain 1,319 and 5,000 questions, respectively.

450   4.5                  A NALYSIS ON N INE C OGNITIVE D IMENSIONS
451
452   Furthermore, we analyze the performance of LLMs in each dimension, which allows us to explore in
453
      greater detail the model’s robustness and sensitivity. Specifically, for each dimension i, we calculate
454   a Relative Pass Rate (RPR) defined as: RPR = |P ass      i ∩P ass|
                                                             |P ass|     . Here, P assi denotes the problems
455   where the LLM successfully passes their corresponding inquiry qi , and P ass refers to the problems
456   correctly answered in the original dataset. It is important to note that a higher RPR indicates better
457
      robustness and stability of the LLM’s capabilities in that dimension. This is because the model’s
      performance on corresponding inquiries is highly consistent with its performance on the original
458
      problems, making it less likely to exhibit defects when it answers the original problem correctly.
459
      Conversely, a lower RPR signifies a more detrimental impact on LLM performance, suggesting that
460   the model exhibits lower adaptability to that type of inquiry.
461
462   The results are presented in Figure 2. Overall, Deepseek-v2.5 and GPT-4 exhibit the most bal-
463
      anced performance across multiple dimensions, followed by GPT-3.5, Mixtral-8x7B, Gemini-1.5,
      and Llama3-8B, with Llama2-13B performing the worst. Secondly, regarding the four dimensions
464
      in problem comprehension stage, an important observation is that GPT-4, GPT-3.5, Gemini-1.5, and
465
      Deepseek-v2.5 underperform in Dimensions 2 and 3, even lagging behind Llama2-13B and Llama3-
466   8B. We speculate that this is because most training data for current LLMs is composed of solvable
467   math problems. After being trained on such data, when facing an unsolvable problem, current LLMs
468   may inherently “over-correct” the problem into a solvable one by rephrasing, adding conditions, or
469   reorganizing, aligning it more closely with their training data. This insight suggests that in order to
470   equip LLMs with more human-like cognitive capabilities, it is necessary to cultivate critical thinking
471   skills rather than mere imitation of training data. Thirdly, for the three dimensions associated with
472   problem solving stage, Dimension 7 accounts for the low pass rate discussed in Section 4.4. This
473   indicates that current LLMs treat knowledge more as rigid memorization and application, rather than
474
      integrating it organically and flexibly into the reasoning process. Lastly, in solution summarization
      stage, nearly all LLMs demonstrate higher RPR values in Dimension 8, suggesting that they are
475
      quite adept at explaining reasoning steps. However, the performance in Dimension 9 indicates that
476
      these models struggle to use conclusions to reversely derive conditions, which explain why they are
477   difficult to self-verify the correctness of their own answers.

      4.7                        E RROR A NALYSIS
514
515   From Sections 4.3 to 4.5, we verify that the primary reason for LLMs making errors in our CogMath
516   is due to their deficiencies in abilities corresponding to Dimensions 2, 3, 7, and 9. In this section, we
517   further investigate how the characteristics of the problems influence LLMs’ errors. Specifically, we
518   take the MATH dataset as an example and explore the relationship between problem difficulty and
519   the pass rate, as well as between problem length and the pass rate. Problem difficulty is measured
520   by the dataset’s inherent “level” labels, which include five tiers. For problem length, we divide all
521   problems into five levels using an equal-frequency binning approach.
522   From Figure 3, we can first observe that as problem difficulty increases, the performance of all LLMs
523   declines significantly. More specifically, most models only perform well on level 1 problems, while
524   only GPT-4 and Deepseek demonstrate proficiency on more than half of the problems at both levels
525   1 and 2. Secondly, as problem length increases, the LLM performance also shows some decline,
526   though it is less significant compared to the impact of problem difficulty. This suggests that problem
527   length has a relatively lower correlation with model performance. Based on these observations, we
528
      think future improvements in LLMs’ mathematical abilities could focus on enhancing their capacity
      to handle more complex problems, particularly those in higher difficulty levels.

      In this paper, we introduced CogMath, a comprehensive and scientific evaluation framework that
534   assesses the mathematical abilities of large language models across three cognitive stages and nine
535   dimensions of humans. The findings indicated that the mathematical capabilities of current main-
536   stream LLMs are overestimated by approximately 30-40%. Specifically, weaker LLMs like Llama2-
537   13B struggled with problem comprehension, while more advanced LLMs like GPT-4 demonstrated
538   an insufficient grasp of knowledge during problem-solving. Moreover, we verified that prompting
539   techniques such as CoT and ICL do not genuinely enhance the mathematical proficiency of these
      models. For future work, we discuss some valuable directions in Appendix C.

669   A    P ROMPTS IN C OG M ATH FRAMEWORK
670
671   The prompts for all agents across the 9 dimensions are presented in Figures A.1.1 to A.8.2. Notably,
672   in CogMath, the expected answers for Dimensions 1 to 4 are the original answers A of problem P ,
673   so we omit the corresponding Reference agents for these dimensions. For Dimension 2, the Inquiry
674   agent automatically disrupts the word order in each clause according to rules, and this process does
675   not require a special prompt or a Judge agent for evaluation. Hence, all agents for Dimension 2 are
676   omitted here. As for Dimension 9, as shown in Figure A.8.1, its Inquiry agent also automatically
677   determines the answer for inquiry q9 (marked with “[]”), so there is no need to design an additional
678
      Reference agent prompt, which is therefore omitted.
679
680   B    I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS .
681
682   All the Inquiry agents, Reference agents, and Judge agents are implemented with GPT-4. Besides,
683   the maximum number of iterations for Inquiry agent is set to δ = 10. If after 10 iterations, we still
684   fail to obtain a satisfactory inquiry, we consider the problem to be unsuitable to be evaluated from
685
      that dimension. For such problems, we omit consideration of that dimension during the evaluation.
686
687   C    D ISCUSSION
688
689   First, our CogMath framework is highly generalizable, as it does not rely on specific problem types
690   or formats, making it applicable to testing LLMs’ cognitive abilities in other mathematical tasks,
691   such as theorem proving. Second, our framework can be easily extended to tasks in other domains.
692   For instance, in visual reasoning tasks, a visual comprehension stage could be added into our frame-
693
      work, along with dimensions like image perturbation to evaluate the capabilities and robustness of
      visual LLMs like GPT-4v. Third, through experiments in Sections 4.3 to 4.7, we have conducted a
694
      detailed examination of LLMs’ mastery across different dimensions, providing valuable insights for
695
      future model improvements. For example, as observed in Section 4.5, existing LLMs may exhibit
696   an “over-correction” behavior when faced with unsolvable problems. To address this, we need to
697   introduce critical thinking mechanisms that enable them to reconsider the fundamental nature of
698   each problem, rather than merely imitating patterns from training data. Lastly, from the results of
699   Section 4.6, we found that CoT and ICL may not fundamentally improve the mathematical capabil-
700   ities of LLMs. However, these techniques have been shown to enhance performance in many NLP
701   tasks. Therefore, we believe that understanding the underlying mechanisms of these methods from
      a theoretical perspective remains a critical research question.
