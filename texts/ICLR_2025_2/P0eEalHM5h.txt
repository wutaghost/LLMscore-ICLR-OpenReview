011                                                  A BSTRACT
012
013            We study the problem of constructing an efficient LLM-based instruction-following agent
014            capable of comprehending and executing open-ended instructions in an embodied envi-
015
               ronment. We propose a method called LLMs Synergy for rapid domain adaptation in
               the instruction-following task without requiring additional manual annotations. This ap-
016
               proach leverages a large general-purpose LLM to establish task baselines and generate
017
               domain-specific data. The knowledge from the larger model is then gradually transferred
018            to a domain-tuned open-source LLM through a model transition process, enabling faster
019            and more efficient adaptation. Accordingly, we developed the Dynamic Instruction De-
020            composition (DID) framework, specifically designed for LLM integration within this task
021            scenario. The DID framework enables the agent to progressively align open-ended natural
022            language commands with dynamic environmental contexts. Experimental results demon-
023            strate significant improvements in task accuracy, leading to more effective instruction fol-
024            lowing and enhanced human-agent collaboration.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
028
      Recent advancements in Large Language Models (LLMs) have marked a new phase in the development of AI
029
      agents (Yao et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2023; Dong et al., 2022), facilitating more natural
030   human-agent collaboration. Traditional agents have often struggled to comprehend open-ended instructions
031   within embodied environments. The enhanced natural language understanding and reasoning capabilities
032   exhibited by LLMs offer promising solutions to these challenges.
033
      In this paper, we address the challenge of incorporating LLMs to develop instruction-following agents within
034
      a collaborative research environment, specifically the CB2 scenario (Sharf et al., 2023). CB2 is a real-time
035
      collaborative framework in which a human leader and an agent follower cooperate to collect matching card
036   sets in a shared 3D space. The primary difficulty arises from the differing perspectives of the human leader
037   and the agent follower, compounded by the open-ended nature of the instructions. This often results in
038   ambiguity, requiring the agent to autonomously interpret high-level guidance and translate it into concrete
039   actions based on its observations.
040
      While proprietary closed-source LLMs like Gemini 1.5 (Team, 2024), GPT3.5 Ouyang et al. (2022) and
041
      GPT4 (OpenAI et al., 2024), have demonstrated remarkable capabilities as general-purpose agents in solv-
042   ing a variety of tasks, their model opacity and limited accessibility hinder their applications in specific
043   domains. In contrast, open-source, low-parameter LLMs, which offer reduced computational costs and
044   greater adaptability, are often preferred for domain-specific tasks. However, these models typically face
045   an initial performance gap when compared to proprietary models. The key challenge lies in efficiently en-
046   hancing the domain-specific performance of smaller LLMs. This can be achieved through (1) the design of

      domain-specific execution frameworks that leverage LLM strengths, and (2) the acquisition of high-quality
048
      domain-specific data for fine-tuning. Notably, large, general-purpose closed-source models, owing to their
049   superior generalization, can contribute significantly to both strategies.
050
051   We introduce LLMs Synergy, a novel approach to rapidly adapt large language models (LLMs) for domain-
052
      specific tasks, leveraging the complementary strengths of both proprietary closed-source and open-source
      models. Our contributions are as follows:

      2.1   I NSTRUCTION F OLLOWING
072
073   Recent advancements in instruction following for robotics have demonstrated notable progress, particularly
074   in the comprehension and execution of specific commands (Huang et al., 2022; Ahn et al., 2022). Nonethe-
075   less, substantial challenges remain in handling open-ended instructions and dynamic environments, as these
076   scenarios demand a deeper integration of real-world commonsense reasoning and the ability to process com-
077   plex natural language instructions within context (MAV, 2015). Furthermore, existing instruction-following
078   models are typically trained on narrowly defined tasks, limiting their generalization capabilities (Chen et al.,
079   2023), and frequently lacking the contextual awareness required for robust decision-making (Wang et al.,
080
      2021). This issue is exacerbated by the reliance on large-scale annotated datasets (Shridhar et al., 2020;
      Misra et al., 2017; Suhr et al., 2018), which are both costly and labor-intensive to curate. In contrast, our
081
      approach mitigates these limitations by leveraging the broader generalization and generative capacities of
082
      large proprietary models, thereby reducing the dependency on extensive annotated data.
083
084
085   2.2   LLM- BASED AGENT
086
      Many prior works have explored various methods for using frozen LLMs to build agents across different
087
      domains. A significant amount of research focuses on prompting techniques (Wei et al., 2022; Yao et al.,
088
      2024; 2022; Dong et al., 2022) to enhance the performance of large foundation models in specific domains.
089   Among them, in-context learning, which incorporates feedback from the environment, is commonly used.
090   For instance, Voyager (Wang et al., 2023) and Ghost(Zhu et al., 2023) iteratively prompt LLMs to regenerate
091   action code based on error messages, continually refining the prompt with this information. Our approach
092   differs from these prompting-based methods by conducting domain-specific fine-tuning at a lower cost,
093   which leads to more robust and controllable model performance.

                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


094
      Additionally, some works have concentrated on supervised fine-tuning, for example, E2WM (Xiang et al.,
095
      2024) and LLAMARider (Feng et al., 2023) focus on collecting high-quality data to fine-tune LLMs. Specif-
096   ically, E2WM gathers embodied experience in VirtualHome using Monte Carlo Tree Search and random
097   exploration, while LLAMARider collects experience in the game engine Minecraft via self-reflection with
098   feedback. Both approaches illustrate that fine-tuning on collected experiences significantly improves LLMs’
099   capability to address tasks within their respective environments. However, these methods often necessitate
100   substantial effort to amass environment-specific data due to initial model performance limitations, thereby
101   requiring extensive searches for high-quality data. Our approach mitigates this challenge by employing
102   larger models to swiftly establish a baseline, facilitating the efficient accumulation of data to guide the fine-
103   tuning of smaller models.

105   3   C OLLABORATIVE E NVIRONMENT
106
107   Overview In this study, we explore human-agent collaboration in the CB2 scenario Sharf et al. (2023), a
108   real-time collaborative environment where a human leader and an agent follower work together to collect
109   matching card sets in a shared 3D space.
110   A valid set comprises three cards, each differing in color, shape, and count. When the selected cards in
111   the environment form a valid set, the players are awarded a point. The game is turn-based, with a limited
112   number of total turns. The leader plans and provides instructions in natural language, while the follower
113   executes these commands. The goal is to maximize the final score by successfully collecting card sets
114   through effective collaboration, where the task score reflects the efficiency of the cooperation. More details
115   of the environment are provided in Appendix A.
116   This game involves two key aspects: the Observability Gap and the Ability Gap. The leader has an over-
117   head view of the environment, while the follower sees only from a first-person perspective, with some card
118   patterns hidden. As a result, the descriptions within leader’s instructions focus on the surroundings rather
119   than specific details, such as “the card near the stone” or “the card between the lake and the yellow house”.
120   Additionally, the follower has greater mobility, covering more ground per turn, making task success mainly
121   dependent on the follower’s ability to correctly execute each instruction.
122   CB2’s design effectively captures real-world collaborative challenges. Differences in perspective and the
123   use of open-ended language can lead to ambiguous or unclear instructions, sometimes with inaccuracies.
124   This necessitates that the follower autonomously interpret and translate high-level instruction into concrete
125   actions based on their observations.
126
      Data The CB2 research team also released a dataset of human-human interactions, where trained human
127
      workers excel as leaders and followers. This dataset includes leader instructions, follower actions, final card
128   selections, and game states including map information and final scores. The data is divided into two parts:
129   training and evaluation sets. The training data, consisting of 185 games and 3,439 instructions, can be used
130   for model development, while the evaluation set of 187 games and 3,404 instructions is used for comparing
131   agent’s performance. Each instruction is recorded with the human leader’s instruction x, follower’s first-view
132   map map, the follower’s position pos and the states of all cards C during execution.

      4.1       DYNAMIC I NSTRUCTION D ECOMPOSITION F RAMEWORK
179
180   To address the discussed challenges in embodied instruction grounding and execution, we design the Dy-
181   namic Instruction Decomposition (DID) framework. It leverages the LLM’s language comprehension to
182   decompose instructions and dynamically align them with the evolving environmental context through pro-
183   gressive exploration. A domain-specific prompt PF (detailed in next section) is designed to guide the LLM
184   in breaking down the original instruction into two types of tasks:
185
186                • Immediate Tasks: Tasks can be completed right away within the current contexts
187                • Deferred Tasks: Tasks require a change in perspective to gain additional information.

      Figure 2 illustrates the entire process of instruction decomposition. When the Leader issues a new instruc-
189
      tion, the instruction, together with a structured text sequence representing the current first-person view, is
190   fed to the LLM. The LLM then decomposes the instruction into Immediate Task and Deferred Tasks based
191   on the current input. Next, the agent executes the immediate task in the environment and updates the first-
192   person view map. If any deferred task remains, it will be input to the LLM along with the updated map for
193   the next decomposition. The agent iteratively repeats the decomposition and execution process until all tasks
194   are completed. The initial testing with LLMs demonstrated promising results in the decomposition of open-
195   ended instructions. However, the decomposed tasks sometimes can not be converted into executable actions
196   in the environment. While LLMs excel in reasoning, they may lack precision in tasks such as numerical
197   calculations and format-specific mapping. Therefore, leveraging the LLM’s function-calling capability, we
198
      equipped the agent with tools for accurate map-based action generation. These include: A Path Planner to
      generate movement sequences (e.g., ‘Forward, Turn left’) to guide the agent towards a specific position and
199
      an Executable Action Converter to transform other types of tasks into executable actions. The implementa-
200
      tion details of these two tools can be found in Appendix C. The agent’s execution process is also outlined in
201   Algorithm 1. A vivid example of the dynamic instruction decomposition process is shown in Appendix D.
202
203   Through rapid adjustments and testing of the framework, even with a small subset of training samples,
204
      the LLM-enhanced framework has already demonstrated competitive performance compared to traditional
      methods that rely on extensive data. Unlike traditional behavioral cloning models, Our LLM-based instruc-
205
      tion follower agent clearly demonstrates agent characteristics: it operates on open-ended instructions and
206
      goals, reasons about them, formulates plans, utilizes tools and interacts with dynamic environments.

      4.2     M ODEL T RANSITION
236
237   In this section, we will demonstrate how to iteratively fine-tune a domain-specific small-scale LLM by
238   gradually transferring knowledge from a larger general-purpose model. The larger model would play a
239   crucial part in the construction of the fine-tuning dataset, helping to eliminate the need for additional manual
240   annotations.
241   Basic Dataset Construction The previously developed agent follower, a general-purpose LLM integrated
242   into the DID framework, is used for data labeling by generating decomposition outputs (yI , yD ) given an
243   instruction and the according first-view map (x, map). The training set in CB2 would be served as input
244   dataset X. Specifically, the game state for each instruction is loaded to initialize the environment, where
245   the agent follower decomposes the instruction and interacts with the environment until the task is completed
246   or the step limit is reached, and intermediate data tuples (x, map, yI , yD ) are collected. Human follower
247   performance is then used as the ground truth to evaluate the agent’s execution and filter out invalid data. By
248   comparing card states post-execution, only matching intermediate data is retained, forming the basic training
249
      dataset D1lab during the process.This process can be formulated as follows:

333
334   Metrics The agent’s performance is evaluated against human performance at the instruction level using the
335   evaluation dataset of human-human game records released along with the CB2 platform. Mean card state
336
      accuracy is assessed by comparing the final states of the cards between the human and agent, with only
      exact matches considered as correct. We also compare the mean distance of the final position between
337
      human followers and the agent across all instructions. This metric complements the card state accuracy by
338
      assessing the efficiency of movements.
339
340   Data However, directly using all human follower execution results from the evaluation set as ground truth
341   for comparison may lead to certain errors, as some instructions may have poor execution outcomes even by
342
      human followers. To enhance evaluation data quality, the poorly executed instructions would be removed
      automatically based on two criteria: those canceled by the leader during execution and those with no changes
343
      in the card set before and after execution. This filtering process resulted in the CB2-Eval dataset, with 1,417
344
      remaining test instructions from 109 games. However, during the evaluation, we observed that the filtered
345   dataset still has has some limitations. There are two types of issue: the follower selected a card but did not
346   do so correctly, and the leader’s instructions were unclear or ambiguous to understand. The automated rules
347   could not filter out these errors. To ensure rigor in the evaluation, we further filtered out these problematic
348   instructions and constructed a higher-quality evaluation dataset with 786 instructions from 97 games, CB2-
349   Eval-Filtered. This dataset will also be released to facilitate more objective and accurate evaluations.
350
      To ensure objectivity in our evaluations, we will report results on both datasets, CB2-Eval and CB2-Eval-
351   Filtered, when comparing with other instruction-following methods. For internal comparisons, as CB2-Eval-
352   Filtered is more reliable, we will conduct a more detailed analysis only on this dataset.

      A    D ETAILS OF CB2
548
549
550
      The collaboration in CB2 interaction involves two agents: a leader and a follower, who work together to
      complete tasks but differ in their environment observations and abilities. Both agents can move between
551
      adjacent hexagons or turn in place to change orientation. They interact with cards by moving over them to
552
      select or deselect .The goal is for the agents to select valid sets of cards. A valid set consists of three cards
553   with unique combinations of color, shape, and count. When a valid set is selected, the cards disappear, and
554   the agents earn one point. Three new random cards appear in random positions, and the agents receive extra
555   turns, though the number of additional turns decreases after each set completion.
556
      As shown in Figure 3 The leader has a full overhead view of the environment, while the follower only
557
      sees what’s directly ahead from a first-person perspective. Initially, the patterns on unselected cards are
558
      hidden from the follower, showing a question mark instead. The agents take turns, with each turn allowing
559   a limited number of steps. Every movement (forward, left, right, or backward) consumes one step. Turns
560   are time-limited to keep the interaction dynamic and minimize waiting for the other agent. The time limit
561   can be adjusted, but typically, the leader is given more time to plan their moves. Turns alternate between the
562   follower and the leader. Instruction writing and sending by the leader, and marking them as complete by the
563   follower do not consume steps
