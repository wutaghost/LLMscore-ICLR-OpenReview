009                                                   A BSTRACT
010
011                The rapid expansion of large language models (LLMs) has underscored the need
012                for parameter-efficient fine-tuning methods, with LoRA (Low-Rank Adaptation)
013                emerging as a popular solution. Although LoRA reduces the number of train-
014                able parameters, serving multiple (task or user-specific) LoRA modules on top
015                of a base model still creates significant storage challenges. To address this, us-
016
                   ing theoretical derivation, we introduce LoRA-XS (Low-Rank Adaptation with
                   eXtremely Small number of parameters), a novel low-rank adaptation method that
017
                   considerably reduces the trainable parameters while showing superior or compet-
018
                   itive performance. LoRA-XS achieves this by inserting a small, trainable r × r
019                weight matrix between frozen low-rank matrices, which are constructed by Sin-
020                gular Value Decomposition (SVD) of the original weight matrix. This lightweight
021                matrix enables fine-tuning with drastically reduced storage requirements, making
022                it feasible to deploy millions of personalized models while minimizing memory
023                overhead. For instance, LoRA-XS achieves a remarkable reduction of trainable
024                parameters by over 100x in 7B models compared to LoRA. Our evaluations across
025                various benchmarks (including GLUE, GSM8K, MATH, and eight commonsense
026                reasoning datasets) demonstrate that LoRA-XS performs competitively or better
027
                   than LoRA and other recent methods like VeRA while being significantly more
                   parameter efficient. We also provide an extensive ablation study on the impor-
028
                   tance of singular vectors in transformer weights, shedding light on the underlying
029
                   mechanisms driving LoRA-XS’s enhanced efficiency. These findings suggest that
030                LoRA-XS is not only a storage-efficient alternative, but also a powerful tool for
031                scaling and personalizing LLMs at unprecedented scales.1

034   1       I NTRODUCTION
035
036   In recent years, the development of large language models (LLM) has revolutionized the field of nat-
037
      ural language processing (NLP), enabling unprecedented performance across various tasks. How-
      ever, these state-of-the-art models often come with a huge number of parameters, presenting signif-
038
      icant challenges for fine-tuning and adaptation to specific downstream tasks. Modifying and storing
039
      these immense models introduces computational and storage challenges.
040
041   Given these challenges, Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT) methods, where only a relatively
042   small number of parameters are fine-tuned, emerged as a potential solution to compensate for the
043
      tremendous compute/storage cost of full parameter fine-tuning (Houlsby et al., 2019; Hu et al.,
      2021; Lester et al., 2021; Li & Liang, 2021; Zaken et al., 2021). Among PEFT methods, LoRA (Hu
044
      et al., 2021) is widely used in recent literature due to its good generalization and the fact that it
045
      does not introduce extra modules during the inference phase. Nevertheless, even these techniques
046   can require considerable storage and computational resources, particularly when the objective is
047   to enable large-scale personalized or task-specific adaptation. As an example, applying LoRA on
048   the GPT-3 model (Brown et al., 2020) with a rank of 16 and only query and value matrices being
049   adapted, would result in 144MB of memory per checkpoint, which would amount to 144TB of
050   memory when serving 1 million personalized models.
051
      Following LoRA, many successors have been proposed to further reduce the number of parame-
052   ters and improve efficiency (Kopiczko et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2023). One such

125   2   R ELATED WORK
126
127   Efficient Adaptation Recently, there has been many variants of adapter-based fine-tuning meth-
128   ods proposed where a set of adapter modules are introduced into the transformer model (Vaswani
129
      et al., 2017). These modules can either be introduced as extra adapter layers into the transformer
      block (Houlsby et al., 2019; Liu et al., 2022; Pfeiffer et al., 2020), or as an additional set of param-
130
      eters modifying input layer activations (Asai et al., 2022; Lester et al., 2021; Li & Liang, 2021; Liu
131
      et al., 2023). Although these approaches introduce a relatively small number of parameters, they
132   deteriorate model’s latency in online inference, especially in large-scale production scenarios.
133
134   Low-rank Adaptation Low-rank adaptation of transformer models, proposed by LoRA (Hu et al.,
135   2021), offers a strong alternative to previous PEFT methods, where the generalization perfor-
136   mance is competitive to full fine-tuning while not introducing any further latency during inference
137   phase. Building upon the LoRA method, there has been many recent efforts to improve its learn-
138   ing curve (Liu et al., 2024; Hayou et al., 2024; Meng et al., 2024), reduce the trainable parame-
139   ters (Zhang et al., 2023; Kopiczko et al., 2023; Renduchintala et al., 2023), or even training it with
140   quantized pre-trained weights to improve memory footprint during training (Dettmers et al., 2024;
141   Li et al., 2023). Our method, LoRA-XS, falls in the second category, where we aim to significantly
142
      reduce trainable parameters while performing competitively to LoRA over various benchmarks and
      different model scales.
143
144
      Parameter-constrained LoRA variants Several recent works propose variants of LoRA
145
      that reduce the number of trainable parameters while maintaining competitive performance.
146
      AdaLoRA (Zhang et al., 2023) investigates a dynamic rank adjustment for different modules’ low-
147   rank matrices as opposed to uniform parameter allocation in LoRA. Tied-LoRA (Renduchintala
148   et al., 2023) improves the parameter efficiency by tying LoRA matrices across all layers of the
149   transformer model. VeRA (Kopiczko et al., 2023), which is closely related to our work, shares ran-
150   domly initialized frozen LoRA matrices across layers and adds trainable scaling vectors. However,
151   unlike VeRA, LoRA-XS initializes its low-rank matrices using the SVD of the pre-trained model
152   weights, providing both theoretical justification (see Section 3.1) and strong empirical performance
153   (see Section 5). Additionally, LoRA-XS’s number of trainable parameters is independent of the
154   model’s hidden dimensions, allowing for a significant reduction in parameters, particularly in large-
155
      scale models.

      addresses scalability and storage issues while maintaining highly competitive performance.

184   In recent years, LoRA (Hu et al., 2021) has been pivotal in parameter-efficient tuning by introducing
185   low-rank matrices for adaptation, significantly lowering the number of trainable parameters. How-
186   ever, deploying LoRA at scale, particularly across large-scale task-specific or user-specific models,

      can substantially increase the required storage needs. As modern models grow in size and complex-

188
189   LoRA demonstrated that its low-rank weight updates (i.e., ∆W ) align with certain directions already
190   present in the model’s weights. Building on this insight and our theoretical derivation (see Sec-
191   tion 3.1), we propose initializing the LoRA adaptation matrices (A and B in Figure 2) using the top
192   singular vectors from the SVD of the pre-trained weight matrix W . These matrices are kept fixed
193
      during training. To introduce flexibility, we add a trainable r × r matrix R between A and B, mak-
      ing R the only learnable component. This drastically reduces the number of trainable parameters,
194
      with the parameter count independent of model dimensions. Figure 2 provides an overview of the
195
      LoRA-XS method, highlighting its differences from the original LoRA framework.

      4       E XPERIMENTS
351
352   This section describes experiments evaluating the effectiveness of LoRA-XS. We begin by detailing
353
      the experimental setup and then then provide results for the GLUE benchmark (Wang et al., 2018),
      where we compare LoRA-XS with full fine-tuning, LoRA, and VeRA across six tasks. We explore
354
      various ranks for LoRA-XS to highlight their effect on performance and parameter efficiency.
355
356   We also report results on instruction tuning experiments with decoder-only language models. These
357   experiments test LoRA-XS’s ability to enable large language models (LLMs) to follow instructions
358   with minimal parameter overhead. The first set of experiments involves training models on the
359
      MetaMathQA dataset (Yu et al., 2023) and evaluating them on the GSM8K (Cobbe et al., 2021) and
      MATH (Hendrycks et al., 2021) benchmarks, focusing on mathematical reasoning. The second set
360
      evaluates commonsense reasoning in the same setting as Hu et al. (2023), using eight benchmarks
361
      to assess model performance.
362
363   Our preliminary findings show that when the number of trainable parameters is limited, using a
364   lower rank r with more LoRA-XS modules yields better results. This guided our strategy of using
365
      smaller ranks while distributing more LoRA-XS modules than LoRA and VeRA, which mainly
      added adaptation modules to the Query and Value matrices in the main experiments. By spreading
366
      LoRA-XS across additional components and keeping the rank low, we achieve a balanced parameter
367
      allocation without significantly increasing the number of trainable parameters.

465   5      A BLATION
466
467   In this section, we present ablation experiments to better understand the efficiency of LoRA-XS,
468   demonstrate our theoretical derivations in practice, and examine the role of singular vectors in trans-
469   former weights and adaptation layers.
470
471   Importance of Singular Vectors in Transformer Weights We begin by analyzing the signifi-
472   cance of singular vectors in the weight matrices of transformer models. Detailed results can be
473   found in Appendix D. By examining different subsets of singular vectors (top, middle, and bottom)
474   for various transformer weights (e.g., attention and feedforward modules), we find that the top sin-
475
      gular vectors retain the most task-relevant knowledge. In contrast, the middle and bottom singular
      vectors contribute less to task performance, suggesting that they encode more subtle or less critical
476
      information. This observation aligns with our proposal to initialize LoRA-XS using top singular
477
      vectors.
478
479
      Delta Weight Approximation and Singular Subspace Retention We evaluate how accurately
480   the full weight update ∆W , obtained during fine-tuning, can be approximated by projecting it onto
481   different subspaces of singular vectors from the SVD of the original pre-trained weight matrix W .
482   Specifically, we experiment with retaining varying fractions of top, middle, and bottom singular
483   vectors and assess their impact on downstream task performance. As summarized in Appendix E, the
484   self-attention modules (query, key, value, attention output) exhibit minimal performance degradation
485   when only 1% or 10% of singular vectors are retained, regardless of whether they come from the top
      or bottom subspaces. In contrast, output dense layers are more sensitive to these approximations and


                                                                9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      require a higher fraction of singular vectors to preserve accuracy. These results suggest that while
487   self-attention layers can tolerate significant dimensionality reduction, output dense layers benefit
488   from retaining a larger portion of the singular spectrum.
489
490   LoRA-XS Initialization The initialization of matrices A and B is a key factor in the performance
491   of LoRA-XS. We investigate three initialization strategies: random initialization, SVD of random
492   matrices (SVD of random), and SVD of pre-trained weights (SVD of W ). Please refer to Appendix F
493   for the details.
494
      As summarized in Table 4, our experiments show that using SVD on the pre-trained weight matrices
495
      generally leads to superior performance. This observation aligns with our theoretical framework,
496   which claims that, assuming the considered task is similar to the task used for pre-training, SVD of
497   the original weight matrix is the most effective initialization choice (see Section 3.1).
498
499
      An exception to this trend is observed in the SST-2 task, where SVD of random matrices slightly out-
      performs SVD of W . We hypothesize that this is due to SST-2 being a sentiment classification task,
500
      which may not align as closely with the pre-training objective of language modeling as other tasks
501
      such as MRPC, CoLA, and QNLI. This insight reinforces our theoretical analysis, which suggests
502   that SVD of pre-trained weights is most advantageous when the fine-tuning task shares similarities
503   with the pre-training objective.
504
505
      Additionally, we show that initializing LoRA-XS with SVD of the pretrained weights accelerates
      convergence in the early stages of LoRA-XS training (see Table 14). This early advantage sets
506
      LoRA-XS apart from other ultra-efficient adaptation techniques, such as soft prompt tuning (Lester
507
      et al., 2021; Li & Liang, 2021), which often exhibit slower convergence. By initializing A and
508   B with information derived from the pre-trained model, LoRA-XS benefits from a more informed
509   starting point, leading to more efficient and effective training.

531   6   C ONCLUSION
532
533
      We introduce LoRA-XS, a novel parameter-efficient fine-tuning method that drastically reduces the
534   number of trainable parameters while preserving or enhancing model performance, supported by
535   solid theoretical foundations. LoRA-XS combines low-rank adaptation with singular value decom-
536   position (SVD), aligning adaptation matrices with the principal components of pre-trained weights.
537   Our experiments on GLUE, GSM8K, MATH, and eight commonsense reasoning datasets across
538   multiple models demonstrate that LoRA-XS outperforms both LoRA and VeRA in parameter ef-
539   ficiency, while achieving competitive results across diverse tasks. This method provides a highly
      efficient approach to model adaptation with substantial parameter savings.

762
      VeRA.
763   For simplicity, let’s consider a transformer model with L fine-tuned layers, each consisting of q
764   number of W ∈ Rn×n matrices.
765

766
767                                          PLoRA = L × q × r × 2n,                                          (1)
768
769   For VeRA, the number of trainable parameters is given by:
770

771

773
774                                           PLoRA-XS = L × q × r2 .                                         (3)
775

777   between the methods. The ratio of trainable parameters for LoRA to LoRA-XS is:
778                                      PLoRA     L × q × r × 2n   2n
779                                              =                =    ,                                      (4)

782

                                               =            2
                                                                 =     .                                      (5)
784                                   PLoRA-XS     L×q×r            r2
785
786   As the model dimension n becomes very large compared to the rank r, the benefit of LoRA-XS over

788                                   PLoRA     2n               PVeRA     n

      D    A BLATION S TUDY ON S INGULAR VALUE R ETENTION
919
920
      To gain insights into the importance of singular vectors in the pre-trained model’s performance,
921   we conduct an ablation study on fine-tuned RoBERTa-large model by performing Singular Value
922   Decomposition (SVD) on selected weight matrices. Specifically, we decompose the matrices into
923   their singular values and vectors, retaining only a fraction rfrac of the singular values: either from the
924   top, middle, or bottom of the spectrum, while zeroing out the remaining ones. This experiment aims
925   to explore which portions of the singular value spectrum contribute most to the model’s performance.
926
      The experiment is carried out on RoBERTa-large fine-tuned on MRPC, SST-2, and MNLI tasks using
927   default hyperparameters, including a learning rate of 2e-5 and 5 epochs for MRPC, 3 epochs for SST-
928   2, and MNLI. We evaluate the performance of various weight matrices within the transformer layers,
929   such as ‘query‘, ‘value‘, ‘key‘, ‘attention.output.dense‘, ‘intermediate.dense‘, and ‘output.dense‘.
930   Each matrix is tested across the following configurations:
931
932          • Top-r: Retaining the top rfrac singular values.
933          • Middle-r: Retaining the middle rfrac singular values.
934          • Bottom-r: Retaining the bottom rfrac singular values.
935
936   The results, shown in Tables 6, 7, and 8, reveal several key insights. First, retaining only the top
937   singular values consistently yields strong performance, particularly in the ‘query‘, ‘value‘, and ‘key‘
938   matrices. Interestingly, retaining a small fraction (rfrac = 0.1) of the top singular values still achieves
939   reasonable accuracy in tasks like SST-2 and MNLI. In contrast, retaining the middle or bottom
940   singular values generally leads to a sharp performance degradation, suggesting their limited role in
941
      maintaining task-specific knowledge.
942   An exception to this pattern is observed in the ‘intermediate.dense‘ matrix, where preserving the bot-
943   tom singular values for MRPC and SST-2 yields better performance than retaining the top or middle
944   singular values. This suggests that the ‘intermediate.dense‘ matrix may store more task-specific in-
945   formation in the lower-ranked singular vectors. One possible explanation is that intermediate layers
946   could be more sensitive to the distribution of information, requiring a broader spread across singular
947
      values, or that they store certain nuanced representations directly in the lower spectrum.
948   These findings provide initial evidence that the top singular vectors capture the most essential infor-
949   mation in transformer weights. This reinforces the intuition that low-rank adaptations, such as those
950   employed in LoRA-XS, can be highly effective in parameter-efficient fine-tuning scenarios.

             S UBSPACES ON D ELTA W EIGHT A PPROXIMATION
1137
       In this ablation study, we explore the influence of retaining different singular vector subspaces on the
1138
       approximation of weight updates ∆W in LLM fine-tuning. Specifically, we assess how well ∆W ,
1139
       representing the full weight updates, can be approximated by projecting it onto a subspace spanned

1141   goal is to evaluate how different subspaces (top, middle, or bottom) affect downstream performance
1142   across several GLUE tasks.

1144   E.1   T HEORETICAL F RAMEWORK
1145
1146   The pre-trained model’s weights W are decomposed using singular value decomposition (SVD) as
1147   follows:
1148
1149                                               W = U ΣV T
1150
1151   where U and V are orthogonal matrices representing the left and right singular vectors, respectively,
1152   and Σ is the diagonal matrix of singular values. During fine-tuning or LoRA adaptation, the model’s
1153   weights are updated, resulting in a weight update ∆W . This leads to the updated weight matrix
1154   being expressed as:
1155
1156                                        W + ∆W = U (Σ + C)V T
1157
1158   where ∆W can be represented in the singular vector basis of W as:
1159
1160                                    ∆W = U CV T ,         C = U T ∆W V
1161
1162   The matrix C captures how the weight update ∆W projects onto the singular vector subspaces of
1163   W . We propose approximating ∆W by retaining only specific subspaces of U and V , focusing on
1164   subspaces corresponding to the top, middle, or bottom singular vectors. This leads to the following
1165   approximation:
1166
1167                                           ∆Wapprox = U Cr V T
1168
1169   where Cr is an r × r block matrix, constructed by selecting singular vector subspaces (top, middle,
1170   or bottom). By retaining different subspaces, we explore the sensitivity of the model’s performance
1171
       to various parts of the singular spectrum.
1172
1173   E.2   F ULLY F INE - TUNED ∆W
1174
       In this experiment, ∆W represents the complete set of weight updates from fine-tuning. We fine-
1175
       tuned RoBERTa-large on MRPC, SST-2, and MNLI, using a learning rate of 2e-5 for 5 epochs on
1176   MRPC and 3 epochs on SST-2 and MNLI. This experiment aims to assess the impact of retaining
1177   different fractions of the singular spectrum on performance for various weight modules, including
1178   query, key, value, intermediate dense, and output dense. The results are summarized in Table 9.
1179
       Our findings reveal that different modules exhibit varying sensitivity to the retention of singular vec-
1180
       tors, suggesting that task-specific fine-tuning impacts each module differently. In the self-attention
1181
       modules (query, key, value, and attention output), retaining even a small fraction of singular vectors
1182   (1% or 10%) resulted in minimal performance degradation across all tasks (in Table 9 we only show
1183   results for the query because for other matrices the behavior was very similar). Both the top and bot-
1184   tom singular vectors maintained performance well. This may indicate that ∆W is relatively small
1185   for self-attention layers compared to W , meaning it has a limited influence on the model’s predic-
1186   tions. This may suggest that these modules can tolerate substantial dimensionality reduction. The
1187   intermediate dense modules exhibited slightly higher sensitivity to the subspaces retained, though
       the impact remained small.

       F     A BLATION S TUDY ON L O RA-XS INITIALIZATION
1243
1244
       In this ablation study, we examine the impact of different initialization strategies for the LoRA-
1245   XS matrices A and B, comparing Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) initialization to random
1246   initialization (used in LoRA and VeRA). Additionally, we investigate whether applying SVD to
1247   random matrices yields any advantages, testing if the benefits of SVD initialization derive solely
1248   from the orthogonality of singular vectors, regardless of the source of the decomposition.
1249
       For the SVD initialization, we decompose the original weight matrix as W ≈ Ur Σr VrT , retaining
1250
       the top r singular vectors. Random initialization follows the Kaiming initialization, commonly used
1251   for linear layers in PyTorch (Paszke et al., 2019).
1252
1253   We conduct our experiments using the RoBERTa-large (Liu et al., 2019) model on selected GLUE
1254
       tasks. The LoRA-XS matrices are applied to specific transformer weights matrices, including the
       query, value, attention output, and intermediate output layers. All experiments are repeated with five
1255
       different random seeds, and we report the median performance.
1256
1257
       F.1   C OMPARING R ANDOM I NITIALIZATION , SVD ON R ANDOM M ATRICES , AND SVD ON
1258
             W EIGHTS
1259
1260   We compare three initialization strategies: random initialization, SVD on random matrices (SVD of
1261   random), and SVD on the corresponding layer’s weight matrix (SVD of W). The results, summarized
1262   in Table 11, Table 12, and Table 13, reveal that initializing the A and B matrices with SVD on the
1263   corresponding module’s original weight matrix (W ) generally provides the best performance. This
1264   supports our theoretical hypothesis that leveraging the structure of the weight matrix through SVD
1265   initialization is more effective than random initialization, as it retains task-relevant information from
1266   the pretrained model (see Section 3.1).
1267   Our results also confirm that the performance benefits do not stem solely from the orthogonality of
1268   the singular vectors, as SVD on random matrices did not consistently outperform random initializa-
1269   tion.
1270
       Notably, for the SST-2 task (Table 10), all initialization methods perform similarly, with SVD on
1271   random matrices yielding the highest score. This is likely due to the nature of SST-2 as a sentiment
1272   classification task, which is less closely aligned with language modeling compared to other tasks
1273   such as MRPC, COLA, and QNLI. This finding aligns with our theoretical analysis (Section 3.1),
1274   which suggests that SVD on the original weights is most beneficial when the fine-tuning task is
1275   similar to the pretraining objective.
1276
1277   F.2   E FFECT OF I NITIALIZATION ON L EARNING C URVE AND R ANK
1278
1279   We further explore the impact of initialization on learning speed and performance at different ranks.
1280   In Table 14, we present the detailed results comparing the initialization of the A and B matrices of
1281   LoRA-XS using SVD versus random initialization. The reported metrics are accuracy for SST-2 and
1282   QNLI, and Matthews correlation for CoLA on the RoBERTa-large model. Hyperparameters were
1283   selected through grid search and are detailed in Table 15. Results are reported as the median over
1284   5 random seeds, with the best epoch result for each run (column ”Best epoch”). Additionally, we
1285   show results after 1 and 2 epochs to illustrate the learning curve and how quickly the models learn
1286
       with each initialization method.
1287   Our results empirically confirm that also for other ranks aligning adaptation matrices with the prin-
1288   cipal components of pre-trained weights can enhance parameter efficiency while maintaining model
1289   accuracy. As shown in Table 14, initializing the A and B matrices with SVD generally leads to
1290   improved performance compared to random initialization.
1291   Moreover, the performance after 1 and 2 epochs is often higher with SVD initialization. This sug-
1292   gests that proper initialization using SVD may help the models converge more quickly and achieve
1293   better overall accuracy. This finding underscores the importance of initialization strategy in fine-
1294   tuning large language models, highlighting that SVD initialization not only boosts performance but
1295   also accelerates the training process.

       In this section, we explore the significance of including singular values in the initialization of ma-

1733   is initialized using only the singular vectors, A = U , while keeping B = V T in both cases. Our
1734   analysis focuses on the top r singular vectors for both methods.
