009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Evaluating the capabilities of Foundation Models has traditionally relied on static
012            benchmark datasets, human assessments, or model-based evaluations — meth-
013            ods that often suffer from overfitting, high costs, and biases. We introduce Ze-
014            roSumEval, a novel competition-based evaluation protocol that leverages zero-
015            sum games to assess LLMs with dynamic benchmarks that resist saturation. Ze-
016            roSumEval encompasses a diverse suite of games, including security challenges
017            (Capture the Flag), classic board games (chess), and knowledge tests (MathQuiz).
018
               These games are designed to evaluate a range of AI capabilities such as strategic
               reasoning, planning, knowledge application, safety, and adaptability. A key nov-
019
               elty is integrating automatic prompt optimization to ensure fair comparisons by
020
               eliminating biases from human prompt engineering and support arbitrary prompt-
021            ing strategies. Furthermore, ZeroSumEval measures AI models’ abilities to self-
022            improve from limited observations and assesses their robustness against adver-
023            sarial or misleading examples during prompt optimization. Building upon recent
024            studies that highlight the effectiveness of game-based evaluations for LLMs, Ze-
025            roSumEval enhances these approaches by providing a standardized and extensible
026            framework for rigorous assessment. We find ZeroSumEval correlates strongly
027            with expensive human evaluations (Chatbot Arena) and disagrees with bench-
028            marks with known overfitting and saturation issues. Inspecting match traces re-
029
               veals models that allocate more tokens to thought processes perform strongly in
               games involving planning capabilities.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      Large Language Models (LLMs) are being developed at an unprecedented pace (Zhao et al., 2024),
035
      requiring significant investment for their training and refinement (Kevin Lee, 2024; Miller, 2022;
036   Kimball, 2024). As the performance and complexity of these models continue to grow (Chen et al.,
037   2024b), selecting the most appropriate model for a specific application has become an increasingly
038   challenging and costly decision(Kaplan et al., 2020; Hoffmann et al., 2022). Benchmarking emerges
039   as a critical tool in this context (Laskar et al., 2023; Qin et al., 2023), providing standardized metrics
040   and evaluations to guide these choices.
041
      With the rapid growth of generative technologies built on top of Large Language Models (OpenAI,
042   2022; Google, 2024; Anthropic, 2024b; Ormazabal et al., 2024; Mistral, 2024; Dubey et al., 2024a;
043   Yang et al., 2024), it has been increasingly difficult to evaluate these models comprehensively (Guo
044   et al., 2023). Current benchmarking practices face several significant issues. Many benchmarks
045   suffer from data contamination (Yang et al., 2023), where models inadvertently train on portions of
046   the test data (Dubey et al., 2024a; Groeneveld et al., 2024), leading to inflated performance metrics.
047   Sensitivity to prompt variations (Alzahrani et al., 2024b) and a lack of diversity in evaluation tasks
048   (Laskar et al., 2024) further undermine the reliability and robustness of these benchmarks. Addition-
049   ally, the high cost and effort required to develop new benchmarks often result in outdated evaluation
050
      methods that do not keep pace with the rapid development of LLMs (Kiela et al., 2021; Vu et al.,
      2023).
051
052   An observed disparity exists between the computational resources measured in floating-point oper-
053   ations per second, or FLOPs used to train LLMs and those allocated for their evaluation. Training
      these models involves massive computational efforts (Hoffmann et al., 2022), yet the evaluation

072   model capabilities. Traditionally, this scaling involves incorporating human-crafted independent and
073   identically distributed (i.i.d.) data (Holland et al., 2018), which is resource-intensive (Hutchinson

077   Previous work has proposed the use of games as benchmarks (Topsakal et al., 2024), offering a
078   promising avenue for evaluating complex reasoning (Wong et al., 2023) and decision-making abil-
079   ities of LLMs (Warstadt et al., 2023; Park et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023). Games provide inter-
080   active and dynamic environments that can test models beyond static datasets. However, existing
081   game-based benchmarks are often (i) inflexible and limited in scope, (ii) not easily extendable, (iii)
082   restricted in their effectiveness for comprehensive model evaluation, and (iv) depend on predefined
083   prompts.
084   Scaling evaluation is fundamental not only for assessing performance but also for uncovering hid-
085   den dynamics within LLMs, such as potential backdoors or biases (Schuster et al., 2020), and for
086   evaluating their emerging reasoning capabilities (Brown et al., 2020; Sanh et al., 2022; Wei et al.,
087   2023b;a). Implementing environments for simulations or games offers a scalable solution to these
088   challenges (OpenAI et al., 2019; OpenAI, 2019; Silver et al., 2016; 2017; Zheng et al., 2021).
089   Existing evaluation protocols possess several key issues:
090
091   (i) Prompt Sensitivity: Previous work (Zheng et al., 2024; Pezeshkpour & Hruschka, 2023; Lu
092
      et al., 2022; Alzahrani et al., 2024a; Wang et al., 2024a) has shown that models are sensitive to
      benchmark formats. By sheer chance, a model could be presented with a prompting method that’s
093
      either favorable or detrimental. These prompt modifications are shown to result in substantially
094
      different relative performance between models (Alzahrani et al., 2024a). By testing models in varied
095   scenarios within a controlled environment, we can assess and improve their robustness to different
096   prompts. Crucially, different models are not optimized for the same prompts due to variations in data
097   mixtures and algorithmic implementations. Using identical prompts across all models may therefore
098   lead to unfair comparisons.
099
      (ii) Limited Diversity: Traditional evaluation methods often rely on static datasets, which are in-
100
      herently limited by their dependency on human curation and annotation. This makes it challenging
101   to continuously introduce new, diverse test data. An extensible simulated environment, however,
102   allows for a wide array of dynamically generated games and scenarios, enhancing the diversity and
103   scalability of evaluation tasks.
104
      (iii) Extensibility: Once established, the environment can be easily expanded to include new games,
105
      rules, and scenarios, facilitating continuous evaluation improvements.
106
107   (iv) Crowd and Annotator Bias: LLM evaluations conducted by large crowds often tend to be sus-
      ceptible to social hacking, and it can depend on geographic, temporal, and narrative factors Gururan-


                                                            2
108
      gan et al. (2018). Controlled and interpretable environments can mitigate these biases by providing
109   consistent, objective evaluation criteria.
110
111   (v) Saturation: With the rapid improvement of LLMs, evaluation benchmarks quickly become
112
      obsolete and saturated, with frontier models achieving almost perfect scores, which necessitates the
      development of new benchmarks. On the opposite extreme, benchmarks that are too difficult would
113
      result in almost random scores. Both extremes result in a lack of granularity to distinguish models.
114
      Therefore, benchmarks posing moderate difficulty to frontier models will need to be continuously
115   developed as models improve. For instance, GSM8K (Cobbe et al., 2021) tests models on grade
116   school-level math, and most state-of-the-art models achieve scores above 90% (Dubey et al., 2024b;
117   Anthropic, 2024a). Thus, the more difficult MATH (Hendrycks et al., 2021b) dataset, which consists
118   of math competition questions, was developed and is now commonly used1 . A similar trend is
119   observed in academic examination benchmarks with the migration from MMLU (Hendrycks et al.,
120   2020) to MMLU-Pro (Wang et al., 2024b) and GPQA. (Rein et al., 2023)2 .
121   To address these challenges, we introduce Z ERO S UM E VAL, a flexible and extensible open-source
122   framework designed to scale LLM evaluation through the simulation of two-player zero-sum games.
123   Our framework allows for comprehensive and robust assessment by providing models with multiple
124   opportunities to make legal moves, thereby accommodating occasional errors and offering a more
125   nuanced understanding of their capabilities.
126
127   1. Scaling Evaluation by Simulation: We demonstrate how simulation environments can effec-
128   tively scale the evaluation process.
129   2. Flexible and Extensible Framework: Z ERO S UM E VAL is designed to be adaptable, allowing
130   researchers and practitioners to customize and extend the evaluation environment to suit diverse
131   needs.
132   3. Robustness to Prompt Sensitivity: By incorporating automatic prompt optimization, our frame-
133   work mitigates issues related to prompt sensitivity, leading to more reliable evaluation outcomes.
134   4. Enhanced Interpretability: The structured environment facilitates easier interpretation of model
135   behaviors, aiding in the identification of strengths and weaknesses.
136
      5. Error Accommodation: Models are given multiple chances to make legal moves, ensuring that
137
      occasional missteps due to inherent stochasticity do not disproportionately affect the overall evalu-
138   ation.

143   2.1   S TATIC LLM B ENCHMARKS
144
      Until recently, LLMs were evaluated on Natural Language Understanding (NLU) tasks from bench-
145   mark collections like GLUE (Wang, 2018) and SuperGLUE (Wang et al., 2019), which included
146   tasks like paraphrase classification and sentiment analysis. As LLMs developed, they acquired
147   emergent capabilities beyond generating plausible text, such as reasoning, generating code, and
148   instruction following (Brown et al., 2020; Wei et al., 2022). With these newly found capabilities,
149   new benchmarks were developed to quantify these abilities. As models improve, more difficult
150   benchmarks are created. For example:
151
152
         • Reasoning: undergraduate level academic questions are tested via MMLU (Hendrycks et al.,
      2020), while GPQA (Rein et al., 2023) tests models with graduate level questions. All aforemen-
153
      tioned benchmarks score models based on the likelihood of specific tokens for the answer keys in a
154
      multiple-choice setting.
155
156
        • Mathematics: GSM8K (Cobbe et al., 2021) evaluates models on elementary level arithmetic,
      while MATH (Hendrycks et al., 2021b) tests on competition level mathematics. Both benchmarks
157
      evaluate the model in a few-shot setting by encouraging models to output chains of thought followed
158
      by the numeric answer in a specific format.

169   2.2       C OMPARATIVE LLM B ENCHMARKS
170
171   LLM Game Evaluations To address the static benchmark issues highlighted in Section 1, the
172   paradigm of evaluating agentic capabilities through simulations has been applied successfully in
173   multiple prior works. Evaluation frameworks comprising multiple games include: (i) ChatArena
174   (Wu et al., 2023), which includes Chess, Tic-Tac-Toe, Rock-Paper-Scissors, and others, (ii)
175
      GridGames (Topsakal et al., 2024), implementing Tic-Tac-Toe, Connect Four, and Gomoku, and
      (iii) GameBench (Costarelli et al., 2024), which is the most diverse, as they developed 9 games,
176
      include non-deterministic and imperfect information games.
177
178
179
      Limitations of LLM Game Evaluations All the aforementioned benchmark frameworks are im-
      plemented with manually written prompts for all models, and sometime suggest a strategy within
180
      the prompt, such as ChatArena prompting models to output a random move in Rock-Paper-Scissors.
181
      GameBench tries to optimize model results by utilizing two prompting strategies: (i) Chain of
182   Though (CoT), and (ii) Reasoning via Planning (RAP), but the issue of static prompt still persists.
183   This could explain the poor performances they observed, such as GPT-4 achieving almost random
184   results on some tasks.
185
186   Comparative Human Evaluations A popular head-to-head LLM evaluation framework is Chat-
187   bot Arena3 (Chiang et al., 2024), which allows users to prompt two anonymous LLMs with arbitrary
188   prompts and to vote for the better response. This creates a diverse evaluation that effectively ranks
189   all models in a leaderboard. However, it suffers from two issues: (i) human evaluations are slow
190   and laborious, and adding new models requires prolonged evaluation periods until sufficient votes
191   are acquired for a confident placement, and (ii) human evaluations contain human biases, such as
192   prompt over-representation (Dunlap et al., 2024) and bias to verbose and “pretty” responses (Chen
193   et al., 2024a; Park et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024).

197   In this section, we describe the technical details of Z ERO S UM E VAL including design choices, the
198   importance of automatic prompt optimization, and game selection/categorization. At its core, Z E -

      3.4   AUTOMATIC P ROMPTING
304
305
      Automatic prompting is an essential component of the Z ERO S UM E VAL framework for several rea-
306   sons. First, it allows models to learn to play new games through self-optimization, demonstrating
307   their ability to adapt to different scenarios without human intervention. Second, it removes the hu-
308   man element in prompt engineering, thereby reducing biases and variations introduced by manual
309   prompt construction (Zheng et al., 2024; Pezeshkpour & Hruschka, 2023; Alzahrani et al., 2024a).
310   Third, automatic prompting serves as a measure of a model’s ability to self-improve at inference
311   time, providing insight into its adaptability and strategic reasoning skills.
312   We leverage the DSPy (Khattab et al., 2023) approach to implement automatic prompt optimization
313   in our framework. DSPy allows models to autonomously explore and select optimal prompts based
314   on the current game context, dynamically adjusting strategies to maximize performance. We also
315   make use of DSPy Assertions (Singhvi et al., 2024) to simulate interactivity between the models
316   and the game environment by allowing a number of retries (with feedback from the game) when
317   the model makes an invalid move. Although we find DSPy has the flexibility and generalizabil-
318   ity to support various models and games, Z ERO S UM E VAL supports alternative automatic prompt
319   optimization techniques if required.
320   Through prompt optimization, models can develop improved strategies as they encounter diverse
321   game scenarios. For example, in a chess game, models equipped with optimized prompting demon-
322   strated a higher proportion of correct moves compared to their counterparts using default prompts
323   Figure 2. This not only reveals the models’ enhanced strategic reasoning but also emphasizes the
      significance of prompt optimization in robust performance evaluation.

324
      By incorporating automatic prompting, Z ERO S UM E VAL addresses benchmark sensitivity. The
325   prompt optimization integrates game validation mechanisms into the optimization process, allow-
326   ing models to observe tangible outcomes and refine their prompt strategies. Consequently, this
327   mitigates the variations in performance due to prompt sensitivity, leading to a more consistent and
328   reliable evaluation of model capabilities.

349   3.5    R ATINGS
350
351   Z ERO S UM E VAL utilizes an easily computable rating system derived from the outcomes of competi-
352   tive games between models. Each model receives a rating based on its win-loss record over multiple
353   games, allowing for a rapid and scalable oversight of model capabilities. This framework seamlessly
354   incorporates new games, providing continuous and dynamic evaluation as models improve.
355   Following recent suggestions for LLM rating systems by Boubdir et al. (2023); Chiang et al. (2023),
356   we employ the Bradley-Terry (BT) rating system, an alternative to the Elo system, to rate models.
357   The BT model is permutation-invariant and assumes a fixed win rate for each model pair, maximiz-
358   ing the likelihood of observed outcomes (Bradley & Terry, 1952). This choice is more suitable than
359   the traditional Elo system, which was designed for human chess players with varying skill levels,
360   whereas LLMs have fixed skill levels defined by their weights (Elo, 1967).
361   Z ERO S UM E VAL’s rating system facilitates analysis of model behaviors. It allows us to observe
362   not only the relative strategic planning capabilities of models but also their capacity for self-
363   improvement through prompt optimization. For instance, analysis of models’ gameplay strategies
364   in chess revealed that prompt-optimized models allocate more reasoning words in their decision-
365   making process, suggesting a deeper level of planning (Figure 4).

      The experiments involve running a multiple round-robin tournaments to simulate competitive game-
379   play among the model (50-100 games per experiment). In addition to measuring model perfor-
380   mance on the games in the Z ERO S UM E VAL suite, we also examine how the models’ performance
381   changes with different prompt optimization techniques. Each tournament round involves all possi-
382   ble match permutations between model variants, after which the models’ ratings are calculated using

467   The dynamic, competitive nature of Z ERO S UM E VAL’s evaluation provides a more robust and trust-

470
      with diverse, evolving tasks, minimizing the risk of overfitting and saturation commonly observed

      namic capacity for self-improvement.
