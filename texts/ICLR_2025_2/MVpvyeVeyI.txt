009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Causal Bayesian Optimization (CBO) is a methodology designed to optimize an
012            outcome variable by leveraging known causal relationships through targeted inter-
013            ventions. Traditional CBO methods require a fully and accurately specified causal
014            graph, which is a limitation in many real-world scenarios where such graphs are
015            unknown. To address this, we propose a new method for the CBO framework that
016            operates without prior knowledge of the causal graph. We demonstrate through
017            theoretical analysis and empirical validation that focusing on the direct causal
018
               parents of the target variable is sufficient for optimization. Our method learns a
               Bayesian posterior over the direct parents of the target variable. This allows us to
019
               optimize the outcome variable while simultaneously learning the causal structure.
020
               Our contributions include a derivation of the closed-form posterior distribution
021            for the linear case. In the nonlinear case where the posterior is not tractable, we
022            present a Gaussian Process (GP) approximation that still enables CBO by inferring
023            the parents of the outcome variable. The proposed method performs competitively
024            with existing benchmarks and scales well to larger graphs, making it a practical
025            tool for real-world applications where causal information is incomplete.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Many real-world applications require optimizing the outcome of a function that is both unknown
031   and expensive to evaluate. For instance, in agriculture, this optimization focuses on determining
032   which fertilizers to apply in order to maximize crop yield. In robotics, it involves fine-tuning control
033
      algorithms to achieve optimal performance. In medical applications, such as in drug discovery, the
      objective is to maximize the efficacy of a drug. Bayesian Optimization (BO) (Jones et al., 1998)
034
      is a widely used framework used to solve these kinds of optimization problems. The framework
035
      uses a surrogate model to estimate uncertainty in the unknown function and strategically optimizes
036   it through a series of well-chosen queries.
037
038   Typically, BO focuses on a black-box setup. As a result, BO methods have no notion of a targeted
039
      intervention and thus requires intervening on all variables at each iteration of the algorithm in order
      to optimize the function of interest. This is limiting as interventions are costly. Causal Bayesian
040
      Optimization (CBO) (Aglietti et al., 2020) leverages causal information in the form of a causal graph
041
      (Pearl, 2009) to provide more structure to this black-box function. In a causal graph, the arrows point
042   from cause to effect, which offers insights into the relationship between the variables. CBO uses this
043   causal information to select targeted interventions that optimize an outcome variable of interest. As
044   a motivating example, CBO can be used for a doctor to prescribe drugs to minimize the prostate-
045   specific antigen (PSA) levels of a patient. By incorporating causal knowledge, we can make more
046   informed decisions about which interventions are likely to improve the outcome. This means we
047   can optimize the outcome variable with targeted interventions. Leveraging causal information can
048   therefore reduce the dimensionality of the BO problem.
049   Although the CBO methodology has been extended to various settings, such as model-based ap-
050   proaches (Sussex et al., 2023; 2024), functional interventions (Gultchin et al., 2023), dynamic set-
051   tings (Aglietti et al., 2021), and constrained settings (Aglietti et al., 2023), a fundamental limitation
052   of these approaches is the assumption of a known causal graph. In practice, the causal graph is
053   almost never fully known, and even domain experts may not correctly identify all causal relation-
      ships. This limitation motivates the setting where we consider the CBO problem in the case where

078
079         1. We derive a posterior distribution that learns the direct parents of the target variable using
080            both observational and interventional data, and incorporate this posterior into the surrogate

082         2. We theoretically establish that specifying only the direct parents is sufficient for optimiza-

      Causal Bayesian Optimization. There are various methods that build on the CBO methodology
      from Aglietti et al. (2020). Aglietti et al. (2021) consider the case where both the target variable and
094
      the input variables evolve over time. Furthermore, Sussex et al. (2023; 2024) propose model-based
095
      CBO methods that have cumulative regret guarantee. Aglietti et al. (2023) explore the constrained
096   case where a constraint condition is added to CBO. Gultchin et al. (2023) consider a functional
097   intervention. In contrast to these, our work considers the CBO problem in scenarios where the causal
098   graph is unknown. Branchini et al. (2023) address the same problem, but introduce an acquisition
099   function to jointly optimize the target and learn the structure; however, their approach fails to scale to
100   larger graphs. In contrast, our work presents a scalable framework specifically designed for solving
101   the CBO problem with completely unknown causal graphs. Unlike (Branchini et al., 2023), we focus
102   on identifying the direct causal parents of the target variable. We also consider the case where hard
103   interventions are performed.
104   Causal Discovery. There has been a recent line of work for Bayesian inference over causal graphs
105   using continuous optimization (Annadani et al., 2021; Lorch et al., 2021; Cundy et al., 2021; Hägele
106   et al., 2023; Annadani et al., 2024), but learning the full graph is not needed in our optimization
107   problem. Causal Discovery methods that combine observational and interventional data is known
      as joint causal inference (Mooij et al., 2020). There are various methods that learn the full graph

      using joint causal inference (Hauser & Bühlmann, 2012; Magliacane et al., 2016; Wang et al., 2017;
109   Yang et al., 2018), but all these methods learn a point estimate of the graph. Local Causal Discovery

      for the causal structure. We are interested in a posterior distribution of the direct parents. This
      captures the epistemic uncertainty over the direct causal parents.

119   Causal Bandits. Since the introduction of causal bandits by Lattimore et al. (2016), there has been

      functional relationships between the variables.

      We will use the standard GP for CBO as the surrogate model (Aglietti et al., 2020) for CBO-U.
341
      Since the graph is unknown, estimating the prior mean and kernel function becomes more difficult.
342   As in Branchini et al. (2023), we will introduce a latent variable g, which denotes the unknown set
343   of parent variables. We can then use the law of total expectation and the previously derived posterior
344   distribution to compute the mean and variance as
345
                         E[Y | ξ, C] = EP (g) [E[Y | ξ, C, g]]                                              (12)
346
347                      V[Y | ξ, C] = VP (g) [E[Y | ξ, C, g]] + EP (g) [V[Y | ξ, C, g]]                    (13)
348   We use Dobs and do-calculus to estimate the inner expectation, and then the posterior distribution to
349   estimate the final mean and variance function. This will capture the uncertainty over both the graph
350   and the surrogate model. The final algorithm is then given in Algorithm 1. We use the invariance
351   property (Peters et al., 2016) to update the posterior probability with the new interventional data
352   point. It is shown that using interventional data improves the the accuracy of causal effect estimation
353
      (Hauser & Bühlmann, 2015).

525   7     C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
526
527   Conclusion. In this work, we proposed a CBO methodology for cases where the causal graph
528
      is unknown that scales to larger graphs. We introduced a novel approach that learns a Bayesian
      posterior over the direct parents of the target variable. Our theoretical analysis and experiments
529
      demonstrated that focusing on direct parents suffices for the CGO problem under some assump-
530
      tions. We derived a closed-form posterior of the direct parents for the linear case and provided an
531   approximation for the nonlinear case.
532
533
      Limitations and Future Work. The main limitation of our work is the restriction to hard inter-
534   ventions. Future research should focus on extending CBO-U to cases where other interventions can
535   be performed, or to cases where the assumptions of this paper do not hold. The framework is gen-
536   eral, and similar approaches should be considered in these cases. For example, Lee & Bareinboim
537   (2018) give optimality conditions in the presence of unobserved confounders and Elahi et al. (2024)
538   show that not all confounders need to be discovered to uncover the possibly optimal intervention
539   set. This is a clear future direction for relaxing Assumption 4.3. One can use such an approach to
      identify the most relevant part of the causal graph for and then optimize the CBO objective.
