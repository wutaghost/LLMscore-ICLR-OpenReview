009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011              Meshes are fundamental representations of 3D surfaces. However, creating high-
012              quality meshes is a labor-intensive task that requires significant time and expertise
013              in 3D modeling. While a delicate object often requires over 104 faces to be ac-
014              curately modeled, recent attempts at generating artist-like meshes are limited to
015              1.6K faces and heavy discretization of vertex coordinates. Hence, scaling both the
016              maximum face count and vertex coordinate resolution is crucial to producing high-
017              quality meshes of realistic, complex 3D objects. We present M ESHTRON, a novel
018
                 autoregressive mesh generation model able to generate meshes with up to 64K
                 faces at 1024-level coordinate resolution –over an order of magnitude higher face
019
                 count and 8× higher coordinate resolution than current state-of-the-art methods.
020
                 M ESHTRON’s scalability is driven by four key components: (i) an hourglass neural
021              architecture, (ii) truncated sequence training, (iii) sliding window inference, and
022              (iv) a robust sampling strategy that enforces the order of mesh sequences. This
023              results in over 50% less training memory, 2.5× faster throughput, and better con-
024              sistency than existing works. M ESHTRON generates meshes of detailed, complex
025              3D objects at unprecedented levels of resolution and fidelity, closely resembling
026              those created by professional artists, and opening the door to more realistic gen-
027              eration of detailed 3D assets for animation, gaming, and virtual environments.1

      1   I NTRODUCTION
055
056   Meshes are one of the most important and widely used representations for 3D assets. They are the
057   de facto standard in the film, design, and gaming industries and are natively supported by virtually
058   all 3D softwares and graphics hardwares. Currently, meshes can be generated either manually by
059   artists or automatically through 3D generation algorithms. However, high-quality meshes suitable
060   for games and movies –that is, those that can be manipulated, articulated, and animated– are still
061   almost exclusively created by artists. Artist-created meshes capture not only the external appearance
062   of objects but also their intrinsic properties and construction details through the mesh topology –the
063   arrangement of vertices and faces in a specific tesselation. For instance, symmetrical objects should
064
      have a tesselation that mirrors their symmetry along the symmetry plane, and the edge flow should
      follow the natural curvature of the object to ensure smooth transitions and facilitate editing.
065
066   The availability of large-scale datasets and scalable models has driven remarkable advancements in
067   generative models, such as autoregressive Large Language Models (LLMs) (Achiam et al., 2023;
068   Team et al., 2023; Dubey et al., 2024), up to the point where generated data is often indistinguish-
069   able from real examples. Generating 3D assets directly as meshes, however, remains a significant
070
      challenge due to several factors: (i) the complexity of representing meshes properly due to their
      unordered, discrete nature, (ii) the large size of resulting representations, and (iii) the scarcity of
071
      high-quality training data. As a result, most of the 3D generative models avoid modeling meshes
072
      directly, and instead rely on alternative 3D representations such as neural fields (Poole et al., 2022;
073   Wang et al., 2024), 3D Gaussians (Tang et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2024b), voxels (Brock et al., 2016;
074   Wu et al., 2016), or point clouds (Vahdat et al., 2022; Jun & Nichol, 2023). These representations are
075   then converted to meshes for downstream applications, for instance by iso-surfacing methods such
076   as Marching Cubes (Lorensen & Cline, 1998; Chen & Zhang, 2021; Shen et al., 2023; Wei et al.,
077   2023) and Marching Tetahedra (Shen et al., 2021). Unfortunately, the resulting meshes often ex-
078   hibit poor topology, characterized by overly dense tesselation, over-smoothing, and bumpy artifacts,
079   leaving a significant quality gap between AI-generated meshes and those crafted by artists (Fig. 2).
080   To close this gap, recent works propose generating 3D as meshes by modeling the vertices and faces
081   of the mesh directly, thereby avoiding the need for post-generation mesh conversion (Nash et al.,
082   2020; Alliegro et al., 2023; Siddiqui et al., 2024; Weng et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024a;b;c). While
083   these approaches show promise for low face-count meshes, they are constrained by the extreme
084   lengths of the resulting sequences. Consequently, they are limited to generating meshes up to 800
085   faces, with the sole exception of Chen et al. (2024c), which is capped at 1.6K faces. As a result,
086   they fail to generate detailed, realistic shapes, which often consist of more than 10K faces. Figure 3
087
      shows the face count distribution of our curated artist mesh dataset, which roughly follows a log-
      normal pattern with a mean of 32K faces and a median of 10K faces. As shown, existing methods
088
      are incapable of capturing the majority of artist-created meshes –indicated by the green and yellow
089
      vertical lines in Fig. 3– emphasizing the need for more scalable mesh generation methods.
090
091   Scaling up mesh generation to large, realistic meshes is a challenging task. The predominant way to
092   represent a 3D mesh with N faces is by flattening into a mesh sequence of 3nN coordinates, where
093
      n is the number of vertices per face. For a triangle mesh with 32K faces, this results in a sequence of
      288K tokens. Generating sequences of this length presents challenges in terms of both efficiency and
094
      robustness. To address this limitation, recent works focus on developing compact representations
095
      that require less tokens to represent the mesh. For example, Siddiqui et al. (2024) uses a VQ-VAE to
096   shorten the sequence by 33%, and Chen et al. (2024c) uses a lossless mesh compression algorithm to
097   achieve about 50% reduction. Nevertheless, these reductions are still insufficient to close the large
098   gap required to generate high-quality meshes of realistic objects.
099
      In this paper, we take an orthogonal route. We focus on designing a more scalable and robust mesh
100
      generator by addressing the root of the scalability problem: the quadratic cost of vanilla Transform-
101
      ers. Existing methods rely heavily on global self-attention, making them prohibitively expensive
102   when facing long mesh sequences required for detailed objects. We address this limitation through
103   four key components: First, we recognize that mesh sequences have more structure than just being
104   a homogeneous sequence of tokens; they can be equivalently represented at coordinate, vertex and
105   face levels of abstraction. Additionally, emerging from the way mesh sequences are constructed, we
106   identify a periodic pattern where ending coordinates within each face and vertex are harder to pre-
107   dict than starting ones (Fig. 5). Based on these insights, we replace the standard Transformer with
      a hierarchical Hourglass Transformer (Nawrot et al., 2021). This architecture introduces a strong

234   Scaling up autoregressive mesh generation. In autoregressive generation, a causal neural network
235   –typically a Transformer (Vaswani, 2017; Achiam et al., 2023; Dubey et al., 2024)– is used to learn
236
      the conditional distribution p(ci |c<i ). However, due to their quadratic computing complexity and
      linear memory requirements, handling long sequences quickly becomes prohibitively expensive. We
237
      address this limitation through the components detailed in the following subsections.
238
239   3.1          H IERARCHICAL MESH MODELING WITH HOURGLASS TRANSFORMERS
240
      While mesh generation can be treated as a generic sequence generation problem, doing so overlooks
241   the inherent structure of mesh sequences, which can be leveraged to build more effective models.
242   As shown in Eq. 1, mesh tokens follow a two-level hierarchy where every 3 tokens represent a
243   vertex, and every three vertices, i.e., 9 tokens, form a triangle. Unlike text tokens, individual mesh
244   tokens carry limited information and become meaningful only when processed in the respective
245   hierarchical groups. By treating mesh tokens as cohesive hierarchical units, models could better
246   capture the structure of mesh sequences, leading to improved understanding and generation. In
247   addition to the hierarchical structure of mesh sequences, we observed a distinctive repetitive pattern
248   in the difficulty of generating tokens resulting from the ordering used to construct mesh sequences
249
      (Sec. 2). As shown in Fig. 5, early tokens within each triangle are easier to generate, as evidenced
      by lower average perplexity values, than later ones. Similarly, within each vertex, later tokens tend
250
      to be harder to predict. This pattern arises from vertex sharing across adjacent triangles, which
251
      introduces repeated tokens in the mesh sequence (Fig. 5a).
252
253   These insights draw our attention to the Hourglass Transformer architecture (Nawrot et al., 2021),
254   an autoregressive hierarchical model designed to process inputs at multiple levels of abstraction
255
      (Fig. 4). The architecture employs multiple Transformer stacks at each level, with transitions be-
      tween levels managed by causality-preserving shortening and upsampling layers that bridge these
256
      hierarchical levels. Shortening layers compress groups of token embeddings into a single embedding
257
      via average, linear or attention-based pooling. Upsampling layers reverse this process by expanding
258   a single embedding back into multiple tokens using repeating, linear upsampling or attention-based
259   upsampling. The expanded sequence is then combined with the higher-resolution sequences from
260   early levels via residual connections, similar to U-Nets (Ronneberger et al., 2015). Both shortening
261   and upsampling layers are carefully designed to preserve causality, as detailed in Fig. 10. In addi-
262   tion, the Hourglass architecture offers a static routing mechanism that allocates compute differently
263   to tokens based on their positions within the sequence. For a shortening factor of s, only every sth
264   token in the sequence passes through the inner Transformer stacks, while other tokens bypass it.
265   For instance, with s=3, every 3rd token is processed through the full stack, while every 1st and 2nd
266
      token skip the inner stacks. This selective routing enables the model to distribute compute efficiently
      given a prespecified structure of the input. An animation illustrating this process is available here.
267
268   We design the backbone of M ESHTRON as an Hourglass Transformer with two shortening layers,
269   each reducing the sequence by a factor of 3. This results in a three-stage model, where the shortened
      stages correspond to token groups representing the vertices and faces of a mesh (Fig. 4). By align-

296   3.2    T RAINING ON TRUNCATED SEQUENCES AND INFERENCE WITH SLIDING - WINDOW
297   Mesh sequences can be extremely long, ranging from a few to hundreds of thousands of tokens
298   (Fig. 3a). As a result, training on full mesh sequences can still be prohibitively expensive, even with
299   the memory and computation savings of an Hourglass architecture. Moreover, the wide variation
300   in sequence lengths hinders the implementation of efficient training setups, even with advanced
301   parallelization techniques in place (Liu et al., 2023a; Korthikanti et al., 2023).
302   Fortunately, mesh generation has a special property that can be exploited to scale generation to very
303   long mesh sequences. The ordering of the mesh sequence sorts triangles from bottom to top, layer
304   by layer, promoting the locality of adjacent triangles within the sequence (Sec. 2). Hence, assum-
305   ing proper global conditioning (see Sec. 3.3 for details on how to achieve this), the generation of
306   subsequent triangles only requires information from adjacent tokens, –specifically, the vertex posi-
307   tions of nearby triangles. This special property allows us to adopt a sliding window approach for
308   efficient training and inference ((Fig. 6 left). Specifically, we train our model with fixed-length trun-
309   cated segments of mesh sequences to significantly reduce compute and memory consumption during
310
      training. Then, during inference, we use a rolling KV-cache with a buffer size equal to the atten-
      tion window, to achieve linear complexity. Importantly, while there is a small discrepancy between
311
      training and inference due to cached embeddings carrying information from outside the current at-
312
      tention window during inference (Fig. 6 right), Sec. 4.2 shows that this has no negatively impact on
313   performance, allowing for efficient generation without the need to recompute previous contexts.
314
315   3.3    G LOBAL CONDITIONING ON TRUNCATED SEQUENCES WITH CROSS - ATTENTION
316
      Recent works, such as MeshXL (Chen et al., 2024a) and MeshAnything (Chen et al., 2024b;c)
317
      perform conditional generation by attaching the embedding of the conditional variables, e.g., point-
318   clouds, at the beginning of mesh sequences. However, as our scaling strategy involves training on
319   truncated mesh sequences, prepending would either (i) make the conditional signal visible to only a
320   few mesh segments, or (ii) require complex concatenation strategies during training and inference.
321   To overcome these limitations, we use cross-attention to condition all mesh segments on global
322   conditioning signals, irrespective of their position within the sequence. This enables our model to
323   effectively combine local and global information during both training and inference, resulting in
      accurate predictions while keeping low resource usage.

      M ESHTRON is designed for point-cloud conditioned mesh generation model. Point clouds are a flex-
341
      ible and universal 3D representation that can be efficiently derived from other 3D formats, including
342   meshes. We encode the input point-cloud into 1024 embeddings via a jointly-trained Perceiver en-
343   coder (Jaegle et al., 2021; Zhao et al., 2024). Additionally, we condition generation on the face count
344   and the proportion of quad faces in the mesh before triangulation. These variables allow control over
345   mesh density and quad-dominance during inference. Each variable is encoded into 1 embedding via
346   an MLP and concatenated to the point-cloud embeddings for conditioning (Fig. 4). Following Dubey
347   et al. (2024), we replace every 4th layer in the Transformer stack with a cross-attention layer to
348   enable interaction between the main model and the conditioning embeddings.
349   3.4 ROBUST MESH GENERATION WITH MESH SEQUENCE ORDERING ENFORCEMENT
350
      To ensure robust generation, we enforce generated mesh sequences to adhere to the ordering with
351
      which they are constructed (Sec. 2). Specifically, we constrain generation such that vertex coordi-
352   nates within each face follow a lexicographic ascending order, and the coordinates of subsequent
353   faces also follow a lexicographic ascending order relative to previous faces. Additionally, we con-
354   strain end-of-sequence tokens to appear only at the start of a new face. These constraints prevent the
355   generation of inconsistent sequences, ensuring that the outputs remain within the data distribution.
356
      We benchmark our order enforcement algorithm on the validation dataset by simulating the genera-
357
      tion process. For each token, we calculate the number of invalid categories in the N -way categorical
358   distribution that would violate the sequence ordering based on prior tokens. Our algorithm prevents
359   32% invalid predictions at 1024-level quantization and 27% at 128-level quantization, effectively
360   narrowing the model’s sample space and enhancing both generation quality and robustness.
361
362   4   E XPERIMENTS
363   This section is divided in two parts. First, we validate the components introduced in M ESHTRON on
364   a small-scale setup with models of 500M parameters and a dataset with meshes up to 4096 faces and
365   128-level coordinate quantization. Next, based on the insights from these experiments, we conduct
366   a full-scale study using a model with 1.1B parameters and a dataset with meshes up to 64K faces and
367   1024-level coordinate quantization. During training, we employ strategies to enhance M ESHTRON’s
368   generalization to imperfect, non-artist meshes. First, we avoid sampling points from internal mesh
369   structures, often absent in non-artist meshes. Then, we perturb the point cloud with Gaussian noise,
370
      applying up to σpos =0.1 to the point positions, and σnormal =0.2 to the point normals. This improves
      generalization and provides a mechanism to balance creativity and faithfulness by increasing noise
371
      or avoiding noise (Fig. 11). Further details on architectural configurations, augmentations, training
372
      setups, hyperparameters and datasets used are provided in Appx. C.1.

409   4.2 G ENERATING FULL MESH SEQUENCES WITH MODELS TRAINED ON TRUNCATED DATA
410   Next, we study the sequence length generalization capability of the Hourglass model by training it on
411   truncated mesh sequences of up to 1024 faces and evaluating its performance generating full mesh
412   sequences with and without sliding window attention (SWA). Surprisingly, as shown in Table 2, the
413   model trained on truncated sequences outperforms the model trained on full mesh sequences, while
414   reducing memory usage by over 50%. This result confirms that, with proper global conditioning,
415   mesh generation does not require access to the entire mesh sequence. However, omitting SWA
416   during inference significantly degrades performance, highlighting its importance in bridging the
417   train-test gap. As shown in Fig. 7, validation perplexity degrades rapidly beyond the training length
418
      without SWA –a known phenomenon in the LLM community (Press et al., 2021; Sun et al., 2022).
419   4.3 S CALING M ESHTRON TO 64 K FACES
420   Our previous studies confirm that the Hourglass architecture offers better inductive biases for mesh
421   generation and that, with proper global conditioning and sequence ordering, mesh generation can
422   effectively rely on local information without sacrificing performance. With our design validated at a
423   small scale, we now scale up M ESHTRON to 1.1B parameters and a dataset containing meshes up to
424   64K faces with 1024-level coordinate quantization. Higher quantization is required to accommodate
425   for the smaller face sizes encountered at this scale (Fig.3b) and to enhance geometric quality.
426   We evaluate M ESHTRON as a retopologization tool based on meshes from three different sources:
427   artist-created, 3D-scanned, and generated through iso-surfacing by online text-to-3D services. Point
428   clouds sampled from these meshes are used to condition our model. We compare M ESHTRON with
429   artist-like mesh generators MeshAnythingV1/V2 (Chen et al., 2024b;c) –the state-of-the-art point
430   cloud conditioned artist-like mesh generator– and MeshGPT (Siddiqui et al., 2024) (Figs. 8, 9, 14),
431   as well as point-cloud conditioned iso-surfacing methods DMTet (Shen et al., 2021) and FlexiCubes
      (Shen et al., 2023) (Fig 2). An extended gallery of results is available on our website.

457   5                 L IMITATIONS AND FUTURE WORK
458
      Despite the advancements offered by M ESHTRON, several areas for improvement remain. First ,
459   even with M ESHTRON’s greatly improved efficiency, generating large meshes still requires consid-
460   erable time –our largest model inferences at 140 tokens / sec. Advancements in efficient autoregres-
461   sive models (Gu & Dao, 2023; Poli et al., 2023), speculative decoding (Cai et al., 2024; Leviathan
462   et al., 2023) and advanced inference systems (Kwon et al., 2023), could help accelerate this process.
463   Second, while M ESHTRON exhibits impressive generation capabilities, it is limited by the low-level
464   nature of its point cloud conditioning. As a result, it struggles to add significant detail to degraded
465   3D shapes, e.g., from marching cube text-to-3D generators. Incorporating higher-level condition-
466   ing signals such as text, or additional guidance, e.g., high-resolution surface normal maps, could
467
      further enhance its capabilities. Another challenge lies in ensuring the robustness of autoregressive
      generation for long sequences –an area that remains underexplored. Despite our sequence ordering
468
      enforcement, occasional failures still occur during inference, expressed by missing parts, holes, or
469
      non-termination. This challenge warrants further investigation. Lastly, the scarcity of high-quality
470   3D data may hinder the advancement of data-driven methods like M ESHTRON. While M ESHTRON
471   is very scalable, it relies on a massive amount of 3D data to train. However, the availability of high-
472   quality 3D data significantly lags behind other modalities. Leveraging data from other domains, e.g.,
473   images or videos, to support mesh generation is a promising direction to address this limitation.
474
475   6                 C ONCLUSION
476
477
      We introduce M ESHTRON, a point cloud-conditioned autoregressive mesh generation model capable
      of producing artist-quality meshes with up to 64K faces at 1024-level coordinate resolution. Lever-
478
      aging the Hourglass architecture, M ESHTRON captures the intrinsic structure of mesh sequences,
479
      improving efficiency and performance. By combining effective global conditioning with training on
480   truncated mesh sequences, it efficiently utilizes both local and global information during generation.
481   Controlled experiments validate its components, guiding an optimized scaling strategy. M ESHTRON
482   exhibits unprecedented capabilities, generating high-quality, artist-like meshes directly from point
483   cloud inputs with fine control over density and tessellation style, significantly outperforming prior
484   work in face count, spatial resolution and quality, enabling the generation of complex, realistic 3D
485   meshes. M ESHTRON marks a major step toward practical, artist-friendly mesh generation tools,
      automating retopologization and reducing the manual effort required for high-quality 3D modeling.

      A    E XTENDED RELATED WORK
814
      3D generation. There is a plethora of work on 3D generation, where text or images are commonly
815
      used as inputs to generate corresponding 3D shapes. These works utilize a wide range of internal 3D
816
      representations. Poole et al. (2022); Wang et al. (2024); Liu et al. (2023b); Qian et al. (2023); Tang
817   et al. (2023b); Shi et al. (2023); Li et al. (2023); Lorraine et al. (2023) represent the shapes using
818   neural radiance fields (NeRF) (Mildenhall et al., 2021), enabling supervision through differentiable
819   volumetric rendering. Gao et al. (2022); Lin et al. (2023); Liu et al. (2024); Chen et al. (2023); Jun
820   & Nichol (2023); Qian et al. (2023); Long et al. (2024); Wei et al. (2024); Bensadoun et al. (2024)
821   adopts signed distance fields (SDF), another popular 3D representation. Closely related to SDF is
822   occupancy fields, which is used in Zhang et al. (2024); Peng et al. (2020). Finally, 3D Gaussian
823   (Kerbl et al., 2023) is also gaining popularity in 3D generation literature (Tang et al., 2023a; Ren
824   et al., 2023; Tang et al., 2024), bringing improved efficiency and quality.
825   Mesh extraction and reconstruction. The above-mentioned body of work on 3D generation often
826   requires additional mesh extraction steps to convert the outputs into meshes suitable for downstream
827   applications. The foundation of these methods are Marching Cubes (Lorensen & Cline, 1998) and
828   the closely related Marching Tetrahedra (Doi & Koide, 1991) algorithms, each implementing a set
829   of hand-designed rules to extract a mesh from the iso-surface of a 3D volume. Their modern deriva-
830   tives (Shen et al., 2023; 2021; Chen & Zhang, 2021; Wei et al., 2023) are extensively utilized in the
831
      3D generation literature to refine the geometry and extract the meshes.
832   Data-driven mesh generation. The iso-surfacing methods discussed above focus on modeling the
833   geometry of the 3D representations and do not consider the resulting mesh tesselation during op-
834   timization. Consequently, resulting meshes are often overly tessellated, and do not resemble those
835   created by artists (Fig. 2). Recent works have begun addressing this issue by learning the tessellation
836   directly from artist-created meshes. These models often employ an autoregressive backbone: Poly-
837
      Gen (Nash et al., 2020) adopts two autoregressive models for generating the vertices and faces of
      a polygon mesh respectively. MeshGPT (Siddiqui et al., 2024) couples a VQ-VAE, which shortens
838
      the sequence length by 1/3, with an autoregressive model. PivotMesh (Weng et al., 2024) introduces
839
      pivot vertices to improve generation quality. MeshXL (Chen et al., 2024a) scales up the model and
840   data while using a single autoregressive model to generate raw mesh sequence directly. MeshAny-
841   thingV1 (Chen et al., 2024b) adds conditional generation capability to MeshGPT by including a
842   point cloud encoder. MeshAnythingV2 (Chen et al., 2024c) extends support to larger meshes of up
843   to 1.6K faces by replacing the VQ-VAE with a more efficient, lossless mesh compression algorithm.
844
      There also exist works that use different generative formulations or mesh representations. BSP-
845
      Net (Chen et al., 2020) generates compact meshes via binary space partitioning. PolyDiff (Alliegro
846   et al., 2023) generates meshes using a discrete diffusion model. BrepGen (Xu et al., 2024a) gener-
847   ates parametric surfaces instead of polygons using diffusion models. DeepCAD (Wu et al., 2021)
848   generates CAD commands that constructs the shape instead of the mesh itself.
849
      Efficient Transformer models. There has been pursuit for efficient long sequences support for
850
      autoregressive Transformer models driven mainly by the LLM community. Several works utilize
851
      sparse attention to reduce complexity (Child et al., 2019; Beltagy et al., 2020; Zaheer et al., 2020;
852   Ding et al., 2023). Notably, Longformer (Beltagy et al., 2020) proposes using sliding-window at-
853   tention to achieve linear computing cost with regard to the sequence length, which has been adopted
854   in popular LLMs such as Mistral (Jiang et al., 2023), Gemma 2 (Team et al., 2024), and GPT-
855   NEO (Black et al., 2021). As an under-explored approach that also reduces model complexity,
856   Nawrot et al. (2021) proposes a hierarchical transformer architecture that considerably conserves
857   memory and computing by reducing the sequence length using causal shortening and upsampling.
858   Apart from reducing model complexity, another promising avenue of reducing the training cost is
859   to train on shorter sequences while still generating full sequences during inference. Training-free
860   long-context methods such as An et al. (2024); Xiao et al. (2023) extend pretrained models beyond
861   its training sequence length without finetuning. As for training-based methods, Dai et al. (2019)
862   finds the positional embedding crucial for chunk-based training. Sun et al. (2022) discovers that
863   damping the magnitude of RoPE positional embedding improves the extrapolation performance,
      and windowed attention is also beneficial by improving the “attention resolution”.

      C.2   DATA HANDLING
937
938   Data curation. Our training data is licensed from a major 3D content provider. We curate the
939
      data by reviewing rendered images to remove meshes with low geometric quality. Additionally,
      we remove all scanned, reconstructed and decimated meshes, as well as those produce by a CAD
940
      software, using a combination of metadata keyword filtering and geometric heuristics. This ensures
941
      that non-artist meshes do not compromise the quality of the trained model. The final dataset contains
942   of 700K meshes containing fewer than 64K faces.
943
944   Many meshes in the dataset contains quad faces, with a small subset being consisting entirely of
945
      quads. During training, we triangulate these meshes but retain the original quad percentage and use
      it as conditioning. This allows for control over the generation of (triangulated) quad meshes.
946
947   Training time augmentations. During training, we apply data augmentations including random
948   rotations, translations and scaling. For point cloud conditioning, we sample points from the mesh
949   surface by rasterizing the mesh from 20 viewpoints corresponding to the 20 faces of an icosahedron.
950   Then, depth maps are extracted from these views and unprojected onto point clouds to filter points
951
      coming from inner structures in the mesh. Next, we subsample the point cloud using farthest-point
      sampling. This approach avoids sampling points inside objects, improving M ESHTRON’s general-
952
      ization to non-artist meshes, which often lack internal structure.
953
954   Additionally, we perturb the point cloud with Gaussian noise, applying of up to σpos =0.1 to the
955   point positions, and σnormal =0.2 to the point normals. We also randomly set the point normals
956   to zero vectors for the whole point cloud with a chance of 0.5. These perturbations enhance the
957
      model’s generalization and provides a mechanism to balance creativity (by adding more noise) and
      faithfulness (by reducing or avoiding noise) during inference (Fig. 11).
958
959   C.3   E VALUATION PROTOCOL
960   Since our models are always conditioned on face count, we use the face count of the ground truth
961   mesh as the conditioning input when evaluating reconstruction performance. The generation process
962   is halted when the number of generated faces exceeds twice the specified face count value.
963
      C.4   T RAINING ON LARGE MESH SEQUENCES BENEFIT SHORT MESH SEQUENCE GENERATION
964
965   Previous works, capped at 800 or 1600 faces, are unable to leverage datasets containing larger, more
966   complex meshes during training. This raises a question: can training on longer mesh sequences,
967   even if they exceed the target length, improve the generation of smaller meshes?
968   To answer this question, we trained a model on data with up to 8K faces –which includes the 4K face
969   training set used for architecture ablations in the main paper–. As shown in Fig. 12, additional train-
970   ing data, even beyond the target sequence length, improves performance on shorter sequence. This
971   finding suggests that training on cropped sequences is a viable strategy for enhancing performance,
      even when the target is to generate shorter sequences.

       C.5 M ESHTRON ’ S ROBUSTNESS OVER POINT CLOUD DENSITIES
1006   We evaluate M ESHTRON’s robustness over changing point cloud densities. As observed in Fig. 15,
1007   M ESHTRONdemonstrates strong robustness to variations in the density of the conditioning point

       densities observed during training.
1009
1010   As one could expect, higher point densities provide more detailed geometric information, allowing
1011   M ESHTRON to generate meshes with finer detail, particularly for complex geometries. Interestingly
1012   however, this behavior is observed, even when the specific point cloud density used exceeds those
1013   observed during training. Conversely, lower point densities may limit the detail level but still result in
1014
       coherent and well-structured outputs. This flexibility ensures that M ESHTRON can adapt to varying

1015
       resolution may vary.
