009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011                Sparse Autoencoders (SAEs) have recently been employed as an unsupervised ap-
012                proach for understanding the inner workings of Large Language Models (LLMs).
013                They reconstruct the model’s activations with a sparse linear combination of in-
014                terpretable features. However, training SAEs is computationally intensive, es-
015                pecially as models grow in size and complexity. To address this challenge, we
016                propose a novel training strategy that reduces the number of trained SAEs from
017                one per layer to one for a given group of contiguous layers. Our experimental
018
                   results on Pythia 160M highlight a speedup of up to 3x without compromising the
                   reconstruction quality and performance on downstream tasks. Therefore, layer
019
                   clustering presents an efficient approach to train SAEs in modern LLMs.
020

022   1       I NTRODUCTION
023
024   With the significant adoption of Large Language Model (LLM)s in world applications, understand-
025   ing their inner workings has gained paramount importance. A key challenge in LLMs interpretability
026   is the polysemanticity of neurons in models’ activations, lacking a clear and unique meaning (Olah
027   et al., 2020). Recently, SAEs (Huben et al., 2024; Bricken et al., 2023) have shown great promise
028   to tackle this problem by decomposing the model’s activations into a sparse combination of human-
029   interpretable features.
030   The use of SAEs as an interpretability tool is motivated by two key reasons: the first is the substantial
031   empirical evidence supporting the Linear Representation Hypothesis (LRH), or that LLMs exhibit
032   interpretable linear directions in their activation space (Mikolov et al., 2013; Nanda et al., 2023; Park
033   et al., 2023); the second is the Superposition Hypothesis (SH) (Elhage et al., 2022), which supposes
034   that, by leveraging sparsity, neural networks represent more features than they have neurons. Under
035   this hypothesis, we can consider a trained neural network as a compressed simulation of a larger
036   disentangled model, where every neuron corresponds to a single feature. To overcome superposi-
037
      tion, SAEs leverage the LRH to decompose model activations into a sparse linear combination of
      interpretable features.
038
039   However, training SAEs is computationally expensive and will become even more costly as the
040   model size and parameter counts grow. Indeed, one Sparse Autoencoder (SAE) is typically learned
041   for a given component at every layer in a LLM. Moreover, the number of features usually equals
042   the model activation dimension multiplied by a positive integer, called the expansion factor. For
043
      example, a single SAE trained on the Llama-3.1 8B model activations with an expansion factor of
      32 has roughly 40962 × 32 × 2 ≈ 1.073 billion parameters, resulting in a total of more than 32
044
      billions parameters when training one for each of the 32 layers. Additionally, every SAE is trained
045
      on a fixed number of tokens T , typically in the billions, leading to a cumulative training requirement
046   of L × T tokens, where L is the total number of layers—for the Llama-3.1 8B model, this amounts
047   to 32 billion tokens. This high computational demand not only increases training time but also
048   requires substantial hardware resources and energy consumption, making the approach increasingly
049   impractical as models scale.
050
      In this work, we reduce the computational overhead of training a separate SAE for each layer of
051
      a target LLM by learning a single SAE for groups of related and contiguous layers, thus reducing

      2.4   I MPROVING SAE S T RAINING
195
196   As SAEs gain popularity for LLMs interpretability and are increasingly applied to state-of-the-art
197   models (Lieberum et al., 2024), the need for more efficient training techniques has become evident.
198   To address this, (Gao et al., 2024) explored the scaling laws of Autoencoders to identify the optimal

200
      Recent work also explored using transfer learning to improve SAE training. For example, Kissane

204   layers of a single model, both in forward and backward directions.

233   3.2    SAE S TRAINING AND EVALUATION
234
      We denote SAEi as the baseline SAE trained to reconstruct the activations of layer i. For every
235
      1 ≤ j ≤ k ≤ 5 with j, k ∈ N, we define SAEjk as the SAE trained to reconstruct the activations for
236
      all layers in the j-th group of a partition consisting of k groups. Furthermore, let [jk ] represent the
237   set of layers belonging to the j-th group within this partition. Each SAEjk is specifically trained to
238   reconstruct the activations of each layer s ∈ [jk ] individually, using the formulations described in
239   equations 3 and 2.
240
      To ensure both computational efficiency and fair comparison with baselines, we allocate a fixed total
241
      of 1 billion tokens for training both every SAEi and SAEjk . In particular, for SAEjk these tokens are
242
      evenly distributed across the layers in [jk ], assigning 1B/|[jk ]| tokens to each layer, with a randomly
243   chosen layer’s activation per token. This approach ensures balanced training across layers within the
244   group while keeping the total token budget consistent with the baseline configurations, thus reducing
245   the total number of training tokens from LT to kT .
246
      For evaluation, we train a SAEjk for every combination of k and j and compare its performance
247
      with the corresponding baseline SAEs , where s ∈ [jk ]. Section 4 presents the results of these
248
      comparisons based on standard reconstruction and sparsity metrics. Furthermore, in Section 5, we
249   show how our approach performs on popular downstream tasks (e.g., (Marks et al., 2024; Hanna
250   et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023)), while Section 6 reports the human interpretability scores.

      While achieving good reconstruction metrics is crucial for a trained SAE, it is insufficient for a
      comprehensive evaluation of its performance. For instance, unsupervised metrics alone cannot de-
352
      termine whether the identified features capture causally influential directions for the model. To
353
      address this, following Marks et al. (2024), we applied SAEs to three well-known tasks: Indirect
354   Object Identification (IOI), Greater Than, and Subject-Verb Agreement.
355
356   Each task can be represented as a set of counterfactual prompts paired with their respective answers,
357
      formally denoted as T : {xclean , xcorrupted , aclean , acorrupted }. Counterfactual prompts are similar to
      clean ones but contain slight modifications that result in a different predicted answer.
358
359   Ideally, a robust SAE should be able to recover the model’s performance on a task when recon-
360   structing its activations. Furthermore, we expect the SAE to rely on a small subset of task-relevant
361   features to complete the task. To assess this, we filtered the features to include only the most impor-
362   tant ones, where importance is defined as the indirect effect (IE) (Pearl, 2022) of the feature on task
363
      performance, measured by a real-valued metric m : Rdvocab → R. Specifically,
364
365                              IE(m; fi ; xclean , xcorrupted ) = m(xclean |do(fi = fi;corrupted )) − m(xclean )                        (6)

368
      m(xcorrupted ), and m(xclean |do(fi = fi;corrupted )) refers to the value of m for xclean under an interven-
      tion where the activation of feature fi is set to fi;corrupted . Moreover, m is defined as the difference
369
      in logits between the clean and the corrupted answer.

      7   C ONCLUSION
472
473
      This work introduces a novel approach to efficiently train Sparse Autoencoders (SAEs) for Large
474
      Language Models (LLMs) by clustering layers based on their angular distance and training a single
475   SAE for each group. Through this method, we achieved up to a 3x speedup in training without com-
476   promising reconstruction quality or performance on downstream tasks. The results demonstrate that
477   activations from adjacent layers in LLMs share common features, enabling effective reconstruction
478   with fewer SAEs.
479
      Our findings also show that the SAEs trained on grouped layers perform comparably to layer-specific
480
      SAEs in terms of reconstruction metrics, faithfulness, and completeness on various downstream
481   tasks. Furthermore, human evaluations confirmed the interpretability of the features learned by our
482   SAEs, underscoring their utility in disentangling neural activations.
483
484
      The methodology proposed in this paper opens avenues for more scalable interpretability tools, fa-
      cilitating deeper analysis of LLMs as they grow in size. Future work will focus on further optimizing
485
      the number of layer groups and scaling the approach to even larger models.

      8   L IMITATIONS AND FUTURE WORKS
487
488
      One limitation of our approach is the absence of a precise method for selecting the optimal number
489   of layer groups (k). This choice is due to the lack of a clear elbow rule for identifying the correct
490   number of groups.
491
492
      Based on our results on Pythia-160m, Appendix B provides the Maximum Average Angular Dis-
      tance (MAAD) within groups for Pythia-160m and larger models (Gemma-2 2b and 9b). This
493
      heuristic can guide the selection of the number of groups k for clustering layers in larger models,
494
      relying on the assumption that angular distance between activations from different layers is an im-
495   portant indicator of how the Group SAE will perform on them. While further investigation is needed
496   to refine group configurations and assess scalability, these heuristics offer a practical reference for
497   training Group-SAEs in larger models.
498
      Additionally, we tested our method primarily on the Pythia-160M model, a relatively small LLM.
499
      While our findings demonstrate significant improvements in efficiency without sacrificing perfor-
500
      mance, the scalability of our approach to much larger models remains an open question. Future
501   work could explore how the grouping strategy and training techniques generalize to models with
502   billions of parameters, where the computational benefits would be even more pronounced.
503
504
      Another important direction for future research involves understanding how Sparse Autoencoders
      (SAEs) handle the superposition hypothesis when encoding information from multiple layers. While
505
      our method effectively grouped layers and maintained high performance, how SAEs manage the po-
506
      tential overlap in feature representation across layers remains unclear. Investigating this aspect could
507   lead to a more clear understanding of the trade-offs between sparsity and feature disentanglement in
508   SAEs, and inform strategies for improving interpretability without compromising task performance.
509
510
      In summary, while our work represents an efficient step forward in training SAEs for interpretability,
      extending this approach to larger models and exploring the handling of superposition will provide
511
      valuable insights for both practical applications and the theoretical understanding of sparse neural
512
      representations.
