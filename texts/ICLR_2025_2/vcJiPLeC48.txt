009                                                      A BSTRACT
010
011                Recurrent neural networks are a successful neural architecture for many time-
012                dependent problems, including time series analysis, forecasting, and modeling of
013                dynamical systems. Training such networks with backpropagation through time
014                is a notoriously difficult problem because their loss gradients tend to explode or
015                vanish. In this contribution, we introduce a computational approach to construct
016                all weights and biases of a recurrent neural network without using gradient-based
017                methods. The approach is based on a combination of random feature networks
018                and Koopman operator theory for dynamical systems. The hidden parameters
019
                   of a single recurrent block are sampled at random, while the outer weights are
                   constructed using extended dynamic mode decomposition. This approach alleviates
020
                   all problems with backpropagation commonly related to recurrent networks. The
021
                   connection to Koopman operator theory also allows us to start using results in this
022                area to analyze recurrent neural networks. In computational experiments on time
023                series, forecasting for chaotic dynamical systems, and control problems, as well as
024                on weather data, we observe that the training time and forecasting accuracy of the
025                recurrent neural networks we construct are improved when compared to commonly
026                used gradient-based methods.

029   1       I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Recurrent neural networks (RNNs) are notoriously difficult to train because their loss gradients
032   backpropagated in time tend to saturate or diverge during training, commonly referred to as the
033   Exploding and Vanishing Gradient Problem (EVGP) (Pascanu et al., 2013; Schmidt et al., 2019). To
034   alleviate these problems and improve the computational load of training such networks, we consider
035   a model that completely avoids iterative gradient-descent optimization. We propose to sample the
036   hidden layer parameters of the RNN at random, before solving for the outer linear layer by least-
037   squares methods. Our first major contribution is that we consider data-dependent distributions for the
038
      weights and biases, which improves upon the accuracy and interpretability compared to data-agnostic
      distributions. Our second major contribution adds more structure to the outer layer and improve the
039
      models performance even further. We achieve this by connecting RNNs and the Koopman operator
040
      (cf. Korda & Mezić (2018a)) through a linear state-space model in a higher-dimensional space, i.e.,
041

073   2    R ELATED WORK
074
075   Exploding and vanishing gradients. For all major types of RNNs, including LSTMs and GRUs,
076   the dynamics and loss gradients of RNNs are closely linked. If the RNN dynamics converge to
077   a stable fixed point or cycle, loss gradients will remain bounded, but they may vanish (Mikhaeil
078   et al., 2022). Yet, established remedies (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997; Schmidt et al., 2019)
079   can be used to effectively prevent their gradients from vanishing. However, in chaotic dynamics,
080   gradients invariably explode, posing a challenge that cannot be mitigated through RNN architectural
081   adjustments, regularization, or constraints; instead, it necessitates addressing the problem during
082
      the training process (Mikhaeil et al., 2022). Bifurcations may also contribute to sudden jumps in
      loss observed during RNN training, potentially hindering the training process severely (Doya et al.,
083
      1992; Eisenmann et al., 2023). In Eisenmann et al. (2023), it has been demonstrated that specific
084
      bifurcations in ReLU-based RNNs are always associated with EVGP during training. Therefore, to
085   harness the full potential of RNNs, the training algorithm needs careful design to tackle challenges
086   posed by bifurcations and the possible emergence of EVGP.
087
      Curse of memory. The existence of long-term memory adversely affects the learning process of
088
      RNNs (Bengio et al., 1994; Hochreiter et al., 2001; Li et al., 2021). This negative impact is captured
089
      by the concept of the “curse of memory”, which states that when long-term memory is present in
090   the data, approximating relationships demands an exponentially large number of neurons, resulting
091   in a significant slowdown in learning dynamics. Specifically, when the target relationship includes
092   long-term memory, both the approximation and optimization of the learning process become very
093   challenging (Li et al., 2021).
094
      Loss function for chaotic and multistable dynamics reconstruction. To effectively train RNNs and
095
      evaluate reconstruction, it is crucial to carefully choose a proper loss function. The Mean Squared
096   Error (MSE) is a commonly used loss function for reconstruction tasks. MSE is derived under the
097   assumption of Gaussian noise, and it may not be the most appropriate choice when dealing with
098   chaotic systems or multistable dynamics, where the underlying noise characteristics may deviate
099   from Gaussian distribution assumptions. It is not suitable as a test loss for chaotic dynamical systems
100   due to their unpredictable behavior and abrupt changes (Wood, 2010). In multistable systems, where
101   there are multiple stable states, MSE may struggle to distinguish between these states. The loss
102   function may not adequately penalize deviations between different attractors, leading to a less accurate
103   reconstruction of the system’s multistable behavior. Despite proposed alternatives, challenges persist,
104
      and an optimal loss function for reconstructing chaotic or multistable dynamics is still lacking
      (Ciampiconi et al., 2023).
105
106   Interpretability deficiency. Deep learning models are commonly regarded as “black boxes”, and
107   existing methods to comprehend the decision-making processes of RNNs offer restricted explanations
      or rely on local theories. The lack of theory behind analyzing the training algorithms of RNNs, as


                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      well as the training process itself, are outstanding open questions in the field (Redman et al., 2023)
109   beyond models for linear systems (Datar et al., 2024).
110
111   Randomly choosing the internal network parameters. The general idea of randomly choosing
112
      the internal parameters of neural networks is studied in random feature models (including deep
      architectures).Barron (1993); Rahimi & Recht (2008) developed the basic theory, and Gallicchio &
113
      Scardapane (2020) provide a review. Reservoir computing (also called echo-state networks, cf. Jaeger
114
      & Haas (2004)) is potentially the closest idea to what we are proposing here. In a reservoir computer,
115   the internal weights are also randomly sampled, and a recurrent, time-delayed model is constructed
116   to approximate a given dynamical system. This type of architecture has been used successfully to
117   model chaotic systems (cf. Pathak et al. (2018); Gauthier et al. (2021)). While there are similarities to
118   the recurrent architecture (cf. Lukoševičius & Jaeger (2009)), the concept, as well as the architecture
119   of a reservoir computer, is often treated separately from classic recurrent neural networks that are
120   trained with backpropagation-in-time. In our work, we directly compute all parameters of classical
121   recurrent neural networks without the time-delay component present in reservoir computers.
122   Koopman operator theory. The Koopman operator is an object associated to every dynamical system.
123   It evolves observables of the state of the system in time. This evolution is linear, which is the main
124   reason the operator is employed and studied extensively for modeling dynamical systems (Mezić,
125   2005; 2013; Korda & Mezić, 2018b). Many numerical approximation algorithms exist (Schmid,
126   2010; Williams et al., 2015a; Li et al., 2017; Mezic, 2020; Schmid, 2022). The dictionary for the
127   approximation of the Koopman operator has been constructed with neural networks using gradient
128   descent (Li et al., 2017) and using random features (Salam et al., 2022). Reservoir computing has
129
      also been related to Koopman operator approximation by Bollt (2021); Gulina & Mauroy (2020).
      To our knowledge, the relation of the Koopman operator to the weight matrices of recurrent neural
130
      networks has not been observed before. This is what we discuss in this work. We also provide a
131
      data-dependent probability distribution for the hidden parameters, which is the strongest deviation
132   from the data-agnostic distributions (e.g., normal, uniform) typically used in reservoir computing.

      We start by defining a general framework of recurrent neural networks (RNNs) and the underlying
138   dynamical system before introducing sampling and its connection to the Koopman operator. Let
139   X ⊆ Rdx be an input space, Y ⊆ Rdy an output space, and H ⊆ Rdh a state space. We assume
140   that these spaces are associated with the measures µx , µy , and µh , respectively. The underlying
141   dynamical system is then defined through the evolution operator F , where we may be working in an
142   uncontrolled system ht = F (ht−1 ) or a controlled system ht = F (ht−1 , xt ). We also denote the
143   input dataset X = [x1 , x2 , . . . , xN ] and the dataset of observations Y = [y1 , y2 , . . . , yN ]. In this
144   paper we are interested in recurrent neural networks modelling dynamical systems where the state is
145   observable, and we therefore usually assume access to the dataset H = [h1 , . . . , hN ] as well as its
146
      copy after one time step H ′ = [h′1 , . . . , h′N ], where h′n = F (hn ), n ∈ {1, . . . , N }.
147   We denote activation functions as σ : R → R, where we are mainly working with σ = tanh in this
148   paper, as it is an analytic function and connects to SWIM (Bolager et al., 2023). Other functions such
149   as ReLU are also a valid choice. The following definition outlines the models we consider.
150
      Definition 1. Let Wh ∈ RM ×dh , Wx ∈ RM̂ ×dx , bh ∈ RM , bx ∈ RM̂ , Ch ∈ Rdh ×M , Cx ∈ Rdh ×M̂ ,
151
      and V ∈ Rdy ×dh . For time step t and h0 ∈ H, we define a recurrent neural network (RNN) by
152
153
                          ht = σhx (Ch σ(Wh ht−1 + bh ) + Cx σ(Wx xt + bx ) + bhx ),                            (2)
154
                          yt = V ht .                                                                           (3)

156
      Remark 1. For completeness we have added σhx as an arbitrary activation function. We choose to set
157   σhx as the identity function to let us solve for the last linear layer in the procedure described below.
158   Other activation functions such as the logit is possible as well.

160   The classical way to train this type of RNN is through iterative backpropagation, which suffers
161   the aforementioned issues such as EVGP and high computational complexity. We instead start by
      sampling the hidden layer parameters to circumvent backpropagation, as explained next.

164   Sampled neural networks are neural networks where the parameters of the hidden layers are sampled
165   from some distribution, and the last linear layer is either sampled or, more typically, solved through

167   layers of both FH and FX by sampling pairs of points from the domain H and X , and construct
168   the weights and biases from these pairs of points. One then solves a linear (general) regression
169   problem at the end for the last linear layer that maps to the next state. Concretely, let PH and PX be
170
      probability distributions over H2 and X 2 respectively. For each neuron in the hidden layer of FH ,

196   3.2   I NVOLVING THE KOOPMAN OPERATOR
197
198   We already introduced the network FM : Rdh → RM = σ(Wh · +bh ), where M is the number of
199   neurons in its single hidden layer, and likewise with GM̂ : Rdx → RM̂ = σ(Wx · +bx ). We then
200   project down to Rdh by applying Ch and Cx respectively. To add more structure and interpretability
201
      to the matrices Ch and Cx , we will set Ch = CK and Cx = CB, where K ∈ RM ×M , B ∈ RM ×M̂ ,
202   and C ∈ Rdh ×M . We then end up with the function
203
204                        ht = Czt = C(Kσ(Wh ht−1 + bh ) + Bσ(Wx xt + bx )).                               (6)
205   Note that if the output y differs from h, we take advantage of the high dimensionality and rather set
206   yt = V zt than first projecting down by C. This completes the setting from Equation (1).
207
      The reason for the splitting of matrices Ch and Cx is the following: the hidden layers FM and GM̂
208
      map their respective input to a higher dimensional space. In a higher dimensional space, the possibly
209   nonlinear evolution described by F becomes more and more linear (Korda & Mezić, 2018b). This
210   evolution is then captured by K and B before we map down to the state space through C. This also
211   allows us to connect K and B to the Koopman theory applied to the dynamical system. Given a
212   suitable functional space F, the Koopman operator K : F → R is defined as
213
                                          [Kϕ](h) = (ϕ ◦ F )(h),        ϕ ∈ F.
214
215   The Koopman operator captures the evolution of the dynamical systems in the function space F and
      not in the state space itself. In most cases, this makes the operator infinite-dimensional, but in return,

518   5          C ONCLUSION
519
520   We introduce an efficient and interpretable training method for recurrent neural networks by combin-
521   ing ideas from random feature networks and Koopman operator theory.
522   Benefits of the approach In many examples, we demonstrate that we can train accurate recurrent
523   networks orders of magnitude faster compared to networks trained by iterative methods. We also
524   observe that the training approach works with a very small amount of training data. The direct
525   connection to Koopman operator theory allows us to draw on existing theoretical results for dynamical
526   systems, which we use to prove convergence in the limit of infinite width.
527
      Limitations compared to other methods The training method we use involves the solution of a
528   large, linear system. The complexity of solving this system depends cubically on the minimum
529   number of neurons and the number of data points (respectively, time steps). This means if both the
530   network and the number of data points grow together, the computational time and memory demands
531   for training grow too quickly. With backpropagation-in-time, the memory requirements are mostly
532   because many gradients must be stored for one update pass.
533
      Remaining challenges and future work Recurrent networks are often used for tasks in computer
534   vision and natural language processing. These tasks require network architectures beyond feed-
535   forward networks, like convolutional neural networks or transformers, which means we currently
536   struggle with high dimensional data. The sampling scheme we use to construct the hidden weights
537   of the neurons is currently not useful in constructing parameters for such architectures, but it is
538   an intriguing challenge to work towards sampling them. Remaining challenges in the theoretical
539   work include extending the theory shown in this paper to controlled systems, as well as bridging the
      Koopman theory for continuous dynamical systems and NeuralODEs.

541
542
      For comparatively small problems, our construction method is orders of magnitude faster to construct
543   the parameters of a recurrent network. As neural networks are generally dual-use, our work potentially
544   also allows faster training for misuse of this technology. Still, we connect the construction of
545   recurrent neural networks to Koopman operator theory and dynamical systems. This connection
546   allows researchers in these fields to better understand the behavior, failure modes, and robustness
547   of recurrent architectures. In addition, by sampling weights and bias from the input spaces it adds
548   interpretability to the models which again lets users understand better the underlying networks used
549   (see Appendix C for an example of this). We believe that this far outweighs the potential downsides
550   of misuse because recurrent architectures that are understood much better can also be regulated in a

815   In this section we give a more thorough introduction to the Koopman operator and its use in dynamical
816
      system theory. In addition, we also walk the reader through extended dynamic mode decomposition
      (EDMD), which is the finite approximation of the Koopman operator we use in the main paper.
817
818   Consider a dynamical system (H, F ), where the state space H is a topological space and F : H → H
819   is an evolution operator/map. We impose more structure on our system by requiring our state space
820   H to be a measure space (H, F, µ), and F to be F-measurable. We start by considering (H, F ) to
821   be a discrete-time system, which are the systems we mainly work with in this paper. We can then

1496   With the forced Van der Pol example, presented in Section 4.4, we use the sampled RNN as a
1497   surrogate model to a controller, essentially making this a model-based control example. In order to
1498   be able to accurately control the state, the surrogate needs to capture the dynamics of the system with
1499   sufficient accuracy. The hyperparameters are listed in Table 2. We opted for an LQR controller as our
1500   optimal controller, as a simple choice for linear systems. Although the Van der Pol oscillator is not a
1501   linear system, the surrogate sampled RNN captures the dynamics via a linear map with the Koopman
1502
       operator, allowing minimizing the cost. For our LQR, R = 1, and Q = diag(10). The dataset we use
       contains randomly initialized trajectories evolved in time, as well as randomly chosen control inputs.
1503
       We observed that a dataset with many shorter trajectories is more useful than a dataset with a few
1504
       longer trajectories since for this problem diverse initial conditions are more informative of the vector
1505   field of the system, as opposed to long trajectories which become periodic. The necessary effort for
1506   tuning the sampled RNN hyperparameters and LQR controller parameters was low.

1519   H.1.1    LQR WITH NONLINEAR LIFTING
1520
1521   In this section we describe shortly how we perform model predictive control described in Algorithm 4
1522   with nonlinear lifting GM̂ , i.e. applying a sampling hidden layer on the input x. The experiment in
1523   the main paper was mainly done by setting GM̂ to the identity, but adding nonlinearity with M̂ > dx
1524   is possible and may beneficial for future testing, even though for the particular system in the main
1525   paper, it yielded equivalent results as with GM̂ ≡ Id.
1526
       Following Algorithm 4, we lift xt to RM̂ through the hidden layer GM̂ . Then we solve for B exactly
1527   as described in Algorithm 2 and fit the LQR. The LQR computes the control input, but now in the
1528
       RM̂ space. Before we can pass it to F , we approximate a linear map P , such that P GM̂ (xt ) ≈ xt .
1529
       Once projected down, we can pass the projected value x̂t to F and continue as usual.

1627   We use Tensorflow (Abadi et al., 2015) to train a baseline LSTM for the weather data problem of
1628   predicting the temperature. An LSTM is trained similarly to the process by TensorFlow (2024).
1629   After training the weather models, we compute the predictions with the specified horizon and
1630   concatenate them into a single time series. More specifically, if the horizon is set to one day with a
1631   time delay of one week, the prediction on a dataset split goes as follows. First, we use ground-truth
1632   values of days one to seven to predict the value for day eight. Then, we use the ground-truth values
1633   of days two to eight to predict the value for day nine. This process is repeated until we reach the
1634   end of the split. Then, the resulting predictions are concatenated and compared to the ground-truth
1635
       measurements. This prediction process alligns with the one used for sampled RNNs to ensure a
       consistent comparison.
