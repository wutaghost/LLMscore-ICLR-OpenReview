011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Recent research has shown significant progress in the field of zero-shot text
014            reranking for large language models (LLMs). Traditional pointwise approaches
015
               prompt the LLM to output relevance labels such as ”yes/no” or fine-grained la-
               bels, but they have several drawbacks. Firstly, these prompts struggle to capture
016
               complex correlations between queries and passages and lack robustness for out-
017
               puts not covered by predefined labels. Secondly, ranking scores rely solely on the
018            likelihood of relevance labels, leading to potential noise and bias. Lastly, exist-
019            ing pointwise approaches are not supported by decoder-only LLMs, as ranking
020            requires LLMs to output prediction probabilities. In response to these challenges,
021            a novel pointwise approach called yesno-pro has been designed, which redefines
022            both prompt design and score computation mechanisms to better align with the
023            intrinsic nature of text reranking. Additionally, a comprehensive reranking frame-
024            work based on LLM services has been proposed to support concurrent ranking
025            calls and quickly adapt to any open-source decoder-only large models. Experi-
026
               mental results have demonstrated that this method outperforms existing pointwise
               and some pairwise/listwise methods on TREC19/20 and BEIR datasets, achiev-
027
               ing the state-of-the-art performance. Due to its concurrency features, this work is
028
               applicable to practical applications with high real-time requirements.
029
030
031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033   In recent years, LLMs(large language models) have significantly empowered an increasing number
034   of domains. In this paper, we focus specifically on zero-shot text reranking. Compared to traditional
035   methods, using LLMs to perform zero-shot text reranking demonstrates significant advantages due
036   to their capability to understand knowledge across diverse domains. For example, utilizing LLMs
037   for text reordering mitigates the necessity for repeated fine-tuning of small models in situations
038   where knowledge is subject to frequent changes across different domains. This approach effectively
039
      reduces both resource expenditure and time. Recent works related to zero-shot LLM rankers can be
      categorized into three types: pairwise, listwise, and pointwise methods.
040
041   In pairwise approaches, LLMs are prompted with a query along with a pair of passages to perform
042   ranking tasks(Qin et al., 2023)(Luo et al., 2024). Conversely, listwise approaches involve prompting
043   the LLM with a query and a comprehensive list of passages(Sun et al., 2023). The two approaches
044   share the following common points: (1) They require the design of sampling strategies, such as
045
      ”sliding window” and ”all pair”, to obtain candidate passage sets that cover all passages of a query.
      These candidate passage sets can be structured as either pairs or lists; (2)The ranking of any given
046
      passage is inherently relative, being contingent upon comparisons with other passages. This leads
047
      to three drawbacks: (1) Due to the max token length limitation of LLMs, pairwise and listwise ap-
048   proaches cannot be scaled to long lists. (2) The required sampling strategies introduce additional
049   time overhead. Particularly in pairwise methods, the total number of candidate passage sets often
050   exceeds the number of original passages, leading to an increase in overall inference times. (3) The
051   comparative nature prevents the two approaches from supporting concurrent invocations. These fac-
052   tors render pairwise and listwise approaches relatively slow, and limit their application in practical
053   scenarios with high real-time requirements, thereby confining their use predominantly to research
      settings.

054
      Existing pointwise rankers can be categorized into two classes: query generation and relevance
055   generation. Query generation often ranks documents based on the query likelihood from LLM
056   given the passage(Sachan et al., 2022a). With superior performance, relevance generation generally
057   prompts the LLM to generate relevance labels given a query and a passage,such as ”yes/no” or
058   ”0,1,2,3”, and subsequently derives ranking score based on their likelihood. However, this approach
059   suffer from these drawbacks: (1) Existing pointwise prompts are insufficient to capture the intrinsic
060   relevance between queries and passages. This indicates that, although there may be some connection
061   between the passage and the query, it does not provide actual assistance in answering or resolving
062   the query. We will discuss this in detail in Section 2.1; (2) At times, the outputs of LLMs do
063
      not conform to predefined relevance labels, and current approaches fail to effectively mitigate this
      issue, lacking corresponding solutions; (3) Zero-shot text reranking is generally applied to reorder
064
      passages based on the ranking results from the first-stage, but existing methods do not fully leverage
065
      these first-stage ranking results, which constrains further improvements in ranking performance; (4)
066   Current pointwise approaches are applicable to encoder-decoder LLMs but do not support decoder-
067   only LLMs. Furthermore, there exists a lack of an efficient and deployable framework for text
068   rankers using LLMs in the open-source domain.
069
      A question naturally arises: Is it possible to propose a new zero-shot LLM reranking method that
070
      overcomes the shortcomings of pairwise/listwise and pointwise approaches while balancing both
071
      accuracy and efficiency?
072
073   In this work, we present a novel pointwise method: Yesno-Pro. This approach introduces a effective
074   prompt template that guides LLMs in capturing the key correlations between queries and passages
075
      and employs a comprehensive scoring computation method that leverages the first-stage ranking
      results, mitigating biases and noise arising from reliance on a singular information source. Further-
076
      more, we developed a VLLM-based text reranking framework that supports concurrent calls and
077
      significantly enhances the ranking speed. Our contributions can be summarized as follows:
078
079   1. We propose a new approach: Yesno-Pro. By optimizing the prompt and ranking score deriving
080   methods, this approach substantially improves the performance and surpass other pointwise llm
081
      rankers and some pairwise/listwise approaches.
082   2. Yesno-Pro is the first pointwise approach capable of supporting both encoder-decoder and
083   decoder-only LLMs.
084
      3. We develope a LLM text reranking framework that can swiftly adapt to any LLM model supported
085   by open VLLM, which support concurrent calls and demonstrates superior speed compared to all
086   existing LLM text rankers.

      2     M OTIVATION
090
091
092   The introduction has briefly outlined the drawbacks of existing listwise/pairwise and pointwise
093   methods. This section will delve deeper into the fundamental reasons behind the suboptimal perfor-
094   mance of current pointwise approaches, focusing on two key aspects: prompt design and ranking
095
      score computation, illustrated with concrete examples.

      2.1   PROMPT DESIGN
098
099
      Fig 1 lists the prompts in popular pointwise methods. Among these, prompts (a), (b), and (c) guide
100
      the large model to output relevance labels between queries and documents. However, such prompts
101
      may hinder LLMs to effectively capture the critical relevance between them. For instance, consider
102   a user query such as ”What are the market trends in the electric vehicles?” Document 1 states: ”The
103   market trend for traditional gasoline vehicles is gradually declining, influenced by electric vehicles
104   and environmental policies, leading to shifts in consumer preferences.” In contrast, Document 2
105   asserts: ”Sales of electric vehicles have increased by 30% over the past year.” In reality, Document
106   2 should be prioritized over Document 1, as it directly addresses the user’s query. However, if
107   relevance-based prompts are employed, the model might inaccurately prioritize Document 1 over
      Document 2, as it could focus on some irrelevant aspects of information.

139
      2.2    RANKING SCORE COMPUTATION
140
141   Existing LLM rerankers are typically employed to refine the ranking of passages subsequent to the
142   initial ranking stage. Popular pointwise approaches derive ranking scores based on the probabilities
143   of relevant labels generated by LLMs. Inspired by (Luo et al., 2024), we identify a significant
144   limitation of this approach: it does not effectively integrate information from the first-stage ranking.
145   In order to avoid noise and bias, we should consider how to effectively integrate ranking results of
146   the two stages to enhance overall ranking performance.
147   In addition, current pointwise approaches only support LLMs of encoder-decoder structur, but the
148   majority of contemporary and advanced LLMs utilize a decoder-only structure. This limitation
149   constrains the applicability of pointwise LLM rerankers across diverse contexts.

154
      Considering the aforementioned in-depth analysis of the suboptimal performance and usability chal-
      lenges of existing pointwise approaches, we propose Yesno-Pro, which optimizes prompt design and
155
      ranking score computation.

      3.1    PROMPT DESIGN

159
      We designed the following prompt:
160
161   ”Passage:{text} Query:{query} Does this passage contain the information needed to answer the
      question? Please respond directly with ’Yes’ or ’No’.”

      In this prompt, ”Does this passage contain the information needed to answer the question?” ad-
163   dresses the challenge that relevant-based prompts’ inability to adequately guide LLMs to capture
164   the critical relevance between passages and queries. It also mitigates the confusion that arises from
165   ”Does the passage answer the query?”, as some passages may not directly respond to the query
166   posed. Furthermore, the instruction ”Please respond directly with ’Yes’ or ’No’.” effectively re-
167   duces the negative impact on the ranking results caused by the fact that LLMs do not always produce
168   responses aligned with the predefined relevant labels.
169   We will conduct a detailed investigation into the effects of different prompts in Ablation Study.

      3.2   RANKING SCORE COMPUTATION
172
173   Given a query q and its first-stage ranking results D = {(d1 , r1 ), (d2 , r2 ), ..., (dn , rn )}, where di
174   denotes the i-th passage, ri represents the score of di , and n is the total number of passages, the
175   process of reranking is to prompt LLMs, derive ranking scores for each (q, di )and subsequently
176   perform sorting based on ranking scores.
177   For encoder-decoder LLMs, following traditional methods, we can caculate ranking scores by:
178
179                                                            epi,1

                                                           epi,1 + epi,0

      where,

                                 pi,1 = LLM (Y es|q.di ), pi,2 = LLM (N o|q, di )

184   Given a query q and the i-th passage di , pi,1 denotes the likeklihood that LLM outouts ’Yes’ , and pi,2
185   denotes the likelihood that LLM outputs ’No’. The ranking score si is calculated using a softmax
186   function.
187
      For decoder-only LLMs, to overcome the limitation that pointwise approaches only support encoder-
188   decoder LLMs, we optimized the vllm framework(Kwon et al., 2023), a widely used LLM server, to
189   enable the LLMs to ouput not only generated tokens but also the logits corresponding to each token.
190

192                                      S = {(t1 , l1 ), (t2 , l2 ), ..., (tN , lN )}

194   where N denotes the number of output tokens, ti denotes the i-th output token, and li ∈ R1×K denotes
195   the logits corresponding to the i-th output token, here K is the vocabulary size.
196

197
      situations here. In the first scenario, the output contains either ’Yes’ or ’No’, and we denote the posi-

199   to token p for ranking score calculation. Let us denote the token id for ’Yes’ as m and the token id

231   TREC encompasses a diverse array of topics and text types, including news articles, web content,

      the test sets of passage reranking tasks in TREC-DL 19 and TREC-DL 20. Sharing the same MS

412   5   C ONCLUSION
413
414   In this paper, we introduce YesNo-Pro, a novel pointwise ranking method. By optimizing both the
415   prompt design and the ranking score derivation, our approach significantly surpasses existing point-
416   wise and listwise methods, achieving state-of-the-art results. Furthermore, we have enhanced the
417   VLLM framework to support YesNo-Pro, substantially accelerating the ranking speed and making
418   it the fastest among current pointwise, pairwise, and listwise methods. This adaptation offers ad-
419
      ditional advantages, such as bridging encoder-decoder and decoder-only LLMs, and enabling the
      algorithm to support concurrent calls. Consequently, it can be rapidly adapted to any open-source
420
      LLMs and efficiently deployed in practical scenarios. We are looking forward to further study in
421
      highly effective and rapid pointwise LLM rerankers.
