009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Reinforcement learning is a general method for learning in sequential settings, but
012            it can often be difficult to specify a good reward function when the task is com-
013            plex. In these cases, preference feedback or expert demonstrations can be used
014            instead. However, existing approaches utilising both together are either ad-hoc or
015            rely on domain-specific properties. Building upon previous work, we develop a
016            novel theoretical framework for learning from human data. Based on this we in-
017            troduce LEOPARD: Learning Estimated Objectives from Preferences And Ranked
018
               Demonstrations. LEOPARD can simultaneously learn from a broad range of data,
               including negative/failed demonstrations, to effectively learn reward functions in
019
               general domains. It does this by modelling the human feedback as reward-rational
020
               partial orderings over available trajectories. We find that when a limited amount of
021            human feedback is available, LEOPARD outperforms the current standard prac-
022            tice of pre-training on demonstrations and finetuning on preferences, as well as
023            other baselines. Furthermore, we show that LEOPARD learns faster when given
024            many types of feedback, rather than just a single one.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Reinforcement Learning (RL) is a branch of machine learning where an agent learns a behavioural
030   policy by interacting with an environment and receiving rewards. These rewards are determined by
031   a reward function that mathematically encodes the objective of the agent. For real-world practical
032   applications of RL, such as robotics or Large Language Model (LLM) finetuning, the specification of
033
      the reward function poses a difficult challenge. Two popular RL subfields try to solve this problem by
      leveraging human data in order to learn what the reward function should be, typically by optimising
034
      a parameterised function such as a neural network.
035
036   Inverse RL (IRL) utilises human-provided demonstrations of the correct behaviour and tries to learn
037   a reward function for which only the demonstrations, or similar behaviour, are near-optimal (Ng
038   et al., 2000; Ziebart et al., 2008; Wulfmeier et al., 2015). RL from Human Feedback (RLHF)
039
      presents the human with pairs of agent–behaviour examples. For each pair, the human decides
      which piece of behaviour is better, and the reward function is trained to re-produce this preference
040
      (Christiano et al., 2017). Both methods iterate between reward model and agent training. For more
041
      details on IRL and RLHF, see sections 2.1 and 2.2, respectively. For many applications it might be
042   possible and desirable to generate and learn from both of these feedback types, rather than com-
043   mitting to a single one. The current standard approach is to first train on demonstrations and then
044   finetune the resulting model with preferences (Ibarz et al., 2018; Palan et al., 2019; Bıyık et al.,
045   2022). Some methods have been proposed to more effectively leverage the information encoded in
046   both the preferences and demonstrations, but this is still largely ad-hoc or specific to certain domains
047   (Krasheninnikov et al., 2021; Mehta & Losey, 2023; Brown et al., 2019). We discuss these methods
048   further in section 2.3.
049   In an attempt to solve this problem for general domains—and for many types of feedback including
050   preferences and demonstrations—Jeon et al. (2020) propose Reward-Rational Choice (RRC). This
051   frames the human feedback data as Boltzmann-Rational choices according to a probability distribu-
052   tion which has been induced by some unknown true reward function. Learning the reward function
053   can then be cast as a supervised learning problem where we try to replicate these choices. Unfortu-
      nately, RRC is often difficult to implement in practice. For example, in the case of demonstration

101
102   To summarise, we make the following contributions:
103          1. We introduce RRPO, a practical and general framework for interpreting human feedback.
104

105

106
107

      2       R ELATED W ORK AND BACKGROUND

      2.1       D EMONSTRATION -BASED RL
111
112   A popular paradigm for learning from demonstrations is Inverse RL (IRL), where the demonstrations
113   are used to learn a reward function (Ng et al., 2000). This overcomes many issues of behavioural
114   cloning, which aims to directly mimic the given demonstrations (Bratko et al., 1995). Many cur-
115   rent methods for IRL are based on the principle of maximum (causal) entropy (MaxEnt; MCE),
116   established by Ziebart et al. (2008; 2010). This learns a reward function that captures the fact
117   that the human demonstrations are optimal, but beyond this, it tries to have as much uncertainty
118
      about the reward dynamics as possible. Assuming a deterministic environment simplifies MCE into
      MaxEnt, and this assumption has been used to extend this class of methods into settings with high-
119
      dimensional observation spaces, e.g. DeepIRL (Wulfmeier et al., 2015). Advanced extensions of
120
      DeepIRL have been proposed, leveraging methods such as importance sampling (Finn et al., 2016),
121   or GAN-style architectures (Fu et al., 2018). For a more comprehensive introduction to MCE and
122   its derivatives, see Gleave & Toyer (2022). Our proposed algorithm does not reduce to a MaxEnt-
123   derived method in the demonstration only case, but is still inspired by the principle and is of a
124   similar form. Bayesian methods in IRL have also been explored (Ramachandran & Amir, 2007;
125   Brown et al., 2020), highlighting how a probabilistic framing of the inverse learning problem can be
126   useful.
127
128   2.2       P REFERENCE -BASED RL
129

132
      recent agent behaviour, fitting the reward model to the comparison dataset, and training of the policy
133
      on the learnt reward function. The reward model is fitted by minimising the following loss function:

144   Recently, RLHF has been used for instruction and safety-finetuning large language models (LLMs)

152
      be compatible with other sources of feedback, whilst still being practically applicable to problems
      like LLM finetuning.

155   2.3       C OMBINING D EMONSTRATIONS AND P REFERENCE F EEDBACK
156
      As mentioned in the case for LLMs, demonstration and preference feedback are typically combined
157
      by pre-training on the demonstration data using IRL/behavioural-cloning methods, and then fine-
158   tuning the resulting reward model on preferences using RLHF (Ibarz et al., 2018; Palan et al., 2019;
159   Bıyık et al., 2022). This works well in practice, but it is unclear how to add in further reward infor-
160   mation, such as negative demonstrations or the relative rankings of demonstrations. Additionally,

163   strong regularisation is applied to the reward model, or the RL policy does not sufficiently explore
164   when training on the demonstrations.

166
      More sophisticated combinations of preferences and demonstrations have been considered.
      Krasheninnikov et al. (2021) sampled trajectories according to reward functions optimal for the pref-
167
      erences, and applied MCE-IRL. This approach is computationally expensive and limited to linear
168
      reward functions over tabular MDPs. Mehta & Losey (2023) combine preferences and demonstra-
169   tions alongside corrections (Bajcsy et al., 2017), but leverage domain-specific properties of robotics
170   and encode their demonstrations using trajectory-space perturbations. This method is not applica-
171   ble outside of robotics, and loses information about how demonstrations are better than most of
172   trajectory-space, not just better than nearby trajectories. Brown et al. (2019) and Brown & Niekum
173   (2019) both subsample ranked demonstrations to produce preferences for training the reward model,
174   giving good results but still losing information about how those demonstrations might be preferred
175   to other trajectories. Taranovic et al. (2022) combines a novel preference loss with adversarial imi-
176   tation learning. This is the closest to our work, and so we test against it as a baseline. We also note
177
      that none of these methods can be easily extended to other types of feedback.
178   Our method enables learning from preference and demonstration feedback in a principled manner,
179   without leveraging domain-specific properties, and in a way that can be readily extended.

181   2.4       L EARNING F ROM OTHER T YPES OF F EEDBACK
182
183   Other types of feedback have been explored in isolation, such as negative demonstrations (Xie et al.,
184
      2019),2 improvements (Jain et al., 2015), off-signals (Hadfield-Menell et al., 2017a), natural lan-
      guage (Matuszek et al., 2012), proxy reward functions (Hadfield-Menell et al., 2017b), and even the
185
      initial state (Shah et al., 2019). Jeon et al. (2020) interpret many of these types of feedback as part
186
      of an overarching formalism, reward-rational (implicit) choice (RRC), providing a mathematical
187   theory for reward learning that combines different types of feedback.
188
189   RRC interprets each piece of human feedback as a Boltzmann-Rational choice C from some (possi-
190
      bly implicit) set of choices D with rationality coefficient β. A grounding function, ψ, maps choices
      to distributions over trajectories. The expected reward over these distributions gives the value for
191
      each choice under the Boltzmann-Rational model, according to some reward function Rθ .

207   3       M ETHOD
208
209   We propose LEOPARD, a method for learning from preferences, positive demonstrations, negative
210   demonstrations, and partial rankings over the given demonstrations. It is practical, flexible, and
211
      applicable to many environments. The aim is that a practitioner can give any and all feedback
      possible to the learning algorithm, and this feedback can be continuously learnt from and added
212
      to. First, we develop a general theoretical framework, reward-rational partial ordering (RRPO),
213
      extending that of deterministic reward-rational choice (RRC, Jeon et al. (2020)). Then, we apply
214   this to the specific case of learning from preferences and mixed demonstrations.

      3.1    R EWARD R ATIONAL PARTIAL O RDERINGS
217
218   To ensure the general applicability of our theoretical formalisms, we assume that only the trajectories
219   our reward optimisation procedure has access to are provided directly. These could be generated dur-
220   ing the agent’s training or provided by the human in the case of demonstrations. This is assumed as
221   sensible/relevant trajectories could sit on an unknown manifold in (a high-dimensional) observation
222   space, crippling random-sampling based approaches.3 We’d expect that reward functions captur-
223   ing complex desirable behaviour would not be linear, but that they could at least be approximated
224
      sufficiently by some differentiable parameterised function.
225   Our key insight is to interpret human feedback as a set of Boltzmann-Rational choices encoding
226   strict partial orderings over the trajectory-fragments we have direct access to, where a fragment
227   is a contiguous subsequence of a trajectory. For each item in the partial order, we ‘choose’ that
228   element out of a set containing itself and all elements strictly less than it. This is analogous to the
229   Plackett-Luce ranking model (Marden, 1996), and is equivalent when the ordering can be viewed as
230
      a total ordering embedded in some larger set. Similar to RRC, each partial ordering is assumed to
      be independent given the reward function. Since a partial order may encode a single element being
231
      greater than all others with no other relations, this generalises deterministic choices of RRC.
232

234   C = {<j }j the set of human feedback, and Rθ our non-linear reward function parameterised by θ.

287   4    E XPERIMENTS
288
289   We test our method on several environments in order to evaluate its performance across a broad
290   variety of domains. Additionally, we also vary the proportions and amounts of different types of
291   feedback used for learning to demonstrate that combining demonstrations and preferences can give
292   stronger performance than just relying on either one. In order to reduce the cost of testing our method
293
      and facilitate hyperparameter tuning with many repetitions, we synthetically generate preferences,
      demonstrations, and their rankings. We generate preferences by sampling using the sigmoid of the
294
      reward difference between the two fragments under comparison as the probability of preference. We
295
      generate demonstrations by training an agent on the ground truth reward function and then sampling
296   its trajectories, with their ground truth reward determining their relative rankings. For further details,
297   see Appendix A.2.
298
299
      We experimentally evaluate LEOPARD on four environments from the Gymnasium (Towers et al.,
      2024) test suite: Half Cheetah (MuJoCo), Cliff Walking (Toy Text), Lunar Lander (Box2D), and Ant
300
      (MuJoCo). This covers a range of continuous and discrete observation and action spaces, reward
301
      sparsities, and overall complexities. We require a finite horizon to reduce complications from the
302   preference and demonstration learning, so some environments required modification. These and
303   other environment details are given in Appendix B.
304
305
      We organise our experiments into two sections. In the first, we compare our method to baselines.
      For the case of preferences and positive demonstrations, we compare against Adversarial Imitation
306
      Learning with Preferences (AILP, Taranovic et al. (2022))6 and a standard pipeline of training on
307
      demonstrations with DeepIRL and then preference finetuning with RLHF. As an ablation, on Half
308   Cheetah we also test first training on preferences with RLHF, and then on demonstrations with
309   DeepIRL. We find that, except on Ant, LEOPARD always outperforms all baselines. On Ant, it lags
310   behind AILP but is still far better than the standard pipeline.
311
      With positive demonstrations only, we show that LEOPARD either performs similarly or beats the
312
      baselines, depending on the environment, For preferences only, our method directly reduces to
313
      RLHF and so no comparison is needed. For LEOPARD and AILP, when training the reward model,
314   we keep training until the loss has loosely converged (see Appendix A.1.3 for details). This is not
315   possible with DeepIRL as the maximum-entropy ‘loss’ function is not bounded from below. There-
316   fore, we use a fixed number of training epochs for the reward model with the associated baselines,
317   and give results for a variety of values.

476
477   6.1               G ENERALITY OF RRPO
478
      Reward-rational preference orderings, the basis of LEOPARD, are a generalisation of the determin-
479
      istic reward-rational choice framework (Jeon et al., 2020), but offers several distinct advantages.
480   Recall that RRC frames the human feedback as a choice over some set, and then maps elements of
481   that set into distributions over trajectories. Instead, RRPO maps the human feedback directly into
482   a set of partial orderings. These two approaches have differing flexibility, and different feedback
483   types might lend themselves more readily to one or the other. However, as RRPO is explicit in its
484   construction that it operates only over directly-accessible trajectories, it becomes much more gen-
485   eral in a practical sense. For example, in RRC, demonstration feedback requires optimising over the
      entire trajectory space, while RRPO does not.


                                                                                                                 9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      Furthermore, RRPO does not assume any particular properties about the space of reward functions,
487   nor the space of trajectories. In general, one can think of optimal trajectories as a small part of
488   some feasible-trajectory manifold, which itself is a small part in a larger trajectory feature space.
489   Methods which rely on domain-specific properties of these spaces, such as linearity or computable
490   perturbations, inherently limit themselves from being more broadly applied. For example, Mehta &
491   Losey (2023) leverages inverse kinematics models to interpret demonstration feedback (alongside
492   preferences) in robotics domains. Whilst effective for this application, it renders the broader method
493   impossible outside of robotics. RRPO and LEOPARD on the other hand, could be easily applied
494   to environments very different to the ones we have tested on. For example, they could be used for
495
      Large Language Model (LLM) and foundation-model finetuning.
496
497   6.2   L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
498
      Whilst we have tested LEOPARD on a range of environments with differently structured observation
499
      and action spaces, a more comprehensive study would investigate an even wider range of tasks, such
500   as more complex robotics, Atari games, and even LLM finetuning. Furthermore, with additional
501   resources, it would be instructive to more closely interrogate how performance depends on the pro-
502   portions of different feedback used for learning. For instance, future work could vary the feedback
503   proportions with greater precision, and include additional repetitions.
504
      Additionally, there are other methods that seek to learn from both preference and demonstration
505
      data, or even negative/failed demonstrations, as detailed in sections 2.3 and 2.4. Whilst these are
506   less general in application than LEOPARD; a comparison of performance would still be interesting.
507   We have chosen the baselines of AILP and ‘DeepIRL followed by RLHF’ to test against as they have
508   similar simplicity and generality to our own method, as well as the latter being common practice.
509
      We introduce RRPO as a theoretical backdrop for LEOPARD, however our investigation of its prop-
510
      erties and encodings for many types of feedback is limited. Due to its similarity to RRC and the
511
      Placket-Luce choice model, we do not see this as a critical failing, as it will inherit many proper-
512   ties from those models, and deterministic RRC formulations can be trivially encoded under RRPO.
513   Nevertheless, there are likely important theoretical properties and applications of RRPO that are of
514   relevance to reward learning that ought to be investigated.
515
      These limitations largely stem from constraints on time and computational resources. Thus, they are
516
      left to be resolved by future work.
517
518
      6.3   C ONCLUSION
519
520   We have shown that LEOPARD can perform effective reward inference, learning from many sources
521   of reward information simultaneously. It is more effective than standard baselines for learning from
522   preferences and demonstrations, and can additionally incorporate more information such as demon-
523   stration rankings and negative/failed demonstrations. We have also shown that using many sources
524   of reward information can be more beneficial than relying on only large amounts of a single type.
525   Whilst our empirical work is non-extensive, the generality and simplicity of the method makes it
526   very powerful and potentially applicable to important current problems such as high dimensional
527   robotics, and LLM / foundation-model finetuning. Furthermore, it opens the door to exploring the
528
      use of a much wider range of feedback in many RL settings.

780   A.1.3    S TOPPING C ONDITIONS
781
      Generally, the reward function loss from poorly-fitted demonstration rankings are much higher than
782
      poorly fitted preferences. This is because trajectories are typically longer than trajectory-fragments
783
      and demonstrations generate more ‘<’ comparisons than a preference. However, the distribution of
784   demonstrations are typically quite far from that of the agent trajectories, which the preferences have
785   been generated over. This makes it much easier for the reward function to separate the demonstra-
786   tions from agent behaviour and thus achieve a low loss on the demonstration ordering, than it does
787   for it to get low loss on all the preference orderings.
788
      The consequence of the above two facts is that if we were training on just the demonstrations, we’d
789
      want to do at most a few epochs (to learn fast and avoid overfitting), but if we were training on just
790   the preferences we might want to do more (as learning is slower and overfitting less of a potential
791   issue). Thus, as the amount of data in each dataset varies in each iteration, it does not make sense to
792   have a pre-specified number of training steps, and instead a stopping condition should be used.
793
      Our stopping condition simply checks if the training loss has loosely converged. At each step we
794
      check if the training loss is within ±0.001 of the last step’s training loss. If this occurs 3 times in a
795
      row, we stop training the reward model for that iteration, and return to agent training. Empirically

1173   E.1     T HEORETICAL P ROPERTIES
1174
1175   Neither PSum-of-Choices nor PChoose-Best-Average have the property that upper bounds on their negative-
1176   log-likelihood give rise to lower bounds on reward differences between demonstrated trajectories and
1177   ones sampled from the agent, unlike PRRPO . We prove this in theorems 2 and 3 in Appendix E.2.1.
1178
       Whilst this may not seem too critical, its combination with the potential effects of PRank , and its
       interaction with exploration in RL, can cause a very undesirable failure mode.
1179
1180   Imagine an environment where three distinct behaviours are possible, A, B, and C. We prefer C to
1181   B, and B to A, so we provide a demonstration of B and C each, τb , τc , and express via the ranking
1182   model that τc ≻ τb . This ranking is fitted by assigning high reward to C, and low to B. Our agent is
1183   initialised generating from A. Our demonstration model, seeing τc have high reward, does not lower
1184
       the reward of A that much, and does not mind that τb has low reward. We’re left with low loss and
       yet a reward model that could prefer A to B.
1185
1186   Now consider that our environment has some unfavourable dynamics. Policies that generate A, are
1187   quite different from those that generate C, with B being somewhere between the two. Thus, to
       eventually generate C, our policy will first need to explore B. However, our reward model gives it


                                                                          22
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1188
       lower reward when it tries this, and so the agent sticks to what it thinks is best, behaviour A, much
1189   to our disappointment.
1190
1191   Whilst a little contrived, the above story highlights a certain failure mode that could occur if one
1192
       combined demonstration rankings with a demonstration model that does not satisfy theorem 1. If it
       did satisfy it, such as for RRPO and LEOPARD, then low loss cannot be achieved unless the reward
1193
       model prefers B to A, preventing the issue.
1194
1195   Alleviating this problem by omitting the rankings is suboptimal, as we lose information. However,
1196   PSum-of-Choices suffers further. It is shown in Appendix E.2.2 that the gradient of LSoC with respect to
1197   θ can be expressed in the following form.

1203   trajectories are pushed down proportional to the probability that they would be chosen out of the
1204   combined set of trajectories. This makes sense—if our reward model thinks highly of specific agent
1205   trajectories, it ought to adjust its beliefs so that it no longer favours them.
1206   However, the demonstration trajectories are also pushed up in reward proportional to the probability
1207   that they would be chosen. That is to say, the better the reward model thinks the demonstrated
1208   trajectory is, the more it thinks it should increase its reward, a positive feedback loop! In practice,
1209   the reward model is going to have some initial preferences over the demonstrated trajectories due to
1210   its initialisation. Since this will be random, it will most likely be incorrect. It will then proceed to
1211   reinforce its own incorrect beliefs and lock-in its own ranking of the demonstrations. This means
1212
       our reward model will not provide correct rewards to guide the agent towards better behaviour in
       the trajectory space around the demonstrations. Furthermore, if it generalises from these incorrect
1213
       beliefs, it could also become wrong about other parts of trajectory space, further reducing the quality
1214
       of the reward signal for the agent.
