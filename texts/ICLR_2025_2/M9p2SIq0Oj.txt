011                                                    A BSTRACT
012
013                Continual Reinforcement Learning (CRL) is a powerful tool that enables agents
014                to learn a sequence of tasks, accumulating knowledge learned in the past and us-
015
                   ing it for problem-solving or future task learning. However, existing CRL meth-
                   ods all assume that the agent’s capabilities remain static within dynamic environ-
016
                   ments, which doesn’t reflect real-world scenarios where capabilities evolve. This
017
                   paper introduces Self-Evolution Continual Reinforcement Learning (SE-CRL), a
018                new and realistic problem where the agent’s action space continually changes. It
019                presents a significant challenge for RL agents: How can policy generalization
020                across different action spaces be achieved? Inspired by the cortical functions
021                that lead to consistent human behavior, we propose an Action Representation
022                Continual Reinforcement Learning framework (ARC-RL) to address this chal-
023                lenge. Our framework builds an action representation space by self-supervised
024                learning on transitions, decoupling the agent’s policy from the specific action
025                space. For a new action space, the decoder of the action representation is ex-
026
                   panded or masked for adaptation and regularized fine-tuned to improve the sta-
                   bility of the policy. Furthermore, we release a benchmark based on MiniGrid
027
                   and Procgen to validate the effectiveness of methods for SE-CRL. Experimental
028
                   results demonstrate that our framework significantly outperforms popular CRL
029                methods by generalizing the policy across different action spaces. 1

089   Animals, including humans, consistently perform behaviors even years after learning (Georgopoulos
090   & Pellizzer, 1995; Emmons-Bell et al., 2019; Blackiston et al., 2015). It is due to the brain’s abil-
091   ity to represent actions in a latent space, allowing for the generalization across different contexts.
092
      Precisely, the stability of latent dynamics of neural activity reflects a fundamental feature of learned
      cortical function, leading to stable and consistent behavior (Gallego et al., 2020). In addition, the
093
      research on self-supervised learning for reinforcement learning has been shown to be effective in
094
      improving the generalization ability of the agent (Chandak et al., 2019; Liu et al., 2024; Fang &
095   Stachenfeld, 2024).
096
097   Inspired by these, we propose an Action Representation Continual Reinforcement Learning frame-
098
      work (ARC-RL) to address the challenge of SE-CRL by generalizing policy across action spaces
      with different sizes. ARC-RL first learns an action representation space by learning a pair of en-
099
      coder and decoder. They are trained through self-supervised learning on transitions collected from
100
      the agent’s exploration of the environment. The encoder maps the agent’s actions to action repre-
101   sentations, and the decoder maps them to action probabilities. Once trained, the encoder and the
102   decoder are fixed, and the agent’s policy is trained based on the action representation space. When
103   the action space changes, the decoder’s structure is updated to accommodate the size of the new
104   action space. The agent then explores the environment with the new action space and fine-tunes the
105   encoder and the decoder, adding regularization for the fine-tuning of the decoder to maintain stabil-
106   ity. In this process, the function of the decoder is similar to that of the cerebellum of humans, while
107   the policy corresponds to that of the primary motor cortex. The former is essential for learning new

129   2.1   S ELF -S UPERVISED L EARNING FOR R EINFORCEMENT L EARNING
130   Existing reinforcement learning methods often require extensive data interactions with the environ-
131   ment, particularly in image-based RL tasks, which suffer from low sample efficiency and general-
132   izability (Schrittwieser et al., 2020; Ye et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2024b). Recently, Self-Supervised
133   Learning (SSL) has emerged to address these issues by learning a compact and informative repre-
134   sentation of the environment (Li et al., 2022; Stooke et al., 2021). SSL approaches in RL encompass
135
      auxiliary tasks, contrastive learning, and data augmentation, each contributing to improved perfor-
      mance and efficiency.
136
137   Auxiliary tasks in SSL for RL involve learning additional objectives that aid in representation learn-
138   ing. These tasks include reconstruction loss Chandak et al. (2019); Liu et al. (2024), world model-
139   ing (Hafner et al., 2020), and information-theoretic techniques (Pong et al., 2020) to obtain efficient
140   representations. Contrastive learning has gained traction in RL for its ability to learn valuable rep-
141
      resentations without requiring labeled data (Laskin et al., 2020a). Additionally, contrastive learning
      has shown success in goal-conditioned RL tasks without needing extra data augmentation or aux-
142
      iliary objectives (Eysenbach et al., 2022). Data augmentation strategies, as demonstrated by DrQ,
143
      apply simple image augmentations to standard model-free RL algorithms, enhancing robustness and
144   efficiency (Yarats et al., 2021) RAD further explores data augmentations for both pixel-based and
145   state-based inputs, significantly improving data efficiency and generalization (Laskin et al., 2020b).
146
147
      While SSL for RL has significantly improved sample efficiency and generalization, the open re-
      search challenge of using SSL in CRL is an intriguing area that requires further exploration. Our
148
      proposed framework uses self-supervised learning to build an action representation space that de-
149
      couples the agent’s policy from the specific action space, enabling policy generalization.
150
151
152   2.2   C ONTINUAL R EINFORCEMENT L EARNING
153   Continual reinforcement learning focuses on training RL agents to learn multiple tasks sequentially
154   without prior knowledge, generating significant interest due to its relevance to real-world artificial
155   intelligence applications (Khetarpal et al., 2022).
156   A central issue in CRL is catastrophic forgetting, which has led to various strategies for knowledge
157   retention. PackNet and related pruning methods (Mallya & Lazebnik, 2018; Schwarz et al., 2021)
158   preserve model parameters but often require knowledge of task count. Experience replay techniques
159   such as CLEAR (Rolnick et al., 2019) use buffers to retain past experiences but face memory scal-
160   ability challenges. In addition, some methods prevent forgetting by maintaining multiple policies
161   or a subspace of policies (Schöpf et al., 2022; Gaya et al., 2022). Furthermore, task-agnostic CRL
      research indicates that rapid adaptation can also help prevent forgetting (Caccia et al., 2023).


                                                        3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      Another issue in CRL is transfer learning, which is crucial for efficient policy adaptation. Naive
163   approaches, like fine-tuning, train a single model on each new task and provide good scalability and
164   transferability but suffer from catastrophic forgetting. Regularization-based methods, such as EWC
165   (Kirkpatrick et al., 2017; Wang et al., 2024a), have been proposed to prevent this side effect, but
166   often reduce plasticity. Some architectural innovations have been proposed to balance the trade-off
167   between plasticity and stability (Rusu et al., 2016; Berseth et al., 2022). Furthermore, methods like
168   OWL (Kessler et al., 2022) and MAXQINIT (Abel et al., 2018) leverage policy factorization and
169   value function transfer, respectively, for improved learning across tasks.
170   Most existing methods perform well when applied to sequences of tasks with static agent capabilities
171   and dynamic environments, such as when environmental parameters are altered, or the objectives
172   within the same environment are different (Pan et al., 2024). However, their effectiveness is greatly
173   diminished when the agent’s capabilities evolve. Our proposed framework aims to overcome this
174   limitation by building an action representation space.
175   Additionally, LAICA (Chandak et al., 2020) and DAE (Ding et al., 2023) are particularly relevant in
176   this context. LAICA primarily addresses changes in the action space but focuses on expansion rather
177   than contraction and others, and does not account for catastrophic forgetting. DAE investigates
178   incremental reinforcement learning with expanding action spaces and state spaces but also lacks
179   consideration of more complex action space changes and the critical aspects of CRL. In contrast,
180   our work proposed a more general problem in the context of CRL, considering both the expansion
181   and contraction situations of the action space and the catastrophic forgetting problem.

      3.2   P ROBLEM F ORMALIZATION
203
204   In real-world scenarios, the capabilities of an agent may evolve over time. To explore this,
205   we introduce a new problem called Self-Evolution Continual Reinforcement Learning (SE-CRL).
206   This problem can be formally defined as a sequence of Markov Decision Processes (MDPs)
207   {(S, Ai , P i , R)|i = 1, 2, ..., N }, where N is the total number of MDPs and Ai represents the
208   action space available to the agent at MDP i. Following the convention in CL, we still use “task” to
209   represent each MDP in the sequence. Each task in the sequence shares a common state space S and
210   reward function R, but differs in the action space and implicitly in the transition probability function
211   P i , which is influenced by the action space Ai . To simplify the problem, we assume that the action
212
      space is discrete and finite, and we focus on the impact of changing action spaces on the learning
      process while assuming P i remains conceptually similar across tasks.
213
214   Then, the dynamics of the action space can be characterized by differences in successive action
215   spaces. For each task i > 1, the action space Ai can be related to the previous action space Ai−1 in
      one of the following situations (Ai ̸= Ai−1 ):

      4.1   F RAMEWORK
257
      Our goal is to design a framework for generalized policy learning that can adapt to the chang-
258   ing action space, enhancing agent adaptation to evolving action capabilities. Recent neuroscience
259   research has shown that the stability of latent dynamics of neural activity reflects a fundamental
260   feature of learned cortical function that leads to long-term and consistent human behavior (Gal-
261   lego et al., 2020). Furthermore, research on SSL for RL has demonstrated its effectiveness in im-
262   proving the agent’s generalization ability (Chandak et al., 2019; Liu et al., 2024; Fang & Stachen-
263   feld, 2024). Drawing inspiration from these findings, we propose a new framework, named Action
264   Representation Continual Reinforcement Learning (ARC-RL), to enables the agent to generalize
265   policy across different action spaces.
266   Figure 3 illustrates the overview of ARC-RL. By decoupling the policy of the agent from the action
267   space, the policy can be generalized to new action spaces efficiently. The interaction between the
268   agent and the environment at each task is achieved through the action representation space. Each
269   task in ARC-RL consists of a two-stage process: Exploration Stage) As shown in the left part of
      Figure 3, the agent explores the current action space in the environment, collects the transitions


                                                          5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      (state-action-state pairs), and learns an encoder-decoder pair through SSL. The encoder maps the
271   action space to an action representation space, and the decoder maps the action representation space
272   to the action space. When the action space changes, the agent can adapt by modifying the decoder’s
273   structure, while the parameters of the encoder and the decoder are updated. Furthermore, to maintain
274   the stability of the policy, the regularization term is added to the fine-tuning process of the decoder.
275   Learning Stage) As shown in the right part of Figure 3, the agent learns a policy based on the
276   learned action representation space, rather than the specific action space of each task. Specifically,
277   the action representation space is treated as the action space, and a standard RL policy is used to
278   maximize the expected return. Once the action space changes, the agent merely needs to use the
279
      updated decoder to interact with the environment. In this way, the policy can maintain stability
      through the action representation space, and the agent can adapt to the new action space efficiently.

      4.2   ACTION R EPRESENTATION S PACE B UILDING
283
      We use self-supervised learning to build an action representation space for the agent. The agent
284   explores the action space in the environment of task i before learning the policy. It collects the
285   transitions T i = {(s, a, s′ )m |m = 1, 2, · · · , M } without reward, where s′ is the next state of s
286   after taking action a and M is the number of transitions. Based on the work of auxiliary tasks
287   in reinforcement learning (Chandak et al., 2019; Fang & Stachenfeld, 2024), we believe that the
288   features of the actions can be naturally represented by their influences of state changes. Therefore,
289   the auxiliary task of the action representation is to predict the next state s′ given the current state s
290   and the action a. Specifically, for a transition (s, a, s′ ), the encoder fϕi parameterized by ϕi maps
291   an action a to an action representation e ∈ E. The decoder gδi i parameterized by δ i maps the action
292   representation e ∈ E to the action probability. The processes are formulated as:

      5     E XPERIMENTS
343
344   5.1   B ENCHMARK
345
346   To evaluate the performance of CRL methods in SE-CRL, we establish a benchmark with changing
347   action spaces. This benchmark comprises sequences with tasks that share identical state, reward,
348   and transition dynamics but possess different action spaces. [Detailed descriptions of experimental
349   settings, network structures, hyperparameters, and the metrics are provided in Appendix B.]
350   Environments. The environments of these tasks are based on MiniGrid (Chevalier-Boisvert et al.,
351   2023) and Procgen (Cobbe et al., 2020). These environments feature image-based observations, a
352   discrete set of possible actions. For expeditious training and evaluation, we use Empty of MiniGrid
353   and Bigfish of Procgen in our experiments. Other environments are also provided in the Appendix
354   C.7. The agents in these environments can move in different directions. To simulate the evolving
355   capabilities, we introduce some additional actions and remove some existing actions. Then, we
356   design three tasks with different numbers of actions for both MiniGrid and Procgen, specifically
357
      incorporating tasks with three, five, and seven actions for MiniGrid, and tasks with three, five, and
      nine actions for Bigfish. When the agent switches from one task to another, the set of available
358
      actions may either increase or decrease. The agent can only observe the action space of the current
359
      task. Based on these tasks, we evaluate the performance of CRL methods in three situations of task
360   sequences: expansion (the action space is expanding), contraction (the action space is contracting),
361   and the combination of both.
362
363
      Compared Methods. We select three types of CRL methods to compare with SE-CRL: one replay-

409   reliability. During each task’s training phase, the agent is not only trained on the current task but also
410   periodically evaluated on all tasks, including those it has previously encountered. This evaluation

      prior tasks. The results presented in the evaluation plots and the total evaluation metric reported

476
477
      We conduct an ablation study on MiniGrid tasks to investigate what affects ARC-RL’s performance
      in SE-CRL. We consider two variants: ARC-RL-O, which omits the regularization during fine-
478
      tuning, and ARC-RL-E, which uses the same regularization for the encoder and decoder. The
479
      results, presented in Table 1, reveal that ARC-RL-O exhibits better forward transfer than other
480   methods, demonstrating that the action representation space is helpful for a more generalized pol-
481   icy. The comparison between ARC-RL and ARC-RL-O in forgetting and forward transfer suggests
482   that regularization may improve policy stability. Nevertheless, ARC-RL’s superior continual return
483   demonstrates that this balance is beneficial for the stability-plasticity trade-off essential in continual
484   learning systems (Wang et al. 2024). Compared with ARC-RL, additional regularization in ARC-
485   RL-E damages the forward transfer but does not mitigate forgetting. This may indicate that the
      plasticity of the encoder is essential for the learning of the action representation space.

532
533
      In this paper, we first propose a new and practical problem to supplement CRL called Self-
534   Evolution Continual Reinforcement Learning (SE-CRL), in which the agent’s action space con-
535   tinuously changes. To tackle the challenges in this problem, we introduce a new framework called
536   Action Representation Continual Reinforcement Learning (ARC-RL). This framework leverages
537   self-supervised learning and regularized fine-tuning to build an action representation space to gen-
538   eralize the policy across different action spaces. We release a benchmark based on MiniGrid and
539   Procgen to validate the effectiveness of CRL methods in SE-CRL. Experimental results demonstrate
      the superior performance of ARC-RL compared to popular CRL methods.

826   MiniGrid1 The MiniGrid contains a collection of simple 2D grid-world environments with a variety
827   of objects, such as walls, doors, keys, and agents. These environments feature image-based partial
828   observations, a discrete set of possible actions, and various objects characterized by their color and
829   type. For expeditious training and evaluation, we only use the empty room environments of Mini-
830
      Grid in our experiments. By default, they have a discrete 7-dimensional action space and produce
      a 3-channel integer state encoding of the 7 × 7 grid directly including and in front of the agent.
831
      Following the training setup for Atari (Schrittwieser et al., 2020), we modified the environments to
832
      output a 7 × 7 × 9 by stacking three frames. Furthermore, we only use three basic movement actions
833   from the original action space of MiniGrid: turn left, turn right, and move forward. Then, we expand
834   the action space by adding four more actions to simulate SE-CRL: move left, move right, forward
835   left, forward right. The screenshots of these actions are shown in Figure 7. Finally, we design three
836   tasks with different action spaces: a three-action task (turn left, turn right, forward), a five-action
837   task (turn left, turn right, forward, left, right), and a seven-action task (turn left, turn right, forward,
838   left, right, forward left, forward right).
839   Procgen.2 The Procgen benchmark is a collection of procedurally generated environments designed
840   to evaluate generalization in RL algorithms. It was proposed as a replacement for the Atari games
841   benchmark while being computationally faster to simulate than Atari. For faster training and evalu-
842   ation, we chose Bigfish with the easiest level as the base environment in our experiments, in which
843   the agent starts as a small fish and needs to become bigger by eating other fish. Figure 8 shows the
844   screenshots of this environment. The input observations are RGB images of dimension 64 × 64 × 3,
845   along with 15 possible discrete actions. Similar to MiniGrid, we only use nine basic movement
846   actions from the original action space of Bigfish: stay, up, down, left, right, up-left, up-right, down-
847
      left, and down-right. Then, we design three tasks with different action spaces: a three-action task
      (stay, up, down), a five-action task (stay, up, down, left, right), and a nine-action task (stay, up, down,
848
      left, right, up-left, up-right, down-left, down-right).

1428   In the human brain, motor control is distributed across several anatomical structures that operate
1429   hierarchically (D’Mello et al., 2020; Friedman & Robbins, 2022). At the highest levels, planning is
1430   concerned with how an action achieves an objective, while lower levels translate goals into specific
1431
       movements. This hierarchical organization allows for flexible and adaptive behavior, as higher-level
       goals can be achieved through various lower-level actions depending on the context. Similarly,
1432
       in ARC-RL, the agent’s policy can be seen as operating at a high level, focusing on achieving task
1433
       objectives, while the action representation space operates at a lower level, translating these objectives
1434   into specific actions. By decoupling the policy from the specific action space, ARC-RL leverages
1435   a hierarchical approach that mirrors the brain’s strategy for motor control. This allows the agent to
1436   adapt to changes in the action space without needing to relearn the entire policy.
1437
       Neurophysiological studies have shown that the activity of neurons in the motor cortex is often cor-
1438
       related with movement direction rather than specific muscle activations (Georgopoulos & Pellizzer,
1439
       1995; Kakei et al., 1999). Neurons exhibit directional tuning, and their collective activity can be rep-
1440   resented as a population vector that predicts movement direction. This concept of population coding
1441   suggests that the brain represents actions in a high-dimensional space, allowing for generalization
1442   across different contexts. In ARC-RL, the action representation space serves a similar function. By
1443   encoding actions in a high-dimensional space, the agent can generalize its policy across different ac-
1444   tion spaces. The encoder-decoder architecture in ARC-RL can be likened to the neural mechanisms
1445   that map cortical activity to specific movements. When the action space changes, the update of the
1446   ecoder and the decoder, is akin to how the brain might update its motor representations in response
1447   to changes in the body or environment.
1448   Recent research in motor neurophysiology has highlighted the dynamic nature of neural represen-
1449   tations. Neurons do not have fixed roles but instead can represent different features depending on
1450   the context and time (Churchland et al., 2012; Gallego et al., 2020). This flexibility allows the
1451   motor system to adapt to a wide range of tasks and environments, providing maximum behavioral
1452   flexibility. ARC-RL incorporates this idea by allowing the action representation space to be dy-
1453   namically updated. This dynamic updating process is analogous to how the brain adjusts its neural
1454
       representations to maintain consistent behavior despite changes in the body or environment.
1455   By drawing inspiration from neuroscience, ARC-RL achieve policy generalization and adaptability
1456   in the face of changing action spaces. This connection between neuroscience and artificial intelli-
1457   gence not only enhances our understanding of both fields but also provides feasible ideas for more
       sophisticated and adaptable AI systems.

       E.1   E XTENDING TO OTHER S ITUATIONS
1459
1460   Our framework can be extended to other situations as it is not specifically designed for expansion and
1461   contraction situations. Both the partial change situation and the complete change situation can be
1462   viewed as simultaneous occurrences of expansion and contraction. In the partial change situation,
1463   some actions from the previous action space remain, while in the complete change situation, all
1464   previous actions are removed.
1465   In the complete change situation, incorporating experience replay is a straightforward improvement
1466   of our framework. However, this approach incurs additional storage and computational costs. Thus,
1467   the trade-off between performance and efficiency is necessary. In addition, the method of generating
1468   pseudo samples (Shin et al., 2017; Yue et al., 2023) via representation space may be more suitable
1469   for practical privacy-preserving scenarios, where the agent cannot access the previously collected
1470   data.
1471   For the partial change situation, the challenge lies in identifying the differences between action
1472   spaces. Our framework can be improved by integrating mechanisms to detect removed and newly
1473   added actions, similar to novelty detection and class-incremental learning in the open-world setting
1474   (Masana et al., 2022; Sahisnu Mazumder, 2024; Li et al., 2024a). While clustering or classifying
1475   action representations can achieve this, it imposes higher demands on the self-supervised learning
1476   method. The action representations need to be well-separated in the representation space. Therefore,
1477   leveraging more information beyond state changes to learn action representations could be a valuable
1478
       extension of our framework.
1479
1480   E.2   L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
1481
       Despite the promising advancements, we acknowledge the current limitations, primarily the scal-
1482   ability concerns when dealing with large action spaces in complex environments. Future avenues
1483   of research should focus on enhancing adaptability by incorporating existing exploration methods
1484   and developing more effective action representation learning algorithms. In addition, more com-
1485   plex changes in the action space, such as the changes of continuous action space, require further
1486   investigation. This require more sophisticated representation learning methods to capture the action
1487   representations. The exploration policy in ARC-RL is also not suitable for continuous action spaces,
1488   and future work should explore more advanced exploration strategies for such scenarios. Moreover,
1489   we plan to extend our work to a broader range of CRL, including changes in the agent’s capabilities
1490
       and the external environment. This may involve utilizing meta-learning strategies to improve gen-
       eralization across a broader spectrum of capabilities evolutions and incorporating advanced transfer
1491
       learning mechanisms to seamlessly integrate knowledge from a wider range of environments and
1492
       varying agent capabilities.
