011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) on specialized domain datasets has shown
014            improved performance when large language models (LLMs) are fine-tuned for
015            generating responses to user queries. In this study, we develop a cybersecurity
016            question-answering (Q&A) dataset, called AttackQA, and employ it to build a
017            RAG-based Q&A system designed for analysts in security operations centers. The
018            dataset comprises 25,335 Q&A pairs, accompanied by rationales to facilitate fine-
019
               tuning and evaluation. 80% of the dataset was generated with help of a lightweight
               open-source LLM (LLama 3 8B), which produced over 1100 tokens per second
020
               with full 16-bit precision on specialized hardware. To ensure dataset quality, we
021
               fine-tuned LLama 3 70B to detect and reject low-quality Q&A pairs. In using the
022            dataset for RAG, we demonstrate that fine-tuning open-source embeddings and
023            LLMs can yield superior accuracy compared to OpenAI’s state-of-the-art propri-
024            etary embedding and LLM (GPT-4o). Furthermore, we use Llama 3.1 405B as a
025            judge to evaluate answer correctness, enabling the creation of a fully open-source,
026            high-speed RAG and evaluation pipeline with an associated benchmark.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031
032   Security operations centers (SOCs) house information security teams who are responsible for de-
033
      tecting, analyzing, and responding to cybersecurity incidents using a variety of tools, technologies,
      and processes. According to the Hershberger (2023) survey, the top challenges facing SOCs include
034
      a lack of expertise in security, too much time spent in investigating alerts, and a slow response time
035
      to advanced threats. To address those challenges and to enable quicker attack prevention and recov-
036   ery, we propose an LLM-based question-answering (Q&A) system to help SOC analysts get quick
037   answers to time-sensitive questions about cyberattacks. Our solution leverages entirely open-source
038   large language models (LLMs) that are becoming increasingly powerful and, on domain-specific
039   datasets, can be tuned to exceed the performance of proprietary LLMs that are many times as large.
040
      We used the MITRE ATT&CK® (The MITRE Corporation, 2024) knowledge base of cyberattack
041
      techniques, tools, campaigns, detection approaches, and mitigation approaches to generate a Q&A
042   dataset called AttackQA for use in Q&A systems or general-purpose chatbots. That knowledge base,
043   grounded in real-world observations and updated biannually, was chosen because the ATT&CK®
044   framework is widely adopted for cyber threat intelligence across the private sector, government,
045   and the broader cybersecurity product and service community (Roy et al., 2023; AL-SADA et al.,
046   2024; Al-Shaer et al., 2020). It is stored in an esoteric database format called Structured Threat
047   Information Expression (STIX), making it ill-suited for direct use in Q&A systems, so we extracted
048   the data and processed it in a way that makes it easier for training and inferencing with LLMs.
049   The structure of this paper and our approach are outlined in Fig. 1. The first phase involves the
050   creation of the AttackQA dataset. Initially, we generated 28,686 Q&A pairs derived from the MITRE
051   knowledge base. Subsequently, we fine-tuned Llama 3 70B to perform quality control (QC) on those
052   Q&A pairs, retaining 25,335 high-quality examples. In the second phase, AttackQA was used to
053   fine-tune both Microsoft’s E5 Large V2 embedding (Wang et al., 2022) and Meta’s Llama 3 8B
      LLM AI@Meta (2024) for retrieval-augmented generation (RAG). The accuracy of the results was

      2   R ELATED W ORK

      The use of LLMs for synthetic dataset generation, curation, and evaluation has been surveyed
102   by Long et al. (2024). Although AttackQA is synthetically generated, it is grounded in the widely
103   reputed MITRE ATT&CK® knowledge base. Levi et al. (2024) also fine-tune models on the MITRE
104   ATT&CK® knowledge base, among other datasets, to teach models the relationships between source
105   types. Similar to us, they too perform instruction tuning with chain of thought reasoning, but do not
106   provide an approach to quality control their dataset or compare results with SOTA proprietary LLMs.
107   The CyberQA dataset (priamai, 2024) is a related work in progress based off a dataset used in edu-
      cational games. The use of LLMs in cybersecurity has been surveyed in (Motlagh et al., 2024).

108
      The work of Hsieh et al. (2023) demonstrates that fine-tuning a 770M T5 model, using extracted
109   rationales during the fine-tuning process, can outperform a few-shot prompted 540B PaLM model.
110   Similarly, Zhang et al. (2024) fine-tuned the generation model within a RAG pipeline, enabling it
111   to predict rationales alongside answers. Moreover, they fine-tuned the model using context that
112   included both relevant and irrelevant (distractor) documents to improve its ability to answer ques-
113   tions. We employ the same set up in our work and show that greater accuracy improvements can be
114   obtained by fine-tuning the embedding in addition to the LLM.
115   Yu et al. (2024) fine-tuned large language models to generate answers while simultaneously ranking
116   context based on relevance. Izacard et al. (2022) introduced the pretrained LLM Atlas, specifically
117   designed for retrieval-augmented generation, which achieved a 3% performance improvement over a
118   540B parameter model, despite having 50 times fewer parameters. Our fine-tuned embeddings per-
119   form well on ranking without requiring any unique training approaches and our generation models
120   produce a 9% improvement on proprietary SOTA models that are much larger.
121   The fine-tuning of embeddings has been previously shown to enhance performance on tasks in-
122   volving domain-specific datasets (Fabian et al., 2020). Synthetic dataset generation for the explicit
123   purpose of fine-tuning such embeddings has been explored by Wang et al. (2024).

      3.1   S UMMARY OF THE SOURCE DATA
131
132   The MITRE ATT&CK® knowledge base encompasses multiple categories of information, such as
133   attack techniques, tactics, software tools used by attackers, well-known attacker groups, well-known
134   attack campaigns executed by the attacker groups, mitigation strategies, and relationships.
135
      The data for techniques, tactics, software, groups, campaigns, and mitigation approaches include
136   a unique ID, name, description, and URL (an example is provided in Appendix A.1). From that
137   data, we extracted the descriptions as text documents for use in Q&A tasks. The relationships table
138   maps a source type to a target type via a mapping type. Source types include ‘software’, ‘group’,
139   ‘data component’, ‘mitigation strategy’, and ‘campaign’, while target types consist of ‘technique’,
140   ‘software’, and ‘group’. The mapping types include ‘uses’, ‘detects’, ‘mitigates‘, and ‘attributed-to’.
141   A mapping description was also provided and included in our set of Q&A documents.
142   Note that each instance of a source type is associated with a unique identifier. E.g., ‘T1562’ refers
143   to an attack ‘technique‘ and ‘C0002’ refers to an attack campaign. Specifying the identifier allows
144   LLMs to make specific references with URLs to source data when answering questions.
145
      AttackQA was generated using a mix of questions and answers generated by humans and LLMs.
146
      Each Q&A pair was derived from a single document, eliminating the need for multi-hop reasoning
147
      because comprehensive answers did not require information from multiple documents.
148
149
      3.2   D OCUMENT PREPROCESSING
150
151   Newline characters were removed from within individual documents, ensuring that they appeared
152   only between documents in the final retrieval context presented to the generation model. In all
153   the documents, hyperlinks and special tags were replaced with plain text to ensure that neither the
154   embeddings nor the generation models needed to process special tags that would not be encountered
155   in questions and not be expected in answers.
156   From each document, one to three triplets of {question, thought, answer} were generated, where
157   thought represents the rationale necessary to accurately answer the question.
158

      3.5     E NSURING Q UALITY OF LLM- GENERATED DATA
233
234   Through careful prompt engineering and post-processing of special characters (e.g., the ‘\’ character
235   in file paths), we achieved a 99% success rate in ensuring that Llama 3 8B produced valid JSON in
236   the required format. That was despite the present lack of a JSON mode in the SambaNova API.
237
      To ensure the quality of the LLM-generated questions and answers, we employed three strategies:
238
239           1. We mandated that the LLM generate a citation for each question-answer-rationale entry
240              that included verbatim text from the document supporting the answer. That requirement
241              ensured that the entry was grounded in the source material.
242           2. All instances of duplicated questions were removed from the dataset, allowing the remain-
243              ing questions to act as a unique index. The reason is that the presence of duplicate questions
244              implies that the same inquiry could pertain to two or more different documents, indicating
245              that the question lacked specificity to any particular document.
246           3. We leveraged an independent grader LLM to grade each entry in the dataset on the quality
247              of the question and answer. We refer to that LLM as the quality control (QC) LLM and the
248              remainder of this subsection will describe that automated curation approach.
249
250   3.5.1     C RITERIA FOR ASSESSING Q&A QUALITY
251
252   We employed the G-Eval metric Confident AI, 2024 in the DeepEval framework to facilitate au-
253   tomatic curation using the quality control (QC) LLM. The G-Eval metric is a custom metric that
254
      scores each Q&A pair by assessing it in conjunction with the retrieved context. In addition to gen-
      erating a score, the prompts formulated by DeepEval require the LLM to provide a rationale for the
255
      assigned score. The scoring is based on specific evaluation steps that establish the scoring criteria.
256
      We developed distinct metrics for assessing both question quality and answer quality.
257
258   For the assessment of question quality, we evaluated whether the questions were ambiguous, failed
259   to reference specific topics in the context, referred to topics not present in the context, or were so
260
      broad that they could be answered with information outside of the provided context.
261   For the assessment of answer quality, we examined whether the answers were irrelevant to the ques-
262   tion, did not reference pertinent content from the context, lacked comprehensiveness, were vague,
263   or included information not contained within the context.
264   In DeepEval, the model is prompted to generate a score ranging from 0 to 10. However, that score
265   is subsequently divided by 10 during post-processing to yield a normalized range from 0 to 1.
266
267   3.5.2     F INE - TUNING THE QC LLM
268
269   We manually annotated 400 Q&A pairs, assigning scores along with reasons to justify the scores.
      We kept 80 of those pairs as a hold-out validation set to evaluate models ensuring that the score


                                                          5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      distributions were preserved (see Appendix A.2). A high score indicates that the Q&A pair is of
271   high quality and worth retaining, whereas pairs with low scores were filtered out.
272
273   According to our annotations, a score of 1 denoted a perfect example, while scores of 0.8 and 0.9
274
      were deemed acceptable, but not flawless. Any score below 0.7 indicated significant quality issues
      and the example was marked for removal from the dataset. We classified examples from the dataset
275
      that are deemed worth retaining (i.e., score > 0.7) as positives, and those that are better suited for
276
      removal (i.e., score ≤ 0.7) as negatives. Under this classification, 31% of the training set and 32.5%
277   of the validation set were identified as negatives.
278
279   At the time of writing, OpenAI’s GPT-4o is recognized as a SOTA proprietary model. However, its
280
      ability to judge data quality was found to be inadequate. Despite the validation set containing 26
      negatives, GPT-4o identified only 2 as negatives, while Llama 3 70B did not predict any negatives.
281
      This resulted in (precision, recall) values of (79%, 98%) for GPT-4o and (71%, 100%) for Llama
282
      3 70B. Neither model demonstrated proficiency in predicting negatives, and the high recall values
283   were largely attributable to the trivial strategy of predicting all examples as positives.
284
285   To ensure that the LLM-based QC process would yield a filtered dataset with a high precision (in
286
      which case most predicted positives turn out to be true positives), we opted to fine tune Llama 3 70B
      on the annotated training set. Full parameter tuning was conducted using the AdamW (Loshchilov
287
      & Hutter, 2019) optimizer with a fixed learning rate of 10(−5) and a weight decay of 0.1. As
288
      previously mentioned, the base model achieved a perfect recall due to its failure to predict any
289
      negatives. However, as we fine-tuned the model over additional steps, we observed a decrease in
290   recall accompanied by an increase in precision (see Appendix A.3 for more details). The final QC
291   assessment was performed using a checkpoint of Llama 3 70B that was fine-tuned for 65 steps,
292   resulting in a validation precision of 84.2% and a recall of 89%. By prioritizing precision over
293   recall, we chose a model that was better at detecting negatives (bad examples) and minimizing
294   their presence in the final dataset. The consequent reduction in recall led to the exclusion of some
295   positive examples (along with the negatives) from the final dataset, a trade-off that ensured quality
296   over quantity.

310   4     M ODEL F INE - TUNING FOR RAG
311
312   A basic RAG framework is illustrated in Fig. 2. In this section, we used AttackQA to fine-tune the
313   LLMs and embeddings to improve answer accuracy in that framework.
314   Prior to any user interaction, documents are embedded by the embedding model and stored in a
315   vector database. When a user asks a question, the question is embedded by the same embedding
316   model and k documents relevant to the question are retrieved from the vector database. In our
317   analysis, we retrieve the k documents based on the similarity between their embeddings and the
318   question’s embeddings. The k documents are then presented to the generation model in a prompt
319   that asks the model to answer the user’s questions using information from the documents. The
320
      answer is then returned to the user.
321   Note that there a more complex implementations of the framework involving multiple generation
322   models, re-ranking models, and multiple types of data stores. Such implementations are beyond on
323   the scope of this analysis, which seeks to measure the contributions of fine-tuning individual models
      on the overall accuracy.

337   4.1   T RAINING AND E VALUATION S PLIT
338
339   We split the 25, 335 Q&A pairs into a training set (90%) and an evaluation set (10%) using uniform
340   random sampling. Similar to Zhang et al. (2024), we ensured that all documents were represented
341
      in the training set so that the trained models would be familiar with the knowledge base from which
      questions would be asked. However, the questions in the evaluation set were not present in the
342
      training set. That resembles a live production usage setting, in which the end user wants to ask
343
      questions of a dataset, and the chatbot is familiar with the source documents but may not have
344   previously seen the questions.
345
346   When fine-tuning the models, we used 10% of the training set for validation to ensure that we would
347
      eventually evaluate on a checkpoint that was not over-fitting on the training set.
348
349   4.2   E MBEDDING M ODEL
350
351   We performed full parameter fine-tuning for two epochs using Microsoft’s E5 Large V2 embedding
352   model (Wang et al., 2022) on one Reconfigurable Dataflow Unit (RDU) (Prabhakar et al., 2024).
353   The model has 335M parameters and encodes up to 512 tokens into an embedding of length 1024.
354   The training dataset comprised of a list of questions from the training set. For each question, a list
355   of positive documents (containing the answer) and negative documents (not containing the answer)
356   were provided. By construction of the dataset, only one positive document existed for each question
357
      (since the question was generated from that document).
358   Having negative documents helps the model learn to distinguish between relevant and irrelevant
359   documents for a given question using contrastive learning (Chopra et al., 2005). The negative docu-
360   ments were randomly sampled from a set that excluded documents whose entities were related to the
361   entity associated with the question. That ensured that the answers could not accidentally be obtained
362   from the negative documents, leading to poor contrastive learning. Related entities can be identified
363
      in the MITRE dataset based on their IDs (e.g., T1562.001 and T1562.002 are related techniques and
      should not be included in negative documents for any question relating to T1562.xxx).
364
365
366   4.3   G ENERATION M ODEL
367
368   Llama 3 8B Instruct was fine-tuned using full-parameter fine-tuning for two epochs (batch size of 64)
369   on 8 RDUs. the same questions that were used to train the embedding model, but the dataset prepa-
370   ration for training was different. For each question, a set of k documents were retrieved using Mi-
371   crosoft’s E5 Large V2 embedding. We denote the retrieved set of documents by d(k) = {d1 , ..., dk }.
372   Let d∗ denote the golden document, which contained the answer to the question. For all the 23,263
373   Q&A pairs in the training set, we used post-processing to ensure that their corresponding d∗ ∈ d.
374   Specifically, when d did not contain d∗ from the retrieval, we used code to replace dk with d∗ . The
375   remaining k−1 documents, di ̸= d∗ , were distractor documents that the LLM would need to learn to
376   ignore because the model would be presented with all k documents even in production. We shuffled
377   the k documents in d to ensure that the model did not learn to pick d∗ based on its retrieval rank in
      d, and focused on the contents of the documents instead of the ordering.

403   5.1   R ETRIEVAL M ODEL
404
      The retrieval component of the pipeline refers to steps 2-4 in Fig 2. We evaluated that component
405
      using the context recall metric, which captures whether or not the retrieved context contains the

422   5.2   G ENERATION M ODEL
423
424   Because the answers generated by the generation models are all free-form text, it was difficult to
425   come up with an objective evaluation of their correctness. Objective metrics like Bleu (Papineni
426   et al., 2002) and Rouge (Lin, 2004) perform N-gram comparisons between expected and actual
427   answers and may not recognize when the two are semantically equivalent if they use different words.
428
      For that reason, we used an LLM-as-a-judge to score the answers for correctness.
429   Once again, we used the G-Eval metric with DeepEval to score answers and provide reasons for the
430   scores. With regard to evaluation criteria, we required that the generated answers be penalized for
431   correctness if they 1) contradicted the true answer, 2) omitted details from the true answer that were
      relevant to the question, and 3) included irrelevant detail that were not present in the true answer.

519   6       C ONCLUSION
520
521
      In this work, we created a Q&A dataset based on the MITRE ATT&CK® database of cyberattack
522   techniques, software, campaigns, mitigation approaches, and detection approaches. The dataset,
523   AttackQA, can be used to train models and create a chatbot to help security operations center ana-
524   lysts decrease their time to mitigate cyberattacks by giving them easy and fast answers to questions
525   that they may have about the attacks. We presented an approach to automatically generate data and
526   perform quality control on that data using SOTA open-source LLMs.
527
      We evaluated a RAG pipeline using our dataset and showed that fine-tuning both the generation and
528   embedding models can lead to an increase in hard accuracy of 26 percentage points. Fine-tuning the
529   embedding model alone can lead to an improvement of 16 percentage points. Finally, fine-tuning the
530   generation model alone, as proposed by Zhang et al. (2024), leads to an accuracy improvement of
531   10 percentage points. Open AI’s SOTA models produced high accuracy but could be outperformed
532   by tuning openly available embedding models. Even when GPT 4-o was combined with our tuned
533   embeddings, it underperformed a fine-tuned Llama 3 8B model, which was many times smaller. That
534   is particularly advantageous when deploying Q & A systems to hardware-constrained resources;
535   LLama 3 8B can run on a commodity laptop, which can be air-gapped for security purposes. GPT
536
      4-o, however would require an API call because it is too large to run on commodity hardware.
537   The AttackQA dataset and associated benchmarking code are made openly available (author, 2024).
538   Researchers may use the dataset to evaluate several other open-source embeddings and LLMs (e.g.,
539   Qwen, Mistral, etc.) that may outperform Llama 3 8B. However, since AttackQA contains domain-
      specific jargon, we expect that the best performing models would need to be tuned on the dataset.
