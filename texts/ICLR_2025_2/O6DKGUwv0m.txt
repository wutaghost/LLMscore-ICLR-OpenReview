011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Knowledge distillation, a widely used model compression technique, enables a
014            smaller student network to replicate the performance of a larger teacher network
015            by transferring knowledge, typically in the form of softened class probabilities
016
               or feature representations. However, current approaches often fail to maximize
               the teacher’s feature extraction capabilities, as they treat the semantic information
017
               transfer between teacher and student as equal. This paper presents a novel frame-
018
               work that addresses this limitation by enhancing the teacher’s learning process
019            through the Variable Scale Distillation Framework. Central to our approach is
020            the Rescale Block, which preserves scale consistency during hierarchical distilla-
021            tion, allowing the teacher to extract richer, more informative features. In extensive
022            experiments on the CIFAR100 dataset, our method consistently outperforms state-
023            of-the-art distillation techniques, achieving an average accuracy improvement of
024            2.12%. This demonstrates the effectiveness of our approach in fully leveraging
025            the teacher’s capacity to guide the student, pushing the boundaries of knowledge
026            distillation.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Knowledge distillation, introduced by Hinton et al. (2015), revolutionized model compression by
032   showing how knowledge from a large teacher network can be distilled into a smaller student network.
033
      This process typically involves training a teacher network with extensive parameters and transferring
      its soft logits to guide the training of the student network. This technique is commonly used in
034
      various downstream fields, including object detection Chen et al. (2017); De Rijk et al. (2022),
035
      segmentation Liu et al. (2019), etc. Previous work has proposed multiple approaches, including
036   distilling knowledge via feature-based methods Romero et al. (2014); Ahn et al. (2019); Chen et al.
037   (2022); Guo et al. (2023); Tian et al. (2019), logit-based methods Chen et al. (2020a); Hinton et al.
038   (2015); Zheng et al. (2020); Mirzadeh et al. (2020), and relation-based methods Huang et al. (2022);
039   Li et al. (2022); Park et al. (2019); Peng et al. (2019). Further advancements have integrated self-
040   supervised learning into the knowledge distillation framework Xu et al. (2020); Yang et al. (2021);
041   Lee et al. (2020), leveraging pretext tasks to enhance the distillation process and improve model
042   performance.
043   However, in previous work, both networks receive identical input, neglecting the teacher’s stronger
044   feature extraction and under-utilizing its complex architecture and larger parameter set. To address
045   this limitation, our work presents a novel approach inspired by image super-resolution Ledig et al.
046   (2017). It leverages the teacher network’s superior feature extraction capability by presenting it
047   with more detailed, fine-grained image input. Unlike previous methods, we propose Variable Scale
048   Distillation Framework, where the teacher network is trained on images of varying resolutions to
049   capture both high-level semantic features and finer details. This methodology allows the teacher
050
      model to fully exploit its larger capacity without altering the original semantic content of the images.
051   Furthermore, we propose a Rescale Block that ensures scale consistency during hierarchical distilla-
052   tion. This is critical in maintaining the alignment of feature maps between teacher and student net-
053   works, addressing the common issue of feature mismatch when applying Variable Scale Distillation
      Framework. Different from most previous methods, our method incorporates multi-task training,

      combining self-supervised learning with image classification. It enables the teacher model to aggre-

090   2.1   K NOWLEDGE D ISTILLATION
091
092   Knowledge distillation trains a smaller network using the supervision signals from both ground truth
093   labels and a larger network Gou et al. (2021). The seminal concept of knowledge distillation was
094   introduced by Hinton et al. Hinton et al. (2015), which achieved knowledge transfer through soft
095   probability distributions. When comparing our method with common state-of-the-art knowledge dis-
096   tillation methods, FitNet Romero et al. (2014) proposes a method where the student network learns
097   the intermediate features of the teacher network to achieve better performance. AT Zagoruyko &
098   Komodakis (2016b) improves the performance of a student CNN network by forcing it to mimic the
099
      attention maps of a powerful teacher network. RKD Park et al. (2019) transfers mutual relations of
      data examples to the student network. CRD Tian et al. (2019) trains the student network to capture
100
      significantly more information in the teacher’s representation of the data. SSKD Xu et al. (2020)
101
      demonstrates that the seemingly different self-supervision task in knowledge distillation can serve
102   as a simple yet powerful solution. HSAKD Yang et al. (2021) proposes appending several auxiliary
103   classifiers to hierarchical intermediate feature maps to generate diverse self-supervised knowledge
104   and perform the one-to-one transfer to thoroughly teach the student network. To summarize, while
105   these methods have advanced knowledge distillation by enhancing the transfer of knowledge, they
106   often overlook the potential for leveraging diverse input transformations. In response to these lim-
107   itations, our work enables the teacher network to provide richer, more comprehensive guidance to
      the student network.

      2.2     S ELF -S UPERVISED L EARNING
131
      Self-supervised learning is a paradigm for learning visual features from large-scale unlabeled data
132   Jing & Tian (2020); Liu et al. (2021). It allows us to train deep neural networks without artifi-
133   cial labels, providing useful representations for specific downstream tasks. For the pretext tasks
134   of self-supervised learning, many effective methods have been proposed in SimCLR Chen et al.
135   (2020b), including the restoration of the original image Pathak et al. (2016); Zhang et al. (2016);
136   Kolesnikov et al. (2019); Noroozi & Favaro (2016); Gidaris et al. (2018b), and the classification of
137   original categories of processed images Dosovitskiy et al. (2016); Gidaris et al. (2018a); Misra et al.
138   (2016), among others. The color permutation and rotation in Lee et al. (2020) feed the network with
139   augmented images and force it to recognize the rotation angle and channel sorting order. During
140
      the training of the student network, it has to understand the semantic information contained in the
      image, thereby achieving better performance. While previous methods have significantly advanced
141
      self-supervised learning to extract rich semantic features, our work integrates self-supervised tasks
142
      directly into the knowledge distillation process, forming an aggregated task that combines both
143   classification and self-supervision. This allows for an end-to-end training framework, improving
144   student’s performance by leveraging the full potential of the teacher’s guidance.

      semantic information from the images. Additionally, the student’s training is guided by both the
153
      teacher network’s logits and the parameters from the intermediate feature layers.

184   3.1.2   AGGREGATED -TASK L EARNING WITH S ELF - SUPERVISION
185
186   To enhance the teacher and student networks’ ability to generalize, we introduce an additional self-
187   supervised task that broadens the dimensions of the final vector space during training. This self-
188   supervised transformation preserves the semantic information of the input images, helping the net-
189
      work better capture the underlying structure. To reduce training costs and fully leverage the existing
      label information in the dataset, we perform training in an aggregated vector space that combines
190
      both image classification and self-supervised tasks. This joint training approach allows the model to
191
      infer as effectively as an aggregated model after a single training process.
192
193   Let the network function be represented as f (·), which can be decomposed into a feature extractor
194   fe (·, ωe ) and a linear classification head fc (·, ωc ), where ω represents the weight tensors. Consider
195
      x ∈ X as a mini-batch from the training dataset X .
196   We define a total of M various self-supervised transformations {ti }M
                                                                          i=1 within the transformation
197   space M = {1, ..., M }, and N classification labels within the label space N = {1, ..., N }. The
198   aggregated vector space for the task is A = M ⊗ N = {(1, 1), ..., (M, N )}, where ⊗ means the
199   cartesian product, and the dimension of A is ||A|| = M × N .

218   3.2.1    H IERARCHICAL K NOWLEDGE D ISTILLATION
219
220   During inference, feature maps at different levels capture various semantic features. Lower-level
221   feature maps typically focus on fine-grained details, such as texture, while higher-level feature maps
222   represent more abstract, global information. To effectively utilize the hierarchical feature maps from
223
      the teacher network, we employ a Hierarchical KD Branch to transfer the knowledge from multiple
      layers of the teacher. This allows the student network to learn not only from the final soft targets but
224
      also from the intermediate representations that are critical in the teacher’s evaluation process.
225
226   To further refine the feature representations for guiding the student model, one transformation in
227   the pretext task is doubling the input image’s width and height. However, this creates a mismatch in
228   input sizes between the teacher and student networks, posing a challenge for hierarchical distillation.
229
      To address this, we designed a Rescale Block that ensures scale consistency between the feature
      maps during the distillation process. This module also captures the hierarchical feature maps in the
230
      aggregated task training, allowing the student to learn from both detailed and high-level features.

      equips the network with knowledge that transcends the specific images within the training dataset.
460
      The integration of Variable Scale Distillation Framework and self-supervised tasks facilitates a fo-
461
      cus on more salient semantic features during the learning process, thereby enabling the network to
462   achieve superior generalization performance across diverse datasets.
463
464
      4.4   L IMITATIONS
465
466   In this study, we focused on specific pretext tasks such as image rotation and channel permutation
467   to improve the generalization ability of the student model. That said, we did not explore other
468   potentially effective self-supervised tasks, such as random masking, which could present another
469   promising avenue for enhancing the feature extraction capabilities of both the teacher and student
470   models. The primary reason is to maintain computational efficiency and simplicity in our exper-
471   imentation pipeline, as the training of models with random masking typically requires additional
472
      resources and longer convergence times due to the need for feature recovery mechanisms.
473   We acknowledge that incorporating random masking could potentially further improve the student’s
474   ability to learn fine-grained and contextually rich representations from the teacher. Future work
475   could explore the combination of our framework with random masking to assess its effectiveness in
476   distillation scenarios.
477
478   5     C ONCLUSION
479
480   In this study, we propose the Variable Scale Distillation Framework, and integrating it with self-
481   supervised tasks to create a unified framework. Furthermore, to maintain size consistency among
482   feature maps during hierarchical distillation, we present the Rescale Block, which facilitates effec-
483   tive feature map alignment. This innovative approach empowers student networks to acquire richer
484   and more generalized semantic feature representations, ultimately achieving state-of-the-art perfor-
485   mance on standard image classification benchmarks within the realm of knowledge distillation.
