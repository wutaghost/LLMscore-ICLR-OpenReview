009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011
               The remarkable achievements of both generative models of 2D images and neural
012            field representations for 3D scenes present a compelling opportunity to integrate
013            the strengths of both approaches. In this work, we propose a methodology that
014            combines a NeRF-based representation of 3D scenes with probabilistic modeling
015            and reasoning using diffusion models. We view 3D reconstruction as a perception
016            problem with inherent uncertainty that can thereby benefit from probabilistic in-
017            ference methods. The core idea is to represent the 3D scene as a stochastic latent
018            variable for which we can learn a prior and use it to perform posterior inference
019            given a set of observations. We formulate posterior sampling using the score-
020
               based inference method of diffusion models in conjunction with a likelihood term
               computed from a reconstruction model that includes volumetric rendering. We
021
               train the model using a two-stage process: first we train the reconstruction model
022
               while auto-decoding the latent representations for a dataset of 3D scenes, and then
023            we train the prior over the latents using a diffusion model. By using the model to
024            generate samples from the posterior we demonstrate that various 3D reconstruc-
025            tion tasks can be performed, differing by the type of observation used as inputs.
026            We showcase reconstruction from single-view, multi-view, noisy images, sparse
027            pixels, and sparse depth data. These observations vary in the amount of infor-
028            mation they provide for the scene and we show that our method can model the
029            varying levels of inherent uncertainty associated with each task. Our experiments
030            illustrate that this approach yields a comprehensive method capable of accurately
031
               predicting 3D structure from diverse types of observations.

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   3D prediction based on neural network representations has been a major focus of research in recent
037   years. This line of research, based on the work of Mildenhall et al. (2020); Sitzmann et al. (2019);
038   Park et al. (2019), focuses on two distinct problems. The first problem is 3D reconstruction, where
039   a 3D representation is predicted from a given set of images of a scene, possibly containing very few
040   or even only one image. A second problem is 3D generation, where a generative model is used to
041   generate samples of new 3D scenes using various types of conditioning signals like text descriptions
042   or visual data.
043   While 3D generation is typically addressed using a probabilistic generative model used to sample the
044   representation of new scenes, the 3D reconstruction problem is usually approached with determin-
045   istic gradient based optimization methods. However, 3D reconstruction is in most cases an ill-posed
046   problem that requires incorporating prior knowledge and could therefore benefit from probabilistic
047   inference methods.
048   In this work we propose to tackle the 3D reconstruction problem using a probabilistic framework.
049   Our goal is to predict the full distribution over the 3D scene structure given observations of various
050   types that can carry different levels of information about the scene. The approach consists of a
051   generative model of the latent 3D representation that is used as a prior, and a reconstruction model
052   that is used to compute the likelihood term. Given a set of observations, predicting the 3D scene
053   becomes a probabilistic inference problem that involves computing the posterior distribution over
      the latent representation.

084   To implement this, we follow the work of Dupont et al. (2022) and use a reconstruction model that
085   consists of a volumetric renderer based on a shared conditional neural field (CNF) and a latent
086   representation per scene. In order to train the CNF and a prior over the latent representations we
087   use a two-stage training process. In the first stage we use auto-decoding to train the shared CNF
088   and optimize the latent representation of each scene in the training set. In the second stage we train
089   a diffusion model over the latent representations of the training set in order to capture the prior
090   distribution.
091   We use a latent representation that is mapped to the CNF through a tri-plane structure, a repre-
092   sentation that provides a tradeoff between global information and local structure and was proven
093   to be effective in previous work (Chan et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2022). As was also shown for 2D
094   data (Bauer et al., 2023), we argue that spatial structure is important for training downstream models
095   such as generative models of the representation.
096   Training a diffusion model over the latents and combining it with the reconstruction model as a prior,
097   results in a latent variable model that can be used to perform probabilistic reasoning about 3D struc-
098   ture. Here, we propose to use guided iterative Langevin sampling from the diffusion model in order
099   to cover the posterior distribution of the latents. This is in contrast to prior work that suggested
100   to amortize posterior inference (Kosiorek et al., 2021a) in a variational autencoder setting, which
101   empirically demonstrated only limited results. By guiding the Langevin sampling with the gradi-
102   ent of the reconstruction model we combine the strength of two recently successful methods: (1)
103   iterative sampling with diffusion models and (2) gradient based optimization for translating obser-
104
      vations to 3D representations. Our approach unifies both methods leading to efficient and accurate
      3D reconstruction from observations.
105
106   We demonstrate our method on various tasks, highlighting the benefits of generating multiple sam-
107   ples from the posterior in cases where uncertainty is large. We show that this leads to better coverage
      of the ground truth structure and allows constructing uncertainty maps of the 3D scene.

      Our method achieves good results on well studied tasks like single-view reconstruction, and can
109   also be used for infinitely many other tasks which can be defined at test time by formulating a
110   reconstruction term. We show results for 3D reconstruction from noisy observations, sparse pixels
111   and sparse depth information.

      Our contributions can be summarized as:
113
114         1. We propose to reconstruct 3D scenes by sampling the posterior of a compressed 3D latent
115            representation, using a pre-trained diffusion prior and a conditional NeRF-based decoder.
116
            2. We develop an efficient two-stage training method that (1) auto-decodes a compressed rep-
117
               resentation of 3D scenes, and (2) trains a diffusion model as a prior over the representation.
118
119         3. We demonstrate the usefulness of this approach on various 3D reconstruction tasks, namely,

128   Latent variable models over 3D scenes Early work on learning probabilistic latent variable models
129   of 3D scenes like GQN (Eslami et al., 2018), was based on the variational autoencoder and did not
130   include any specialized 3D-geometry-based architectures. While the experiments were performed
131   on data with limited complexity, the results already demonstrated the ability to perform probabilistic
132   reasoning and capture uncertainty in a 3D consistent way. Later attempts to equip such models with
133   a 3D rendering pipeline based on NeRF (Kosiorek et al., 2021b), retained the good probabilistic
134   abilities of the model but did not seem to leverage the capacity of NeRF in modeling complex 3D
135
      scenes. Our work replaces the amortized variational inference approach in favor of a high capacity
      diffusion model and Langevin posterior sampling. More recent works (Shen et al., 2022; Sünderhauf
136
      et al., 2022; Goli et al., 2023) target uncertainty modeling using different stochastic and Bayesian
137
      methods to detect reconstruction inconsistencies in a single scene, however they do not use a data-
138   driven learned prior like we do.
139
140   Generating 3D with 2D Generative models The scarcity of ground truth data in 3D has led various
141
      work on methods to generate 3D data using generative models of 2D images. Notable works use
      pretrained image diffusion models in order to train a NeRF representation (Poole et al., 2022; Watson
142
      et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2023; Sargent et al., 2023). In this approach text conditional diffusion models
143
      can be used to generate 3D from text descriptions, and multiview 2D diffusion models can be used
144   to generate 3D scenes conditioned on a single input image. In a similar approach, Liu et al. (2024)
145   use a 2D prior to compute 3D uncertainty maps. While these models lead to impressive results for
146   3D generation and clearly demonstrate a level of 3D understanding, they do not explicitly reason
147   about the latent 3D structure of the scene, preventing them from performing the full range of 3D
148   probabilistic reasoning tasks, e.g. reconstruction from depth information.
149
      Generative models of observed 3D representations Despite the scarcity of 3D data, some work has
150   studied generative modeling directly on various datasets of 3D representations. Shue et al. (2022)
151   trains a diffusion model on a tri-plane representation. Erkoç et al. (2023) trains a diffusion model
152   on 3D shape data, by first training neural field MLPs and then modeling a prior over them using
153   a transformer based denoiser. Jun & Nichol (2023) uses a dataset of point clouds. It first trains
154   an encoder that maps the point cloud to neural field MLP weights, and then trains a (conditional)
155   diffusion model on the weights. In our work there is no need for ground truth 3D representations as
156   we only rely on 2D image datasets of 3D scenes.
157   Generative models of latent 3D representations Our work is inspired by Dupont et al. (2022),
158   where it is proposed to represent continuous functions such as images and 3D scenes as conditional
159   neural fields (CNF) by first training a CNF, and then using the auto-decoded conditioning vector
160   as data for training downstream models such as generative models. Their paper provides a simple
161   proof of concept of using such a generative model as a prior and perform MAP inference. Com-
      pared to their models and experiments on 3D data, we introduce the tri-plane representation which

      results in a better and more compressed latent representation, we circumvent the expensive meta-
163   learning process, and we demonstrate better results by using a diffusion model to sample from the
164   full distribution of the posterior.

166
      Another work that provides inspiration to our model is Bautista et al. (2022). As far as we can tell our

177
      3D generative model is trained based on a Gaussian splatting representation which could also be
      combined with posterior sampling in future research.

      3.1   AUTO - DECODING 3D REPRESENTATIONS
184
185
      Recent years have witnessed breakthroughs in the representation of 3D scenes with deep neural
186
      networks. Most notable is NeRF (Mildenhall et al., 2020) - a model that employs a neural network
187   to capture a 3D scene by training it with a reconstruction loss based on rendering various views of the
188   scene. While in NeRF, a different model needs to be trained for each 3D scene, follow-up work has
189   focused on developing a generalizable model that can leverage prior information of 3D scenes, and
190   can consequently be trained with fewer views per scene. Earlier models like PixelNeRF (Yu et al.,
191   2021) and IBRNet (Wang et al., 2021) apply encoder networks in order to learn image features,
192   which are then projected into the 3D scene. In contrast to vanilla NeRF models that consist of a
193   neural field (NF) network, generlizable NeRF models often rely on a conditional neural field (CNF)
194   where the NF network is shared across scenes, but conditioned on different representations per scene.
195   More recently, different papers propose to use CNFs to train representations of scenes that can later
196   be used in downstream tasks (Dupont et al., 2022; Bautista et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2023; Yang et al.,
197   2023). Rather than training an encoder to map images to a scene representation, these models use an
198   auto-decoding approach (Bojanowski et al., 2019; Park et al., 2019), where the representations are
199   optimized for each scene concurrently with the training of the shared CNF. Most of these models
200   are based on a tri-plane representation (Chan et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2022). This representation
201
      is structured as three planes posed in three orthogonal directions in the scene (Fig. 2). The repre-
      sentation is used as a conditioning input to the CNF by projecting the queried 3D position into the
202
      different planes, interpolating the values for each plane, and concatenating the results. This repre-
203
      sentation has proven very effective in 3D modeling, as it maintains a spatial structure in 3D space,
204   and balances between global and local information.

246   4     M ETHOD

248   In this section we describe our method both at training time and at inference time. Training is based
249   on two stages: (1) training the reconstruction model while optimizing the latent representation of
250   the training scenes (auto-decoding), and (2) training a diffusion model over the latents as a prior. At
251   inference time we use the trained prior and reconstruction models to perform posterior sampling of
252   the latents. For all implementation details please refer to Sec. A in the appendix.
253
254   4.1   T RAINING THE REPRESENTATION AND RECONSTRUCTION
255
      The reconstruction model is a CNF followed by a volumetric renderer. Conditioned on a scene rep-
256
      resentation, the CNF predicts the values of 3D positions within the scene that are subsequently used
257
      by the volumetric renderer. The CNF is trained while concurrently auto-decoding the representation
258   of each scene. The role of the reconstruction model is to form a mapping from the representation
259   vectors to the values of the observations, i.e. image pixels, and also serve as the model through
260   which the representation is optimized, effectively mapping the 3D scene observations back into the
261   representation.
262
      The model is depicted in Fig. 2. The latent vector zi ∈ Rd , corresponding to the i-th scene, is first
263
      reshaped into a 2D map of shape r × r × c. The latent decoder D1 decodes zi into a 3D tensor
264   T ∈ RR×R×3C using a series of ResNet blocks. T is reshaped to form a tri-plane representation
265   T1i , T2i , T3i ∈ RR×R×C . The tri-planes structure is used for reconstruction as follows: given an
266   image of a scene, rays are projected from each pixel into the 3D scene, and multiple 3D points are
267   sampled along each ray. Each 3D point is projected onto the tri-planes, and using bi-linear interpo-
268   lation, each plane produces a single corresponding feature vector f ∈ RC . The three feature vectors
269   are concatenated to form f ∗ , which is used as the input to the decoder D2, an MLP that trans-
      forms the tri-plane interpolated vector into RGB and σ values for the corresponding 3D position.

523   6   C ONCLUSION
524
525
      In this work we introduced a methodology that combines the strengths of NeRF-based 3D recon-
526
      struction together with the probabilistic reasoning of diffusion models. Our method views 3D recon-
527
      struction as an ill-posed perception problem that requires reconciling the observed information with
528   prior knowledge. We showed that (1) 3D scenes can be efficiently represented by compact latent
529   vectors, using a reconstruction model that consists of a tri-plane representation, which preserves
530   spatial structure within the 3D model; and (2) this representation is amenable to training a strong
531   diffusion-model based prior that can later be used to solve various inference tasks. We highlight the
532   importance of predicting the full posterior distribution rather than relying on point estimates, and
533   demonstrate a comprehensive method that can be used to solve various 3D reconstruction tasks.
534   Limitations and future work: A main challenge that remains in 3D reconstruction is scaling to
535   more complex and more diverse data towards developing methods that can reliably predict real 3D
536   scenes from different levels of observations. Another challenge is the slow sampling time with diffu-
537   sion models. While our results are demonstrated on small scale data, we believe that the compressed
538   representation and the principled way of handling uncertainty that we propose, combined with re-
539   cent developments in accelerating diffusion model sampling, are key for scaling up these models to
      larger and more complex datasets.

707   T RAINING THE R EPRESENTATION AND R ECONSTRUCTION MODEL
708
709   The dataset size corresponds to the number of latent vectors, with each latent representing a sin-
710   gle scene {zi }N i=1 (N scenes = N latents). During training, each scene’s images optimize only
711   its respective latent, while the entire model, including latents and decoders, is jointly trained.
712
      The latent representation zi dimensions are d = 1024, r = 16, c = 4. D1 is constructed us-
      ing a series of six ResNet blocks where at each block the number of channels is the following:
713
      [4,32,64,96,128,192]. Blocks are followed by a self-attention layer and alternating upsampling.
714
      The resulting 3D tensor T is divided into two tensors responsible for generating RGB and den-
715   sity, TRGB ∈ RR×R×3CRGB , Tσ ∈ RR×R×3Cσ , respectively. TRGB is reshaped to form a tri-
716   plane representation T1i , T2i , T3i ∈ RR×R×CRGB . Similarly, Tσ forms a tri-plane representation
        ′    ′     ′
717   T1i , T2i , T3i ∈ RR×R×Cσ . Dimensions are R = 128, CRGB = 48, Cσ = 16. For each scene
718   i, we randomly select 4096 rays from pixels in the training images. Along each ray, we sample
719   220 3D points and project them onto the tri-planes of both the RGB and density planes separately.
720   This projection extracts 3 feature vectors from each of the respective planes for further processing.
721   Three vectors are concatenated into a single feature vector f ∗ ∈ R3C for RGB calculation and
722   f ∗ ∈ RC                                                        ∗
                RGB/σ for density. While the density feature vector fσ produces density for 3D points by
                                                               ∗
723   simply summing its elements, the RGB feature vector fRGB is passed through D2 to produce a sin-
724   gle RGB value. D2 is an MLP of 7 layers. Once all 3D points along the ray have RGB and density
725
      values, volumetric rendering, a parameterless process, produces a single RGB value to be compared
      with the pixel’s color.
726
727   We train the model with a minibatch B size of 2 scenes, and with an Adam optimizer using three
728   different learning rates: 1e-3 for the latents, 1e-4 for the D1 parameters and 1e−3 for D2 parameters.
729   Our model is based on the code published in Chen et al. (2022).
730
731   At test time, a new latent (initialized to zeros) is coupled with the new scene and optimized using
732
      the learned/frozen decoders.

735   As in section 4.1, latent representation zi dimensions are d = 1024, r = 16, c = 4. The diffusion
736   model used is implemented by Graikos et al. (2022) with the following parameters: The noise
737   scheduler is a linear schedule with parameters T = 1000, β0 = 1e − 4, βT = 2e − 2. The U-
738   net parameters are model channels = 64, num resnet blocks = 2, channel mult = (1, 2, 3, 4),
739   attention resolutions = [8, 4], num heads = 4. We train the model with a minibatch B size of
