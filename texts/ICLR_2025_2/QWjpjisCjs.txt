009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Model Inversion (MI) attacks aim at leveraging the output information of target
012            models to reconstruct privacy-sensitive training data, raising widespread concerns
013            on privacy threats of Deep Neural Networks (DNNs). Unfortunately, in tandem
014            with the rapid evolution of MI attacks, the lack of a comprehensive, aligned, and
015            reliable benchmark has emerged as a formidable challenge. This deficiency leads
016
               to inadequate comparisons between different attack methods and inconsistent
               experimental setups. In this paper, we introduce the first practical benchmark for
017
               model inversion attacks and defenses to address this critical gap, which is named
018
               MIBench. This benchmark serves as an extensible and reproducible modular-
019            based toolbox and currently integrates a total of 16 state-of-the-art attack and
020            defense methods. Moreover, we furnish a suite of assessment tools encompassing
021            9 commonly used evaluation protocols to facilitate standardized and fair evaluation
022            and analysis. Capitalizing on this foundation, we conduct extensive experiments
023            from multiple perspectives to holistically compare and analyze the performance
024            of various methods across different scenarios, which overcomes the misalignment
025            issues and discrepancy prevalent in previous works. Based on the collected attack
026            methods and defense strategies, we analyze the impact of target resolution, defense
027
               robustness, model predictive power, model architectures, transferability and loss
               function. Our hope is that this MIBench could provide a unified, practical and
028
               extensible toolbox and is widely utilized by researchers in the field to rigorously
029
               test and compare their novel methods, ensuring equitable evaluations and thereby
030            propelling further advancements in the future development.

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035   In recent years, Model Inversion (MI) attacks have raised alarms over the potential privacy breaches
036   of sensitive personal information, including the leakage of privacy images in face recognition models
037   (He et al., 2016), sensitive health details in medical data (Wang et al., 2022), financial information
038   such as transaction records and account balances (Ozbayoglu et al., 2020), and personal preferences
039
      and social connections in social media data (Feng et al., 2022). In the MI attacks, an attacker aims to
      infer private training data from the output information of the target model. Fredrikson et al. (2014)
040
      proposed the first MI attack against linear regression models to reconstruct sensitive features of
041
      genomic data. Subsequent studies (Fredrikson et al., 2015; Song et al., 2017; Yang et al., 2019) have
042   extended MI attacks to more Machine Learning (ML) models. Zhang et al. (2020b) first introduces
043   the GANs as stronger image priors, paving the way for more applications in GAN-based methods
044   (Chen et al., 2021a; Yuan et al., 2022; 2023b; Wang et al., 2021b).
045
      However, some critical challenges are posed owing to the lack of a comprehensive, fair, and reliable
046
      benchmark. Specifically, the evaluation of new methods is often confined to comparisons with a
047
      narrow selection of prior works, limiting the scope and depth of analysis. For instance, some methods
048   (Nguyen et al., 2023b; Yuan et al., 2023a) exhibit superior performance for lower-resolution images
049   while other methods (Struppek et al., 2022; Qiu et al., 2024) perform better at higher resolutions.
050   However, these studies only evaluate under their predominant resolutions, and thus do not provide a
051   unified and comprehensive comparison. Additionally, the absence of unified experimental protocols
052   results in a fragmented landscape where there is less validity and fairness in the comparative studies.
053   For example, the original GMI (Zhang et al., 2020a) achieves the attack accuracy of 28%, 44%,
      46% when attacking three different classifiers trained on the CelebFaces Attributes(CelebA) (Liu


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      et al., 2015), while merely maintaining lower attack accuracy of 21%, 32%, 31% and 21%, 31%,
055   29% when compared in the KEDMI (Chen et al., 2021b) and PLGMI (Yuan et al., 2023a) under
056   the same experimental setup. Compounding these issues, discrepancies in the evaluation metrics
057   further obscure the reliability of reported conclusions, potentially steering the field towards biased or
058   misleading insights. These shortcomings impede both the accurate measurement of advancements in
059   the MI field and the systematic exploration of its theoretical underpinnings, underscoring the urgent
060   need for a harmonized framework to facilitate robust and transparent research practices.
061   To alleviate these problems, we establish the first benchmark in MI field, named MIBench. For build-
062   ing an extensible modular-based toolbox, we disassemble the pipeline of MI attacks and defenses into
063   four main modules, each designated for data preprocessing, attack methods, defense strategies and
064   evaluation, hence enhancing the extensibility of this unified framework. The proposed MIBench has
065   encompassed a total of 16 distinct attack and defense methods, coupled with 9 prevalent evaluation
066   protocols to adequately measure the comprehensive performance of individual MI methods. Further-
067   more, we conduct extensive experiments from diverse perspectives to achieve a thorough appraisal of
068   the competence of existing MI methods, while simultaneously venturing into undiscovered insights
069
      to inspire potential avenues for future research. We expect that this reproducible benchmark will
      facilitate the further development of MI field and bring more innovative explorations in the subsequent
070
      study. Our main contributions are as follows:
071
072          • We build the first comprehensive benchmark in MI field, which serves as an extensible and
073            reproducible modular-based toolbox for researchers. We expect that this will foster the
074            development of more powerful MI attacks by making it easier to evaluate their effectiveness
075            across multiple distinct dimensions.
076          • We implement 16 state-of-the-art attack methods and defense strategies and 9 evaluation
077            protocols currently in our benchmark. We hope that the benchmark can further identify the
078            most successful ideas in defending against rapid development of potential MI attacks.
079          • We conduct extensive experiments to thoroughly assess different MI methods under multiple
080            settings and study the effects of different factors to offer new insights on the MI field. In
081            particular, we validate that stronger model predictive power correlates with an increased
082            likelihood of privacy leakage. Moreover, our analysis reveals that certain defense algorithms
083            also fail when the target model achieves high prediction accuracy.

      2    R ELATED W ORK
086
087
      Model Inversion Attacks. In the MI attacks, the malicious adversary aims to reconstruct privacy-
088   sensitive data by leveraging the output prediction confidence of the target classifier and other auxiliary
089   priors. Normally, the attacker requires a public dataset that shares structural similarities with the
090   private dataset but without intersecting classes to pre-train the generator. For example, an open-source
091   face dataset serves as essential public data when targeting a face recognition model. For a typical
092   GAN-based MI attack, attackers attempt to recover private images x∗ from the GAN’s latent vectors
093   z initialized by Gaussian distribution, given the target image classifier fθ parameterized with weights
094   θ and the trained generator G. The attack process can be formulated as follows:
095                              z∗ = arg min Lid (fθ (G(z)), c) + λLaux (z; G),                            (1)
096                                        z
097   where c is the target class, Lid (·, ·) typically denotes the classification loss, λ is a hyperparameter,
098   and Laux (·) is the prior knowledge regularization (e.g., the discriminator’s classification loss) used
099   to improve the reality of G(z). By minimizing the above equation, the adversary updates the latent
100   vectors z into the optimal results ẑ and generate final images through x̂ = G(ẑ).
101
      Fredrikson et al. (2014) first introduce the concept of MI attacks in the context of genomic privacy.
102   They find that maximizing the posterior probabilities of a linear regression model can reconstruct
103   the original genomic markers. Ensuing works (Fredrikson et al., 2015; Song et al., 2017; Yang et al.,
104   2019) manage to design MI attacks for more kinds of models and private data, but are still limited
105   to attacking simple networks and grayscale images. To enhance the reconstruction performance
106   on complex RGB images, GMI (Zhang et al., 2020a) first propose to incorporate the rich prior
107   knowledge (Fang et al., 2023; Gu et al., 2020; Fang et al., 2024) within the pre-trained Generative
      Adversarial Networks (GANs) (Goodfellow et al., 2014). Specifically, GMI starts by generating a


                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      series of preliminary fake images, and then iteratively optimizes the input latent vectors that are used
109   for generation. Based on GMI, KEDMI (Chen et al., 2021b) refine the discriminator by introducing
110   target labels to recover the distribution of the input latent vectors. VMI (Wang et al., 2021a) utilize
111   variational inference to model MI attacks and adopts KL-divergence as the regularization to better
112   estimate the target distribution. PPA (Struppek et al., 2022) introduce a series of techniques such as
113   initial selection, post-selection, and data argumentation to enhance MI attacks and manages to recover
114   high-resolution images by the pre-trained StyleGAN2 (Karras et al., 2019). LOMMA (Nguyen et al.,
115   2023b) integrate model augmentation and model distillation into MI attacks to tackle the problem of
116   over-fitting. PLGMI (Yuan et al., 2023a) leverage a top-n selection technique to generate pseudo
117
      labels to further guide the training process of GAN.
118   Besides, based on whether the parameters and structures of the victim model are fully accessible
119   to the attackers, MI attacks can be further split into white-box attacks and black-box attacks. Note
120   that in black-box settings, the gradients can no longer be computed by the back-propagation process.
121   Thus, Yuan et al. (2022) address this problem by sampling numerous latent vectors from random
122   noise, selecting the ones that can generate correct labels, and updating the latent vectors solely on
123
      discriminator loss. Nguyen et al. (2023a) propose to conduct MI attacks on various surrogate models
      instead of the unfamiliar victim model, transforming the black-box settings into white-box settings.
124
125   Model Inversion Defenses. To defend MI attacks, most existing methods can be categorized into two
126   types: model output processing (Yang et al., 2020; Wen et al., 2021; Ye et al., 2022) and robust model
127   training (Gong et al., 2023; Titcombe et al., 2021; Li et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2021c; Peng et al.,
128   2022; Struppek et al., 2023). Model output processing refers to reducing the private information
129
      carried in the victim model’s output to promote privacy. Yang et al. (2020) propose to train an
      autoencoder to purify the output vector by decreasing its degree of dispersion. Wen et al. (2021)
130
      apply adversarial noises to the model output and confuse the attackers. Ye et al. (2022) leverage a
131
      differential privacy mechanism to divide the output vector into multiple sub-ranges. Robust model
132   training refers to that incorporating the defense strategies during the training process. MID Wang
133   et al. (2021c) penalizes the mutual information between model inputs and outputs in the training
134   loss, thus reducing the redundant information carried in the model output that may be abused by the
135   attackers. However, simply decreasing the dependency between the inputs and outputs also results in
136   model performance degradation. To alleviate this issue and strike a better balance between model
137   utility and user privacy, Bilateral Dependency Optimization (BiDO) (Peng et al., 2022) minimizes the
138   dependency between the inputs and outputs while maximizing the dependency between the latent
139   representations and outputs. Gong et al. (2023) propose to leverage GAN to generate fake public
140
      samples to mislead the attackers. Titcombe et al. (2021) defend MI attacks by adding Laplacian noise
      to intermediate representations. LS (Struppek et al., 2023) finds that label smoothing with negative
141
      factors can help privacy preservation. TL (Ho et al., 2024) leverages transfer learning to limit the
142
      number of layers encoding sensitive information and thus improves the robustness to MI attacks.

      3.1   DATASET
148
149
      Considering existing MI attacks primarily focus on reconstructing private facial data from image
150   classifiers, we select 4 widely recognized face datasets as the basic datasets for our benchmark, which
151   include Flickr-Faces-HQ (FFHQ) (Karras et al., 2019), MetFaces (Karras et al., 2020a), FaceScrub
152   (Ng & Winkler, 2014), and CelebFaces Attributes (CelebA) (Liu et al., 2015). Generally, FFHQ
153   and MetFaces are employed as public datasets for pre-training auxiliary priors, whereas FaceScrub
154   and CelebA serve as the target private datasets to attack. Our benchmark facilitates researchers to
155   freely combine public datasets with private ones, thereby enabling customized experimental setups.
156   Extensive evaluation on more non-facial datasets are presented in Sec.C.3
157   Notably, the target resolutions across different MI attacks are not uniform. The majority of attack
158   methods concentrate on low-resolution images of 64 × 64, while recent attack methods have begun
159   to focus on higher resolutions, such as 224 × 224. Therefore, our benchmark offers 2 versions
160   of low-resolution and higher-resolution for the aforementioned 4 datasets and prepares multiple
161   transformation tools for processing images, freeing researchers from the laborious tasks of data
      preprocessing. More details regarding the datasets can be found in the Sec. B.2 of the Appendix.

167   computer vision or machine learning domains. This criterion ensures the reliability and validity of
168   the implemented methods. Considering the main targets in our benchmark are image classifiers for
169   RGB images, the learning-based MI attacks (Fredrikson et al., 2015; Song et al., 2017; Yang et al.,

247   image transformations (e.g. center crop and resize) based on the split file and chosen resolution

249   designated classifiers. We abstract the general procedures in classifier training into a base trainer class
250   to facilitate users in extending and customizing their own classifiers. For parameter management,

253   organized and efficient parameter handling across various operations in the workflow.
254   Attack Module. The workflow of MI attacks can be roughly divided into two stages. The first stage is
255   GAN training, where the module abstracts the general training process of GANs into a basic trainer
256   class. This allows users not only train GANs that are pre-built into the benchmark, but also extend to
257   their uniquely designed GANs. The second stage is the core inversion attack, which we split into
258   three parts: latent vectors initialization, iterative optimization, and an optional post-processing step.
259   After completion of the attack, the module preserves essential data such as the final optimized images
260
      and latent vectors, facilitating subsequent evaluation and analysis.
261   Defense Module. Considering the mainstream MI defense strategies are applied during the training
262   process of target classifiers, we design the defense module following the target model training
263   functionality within the Data Preprocessing Module. To enhance extensibility, we incorporate
264   defense strategies as part of the training parameters for classifiers to enable the defense during the
265   training process of target models, which decouples the defense from the training pipeline. In this way,
266
      we allow users to customize their own defense strategies against MI attacks.
267   Evaluation Module. Our benchmark concentrates on the evaluation at distribution level instead
268   of sample level, assessing the overall performance of the whole reconstructed dataset. Therefore,
269   we provide a total of 9 widely recognized distribution level evaluation metrics for users, which
      can be categorized into four types according to the evaluated content: Accuracy, Feature Distance,

270
      FID (Heusel et al., 2017) and Sample Diversity. For Accuracy, this metric measures how well the
271   reconstructed samples resemble the target class, consisting of Acc@1 and Acc@5. For Feature
272   Distance, it computes the average shortest l2 distance from features between each reconstructed
273   sample and private data to measure the similarity between the feature space of reconstructed samples
274   and private dataset. For FID, the lower FID score shows higher realism and overall diversity (Wang
275   et al., 2021a). For Sample Diversity, the higher values indicate greater intra-class diversity. Moreover,
276   we provide convenient tools for analysis, including Standard Deviation Calculation and Visualization.

      4.4   E VALUATION ON D IFFERENT M ODEL P REDICTIVE P OWER
417
418
      The predictive power of the target model is a crucial factor in determining the effectiveness of MI
419
      attacks. Previous work (Zhang et al., 2020a) has conducted preliminary experimental validations
420   on simple networks (e.g. LeNet (LeCun et al., 1989)), demonstrating that the performance of the
421   first GAN-based attack GMI is influenced by the predictive power of the target model. Therefore,
422   we evaluate the state-of-the-art MI attacks on target models with varied predictive power to further
423   validate the consistency of this characteristic in the recent attacks. The resolution of datasets is set to
424   64 × 64. The evaluation is stated in Fig. 4.
425
      The comparison in Fig. 4 reveals most MI attacks maintain the trend that higher predictive power
426   contributes to better attack performance, which is consistent with the aforementioned characteristic.
427   Specifically, the earlier attack methods (e.g., GMI and KEDMI) presents more fluctuation on the
428   trend across different predictive power, while the recent attack methods (e.g., PLGMI and PPA) show
429   in more stable trend when predictive power increases. This indicates the predictive power of target
430   models plays a crucial role in measuring the performance of MI attacks. Thus one can expect lower
431   privacy leakage in the robust model by balancing the accuracy-privacy trade-off. This study of the
      predictive power of target models illustrates another useful aspect of our MIBench.

      4.5    E VALUATION ON D EFENSE S TRATEGIES
433
434
      This analysis concentrates on the robustness of MI attacks when applied to target models with defense
435
      strategies. Notably, we select the first 100 classes subset from FaceScrub as the target dataset due
436   to the time cost. The assessment results are listed in Fig.5. The configuration of defenses is set
437   following the official parameters, as detailed in Table 8.
438
439
      Overall, the TL (Ho et al., 2024) achieves the state-of-the-art decrease in Accuracy. However,
      advanced attack methods have overcome current defense strategies to some extent, such as PLG
440
      and PPA. Additionally, some older defense strategies (e.g. MID (Wang et al., 2021c)) are no longer
441
      effective against the latest attacks. From Fig.5, we observe that LS (Struppek et al., 2023) exhibits
442   unexpectedly poor performance in Acc@1 metric while it was recently published in the top-tier
443   conference. The potential reason might be the utilized target classifier with relatively high test
444   accuracy, as validated in the Sec. C.4. Furthermore, we conduct further experiments with PPA
445   as the attack method against ResNet-152 trained on high resolution (224×224) scenario, proving
446   that this phenomenon can be extended into other defenses. The results are listed in the Table 5,
447   demonstrating that all the defenses become invalid even with the recommended parameters. More
448   in-depth evaluation on defenses is exhibited in Sec C.5.
449   Combining this phenomenon with the above experiment on predictive power, our empirical analysis
450   indicates that the leaked information is strongly correlated to the model prediction accuracy and
451   current defenses cannot effectively reduce the privacy information without sacrifice of model perfor-
452   mance. Our findings emphasize that more reliable and stable defense strategies should be studied due
453   to the fact that high model prediction accuracy is crucial for application of AI technology.

470   5     C ONCLUSION
471
472
      In this paper, we develop MIBench, a comprehensive, unified, and reliable benchmark, and provide
473
      an extensible and reproducible toolbox for researchers. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first
474   benchmark and first open-source toolbox in the MI field. Our benchmark encompasses 16 of the
475   state-of-the-art MI attack methods and defense strategies and more algorithms will be continually
476   updated. Based on the implemented toolbox, we establish a consistent experimental environment and
477   conducted extensive experimental analyses to facilitate fair comparison between different methods.
478   In our experiments, we explore the impact of multiple settings, such as different image resolutions,
479   model predictive power and defense. With in-depth analysis, we have identified new insights and
480   proposed potential solutions to alleviate them.
481   Societal Impact and Ethical Considerations. A potential negative impact of our benchmark could
482   be malicious users leveraging the implemented attack methods to reconstruct private data from public
483   system. To alleviate this potential dilemma, a cautious approach for data users is to adopt robust and
484   reliable defense strategies, as shown in the Sec.4.5 of our paper. Additionally, establishing access
485   permissions and limiting the number of visits for each user is crucial to build responsible AI systems,
      thereby alleviating the potential contradictions with individual data subjects.

767   datasets in high and low versions of the resolution. Low resolution versions include 64 × 64 and
768
      112 × 112, and high resolution versions include 224 × 224 and 299 × 299. Here are the default
      pre-processing method for the four datasets.
769
770
              • CelebA (Liu et al., 2015). For the low version, a center-croped with a crop size 108 × 108
771
                and a resize function is applied to each sample from the origin images. For the high version,
772             only a direct resize will be used in the images pre-processed from HD-CelebA-Cropper (He,
773             2020).
774
              • FaceScrub (Ng & Winkler, 2014). In the low-resolution version, the original data is center
775
                cropped at 54/64 and then scaled, and the high-resolution is scaled directly.
776
777           • FFHQ (Karras et al., 2019) & MetFaces (Karras et al., 2020a). In low and high resolution
778             versions, we center cropped the data with 88/128 and 800/1024 factors, respectively, and
779
                scaled to the specified resolution.

799   A.3     I MPLEMENTATION OF C LASSIFIER T RAINING & D EFENSE M ETHODS
800
      Our toolbox supports the training of a wide range of classifiers, including those used by the official
801
      code of most implemented algorithms, as well as those supported by TorchVision.
802
803   To ensure consistency across different defense strategies, we provide a unified framework for classifier
804   training. Here we present the general idea of implemented methods.
805
806
            1. NO Defense. The classifiers are trained with the cross-entropy loss function.
807         2. MID (Wang et al., 2021c). It applies a Gaussian perturbation to the features in front of
808            the classification head to reduce the mutual information of the model inputs and outputs.
809            According to the official code, the method can also be called VIB, and we adopt this name in
               our toolbox. The hyperparameter α controls the factor of the Gaussian perturbation term.


                                                         15
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
            3. BiDO (Peng et al., 2022). It adds the regular term loss (COCO or HSIC) function so that
811            the intermediate features decrease the mutual information with the input and increase the
812            mutual information with the output. It has two hyperparameters, α and β. The former is
813            the factor of the regular term loss between inputs and features, and the latter means that
814            between features and model outputs.
815
            4. Label Smoothing (LS) (Struppek et al., 2023). It adds an label smoothing term with a
816            negative smoothing factor α into the cross-entropy loss.
817
            5. Transfer Learning (TL) (Ho et al., 2024). This method uses a pre-trained model for
818
               fine-tuning. The parameters of the previous layers are frozen and those of the later layers
819
               will be fine-tuned. In our implementation, the hyperparameter α is defined as a ratio of the
820            number of frozen layers.
821
822   In order to fairly compare the various defense methods, all defense methods are trained using the
823   same pre-trained model in our experiments.

825   A.4   D ETAILS OF ATTACK P ROCESS
826
827   The attack process follows a sequential workflow, containing latent vectors sampling, labeled latent
828   vectors selection, optimization and final images selection. Here are the details of the workflow.
829
830   Latent Vectors Sampling. This step generate random latent vectors and distributes them for each
831   attack target. Most attack methods use a random distribution strategy. Mirror, PPA and BREP
832   calculate the score of each latent vector corresponding to each label, and for each label the vectors
833   with the highest scores are selected.
834
835   Labeled Latent Vectors Selection (Optional). The previous step distributes latent vectors for
836   each label, and this step optimizes the latent vectors by calculating the scores of the latent vectors
837
      corresponding to the labels and selecting the few vectors with the highest scores. Although currently
      there are no algorithms use this step, it can be added into the attack algorithms that use conditional
838
      generators, e.g. PLGMI (Yuan et al., 2023a) and LOKT (Nguyen et al., 2023a).
839
840
      Optimization. Optimization is the key step for the attack process, it accepts the initialized latent
841
      vectors and attack labels as input and outputs the optimized inverted images. We provide several
842   kinds of optimization strategies of each attack method as follows.
843
844         1. Simple White-Box Optimization. An optimizer for attack algorithms that use the gradient
845            except KEDMI (Chen et al., 2021b) and VMI (Wang et al., 2021a). It optimize and generate
846            an image for each input vector. Some implement details are displayed in Table 6.
847         2. Variance White-Box Optimization. It optimizes results from a Gaussian distribution
848            of latent vectors corresponding to the target labels, and images are generated by random
849            sampling from the optimized distribution. It is used by KEDMI (Chen et al., 2021b).
850
            3. Miner WhiteBox Optimization. This optimizer aims to iteratively update parameters of
851
               networks that are utilized to produce high-quality latent vectors, such as Flow models (Xu
852            et al., 2022). It is used by VMI (Wang et al., 2021a).
853
854
            4. Genetic Optimization. An optimizer using genetic algorithms for optimization. The black-
               box version of Mirror (An et al., 2022) and C2FMI (Ye et al., 2023) use this optimization
855
               strategy.
856
857         5. BREP Optimization. A specific optimizer for BREPMI algorithm (Kahla et al., 2022). It
858
               uses a boundary repelling strategy for gradient simulation.
859         6. Reinforcement Learning Optimization. Optimizing the latent vectors via reinforcement
860            learning. Used by RLB attack method (Han et al., 2023).
861
862   Final Images Selection (Optional). This step works by calculating the scores of each image and
863   selecting the part of the image with the highest score as the result of the attack. It is used by PPA
      (Struppek et al., 2022).

      A.5     D ETAILS OF E VALUATION
876
877   We provide the following four evaluation metrics to evaluate the effectiveness of the attack.
878
879          1. Classification Accuracy. The metric uses a given classifier to classify the inverted images
880             and measures the top-1 and top-5 accuracy for target labels. The higher the reconstructed
881
                samples achieve attack accuracy on another classifier trained with the same dataset, the more
                private information in the dataset can be considered to be exposed (Zhang et al., 2020a).
882
883          2. Feature Distance. The feature is defined as the output of the given classifier’s penultimate
884             layer. We compute the shortest feature l2 distance between inverted images and private
885
                samples for each class and calculate the average distance. Smaller feature distance means
                more similar features to the private image.
886
887          3. Fréchet Inception Distance (FID). FID (Heusel et al., 2017) is commonly used to evaluate
888
                the generation quality of GANs. The lower FID score shows higher inter-class diversity and
                realism (Wang et al., 2021a).
889
890          4. Sample Diversity. The metric contains Precision-Recall (Kynkäänniemi et al., 2019) and
891             Density-Coverage (Naeem et al., 2020) scores. Higher values indicate greater intra-class
892
                diversity of the inverted images.

1230   D     L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE P LANS
1231
1232   Our benchmark mainly focuses on GAN-based MI attacks and MI defenses applied in the classifier
1233   training stage. We will extend our benchmark to wider range of MI methods, including learning-based
1234   MI attacks and MI defenses applied to the classifier output. Furthermore, the concept of MI also
1235   pervades across modalities (e.g. text (Parikh et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2023; Carlini et al., 2019;
1236   2021; Yu et al., 2023; Nasr et al., 2023) and graph learning (Zhang et al., 2021; 2022b; Zhou et al.,
1237   2023; Wu et al., 2022; He et al., 2021)) beyond computer vision domain, where our benchmark
1238
       concentrates in current stage. Expansion to new modalities is a promising direction for our benchmark
       to further explore the privacy threats in other fields, leading to more generalization in the AI security.
1239
       In addition to developing new algorithms, it is also essential to conduct further research on the MI
1240
       attacks and defenses to make in-depth analysis about their characteristics and bring valuable new
1241   insights.
