009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               Self-supervised learning (SSL) has revolutionized image processing, but extend-
012            ing its success to video understanding presents unique challenges due to increased
013            data complexity and computational demands. We introduce ViDROP (Video
014            Dense Representation thrOugh spatio-temporal sParsity), a novel SSL architec-
015            ture for video understanding that combines token dropping and masking strategies.
016            Our approach eliminates the need for a decoder and enables per-patch loss compu-
017            tation, overcoming limitations of previous video SSL methods. Moreover, we pro-
018            pose a simple yet effective video compression technique using k-means clustering
019            in pixel space, significantly accelerating data loading and facilitating rapid ex-
020
               perimentation. ViDROP demonstrates remarkable scalability across model sizes,
               from ViT-Small to ViT-Huge, when starting from pretrained models (VideoMAE
021
               or V-JEPA), achieving significant performance gains. Pushing the boundaries even
022
               further, we leverage network expansion techniques to successfully train ViT-Huge
023            from scratch using modest computational resources, achieving comparable accu-
024            racy to VideoMAE 25× faster in training time. This marks a significant break-
025            through in large-scale video SSL, enabling the training of state-of-the-art mod-
026            els with limited resources. Extensive experiments show that ViDROP achieves
027            state-of-the-art performance on various video understanding benchmarks, includ-
028            ing Kinetics400, SSv2, UCF101, and HMDB51, as well as in temporal action
029            detection (THUMOS14). These results highlight the effectiveness of our fine-
030            grained token-level learning strategy in a domain traditionally dominated by fine-
031
               tuned SSL models, while enabling the training of large-scale models with limited
               computational resources.

035   1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037   Self-supervised learning (SSL) has transformed computer vision by enabling models to learn rich
038   representations from vast amounts of unlabeled data. While SSL methods like DINOv2 Oquab
039   et al. (2023) have achieved remarkable success in image processing, extending these techniques to
040   video understanding presents unique challenges due to increased data complexity and computational
041   demands.
042   The temporal dimension in videos captures essential information about object movement and scene
043   changes, crucial for action understanding. However, it also significantly increases the amount of
044   data to be processed, exacerbating computational burdens. Recent works such as VideoMAE Tong
045   et al. (2022) have addressed these challenges by employing sparse encoder and dense decoder archi-
046   tectures, leveraging vision transformers Dosovitskiy et al. (2020) to efficiently process video data
047   split into tokens or patches.
048   Despite their promise, these approaches face two significant limitations. First, the computational
049   cost of the decoder remains considerable, as it still operates on all or a large portion of video tokens
050   (see e.g., Wang et al. (2023a); Hwang et al. (2022)). Second, the split between encoder and decoder,
051   coupled with a low-level reconstruction objective, can lead to suboptimal representation learning.
052   The encoder may not capture all relevant high-level features, as some information is relegated to
053   the decoder for reconstruction purposes. This division of representational power can result in less
      robust or comprehensive features, potentially misaligning with high-level downstream tasks.

      To address these challenges, we propose ViDROP (Video Dense Representation thrOugh spatio-
      temporal sParsity), a novel SSL architecture for video understanding that combines token dropping
076
      and masking strategies. ViDROP employs a sparse encoder with masked tokens and eliminates the
077
      need for a decoder, enabling per-patch loss computation for robust representations while maintain-
078   ing exceptional efficiency. This approach overcomes the limitations of previous methods, providing
079   a more effective and computationally efficient solution for self-supervised learning in video under-
080   standing tasks (see Figure 2).
081
      To accelerate training, we introduce a simple yet effective video compression technique using k-
082
      means clustering Lloyd (1982) in pixel space. This innovation addresses the data loading bottleneck
083
      that emerges as our model accelerates computation, especially for smaller models.
084
085   ViDROP achieves state-of-the-art linear probing results using only minimal data augmentations (ran-
086   dom resized cropping and flipping), in contrast to top-performing models like SVT Ranasinghe et al.
087
      (2021) and ρBYOL Feichtenhofer et al. (2021) that rely on heavy, potentially dataset-specific aug-
      mentations. This approach aims for more robust and generalizable representations. Additionally,
088
      ViDROP produces high-quality per-patch representations alongside strong global representations,
089
      distinguishing it from traditional contrastive methods that excel in global video clip representations
090   but may struggle with fine-grained, per-patch understanding (see Figure 1). This versatility enables
091   ViDROP to be effective across a wide range of downstream tasks, from those requiring local under-
092   standing to those needing global video comprehension.
093
      Our contributions can be summarized as follows:
094
095          • A novel, efficient SSL architecture for video understanding: We introduce ViDROP,
096            combining sparse token processing with masked learning, achieving state-of-the-art per-
097            formance without a decoder while producing high-quality per-patch and global representa-
098            tions.
099          • Scalable and resource-efficient training: We demonstrate exceptional scalability across
100            model sizes (ViT-Small to ViT-Huge) and initialization strategies (VideoMAE Tong et al.
101            (2022), V-JEPA Bardes et al. (2024)). By leveraging network expansion techniques Wang
102            et al. (2023b) and introducing a novel k-means clustering-based video compression method,
103            we train a ViT-Huge model with comparable accuracy to VideoMAE in just 4% of the total
104            training time, enabling large-scale video SSL with limited computational resources.
105          • Comprehensive evaluation: ViDROP achieves superior performance across a wide range
106            of video understanding tasks, including action recognition, temporal action detection,
107            frame-wise tracking, and copy detection. Our method’s versatility extends to image classi-
               fication, showcasing the robustness and generalizability of the learned representations.

      Evolution of image SSL: Self-supervised learning (SSL) in computer vision has evolved from
      image-based to video understanding approaches. Image SSL progressed from contrastive learn-
113
      ing van den Oord et al. (2018); Chen et al. (2020a;b) to clustering-based methods Caron et al. (2018;
114
      2020; 2021), then to direct feature prediction Grill et al. (2020); Chen & He (2020) and cross-
115   correlation techniques Zbontar et al. (2021). These discriminative approaches have consistently
116   advanced representation learning in static images.
117
118
      Masked modeling and diverse SSL approaches: Complementing these discriminative methods,
      various other approaches have played a crucial role in SSL. Notably, masked modeling approaches
119
      have gained significant traction. BEiT Bao et al. (2021) introduced the concept of infilling in latent
120
      space, inspired by BERT Devlin et al. (2019) in natural language processing. Masked Autoencoders
121   (MAE) He et al. (2021) adapted this idea to regress missing pixel patches directly in pixel space,
122   while AIM El-Nouby et al. (2024) advanced these concepts with next token prediction in latent
123   space. Hybrid models such as iBOT Zhou et al. (2021) and DINOv2 Oquab et al. (2023) have
124   further pushed the boundaries, combining generative and discriminative elements to produce high-
125   quality representations. These approaches have demonstrated exceptional performance in tasks like
126   KNN classification Fix & Hodges (1989) and unsupervised semantic segmentation Hamilton et al.
127   (2022), showcasing the power of integrating multiple SSL paradigms.
128   Video SSL challenges: The transition from image to video SSL introduces unique challenges due to
129   the temporal dimension and increased data scale. Video SSL methods often employ pretext tasks de-
130   signed to capture both spatial and temporal aspects, such as determining the correct order of shuffled
131   video frames Misra et al. (2016); Jenni et al. (2020); Dorkenwald et al. (2022); Dave et al. (2023).
132   Generative models like VideoMAE Tong et al. (2022) and VideoMAEv2 Wang et al. (2023a) have
133   been adapted to reconstruct video segments or entire frames, while consistency-based approaches
134   like ρBYOL Feichtenhofer et al. (2021), BRAVE Recasens et al. (2021), and SVT Ranasinghe et al.
135
      (2021) ensure feature consistency across different video segments.
136   Computational optimization: The computational demands of processing videos, especially with
137   Vision Transformers (ViT) Dosovitskiy et al. (2020), have led to various optimization strategies.
138   Factorized attention, as used in SVT Ranasinghe et al. (2021), offers one approach, although com-
139   prehensive spatio-temporal attention, as in ViViT Arnab et al. (2021), typically yields superior re-
140   sults. Some methods leverage pretrained image or short video clip encoders to derive representations
141
      for longer clips Sameni et al. (2022); Lei et al. (2021). Inspired by MAE He et al. (2021), sparse
      encoder inputs paired with dense decoder outputs have been employed to reduce computational re-
142
      quirements, with models like VideoMAEv2 Wang et al. (2023a) and EVEREST Hwang et al. (2022)
143
      further economizing resources by introducing sparse reconstruction tokens to the decoder.
144
145   Data processing bottlenecks: Addressing the data bottleneck in video SSL remains a significant
146   challenge. While tools like FFCV Leclerc et al. (2023) and its SSL variant Bordes et al. (2023) are
147
      effective for images, they fall short for video processing. Wrappers such as decord and Avion Zhao
      & Krahenbuhl (2023) offer some improvements but remain relatively slow. Data remasking tech-
148
      niques Bardes et al. (2024); Feichtenhofer et al. (2022) provide a cost-effective way to alleviate
149
      data loading bottlenecks. Another approach involves using precomputed latent representations of
150   videos Wiles et al. (2022); Jaegle et al. (2021), which can be integrated with learned data augmen-
151   tations Lee et al. (2023). However, this method introduces higher costs at the inference stage due to
152   the need for an expensive encoder to convert raw pixel videos into the latent representation.
153
      Role of data augmentation: The role of data augmentation in SSL has been a subject of significant
154
      research Zhai et al. (2023); Wagner et al. (2022); Kalibhat et al. (2023). While heavy augmentations
155
      have been crucial for the success of many image SSL methods Chen et al. (2020a); Grill et al. (2020),
156   their application to video data is more complex due to the need to preserve temporal coherence Fe-
157   ichtenhofer et al. (2021). Some video SSL approaches have adapted image augmentation techniques
158   to the video domain Ranasinghe et al. (2021), while others have developed video-specific augmenta-
159   tions Qian et al. (2020). However, the computational cost of these augmentations can be substantial,
160   especially for large-scale video datasets. Moreover, the reliance on carefully designed augmenta-
161   tions raises questions about the broader applicability and robustness of SSL methods across diverse
      video datasets and tasks.

      3     M ETHOD
188
189   ViDROP introduces a novel approach to self-supervised learning for video understanding, com-
190   bining efficient processing with effective representation learning. Our method builds upon the DI-
191
      NOv2 Oquab et al. (2023) framework, adapting it for video data through a sparse encoder archi-
      tecture that integrates token dropping and masking within a single network. We employ a video
192
      sampling and processing strategy using both large and small crops, along with a multi-component
193
      loss function ensuring global and local feature consistency. Additionally, we introduce a lossy data
194   loading scheme based on k-means clustering for efficient video compression. In the following sub-
195   sections, we detail these components and explain how they work together to overcome the limitations
196   of existing video SSL methods.
197
198
      3.1   V IDEO S AMPLING AND P ROCESSING
199
200
      Given a video, we sample multiple clips, creating two large crops and eight small crops. The
201
      large crops are fed directly to an exponential moving average (EMA) of the network, serving as
202   the teacher. For the student network, we apply sparsification (token dropping) to these same views
203   and replace some of the remaining tokens with a special MSK token. Small crops maintain dense
204   inputs without sparsification or masking Assran et al. (2022). Both student and teacher networks
205   include a CLS token for global representation.
206
207
      3.2   L OSS F UNCTION
208
209
      ViDROP’s loss function consists of two main components. The first is a DINO-style loss calculated
210   between the CLS tokens of the student and teacher Caron et al. (2021). The second is a patch-
211   level loss for the masked tokens in the student, using the corresponding outputs from the teacher
212   as targets Zhou et al. (2021). We employ a global-global consistency loss to ensure coherence
213   between different views of the same video and a global-local consistency loss that aligns features
214   from large crops (global) with those from small crops (local). The patch-level loss enables fine-
215   grained representation learning, complemented by the KoLeo regularization adapted from DINOv2.
      For detailed formulations of these losses, we refer readers to the DINOv2 paper Oquab et al. (2023).

241   3.3   A RCHITECTURE
242
243   ViDROP employs a sparse encoder with masked tokens, eliminating the need for a separate decoder
244   (Figure 2). This design differs from asymmetric encoder-decoder architectures He et al. (2021);
245
      Tong et al. (2022); Liu et al. (2022); Bardes et al. (2024); Assran et al. (2023); Baevski et al. (2022a)
      by uniquely combining token dropping and masking strategies within a single encoder. Our approach
246
      achieves the efficiency of sparse processing while maintaining the rich representational learning of
247
      mask-based self-distillation methods Zhou et al. (2021); Oquab et al. (2023); Bao et al. (2021);
248   Xie et al. (2021); Baevski et al. (2022b). Crucially, our design enables per-patch loss computation,
249   allowing fine-grained representation learning without the computational overhead of dense decoders.
250   This bridges the gap between token dropping efficiency and mask-based representational power.
251
252   3.4   L OSSY DATA L OADING
253
254   To address the data loading bottleneck in video processing, we introduce a lossy data loading scheme
255   based on k-means clustering Lloyd (1982) of video patches (see Figure 3). By applying k-means
256   clustering directly in pixel space on 10 × 10 pixel patches, we achieve a compression rate of 150
257   (using 65536 clusters), significantly accelerating the data loading process.
258   This approach offers several advantages over VQVAE-based methods van den Oord et al. (2017);
259   Esser et al. (2020); Park et al. (2023); Wiles et al. (2022). It allows for faster processing during both
260   training and inference by avoiding complex encoding and decoding steps. Our method maintains
261   compatibility with pretrained models that operate on raw pixel data, enabling us to leverage exist-
262   ing large-scale pretrained checkpoints. Furthermore, our approach provides a flexible compression
263   scheme that can be easily adjusted to balance between compression rate and representation quality.

345   Number of small crops. A key success element of DINO Caron et al. (2021) and MSN Assran et al.
346   (2022) was using small crops. We see a similar pattern in Table 1b, where small crops significantly
347   boost performance. We use the same setting as DINOv2 Oquab et al. (2023), but observe that fewer
348   crops are viable. If computational load is an issue (as small crops are not sparsified), the number
349   can be reduced while maintaining considerable performance gains.
350   Drop pattern. While previous video reconstruction methods emphasized specific token dropping
351   patterns Tong et al. (2022); Bardes et al. (2024); Feichtenhofer et al. (2022), Table 1c shows that
352   random patch dropping outperforms tube dropping Tong et al. (2022); Wang et al. (2023a) and block
353   masking Bardes et al. (2024). We observe higher self-supervised loss for these patterns compared to
354   random dropping, suggesting that their difficulty may hinder the model’s representational power.
355   Drop rate. Token dropping is an essential component for reducing the computational load of our
356   model. In Table 1d, we can see that there is a trade-off in terms of training time and quality. Reducing
357   the drop rate improves the quality but at the cost of extra training time. Note that in this experiment,
358   we had to reduce the batch size of the model trained with an 80% token drop rate to be able to train
359   it on our hardware. We further studied this trade-off and trained a dense model (i.e., a model with a
360   drop rate of 0%) for 100 hours (almost 4× the base model) and achieved an accuracy of only 47.1%,
361   which is significantly lower than the 54.9% accuracy of the base model (see Figure 4).
362   Number of clusters. Since we are using Sinkhorn-Knopp centering for our DINO Caron et al.
363   (2021) losses, we are significantly more compute-heavy in the loss (compared to V-JEPA Bardes
364   et al. (2024) and VideoMAE Tong et al. (2022)). Additionally, we can’t apply gradient accumulation
365   with many steps, since the centering operation depends on the whole batch. Changing the loss is
366   beyond the scope of this paper, so instead, we reduced the number of clusters both for the global loss
367   and patch loss. Results in Table 1e show that, similar to the findings of MSN Assran et al. (2022),
368
      we can significantly reduce the number of clusters and maintain the same performance. We also
      notice that, similar to the findings of DINOv2 Oquab et al. (2023), having different heads for the
369
      two loss terms is beneficial. For the rest of the ablation studies, we used 16k clusters.

374
      we can use larger probabilities and achieve slight improvement. This likely stems from video data’s

      et al. (2022) vs 75% for images He et al. (2021)).

457   For our main results, we train seven different models to comprehensively evaluate the performance of
458   ViDROP under various conditions. We begin with two ViT-Large models based on VideoMAE Tong
459   et al. (2022), one with minimal data augmentation and another incorporating heavier augmentations
460
      used in DINO Caron et al. (2021). These are followed by two similarly configured ViT-Large models
      based on VJEPA Bardes et al. (2024), demonstrating the compatibility and versatility of our method
461
      across different pretraining paradigms. To showcase scalability, we include two ViT-Huge mod-
462
      els: one initialized from a VideoMAE checkpoint and another trained using LEMON Wang et al.
463   (2023b) expansion techniques from random initialization, highlighting the efficiency and potential of
464   our method for training large-scale models with limited computational resources. All VideoMAE-
465   based models are initialized with checkpoints pretrained for 1600 epochs on Kinetics400, while
466   VJEPA-based models use the original VJEPA checkpoint. For one VideoMAE-based ViT-Large
467   model, we utilize KMeans compressed data. While KMeans compression doesn’t offer significant
468   speed benefits for ViT-Large models, we include this configuration to demonstrate the robustness
469   of our findings. By using compressed data, we establish a lower bound on performance (as shown
470   in Table 4), yet still outperform the original VideoMAE model, underscoring the effectiveness of
471
      ViDROP even under potentially suboptimal data conditions. Additionally, to facilitate direct com-
      parisons with academic papers, we separately train a ViT-Base model based on VideoMAE trained
472
      for 800 epochs on Kinetics400.

521   5   C ONCLUSION
522
523
524   We presented ViDROP, a novel self-supervised learning approach for video understanding that
525
      combines token dropping and masking strategies within a single encoder. Our method achieves
      state-of-the-art performance across various video understanding tasks, including action recognition,
526
      temporal action detection, and low-shot learning, while maintaining computational efficiency. Key
527
      innovations include a sparse encoder architecture that eliminates the need for a separate decoder, en-
528   abling fine-grained representation learning without computational overhead, and a lossy data loading
529   scheme based on k-means clustering, significantly accelerating data processing while maintaining
530   compatibility with pretrained models. We demonstrated scalability across model sizes and initializa-
531   tion strategies, including successful training of ViT-Huge models from scratch using limited com-
532   putational resources. ViDROP’s remarkable data efficiency in low-shot learning scenarios and its
533   effectiveness in both action classification and temporal action detection tasks highlight its versatility
534   and potential for real-world applications.
535   Looking forward, our work opens up possibilities for training state-of-the-art models with limited
536   computational resources, making advanced video understanding more accessible. However, our re-
537   sults with VJEPA-initialized models highlight the importance of training on more diverse datasets to
538   enhance generalization capabilities. Additionally, while our LEMON experiments for training ViT-
539   Huge models from scratch show promising results, they are not yet on par with models initialized
      from pretrained weights, indicating a need for further exploration of optimal scaling strategies.
