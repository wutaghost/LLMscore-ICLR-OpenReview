009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Optimizing opaque functions in high-dimensional search spaces has been known
012            to be challenging for traditional Bayesian Optimization (BO). In this paper, we
013
               introduce HiBO, a novel hierarchical algorithm integrating global-level search
               space partitioning information into the acquisition strategy of a local BO-based
014
               optimizer. HiBO employs a search-tree-based global-level navigator to adaptively
015
               split the search space into partitions with different sampling potential. The local
016            optimizer then utilizes this global-level information to guide its acquisition strat-
017            egy towards the most promising regions within the search space. A comprehensive
018            set of evaluations demonstrates that HiBO outperforms state-of-the-art methods in
019            high-dimensional synthetic benchmarks and presents significant practical effec-
020            tiveness in the real-world task of tuning configurations of database management
021            systems (DBMSs).

023   1   I NTRODUCTION
024
      Bayesian Optimization (BO) has emerged as a powerful approach for optimizing opaque functions
025   that exhibit little information of its form and requires iterative evaluations for reaching the optima
026   with significant computational cost. BO has been applied to a wide range of real-world scenar-
027   ios requiring optimization over opaque functions within a limited budget of sampling times, such
028   as Neural Architecture Search (Kandasamy et al., 2018; Ru et al., 2020), hyperparameter tuning
029   (Hvarfner et al., 2022; Zimmer et al., 2021) and automatic database management system (DBMS)
030   configuration tuning (Van Aken et al., 2017; Zhang et al., 2021; 2022). Typically, BO iteratively
031   builds a probabilistic surrogate model on the objective function and uses an acquisition function
032   to decide where to sample next, balancing exploration and exploitation to efficiently locate the op-
033
      timum. However, it is well known that BO struggles to scale in high-dimensional search spaces
      (Binois & Wycoff, 2022; Malu et al., 2021). As dimensionality increases, the size of the search
034
      space grows exponentially and uniformly sampled points are more likely to be distant from each
035
      other (Rashidi et al., 2024). Such sparsity of nearby data increases uncertainty in the posterior dis-
036   tribution, which can cause the acquisition function to favor unexplored regions excessively, leading
037   to over-exploration.
038
039
      Even worse, optimization over high-dimensional search spaces in real-world systems is more chal-
      lenging. The task of DBMS configuration tuning exemplifies this challenge, which involves a high-
040
      dimensional configuration knob space, complex interdependencies between knobs, and noisy per-
041
      formance observations (Zhao et al., 2023). Existing approaches towards this problem either rely
042   on extensive pre-collected random samples (Van Aken et al., 2017) or metadata of prior workloads
043   (Zhang et al., 2021), or restrict evaluations to manually selected low-dimensional spaces (Zhang
044   et al., 2022; Cereda et al., 2021). Moreover, methods aiming to scale BO to high dimensionali-
045   ties (to be discussed below) often lack evaluation on real-world systems, limiting insights into their
046   practical effectiveness.
047
      Various approaches have been proposed towards mitigating the curse-of-dimensionality. Some rely
048   on additive models applied to separate groups of dimensions (Duvenaud et al., 2011; Kandasamy
049   et al., 2015), while others project the high-dimensional input space into a lower-dimensional sub-
050   space using linear transformations (Wang et al., 2016; Letham et al., 2020; Nayebi et al., 2019).
051   But these methods assume specific structural properties, such as decomposability of the objective
052   function or the existence of a hidden low-dimensional active subspace. Another line of work avoids
053   these assumptions by restricting the sampling scope into a promising region at each iteration. Among
      them, some define this region as the trust region (TR) maintained around the best samples found so

054
      far (Eriksson et al., 2019; Daulton et al., 2022) while others partition the search space and focus
055   on the most promising regions (Wang et al., 2020; Munos, 2011; Bubeck et al., 2011; Kim et al.,
056   2020). However, these methods greedily confine the sampling scope to the only region with high-
057   est immediate potential, underutilizing the structural information generated during the partitioning
058   process, such as the way the search space is partitioned. Such inefficient utilization of generated
059   structural information and inflexible sampling scope confinement can lead to suboptimal perfor-
060   mance. Moreover, with a limited initial sample budget, the constructed tree may lack reliability, and
061   strict confinement can further prevent effective exploration of the search space. Our experiments em-
062   pirically demonstrate the suboptimal performance of such approaches, where our proposed method
063
      offers ∼28% performance improvement in real-world systems than them. While incorporating such
      structural information into the original BO algorithm for optimal performance introduces challenges,
064
      including designing an effective hierarchy to combine the extra module extracting structural infor-
065
      mation with the original BO, and ensuring that the structural information are utilized optimally.
066   On the other hand, the way of partitioning the search space is critical in terms of exploitation-and-
067   exploration trade-offs and the computational cost. Splitting the search space into coarser or fine
068   partitions directly impacts the generated structural information and the computational overhead in
069   this process. An adaptive mechanism for adjusting the partitioning process is needed.
070
      Motivated by the limitations of existing high-dimensional BO methods and the need for effective
071
      real-world system tuning, we have the following contributions:
072
073   Hierarchical Framework for Bayesian Optimization. We propose Hierarchical Bayesian
074   Optimization (HiBO), a hierarchical BO variant that is the first to integrate global-level space par-
075   titioning information into the local BO model’s acquisition strategy instead of rigidly confining the
076
      sampling scope. HiBO is shown to provide ∼28% more performance improvements than only us-
      ing the local optimizer in high-dimensional DBMS configuration tuning tasks with limited sample
077
      budget.
078
079   Search Space Partitioning with Adaptive Control. We introduce the search-tree-based space
080   partitioning for the global-level navigator, recursively splitting the search space into the partitions
081   with different sampling potential. Besides, an adaptive mechanism on controlling the maximum
082
      search tree depth is proposed for effective exploration-and-exploitation trade-offs and computational
      cost reduction.
083
084   Practical Evaluation on Real-world Benchmark. Besides experiments on synthetic and simulated
085   “real-world” benchmarks (Jones, 2008; Wang et al., 2017), we demonstrate HiBO’s effectiveness
086   and practical value via evaluation on a real-world DBMS configuration tuning task, comprehensively
087   considering performance improvement, tuning time cost and failure rate.

089   2    BACKGROUND
090   2.1 P RELIMINARIES
091
      Bayesian Optimization (BO) is an effective technique for optimization that iteratively refines a prob-
092
      abilistic surrogate model of the objective function and employs an acquisition function to determine
093   the next sampling points (Candelieri, 2021).
094
095   The goal is to solve the following problem:
096                                             x∗ = arg max f (x)                                           (1)
097                                                      x∈X
098
099   where x is typically a data point in d-dimensional search space X ⊂ Rd and f (x) is a opaque
100   function mapping the input data into the performance metric of interest. This function is typically
101   computationally expensive to evaluate and provides limited explicit information, hence the term
102   “opaque”.
103   A typical BO-based algorithms begins with an initial random sampling of data points. In each itera-
104   tion of its optimization loop, it fits a surrogate model to estimate the posterior probability distribution
105   over the objective function based on the collected data. It then selects the next point maximizing the
106   acquisition function, evaluates this point by the objective function and updates the history dataset.
107   As iterations progress, the surrogate model becomes increasingly accurate at estimating the distri-
      bution of the objective function, leading to better sampling decisions.

120   Though BO has been recognized as a powerful approach across various problem domains, vanilla
121   BO is known to be inherently vulnerable to the “curse-of-dimensionality” (Binois & Wycoff, 2022)
122   when the search space has a large number of dimensions. As dimensionality increases, the average
123   squared distance between uniformly sampled points grows, making it harder for distance-based
124   surrogate models to capture correlations between data points (Rashidi et al., 2024). Additionally,
125
      the increased distance can result in greater uncertainty assigned to data points by the estimated
      posterior, increasing acquisition functions unreasonably and trapping BO algorithms in cycles of
126
      excessive exploration (“over-exploration”; Siivola et al. (2018)).

128   2.2   R ELATED W ORK
129
      Several paradigms of approaches have been developed to scale Bayesian Optimization (BO)
130
      to high-dimensional search spaces. As discussed in Section 1, some methods leverage struc-
131   tural assumptions about the objective function, such as additive kernels (Duvenaud et al.,
132   2011; Kandasamy et al., 2015; Wang et al., 2018) or subspace embeddings (Wang et al.,
133   2016; Letham et al., 2020; Nayebi et al., 2019; Papenmeier et al., 2022). Alternatively,
134   variable-selection-based strategies are employed to identify the most important dimensions
135   (Li et al., 2017; Spagnol et al., 2019; Shen & Kingsford, 2023; Song et al., 2022). However, these
136   methods often rely on assumptions of the input space that may not always hold in practice, or in-
137   volve uncertain user-estimated parameters for these assumptions, such as the existence of the latent
138   subspace and its dimensionality respectively.
139   Another line of work focuses on optimizing the sampling scope without such assumptions, which
140   can be further divided into 1) local modeling (Eriksson et al., 2019; Daulton et al., 2022) and 2)
141   space partitioning (Munos, 2011; Bubeck et al., 2011; Wang et al., 2021; 2020). TuRBO, a rep-
142   resentative of local modeling, maintains trust regions around the best samples to mitigate over-
143   exploration. While prior space-partitioning-based methods focus on splitting the search space into
144   different partitions and limiting the sampling scope to the most promising one. A detailed compar-
145   ison space-partitioning-based optimization is presented in Table 1, which includes HOO (Bubeck
146
      et al., 2011), LA-NAS (Wang et al., 2021), LA-MCTS (Wang et al., 2020) and HiBO. HOO tradi-
      tionally applies axis-aligned K-nary partitioning for splitting the space while LA-NAS extends it by
147
      leveraging linear regression to learn the linear decision boundary for bifurcation. As a more recent
148
      work, LA-MCTS applies MCTS-inspired ideas to partition the search space based on clustering and
149   classification for more flexible space bifurcation, and becomes the state-of-the-art (SOTA) approach
150   among space-partitioning-based work. In our work, HiBO extends these MCTS-based concepts in
151   LA-NAS and LA-MCTS by formalizing the notion of sampling potential and employing a general
152   search tree instead of MCTS. HiBO’s partitioning search tree can be adaptively adjusted during the
153   search to balance exploration-exploitation trade-off, and reduce unnecessary computational cost.
154   Additionally, HiBO does not restrict sampling scope to the only specific region, allowing greater
155   flexibility and more comprehensive consideration collected samples.
156   It is important to distinguish our use of the term “Hierarchical Bayesian Optimization” from its
157   usage in Pelikan et al. (2007). While Pelikan et al. (2007) also employs this term, their discussions
158   focus on scalable solutions for nearly decomposable and hierarchical problems. While HiBO does
159   not assume any decomposability or hierarchical properties in the optimization problems it addresses.
160   The “hierarchicalness” of HiBO mainly lies in the methodology, instead of the target problem. As
161   our focus is scaling up BO to high-dimensional search space, no further discussion about their
      method is provided considering no direct relevance.

      3     S YSTEM OVERVIEW
182
183   In this section, we will introduce the high-level design of HiBO. HiBO is a hierarchical BO ap-
184   proach consisting of two parts: 1) a global-level navigator, which conducts search-tree-based search
185   space partitioning based on collected data with dynamic and adaptive constraints posed when con-
186   structing the search tree; 2) a local optimizer with a BO-based algorithm as its core. Specifically
187   we choose TuRBO (TuRBO-1; Eriksson et al. (2019)) as the local optimizer throughout this paper
188
      considering its practical effectiveness and simplicity for implementation.
189   Before the optimization loop starts, similar to typical BO-based algorithms, HiBO collects an initial
190   set of data points (history dataset) based on random sampling, ensuring the basic coverage of the
191   search space. The initial dataset allows the global-level navigator to construct the initial search
192   tree and enables the local optimizer to train the initial surrogate model. After the initial sampling,
193   HiBO enters the main optimization loop. During each iteration of the loop, the global-level navigator
194   partitions the search space based on the history dataset, and the local optimizer integrates the global-
195
      level information into its acquisition strategy for biasing the sample selection towards data points
      from most promising regions. The high-level workflow for each iteration is outlined below, where
196
      Figure 1 illustrates this procedure:
197
198       • Step 1 : Based on the history dataset, the global-level navigator constructs the search tree
199         to partition the search space and generates a distribution of partition scores. While the local
200         optimizer trains its surrogate model. The details of search space partitioning is explained in
201         Section 4.1.
202       • Step 2 : The local optimizer randomly samples a set of candidate points within the trust region
203         and calculates the modified acquisition function values based on the trained surrogate model
204         and partition-based scores.
205       • Step 3 : The candidate with the highest adapted acquisition function value is selected for ob-
206         jective function evaluation, and the new observation is added to the history dataset.
207
208
      The key components of HiBO mentioned in the procedure above will be explained in the following
      sections.

214   partitions balancing exploration and exploitation. We intuitively describe the sampling potential of
215   a search space partition as the potential of sampling high-performing data points within this partition
      in the following sections.

234   The core motivation of introducing this global-level navigator is to partition the search space in a
235   way maximizing the difference in sampling potential across resulting partitions. It generates a dis-
236   tribution of sampling potential over these partitions, which will be utilized by the local optimizer for
237   augmenting its acquisition strategy to boost its optimization efficiency in high-dimensional search
238   spaces (see Section 5). Besides the space partitioning itself, the design of the global-level navigator
239   includes mechanisms for reducing computational cost and balancing trade-offs between exploration
240   and exploitation.
241
      4.1   DATA -D RIVEN S EARCH -T REE - BASED S PACE PARTITIONING
242
243   The navigator constructs a search tree in a data-driven manner, where each node represents a parti-
244   tion of the original search space and is associated with the samples falling into this partition. Starting
245   from the root node representing the entire search space, this method recursively splits the space into
246   smaller and more specific partitions, as illustrated in Step 1 of Figure 1.
247   To partition a node, clustering techniques like K-Means (MacQueen et al., 1967) are used to group
248   the samples from the history dataset that fall within this node’s partition into two clusters, assigning
249   each sample a binary label. Given each sample has its cluster label, a classification model is trained
250   on the sample features to predict the cluster labels. This classifier hence learns a decision boundary
251   dividing the current search space partition into two parts. Each of these two parts thus forms a
252   bifurcation of the original partition.
253
      By bifurcating the original partition into two sub-partitions, two child nodes are generated, each cor-
254
      responding to one of the sub-partitions and containing the samples that fall within them. Recursively
255   repeating this ML-based bifurcation starting from the root node will finally result in a binary tree of
256   nodes, where each node represents a progressively refined region of the search space.
257
258   A key aspect of this approach is that clustering is performed on both sample features and their
259
      performance data. As a result, samples in the two resulting sub-partitions are expected to differ
      significantly in terms of both performance and feature similarity, enabling the differentiation of
260
      valuable data points within different partitions. Once the tree is fully constructed, each leaf node
261
      corresponds to a sequence of split operations, and represents a highly specific partition of the original
262   search space. These refined partitions provide global-level insights into which regions of the search
263   space are likely to be valuable for further sampling.
264
265   4.2   S AMPLING P OTENTIAL E VALUATION VIA UCT
266   Once the search tree is constructed, HiBO utilizes Upper Confidence bounds applied to Trees (UCT;
267   Kocsis & Szepesvári (2006)) to evaluate each node’s sampling potential by balancing exploitation
268   and exploration:

      Here, Cp is a hyperparameter controlling the exploration-exploitation trade-off, nj and np denote the
271   number of visits to node j and its parent node respectively, and v̂j represents the average objective
272   value within node j. By integrating UCT, HiBO prioritizes data points from partitions with higher

275
276   In summary, the essence of HiBO’s search-tree-based partitioning strategy involves: 1) utilizing
277
      machine learning techniques to partition the search space, aiming to maximize the difference of
      sampling potential across partitions; and 2) applying UCT to quantitatively assess such potential of
278
      each partition while balancing the exploration and exploitation.

280   4.3    DYNAMIC AND A DAPTIVE S EARCH T REE C ONSTRUCTION
281
      In addition to the procedures described for search space partitioning, we address two additional
282
      concerns regarding the proposed strategies:
283
284       • Computational Cost. Building a search tree at each iteration can be computationally expensive,
285         where multiple classifiers such as Support Vector Machines (Boser et al., 1992) need to be
286         trained and are used for inferring labels of thousands of candidate data points.
287
          • Balance of Exploration and Exploitation The depth of the tree, determined by the number
288
            of bifurcations from root to leaf, affects the range of the search space represented by each leaf
289
            node. As the tree deepens, leaf nodes represent smaller and more specific partitions, potentially
290         leading to insufficient exploration.
291
292   The two concerns necessitate a dynamic and adaptive strategy to control the maximum depth of the
293   constructed search tree during optimization. Towards this target, inspired by TuRBO’s approach
294   (Eriksson et al., 2019) for exploration control via dynamic trust region adjustment, HiBO allows the
295   tree to expand or shrink based on consecutive successes or failures. Consecutive successes reduces
296
      the number of bifurcation and hence increases the range covered by each leaf node, thereby promot-
      ing more exploration by considering a broader set of samples with equal partition-score weights.
297
298   The additional rules for building the tree are explained as follows:
299
300   Rule 1 (Breadth-First Search Tree Construction) Strictly follow the breadth-first order to build the
301   tree layer by layer. Track the depth of each newly split child node and stop splitting once they
302
      reaches the current maximum tree depth, which is shown by the red line in Figure 2a.
303   Rule 2 (Adaptive Maximum Tree Depth) After a certain number of consecutive successes (improve-
304   ments over the current best), reduce the maximum tree depth to broaden the range covered by each
305   leaf node. Conversely, increase the maximum depth after consecutive failures for more focused
306   biasing and hence more exploitation.
307
      Rule 3 (Restart When Too Deep) If the maximum tree depth goes beyond a predefined threshold,
308
      instruct the local optimizer to restart with a new round of initial sampling and reset the maximum
309
      tree depth.
310
311   This dynamic and adaptive control on the maximum depth of the search tree aims to prevent unnec-
312   essary tree growth, reducing computational cost while maintaining a balance between exploration
313   and exploitation. A further validation of the effectiveness of such adaptive tree depth settings are
314   shown in Appendix C.

      6.2.1 E VALUATION BACKGROUND
411
412   Besides evaluation on synthetic benchmarks, we evaluate the practical value of HiBO on a more
413
      complex real-world application, specifically automatic DBMS configuration tuning. The perfor-
      mance of DBMS highly depends on the settings of their configuration knobs, which control vari-
414
      ous aspects of their behaviours such as memory allocation, I/O behaviors, and query optimization.
415
      However, default configurations are typically suboptimal and require further tuning for optimal per-
416   formance (Zhao et al., 2023). We select DBMS configuration tuning as a critical evaluation scenario
417   due to its significant practical importance and inherent complexities, especially for the following
418   considerations:
419
420     • (High-dimensionality) DBMS configuration tuning involves a high-dimensional search space
421
          with over 100 configuration knobs;
422     • (Knob Correlation) Many of these configuration parameters are interdependent, where certain
423
          knobs are correlated and can lead to combined effects on the performance;
424     • (Noisy Observations) Measurements of performance data in real-world environments are much
425       noiser compared to cases in synthetic benchmarks.
426   These factors make manual and heuristic tuning impractical (Huang et al., 2019; Zhao et al., 2023),
427   making DBMS configuration tuning an ideal target for evaluating practical values of algorithms
428   in real-world applications. In comparison, though not limited to simple mathematical synthetic
429   functions, commonly-seen “real-world” benchmarks in literature are still large based on simulation
430   (Jones, 2008; Wang et al., 2017; Eriksson & Jankowiak, 2021). For example, Mopta08 (Jones,
431   2008) originates from real-world problems in vehicle design but is abstracted into a mathematical
      optimization problem in practice. Considering the page limits, we included the evaluation on two

531   7             C ONCLUSION
532   In this paper, we introduce HiBO, a novel Bayesian Optimization algorithm designed to efficiently
533   handle high-dimensional search spaces via a hierarchical integration of global-level space partition-
534   ing and local modeling. HiBO dynamically refines global partitioning information and seamlessly
535   integrates it into local acquisition strategies, thereby accelerating the convergence towards global
536   optima. Our comprehensive evaluation demonstrate its effectiveness and practical value on both
537   synthetic benchmarks and, more importantly, on real-world systems. Additionally, the visual anal-
538   ysis presents a straightforward validation of its effects in navigating on complex optimization land-
539   scapes. More discussions on limitations and scalability of HiBO can be found in Appendix F and G
      respectively.

1057   F     L IMITATIONS
1058
       We summarized the following limitations with HiBO based on reasoning and experiment results:
1059
1060       • Performance Improvment on Dense Benchmarks. One limitation of HiBO lies in its rela-
1061         tively less pronounced improvement over the local optimizer on dense high-dimensional bench-
1062         marks compared to sparse benchmarks. While HiBO demonstrates significant performance
1063         gains on sparse benchmarks, its improvement is less evident on dense tasks where all dimen-
1064
             sions are uniformly effective such as Levy-200D and Rastrigin-200D). Our interpretation is that
             in sparse spaces, HiBO efficiently identifies promising regions to guide the local optimizer and
1065
             it is one of its greatest advantages. While in dense spaces, it requires more samples to achieve
1066
             accurate estimations across the entire space than in sparse scenarios. But HiBO still converges
1067         efficiently towards the optimal point in these tasks and achieves the best in Levy-200D and
1068         Rastrigin-200D.
1069
1070
           • Unobservable Configurations. HiBO may also struggle in scenarios where certain critical con-
             figuration variables are hidden or unobservable during optimization. Such situations can arise
1071
             in practical tasks where parts of the configuration space are inaccessible due to hardware con-
1072
             straints, legacy systems, or incomplete problem specifications. In these cases, the global-level
1073         navigator may build an inaccurate model of the search space, leading to suboptimal partitioning
1074         and sampling.

1120   G.2 S CALABILITY WITH RESPECT TO LARGE - SCALE DATASETS
1121
       Besides the scalability with respect to dimensionality, here we provide clarification on the scalability
1122   with respect to large-scale data samples. In fact, there is no easy answer to such scalability of HiBO
1123   especially because the search tree depth is adaptively controlled and the total computational cost can
1124   vary from different benchmarks. Further, it also depends on the types and specified configurations of
1125   clustering and classification algorithms used for search space partitioning, which can have different
1126   computational complexity. For example, the classifier used in the search space partitioning can be
1127   SVMs based on linear kernels with O(n) complexity or non-linear kernels with O(n2 ) or O(n3 ),
1128   where n is the number of data samples. However, the focus of this paper is the design of the
1129   hierarchical framework and we do not conduct specific analysis on designing specific component
1130
       combination to large scale dataset.
1131   Moreover, HiBO is designed for high-dimensional tasks with limited sample budgets, which aligns
1132   with real-world scenarios where objective evaluations are expensive to be evaluated. The DBMS
1133   configuration tuning serves as a typical example because ideally we expect an important DBMS
       instance can keep running instead of spending time on tuning. Correspondingly, related works in the


                                                                                        21
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1134
       field of DBMS configuration tuning typically control their sample budget to less than 500 iterations
1135   (Zhang et al., 2021; 2022; Cereda et al., 2021; Kanellis et al., 2022). This work even adopts the very
1136   limited sample budget (50 iterations) to form a challenging benchmark, while HiBO can improve
1137   the default performance by more than 2 times within this sample budget. In fact, tuning towards
1138   opaque problems within limited sample budget is the one of core design principles of BO and HiBO
1139   adequately exemplifies this consideration. From this perspective, scalability with respect to large
1140   scale dataset is out of scope of this work.
