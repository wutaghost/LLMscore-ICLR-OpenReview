011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            We present Lumina-mGPT, a family of multimodal autoregressive models capable
014            of various vision and language tasks, particularly excelling in generating flexible
015            photorealistic images from text descriptions. By initializing from multimodal
016            Generative PreTraining (mGPT), Lumina-mGPT demonstrates that decoder-only
017            Autoregressive (AR) model can achieve image generation performance comparable
018            to modern diffusion model with high efficiency through Flexible Progressive Su-
019
               pervised Finetuning (FP-SFT). Equipped with our proposed Unambiguous image
               Representation (Uni-Rep), Lumina-mGPT can flexibly generate high-quality im-
020
               ages of varying aspect ratios. Building on the strong image generation capabilities,
021
               we further explore Ominiponent Supervised Finetuning (Omni-SFT), an initial at-
022            tempt to elevate Lumina-mGPT into a unified multi-modal generalist. The resulting
023            model demonstrates versatile multimodal capabilities, including visual generation
024            tasks like text-to-image/multiview generation and controllable generation, visual
025            recognition tasks like segmentation and depth estimation, and vision-language
026            tasks like multi-turn visual question answering, casting light on the rosy potential
027            of this direction. We release all code and checkpoints, hoping to facilitate the
028            progress toward building artificial general intelligence.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Seminal models, including DALL-E 3 (Betker et al., 2023), Stable Diffusion 3 (Esser et al., 2024),
035   and SoRA (Brooks et al., 2024), have demonstrated superior performance in photorealistic image and
036   video generation using diffusion-based generative modeling over continuous latent image features.
037   In contrast, autoregressive (AR) generative models, which rely on “next-token prediction,” have
038   revolutionized text generation with groundbreaking reasoning abilities, as exemplified by models
039
      like GPT-4 (Achiam et al., 2023) and Gemini (Team et al., 2023), over discrete token representation.
      However, AR-based generative modeling over vector-quantized image features still lags far behind
040
      diffusion-based counterparts in terms of photorealistic and controllable image generation.
041
042   Although previous autoregressive efforts, such as DALL-E (Ramesh et al., 2021), CogView (Ding
043   et al., 2021), Parti (Yu et al., 2022), OFA (Wang et al., 2022), Unified-IO (Lu et al., 2022; 2024a),
044   LlamaGen (Sun et al., 2024), and Chameleon (Team, 2024a), have extensively explored generative
045
      modeling over vector-quantized image features following the paradigm of large language models
      (LLMs), their results on text-to-image generation have either been unsatisfactory or produced high-
046
      quality samples limited to academic benchmarks like ImageNet (Deng et al., 2009). We summarize the
047
      limitations of previous approaches: (1) AR-based image generation models often rely on randomly-
048   initialized transformers, resulting in poor image quality and slow convergence due to the lack
049   of pretrained representations; (2) the verbose encoder-decoder architectures used complicate the
050   framework and hinder scalability and generalization to other tasks; (3) these models are limited in
051   decoding resolution and flexibility, often producing low-quality images with fixed resolutions; (4)
052   they lack task extensibility, restricting their applicability to a narrow range of scenarios without
053   integration with other tasks such as dense labeling and controllable image generation. More detailed
      discussion of these challenges can be found in Appendix C.

125   To address the aforementioned challenges, we present Lumina-mGPT, a decoder-only transformer
126   initiated with effective multimodal Generative PreTraining (mGPT) and then supervised-finetuned
127   over flexible, high-quality, high-resolution discrete image tokens in a progressive manner. This
128   framework illuminates flexible high-resolution photorealistic image generation and can be easily
129
      extended to solve various component tasks in a unified manner. We provide a detailed comparison
      of the architecture design choices and model capabilities of existing multimodal autoregressive
130
      approaches in Table 1. The key features of Lumina-mGPT are outlined below:
131
132   ➀ Effective Multimodal Generative Pretraining: Unlike commonly adopted approaches that utilize
133   a randomly-initialized causal transformer to generate discrete image tokens in an autoregressive
134   manner, Lumina-mGPT is initialized from an effective multimodal Generative PretTraining (mGPT)
135
      representation, which is a multimodal autoregressive transformer trained at large scale using a
      simple “next-token prediction” loss. We find that by leveraging the rich knowledge from large-scale
136
      multimodal pretraining, mGPT can learn broad and general multimodal representation. This serves as
137
      an excellent starting point, remarkably simplifying the task of text-to-image generation, accelerating
138   training convergence on downstream tasks, and unifying various vision-language tasks. Due to
139   the lack of training resources, this mGPT representation is directly adopted from the pretrained
140   Chameleon 7B and 30B models released by Meta (Team, 2024a).
141
      ➁ Simple Decoder-Only Architecture: Lumina-mGPT adopts the simple decoder-only architecture.
142
      Compared to more complex designs like encoder-decoder architectures, decoder-only models have
143
      a significant innate advantage: they provide a simple, elegant, and extensible framework to unify
144   various understanding and generation tasks across different modalities. This makes them a promising
145   candidate for achieving true unification. Besides, by sharing the same architecture as the flourishing
146   text-only LLMs, Lumina-mGPT can benefit from well-established theories and infrastructures in
147   the LLM community, such as scaling properties (Chowdhery et al., 2022; Brown et al., 2020) and
148   techniques to optimize the training and inference processes (Dao et al., 2022; Dao, 2024; Kwon et al.,
149   2023). We train a family of Lumina-mGPTs with up to 30 billion parameters to provide a flexible
150   trade-off between efficiency and performance and to explore the limit of its multimodal capabilities
151   as model parameters scale.
152   ➂ Flexible High-quality Image Generation: Despite the aforementioned strengths of decoder-only
153   architecture, the image generation capabilities of such models remain limited, creating a gap between
154   the potential and the reality of this architecture. We thus propose Flexible Progressive Supervised
155   Finetuning (FP-SFT) to fully fulfill the potential of high-quality text-to-image generation. This
156   approach starts with low-resolution discrete tokens and progressively transitions to high-resolution
157   discrete tokens. Combined with Unambiguous image Representation (Uni-Rep), this weak-to-strong
158
      SFT strategy effectively grants the model with the ability to generate high-quality photorealistic
      images with flexible aspect ratios.
159
160   ➃ Omnipotent Task Unification: The high-quality image generation capabilities achieved during
161   the FP-SFT stage provide the prerequisites for further exploring the fundamental advantages of
      the decoder-only architecture. We thus propose Omnipotent Supervised Finetuning (Omni-SFT), a


                                                          3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      preliminary attempt to create an omnipotent generalist. Specifically, Omni-SFT treats various tasks–
163   such as multi-turn dialog, visual multi-turn understanding, dense labeling, text-to-image generation,
164   text-to-multiview generation, image editing, and spatial-conditional image generation–as a unified
165   discrete modeling task, allowing the model to achieve omnipotent task unification via a natural
166   language interface.
167
      We summarize our contributions as follows: (1) We are the first (especially in the open-source
168
      domain) to demonstrate that a decoder-only AR model can achieve image generation performance
169   comparable to modern diffusion models. Furthermore, by initializing from mGPT, the aforementioned
170   capability can be achieved at low computational cost (32 A100 GPUs × 7 days for 7B model). (2)
171   We propose UniRep, an image representation that empowers decoder-only AR models with the ability
172   to flexibly generate images of varying aspect ratios. (3) Building on the strong image generation
173   capabilities, we further explore Omni-SFT, an initial attempt to elevate the model into a unified
174   generalist. Experiments underscore the promising potential of this direction. (4) We open-source the
175   entire pipeline to encourage the community’s further exploration of this topic.

177   2     M ETHODOLGY
178
179   Lumina-mGPT is a decoder-only transformer initialized with multimodal Generative PreTraining
180   (mGPT) and finetuned over high-quality multimodal tokens derived from various tasks. Based on the
181   robust mGPT representation and our proposed supervised finetuning strategies with unambiguous
182   image representation, Lumina-mGPT achieves superior performance in photorealistic image genera-
183   tion and omnipotent task unification with high flexibility in image resolution and aspect ratio. In this
184   section, we first introduce mGPT, followed by the training and inference details of Lumina-mGPT.
185
186   2.1   R EVISITING M GPT WITH C HAMELEON
187
188   mGPT represents the family of models utilizing a decoder-only transformer architecture, pretrained
189
      on extensive multimodal token sequences. These models exhibit exceptional native multimodal
      understanding, generation, and reasoning capabilities, offering the potential for universal modeling
190
      across various modalities and tasks. We use the recent open-source model, Chameleon (Team, 2024a),
191
      as an example to illustrate the design choices and implementing details of mGPT.
192
193
      Multimodal Tokenization To unify text and images into a multimodal token sequence, it is
194   essential first to tokenize both text and image into discrete space. Especially for images, the choice of
195   tokenizer is crucial as it determines the upper limit of generation quality. Specifically, Chameleon
196   trains a new byte pair encoding tokenizer for text. For images, it adopts the quantization-based
197   tokenization method following prior works (Esser et al., 2021; Yu et al., 2022; Ramesh et al., 2021),
198   converting continuous image patches into discrete tokens from a fixed codebook while reducing spatial
199   dimensions. The quantized image tokens are then flattened into a 1D sequence and concatenated with
200   text tokens in various ways to form a multimodal token sequence for unified modeling.
201
202   Decoder-Only Transformer Unlike Unified-IO and Parti using an encoder-decoder architecture
203   with pretrained encoders, mGPT trains a decoder-only autoregressive transformer from scratch after
204
      transforming text and image inputs into a unified sequence of discrete tokens x = (x1 , x2 , ..., xT ),
      leading to a simpler and more unified approach for multimodal generative modeling. mGPT adopts a
205
      standard dense transformer architecture for scalability and generalizability, with minor adaptations
206
      like RoPE (Su et al., 2024) and SwiGLU (Shazeer, 2020) activation function, following the LLaMA
207   series (Touvron et al., 2023). However, this standard transformer architecture exhibits training
208   instability when scaling up the model size and context length of multimodal token sequences, as
209   observed in existing works (Team, 2024a; Zhuo et al., 2024). It has been found that this instability
210   is caused by the uncontrollable growth of network activations in transformer blocks. Therefore,
211   Pre-Norm, Post-Norm, and QK-Norm (Henry et al., 2020) are added to each transformer block to
212   preserve the magnitude of intermediate activation and further stabilize the training process.
213
214   Training Objective During training, mGPT models the conditional probability p(xt |x1 , ..., xt−1 )
215   of multimodal sequences using the standard next-token prediction objective. Additionally, Chameleon
      applies z-loss (Chowdhery et al., 2022) to stabilize the training of 7B and 30B models. Initially, we

242   Limitations of Chameleon Although Chameleon demonstrates potential for joint image and text

249   as segmentation and depth prediction, as well as creative visual generation tasks like controllable
250
      generation and image editing. Lumina-mGPT is built upon Chameleon to unlock its full potential for

      2.2      L UMINA - M GPT
254
      2.2.1        E FFECTIVE I NITLIZATION
255
256   Large-scale pre-training and scalable model architecture have been widely verified as the golden
257   path to advanced intelligence. As mGPTs like Chameleon are pretrained on large-scale image-text
258   interleaved datasets and have developed effective and generalizable representations for both image
259   and text, they can better serve as the starting point for flexible photorealistic image generation and
260   beyond than random initialization or language-only models. Furthermore, the LLaMA (Touvron et al.,
261
      2023) architecture integrated with features like QK-Norm has demonstrated strength and scalability
      through extensive validation (Touvron et al., 2023; Meta, 2024; Sun et al., 2024; Gao et al., 2024). By
262
      initializing from the Chameleon mGPT, which adheres to the LLaMA architecture, we can leverage
263
      these architectural advantages. Consequently, initializing from mGPTs allowed us to efficiently train
264   high-performing Lumina-mGPT models, with parameters ranging from 7B to 30B, using just 10M
265   high-quality image-text data points.
266
267   2.2.2        S UPERVISED F INETUNING FOR L UMINA - M GPT
268
269   Unambiguous Image Representation Existing methods represent images as 1D flattened se-
      quences of 2D discrete image codes. While adequate for fixed resolutions, this approach becomes


                                                                                              5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      ambiguous when supporting variable resolutions, as with Lumina-mGPT. For instance, images with
271   resolutions of 512 × 512, 256 × 1024, and 1024 × 256 can all be encoded into the same number of
272   tokens, making it impossible to infer the original shape without examining the token contents. This
273   ambiguity poses significant challenges for both image perception and generation.
274
      To address this problem, we propose Unambiguous image Representation (Uni-Rep), which augments
275
      the image representations by adding extra height/width indicator tokens immediately after the
276
      <start-of-image> token and inserting <end-of-line> tokens after image tokens belonging
277   to the same row. As shown in Figure 2, this modification ensures that the original shape of the images
278   can be accurately parsed from the 1D representation without additional context or delving into the
279   contents of the image tokens. This enhancement provides the foundation for Lumina-mGPT’s ability
280   to perform image-related tasks at any resolution and aspect ratio.
281
      Note that while either the height/width indicators or the <end-of-line> tokens can independently
282
      achieve disambiguation, we still use both simultaneously because they have distinct benefits. When
283   generating images, the height/width indicators, generated before any image tokens, pre-determine
284   the shape of the image, aiding Lumina-mGPT in composing the image contents. On the other hand,
285   the <end-of-line> tokens can serve as anchors, offering the 1D tokens sequence with additional
286   explicit spatial information. We detailed in Section 3.3 to illustrate the role of these indicators.
287
288
289   Flexible Progressive Supervised Finetuning (FP-SFT) The FP-SFT process equips the pretrained
290   mGPT with the capability to generate high-resolution images with flexible aspect ratios in a pro-
291   gressive manner. The process is divided into three stages, where the product of width and height
292   approximates 5122 , 7682 , and 10242 , respectively. In each stage, a set of candidate resolutions with
293
      similar areas but different height-width ratios are prepared, and each image is matched to the most
      suitable resolution. In the low-resolution stage, shorter sequence lengths and the resulting high
294
      training throughput allow the model to quickly traverse a large amount of data, learning the general
295
      composition of images and a broad spectrum of visual concepts. Conversely, in the high-resolution
296   stage, the model is expected to focus on learning high-frequency fine-grained details unique to
297   high-resolution images. Benefiting from the strong foundation built during the high-throughput
298   pretraining and low-resolution finetuning stages, the low-throughput high-resolution finetuning stage
299   is data-efficient, thereby enhancing the overall efficiency of the FP-SFT process.
300
      A meticulously curated dataset of high-resolution photorealistic image-text pairs is used for FP-SFT.
301
      Moreover, the pure-text data from OpenHermess (Teknium, 2023) and the image-to-text data from
302   Mini-Gemini (Li et al., 2024b) are also incorporated during training to prevent catastrophic forgetting.
303   To provide users with a natural way to specify the desired resolution of generated images, we
304   developed the resolution-aware prompt (Figure 2). For each image and its corresponding description,
305   the prompt is structured as follows:
306
       Generate an image of {width}x{height} according to the following
307
      prompt: \n {description}
308
309
310
      Omnipotent Supervised Finetuning (Omni-SFT) While flexible photorealistic image generation
311   is the primary target of Lumina-mGPT, we find that the resulting model after FP-SFT can be efficiently
312   transferred to a wide spectrum of image understanding and generation tasks. We thus present Omni-
313   SFT, a preliminary exploration toward boosting Lumina-mGPT to a visual generalist. Training tasks
314   and data for Omni-SFT consists of the following:
315
      1. Single- and multi-turn language-guided image-editing with data from MagicBrush (Zhang et al.,
316
      2024) and SEED (Ge et al., 2024) (only involving the real-world and multi-turn subsets).
317
318   2. Dense prediction tasks, including surface norm estimation from NYUv2 Silberman et al. (2012)
319   and ScanNet Dai et al. (2017), depth estimation from Kitti v2 Cabon et al. (2020) and Sintel Butler
320
      et al. (2012), pose estimation from MSCOCO Lin et al. (2014), semantic segmentation data annotated
      with OneFormer (Jain et al., 2023) on image from Laion (Schuhmann et al., 2022), and grounding
321
      data from RefCOCO (Kazemzadeh et al., 2014).
322
323   3. In-house spatial-conditional image generation following ControlNet (Zhang et al., 2023), with
      conditions including surface norm, depth, pose, and segmentation.

      3.1   F UNDAMENTAL P HOTOREALISTIC T EXT- TO -I MAGE G ENERATION
407
408   We first demonstrate the fundamental text-to-image generation capabilities of Lumina-mGPT with
409   FP-SFT. As shown in Figure 13, Lumina-mGPT can generate photorealistic images in a variety
410   of resolutions, achieving the first native 1K autoregressive generation without cascaded models, a
411   common technique in text-to-mage generation (Yu et al., 2022; Saharia et al., 2022; Chang et al.,
412   2023; Pernias et al., 2024). These generated images exhibit strong semantic coherence with intricate
413   visual details, despite being finetuned on limited computational resources and text-image pairs.
414
415   Qualitative Comparison with SoTA AR-based Approaches We compare the text-to-image
416   synthesis ability of Lumina-mGPT with LlamaGen (Sun et al., 2024) and Parti (Yu et al., 2022).
417   LlamaGen beats state-of-the-art diffusion models on ImageNet FID score. Compared with LlamaGen,
418   Lumina-mGPT can achieve better visual quality as shown in Figure 3 in text-to-image generation.
419
      Note that Lumina-mGPT only requires 10M image-text pairs while LlamaGen is trained over 50M

      3.3       ATTENTION V ISUALIZATION
516
517
      To better understand the sampling behavior of Lumina-mGPT, we visualize the average attention
518   logits of the last image token during text-to-image generation, as shown in Figure 5. The results
519   reveal that the attention score decreases for distant tokens, indicating that the model pays more
520   attention to local tokens compared to distant ones. This behavior aligns with the long-term decay
521   property designed in RoPE. Besides, we observe a similar pattern known as “attention sinks” in
522   LLMs (Xiao et al., 2024), where a large proportion of attention score is allocated to a small number
523   of tokens. After looking into details, these sink tokens are identified to be the indicators, including the
524   <start-of-image> and <end-of-line> tokens. Notably, the attention scores of text tokens
525   are significantly smaller than those of the <start-of-image> token. This suggests that most of
526
      the semantic information in text tokens may have been encapsulated into the <start-of-image>
      token. These intriguing findings indicate that Lumina-mGPT aggregates more information from these
527
      sink tokens, demonstrating the effectiveness of these indicators in our proposed Uni-Rep and opening
528
      up the potential for accelerating the sampling process with these sink tokens.

      In this work, we introduce Lumina-mGPT, a decoder-only transformer that can produce diverse, photo-
534   realistic images at any resolution from text prompts. Instead of random initialization, Lumina-mGPT
535   features initializing from an autoregressive transformer with multimodal Generative PreTraining
536   (mGPT). Leveraging the general multimodal representation learned from massive interleaved data,
537   we design two efficient finetuning strategies named FP-SFT and Omni-SFT to unleash the potential

      Multimodal Large Language Models Recent large language models (LLMs) (Chowdhery et al.,
867
      2022; Touvron et al., 2023; Brown et al., 2020; Achiam et al., 2023; Team et al., 2023) demonstrate
868
      strong instruction-following and reasoning capabilities, coupled with extensive world knowledge. To
869   extend LLMs’ expertise from the text domain to multimodal domains such as images and videos,
870   prior works (Liu et al., 2023a; Lin et al., 2023a; Maaz et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2023b) have aligned
871   pretrained encoders from various modalities with LLMs by curating multimodal instruction tuning
872   datasets. Although these multimodal large language models (MLLMs) have exhibited powerful visual
873   understanding capabilities, their multimodal functionality is primarily limited to perceiving the visual
874   world, exemplified by tasks such as visual question answering and image captioning, rather than
875   generating visual outputs. Another line of research Koh et al. (2024); Dong et al. (2024); Sun et al.
876   (2023); Wu et al. has proposed augmenting MLLMs with the ability to generate images, videos, and
877
      audio from text instructions. These approaches introduce additional visual tokens for generation and
      align these generative tokens as conditional information with a pretrained generator, such as Stable
878
      Diffusion (Esser et al., 2024; Podell et al., 2023) for text-to-image generation. Consequently, the
879
      generation capabilities heavily rely on the external expert generator rather than MLLMs themselves,
880   resulting in inconsistent and inferior generation results. To combine the strength of both approaches,
881   our model aims to learn both understanding and generation of images using an MLLM with native
882   multimodal capabilities, drawing inspiration from Chameleon (Team, 2024a), a mixed-modal early-
883   fusion foundation model.
884
885
      Text-to-Image Generation The task of text-to-image generation seeks to synthesize photorealistic
886   and diverse images based on textual descriptions. Nowadays, diffusion models, whether in pixel
887   space (Saharia et al., 2022) or in latent space (Podell et al., 2023; Esser et al., 2024; Pernias
888   et al., 2024), have become the de-facto approaches due to their superior performance, particularly in
889   producing extremely high-aesthetic images. Among these models, the recent trend of scaling diffusion
890   transformers (DiTs) (Chen et al., 2024b;a; Gao et al., 2024; Esser et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024c; Team,
891   2024b) suggests a unified architecture for both text and image modeling. However, existing DiTs still
892   leverage separate language models, such as CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) or T5 (Raffel et al., 2020), as
893   text encoders. This modality gap between text and image representations not only leads to inaccurate
894
      generation but also hinders the development of a unified multimodal foundational generative model.
      Compared to the dominance of diffusion models, the progress of autoregressive image generation has
895
      received less attention in recent years. Early works (Ramesh et al., 2021; Ding et al., 2021) proposed
896
      a two-stage generation approach: first, training a VQ-VAE Van Den Oord et al. (2017); Esser et al.
897   (2021) for image tokenization and de-tokenization, and then using an autoregressive transformer to
898   model discrete image token sequences, akin to language modeling. Parti (Yu et al., 2022) scaled up
899   the autoregressive transformer to 20 billion parameters, demonstrating promising high-fidelity image
900   generation results. LlamaGen (Sun et al., 2024) further improved the image tokenizer and integrated
901   advanced techniques in LLMs, bridging the performance gap with diffusion counterparts. Unlike
902   Parti and LlamaGen, Lumina-mGPT proposes multimodal generative pertaining on unified text-image
903   sequences, followed by supervised finetuning on high-quality text-to-image pairs, achieving flexible
904   high-aesthetic image generation with autoregressive models.

1048
1049   In autoregressive models, various configuration parameters during Lumina-mGPT’s decoding stage significantly
1050   affect sample quality (Holtzman et al., 2020; Radford et al., 2018; 2019). Hyperparameters such as temperature
1051   (T), top-k, and classifier-free guidance scale (CFG) have not been extensively investigated in the visual domain.
1052   In this section, we explore how these hyperparameters influence the generated image in terms of quality, texture,
       and style.
1053
1054
       Different Decoding Hyperparameters for Image and Text The sampling strategy of autoregressive
1055   models involves numerous hyperparameters that significantly influence the sampling results. We find that the
1056   optimal decoding hyperparameters differ greatly between text decoding and discrete image code decoding. For
1057   example, the top-k=5 setting performs well in generating text. However, when generating images, the value of
1058   top-k should be much larger (e.g. 2000) to avoid repetitive and meaningless patterns. Therefore, we implement
       a status-aware control mechanism for inference. Specifically, a set of default hyperparameters is used for text
1059
       decoding; once a <start-of-image> token is generated, the hyperparameters switch to those optimized for
1060   image generation. After the <end-of-image> token is generated, the parameters revert to the initial settings.
1061
1062   Classfier-Free Guidance Classifier-Free Guidance (CFG) (Ho & Salimans, 2022) is originally proposed to
1063   enhance the quality and text alignment of generated samples in text-to-image diffusion models. We incorporate
1064
       this technique into autoregressive models during inference. When generating an image token, the CFG-processed
       logits lcfg are formulated as lcfg = l + s(l − l′ ), where l represents the original logits conditioned on the complete
1065   context; l′ represents the context-independent logits, which are conditioned solely on the tokens following
1066   the <start-of-image> token of the currently generating image, and are independent of any prior context;
1067   s denotes the guidance scale of Classifier-Free Guidance. To make CFG work, during training, the context
1068   before <start-of-image> is randomly dropped by a probability of 10%. In practice, KV cache can be used for
1069
       accelerating the computation of both l and l′ . As shown in Fig. 8, similar to the trend of diffusion models,
       increasing CFG initially raises the quality and stability of generation, but increasing it further would make the
1070   quality deteriorate.
1071
1072   Influence of Temperature To evaluate the effect of these decoding parameters, we first set a standard
1073   decoding configuration: T=1.0, top-k=2000, CFG=4.0, which serves as a good-to-use setting. From this baseline,
1074   we gradually shift T from 0.7 to 1.0 to generate corresponding images at different temperatures. As shown
1075
       in Figure 9, it is evident that when setting the temperature low, visual details diminish and objects tend to be
       over-smoothed. Conversely, when setting the temperature high, the generated images contain rich visual content
1076   but are prone to contain more artifacts.

1079   where 8192 is equal to the size of the VQ-VAE codebook used usedin Lumina-mGPT. The results, visualized in
       Figure 9, indicate a similar trend with increasing temperature. When top-k is low, the image content and texture

       F                      C OMPARISON WITH D IFFUSION - BASED A PPRAOCHES
1158
1159   For a long period of time, diffusion models have dominated the field of text-to-image generation compared
1160
       to autoregressive models. Although LlamaGen claims to beat diffusion models, their results are limited to
       the ImageNet benchmark and there has been no direct comparison between these two architectures so far. In
1161   this section, we aim to provide a detailed comparison of autoregressive and diffusion-based methods trained
1162   on these same text-image datasets, focusing on image quality, diversity, text-rendering, and multilingual
1163   capabilities. Specifically, we adopt Lumina-mGPT and Lumina-Next-SFT (Zhuo et al., 2024) as representatives
1164   of autoregressive and diffusion-based methods, respectively. A direct visual comparison between Lumina-Next-
1165
       SFT and Lumina-mGPT reveals both the similarities and differences between autoregressive and diffusion-based
       generative modeling approaches.
1166
1167   On the Similarity between Diffusion- and AR-based Generation Given the same set of text prompts, both
1168
       diffusion- and AR-based approaches generate photorealistic images with similar aesthetic style and fine-grained
       details, illustrated in Figure 10. This reveals the fact that both architectures can achieve satisfactory text-to-image
1169   generation performance when provided with the same training data, training budget, and comparable model sizes.
1170   The AR-based methods display remarkable visual aesthetics on par with their diffusion counterparts, challenging
1171   the notion that diffusion models are more effective and promising architecture in generative modeling. This
1172   finding also aligns with the platonic representation hypothesis (Huh et al., 2024) that neural networks are
1173
       converging to learn a shared representation space despite being trained with different architectures and objectives.
       Therefore, this hypothesis highlights the importance of collecting more high-quality data and optimizing training
1174   infrastructure as directions for data and model scaling, to boost the overall model performance that is agnostic to
1175   any specific architecture.
1176
       On the Differences between Diffusion- and AR-based Generation As shown in Figure 10, Lumina-mGPT
1177   exhibits more diversity using different random seeds, while Lumina-Next-SFT generates similar images with
1178   identical layouts and textures. This can be partly attributed to the use of high temperature and top-k values
1179   in Lumina-mGPT. However, excessive diversity also causes our model to be less stable and more prone to

1375                     A bat and a ball together cost $1.10. The bat costs $1.00 more than
                           the ball. How much does the ball cost? Let's think step by step.
1376
1377
                         Step 1: Identify the given information
1378                     We know that the total cost of the bat and the ball combined is $1.10. We also know that the bat costs $1.00
                         more than the ball.
1379                     Step 2: Create an equation
                         Let's denote the cost of the ball as 'x' (since it's the unknown variable). Then, the cost of the bat is 'x + $1.00'
1380                     (since it's $1.00 more than the ball).
                         Step 3: Use the given information to form an equation
1381                     Using the given information, we can create an equation:
                         (x + $1.00) + x = $1.10
1382                     Step 4: Simplify and solve for the unknown variable 'x'
                         Combining the terms with 'x', we get:
1383                     2x + $1.00 = $1.10
                         2x + $1.00 - $1.00 = $1.10 - $1.00
1384                     2x = $0.10
                         Step 5: Solve for the unknown variable 'x'
1385                     To find the cost of the ball (x), we need to divide both sides of the equation by 2:
                         x = $0.10 / 2
1386                     x = $0.05
1387                     Step 6: State the conclusion
                         The ball costs $0.05, which is 5 cents.
