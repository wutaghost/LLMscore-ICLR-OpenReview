009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) have shown remarkable performance in various
012            scientific domains, but their lack of interpretability limits their applicability in
013            critical decision-making processes. Recently, intrinsic interpretable GNNs have
014            been studied to provide insights into model predictions by identifying rationale
015            substructures in graphs. However, existing methods face challenges when the un-
016
               derlying rationale subgraphs are complicated and variable. To address this chal-
               lenge, we propose T OP I N G, a novel topological framework to interpretable GNNs
017
               that leverages persistent homology to identify persistent rationale subgraphs. Our
018
               method introduces a rationale filtration learning technique that models the gen-
019            erating procedure of rationale subgraphs, and enforces the persistence of topo-
020            logical gap between rationale subgraphs and complement random graphs by a
021            novel self-adjusted topological constraint, topological discrepancy. We show that
022            our topological discrepancy is a lower bound of a Wasserstein distance on graph
023            distributions with Gromov-Hausdorff metric. We provide theoretical guarantees
024            showing that our loss is uniquely optimized by the ground truth under certain con-
025            ditions. Through extensive experiments on varaious synthetic and real datasets,
026            we demonstrate that T OP I N G effectively addresses key challenges in interpretable
027
               GNNs including handling variiform rationale subgraphs, balancing performance
               with interpretability, and avoiding spurious correlations. Experimental results
028
               show that our approach improves state-of-the-art methods up to 20%+ on both
029
               predictive accuracy and interpretation quality. Our code is available through the
030            link: https://anonymous.4open.science/r/TopoEx-1EE2/

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) have emerged as powerful tools for learning graph-structured data,
036   in various scientific domains, such as chemistry, biology, physics, and materials science, achieving
037   remarkable success in applications of predicting molecular properties (Kamberaj, 2022; Chen et al.,
038   2023), modeling protein-protein interactions (Görmez et al., 2021; Ravichandran et al., 2024; Li
039
      et al., 2023), analyzing phase transitions (Qu et al., 2022), characterizing material characteristics (Hu
      & Latypov, 2024; Sheriff et al., 2024; Gurniak et al., 2024; Xiao et al., 2024), etc. As GNNs are
040
      increasingly applied to critical scientific and decision-making tasks, there is a growing need for
041
      interpretability and explainability in these models (Zhang et al., 2024a). Scientists and practitioners
042   often ask for not only accurate predictions, but also insights into why and how these predictions
043   are made. This is particularly crucial in scientific applications where understanding the underlying
044   mechanisms and causal relationships is as important as the predictions themselves.
045
      A recent trend in GNN research focuses on enhancing interpretability by developing methods that
046
      identify and visualize the nodes, edges, subgraphs, or features most influential or causal for a
047
      given prediction. Existing approaches to GNN interpretation can be broadly categorized into two
048   classes (Zhang et al., 2024a): post-hoc explainer methods (Ying et al., 2019a; Luo et al., 2020a;
049   Schlichtkrull et al., 2021; Wu et al., 2023; Bui et al., 2024) and intrinsically interpretable mod-
050   els (Wu et al., 2022; Miao et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2024). Post-hoc Explainer methods analyze a
051   pre-trained GNN model to generate intuitive explanations after the fact. It enjoys flexibility and can
052   be integrated into different kinds of models. However, recent research (Miao et al., 2022) shows that
053   post-hoc methods might provide explanations that are suboptimal or inconsistent with the model’s
      actual decision-making processes. On the other hand, intrinsically interpretable models incorporate

054
      interpretability directly into the model architecture and training process. The fundamental idea of
055   intrinsical interpretability stems from the concept of graph attention (Veličković et al., 2018). As
056   attention weights may not always correlate with actual feature importance, a Naı̈ve application of
057   attenion weights is not reliable for real graph data (Ying et al., 2019a; Yu et al., 2020). Moreover,
058   the potential trade-off between interpretability and predictive performance (Du et al., 2019) may not
059   be acceptable in real-world applications. Therefore, various methods have been developed regard-
060   ing how to use attention weights for interpretation. Miao et al. (2022) proposed stochastic attention
061   mechanism (GSAT) to use the graph information bottleneck (Wu et al., 2020; Tishby et al., 1999) as
062   target function, employ attention weights to control the information bottleneck, and sample rationale
063
      subgraphs using Gumbel-softmax reparameterization, to achieve strong performance in both predic-
      tion and interpretation. Similarly, Chen et al. (2024) approached interpretation by searching for
064
      rationale subgraphs within the framework of subgraph multilinear extension (SubMT) and propos-
065
      ing a graph multilinear net (GMT) for better SubMT approximation. Wu et al. (2022) proposed
066   Discovering Invariant Rationales (DIR), applying interventions on training distributions to obtain
067   invariant causal rationales while filtering out spurious correlations.
068
069
      Despite these advancements, existing interpretable models often assume either explicitly or im-
      plicitly that the subgraph rationales are nearly invariant across different instances within the same
070
      category of graphs, even a strong one-to-one correspondence between subgraph rationales and pre-
071
      dictions. However, this is overly restrictive and unrealistic in many real-world scenarios, where the
072   graph dataset and the downstream tasks might be complicated with varriform subgraph rationales,
073   which can vary significantly in form, size, and topology, even among graphs belonging to the same
074   category. For example, in molecular biology, molecules with the same bioactivity can have different
075   functional groups responsible for that activity Patani & LaVoie (1996); Brown (2012). An aromatic
076   ring, a sulfonamide group, or a heterocyclic compound can each be the key substructure leading to
077   the same pharmacological effect in different molecules. Another example can be drawn from social
078   networks. In the scenario of identifying influential users, the structural reasons for the influence
079   vary significantly. An influential user might have high degree centrality, being directly connected to
080
      many other users, or they might act as bridge nodes connecting different communities. Our obser-
      vations, supported by experiments on a synthetic dataset we created (see Figure 3 for the results and
081
      Appendix C for the dataset construction), also show that existing intrinsically interpretable models
082
      struggle with such variability. Models obtained under these assumptions may fail to accurately cap-
083   ture the true causal mechanisms underlying the predictions, resulting in unreliable interpretations
084   and bad generalization performance.
085
086
      To address the above challenges, we propose Topologically Interpretable Graph Learning
      (T OP I N G), a novel topological approach to intrinsically interpretable GNNs that leverages tech-
087
      niques from topological data analysis to identify stable and persistent rationale subgraphs, effec-
088
      tively handling the variability in subgraph structures. Our method is inspired by the concept of
089   persistent homology, originating from algebraic topology and recently applied to data analysis and
090   machine learning Wong & Vong (2021); Yan et al. (2021; 2022a); Zhao et al. (2020); Immonen et al.
091   (2023); Ye et al. (2023). Persistent homology studies the dynamics of topological invariants over
092   various scales through a filtration process, allowing us to capture all the changes and persistence of
093   topological features in the data.
094
      Based on this foundation, we introduce a new perspective on the rationale subgraph identification
095   problem. We model the graph attention mechanism as an underlying graph generation process,
096   which ideally constructs the rationale subgraph first, followed by the addition of auxiliary struc-
097   tures. We use persistent homology tools to capture and track the representations and life cycles of
098   topological features during the generating process. To effectively distinguish the rationale subgraph
099   from the complement subgraph, we optimize the parameterized generation procedure to enhance the
100   stability of the rationale subgraph. Specifically, our goal is to amplify the topological differences
101   between the rationale subgraph and the complement subgraph, creating a persistent gap in their
102   topological features throughout the generation process. To achieve this goal, we propose a novel
103
      self-adjusting topological constraint, topological discrepancy, which measures the statistical differ-
      ence between two graphs with respect to their topological structures. The topological discrepancy
104
      serves as a metric to quantify how well the rationale subgraph is preserved and distinguished from
105
      the complement subgraph during the filtration process. We also provide a tractable approximation of
106   our topological discrepancy and provide theoretical guarantees that our models are able to achieve
107   ground truth as the unique optimal solution under our loss function.

      Our main contributions of the paper can be briefly summarized as follows:
109
110          • We introduce T OP I N G, a novel intrinsically interpretable GNN framework that incorpo-
111            rates topological data analysis to identify stable rational subgraphs via persistent rationale
112            filtration learning. We propose a new loss function, topological descrepency, to measure
113            the statistical difference between two graphs with respect to their topological structures.
114          • We provide a tractable approximation of our topological discrepancy and provide theo-
115            retical guarantees that our models are able to achieve ground truth as the unique optimal
116            solution under our loss function. This establishes a solid theoretical foundation for the
117            effectiveness of our approach.
118          • We empirically demonstrate that T OP I N G improves existing methods in both prediction
119            and interpretation tasks on multiple benchmark datasets, up to 20%+ on both interpretation
120            and prediction performance. Additionally, we created a synthetic dataset with variiform
121            rationale subgraphs to specifically target challenges faced by previous methods. Our results
122            show that T OP I N G effectively handles such variability, confirming its ability to address this
123            critical challenge.

140   2.2   I NTRINSICALLY I NTERPRETABLE G RAPH L EARNING
141
      Intrinsically interpretable graph learning aims to build a model simultaneously targeting for both per-
142
      formance and interpretability during the training procedure. Formally, given a collection of labeled
143   graphs (G, Y ) = {(G, yG )}, assume each graph G is composed with two edge disjoint subgraphs
144   G = GX ⊔ Gϵ with vertex correspondence for some GX ∈ GX and Gϵ ∈ Gϵ . GX and Gϵ are two
145   families of graphs. GX is usually a small finite set. Given a graph G, GX is the rationale subgraph
146   in G that almost determines the label yG ≈ h∗ (GX ) up to some random noise, for some unknown
147   oracle h∗ : G → [0, 1]. Gϵ is the noise or less relevant part of in the graph. Both GX and Gϵ are
148   unknown and they have to be learned from the data. The goal is to predict the label ŷG for each
149   graphs G and simultaneously identify its rationale subgraphs GX .

151   2.3   T OPOLOGICAL DATA A NALYSIS (TDA)
152
153
      Recent year, TDA has found its applications in various areas such as machine learning, artificial
      intelligence, data science, neuroscience, an so on (Giunti et al., 2022). Especially in the area of
154
      graph representation learning, TDA has shown the power of enhancing popular GNNs on different
155
      tasks by augumenting potentially useful topological features represented by TDA methods Hofer
156   et al. (2017); Dehmamy et al. (2019); Carrière et al. (2020); Horn et al. (2022). One successful
157   tool is persistent homology. On graphs, the persistent homology is mainly determined by a graph
158   filtration which is usually induced by some edge filtration function. With the spirit of machine
159   learning, it is natural to consider learning the edge filtration function from data to search for an
160   optimal filtration for downstream tasks. Along this approach, various models have been proposed
161   for the graph filtration learning (Carrière et al., 2020; Horn et al., 2022; Hofer et al., 2020; Xin et al.,
      2023; Yan et al., 2022a; Zhao et al., 2020; Carrière & Blumberg, 2020; Zhang et al., 2024b). Just

      In the following context, for a given G, we denote the oracle rationale subgraph and its complement
236
      as G∗X and G∗ϵ . Use GX and Gϵ to represent a candidate rationale subgraph predicted by our model.
237
238   In contrast to existing methods, our approach reconsiders the problem from a more global perspec-
239   tive through the lens of topology. If the graph classification/prediction task indeed can be captured
240   by a rationale subgraph as a core structure in a relatively small family GX , then the graph G can be
241
      considered as ‘growing’ from the core with additional auxiliary structures attached to the core. Nev-
      ertheless looking for this core rationale substructure is highly non-trivial, as this requires maintaining
242
      consistency across the growing procedure (i.e., not losing edges in the middle) and identifying com-
243
      mon subgraphs across many instances in the data input. We propose to learn a filtration function that
244   captures the importance of edges in the graph generating process, allowing us to identify stable and
245   persistent substructures that are most relevant for predictions. This approach aims to leverage the
246   power of topological data analysis to improve the interpretability and generalization of GNNs while
247   maintaining high predictive performance.
248
      Based on our assumption, we consider a generating process of G = (V, E) by first generating the
249
      most important part which corresponds to the candidate rationale subgraph GX , and then combined
250   with some noisy graph Gϵ as complement to get the final graph G. Following this idea, for a
251   given graph G, we consider a filtration on the graph F(G) which is a sequence of step-by-step
252   generating process of G based on an ordering of the edges in G. More precisely, we construct
253   an ordering on edges (e1 , e2 , · · · , e|E| ) and induced graph filtration F(G) = {G0 , G1 , . . . , G|E| },
254   where ∀i ∈ [|E|], Gi = Gi−1 ∪ ei with G0 initialized to be the empty graph and G|E| = G.
255   Intuitively, we hope such ordering can capture the importance of the edges in G. We use a filtration
256   function f : E → [0, 1] to represents the importance of each edge in G and the order of edges is
257   following 1 − f (e). It is natural to assume that the more important the edge is, the earlier it appears
258   in the ordering (and those pairs (u, v) ∈    / E are out of the generating process). Following such idea,
259
      we also require this ordering to be consistent with the importance of GX and Gϵ . That is to say,
260
      f (e ∈ GX ) > f (e′ ∈ Gϵ ) . In our model, we will learn a filtration functional fϕ : G → [0, 1]|E|
261
      to construct for each graph a function fϕG : E → [0, 1] mapping edges to their importance score.
262   For concise of notations, we might omit the upper and lower indices for f = fϕG and F(G) =
263   Fϕ (G) if they are clear in the context. We denote the subfiltration F(G≤t ) ⊆ F(G) to be the
264   filtration consisting of the subgraphs in Fϕ (G) whose edges’ filtration values are all below or equal
265   t. Symmetrically, let F(G≥t ) to be the filtration consisting of the subgraphs whose edges’ filtration
266
      values are all above t.

338   Finally, we give the following theorem to show when our model is guaranteed to be optimized by
339   the ground truth. The proof is a bit technical. We provide it in the appendix B.
340   Theorem 3.4. Assume ∀G, |EX | < |Eϵ |, and G∗X is minimal with respect to yG in the sense that
341   any subgraph GX ⊂ G∗X losses some information of label, then dtopo is uniquely optimized by
342   fϕ∗ (e) = 1{e ∈ G∗X }.
343
      Remark 3.5. Note that our guarantee does not depend on any stability or invariance assumptions
344   on GX , therefore, it will not be affected by variiform rationale subgraphs in theory.

374   3.3   R ELATED W ORK
375
      Two works are most related to ours: DIR (Wu et al., 2022) and GSAT (Miao et al., 2022).
376
377   Compared to DIR, our model also considers the distribution of the complement graph, but in a “soft
      way”, which is more efficient since we do not store those graphs exactly. Intuitively, our methods

                                                         7
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


378
      can be viewed as storing a learnable distribution of topological summary of the complement graphs.
379   Also, in practice we do not use a hard threshold to filter the graphs. What we do is computing the
380   persistent homology along ascending ordering and descending ordering separately, to mimic a hard
381   cut for some threshold t. Since our TDA method is robust enough, in practice it works good.
382
      Compared to GSAT, our loss function can also be viewed as a variational lower bound of the GIB
383
      loss. However, we use a totally different prior distribution of the rationales GX , and get rid of the
384
      hyperparameter r used in GSAT to specify the mean values of edge attentions. Instead, our topolog-
385   ical loss can be viewed as a self-adjusted cut to separate GX from G. In practice, we observe that
386   the attention learned by GSAT can collapse to the constant value r if it is not tuned carefully, which
387   is also mentioned in Chen et al. (2024). But our method does not have this issue. We consider that
388   such an issue might be caused by the unimodality of the prior distribution used in GSAT. However,
389   our prior is bimodal, which is essentially doing an unsupervised clustering over two Gaussians, like
390   k-means. In practice, we find the position of the two centers of the prior distribution does not matter
391   too much, as long as they do not collapse into one. Therefore, we just fix them to be 0.25 and 0.75,
392   with a penalty term to prevent component collapse.

510   5     C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
511
512   In this work, we reconsider the intrinsically interpretable graph learning problem via learning a
513   persistent rationale filtration. We propose our novel T OP I N G model that leverages the persistent ho-
514   mology to represent topological features of graphs. Based on that, we propose a novel self-adjusted
515   topological constraint, topological discrepancy, to measure the statistical topological difference be-
516
      tween two graph distributions. We provide a theoretical guarantee that our target function can be
      uniquely optimized by ground truth under certain conditions. We empirically show that our model
517
      can handle a newly targeted challenge on one simple synthetic dataset. From experiments, we also
518
      see that our model can solve other challenges including balancing performance of interpretability
519   and prediction and avoiding spurious correlations.
520
521
      5.1   L IMITATION
522
523   One limitation of our model is the computational cost. Currently the bottleneck is limited by the
524   computation of the topological invariants. The main technique issue is that there is no efficient GPU
525   implementation of the core algorithm to compute the persistent homology. The data transfer between
526   GPU memory and CPU memory takes much I/O cost. Maybe some system-level optimization based
527   on the CUDA framework can help. Some attempts have been made to use GPU to accelerate the
528   computation of persistent homology (Zhang et al., 2020), but the performance is still not satisfactory
529
      enough. Another possible solution is to use some approximation algorithms to compute the topolog-
      ical invariants. For example, some efficient sparsification methods (Dey et al., 2019), or pretained
530
      NNs for computing persistent homology (Yan et al., 2022b). We leave these problems for the future.
531
532
      5.2   F UTURE W ORK
533
534   Another potential extension is to use multi-parameter filtration. Our current model is based on a
535   linear graph filtration, which is based on the assumption that the importance of the edges in a graph
536   generating procedure is a 1-dimensional scalar. But if multi-dimensional vector can represents a
537   more sophisticated importance relations among all edges, which should in theory enrich the ex-
538   pressive power of our model. Some corresponding works are available in the literature that study
539   multi-parameter persistence (Xin et al., 2023; Mukherjee et al., 2024; Dey & Xin, 2021b; 2019b;a;
      2021a; Botnan et al., 2024). We also leave this for future work.

      The reason is that since we only care about edge filtrations, the filtration values on nodes can be

852   points in q0 and minimize the death times of points in p0 to maximize dB (p0 , q0 ), which gives
853   us the constant filtration function fϕ (e) = 1{e ∈ GX } on each partition. Then, for constant
854   filtration functions, the induced graph filtrations are essentially reduced to static graphs, and in
855   consequences, persistent homology is essentially reduced to homology. For 0-degree homology,
856   we just need to compare the 0-th Betti numbers β0ϵ and β0X between Gϵ and GX . In that case,
857   dB (p, q) = C(β0ϵ − β0X ) = C(|Eϵ | − |EX |) = C(|GE | − 2|EX |) for some constant C independent

887   C.1   DATASETS
888
889   Mutag (Kazius et al., 2005): The dataset involves a task of predicting molecular properties, specif-
890
      ically determining whether a molecule is mutagenic. The functional groups -NO2 and -NH2 are
      regarded as definitive indicators that contribute to mutagenicity, as noted by (Luo et al., 2020b).
891
892   Benzene (Sanchez-Lengeling et al., 2020): The dataset comprises 12,000 molecular graphs sourced
893   from ZINC15 (Sterling & Irwin, 2015). The objective is to identify the presence of benzene rings
894   within a molecule. The carbon atoms in these benzene rings serve as the ground-truth explanations.
895   BA-2Motifs (Luo et al., 2020b): The dataset involves a binary classification task in which each
896   graph combines a Barabasi-Albert base structure with either a house motif or a five-cycle motif. The
897   graph’s label and ground-truth explanation are based on the motif it includes.
898
      SPmotif (Wu et al., 2022): The dataset consists of graphs that merge a base structure (such as a Tree,
899
      Ladder, or Wheel) with a motif (either a Cycle, House, or Crane). Each graph is manually infused
900   with a spurious correlation between the base and the motif. The graph’s label and the ground truth
901   explanation are determined by the motif it contains.
902
903
      BA-HouseGrid: The house and grid motifs are chosen because they do not have overlapping struc-
      tures, such as those found in the house and 3 × 3 grid.
904
905   BA-HouseAndGrid (Bui et al., 2024): Each graph is based on a Barabasi-Albert structure and may
906   be linked with either a house motif or a grid motif. Graphs that contain both types of motifs are
907   labeled as 1, while those containing only one type are labeled as 0. Note that each motif appears at
908   most once in each graph.
909   BA-HouseOrGrid (Bui et al., 2024): Similar to BA-HouseAndGrid, graphs that contain either
910   house motif or grid motif are labeled as 1, while those containing neither type are labeled as 0. Note
911   that each motif appears at most once in each graph.
912
      BA-HouseOrGrid-nRnd: Similar to BA-HouseOrGrid, graphs that contain either n house motifs
913   or n grid motifs are labeled as 1, where n is a random integer between 1 (inclusive) and n (inclusive).
