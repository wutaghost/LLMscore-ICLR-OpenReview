009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Synthetic data generation has been proven successful in improving model per-
012            formance and robustness in the context of scarce or low-quality data. Using the
013            data valuation framework to statistically identify beneficial and detrimental ob-
014            servations, we introduce a novel augmentation pipeline that generates only high-
015            value training points based on hardness characterization. We first demonstrate
016            via benchmarks on real data that Shapley-based data valuation methods perform
017            comparably with learning-based methods in hardness characterisation tasks, while
018
               offering significant theoretical and computational advantages. Then, we show that
               synthetic data generators trained on the hardest points outperform non-targeted
019
               data augmentation on simulated data and on a large scale credit default prediction
020
               task. In particular, our approach improves the quality of out-of-sample predictions
021            and it is computationally more efficient compared to non-targeted methods.

024   1   I NTRODUCTION
025
026   Training complex machine learning models requires large amounts of data, but in real-world applica-
027   tions data may be of poor quality, insufficient in amount, or subject to privacy, safety, and regulatory
028   limitations. Such challenges have sparked an interest in synthetic data generation (SDG), represent-
029   ing the practice of using available data to generate realistic synthetic samples (Lu et al., 2024). In
030   this work, we argue that, when the objective is to use synthetic data to make an existing machine
031
      learning model better generalize to unseen data, augmenting only the hardest training points is more
      effective than augmenting the entire training dataset. In other words, we propose a scalable targeted
032
      synthetic data generation framework, focusing on binary classification tasks. The underlying intu-
033
      ition is that, within a dataset, some of the observations are obvious to classify, whereas others play
034   a more significant role in determining the decision boundary of the trained model. Focusing only
035   on these harder examples when generating additional data may allow the model to efficiently learn
036   a more robust decision boundary.
037
      In particular, the method proposed in this work is specifically targeted towards binary classifiers
038
      on tabular data, which often require adaptations such as oversampling, undersampling, or synthetic
039
      data generation to improve model performance (Chawla et al., 2002; He et al., 2008) under class
040   imbalance. While deep neural networks have proven successful across domains such as images,
041   audio or text, they are still regularly outperformed on tabular data by simpler and more interpretable
042   tree-based architectures (Shwartz-Ziv & Armon, 2021). Therefore, we choose trees as the reference
043   model for binary classification on tabular data, while deep learning models are used for synthetic
044   data generation. In general, we will use D = {(xi , yi )}ni=1 to denote the training data, where
045   xi ∈ Rd is a d-dimensional feature vector and yi ∈ {0, 1} is a binary label.
046
      Our targeted synthetic data generation approach is divided into two main steps: (i) hardness char-
047
      acterization of training data points, followed by (ii) training of a synthetic data generator only on
048   the hardest data points of the initial training dataset. In order to identify “hard” data points, we
049   propose a novel hardness characterizer specific to tabular data, based on KNN data Shapleys (Jia
050   et al., 2020), capable of achieving performances comparable to state-of-the-art methods in hardness
051   detection benchmarks, while offering key advantages such as being deterministic, model agnostic
052   and computationally efficient. Hardness characterization has already been applied in the literature
053   with rationales similar to this work, but mainly with the objective of pruning the easier examples
      (Jia et al., 2021; Paul et al., 2023), rather than augmenting the harder ones.

054
      Once the hardest points have been identified, synthetic data generation models are trained only on
055   the most difficult points identified by our hardness characterizer. In this work, we focus on two
056   widely used deep learning architectures for synthetic data generation for tabular data, namely Tab-
057   ular Variational Autoencoders (TVAE) and Conditional Tabular Generative Adversarial Networks
058   (CTGAN) (both introduced in Xu et al., 2019). To mitigate instabilities encountered during training,
059   we devise a custom early stopping condition which allows to interrupt training when the quality
060   of the synthetically generated samples is maximised. The best synthesizers are then found using a
061   highly efficient routine which combines model selection and hyperparameter tuning. From a com-
062   parison of the best models we find that, given a fixed number of points to generate, augmenting only
063
      hard data results in a larger performance improvement on unseen data with respect to non-targeted
      data augmentation, while being computationally cheaper.
064
065   The rest of the work is structured as follows: after a recap on the related literature in Section 2,
066   the proposed approach is discussed and empirically verified in Section 3. Next we explore the use
067   of KNN Shapleys as hardness characterizers via a benchmark study in Section 4.1, followed by a
068   discussion on how to utilize them for synthetic data generation on real (Section 4.2) and simulated
069
      data (Section 4.3). The results are extensively discussed in Section 5.

071   2     BACKGROUND AND RELATED LITERATURE
072
073   In this work, we aim to bridge the gap between existing methods for hardness characterization and
074   game theoretic approaches to data valuation, with the objective of improving the performance of
075   synthetic data generators. Therefore, our work closely aligns with existing techniques in hardness
076   characterization, data valuation, and synthetic data generation, briefly reviewed in this section.
077
078   2.1   H ARDNESS CHARACTERIZATION
079
080   As remarked in the introduction, the first aim of this work is the identification of a subset of points
081   that are hard for the model to learn. Existing literature on the topic of hardness characterization is
082   often qualitative and based on different definitions of “hardness”. Following the taxonomy presented
083   in Seedat et al. (2024), three main categories of hardness can be identified: (i) Mislabeling: the true
084
      label is replaced with an incorrect one. The label perturbation probability can be uniform across
      classes or label-dependent; (ii) Out-of-distribution: features are transformed or shifted, leading to
085
      observations distributed differently from the main data generating process; (iii) Atypical: observa-
086
      tions that are compatible with the main data generating process, but located in tails or rare regions of
087   the feature distributions. These distinctions normally dictate different courses of actions: datapoints
088   that are mislabeled or out of distribution are symptomatic of errors in the data collection process
089   and should be removed from training data, while atypical datapoints are valid points and may in fact
090   need to be augmented to train a robust model (Yuksekgonul et al., 2023).
091
      While many different hardness characterizers have been proposed, most of them are based around
092
      keeping track of some statistic of interest for each datapoint during the training process, meaning
093   they can be adapted to any model that is trained iteratively (such as XGBoost or a neural network).
094   Examples include GraNd (Paul et al., 2023), which uses large gradients as an indicator of hardness,
095   and Forget (Toneva et al., 2019), which counts how many times each point is learnt and then forgot-
096   ten during training, with the simple intuition that hard points are forgotten more often. Based on the
097   quantitative benchmarks reported in Seedat et al. (2024), Data-IQ (Seedat et al., 2022) emerges as
098   one of the best performers on tabular data, and it is chosen as benchmark hardness characterizer in
099   this work because of its interpretability and compatibility with XGBoost.
100
101   Data-IQ Data-IQ (Seedat et al., 2022) partitions the training data into “easy” and “hard” points
102   via the concepts of epistemic and aleatoric uncertainty, combined with a model confidence score
103   calculated from P(x, θ), representing the probability of assigning a correct label to an observation,
104
      given input features x and model parameters θ. The epistemic uncertainty is caused by the variabil-
      ity in the estimated model parameters, whereas the aleatoric uncertainty captures the inherent data
105
      uncertainty. Given parameter estimates {θe }E e=1 obtained during training, a Data-IQ confidence

117   i-th training point, which takes the following form:

123

148   2.3    S YNTHETIC DATA GENERATION
149
      Synthetic data generators (SGDs) for tabular data have been actively researched in recent years (see
150
      for example Fonseca & Bacao, 2023). Here, we discuss three of the most popular SDGs used in
151
      practice: SMOTE, CTGAN, and TVAE.
152
153
      SMOTE In Synthetic Minority Over-sampling Technique (SMOTE; Chawla et al., 2002), obser-
154   vations are picked at random among the K nearest neighbours of the same class for each datapoint,
155   and new data is generated by random sampling along the segment connecting the point to the chosen
156   neighbour. The number of neighbours is determined based on the over-sampling ratio.
157
158   CTGAN Tabular GAN (TGAN; Xu & Veeramachaneni, 2018) was the first attempt at using Gen-
159   erative Adversarial Networks (GAN; Goodfellow et al., 2014) to generate tabular data, which was
160   then extended to Conditional TGANs (CTGANs; Xu et al., 2019). A GAN consists of two models:
161   a generator that takes as input random noise and tries to output a point following the data distri-
      bution, and a discriminator that given observations (either from the original dataset or synthesized


                                                                 3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      by the generator) outputs the probability of them being fake. Both the generator and discriminator
163   are typically neural networks. In CTGANs, the possible non-Gaussianity and multimodality in the
164   data is handled by fitting a variational Gaussian mixture model to each normalized numeric feature,
165   whereas categorical features are represented as one-hot encoded vectors. Both the generator and the
166   discriminator networks within the underlying GAN are multilayer perceptrons (MLPs). The name of
167   the CTGAN architecture derives from the form of its conditional generator, which allows to generate
168   data conditional on specific values of the discrete features, for better handling imbalanced datasets.
169
170   TVAE A Tabular VAE (TVAE; Xu et al., 2019) is an adaptation for tabular data of a Variational
171   Auto-Encoder (VAE; Kingma & Welling, 2013), using similar preprocessing to CTGAN. A VAE
172   consists in an encoder that learns to represent high-dimensional data into a low-dimensional latent
173
      space, and a decoder that reconstructs the compressed representation into the original domain. Both
      the encoder and decoder networks are normally chosen to be MLPs (Kingma & Welling, 2013).

      4.2                           A MERICAN E XPRESS DATASET
304
305   The main real-world benchmark for the methodologies discussed in this work is the American Ex-
306   press credit default prediction dataset (Addison et al., 2022), referred to as the Amex dataset. The
307   dataset consists of observations for n = 458 913 customers. For each of them, we consider 188 fea-
308   tures available at the latest credit card statement date, in addition to a binary label yi ∈ {0, 1} which
309   specifies whether or not the customer was able to repay their credit card debt within 120 days of the
310   statement. The n = 458 913 available observations are split into nvalid = 50 000 datapoints used
311
      to validate model performance on unseen data, ntest = 50 000 observations used to calculate KNN
      Shapleys, and the remaining datapoints are used for training. The splits are performed randomly
312
      stratifying on the target, so that the same ratio of defaulters and non-defaulters is maintained across
313
      datasets. Performance is measured via the normalized Gini coefficient, which is 2 · AUCROC − 1
314   with AUCROC denoting the Area Under the Receiver Operating Characteristic curve.
315
316   The Amex dataset has been first released as part of a Kaggle1 competition, with winning submis-

350   4.2.1                     H ARDNESS CHARACTERIZATION
351
      Data-IQ’s confidence scores and aleatoric uncertainties are estimated for each training observation
352
      as per Section 2.1. Figure 4b displays the relationship between the two: in the top left a large
353
      amount of “easy” points, with high confidence and low aleatoric uncertainty; in the bottom left a
354   few “hard” points, with low confidence and low aleatoric uncertainty; lastly, the “ambiguous” points
355   are located around the elbow, with high aleatoric uncertainty. Looking at the marginal distributions
356   in Figure 4c, it is noticed that XGBoost is generally very confident in its predictions, causing the
357   number of hard points to be low. Setting a low confidence threshold of 0.25, a high confidence
358   threshold of 0.75 and a low aleatoric uncertainty threshold of 0.2 allows to assign to each point a
359   tag in {Easy, Hard, Ambiguous}. Hard points are less than 3% of training data and both hard and
360   ambiguous points have a much higher proportion of defaulters than the full data (cf. Table 4a).
361   Beyond these initial statistics, it is interesting to know where these points lie in feature space. By
362   looking at the total reduction in loss due to a feature in the nodes where said feature is used to
363   split the data, we can calculate the feature importances of a trained XGBoost classifier. In the case
364   of the Amex dataset, the first feature dominates these scores, with an importance comparable to
365   the bottom 100 features combined. Figure 4d displays the distribution of this feature separated by
366   Data-IQ tag for both defaulters and non-defaulters: hard defaulters somewhat overlap with easy non-
367   defaulters and vice-versa, while ambiguous defaulters are almost indistinguishable from ambiguous
368
      non-defaulters. This is a common issue with tabular data, referred to as outcome heterogeneity and
      is known to be captured by Data-IQ (Seedat et al., 2022).
369
370   KNN Shapleys are then computed for K ∈ {1, 5, 100} under the assumption that the ntest available
371   out-of-sample data points are representative of the entire data distribution. Figure 5 displays the
372   distribution of 100NN Shapleys separated by Data-IQ’s tag: hard points as per Data-IQ exhibit low
373   100NN Shapleys, with very good adherence for the hardest points. In addition, the left tail of the
374
      ambiguous points lies in the region of low 100NN Shapleys and would thus be recognized as hard
      by our novel characterizer.
375
376   To choose the best K, validation Gini is monitored as we re-train XGBoost after gradually removing
377   the hardest datapoints. These points are expected to be the most valuable for XGBoost and thus the
      best hardness characterizer should be the one displaying the fastest drop in validation performance.

413
414   The 10% hardest datapoints are chosen as training set for synthetic data generators, with the purpose
415   of establishing whether augmenting only the hardest datapoints makes XGBoost more robust than
416   non-targeted data augmentation. The choice of a hard threshold is imposed by the expensive compu-
417   tational cost of fine-tuning neural networks, and 10% is justified as it is just before the elbow region
418   where the decrease in validation performance when removing hard data slows down (cf. Figure 6).
419   Both CTGAN and TVAE are implemented in Python using a heavily customized version of the sdv
420   package (Patki et al., 2016), with extensive details about the training given in Appendix A.1. The
421   quality of the synthetic samples can be examined directly for the first feature: Figure 7a (CTGAN)
422
      displays a good but not perfect overlap with real data, whereas from Figure 7b (TVAE) we can see
      better overlap of synthetic and real data with respect to CTGAN for the first feature.
423
424   For models trained on the 10% hardest points we augment by 100%, while for models trained using
425   the entire dataset we augment by 10%, thus guaranteeing the same amount of synthetic samples
426   across all experiments. Notice that for each attempt synthetic data has to be generated multiple times
427   with different seeds to mitigate randomness and quantify uncertainty on the performance metric.
428
      The proposed routine, implemented in Python with the baytune package (Smith et al., 2019),
      requires very few attempts to find good proposals, and is thus more efficient than a grid search over
429
      a hyperparameter space, reducing the computational complexity of the problem.

521   5                                          C ONCLUSION AND DISCUSSION
522
523   In this work, we verified empirically that, when it comes to data augmentation, focusing only on the
524   most challenging points for the model can be beneficial, both in terms of performance improvement
525   and computational efficiency. To achieve this result, we first devised a novel hardness characterizer
526   based on KNN Shapleys, capable of achieving performances comparable to state-of-the-art meth-
527   ods in hardness detection benchmarks on tabular data. Then, we performed a complex fine-tuning
528   routine on synthesizers trained either on the entire dataset or on the hardest points only, allowing to
529   rigorously establish which approach leads to the largest performance improvement.
530   Methodologically, this work aims at bridging the gap between learning-based hardness characteri-
531   zation and game theoretic data valuation. The benchmarks reported in Section 4.1 represent the first
532   quantitative comparison between a Shapley-based evaluator and existing hardness characterizers.
533   While research on the two topics has often dealt with similar problems, such as mislabeling or data
534   summarization (Paul et al., 2023; Jia et al., 2021), the two paths have never crossed.
535   Practically, this work attempts to change the perspective on data summarization: while existing
536   literature has mostly focused on pruning the least valuable points from training data (Seedat et al.,
537   2022; Kwon & Zou, 2023), here we propose augmenting the most valuable ones. We demonstrate
538   that not all datapoints are created equal, and some play a more significant role in determining the
539   predictive power of the final model: high-value data dictates which points we should either collect
      in larger amounts or, when this is not possible, synthetically generate.

      A.1   T RAINING CTGAN AND TVAE – A MEX DATASET

      This section provides details about CTGAN and TVAE SDGs on the Amex dataset. A common issue
642   when training GANs is the instability of both generator and discriminator losses. More specifically,
643   the two typically move in opposite ways with oscillatory patterns, making it difficult to decide when
644   to stop training. For this reason, we introduce a novel early stopping condition which tracks epoch
645   after epoch a weighted average of the Kolgomorov-Smirnov (KS) statistic between real and synthetic
646   data across all individual features. In particular, we choose to use as weights the feature importances,
647   in order to focus more on the features relevant to XGBoost. The patience is set to 50 epochs with
      a maximum number of epochs of 500. If the early stopping condition is triggered, the model is

                                                        12
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


648
      reverted to the best epoch. A large batch size of 10 000 is chosen to limit the number of updates per
649   epoch and guarantee a smoother training process. Both the generator and discriminator are trained
650   using the Adam optimizer (Kingma & Ba, 2017), setting the learning rate to 2 · 10−4 , weight decay
651   to 10−6 , and the momentum parameters to β1 = 0.5 and β2 = 0.9. Figure 10a shows the losses and
652   the statistic epoch by epoch: we can see that the losses move against each other and then more or
653   less converge once the model cannot further improve. We notice oscillatory patterns in the tracked
654   statistic, symptomatic of the training dynamics of the generator and discriminator pair, and the early
655   stopping condition kicking in after around 100 epochs when the weighted KS statistic peaks at 0.83.
656   Training of the VAE relies on the Adam optimizer with a learning rate of 10−3 , weight decay of
657   10−5 , β1 of 0.9 and β2 of 0.999. To avoid overfitting of training data and the subsequent generation
658   of exact replicas of training points, we stop training once the maximum number of epochs is reached.
