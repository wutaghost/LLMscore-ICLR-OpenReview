009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Recently, large language models (LLMs) with extensive general knowledge and
012            powerful reasoning abilities have seen rapid development and widespread appli-
013            cation. A systematic and reliable evaluation of LLMs or visual language model
014            (VLMs) is a crucial step in applying and developing them for various fields. There
015            have been some early explorations about the usability of LLMs for limited urban
016
               tasks, but a systematic and scalable evaluation benchmark is still lacking. The
               challenge in constructing a systematic evaluation benchmark for urban research lies
017
               in the diversity of urban data, the complexity of application scenarios and the highly
018
               dynamic nature of the urban environment. In this paper, we design CityBench, an
019            interactive simulator based evaluation platform, as the first systematic benchmark
020            for evaluating the capabilities of LLMs for diverse tasks in urban research. First,
021            we build CityData to integrate the diverse urban data and CitySimu to simulate
022            fine-grained urban dynamics. Based on CityData and CitySimu, we design 8
023            representative urban tasks in 2 categories of perception-understanding and decision-
024            making as the CityBench. With extensive results from 30 well-known LLMs and
025            VLMs in 13 cities around the world, we find that advanced LLMs and VLMs can
026            achieve competitive performance in diverse urban tasks requiring commonsense
027
               and semantic understanding abilities, e.g., understanding the human dynamics and
               semantic inference of urban images. Meanwhile, they fail to solve the challenging
028
               urban tasks requiring professional knowledge and high-level reasoning abilities,
029
               e.g., geospatial prediction and traffic control task. These observations provide
030            valuable perspectives for utilizing and developing LLMs in the future. The dataset,
031            benchmark and source codes are openly accessible to the research community via
032            https://github.com/CityBench24/CityBench.

      As presented in Figure 1, CityBench is a simulator based evaluation platform with three core

      components: CityData for collecting and processing diverse urban data, CitySimu for simulating
185   human dynamics and providing an interactive simulation environment, and CityBench for model
186   evaluation on 8 representative urban tasks with different modalities.

188   3.1 C ITY DATA
189   To present a complete picture of the city’s geospatial structure, semantic features, and human activities,
190   CityData integrates the following globally available data from multiple sources.
191
      Geospatial Data Geospatial data, represented by maps, is the most fundamental data for describing
192
      the urban structure including road networks, points of interest (POIs), areas of interest (AOIs), etc.
193   OpenStreetMap (OSM) 1 is most widely used open source map data. However, the raw data provided
194   by OSM cannot support the simulation of urban dynamics directly because the relationship between
195   different elements is incomplete such as the connection between buildings and roads. Therefore, we
196   provide a globally available rule-based map building tool within CityData that reconstructs lanes,
197   lane topology, and building-lane connections based on the raw OSM data. The reconstructed map is
198   used as the geospatial base and simulation input in CitySimu.
199   Urban Visual Data Street view data and satellite images are two types of globally available urban
200   data that contains rich semantic information, which represents the visual of human. Therefore,
201   CityData also integrates the two types of data, the former obtained via Google Maps API and Baidu
202   Maps API, and the latter using the Esri World Imagery as data source. In the open-source CityData
203   python package, street view data is accessed through spatial location and facing direction, and satellite
204   images are acquired through spatial ranges.
205   Human Activities Data We use the global Foursquare-checkin Yang et al. (2016) data and a synthetic
206   global origin-destination data (OD data) loooffeeeey (2024) as the proxy of human activities to enable
207   the fine-grained human movement simulation. The Foursquare-checkin dataset Yang et al. (2016) is a
208   long-term user check-in dataset collected from Foursquare 2 in approximately 400 cities worldwide. It
209   has been widely used in the community over the past ten years Chen et al. (2024). Origin-destination
210   data is generated by a diffusion model with population from Worldpop3 and satellite image from Esri
211   World Imagery as input. While all the user information are anonymized, we follow the license from
212   Foursquare-checkin Yang et al. (2016) to protect the public privacy.

      3.2 C ITY S IMU
217
      Building on CityData, CitySimu simulates the urban dynamics and provide diverse easy-to-use APIs
218
      for the interactive operation. As shown in Figure 3, CitySimu contains the base environment APIs
219   for obtaining the static information of environment, three simulation APIs for human and vehicle
220   behavior simulations, language APIs to enable the interaction between CitySimu and LLMs.
221
222
      Individual Mobility Simulation Based on the geospatial data, the individual mobility simulation
      constructs a simulator that can simulate an agent moving and exploring within the city. Agents can
223
      obtain the POIs and roads around them through API provided by CityData, and thus plan and decide
224
      the next lane or POI to travel in to update their locations. For the mobility prediction task in the city
225   scale, the available actions are defined as the POIs around the city. For the urban exploration task in
226   the local street scale, the available actions are defined as the nearby lanes.
227
228
      Urban Visual Environment Simulation To further support the study of urban visual intelligence Fan
      et al. (2023), we follow Chen et al. (2019); Mirowski et al. (2018) to construct a urban visual
229
      environment simulation with real street view images and map data. In the environment, agent can
230
      access the panoramic images of its location via APIs and then select the available actions to move
231   along the road to arrive the destination. In the outdoor visual-language instruction navigation task,
232   given the human-like instruction, agent can observe the panoramic images of its location, extract
233   key elements from them and then decide one direction to go. This can be saw as an extension of
234   individual mobility simulation with visual input.
235
      Traffic Simulation In the former two simulations, we only simulate the individual actions without
236
      the interaction with others. Here, we introduce microscopic traffic simulation to model the interaction
237   behaviors between vehicles and provide a traffic control environment. The simulator takes the
238   geospatial data reconstructed from OSM within CityData and the travel demand described by the
239   synthetic global OD data as inputs. It simulates the vehicle behaviors through realistic driving
240   simulation models including the intelligent driver model (IDM) Treiber et al. (2000) as the car-
241   following model and the randomized MOBIL model Kesting et al. (2007); Feng et al. (2021) as the
242   lane-change model to obtain the dynamics of all vehicles in the city at each second. Through the
243   sensing APIs, LLMs can obtain data about urban dynamics such as junction queue length, vehicle
244   speed, and road average speed. Through the control APIs, LLMs can intervene in the city’s operation,
245
      such as modifying traffic signal lights, modifying the speed limit of the road, etc.

      3.3     C ITY B ENCH
247
248   Based on CityData and CitySimu, we design a multi-modal urban evaluation benchmark CityBench to
249   evaluate the capability of LLMs and VLMs. In the following section, we first summarize the whole
250   pipeline and then give introduction to each task. Due to the limits of space, detailed templates and
251   prompts for each task can refer to the appendix.
252
253   3.3.1    P IPELINE
254   Figure 2 describe the procedure of building evaluation benchmark. As introduced before, CityData
255   works in the data collection and data processing stage and CitySimu works in the data processing
256   stage. We focus on introducing the evaluation generation stage and quality control stage as follows.
257
      In the evaluation generation stage, we use template based methods and LLMs/VLMs based methods
258   to generate the evaluation questions. For example, for the image geolocalization task, the groundth
259   location is already known when collecting, thus we directly design template based question to convert
260   the image geolocalization task into question answer pair. As for the outdoor navigation task, we
261   employ VLM to act as human annotation experts to annotate the data to generate the navigation
262   instruction with additional inputs. In CityBench, the instructions for urban exploration task and the
263   outdoor navigation task are generated by LLM assisted methods. Instructions for other tasks are
264   generated from template based methods.
265   Due to the hand-craft designs and potential issues of LLMs, we apply a quality control stage to
266   filter and rewrite the generated questions to obtain a high quality evaluation questions. For questions
267   generated from template based methods, we use LLM as data quality expert to filter the low-quality
268   data and use LLM as data rewritter to rewrite the questions with diverse formats and expressions.
269   For questions generated from the LLMs/VLMs based methods, we use LLM/VLM as the agent
      with additional information to execute the task to verify the quality of generated instructions. If

      3.3.3   P LANNING AND D ECISION M AKING TASK
314
315   Different from the static evaluation introduced in the last section, we design four interactive decision
316   making tasks to evaluate the capabilities of LLMs in dynamic and partial observed environments
317   which are more challenging and realistic. With the interaction with the CitySimu and dynamic
318   human activities, LLMs need to understand the important mechanisms and regularity in the urban
319   environments to complete the decision-making tasks.
320   Mobility Prediction As one of the fundamental task for understanding the human behaviors and
321   urban dynamics, mobility prediction task is to predict the next location of user in the next time
322   window with given the past mobility trajectory. Here we use the the global Foursquare checkin data
323   to support the mobility prediction in the simulator. We follow Wang et al. (2023b) to conduct the

342   Outdoor Navigation Outdoor navigation task is widely used in neurocognitive science Epstein
343   et al. (2017) as the important benchmark for evaluating the spatial cognition of human and models.
344
      As one of the most widely-used settings in outdoor navigation task, visual-language navigation
      task Schumann et al. (2024); Yang et al. (2024) requires the model to follow the human-annotated
345
      language instruction to arrive to the destination with the nearby street view images as additional input.
346
      This task requires the VLMs to acquire the ability of urban visual scene understanding, language
347   understanding and decision-making.
348
349   Urban Exploration Here, we define a text based street exploration task to evaluate the zero-shot
350
      navigation capability of LLMs in a new city without visual input and instructions. Different from the
      visual language navigation which require model to follow the language instruction and understand the
351
      scene via street view image, our urban exploration task require model to explore the region via the
352
      local information (e.g. accessed road names) provided by the simulator during action and its intrinsic
353   knowledge of the whole urban space in the city.
354
355   Traffic Signal Control Traffic signal control task is one of the widely studied realistic urban decision
356
      making task in recent years Wei et al. (2019). It is challenging for existing methods due to the dynamic
      traffics and the generalization issues. It is to generate the future traffic signal schedule by considering
357
      the current traffic states and the future traffics. Lai et al. Lai et al. (2023) propose LLMLight to solve
358
      the single traffic signal control in simple intersection and demonstrate the generalization of LLMs.
359   Follow this work, we extend it into multiple traffic signal control in a region with more than one
360   intersections, which are more challenging and require cooperation between different intersections.

      Performance of VLMs on Urban Visual Tasks Main results on urban visual tasks are presented in
409   Table 2. We find three interesting observations: 1) VLMs perform much better than the traditional
410   baselines on the urban visual task, e.g, city inference via street view image and infrastructure
411   inference, which require commonsense and semantic understanding ability of models. 2) But,
412   VLMs perform poorly on tasks requiring precise numerical values, or have a diminished advantage,
413   e.g., the geospatial prediction task on population and precise geo-coordindates prediction task. 3)
414   Most of the VLMs’ performance on all the tasks are not robust, e.g., some VLMs’ performance
415   is close to zero and large model within the same model series not always perform better than the
416   smaller models, e.g., InternVL2-26B performs worse than InternVL2-8B. Finally, we find that the
417
      performance variability across different LLMs and VLMs is primarily influenced by the capabilities
      of the LLMs backbone. For example, in widely used VLMs for urban tasks, Intern2.5-7B consistently
418
      outperforms Qwen2-7B and Mistral-7B in most tasks, which can be attributed to Intern2.5-7B’s
419
      superior performance in general NLP tasks at the same parameter scale. Similarly, LLaVA-NeXT-8B
420   demonstrates performance comparable to MiniCPM-V2.5-8B, as both models leverage the same
421   LLM backbone, LLaMA3-8B.

                     Error Analysis of LLMs We find that LLMs often display errors such as logic error, format error,
450
                     invalid action, refusal to answer, and hallucinations. As shown in Figure 6, Llama3-8B exhibited
451
                     a logical error in its judgment of time in the task mobility prediction. For the urban exploration
452                  task, Qwen2-7B refused to choose the option and instead demanded the user to use a navigation
453                  service to solve the problem. Intern2.5-7B directly stated that it lacks expertise in this area and needs
454                  more information to answer the question. Llama3-8B provided an invalid option in the traffic signal
455                  task, rendering CitySimu unable to perform next action. We notice that the most frequent error is
456                  Misformatted, and several instances close to 0 in Table 3 are mostly caused by formatting errors.
457                  Thus, one of the promising direction is to reduce these error from LLMs to improve their practicality.
458                    Error Analysis of VLMs Urban visual tasks require the model to make decisions directly without
459                    going through an explanation process. As a result, as shown in Figure 7, common errors include
460                    format errors, invalid actions, refusal to answer and hallucinations. In the image geolocalization
461                    task, InternVL2-2B provides a response that do not follow the required format, while Yi-VL-34B
462                    gives an irrelevant invalid response. CogVLM2-19B and Yi-VL-34B, in the geospatial prediction and
463                    infrastructure inference tasks respectively, repeat the examples provided in the question and refuse to
464                    answer the actual question. Due to the response format requirements of the tasks, the most common
465                    error made by VLMs in urban visual tasks is misformatted responses.
466                  Geospatial Bias of LLMs and VLMs To further investigate the difference between LLMs and
467                  VLMs, we report the detailed results of mobility prediction task and image geolocalization tasks
468                  from 13 cities in Figure 5. Based on the above results, we have made several interesting discoveries.
469                  First, we find that the performance of different LLMs varies a lot across different cities, no LLM can
470                  always perform best in mobility prediction tasks. Second, we find that the performance of VLMs
471                  on visual task like image geolocalization task are significantly biased. Most VLMs perform well in
472
                     major international cities, but poorly in some lesser-known cities (e.g., CapeTown and Nairobi). We
                     provide simple evidence for this phenomenon by analyzing the number of public websites available
473
                     on Google and Wikipedia. For more details, see Section A.3 in the appendix. The variability in
474
                     evaluation results demonstrate the necessity of establishing a global evaluation benchmark, and also
475                  highlights the potential shortcomings and areas for improvement of LLMs.

477                      5                                              C ONCLUSION
478                  In this paper, we propose CityBench, a systematic evaluation benchmark for LLMs and VLMs in
479                  diverse urban tasks. With the data support from CityData and simulation support from CitySimu, we
480                  design 8 important urban tasks in 13 cities to constitute the CityBench for evaluating the capabilities
481                  of LLMs and VLMs. Extensive experiments present that LLMs and VLMs exhibit exceptional
482                  performance in various urban tasks requiring commonsense and semantic understanding, but fail
483                  in challenging urban tasks which require professional domain knowledge and precise numeric
484                  calculations. The extensive results from CityBench demonstrate the potential the applying LLMs and
485
                     VLMs in various urban tasks and also shed light for the future research of developing more powerful
                     LLMs and VLMs for urban tasks.

779   A.2     D ETAILS OF Q UALITY C ONTROL
780
781   In CityBench, the authors will participate in the quality control process for some tasks, following the
782   automatic quality control stage. Taking the manual checking of GeoQA as an example, the original
783   data from OpenStreetMap contains low-quality information, with missing or incorrect details about
784
      AOI, POI, and roads. When this low-quality data is used in the evaluation task, LLMs may become
      confused and generate meaningless answers. In such cases, the authors review the questions to ensure
785
      that the information in the context is meaningful. However, due to time limitations for participants,
786
      we can only randomly sample the evaluation cases. For instances from cities and regions where
787   authors are unfamiliar, we filter out low-quality instances. For instances from cities and regions where
788   authors are familiar, we rewrite low-quality instances using external information, such as commercial
789   map services. Finally, if data quality remains unsatisfactory after filtering and rewriting, we will
790   regenerate a certain number of cases to fill the gaps. In fact, while considering the probabilities of
791   filtering, we generate enough candidate instances during the initial generation.

      A.5    D ISCUSSION
865
866
      Limitations. While our platform is based on the public data from various sources, the quality
867   of different data may play a important role in the evaluation results. In the future, we plan to
868   collect more kinds of tasks with global scale groundtruth data to further improve the reliability and
869   representativeness of benchmark.
870
      Ethical considerations and potential societal impact. Our benchmark is designed for enable the
871
      global evaluation of LLMs and VLMs for various cities with different cultures and countries. We
872
      try our best to improve the ease-of-use and fairness for cities with different development levels.
873   However, due to the limitation of accessed data, the evaluation results for different cites varies a lot.
874   Therefore, the variation in evaluation results caused by data quality may lead to a certain degree of
875   misunderstanding regarding the performance on some urban problems. We call the whole community
876   for attention to this issue to improve the usability of LLMs across different races and countries,
877   promoting fairness and sustainable development of the world.
878
      Develop foundation model for urban domain. Based on the results of our benchmark, we find
879   existing LLMs perform poorly on many urban tasks, even worse than some classic simple baseline
880   algorithms. Developing LLMs tailored for urban domain is urgently necessary. We hope our
881   benchmark can accelerate this development and we look forward to a more comprehensive and robust
882   evaluation framework for urban domain.
