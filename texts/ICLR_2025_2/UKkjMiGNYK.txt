009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011
               This research explores the application of Multimodal Generative AI to enhance
012
               story point estimation in Agile software development. By integrating text, image,
013
               and categorical data using advanced models like BERT, CNN, and XGBoost, our
014            approach surpasses the limitations of traditional single-modal estimation methods.
015            The results demonstrate good accuracy for simpler story points, while also high-
016            lighting challenges in more complex categories due to data imbalance. This study
017            further explores the impact of categorical data, particularly severity, on the esti-
018            mation process, emphasizing its influence on model performance. Our findings
019            emphasize the transformative potential of multimodal data integration in refin-
020            ing AI-driven project management, paving the way for more precise, adaptable,
021            and domain-specific AI capabilities. Additionally, this work outlines future direc-
022
               tions for addressing data variability and enhancing the robustness of AI in Agile
               methodologies.

026   1   INTRODUCTION
027
028
      Story points (SP) are a key metric in Agile methodologies used to estimate the size, complexity,
029
      and effort for each user story, which is a brief description of a software feature from the end user’s
030
      perspective, outlining their needs and reasons. They are also employed to estimate the remain-
031   ing useful life of software products (Islam & Sandborn, 2021, 2023, 2024). Agile teams typically
032   use subjective methods such as planning poker to estimate these points, but this process often ex-
033   hibits inconsistency and variable accuracy (Jorgensen, 2001 & Usman et al., 2014). The inherent
034   complexity of software development within Agile frameworks demands more precise and adaptable
035   techniques for estimating story points (Menzies et al., 2006). Recent advancements in Generative
036   AI, particularly multimodal models that integrate various data formats such as text, images, graphs,
037   and categorical data, present a groundbreaking solution to these challenges (Devlin et al., 2019; He
038   et al., 2016). Deep learning architectures in these models process and integrate multimodal inputs,
039
      enabling a more nuanced analysis of text-based data and resulting in predictions that are both more
      accurate and consistent (Radford et al., 2021). Multimodal Generative AI exploits the synergistic
040
      potential of diverse data types, uncovering complex relationships among textual descriptions, visual
041
      elements, historical data, and categorical features. This comprehensive approach not only improves
042   the accuracy of story point estimation, aligning with Agile principles, but also enhances the re-
043   sponsiveness and adaptability of the development process (Vaswani et al., 2017). Integrating these
044   models within software development workflows reduces human bias and shortens project timelines,
045   leading to substantial cost savings by minimizing delays and avoiding unnecessary rework (Lin et
046   al., 2014).
047
      This paper proposes a novel framework that uses state-of-the-art multimodal machine learning tech-
048   niques, including Ordinal Encoding, BERT (Bidirectional Encoder Representations from Transform-
049   ers), CNN (Convolutional Neural Networks), XGBoost (Extreme Gradient Boosting), and other
050   models, to refine the task of story point estimation. Through empirical analysis, we aim to show
051   how multimodal Generative AI can significantly advance Agile software development by effectively
052   addressing the complexities associated with story point estimation. Our findings support the adop-
053   tion of these technologies to foster more reliable, consistent, and adaptable development practices,
      setting a new benchmark for future advancements in the field.

      2       RELATED WORK
055
056
      Researchers have extensively studied the field of story point estimation within Agile software de-
057
      velopment, with traditional approaches predominantly relying on expert judgment, historical data
058   analysis, and machine learning techniques such as regression models and decision trees. While
059   useful, these methods often struggle with inconsistencies and inaccuracies due to their reliance on
060   single-modal data inputs, such as text descriptions of user stories (Friedman, 2001). Recent advances
061   in machine learning, particularly with the advent of deep learning and natural language processing
062   (NLP), have introduced more sophisticated approaches. However, even these advanced techniques
063   face limitations in integrating the diverse data types often present in software development processes.
064   One significant development in machine learning has been the adoption of Generative AI models,
065   particularly those based on transformer architectures, to enhance the accuracy of story point esti-
066   mation. Models like BERT (Devlin et al., 2019) and GPT (Brown et al., 2020) have demonstrated
067   promise in processing textual data and capturing the nuances of user stories with a level of detail
068   previously unattainable. However, these models typically focus solely on textual analysis and do
069   not fully exploit the potential of multimodal data integration, limiting their effectiveness in contexts
070   where visual or categorical data are also relevant. Multimodal learning has emerged as a promising
071   approach to overcome these limitations by integrating various data formats such as text, images,
072
      graphs, and categorical data. Research in this domain has shown that multimodal models can cap-
      ture more complex relationships between different types of data, leading to improved performance
073
      in tasks like image captioning (Radford et al., 2021), sentiment analysis (Wang & Deng, 2018), and
074
      medical diagnosis (Wang et al., 2020). Despite these advancements, applying multimodal learning
075   to story point estimation in Agile software development remains underexplored. Our work builds
076   upon these foundations by introducing a Multimodal Generative AI approach that integrates not
077   only textual but also visual and categorical data, thereby creating a more comprehensive and accu-
078   rate estimation model. Unlike previous single-modal methodologies, our framework leverages the
079   strengths of multimodal integration, offering a holistic perspective of user stories and their inher-
080   ent complexities. This approach promises a significant improvement over traditional methods by
081   providing a deeper understanding of the multifaceted aspects of story points.
082   Addressing a critical gap in existing research, our study specifically tailors multimodal learning
083   to the unique challenges of Agile methodologies, which require rapid iteration and adaptability.
084   This customization ensures that our model integrates seamlessly into Agile workflows, delivering
085   real-time, adaptive story point estimates. By extending multimodal learning techniques to Agile
086   story point estimation, our paper advances the state of the art, overcoming previous limitations and
087   illuminating new ways to incorporate diverse data types for more accurate and efficient software
088   development practices. Our research presents a novel framework for integrating multimodal data
089
      into Agile software development, paving the way for more reliable, consistent, and adaptable prac-
      tices. This framework makes a significant contribution to the field, offering a robust solution to the
090
      longstanding challenges of story point estimation.

      3.2   DATA P RE - PROCESSING AND F EATURE E NGINEERING
143
144
      We meticulously pre-processed the raw data for this project to prepare it for use in machine learning
145   models. We refined the text data by removing extraneous details, normalizing the language, and
146   tokenizing the content, while pre-processing the image data involved resizing, normalization, and
147   feature extraction to ensure effective representation of the visual and textual content in the form of
148   embeddings. Our entire corpus consists of 113 observations. For feature extraction and embedding,
149   we utilized BERT (Bidirectional Encoder Representations from Transformers) for text data and CNN
150   (Convolutional Neural Networks) for image data. We chose BERT for its ability to understand the
151   context within user stories, making it ideal for tasks requiring deep semantic comprehension, such
152   as classification or sentiment analysis (Table 2). We selected CNNs for their exceptional ability to
153
      process and analyze visual data. Additionally, we applied ordinal encoding to categorical data such
      as severity and story points, leveraging the inherent order within these categories to enhance model
154
      interpretability. We used Fibonacci sequencing to estimate story points. Ordinal encoding is particu-
155
      larly valuable for encoding categorical features that follow a natural sequence or hierarchy, ensuring
156   that the encoded data accurately reflects the structured relationships inherent in the project’s cate-
157   gories. We integrated these processed features into a multimodal dataset ready for machine learning
158   in the final step. This fusion combined cleaned text, image features, and encoded categorical data
159   into a unified format. To facilitate effective model training, we flattened multi-dimensional arrays
160   into one-dimensional formats and normalized these to ensure a consistent scale across all data types,
161   thereby optimizing the performance of subsequent algorithms. This comprehensive approach to
      data preparation is crucial for accurately predicting and categorizing story points in our models. We

      When we compare the model’s performance with and without severity data, several key trends
312   emerge. The precision, recall, and F1 scores for story point categories 1 and 3 remain consis-

314   3-5). However, excluding severity data leads to a noticeable improvement in overall model accu-
315   racy, which increases from 0.63 to 0.77 (Table 4). This improvement is also reflected across the
316   macro and weighted averages, showing more balanced performance across categories.
317
      Story point category 8, which represents more complex or rare story points, shows significant dif-
318   ferences. With severity data included, the model fails to effectively predict this category, resulting
319   in a precision, recall, and F1 score of 0.00 (Figure 3). However, excluding severity data, the model’s
320   recall for story point category 8 improves to 1.00 (Figure 5), and the F1 score reaches 0.5 (Table 4),
321   though precision remains low at 0.33 (Figure 4). This indicates the model’s ability to identify more
322   complex cases, albeit with some inaccuracies. This comparison suggests that while severity data
323   might add complexity, removing it allows the model to generalize better across different categories,
      particularly improving its performance on rare or complex story points.

      5   CONCLUSION
514
      This research demonstrated a novel approach to story point estimation in Agile software develop-
515
      ment by leveraging a Multimodal Generative AI framework. The integration of text, image, and
516
      categorical data using advanced machine learning techniques such as BERT for text embeddings,
517   CNN for image processing, and XGBoost for classification has shown potential to improve the ac-
518   curacy and consistency of story point predictions. The study’s main findings highlight that while
519   the model performs well in estimating simpler story points, it faces challenges with more complex
520   categories, particularly due to data imbalance and the varying quality of image inputs.
521
      The significance of this work lies in its contribution to the growing field of AI-driven software
522
      project management and development tools. By demonstrating how multimodal data can be effec-
523   tively integrated to provide more nuanced and accurate estimates, this research opens new avenues
524   for enhancing the efficiency of Agile workflows. The ability to more accurately estimate story points
525   has direct implications for project planning, resource allocation, and overall software development
526   efficiency, making this approach highly relevant to both academic research and industry practices.
527   While this study shows promise, further exploration is needed. Addressing data imbalance, refining
528   multimodal inputs, and tailoring AI models to the language and context of software development are
529   key areas for advancement. Future work should focus on these aspects and extend the approach to
530   other project management domains, bringing us closer to fully realizing AI’s potential in transform-
531
      ing Agile software development

      E THICS S TATEMENT

      XGB OOST (E XTREME G RADIENT B OOSTING )
624
625   XGBoost is an optimized distributed gradient boosting library designed to be highly efficient, flex-

627   work, which builds models sequentially, each new model attempting to correct the errors made by
628   the previous ones. XGBoost is used in our research to handle categorical data and produce predic-
629   tions based on the combined inputs from text and image features.
