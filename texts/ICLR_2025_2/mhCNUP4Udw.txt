                                                     A BSTRACT
008
009
                  The potential of the vision modality for enhancing graph structural awareness has
010
                  long been overlooked in the mainstream graph neural network (GNN) community.
011
                  In this paper, we propose a simple yet effective framework called Graph Vision
012               Networks (GVN), which first incorporates vision awareness into Message Passing
013               Neural Network (MPNN) and achieves effective performance for link prediction,
014               highlighting this unexplored but promising direction. Specifically, GVNs transform
015               graph structures into images and extract Visual Structural Features (VSFs) from
016               those images, where VSFs are considered a novel type of structural feature. Similar
017               to previous structural features, VSFs also mitigate the limitations of traditional
018               MPNNs in expressive power and substructure awareness. Additionally, unlike
019               most previous heuristic-based structural features (e.g., common-neighbor-based
020
                  and path-based ones), which typically depend on fixed structural priors, VSFs are
                  adaptive and capable of capturing varying structural insights to better suit different
021
                  scenarios. Extensive experiments across seven commonly used benchmark datasets
022
                  demonstrate that GVNs and their variants can significantly enhance MPNNs in link
023               prediction tasks. Additionally, the straightforward design of the framework makes
024               it highly compatible with current methods, providing additional performance gains
025               to achieve new state-of-the-art performance.

028   1       I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Link prediction is a fundamental task in graph machine learning, and has been widely used across
031   various application domains. Examples include recommendation systems (He et al., 2020), drug
032   interaction prediction (Yamanishi et al., 2008), and knowledge-based reasoning (Bordes et al., 2013).
033   A class of powerful link predictors are the Graph Neural Networks (GNNs), which produce node
034   representations and then aggregate them to link representations for the prediction of link existence.
035   While GNNs are very popular, they suffer from limited expressive power. In particular, they produce
036   the same representations for links involving isomorphic nodes1 (Morris et al., 2019; Xu et al., 2018),
037   and ignores the pairwise structural relations between the two nodes in the target link (Zhang et al.,
038   2021; Chamberlain et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2024). Second, the structure awareness ability of
039   MPNNs is coarse-grained. It can be proved that MPNNs are incapable of counting local structural
040   patterns such as triangles (Chen et al., 2020). Empirically, as will be demonstrated in our experiments,
041
      MPNNs cannot estimate link prediction heuristics such as Common Neighbor Counts (CN) (Barabási
      & Albert, 1999), Resource Allocation (RA) (Zhou et al., 2009), and Adamic Adar (AA) (Adamic &
042
      Adar, 2003).
043
044   To address the aforementioned issues, a number of strategies have been proposed to improve MPNNs
045   for link prediction. One direct approach involves assigning labels or random node features to all
046   nodes, thereby enabling MPNNs to generate distinct node representations for isomorphic nodes and
047
      facilitating the differentiation of links involving such nodes. However, this comes at the cost of
      inductive ability and training convergence (Abboud et al., 2020; Sato et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2021).
048
      A more effective approach involves designing and computing heuristic structural features (HSFs),
049
      also known as labeling tricks, that are derived from the local graph structure. These HSFs supplement
050   MPNNs with more detailed and sophisticated structure characteristics, therefore enhancing expressive
051   power and structural awareness. This approach has shown remarkable success on link prediction. For
052   instance, SEAL (Li et al., 2020) utilizes the shortest path distance (SPD) between the nodes (target

055   as HSFs. More recently, models such as Neo-GNN (Yun et al., 2021), BUDDY (Chamberlain et al.,
056   2022), and NCNC (Wang et al., 2024) leverage different types of one-hop and multi-hop common
057   neighbor information to construct common-neighbor-based HSFs, leading to state-of-the-art (SOTA)
058   link prediction performance.
059
      Despite the outstanding performance, each HSF is derived from a pre-defined structural prior, thus
060
      encapsulating structural information from only one predefined perspective. However, real-world
061   situations are complex and variable, and may demand structural insights from diverse perspectives.
062   For instance, while SEAL and NBFNet (using path-based HSFs) have superior link prediction
063   performance on the planetoid dataset (Yang et al., 2016), they perform even worse than the simple
064   Graph Convolutional Network (GCN) on the ogbl-ddi dataset (Hu et al., 2020), which contains dense
065   graphs and most node pairs are reachable in two hops, making path-based HSFs not sufficiently
066   informative. Consequently, due to the fixed structural insights of HSFs, users are often required to
067   try repeatedly to find the best-suited HSF. Hence, there is a growing demand for methods capable of
068   generating adjustable and adaptive structural features tailored to different application scenarios.
069
      Ideally, this approach should be compatible with existing methods that use fixed HSFs, and provide
      performance enhancements for scenarios with already-identified heuristic preference.
070
071   To achieve this, we propose the Graph Vision Network (GVN), which innovatively utilizes the visual
072   modality to extract dynamic and learnable structural features (called Visual Structural Features,
073   or VSFs) from the visual representations of graphs, thereby enhancing the expressive power and
074   structural awareness of MPNNs. Specifically, GVN first visualizes local graph structures as visual
075
      graph images. A learnable vision encoder is then employed to dynamically extract VSFs from these
      images. Subsequently, the VSFs are integrated into MPNNs through a learnable attention-based
076
      fusion module, which adaptively enhances link prediction for different scenarios. The proposed
077
      GVN framework includes two variants: GVN-Link and GVN-Node, where the latter is particularly
078   designed for large graphs. Due to the simple but effective design, both variants are compatible
079   with existing HSF-based methods. We demonstrate that VSF, as a novel type of structural feature,
080   possesses flexible and comprehensive structural awareness. The extensive experiment results on
081   seven common datasets including challenging large-scale graphs demonstrate both GVN-Link and
082   GVN-Node can significantly enhance traditional MPNN in link prediction (28.20% and 36.15%
083   respectively). Besides, when incorporated into existing methods, both GVN-Link and GVN-Node
084   achieve new state-of-the-art (SOTA) performance.
085   In summary, the contributions of this paper are three-fold.
086
      • We are the first practice to integrate the vision modality into MPNNs for link prediction by
087
        proposing a novel structural feature: visual structural features (VSFs), highlighting a promising
088     direction to combine vision awareness into GNNs.
089
      • By incorporating adaptive VSFs to MPNNs, we propose the GVN framework, which has a simple
090     but effective design and is able to be compatible with existing methods.
091
      • Extensive experiments demonstrate that GVNs significantly enhance MPNNs in link prediction
092     and can achieve SOTA performance by further improving existing methods with vision awareness.

095   2   R ELATED W ORK
096
097   Link Predictor. Link predictors can be divided into three classes: node embedding methods, link
098   prediction heuristics, and MPNN-based link predictors. 1) Node embedding methods (Perozzi et al.,
099
      2014; Tang et al., 2015; Grover & Leskovec, 2016) represent each node as an embedding vector and
      utilize the embeddings of target nodes to predict links. 2) Link prediction heuristics (Liben-Nowell &
100
      Kleinberg, 2003; Barabási & Albert, 1999; Adamic & Adar, 2003; Zhou et al., 2009) create structural
101
      features through manual design. 3) MPNN-based link predictors explicitly model the enclosing
102   subgraphs around the nodes through MPNNs and generate/update node embeddings via the message-
103   passing mechanism, thus fully leveraging node attributes and aggregating node representations.
104   However, the expressive power of naive MPNN architectures is proven to be limited (Zhang et al.,
105   2021), constrained by the 1-WL test (Morris et al., 2019), and they fail to finely perceive substructures
106   like triangles (Chen et al., 2020). To overcome these limitations, more advanced MPNNs are proposed
107   that integrate link prediction heuristics and their extended form as structural features (i.e., HSFs)
      into MPNNs. For instance, SEAL (Zhang & Chen, 2018) incorporates path-based SPD structural

      features into MPNNs, which concatenates the SPD from each node to the target nodes u and v with
109   the node features to form the augmented node features X ′ and apply MPNN on a k-hop subgraph

      Su,v centered around (u, v). Other common-neighbors-based HSFs have also been incorporated
111   into MPNNs. For example, Neo-GNN (Yun et al., 2021) and BUDDY (Chamberlain et al., 2022)
112   use the heuristic function to model high-order common neighbor information. NCNC (Wang et al.,
113   2024) directly concatenates the weighted sum of node representations of common neighbors with the
114   Hadamard product of MPNN representations of u and v.
115   Graph Learning with Vision. Recently, there are a number of explorations on leveraging vision
116   to enhance graph learning. Das et al. (2023) find that the vision modality, combined with a vision-
117   language model (VLM), can outperform GNN baselines for node classification on the planetoid
118   datasets. Wei et al. (2024) shows that the vision modality excels at capturing graph substructures such
119   as local cycles and triangles with the help of VLMs. However, using vision as structural features with
120   MPNNs or integrating vision in link prediction remains unexplored, which is the focus of this paper.

124   Notations. An undirected graph G = (V, E) comprises a set V of n nodes (vertices) and a set E
125   of e links (edges). We denote the adjacency matrix of G by A ∈ Rn×n , where A(u,v) > 0 if and

127   v, and Nk (v) as the set of neighbors of node v within k hops, where a node u ∈ Nk (v) if and only
128   if SPD(u, v) ≤ k. The node feature matrix XG ∈ Rn×F contains the node features in G, where

254   4.3     T IME C OMPLEXITY A NALYSIS
255
      Let n be the number of nodes, d be the maximum node degree, F be the node feature dimension, F ′
256
      be the dimension of node representation produced by MPNN, and l be the number of target links.
257
      The time complexity of GVN-Link is determined by the following components: 1) Complexity of
258   the base model, which includes the MPNN and its associated read-out function. For example, the
259   complexity of GCN is O(ndF + nF 2 ) + O(lF 2 ). For the NCNC model (Wang et al., 2024) that
260   incorporates common-neighbor HSFs, the complexity is O(ndF + nF 2 ) + O(ld2 F + ldF 2 ). We
261   denote this part by O(Base). 2) Complexity of generating visual images for the target links is O(l).
262   3) Complexity of extracting visual structural features with Vision Encoder is O(l). 4) Complexity of
263   linear projection which converts the S dimensional VSFs vuv to the F dimensional ṽuv is O(lSF ) 4)
264   Complexity of the attention mechanism is O(lF 2 ). Therefore, the total time complexity of GVN-Link
265   is O(Base) + O(l) + O(lSF ) + O(lF 2 ) = O(Base) + O(lSF + lF 2 ).
266   For GVN-Node, the difference lies in its use of node-centered subgraph VSFs. Therefore, the
267   complexity of generating visual images for all nodes is O(n), the complexity of the VSF projection
268   becomes O(nSF ′ ), the complexity of the attention mechanism becomes O(nF ′2 ), and the other
269   parts remain the same with GVN-Link. As a result, the total time complexity of GVN-Node is
      O(Base) + O(n) + O(nSF ′ ) + O(nF ′2 ) = O(Base) + O(nSF ′ + nF ′2 ).

      4.4      C OMPARISON BETWEEN GVN-L INK AND GVN-N ODE
271
272   GVN-Link and GVN-Node have their own advantages and disadvantages. First, in most graphs,
273   the number of links l is significantly larger than the number of nodes n (with l having an upper
274   bound of n2 ). Consequently, GVN-Node demonstrates a higher computational efficiency compared
275   to GVN-Link, making it more suitable for large and dense graphs, where GVN-Link can become
276   computationally intensive. Second, the VSFs in GVN-Link include the visual perception of the target
277   link’s neighborhood structure. These VSFs explicitly reveal the pairwise relationship between the two
278   nodes in the target link, which is advantageous for link prediction. Third, the VSFs in GVN-Node
279
      encompass the visual perception of all nodes’ neighborhoods and participate in message passing.
      This allows the VSFs in GVN-Node to capture more structural details and integrate them more
280
      deeply into the link prediction process. In contrast, GVN-Link only provides visual perception of the
281
      substructures surrounding the two nodes in the target link.
282
283   It is worth noting that although the VSFs in GVN-Node do not explicitly model pairwise relationships,
284   the base model can still learn these relationships from the neighborhood connections between nodes,
285
      such as through similar substructures, as a compensation. Furthermore, employing an MPNN method
      with pairwise HSFs, such as NCNC, as the base model can effectively address this limitation.

375
      We delve into the reasons how vision helps MPNN-based link prediction from two aspects:
376
377   Vision alleviates two limitations of MPNNs in link prediction. 1) Distinguish Links with Iso-
      morphic Nodes. As highlighted in Section 1, MPNNs exhibit limited expressive power due to their

      inability to differentiate links with isomorphic nodes (see the illustration in Appendix A), thus
379   producing the same link prediction p(u, v) for these links that have distinct enclosing subgraphs.
380   To illustrate the improvement of GVNs over MPNNs in this regard, we compute the proportion of
381   links that produce the same prediction (i.e., p(u, v)) with at least one other link by GVN-LGCN ,
382   GVN-NGCN , and GCN during link prediction on the non-attributed5 Cora dataset. We expect to
383   find whether there are cases where links with distinct enclosing subgraphs are treated the same by
384   GCN but corrected by VSFs in GVN. Empirically, 6.79% links in GCN share identical predictions
385   with the other links. In contrast, the ratio is only 0.88% (0.94%) for GVN-LGCN (respectively, for
386   GVN-NGCN ). Therefore, the gap between the ratios demonstrates that GVNs make the links with
387
      distinct enclosing subgraphs distinguishable by incorporating VSFs.
388   2) Capture the substructures. Besides, MPNNs are proven to have only coarse-grained structural
389   awareness on substructures like triangles. To evaluate whether GVNs achieve progress in this aspect,
390   we extract 3200 triangles from the Cora dataset and then randomly sample another 3200 non-triangle
391   triplets as negative samples. Each model is then tasked with distinguishing triangles from the negative
392   samples. Empirically, GVN-LGCN achieves an accuracy of 91.88%, and GVN-NGCN achieves
393
      an accuracy of 88.91%, while GCN attains only 63.25%. This underscores the more fine-grained
      structural perception of GVN models compared to traditional MPNNs.
394
395   VSFs encapsulate diverse structural information and can be tailored to specific scenarios.
396

397   (HSFs) that depend on a single structural prior
398   like common-neighbors (e.g., CN, RA, AA)
399   or path information between target nodes (e.g.,

401   sights from multiple perspectives, as illustrated
402   in Figure 2. The adaptability of VSFs allows
403   them to shift focus based on varying scenarios.

531   6    C ONCLUSION
532
533
      We propose the Graph Vision Networks (GVN) framework, which innovatively incorporates vision
534   features as a new type of structural feature, termed visual structural features (VSFs), to enhance
535   MPNNs in link prediction tasks. Unlike previous methods that rely on fixed heuristic structural
536   priors, VSFs are adaptively extracted and fused to suit the current scenario and are also compatible
537   with existing methodologies. Experimental results demonstrate that VSFs are both informative and
538   adaptive, leading to significant performance improvements beyond base models. Building on the
539   previous SOTA model NCNC, both GVN-Link and GVN-Node achieve SOTA performance. In our
      future work, we are interested in extending GVN to other graph tasks.

      In Figure 4, nodes v2 and v3 are isomorphic because of their sym-
705
      metric positions in the graph, and they have the same h-hop neigh-
706   borhoods for any h. Hence, without node features, permutation-
707   equivariant MPNNs produce the same node representations for v2
708   and v3 (i.e., yv2 = yv3 ). As a result, when predicting distinct links
709   (v1 , v2 ) and (v1 , v3 ), the input fed into the Readout function are ex-
710   actly the same (i.e., (yv1 , yv2 ) = (yv1 , yv3 )). Therefore, the same Figure 4: Example graph with
711   predictions are produced for the two links.                                isomorphic nodes.
712   However, the two links can have distinct pairwise structural relations w.r.t. the target node (v1 in this
713   case). For example, v3 is closer to v1 than v2 . This difference in structural relations is overlooked
714   by an MPNN but can be effectively captured by the SPD structural feature (SP D(v1 , v2 ) = 5,
715   SP D(v1 , v3 ) = 2). Similarly, common-neighbor-based heuristics such as CN, RA and AA can also
716   help the MPNN to distinguish the two links (v1 , v2 ) and (v1 , v3 ), as v1 and v2 share no common
717   neighbor while v1 and v3 have one.
718   Therefore, structural features can enhance the expressive power of MPNN in link prediction, by
719   providing extra structural information which are ignored by MPNNs.

787   Link Prediction Setups
788   In link prediction, links play dual roles: serving as supervision and acting as message-passing
789   paths. Following the standard practice in link prediction, training links fulfill both supervision labels
790   and message-passing paths. In terms of supervision, the training, validation, and testing links are
791   mutually exclusive. For message passing, we follow the common setting where the validation links in
792   ogbl-collab additionally function as message-passing paths during test time.
793   For the Planetoid datasets (Cora, Citeseer, and Pubmed), since the official data splits are not available,
794   we adopt the common random splits of 70%/10%/20% for training/validation/testing. For the OGB
795   benchmarks ogbl-collab, ogbl-ppa, ogbl-ddi, and ogbl-citation2 (Hu et al., 2020), we utilize the
796   official fixed splits.
797
      For the baselines, we directly use the results reported in (Wang et al., 2024) since we adopt the same
798
      experimental setup.

803   F.1      I MPLEMENTATIONS OF F USION S TRATEGIES
804
805   “concat" and “mixture of experts" in GVN-Link. In GVN-Link, feature integration occurs after
806
      message passing and the different fusion strategies will affect how to integrate vision structural
      awareness to the output of MPNNs.
807
808   In GVN-Link, the “concat" strategy is achieved by concatenating the link-centered VSF with the
809   target node features directly, and then use the concatenated features as input to the Readout function.
      Thus, for each node pair (u, v) in the target link, their MPNN node representations (yu and yv ) are


                                                                15
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
      updated to ỹu and ỹv as:
811
                                         ỹu = yu ||vuv , ỹv = yv ||vuv ,
812
813   where yuv is the link-centered VSF.
814   For the Mixture of Experts (MoE) fusion strategy, we use a three-layer multilayer perceptron (MLP)
815   as vision-based expert for link prediction. This MLP relies solely on the vision modality by only
816   receiving VSFs as input to output the link prediction probability pvision
                                                                         (u,v) . The MPNN produces another
817   link prediction probability pgraph
                                   (u,v) based on message passing and node attributes. Finally, the mixture-
818                                                                                       graph
      predicted link probability p(u,v) is computed as a weighted sum of pvision
                                                                               (u,v) and p(u,v) , combining the
819
820
      capabilities of both the vision-based expert and the MPNN expert with a learnable weight balance
      parameter δ:
821                                                                    graph
822                                   p(u,v) = δ · pvision
                                                    (u,v) + (1 − δ) · p(u,v) .
823
824   “concat" and “mixture of experts" in GVN-Node In GVN-Node, feature integration occurs on
825
      the node feature before MPNN, and different fusion strategies use different approaches to obtain the
      vision-aware node feature x̃v .
826
827   The “concat" fusion strategy obtains the vision-aware node feature of node v by appending the
828   node-centered VSF vv after the central node’s features xv .
829
                                                   x̃v = xv ||vv .
830
831   For “mixture of experts", we use two linear experts to encode the original node feature xv and the
832   corresponding node-centered VSF vv . Therefore, computation of the vision-aware node feature can
833   be expressed as:
834                             x̃v = δLinearϕ1 (xv ) + (1 − δ)Linearϕ2 (vv ),
835   where δ is a learnable parameter to balance the contributions from the two linear experts, Linear1
836   and Linear2 are linear experts with trainable parameters ϕ1 and ϕ2 .
