011                                                       A BSTRACT
012
013                A neural network with ReLU activations may be viewed as a composition of piece-
014
                   wise linear functions. For such networks, the number of distinct linear regions ex-
                   pressed over the input domain has the potential to scale exponentially with depth,
015
                   but it is not expected to do so when the initial parameters are chosen randomly.
016
                   Therefore, randomly initialized models are often unnecessarily large, even when
017                approximating simple functions. To address this issue, we introduce a novel train-
018                ing strategy: we first reparameterize the network weights in a manner that forces
019                the network to exhibit a number of linear regions exponential in depth. Training
020                first on our derived parameters provides an initial solution that can later be refined
021                by directly updating the underlying model weights. This approach allows us to
022                learn approximations of convex, one-dimensional functions that are several orders
023                of magnitude more accurate than their randomly initialized counterparts. We fur-
024                ther demonstrate how to extend our approach to multidimensional and non-convex
025
                   functions, with similar benefits observed.
026
027   1       I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Beyond complementary advances in areas like hardware, storage, and networking, the success of
030   neural networks is primarily due to their ability to efficiently capture and represent nonlinear func-
031   tions (Gibou et al., 2019). In a neural network, the goal of an activation function is to introduce
032   nonlinearity between the network’s layers so that the network does not simplify to a single linear
033   function. The rectified linear unit (ReLU) has a unique interpretation in this regard. Since it either
034   deactivates a neuron or acts as an identity, the resulting transformation on each individual input re-
035   mains linear. However, each possible configuration of active and inactive neurons can produce a
036   unique linear transformation over a particular region of input space. The number of these activa-
037
      tion patterns and their corresponding linear regions provides a way to measure the expressivity of a
      ReLU network1 and can theoretically scale exponentially with the depth of the network (Montufar
038
      et al., 2014a; Serra et al., 2018). Hence deep architectures may outperform shallow ones.
039
040   Surprisingly, though, a sophisticated theory of how to best encode functions into ReLU networks is
041   lacking, and in practice, adding depth is often observed to help less than one might expect from this
042   exponential intuition. Lacking more advanced theory, practitioners typically use random parameter
043
      initialization and gradient descent, the drawbacks of which often lead to extremely inefficient solu-
      tions. Hanin & Rolnick (2019) show a rather disappointing bound pertaining to randomly initialized
044
      networks: they prove that the average number of linear regions formed upon initialization is entirely
045
      independent of the configuration of the neurons, so depth is not properly utilized. They observed
046   that gradient descent has a difficult time creating new activation regions and that their bounds ap-
047   proximately held after training. As we will discuss later, the number of linear regions is not actually
048   a model property that gradient descent can directly optimize. Gradient descent is also prone to re-
049   dundancy; Frankle & Carbin (2019) show how around 95% of weights may ultimately be eliminated
050   from a network without significantly degrading accuracy.
051
      The present work aims to begin eliminating these inefficiencies, starting in a simple one-dimensional
052   setting. Drawing inspiration from existing theoretical ReLU constructions, our novel contributions
053

      include a special reparameterization of a ReLU network that forces it to maintain an exponential
055   number of activation patterns over the input domain. We then demonstrate a novel pretraining strat-
056   egy, which trains these derived parameters before manipulating the underlying matrix weights. This
057   allows the network to discover solutions that are more accurate and unlikely to be found other-
058   wise. Our technique directly rectifies the issues raised by Hanin & Rolnick (2019). Firstly, we
059   initialize immediately with an exponential number of linear regions, which would not be expected
060   to happen otherwise. Secondly, we are not reliant on gradient descent to “discover” new regions;
061   we only need to maintain the existing ones, which is already guaranteed by the reparameterization
062   during the pretraining stage. Additionally, this pretraining step can avoid areas of the loss landscape
063
      where unassisted gradient descent might make short-sighted optimizations that eliminate activation
      regions. Our results demonstrate that minimizing the reliance of network training on unassisted
064
      gradient descent can reliably produce error values orders of magnitude lower than a traditionally-
065
      trained network of equal size. Although preliminary numerical demonstrations in this theoretical
066   study pertain to relatively low-dimensional functions, the paper concludes with our views on ex-
067   tending these theoretical exponential benefits for ReLU networks to arbitrary smooth functions with
068   arbitrary dimensionality, which would have significant practical utility.

072
073   This work is primarily concerned with a novel training methodology, but it also possesses a signif-
074   icant approximation theoretic component. In one dimension, where we initially focus, our work is
075   a generalization of the approximation to x2 we review in this section. The reparameterization we
076
      employ modifies this method to become trainable to represent other convex one-dimensional func-
      tions, and then converts that result back into a matrix representation. The goal of this method is not
077
      to re-express the full set of neural network weights, but to constrain them into efficient patterns.

      The same effect can be achieved with non-symmetric triangle waves (Huchette et al., 2023) (or any
109   shape that sufficiently “folds” the input space (Montufar et al., 2014b)). Our reparameterization
110   strategy focuses on non-symmetric triangle waves. The location on (0, 1) of a triangular function’s
111   peak acts as an alternative parameterization of the function, instead of its weight matrix representa-
112   tion. Unlike the raw weights of a depth d ReLU network, any values chosen for the triangle peak
113   parameters will result in the creation of 2d linear regions. Furthermore, this parameterization is
114   trainable. By using the peak location to set the raw weights of a layer, the gradients can backprop-
115   agate through the raw weights to update the triangle peaks, effectively confining the network to a
116   subspace of weights that generates many linear regions.
117   The dilated triangular waveforms produced in this manner are not particularly useful on their own.
118   Their oscillations quickly become excessively rapid, and their derviatives do not exist everywhere
119   (especially in the infinite depth limit). But these problems can be rectified by taking a sum over the
120   layers of a network. Yarotsky (2017) and Liang & Srikant (2016) construct y = x2 on [0, 1] with
121   exponential accuracy using symmetric triangle waves. To produce their approximation, one begins
122   with f0 (x) = x, then computes f1 (x) = f0 (x) − T (x)/4, f2 (x) = f1 (x) − T (T (x))/16, f3 (x) =
123
      f2 (x) − T (T (T (x)))/64, and so forth, as pictured in Figure 1. As these successive approximations
      are computed, Figure 1 plots their convergence to x2 , as well as the convergence of the derivative to
124
      2x. Our reparameterization generalizes this approximation to use non-symmetric triangle waves to
125
      approximate a wider class of convex, differentiable, one-dimensional functions.
126
127   The x2 approximation is used by other theoretical works as a building block to guarantee exponential
128   convergence rates in more complex systems. Perekrestenko et al. (2018) construct a multiplication
129
      gate via the identity (x + y)2 = x2 + y 2 + 2xy. The squared terms can all be moved to one side, ex-
      pressing the product xy as a linear combination of squared terms. They then further assemble these
130
      multiplication gates into an interpolating polynomial, which can have an exponentially decreasing
131
      error when the interpolation points are chosen to be the Chebyshev nodes. Polynomial interpo-
132   lation does not scale well into high dimensions, so this and papers with similar approaches will
133   usually come with restrictions that limit function complexity: Wang et al. (2018) requires low input
134   dimension, Montanelli et al. (2020) uses band limiting, and Chen et al. (2019) approximates low-
135   dimensional manifolds. These works all make use of a fixed representation of x2 . If our networks
136   were substituted in for the x2 approximation, these works would provide theoretical guarantees
137   about the capabilities of the resulting model. Even though the approximation rates will not scale
138   well with input dimension, they serve as a bound that can be improved upon. In Section 5 we further
139   elaborate on how to use our networks to represent higher-dimensional or non-convex functions.
140   Other works focus on showing how ReLU networks can encode and subsequently surpass traditional
141   approximation methods (Lu et al., 2021; Daubechies et al., 2022), including spline-type methods
142   (Eckle & Schmidt-Hieber, 2019). Interestingly, certain fundamental themes from above like com-
143   position, triangles, or squaring are still present. Another interesting comparison of the present work
144   is to Ivanova & Kubat (1995), which uses decision trees as a means to initialize sigmoid neural
145   networks for classification. Similar to the spirit of our work, which restricts parameterizations
146
      of ReLU networks, Elbrächter et al. (2019) explores theoretical aspects of the conditionining of
      ReLU network training and provides constructive results for a parameterization space that is well-
147
      conditioned. Chen & Ge (2024) present a creative approach where they explore reparameterizing
148
      the direction of weight vectors using hyperspherical coordinates to improve training dynamics. Un-
149   like their reparameterization, ours will restrict the network’s expressivity in order to prevent it from
150   learning inefficient weight patterns. Lastly, Park et al. (2021) approaches the problem of linear
151   region maximization from an information theory perspective and uses a loss penalty rather than a
152   reparameterization to increase the number of linear regions.

      2.2   N EURAL N ETWORK I NITIALIZATION
155
156   Our work seeks to improve network initialization by making use of explicit theoretical constructs.
157   This stands in sharp contrast the current standard approach, which treats neurons homogeneously.
158   Two popular initialization methods implemented in PyTorch are the Kaiming (He et al., 2015) and
159   Xavier initialization (Glorot & Bengio, 2010). They use weight values that are sampled from dis-
160   tributions defined by the input and output dimension of each layer. Aside from sub-optimal ap-
161   proximation power associated with random weights, a common issue is that the initial weights and
      biases in a ReLU network can cause every neuron in a particular layer to output a negative value.


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      The ReLU activation then sets the output of that layer to 0, blocking any gradient updates. This is
163   referred to as the dying ReLU phenomenon (Qi et al., 2024; Nag et al., 2023). Worryingly, as depth
164   goes to infinity, the dying ReLU phenomenon becomes increasingly likely (Lu et al., 2020). Sev-
165   eral papers propose solutions: Shin & Karniadakis (2020) use a data-dependent initialization, while
166   Singh & Sreejith (2021) introduce an alternate weight distribution called RAAI that can reduce
167   the likelihood of the issue and increase training speed. We observed during our experiments that
168   RAAI greatly reduces, but does not eliminate the likelihood of dying ReLU. Our approach enforces
169   a specific network structure that does not collapse in this manner.

280   4     O NE -D IMENSIONAL E XPERIMENTS
281
282   The goal of deep learning is to train a network to approximate a (generally unknown) nonlinear func-
283   tion. Accordingly, in this section, we implement our method to learn several convex one-dimensional
284   curves. These nonlinear functions are known—so that we can measure error—and we remark and
285   demonstrate results on more difficult functions in Section 5, Section 6, and Appendix A.9. The aim
286   of these experiments is twofold: (1) we would like to determine how to learn the most effective
287
      function representations possible, and (2) to explore how the utilization of an increased number of
      linear regions can affect a network’s ability to capture underlying nonlinearity in its training data.
288
      To demonstrate that our networks can learn function representations that better utilize depth, we
289
      benchmark against PyTorch’s (Paszke et al., 2019) default settings (nn.linear() uses Kaiming
290   initialization), as well as the RAAI distribution from Singh & Sreejith (2021), and produce errors
291   that are orders of magnitude lower than both. For our experimental networks, we use a common set
292   of initialization points where the triangle peaks and scaling parameters are chosen according to our
293   main theorem (Equation 3.1). We compare the effect of reparameterized pretraining against skip-
294   ping immediately to standard gradient descent (from a reparameterized initialization as in Section
295   3). We also compare training the scaling parameters freely during pretraining, instead of choosing
296   them to achieve differentiability. The intent of both comparisons is to see if pretraining and further
297   differentiability constraints facilitate smoother navigation of the loss landscape. Lastly, we conduct
298
      a second round of tests to determine if pretrained networks display an enhanced predictive capacity
      on unseen data points, as might be expected if they can leverage greater nonlinearity in their outputs.

301   4.1   E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
302
      All models are trained using Adam (Kingma & Ba, 2017) as the optimizer with a learning rate of
303
      0.001 for 1,000 epochs to ensure convergence (see Appendix for a brief analysis of different learning
304   rates). Each network is four neurons wide with five hidden layers, along with a one-dimensional
305   input and output. The loss function used is the mean squared error, and the average and minimum
306   loss are recorded for 30 models of each type. The networks unique to this paper share a common set
307   of starting locations using the construction in Section 3, so that the effects of each training regimen
308   are directly comparable. As in related papers (Perekrestenko et al., 2018; Daubechies et al., 2022),
309   we focus for the moment on one-dimensional examples, which are sufficient to demonstrate our
310   proposed theory and methodology. The four curves we approximate are x3 , x11 , tanh(3x), and a
311   quarter period of a sine wave. The curves are chosen to capture a variety of convex one-dimensional
312
      functions. To approximate the sine and the hyperbolic tangent, the triangle waves are added to the
      line y = x. For the other approximations, the waves are subtracted. This requires the first scaling
313
      factor to be a0 ∗ a1 instead of (1 − a0 ) ∗ a1 . The first set of data is 500 evenly spaced points on the
314
      interval [0, 1] for each of the curves. This is chosen to be very dense deliberately, to try to evoke the
315   most accurate representations from each network. We determine from these tests that pretraining
316   with differentiability enforced produces the best results, so we compare it to standard networks in
317   our second set of experiments. We use a second set of data consisting of only 10 points, with a test
318   set of 10 points spaced in between so as to be as far away from learned data as possible. The goal of
319   this set of experiments is to compare the predictive capacities of the networks on unseen data.

523   6    C ONCLUDING R EMARKS
524
525   This paper focused on exploiting the potential computational complexity advantages neural net-
526   works offer for the problem of efficiently learning nonlinear functions; in particular, compelling
527   ReLU networks to approximate functions with exponential accuracy as network depth is linearly
528   increased. Our results showed improvements of one to several orders of magnitude in using our
529   initialization and pretraining strategy to train ReLU networks to learn various nonlinear functions,
530   including non-convex and multi-dimensional functions. This finding is particularly powerful since
531
      random initialization and gradient descent are not likely to produce an efficient solution on their
      own, even if it can be proven to exist in the set of sufficiently sized ReLU networks. Although we
532
      proposed one preliminary strategy for extending our network for higher-dimensional functions, this
533
      strategy has limitations, such as leaving many weights to be filled in by gradient descent (which it
534   generally does not do effectively). We anticipate continuing investigating strategies for improving
535   the generalizability of our work, which we believe is critically important since it offers the possibility
536   of an exponentially more accurate drop-in replacement of linear layers in any architecture. We note
537   that there are other metrics for evaluating a neural network’s expressivity (e.g., Raghu et al. (2017)),
538   which would be interesting to measure for our networks in future work. Overall, we are hopeful
539   that future works by our group and others will help illuminate a complete theory for harnessing the
      potential exponential power of depth in ReLU and other classes of neural networks.

      A.2   T ERMINOLOGY
757
758   Before presenting further formal mathematical details of our method, we first briefly review a
759   few pieces of basic ReLU network terminology used in the paper. The reader is referred to the
760   Chmielewski-Anders (2020) for an excellent alternative presentation of these terms.
761
      An activation pattern is a boolean mask that tracks which neurons in a network have their output
762   zeroed by ReLU activations. The activation regions of a ReLU network are connected (and can be
763   shown to be convex) sets of inputs on which the activation pattern is constant. Since the action of the
764   ReLU activation function is constant, the network output over an activation region is equivalent to the
765   case where there is no activation function and the associated zeroed neurons are absent; therefore, the
766   network output behaves linearly over the inputs in the activation region. Relatedly, a linear region
767   is a set of inputs on which the network output behaves linearly with respect to its inputs. It may
768   consist of multiple neighboring activation regions. Another important concept is the boundary of a
769   neuron, as described in Rolnick & Kording (2020): the set of inputs for which the neuron outputs
770
      0, independently of ReLU. The boundary of a neuron is precisely the boundary of the activation
      regions it adds to the network. Chen & Ge (2024) refers to this set as the characteristic activation
771
      boundary since these are the boundaries of the activation regions.
772
773   We note that although works like ours are theoretical papers that leverage these concepts, studying
774   these ideas can lead to interesting applied learning research. For instance, Rolnick & Kording (2020)
775   leverages theoretical works like those cited in our related work discussion for an exciting privacy
776

       Here we present a few preliminary results on extending our pretraining technique to classification
1169
       problems and real-world datasets. The classification problem we chose is the classic two spirals
1170   dataset, and we selected the UCI dataset “Concrete Compressive Strength” (Yeh, 1998) for our real-
1171   world regression task. The concrete dataset has 8 numerical features that can be used to predict the
1172   compressive strength of a concrete sample.
1173
       The networks are set up as described before, with our 4-neuron-wide networks acting as 1D to 1D
1174
       activation functions inside randomly initialized standard linear layers. In these experiments, we use
1175
       one “hidden layer” of our networks (which we choose to have depth 5 in our tests). The geometric
1176   interpretation of this is that we are setting up one-dimensional convex functions oriented in random
1177   directions on the input space, and then taking a linear combination of them as the network output.
1178   In the case of classification, the loss function is simply swapped for cross entropy.
1179
       After our pretraining phase, there are two choices of how to conduct the second phase of training
1180
       (training matrix entries directly). All the parameters could be freed from constraints (“dense”),
1181
       or the smaller 4-neuron subnetworks could be kept isolated from each other (but otherwise have
1182   their parameters freed). In the case where the 4-neuron networks remain in isolation, the weight
1183   matrices of the hidden layers will have a block diagonal structure (block size 4—the width of each
1184   subnetwork). Thus, we consider two fair (same number of free parameters) comparisons in this
1185   subsection: (1) dense versions of our and Kaiming-initialized networks, and (2) block diagonal
1186   versions of our and Kaiming-initialized networks. As discussed below and in the figure captions, we
1187   find that while the dense variant of our method ties or is slightly worse than a regular fully-connected
       network of the same dimensions, in the block diagonal case, our experimental networks significantly

1191   7) get filled in with noise by gradient descent during the second training stage. This again highlights

       Our two-step training approach adds some practical challenges, such as deciding when to switch
1248
       parameterizations for optimal convergence time. Additionally, since the optimization variables are
1249   different, the optimizer will have to restart any momentum or adaptive learning rates when the switch
1250   is made, which can sometimes cause loss to temporarily spike. We lowered the learning rate on the
1251   second step to avoid this. Further interesting optimizations of our approach (e.g., training another
1252   network to inform our algorithm when to switch parameterizations) are imagined as future work.
