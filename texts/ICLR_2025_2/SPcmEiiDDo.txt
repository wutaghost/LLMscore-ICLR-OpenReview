009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Training adversarial agents to attack neural network policies has proven to be both
012            effective and practical. However, we observe that existing methods can be further
013            enhanced by distinguishing between states leading to win or lose and encouraging
014            the policy training to prioritize winning states. In this paper, we address this gap
015            by introducing an episodic control-based approach for adversarial policy train-
016
               ing. Our method extracts the historical evaluations for states from historical ex-
               periences with an episodic memory, and then incorporating these evaluations into
017
               the rewards to improve the adversarial policy optimization. We evaluate our ap-
018
               proach using two-player competitive games in MuJoCo simulation environments,
019            demonstrating that our method establishes the most promising attack performance
020            and defense difficulty against the victims among the existing adversarial policy
021            training techniques.

024   1   I NTRODUCTION
025
026   It has been proved that deep reinforcement learning (DRL) policies are vulnerable to adversarial
027
      attacks (Huang et al., 2017; Kos & Song, 2017). Most existing attacks on DRL policies are executed
      by searching the adversarial examples and manipulating the environment (Huang et al., 2017; Kos &
028
      Song, 2017; Nguyen & Reddi, 2019). However, such adversarial examples may not be applicable in
029
      the real world (Gleave et al., 2020). Recently, training adversarial agents as attackers to DRL policies
030   in two-player games has been proven effective and practical (Gleave et al., 2020; Wu et al., 2021;
031   Guo et al., 2021; Bui et al., 2022). These kind of attacks first reduce the two-player environments
032   to single-player environments by fixing the victim agents, and then train the other agent to be an
033   adversarial agent which can be trained by conventional single-agent policy training method. Known
034   as adversarial policy training, these attacks generate natural observations that are adversarial to the
035   victim agents, achieving significant results.
036   While the aforementioned adversarial policy training methods have proven effective, there is still
037   room to improve adversarial training by exploring how states influences game outcomes. We believe
038   that utilizing the adversarial agent’s historical experiences can enable policy training to distinguish
039   between states that lead to win or lose and facilitate the learning of winning states through reward
040   adjustment, leading to an improvement in the effectiveness of adversarial policy training. In this
041   paper, we introduce a novel adversarial policy training approach that leverages the analysis of infor-
042   mation from past episodes to assess game states and adjust rewards, thereby assisting the adversarial
043   agent in achieving better performance.
044   Technically, we propose an episodic control-based adversarial policy training method for two-player
045   competitive games. Inspired by previous works on improving the performance of DRL using
046   episodic control (Blundell et al., 2016; Pritzel et al., 2017; Li et al., 2023), our method develops
047   a neural network-based episodic memory to store historical experiences. We utilize this episodic
048   memory to generate historical evaluations, which are used for reward revision. In our experiments,
049   we evaluate our method on two-player competitive games in MuJoCo domains (Todorov et al., 2012)
050
      and compare it with state-of-the-art adversarial policy training approaches (Gleave et al., 2020; Guo
      et al., 2021; Wu et al., 2021). Our experimental results show that our method establishes the most
051
      promising attack performance and defense difficulty.
052
053   In summary, this paper presents four contributions. First, we propose an episodic control-based
      adversarial policy learning method for two-player competitive games. Second, we introduce a


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      method for generating state evaluation from historical experiences, implemented through our pro-
055   posed episodic memory. Third, we propose a reward revision approach to incorporate the historical
056   evaluations into the rewards. Fourth, our work demonstrates that by identifying and highlighting
057   the winning states with historical experiences, adversarial agents can achieve higher winning rates
058   against fixed victim agents and possess the capability to integrate multiple winning strategies to
059   defeat the victims.

      2.1   A DVERSARIAL ATTACKS ON DRL P OLICIES
065
066
      Previous attacks against DRL policies mainly focus on manipulating the environment to fail the
067
      victim agents. One type of attack focuses on perturbing the victim’s observations, forcing its policy
068
      network to output sub-optimal actions, and thus failed the victim agent (Russo & Proutiere, 2019;
069   Sun et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2021; Madry et al., 2018; Pattanaik et al., 2018; Pan et al., 2022; Zhao
070   et al., 2020). Another kind of attack directly perturbs the trajectory of the victim, specifically actions
071   the victim agent takes (Lee et al., 2020; Pan et al., 2022) or the rewards it receives (Ma et al., 2019;
072   Yang et al., 2019; Lykouris et al., 2021) to effectively attack the victim. However, the above attacks
073   are argued to be unrealistic since the real-world environment can not be manipulated (Gleave et al.,
074   2020; Guo et al., 2021; Wu et al., 2021).
075   Unlike the above attacks, to simulate the real-world scenarios, Gleave et al. has successfully trained
076   adversarial agents by PPO algorithm (Schulman et al., 2017) in two-player competitive games un-
077   der a strict zero-sum assumption and demonstrated the effectiveness of training adversarial agents
078   against fixed black-box victims (Gleave et al., 2020). Wu et al. further improved the attack per-
079   formance by exploring the minimal observation differences of the shared environment to maximize
080   deviations of the victim actions (Wu et al., 2021). Guo et al. relaxed the zero-sum assumptions
081   of previous works and demonstrated that such attack could be achieved by maximizing the gap be-
082   tween the adversary and victim rewards which are approximated by observations of the adversarial
083
      agent (Guo et al., 2021). On the other hand, Bui et al. adopted imitators of the victim policies
      learned by imitation learning algorithms (e.g., GAIL (Ho & Ermon, 2016)) to roll out the victim
084
      actions for the attacker and reached better performances (Bui et al., 2022). However, this attack is
085
      based on the specification that the victim’s actions are visible and accessible to the imitators.
086
087   This paper adopts the same setting as (Gleave et al., 2020; Guo et al., 2021; Wu et al., 2021),
088   wherein we have control solely over the adversarial agent and treat the victim agent as a black box,
089
      rendering its observations, actions, and rewards inaccessible. Meanwhile, unlike (Gleave et al.,
      2020; Guo et al., 2021; Wu et al., 2021), we concentrate on leveraging the historical experiences
090
      from adversarial agents to emphasize the winning states to improve the adversarial policy training.
091
092
093   2.2   M ODEL -F REE E PISODIC C ONTROL
094
095
      Episodic Control (Lengyel & Dayan, 2007) has demonstrated the effectiveness of utilizing past
096   experiences to address sample inefficiency in various tasks, such as multi-agent tasks (Zheng et al.,
097   2021), model-based reinforcement learning (Le et al., 2021), and continuous control (Zhang et al.,
098   2019; Kuznetsov & Filchenkov, 2021). Previous works primarily adopt a tabular episodic memory to
099   save experiences of past scenes, leveraging the information gained during exploration and retrieving
100   past experiences of similar scenes to expedite policy optimization (Blundell et al., 2016). This
101   memory uses the state as a key and a measurement of the state (e.g., the Q-value of the state) as
102   the value, storing these key-value pairs. Then, a distance-based analysis (e.g., KNN) is adopted to
103   retrieve a summary statistic of similar states from the episodic memory, and the retrieved statistic
104
      can be used to guide the training process (Hansen et al., 2018).
105   In this paper, we adopts the episodic control to generate historical evaluations to measure the quality
106   of the states. Unlike the episodic memory proposed by previous works, our method proposes a neural
107   network-based episodic memory which uses state sequences as the basis of historical experiences
      analysis and predict historical evaluations for states based on the learned experiences.

138   3.1   A DVERSARY IN T WO - PLAYER M ARKOV G AME E NVIRONMENT
139
      A two-player Markov game environment can be modeled as E = (S, (Aα , Aν ), T, (Rα , Rν )). Here,
140
      we use α and ν to represent the adversary and victim respectively. S represents a state set, and both
141
      Aα and Aν are action sets. T denotes a joint state transition function T : S × Aα × Aν → ∆(S),
142   where ∆(S) is a probability distribution on S. The reward function Ri : S × Aα × Aν × S → R
143   depends on the current state, actions taken by both agents and the next state.
144
145
      Gleave et al. discovered that by fixing the victim agents, the two-player competitive games can be
      reduced to single-player games. In such environment, the other agent can be trained as an adversarial
146
      agent with conventional single-agent policy training methods (e.g., PPO) to attack the victim (Gleave
147
      et al., 2020). The training for the adversarial agent to defeat the fixed victim agent is called adver-
148   sarial policy training. Under this setting, the two-player game reduces to a single-player MDP:
149                          ′
      Eα = (S, Aα , Tα , Rα    ) as the victim policy can be treated as a part of the environment. S be-
150   comes the state set of the adversary and the state transition function and reward function change to
                                          ′
151   Tα : S × Aα × S → ∆(S) and Rα          : S × Aα × S → R.
152
      Our threat model We use the same setting as Gleave et al. and assumes that our threat model
153
      has control over the adversarial agent and black-box access to the information of the victim agent.
154   The adversarial agent can only interact with the environment, which is the common practice in
155   adversarial policy training.

157   3.2   R EWARD R EVISION BASED ON H ISTORICAL E XPERIENCES
158
                     ′
159   The reward Rα    obtained by the adversarial agent only includes the evaluations from a single game
160   and does not contain evaluations from the historical experiences. To enhance the adversarial policy
161   training, our key insight is to integrate state evaluations from historical experiences into the rewards,
      thereby providing the adversarial policy with more comprehensive rewards for learning.

162
      As a two-player Markov game is not deterministic, past episodes starting from the same state may
163   include different state sequences. Therefore, we use state sequences, referred to as patterns, as
164   the basis for performance evaluation in past episodes. Our choice of utilizing state sequence for
165   performance evaluation aligns with existing research (Sutton & Barto, 2018; Li et al., 2023), which
166   has proved that failures in adversarial games are often the result of a series of poor decisions rather
167   than isolated states. Based on the qualification of these patterns from historical experiences, we can
168   assign higher rewards to states that lead to good patterns and lower rewards to states that lead to bad
169   patterns, thereby integrating evaluations from historical experiences into the rewards.
170
171   3.2.1    E VALUATION OF PATTERN P ERFORMANCE
172
      As mentioned above, a pattern refers to a sequence of states within an episode. For a given episode
173   e, a k-step pattern starting from time step t, denoted as pt , refers to k consecutive states in e, i.e.,
174   pt = st , . . . , st+k−1 . For example, p1 = s1 , . . . , sk exemplifies a k-step pattern from the first state
175   s1 . Notice that a state can be considered a special case of a pattern, specifically a 1-step pattern.
176
      To evaluate the past performance of the patterns, we propose that a pattern can be considered high-
177
      performing if the past episodes containing this pattern result in more wins than losses. Conversely,
178
      a pattern can be considered poor-performing if the past episodes containing it result in more losses
179   than wins. Based on this idea, we introduce a historical score for each pattern to quantify its past
180   performance, defined as the average cumulative reward received in past episodes that include that
181   pattern. The cumulative reward for an episode, which quantifies the adversarial agent’s performance,
182   tends to be higher in episodes where the agent wins than in those it loses. As the performance
183   of adversarial agents improves, these cumulative rewards increase. Therefore, using the average
184   cumulative reward of episodes including the pattern effectively reflects its past performance. The
185   historical score h score(pt ) can be calculated as:
186                                             h score(pt ) = M (pt ),                                         (1)
187
      where M is an episodic memory we proposed to collect and analyze patterns in past episodes. In
188
      Section 3.3.1, we will further explain how we implement the episodic memory.

      As we stated in Section 3.2.1, we propose an episodic memory to generate historical scores for
224
      patterns based on the historical experiences. The architecture of our episodic memory, as shown
225
      in Figure 1, includes an LSTM network followed by a multi-layer perceptron (MLP). The LSTM
226   encodes the patterns into abstract vectors, while the MLP aggregates these encodings to produce
227   historical scores for the patterns.
228
229
      During the training, new episodes are used to update the episodic memory. Assume a newly pro-
      duced episode e has a cumulative reward of R and contains the set of patterns P . The episodic
230
      memory M is then trained to map patterns in P to the new cumulative reward R. Note that for a
231
      pattern in P , the learning target is the historical score of the pattern, which is not a fixed value and
232   will change as new episodes occur. Then, during the reward revision, M predicts historical scores
233   for the patterns as Equation 1 to generate historical evaluations for reward revision. More details of
234   our implementation of the episodic memory can be found in Appendix A.2.

      position so that the adversarial actions may not be effective. We show the performances of our

      adversarial agents and baseline agents against masked victims in Table 1. We observe a significant
411   decline in the non-loss rates of the three baseline agents, while our agent maintains a high non-loss
412   rate against the masked victim in YouShallNotPassHumans. This could be attributed to the fact
413   that our agent not only relies on adversarial actions to attack the victim, but also incorporates non-

479
480   As stated in Section 4.1, our method utilizes PPO algorithm as the single-agent training method
481   to train the adversarial agents. It is also important to note that our method is scalable and can be
482   applied to various DRL algorithms. In the Appendix, we demonstrate the performances of our ap-
483   proach applied to the baseline algorithms (Wu et al., 2021; Guo et al., 2021) and compare the results
484   with those of the original baseline algorithms in YouShallNotPassHumans and KickAndDefend. The
485   results show that by leveraging historical evaluations to revise the rewards, the performances of all
      baseline approaches get improved.

486
      In this paper, we propose an episodic control-based adversarial policy training method to train an
487   adversarial agent more effectively and efficiently. Our method introduces an episodic memory to
488   utilize the historical experiences to generate historical evaluations for the states and consequently
489   revise the rewards of the states based on the evaluation, thereby integrating the historical evaluations
490   into the rewards used for adversarial training to emphasize the high-performing states. In our ex-
491   periments, we demonstrate that agents trained with our approach achieve the most promising attack
492   performance and defense difficulty. Additionally, by comparing the behaviors of adversarial agents,
493   we discover that our attack method can explore optimal strategies by integrating multiple winning
494   approaches. We believe our exploration of game states and use of historical experiences advance ad-
495
      versarial policy training methods. Future research could focus on extracting more specific insights
      from these historical experiences to further enhance the effectiveness of adversarial learning.
496
497   Limitation Our method is designed for two-player competitive game environments. For other envi-
498   ronments, the reward revision needs to be redesigned.
