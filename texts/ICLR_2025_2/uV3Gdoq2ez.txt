011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated wide-ranging applications
014            across various fields and have shown significant potential in the academic peer-
015
               review process. However, existing applications are primarily limited to static re-
               view generation based on submitted papers, which fail to capture the dynamic
016
               and iterative nature of real-world peer reviews. In this paper, we reformulate the
017
               peer-review process as a multi-turn, long-context dialogue, incorporating distinct
018            roles for authors, reviewers, and decision makers. We construct a comprehensive
019            dataset containing over 30,854 papers with 110,642 reviews collected from the
020            top-tier conferences. This dataset is meticulously designed to facilitate the ap-
021            plications of LLMs for multi-turn dialogues, effectively simulating the complete
022            peer-review process. Furthermore, we propose a series of metrics to evaluate the
023            performance of LLMs for each role under this reformulated peer-review setting,
024            ensuring fair and comprehensive evaluations. We believe this work provides a
025            promising perspective on enhancing the LLM-driven peer-review process by in-
026
               corporating dynamic, role-based interactions. It aligns closely with the iterative
               and interactive nature of real-world academic peer review, offering a robust foun-
027
               dation for future research and development in this area.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Academic paper peer-review is a critical component of the academic publishing system, ensuring
033   the quality of scientific research. Despite its essential role, the traditional peer-review process faces
034   significant criticism (Morris et al., 2023; Shah, 2022; Liu & Shah, 2023) for its inefficiency, bias,
035   and lack of transparency. While applying LLMs in peer-review presents a promising solution, re-
036   cent studies have demonstrated the potential of LLMs in generating high-quality reviews for given
037   papers (Robertson, 2023; Liang et al., 2023; D’Arcy et al., 2024). However, existing research pri-
038   marily focuses on generating static reviews based on submitted papers, which severely simplifies
039
      the process and fails to capture the dynamic and iterative nature of real-world peer reviews. In this
      work, as shown in Figure 1, we offer a perspective on the complete peer-review process based on
040
      realistic scenarios by reformulating it as a multi-turn, long-context dialogue involving three distinct
041
      roles: authors, reviewers, and decision makers. This reformulation includes several key aspects:
042
043    • Long-Context: The entire dialogue is grounded in the extensive context of the paper, ensuring
044      that all interactions of different roles are informed by the full scope of the manuscript.
045    • Multi-Turn: The dialogue is conducted over multiple rounds, mimicking the real world where
046      reviewers write reviews based on the paper, authors provide rebuttals to the reviews, reviewers
047      respond to rebuttals, and decision makers make decisions based on the comprehensive exchange.
048
       • Role-Based: Each role in the dialogue has specific responsibilities. Reviewers critically evaluate
049      the paper and provide feedback, authors respond to this feedback to clarify their work, and
050      decision makers synthesize the dialogue to make an informed publication decision.
051
052   With these principles in mind, we constructed a comprehensive dataset named ReviewMT, sourced
053   from multiple venues including the top-tier AI conference ICLR and NeurIPS. This dataset is metic-
      ulously designed to embody the dynamic, iterative nature of the peer-review process. By incorporat-

097   2.1    I NSTRUCTION T UNING DATASET
098
099
      Instruction tuning is a specialized training process applied to LLMs to enhance their ability to follow
      specific instructions and perform designated tasks with greater precision and reliability. Instruction
100
      tuning datasets, which are collections of task-specific examples paired with explicit instructions,
101
      play a crucial role in this process. Early efforts in this field, such as Dolly (Conover et al., 2023)
102   and InstructGPT (Ouyang et al., 2022), relied heavily on manual or expert annotations. Over time,
103   the field has seen the emergence of semi-automated and fully automated approaches for instruction
104   creation. These approaches have transformed existing datasets and facilitated more efficient train-
105   ing of LLMs (Chowdhery et al., 2023; Sanh et al., 2021; Chung et al., 2024). A notable example
106   is Stanford Alpaca (Taori et al., 2023), which employs a bootstrapping technique grounded in a
107   set of handcrafted instructions to generate 52,000 diverse instructions. This approach has inspired
      the development of model-aided data collections, such as Baize (Xu et al., 2023), COIG (Zhang


                                                            2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      et al., 2023), and UltraChat (Ding et al., 2023), enabling automatic data generation and reducing
109   the need for human effort (Honovich et al., 2022; Nayak et al., 2024; Rajpurkar et al., 2016; Wang
110   et al., 2018). Despite these advancements, the majority of instruction-tuning datasets still emphasize
111   single-turn interactions (Bai et al., 2024; Ou et al., 2023), lacking the multi-turn, long-context dia-
112   logues that are essential for tasks like peer reviews. This limitation highlights an ongoing challenge
113   in the field as it evolves towards more complex and nuanced interactions.
114
115   2.2   LLM IN R EVIEW
116
117   LLMs have demonstrated significant potential in reviewing and comprehending complex arti-
118   cles (Goldberg et al., 2023; Kuznetsov et al., 2024; Rastogi et al., 2024). Early studies (Robertson,
119   2023) suggested that GPT-generated reviews are comparable to those of human reviewers. By com-
120   paring reviews generated by humans and GPT models for academic papers submitted to a major
121   machine learning conference, it was initially demonstrated that LLMs can effectively contribute to
122   the peer review process. Further research (Liang et al., 2023) revealed that GPT-4’s feedback had
123
      a substantial overlap with human reviewers, with over half of the users rating GPT-4’s feedback as
      helpful, underscoring the growing role of LLMs in the peer review process. MARG (D’Arcy et al.,
124
      2024) employs multiple LLM instances to internally discuss and assign sections of a paper to dif-
125
      ferent agents, providing comprehensive feedback across the entire text, even for papers exceeding
126   the model’s context size. Concurrently, AgentReview (Jin et al., 2024) focuses on analyzing LLM-
127   generated reviews, taking into account factors like privacy concerns, while AI Scientist (Lu et al.,
128   2024) leverages GPT-4 to automate the peer review process for scientific papers. SEA (Yu et al.,
129   2024) introduces an automated paper reviewing framework including standardization, evaluation,
130   and analysis modules. While existing research focuses on simply generating reviews, we aim to
131   simulate the complete review process into a multi-round dialogue, emphasizing the iterative nature
132   of real-world peer review.

135
136
      In existing works on LLM-based peer review research, the focus is primarily on generating a static
137
      review R for a given paper P by a reviewer R. This process can be formulated as follows:
138
139                                                R : P → R,                                              (1)
140
141   where R is implemented by an LLM Fθ parameterized by θ. This process is typically conducted in
142   a single turn, with the reviewer R providing feedback on the paper P without further interaction.
143   In our peer-review framework, we extend this process to a multi-turn dialogue with three distinct
144   roles: Reviewer, Author, and Decision Maker. Each role has specific objectives and interactions:
145
146    • Reviewer (R): The reviewer is responsible for generating an initial review Ri for the paper P
147      in the first turn, which includes a critical assessment of the paper and questions for the author to
148      address: Ri : P → R. It is worth noting that there are N reviewers for each paper P . After
149      the author responds with rebuttals Ai in the second turn, the reviewer evaluates the author’s
150
         response and generates a final review Ri0 , which reflects their updated opinion on the paper after
         considering the author’s clarifications and revisions: Ri : Ai → Ri0 .
151
152    • Author (A): The author plays a crucial role in the second turn by responding to the initial review
153      {Ri }Ni=1 provided by each reviewer. The author carefully addresses the reviewer’s comments,
154      clarifies misunderstandings, and outlines any changes or improvements made to the paper in
155      response to the feedback. The rebuttal Ai serves to defend the paper’s validity and significance
156      while showing a willingness to incorporate constructive criticism: A : {Ri }N              N
                                                                                     i=1 → {Ai }i=1 .
157    • Decision Maker (D): The decision maker synthesizes the entire dialogue, including the pa-
                                                                                                       0 N
158      per P , the initial review {Ri }N                                N
                                         i=1 , the author’s rebuttal {Ai }i=1 , and the final review {Ri }i=1 ,
159      to make an informed decision D. This role is pivotal in the fourth turn, where the decision
160      maker evaluates the coherence and validity of the arguments presented by both the reviewer
161      and the author to reach a final decision on whether the paper should be accepted or rejected:
         D : {Ri , Ai , Ri0 } → D.

      4.1    DATA S OURCE
175
176   The primary source of ReviewMT dataset was the OpenReview platform (Soergel et al., 2013),
177   specifically drawing from the International Conference on Learning Representations (ICLR) (confer-
178   ence organizers, a) and the Conference on Neural Information Processing Systems (NeurIPS) (con-
179   ference organizers, b), both of which are prestigious and widely recognized in the fields of artificial
180   intelligence. These conferences serve as leading venues for cutting-edge research and provide a
181   rich collection of papers and peer reviews across a broad range of AI topics. The openly accessible
182
      and detailed peer reviews available on OpenReview make these conferences especially valuable as
      data sources, offering diverse and high-quality review data that can be leveraged for constructing a
183
      comprehensive peer-review dataset. The breadth and depth of feedback provided in these reviews
184
      ensure that the dataset captures a wide range of evaluative criteria, critiques, and insights, making it
185   an ideal foundation for advancing research in automated review generation and evaluation tasks.
186
187   4.2    DATA P ROCESSING
188
189   As depicted in Figure 2, the data collection process for the ReviewMT dataset involved sourcing
190   papers from ICLR covering the years 2017 to 2024 and from NeurIPS for the years 2021 to 2023. To
191   facilitate this process, we utilized the official ICLR API (OpenReview) to efficiently extract meta-
192   data, such as titles and abstracts, for each paper. For full-text extraction, we employed the software
193   tool Marker (Paruchuri), which converts PDFs into text while preserving the original document
194
      structure and formatting using markdown syntax.
195   The dataset construction was specifically designed to capture the nuanced, multi-turn interactions
196   inherent in the peer review process. Each paper is associated with a comprehensive set of fields that
197   reflect the full cycle of the review process, from initial submission to final decision. Specifically, the
198   dataset includes fields for each turn of the dialogue: “Title”, “Abstract”, and “Main Text” provide
199   the long context; “Summary”, “Strengths”, “Weaknesses”, and “Questions” are included in the first
200
      turn for reviewers to write the initial review for a given paper; “Response” is in the second turn for
      authors to address each reviewer; “Final comment” and “Score” are in the third turn for reviewers to
201
      provide the final review and assign a score; and “Meta review” and “Final decision” are in the fourth
202
      turn for decision makers to make the final publication decision. All the files are stored in JSON
203   format to ensure easy access and compatibility with various programming languages and tools.
204
205   It is important to note that the review process may vary even within the same conference across
206
      different years. For instance, in certain years, the initial review phase may omit an explicit numerical
      rating for the paper, whereas other years include this evaluation. In such instances, we adapted
207
      our data collection methodology by focusing exclusively on the final rating provided later in the
208
      review cycle. By meticulously collecting and organizing this data, the ReviewMT dataset aims to
209   provide a comprehensive resource that captures the iterative nature of the peer review process. The
210   resulting interactions modeled in the dataset are expected to drive more nuanced LLM applications
211   in academic peer review, promoting constructive feedback mechanisms in scholarly publishing.

      5.1   E VALUATION M ETRICS
364
365   We introduce a comprehensive set of evaluation metrics tailored to the peer-review dialogue. These
366   metrics are designed to evaluate the quality of text replies and the validity of responses.
367
368
      (1) Text quality evaluation For all text replies, including the reviewers’ initial reviews, the authors’
      responses, the reviewers’ final reviews, and the decision makers’ meta reviews, we employ text
369
      similarity metrics to assess the quality of the generated text. These metrics include:
370
371    • BLEU-2 and BLEU-4 (Papineni et al., 2002): Measures n-gram precision by comparing the
372
         generated text to a reference text, focusing on 2-gram and 4-gram overlaps respectively.
373    • ROUGE-1, ROUGE-2, and ROUGE-L (Lin, 2004): Measures f1-score of unigram, bigram,
374
         and longest common subsequence overlaps between the generated text and the reference text.
375
       • METEOR (Banerjee & Lavie, 2005): A measure of alignment between the generated and refer-
         ence texts, incorporating stemming and synonymy for more sensitivity to variations in wording.
376
377    • BERT Score (Zhang et al., 2020): Evaluates the similarity between the generated and reference
         texts using contextual embeddings from BERT (Devlin et al., 2018).


                                                         7
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


378
      (2) Validity of response Given the long-context nature of peer-review documents, which average
379   over 20,000 tokens per paper, LLMs may occasionally fail to provide valid responses. To address
380   this, we use the following hit rates to evaluate the validity of the responses:
381
       • Paper hit rate (P-hr): Measures whether the LLM-generated response addresses the paper con-
382
         tent. If the LLM fails to respond to the paper, the hit rate is 0.
383
384
       • Review hit rate (R-hr): Evaluates whether the LLM-generated final review includes a score. If
         the LLM fails to provide the score, the hit rate is 0.
385
386    • Decision hit rate (D-hr): Assesses whether the LLM-generated decision includes a clear accept
387
         or reject outcome. If the LLM fails to respond with a decision, the hit rate is 0.
388
      (3) Score and decision evaluation To evaluate the accuracy of the final review scores provided by
389
      the reviewers and the decisions (accept or reject) made by the decision makers, we use:
390
391    • Mean Absolute Error (MAE): Measures the average absolute difference between the scores
392
         given by the LLM and the actual scores provided by human reviewers.
393    • F1-score: Combines precision and recall to measure the binary classification of decisions.
394
395
      (4) Human evaluation To ensure a robust assessment of the quality of reviews, author responses,
      and decision-maker comments, we incorporate blind human evaluation as a key component of our
396
      experimental setup. We engage five experienced reviewers, all of whom have extensive expertise in
397
      peer reviewing for top-tier AI/ML conferences. They are tasked with evaluating the outputs of the
398   LLMs, including the initial peer reviews (H-R), the author rebuttals (H-A), and the final decisions
399   (H-D). The human evaluators are instructed to assess each generated output based on a 10-point
400   scale, where a score of 0 indicates the lowest quality, and 10 represents the highest level of quality.
401
      (5) Pre-trained model evaluation We implement GPTScore (Fu et al., 2023) that a large gener-
402
      ative pre-trained model will be used to calculate how likely the text could be generated based on
403
      the specific protocol among consistency, conherence, fluency, correctness, semantically appropriate
404   aspects. The detailed evaluation metrics are in the Appendix D.

521   6      C ONCLUSION AND L IMITATION
522
523   In this paper, we presented the construction and evaluation of the ReviewMT dataset, designed for
524   the application of LLMs in the peer review process. By reformulating peer review as a multi-turn
525
      dialogue involving distinct roles for reviewers, authors, and decision makers, we aim to capture
      the dynamic and iterative nature of real-world academic peer review. Our comprehensive dataset,
526
      drawn from top-tier conferences like ICLR and NeurIPS, supports this complex interaction model
527
      and provides a rich resource for fine-tuning and evaluating LLMs. Our benchmark dataset includes
528   detailed annotations for each turn of the peer review process, allowing LLMs to generate and respond
529   to reviews. By addressing various aspects of the peer review cycle—initial reviews, author rebuttals,
530   final reviews, and decision-making—the ReviewMT dataset facilitates the development of LLMs
531   that can engage in meaningful, constructive peer review dialogues. This advancement holds promise
532   for improving the efficiency and fairness of the peer review process.
533
      Despite its potential, our work has certain limitations that need to be acknowledged. Firstly, no
534   figures are included in the main text, which could limit the dataset’s ability to handle visual data
535   integral to some academic papers. Additionally, the dataset is currently limited to specific confer-
536   ences, primarily ICLR and NeurIPS. This scope may not fully represent the diversity of academic
537   publishing, and future work should aim to extend the dataset to include a broader range of sources
538   across various disciplines. The primary concern about societal impact is the potential for bias. If
539   the training dataset includes biased reviews or decisions, the LLM might learn and replicate these
      biases, leading to unfair evaluations of certain groups or topics.

1077   In the main text, we introduce several metrics to evaluate the validity and quality of the responses
1078   generated by the LLMs, including paper hit rate, review hit rate, decision hit rate, mean absolute

1080
       Paper Hit Rate
1081
1082    • Definition: Measures whether the LLM-generated reply addresses the paper content.
1083
        • Criteria: If the reply is empty, the hit rate is 0. This metric ensures that the LLM provides a
1084      substantive response.
1085
1086   Review Hit Rate
1087
1088    • Definition: Evaluates whether the LLM-generated final review includes a valid score.
1089    • Criteria: We use a regular expression to match the phrase “Score: “ followed by a number in
1090      the final review. If no number is found, or if the number is outside the threshold range of [1,10],
1091      the review hit rate is 0. This ensures that the LLM correctly identifies a valid score in its review.
1092
       Decision Hit Rate
1093
1094    • Definition: Assesses whether the LLM-generated decision includes a clear accept or reject.
1095
        • Criteria: If the LLM fails to include the words “accept” or “reject” in its reply, the decision hit
1096      rate is 0. This metric ensures that the LLM provides a definitive decision regarding the paper.
1097
1098   Mean Absolute Error (MAE)
1099
1100    • Definition: Measures the accuracy of the review scores provided by the LLM.
1101    • Criteria: We calculate the absolute difference between the score matched in the LLM’s final
1102      review and the ground truth score provided by human reviewers. This metric quantifies the
1103      numerical accuracy of the LLM’s scoring.
1104
       F1-Score for Decision Accuracy
1105
1106    • Definition: Evaluates the accuracy of the LLM’s binary classification of decisions (accept or
1107      reject).
1108
        • Criteria: Since LLMs may generate multiple instances of “accept” or “reject,” we count the
1109      frequency of each term. If “accept” is mentioned more frequently than “reject,” we interpret the
1110      LLM’s decision as an acceptance, and vice versa. The F1-score is then calculated based on the
1111      precision and recall of these decisions compared to the ground truth.
