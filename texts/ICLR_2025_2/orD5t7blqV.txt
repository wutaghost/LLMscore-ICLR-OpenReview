009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            PC is an efficient constraint-based algorithm for learning the structure of a Bayesian
012            network. However, the required number of conditional independent (CI) tests can
013            make the algorithm practically infeasible or slow for large graphs. We developed
014            a constrained-based algorithm, called Path-Driven Independence Testing (PIT)
015            Algorithm, which during the learning process, utilizes the information of the
016
               partially learned network to reduce the number of CI tests. The idea is that for each
               pair of variables X and Y , instead of checking independence conditioned on every
017
               subset of all the neighbors of X (resp. Y ) as in PC, the search is restricted to only
018
               the common neighbors of X and Y and to neighbors connected to Y (resp. X) by a
019            path. Also, paths connecting X and Y without a descendant of a common neighbor
020            can be blocked by observing two consecutive nodes on the path. Compared to PC,
021            PIT is proven to conduct at most the same number of CI tests, and experimentally
022            shown to be significantly (up to 7 times) faster and more accurate.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
026
027
      Causal discovery, the task of identifying the relationship between random variables, is the key to
      understanding the underlying governing mechanisms and is necessary to answer interventional queries
028
      (Kitson et al., 2021). Causal relationships can be represented by a directed acyclic graph (DAG) with
029
      random variables as nodes and where links imply causal relations. Similarly, yet in a non-causal
030   context, a DAG (the structure) together with the probability distributions of each variable conditioned
031   on its parents (the parameters) form a Bayesian network over the variables, which factorizes the joint
032   probability distribution of the variables as a multiplication of the conditional probability distributions,
033   and in turn allows answering probabilistic queries. In either context, the task of identifying the “true”
034   DAG from data collected on the variables is referred to as structure learning (Guo et al., 2020).
035
      One approach to fulfill this task is constraint based, which is based on detecting (in)dependencies
036   between the variables by performing conditional independence (CI) tests (Koller & Friedman, 2009).
037   The idea is that two variables are not connected in the true DAG should they be independent
038   conditioned on a subset of the other variables. The result is a class of independence-equivalence (I-
039   equivalence) graphs that is presented as a partially DAG (PDAG). Under the Markov and faithfulness
040   assumptions, constraint-based methods asymptotically output the “true” PDAG (Ng et al., 2021).
041
      The PC algorithm is one of the most fundamental constraint-based approaches for causal discovery,
042   following a two-step process (Spirtes et al., 2000). First, it identifies the direct dependencies between
043   every pair of variables by starting from a fully connected graph and iteratively removing edges where
044   conditional independence is detected. Second, the surviving edges are oriented to reflect causal
045   directions. Despite its effectiveness, the number of conditional independence (CI) tests required by
046   the PC algorithm can scale exponentially, with a worst-case complexity of O(2N ), where N is the
047   number of variables (Spirtes et al., 2000).
048   In practice, this number is often lower due to the removal of edges and reduction of adjacency sets,
049   particularly in sparse graphs (Spirtes et al., 2000; Peters et al., 2017). However, the high number of CI
050   tests remains a critical bottleneck (van den Boom et al., 2022; Wadhwa & Dong, 2021), especially for
051   large-scale problems. To address this, several techniques have been developed, including distributed
052   and parallel learning approaches (Hwang et al., 2006; Gu & Zhou, 2020; Bouhamed et al., 2015;
053   Zarebavani et al., 2019; Le et al., 2016; Shahbazinia et al., 2023), limiting the size of conditioning sets
      (Sondhi & Shojaie, 2019), and pre-processing strategies (Cai et al., 2022). While these methods offer

054
      improvements, they either demand extensive computation resources, rely on specific assumptions, or
055   did not consistently outperform PC.
056
057   In this paper, we developed the Path-Driven Independence Testing (PDIT) Algorithm to reduce the
058
      number of CI tests in the PC algorithm. In PC, the candidate conditioning set for a pair of adjacent
      variables X and Y is every subset of the neighbors of X and every subset of the neighbors of Y .
059
      However, some neighbors of X are not connected to Y and vice versa, and hence, do not render X
060
      and Y independent. Thus, they can be excluded from the conditioning set. On the other hand, some
061   paths between X and Y may always be blocked without activating any other connecting path. Thus,
062   these paths or a sufficient number of their nodes can be always included in the conditioning set and do
063   not need to be searched through. PIT and its variants leverage these properties to reduce the number
064   of candidate conditioning sets. PIT is sound and complete and never requires more CI tests than
065   those in PC–should an oracle be used for the CI tests. In practice, our extensive evaluation across 16
066   datasets shows that PIT requires significantly fewer CI tests, and is significantly faster and often more
067   accurate than PC and PC-stable (Colombo & Maathuis, 2014) as well as Hill Climbing and Tabu.

069   2    BACKGROUND
070
071   Given random variables X = {X1 , . . . , XN }, their joint probability distribution P (X ) can be
072   factorized according to the chain rule into ΠNi=1 P (Xi | X1 , . . . , Xi−1 ). Each conditional probability
073   term may be simplified if a corresponding conditional probability independence holds. For example,
074   P (X1 | X2 , X3 ) becomes P (X1 | X2 ) should X1 ⊥ X3 | X2 hold. The problem is to find (one of)
075   the “simplest” factorization(s) of the joint probability distribution.
076   A directed acyclic graph (DAG) G can be attributed to each factorization, where nodes are the random
077   variables X and for each conditional term P (Xi | Xi1 , . . . , Xik ), k ≥ 1, there is an incoming link
078   from each of the conditioned variables Xi1 , . . . , Xik to Xi . Thus, Xi1 , . . . , Xik form the parents
079   of Xi , denoted by the set PaXi . A sequence of nodes T = (X1 , X2 , . . . , Xn ), n ≥ 1, where Xi
080   and Xi+1 are linked in G for i = 1, . . . , n − 1, is referred to as a trail (path) between X1 and
081   Xn . The length of the trail is the number of its links, i.e., n − 1. Define the interior of the trail as
082   int(T ) = {X2 , . . . , Xn−1 }, that is, the set of all but the ending nodes. The descendants of a node X
083   are those nodes to which X is connected by a directed path, including X itself. The reachable set of
084
      node Y , denoted RY , is the set of nodes, including Y , that are connected to Y by some trail. Path T
      is a directed path from X1 to Xn if node Xi is linked to node Xi+1 for all i = 1, . . . , n − 1. Define
085
      a collider as a length-two trail (X, Z, Y ) where nodes X and Y are linked to Z, i.e., X → Z ← Y .
086
      Node Z is referred to as the collider node or center. The collider is an immorality if there is no edge
087   between X and Y , referred to as a covering edge.
088
089   The DAG implies certain conditional independencies on the variables. For example, each variable
090
      is independent of its non-descendants conditioned on its parents (Koller & Friedman, 2009). More
      generally, the notion of d-separation is defined to capture all conditional independencies imposed
091
      by the DAG (Definition 5 in the appendix). Let I(P ) be the set of all conditional independencies
092
      satisfied by the joint distribution of variables X . Back to the aforementioned factorization problem, it
093   can be shown that every d-separation in the DAG that corresponds to the factorization is also satisfied
094   by the distribution P . So DAG G corresponding to the desired factorization must fulfill the so-called
095   Markov condition (MC), that is, I(G) ⊆ I(P ). A DAG G satisfying MC is known as an I-map for P .
096   On the other hand, we are interested in the “simplest” factorization, that is, the “sparsest” DAG. This
097   is captured by the notion of minimal I-map, which is a DAG that is an I-map for P but not if any of
098   its edges are deleted. We make the more restrictive yet common assumption that I(P ) ⊆ I(G). The
099   distribution P is said to be faithful to the DAG G if it satisfies this assumption. We assume that there
100   exists a DAG G that satisfies both the Markovness and faithfulness assumptions for the distribution P .
101
      Such a DAG is called a P-map for P , also referred to as the true DAG.
102
      Assumption 1 There exists a DAG G that is a P-map for the distribution P , i.e., I(G) = I(P ).
103

105   is often more than one P-map for a distribution P , e.g., both DAGs X → Y and Y → X are a P-map
106   for the distribution I(P ) = ∅. The set of all P-maps for a given distribution P share the same skeleton
107   (the undirected graph obtained by removing the orientations of the edges) and immoralities (Koller
      & Friedman, 2009). Thus, a partially DAG (PDAG), that is a graph that can have both directed and

129   The idea behind the algorithm is that two adjacent nodes in the P-map never become independent,
130   regardless of what they are conditioned on, because they never become d-separated.
131
132   Lemma 1 (Based on (Pearl, 2009)) Consider random variables X with joint distribution P that
133   admits a P-map G. Vertices X and Y are not adjacent in G if and only if X ⊥ Y | U for some
134   U ⊆ X.
135
136   PC is based on a sharpened version of this lemma, restricting U to the parents of X and Y .
137
138   Lemma 2 [Lemma 3.2 in (Koller & Friedman, 2009)] Consider random variables X with joint
139   distribution P that admits a P-map G. Vertices X and Y are not adjacent in G if and only if
140   X ⊥ Y | PaX or X ⊥ Y | PaY .
141
142
      Thus, in order to remove the edge between X and Y , only CI tests conditioned on the parents of X
143   and conditioned on the parents of Y are needed. As the parents are unknown initially in the undirected
144   graph, PC searchers over all subsets of the neighbors of X, denoted NX , and the neighbors of Y ,
145   denoted NY , to ensure that their parents will be conditioned on during the search.

178   3.1   T HE IDEA
179
180
      Is it possible to reduce the search over all subsets U of the neighbors NX and NY in the PC algorithm?
      Lemma 2 is conservative in the sense that among the parents of X (resp. Y ), some may not be even
181
      indirectly connected to Y (resp. X). When checking the link X1 → Y in Example 1, node Y has
182
      many parents, but none are connected to X1 . Therefore, X1 and Y are not d-separated in the true
183   DAG conditioned on any combination of the parents of Y . Similarly, X1 has no parent. Hence,
184   an efficient algorithm should perform only n marginal independent tests between Y and each of
185   X1 , . . . , Xn once the undirected structure in Fig. 1-b is reached. This observation suggests that the
186   parents PaX (resp. PaY ) in Lemma 2 should be limited to those that are connected to the other node
187   Y (resp. X) by some path. We define the notion of covering paths accordingly.
188
189   Definition 1 (Covering path) A covering path/trail for nodes X and Y is a trail between X and Y
190   of length at least two. A covering path is short if the length is exactly two and long otherwise. The
191
      set of all covering, short-covering, and long-covering paths between X and Y are denoted by TXY ,
        s           l
      TXY  , and TXY   .
192
193   Rather than the whole parents PaX , one may condition only on those that belong to the interior of
194   the covering paths between X and Y , which we refer to as the essential parents of X (with respect to
195   Y ), denoted by PaX,Y = PaX ∩ {int(T ) : T ∈ TXY }. This is the same as those parents of X that
196   can reach node Y (excluding Y itself), i.e., PaX,Y = PaX ∩ RY \ {Y }. In Figure 1-a, PaXi ,Y = ∅
197   for all i, and in Figure 2-a, PaX5 X8 = {X3 }.
198
      A second conservative aspect of PC is in the way Lemma 2 is used. As discussed earlier, since the
199   parents are not a priori known in the undirected graph, PC may search through eventually all possible
200   combinations of NX . However, to reduce the number of possible combinations, is it possible to
201   always include some nodes in the search, namely, to always include a neighbor, say Z ∈ NX , in
202   the CI tests as X ⊥ Y |Z, U? Generally, no, because node Z may be the center node of a collider
203   on a trail connecting X and Y . Then observing Z may activate the trail and prevent X and Y from
204   becoming independent. An example is in Fig. 2-a in Example 2, where X2 is not a parent of X5 and
205   observing it makes the trail (X5 , X2 , X1 , X4 , X8 ) active. Nevertheless, there are situations where
206   observing some nodes does not make the connecting trails active. For example, if instead of just X2 ,
207
      we observe all of the interior nodes of the trail, i.e., X2 , X1 , and X4 , then the trail becomes inactive.
      So we can include these nodes in the conditioned nodes in all of the CI tests. That is, rather than
208
      checking X5 ⊥ X8 | U for all U ⊆ NX and U ⊆ NY , we can check X5 ⊥ X8 | X2 , X1 , X4 , U ′
209
      for all U ′ ⊆ NX \ {X2 } and U ′ ⊆ NY \ {X4 }, reducing the search space. The following result
210   illustrates the nodes that can be always observed.

474   5    C ONCLUSION
475
476   We introduced PIT as efficient enhancements to the PC algorithm for Bayesian network structure
477   learning. By restricting the conditioning sets to neighbors that have paths to the target variable when
478   verifying a link, PIT substantially reduces the number of required conditional independence (CI) tests.
479   This straightforward modification leads to significant improvements in both speed and accuracy.
480   The algorithms are both sound and complete, ensuring the identification of the correct P-map under
481   ideal conditions–something many fast algorithms compromise on. Unlike score-based methods that
482   rely on local search approximations, PIT delivers high accuracy without relying on extensive CI testing.
483   Moreover, PIT achieves these performance gains without necessitating additional computational
484   resources, such as distributed or parallel computing. Instead, it complements these methods by
485   seamlessly integrating into existing parallel or distributed frameworks where local learning algorithms
      are employed.

517   Empirical evaluations demonstrated that PIT outperforms PC and PC-stable in terms of accuracy for
518   small to medium-sized datasets and offers considerable speed advantages for large datasets. This dual
519   benefit addresses the core challenges in structure learning: maintaining high accuracy with limited
520   data and ensuring computational efficiency with large-scale data.
521   Advanced versions, BPIT and Opt-BPIT , build upon the path-driven approach by distinguishing
522   between different types of connecting paths and determining if they can be “blindly” blocked. While
523   this blind-blocking technique reduces CI tests even further, it also presents new challenges in terms
524   of conditioning set size. However, if future CI tests can maintain accuracy while scaling linearly with
525   conditioning size, this approach could become a game-changing advancement in the field.
526   Future work includes optimizing path computations to enhance algorithm efficiency and exploring
527   robust CI testing methods that maintain accuracy with larger conditioning sets. Furthermore, integrat-
528   ing our approach with distributed and parallel learning techniques could enable scalability to even
529   larger datasets, broadening the applicability of our methods in diverse real-world scenarios.
530
      Ultimately, the contributions of PIT and BPIT strike a critical balance between precision, speed, and
531   scalability, offering an algorithm that not only pushes the boundaries of current graph-based learning
532   methods but also sets the stage for future innovation in causal discovery.
