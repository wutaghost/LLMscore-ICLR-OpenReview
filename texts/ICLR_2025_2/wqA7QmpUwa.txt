                                                                           A BSTRACT
031
032
               Expanding the long-context capabilities of Multi-modal Large Language Mod-
               els (MLLMs) is crucial for video understanding, high-resolution image under-
033
               standing, and multi-modal agents. This involves a series of systematic optimiza-
034
               tions, including model architecture, data construction and training strategy, par-
035            ticularly addressing challenges such as degraded performance with more images
036            and high computational costs. In this paper, we adapt the model architecture to a
037            hybrid of Mamba and Transformer blocks, approach data construction with both
038            temporal and spatial dependencies among multiple images and employ a progres-
039            sive training strategy. The released model LongLLaVA (Long-Context Large
040            Language and Vision Assistant) is the first hybrid MLLM, which achieved a bet-
041            ter balance between efficiency and effectiveness. LongLLaVA not only achieves
042            competitive results across various benchmarks, but also maintains high through-
043
               put and low memory consumption. Especially, it could process nearly a thousand
               images on a single A100 80GB GPU, showing promising application prospects
044
               for a wide range of tasks.
045

047   1   I NTRODUCTION
048
049   The rapid advancement of MLLMs (Liu et al., 2024b; 2023a; Dong et al., 2024a; Chen et al., 2024a)
050   has demonstrated their remarkable capabilities across various applications (Chu et al., 2024; Yang
051   et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2023b; Chen et al., 2024b). However, multi-image scenario remain an impor-
052   tant yet to-be-explored aspect. In particular, expanding the context of MLLMs to understand longer
053   videos (Zhang et al., 2023; Cheng et al., 2024b), higher-resolution images (Xu et al., 2024c; Wu &
      Xie, 2023b), and make decisions based on more historical messages (Wang et al., 2024b; Liu et al.,

054
      2024c) is crucial for enhancing user experience (Li et al., 2024b) and further broadening MLLMs’
055   application scope (Apple, 2024).
056
057   However, extending the context length of MLLMs to improve their usability poses challenges related
058
      to degraded performance and high computational costs when processing more images. To maintain
      the performance in longer context, some studies (Zhang et al., 2024a; Zhao et al., 2024c) have con-
059
      centrated on curating long-context training data involving multiple images to enhance performance.
060
      Additionally, other research efforts have explored innovative training strategies (Liu et al., 2024a;
061   Zhang et al., 2024b; Li et al., 2024a; Zhang et al., 2024d) to mitigate performance declines. Re-
062   garding the issue of high computational costs, Xue et al. (2024) have made strides in improving
063   multi-node efficiency by reducing communication costs. However, there remains a gap in solutions
064   for accelerating the computation itself when managing longer contexts.
065
      To address the challenges mentioned above, we propose a systematic solution called LongLLaVA,
066
      especially using a hybrid architecture for acceleration. This solution comprehensively optimizes
067   across three dimensions: Multi-modal Architecture, Data Construction, and Training Strategy.
068
069          • For Multi-modal Architecture, we adopt a hybrid hybrid Transformer-Mamba architec-
070            ture and an efficient image representation method that applies 2D pooling to compress
071            image tokens, significantly reducing computational costs while maintaining performance.
072          • For Data Construction, we have designed unique formats for different tasks, enabling the
073            model to distinguish between temporal and spatial dependencies among images.
074
             • For Training Strategy, we use a three-stage method for multi-modal adaptation—Single-
075
               image Alignment, Single-image Instruction-tuning, and Multi-image Instruction-
076            tuning—to incrementally enhance the model’s ability to handle multi-modal long contexts.
077
078   Experiemntal results show that LongLLaVA excels in understanding multi-modal long contexts with
079   high efficiency. It leads in retrieval, counting, and ordering tasks in VNBench (Zhao et al., 2024e)
080   and achieves nearly 100% accuracy with 1,000 images on a single 80GB GPU for Needle-In-A-
081   Haystack evaluation (Zhang et al., 2024b). Our summarized contributions are as follows:
082
083
             • We introduce LongLLaVA, a solution optimized through data construction, training strate-
               gies, and multi-modal architecture, effectively balancing performance and efficiency. To
084
               the best of our knowledge, this is the first hybrid architecture for MLLMs.
085
086          • LongLLaVA demonstrates exceptional performance in multi-modal long-context under-
087            standing, excelling in retrieval, counting, and ordering tasks. In our commitment to trans-
088
               parency and community research, we will open source all models, codes, and datasets
               associated with LongLLaVA.

093   2.1   T HE C URSE OF I MAGE N UMBERS
094
095   Degraded Performance with More Images. While many open-source MLLMs match closed-
096   source models on single-image tasks (Bai et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024a; Zhang et al., 2024a; OpenAI,
097   2024; Google, 2024), their performance degrades significantly in multi-image scenarios, particularly
098
      in tasks involving temporal or semantic relationships (Song et al., 2024). This limitation restricts
      their usability and calls for systematic solutions from the open-source community.
099
100   Excessive Input Length. Processing multiple images results in excessive input length due to the
101   large number of tokens generated by visual encoders like CLIP (Radford et al., 2021). For example,
102   representing a three-minute video at 1 FPS requires 103,680 tokens, causing increased computa-
103   tional demand and memory usage. Compression methods (Chen et al., 2023a; Zhang et al., 2024b;
104
      Xu et al., 2024b) partially alleviate this issue but often compromise performance.
105   High Computational and Memory Complexity. The quadratic scaling of Transformer architec-
106   tures with sequence length leads to high computational and memory overhead when handling mul-
107   tiple images. Techniques like ring attention (Liu et al., 2024a; Zhang et al., 2024b), sequence par-
      allelism (Xue et al., 2024), and Mamba architectures (Gu & Dao, 2024; Zhao et al., 2024a) offer


                                                       2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      partial relief but introduce trade-offs, such as time overhead or reduced in-context learning capabil-
109   ities (Lieber et al., 2024). A balanced solution is needed to address these challenges in multimodal
110   contexts.

121   Transformer architectures are highly effective in multimodal tasks but face significant computa-
122   tional challenges due to their quadratic complexity with sequence length. This inefficiency becomes
123   a bottleneck in long-context scenarios, requiring high memory and computation resources. Mamba
124   architectures address this issue with their linear computational complexity, making them signif-
125   icantly more efficient. However, they exhibit notable weaknesses in In-Context Learning (ICL)
126   tasks, particularly those involving complex retrieval or reasoning (Park et al., 2024). These limi-
127   tations may attributed to Mamba’s reliance on reduced attention mechanisms (Olsson et al., 2022),
128
      which constrain its ability to learn contextual patterns effectively. While explicit training can enable
      Mamba models to perform simple ICL tasks, this approach restricts the utilization of the model’s
129
      full capacity and training data (Dao & Gu, 2024).
130
131   Recent advancements have demonstrated the potential of hybrid Mamba-Transformer architectures,
132   which integrate Mamba’s efficiency with the robust ICL capabilities of Transformers (Dao & Gu,
133   2024; Wang et al., 2024a). Comparative experiments show that these hybrids achieve superior per-
134
      formance on ICL tasks and maintain computational efficiency. For instance, Jamba (Lieber et al.,
      2024), a hybrid model, can process 256K tokens with only 4GB of KV-Cache memory, far sur-
135
      passing the capabilities of Mixtral (Jiang et al., 2024a), which has the same activation parameters.
136
      This balance between effectiveness and efficiency makes hybrid architectures an ideal solution for
137   long-context multimodal tasks, addressing both computational and functional limitations.
138
139   2.3   T HE B ENEFIT OF S CALING U P THE I MAGE N UMBER
140
141   Adopting more images significantly broadens the application scenarios for current MLLMs. We will
142   explore this from two dimensions: Temporal Expansion and Spatial Expansion.
143   Temporal Expansion. Understanding the temporal dependencies between images is crucial for a
144   variety of applications. In multi-modal assistants, it enhances real-time recall capabilities, which is
145   particularly beneficial for the elderly (Li et al., 2024b; Loveys et al., 2022). For mobile agents, it
146   enables more personalized services and improves task planning (Deng et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024f;
147   Wu et al., 2023a). In the healthcare sector, it assists in anomaly detection in 3D medical videos,
148   thereby reducing diagnostic errors (Bai et al., 2024a).
149   Spatial Expansion. When dealing with high-resolution images (Xu et al., 2024c; Dong et al.,
150   2024b) or when detailed understanding of images (Wu & Xie, 2023b) is required, images are often
151   decomposed into sub-images. This process highlights the importance of grasping spatial dependen-
152   cies among these sub-images. In remote sensing, an increased number of images enhances both
153   coverage and granularity (Guo et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2022). In pathology, it minimizes information
154   loss and improves diagnostic accuracy (Sun et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2024a). In the field of Molecu-
155   lar Learning, it facilitates the processing of complex reactions and the analysis of larger molecular
156
      graphs (Zhang et al., 2024c; Le et al., 2024).

189
      of hybrid layers, each integrates Transformer and Mamba layers in a 7:1 ratio, as depicted in Fig-
      ure 2. It also features a Mixture of Experts (MoE) approach in every other layer, utilizing 16 experts
190
      and selecting the top-2 experts for each token. RMSNorm (Zhang & Sennrich, 2019) is used be-
191
      tween layers to enhance normalization, although positional embeddings are omitted. The model
192   incorporates Grouped Query Attention (GQA) (Ainslie et al., 2023) and SwiGLU activation func-
193   tions (Shazeer, 2020), similar to other large language models. The total parameter count of the
194   model is 53B, with activation parameters during inference totaling 13B; we designate this model
195   as LongLLaVA-A13B. In an effort to make the model more efficient, we have retained only the
196   Expert-0 in the Mamba MoE Layer2 , thereby constructing LongLLaVA-9B.

198   3.2      DATA P ROCESSING P ROTOCOL
199
200   To ensure that the model effectively distinguishes between temporal and spatial dependencies among
201   images in multi-image scenarios and performs well across various tasks, we meticulously differenti-
202
      ated special characters in different scenarios. As shown in Figure 3, these special characters compre-
      hensively address the various relationships between images in different contexts, thereby enhancing
203
      the model’s adaptability to diverse tasks.
204
205   Regular Single and Multiple Images. For this type of inputs, we use <img> and </img> to
206   enclose image tokens, helping the model differentiate between image and text tokens.
207   Video. For video inputs, to enable the model to understand the temporal relationship between
208   frames, we first use <vid> and </vid> to enclose image tokens. Additionally, we add the special
209   symbol <t> between different frames to represent the temporal dependency between them.
210
      High Resolution Image. For complex single-image understanding that require dividing an image
211
      into multiple sub-images, we use \n to separate the main image from its sub-images. For the ar-
212   rangement of sub-images, we traverse from the top-left to the bottom-right, adding \n between split
213   lines to preserve the relative spatial positions of the sub-images.

239   Figure 4: Dataset Taxonomy of LongLLaVA. Replay refers to data sampled from former phase
240   to maintain single-image and dialogue understanding ability. SubImage denotes a constructed
241   dataset for understanding complex single images divided into sub-images. Ins-T. and Align. refer to
242   instruction-tuning and alignment, respectively.
243
244   Pure-text Instruction Tuning. We initially enhance the pre-trained language model’s ability to
245   follow instructions of varying lengths in pure-text contexts. This is achieved using a comprehensive
246   dataset totaling 278k pure text entries from Evol-instruct-GPT4 (Xu et al., 2023), WildChat (Zhao
247   et al., 2024d), and LongAlign (Bai et al., 2024b).
248   For multi-modal adaptation, following the Single-image Alignment and Single-image Instruction-
249   tuning stages in LLaVA (Li et al., 2024a), we introduce a Multi-image Instruction-tuning stage to
250   progressively enhance the model’s long-context capabilities. We adopt progressive training not only
251   for better control of variables but also to increase model reusability (Fu et al., 2024b). The specific
252   dataset usage is detailed in Figure 4.
253   Stage I: Single-image Alignment. This stage is to align visual modal features with textual modality.
254   We utilize datasets such as ALLaVA-Caption (Chen et al., 2024a) and ShareGPT4V (Chen et al.,
255   2023b), which comprise approximately 600K high-quality image-caption pairs. During this phase,
256   only the projector is trained while freezing the parameters of the Visual Encoder and LLM.
257
      Stage II: Single-image Instruction Tuning. This stage aims to endow the model with multimodal
258   instruction-following capabilities. We use datasets like LLaVA-1.5 (Liu et al., 2023b) and Mantis-
259   Single (Jiang et al., 2024b), totaling around 932K high-quality question-answer pairs. Here, only
260   the Visual Encoder is frozen, and the projector and LLM parts are trained. This process ultimately
261   results in the development of LongLLaVA (single image).
262
      Stage III: Multi-image Instruction Tuning. In this stage, the model is trained to follow instruc-
263
      tions in multimodal long-context scenarios. We sample 200K, 200K and 50K data items from Man-
264   tis (Jiang et al., 2024b), VideoChat2 (Li et al., 2024d) and ShareGPT4Video (Chen et al., 2024c)
265   respectively. To preserve the model’s single-image comprehension and pure-text dialogue capabil-
266   ities, we include an additional 200K and 50K data items from the Single-image Instruction-tuning
267   and Pure-text Instruction-tuning phases as the Replay component. Furthermore, to enhance the
268   model’s ability to interpret complex single images segmented into multiple sub-images, we extract
269   50K data items from the Single-image Instruction-tuning phase, perform padding and segmentation,
      and divide the original images into sub-images of size 336 × 336 as the Sub-Image component.

292   For training, we utilize random sampling to concatenate data items into a token length of 176,000,
293   separated by the <eos> token. This approach helps in managing extensive datasets and ensuring
294   diverse coverage of different data segments. Training is executed across three compute nodes, each
295   equipped with eight A800 GPUs, leveraging DeepSpeed Zero-3 as the distributed strategy to en-
296   hance scalability and efficiency. We employ a cosine learning rate scheduler with a warm-up rate of
297   0.03, set the training epoch to 1, and the learning rate to 1e-5.

       Single-image Benchmarks. We select seven commonly used evaluations to assess the model’s
       single-image understanding capabilities. These include:
997
998          • GQA (Hudson & Manning, 2019): A benchmark for real-world visual reasoning and com-
999            positional question answering.
1000
             • MME (Fu et al., 2023): A comprehensive benchmark focused on perception and cognition,
1001           from which we use the perception component.
1002
             • MM-Vet (Yu et al., 2023): Examines six core visual-linguistic (VL) capabilities and sixteen
1003
               integrations derived from these capabilities.
1004
             • ScienceQA (Lu et al., 2022): Consists of 4,210 questions across various science topics,
1005
               with detailed annotations.
1006
1007         • SEED-Bench-v1 (Li et al., 2023): Evaluates comprehension across twelve dimensions in
1008
               both image and video modalities; we use the image set.
1009         • MMBench (Liu et al., 2023c): A systematically-designed benchmark across twenty ability
1010           dimensions.
1011         • MMMU (Yue et al., 2024): Tests multi-modal models on multidisciplinary tasks requiring
1012           university-level knowledge, covering 183 subfields and 30 types of images.
1013
