                                                        A BSTRACT
008
009
010
                   What is the worth of a node? We answer this question using an emerging set of
                   data valuation techniques, where the value of a data point is measured via its
011
                   marginal contribution when added to the (training) dataset. Data valuation has
012
                   been primarily studied in the i.i.d. setting, giving rise to methods like influence
013                functions, leave-one-out estimation, data Shapley, and data Banzhaf. We conduct
014                a comprehensive study of data valuation approaches applied to graph-structured
015                models such as graph neural networks in a semi-supervised transductive setting.
016                Since all nodes (labeled and unlabeled) influence both training and inference we
017                construct various scenarios to understand the diverse mechanisms by which nodes
018                can impact learning. We show that the resulting node values can be used to identify
019                (positively and negatively) influential nodes, quantify model brittleness, detect
020                poisoned data, and accurately predict counterfactuals1 .

023   1       I NTRODUCTION
024
025   When we learn from data, a natural question to ask is how each data point influences learning. We
026   can study how a given training point affects the learned weights, the accuracy, or the prediction for a
027
      given test point. Beyond offering insights into our models, this question is also practically important,
      especially for modern machine learning methods that rely on increasingly larger datasets (Zhou et al.,
028
      2017). For example, if we gather data from unreliable sources (e.g. the internet) we may want to
029
      filter out low quality instances. When we obtain data from data markets, different data providers (e.g.
030   individuals, hospitals) should be equitably compensated for the data that they provide. Data values
031   have been used to quantify train-test leakage, find semantically similar examples (Ilyas et al., 2022),
032   detect mislabeled instances (Wang & Jia, 2023) and for dataset selection (Engstrom et al., 2024).
033
      A classical answer to the data valuation question is influence functions (Cook & Weisberg, 1982).
034
      They approximate the effect of removing a data point on the model performance. From linear
035
      regression to deep learning, they have been thoroughly studied in various settings, including their
036   limitations (Basu et al., 2021; Bae et al., 2022). Broadly, data valuation encompasses techniques that
037   try to relate some output of a model with the data it is trained on. While there is growing research that
038   explores different notions of value, the vast majority is focused on supervised learning for i.i.d. data.
039
      We conduct the first comprehensive data valuation study for graph-based models in the semi-
040
      supervised transductive setting. Only two recent works have explored data valuation for graphs.
041
      Chen et al. (2023) derive closed-form estimate for the leave-one-out influence of nodes and edges us-
042   ing SGC (Wu et al., 2019) as a surrogate model. Chi et al. (2024) propose the precedence-constrained
043   winter value where nodes are grouped in coalitions based on the graph structure. Our focus is not
044   on proposing new notions, but rather rigorously evaluating existing notions. Our results show that
045   both data Banzhaf (Wang & Jia, 2023) and datamodels (Ilyas et al., 2022) significantly outperform
046   other valuation methods, but neither of these notions has been studied for graphs so far. Given the
047   importance and ubiquity of graph-based models such as graph neural networks (Ju et al., 2024), we
048   hope that our work provides a solid foundation for the study of data valuation on graphs.
049   It is clear that not all data are created equally, e.g. some instances may be noisy or mislabeled. How-
050   ever, even a “high-quality” instance may not be valuable if there are already similar instances in the
051   dataset, indicating that to properly evaluate the worth of an instance we need to carefully consider the
052   appropriate context. Lack of context is why leave-one-out estimation often fails in practice – its focus

073   The right context is even more important in the graph setting. First, the prediction of a node depends
074   on its neighborhood – nodes are not i.i.d. This suggests that looking at nodes in isolation is likely
075
      suboptimal and we should consider different subgraphs. The main idea is illustrated in Fig. 1. To
      estimate the influence of node i on some utility function u(·) such as the accuracy, we consider the
076

077

078   average to obtain the final node value ϕ(i). Different weights recover many of the established data
079   value notions, including data Shapley, beta Shapley, data Banzhaf, and leave-one-out.
080
081
      Second, all nodes – labeled and unlabeled – influence both training and inference. We account for
      this by constructing different variants (§ 3) of each data value notion that aim at disentangling these
082
      effects. Unsurprisingly, even though the number of training nodes is a small percentage of all nodes in
083
      the sparsely-labeled scenario (Shchur et al., 2018), the top most influential nodes are mostly training
084   nodes. At the same time, we can find unlabeled nodes which have greater influence than many training
085   nodes. This reflects the complex interplay between training and test nodes in graphs.
086
087
      In addition to influence-based and game-theoretic notions, predictive approaches such as datamodels
      (Ilyas et al., 2022) and MLPbV (Wu et al., 2024) construct surrogate (proxy) models that can predict
088
      the utility function for any subset without the need to train the model from scratch. They allow us to
089
      answer counterfactual questions, i.e. predicting the performance on arbitrary unseen subsets of data.
090   Proxy models are trained on subset of all possible {(Si , u(Si )} pairs, where Si ⊆ V. As we discuss
091   in § 2.1, we can trivially turn any set of data values into a predictive surrogate. Moreover, the resulting
092   data values from predictive and game-theoretic approaches are equivalent for certain configurations
093   (see § 2.2). Thus, this distinction between these types of notions may not be very useful.
094
      Overall, our results show that approaches accounting for subgraphs instead of single-node contribution
095
      result in more accurate data values. We demonstrate the usefulness of the data values for various
096   downstream applications: i) finding highly influential nodes – pruning nodes with high positive
097   (negative) influence results in a significant drop (rise) in model performance; ii) spotting brittle
098   predictions which depend on a small set of support nodes whose removal results in misclassification;
099   iii) detecting poisoned (mislabeled) data; iv) estimating counterfactuals such as predicting the
100   performance on an arbitrary subset; and v) visualizations that provide further insight in the data.
101
      We thoroughly study the problem of data valuation for graphs in a transductive semi-supervised
102   setting, including variants that attribute importance to both labeled and unlabeled nodes. We analyse
103   different valuation approaches, including game-theoretic and predictive notions. We show that
104   datamodels and data Banzhaf, neither of which was previously considered in the context of graphs,
105   significantly outperform other valuation techniques, including the two recently proposed graph-
106   specific approaches. Our study was computationally expensive (over 2500 compute hours in total) and
107   storage-intensive (more than 10TB of raw data). Nonetheless, we show that one can obtain accurate
      estimates even with modest computational resources, and we plan to publicly release the raw data.

      2     DATA VALUATION
109
110
      Broadly, data valuation methods try to relate the presence or absence of an instance in the dataset
111   with the resulting change in some user-defined utility function. Traditionally, this is some measure of
112   model performance such as accuracy, where a high positive (negative) value indicates that including
113   the instance in the data improves (deteriorates) performance. For both game-theoretic and predictive
114   approaches the main idea is to consider different subsets of the training set D and assess the value of
115   missing training samples according to the change in the utility. We denote with ϕ(i) ∈ R the value
116   assigned to instance i.
117
      Utility. Let u : 2|D| → R be a utility function which maps any subsets S ⊆ D to a score indicating
118   the usefulness of the subset. For classification tasks, we commonly have u(S) = acc(A(S)) where
119   A is a learning algorithm that returns a model fS trained on S, and acc computes the accuracy on a
120   held-out set. Sometimes we are not interested in the overall performance but rather how the training
121   data influences a specific test point. For example, the utility can be the prediction margin for the test
122   point xtest . That is u(S) = u(S, xtest ) = margin(fS , xtest ) = fS (xtest )y∗ − maxy̸=y∗ fS (xtest )y
123   where fS (·)y returns the logit-score or probability for label y, and y ∗ is the ground-truth label. Any
124   other function of (the outputs of) fS is valid.

167   Influence functions. The number of possible subsets increases exponentially with the size of the
168
      dataset. Moreover, for each subset, we need to train the entire model from scratch. If the dataset is

169
      expensive. In contrast, influence functions (Cook & Weisberg, 1982) aim to approximately compute
170
      the change in the model output (or model weights) when removing a training sample. They rely on
171   first and second-order information (gradient, Hessian) to obtain estimates without model retraining.
172   Nevertheless, influence functions perform poorly in non-convex settings as in deep neural networks
173   (Basu et al., 2021; Bae et al., 2022). At best, they perform as well as leave-one-out errors which are
174   suboptimal since they consider instances in isolation. Chen et al. (2023) derive different data values
175   using influence functions for graph neural networks.

177   assuming that the values are the weights of an implicit linear model (without a bias term). This

      Sampling subsets. Since it is infeasible to evaluate the utility for all 2|D| subsets a common
182
      approximation technique is to sample subsets. A simple but effective choice is to independently
183
      include each instance with some probability α, that is Pr[i ∈ S] = α, ∀i ∈ D. This implies that the
184   subset size follows a Binomial distribution with an expected set size of α·|D|. We follow this approach
185   for data Banzhaf and datamodel values. As we show in § E.4, α is an important hyperparameter to
186   tune. To approximate the Shapley value we first randomly sample a permutation, and then scan the
187   nodes in order until reaching a user-defined truncation threshold (see § 4). For a fair comparison, we
188   make sure that each method uses the same number of subsets.

      Limitations of data values. The utility function can be viewed as a vector in R2 which is
199
      transformed into a vector of data values ϕ ∈ Rn . This mapping is not injective, so there are different
200
      utility functions that yield identical values. A natural question is which utility functions are well
201   approximated by data values. Wang et al. (2024) show that a sufficient condition is for the utility
202   to be a monotonically transformed modular function. In general, data values may be no better than
203   a random baseline for dataset selection. They also generalize the result from Saunshi et al. (2022)
204   which states that the the residual error of the unregularized linear approximation equals to the sum of
205   all u’s Fourier coefficients of order 2 or higher. Nonetheless, data values work well in practice. This
206   is remarkable since they approximate u(S) which involves training of a model on S from scratch
207   (often a complicated neural network) and then computing some function of the model outputs.
208   Robustness and efficiency. In our context the utility function is stochastic due to the randomness
209   in the training process, making the influence of a data point a random variable with an expectation

      Semi-supervised setting. Consider a graph G = (V, E) where V is the set of nodes and E is the
229   set of edges. Let X ∈ R|V|×d be the matrix of d-dimensional node features, A ∈ {0, 1}|V|×|V| be
230   the adjacency matrix and y ∈ R|V| be the vector of labels. Let Vℓ and Vu be the subsets of labeled
231
      and unlabeled nodes respectively, and let Vℓ be further split into training nodes Vt and validation
      nodes Vv . Usually, Vℓ is a small subset of nodes while the majority is in Vu (Shchur et al., 2018).
232

233
      the training, such that it can propagate label information from the few labeled nodes to the many
234   unlabeled nodes leveraging the underlying graph connectivity and node features.

296
297
      We compare different data values in a diverse range of applications. Unless specified otherwise, the
298   presented results are in the all setting and the learning signal variant.
299
300   Methods. As representative game-theoretic notions, we consider the three well-known semivalues –
301
      leave-one-out (LOO), data Shapley (SHAP), and data Banzhaf (α-BANZ), where α = 0.5 recovers the
      original Banzhaf value. As a representative predictive notion, we consider datamodels (DM). Finally,
302
      we consider the precedence-constrained winter value (PCW) as the only data value custom-designed
303
      for graphs since the method by Chen et al. (2023) approximates LOO, which we already cover. We
304   introduce a sampling improvement to PCW which we call PCWP (see § E.3 for details). We include
305   two more baselines, namely degree (DEG) where a node value corresponds to its degree, and randomly
306   assigning node values (RND). We test these methods on the largest connected component (LCC)
307   of different citation graphs (Citeseer, CoraML, PubMed, and CoPhysiscs) and co-purchase
308   graphs (Photo and Computers). We consider three models SGC, GCN and GAT. Details about
309   datasets, models, and training are provided in § E.
310   Subsets sampling. We sample 50 000 subsets for each method. For α-BANZ and DM we explored
311   different values for α (see § 2.1) and determined the optimal value for CoraML (see § E.4). This
312   optimal value, α = 0.1, is then used across the other graphs. For SHAP, we adopt the implementation
313   from Ghorbani & Zou (2019), which stops processing a permutation after having scanned 25% of the
                                                              50 000
314   nodes. Thus, we set the number of permutations to ⌈N       ·tr⌉ , where tr = 0.25 is the truncation ratio.
315   When computing node values in the all setting, permutations may not start with training nodes
316   resulting in injecting noise (resulting from the random model’s prediction). Thus, we start updating
317   node values from the first training node in the permutation and truncate after 25% counting from
318   the latter. Similarly, we select the number of permutations for PCW s.t. the total number of subsets
319   equals 50 000. In Chi et al. (2024) the default ratios tr for 1-hop and 2-hop neighbors is 0.5 and 0.7
320
      respectively. We optimize these ratios to obtain PCWP that performs slightly better (see § E.3).

322   and its associated edges – because of the message passing – we consider the value of a node as the
323   contribution of these two components. To evaluate the quality of the node values, we can use the
      rank of the nodes according to their values. Nodes with high positive value should have positive

      Brittle predictions. The support of a node v is the minimum set of nodes such that if removed,

369   brittle – more than half of the nodes (y-axis) have a support size of 15 or less (x-axis). By removing

516   5   R ELATED WORKS
517
518   Data valuation has mainly been studied in the i.i.d. settings. Ghorbani & Zou (2019) was the first
519   paper proposing to investigate how to attribute training datapoints generalizing Shapley values from
520   features to datapoints. Similarly, Wang & Jia (2023) makes use of the Banzhaf values. More recent
521   efforts investigate predictive approaches where surrogates are leveraged to approximate utility (Ilyas
522
      et al., 2022; Park et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2024). Approaches identifying input patterns to explain the
      model’s prediction have been studied in the context of graphs as well (Duval & Malliaros, 2021;
523
      Akkas & Azad, 2024; Bui et al., 2024). They all use different semivalues at inference time to identify
524
      patterns in the input that explain the predictions. Finally, Chen et al. (2023); Chi et al. (2024) are
525   the only two works accounting for data valuation in the context of graphs to relate input data to the
526   model’s performance. See § G for a more exhaustive discussion.

      6   D ISCUSSION AND CONCLUSION
530
531
      In this paper, we present the first extensive study of data valuation methods for graph-structured data
      in a transductive semi-supervised setting. We introduce different data valuation scenarios, and apply
532
      state-of-the-art data valuation approaches that have not yet been investigated in the context of graphs.
533
      Our results demonstrate that these methods significantly outperform the latest efforts in graph data
534   valuation across multiple applications. Moreover, we show how different utility functions can open
535   up several applications. Due to the need to train numerous models on different subsets, the study we
536   conducted was computationally intensive, and more effort is needed to develop more efficient data
537   valuation methods for node values – both in terms of resources and time efficiency – in particular for
538   very large graphs. Future directions also include exploring alternative proxies for datamodels that
539   account for graph structures and a deeper investigation into settings where nodes are simply unlabeled
      rather than removed, as well as extending the analysis to the semi-supervised inductive setting.

      A    K EY TAKEAWAYS
703
704
      In this section, we summarize the lessons learned and key insights from our study to guide future
705   advancements in data valuation for graphs. Our findings contribute to understanding the strengths,
706   limitations, and potential directions in this domain.
707
708         • Context awareness: Ad-hoc data valuation methods for graphs do not always perform
709           better than state-of-the-art i.i.d. methods. Establishing a clear context and understanding
710           domain-specific variations is critical before designing specialized approaches.
711         • Brittle GNNs: Data values are powerful tools for analyzing a model’s behavior, such as the
712           brittleness of GNNs to structural changes. For example, removing a few dozen nodes can
713           lead to mispredictions for the majority of test nodes (§ 4). This complements adversarial
714           analysis, opening opportunities for broader applications of data values. We believe data
715           values can also contribute to exploring other related areas in a complementary manner.
716         • Scalability: Current data valuation approaches struggle with scalability due to the computa-
717           tional expense of a high amount of subset evaluations. Efforts should focus on developing
718           methods that reduce this burden.
719         • Maximal Sample Reuse (MSR): Leveraging MSR allowed data Banzhaf to be more effi-
720           cient in computing data values without compromising performance. Indeed, we empirically
721           demonstrated that extending the latter from a uniform subset sampling to a binomial and
722           adopting the MSR anyway, performs really well in practice while saving computation costs.
723         • Simpler models for data valuation: Data values are intrinsic to the data rather than specific
724           models. Employing simpler, efficient models to compute data values (as supported by Fig. 8)
725           can significantly lower computational costs.
726
727   These takeaways underscore the importance of efficient and context-aware approaches to data
728
      valuation for graphs. We hope our findings inspire further research into scalable, transferable, and
      computationally efficient methods that harness the full potential of data values.

753   C    E MPIRICAL LIMITATION OF GAME - THEORETIC APPROACHES
754
755   SHAP spends many evaluations for degenerate subsets containing very few nodes, as the value for
      a node is updated while progressively scanning each permutation. In contrast, approaches such as


                                                      14
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


756
      DM and BANZ focus on subsets controlled by a probability parameter α. This allows to evaluate
757   subsets of more meaningful size, and a better assessment of a node’s influence within some structural
758   context. To address this, we slightly modified the data Shapley approach to consider more meaningful
759   subsets. Specifically, during the permutation scan, subsets are independently augmented to include
760   10% of additional nodes (to align with α = 0.1). This ensures that the value of the current node in the
761   permutation is evaluated with a structural context influenced by other nodes in the subset. As in the
762   original data Shapley, only the value of the current node in the permutation is updated. Fig. 12 verifies
763   this showing that when the node’s influence is evaluated with some structure context (dashed line),
764   the computed Shapley values are more realistic. We leave it as future work exploring the optimal
765
      percentage of nodes to include for maximizing the effectiveness of SHAP.

       T (bottom). From the first case, as we add back the removed nodes, the utility will capture the signal
       of removing nodes only during learning – from this, the name learning signal. In the second
1040

1041

1044   E.2      N ODE INFLUENCE - FROM MARGINS TO INFLUENCE SCORE .
1045
1046   In order to rank the nodes for the node influence experiment, we need a score for each node. However,
1047   when using margins as the utility, for each node we get the influence value of each other node for
1048   its prediction. To obtain the final score and then compute the ranking, we consider the average
1049   contribution of each node on the prediction of all the others. Practically, given the matrix of the
1050   influence scores, where the row i contains the influence of the other nodes for its prediction, then we
1051   take the column-wise mean as the final score to use for ranking the nodes.
1052
1053
1054   E.3      O PTIMIZED PCW – PCWP
1055
1056   In PCW we only compute marginal contributions for nodes in the first (1 − tr) portion of each node’s
1057   child subtrees, approximating the marginal contributions of players in the remaining subtrees as 0
1058
       and stop this procedure when the exact number of subsets (50 000) is reached. As this approach was
       developed for the inductive setting, we figured out that the selected truncation ratios were too tailored
1059
       for the datasets used in that setting. It turns out that optimizing the truncation ratios to maximize the
1060
       number of considered permutations leads to better performance for the approach in the transductive
1061   setting. For this reason, we set both the 1-hop and 2-hop neighbors truncation ratio to 0.99 and we
1062   refer to this approach as PCWP.

1065   E.4      C HOICE OF THE HYPERPARAMETER α
1066
1067   As mentioned in § 4, the parameter α represents the probability with which each node from D is
1068   kept in the subset or not. In other words, it establishes the size of the subsets. For instance, in the
1069   train setting where D = Vt , selecting α = 0.1 results is subsets that are the 10% of Vt . This is a
1070   hyperparameter for the approaches DM and α-BANZ and as such, it can be selected via a search. Given
1071   the time complexity of performing such a search for each dataset, we opted to select the best α for
1072   CoraML and adopt this across all the other datasets even though it could be suboptimal. However, as
1073
       it is shown in § 4, the picked α is enough to outperform the other approaches for most of the datasets.
1074   Fig. 21 shows how α-BANZ performs in the most influential pruning experiment when subsets
1075   sampled with different αs are provided to predict the node values. We can see that α = 0.1 and
1076   α = 0.25 are the best choices for realistic value estimates. However, as having fewer training samples
1077   results in faster training of the models, we decided to opt for α = 0.1 as the best choice for α.

       G    M ORE RELATED WORKS
1195
       In the literature, data valuation has been adopted mainly in an i.i.d. setting to study relations between
1196
       training data and changes in the model’s utility or to support prediction’s explanations at inference
1197
       time. This section presents an overview of the works that are closest to ours.
1198
1199   Graph data valuation at inference time. When applied at inference time, a data valuation ap-
1200   proach assesses the most important input patterns that mostly influence the final model’s prediction.
1201
       Particularly for graphs, this translates into highlighting the graph structure or node features that cause
       the prediction for the input. For instance, Duval & Malliaros (2021) compute graph structure and
1202
       node feature explanations for a single example by constructing a surrogate model on a perturbed
1203
       graph and computing Shapley values as explanations. Akkas & Azad (2024) explain predictions by
1204   computing Shapley values edge-wise and outputting the subgraph with edges from the top-k values.
1205   Chhablani et al. (2024) use Banzhaf value in combination with thresholded utility functions on edges
1206   to provide counterfactual explanations. Finally, Bui et al. (2024) introduce the Myerson-Taylor index
1207   which includes structure information inside the Shapley value to identify important motifs for the
1208   prediction. Differently from the mentioned works, we employ data valuation to discover relations
1209   between nodes involved in the training and the final output of the model. Namely, we do not treat the
1210   GNN as a fixed black box function.
1211   Data valuation in the i.i.d. setting. The seminal work by Ghorbani & Zou (2019) extends Shapley
1212   value from feature-level to data point granularity to assess training data importance. As this may
1213   result in a computationally expensive procedure, Jia et al. (2021) compare the utility of different data
1214   attribution methods and propose a fast estimator for Shapley values based on k-nearest neighbors
1215   surrogate. A more recent research direction uses linear surrogates to learn a mapping from a subset
1216   of the training set to the model’s utility (Ilyas et al., 2022). The surrogate is then used to estimate
1217   the utility from newly sampled subsets and the surrogate weights represent the data values. As the
1218
       number of possible training subsets is exponential w.r.t. the number of training data points, the
       learning of the surrogate may be time-consuming. In a follow-up work, Park et al. (2023) employ
1219
       Taylor approximation to linearize a considered deep neural network such that the one-step Newton
1220
       approximation can be applied in a non-linear setting. They show that in such a way, just a few training
1221   subsets are needed to attribute importance to training data accurately. Ultimately, Wang & Jia (2023)
1222   introduce the concept of data Banzhaf for assessing the values of training data and showing its
1223   robustness in differentiating data. Except for data Shapley, these approaches have not yet been applied
1224   to the graph domain. In our work, we incorporate and compare all these approaches to investigate
1225   their behaviour when structural information comes into play.
1226
       Data valuation for graph-structured data. Graph data valuation relating data to model predictions
1227   is still under investigation. As far as we are concerned, only two works try to tackle this direction.
1228   Chen et al. (2023) adapt influence functions to a graph-structured model to approximate the leave-
1229   one-out training. However, they consider only training nodes in a transductive setting, ignoring in
1230   such a way the interactions between unlabeled and labeled nodes. Differently from them, we consider
1231   a more comprehensive picture by studying the transductive scenario, looking at the influence of all
1232   nodes, whether labeled or not. Instead, Chi et al. (2024) propose a new coalition sampling based on
1233   the Winter value that considers the graph’s structure when creating a permutation of nodes to process
1234   and estimate each node’s contribution. However, this work focuses on the inductive scenario where
1235
       unlabeled nodes do not play a role during the model training.
