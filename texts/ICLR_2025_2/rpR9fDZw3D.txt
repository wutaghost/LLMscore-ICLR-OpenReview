009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011
               A critical component in knowledge distillation is the means of coupling the teacher
012            and student. The predominant sequence knowledge distillation method involves
013            supervised learning of the student against teacher-decoded outputs, and is exem-
014            plified by the current state of the art, which incorporates minimum Bayes risk
015            (MBR) decoding. In this paper we seek to integrate MBR more tightly in distilla-
016            tion training, specifically by using several high scoring MBR translations, rather
017            than a single selected sequence, thus capturing a rich diversity of teacher outputs.
018            Our experiments on English to German and English to Japanese translation show
019            consistent improvements over strong baseline methods for both tasks and with
020
               varying model sizes. Additionally, we conduct a detailed analysis focusing on data
               efficiency and capacity curse aspects to elucidate MBR-n and explore its further
021
               potential.

025   1    I NTRODUCTION
026
027   Large language models (LLM) have shown remarkable capabilities in multilingual language un-
028   derstanding and translation. With careful prompting, LLMs can produce high quality translations
029   for a range of translation languages, rivaling or exceeding that of the traditional encoder-decoder
030   architectures translation systems (Anil et al., 2023). However, despite their superior performance,
031   LLMs are substantially bigger, more resource-intensive, and slower than the encoder-decoder trans-
032   lation systems. This raises the question of how the advanced translation capabilities of the LLM
033
      can be transferred to cheaper and more efficient models that can be deployed more widely, and
      with a lower carbon footprint. Knowledge Distillation (KD) presents a practical solution to this
034
      issue, such that the translation outputs of a complex LLM teacher can be used to train a simpler
035
      student model. This builds on a history of distillation research, starting with Hinton et al. (2015), and
036   extending to sequential generation models (Kim & Rush, 2016). Despite many intervening years,
037   Kim & Rush’s SeqKD approach is still widely used in translation research (Tan et al., 2019; Wang
038   et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2023b), specifically in its simplest version which trains the student using
039   standard cross-entropy loss on translation sentences generated by the teacher. Recently, this black-box
040   approach has also gained widespread use in LLM knowledge distillation (Peng et al., 2023; Zhou
041   et al., 2023; Finkelstein et al., 2023), as many proprietary LLMs only offer APIs for user interaction.
042   A key question in distillation is what information from the teacher will best inform the learning of
043   the student. While most SeqKD approaches have used greedy or beam decoding to generate teacher
044   samples, Finkelstein et al. (2023) proposed the use of minimum Bayes risk (MBR) decoding. This
045   decoding method generates a large pool of candidate samples, which are then compared in a pair-wise
046   manner using a reference-based evaluation metric, and the most central sample is selected. This
047   results in an improvement in translation accuracy over beam search decoding, as well as better student
048   performance when used as a distillation target.
049   In this paper we explore ways to use deeper information from the MBR computation for better
050   learning of student models. In particular, we show that presenting several candidates to students
051   instead of the single sequence, results in better distilled student output. Our experiment results over
052   two language pairs and with varying sizes of teacher and student models show that providing several
053   sequences provides consistent improvements over 1-best MBR supervision, and generally observe
      increasing student performance with increasing number of supervision sequences. Overall this work

      Sequence-level knowledge distillation (SeqKD) is a popular distillation method for translation
091
      (Kim & Rush, 2016). It works by training the student to match the sequence outputs of the teacher,
092
      commonly formulated as minimizing

097
098   where H is the set of all possible sequences, p(t|s) is the student model, and ŷ is the output generated
099   by teacher model, e.g., via beam search decoding. This approach is motivated by mimicking the
100   full distribution of the teacher, however the intractability of considering infinitely many sequences
101   necessitates approximation, here by approximating the teacher’s full distribution by its mode.
102   Intuitively, a better approximation of the distribution, as expounded in Kim & Rush (2016), is to
103   generate the n-best translations from the teacher model for each source sentence. Their experiments
104   indicate that this approach is not as effective for distillation as using beam search. One possible reason
105   for this is the low diversity in beam search outputs, and thus small settings of n do not expose the
106   student to meaningful variation. In this paper we propose instead to use MBR decoding to generate
107   n-best candidates, based on selecting the top MBR scoring candidates from a diverse pool of sampled
      outputs.

      MBR (Kumar & Byrne, 2004) is based on the principle of minimizing the expected loss under
109   given loss function. It incorporates both model uncertainty—through operating over a diverse pool
110   of candidates—and a reference-based evaluation metric, which brings complementary signal about
111   translation equivalence. MBR scoring starts with a set of candidate translations H sampled from a
112   model, and then uses a reference-based metric u to estimate the expected loss with respect to the
113   other candidates, i.e.,
114                                                         1 X
                                       y mbr = argmaxh∈H           u(h, r)                          (2)
115                                                       |H|
                                                              r∈H
116                                                                                 P
      This has a time complexity quadratic in |H|, arising from the nested max and     operations.
117
118   The MBR technique incorporates several plausible translations from the model, not just the mode.
119   This feature, as well as the ability to integrate strong reference-based metrics, leads to the substantial
120   gains in output quality over beam search. Previous research (Finkelstein et al., 2023) has shown that
121
      MBR decoding can be used with knowledge distillation, by using y mbr to replace ŷ in Eq 1, resulting
      in improvements over beam search baselines.

124

125
      the set Y mbr , comprising the top n scoring candidates from H (computed as the n max solutions to
126   Eq. 2). This gives the distillation loss,

131   In summary, our method (‘MBR-n’ hereafter) works as follows: given a teacher model and a
132   set of monolingual input sentences, we generate a set of candidate translation samples for each
133   input sentence. Next, we conduct MBR scoring on the candidate set and select the top n scoring
134   candidates to form a training set. Finally, we train the student model using supervised training on this
135   dataset. Letting the student model see more high-quality outputs from teacher can help them better
136   approximate the teacher’s distribution. The workflow of MBR-n shown in Figure 1.

140   In this section, we will introduce the translation language, datasets, models and evaluation.
141
142   Languages and dataset We conduct experiments on two language pairs: English to German (en-
143   de) and English to Japanese (en-ja), following Finkelstein et al. (2023), where en-de represents a
144   high-resource language pair and en-ja a medium-resource language pair.
145
      We used two types of training data. The first is base finetuning data, which primarily serves to train
146   teacher models, ensuring satisfactory translation performance. Additionally, we used this dataset
147   to fine-tune the student model, observing how our method enhances the performance of a student
148   model already proficient in translation. We employ the WMT22 en-de and en-jp training sets for this
149   purpose, which consist of about 20M and 8M sentence pairs, respectively. The second type of data is
150   KD data, employed for SeqKD training. As the teacher’s outputs are used, only monolingual input
151   text is required, however to allow for benchmarking against human-annotated reference training, we
152   use a high-quality parallel corpus, the source sentences serve as KD data, while the target sentences
153   are exclusively used for the Reference-based training baseline. Following (Finkelstein et al., 2023),
154
      for the KD dataset we use the aggregated test sets from WMT09-19 (en-de) and the WMT21 test set
      for en-ja, which contain 30k and 1k instances, respectively.

334   However, we were surprised to observe that this does not map to output uncertainty: MBR distilled
335   models display the most uncertainty, despite each input having only one corresponding translation.
336   As n increases in MBR-n, we noticed a decrease in the distilled student’s output diversity. Why
337   this happens is an open question, that requires further exploration. Note that unlike Beam, MBR-40
338   distillation also enhances model performance substantially.

340   Capacity curse The results from Table 2 and Table 3 also illustrates the capacity gap curse
341   phenomenon. Essentially, employing a better and larger teacher model to instruct the student model
342
      doesn’t always lead to better student performance; instead, performance degrades. This curse can
      be seen for our MBR-n method, and most other distillation strategies. Notably the PaLM2-XXS
343
      teacher has the lowest performance on its own, however it produces some of the best results for
344
      student distillation (e.g., with student XXXS, for both en-de and en-jp). This phenomenon has been
345   extensively discussed in prior literature (Zhang et al., 2023a). Our experiments, conducted on a
346   broader scale, validate that this issue persists for translation distillation with LLMs.

449   5    R ELATED WORKS
450
451   Minimum Bayes risk Minimum Bayes Risk (MBR) decoding, originating from statistical decision
452   theory, aims to minimize the expected risk by selecting the decision that minimizes the expected
453   loss over all possible decisions. It outperform MAP beam search in various tasks (Suzgun et al.,
454   2023; Shi et al., 2022), including machine translation. Initially explored in statistical machine
455
      translation (Kumar & Byrne, 2004), MBR has recently garnered attention in NMT (Eikema & Aziz,
      2022; Freitag et al., 2022) as a method to mitigate biases inherent in MAP decoding techniques
456
      such as beam search. However, MBR comes with a significant computational cost, necessitating
457
      the generation of numerous samples and the computation of utility metric functions quadratically
458   proportional to the number of samples. Thus, efforts have been made to mitigate this computational
459   burden, with some studies (Cheng & Vlachos, 2023) focusing on reducing the resource consumption
460   of MBR. Additionally, besides using MBR for decoding during the inference stage, there have been
461   efforts to integrate MBR into the training process. Shen et al. (2016) introduced MBR training, which
462   involves training with evaluation metrics, while Finkelstein et al. (2023) proposed using the outputs
463   of MBR decoding instead of beam search for KD training of the student model.
464
465   Knowledge distillation Knowledge distillation (KD) (Hinton et al., 2015) is a model compression
466   technique that employ a teacher model to guide the training of the student model, aiming to procure a
467   smaller model that closely mirrors the teacher’s behaviours and performance. Kim & Rush (2016)
468
      introduced the first KD technology for machine translation, known as sequence-level knowledge
      distillation (Seq-KD). This straightforward approach, training the student model with text generated
469
      by the teacher, has been widely employed in machine translation (Tan et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2021;
470
      Zhang et al., 2023b) and the other generation tasks. With the remarkable success of LLMs, many
471   studies have begun employing KD techniques on LLMs. Some approaches (Peng et al., 2023; Zhou
472   et al., 2023; Finkelstein et al., 2023) follow Seq-KD and fine-tune LLMs using teacher-generated text.
473   Gu et al. (2023) and Agarwal et al. (2024) leverage imitation learning and frame distillation, framing
474   the problem as a reinforcement learning (RL) task. They both replace forward KL divergence with
475   reverse KL divergence, as it better suits generation tasks. Finkelstein et al. (2023) present a method
476   similar to ours, employing MBR and Quality Estimation to rank candidates for distillation. However,
477   their approach stops at selecting the best candidate, while our method explores the impact of multiple
478   candidates and conducts a deeper analysis of multiple inputs.
479
480   6    C ONCLUSION
481
482   We present a novel approach to KD of LLMs, MBR-n. By leveraging multiple outputs of MBR
483   scoring, we train the student model to more effectively capture the teacher’s distribution, resulting
484   in improved performance. Our extensive experimentation spans en-de and en-jp translation tasks,
485   encompassing diverse student and teacher model configurations. The findings underscore the efficacy
      of MBR-n.
