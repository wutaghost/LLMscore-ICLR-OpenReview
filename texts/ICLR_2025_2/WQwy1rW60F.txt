009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            State-of-the-art large language models (LLMs) are now claiming remarkable sup-
012            ported context lengths of 256k or even more. In contrast, the average context
013            lengths of mainstream benchmarks are insufficient (5k-21k), and they suffer from
014            potential knowledge leakage and inaccurate metrics, resulting in biased evalua-
015            tion. This paper introduces LV-Eval, a challenging long-context benchmark with
016
               five length levels (16k, 32k, 64k, 128k, and 256k) reaching up to 256k words. LV-
               Eval features two main tasks, single-hop QA and multi-hop QA, comprising 11
017
               bilingual datasets. The design of LV-Eval has incorporated three key techniques,
018
               namely confusing facts insertion (CFI), keyword and phrase replacement (KPR),
019            and keyword-recall-based metric design. The advantages of LV-Eval include con-
020            trollable evaluation across context lengths, challenging test instances with con-
021            fusing facts, mitigated knowledge leakage, and more objective evaluation. We
022            evaluate 15 LLMs on LV-Eval and conduct ablation studies on the benchmarking
023            techniques. The results reveal that: (i) Moonshot-v1 and recent large-scale open-
024            source models, such as Qwen-2.5-72B and Llama-3.1-70B, achieve the highest
025            performance on LV-Eval, particularly at lengths below 64k. (ii) Models exhibit
026            distinct score trends. For example, GLM-4-9B-128k, Yi-6B-200k, and Llama3-
027
               8B-1M exhibit a relatively gentle degradation of performance, but their absolute
               performances may not necessarily be higher than those of LLMs with shorter con-
028
               text lengths. (iii) LLMs’ performances can significantly degrade in the presence
029
               of confusing information, especially in the pressure test of “needle in a haystack”.
030            (iv) Issues related to knowledge leakage and inaccurate metrics introduce bias in
031            evaluation, and these concerns are alleviated in LV-Eval.

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated exceptional performance on a variety of natural
037   language processing tasks. The ability of long-context understanding is crucial for LLMs to deal
038   with tasks based on longer contexts, such as books, lengthy chat history, and so on. Recently,
039
      extensive efforts have been devoted in enlarging the supported context length (i.e., the number of
      tokens that the model can accept as input) of LLMs. These efforts have pushed the supported context
040
      length of LLMs from 2k tokens to 32k tokens (Touvron et al., 2023; Zheng et al., 2023; Bai et al.,
041
      2023a; Zeng et al., 2022; Li et al., 2023a), and some models have achieved a remarkable context
042   length of 128k and 200k (Peng et al., 2023a; Achiam et al., 2023; Yi, 2023).
043
044   In contrast to the rapid evolution of the models’ supported context length, existing benchmarks
045
      have lagged behind. The average word count in current long-context benchmarks typically falls
      within the range of 32k (Bai et al., 2023b; Li et al., 2023b; An et al., 2023; Shaham et al., 2023;
046
      Dong et al., 2023), considerably shorter compared to the supported context lengths of state-of-
047
      the-art long-context models. Moreover, previous benchmarks primarily consist of unaltered public
048   documents and articles. This could be problematic for two reasons: (i) the data might be involved
049   in LLMs’ training processes, and (ii) the facts within them might be common-sense facts found in
050   other training resources. The presence of this issue, known as “knowledge leakage” (Zhou et al.,
051   2023a), can lead to models answering questions with memorization or common-sense knowledge
052   instead of understanding long-range contexts. Last but not least, the automatic metrics employed in
053   most of the existing benchmarks are susceptible to the variations in answer format and the inclusion
      of irrelevant words. Such metrics struggle to accurately assess the answer quality.

064   To address these issues, we propose LV-Eval, a bilingual benchmark with up to 256k words. LV-
065   Eval incorporates distractions and confusions to make the test more challenging, replaces keywords
066   and rephrases sentences to prevent knowledge leakage, and employs a more accurate metric. We
067   summarizes the key characteristics of LV-Eval as follows:
068
             • Sufficiently long context length to evaluate state-of-the-art models: LV-Eval comprises
069
               5 length levels with word counts of 16k, 32k, 64k, 128k, and 256k. Test instances across
070            these levels share the same set of question-answer (QA) pairs, and only differ in the context
071            content and length. Testing on the same QA pairs with different context lengths facilitates
072            a controllable evaluation of models’ long-context ability.
073
             • Incorporation of distraction and confusion to increase difficulty: When constructing the
074            context for each test instance, we mix up distracting documents and supporting documents.
075            This approach evaluates the model’s ability in pinpointing key information in a large bunch
076            of distracting texts. In addition, we insert confusing facts generated by GPT-4 and revised
077            by human annotators into the context. This assesses the model’s capability to accurately
078            reason in the presence of interference.
079          • Keyword and phrase replacement to mitigate knowledge leakage: To mitigate the bi-
080            ased evaluation of long-context ability caused by knowledge leakage, we replace the key-
081            words and phrases in the context and QA pairs. The replacement rules are annotated by
082            human annotators. In this way, LV-Eval requires LLMs to rely on the understanding of
083            context to answer questions rather than relying on memorization or common-sense knowl-
084            edge.
085          • Keyword-recall-based metric for more objective scoring: Existing N -gram metrics such
086            as the F1 score are sensitive to the format variations and non-informative words in the
087            answer, which results in inaccurate scores. To address this, we manually annotate answer
088            keywords and a blacklist of unrelated words. The golden answers are the critical words
089
               or sentences extracted from original ground-truth (GT) answers, while the word blacklist
               contains common and non-informative words such as ‘the’, ‘a’, ‘of’, and so on. The metric
090
               calculation follows a two-stage procedure: the first stage calculates the recall of golden
091
               answer keywords. if the recall exceeds a certain threshold, the second stage will remove
092            all the blacklisted words and then calculate the F1 score between the prediction and the GT
093            answer. This metric design can get scores with higher objectivity.
094
095   Findings. We evaluate 15 LLMs on LV-Eval and summarize the main findings as follows: (i)
096   Moonshot-v1 and recent large-scale open-source models, such as Qwen-2.5-72B and Llama-3.1-
097   70B, achieve the highest performance on LV-Eval, particularly at lengths below 64k. (ii) Models
098   exhibit distinct score trends. For example, GLM-4-9B-128k, Yi-6B-200k, and Llama3-8B-1M ex-
099   hibit a relatively gentle degradation of performance, but their absolute performances may not nec-
100   essarily be higher than those of LLMs with shorter context lengths. (iii) LLMs’ performances can
101
      significantly degrade in the presence of confusing information, especially in the pressure test of
      “needle in a haystack”. (iv) Issues related to knowledge leakage and inaccurate metrics introduce
102
      bias in evaluation, and these concerns are alleviated in LV-Eval.

145
146   Long-Context Techniques. Considerable efforts have been devoted to enhancing the long-context
147   abilities of LLMs. One line of work focuses on making LLMs have extended context sizes without
148   fine-tuning and behave normally on inputs longer than their training context lengths. The design and
149   extrapolation method of the position encoding module (Su et al., 2024; Press et al., 2021; bloc97,
150   2023) is crucial for this goal. Besides, several sparse attention techniques (Han et al., 2023; Xiao
151   et al., 2023) have also been proposed to avoid model collapse. These sparse attention techniques
152
      also alleviate the quadratic complexity w.r.t. the sequence length.
153   There are many other strategies aimed at enabling LLMs to effectively leverage long input contexts.
154   The most commonly utilized strategy is long-context fine-tuning (Xiong et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023a;
155   Peng et al., 2023b). For instance, YaRN (Peng et al., 2023b) conducts fine-tuning with 64k and
156   128k context lengths starting with Llama2-7B/13B, and Yi-6B-200k (Yi, 2023) is trained with 200k
157   context length starting with its 4k variant. Other strategies include the recurrent- or memory-based
158
      architecture (Dai et al., 2019; Wu et al., 2021; Martins et al., 2022; Zhou et al., 2023b; Liang et al.,
      2023), and the retrieval- or summarization-based context compression techniques (Khandelwal et al.,
159
      2019; Borgeaud et al., 2022; Bai et al., 2023b; Zhou et al., 2023b), and so on.
160
161   In this work, we evaluate LLMs of diverse context sizes, ranging from 4k to 200k, most of which
      have incorporated advanced position encoding design and undergone long-context fine-tuning.

179   LV-Eval focuses on two types of QA tasks: single-hop QA and multi-hop QA, and is comprised
180
      of 11 QA datasets (6 in English and 5 in Chinese). The data statistics for LV-Eval are outlined in
      Table 2. Each test instance in LV-Eval comprises three parts: a context (C), a question (Q), and a
181
      GT answer (A), where C is a synthetic document containing the information required to answer Q.
182
183   Datasets in LV-Eval are constructed with existing public datasets as the source, except for factrecall-
184   en and factrecall-zh, which are constructed using the data from PG19 (Rae et al., 2019) dataset and
185   Journey to the West book. Each dataset consists of five subsets of different lengths: 16k, 32k, 64k,
186
      128k, and 256k. All five subsets share the same question-answer (QA) pairs, meaning there are five
      contexts of varying lengths for each QA pair. This allows for a controllable evaluation of models’
187
      long-context ability when testing the same set of questions with different context lengths. In total,
188
      LV-Eval comprises 1,729 QA pairs and 1,729×5 = 8,645 synthetic contexts.
189
190   Figure 1 illustrates the construction process of LV-Eval. For factrecall-en and factrecall-zh, we
191   write one QA pair for each dataset. For the rest 9 out of the 11 datasets, we first choose a specific
192
      number of QA pairs from existing QA datasets (Section 3.1). Then, for each unique QA pair, we go
      through three procedures to construct the context (Section 3.2):
193
194
195
               1. Context mixing up (Section 3.2.1): We first construct five contexts of different lengths
                  by mixing up supporting documents corresponding to the QA pair and several distracting
196
                  documents. For factrecall-en and factrecall-zh, we mix the supporting evidence of the
197
                  single QA pair with distracting documents from two books. For other datasets, the distract-
198               ing documents are unrelated to the question and are chosen from the context documents
199               corresponding to non-selected QA pairs in the same source dataset.
200
201            2. Confusing Facts Insertion (CFI) (Section 3.2.2): Then, in some datasets, we introduce
202               confusing facts by generating them with GPT-4, manually revising them, and randomly
203               inserting these into the context. These confusing facts bear similarities to the original
204               supporting facts but are factually different, without contradicting the original information.
205               This helps make the test instances more challenging.
206
207            3. Keyword and Phrase Replacement (KPR) (Section 3.2.3): Finally, to reduce the im-
208               pacts of knowledge leakage on evaluation results, we manually replace some keywords and
209
                  phrases in the context and the QA pairs.
210
211   When evaluating the generated answer, to mitigate the bias in existing metrics, we manually annotate
212
      the keywords in the GT answer and adjust the metric to focus more on the keywords (Section 3.3).
213   During the benchmark construction, we employ and guide five human annotators to revise the con-
214   fusing facts, replace the keywords and phrases, and annotate the keywords in GT answers. These
215   human annotators include three master students involved in LLM research and two master students
      majored in linguistics. The details of our annotation process are introduced in Appendix C.

303   3.2.3    K EYWORD AND P HRASE R EPLACEMENT
304
305   Knowledge leakage is an important concern in LLM evaluation (Zhou et al., 2023a). On the one
306
      hand, the test data are usually collected from open-access sources, and we cannot fully rule out
      the possibility of their being involved in some LLMs’ training process. On the other hand, some
307
      common-sense questions can be answered without referencing the provided context. Consequently,
308
      LLMs might rely on memorization and common-sense knowledge to answer the questions rather
309   than fully understanding the context. This will cause inflated benchmark scores to overrate the
310   long-context ability of models.
311
312
      To mitigate the influences of knowledge leakage on the evaluation results, we conduct KPR ac-
      cording to manually crafted rules in hotpotwikiqa-mixup, cmrc-mixup, multifieldqa-en-mixup,
313
      multifieldqa-zh-mixup, factrecall-en, and factrecall-zh. Specifically, given a QA pair, the annota-
314
      tors are asked to select keywords or phrases for replacement and write a substitute for each. After the
315   selected keywords and phrases are replaced throughout the entire context, the annotators review the
316   modified context to check and resolve any conflicts: If there are conflicts, the annotators are asked
317   to revise the replacement rule until all conflicts are resolved. One example of the KPR process is
318   shown in Figure 3. See Table 2 for the statistics of the number of replacement rules.

320   3.3     M ETRIC D ESIGN
321
322   The quality evaluation of natural language generation is challenging. Current N -gram metrics, such
323   as the F1 score, treat all words equally. The neglect of differences in word importance leads to
      evaluation bias. For example, in the sentence “Attention is all you need”, the word “attention”


                                                              6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      carries the key information and is more important. However, the answer “Attention matters” will get
325   a lower score than the answer “CNN is all you need”, which is not what we expected. To this end,
326   we adopt a two-stage metric calculation process.
327
328
      Specifically, to evaluate an answer A′ , we first calculate the recall of several “answer keywords”
      in A′ . When the recall exceeds a certain threshold (0.2 for Chinese dataset, 0.4 for English
329
      datasets), we calculate the F1 score between A′ and GT answer A as the final score for A′ . oth-
330
      erwise, A′ gets a zero score. We manually annotate the answer keywords in the GT answer
331   A for hotpotwikiqa-mixup, lic-mixup, loogle-CR-mixup, loogle-MR-mixup, loogle-SD-mixup,
332   multifieldqa-en-mixup, and multifieldqa-zh-mixup. Figure A6 (a) shows an example, demon-
333   strating how this two-stage calculation helps avoid some inflated high evaluation scores.
334
      When calculating the F1 score between A′ and A in the second stage, we exclude common but non-
335
      informative words like ‘the,’ ‘a’, ‘of’, and so on. The word blacklist is constructed as follows. We
336
      first summarized the word counts in the generations of Llama2-7B-Chat-hf and ChatGLM3-6B-32K
337   on all datasets and chose the top 100 words that matched the GT answer most frequently. Then,
338   we manually annotate the non-informative words from the 100 words to construct the blacklist.
339   Figure A6 (b) shows an example of how the word blacklist aids in calibrating the evaluation scores.

343
344   Models and Inference. We evaluate 3 commercial and 12 open-source LLMs on LV-Eval. Their

      Case study on the fact-recall tasks. The factrecall-en and factrecall-zh datasets are constructed
456
      to evaluate the enhanced NIAH (Kamradt, 2023) ability. The traditional NIAH evaluation is basi-
457   cally a retrieval task, asking LLMs to find the answer or passkey in long context, which is too simple
458   for majority of LLMs that they can easily get high scores after task oriented training. Therefore we
459   enhance the NIAH evaluation with CFI and KPR to assess LLM’s positional consistency of retrieval
460   while challenging their comprehension and anti-interference abilility.
461
      We show the ablation results of CFI and KPR in Figure 5 and Table A11. From the first column
462
      in Figure 5, we can see that ChatGLM3-6B-32k attains high accuracy on datasets without CFI and
463   KPR, as long as the input context length is within its context size (32k). However, when either CFI
464   (second column of sub-figure) or KPR (third column sub-figure) is applied, the retrieval accuracy
465   decreases. The accuracy experiences a more severe degradation when both CFI and KPR are applied,
466   particularly evident in factrecall-zh, where a performance collapse is observed (See Appendix G for
467   an example failure case). This indicates that there is room for improvement in the model’s ability to
468   accurately identify a specific piece of information from a long context in the presence of interference.

471
      Keyword-recall-based metric. For a given length level Ld of the dataset, if the single key infor-
472
      mation is uniformly distributed in the context, an LLM with a context window size Lm can only
473
      observe the key information for approximately LLmd of the time. Thanks to our KPR technique,
474
      we can reasonably expect that the LLM cannot get the correct answer through memorization or
475
      common-sense knowledge. Furthermore, in our free-form QA tasks, unlike in multiple-choice set-
476   tings, the LLM cannot easily guess the correct answer. Therefore, we would not expect to see a
477   metric score much higher than LLmd . In other words, LLmd is the ideal score assuming that all an-
478   swers are correctly extracted from and only from the context, and the metric accurately measures
479   the answer correctness.
480
481
      However, as shown in Table 4, when using the original F1 metric, the metric score can be signifi-
482
      cantly higher than LLmd due to the undesired matching of non-keywords and non-informative words.
483   For example, ChatGLM3-6B-32k achieves a score of 26.43% on the 256k length level of the cmrc-
484   mixup dataset, which greatly exceeds LLmd = 12.5%. In contrast, our keyword-recall-based metric
485   with the word blacklist is a more meaningful metric, as its scores are more aligned with our expec-
      tations (i.e., smaller than LLmd ).

      5   L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
522
523   LV-Eval does not encompass other task types such as summarization. Additionally, due to the high
524
      cost, we do not test some of the most recent LLMs, such as GPT-4-128k, GPT-4o, and so on. As
      we release all the test data, one can intentionally overfit the benchmark by training on the test data
525
      to get a high score. In this case, training on LV-Eval datasets with KPR might lead to mistakes in
526
      common-sense knowledge, resulting in a very unreliable evaluation.
527
528   As LV-Eval primarily focuses on extracting and manipulating information from long inputs, we do
529   not include questions that require long outputs. In future benchmarks where evaluating long outputs
530
      is necessary, a more appropriate metric might involve using an LLM rater to assess whether the
      answer matches the ground truth. However, since our benchmark does not focus on long outputs,
531
      we rely on human efforts to provide a simpler metric, with the goal of avoiding the use of a strong
532
      LLM in the evaluation process, as this could introduce additional biases and costs.
533
534   A worth-noting issue about KPR is that as KPR modifies the fact, there exist some cases where the
535   model identifies factual errors and then insists on providing a common-sense response. We view
536
      these cases as an issue with the model’s instruction-following ability, as our prompt explicitly states
      that the answer should be solely based on the context instead of its existing knowledge.
537
538   Our CFI technique relies on manual revision of the confusing facts to ensure the benchmark quality.
539   A possible way to alleviate the human efforts when applying the CFI technique to future corpus
      might be leveraging stronger LLMs to substitute manual revision efforts.

752   Could the CF generated by GPT-4 give an unfair effect to the evaluation results of GPT-4?
753   There is a possibility that the CF generated by GPT-4 could lead to either an unfair advantage or an
754   unfair disadvantage in its evaluation results. On the one hand, the generated CF is aligned to GPT-4’s
755   internal concept, which may lead to an unfair advantage, due to the problem of data leakage. On the
      other hand, it may lead to an unfair disadvantage, because the CF generated by GPT-4 itself may be

      the ones that are hardest for GPT-4 to distinguish, unless explicitly prompted to consider CF within

      Comparison of LV-Eval with two concurrently developed benchmarks: XLBench (Ni et al.,
760   2024) and Loong (Wang et al., 2024) There are several key differences between LV-Eval and
761   these two benchmarks: 1. Context Length Balance: Both Loong and XLBench are not length-
762   balanced, meaning that test instances of different lengths do not share the same QA pairs. In con-
763   trast, LV-Eval ensures that the supporting documents and QA pairs are shared across different length
764   levels, making it easier to track performance degradation as context length increases. 2. Application
765   Focus: Loong is primarily focused on information extraction tasks, such as sheet analysis, which
766   limits its scope. In contrast, LV-Eval is a more general-purpose benchmark, assessing not only
767   knowledge extraction but also manipulation and understanding in complex long-context scenarios.
768
      3. Benchmarking Techniques: We introduce the KPR benchmarking technique to decouple the eval-
      uation of common-sense knowledge from the evaluation of long-context understanding, addressing a
769
      key limitation in existing long-context benchmarks. Additionally, we propose an optimized metric,
770
      leveraging manual efforts to label keywords, which makes the scoring more intuitive and inter-
771   pretable (see Table 4). We hope this comparison clarifies the distinct contributions of LV-Eval to the
772   community.

      Multi-hop QA. In a multi-hop QA task, the reasoning to derive the answer needs to gather mul-
      tiple pieces of information from various locations in the context. We construct four multi-hop QA
781
      datasets: dureader-mixup, loogle-CR-mixup, loogle-MR-mixup, and hotpotwikiqa-mixup.
782
783          • hotpotwikiqa-mixup is originated from two Wikipedia-based multi-hop QA datasets: Hot-
784            potQA and 2WikiMultihopQA. HotpotQA contains 112,779 2-hop questions that are writ-
785            ten by native speakers according to two given paragraphs as the context. 2WikiMultihopQA
786            contains 192,606 5-hop questions that are synthesized using manually designed templates
787            to prevent shortcut solutions. We select 124 samples from the two datasets.
788          • loogle-MR-mixup and loogle-CR-mixup originate from LooGLE’s Long-dependency QA
789            task, specifically the Multiple information Retrieval and Comprehension and Reasoning
790            subtasks. The Multiple information Retrieval task requires aggregation of the evidence that
791            can be directly located in original sentences, while the Comprehension and Reasoning task
792            contains implicit evidence within the context, it requires multi-step reasoning to get the
793            correct answers. We select 139 and 99 questions for loogle-MR-mixup and loogle-CR-
794            mixup, respectively.
795          • dureader-mixup is built from the DuReader dataset. We first randomly select 200 in-
796            stances and then manually remove 24 samples whose answers are longer than 360 words.
797
798   Single-hop QA. In a single-hop QA task, only a single evidence in the context is needed to de-
799   rive the answer. We construct seven single-hop QA datasets: lic-mixup, loogle-SD-mixup, cmrc-
800   mixup, multifieldqa-en-mixup, multifieldqa-zh-mixup, factrecall-en, and factrecall-zh.

826
827
            • Annotators: We hire a total of five annotators, including three master students involved
828           in LLM research and two master students majored in linguistics. They are hired to work
829           on-site as full-time annotators.
830
            • Annotation time: On average, annotators worked 8 hours per day. (1) For CFI, six datasets
831
              comprising a total of 557 instances of confusing facts were reviewed, with the verification
832           process completed by two annotators over a period of three days. This task was assigned to
833           master’s students in linguistics to ensure semantic consistency. (2) For KPR, six datasets
834           containing 1,924 pairs were processed. Replacing key words and phrases in the Chinese
835           datasets involved five individuals over three days, whereas the English datasets required two
836           individuals over two days. (3) For answer keyword annotation, nine datasets comprising
837           955 instances of answer keywords were annotated by two individuals within a single day.
838         • Annotation guidelines: We provided guidelines and examples to the five annotators and
839           conducted a trial annotation in accordance with these guidelines to ensure they understand
840           the guidelines. The guidelines are summarized as follows. For CFI, two types of cases
841           require manual modification: (a) The confusing facts generated by GPT-4 fail to produce
842           interference; (b) The confusing facts generated by GPT-4 conflict with the original facts.
843           For KPR, the guidelines are: (a) Ensure that the replaced words or phrases are distinct
844           from the original and not synonyms; (b) Maximize the differences between the revised and
845           original sentences by replacing as many words as possible; (c) Prioritize the replacement
846           of words that do not have synonyms, as it is challenging to ensure that all synonyms are
847
              correctly replaced for words with multiple synonyms; (d) After replacement, the resulting
              statement may be inconsistent with common knowledge; (e) After replacement, it’s OK
848
              that the resulting statement may be inconsistent with common knowledge, as long as the
849
              answer can be derived from the context and that all information related to this answer is
850           consistent within the context.
851
            • Measurement of inter-annotator agreement: We did not measure inter-annotator agree-
852
              ment during the annotation process. Since the principles of the annotation task are simple
853
              and clear, and the number of annotations is relatively small, we had the two annotators
854           review each other’s work to resolve any disagreements after completing their independent
855           annotations.
