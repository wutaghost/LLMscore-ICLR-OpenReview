009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Multimodal large language models (MLLMs) capable of interpreting images can
012            generate highly detailed and extensive captions, owing to their advanced language
013            modeling capabilities. However, the captions they produce frequently contain hal-
014            lucinations. Furthermore, our empirical analysis reveals that existing hallucination
015            detection methods are less effective in detailed image captioning tasks. We attribute
016
               this to the increasing reliance of MLLMs on their own generated text, rather than
               the input image, as the sequence length grows. To address this issue, we propose a
017
               novel corrector-based method that decomposes a given caption into atomic propo-
018
               sitions, evaluates the factuality of each unit, and revises the caption accordingly.
019            Our method is training-free and can be applied in a plug-and-play manner to
020            any captioning model. Additionally, we introduce an evaluation framework and a
021            benchmark dataset to facilitate the systematic analysis of detailed captions. Our
022            experiments demonstrate that existing approaches to improve the factuality of
023            MLLM outputs may fall short in detailed image captioning tasks. In contrast, our
024            proposed method significantly enhances the factual accuracy of captions, even
025            improving those generated by GPT-4V. Finally, we highlight a limitation of VQA-
026            centric benchmarking by demonstrating that an MLLM’s performance on VQA
027
               benchmarks may not correlate with its ability to generate detailed image captions.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Numerous image captioning methods utilizing deep neural networks (DNNs) have been proposed
032   (Vinyals et al., 2015; Xu et al., 2015). However, they are generally limited to generating short and
033   concise captions, which constrains their broader application in real-world scenarios. For instance, in
034   cases such as assistance for visually impaired individuals, where it is necessary to provide highly
035   detailed descriptions of the scene in front of the user, these methods may not be suitable.
036   Following the recent success of large language models (LLMs) (Brown et al., 2020), there have been
037   attempts to use not only text but also information from other modalities as input to LLMs. Notably,
038   many studies have explored multimodal large language models (MLLMs) that incorporate visual
039   information (Li et al., 2023a; Dai et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024b). These models have demonstrated
040   significantly superior performance compared to traditional models in tasks such as visual question
041   answering (VQA) and captioning (Liu et al., 2024a). In particular, MLLMs, leveraging the advanced
042   language capabilities of LLMs, are able to generate much longer and more detailed captions than
043   conventional captioning models. However, these generated captions frequently contain inaccurate
044
      information, including descriptions of objects that are not present in the input image (Leng et al.,
      2024). Such hallucination problems hinder the practical application of MLLMs in real-world settings.
045
046   Three major approaches have been recently proposed to improve the factuality of MLLM outputs:
047   (i) Decoding-based methods (Leng et al., 2024) reduce the probabilities of hallucination-related
048   tokens during the model’s decoding process without requiring additional training; (ii) Training-based
049   methods (Liu et al., 2023a) further train the models on curated multimodal datasets to ensure they
050
      generate only accurate responses; (iii) Corrector-based methods (Zhou et al., 2024) employ a corrector
      model that detects and either removes or revises hallucinations present in the model’s responses.
051
052   In this paper, we propose a novel corrector-based method called Visual Factuality EnhanceR
053   (V-FactER). Unlike existing approaches that require training a corrector (Lee et al., 2024), V-FactER
      improves the factuality of detailed image captions by leveraging the collaboration between an LLM

054
      and MLLM, without the need for additional training. Moreover, unlike methods that target specific
055   types of hallucinations (Li et al., 2023b; Zhou et al., 2024), our approach does not pre-define the
056   hallucination types, allowing it to address a broader range of issues. The method proceeds as follows:
057   (i) an LLM decomposes a given detailed caption into atomic propositions; (ii) an MLLM verifies the
058   truthfulness of each atomic proposition based on the corresponding image; and (iii) the LLM revises
059   the caption accordingly. Our design is particularly motivated by the observation that, as the length of
060   a model’s response increases, hallucinations generated later in the sequence become more difficult
061   for existing methods (Wang et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2024) to detect.
062   Evaluating the factuality of detailed captions is not straightforward. Through experiments, we
063   demonstrate that conventional caption evaluation metrics such as BLEU (Papineni et al., 2002),
064   ROUGE (Lin, 2004), METEOR (Banerjee & Lavie, 2005), and CIDEr (Vedantam et al., 2015), as
065   well as recently proposed methods (Hessel et al., 2021; Petryk et al., 2024), fail to accurately assess
066   the factuality of detailed captions. To address this issue, we propose a novel GPT-based method
067   for factuality evaluation and validate its effectiveness through experiments. Even if a caption
068   contains factual information, however, it may still be considered inadequate if it does not sufficiently
069
      capture the visual information. To measure the coverage of captions, we construct a detailed VQA
      dataset through a collaboration between humans and an AI agent (Achiam et al., 2023). If a
070
      caption fully encapsulates the information of a given image, questions about the image should be
071
      answerable accurately using only the caption, without referencing the image itself.
072
073   Our experiments surprisingly reveal that methods designed to improve the factuality of MLLMs,
074   which have proven effective in tasks like VQA (Huang et al., 2024), may be ineffective for detailed
075
      image captioning tasks that require longer responses. In contrast, V-FactER significantly enhances
      the factuality of captions and can be applied in a plug-and-play manner to any captioning model.
076
      Our experiments further demonstrate that this improvement extends to captions generated by the
077
      state-of-the-art closed model, GPT-4V (Achiam et al., 2023). Finally, we highlight an issue with the
078   current VQA-centric benchmarking (Duan et al., 2024) by showing that an MLLM’s performance on
079   VQA benchmarks may not correlate with its ability to generate detailed image captions.
080
081
      In summary, our contributions are as follows:
082   • We demonstrate that existing hallucination detection methods may perform worse as MLLM
083     response length increases, and we propose a method that can circumvent this issue.
084   • We introduce V-FactER, a method that significantly enhances the factuality of given detailed image
085     captions. V-FactER is a pipeline that leverages a pre-trained LLM and MLLM.
086   • We propose an evaluation framework and benchmark dataset that overcome the limitations of
087
        existing caption evaluation methods and enable the systematic analysis of detailed image captions.
      • We show that while existing methods designed to improve the factuality of MLLM responses may
088
        be ineffective for detailed image captioning tasks, V-FactER significantly improves their factuality.
089
      • Our experiments demonstrate that current VQA benchmarks fail to reliably capture the potential of
090     MLLMs in real-world applications, such as visual assistants for the visually impaired.

      MLLM hallucinations and mitigation strategies. MLLMs sometimes generate inaccurate re-
      sponses. For example, they may incorrectly describe the characteristics of objects in an input image,
128
      misrepresent relationships between objects, or even describe objects that do not exist. To mitigate
129
      these hallucination problems, decoding-based methods identify factors that induce hallucinations and
130   apply penalties to the probabilities of tokens that are likely to be hallucinations during the decoding
131   process. For instance, VCD (Leng et al., 2024) induces hallucinations using corrupted images, while
132   OPERA (Huang et al., 2024) leverages the correlation between high attention weights assigned to a
133   few summary tokens and hallucinations. Training-based methods focus on exploring training data
134   that can suppress the generation of hallucinations. Liu et al. (2023a) demonstrated that hallucinations
135   can be alleviated by incorporating negative samples—descriptions that explicitly state the absence of
136   certain objects in a given image—into visual instruction tuning datasets. Corrector-based methods
137   (Zhou et al., 2024; Lee et al., 2024) detect, remove, and revise hallucinations present in MLLM
138
      responses by using a corrector model. This model is obtained by supervised fine-tuning a pre-trained
      MLLM. The corrector model then revises the initial response based on the given image.

141   Caption evaluation methods. Since short image captions are relatively easy to obtain reference
142
      captions for, we can use matching-based caption evaluation methods (Hossain et al., 2019) to assess
      them. However, for long and detailed captions generated by MLLMs, the number of reference
143
      captions required for such evaluations becomes exceedingly large. Thus, it becomes impractical to
144
      evaluate detailed captioning using traditional approaches. Hessel et al. (2021) proposed CLIPScore, a
145   reference-free evaluation method. CLIPScore measures the distance between an image and its caption
146   within the pre-trained joint representation space of CLIP (Radford et al., 2021). Additionally, the
147   authors introduced RefCLIPScore, which uses both the image and reference captions within that
148   same representation space. Chan et al. (2023) addressed the limitations of matching-based methods
149   by utilizing an LLM. The LLM-based metric they proposed, CLAIR, assigns scores to captions based
150   on reference captions using an LLM. Similarly, ALOHa (Petryk et al., 2024) detects hallucinations
151   by comparing a generated caption and its reference information through the use of an LLM.

251   3.2    V ISUAL FACTUALITY E NHANCE R
252
253   Our motivational observations demonstrate that asking about the presence of objects using a prompt
254   template effectively detects object hallucinations in detailed captions. However, this approach has
255   limitations, as it fails to detect various types of hallucinations. To overcome this limitation, we first
256
      decompose each detailed caption into atomic propositions using an LLM. An atomic proposition is a
      claim or statement that must either be true or false. For example, the caption “A house has a red roof
257
      and a chimney” is broken down into “A house has a red roof” and “A house has a chimney.” We use
258
      an LLM to perform this process, but we allow flexibility in cases where the results do not strictly
259   conform to the definition of an atomic proposition. We then investigate the truth of each decomposed
260   unit using an MLLM. Each unit is converted into a True/False question and independently fed to the
261   MLLM. The hallucination score H(u) for the unit u is defined as follows:
262
263                             H(u) = − log (min (p (“True”|x, Q(u)) − p (“False”|x, Q(u)) , ϵ))                                     (1)
264   p (“True”) and p (“False”) represent the MLLM’s token probabilities for the “True” and “False”
265   tokens, respectively. x and ϵ denote the input image and a very small constant near zero. Q(·) is a
266   function that converts the input text into a True/False question, which we implement by prepending
267   “True or False?” to the input. Each unit is included in either the True set T or the False set F, based on
268   its hallucination score. To achieve this, we introduce a hyperparameter π, such that T = {u|H(u) ≤
269   π} and F = {u|H(u) > π}. Finally, the initial caption, along with the corresponding sets T and F,
      is provided to an LLM, which corrects the initial caption to ensure it contains only factual information.

287   3.3   E VALUATION M ETHODS
288
289   Traditional caption evaluation methods rely on word matching between a predicted caption and its
290
      reference captions. This approach works because conventional captioning models generate short
      captions. However, modern MLLMs produce much longer and more detailed captions, making it
291
      impractical to obtain sufficient reference captions for accurate evaluation. Given the enriched content
292
      of these image captions, rather than simply evaluating them as good or bad, we aim to assess them
293   systematically by considering two key perspectives:
294
295   • Factuality: The degree to which the content of the caption is factual and free from hallucinations.
296
      • Coverage: The extent to which the caption captures the information contained in the image.
297   We propose evaluation methods for detailed image captions from these two perspectives.
298
299   Factuality. If a human were to measure the factuality of a text, it would be natural to decompose
300   the text into units that can be classified as true or false, and then calculate the proportion of true
301   units (Maynez et al., 2020). We adopt this approach to measure the factuality of captions, utilizing
302   the state-of-the-art model GPT-4o. In our framework, GPT-4o decomposes each caption into atomic
303   propositions and determines their truthfulness based on the corresponding image and reference
304   caption. If the number of atomic propositions judged as true and false are T and F , respectively, the
                                                T
305   factuality of the caption is defined as T +F . This approach enables reliable factuality evaluation using
306   only a single reference caption, unlike conventional methods (Vedantam et al., 2015).
307   To validate this evaluation method, we use the DOCCI dataset (Onoe et al., 2024), which contains
308   human-annotated detailed image captions. Specifically, for each image in a subset of the dataset, we
309   prepare the following four types of captions (details provided in Appendix B):
310
      1. Clean: The original caption (e.g., An indoor top-down view captures a white cat with black patches
311      on a wooden floor, attempting to catch a large pale peacock feather flying above it.).
312   2. Object: An additional description of an object likely to exist in the image but not actually present
313      is added to the Clean caption (e.g., An indoor top-down view captures a white cat with black
314      patches on a wooden floor, attempting to catch a large pale peacock feather flying above it. A
315      small red ball is rolling near the cat.).
316   3. Attribution: Some object attributions in the Clean caption are modified to be inconsistent with the
317      image (e.g., An indoor top-down view captures a white cat with black patches on a metal floor,
318      attempting to catch a small dark peacock feather flying above it.).
319
      4. Relation: Some relationships between objects in the Clean caption are altered to be inconsistent
         with the image (e.g., An indoor top-down view captures a white cat with black patches on a
320
         wooden floor, attempting to catch a large pale peacock feather flying below it.).
321

323   ROUGE, METEOR, CIDEr, CLIP-S, RefCLIP-S, CLAIR, and ALOHa), including our own, to
      determine whether the hallucinations in the three modified types are reflected in the scores. For a fair

      requiring reference captions (BLEU, ROUGE, METEOR, CIDEr, CLAIR, ALOHa, and ours) use a
343
      separate set (Garg et al., 2024) of human-annotated captions (one reference caption per image).
344
345   Table 2 shows that existing metrics are unreliable for evaluating the factuality of detailed image
346   captions. Specifically, BLEU, ROUGE, METEOR, and CIDEr fail to account for hallucinations in
347   the scores and do not assign the highest score to the Clean captions. CLIP can only process up to 77
348
      tokens and operates like a bag-of-words model (Yuksekgonul et al., 2023). This prevents CLIP-based
      metrics from capturing the full content of detailed image captions, particularly missing Relation
349
      hallucinations. ALOHa effectively addresses Object and Attribution hallucinations but fails to capture
350
      Relation hallucinations due to its algorithmic limitations. CLAIR detects and reflects all three types
351   of hallucinations in the scores. However, CLAIR does not focus solely on factuality; instead, it allows
352   the GPT model to directly score each caption, applying the evaluation criteria implicitly defined by
353   the GPT model. In contrast, our evaluation method exclusively considers the factuality of the caption.
354   While it does not assign a perfect score to the Clean captions due to GPT-4o’s limitations in image
355   understanding, it successfully assigns the highest score to Clean among the four caption sets.
356
357   Coverage. Even if an image caption contains only factual information, it would not be highly
358   rated if it reflects only trivial aspects of the image. To assess the coverage of captioning models, we
359   propose a QA-based metric and a benchmark dataset. Our coverage evaluation method is based on
360   the assumption that if an image caption fully captures the information in the image, visual questions
361
      about that image should be answerable by referencing the caption alone.
362   Our goal is to evaluate detailed image captioning models. Therefore, the visual questions for evaluation
363   must include a variety of detailed and nuanced questions about the images. Given the limitations of
364   existing VQA datasets in this regard (Lu et al., 2022; Yin et al., 2023b; Li et al., 2023b; Yue et al.,
365   2024), we construct a new VQA dataset. However, creating a new VQA dataset that includes a variety
366   of detailed questions requires substantial labor. To reduce the associated costs, we follow the process
367
      outlined below to construct our dataset:
368   1. Generating more than 50 questions per image in the IIW-400 dataset using GPT-4o.
369   2. Deduplicating the questions for each image using Sentence-BERT (Reimers & Gurevych, 2019).
370   3. Instructing human labelers to remove or revise questions that can be answered without specific
371      image information, or that are ambiguous or flawed, making them difficult to answer.
372   4. Annotating the correct answers to the remaining and revised questions by human labelers.
373   Our coverage evaluation dataset contains a total of 19,899 multiple-choice questions, with each image
374   averaging 49.8 questions. Although we did not explicitly instruct the GPT model to generate detailed
375   questions, it naturally includes them while generating a large number of questions. We present an
376   example of our dataset in Figure 4. While our benchmark dataset can also be used to assess the visual
377   understanding capabilities of MLLMs, we use it to evaluate the coverage of captioning models by
      having an LLM answer the questions based on the captions generated by those models.

403   We adopt LLaVA-v1.5-7B, LLaVA-NeXT-7B, LLaVA-NeXT-13B, InternVL-Chat-V1.5, and GPT-4V

407   caption. These high-quality reference captions enable precise evaluation of the captioning models.
408   We employ our proposed factuality and coverage evaluation methods, along with CLAIR, all of which
409   use GPT-4o to evaluate the generated captions. To ensure robust evaluation and assess the recall
410   potential of the captioning methods, we summarize the captions (Ge et al., 2024) generated from five
411   different input prompts using LLaMA-3-8B. The only hyperparameter in V-FactER, π, is determined
412   using a validation set composed of five images, their QAs, and reference captions. This validation set
413   is constructed by sampling five examples from the DCI dataset (Urbanek et al., 2024). The prompt
414   templates used in our experiments are provided in Appendix B.

418
      ter π. Specifically, as π decreases, the threshold for determining factual propositions becomes stricter,
419
      leading to more propositions being identified for correction. Consequently, factuality increases while
420   coverage decreases (an ablation study on π is provided in Appendix A). We first investigate whether

425

465   4.3   C OMPARISON WITH OTHER METHODS
466
467
      Various methods have been proposed to mitigate hallucinations in MLLMs, and they have primarily
      been validated on VQA and simple captioning benchmarks. We compare V-FactER with two recent
468
      decoding-based methods (VCD and OPERA), one corrector-based method (Volcano), and one training-
469
      based method (LRV-Instruction) from the perspective of detailed image captioning. All methods,
470   except for LRV-Instruction, use LLaVA-v1.5-7B, while the LRV-Instruction method employs the
471   MiniGPT-4 model (Zhu et al., 2023), as provided by its authors.
472
473
      Table 4 shows that the VCD, OPERA, and LRV-Instruction methods are ineffective for detailed image
      captioning. Ironically, applying VCD significantly reduces the factuality of the LLaVA model while
474
      increasing coverage. Volcano yields only slight improvements in LLaVA’s captions. However, V-
475
      FactER substantially enhances the factuality of the captioning model compared to the other methods.
476   These results suggest that methods proposed to enhance MLLM factuality should be evaluated not
477   only on tasks requiring short responses, such as VQA, but also on detailed image captioning tasks.

      5   C ONCLUSION
525
      Detailed image captioning tasks are closely linked to critical applications, such as visual assistance
526
      for the impaired. Our research aims to assess and enhance the potential of MLLMs in these real-
527   world contexts. We propose V-FactER, a method that improves detailed image captions through
528   the collaboration of a pre-trained MLLM and LLM. In addition, we introduce a framework and
529   benchmark dataset for evaluating the factuality and coverage of captioning models. Our experiments
530   validate the proposed evaluation framework and demonstrate that V-FactER significantly improves
531   the factuality of captioning models. We additionally present the following two key observations:
532
      • Methods designed to improve MLLM factuality, which have been validated primarily on VQA or
533     short captioning tasks, may be ineffective for detailed image captioning and can even reduce the
534     factuality of the backbone model’s responses.
535   • High performance on commonly used VQA-centric benchmarks does not necessarily indicate that
536     the model will excel in detailed image captioning.
537
      These observations raise concerns about the current VQA-centric trend in MLLM evaluation. We
538
      encourage the community to evaluate MLLMs and related algorithms not only on VQA tasks but also
539   on detailed image captioning tasks to gain a more comprehensive understanding of their potential.
