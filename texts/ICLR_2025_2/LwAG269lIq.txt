009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            In this work, we present an adjoint-based method for discovering the underlying
012            governing partial differential equations (PDEs) given data. The idea is to con-
013            sider a parameterized PDE in a general form and formulate a PDE-constrained
014            optimization problem aimed at minimizing the error of the PDE solution from
015            data. Using variational calculus, we obtain an evolution equation for the Lagrange
016
               multipliers (adjoint equations) allowing us to compute the gradient of the objective
               function with respect to the parameters of PDEs given data in a straightforward
017
               manner. In particular, we consider a family of parameterized PDEs encompassing
018
               linear, nonlinear, and spatial derivative candidate terms, and elegantly derive the
019            corresponding adjoint equations. We show the efficacy of the proposed approach in
020            identifying the form of the PDE up to machine accuracy, enabling the accurate dis-
021            covery of PDEs from data. We also compare its performance with the famous PDE
022            Functional Identification of Nonlinear Dynamics method known as PDE-FIND
023            Rudy et al. (2017), on both smooth and noisy data sets. Even though the proposed
024            adjoint method relies on forward/backward solvers, it outperforms PDE-FIND for
025            large data sets thanks to the analytic expressions for gradients of the cost function
026            with respect to each PDE parameter.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030
      A large portion of data-driven modelling of physical processes in literature is dedicated to deploying
031   Neural Networks to obtain fast prediction given the training data set. The data-driven estimation
032   methods include Physics-Informed Neural Networks Raissi et al. (2019), Pseudo-Hamiltonian neural
033   networks Eidnes and Lye (2024), structure preserving Matsubara et al. (2020); Sawant et al. (2023),
034   and reduced order modelling Duan and Hesthaven (2024). These methods often provide efficient
035   and somewhat "accurate" predictions when tested as an interpolation method in the space of input
036   or boundary parameters. Such fast estimators are beneficial when many predictions of a dynamic
037   system is needed, for example in the shape optimization task in fluid dynamics.
038
039
      However, the data-driven estimators often fail to provide accurate solution to the dynamical
      system when tested outside the training space, i.e. for extrapolation. Furthermore, given the
040
      regression-based nature of these predictors, often they do not offer any error estimator in prediction.
041
      Since we already have access to an arsenal of numerical methods in solving traditional governing
042   equations, it is attractive to learn the underlying governing equation given data instead. Once
043   the governing equation is found, one can use the standard and efficient numerical methods for
044   prediction. This way we guarantee the consistency with observed data, estimator for the numerical
045   approximation, and interoperability. Hence, learning the underlying physics given data has motivated
046   a new branch in the scientific machine learning for discovering the mathematical expression as the
047   governing equation given data.
048   The wide literature of data-driven discovery of dynamical systems includes equation-free modeling
049   Kevrekidis et al. (2003), artificial neural networks González-García et al. (1998), nonlinear regression
050   Voss et al. (1999), empirical dynamic modeling Sugihara et al. (2012); Ye et al. (2015), modeling
051   emergent behavior Roberts (2014), automated inference of dynamics Schmidt et al. (2011); Daniels
052   and Nemenman (2015a;b), normal form identification in climate Majda et al. (2009), nonlinear
053   Laplacian spectral analysis Giannakis and Majda (2012) and Koopman analysis Mezić (2013) among
      others. There has been a significant advancement in this field by combining symbolic regression

      with the evolutionary algorithms Bongard and Lipson (2007); Schmidt and Lipson (2009); Tohme
055   et al. (2022), which enable the direct extraction of nonlinear dynamical system information from data.

057   robustly deduce the underlying principles of dynamical systems Brunton et al. (2016); Mangan et al.

059
060   Related work. Next, we review several relevant works that have shaped the current landscape of
061   discovering PDEs from data:
062   PDE-FIND Rudy et al. (2017). This method has been developed to discover underlying partial
063   differential equation by minimizing the L22 -norm point-wise error of the parameterized forward model
064   from the data. Estimating all the possible derivatives using Finite Difference, PDE-FIND constructs a
065   dictionary of possible terms and finds the underlying PDE by performing a sparse search using ridge
066   regression problem with hard thresholding, also known as STRidge optimization method. Several
067   further developments in the literature has been carried out based on this idea, namely Champion et al.
068   (2019); Kaheman et al. (2020). In these methods, as the size (or dimension) of the data set increases,
069
      the PDE discovery optimization problem based on point-wise error becomes extremely expensive,
      forcing the user to arbitrarily reduce the size of data by resampling, or compress the data using proper
070
      orthogonal decomposition. Needles to say, in case of non-linear dynamics, such truncation of data
071
      can introduce bias in prediction leading to finding a wrong PDE.
072
073   PDE-Net Long et al. (2018; 2019). In this method, the PDE is learned from data using convolution
074   kernels rather than brute-force use of Finite Differences, and apply neural networks to approximate
075
      nonlinear responses. Similar to PDE-FIND, the loss function of PDE-Net is the point-wise error from
      data which leads to a regression task that does not scale well with the size of the data set.
076
077   Hidden Physics Models Raissi and Karniadakis (2018). This method assumes that the relevant terms
078   of the governing PDE are already identified and finds its unknown parameters using Gaussian process
079   regression (GPR). While GPR is an accurate interpolator which offers an estimate for the uncertainty
080   in prediction, its training scales poorly with the size of the training data set as it requires inversion of
081
      the covariance matrix.
082   PINN-SR Chen et al. (2021). One of the issues with the PDE-FIND is the use of Finite Difference in
083   estimating the derivatives. The idea of PINN-SR is to extend PDE-FIND’s optimization problem to
084   also find a PINN fit to the data in order to find smooth estimates for spatial derivatives. In particular,
085   the training of PINN-SR combines the search for weights/biases of PINN approximation of the PDE
086   with the sparse search in the space of possible terms to find the coefficients of the PDE given data.
087
      However, similar to PDE-FIND, the point-wise error from data is used as the loss for the regression
      task which does not scale well with the size of the data set.
088
089
090   Contributions. In this paper, we introduce a novel approach for discovering PDEs from data
091   based on the well-known adjoint method, i.e. PDE-constrained optimization method. The idea is to
092   formulate the objective (or cost) functional such that the estimate function f minimizes the L22 -norm
093
      error from the data points f ∗ with the constraint that f is the solution to a parameterized PDE using
      the method of Lagrange multipliers. Here, we consider a parameterized PDE in a general form and
094
      the task is to find all the parameters including irrelevant ones. By finding the variational extremum of
095
      the cost functional with respect to the function f , we obtain a backward-in-time evolution equation
096   for the Lagrange multipliers (adjoint equations). Next, we solve the forward parameterized PDE as
097   well as the adjoint equations numerically. Having found estimates of the Lagrange multipliers and
098   solution to the forward model f , we can numerically compute the gradient of the objective function
099   with respect to the parameters of PDEs given data in a straightforward manner. In particular, for a
100   family of parameterized and nonlinear PDEs, we show how the corresponding adjoint equations can
101   be elegantly derived. We note that the adjoint method has been successfully used before as an efficient
102   method for uncertainty quantification Flath et al. (2011), shape optimization and sensitivity analysis
103   method in fluid mechanics Jameson (2003); Caflisch et al. (2021) and plasma physics Antonsen et al.
104
      (2019); Geraldini et al. (2021). Unlike the usual use of PDE-constrained adjoint optimization where
      the governing equation is known, in this paper we are interested in finding the form along with the
105
      coefficients of the PDE given data.
106

254   Training with noisy data set. Often the data set comes with some noise. There are several pre-
255   processing steps that can be done to reduce the noise at the expense of introducing bias, for example
256   removing high frequencies using Fast Fourier Transform or removing small singular values from data
257   set using Singular Value Decomposition. However, we can also reduce the sensitivity of the training
258   algorithm to the noise by averaging the gradients before updating the parameters. Assuming that the
259   noise is martingale, the Monte Carlo averaging gives us the unbiased estimator for the expected value
260
      of the gradient over all the data set. We adapt the training procedure by averaging gradients over all
      available data points and then updating the parameters (see Algorithm 2 and the flowchart in Fig. 5
261
      of Appendix A for more details). Clearly, this will make the algorithm more robust at higher cost
262
      since the update happens only after seeing all the data.

      7         D ISCUSSION
469
      Below we highlight and discuss strengths and weaknesses of the proposed adjoint method.
470
471
      Strengths. The proposed method has several strengths:
472
473               1. The proposed adjoint-based method of discovering PDEs can provides coefficients of the
474                  true governing equation with significant accuracy.
475
                  2. Since the gradient of the cost function with respect to parameters are derived analytically, the
476                  optimization problem converges fast. In particular, the adjoint method becomes cheaper than
477                  PDE-FIND as the size of the data set increases. The adjoint method by construction finds
478                  the optimal relation between gradient of the cost function and the error in the data points.
479                  This was achieved by finding the extremum of the objective functional using variational
480                  derivative. We note that a clear difference from the point-wise loss ||f − f ∗ ||2 equipped with
481                  backprobagation used in PDE-FIND method is that the adjoint method weights the error
482                  at discrete points with the Lagrange multipliers, see Eq. equation 4 and the final condition
483                  Eq. equation 8.
484               3. Since the adjoint method uses a PDE solver to find the underlying governing equation, there
485                  is a guarantee that the found PDE can be solved numerically with the same PDE solver as
                     the one used by the adjoint method.


                                                                                                           9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
            4. The adjoint method can use a finer mesh in time compared to the available discretization of
487            the data set. This allows an accurate recovery of the underlying PDE compared to the PDE-
488            FIND, where the error in the latter increases as the data set gets coarser since it estimates
489            derivatives directly (either with Finite Difference or a polynomial fit) using the given data
490            set.
491
492   Weaknesses. Our proposed method has some limitations:
493
494         1. In order to use the proposed adjoint method for discovering PDEs, a general solver of PDEs
495            needs to be implemented. Here, we used Finite Differences which can be replaced with
496
               more advance solvers. Clearly, the proposed adjoint method is most effective when there
               is a prior knowledge of the underlying PDE form, and an appropriate numerical solver is
497
               deployed.We note that an inherent limitation of the proposed adjoint method is the possibility
498
               of encountering either ill-posed forward or backward equations during optimization, which
499            limits the time step size.
500
            2. In this work, we used the same spatial discretization as the input data. If the spatial grid of
501
               input data is too coarse for the PDE solver, one has to use interpolation to estimate the data
502
               on a finer spatial grid that is more appropriate for the PDE solver.
503
504
            3. In this work, we made the assumption that the underlying PDE can be solved numerically.
               This can be a limitation when there are no stable numerical methods to solve the true PDE.
505
               In this scenario, the proposed method may find another PDE that is solvable and fits to the
506
               data with a notable error.
507

509   8   C ONCLUSION
510
511   In this work, we introduce a novel mathematical method for the discovery of partial differential
512
      equations given data using the adjoint method. By formulating the optimization problem in the
      variational form using the method of Lagrange multipliers, we find an analytic expression for the
513
      gradient of the cost function with respect to the parameters of the PDE as a function of the Lagrange
514
      multipliers and the forward model estimate. Then, using variational calculus, we find a backwards-in-
515   time evolution equation for the Lagrange multipliers which incorporates the error with a source term
516   (the adjoint equation). Hence, we can use the same solver for both forward model and the backward
517   Lagrange equations. Here we used Finite Differences to estimate the spatial derivatives and forward
518   Euler for the time derivatives, which indeed can be replaced with more stable and advanced solvers.
519
      We compared the proposed adjoint method against PDE-FIND on several test cases. While PDE-
520
      FIND seems to be faster for small size problems, we observe that the adjoint method equipped with
521   forward/backward solvers becomes faster than PDE-FIND as the size of the data set increases. Also,
522   the adjoint method can provide machine-accuracy in identifying and finding the coefficients when the
523   data set is noise-free. Furthermore, in the case of discovering PDEs for PDFs given its samples, both
524   methods seem to suffer enormously form noise/bias associated with the finite number of samples
525   and the Finite Difference on histogram. This motivates the use of smooth and least biased density
526   estimator in these methods such as Tohme et al. (2023) in future work. In the future work, we intend
527   to combine the adjoint-based method for the discovery of PDE with PINNs as the solver instead of
528   Finite Difference method. This would allow us to handle noisy and sparse data as well as deploying
529
      larger time steps in estimating the forward and backward solvers.
