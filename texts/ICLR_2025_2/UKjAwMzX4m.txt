                                                   A BSTRACT
010
011            Post-training quantization (PTQ) is a promising approach to reducing the stor-
012            age and computational requirements of large language models (LLMs) without
013            additional training cost. Recent PTQ studies have primarily focused on quantiz-
014            ing only weights to sub-8-bits while maintaining activations at 8-bits or higher.
015            Accurate sub-8-bit quantization for both weights and activations without rely-
016            ing on quantization-aware training remains a significant challenge. We propose
017            a novel quantization method called block clustered quantization (BCQ) wherein
018            each operand tensor is decomposed into blocks (a block is a group of contigu-
019
               ous scalars), blocks are clustered based on their statistics, and a dedicated optimal
               quantization codebook is designed for each cluster. As a specific embodiment of
020
               this approach, we propose a PTQ algorithm called Locally-Optimal BCQ (LO-
021
               BCQ) that iterates between the steps of block clustering and codebook design to
022            greedily minimize the quantization mean squared error. When weight and acti-
023            vation scalars are encoded to W4A4 format (with 0.5-bits of overhead for storing
024            scaling factors and codebook selectors), we advance the current state-of-the-art by
025            demonstrating < 1% loss in inference accuracy across several LLMs and down-
026            stream tasks.

      1 I NTRODUCTION
030   Quantization is a highly effective and widely adopted technique for reducing the computational and
031   storage demands of Large Language Model (LLM) inference. While recent efforts (Wang et al.,
032   2023; Tseng et al., 2024; Egiazarian et al., 2024; Frantar et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2023) have largely
033
      focused on weight-only quantization targeting single-batch inference, activation quantization be-
      comes critical for improving throughput during multi-batch inference scenarios such as cloud-scale
034
      deployments serving multiple users. Previous works (Yao et al., 2023; Dai et al., 2021) on sub-8-bit
035
      quantization of both weights and activations have relied on quantization-aware training (QAT) to re-
036   cover accuracy loss during inference. However, the prohibitive cost of training and unavailability of
037   training data in recent LLMs has made QAT increasingly difficult and motivated recent post-training
038   quantization (PTQ) efforts (Xiao et al., 2023; Rouhani et al., 2023a; Wu et al., 2023).
039
      Block quantization techniques where each block, typically consisting of 16-to-32-scalar elements,
040
      has its own scaling factor (Rouhani et al., 2023a; Dai et al., 2021) achieve the current state-of-the-art
041
      accuracy for sub-8-bit quantization of both weights and activations. While these works deploy the
042   same quantizer (number-format) across blocks, we hypothesize that one way to achieve lower quan-
043   tization mean squared error (MSE) would be to design a dedicated codebook for each block through
044   an MSE-optimal algorithm such as Lloyd (1982). However, such a design would be expensive in
045   terms of computational effort and memory footprint. Therefore, we propose to amortize this cost
046   via codebook sharing among clusters of blocks. Our method is called block clustered quantization
047   (BCQ) and is comprised of two steps: (1) a clustering step applied to operand blocks, and (2) a
048   quantization step individually applied to operand scalars based on their cluster membership.
049   We propose an iterative PTQ algorithm called LO-BCQ (locally optimal block clustered quantiza-
050   tion) that jointly optimizes the block clustering and the per-cluster codebook. We prove that LO-
051   BCQ greedily minimizes quantization MSE across iterations by performing locally optimal steps at
052   each iteration. With the optimal codebooks derived through LO-BCQ, we demonstrate state-of-the-
053   art bitwidth-vs-accuracy across a suite of GPT3, Llama2 and Nemotron-4 models on a wide range
      of downstream tasks. For all our results, we employ PTQ on frozen model parameters.

      LO-BCQ provides the quantization operation the flexibility to assign data to any of the sign posts
325   (codewords) in Figure 6. The union of these sign posts covers the real line with a resolution that
326   is clearly superior to that of a 4-bit quantizer. Therefore, we hypothesized that these codebooks

329   we calibrated a set of codebooks on data sampled from GPT3 models on Wikitext-103 dataset and
330   froze it. We find that these codebooks achieve comparable quantization MSE compared to those
331   calibrated individually on each operand as shown in Figure 7 which verifies our hypothesis. In our
332   subsequent results, we always employ universally calibrated codebooks.
333   Finally, we note that in a real implementation, activations can be efficiently quantized on the fly.
334   Indeed, LO-BCQ involves computing the following values – per-block array scale-factor sA , per-
335   block codebook selector sb which is the result of the mapping function f (Eq. 4), and the index to
336   closest representation b̂ in the selected codebook (Eq. 2). Note that the computation of sA simply
337   corresponds to a max-reduction (followed by quantization) over the block array, whose size is small
338   (<= 64). Importantly, with LO-BCQ, the size of codebooks (<= 0.19KB) is small enough such
339   that it easily fits within the shared memory of modern GPUs. This is an important distinction with
340   other works on codebook quantization (Tseng et al., 2024; Egiazarian et al., 2024). As such, sb and
341   b̂ can be concurrently computed in a thread-local sub-routine within a custom CUDA kernel. The
342   locality of computation circumvents the need for any synchronization of streaming multiprocessors.

      We apply the LO-BCQ algorithm to the operands before inference and pre-calibrate the optimal
358
      codebooks. In our experiments, we perform this calibration on one batch of activations from the
359   training data of the GPT3-126M model and the Wikitext-103 dataset. We freeze these optimal
360   codebooks across operands and models during all of our accuracy evaluations. Further, we represent
361   each entry of the codebooks as a 6-bit integer. That is, once decoded, the inner product computations
362   with a block array during inference can be performed at 6-bit precision3 . Furthermore, we perform

366   weights and activations such as VSQ (Dai et al., 2021), MX (Rouhani et al., 2023a) and MXFP
367   (Rouhani et al., 2023b). VSQ and MX perform per-block quantization of 16-element blocks with
368   an 8-bit scale-factor per-block resulting in an effective bit-width of 4.5 bits. VSQ quantizes each
369   scalar to INT4 format and per-block scale-factor to INT8 format. MX performs micro-scaling at
370   per-block level with a 1-bit exponent shared by 2-element blocks. Each scalar is quantized to INT3.
371   In this paper, we overestimate accuracy of MX by allowing each scalar to have its own exponent,
372   resulting in INT4 precision. The per-block array scale factors of MX are quantized to E8M0 format.
373
      Therefore, our evaluation results in a bitwidth of 4.5 bits. Further, MXFP explores 32-element
      blocks with 8-bit scale-factor per block resulting in an effective bitwidth of 4.25 bits. The number
374
      format of scalars and per-block scale factors are E2M1 and E8M0, respectively. The quantization
375
      methodology with these block formats is detailed in A.4.4.

      4.2.1   P ERPLEXITY ON W IKITEXT-103
388
389   Across large models such as Llama2-70B and GPT3-22B, 4.5-bit LO-BCQ achieves < 0.1 loss in
390   perplexity compared to the unquantized baseline on the Wikitext-103 dataset. Further, LO-BCQ
391   achieves significant benefits compared to the baselines of equivalent bit-widths. When the quanti-
392   zation bitwidth is 4.5-bits, LO-BCQ achieves an average improvement of 0.9 and 0.76 in perplexity
393   compared to VSQ and MX, respectively, and 1.19 average improvement with 4.25-bits compared
394
      to MXFP across models. We achieve these improvements during PTQ, i.e., without any additional
      training or finetuning.
395
396   MX, MXFP and VSQ perform per-block quantization by associating a scale-factor to each block
397   (or a block array) and with a single number format (quantizer) across blocks. On the other hand, in
398   addition to per-block array scaling, LO-BCQ allows a block to flexibly map to a codebook that best
399   represents it from a set of codebooks. This flexibility allows LO-BCQ to achieve better perplexity.
400
      Furthermore, we find that with a larger quantization bitwidth, LO-BCQ achieves better perplexity
      across models as expected.
401
402   Further, the number format of per-block (or block array) scale-factor has a significant impact on
403   accuracy. VSQ is unable to sufficiently capture the range of activations with its INT8 scale-factors
404   as observed in Llama2-7B, while it outperforms the E8M0 scale-factors of MX in GPT3-22B due
405   to better resolution when representing large values. Across various models, we find that the E4M3
406
      format of LO-BCQ provides sufficient range and resolution to represent the scale-factors.
407
      4.2.2   ACCURACY ON LM EVALUATION HARNESS TASKS
408
409   Across 0-shot LM evaluation harness tasks LO-BCQ shows significant improvement in average ac-
410   curacy compared to MX, MXFP and VSQ at equivalent bitwidth. Further, across models during
411   4.5-bit quantization, LO-BCQ achieves < 1% loss in average accuracy compared to the respective
412   unquantized baselines. When the bitwidth of LO-BCQ is increased by varying its configuration,
413   we find that the average accuracy generally increases albeit with a few exceptions. Although these
414   variations are small (< 0.5%), we believe that they arise due to the universal calibration of code-
415
      books. Our codebooks are calibrated on a batch of training data from the Wikitext-103 dataset and

531   5        C ONCLUSION
532
      The inference accuracy of LLMs during per-block (fine-grained) quantization is significantly influ-
533
      enced by the number format of the operands and per-block scale factors. Several previous works
534   have explored novel number formats to improve accuracy. However, none have explored per-block
535   quantization methods involving clustering that minimize quantization MSE. In this work, we pro-
536   pose LO-BCQ, an iterative block clustering and quantization algorithm that greedily minimizes
537   quantization MSE for any operand (weights and activations) through locally optimal steps at each
538   step of the iteration. We demonstrate that LO-BCQ achieves state-of-the-art perplexity across a
539   suite of GPT3, LLama2 and Nemotron4 models on various downstream tasks such Wikitext-103,
      LM evaluation harness and MMLU.

933   A.4.3    MX F ORMAT
934
      The MX format proposed in (Rouhani et al., 2023a) introduces the concept of sub-block shifting. For
935
      every two scalar elements of b-bits each, there is a shared exponent bit. The value of this exponent
936
      bit is determined through an empirical analysis that targets minimizing quantization MSE. We note
937   that the FP format E1 Mb is strictly better than MX from an accuracy perspective since it allocates
938   a dedicated exponent bit to each scalar as opposed to sharing it across two scalars. Therefore, we
939   conservatively bound the accuracy of a b + 2-bit signed MX format with that of a E1 Mb format in
940   our comparisons. For instance, we use E1M2 format as a proxy for MX4.
