011                                                  A BSTRACT
012
013            The crew pairing problem (CPP) is central to optimal planning and scheduling of
014            operations in the airline industry, where the objective is to assign crews to cover a
015
               flight schedule at minimal cost while adhering to various logistical, personnel, and
               policy constraints. Despite the implementation of optimized schedules, operations
016
               are frequently disrupted by unforeseen events. This vulnerability stems from
017
               the deterministic nature of the CPP’s base formulation, which fails to account
018            for the uncertainties inherent in real-world operations. Existing solutions either
019            aim to safeguard against a specified level of uncertainty or focus on worst-case
020            scenarios.To this end, we propose a reliability-centric CPP formulation amenable to
021            solution by column-generation (CG) SurvCG, that leverages survival analysis for
022            dynamic quantification of uncertainty using the operation patterns in historical data.
023            Applied to CPP, SurvCG forecasts and incorporates flight connection reliability
024            into the optimization process. Through rigorous experiments on a large-scale first-
025            of-its-kind real-world instance under regular and irregular operating conditions,
026
               we demonstrate that SurvCG achieves unprecedented improvements (up to 61%)
               over baseline in terms of total propagated delays, establishing SurvCG as the first
027
               data-driven solution for uncertainty-aware reliable scheduling.

030   1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Airline operations planning involves complex decision making on optimal flight scheduling, aircraft
033   assignment, and crew pairing. A pairing is a sequence of flights assigned to a crew under strict rules
034   governed by aviation regulating bodies and union policies. Crew expenses are a major component of
035   airline costs and are highly sensitive to disruptions, since airlines incur additional costs due to any
036   delayed flights, swaps or call-ins (IATA). In this context, the crew pairing problem (CPP) is critical
037   for determining an optimal set of pairings with minimum crew cost under the said constraints.1
038   The CPP is a highly constrained NP-hard combinatorial optimization problem (Aydemir-Karadag
039   et al., 2013; Lu & Gzara, 2015; Deveci & Demirel, 2018), and is typically modelled as a set-
040   partitioning problem, and, the state-of-art solution methods are based on the column generation
041   (CG) method (Zeren & Özkol, 2016; Quesnel et al., 2020). CPP is often solved by minimizing
042   planned costs based on known schedules and flight times assuming no disruption, months prior to the
043   actual day of operations; also used as the benchmark for evaluation, referred to as the nominal cost
044
      ((Erdoğan et al., 2015; Eltoukhy et al., 2017)). However, unforeseen events, such as crew absenteeism
      and adverse weather, introduce disruption, making the actual costs considerably higher than the
045
      planned ones (Antunes et al., 2019). Such disruptions cause pairings to violate operational constraints,
046
      such as union regulations, requiring overtime, crew swaps, or additional crews, often leading to
047   deadheading or last-minute crew assignments to maintain coverage, resulting in inflated costs, carbon
048   emissions, and customer dissatisfaction (Huang et al., 2020).
049
050
      Using historical flight data at the time of planning can help with uncertainty management and crew
      utilization (Sohoni et al. (2011)). To this end, current approaches introduce uncertainty in CPP by

087   of irregular (disrupted) operations reveals that SurvCG leads to significant performance gains in

093             analysis with column generation, integrating reliability into the crew pairing optimization,
094             ensuring schedules are rigorously penalized for low reliability, thus optimizing for both
095

098             tional survival metrics (e.g., C-index, Brier score) only consider event ordering, inadequate

105   1.1   R ELATED W ORKS
106
107   Traditional crew pairing models are deterministic and fail to account for disruptions such as weather,
      delays, or maintenance issues. To this end, stochastic programming has been applied to introduce


                                                                                              2

108
      randomness and develop more disruption-resilient solutions. For instance, Yen & Birge (2006) aimed
109   to minimize expected total costs through stochastic programming, though scalability remains an issue.
110   Ionescu & Kliewer (2011) and Dück et al. (2012) extended this work by incorporating crew swaps
111   and combining crew scheduling with aircraft assignment for operational resilience. Schaefer et al.
112   (2005) estimate crew pairing costs via simulation, without reflecting this in the optimization. They
113   propose heuristic-based improvements over nominal solution using penalties on undesirable features.
114
      Robust optimization approaches, which account for uncertainty by modeling worst-case scenarios,
115   have also been explored. These models introduce additional constraints and variables, increasing
116   computational complexity. Antunes et al. (2019) developed a robust crew pairing model that accounts
117   for delay propagation and operational disruptions, while Lu & Gzara (2015) proposed a robust
118   optimization approach using Lagrangian relaxation to handle crew costs under worst-case conditions.
119   However, these methods often rely on historical delays or predefined uncertainty sets. This is limiting
120   because they averages are not sufficient to capture the long-tail distribution of delays, and therefore
121   these methods’ ability to handle irregular disruptions in the real-world.
122   Time-to-event modeling is used in a number of domains while survival analysis is a popular choice in
123   clinical studies to analyze disease progression Collett (2015); In & Lee (2018); George et al. (2014),
124   in engineering it is referred to as reliability engineering; we use survival analysis w.l.o.g. since recent
125   developments use this terminology. Survival analysis has been successfully applied in traffic incident
126   modeling Nam & Mannering (2000); Hojati et al. (2014); Li et al. (2020) and predictive maintenance
127   Vianna & Yoneyama (2017); Verhagen & De Boer (2018). A standard approach in survival analysis
128   is to use the Cox proportional hazard (CoxPH) model Cox (1972)], which is a semi-parametric model
129
      that assumes that 1) the logarithm of risk (hazard) of an event has a linear dependence on their
      covariates Breslow (1975), and 2) hazard of two data samples remains constant over time, known
130
      as the proportional hazard assumption. The linearity and the proportional hazard assumptions are
131
      limiting in real-world applications, and works such as Liestbl et al. (1994); Faraggi & Simon (1995);
132   Ishwaran et al. (2008a), and more recently neural network-based models Katzman et al. (2018); Lee
133   et al. (2018); Zhong et al. (2021) have become popular.
134
135
      In the context of aviation, survival analysis has been used to analyze individual flight delays, such
      as factors influencing delay recovery in an airline Wong & Tsai (2012), and assess delays in South
136
      Korea’s air transportation Kim & Bae (2021). To the best of our knowledge, time-to-event models
137
      have not been used to predict connection reliability or in any optimization settings.

141
142   This section develops time-to-event (TTE) terminology for survival analysis in the context of flight
143   events, such as arrivals and departures, with examples. We then introduce flight connection reliability
144   forecasting based on a flight’s likelihood of arriving within the necessary time window.

      2.1     M ETHODOLOGY FOR R ELIABILITY P REDICTION
147
148   The success of a connection depends on whether the flight i lands within a feasible window before
149   flight j’s departure, to allow crew to transition to the next flight. Consequently, delays in flight i can
150   disrupt the entire sequence to be completed by a crew, causing reassignments or missed connections.
151   Hence, we will define the reliability based on flight i’s timely arrival within the connection window.
152   To this end, we use survival analysis to determine the probability that a flight i can connect to a
153   subsequent flight j departing at scheduled departure time, SDTj . The time-to-event (TTE) for flight i
154   is defined as TTEi = AATi − SDTi , where AATi is the actual arrival time, demonstrated as follows.

      3.1           N OMINAL VS R ELIABLE C OST F UNCTIONS
293
294   The cost of arc (i, j), cij , corresponds to the cost of the crew covering flight j after flight i in their
295   pairing, and is calculated apriori in function of scheduled departure and arrival times. As such, it
296   is referred to as the nominal cost to distinguish from the actual crew cost, which is based on actual
297   departure and arrival times. cij is calculated based on the elapsed time of flight i and the connection
298   time between the two consecutive flights i and j, expressed as:
299
                                                cij = (cei + α · ccij ), where cei = SETi , ccij = SDTj − SATi                                                                               (13)
300
301   The scaling factor α penalises longer connection times, making pairings with shorter layovers between
302   flights more attractive from a cost perspective. Specifically, α reflects the operational priorities, such
303   as reducing crew downtime or enforcing extended layovers. The nominal cost function 13 is not
304   equivalent but mimics the pay-and-credit model, which itself does not accurately reflect the complexity
305
      of crew pay in practice. Our function is motivated by discussions with an industry partner.
306   The nominal cost cij assumes perfect operation of the airlines, which is rarely the case. Delays in
307   one flight may cause a cascading effect of delays and disruptions in subsequent flights in the pairing,
308   leading to actual costs significantly different from the nominal. The reliable cost function ϕij (cij )
309   that we propose makes use of the reliability score of a connection to augment the nominal cost and
310   account for delays and disruptions under a push-back recovery policy that is commonly used in the
311
      literature (Schaefer et al. (2005); Antunes et al. (2019); Lu & Gzara (2015)). It is expressed as:
312                                                   rcij = ϕij (cij ) = cij (λ1 e−λ2 rij + 1) − cij (λ1 e−λ2 )                                                                             (14)
313
314   where λ1 is a parameter that adjusts how significantly the reliability score impacts the reliable cost.
315
      A higher value of λ1 increases the sensitivity of the cost adjustment to changes in rij . The parameter
      λ2 controls the rate of exponential decay, i.e, the rate of increase of the reliable cost as rij decreases.
316
      A larger λ2 results in a steeper decay curve, which more aggressively penalizes lower rij values.
317
      The term cij (λ1 e−λ2 ) is the vertical adjustment, it shifts the cost function such that the reliable cost
318
      equals the nominal cost under ideal conditions (rij = 1). The effects of varying λ1 and λ2 on the
319   reliable cost function are illustrated in Figure 4.
320

322
      is nonlinear in cij . Furthermore, similar incorporation of reliability is possible into a pay-and-credit
323
      costing model. Consequently, ϕ(ckp ) may be calculated for a given pairing p ∈ P k and the objective

513   5                         D ISCUSSION
514
515   We introduce a data-driven approach for the Crew Pairing Problem (CPP) to tackle the uncertainty in
516   real-world planning using an exposition of crew pairing in aviation operations. Our results indicate the
517   tremendous potential to impact operational efficiencies in the real world by leveraging historical on
518   time performance data. We accomplish this by incorporating the reliabilities, predicted using survival
519
      analysis – a popular time-to-event model, in the cost function of the CPP task and combining this
      with column generation algorithm – the state-of-the-art algorithm to solve CPP. SurvCG significantly
520
      reduces total propagated delay and deadheading connection costs compared to the nominal solution.
521
522   Reliable solutions also show a significant reduction in higher percentiles across various scenarios,
523   demonstrating their robustness under mixed and highly irregular conditions. While recent methods
524   Antunes et al. (2019) report a reduction of 18 − 20% as compared to the nominal solution in terms
525
      of total propagated delays, we demonstrate that SurvCG can lead to a reduction of up to approx.
      60% over nominal on this metric, that too under the challenging irregular operating conditions. For
526
      instance, in the 75R, 25IR-70 scenario in Table 3, reliable solutions save 1797.78 minutes in TPGD at
527
      the 99th percentile. Similar trends are observed in the 75R, 25IR-80 and 75R, 25IR-90 scenarios, with
528   savings of 1955.12 minutes and 1724.70 minutes, respectively. Our solution also reduce certain costs
529   and significantly decrease deadheading, resulting in lower operational expenses over the nominal.
530
531
      SurvCG, is also, to the best of our knowledge, the first algorithm to incorporate data-driven reliabili-
      ties for this long-term planning problem. As a result, this investigation also lays the foundations of
532
      developing other machine learning for optimization methods. While, a limitation of our approach is
533
      that the optimal pairings obtained on solving the CPP using column generation algorithm depends on
534   how accurate the reliability predictions are from the survival model. It is worth noting that while we
535   use a specific survival analysis model – CoxTime, which worked well for this dataset, SurvCG is
536   not constrained to using this model and practitioners can incorporate any appropriate time-to-event
537   model. Future work on this thread can extend the use of reliabilities for other optimizations, or even
538   combine this with recent works on reinforcement learning for column generation Chi et al. (2022).
539   Overall, our work opens new avenues to usher operational efficiencies in the aviation industry and
      beyond in the backdrop of high competition, climate impacts, and customer retention.

      C    I NSTANCE G ENERATION
757
758   To set up a crew pairing experiment, we first generate an instance of crew operations consisting of
759   a closed network of flights and connections over a fixed period. The crew starts and finishes at a
760   specified crew base. Specifically, an instance is described by a network N containing a set of flights
761   F̂, connections Â, and costs Ĉ. Using a set of spatiotemporal constraints C, we construct N as
762   follows:
763
764         1. Filter D according to C to obtain a set of flights F0 .
765
            2. Construct an initial network N0 by applying space and time constraints to F0 . A connection
               between flight i and flight j is feasible if originj = desti and δmin ≤ SDTj − SATi ≤
766
               δmax , where, δmin , δmax are the minimum and maximum connection times.
767
            3. Prune N0 to remove redundant flights and extract the subgraph N describing our instance.
768
769   We create an instance for December 2-5, 2019, using the flight operations of Endeavor Air between
770   all the airports in the network on these dates, with John F. Kennedy International Airport (JFK) as the
771   crew base.

786
787   Filtering: During the filtering phase, we specify space and time constraints to select relevant flights
788   from the dataset. The constraints include the specific dates and the crew base for starting and ending
789   operations. This phase aims to narrow down the vast dataset to a manageable subset that is relevant
790   to the instance we want to create. By applying these constraints, we extract a filtered set of flights F0
791   from the dataset D, such that F0 = {i ∈ D|i satisfies C}. This step ensures that only flights within
792
      the specified dates and that either start or end at the crew base are included in the instance.
793   Constructing a Network: In the network construction phase, we form connections between the
794   filtered flights. The connections represent possible pairings of flights that a crew can operate within
795   the given constraints. The network includes nodes for the crew base and connections between flights
796   that are feasible based on time constraints. Specifically, a connection between flight i and flight
797   j is feasible if the destination of i matches the origin of j, and the time difference between the
798
      scheduled departure time of flight j and the scheduled arrival time of flight i falls within the allowable
      connection time range. The cost for a connection is calculated as SDTj − SATi + SETi , where SDT
799
      is the scheduled departure time, SAT is the scheduled arrival time, and SET is the scheduled elapsed
800
      time.
801
802   Network Pruning: The pruning phase ensures that the network remains practical and feasible for
803   crew pairings. During this phase, we remove redundant or infeasible flights and connections that do
804
      not contribute to viable pairings. This is done by identifying and retaining only those flights that can
      form a continuous path from the origin to the destination crew base. Flights that do not participate in
805
      any such path are pruned out.
