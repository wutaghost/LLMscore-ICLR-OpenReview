009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            We present a novel method to identify parameter of nonlinear Ordinary Differen-
012            tial Equations (ODEs) using time series data. Our approach fits parameters by
013            matching a collocation-based estimate of the integral of the learned derivative to an
014            interpolation of the trajectory, thus avoiding the computational cost of ODE solvers
015            in adjoint methods and the sensitivity to noise of derivative estimates in gradient
016            matching methods. By employing batching strategies based on time subintervals
017            and state components, our method achieves linear complexity in relation to system
018
               dimensions and dataset sizes. The method is highly parallel enabling fast gradient
               evaluations and a faster convergence than adjoint methods. For fully observed
019
               systems, we demonstrate the method on canonical dynamical systems, where the
020
               method achieves speed-ups of three orders of magnitude over adjoint methods and
021            an increased robustness against observational noise. We provide an extension to
022            partially observed systems and demonstrate the method on the Lorenz63 attractor.

027   Ordinary Differential Equations (ODEs) are widely used to model dynamical systems in fields such
028   as physics, biology, and engineering, Strogatz (2014); Villaverde and Banga (2014). Estimating
029   unknown parameters of arbitrary nonlinear ODEs -derived from physical laws, or postulated like SIR
030   models in epidemiology- from noisy, partially observed time-series data is important.

150   2.1   C OLLOCATION METHODS
151
152   Collocation methods have become increasingly popular to solve optimal control problems; see
153
      Betts (2010). These methods are implicit integration methods where the value of state and control
      (the parameters Θ) at specific discretization nodes are decision variables of a nonlinear program.
154
      Although, as noted in Varah (1982), these methods are the backbone of gradient matching, their direct
155
      use for system identification is original. We selected the Legendre-Gauss-Radau (LGR) approach for
156   its suitability to initial value problems and its properties: it is A-stable, i.e., with numerical stability
157   guarantees for classes of initial value problems, symplectic, i.e., preserving the Hamiltonian of the
158   system, and has an approximation error is o(h2K−1 ), where K is the degree of the approximating
159   polynomial and h is the size of the time step, see Fahroo and Ross (2008); Garg et al. (2011b) for
160   discussions and proofs. While other collocation methods are compatible with our approach, our
161   choice ensures that the KKT conditions discretize the Pontryagin Principle, which connects our
      method to the adjoint sensitivity method, see Wei et al. (2016).

      3.2   R ESOLUTION METHOD AND QUALITATIVE DISCUSSION
231
      Problem (6) is solved by alternate descent with Algorithm 1. Estimations of the trajectory at LGR
232
      nodes using linear regressions (ril terms / L. 6 of the pseudo-code) alternate with gradient descent on
233   system inversion problem (ℓ(Θ, X) / L. 7 of the pseudo-code). Solving Problem (3) directly suffers
234   in practice from the same issues detailed in Roesch et al. (2021): gradients are relevant only when the
235   integrated trajectory is close to data, although numerical integration remains computationally costly,
236   as evidenced in Figure 1 by the initial plateau of ODE Solver methods from a random initialization.
237   Allowing numerical integration error and computing the gradient along the interpolation avoids these
238   issues. We set ρ=1 as this scales gradients that are rescaled after by Adam, Kingma and Ba (2014).
239   Denoising, filtering and connection to other methods
240   Noise on data translates into interpolation error which is

242   nodes over the interval [0,1] (see Figure (2). To mitigate this,

245   advanced approaches can be beneficial (L. 2 of Algorithm

495   6    L IMITATIONS AND FURTHER WORK
496
497   As mentioned in section (3.2), the accuracy of the interpolation is key for the performance of
498   the method as it impacts two parts of the loss. The 1D, 1 subinterval system inversion term
499
      ∥D−1 F(X, Θ) − (X − X0 )∥2 is impacted in two ways by the interpolation error:
500
             • In the numerical integration term along the trajectory via the D−1 F (X, Θ) terms, the non
501            linearity F can create a bias: the distribution of the error on F (X + ϵ, Θ) is not centered
502            around F (X, Θ), even if the approximation error ϵ is.
503
             • In the difference (Xi − X0 ), systematic bias is neutralized by the difference. As X0 is
504
               involved every integral of the subinterval, the variance on the initial condition is a key
505            component for global accuracy and has motivated the Savitsky-Golay filter in Appendix (A).
506            The use of multiple overlapping and random subintervals is a way to balance this variance
507            on multiple points and subintervals, mitigating this single point of failure issue.
508
509   The impact of systematic bias, in the absence of filtering based on current equations, with the caveats
510   on local optima mentioned in section (3.2), can often be mitigated by denoising with more data, and
511   longer horizons. If not, our method can be used to initialize parameters prior to adjoint methods.
512   Another failure mode is about generalizability in the absence of prior structure on equations: in
513   particular for Neural ODEs the trajectory may leave the manifold of the training data, and, in
514   the absence of prior or additional penalty, the integrated trajectory becomes completely irrelevant.
515   Oscillators such as the damped one in Chen et al. (2018) or Lokta-Volterra models Lotka (1926), as
516   well as attractors have the characteristic that the system is trapped in a bounded manifold possibly
517   and will visit regions multiple times over long horizons. In the case of the damped oscillator, as long
518
      as the trajectory remains in the envelope of interpolated trajectories, as solutions of ODEs do not
      intersect, the trajectory will generalize as it will cross a trained region.
519
520   The algorithm in this paper is based on fixed length subintervals and orders, but this is not a hard
521   limitation. The integration order K needs not be fixed (aside from easing implementation and
522   providing a rigid computation graph), we can in principle use variable-length subintervals. Similarly
523   as for SINDy methods, our method can be used to learn Partial Differential Equations using the
524
      method of lines to convert PDEs into a system of coupled ODEs.
525
526   7    C ONCLUSION
527
528   We have studied the utilization of a particular collocation method for system identification of nonlinear
529   dynamical systems, leveraging data to simplify the typically computationally intensive computations.
530   Our efficient method requires fewer backpropagations to evaluate gradients at each step of the descent
531   and introduces batching strategies, such as by subintervals and state components, to enable high
532   parallelism and scalability linearly with dataset size, horizon length T , and system dimensions,
533
      contrasting with autoregressive ODE Solver methods.

802   computationally cheap sparsifying method is scalable to large dimensions. However, on large
803   dimensions, for instance, N = 200, the number of monomials grows to more than 20,000 terms for a

      are no longer cheap, though easily treated in parallel.
