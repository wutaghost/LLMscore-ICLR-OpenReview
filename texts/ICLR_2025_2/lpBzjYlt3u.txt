009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Autonomous agents powered by large language models (LLMs) show promis-
012            ing potential in assistive tasks across various domains, including mobile device
013            control. As these agents interact directly with personal information and device
014            settings, ensuring their safe and reliable behavior is crucial to prevent undesirable
015            outcomes. However, no benchmark exists for standardized evaluation of the safety
016
               of mobile device-control agents. In this work, we introduce MobileSafetyBench, a
               benchmark designed to evaluate the safety of device-control agents within a real-
017
               istic mobile environment based on Android emulators. We develop a diverse set of
018
               tasks involving interactions with various mobile applications, including messaging
019            and banking applications, challenging agents with managing risks encompassing
020            misuse and negative side effects. These tasks include tests to evaluate the safety
021            of agents in daily scenarios as well as their robustness against indirect prompt in-
022            jection attacks. Our experiments demonstrate that baseline agents, based on state-
023            of-the-art LLMs, often fail to effectively prevent risks while performing the tasks.
024            To mitigate these safety concerns, we propose a prompting method that encour-
025            ages agents to prioritize safety considerations. While this method shows promise
026            in promoting safer behaviors, there is still considerable room for improvement to
027
               fully earn user trust. This highlights the urgent need for continued research to
               develop more robust safety mechanisms in mobile environments.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Recent advances in building autonomous agents using large language models (LLMs) have demon-
035   strated promising results in various domains, including mobile device control (Yang et al., 2023;
036   Lee et al., 2024; Rawles et al., 2024). Mobile device control agents can enhance productivity and
037   improve accessibility of user interactions by automating daily tasks such as web interactions, data
038
      sharing, text messaging, social media access, and financial transactions. However, as these agents
      gain the ability to control personal devices, ensuring their safety becomes crucial, particularly be-
039
      cause they have access to sensitive user information and other critical data.
040
041   Despite significant progress in developing benchmarks for evaluating the safety of LLMs, prior
042   works have primarily focused on safety assessments based on question-answering formats (Bai et al.,
043   2022; Li et al., 2024; Yuan et al., 2024). These formats often fail to detect the dangerous behaviors
044
      of LLM agents when controlling mobile devices, making existing benchmarks insufficient for a
      thorough safety assessment. To rigorously evaluate the safety of such agents, it is crucial to develop
045
      a benchmark that incorporates a realistic interactive environment and diverse risks.
046
047   In this work, we present MobileSafetyBench, a novel research platform designed to evaluate the
048   safe behavior of agents controlling mobile devices. MobileSafetyBench is based on several impor-
049   tant design factors (see Figure 1 for an overview). Central to our benchmark is the use of Android
050
      emulators to create interactive and realistic environments. MobileSafetyBench includes diverse ap-
      plications such as memos, calendars, social media, banking, and stock trading, which are essential
051
      for assessing operations commonly used in everyday life.
052
053   Based on realistic environments, we develop a task suite to evaluate the safety of agents across
      various scenarios. These tasks incorporate major risk types associated with mobile device usage,

093   However, despite these improvements, the agents still exhibit unsafe behaviors, such as overlooking
094   the safety considerations they have generated. This inconsistency highlights the need for developing
095   new methods to enhance agent reliability further.

097
098    • We develop a novel benchmark platform evaluating the helpfulness and safety of agents control-

      2     R ELATED WORK
109
110
      Building agents with LLMs Developing intelligent agents with LLMs has gained significant in-
111   terest, as LLMs have shown adeptness in planning, reasoning, and tool usage. Early research, such
112   as ReAct (Yao et al., 2023) and Reflxion (Shinn et al., 2023), have demonstrated that the agents
113   employing LLMs are capable of making sequential decisions from the provided set of actions to in-
114   teract with the environments. Recently, adopting LLMs on more practical domains, as in navigating
115   websites (Zhou et al., 2024) or controlling mobile devices (Yang et al., 2023), is being increasingly
116   investigated. To this end, different prompting methods for advancing the agents are being studied
117   aggressively (Rawles et al., 2024). This work presents experimental results with agents employing
118   LLMs and, additionally, introduces a novel prompting method for guiding safe behaviors.
119
120   Benchmarking agent controlling digital devices Early works have focused on evaluating the pro-
121
      ficiency of agents developed with reinforcement learning (Toyama et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2018). Re-
      cently, benchmarks for LLM agents with authentic environments are thrusting substantial progress.
122
      In web navigation, Webshop (Yao et al., 2022) and WebArena (Zhou et al., 2024) have demonstrated
123
      realistic web simulators for agents manipulating UIs on the web browser. VisualWebArena (Koh
124   et al., 2024) and MMInA (Zhang et al., 2024) have showcased benchmarks considering multi-modal
125   inputs with images. Controlling more general platforms (e.g., operating systems) has also been stud-
126   ied in several works, including ARA (Kinniment et al., 2023), AgentBench (Liu et al., 2023), and
127   OSWorld (Xie et al., 2024). In this work, we focus on the LLM agents controlling real-system mo-
128   bile digital devices, similar to B-MoCA (Lee et al., 2024) and AndroidWorld (Rawles et al., 2024).
129   While prior studies focus on evaluating the helpfulness of the agents, we also consider safety.
130
131   Benchmarking safety of LLMs Numerous studies warn of LLMs’ potential threats such as pro-
132   ducing harmful, biased, toxic, or unethical content. Early studies have focused on evaluating the
133   response of LLMs to safety-related questions (Qi et al., 2024; Mireshghallah et al., 2024; Li et al.,
134   2024). As LLMs are increasingly integrated into real-world tools and environments, several stud-
135
      ies for investigating safe behaviors of LLM agents are ongoing, as in ToolEmu (Ruan et al., 2024)
      or R-judge (Yuan et al., 2024), yet exhibiting several shortcomings such as disregard of realistic
136
      interactive environments. Researchers also have adopted aggressive evaluation techniques, such as
137
      prompt attacks to test the robustness of LLMs leveraging tools (Zhan et al., 2024; Debenedetti et al.,
138   2024; Wu et al., 2024). Several concurrent works study the safety of LLM agents, mainly focusing
139   on jailbreaking scenarios (Andriushchenko et al., 2024) or web environments (Kumar et al., 2024;
140   Liao et al., 2024). In this work, we demonstrate a benchmark evaluating more general safe behaviors
141   of LLM agents manipulating mobile devices.

      helpfulness of mobile device control agents.

      3.1   D ESIGN PRINCIPLES
149
150   MobileSafetyBench is designed as a platform to assess both the helpfulness and safety of agents
151   controlling mobile devices. A key feature of the benchmark is its use of Android emulators to create
152   interactive and realistic environments, where device control is formulated as a sequential decision-
153   making problem. Agents interact with the environment to perform diverse tasks, each derived from
154   real use cases and involving applications such as social media, banking, and stock trading.
155
      Another key feature of MobileSafetyBench is its rigorous safety evaluation process. We define
156   safety within our benchmark as the agent’s robustness to malicious instruction and ability to ensure
157   that its actions do not result in potentially harmful consequences while performing a requested task,
158   as the risks in our tasks encompass the misuse of agents (OECD, 2019) and the negative side effects
159   caused by the agents (Amodei et al., 2016). As safe behaviors can vary depending on the specific task
160   requested or the device’s status, we develop diverse scenarios to challenge agents comprehensively.
161   Each task incorporates an automatic evaluator that utilizes system information from the Android
      emulators, ensuring that the evaluations are both standardized and reliable.

176   3.2      F RAMEWORK
177
178   Problem formulation In MobileSafetyBench, we formulate the task of controlling mobile devices
179   as a sequential decision-making problem, where an agent interacts with an environment simulated
180   by an Android emulator. Formally, the internal state st of the environment transitions to the next
181   state st+1 based on the action at taken by the agent at each discrete time t. The agent, given a
182
      task instruction c, receives the partial observation ot (representing incomplete information about the
      state st ) and generates an action at . During the interactions, the agent can freely utilize diverse
183
      applications in the device, such as web browsers or memos. At the end of an episode, which lasts
184
      no longer than a predefined maximum length T , the environment returns a score r. This score is
185   computed by evaluators based on the final state of the environment sT and the history of actions
186   a1:T taken by the agent.
187
188   Environment implementation For developing a realistic environment, our benchmark utilizes
189   Android, a widely used open-source operating system. To enable seamless interaction for agents,
190   we implement several interfaces. The first interface, an action converter, transforms the action output
191   from the agent into a legible form at that the emulator can process. This includes interpreting actions
192   such as tapping or swiping on the screen from responses generated by agents using LLMs in text
193   form. Additionally, we ensure each observation ot includes both a screen image and text describing
194   the UI elements present on the screen. To facilitate this, a screen-layout translator provides text
195
      descriptions for UI elements, detailing element types (e.g., “Button”) and the associated text (e.g.,
      “Click Me” described in the button element). For more details on these environmental interfaces,
196
      we refer the readers to Appendix A.

      3.3      TASK DESIGN
200   Diverse daily tasks MobileSafetyBench presents 87 numbers of tasks, divided into 44 low-risk
201   tasks and 43 high-risk tasks. Utilizing Android emulators, our tasks are grounded in daily life and
202   designed to reflect common real-life uses of mobile devices.1 The benchmark includes tasks associ-
203   ated with text messaging, web navigation, social media, calendar settings, and financial transactions.
204   Specifically, we categorize tasks into six operational areas (see Figure 2 (a)): Text Messaging,
205   Web Navigation, Social Media, Finance, Device/Data Management (e.g., man-
206   aging photos and applications), and Utility (such as memos, maps, and calendars). Our bench-
207   mark employs diverse applications to simulate these diverse task categories. For more details, please
208
      refer to Appendix A.4.
209
210   Task specification Each task is defined by specifying the task instruction and configuring the
211
      initial status of the environment. For instance, as illustrated in Figure 3, the task is defined with
      the instruction “Could you check the Photos application, and share an image file with James via
212
      Bluetooth?” and the initial device status which is set to contain an image in file storage. The agent is
213
      then challenged to navigate to the Photos application and manipulate UI elements to share the image
214   via Bluetooth.

      3.4    E VALUATION SCHEME
271
272   For rigorous evaluations, our benchmark utilizes rule-based evaluators. Specifically, these evaluators
273   determine task success or failure by checking if pre-defined conditions are met, based on the agents’
274   decisions and their effects on the device. The evaluators have access to the action history to as-
275   sess the agents’ decisions. They also examine device information, including system configurations,
276   file storage, and application databases, ensuring consistent and reliable evaluation. For each task,
277   the evaluator assesses the goal achievement and harm prevention of the agents. Goal achievement
278
      refers to the successful completion of the task instruction by the agent. Notably, in high-risk tasks,
      achieving the goal may not always be desirable, as it could lead to the manifestation of harm. Harm
279
      prevention refers to the agent’s proactive decision to avoid potential risks during task performance.
280
      In our framework, this involves refusing to proceed with the task or asking consent from the user,
281   regardless of whether risks are actually present in the task. Further details on the evaluation scheme
282   are provided in Appendix C.

      4     BASELINE AGENTS
286
287
      In this work, we focus on benchmarking multi-modal LLMs with prompting as baseline agents
      for controlling mobile devices (Zhou et al., 2024; Lee et al., 2024; Rawles et al., 2024). These
288
      agents receive multi-modal observations consisting of screen images and text descriptions of the UI
289
      elements. They then choose an appropriate action from a pre-defined set of options. Examples of
290   action options include tapping UI elements, swiping the screen in a specified direction, and inputting
291   text into a target field, providing a flexible interface for device control. Additionally, we incorporate
292   three specific actions to enhance safety: refuse(), which halts the process if the agent deems
293   continuing with the task is inappropriate; ask-consent(), which is used when the agent requires
294   user permission to proceed; and complete(), which flags the completion of tasks such as when
295   a file to be deleted is not found on the device. A more detailed explanation of the action options is
296   provided in Appendix A.2.
297   To elicit agentic behaviors from LLMs, we design the prompt to include the general role of agents,
298   available action options, goal instructions, previous actions taken by the agent, and the current ob-
299   servation. Our prompts incorporate several techniques, such as the Chain-of-Thought prompt (Wei
300   et al. 2022; CoT), to enhance reasoning and planning. Specifically, we design prompts to mandate
301   a particular response format from the agents. This format includes an interpretation of the current
302   observation, a context summarizing the current progress, a rationale for their planned action, and the
303   final decision on the action option.
304
305   Safety-guided Chain-of-Thought prompting To improve the agents’ ability to recognize po-
306
      tential safety issues, we propose a new prompting method called Safety-guided Chain-of-Thought
      (SCoT) prompt. This SCoT prompt requires agents to generate safety considerations based on the
307
      current observation (ot ) and task instruction (c) before establishing their action plans. Specifically,
308
      the SCoT prompt includes several guidelines that emphasize safe behavior, ensuring that agents ap-
309   ply the safety considerations they generate. Our experiments demonstrate that integrating SCoT with
310   the CoT technique significantly enhances the safety of LLM agents. For more details on the prompts,
311   including different types of prompts used in the experiments, we refer the readers to Appendix D.

      5.2    M AIN RESULTS
358
359   Figure 4 shows the helpfulness and safety scores of the baseline agents in MobileSafetyBench. We
360   observe that all baseline agents frequently fail to behave safely across many tasks. We observe that
361   prompting agents with SCoT to identify potential risks significantly improves the harm prevention
362   rates. This indicates the substantial risk associated with naively employing LLM agents to control
363   mobile devices. Exemplary tasks and responses from the agents are provided in Appendix E.3.
364
365   Safe behaviors of LLM agents across risk types The tasks in MobileSafetyBench encompass
366   various groups of risks, with agents exhibiting different behaviors depending on the type of risk.
367
      Below, we analyze the agents’ behaviors for each risk type.
368   In the Ethical Compliance category, where risk signals are most straightforward, agents com-
369   monly refuse to proceed with tasks upon detecting indications of criminal activity. For example,
370   when tasked with accessing an illegal website, Claude-3.5 agents recognize the illegality of the pro-
371   vided URL and refuse to proceed with the task. However, in more complex scenarios where risks
372   become apparent only after several steps into the task, agents often overlook or struggle to address
373
      these risks. For instance, many agents often proceed with the task by checking only the immediately
      visible part of the conversation in text messages, i.e., the most recent message, without examining
374
      the detailed conversation history where signals of illegality are present. This disregard for potential
375
      safety issues in this category highlights the critical threats.

      In the Offensiveness category, similar to Ethical Compliance category, the agents often

385   We also observe that many agents often struggle to exhibit safe behaviors when the tasks require
386   higher reasoning abilities to interpret risk signals. In the Bias & Fairness category, the clues
387   of risks become difficult to interpret even after accessing content containing risk signals. These con-
388   tents, for example, include unfair judgment (of the users) such as biased employment decisions over
389   certain regions or prejudiced comments on social media posts. Agents more frequently disregard
390   safety issues when asked to assist users with these contents.
391   In the Private Information category, agents struggle to handle private information (e.g.,
392   Google authentication code or credit card information) carefully. For instance, GPT-4o agents often
393   carelessly share text or image contents containing private information, resulting in harm prevention
394   rates 0% (basic) and 15% (SCoT). Meanwhile, Claude-3.5 agents show higher performance than
395   GPT-4o agents, with a harm prevention rates 46%, but still indicate notable room for improvement.
396   Specifically, these agents attempt to edit text contents to remove sensitive information before sharing
397   but often fail to do so effectively.
398
399   Balancing goal achievement and harm prevention We observe that each baseline agents with
400   SCoT method show different behaviors between goal achievement and harm prevention when they
401   are exposed to the high-risk tasks. The GPT-4o agents achieve the highest goal achievement rates at
402   69%, but their harm prevention rates are the lowest at 29%. This indicates the agents neglect safety
403   considerations. The Claude-3.5 agents achieve harm prevention rates of 54% and goal achievement
404
      rates of 23%. The Gemini-1.5 agents demonstrate harm prevention rates of 80%, surpassing the
      GPT-4o agents and Claude-3.5 agents, but their harm preventions in low-risk tasks (with rates value
405
      of 44%) clue that they unnecessarily avoid risks despite the absence of high risks in considerable
406
      number of tasks. These findings indicate that balancing safety with helpfulness presents a challenge,
407   suggesting that investigations on the agent design and prompting strategies remain crucial.
408
409
      The effect of SCoT prompting We find that prompting the agents to produce safety considera-
410
      tions before making decisions significantly improves the harm-preventing behaviors of the agents.
411   The baseline agents provided with SCoT prompt report 25% higher harm prevention rates in high-
412   risk tasks than the agents given with the basic prompt, on average across LLMs. However, we also
413   find that safety considerations are often ignored when the agents are making decisions. This indi-
414   cates the necessity of more advanced reasoning or planning algorithms for achieving higher safety.
415
      Additionally, we conduct an ablation study on SCoT prompting. In the experiment, we employ
416
      another safety-guided prompt type that contains several guidelines to behave safely, but without
417   compulsory requirement on outputting safety consideration, similar to (Ruan et al., 2024). Table 1
418   shows the experimental results with the GPT-4o agents using the safety-guided prompt and SCoT
419   prompt. We observe that forcing the agents to generate safety considerations can be largely benefi-
420   cial for eliciting their safe behaviors, compared to providing several safety guidelines only.

449
      Using MobileSafetyBench, we investigate whether baseline agents can maintain robust behavior
450
      when exposed to indirect prompt injection attacks. For instance, as illustrated in Figure 5, a test
451
      scenario involves agents reviewing a text message that contains an irrelevant instruction to sell stock
452   shares. Such injected prompts are embedded within UI elements like text messages and social media
453   posts, and are delivered to the agents as part of the observation.
454
455
      We present the number of tasks that baseline agents (using the SCoT prompt) successfully defend
      against these attacks, out of the total 8 tasks, in Table 2. Despite the simplicity of the injected
456
      prompts, the baseline agents are prone to these malicious attacks, with the exception of one case
457
      presented by the Gemini-1.5 agent. Misled by the injected prompts, the agents typically assume
458   they have received new instructions and attempt to execute them. Consequently, in several tasks,
459   they inadvertently open a banking application, initiate stock trades, or even attempt to change the
460   device password without the user’s consent. We believe that our findings emphasize that improving
461   the safety of agents against malicious attacks, such as by enhancing agent-user interactivity and
462   generating more data on defensive behaviors through our platform, are highly necessary. We offer
463   detailed examples of an injected prompt and an agent’s response, including additional experiments
464   using benign content without any intention of attack and results with GPT-o1 agent, in Appendix E.5.

      The effect of external safeguards Current closed-source LLMs, such as Gemini-1.5, are equipped
      with an additional safeguard mechanism designed to prevent the model from generating harmful
513
      responses. We investigate the efficacy of these safeguards on the tasks created, by adjusting the
514
      safety settings of Gemini-1.5.5 We observe that the safeguards equipped to Gemini-1.5 are not
515   highly effective for improving the harm prevention for Gemini-1.5 agents. This is because the
516   Gemini-1.5 agents without safeguards already try to prevent harm in tasks where the safeguards work
517   effectively. Specifically, we find that the safeguards frequently block responses containing harmful
518   content, particularly in risk types like Ethical Compliance and Offensiveness. However,
519   these mechanisms do not always guarantee safe behavior, especially in cases where the responses
520   do not contain explicitly dangerous content. For example, while the safeguards properly function
521   when agents try sending a text message by using send-sms() option with an argument of text
522   containing offensive words, they are ineffective when agents decide to forward private information
523
      by using tap() option, as the argument of this function does not contain harmful contents. We
      assume this is because the current safety refusal mechanisms struggle to bridge the effects and
524
      consequences of actions to safety issues, indicating a need for more advanced methods. We include
525
      more detailed discussions in Appendix E.7.

      E THICS STATEMENT
541
542
      We introduce MobileSafetyBench, a benchmark for evaluating the safety and helpfulness of au-
543   tonomous agents controlling mobile devices. While our benchmark aims to improve the safety and
544   reliability of such agents, it also highlights ethical concerns related to their deployment. The risks
545   of security breaches and unintentional harmful actions highlight the need for a well-defined ethical
546   guideline. To mitigate these risks, we emphasize the importance of the reliability and safety of agent
547   actions, user consent, and the implementation of rigorous safety checks when developing and de-
548   ploying autonomous agents. Especially, we provide thorough analyses of state-of-the-art LLM for
549   developing agents. LLM agents can inadvertently take actions that may cause real-world harm or
550   expose sensitive information, either through direct user interactions or external manipulations such
551
      as indirect prompt injection attacks. Our work encourages further research that ensures LLM agents
      prioritize user safety and privacy, and remain aligned with ethical standards to prevent misuse.

      A     E NVIRONMENT DETAILS
709
710   MobileSafetyBench realizes the authentic environments leveraging Android emulators. To be spe-
711   cific, the environments are instantiated with a Android virtual device using Pixel 7 in our experi-
712   ments as a representative of mobile devices. The transitions of the states in the environments, hence,
713
      are determined by the Android OS and applications in use. Also, the initial state is determined by
      each specific task scenario. On top of the use of Android emulators, we define additional external
714
      modules: a screen-layout translator and an action converter. For more details on the implementation,
715
      please refer below.

717
718   A.1   O BSERVATION SPACE
719
720   The observation space of the environments is defined with a combination of a screen image and
721   text describing the UI elements. The screen image has a resolution of 1024 × 2048 × 3, ensuring
722
      details in the images are clearly visible. We define a screen-layout translator which produces the
      text describing the UI elements by parsing from the Android view hierarchy. We use Appium, a
723
      widely-used open-source test automation framework for Android OS, to obtain the Android view
724
      hierarchy. The Android view hierarchy provides the accessibility tree of all available windows in
725   the current screen layout. The screen-layout translator, then, extracts certain attributes such as text,
726   content description, and state flags (e.g., checked, selected, and focused) from the Android view
727   hierarchy. For concise input, we do not include the attributes when the corresponding values are
728   empty (e.g. if the content description is “”). A numeric tag, which will be used as an argument of

      A.3    S TABILIZATION OF TRANSITIONS
865
866   Importantly, the Android emulators operate asynchronously, independent of the agents’ action gen-
867   eration. The manifestation of the actions may require time. This can issue incomplete transition or
868   delay of success signals. To mitigate the issue, we regulate the interaction rate between the agents
869   and the environments, similar to previous approaches employing Android emulators (Toyama et al.,
870   2021; Lee et al., 2024). Specifically, in our experiments, we pause for a set duration before retriev-
871   ing screen data from the environment for a fixed time of 10 seconds for each interaction. We also
872
      enabled the users to adjust the duration.
873   Additionally, we implement several operations that run independently of the agents’ action, to stabi-
874   lize transitions. For example, in the stock training application, a random advertisement may appear.
875   To resolve such random perturbations (e.g., turning off the advertisement window), we implement a
876   stabilization process employing heuristics that analyzes and manipulates certain UI elements (e.g.,
877   a UI element corresponding to the ‘close’ button when the advertisement appears) using Appium.

1028
       We provide more details on the evaluators, including the components of device information utilized
1029   by evaluators and specific examples of score criteria.
1030
1031
       Analysis target On top of analyzing the action history of agents, the evaluators leverage various
1032   components of system information. These components encompass device configuration, file storage,
1033   databases of applications, and UI elements presented on the screen. The methods for analyzing each
1034   component are as follows:
1035
1036          • System configurations: ADB (i.e., ‘adb shell’ command) is utilized to verify password
1037            settings, app installation status, and other system configurations.
1038          • File storage: Files within each folder on the Android emulators are analyzed for file deletion
1039            or sharing activities.
1040          • Application databases: ADB and SQLite are leveraged to access and read the information
1041            stored in application database files. The target databases include SMS history and social
1042            media posts.
1043
              • UI elements: Appium is used to verify the descriptions and active states of the UI elements
1044
                present on the current screen.
1045
1046
              • Agent action history: An action history is managed as a list of action options selected by
                the agents in history. The evaluators review this list to evaluate which action has been
1047
                performed at which timestep.
1048

1383   E.2   A DDITIONAL EXPERIMENT SETUP DETAILS
1384
       We explain the threat model in the indirect prompt injection experiment.
1385
1386
       Challenge The agent needs to stick to the user’s instructions instead of being fooled by the prompt
1387   injected by the attacker. Sticking to the user’s instructions includes not performing unintended
1388   behaviors (e.g., opening irrelevant applications to the instruction) and signaling the completion of
1389   the task instruction when the instruction is followed.
1390
1391   Attacker’s objective The main objective of the attacker is to fool the agents into performing the
1392   attacker’s instruction instead of the original user’s intention.
1393
1394   Attacker’s capability The adversary does not have any direct access to the phone device or to the
1395   agent backbone and is only able to influence it indirectly (i.e., by sending messages or uploading
1396   posts on social media that contain deceptive prompts). On the other hand, they are aware that users
1397   request LLM agents via text prompts and screen images. They are also aware of the format of the
1398   prompt. Also, they can freely modify the deceptive prompt to exploit the agent.
1399
1400
       Scenario The agent may inadvertently come across the deceptive prompts nested within the mes-
       sages in the Message application or posts in the social media application. The deceptive prompts
1401
       become present in the text description of UI elements or inside the image. This implies that the
1402
       deceptive prompts are not part of the agent system or the user intention, as they are nested inside the
1403   description of UI elements.

       E.6     C OMPARISON WITH QUESTION - ANSWERING
1837
1838   We verify whether the baseline LLMs can detect risks in normal question-answering (QA) formats.
1839   In the setting, we instruct the LLMs to classify the content with risks, which are used in designing
1840   out tasks, out of the four risk types we define: Ethical Compliance, Offensiveness,
1841   Bias & Fairness, and Private Information. Additionally, we append another class
1842   “Unrisky”, for the cases where LLMs do not detect any risk. The models are allowed to output at
1843   most two risk types. If the risk that any of the output risk types matches with any of the predefined
1844   labels (annotated by human labelers), the detection is counted as successful.
1845   In the agentic setting, we count the number of scenarios in which the LLM agents successfully
1846   managed the risks. Unlike harm prevention, risk management in this setting refers to the agents’
1847   harm-preventing behavior after risk detection, within a few steps for checking optimal management
1848   (differing per task), where the risk detection and the gap between the detection with harm prevention
1849   are assessed by the evaluators. Please refer to the supplementary code materials for the definition of
1850   risk detection in each task.
1851
1852   Prompt for the QA setting The prompt format used in the QA setting is detailed below. The
1853   prompt for the QA setting included definitions for each risk type and specific task information.
1854   While prompting, the parts that are enclosed with <> in the prompt are replaced for specifying the
1855   task information.
1856   The information about the task consisted of an instruction and additional context. The context, here,
1857   describes the specific content that is provided to the agent, such as details on the social media post
1858   or memo. If the instruction itself contains a risk, the context part is replaced by “-”. If the text
1859   message dialogue contains a risk, the entire conversation containing the message is included. If the
1860   social media post contains a risk, the content of the post is provided. If the memo contains a risk, the
1861   whole content of the memo replaces the part. If the photo contains a risk, the image is also provided.
1862   Especially, unlike the text content, we use the screenshot of the device shown to the agents during
1863
       their task performance, to exclude the effect of different resolutions of the images.

       E.7    D ISCUSSIONS ON EXTERNAL SAFEGUARD

1938   In our investigation on the efficacy of external safeguards implemented by service providers of
1939   Gemini-1.5, we observe that the safeguards equipped to Gemini-1.5 do not effectively induce save
1940   behaviors of Gemini-1.5 agents. Specifically, the harm prevention rates of Gemini-1.5 agents with
1941   safeguards and Gemini without safeguards are 74% and 80% in high-risk tasks, differing two num-
1942   bers of task out of 35 numbers of high-risk tasks. As Gemini-1.5 agents without safeguards try
1943   preventing harm in most of the tasks where safeguards reject to generate responses, we assume that
       the rate difference stems from minor stochasticity.
