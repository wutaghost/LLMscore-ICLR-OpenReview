009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            With the rapid development of generative technology, current generative models
012            can generate high-fidelity digital content and edit it in a controlled manner. How-
013            ever, there is a risk that malicious individuals might misuse these capabilities for
014            misleading or unlawful activities. Although existing research has attempted to
015            shield photographic images from being manipulated by generative models, there
016            remains a significant disparity in the protection offered to video content editing.
017            To bridge the gap, we propose a protection method named VideoGuard, which can
018
               effectively protect videos from unauthorized malicious editing. This protection is
               achieved through the subtle introduction of nearly unnoticeable perturbation that
019
               interferes with the functioning of the intended generative diffusion models. Dif-
020
               ferent from images, videos consist of sequential frames, containing not only visual
021            content but also motion dynamics. Due to the redundancy between video frames,
022            and inter-frame attention mechanism in video diffusion models, simply applying
023            image-based protection methods separately to every video frame can not shield
024            video from unauthorized editing. To tackle the above challenge, rather than op-
025            timize perturbation in a frame-wise manner like image-based methods, we adopt
026            joint frame optimization, treating all the video frames as an optimization entity.
027            Furthermore, we extract video motion information and fuse it into optimization
028            objectives. Thereby, these alterations can effectively compel the models to pro-
029
               duce outputs that are implausible and inconsistent. We provide a pipeline to op-
               timize such a perturbation. Finally, we use both objective metrics and subjective
030
               metrics to demonstrate the efficacy of our method, and the results show that the
031
               protection performance of VideoGuard is superior to all the baseline methods.

091   To raise the cost of unauthorized video editing, we propose a two-stage motion-based perturbation
092   method. Our method extracts not only content information but also motion information and hides
093   them from the edit model. When the perturbation is added to a video and the perturbed video is
094   fed into edit models, the frame consistency will be disrupted, thereby leading to distorted content.
095   Specifically, given a video V and its inversion latent Z0 , in stage 1, we formulate an optimization
096   problem of minimizing motion loss and content loss to seek an inversion latent Zlatent in ϵ-spherical
097   neighborhoods of Z0 , and then a projection gradient descent method(Madry (2017)) is provided to
098   solve the problem. In stage 2, we regard this inversion latent as a pseudo label and formulate
099
      another optimization problem of minimizing the loss between the current inversion latent and the
      target inversion latent(pseudo label), and then the Particle Swarm Optimization method(Kennedy &
100
      Eberhart (1995)) is adopted to search for the best video perturbation. Fused with this protective video
101
      perturbation, we can obtain an immunized video. Consequently, when doing an editing task with
102   the immunized video, the adversary will obtain a manipulated inversion latent from this perturbed
103   video, and this inversion latent will be fed into the denoise process, resulting in a distorted video
104   that can be easily perceived as fake. During the inversion latent optimization process, we treat all
105   frames’ inversion latent as a whole entity and optimize jointly with the projection gradient descent
106   method. Through this approach, we can take inter-frame consistency into consideration, which can
107   effectively destroy the original video’s motion information. Furthermore, we use the perturbation
      vector searching method to optimize video perturbation in the video pixel space, which requires

      2.1   LDM S BASED V IDEO E DITING
140
141   Generative models have demonstrated impressive performance in image editing, with approaches
142   ranging from GANs(Gal et al. (2022); Park et al. (2019); Wang et al. (2018)) to diffusion mod-
143   els(Avrahami et al. (2022); Kawar et al. (2023)). Inspired by image editing techniques, an increas-
144   ing amount of research is dedicated to transforming latent diffusion models (LDMs) into zero-shot
145   image editors(Liew et al. (2023); Wu et al. (2023); Qi et al. (2023)), achieving significant advance-
146   ments. These strides have sparked interest in the realm of video editing, which is a specialized task
147
      within the broader field of video generation. Unlike the standard video generation process that relies
      solely on conditional prompts to direct the creation, video editing necessitates both a source video
148
      and a guiding prompt(Geyer et al. (2023); Wang et al. (2023)). Given an edit prompt, the attributes
149
      of the reference video can be manipulated including shape, style, and scene(Qi et al. (2023)). In ad-
150   dition to appearances, videos are also characterized by the motion dynamics of subjects and camera
151   movements across frames. Recently, the idea of customizing the motion with given reference videos
152   has also been emerging and evolving rapidly(Zhao et al. (2023); Jeong et al. (2024)). They extract
153   the motion pattern from the original video and transfer it to a new video generation process, thus
154   they can obtain a new video that possesses the same motion pattern.
155
      Editing a video essentially involves manipulating a sequence of images arranged sequentially in
156   time. Yet, ensuring consistency across the frames of the edited video remains a challenging issue.
157   Directly altering each frame of the source video might result in inconsistencies such as varying
158   backgrounds or altered positions of the foreground objects(Shin et al. (2024); Ceylan et al. (2023)).
159   The latest video editing frameworks leverage various pre-trained LDMs, to maintain frame-to-frame
160   consistency, these frameworks commonly incorporate cross-frame global attention mechanisms(Wu
161   et al. (2023); Yang et al. (2023)). Additionally, some systems incorporate supplementary conditions
      like depth, pose, and edge data to further enhance consistency(Khachatryan et al. (2023)). Given the


                                                        3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      proliferation of sophisticated techniques for producing high-fidelity videos, there is a rising concern
163   that these editing tools could be exploited for nefarious purposes, potentially leading to the creation
164   of videos that are unlawful, deceptive, or damaging. In light of this, we are pioneering research in
165   this domain.

      2.2   P ROTECTION AGAINST LDM S - BASED D IGITAL C ONTENT E DITING
168
169
      Latent diffusion models (LDMs) can edit images and videos based on conditional prompts, which
170
      can potentially be exploited to generate malicious content(Chen et al. (2023); Liu et al. (2024a)). To
171   counter this threat, Photoguard(Salman et al. (2023)) has been introduced as a protective measure
172   for images, aiming to hinder the efforts of LDMs. This method incorporates adversarial pertur-
173   bations into images, effectively perplexing LDMs and preventing unauthorized editing. Further-
174   more, LDMs can quickly learn specific objects or artistic styles through personalized techniques
175   like DreamBooth(Ruiz et al. (2023)). To protect intellectual property or portrait rights, some works
176   add perturbation to images before releasing them on the Internet(Peng et al. (2023)). With such
177   perturbed images, the fine-tuned LDMs are only capable of producing low-quality results. Recently,
178   there has been great advancement in video generation and video editing techniques, though brought
179
      convenience, the threat of malicious video editing is being taken into consideration. However, video
      edit protection has been less explored in the community. One intuitive protection method is to trans-
180
      fer the image-based method to a video protection task(Li et al. (2024)), applying the image-based
181
      method to every single frame. Nevertheless, this method ignores the relation between video frames.
182   Frames’ content redundancy and inter-frame attention mechanism in video diffusion models convey
183   content information and motion information, which indicates that frame-wise protection manner will
184   not have good protection performance.

189   3.1   T HREAT M ODEL
190
191   Firstly, we will clarify the threat model from the attacker’s and protector’s perspective.
192   Attacker’s Capability and Goal. Individuals with malevolent motives have the capability to effort-
193   lessly procure a pre-trained video editing model and make alterations to videos of targeted individ-
194   uals. As a result of the open-source characteristics of the LDM models and the ready accessibility
195   of videos, perpetrators can falsify identities or fabricate misinformation, subsequently leading to
196   potentially detrimental consequences.
197
      Protector’s Capability and Goal. Protectors are merely endowed with access to the video, thereby
198   confining their operations solely to manipulating the visual content. Their task involves introducing
199   imperceptible perturbations to the original video. The primary objective of the protector is to raise
200   the cost of modifying the safeguarded video, thereby impeding video editing models from easily
201   altering the video content.

208   makes it classified as a specific target label ytarget . This construction is achieved by minimizing the
209   loss of a classifier fθ for that image:

236   3.3   M OTIVATION AND OVERVIEW
237
      Motivation. Motivated by Jeong et al. (2024), we conducted some experiments on the inversion la-
238
      tent. As shown in Figure 1 (right), once we get the inversion latent from a video with a skiing motion
239
      pattern, this inversion latent will be fed into the denoising process guided by the target prompt and
240   obtain edited video with skiing motion. At the same time, when we use the same inversion latent
241   and the ’running’ prompt, we can get a video with the dog running. More precisely and importantly,
242   the dog’s motion pattern is still consistent with the original one. However, a random inversion latent
243   generates a random motion pattern as illustrated on the fourth row in Figure 1 (right). The inversion
244   latent contains a precise motion pattern of the original video. This observation motivates us that we
245   may spare more attention to inversion latent in video edit protection tasks. What’s more, it indicates
246   that we should regard the video and its inversion as a whole.
247   Protect Pipeline Overview. The video editing process consists of two stages. Firstly, the initial
248   noise latent is obtained from the original video in the first stage through the DDIM inversion process.
249   Then, the start noise will be fed into the denoising process guided by target edit prompt P. Based on
250   this edit pipeline, we propose a corresponding protection pipeline. Figure 2 shows the pipeline. To
251   be specific, firstly, we optimize an initial inversion latent as a pseudo label, which has content and
252   motion distortion compared to the original initial inversion latent. Then we regard it as an anchor
253   in the following video space optimization. In the second stage, we employ the gradient-free PSO
254
      algorithm(Kennedy & Eberhart (1995)) to search for a perturbation in video space that can make the
      DDIM inversion latent obtained from the perturbed video close to the target initial inversion latent.
255
      Thus, the perturbation in video space we found can affect the start point of the editing pipeline’s
256
      denoising process, and finally disrupt the edit video generation.

479
480   We present a novel framework for video editing protection powered by diffusion models. We study
481   the motion dynamics of a video in the diffusion latent space and construct optimization objectives
482   accordingly. We propose joint optimization in latent space to tackle the challenge of frame informa-
483   tion redundancy, and we suggest applying a gradient-free search algorithm for video perturbation
484   optimization in a small vector space rather than in the raw pixel space, which is computationally
485   effective. Our method outperforms existing baselines and demonstrates its transferability between
      different models and instructions.

      Despite the promising results, our study also has some limitations. Our method does not perform

510   that when more and more control conditions are fused into the editing process, the importance of

731
732   The Inflation of Attention Mechanism. Video generation models generally inflate text-to-image
733   models. There exists an inflated 3D attention mechanism that enables the inter-frame interaction.
734   Specifically, for the latent representation zti of source frame fti , query features are derived from
735   spatial features zti , while key and value features are computed from spatial features of concatenated
736   latent [zt1 , ..., ztN ]. The mathematical formulation can be as follows:
737                      Q = W Q · zti , K = W K · [zt1 , ..., ztN ], V = W V · [zt1 , ..., ztN ].
738
739   Particle Swarm Optimization. We use this algorithm in stage 2 to search for a video perturbation.
740   PSO algorithm is inspired by the social behavior of birds flocking or fish schooling. There are
741   some basic concepts in this algorithm. (1) Particles: these are the individual agents or solutions that
742   explore the search space. Each particle represents the video perturbation vector in our experimental
743   setting. (2) Swarm: The group of particles. It collectively explores the search space by sharing
744
      information. Typically, the size of the swarm should cover the search variable’s dimension. (3)
      Position and Velocity: Each particle has a position that represents a potential solution and a velocity
745
      that dictates its movement through the search space. (4) Fitness Function: This function evaluates
746
      how good a particle’s position is. The goal is to find the position that optimizes this function.
747
748   In the search process of the video perturbation vector, our search objective is the MSE loss be-
749   tween the current video’s inversion latent and the anchor inverted latent optimized in stage 1. Video
750
      perturbation vectors represent particles. In our experimental setting, the video perturbation vector
      V ∈ RC×F , which means we optimize a vector for every frame.
