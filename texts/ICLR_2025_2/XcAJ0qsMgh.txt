011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Sampling from high-dimensional, multi-modal distributions remains a fundamen-
014            tal challenge across domains such as statistical Bayesian inference and physics-
015            based machine learning. In this paper, we propose Annealing Flow (AF), a contin-
016
               uous normalizing flow-based approach designed to sample from high-dimensional
               and multi-modal distributions. The key idea is to learn a continuous normaliz-
017
               ing flow-based transport map, guided by annealing, to transition samples from an
018
               easy-to-sample distribution to the target distribution, facilitating effective explo-
019            ration of modes in high-dimensional spaces. Unlike many existing methods, AF
020            training does not rely on samples from the target distribution. AF ensures effective
021            and balanced mode exploration, achieves linear complexity in sample size and di-
022            mensions, and circumvents inefficient mixing times. We demonstrate the superior
023            performance of AF compared to state-of-the-art methods through extensive exper-
024            iments on various challenging distributions and real-world datasets, particularly
025            in high-dimensional and multi-modal settings. We also highlight AFвҖҷs potential
026            for sampling the least favorable distributions.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Sampling from high-dimensional and multi-modal distributions is crucial for various fields, includ-
032   ing physics-based machine learning like molecular dynamics (Miao et al., 2015; Salo-Ahen et al.,
033
      2020), quantum physics (Carlson et al., 2015; Lynn et al., 2019), and lattice field theory (Jay & Neil,
      2021; Lozanovski et al., 2020). With modern datasets, it also plays a key role in Bayesian areas,
034
      including Bayesian modeling (Balandat et al., 2020; Kandasamy et al., 2018; Stephan et al., 2017)
035
      with applications in areas like computational biology (Overstall et al., 2020; Stanton et al., 2022),
036   and Bayesian Neural Network sampling (Cobb & Jalaian, 2021; Izmailov et al., 2021).
037
038   MCMC and Neural Network Variants: Numerous MCMC methods have been developed over the
039
      past 50 years, including Metropolis-Hastings (MH) and its variants (Choi, 2020; Cornish et al., 2019;
      Griffin & Walker, 2013; Haario et al., 2001), Hamiltonian Monte Carlo (HMC) schemes (Bou-Rabee
040
      & Sanz-Serna, 2017; Girolami & Calderhead, 2011; Hoffman et al., 2021; Li et al., 2015; Shahbaba
041
      et al., 2014). HMC variants are still considered state-of-the-art methods. However, they require
042   exponentially many steps in the dimension for mixing, even with just two modes (Hackett et al.,
043   2021). More recently, Neural network (NN)-based sampling algorithms (Bonati et al., 2019; Egorov
044   et al., 2024; Gu & Sun, 2020; Hackett et al., 2021; Li et al., 2021; Wolniewicz et al., 2024) have
045   been developed to leverage NN expressiveness for improving MCMC, but they still inherit some
046   limitations like slow mixing and imbalanced mode exploration, particularly in high-dimensional
047   spaces.
048   Annealing Variants: Annealing methods (Gelfand et al., 1990; Neal, 2001; Sorkin, 1991;
049   Van Groenigen & Stein, 1998) are widely used to develop MCMC techniques like Parallel Tem-
050   pering (PT) and its variants (Chandra et al., 2019; Earl & Deem, 2005; Syed et al., 2022). In
051   annealing, sampling gradually shifts from an easy distribution to the target by lowering tempera-
052   ture. Annealed Importance Sampling (Neal, 2001) and its variants(Chehab et al., 2024; Karagian-
053   nis & Andrieu, 2013; Zhang et al., 2021) are developed for estimating normalizing constants with
      low variance using MCMC samples from intermediate distributions. Recent Normalizing Flow and

054
      score-based annealing methods (Arbel et al., 2021; Doucet et al., 2022) optimize intermediate densi-
055   ties for lower-variance estimates, but still rely on MCMC for sampling. However, MCMC struggles
056   with slow mixing, local mode trapping, mode imbalance, and correlated samples issues. These lim-
057   itations are particularly pronounced in high-dimensional, multi-modal settings (Hackett et al., 2021;
058   Van Ravenzwaaij et al., 2018).
059
      Particle Optimization Methods: Recently, particle-based optimization methods have emerged for
060
      sampling, including Stein Variational Gradient Descent (SVGD) (Liu & Wang, 2016), and stochastic
061   approaches such as (Dai et al., 2016; Detommaso et al., 2018; Li et al., 2023; Liu, 2017; Maddison
062   et al., 2018; Nitanda & Suzuki, 2017; Pulido & van Leeuwen, 2019). However, many of these
063   methods rely on kernel computations, which scale polynomially with sample size, and are sensitive
064   to hyperparameters.
065
      Normalizing Flows: Recently, Normalizing Flows (NFs) (Rezende & Mohamed, 2015) and Stochas-
066
      tic NFs (Hagemann et al., 2022; Wu et al., 2020) have been explored for sampling. However, discrete
067   NFs often suffer from mode collapse, prompting works (Albergo & Vanden-Eijnden, 2023; Arbel
068   et al., 2021; Brofos et al., 2022; Cabezas et al., 2024; GabrieМҒ et al., 2021; 2022; Matthews et al.,
069   2022) to address this with MCMC corrections, which depend on the quality of MCMC samples and
070   thus may struggle in high-dimensional settings. Several Continuous Normalizing Flows (CNFs) al-
071   gorithms (Hertrich & Gruhlke, 2024; Tian et al., 2024) are developed to address mode collapse, but
072   still rely on Monte Carlo procedures to correct bias, which are often sensitive to high-dimensional
073   densities. Besides, these methods may often fail with widely-separated modes, leaving some unex-
074   plored even after extensive training.
075   Challenges persist with multi-modal distributions in high-dimensional spaces. This paper introduces
076   Annealing Flow (AF), a novel sampling scheme that learns a continuous normalizing flow map from
077   an easy-to-sample distribution ПҖ0 (x) to the target q(x), guided by annealing principles. Unlike
078   diffusion sampling (Bruna & Han, 2024; Chung et al., 2022; Shih et al., 2024; Zhou et al., 2023)
079   which requires pre-learning from a dataset of unknown distribution, AF training does not require
080   preliminary samples from the target q(x). AF is not based on MCMC, thus avoiding issues like slow
081
      mixing, sample correlation, and mode imbalance. And unlike particle-based optimization methods,
      AF scales linearly with sample size and dimensions. Once trained, one simply samples from ПҖ0 (x),
082
      and the learned transport map directly pushes these samples towards the target distribution.

      7     D ISCUSSIONS
519
520
      In this paper, we have proposed the Annealing Flow (AF) framework, a novel and flexible approach
521
      for sampling from high-dimensional and multi-modal distributions. AF offers several advantages
522   over existing methods, as thoroughly discussed in D.1. Additionally, we have also compared the
523   training and sampling times in D.1. Extensive experiments demonstrate that AF performs well
524   across a variety of challenging distributions and real-world datasets.
525
      The Annealing Flow framework presented in this paper is highly flexible and accommodates various
526
      challenging distributions. The concept of вҖҳAnnealingвҖҷ in sampling can be interpreted as gradually
527
      transitioning from an easy-to-sample distribution to the target distribution. Therefore, each inter-
528   mediate distribution fk can be defined flexibly without adhering to (4), as long as the transitions
529   between fkвҲ’1 and fk are smooth and the sequence converges to the target q(x). If the density modes
530   are close enough, all fЛңk (x) can simply be set to the target density q(x), making the Annealing Flow
531   objective equivalent to the Wasserstein gradient flow, as discussed in Appendix B. Additionally,
532   we believe that by adding more intermediate distributions, one can obtain intermediate samples at
533   various time points to construct a low-variance estimator for the normalizing constant. Finally, the
534   importance flow discussed in Section 5 may be extended to a distribution-free model, allowing one
535   to learn an importance flow from a dataset for sampling its Least-Favorable Distribution (LFD) with
536   minimal variance, as further detailed in D.2.

1490   Annealing Flow (AF) requires pre-training, typically taking 10-20 minutes for tasks with dimensions < 10,
1491   and around 30 minutes for tasks around dimension 50. For 50D experiments, training a single vk with a neural
1492   network structure of 32-32-32 and 1000 gradient steps takes approximately 2вҖ“3 minutes. Once trained, AF
1493   samplers are very efficient: generating 10,000 samples in just 1.5 seconds. These pre-trained samplers can be
       reused at any time, offering significant speed advantages. In contrast, MCMC methods, such as Metropolis-
1494
       Hastings or Hamiltonian Monte Carlo, require about 1 minute to sample 10,000 points, and their performance
1495   deteriorates in high-dimensional, multi-modal settings. Moreover, particle-based methods, like SVGD, struggle
1496   significantly when generating more than 3,000 samples, requiring about 20 minutes for that many samples.
1497   Therefore, we believe that users can take advantage of AFвҖҷs offline training, as it allows the samplers to be
1498   trained once and then efficiently reused for sampling whenever needed.
