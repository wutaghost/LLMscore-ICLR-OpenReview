                                                    A BSTRACT
010
011
012            Encoder–decoder models, which transform input data into latent variables, have
013            achieved a significant success in machine learning. Although the generalization
014            capability of these models has been theoretically analyzed in supervised learning
015            focusing on the complexity of latent variables, the contribution of latent variables in
016            generalization and data generation capabilities are less explored theoretically in un-
017            supervised learning. To address this gap, our study leverages information-theoretic
018
               generalization error analysis (IT analysis). Using the supersample setting in recent
               IT analysis, we demonstrate that the generalization gap for reconstruction loss can
019
               be evaluated through mutual information related to the posterior distribution of
020
               latent variables, conditioned on the input data, without relying on the decoder’s in-
021            formation. We also introduce a novel permutation-symmetric supersample setting,
022            which extends the existing IT analysis and shows that regularizing the encoder’s
023            capacity leads to generalization. Finally, we guarantee the 2-Wasserstein distance
024            between the true data distribution and the generated data distribution, offering
025            insights into the model’s data generation capabilities.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Encoder–decoder models have achieved a significant success in machine learning (Goodfellow et al.,
033   2016). Typically, the encoder extracts information from input data to generate latent variables, called
034   representations, and the decoder uses these representations to output predictions. In supervised
035   learning, these models are trained by minimizing the empirical loss, and the regularization of latent
036   variables helps prevent overfitting, thereby improving generalization performance. As detailed in
037   Sec 1.1, studies on encoder–decoder models have focused on not only learned parameters but also on
038   the complexity of latent variables, through principles such as the minimum description length (MDL)
039
      (Grnwald et al., 2005), PAC-Bayes theory (McAllester, 1998), information-theoretic generalization
      error analysis (IT analysis) (Xu & Raginsky, 2017), and the information bottleneck (IB) hypothesis
040
      (Tishby et al., 2000). These approaches have demonstrated, both numerically and theoretically, that
041
      generalization can be characterized by the complexity of latent variables (Sefidgaran et al., 2023).
042
043   Encoder–decoder models are also popular in unsupervised learning, particularly in deep generative
044   models. When training these models, we minimize the reconstruction loss, which measures the
045
      difference between the original data and the regenerated data obtained by compressing data into
      latent variables by the encoder and regenerating the data by the decoder. Similarly to supervised
046
      learning, the regularization of the latent variables plays a critical role. For example, in the variational
047
      autoencoder (VAE) (Kingma, 2013), the objective function corresponds to the lower bound of the
048   log-likelihood. In the case of a Gaussian likelihood, the reconstruction loss corresponds to the squared
049   loss, and the regularization term is the Kullback–Leibler (KL) divergence between the prior and
050   posterior distributions of the latent variables. Despite experimental studies examining this relationship
051   using the IB hypothesis, as noted in Sec 1.1, there is a lack of theoretical analysis focusing on latent
052   variables. Most research has concentrated on encoder and decoder parameters, resulting in a limited
053   understanding of how latent variables contribute to model performance. This study addresses this gap
      by applying IT analysis to clarify the roles of latent variables in generalization and data generation.

      1.1   R ELATED WORK
055
056   The IB hypothesis (Tishby et al., 2000; Shamir et al., 2010) is the most well-known concept for
057   learning meaningful representations in latent variable models. It suggests that representations that
058   retain essential information for prediction while minimizing mutual information (MI) between input
059   data and latent variables lead to better generalization. Such MI in the IB hypothesis has been used as
060   an empirical tool to understand deep learning mechanisms (Tishby & Zaslavsky, 2015; Shwartz-Ziv &
061   Tishby, 2017; Saxe et al., 2019; Goldfeld et al., 2019; Achille & Soatto, 2018a;b). However, debates
062   persist about whether MI alone fully captures generalization. Sefidgaran et al. (2023) provided a
063
      detailed discussion of these concerns. For example, Geiger & Koch (2019), Geiger (2021), and
      Amjad & Geiger (2019) raised both numerical and theoretical concerns, whereas Vera et al. (2018),
064
      Hafez-Kolahi et al. (2020), Kawaguchi et al. (2023), and Vera et al. (2023) provided theoretical upper
065
      bounds on the generalization error for classification using such MI. However, these studies have
066   several limitations, such as assuming discrete data, the exponential dependence of the bound on MI,
067   and the entropy of input data.
068
069
      In contrast, in IT analysis (Xu & Raginsky, 2017), the generalization error is evaluated on the basis
      of the MI between learned parameters and training data. This approach is closely related to the PAC-
070
      Bayes theory and has been extended through supersample settings (Steinke & Zakynthinou, 2020) to
071
      exploit the symmetry between test and training data. This setting has been applied to the study of
072   generalization based on outputs of functions (Harutyunyan et al., 2021), losses (Hellström & Durisi,
073   2022; Wang & Mao, 2023), and hypothesis entropy (Dong et al., 2024). The relationship between
074   IT analysis and the IB hypothesis has been discussed from numerical and algorithmic perspectives
075   (Wang et al., 2022; Lyu et al., 2023). More recently, Sefidgaran et al. (2023) theoretically studied
076   latent variable models using IT analysis, demonstrating that generalization can be characterized by
077   the complexity of the encoder and latent variables without relying on decoder information. They also
078   developed a theoretical link among IT analysis, the IB hypothesis, and MDL by using compression
079   bounds (Blum & Langford, 2003).
080   In unsupervised learning, there have been numerous empirical and qualitative studies to explore model
081   performance using the IB hypothesis and rate-distortion theory (Cover & Thomas, 2012) (Alemi
082   et al., 2018; Blau & Michaeli, 2019; Tschannen et al., 2020; Bond-Taylor et al., 2021), but theoretical
083   advances remain limited. For instance, PAC-Bayes bounds for the reconstruction loss were proposed
084   by Chérief-Abdellatif et al. (2022) on the basis of the PAC-Bayes theory of supervised learning
085   using prior and posterior distributions over the encoder and decoder parameters. Similarly, Epstein
086
      & Meir (2019) proposed parameter-based bounds. However, these works fall short of explaining
      the theoretical role of latent variables. Mbacke et al. (2023) have recently introduced PAC-Bayes
087
      bounds that use priors and posteriors over the latent variables, aligning the PAC-Bayes posterior
088
      with the variational posterior. Their approach allows for using the posterior distribution, which is
089   conditionally independent between data points, providing guarantees for generalization under the
090   reconstruction loss and data generation capabilities. However, in their work, they assumed fixed
091   encoder and decoder parameters, without considering learning these parameters.
092
093
      1.2   O UR C ONTRIBUTIONS
094
095
      On the basis of existing research findings, we provide a theoretical analysis that guarantees the
096   generalization and data generation capabilities of unsupervised learning models, focusing on latent
097   variables. However, simply extending the analysis of VAEs (Mbacke et al., 2023) results in gener-
098   alization bounds that depend on learned decoder parameters, obscuring the role of latent variables.
099   Similarly, directly using the IT analysis from supervised learning (Harutyunyan et al., 2021; Hellström
100   & Durisi, 2022) is challenging owing to the difficulty in decoupling the encoder–decoder relationship.
101   Furthermore, the techniques used for classification in latent variable models (Sefidgaran et al., 2023)
102   are insufficient for analyzing the reconstruction loss or data generation, as these require reusing input
103   data and handling conditionally independent posterior distributions.
104   To address these challenges and advance the theoretical analysis of unsupervised learning with
105   a focus on latent variables, we propose a novel information-theoretic generalization error bound
106   (Theorem 2) for models with finite latent variables, such as vector quantized VAEs (VQ-VAEs) (Van
107   Den Oord et al., 2017) (detailed in Section 2.1). This extends the supersample setting in existing
      IT analysis (Steinke & Zakynthinou, 2020) incorporating techniques from Mbacke et al. (2023)

      and Sefidgaran et al. (2023). Furthermore, we introduce a novel permutation-invariant supersample
109   setting, ensuring that the generalization gap vanishes when the encoder’s capacity is appropriately
110   regularized (Theorems 3 and 4). Even without such a constraint, we show that generalization can
111   still be guaranteed if the posterior distribution of latent variables is sufficiently stable (Section 3.3).
112   Finally, we provide a guarantee for the data-generating capability by deriving the upper bound on
113   the 2-Wasserstein distance between the true data distribution and the generated data distribution
114   (Theorem 5). These findings provide the first comprehensive theoretical understanding of how
115   encoders and latent variables contribute to generalization and data generation capabilities.

119   For a random variable (RV) denoted in capital letters, we express its realization with corresponding
120   lowercase letters. Let p(X) denote the distribution of X, and let p(Y |X) represent the conditional
121   distribution of Y given X. We express the expectation of a random variable X as Ep(X) or EX .
122   The symbol I(X; Y ) represents the MI between X and Y , while I(X; Y |Z) is the conditional MI
123   (CMI) between X and Y given Z. The Kullback–Leibler (KL) divergence between p(X) and p(Y )
124   is denoted by KL(p(X)∥p(Y )). We further define [n] = {1, . . . , n} for n ∈ N.

135

332   3.3   D ISCUSSION ABOUT THE STOCHASTICITY OF THE ENCODER
333
334   Here, we discuss the role of the stochasticity of the encoder in generalization. Proofs of this section
335   are given in Appendix G. Theorems 2 and 3 suggest that, in addition to the encoder’s capacity,
336   its stochasticity is critical in enhancing generalization. For instance, in the setting of Theorem 2,
337   let Ẑ = fϕ (x) and q(J|e, ϕ, x) = q(J|e, fϕ (x)). This formulates the Markov chain U − Ẑ − eJ
338   conditioned on X̃. From the data processing inequality (Cover & Thomas, 2012), the CMI between
339   U and eJ is smaller than that between U and Ẑ. A similar argument holds in Theorem 3, implying
340   that composing the conditional distribution reduces the CMI, thereby improving generalization.
341   An example of this composition can be found in the stochastic quantized VAE (SQ-VAE) proposed
342   by Takida et al. (2022). In this model, the input data x is compressed as Ẑ = fϕ (x), and then, using
343
      a conditional distribution p(Zq |Ẑ) (e.g., a Gaussian distribution), a noisy version of Ẑ is obtained.
344
      Then, the latent variable eJ is obtained using q(J = j|Zq , e) ∝ exp −β∥Zq − ej ∥2 , which forms
345
      the Markov chain U − Ẑ − Zq − eJ . From the data processing inequality, the CMI between U and Ẑ
346
      or U and Zq is larger than that between U and eJ , thus improving generalization.

      3.4   C OMPARISON WITH EXISTING BOUNDS
373
374   Here, we compare our bounds with those in existing work. Theorem 2 resembles the results of Mbacke
375   et al. (2023) since both bounds include the empirical KL term in the upper bounds, and the posterior
376   distribution corresponds to the variational posterior distribution. The key difference is that Mbacke
377   et al. (2023) assumed fixed encoder and decoder parameters, whereas our analysis incorporates
      the learning process under the assumption of a finite latent space and a squared reconstruction loss.

      Another distinction is that their generalization bound does not become 0 as n → ∞ due to two reasons.
379   One is the presence of the empirical KL term, which we address in Theorem 3 using permutation
380   symmetry. Our technique can be regarded as developing the appropriate prior  Pn distribution in PAC-
381   Bayes bound. The second reason is the presence of the average distance n1 m=1 EX ∥X − Sm ∥ in
382   the existing bound, which is inherent to the data distribution and may not vanish as n → ∞. Our use
383   of the squared loss in the analysis mitigates this problematic term, as detailed in Appendix C.
384
      Our proof techniques are based on Sefidgaran et al. (2023). However, we could not directly apply
385   their methods, as the reconstruction loss reuses input data, unlike in classification settings. We
386   resolve this by combining the data regeneration technique used in the proof of Mbacke et al. (2023).
387   Additionally, we introduced a new permutation symmetric setting, leading to a bound that controls
388   mutual information in Theorem 3. Our setting is closely related to the type-2 symmetry proposed
389   in Sefidgaran et al. (2023), which   involves random permutations selecting n indices from 2n with
      a uniform distribution 1/ 2n

390                                n   , whereas our setting requires the consideration of the order of the
391   permutation index to evaluate the exponential moment (see Appendix E). Finally, we theoretically
392   studied the behavior of the CMI (Theorem 4) focusing on the complexity of the encoder, whereas
393   Sefidgaran et al. (2023) provided the bounds based on the CMI without such discussion.
394   The existing analyses based on the IB hypothesis (Vera et al., 2018; Hafez-Kolahi et al., 2020;
395   Kawaguchi et al., 2023; Vera et al., 2023) assumed that both the latent variables and data are discrete,
396   and their obtained bounds explicitly depend on the latent space size or show exponential dependence
397   on the MI. In contrast, we assume that only latent variables are discrete and the resulting bound does
398   not explicitly depend on the number of discrete states nor exhibit exponential dependence on MI.

442   5    E XPERIMENTS
443
444   In this section, we present experimental results on MNIST (LeCun et al., 1989) to analyze the
445   behavior of the CMI term (I(J̃; U |e, ϕ, X̃)/n) and the KL term (EX̃,U Eq(e,ϕ,θ|X̃U ) KL(Q∥P)/n)
446   in our generalization error bound and to confirm the validity of our provided in Theorem 2 and to
447   confirm the validity of the discussions based on it. These values were empirically evaluated using
448
      15 models trained under five supersample settings prepared based on three different combinations
      of training and test datasets. We also measured the generalization gap, which is the absolute value
449
      of the difference in the empirical reconstruction loss calculated using the training and test data as
450
      the estimated value of the generalization error. In our experiments, we adopted the SQ-VAE model
451   proposed by Takida et al. (2022) with the ConvResNets architecture. The SQ-VAE utilizes a posterior
452   distribution similar to Eq. (1), based on the Gumbel-Softmax relaxation Jang et al. (2017); Maddison
453   et al. (2017), enabling efficient optimization of discrete latent variables and resulting in the excellent
454   generalization performance. The details of our experimental settings are provided in Appendix I.
455
      We first conducted experiments to elucidate the behavior of the CMI and KL divergence terms
456
      in our bound as n increases. Here, we adopted the following sample-size settings: n =
457   {250, 1000, 2000, 4000}. The results are presented in Figure 1. From these results, it is evident that
458   the CMI term (center plot) decreases as n increases. Moreover, we observe a correlation between
459   the generalization gap (left plot) and the CMI term. In addition, the CMI term values are quite
460   close to the generalization gap. In contrast, the values of the KL divergence term (right plot), which
461   employ a uniform distribution as the prior, take extremely large values, do not correlate well with the
462   generalization gap, and do not necessarily decrease as n increases. This observation aligns with our
463   discussion in Section 3.1 regarding the importance of evaluating the generalization error through the
464   construction of an appropriate prior using supersamples.
465   Next, to analyze the behavior of the CMI and KL term in response to changes in model complexity,
466   we fixed the number of training samples at n = 4000 and evaluated models trained with K =
467   {16, 32, 64, 128}. The results are presented in Figure 2. This result indicates that our bound increases
468   only by log K. In contrast, existing studies (Hafez-Kolahi et al., 2021) show that generalization
469   bounds depend exponentially on MI or entropy, which possibly shows O(K). These differences
470   highlight the superiority of our bound.
471
472   6    C ONCLUSION AND LIMITATIONS
473
474   We conducted the first comprehensive analysis of the generalization and data generation capabilities of
475   encoder–decoder models in unsupervised learning based on the IT analysis. Our work highlights the
476   role of encoder capacity and the posterior distribution of latent variables through a novel permutation
477   symmetric supersample setting. However, our analysis has two limitations. First, it assumes a discrete
478   latent space, limiting its applicability to models such as VAEs with continuous latent variables.
479   Second, it relies on the squared loss for reconstruction. Addressing these limitations in future work
480
      is crucial for developing a more accurate understanding of encoder–decoder models. Furthermore,
      from our observations in Section 5, our bound expressed by the CMI term without the KL term in
481
      Theorem 3 may be more reasonable compared to that in Theorem 2. However, it is challenging to
482
      estimate I(J̃; T|e, ϕ, X̃) since T is a high-dimensional random variable (2n dimensions), which
483
      prevent us verifying its validity through numerical experiments. Exploring estimation methods for
484   such a CMI term with high-dimensional random variables, or deriving a bound based solely on the
485   CMI through alternative approaches, constitutes an important direction for future research.

1452   F.2    D ISCUSSION ABOUT THE METRIC ENTROPY FOR REGULARIZED MODEL
1453
1454
       Here we discuss the upper bound of metric entropy in our setting. Since the latent variable lies in
       Rdz , the encoder network operates as fϕ : Rd → Rdz , making it a multivariate function.
1455
1456   To evaluate the complexity of the metric entropy for such multivariate functions, the concept of
1457   Natarajan dimension with margin has been employed (Guermeur, 2007). According to Lemma 39
       (and also Lemma 37 and 38), if a multivariate function has a finite Natarajan dimension with margin,


                                                                        27
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1458
       then its metric entropy scales as O(log n). To explore the properties of the Natarajan dimension with
1459   margin, Guermeur (2018) demonstrated that it can be bounded by the fat-shattering dimension of each
1460   component of the original multivariate function (Lemma 10) under the certain margin assumption.
1461   Additionally, Guermeur (2017) showed in Lemma 1 that the covering number of the multivariate
1462   function can be bounded by the covering number of each of its components under the certain margin
1463   assumption. To further bound the covering number of each dimension, one can rely on the fat-
1464   shattering dimension of each function, as discussed in Lemma 3.5 of Alon et al. (1997). Thus, it is
1465   essential to bound the fat-shattering dimension in both cases. Examples of fat-shattering dimension
1466   evaluations can be found, for instance, in Bartlett & Maass (2003), which discusses neural network
1467
       models, and Gottlieb et al. (2014), which addresses the fat-shattering dimension of Lipschitz function
       classes. If our encoder network adheres to these properties, we can bound its covering number

1603   I.1        OVERVIEW OF SQ-VAE
1604
1605   The SQ-VAE is a generative model that, similar to VQ-VAE, employs a learnable codebook e =
1606   {ek }K          K
             k=1 ∈ Z . The objective of SQ-VAE is to learn the stochastic decoder x ∼ pθ (x|Zq )
1607   using latent variables Zq to generate samples belonging to the data distribution pdata (x), where
1608   pθ (x|Zq ) = N (gθ (Zq ), σ 2 I), N (m, σI) is the Gaussian distribution with mean and equal variance
1609   parameter {m, σ 2 I}, σ 2 ∈ R+ , and I is the identity matrix. Here, Zq is sampled from a prior
1610   distribution P (Zq ) over the discrete latent space edz .
1611   In the main training process of SQ-VAE, we assume P (Zq ) to be an i.i.d. uniform distribution,
1612   identical to VQ-VAE, meaning each codebook element is selected with equal probability (P (zq,i =
1613   bk ) = 1/K for k ∈ [K]). Subsequently, a second training stage is conducted to learn P (Zq ).
1614   Since computing the posterior pθ (Zq |x) exactly is intractable, we utilize an approximate posterior
1615   distribution qϕ (Zq |x) instead.
1616
       At the encoding process, directly mapping from x to the discrete Zq is challenging due to the discrete
1617   nature of Zq . To overcome this issue, Takida et al. (2022) proposed to construct a stochastic encoder
1618   by introducing the following two processes:

1652   I.2   G AUSSIAN SQ-VAE
1653
1654   Gaussian SQ-VAE assumes that the dequantization process pψ (Z|Zq ) follows a Gaussian distribution.
1655   In this paper, we set the following Gaussian distribution: pψ (Zi |Zq ) = N (Zq,i , σψ2 I), where σψ2 ∈
1656   R+ . Then, the stochastic decoder and the stochastic dequantization process in SQ-VAE can be written
1657   as pθ (x|Zq ) = N (gθ (Zq ), σ 2 I) and pψ (Zi |Ẑq ) = N (Ẑq,i , σψ2 I).
