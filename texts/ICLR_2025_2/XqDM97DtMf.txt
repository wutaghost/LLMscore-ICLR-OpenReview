009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Chaotic dynamics, commonly seen in weather systems and fluid turbulence, are
012            characterized by their sensitivity to initial conditions, which makes accurate pre-
013            diction challenging. Despite its sensitivity to initial perturbations, many chaotic
014            systems observe dissipative behaviors and ergodicity. Therefore, recently var-
015            ious approaches have been proposed to develop data-driven models preserving
016            invariant statistics over long horizons. Although these methods have shown em-
017            pirical success in reducing instances of unbounded trajectory generation, many of
018
               the models are still prone to generating unbounded trajectories, leading to invalid
               statistics evaluation. In this paper, we propose a novel neural network architec-
019
               ture that simultaneously learns a dissipative dynamics emulator that guarantees to
020
               generate bounded trajectories and an energy-like function that governs the dissipa-
021            tive behavior. More specifically, by leveraging control-theoretic ideas, we derive
022            algebraic conditions based on the learned energy-like function that ensure asymp-
023            totic convergence to an invariant level set. Using these algebraic conditions, our
024            proposed model enforces dissipativity through a ReLU projection layer, which
025            provides formal trajectory boundedness guarantees. Furthermore, the invariant
026            level set provides an outer estimate for the strange attractor, which is known to be
027            very difficult to characterize due to its complex geometry. We demonstrate the ca-
028            pability of our model in producing bounded long-horizon trajectory forecasts that
029
               preserve invariant statistics and characterizing the attractor, for chaotic dynamical
               systems including Lorenz 96 and a truncated Kuramoto–Sivashinsky equation.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      Chaos, characterized by exponential divergence after infinitesimal initial perturbations, is ubiquitous
035
      in a variety of complex dynamical systems including climate models (Lorenz, 1963) and turbulence
036   in fluids (Kuramoto, 1978; Ashinsky, 1988). The exponential separation makes it challenging to
037   accurately predict their trajectories using data-driven methods. Despite intrinsic instability, many
038   chaotic systems are dissipative, i.e., their trajectories will converge to a bounded positively invariant
039   set (Stuart & Humphries, 1998), which is also known as a strange attractor. Additionally, trajectories
040   of dissipative chaos will visit almost every state on the attractor, resulting in ergodicity and invari-
041   ant statistics (Guckenheimer & Holmes, 2013). As a result, recent progress in dissipative chaotic
042   systems learning has been focused on applying deep learning to learn a dynamics emulator that
043   preserves invariant statistics over a long forecast horizon instead of pointwise accurate prediction.
044   In order to obtain meaningful statistics from the trajectories generated by the learned dynamics em-
045   ulator, it is often required to roll out the model prediction for a long time horizon. Similar to the true
046   system, an accurate model aiming at preserving the invariant statistics needs to visit every possible
047   state on the strange attractor. Since the training dataset is limited, the model needs to generalize well
048   to states not seen during training. More specifically, it is crucial that the learned model always gen-
049   erate bounded trajectories from all initial states, otherwise the statistics evaluation will be invalid.
050
      However, it can be challenging to keep predicted trajectories bounded for a sufficiently long time,
      as these models are trained to match the chaotic behaviors observed in the training data.
051
052   Despite empirical success in developing data-driven models that preserve invariant statistics well,
053   many of these models still suffer from the risk of generating unbounded trajectories. For specific re-
      gression models such as multi-level ad hoc quadratic regression models, theoretical limitations have

      been investigated by Majda & Yuan (2012), where they found these models could lead to finite-
055   time blowup or unstable statistical solutions, rendering the prediction unreliable. For sequential
056   time-series models, it was shown by Mikhaeil et al. (2022) that training a recurrent neural network
057   (RNN) for chaotic system prediction can lead to unbounded gradients. Other than RNN, reservoir
058   computing (RC) has been a popular choice in modeling chaotic systems due to its relatively sim-
059   ple structure and computation efficiency, which helps avoid unbounded gradient issues occured in
060   RNN-based approaches (Wikner et al., 2022). Although RC models have shown good prediction
061   performance in capturing invariant statistics, Lu et al. (2018); Pathak et al. (2017) have observed
062   that they are prone to step on a state not seen during training and experiencing finite-time blowup,
063
      which reduces the length of reliable forecast.
064   Related Work. To gain deeper understanding of this issue and encourage boundedness in trajec-
065   tories over long forecast horizons, various approaches have been proposed under different settings.
066   For RC-based models, Shirin et al. (2019) proposes a stability analysis approach based on Lyapunov
067   methods to estimate the region of attraction of a fixed point, as an effort to quantify the globally
068   stable region of the learned model. To improve RC-based model’s stability over long time horizons,
069
      a noise-inspired regularization has shown empirical success in promoting stability (Wikner et al.,
      2022), and it is also shown by Haluszczynski et al. (2020) that the predicted trajectory stability can
070
      be improved empirically by reducing network size and removing certain nodes.
071
072   In addition to modifying an architecture or adding noise in the training data, recent literature has
073   shown that empirical improvement in stability can also be achieved by prioritizing on learning statis-
074   tics over pointwise prediction. Platt et al. (2023) propose an RC-based method that constrains the
075
      model to match the Lyapunov exponent spectrum and fractal dimension of the true system. Based on
      the neural operator framework, Jiang et al. (2024) propose to add statistics-based regularization to
076
      the loss function through either expert knowledge or contrastive learning, both of which have shown
077
      to improve the model’s prediction for the invariant statistics. Despite empirical evidence that these
078   methods can reduce the instances of unbounded trajectory generation, there is still a risk of generat-
079   ing unbounded trajectory without any formal treatment regarding dissipativity, which prevents valid
080   statistics evaluation.
081
      Instead of empirical improvement in reducing unbounded trajectory generation instances, a few
082
      works in the literature have been developed to ensure trajectory boundedness by enforcing dissipa-
083
      tivity in the learned model. Based on quadratic nonlinear models, Majda & Harlim (2012) propose
084   a physics-constrained multi-level regression method which employs a linear system and nonlinear
085   system component, and ensures the model to be dissipative by careful construction of the nonlinear
086   term. Based on the neural operator framework, Li et al. (2022) propose to enforce dissipativity in
087   the model by explicitly altering the flow of the learned dynamics emulator in a pre-specified region
088   of the state space. Although both methods show success in predicting chaotic systems with bound-
089   edness guarantees, they often require expert knowledge in constructing the model to pre-specify the
090   dissipativity condition, which can be challenging to obtain for complex systems.
091   Our Key Contributions. In this paper, we propose an approach to learn a neural network model
092   for dissipative chaotic systems that is guaranteed to generate bounded trajectories over long forecast
093   horizons. Unlike previous approaches that require prior information about the system, we simultane-
094   ously learn a dynamics emulator that is formally guaranteed to be dissipative and an energy function
095   that governs the dissipative behavior. Our key contributions are three-fold:
096
            1. By leveraging Lyapunov methods in control theory, we introduce level sets of Lyapunov
097
               functions as an attractor that the dissipative system trajectory converges to, which allows us
098            to derive algebraic conditions for dissipativity that are computationally efficient to verify
099            (Section 3).
100
            2. We enforce the aforementioned algebraic condition in the neural network model, by con-
101            structing a projection layer that ensures the learned dynamics emulator to be dissipative
102            (Section 4.1).
103
            3. By incorporating regularization loss of the invariant level set volume in the training process,
104            our model also learns an outer estimate of the strange attractor which is very difficult to
105            characterize due to its complex geometry (Section 4.2).
106
107   Additionally, we first illustrate the novel stability projection layer that ensures trajectory bounded-
      ness through a numerical experiment on the Lorenz 63 system. We demonstrate the effectiveness

124   Characteristics of Chaotic Dynamical Systems. As discussed in (Strogatz, 2018), a chaotic system
125   typically exhibits the following three properties, albeit there is no universally accepted definition:
126
127
            1. “Aperiodic long-term behavior”: There is a set with non-zero measure of initial conditions
               from which the system’s trajectory does not converge to a fixed point or periodic orbits or
128
               quasiperiodic orbits.
129
130         2. “Deterministic”: There is no stochasticity in either the input or parameters of the system.
131         3. “Sensitive dependence on initial conditions”: Small perturbations in the initial conditions
132            may lead to exponential separation in the trajectory in certain regions of the state space.
133
134
      Among the three properties listed above, the third one is the most quantifiable and also well known
      as the “butterfly effect” (Lorenz, 1963). More formally, the “sensitive dependence on initial condi-
135
      tions” is quantified as the Lyapunov exponent being positive. In what follows, we give an informal
136
      definition for the Lyapunov exponent and explain its implications for prediction problems.
137
138   Intuitive Definition for Lyapunov Exponent (Vulpiani, 2010): Suppose there are two trajectories
139   of the system whose initial conditions differ by δx0 . The Lyapunov exponent λ describes the rate of
140
      exponential growth of the distance between the two trajectories, i.e., ∥δx(t)∥ ≈ exp(λt)∥δx0 ∥, ∀t ∈
      [0, t0 ] for some finite time t0 > 0.
141
142   As a signature of chaotic systems, the Lyapunov exponent being positive implies that neighboring
143   trajectories diverge exponentially. Note that in the presence of a positive Lyapunov exponent, if the
144   initial condition is measured with a tiny error ∥δ0 ∥, the prediction error will grow above a given
145   small threshold ϵ in finite time of O(ln(ϵ/∥δ0 ∥)/λ). In other words, in regions where exponential
146
      separation from neighboring trajectories occur, accurate prediction of chaotic systems is almost
      impossible in the long run (Strogatz, 2018).
147
148   Despite the fact that neighboring trajectories may diverge exponentially in certain regions of the
149   state space, it is important to make the distinction between chaos and instability. As an example
150   in (Strogatz, 2018), the scalar system ẋ = x is deterministic and exhibits sensitive dependence on
151   initial conditions. However, this is not a chaotic system, as every trajectory grows to infinity and
152
      never returns, which makes infinity an attracting fixed point. Such global unstable behaviors exclude
      the system from being chaotic, because it is not aperiodic in the long term.
153
154   The Strange Attractor and Invariant Statistics. As discussed earlier, global instability often dis-
155   qualifies a system from being chaotic. One natural question is what it means to have exponential
156   separation of neighboring trajectories while not being globally unstable. An intuitive explanation is
157   that the exponential divergence only occurs locally within certain regions of the state space, which
158
      corresponds to the “strange attractor”. Due to the disagreement in the literature on a precise defini-
      tion of the strange attractor, we list a few important characteristics of the strange attractor instead of
159
      giving a formal definition (Strogatz, 2018). Interested readers may refer to (Milnor, 1985; Gucken-
160
      heimer & Holmes, 2013) for detailed discussions on the subtleties of such definitions.
161
      The strange attractor M for a chaotic system has the following characteristics (Strogatz, 2018):


                                                          3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
            1. M is a positively invariant set that attracts trajectories that start in an open neighborhood.
163
164         2. M minimal, i.e., there is no proper subset of M that satisfies the above property.
165
            3. Exponential separation of neighboring trajectories occurs with all initial conditions in M .
166
167   The attractivity and invariant set properties of the strange attractor make sure trajectories that are
168   sufficiently close to the attractor will converge to it asymptotically. Intuitively, this behavior prevents
169   exponential separation from growing to infinity by attracting trajectories to a local region.
170
      Furthermore, the third property requires all neighboring trajectories starting on the strange attractor
171
      to separate exponentially. More importantly, it leads to the fact that the system will visit almost
172
      every state on the attractor, in other words, the system will be ergodic once it enters M . As a result,
173   the strange attractor provides invariant statistics of the system (Guckenheimer & Holmes, 2013).
174   Given the challenges in predicting such systems rooted in their chaotic nature, the existence of the
175   strange attractor allows for the possibility of evaluating the statistical properties of the system, rather
176   than only making pointwise predictions.
177
      Dissipative Chaotic Systems. So far we have discussed the importance of the strange attractor
178
      in preserving invariant statistics, but we have not addressed the existence and boundedness of the
179   strange attractor, which are crucial premises for evaluating invariant statistics of the chaotic sys-
180   tem. In general, not every chaotic system has a strange attractor, especially if they are Hamiltonian
181   systems (Ott, 2002). However, over the course of study on chaos, a large class of chaotic systems,
182   dissipative chaos, have been known to have strange attractors, which are bounded and positively
183   invariant sets that attract trajectories in an open neighborhood. As a result, we will focus on this
184   class of chaotic systems in this paper.
185   In fact, many chaotic systems are dissipative, including the well-studied Lorenz 63 system (Lorenz,
186   1963) and the Kuramoto–Sivashinsky equation (Kuramoto, 1978). Note that dissipativity is a prop-
187   erty of dynamical systems that describes its evolution over time, independent of whether the system
188   is chaotic or not. Here we provide a formal definition of dissipativity and then discuss its implica-
189   tions for learning chaos.
190
      Definition 1. We say that the system (1) is dissipative if there exists a bounded and positively invari-
191   ant set M ⊂ Rn such that limt→∞ dist(x(t), M ) = 0, where dist(x(t), M ) = inf y∈M ∥x(t) − y∥.
192   In other words, every trajectory of the system will converge to M asymptotically, and stays within
193   M once it enters. M is said to be globally asymptotically stable.
194
195   The definition is derived based on the one introduced by Stuart & Humphries (1998), where they
196   stated dissipativity as every trajectory will enter M eventually. Here, we quantify this behavior
197   using the notion of asymptotic stability and trajectory distance to M . As alluded to earlier, a chaotic
198   system being dissipative ensures that the trajectory always stays bounded and converges to its strange
199   attractor, in which every state is visited almost uniformly, consequently producing invariant statistics
200
      for evaluation.
201   Our Objective: Learning Dissipative Chaotic Dynamics.
202
      As discussed extensively in this section, we choose to focus on developing models for dissipative
203
      chaotic dynamics because of their invariant statistics. As seen in the recent literature, a tractable goal
204   is to learn a dynamics emulator producing trajectories that match the invariant statistics observed in
205   the original system over long horizons. Although matching statistics is slightly less challenging than
206   pointwise prediction, the chaotic nature of the system, namely exponential separation on the strange
207   attractor, still poses challenges for machine learning models.
208
      More specifically, since the system trajectory will traverse through every state on the attractor in-
209
      finitely often, training directly from trajectory data will often lead to an unstable learned dynamics
210   model as it is trying to match the chaotic behaviors observed in the data. Since the training dataset is
211   limited, during testing the model might not generalize well on the states inside the strange attractor
212   that are not seen during training, resulting in finite-time blowup as reported by Lu et al. (2018).
213   While as surveyed in the introduction, numerous methods have been proposed to address this issue
214   and achieved empirical success in reducing the instances of unbounded trajectory generation, there is
215   still a risk of generating unbounded trajectory without any formal treatment regarding dissipativity,
      which prevents valid statistics evaluation.

      3       A LGEBRAIC D ISSIPATIVITY C ONDITIONS AND ATTRACTOR E STIMATION
223
      In Section 2, we have discussed in detail why learning dissipative chaotic systems is a meaningful
224
      and tractable problem, and we identified the challenges involved. More specifically, dissipativity in
225   chaos ensures the existence of a strange attractor leading to invariant statistics, but in order to obtain
226   valid statistics, the learned models need to generate bounded trajectories over long time horizons. To
227   develop models with embedded dissipativity, we first need to understand theoretical conditions that
228   make a dynamical system dissipative. Note that dissipativity describes a system’s energy behavior
229   over time, independent of whether the system is chaotic or not. In this section, we focus on deriving
230   algebraic conditions that are computationally efficient through the connection between dissipativity
231   and energy, which are crucial for our proposed architecture that guarantees dissipativity.
232
233   3.1       E NERGY- BASED C ONDITIONS FOR D ISSIPATIVITY
234
      Dissipativity, as the name suggests, has a close relationship with energy in a dynamical system.
235
      Intuitively, a dissipative system will lose energy over time, which corresponds to the trajectory con-
236
      verging to a bounded set. Although the trajectory convergence behavior is quantitatively stated in
237   Definition 1, given a system ẋ = f (x) known to be dissipative with access to the true dynamics f ,
238   it is still challenging to quantify the set M that the trajectory converges to. In the specific context
239   of dissipative chaos, there has been a body of literature that tries to address this issue by studying
240   invariant manifolds, volume contraction, and attempting to characterize the strange attractor (Stu-
241   art & Humphries, 1998). Despite the rigorous treatment and the progress over the years that help
242   us understand the strange attractor, these characterizations are often stated in abstract mathemati-
243   cal concepts and a descriptive manner that is intractable to computationally verify, e.g., (Milnor,
244   1985). Given our goal of enforcing dissipativity in neural network models, it is crucial to first derive
245
      computationally efficient conditions that ensure a system is dissipative.
246   In control theory, the concept of Lyapunov functions has been used extensively to formalize asymp-
247   totic stability of dynamical systems, which are also known as “energy-like” functions due to strong
248   connections with the mechanical energy of the system. More importantly, by leveraging the level set
249   of such functions, numerous computationally tractable conditions have been derived and extensively
250   used in designing practical controllers to ensure a system’s asymptotic stability to equilibrium points
251
      (Khalil, 2002). By generalizing asymptotic stability with respect to an equilibrium point to a level
      set of a Lyapunov function, we derive computationally efficient conditions that ensure dissipativity
252
      in a dynamical system.
253
254   Recall in Definition 1, a dissipative system requires the existence of a bounded set M , which satisfies
255   (1) M is an invariant set (2) the system is globally asymptotically stable towards M . By reducing
256   the definition to the existence of a Lyapunov function V and choosing M to be a level set of V , i.e.,
257
      M (c) = {x : V (x) ≤ c} where c > 0 corresponds to the energy level, we derive the following
      conditions for invariance and asymptotic stability of M (c) in Proposition 1 and 2, respectively.

308   Strange Attractor Outer Estimation. Recall the characteristics of the strange attractor discussed
309   in Section 2, if only the first two properties are satisfied by a set S, then this set is known as “the
310   attractor” (Strogatz, 2018). The difference from the strange attractor is that the attractor does not
311   require the exponential separation of neighboring trajectories everywhere, hence it is also a superset
312   of the strange attractor. Note that if a set S ⊂ M (c) is an attractor, then M (c) is both invariant and
313   globally asymptotically stable. Therefore, in addition to certifying a system to be dissipative, the
314

      6     C ONCLUSION
530
531
      In this paper, we propose a novel neural network architecture for learning chaotic systems that en-
      sures the learned dynamics are dissipative, which guarantees the model to always generate bounded
532
      trajectories and provide meaningful statistics evaluation. By leveraging control theoretic ideas, we
533
      have derived algebraic conditions that ensure dissipativity and embed these conditions into the neural
534   network through a projection layer design. Using the level set of an energy function learned simul-
535   taneously with the dynamics emulator, our model also provides an outer estimate for the strange
536   attractor, which is difficult to characterize due to its complex geometry. Numerical experiments for
537   chaotic systems including Lorenz 96 and truncated KS show the model’s capability of preserving
538   invariant statistics of the true dynamics. Experiments also show that a model trained without the
539   stability projection layer leads to finite-time blowup and unreliable statistics, which exemplifies the
      importance of our proposed approach in learning dissipative chaotic dynamics.
