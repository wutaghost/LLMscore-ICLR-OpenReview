009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Complex networks describe important structures in nature and society, composed
012            of nodes and the edges that connect them. The evolution of these networks is
013            typically described by dynamics, which are labor-intensive and require expert
014            knowledge to derive. However, because the complex network involves noisy
015            observations from multiple trajectories of nodes, existing symbolic regression
016            methods are either not applicable or ineffective on its dynamics. In this paper, we
017            propose Physically Inspired Neural Dynamics Symbolic Regression (PI-NDSR),
018
               a method based on neural networks and genetic programming to automatically
               learn the symbolic expression of dynamics. Our method consists of two key
019
               components: a Physically Inspired Neural Dynamics (PIND) to augment and
020
               denoise trajectories through observed trajectory interpolation; and a coordinated
021            genetic search algorithm to derive symbolic expressions. This algorithm leverages
022            references of node dynamics and edge dynamics from neural dynamics to avoid
023            overfitted expressions in symbolic space. We evaluate our method on synthetic
024            datasets generated by various dynamics and real datasets on disease spreading. The
025            results demonstrate that PI-NDSR outperforms the existing method in terms of
026            both recovery probability and error.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Complex networks (Gerstner et al., 2014; Gao et al., 2016; Bashan et al., 2016; Newman et al., 2011)
032   describe important structures in nature and society, which is composed of a set of nodes and a set
033
      of edges that connect them. Complex networks can model various real-world systems, including
      social networks (Kitsak et al., 2010), epidemic networks (Pastor-Satorras & Vespignani, 2001), brain
034
      networks (Laurence et al., 2019; Wilson & Cowan, 1972), and transportation networks (Kaluza et al.,
035
      2010). Extensive works (Zang & Wang, 2020; Murphy et al., 2021; Gao & Yan, 2022) have been
036   conducted to analyze the dynamics of complex networks (Pastor-Satorras et al., 2015; MacArthur,
037   1970; Kuramoto & Kuramoto, 1984), which is crucial for understanding the underlying mechanisms
038   of complex systems and predicting their future behaviors. Among them, the symbolic complex
039   network dynamics is of great importance as it offers a clear and concise depiction of the internal
040   mechanisms and their impact on the overall system behavior.
041
      Obtaining the symbolic expressions for complex network dynamics is challenging and is usually
042   done by mathematicians or physicists. The process of symbolic regression of complex network
043   dynamics typically requires repetitive trial-and-error attempts and profound expertise in the relevant
044   field. Therefore, it is often unlikely to find the symbolic expressions without any prior knowledge of
045   the system (Virgolin & Pissis, 2022). The goal of this paper is to develop a machine-learning method
046   to automatically learn the symbolic expressions of complex network dynamics from data.
047
      Symbolic regression aims to discover the underlying symbolic formula from data (Schmidt & Lipson,
048   2009; Petersen et al., 2019; Cranmer et al., 2020; Shi et al., 2023). Various techniques have been
049   applied to symbolic regression for dynamics, including genetic programming (Gaucel et al., 2014;
050   Kronberger et al., 2020), sparse regression (Brunton et al., 2016), deep-learning-based genetic
051   programming (Qian et al., 2022), and Transformer (d’Ascoli et al., 2024). However, all these methods
052   are designed for symbolic regression of dynamics for a single trajectory. Directly applying them to
053   complex network dynamics ignores common patterns between trajectories and leads to inefficient
      learning and poor performance (Gao & Yan, 2022), as the complex network is an evolving system

      2.1   S YMBOLIC REGRESSION
112
113
114   Throughout the history of physics, extracting elegant symbolic expressions from extensive experi-
115   mental data has been a fundamental approach to uncovering new formulas and validating hypotheses.
116
      Symbolic Regression (SR) is a notable topic in this context (Schmidt & Lipson, 2009; Petersen et al.,
      2019; Cranmer et al., 2020; Kamienny et al., 2022), aiming to mimic the process of deriving an
117
      explicit symbolic model that accurately maps input X to output y while ensuring the model remains
118
      concise. Traditional methods for deriving formulas from data have predominantly relied on genetic
119   programming (GP) (Schmidt & Lipson, 2009; Koza, 1994; Worm & Chiu, 2013), a technique inspired
120   by biological evolution that iteratively evolves populations of candidate solutions to discover the
121   most effective mathematical representations.
122
      More recently, due to the remarkable accomplishments of neural networks across diverse domains,
123
      there has been an increasing interest in leveraging deep learning for symbolic regression. Specif-
124
      ically, some recent works (Cranmer et al., 2020; Chen et al., 2021; Qian et al., 2022; Udrescu &
125   Tegmark, 2020; Martius & Lampert, 2016; Mundhenk et al., 2021; Shi et al., 2023) have explored
126   guiding genetic programming with the output of neural networks to improve the efficiency and
127   accuracy of symbolic regression. This approach takes advantage of the powerful pattern recognition
128   and generalization capabilities of neural networks to inform the evolutionary processes of genetic
129   programming, resulting in more effective and efficient discovery of symbolic expressions. Another
130   line of works (Kamienny et al., 2022; Biggio et al., 2021; Valipour et al., 2021; d’Ascoli et al.,
131   2024) applies Transformer to symbolic regression and achieves comparable performance to GP-based
132   methods.

      2.2   C OMPLEX NETWORK DYNAMICS LEARNING
136
137
138   With the development of machine learning in recent decades, many works have been proposed to
139   learn the dynamics of complex networks from data. The work for learning dynamics of complex
140   networks can be divided into two categories: dynamics learning with neural networks and dynamics
141
      learning with symbolic regression.
142   Dynamics learning with neural networks utilizes deep neural networks to learn dynamics (Hamrick
143   et al., 2018; Zang & Wang, 2020; Liu et al., 2023). Neural dynamics usually follows the encode-
144   process-decode paradigm (Hamrick et al., 2018; Zang & Wang, 2020) with an encoding neural
145   network to pre-process the initial node states by mapping them to latent states. Then the latent states
146   are processed by a graph neural network to capture the evolution and interaction of the complex
147
      network. Murphy et al. (2021) propose to use GNN to model the evolution of complex networks
      with regularly sampled observation. NDCN (Zang & Wang, 2020) models the continuity of the
148
      dynamics with graph neural ODE (Chen et al., 2018) and the interaction of the dynamics with GNN.
149
      Even though neural dynamics learning can capture the complex dynamics of complex networks, it is
150   difficult to interpret the learned dynamics due to the black-box nature of neural networks. Recently,
151   Liu et al. (2023) drop the encoder-process-decoder paradigm and achieve better performance on
152   predicting long-term behavior of complex networks.
153
      On the other hand, dynamics learning with symbolic regression aims to learn symbolic expressions
154
      of complex network dynamics from data. Two-phase sparse identification of nonlinear dynamical
155
      systems (TP-SINDy) (Gao & Yan, 2022) is proposed to obtain the dynamics of complex networks
156   from data. The proposed method includes a function library including a wider range of elementary
157   functions than the orthogonal basis functions in SINDy (Brunton et al., 2016) and a two-phase
158   regression method to learn the dynamics of complex networks. However, TP-SINDy requires
159   an accurate estimation of the time derivative of the node states and can only learn the symbolic
160   expressions from the pre-defined function library. Our work uses the supervision signal and dynamics
161   references provided by neural dynamics, based on genetic programming to learn the symbolic
      expressions of complex network dynamics.

169   In (1), ẋv (t) denotes the time derivative of xv (t), F (xv (t)) denotes the node dynamics term of node
170   v, which includes processes like influx, degradation, or reproduction. G(xv (t), xu (t)) is the edge
171   dynamics describing the interactions between node v and node u, accounting for processes such as
172   spreading and competition. G is shared across all edges in the network because of the universality in

174   node v and node u, and Nv is the set of neighbors of node v.
175   Given observations of the node states {X(t)|t ∈ T }, the symbolic regression of complex network
176   dynamics (Gao & Yan, 2022) aims to find the symbolic expressions of function F and G in (1). Com-
177   pared with traditional symbolic regression, we identify two main challenges of symbolic regression
178   for network dynamics: (a) Directly regressing symbolic function on time derivative is inaccurate
179   and unstable because the estimation of derivative Ẋ(t) is noisy and its accuracy relies on the regular
180   sampling intervals of the observation. (b) The network dynamics consist of two parts, namely node
181   dynamics F and edge dynamics G. In symbolic regression, it is possible that F and G jointly perform
182
      well on the observed (interpolation) trajectory but exhibit significant discrepancies in predicting node
      dynamics and edge dynamics compared to the real dynamics, which results in bad extrapolation
183
      performance (see examples in Appendix B.4). We refer to this phenomenon as the overfitting problem
184
      in symbolic space. In this paper, we propose a physically inspired symbolic regression algorithm,
185   addressing the first challenge with interpolated network observations and the second challenge with a
186   coordinated genetic optimization algorithm.

195   coordinated genetic optimization algorithm based on the algorithmic alignment between neural

      4.1   T RAJECTORY DENOISING AND AUGMENTATION WITH INTERPOLATED OBSERVATIONS
199
200
      In symbolic regression, it is beneficial to regress symbolic expressions with many low-noise network
201
      observations. However, the network dynamics observation is usually sparse (i.e., sample time interval
202   is large) and noisy. To address this issue, we propose Physically Inspired Neural Dynamics (PIND) to
203   interpolate and denoise the network observations, obtaining denser and less noisy trajectories. Except
204   for the interpolated trajectory, the neural dynamics aligns with network dynamics in (1) and provides
205   an estimation of the node dynamics and edge dynamics, which is used as references for coordinated
206   genetic optimization in deriving the symbolic F and G.
207
      Following existing works (Zang & Wang, 2020; Murphy et al., 2021), the neural dynamics is designed
208   based on encode-process-decode paradigm (Battaglia et al., 2018). To obtain the denser and less
209   noisy trajectory and the high-quality estimation for node dynamics and edge dynamics for genetic
210   search, we incorporate the algorithmic inductive bias in (1) to design a physically-inspired neural
211   dynamics and train the neural dynamics from the observed trajectory. In neural dynamics, the raw
212   observation xv (t) is first encoded by an encoding network Enc to obtain the latent state hv (t), and
213   the graph neural network is applied to calculate the time derivative of the latent state ḣv (t), which is
214   later decoded by a decoding network Dec to obtain the time derivatives ẋ◦v (t). Finally, differential
215   ODESolver (Chen et al., 2018) is applied to ẋ◦v (t) to evolve the neural dynamics to obtain more
      network observations.

235   4.2   C OORDINATED GENETIC SEARCH FOR SYMBOLIC REGRESSION
236
237   To regress the symbolic formulas F and G, we propose an algorithm called coordinated genetic search
238
      to obtain the symbolic expressions of complex network dynamics. Based on the physically-inspired
      design of (2), we can obtain the references for symbolic expressions by disentangling the neural
239
      dynamics into node dynamics and edge dynamics. Then, we use the neural references to coordinate
240
      the search process of symbolic expressions of F and G to avoid the overfitted expressions.
241
242   Because of the inductive bias and algorithmic alignment of (1), the neural dynamics often have
243   better generalization performance (Xu et al., 2019; Veličković & Blundell, 2021). Therefore, the
244
      neural node dynamics and edge dynamics can be used as estimations of the node dynamics and edge
      dynamics in (1), which can be used as references for coordinating the search process of symbolic
245
      expressions of F and G. The neural node dynamics and edge dynamics can be computed with the
246
      following equations:

      We compare these algorithms in two aspects: proxy models and formula regression. ‘Proxy models’
343   are trained to fit data and serve as a basis for deriving symbolic expressions. ‘Formula regression’
344   directly extract symbolic expressions from raw data or proxy models. For proxy model, SymDL
345   uses graph networks (GN) with inductive bias, and NASSymDL employs neural architecture search
346   (NAS) for skeleton search. D-CODE can incorporate any suitable regressor. PI-NDSR fits multiple
347   trajectories using PIND, a graph neural ODE aligned with network dynamics for better generalization.
348   SymDL and NASSymDL rely on potentially noisy estimated derivatives for dynamics fitting, whereas
349   D-CODE and PI-NDSR train directly on raw observations for improved accuracy. TP-SINDy operates
350   without a proxy model.
351   In formula regression, methods using genetic search employ elementary operations (e.g.,
352   +, −, ×, ÷, sin, exp) to represent formula, offering more flexibility and requiring less prior knowl-
353   edge than TP-SINDy’s linear combination of functions in predefined function library. SymDL and
354   NASSymDL use the internal functions (Cranmer et al., 2020) from the proxy models as supervision
355   to compute fitness in genetic search. D-CODE uses the interpolated trajectories as supervision.
356   TP-SINDy is based on sparse regression and uses estimated derivatives for symbolic regression,
357
      which can be noisy and inaccurate over large time intervals. PI-NDSR design a coordinated genetic
      search, using (4) from proxy model as references and interpolated trajectories as supervision.

525   6    C ONCLUSION
526
527   In this paper, we propose physically inspired symbolic regression to learn symbolic expressions of
528   complex network dynamics from data. Our approach aims to avoid overfitted symbolic formulas by
529   incorporating the supervision of interpolated and denoised trajectories, as well as referencing neural
530   dynamics. Our method is based on neural dynamics to augment and denoise the trajectory of complex
531   network, and then apply the coordinated genetic search to infer the symbolic expressions based on
532   the dynamics reference from neural dynamics. Compare with existing methods, our method requires
533   less prior knowledge on complex networks, can handle irregularly sampled data, and effectively
534   search the symbolic space and avoid overfitting. See impact statement, future works and limitations
535
      in Appendix C.

733   A.3    D ETAILS OF GENETIC SEARCH
734
      We implement the coordinated genetic search based on gplearn (Stephens, 2015). gplearn (Stephens,
735   2015) represent the symbolic expressions as a syntax tree, where the functions are interior nodes,
736   and the variables and constants make up the leaves. Evolution such as crossover, mutation, and
737   reproduction are performed on the syntax tree. The population size of F and G are set to 200. The
738   maximum generation of the genetic search M is set to 50 and the stopping threshold ϵ = 10−5 .
739   The K in Algorithm 1 equals to 20. The function set includes addition, subtraction, multiplication,
740   division, sine, cosine, and exponential. The constants are constrained in the range [−1, 1]. Other hy-
741   perparameters of gplearn are set as: p crossover=0.6, p subtree mutation=0.1, p hoist mutation=0.05,
742   p point mutation=0.1, parsimony coefficient=0.01. We conduct the genetic search in 256 parallel
743
      threads to speed up the search process. Our CPUs are two AMD EPYC 7763 Processors.
744
745   A.4    C OMPUTATIONAL DETAILS OF R EC . P ROB .
746
      The recovery probability is calculated as the ratio of the number of successful recovery of formula
747
      skeletons to the total number of experiments. We automatically check the correctness of the recovered
748   formula skeletons using the method for verifying skeletons provided in Qian et al. (2022). Basically,
749   we replace the constants in the formulas with placeholders and use the simplify(f ′ − f ) == 0
750   criterion from the Sympy package to determine if the skeleton is correct.
751
752   A.5    D ISCUSSIONS ON THE CHOICE OF METRICS
753
754   Compute MSE between trajectories instead of the constants. We do not directly compute the
755   MSE between predicted and true constants. This is because our goal is to evaluate how well the
      obtained symbolic expressions predict trajectories, which is crucial for real-world scenarios like


                                                         14
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


756
      epidemic forecasting. Directly computing constant errors is insufficient, as different constants impact
757   the trajectory differently. Some constants require high precision, with small deviations causing
758   significant errors, while others are less critical and can tolerate some errors.
759
760
      Compute MSE for formulas with correct skeletons. For simulated datasets, we choose MSE
761   restricted to correctly recovered skeletons because the baseline methods often exhibit large MSE
762   when recovering incorrect skeletons. Filtering out these formulas allows the baselines to achieve
763   comparable performance. For real datasets, since the true dynamics skeleton is unknown, we directly
764   compare the MSE of the trajectories without filtering by the skeletons.

      C    D ISCUSSIONS
959
960   Motivation of neural dynamics design The rationale for decoupling node and edge dynamics
961   is motivated by Xu et al. (2019) which study the reasoning ability of DNN. This paper provides a
962   mathematical framework explaining why certain architectures may be more suitable for particular
963   reasoning tasks. The primary theorem (Theorem 3.6 of Xu et al. (2019)) asserts that improved
964   algorithmic alignment (Definition 3.3 of Xu et al. (2019), structural correspondence between functions
965   of a reasoning task and different modules in DNN) leads to enhanced generalization.
966   PI-NDSR learns neural dynamics with strong generalization ability. Based on algorithmic alignment,
967   (1) provides a strong inductive bias for the neural dynamics design. The designed model (2) is
968   algorithmically aligned with (1), allowing our model to demonstrate strong generalization. With the
969   strong generalization, the decoupling of node and edge dynamics can provide a better reference for
970   coordinated genetic search algorithm to recover symbolic expressions of complex network dynamics.
971   For example, the node dynamics can be seen as the dynamics on a complex network with a single
      node.

       Impact statement The proposed method can be applied to various real-world scenarios, such
973    as epidemic forecasting, brain dynamics, and single-cell RNA forecasting, helping understand the
974    underlying mechanisms of various complex systems.
975
976    Limitations Our method has several limitations:
977
978    • PI-NDSR cannot successfully recover symbolic expressions when the formulations are highly
979      complex or the dimension of node states is high. The highly complex formulations indicate a large
980      search space for the genetic algorithm. Therefore, there should be a large population size and a
981      large number of generations for the genetic algorithm to find the symbolic expressions. Since the
982
         fitness of our method is calculated based on the pairwise combination of node and edge dynamics,
         the fitness evaluation is computationally expensive and memory-consuming.
983
984    • PI-NDSR cannot deal with the complex network dynamics when some variables are missing in
985      the observations. In some complex systems, it is difficult to observe all variables at the same time.
986
         In this case, the prediction accuracy of neural dynamics (3) may not be high enough to provide
         high-quality supervision data for symbolic regression.
987
988
       Future works In the future, we plan to extend our method to handle more complicated network
989
       dynamics with impressive formula manipulation capability of LLM, which includes dynamics
990
       involving time delays, multiple variables, and missing variables. Additionally, we are interested in
991    exploring the application of our method in various real-world scenarios like brain dynamics and
992    single-cell RNA forecasting.
