009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Large language models (LLMs) with one or more fine-tuning phases have become
012            necessary to unlock various capabilities, enabling LLMs to follow natural language
013            instructions and align with human preferences. However, it carries the risk of
014            catastrophic forgetting during sequential training, the parametric knowledge or the
015            ability learned in previous stages may be overwhelmed by incoming training data.
016            This paper finds that LLMs can restore some of the original knowledge by regularly
017            resetting partial parameters. Inspired by this, we introduce Half Fine-Tuning (HFT)
018
               for LLMs, as a substitute for full fine-tuning (FFT), to mitigate the forgetting
               issues, where half of the parameters are selected to learn new tasks. In contrast,
019
               the other half are frozen to retain previous knowledge. We provide a feasibility
020
               analysis from the perspective of optimization and interpret the parameter selection
021            operation as a regularization term. Without changing the model architecture, HFT
022            could be seamlessly integrated into existing fine-tuning frameworks. Extensive
023            experiments and analysis on supervised fine-tuning, direct preference optimization,
024            and continual learning consistently demonstrate the effectiveness, robustness, and
025            efficiency of HFT. Compared with FFT, HFT not only significantly alleviates the
026            forgetting problem, but also achieves the best performance in a series of downstream
027            benchmarks, with an approximately 30% reduction in training time.

030   1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Large language models (LLMs) bring immense revolutions to various natural language processing
033
      applications with powerful language understanding and generation capabilities. Unsupervised large-
      scale pre-training for learning basic world knowledge (hereinafter referred to as basic knowledge),
034
      followed by one or more fine-tuning phases with supervised data or human feedback, is becoming
035
      a new training paradigm in the era of LLMs (Ouyang et al., 2022; Achiam et al., 2023; Touvron
036   et al., 2023). As the fine-tuning phase proceeds, the enormous potential of LLMs is gradually
037   unleashed to handle various downstream tasks, while the parametric knowledge previously learned
038   and stored in the pre-trained model might face a considerable risk of catastrophic forgetting (Lin
039   et al., 2024; Neeman et al., 2023; Dong et al., 2024). To maintain intrinsic basic knowledge, the
040   most straightforward idea is to keep the pre-trained parameters unchanged and include extra modules
041   to learn task-specific abilities (Dou et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2024a). However, such architectural
042   modifications pose significant obstacles to model deployment and continual fine-tuning.
043   Without changing model architecture, vanilla full fine-tuning (FFT) methods update all parameters
044   to improve the performance of downstream tasks (Zhang et al., 2023c), in which the element-wise
045   parameter difference between fine-tuned and pre-trained models (i.e., task vector) represents the
046   knowledge shift during fine-tuning (Ilharco et al., 2023). Herein, a desirable task vector is expected
047   to keep basic knowledge of the pre-trained model and learn new specialized knowledge. Interestingly,
048   recent work shows that partial dropping or trimming of the task vector has only milder impacts on
049   target task (Yadav et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2023). In other words, partial new parameters are sufficient
050
      for the learning of new abilities, so the upcoming question is, is it possible that a portion of old
      parameters could maintain the capabilities of the pre-trained model?
051

053   partial parameters of the chat-model to the pre-trained model, then prob the general abilities and basic
      knowledge of these models (see Figure 1). As a representative general-purpose fine-tuning practice,

078   Inspired by these above observations, we propose Half Fine-Tuning (HFT), a simple yet effective
079   approach for the training of LLMs and further extrapolate it to the continual fine-tuning scenarios.
080   Specifically, in each round of fine-tuning, we randomly select and freeze half of the parameters, and
081   only update the other half, which allows the model to retain the ability of the startup point while
082   learning downstream tasks. Note that HFT does not change the model architecture or traditional
083   fine-tuning paradigm, thus theoretically it can be applied to any setting where the standard full
084
      fine-tuning is previously applicable, including but not limited to supervised fine-tuning (SFT), direct
      preference optimization (DPO), continual learning (CL), etc.
085
086   To evaluate the effectiveness of HFT in instruction fine-tuning settings, we conduct extensive experi-
087   ments with T ÜLU V2 (Ivison et al., 2023) for SFT and UltraFeedback (Cui et al., 2023) for DPO.
088   Simultaneously, we also extend experiments on TRACE (Wang et al., 2023a) for CL (i.e. multi-round
089   fine-tuning) to validate the proposed method in a more extreme scenario. Experimental results
090
      demonstrate that HFT not only exhibits excellent talent in alleviating catastrophic forgetting but also
      achieves comparable or even better performance in learning new abilities compared to FFT. Further
091
      analysis reveals that regardless of which half (or even only about half) of the parameters are selected,
092
      HFT is capable of attaining tolerable performance gains and impressive efficiency improvements,
093   which brings considerable competition to the routine fine-tuning paradigm.
094
095   In summary, the main contributions of this paper are as follows:
096
097
                            • We reveal that by resetting half of the fine-tuned parameters to the startup state, it is possible
                              to preliminary restore the primaeval ability while maintaining new learning ability, which
098
                              poses new opportunities to alleviate catastrophic forgetting and obtain an all-around LLM.
099
100                         • We propose Half Fine-Tuning (HFT), which entails freezing half of the parameters while
101                           training the other half. It allows LLMs to acquire new abilities while retaining and utilizing
102                           previously learned knowledge in various training settings.
103                         • Extensive experiments and analysis demonstrate the effectiveness and efficiency of HFT.
104                           Without any alterations to the model architecture, HFT, as a plug-and-play solution with
105                           only a few lines of code, exhibits the potential to supersede FFT in the era of LLMs.

      Considering that the partial task vector is capable of maintaining new abilities (Yadav et al., 2023; Yu
111   et al., 2023), we attempt to roll back the primaeval abilities of pre-trained models by resetting the
112   remaining part of the task vector, thereby alleviating the catastrophic forgetting problem caused by
113   fine-tuning. In this section, We employ the representative well-aligned LLM, L LAMA 2- CHAT-7 B,
114   and the corresponding pre-trained backbone, L LAMA 2-7 B, as models for analysis.
115
      Setup. To balance the original abilities and the enhanced capabilities gained through instruction
116
      tuning, we simply choose to reset 50% of the parameters in L LAMA 2- CHAT-7 B to L LAMA 2-7 B,
117   so that half of the parameters are hoped to align with the new tasks, while the other half is intended
118   to restore the old capabilities. In the implementation, we randomly select half of each transformer
119   layer according to the category of the parameter matrix. Specifically, we choose two from four
120   self-attention matrices (i.e., WQ , WK , WV , WO ), and for the odd parameter number in L LAMA’s
121   feed-forward layers (i.e., Wup , Wdown , Wgate ), we randomly select half of the transformer layers to
122   choose two matrices and the other half to choose one. Such a fine-grained selection strategy ensures
123   that the Half-Reset operation rolls back exactly 50% of the parameters.
124   To assess the performance of the pre-trained, chat, and half-reset models on both new and old capabil-
125   ities, we follow Ivison et al. (2023) and Dou et al. (2023) to introduce two categories of evaluation
126   benchmarks: (1) General Abilities, including MMLU, GSM8K, BBH, TyDiQA, TruthfulQA, and
127   HumanEval, which measure the LLMs’ newly enhanced abilities to perform specific downstream
128   tasks like examination, reasoning, and coding. (2) Basic Knowledge, including NaturalQuestion,
129   TriviaQA, and HotpotQA, which reflect the parametric world knowledge in the pre-trained model and
130   could be used to evaluate retention of the primaeval capabilities. For more details about the datasets
131
      and evaluation metrics, please refer to Appendix A.2.1 and A.2.2
132   Results. From Figure 1, it is intuitive to observe significant improvement of L LAMA 2- CHAT-7 B on
133   several general ability benchmarks, as well as the comprehensive decline on the basic knowledge
134   benchmarks. When selectively restoring half parameters to the pre-trained L LAMA 2-7 B model,
135   although there is a slight performance loss in the overall performance of general abilities, we witness
136   the remarkable recovery of basic knowledge. In Appendix A.3.1, we attempt other possible half-reset
137
      solutions and provide more numerical results, all of which exhibit similar phenomena.
138   In conclusion, the pilot experiments demonstrate that (1) full parameter fine-tuning with large-scale
139   instruction data disrupts the basic knowledge stored within pre-trained LLMs. (2) Through a simple
140   half-reset operation, it is possible to restore the forgotten knowledge partially. Take another step
141   forward, these findings open a new door for model merging, inspiring us to preserve some mastered
142   abilities of the startup point by freezing partial parameters during fine-tuning.

189   Why Half Fine-Tuning Works. Excluding heuristic motivations, we are also interested in the
190   theoretical principles behind HFT. Theoretically, HFT could be regarded as exerting a parameter-level
191   mask to vanilla FFT. In this part, we borrow the thread in Fu et al. (2022) to interpret why HFT
192   works from the perspective of optimization. Given a pre-trained model M0 with parameters θ0 , the
193
      fine-tuned model M with parameters θ has the same structure as M0 such that ∥θ −θ0 ∥0 ≤ p dim(θ),
      where p = 0.5 in HFT. Next, we denote M ∈ {0, 1}m×m as a mask diagonal matrix on the parameter,
194
      in which the diagonal is equal to 1 if the parameter is selected, thus the fine-tuning procedure can be
195
      formulated as θ = θ0 + M ∆θ, where ∆θ is the task vector. In that case, HFT solves an optimization
196   problem with constraints min∆θ,M L(θ0 + M ∆θ) such that ∥M ∥0 = ⌊mp⌋; Mij = 0, ∀i ̸= j;
197   Mii ∈ {0, 1}. where L is the loss function, ⌊·⌋ is the floor function, m is the parameter numbers. By
198   integrating previous conditions, the optimization procedure of HFT can be reformulated as
199
                                       O = min L(θ) s.t. ∥(I − M )(θ − θ0 )∥2 = 0,                                                                                  (3)
200                                                 θ

201   With Lagrangian duality, solving the constrained optimization problem is equivalent to solving the
202   following unconstrained optimization problem
203
                                       OL = min max L(θ) + λ∥(I − M )(θ − θ0 )∥2 ,                                                                                  (4)
204                                                       θ         λ

205   where λ is the Lagrange multiplier. Based on the Minimax inequality, it is intuitive to derive that
206   minθ maxλ L(θ)+λ∥(I −M )(θ−θ0 )∥2 ≥ maxλ minθ L(θ)+λ∥(I −M )(θ−θ0 )∥2 ≥ minθ L(θ)+
207   ∥(I − M )(θ − θ0 )∥2 . In conclusion, the optimization process of HFT is equivalent to optimizing the
208   upper bound of the FFT loss function L(θ) with a regularization term ∥(I − M )(θ − θ0 )∥2 . From
209   the optimization perspective, such regularization (with an appropriate sparsity M ) contributes to the
210
      stability of the sparse fine-tuned model (Radiya-Dixit & Wang, 2020; Fu et al., 2022), meaning that
      HFT has the opportunity to achieve results comparable to or even better than FFT, theoretically.

254   Results on Improving General Abilities. Results in Table 1 demonstrate the effectiveness of our
255
      proposed HFT method, which simultaneously improves different specialized abilities by selectively
      fine-tuning half of the parameters. Specifically, compared to FFT under the SFT setting, HFT leads
256
      to an overall performance improvement of 1.9% on L LAMA 2-7 B and 2.9% when scaling to L LAMA
257
      2-13 B. Furthermore, as we continue to perform DPO on SFT models, we observe that updating the
258   policy model with HFT does not hinder the model from learning human preferences. In sum, the
259   HFT method has strong robustness to adapt to different fine-tuning algorithms. Besides, we also
260   attempt to review the Half-Reset method in Section 2, but the benefits of this approach are not robust,
261   and we attribute it to the randomness of parameter operations. In comparison, HFT achieves a more
262   stable performance improvement through the learning process, while avoiding the complexity of the
263   two-stage process of fully updating followed by partially resetting.
264   Results on Preserving Basic Knowledge. When it comes to basic knowledge, as depicted in
265   Table 2, both SFT and DPO exhibit a significant decline across all three benchmarks. Notably, HFT
266   demonstrates excellent talent in preserving basic knowledge, consistently outperforming fully updating

268   of 3.4% and 2.9% with L LAMA 2-7 B and L LAMA 2-13 B compared to FFT, respectively. It is worth
269   mentioning that Half-Reset also shows a stable performance in alleviating knowledge forgetting,

442   6    R ELATED W ORK
443
444   Sparse Fine-Tuning. With the continuous increase in the number of language model parameters,
445   sparse fine-tuning (a.k.a. parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT)) offers an effective solution by
446   reducing trainable parameters while achieving comparable performance to FFT (Fu et al., 2022; Ding
447   et al., 2023; Han et al., 2024). Adapter (Houlsby et al., 2019; Mahabadi et al., 2021; Zhang et al.,
448   2023a) and LoRA (Hu et al., 2022; Dou et al., 2023; Dettmers et al., 2023), the two most famous
449   kinds of work, freeze the initial model weight and inject an adapter or a trainable rank decomposition
450
      matrices into each layer. However, these approaches change the model architecture and therefore
      require customized deployment. Keeping the architecture unchanged, DiffPruning (Guo et al., 2021)
451
      learns a sparse diff vector for each task, enabling PEFT to scale well with new tasks. BitFit (Zaken
452
      et al., 2021) only fine-tunes the bias terms of BERT and achieves considerably good performance.
453   Unfortunately, these methods designed for specific tasks or networks (e.g., bias) are unsuitable for
454   modern general-purpose large-scale models. From the perspective of low GPU memory overhead,
455   BAdam (Luo et al., 2024) randomly divides the entire parameter into multiple blocks and updates
456   each block sequentially, LISA (Pan et al., 2024) changes the granularity of blocks at the layer level.
457   Besides, Mixout (Lee et al., 2020) resets a portion of neurons to a pre-trained state in each training
458   step. In this way, all parameters in BAdam, LISA, and Mixout are updated, which is different from
459   HFT and not conducive to continual learning.
460   Continual Learning. Continual learning aims to develop learning algorithms that can accumulate
461   knowledge on non-stationary data, and vanilla FFT has been proven to lead to severe catastrophic
462   forgetting issues when adapting to incoming streaming tasks (Luo et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024).
463   To address this issue, experience replay (Rolnick et al., 2019; Peng et al., 2024) is a widely used
464   technique that incorporates a portion of data from previous rounds into the current training process.
465   Regularization-based models (Kirkpatrick et al., 2017; Lopez-Paz & Ranzato, 2017) introduce
466   additional terms in the loss function to penalize changes in crucial weights. Parameter-allocation
467
      approaches (Li et al., 2019; Gurbuz & Dovrolis, 2022) feature an isolated parameter subspace
      dedicated to each task throughout the network. When LLMs enter the era of billions of parameters,
468
      researchers prefer to use progressive prompts (Razdaibiedina et al., 2023) or PEFT (Dou et al., 2023;
469
      Wu et al., 2024a) to tune a powerful general backbone for specific tasks or domains (Wu et al.,
470   2024b). Instead of introducing auxiliary modules or losses, HFT explores a new direction based on
471   the characteristics of LLMs, proving that random parameter selection is sufficient to achieve passable
472   performance and has the potential to become a successor to FFT.
473

      7    C ONCLUSION
476
477
      In this paper, we observe that rolling back half of the fine-tuned parameters to the pre-trained state
      may recover partial knowledge of the startup model while holding the performance of downstream
478
      tasks. Taking inspiration from this observation, we propose Half Fine-tuning (HFT), which adopts a
479
      category-level strategy to select half of the parameters for updating in each training round, and the
480   remaining parameters are expected to maintain the learned knowledge. Extensive experiments on
481   supervised fine-tuning, direct preference optimization, and continual learning scenarios demonstrate
482   the effectiveness of HFT. It not only alleviates the catastrophic forgetting in preceding capabilities
483   but also achieves comparable or even superior performance than FFT in downstream tasks. Further
484   analysis shows that HFT is robust to selection strategies and selected parameter numbers. Last but not
485   least, HFT does not change the model architecture, making it easy to implement and scale, especially
      under successive fine-tuning scenarios.

      A.1     B ENEFITS AND L IMITATIONS
705
706   Half Fine-Tuning (HFT) achieves a balanced performance in general abilities and basic knowledge
707   benchmarks. It outperforms the Full Fine-Tuning (FFT) strategy while saving approximately 30% of
708   training time, and is scalable for scenarios with continual fine-tuning. In contrast, the widely used
709   Sparse Fine-Tuning methods such as LoRA fall short of HFT in overall performance, and in more
710   challenging scenarios like continual fine-tuning, these methods fail and lead to performance collapses.
711   We believe that HFT has the potential to become a successor to FFT in nearly all scenarios due to its
712
      superior performance and faster training speed. Nonetheless, there are still some limitations to this
      paper. Firstly, due to computational resource constraints, we experiment with the most representative
713
      open-source models L LAMA 2-7 B and L LAMA 2-13 B, without scaling to larger or other family
714
      models. Secondly, we validate HFT on the standard dense transformer architecture, while other
715   architectures such as Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) are not discussed in this paper. We believe that
716   HFT is sufficient to adapt to other architectures and models, which warrants further research and
717   exploration. In the future, we will strive to explore the potential of HFT in a wider range and diverse
718   architecture models, while also refining selection methods to further improve performance.
