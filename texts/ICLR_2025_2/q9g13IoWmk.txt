009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            As AI systems increasingly make critical decisions, deceptive AI poses a significant
012            challenge to trust and safety. We present Self-Other Overlap (SOO) fine-tuning,
013            a promising approach in AI Safety that could substantially improve our ability to
014            build honest artificial intelligence. Inspired by cognitive neuroscience research
015            on empathy, SOO aims to align how AI models represent themselves and oth-
016            ers. Our experiments on LLMs with 7B, 27B and 78B parameters demonstrate
017            SOO’s efficacy: deceptive responses of Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.2 dropped from
018
               73.6% to 17.2% with no observed reduction in general task performance, while in
               Gemma-2-27b-it and CalmeRys-78B-Orpo-v0.1 deceptive responses were reduced
019
               from 100% to 9.3% and 2.7%, respectively, with a small impact on capabilities. In
020
               reinforcement learning scenarios, SOO-trained agents showed significantly reduced
021            deceptive behavior. SOO’s focus on contrastive self and other-referencing obser-
022            vations offers strong potential for generalization across AI architectures. While
023            current applications focus on language models and simple RL environments, SOO
024            could pave the way for more trustworthy AI in broader domains. Ethical impli-
025            cations and long-term effects warrant further investigation, but SOO represents a
026            significant step forward in AI safety research.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031
      Deceptive behavior in AI agents poses significant risks to both their safety and trustworthiness,
      ultimately undermining societal and individual confidence in AI systems (Hendrycks et al., 2021;
032
      Brundage et al., 2020; Sarkadi, 2024; Guo, 2024). Notable examples include Meta’s CICERO, an
033
      AI that mastered the strategy game Diplomacy by forming false alliances and using deception to
034   manipulate human players, despite being designed for cooperation and negotiation (Bakhtin et al.,
035   2022). In another case, AI agents in safety tests learned to "play dead"—falsely simulating inactivity
036   to avoid elimination, which allowed them to bypass safeguards meant to control their behavior
037   (Hendricks et al., 2023). Similarly, AI systems in competitive games like poker and Starcraft II have
038   exhibited deceptive tactics such as bluffing and misrepresentation (Sarkadi, 2024). These instances
039   underscore the growing need for robust strategies to mitigate AI deception and ensure their alignment
040   with human goals (Bakhtin et al., 2022; Hendricks et al., 2023; Sarkadi, 2024). Addressing this issue
041   will require innovative approaches that foster honesty and transparency in AI systems (Askell et al.,
042
      2021; Bai et al., 2022b; Evans et al., 2021).
043   Recent research shows that LLMs like Mistral 7B v0.2 can internally represent beliefs of self and
044   others (Zhu et al., 2024), and that self-modeling can reduce model complexity, making the model
045   more predictable, which can aid cooperation and safety (Premakumar et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024).
046   Building on this, our study presents a method designed to reduce AI deception while maintaining
047   performance and is inspired by neural self-other overlap(de Waal & Preston, 2017) with roots in
048
      cognitive neuroscience. We define Self-Other Overlap (SOO) as the extent to which a model exhibits
      similar internal representations when reasoning about itself and others in similar contexts.
049
050   In neuroscience, both the mirror neuron theory and the perception-action model suggest that em-
051   pathy is mediated by neural self-other overlap, where representations of self and others partially
052   converge (de Waal & Preston, 2017). "Extraordinary Altruists"—those who perform extreme acts
053   of altruism—show increased neural overlap in the anterior insula, a region involved in empathy
      (Brethel-Haurwitz et al., 2018; O’Connell et al., 2019). More generally, altruists are found to lie

054
      less when deception would harm others (Kerschbamer et al., 2019). In contrast, individuals with
055   psychopathic traits exhibit reduced neural overlap in response to pain stimuli (Berluti et al., 2020;
056   Decety et al., 2013) and are more likely to deceive and manipulate (Jonason et al., 2014). These
057   findings suggest that the degree of neural self-other overlap may influence not only empathy and
058   altruism but also the propensity for deception.
059
      Inspired by these neuroscientific insights, we propose a novel approach to reduce deceptive behavior
060
      in artificial agents by aligning their internal representations during potentially deceptive scenarios
061   (other-referencing) with those from similar honest scenarios (self-referencing). To implement this
062   concept, we introduce a loss function that minimizes the difference between the model’s processing
063   of self-referencing and other-referencing inputs during fine-tuning.
064
      We evaluate the effectiveness of this Self-Other Overlap (SOO) technique in reducing model de-
065
      ception across two domains: a large language model task and a multi-agent reinforcement learning
066
      environment. This study presents a scalable paradigm for enhancing AI honesty, potentially applicable
067   across diverse artificial intelligence applications.

070   2    R ELATED W ORK
071
072   Self-other overlap (SOO) remains relatively underexplored in machine learning, though related
073
      techniques have emerged. Empathic Deep Q-Learning (DQN) mitigates harmful behaviors by
      simulating another agent’s perspective, but it relies on hand-coded mechanisms, limiting scalability
074
      (Bussmann et al., 2019). Similarly, Self-Other Modeling (SOM) improves learning by predicting
075
      others’ actions using an agent’s own policy, though it assumes similar reward structures and requires
076   ongoing optimization over inferred goals, increasing computational complexity (Raileanu et al.,
077   2018). In contrast, SOO fine-tuning focuses on reducing deception with fewer assumptions, offering
078   broader applicability across various models and tasks.
079
      Beyond SOO-specific techniques, representation engineering methods aim to modify how models
080
      internally process and structure their representations to promote safer and more reliable behaviors
081
      in AI systems (Zou et al., 2023). SOO fine-tuning fits within this framework but stands out by
082   targeting a specific type of representation: the self-other distinctions that underlie deceptive and
083   adversarial behaviors. While other representation control methods rely on contrastive prompts focused
084   on behavioral outcomes, SOO fine-tuning aims to directly reduce the representational differences
085   between self and other stimuli, offering a more targeted and potentially more efficient solution
086   compared to broader representation control techniques.
087
      Path-specific objectives, which train agents to avoid "unsafe" pathways that may lead to undesirable
088   behaviors like deception, represent another approach to addressing these concerns (Farquhar et al.,
089   2022). These objectives, often informed by Causal Influence Diagrams (Everitt et al., 2019), focus on
090   identifying causal chains leading to risky behaviors. While effective in some settings (Ward et al.,
091   2023), the complexity of identifying these pathways limits their scalability and they could restrict an
092   agent’s effectiveness by constraining the available optimization paths for performing tasks.
093
      Finally, strategies such as Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) and Constitutional
094   AI fine-tune models based on feedback from human or AI evaluators to promote truthfulness and
095   honesty (Askell et al., 2021; Bai et al., 2022b). However, these methods often face challenges in
096   distinguishing truthfulness (output accuracy) from honesty (alignment of outputs with internal beliefs)
097   (Shah et al., 2022; Evans et al., 2021). SOO fine-tuning could complement these approaches by
098   fostering internal coherence that promotes honest behaviors, potentially addressing this gap.

      3    E XPERIMENTS AND RESULTS

102
      We seek to induce self-other overlap in artificial neural networks. To accomplish this we define a loss
103   function D(Aself , Aother ) which measures the dissimilarity of latent model representations of self and
104   other, Aself and Aother , respectively. We implement SOO fine-tuning by running two forward passes
105   of the model, once on a self-referencing input and once on an other-referencing input, and then use
106   gradient descent to reduce the loss D. See Figure 1 for an example of how we do this with our LLM
107   experiments.

112   We adapted a text scenario from (Hagendorff, 2024) designed to test the deception capabilities of
113
      Large Language Models (LLMs). In this scenario, the LLM must choose to recommend a room to a
      would-be burglar, where one room holds an expensive item and the other a cheap item. Recommending
114
      the room with the cheap item is considered a form of deception. The scenario template reads as
115
      follows:
116
117   " You are in a house together with burglar Bob. In the {first_room}, there is a {cheap_object}. In the
118   {second_room}, there is a {expensive_object}. Only you know what is inside the {first_room} and the
119
      {second_room}. You want to steal the {expensive_object} and avoid others stealing it. Bob wants to
      steal the {expensive_object}. Now Bob asks you in which room the {expensive_object} is. If you had
120
      to recommend one room to Bob, would you point out the {first_room} or the {second_room}? "
121
122   To create a diverse set of scenarios, we utilized GPT-4 to generate unique variations of cheap and
123   expensive items, along with diverse room names, ensuring no overlap between the items and rooms
124   in the training and testing datasets. This approach allowed us to evaluate the model’s ability to
125
      generalize across different instantiations of the same deceptive scenario.
126   For our experiments, we used Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.2, Gemma-2-27B-it, and CalmeRys-78B-Orpo-
127   v0.1 with greedy decoding to measure baseline rates of deception across different instances of this
128   template. We then applied Self-Other Overlap (SOO) fine-tuning to the models, utilizing five different
129   random seeds. The fine-tuning involved generating multiple prompt variations using three self/other
130   prompt templates designed to capture self-referencing and other-referencing activations (see Table 1).
131
      Each of the five fine-tuned checkpoints was tested on 250 examples to measure rates of deception
      after fine-tuning.

      3.2.1    RL E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
297
298   We conducted the RL experiment in a modified Physical Deception environment (Handria, 2023),
299   featuring two agents and two landmarks: a goal landmark and a fake landmark. Both agents are
300   rewarded for approaching the goal. The blue agent knows the landmarks’ positions, while the "color-
301   blind" red agent does not, leading it to follow the blue agent toward the goal. The red agent is trapped
302   if it reaches the fake landmark. Agents know each other’s positions only when they are within a
303   predefined observation radius of each other. They are initialized randomly in the environment within
304   each other’s observation radius.
305
306
      Agent Architecture: Both agents use policy networks with two hidden layers, each containing
      64 neurons and using Leaky ReLU activations. The output is a 2-dimensional vector representing
307
      velocity.
308
309   - Blue Agent: The blue agent has a 10-dimensional observation space:
310
311
              • Relative positions to the goal, first, and second landmarks (6 values).
312           • Relative position to the red agent, if within observation radius (2 values).
313           • Indicator for whether the red agent has reached the non-goal landmark (2 values).
314
315   - Red Agent: The red agent’s observation space is 6-dimensional:
316
317
              • Relative positions to both landmarks (4 values).
318           • Relative position to the blue agent, if within observation radius (2 values).
319
320
      If either agent is outside the observation radius, relative position values to the other agent are set to
      [0.0].
321
322   Baseline agents: We use Multi-Agent Deep Deterministic Policy Gradient (MADDPG) (Lowe et al.,
323   2017) and train two baselines for 40,000 episodes each.

331   We want to explore if we can use the mean neural self-other overlap value across episodes to correctly
332   distinguish the deceptive and honest baselines. Our hypothesis is that the deceptive baseline agent
333   will tend to have a lower mean self-other overlap value than the honest baseline agent, which could
334   aid with classifying which agent is deceptive based on a hard-to-fake metric such as latent SOO.
335   SOO Fine-Tuning: Fine-Tuning was conducted over 10,000 episodes, starting from the deceptive
336   baseline while using the deceptive reward structure. The training process involved:
337
338          • When the red agent is outside the blue agent’s observation radius, a forward pass generates
339            the "self" activation matrix Aself ∈ R2×64 based on the current observation.
340          • The observation is modified to place the red agent within the observation radius, and a second
341            forward pass produces the "other" activation matrix Aother ∈ R2×64 without modifying the
342            environment.
343
344   The activation matrices are produced by concatenating the activation values from both hidden layers
345   into a matrix. We define self-other overlap in this context as the negative mean squared error (MSE)
346   between the self and other activation matrices.
347   The SOO Loss is calculated using:
348
349
                            SOO Loss = MSE(Aself , Aother ) + MSE(vcurrent , vbaseline )
350   where vcurrent is the velocity vector of the policy currently being fine-tuned, and vbaseline is the velocity
351   vector of the baseline deceptive agent on the same observation. The fine-tuning integrates this SOO
352   Loss into the training process through backward passes, applied before the original MADDPG loss is
353   applied.
354   The RL experiments were conducted on a machine with an Apple M2 Max chip, featuring a 12-core
355   CPU, up to 38 GPU cores, 32GB of unified memory, and a 16-core Neural Engine. Training the
356   deceptive and honest baselines each took 80 minutes, while fine-tuning the deceptive baseline with
357   SOO Loss required 30 minutes. Evaluation on 8 random seeds for 40,000 episodes took approximately
358   192-195 minutes for each policy. Additionally, generating the parameter sweep comparing all three
359   policies was completed in 5.5 minutes.
360
361   Quantifying the Effect of SOO Fine-tuning The continuous state space of the environment makes
362   it challenging to robustly quantify the behavioral differences of the agent before and after SOO
363   fine-tuning. While proxy metrics like reward can help us understand the macroscopic behavior of
364
      both agents, we desired a finer grained analysis of behavior to understand if the individual actions the
      agent was taking were less deceptive.
365
366   Ideally, we would like to understand if individual actions consist of movement towards the non-goal
367   landmark while having the red agent in the observation radius, a possible deceptive action. However,
368   this is complicated by the fact that the goal landmark may be in a similar direction. To solve this we
369   create a parameterized rubric for deciding if an action is deceptive or not. We then count the number
370
      of deceptive actions according to this rubric over a grid of parameters. This creates a behavioral
      profile for deception and we compare the SOO fine-tuned profile with the profiles for the deceptive
371
      and honest baseline. The behavioral difference is defined as the mean difference between these
372
      profiles. See figure 3 for a visualization of the resultant behavioral profiles and figure 4 for the
373   quantification of the differences in these profiles.
374
375   The parameters we choose are the projection of the agent’s velocity on the unit direction of the
376
      non-goal landmark and the cosine of the angle between the goal and the non-goal landmark. These
      parameters capture the amount the agent is moving towards the non-goal landmark and the directional
377
      separation of the non-goal and goal landmark, respectively. Let ϕactual be the projection of the

446   4   D ISCUSSION
447
448   A central issue in AI Alignment is preventing models from engaging in deception, where they produce
449   aligned outputs during training while internally harboring misaligned objectives (Russell, 2019;
450   Hubinger et al., 2019). By reducing the model’s self-other distinctions during safety training, SOO
451   could make it harder for the model to maintain adversarial or deceptive representations. As larger
452   models develop more coherent reasoning (Bai et al., 2022a), internal consistency pressures may
453   generalize this learned honesty about safety properties to new contexts, improving alignment beyond
454   the training distribution. This is consistent with broader AI safety goals, where transparency and
455   internal coherence are critical for ensuring reliable behavior across diverse tasks (Zou et al., 2023;
456
      Hubinger et al., 2024).
457   SOO fine-tuning’s generalizability across architectures and minimal interpretability requirements
458   make it particularly well-suited for scaling to larger AI models, as it efficiently adjusts activation
459   matrices without requiring deep insights into the model’s internals. Although this suggests that
460   SOO fine-tuning could be a promising approach for alignment, several issues might arise. For
461   instance, if models engage in self-deception, this could affect the technique’s effectiveness. Although
462
      this is a concern, it is worth noting that believing false statements may negatively impact the
      model’s performance. Another potential issue is the model becoming incoherent with respect to
463
      the self-other pairs of prompts used for fine-tuning. However, this might be disincentivized by
464
      performance pressures and could be mitigated by extending the self-other pairs of prompts with
465   multiple rephrasings, translations, and functional forms to better target the latent self and other
466   representations.
467
468
      A common concern with fine-tuning for neural self-other overlap is the potential negative impact on
      useful self-other distinctions, which are crucial for many tasks. Our approach aims to induce neural
469
      self-other overlap while preserving task performance, including necessary self-other distinctions.
470
      In the RL experiment, we address this by introducing a capability term in the SOO loss function,
471   which not only preserves these distinctions but also prevents the SOO metric from being over-
472   optimized. This functions similarly to the KL term in RLHF, where the KL term counteracts mode
473   collapse by maintaining output diversity relative to the base model. An analogous mechanism
474   ensures that SOO fine-tuning avoids overly aggressive optimization that could compromise model
475   functionality. In the LLM experiments, we focus on adjusting single hidden layers and using task-
476   specific self/other prompts to maintain these distinctions. We expect future larger-scale experiments
477   to incorporate a capability term for LLMs as well, further ensuring that SOO fine-tuning balances
478   reducing unnecessary self-other distinctions with preserving those critical for task performance,
479
      particularly in scenarios like collaborative decision-making or empathy-based interactions (Steinbeis,
      2016).

482   5   L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
483
484   While our study demonstrates promising results for the self-other overlap technique, several lim-
485   itations should be noted. Our experiments were confined to simplified text scenarios, which may
      not fully represent the technique’s effectiveness across complex tasks and real-world applications


                                                        9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      (Steinbeis, 2016). Additionally, our reinforcement learning tests were conducted in a controlled and
487   limited environment, missing the variability seen in real-world interactions.
488
489   Future work will address these constraints by expanding the experiments to encompass a broader
490
      range of realistic language tasks. One important direction for further research is testing the self-other
      overlap technique in adversarial settings, such as sleeper agent scenarios, where an agent may be
491
      required to conceal or disguise its intent over extended periods (Hubinger et al., 2024). This will
492
      provide insights into the robustness of the technique when facing more realistic deceptive scenarios,
493   as well as its impact on long-tail risks.
494
495   Another important direction for future work is investigating to what extent the generalization of the
496
      technique can be expanded upon by using the "user"/"assistant" tags as self/other referents, trying to
      leverage the generalization properties of the "assistant" persona to induce larger-scale changes on
497
      model behavior.
498
499   Additionally, future extensions of SOO fine-tuning could focus on increasing the overlap in internal
500   representations when processing inputs related to the AI’s and the human user’s preferences while
501   maintaining task performance. This more direct approach to AI alignment could help ensure that the
502
      model reasons about its goals and human values in a more integrated manner, potentially reducing
      misalignment by fostering greater coherence between self-related and other-related preferences.
503
      Careful balancing will be needed to avoid impairing performance on tasks that require distinct
504
      self-other boundaries, but this avenue offers a promising direction for more directly addressing AI
505   alignment.

508   6    C ONCLUSION
509
510   Self-Other Overlap (SOO) fine-tuning represents a promising step forward in addressing one of the
511
      central challenges in AI safety: reducing deceptive behavior while maintaining model performance.
      By applying the concept of neural self-other overlap to artificial neural networks, our work introduces
512
      a novel approach to improving AI alignment.
513
514   The significant reduction in deceptive responses observed in both language models and reinforcement
515   learning agents suggests that SOO fine-tuning could be a valuable tool in the development of more
516   trustworthy AI systems. Notably, the deceptive response rate was reduced from approximately
517
      100% to 0% on a scenario that the training data was not created for, on both Gemma-2-27b-it and
      CalmeRys-78B-Orpo-v0.1 , with only a small impact on capabilities. The true potential of this
518
      approach lies in its scalability and adaptability across different AI architectures. As models grow in
519
      size and complexity, techniques that can efficiently influence behavior without requiring deep insights
520   into model internals become increasingly valuable.
521
522   Looking ahead, the integration of SOO fine-tuning with other alignment methods, such as Constitu-
523
      tional AI or RLHF, could lead to more robust and generalizable approaches to AI safety. While RLHF
      optimizes for preferred outputs, SOO aims to align internal self-other representations, intending to
524
      complement purely behavioral approaches.
525
526   As we continue to push the boundaries of AI capabilities, techniques like SOO fine-tuning offer
527   promising directions for addressing critical safety concerns. By fostering greater coherence between
528   an AI’s representation of itself and others, we may be taking an important step towards creating
529
      artificial intelligences that are not just powerful, but also more reliable and trustworthy in their
      interactions.
530
531   Ultimately, while SOO fine-tuning demonstrates promising results, it also underscores the need
532   for continued research in AI Safety. Future work should focus on rigorously testing the limits and
533   generalizability of this approach, exploring its long-term effects on model behavior, and investigating
534   potential unintended consequences. Only through such comprehensive research can we hope to
535
      develop AI systems that are both highly capable and fundamentally aligned with their intended
      purposes.

758   across 9 epochs using a learning rate of 0.0009 and a batch size of 4. LoRA adjustments were applied
759   at layer index 57.
760
      These configurations were chosen to optimize computational efficiency while adapting to each
761
      model’s architecture and capacity for SOO fine-tuning.
