009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               RLHF has emerged as a pivotal step in aligning language models with human
012
               objectives and values. It typically involves learning a reward model from hu-
013
               man preference data and then using reinforcement learning to update the gen-
014            erative model accordingly. Conversely, Direct Preference Optimization (DPO)
015            directly optimizes the generative model with preference data, skipping reinforce-
016            ment learning. However, both RLHF and DPO assume uniform preferences,
017            overlooking the reality of diverse human annotators. This paper presents a new
018            method to align generative models with varied human preferences. We propose
019            an Expectation-Maximization adaptation to DPO, generating a mixture of models
020            based on latent preference types of the annotators. We then introduce a min-max
021            regret ensemble learning model to produce a single generative method to minimize
022
               worst-case regret among annotator subgroups with similar latent factors. Our algo-
               rithms leverage the simplicity of DPO while accommodating diverse preferences.
023
               Experimental results validate the effectiveness of our approach in producing equi-
024
               table generative policies.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030
      Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) has emerged as one of the leading methods
031   to align Language Models (LMs) to human preferences Ouyang et al. (2022); Stiennon et al. (2020);
032   Wang et al. (2023b). RLHF focuses on learning a single reward model from human preference
033   data and uses that to fine-tune and align the LM. To sidestep potentially expensive reinforcement
034   learning, Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) Rafailov et al. (2024b) is an alignment method that
035   optimizes the LM policy directly using the preference data. However, DPO implicitly uses the same
036   reward model as RLHF to train the LM. This reward model reflects the majority opinion of the pref-
037   erence data annotators and caters to that majority. If the annotator population is not representative of
038   the general population, then this comes at the cost of neglecting groups underrepresented in the an-
039
      notators, leading to misrepresentation of preferences. On the other hand, if the annotator population
      is representative, then opinions of minority groups in the general population are shunned, causing
040
      bias and discrimination.
041
042   Most papers that try to deal with this issue learn a reward model and then use a standard RL frame-
043   work such as PPO to align the LM. However, DPO has several advantages over RLHF, eliminating
044   the need for a reward model and leading to a more stable pipeline. Zhou et al. (2023) utilizes these
045
      benefits by developing an algorithm that directly optimizes policy by implicitly learning a multi-
      objective reward model. However, methods that rely on a multi-dimensional reward model Wang
046
      et al. (2024b); Zhou et al. (2023) implicitly or explicitly have two main drawbacks. First, these
047
      methods typically require annotators to rate data on a multi-dimensional scale, with each dimen-
048   sion corresponding to a different objective like safety or accuracy. This data is both more costly
049   and harder to obtain compared to binary preference data Casper et al. (2023). Second, the different
050   rating objectives must be determined ahead of the data collection stage. This can be a difficult task
051   as there are many latent factors that might affect the preferences of annotators Siththaranjan et al.
052   (2023), which can be difficult to discern. For example, if we collect ratings based on helpfulness
053   and harmfulness similar to Bai et al. (2022), these rankings might not fully explain some preference
      decisions made because of cultural, political, or geographical inclinations.

      We propose a pipeline of two algorithms to sidestep the need for RLHF for a heterogeneous popu-
055   lation, allowing us to cater to diverse preferences without the need for reinforcement learning, let-
056   ting us reap the added benefits of DPO. In particular, we propose Expectation Maximization Direct
057   Preference Optimization (EM-DPO) and MinMax Direct Preference Optimization (MinMax-DPO).
058   EM-DPO uses an EM algorithm Dempster et al. (1977) to simultaneously learn the distribution of
059   user preference types as well as policies for each type. Note that, if we already knew the group each
060   user belonged to, we could simply train an optimal DPO policy on each group separately. Since we
061   do not, we think of our data as being generated by latent mixture model, where for each user we first
062   draw a latent preference type and then draw a set of annotation data based on the preference type.
063
      We show that one can combine ideas from DPO with the EM algorithm for learning mixture models
      and directly learn a distribution of latent types, as well as a regularized optimal policy for each type.
064
      MinMax-DPO then takes these optimal policies and learns one model to best serve the needs of the
065
      population. Figure 1 shows the proposed pipeline.

091
092   RLHF With Diverse Preferences: One of the chief issues in RLHF is that of diverse populations;
093
      different annotators could have very different preferences Dumoulin et al. (2023). Several studies
      have tried to solve the diverse population problem by learning more expressive reward functions and
094
      then using them to perform RLHF. For example, Rame et al. (2024); Jang et al. (2023); Chakraborty
095
      et al. (2024) maintains and learns several reward models at once. Similarly, Wang et al. (2024b)
096   learns a multi-dimensional reward model where each dimension provides rewards based on a differ-
097   ent objective such as safety or usefulness. Yang et al. (2024) proposes a policy-agnostic method to
098   perform multi-objective LLM alighment. Alternatively, Siththaranjan et al. (2023); Li et al. (2024)
099   learns a distribution over fixed reward models. Finally, these reward models are combined using
100   various strategies Bakker et al. (2022); Jang et al. (2023); Rame et al. (2024) to get a final reward
101   model which is then used to perform RLHF. Chakraborty et al. (2024) also learns multiple reward
102   models, but performs RL by maximizing the minimum reward thereby ensuring that the final model
103   is fair. The paper draws on elements of social choice theory, which Conitzer et al. (2024) argues
104
      is an effective path forward for RLHF research in general, specifically regarding issues with aggre-
      gating preferences. Dai & Fleisig (2024) outlines a correspondence between the key principles and
105
      desiderata of social choice into the RLHF context.
106
107   In an orthogonal approach, Zhong et al. (2024) utilizes meta-learning to learn diverse preferences. In
      general, trying to do RLHF with many reward models becomes expensive, making extending DPO

130   In this section, we discuss traditional alignment methods that assume uniform preference among the
131   whole population, namely RLHF Ziegler et al. (2019); Stiennon et al. (2020); Ouyang et al. (2022)
132   and DPO Rafailov et al. (2024b).

      Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) The RLHF pipeline has two inputs.
134
      The first is an LM πSFT that is pre-trained on internet-scale data and then fine-tuned with supervised
135   learning. The second input is a static annotator preference dataset. To collect this data, first pairs of
136   responses (y1 , y2 ) are generated from πSFT (·|x) where x is a given prompt. Human annotators then
137   choose the best response between the two - in what follows, let y1 denote the winning response and
138   y2 denote the losing response. Also, let H be the population of all human annotators and h ∈ H be
139   the random variable that represents a single human annotator.

      Both RLHF and DPO assume that preferences are uniform across the population and implicitly or
173   explicitly learn a single reward model. However, this is not the case in the real world as humans
174   have diverse preferences and values. Moreover, RLHF and DPO prioritize the majority opinion
175   of the annotator population. This could lead to misalignment if the annotation population is not
176   representative of the general population. Traditional methods like RLHF and DPO can therefore
177   lead to bias and discrimination towards the minority subgroups among the annotator population. We
178   propose a new algorithm, MinMax-DPO, that learns an equitable and fair optimal policy directly
179   from binary preference data to bridge this gap.

282   So far, we have shown how to calculate a separate policy that optimizes for each preference popula-

284   preferences of different groups and find a policy that strikes a good balance.
285

286

287   we will restrict ourselves to selecting an ensemble among the already trained policies. As such, we
288   define the ensemble space of policies as:

305   where [x] = max{x, 0}. Note that we only consider the positive part of the regret.
306   Why min-max regret? Max-min reward is another fairness criterion that can be applied to the
307   RLHF problem to ensure equity, as discussed in Chakraborty et al. (2024). However, this criterion
308   has two major drawbacks. Firstly, the reward model is not uniquely identifiable from preference
309   data. Two reward models r(x, y) and r′ (x, y) are equivalent if r(x, y) − r′ (x, y) = f (x) Rafailov
310   et al. (2024b). Therefore, directly maximizing the minimum reward is ineffective due to this scaling.
311   We could fix this by standardizing the reward model to set the minimum reward to zero - if r(x, y) is
312   the recovered reward function, we can use r′ (x, y) = r(x, y) − miny r(x, y), which is an equivalent
313
      reward model. Even then, there is another issue with the max-min reward criterion: The max-min
      reward focuses on improving rewards for users with the lowest reward, while the min-max regret
314
      function targets users with the highest regrets. These groups differ when users with low rewards
315
      also have low regrets. As an example, consider a setting with fixed context and three responses. If
316   two users have reward vectors [0, 0.01, 0.02] and [0, 10, 1] respectively, then the max-min reward
317   objective will choose response 3 to maximize user 2’s reward. However, user 1 is nearly indifferent
318   between the three choices 2, whereas user 2 strongly prefers option 2. Therefore, it is more ideal to
319   choose option 2, which the min-max regret criteria chooses.

383
384   We can draw parallels between the offline contextual bandit problem and the problem of learning
385   from human preferences Azar et al. (2024). In this setting, the context represents the prompt and the
386   bandit arms represent the possible responses for the given context. For our experiment, we consider
387   a simplified case with three arms.
388   First, we generate 200 annotators drawn randomly from three sub-populations, with 60% of the
389   population coming from the first sub-population, 30% from the second, and 10% from the third.
390   The preferences of annotators within each sub-population is homogeneous and therefore, each
391   sub-population is associated with a single reward model. For our experiment, we model the
392   sub-population reward model using a linear function, similarly to linear contextual bandits Di-
393   makopoulou et al. (2019):
394
                                         rz∗ (y, x) = xT θz (y) + N (0, σ)                               (25)
395
396
      where θz (y) is the model parameters corresponding to the latent variable z for arm y, x is the context,
397
      and N (0, σ) represents noise with 0 mean and standard deviation σ = 0.01. θz (y) is fixed for any
398
      given sub-population with values θi (i) = [10, 10, 10] and θi (j) = [0, 0, 0], j ̸= i for sub-population
399   i. We generate 10 preference data pairs per annotator. For each data point, first we draw a context
400   vector x uniformly randomly from the hypercube [0, 1]3 . Then, a pair of responses is generated from
401   a uniformly random reference policy πSFT = (1/3, 1/3, 1/3). The annotator then chooses a response
402   yw ≻ yl based on their reward model rz∗ (y, x). We implement EM-DPO and MinMax-DPO for this

      6.3   D ISCUSSION & L IMITATIONS
527
528   We provide a robust framework to train equitable policies for a heterogeneous population with di-
529   verse preferences. By extending the DPO algorithm, we are able to sidestep reinforcement learning
530   entirely, enjoying the added stability that DPO provides while making it more applicable to real-
531   world situations and datasets. We demonstrate our findings on a contextual bandit experiment as
532   well as a larger-scale LLM experiment, showing how our algorithm, MinMax-DPO, generates a far
533   more socially equitable policy than standard DPO in diverse populations where some groups may
534   be underrepresented.
535   Based on our results, we raise some limitations and directions for future work. Our derivations op-
536   erate off of the assumption that contexts are uncorrelated given the preference type of the annotator;
537   this may not be the case in the real world as, to increase accuracy of data collection, annotators
538   may be given prompts more tuned to their skill sets. We also assume annotators report their pref-
539   erences honestly, which may not be the case - this raises important questions regarding incentive
      compatibility.

      A    A DDITIONAL R ELATED W ORK
757
758
      Preference-Based Reinforcement Learning: Reinforcement learning from preferences has been
759
      an active research area for some time, providing a way to train on tasks for which explicitly defin-
760   ing rewards is hard Wirth et al. (2017); Lee et al. (2021); Abdelkareem et al. (2022). In particular,
761   Christiano et al. (2017); Ibarz et al. (2018) show that using human preferences to guide reinforce-
762   ment learning (RLHF) is particularly effective on a variety of tasks, such as training robots. More
763   recently, RLHF has become a very popular technique to fine-tune language models to do a variety
764   of tasks such as summarization Ouyang et al. (2022); Ziegler et al. (2019); Stiennon et al. (2020);
765   Wu et al. (2021). RLHF has also been used to align language models Bai et al. (2022); Askell et al.
766   (2021). Casper et al. (2023) details several open problems in the field of RLHF, including those
767   related to the feedback itself, particularly the inverse relation between richness and efficiency. Some
768
      work has been done on this problem with regards to language-based feedback in particular Fu et al.
      (2019); Zhou & Small (2021) as well as in more general settings Hwang et al. (2024), but specific
769
      applications to LLMs have not been fully explored.
770
771   Challenges with Reward Modeling: In general, human preferences can be difficult to represent
772   using reward models Hong et al. (2022), and the validity of reward modeling itself is still somewhat
773   debated Bowling et al. (2023); Bobu et al. (2023); Skalse & Abate (2022). Some work has also
774
      been done to take personality into account when reward modeling Lindner & El-Assady (2022);
      Lee et al. (2021), but this area remains open. In general, taking human irrationality into account
775
      when reward modeling (to optimize a more accurate reward function) leads to a trade-off between
776
      efficiency and accuracy Shah et al. (2019); Nguyen et al. (2017). Work has been done on inverse
777   RL with particular models of suboptimality such as myopia Evans et al. (2016), noise Zheng et al.
778   (2014), and risk-sensitivity Majumdar et al. (2017), but dealing with general irrationalities remains
779   open.
780
      The proper use and collection of data remains an issue with RLHF. Sun et al. (2024) analyzes LLM
781
      fine-tuning as a mechanism design problem where agents may have the incentive to misreport their
782
      preferences. Data can also often have issues or certain data points may not be as effective as others;
783   Wang et al. (2024a) proposes methods to deal with incorrect or ambiguous preference pairs, while
784   Yin et al. (2024) proposes an extension to DPO which uses contrastive learning to discern between
785   more and less preferred responses to prompts.

943   F.1      IMD B DATA G ENERATION
944
      We use the IMDb dataset Maas et al. (2011) to generate a synthetic preference dataset. More specif-
945
      ically, we use a publicly available adaptation of the IMDb dataset1 . This dataset uses the first 20
946
      tokens from the original IMDb datasetMaas et al. (2011) as the prompt and then two responses are
947   generated for each prompt using a GPT-2 Large model that is fine-tuned on the IMDb dataset. We
948   synthetically generate preference data for two user groups using this dataset. We select a random
949   subset of 5,000 prompts and assign it equally among 5 users (1,000 preferences per user). This
950   constitutes the first sub-group and these users always prefer the response that is shorter in length.
951   We automatically label the preference data for this group by simply counting the number of words
952   in the response. Next, we select 15,000 prompts from the remaining data and assign it to 15 users
953   (1,000 preference per user). This is the second sub-group where the users always prefer the response
954   that is more grammatically correct. We use LanguageTool2 to automatically to find the number of
955
      grammatical errors in a given text and divide this number by the length of the text to get a correctness
      score. The users prefer the response with a higher correctness score.
956
957
      F.2      C LUSTER -DPO
958
959   The Cluster-DPO policy is generated as follows. We naively cluster the 20 users into 2 user sub-
960   groups using k-means clustering on the average embedding of all the preferred texts of that user.
961   Embeddings are generated using the RoBERTa-Large model Liu (2019). Then, we train a DPO
962   policy on each cluster separately and combine them using Algorithm 2; we are essentially replacing
963   the EM-DPO step with a naive clustering step.
