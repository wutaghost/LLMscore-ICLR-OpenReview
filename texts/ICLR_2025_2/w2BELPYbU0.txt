011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Rectified Flow Transformers (RFTs) offer superior training and inference effi-
014            ciency, making them likely the most viable direction for scaling up diffusion mod-
015            els. However, progress in generation resolution has been relatively slow due to
016            data quality and training costs. Tuning-free resolution extrapolation presents an
017            alternative, but current methods often reduce generative stability, limiting practi-
018            cal application. In this paper, we review existing resolution extrapolation methods
019            and introduce the I-Max framework to maximize the resolution potential of Text-
020
               to-Image RFTs. I-Max features: (i) a novel Projected Flow strategy for stable ex-
               trapolation and (ii) an advanced inference toolkit for generalizing model knowl-
021
               edge to higher resolutions. Experiments with Lumina-Next-2K and Flux.1-dev
022
               demonstrate I-Max’s ability to enhance stability in resolution extrapolation and
023            show that it can bring image detail emergence and artifact correction, confirming
024            the practical value of tuning-free resolution extrapolation.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030
      Over the past few years, diffusion model Sohl-Dickstein et al. (2015) has made significant break-
031   throughs across various dimensions, including diffusion process Ho et al. (2020); Song et al.
032   (2020a;b); Liu et al. (2022); Lipman et al. (2022), model design Ho et al. (2022); Rombach et al.
033   (2022); Teng et al. (2023), network architecture Peebles & Xie (2023), etc, and yielding numerous
034   practical applications. Building on the accumulated experience from these explorations, rectified
035   flow transformers (RFTs) Ma et al. (2024a) have now been recognized as a potential future direction
036   for scaling up diffusion models, leading to the development of successful open-source text-to-image
037   models like Stable Diffusion 3 Esser et al. (2024), Lumina-T2X Gao et al. (2024), and Flux Black
038   Forest Labs (2024). Although these models have achieved significant improvements in various as-
039
      pects like generation quality, aesthetics, and text-image alignment, their native generative resolution
      has remained limited to the 10242 -20482 range. This restricts the direct application of AI-generated
040
      visual content in scenarios with high-quality demand.
041
042   However, directly training high-resolution generative models is currently less practical, considering
043   the high data quality requirements and the exponential increase in training costs associated with
044   ultra-high-resolution generation. In fact, training a low-resolution diffusion model already requires
045
      dozens of days on hundreds of GPUs Podell et al. (2023). Therefore, some existing works achieve
      a balance by supervised fine-tuning – improving the model’s generative resolution via tuning on a
046
      certain amount of high-resolution samples Cheng et al. (2024); Chen et al. (2024); Ren et al. (2024).
047
      While another line of research focuses on directly hacking the model’s inference stage to achieve
048   tuning-free resolution extrapolation – this allows for high-resolution generation almost as a “free
049   lunch”, but it also significantly decreases the model’s generative stability. In this paper, based on
050   pre-trained text-to-image RFTs, we discover that rectified flow not only facilitates efficient infer-
051   ence but also offers inherent advantages in resolution extrapolation. Combined with the empirical
052   knowledge of transformer’s context length extrapolation, we propose the I-Max framework, which
053   enables stable generation across a range of extrapolated resolutions, enhancing the practical appli-
      cation value of tuning-free resolution extrapolation.

      Reviewing the existing works on resolution extrapolation, we first decouple the challenges they
      address into two perspectives: (i) “how to guide?”, which refers to how to use reliable native-
094
      resolution generation results to guide high-resolution generation, and (ii) “how to infer?”, which is
095
      about enhancing the model’s generative ability to generalize better to extrapolated resolutions during
096   the inference phase. We consider both perspectives equally essential and build the I-Max framework
097   upon them. However, improving the model’s generalization to extrapolated resolutions seems quite
098   intuitive, while using low-resolution generation for guidance may not appear strictly necessary. For
099   this, we demonstrate through experiments that the diffusion model’s generalization ability to the
100   extrapolated generation resolutions varies over time. It exhibits weaker extrapolation capability
101   during the early denoising stages when generating coarse image content from random noise, but
102   demonstrates a more robust ability during the later stages of denoising the coarse content (please
103   refer to Sec. 3.5). In the following paragraphs, we will summarise how existing works address the
104
      questions of “how to guide?” and “how to infer?”, and introduce our design choices in I-Max that
      are tailored for RFTs.
105
106   To solve “how to guide?”, a direct approach is to upsample the low-resolution results, add a certain
107   amount of noise, and then denoise back, similar to SDEdit Meng et al. (2021). DemoFusion Du
      et al. (2024) improves this two-stage paradigm by introducing the concept of “skip-residual”, which

126   integrates the noise-injected low-resolution embeddings at each timestep in a time-conditioned man-

128   timestep, which limits its ability to adjust the subtle change of denoising direction. On the contrary,
129   Upsample Guidance Hwang et al. (2024) downsamples the high-resolution embeddings to the native
130
      resolution to infer the simultaneous low-resolution guidance. Although it carefully balances the dis-
      tribution shift of embeddings and noises caused by downsampling, it still fails to maintain the model
131
      performance at the native resolution. Another more direct approach is integrating low-resolution
132
      generation and high-resolution extrapolation into a single denoising process in a relay manner Teng
133   et al. (2023); Zhuo et al. (2024), but it only improves efficiency. In I-Max, we try to explore the op-
134   timal solution under the context of the rectified flow model. Particularly, we treat the low-resolution
135   space as a low-dimensional projection of the high-resolution space, which means the low-resolution
136   flow can be regarded as the projection of the ideal high-resolution flow. And considering the linear
137   interpolation characteristic of rectified flow – where the optimal direction of the current flow can be
138   directly constructed as the vector from the current position to the endpoint, we can easily build dy-
139   namic guidance in the projected space at each timestep, which we term Projected Flow (see details
140   in Sec. 2.2).
141   As for “how to infer?”, whether UNet or Transformer-based diffusion models have been found to
142   lack good generalization to extrapolated resolutions – direct extrapolation often leads to the collapse
143   of the generated content. A direct solution to this can be found in MultiDiffusionBar-Tal et al. (2023)
144   and its subsequent work Du et al. (2024); Haji-Ali et al. (2024); Lin et al. (2024a;b), which sample
145   overlapping patches at the training resolution to ensure stable inference. In contrast, some other
146   approaches He et al. (2023); Zhang et al. (2023); Huang et al. (2024) adopt dilated convolution to
147
      extend the model’s perception field while doing resolution extrapolation. However, these methods
      are only practical for CNN-based diffusion models and cannot be applied to the DiT architecture.
148
      For this, FiT Lu et al. (2024) and Lumina-T2X Gao et al. (2024) draw on the successful experience
149
      of long-text extrapolation in LLMs and adopt NTK-aware Scaled RoPE loc (2024) to achieve direct
150   extrapolation in a limited range (e.g., 2562 → 5122 or 10242 → 20482 ). In addition to adjusting
151   models’ spatial perception, the SNR shift Esser et al. (2024) and attention entropy shift Jin et al.
152   (2024) are also found to be critical to resolution extrapolation and need to be corrected. This paper
153   identifies the essential inference techniques for RFTs and integrates them as an inference toolkit into
154   the I-Max framework (see details in Sec. 2.3). This ensures that the model can generate expressive,
155   detailed content at extrapolated resolutions.
156   To validate the effectiveness of I-Max, we tune Lumina-Next Zhuo et al. (2024) with self-collected
157   high-quality, high-resolution images and obtain Lumina-Next-2K, a native 2K generation RFT. On
158   Lumina-Next-2K, I-Max can achieve 4× to 16× resolution extrapolation, directly generating im-
159   ages up to 8K resolution, as shown in Fig. 1. We also validate I-Max on Flux.1-dev Black Forest
160   Labs (2024), where it achieve stable 4K image generation, demonstrating its general applicability to
161   MMDiT structured RFT models. Notably, as shown in Fig. 2, even though Flux.1-dev can generate
      ultra-high-quality images, conducting resolution extrapolation through I-Max still provides benefits,

198    Rectified Flow Liu et al. (2022) and Flow Matching Lipman et al. (2022) attempt to simplify the
199   construction of ODE models by linearly interpolating between two distributions. Here, we introduce
200
      the preliminary knowledge in the context of Rectified Flow. Given samples X0 ∈ π0 and X1 ∈ π1 ,
      where π0 is the noise distribution and π1 is the image distribution in this paper. Rectified flow builds
201
      the path from X0 to X1 as a linear flow with the direction of (X1 − X0 ), and the intermediate state
202
      Xt can be represented by Xt = tX1 + (1 − t)X0 . Therefore, we can build the ODE of Xt as
203
204                                             ~\label {} d X_t = (X_1 - X_0) dt.                         (1)
205   Considering that during denoising, the ODE is non-causal – since X1 is the unknown target – we
206   can use vθ (Xt , t, c) to fit in the direction (X1 − X0 ) constructing the neural ODE model, where
207   c is additional conditions like text and class labels. In this way, we can transfer X0 to X1 by the
208   prediction dX̂t = vθ (Xt , t, c)dt.

      3.4      C OMPARISON OF D IFFERENT L OW- RESOLUTION G UIDANCE
410
      In I-Max, we propose Projected Flow, specifically tailored to the characteristics of rectified flow,
411   which leverages low-resolution generation results to improve the stability of high-resolution gener-
412   ation. Here, we compare Projected Flow with existing low-resolution guidance methods to demon-
413   strate its superiority. Specifically, the baseline methods include: (1) SDEdit Meng et al. (2021),
414   which directly upsamples the guidance image, adds noise, and then uses a diffusion model to denoise
415   it. Given its simplicity, SDEdit has been used in popular open-source projects 1 for high-resolution
416   enhancement. (2) Skip-Residual, introduced in DemoFusion Du et al. (2024), constructs a complete
417   progressive diffusion process and then injects low-resolution guidance at each timestep during de-
418   noising, thereby enhancing the guidance’s effectiveness. (3) Time-aware scaled RoPE Zhuo et al.
419
      (2024), achieving the same idea as Relay Diffusion Teng et al. (2023) for butter efficiency but in a
      training-free manner by applying time-conditioned re-scaling to RoPE during the diffusion process.
420
421   In Fig. 8, we visualize the results of comparison. We can observe that, leveraging Projected Flow,
422   I-Max significantly enhances the details of generated images in the 1K→4K extrapolation. While
423   SDEdit also brings finer details, it lacks the ability to guide the flow direction throughout the en-
424   tire denoising process, leading to the appearance of significant artifacts. In contrast, Skip-residual
425
      achieves better stability by injecting guidance at each timestep, but the intermediate guidance con-
      structed by a diffusion process introduces fixed noise that cannot be adjusted based on the current
426
      latent representation, thus reducing image quality. Lastly, while Time-Aware Scaled RoPE offers
427
      superior efficiency, it fails to produce usable results during the 16× extrapolation due to the absence
428   of explicit high-quality guidance. Additionally, in Fig. 7, we also conduct GPT-4o preference evalu-
429   ations between the three baseline methods and Projected Flow. The results were consistent with the
430   visual findings, where Projected Flow achieved significantly higher than 50% win rates.

447   3.5              W HY D O W E N EED L OW- RESOLUTION G UIDANCE ?
448
449
450   In resolution extrapolation, using native-resolution generation as guidance is a common practice in
451   existing works Du et al. (2024); Teng et al. (2023); Hwang et al. (2024); Lin et al. (2024a;b). In this
452
      paper, we also regard it as a core perspective for addressing resolution extrapolation and introduce
      Projected Flow as one of our primary contributions. In this section, we aim to demonstrate why
453
      low-resolution guidance is so critical and how, with the support of low-resolution guidance, reso-
454
      lution extrapolation can lead to the emergence of finer details and improvements in overall quality.
455   Here, we conduct experiments using the 1K Lumina-Next model and 1,000 randomly sampled high-
456   resolution text-image pairs. Specifically, we resize the images to a particular resolution, add noise
457   up to a specific timestep, and then input the noisy images into the model to calculate the velocity
458   loss. In this way, we can evaluate the model’s performance at the given timestep. As shown in Fig. 9
459   (a), we plotted the loss/timestep curves for both native resolution (1K) and extrapolated resolution
460   (2K and 4K). We can observe that as time t progresses, all curves of different resolutions decrease,
461   which is reasonable since the higher the signal-to-noise ratio, the easier it becomes to predict the
462   velocity.
463   Furthermore, we compute the ratio of the loss at extrapolated resolutions to the loss at the native
464   resolution. In that case, we can visualize the degree of the model’s performance degradation at
465   extrapolated resolutions. According to the results shown in Fig. 9 (b), we can observe that the
466   most severe performance degradation occurs in the intermediate timesteps. Previous work Choi
467   et al. (2022) has demonstrated that the core content generation of diffusion models happens during
468   the middle stage of the denoising process, while the later stage is about high-frequency details
469
      refinement. This indicates that when generating at extrapolated resolutions, the model’s ability to
      generate main content degrades noticeably, while the ability to refine local details is less resolution-
470
      sensitive. This finding clarifies the motivation for implementing low-resolution guidance – low-
471
      resolution guidance can alleviate the degradation of the model’s main content generation ability as
472   resolution increases, allowing the model to focus on refining local details, which is less sensitive to
473   resolution changes.
474
475
      Note that we observe a relatively small performance gap in the early stages of the denoising pro-
      cess. This is because when the signal-to-noise ratio is very low, there are many possible correct
476
      flow directions, and the model learns to predict the mean of data distribution. Therefore, the loss
477
      calculated using the per-sample velocity ground truth may not accurately reflect the model’s perfor-
478   mance. Additionally, we notice that the ratio in Fig. 9 (b) can sometimes be smaller than 1. This
479   occurs because, as the sequence length increases, the scale of the RFT’s predictions can change.
480   It does not imply that the model performs better at higher resolutions. Instead of focusing on the
481   numerical values of the curve, the overall trend of the curve offers more valuable insight.

511   4     L IMITATION
512
513   In this paper, we propose I-Max to maximize the resolution potential of text-to-image models, i.e.,
514   enabling them to generate high-quality images far beyond their training resolution. However, we
515   found that the model’s ability to extrapolate is significantly influenced by the foundation model
516   – both the architecture and training strategy determine their behavior and capability during res-
517   olution extrapolation. For example, through sequential ablation studies, we observed that the DiT-
518   structured Lumina-Next-2K and the MMDiT-structured Flux.1-dev exhibit different collapse modes.
519   Moreover, while they both have the capability to generate at 2K resolution, Flux.1-dev is trained to
520   accommodate a range from 2562 to 20482 resolutions, which limits us to generate up to 4K with
521
      the help of I-Max. Nevertheless, we observe an overall trend where generation quality first im-
      proves and then degrades across different models (refer to Sec. 3.3), proving that I-Max has general
522
      applicability to RFTs.
523
524   Besides, in this work, we particularly focus on maximizing the generation quality during resolution
525   extrapolation and do not address the issue of exponentially increasing inference costs associated with
526   high-resolution generation. We experimented with some naive solutions, such as token merging or
527
      token dropping, but they led to noticeable performance degradation in the tuning-free setting. We
      believe this is an area worth exploring in future research.

530   5     C ONCLUSION
531
532
      In this paper, we propose a resolution extrapolation framework for rectified flow transformers, called
533
      I-Max. I-Max consists of two key components: (i) a newly proposed projected flow strategy that
534   leverages the simplicity of rectified flow to implement low-resolution guidance, significantly reduc-
535   ing the inference complexity at extrapolated resolutions; (ii) an inference toolkit that ensures the
536   model’s generalization capability to extrapolated resolutions. Compared to previous approaches,
537   I-Max significantly enhances the stability of the model when generating at extrapolated resolutions,
538   which is why we claim it maximizes the resolution potential of text-to-image models. Through
539   experiments, we demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method and the necessity of each
      design component.

850   For I-Max, a potential concern is that we could synthesise high-resolution images by using a pipeline
851   of low-resolution generation followed by super-resolution, which offers higher inference efficiency
852   than resolution extrapolation. This is particularly relevant since I-Max itself uses low-resolution
853   generated results as guidance. Therefore, we would like to clarify that resolution extrapolation
854
      serves a different purpose from super-resolution. Specifically, high-resolution generation focuses
      on producing high-quality, highly detailed images, while super-resolution has a strong requirement
855
      for maintaining consistency between input and output, which often limits the detail enhancement in
856
      the output. In Fig. 13, we also compare I-Max with the commonly used super-resolution method
857   ESRGAN Wang et al. (2018). It is evident that the rich details introduced by I-Max via high-
858   resolution generation are not present in super-resolution results. Moreover, ESRGAN cannot correct
859   artifacts present in the generated images.
