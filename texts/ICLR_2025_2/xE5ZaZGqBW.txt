009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Recent advances in the field of out-of-distribution (OOD) detection have placed
012            great emphasis on learning better representations suited to this task. While there
013            have been distance-based approaches, distributional awareness has seldom been
014            exploited for better performance. We present HACk -OOD, a novel OOD detection
015            method that makes no distributional assumption about the data, but automatically
016            adapts to its distribution. Specifically, HACk -OOD constructs a set of hypercones
017            by maximizing the angular distance to neighbors in a given data-point’s vicinity
018            to approximate the contour within which in-distribution (ID) data-points lie. Ex-
019
               perimental results show state-of-the-art FPR@95 and AUROC performance on
               Near-OOD detection and on Far-OOD detection on the challenging CIFAR-100
020
               benchmark without explicitly training for OOD performance.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
025
026   Machine learning models are trained on a particular set of data, called the in-distribution (ID) set.
027
      During inference, it is possible that the trained model will receive samples drawn from a different
      distribution to the one it has been trained on. These observations are said to be out-of-distribution
028
      (OOD). It is important to be able to distinguish between ID and OOD instances for safe model
029
      deployment.
030
031   Existing OOD detection methods can be broadly categorized into training-based methods and post-
032   processing distance-based methods Yang et al. (2024). Training-based methods aim to incorporate
033
      OOD detection capabilities directly into the model via train-time regularization Lu et al. (2024);
      Ming et al. (2022a). These methods typically modify the objective function or architecture to enhance
034
      sensitivity to OOD inputs, e.g., using auxiliary classifiers or network branches targeted at OOD
035
      detection Papadopoulos et al. (2021); Mohseni et al. (2020), adversarial training Yi et al. (2021);
036   Chen et al. (2021), or self-supervised learning objectives Sehwag et al. (2021); Mohseni et al. (2020);
037   Hendrycks et al. (2019a); Sun et al. (2022a). They may also take a two-step modeling approach Liang
038   et al. (2017), or directly train an OOD detection model to be applied after the initial model Sun et al.
039   (2022a). While effective, they often require tuning and may sacrifice primary task performance.
040
      Distance-based methods treat OOD detection as a separate post-training step Bibas et al. (2021);
041
      Hendrycks and Gimpel (2016); Liu et al. (2021); Wang et al. (2022); Sehwag et al. (2021); Sun et al.
042   (2022b); Lee et al. (2018a); Ren et al. (2021); Techapanurak et al. (2019); Chen et al. (2022); Huang
043   et al. (2020); Ming et al. (2023); Denouden et al. (2018); Zhou (2023); Yang et al. (2022); Jiang
044   et al. (2023); Li et al. (2023). They assume OOD data falls far from ID data in the output space and
045   utilize scoring functions like maximum softmax probability Hendrycks and Gimpel (2016), maximum
046   logits Hendrycks et al. (2022a), maximum likelihood Bibas et al. (2021), energy Liu et al. (2021),
047   and reconstruction error Denouden et al. (2018); Zhou (2023); Yang et al. (2022); Jiang et al. (2023);
048   Li et al. (2023) to measure distance between samples. Certain works construct scoring functions in
049   the penultimate layer’s feature space Sehwag et al. (2021); Sun et al. (2022b); Chen et al. (2022);
050
      Huang et al. (2020); Ming et al. (2023). Distance-based methods are model-agnostic and applicable
      to pre-trained models if the feature space adequately separates ID and OOD.
051
052   We propose a new approach, HACk -OOD (Hypercone Assisted Contour Generation for OOD
053   Detection), which leverages post-training distance-based OOD concepts. It assumes there is no access
      to OOD samples, as obtaining a representative sample is infeasible. This allows us to flexibly map

054
      the ID feature space by building a set of multidimensional hypercones, and treating the union of the
055   built hypercones as the new ID class shape.
056
057   This paper makes the following contributions. (1) We present, to the best of our knowledge, the
058
      first study of class contour generation by employing hypercone projections, effectively providing a
      new representation for the ID manifold in the feature space. We provide a formalized mathematical
059
      definition of the method, and analyze its dynamics in experimental settings. Our work shows the
060
      efficacy of this method in the OOD detection setting. (2) Contrary to methods in the literature,
061   we do not make strong distributional assumptions about the feature space, other than that ID and
062   OOD data are separable in the space. The generated contour separates ID from OOD data by
063   considering the variability of the ID data in the directions of the projected hypercones from the class
064   centroid. (3) We show through experimental results that HACk -OOD+SHE, a variant of HACk -OOD,
065   reaches state-of-the-art (SOTA) performance in Far-OOD detection and Near-OOD detection using
066   Supervised Contrastive Learning for CIFAR-100, and performs on par with other SOTA methods on
067   benchmark datasets for CIFAR-10. Experiments with Supervised Cross Entropy show that our method
068   is competitive with SOTA methods on models trained with this loss function. The HACk -OOD+SHE
069
      performance in Near-OOD detection detection proves that it performs well even in cases where ID
      and OOD classes have significant semantic overlap.

072   2    P RELIMINARIES
073
074   In line with other distance-based methods for OOD detection Sun et al. (2022b); Sehwag et al. (2021),
075   we frame the task as a multi-class classification problem. We present results on OOD detection for
076   image classification, but this framework can be easily extended beyond image data Liu et al. (2024).
077   Let X ⊆ RD represent the input space, where D = C × W × H, in which C denotes the number of
078
      channels, and W × H denotes the size of the image. The output space Y is defined as {1, . . . , |Y |}.
      The goal of the classification problem is to learn a mapping f : X → Y , which assigns each input
079
      observation to one of the |Y | classes. We employ a neural network f trained on samples drawn from
080
      the joint distribution PXY , where Pin represents the marginal distribution over X. The network
081   outputs a set of logits, used to predict the label for a given input.
082
083   Given a classifier model, such as the one outlined above, our goal during testing is to accurately
084
      classify images into one of the |Y | labels (ID), while also being able to detect unknown observations
      (OOD). Historically, distance-based methods in OOD detection have utilized level set estimation Sun
085
      et al. (2022b) in a binary classification approach to determine whether or not observations are drawn
086
      from Pin .
087
088   Level set estimation involves partitioning the input space into regions where the classifier’s output
089   lies above or below a certain threshold. Let f (x) represent the output (e.g., logits or probabilities) of
090
      the classifier for input x. The decision boundary is determined by a threshold λ, such that:
091
092                                      Decision(x) = 1{S(f (x)) > λ}                                       (1)
093   where 1{·} describes the binary classifier in the form of an indicator function. It classifies a sample
094   as ID when the scoring function S(·) produces a score greater than the scalar threshold value λ. The
095   threshold λ is typically chosen based on properties of the training data and/or through validation
096   techniques to optimize performance. This approach effectively creates a boundary in the input space,
097   separating regions where the model is confident in its predictions (ID) from regions where it is
098   uncertain or likely to make errors (OOD).

100   3    R ELATED W ORK
101
102   As mentioned in Section 1, OOD detection methods can be broadly categorized into post-training
103   and training-based approaches. Our proposed method, HACk -OOD, falls into the post-training
104   category, so we will focus primarily on these techniques while briefly touching on training-based
105   methods for context. In our experiments, we compare HACk -OOD to several post-training methods,
106   demonstrating its effectiveness across various OOD detection scenarios. Our approach builds upon
107   the strengths of existing feature-space methods, while offering greater flexibility in mapping the
      embedding space.

      3.1   P OST-T RAINING M ETHODS
109
110   Post-training methods can be divided into feature space methods, uncertainty estimation methods,
111   gradient-based methods, activation rectification methods, and hybrid methods, each leveraging
112   different information from the trained model. In this section we introduce key post-training based
113   methods from all categories which will later be used as benchmarks for HACk -OOD.
114   Feature Space Methods: These methods leverage the rich information encoded in the feature space
115   of neural networks, typically using embeddings from the classifier’s penultimate layer. SSD+ Sehwag
116   et al. (2021) assumes a Gaussian distribution of ID observations, while the Mahalanobis method Lee
117   et al. (2018b) uses class-conditional Gaussian distributions for features to define a confidence score
118   based on the Mahalanobis distance. In contrast, KNN+ Sun et al. (2022b) and HACk -OOD make no
119   distributional assumptions, offering greater flexibility in mapping the embedding space.
120   Uncertainty Estimation Methods: These methods use the final layer outputs. Probability-
121   based approaches like Maximum Softmax Probability (MSP) Hendrycks and Gimpel (2016) and
122   MaxLogit Hendrycks et al. (2022a) classify observations based on maximum softmax probability and
123   maximum logits, respectively. The Generalized Entropy (GEN) method Liu et al. (2023a) introduces
124   an entropy-based score function applicable to any pre-trained softmax-based classifier, designed to
125   amplify minor deviations from ideal one-hot encodings. The Energy method Liu et al. (2021) com-
126   putes an energy function using logits, attributing higher negative energy values to ID data. Similarly,
127   KL Matching Hendrycks et al. (2022b) forms templates of class posterior distributions, and computes
128
      an anomaly score based on the minimum KL divergence between the test input’s posterior and these
      templates.
129
130   Gradient-Based Methods: GradNorm Huang et al. (2021a) uses gradient space information, noting
131   higher gradient magnitudes for ID data relative to OOD data. It employs the vector norm of gradients,
132   back-propagated from the KL divergence between the softmax output and a uniform probability
133   distribution.
134   Activation Rectification Methods: ReAct and ASH enhance OOD detection by modifying feature
135   activations. ReAct Sun et al. (2021) truncates activations in the classifier’s penultimate layer above a
136   specific value (the p-th percentile of model activations) to reduce noise, and aligns activation patterns
137   with well-behaved cases. ASH Djurisic et al. (2023) employs an on-the-fly method to remove a
138   significant portion of a sample’s activation at a late layer.
139   Hybrid Methods: ViM Wang et al. (2022) and NNGuide Park et al. (2023) combine information
140   from multiple sources. ViM uses both feature space and logit information, while NNGuide guides
141   classifier-based scores for detection.
142
143   3.2   OTHER M ETHODS
144
145   Training-Based Methods: While not the focus of our work, training-based methods offer comple-
146   mentary approaches to OOD detection. Non-parametric Outlier Synthesis (NPOS) Tao et al. (2023)
147   and Mixture Outlier Exposure (MixOE) Zhang et al. (2021) generate artificial OOD training data.
148
      CIDER Ming et al. (2022a), on the other hand, jointly optimizes dispersion and compactness losses
      to promote ID-OOD separability.
149
150   Multi-Modal Approaches: Recent work has explored leveraging multiple modalities for OOD
151   detection. Maximum Concept Matching (MCM) Ming et al. (2022b) and LoCoOp Miyai et al. (2024)
152   align visual features with textual concepts utilizing CLIP local features for OOD regularization.

154   4     M ETHOD
155
156   As embedding-based methods tend to outperform probability-based metrics in distance-based OOD
157   detection, we focus our efforts in this space Ming et al. (2022a); Lu et al. (2024); Sun et al. (2022b);
158   Sehwag et al. (2021). Parametric distance-based methods in the literature, however, necessitate
159   assumptions on the distribution of ID data in the feature space. Thus, they are likely to fail in
160   cases where the distribution has an irregular shape, and are less likely to capture areas of the
161   distribution which do not adhere to the assumptions. HACk -OOD presents a novel approach to OOD
      detection which captures the contour of ID data without making assumptions on the distribution of


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      the embedding space. It uses hypercones which flexibly map the embedding space locally, allowing
163   more precise separation of ID and OOD data.
164
165   More specifically, HACk -OOD extends SSD+ by relaxing the normality assumption that constrains
166
      the class contour to a hypersphere (or multidimensional ellipsoid). Instead, HACk -OOD approximates
      it with a set of hypercones parameterized by a pre-specified angle. Consequently, our method refrains
167
      from assuming a Gaussian distribution for the feature space, and describes its borders not by using
168
      multidimensional ellipsoids, but rather by projecting multidimensional hypercones in appropriate
169   directions. This approach allows for a more flexible representation of the class contour.
170
171   Unlike the classical distance-based methods, where a single distance cutoff threshold is selected for
172
      the full dataset, we adopt a more nuanced strategy. We assign a distinct distance cutoff per projected
      hypercone, determined by the observed variation in ID distances along that direction. The goal is
173
      to accommodate a diverse set of thresholds across different directions. By doing so, our method is
174
      unrestricted in shaping the contour of ID observations, fostering greater flexibility and adaptability.
175   Pseudo-code describing hypercone construction and inference is available in Appendix Section A.5.

190   4.2   H YPERCONE NOTATION
191
192   Let us now introduce key terminology necessary for describing the hypercone. The apex or vertex
193   of a hypercone, V , is the central point from which all generating lines originate. The axis of the
194   hypercone, ⃗a, is a straight line passing through the apex, V , and some other point P . It acts as the
195   central axis of symmetry, defining the primary direction along which the hypercone extends and
196
      maintains its symmetry. The slant height of a hypercone is the length of the line segment connecting
      the apex to any point on the hypercone’s surface. The opening angle of the hypercone is the angle
197
      between the hypercone axis and any line starting at the apex and extending along its slant height.
198
      This opening angle measures how much the hypercone widens or narrows as it extends from the apex
199   along its axis. Mathematically, if we denote a line along the slant height as ⃗s, the opening angle θ can
200   be expressed as:

202

206   the purposes of this paper, we denote a hypercone h according to its parameters as (h(⃗a, θ)). Please

298   4.4   OOD INFERENCE
299
300   During inference, the hypercones are employed to determine whether a new observation in the
301   embedding space, z, is ID or OOD. This decision is made by checking whether or not the observation
302   falls within both the angular and radial boundaries of any of the generated hypercones, in any of the
303   clusters, using the same method described in Section 4.3. We use z ∈ hi to mean that observation z
304   falls within both angular and radial boundaries of hypercone hi . If it does, it is labeled as ID, and
305
      OOD otherwise. Thus, the level-set estimation formulation from Section 2 transforms to an OOD
      detector framework defined as:
306

308                                          ID      if ∃ hi ∈ Hl ∀l ∈ Y s.t. z ∈ hi
                         Decision(z) =                                                                   (13)
309
                                             OOD     otherwise
310
      The hypercones inherently aim to delineate the contour of ID observations by allowing for fluid
311   boundaries between ID and OOD observations in different areas of the embedding space, as opposed
312   to existing approaches that rely on a single distance threshold for the entire space Sehwag et al.
313   (2021); Sun et al. (2022b). Additionally, by utilizing ID observations as the hypercone axes, we not
314   only ensure that we generate the contour by scanning the appropriate directions, but also facilitate the
315   generation of overlapping hypercones in densely populated areas of the embedding space. This ap-
316   proach smooths out the contour’s surface, dimming the effects of outliers, akin to fitting a polynomial
317   curve using interpolation techniques.

      5.4   HACk -OOD PARAMETERS
358
359   As discussed in Section 4, each hypercone’s opening angle is determined by the cosine distance
360   to its k-th nearest neighbor, where k is a tunable parameter. We propose an automatic selection
361   method called Adaptive k, which, though not optimal, performs well across datasets and architectures.
362   Selecting k optimally would require a holdout OOD set, but, as noted in Section 1, we assume no
363   access to such data. Thus, we take a heuristic approach that chooses a specific k for each l ∈ Y
364   by regularizing an informed upper bound for k by a factor of the number of class observations and
365
      feature dimensions, while at the same time incorporating the point density of the class. This is further
      discussed in Appendix Section A.3. The objective of using this method to choose k is to remove the
366
      burden of searching for the best k, which is further explored in 5.6.

469   5.6.1       PARAMETERS
470
471
      HACk -OOD only relies on one key parameter: the number of nearest neighbors (k) used to compute
      the hypercone opening angle. As the opening angle increases, the number of observations within each
472
      hypercone grows. A smaller angle provides a more precise contour of ID observations, assuming
473
      sufficient data-points to avoid gaps between hypercones where ID observations may go undetected.
474   Narrow hypercones may also fail to represent low-density areas accurately. Conversely, a larger angle
475   captures more ID observations but risks including OOD observations. Figure 1 shows FPR95 for
476   different values of k. The blue line represents FPR95 with a fixed k for all labels, while the orange
477   and green lines show FPR95 using Adaptive k. Adaptive k involves an additional regularization
478   factor, so we test the effect with and without regularization, respectively. In the latter two cases, k
479   represents the maximum value of k so the actual value for each class may vary, but will always be
480   less than or equal to this value. The vertical black line marks N4 , the maximum k used in our main
481   experiments. Its intersection with the orange line represents the results reported in Section 5.5.
482   The FPR95 decreases sharply as k increases, reaching a minimum before gradually rising again. As
483   long as k is not too small, its impact on the results remains limited. However, Adaptive k chooses a
484   value of k for each label that yields an FPR95 close to the observed minimum, making it a strong
485   approximation of the optimal k. We find that the regularization factor plays a crucial role in guiding
      Adaptive k to select an effective k, particularly in datasets like CIFAR-10, where class sizes are large.

524   Aligning the hypercone axes with ID train set observations is a highly effective technique for accu-

530   6     C ONCLUSION
531
532   This paper introduces HACk -OOD, a novel approach to post-training OOD detection. It constructs
533   class contours in a classifier’s embedding space using multi-dimensional hypercone projections. Our
534   method demonstrates SOTA performance in challenging feature spaces, and performs comparably to
535   other SOTA methods in simpler feature spaces. We plan to address in the future the optimal selection
536   of both k and the preliminary radial boundary, as well as explore the effect of different centroids
537   on HACk -OOD’s performance. We look forward to exploring the potential of hypercone-assisted
538   contour generation for other applications, such as classification and feature space explainability.

806   A.3     H EURISTIC A PPROACH FOR C HOOSING k
807
808   We use θ as a proxy to arrive at the maximum value for k. Here, we would like to remind the reader
809   that the opening angle of the hypercone is only half of the total angle the hypercone spans (see
      Figure 2). We take the limiting case of a uniformly distributed two dimensional class. To maintain the


                                                       15
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
      convexity of the hypercones, π2 is chosen as the maximum allowable angle. As N → ∞, selecting
811
      k = N4 neighbors yields this angle, where N is the number of observations in a class.
812
813   In higher dimensions, however, π2 covers a much smaller portion of the space, so k = N4 serves as a
814   conservative upper bound for k in a space with greater than two dimensions.
815
      In making a final choice for k per class we:
816
817         1. Scale k using the inverse logarithmic function of the ratio between the number of observa-
818            tions in the class and the dimensionality. The distribution of n points in the d dimensional
819            space should affect the choice of k. A large number of points in a small dimensional space
820            allows for narrower hypercones to be generated, while fewer points in a higher dimensional
821            space necessitates broader hypercones. In order to accommodate this distributional effect on
822            k, we scale it using a regularization factor ζ as follows:
823                                                  k = k ∗ ζ(n, d)                                  (14)
824
825            where,
826
                                                                  1
                                                ζ(n, d) =                                             (15)
827                                                         1 + log(n/d)
828         2. Additionally, given that k = N4 depends on a uniformly distributed space, we scale the
829            value by the ratio of point density between our class and a uniformly distributed class of
830            approximately the same size. We generate a synthetic dataset of features zi drawn from
831            U (α, β) such that Zunif orm = {zi ∈ Rn ∀i ∈ {1, . . . , N }|zi ∼ U (α, β)}, where α is
832            the minimum value of our class feature observations and β the maximum, with the same
833            dimensions as our class. We compute the cosine distance to the i-th nearest neighbor, where
834            i ∈ {f ∗ k ∀f ∈ {0.05, 0.10, 0.15, ..., 1} | k = N4 }, for both the original class in our
835            dataset and the synthetic uniformly distributed class. The ratio of mean cosine distance
836
               values between the two classes is also used to scale the value k.
837
      Note that the final approximation for k is not optimal and in future work we plan to explore optimal
838   selection of k.
