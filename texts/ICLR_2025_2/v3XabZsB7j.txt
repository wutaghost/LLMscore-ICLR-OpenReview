009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Can variational auto-encoders (VAEs) generate flexible continuous latent space
012            for long electrocardiogram (ECG) segments and reconstruct the input? A folded
013            VAE architecture is introduced in this study which is able to encode long ECG
014            segments by splitting an input segment into folds and process them in sequence
015            using a narrow field-of-view in the encoder and concatenate them at the end, in-
016            stead of processing the long segment at a time. The VAE decoder follows similar
017            folding and concatenation strategy for reconstruction of the original ECG seg-
018            ments. The proposed folded VAE architecture is able to generate better recon-
019
               struction of long 30-second ECG segments compared to unfolded classical VAE
               approach which often produce trivial reconstruction of long ECG segments. Ex-
020
               perimental results show that the latent representation generated by our folded VAE
021
               architecture not only retains rich compressed information but also aids designing
022            interpretable models by providing decision-making insights.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
026
027
      Variational autoencoders (VAE) are powerful tool for generative modeling. Due to their inherent
028
      continuous latent space, operations such as random sampling and interpolation becomes possible.
029
      The reconstruction ability of VAEs is an important feature which has many applications includ-
030   ing prototype-based deep learning interpretability methods for time-series (Gee et al., 2019; Zhang
031   et al., 2023; Li et al., 2018). Reconstruction of VAEs for longer physiological time-series, such as
032   electrocardiogram (ECG), of various morphology and signal quality remains under explored.
033
      Prototype classifiers are VAE networks where observations are classified based on their similarity
034
      to one or more prototype observations within the dataset (Li et al., 2018). The proximity between
035
      prototype and observations is measured in a flexible and adaptive latent space (Li et al., 2018; Gee
036   et al., 2019; Zhang et al., 2023). Reconstruction ability of VAE’s decoder module of prototypes and
037   observations is crucial for communicating interpretability to the end users, as well as, to validate
038   the claimed prototypes against a given observation for knowledge exploration. Reconstruction of
039   ECG data using VAEs has been explored in several occasions in the literature including prototype
040   learning in intrinsic interpretability literature of deep learning (Gee et al., 2019; Zhang et al., 2023),
041   and ECG generation using VAEs (Kuznetsov et al., 2021; Beetz et al., 2022; Jang et al., 2021).
042   ECG segment reconstruction, which was often attempted in these studies, either produce short 3-10
043   second ECG segments or healthy heart beats such as sinus rhythms. The quality of VAE-sampled
044
      latent representation and the ability of VAE’s decoder module to reproduce the source sample needs
      to be investigated for long ECG segments and a broad spectrum of heart beats.
045
046   VAEs well reconstruct shorter ECG segments, as shown in Figure 1-a, but generates trivial recon-
047   struction for longer 10 seconds and 30 seconds fragments, shown in Figure 1-b,c. This limitation
048   of VAEs’ reconstruction ability of ECG data needs to be improved to be useful for respective appli-
049   cations such as visualisation of prototype and observation in deep learning interpretability studies
050
      based on prototype learning.
051   For improving the reconstruction ability of VAEs, motivated by the concept of manifold learning
052   which considers that the dimensionality of many data sets is only artificially high, we propose a
053   folded encoder/decoder architecture of VAE which narrows down the input field-of-view by splitting
      a long ECG input segment into small folds at the beginning of the encoder/decoder followed by a

054
      concatenation at the end. The consumption of a long segment into folds by the encoder/decoder
055   backbone allows the network to learn essential ECG features and produce better reconstruction.

096   2.2   VARIATIONAL AUTO - ENCODERS (VAE S )
097
098   Autoencoders (AEs) are one kind of neural network which consists of two parts - encoder e and
099   decoder d (Figure 2-a, excluding the sampler which can sample from latent distribution). The en-
100   coder produces compressed latent representation Z from original high-dimensional input space X
101   by learning a non-linear transformation e : X → Z. The decoder, on the other hand, learns a
102   transformation d : Z → X which tries to recover X from the latent representation Z. AEs map
103   the input to a latent vector in a deterministic way z = e(x) which limits the decoders to reconstruct
104
      x̂ = d(z) = d(e(x)) from a region in the latent space that was never seen by the decoder during the
      training (Ghosh et al., 2019).
105
106   VAEs, on the other hand, sample a latent vector z ∼ N (µ, σ) by parameterizing a diagonal Guassian
107   distribution N (µ, σ) where the parameters µ(x) and σ(x) are mapped from input x (Kingma &
      Welling, 2013).

      2.3   F OLDING AN ECG SIGNAL
109
110   Motivated by the principle of manifold learning, which states the idea that the dimensionality of
111   many data sets is only artificially high (Martinetz & Schulten, 1994; Roweis & Saul, 2000), the
112   problem of encoding an N second ECG recording should be broken down to encoding N number of
113   only one-second ECG segments, which is likely to contain as few as one or two heart beats, followed
114   by a concatenation of encoded folded segments to yield the final encoding. A folded encoding and
115   decoding can be summarised by the Equation 1 and 2,
                                                        n

121   where e(x) and d(z) are encoding and decoding which are achieved by applying them over folded
122   inputs xi and folded sampled vector zi respectively.
123
      Figure 2-a, b show the concept of ECG folding, in encoder - concatenating encoded segments,
124
      sampling from the merged encoding and handing to the decoder, where the opposite is performed
125   including folding the sampled encoding, decoding each fold and finally merging them to yield the
126   final reconstruction. A single network backbone was used by the encoder to encode each fold and
127   the similar shared backbone strategy was followed in the decoder. The use of a shared encoding
128   (or decoding) sub-network forces it to learn distribution of ECG beats (such as, folds in a manifold
129   ECG recording) to encode (or decode). The use of a shared sub-network is expected to enforce in
130   learning general ECG heartbeat features discarding unnecessary information including noise.

132   2.4   VAE ARCHITECTURE
133
134
       Figure 2-a shows the concept of shared backbone encoder and decoder for folded ECG and Figure 2-
      b shows a block diagram of corresponding neural network layers for 30 second long ECG input
135
      sampled at 64Hz.
136
137   The encoder in Figure 2-b consists of four convolution-blocks which receives 30 one-second ECG
138   folds (1920 samples of a 30 second segment sampled at 64Hz turns into 30 64-sample folds) in
139   sequence and outputs a feature-map consisting of 4 spatial samples. A fixed number of 8 output
140
      channels per convolution layer was used to keep the network complexity low (thus, the encoder
      outputs a 8x4 feature-map). A concatenation layer merges 30 8x4 feature-maps in the second di-
141
      mension (spatial dimension in channel X spatial space) yielding a 8x120 feature-map which moves
142
      to the sampling layer. The use of pooling layer was avoided to retain the temporal relationship from
143   bottom to top of the four convolution layers and a convolution stride of 2 was used to down-sample
144   the input similar to a pooling layer.
145
146
      The sampler takes the merged feature-map of 8x120, flattens to 1x960 vector and maps to mean
      vector µ(x) and standard deviation vector σ(x) of a reduced dimension 1x480 which forms the
147
      basis of a Guassian distribution N (µ, σ) from which a latent vector z was sampled and passed to the
148
      decoder.
149
150   The decoder receives a 1x480 latent vector z, maps it back to 1x960 and reshapes to 8x120 which is
151   the dimension same as the concatenated output of the encoder. A folded strategy was adopted, same
152
      as the encoder, by splitting the 8x120 feature-map into 30 8x4 feature-maps which goes through
      four transposed convolution blocks, each producing a 1x64 output feature-map. A concatenation
153
      layer then merges 30 of these feature-maps and produce a final 1x1920 feature-map which is the
154
      same shape as the original input. The transposed convolution block in the decoder consists of an
155   up-sampling layer of scale 2 and a convolution layer with stride 1. A leaky-ReLU non-linearity
156   activation was used in both encoder and decoder to avoid the dying ReLU problem of large number
157   of zero neurons due to negative bias (Maas et al., 2013).

      2.6   U SE CASE : S LEEP STAGE CLASSIFICATION FROM ECG
238   In addition to verifying VAE reconstruction ability using shared encoder/decoder backbone and
239   second-wise folded ECG, the quality of the sampled latent space representation was also tested
240   by using the representation in a context of 3-stage sleep classification from ECG signals including
241   Wake, NREM- and REM-sleep. It was hypothesized that the performance of folded ECG with shared
242   VAE encoder/decoder backbone should not perform lesser than the performance of an unfolded

256   2.8   N ETWORK A RCHITECTURE
257
258    The high-level hybrid explainable architecture is shown in Figure 3. The architecture adds two
259   additional components to the VAE module described above: The Parameterizer and Aggregator.
260   The Parameterizer is inspired by Alvarez-Melis & Jaakkola (2018); Zhang et al. (2022) and is used
261   to generate relevance weights of the different splits for each input sample. Whereas in Zhang et al.
262   (2022) the Parameterizer module was used to generate relevence weights for encoded concepts gen-
263   erated from a self-attention mechanism, here the Parameterizer aims to do the same for the prede-
264   fined splits. It is important to note that unlike the encoder of the VAE, the Parameterizer accepts
265   the entire signal. This provides the Parameterizer with a higher level of context across the entire 30
266
      second sample without the VAE being required to compute over the entire signal. The output of the
      Parameterizer is a vector w ∈ RN where N is the number of splits and wn ∈ w is the weight for
267
      split n, for a given sample.
268
269   The Aggregator first calculates the linear combination of the flattened encoded splits (or folds) with
      the relevance weights and produces a probability weighted by the predicted relevance of each split.

270
      The encoded splits are flattened using a fully connected layer where the output is a vector p ∈ RC
271   where pc is the probability of the entire sample being class c.

      2.9   M ODEL TRAINING AND TESTING
307
308   The classifier sub-network is trained simultaneously with the VAE and the objective of the network
309   is to minimise the combined reconstruction and classification loss,
310
311
                                           L = Lc (Xi , yi ) + LV AE                                     (6)
312   Cross-entropy loss was found suitable to account for the classification error and the combined loss
313   was back-propagated through the whole network (classifier and VAE encoder/decoder sub-networks)
314   by using ADAM optimiser for faster convergence. A small learning rate 0.0001 was found suitable
315   for training the network with maximum epoch as high as 400. A validation loss was calculated end
316   of each epoch and any improvement in validation loss was considered as a milestone throughout
317   the end of the maximum epoch and during each milestone the model was saved and at the end test
318   performance was calculated with the latest model.
319   Subject-wise split was considered for the sleep-stage classification task in both the datasets where a
320   80/20 split of subjects was done for training and testing and the training subjects were further split
321   into 80/20 for training and validation. Recordings in training were then segmented into 30 second
322   sleep epochs, mixed together and used for model training and a similar strategy was adopted for
323   the validation and testing groups. Pre-processing steps include downsampling to a common 100Hz
      frequency and removal of baseline wander in ECG signals.

398   4    D ISCUSSION
399
400
       The results from unsupervised of the VAE architecture indicate a good pattern of reconstruction
401
      for a higher number of splits. This aligns closely with the reconstruction capabilities demonstrated
402
      in previous VAE studies on ECG which demonstrated good reconstruction for 3-10 second samples
403   (Kuznetsov et al., 2021; Beetz et al., 2022; Jang et al., 2021). The poorer reconstruction for 3 and
404   10 fold variants provide further evidence that CNN-based VAE architectures struggle to encode long
405   ECG signals with sufficient granularity as discussed in Figure 1.
406
      Interestingly, though reconstruction was improved by the addition of more data splits, the classifi-
407
      cation performance of the resultant model was poor compared to other architectures trained on the
408
      MESA dataset. For example, Erdenebayar et al. (2020) used a deep network of gated-recurrent units
409   for three-class sleep stage classification achieving an 80.07% test accuracy while Tang et al. (2022)
410   utilised a CNN feature extractor combined with a novel transfer-learning approach to achieve high
411   generalisability for sleep stage classification across subjects with an average accuracy of 80.6%.
412   Previously, Immaculate Joy et al. (2024) demonstrated a high level of accuracy for cardio-vascular
413   disease detection through a similar method of using the latent space encoding of a VAE model as
414   the learned features for a classifier. This discrepancy suggests two possible conjectures for why our
415   current model was unable to perform. Firstly, the model’s hyperparameters may not be correctly op-
416   timised, leading to poor generalisability to the test set. Indeed during training, a level of over-fitting
417
      comparing the loss on training and validation in later epochs. Combined with the good reconstruc-
      tion, this may indicate that the Parameterizer module may be too large. Another conjecture for
418
      the model’s poor performance may be that the information between splits is not being captured by
419
      the VAE encoder and subsequently not being adequately reflected in the relevance weights gener-
420   ated by the Parameterizer when encoding the entire signal. The American Sleep Academy of Sleep
421   Medicine (AASM) defines features from multiple heartbeats as relevant to sleep stage classification
422   as patterns such as sustained bradycardia or tachycardia, which provides some biological precedence
423   for inter-split information (Iber et al., 2007).
424
      The folded-VAE architecture, equipped with Parameterizer and Aggregator modules, provides a
425
      flexible framework to design an intrinsically interpretable deep learning model architecture similar
426   to (Alvarez-Melis & Jaakkola, 2018; Zhang et al., 2023). The only exception is (Zhang et al.,
427   2023) was trying to connect the neighboring sample points to form concepts, while in our folded
428   VAE strategy, each fold is seen to be a concept as granular as required based on the given problem.
429   For example, for a single heart beat level variation, a 30 second long ECG segment can be divided
430   into 30-splits which would be a single second-wise split VAE where the encoder would try to learn a
431   single beat on average in a single second ECG segment. Sleep stage classification problem probably
      require to observe heart-beat pattern from a number of consecutive beats which can be considered


                                                          8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      as a hyperparameter; we have explored 6-fold and 10-fold only in current study and requires further
433   investigation.
434
435   The sampling step in the VAE encoder used the concatenated folds to sample the encoding which
436
      then acts as an input to the decoder (shown in Figure 2). It can be argued that the sampler may find it
      difficult to merge information between folds to form a continuous space. An alternative strategy can
437
      be to take sampling each fold and then concatenate the sampled folds. This sampling-folds first, fol-
438
      lowed by concatenation to form VAE encoder output is used in generating the reconstructions shown
439   in the results (Figure 4 and Figure 5) with no phase shift issue of the folds in the reconstruction. Any
440   further study should consider this alteration of the sampler and concatenation into account.

      5   C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
444
       Variational autoencoders are growing in populatity for ECG analysis because they allow for simple
445
      embedding into a latent space that can be easily sampled from. However, most studies focus on
446
      applications where the duration of ECG signal being analysed is short. The current study aimed
447   to investigate the capability of CNN variational autoencoders on long ECG signals and suggest a
448   potential architecture to address shortcomings by splitting the ECG into smaller chunks. Though
449   good reconstruction was observed for a higher number of splits, classification accuracy for test
450   subjects is lower than other architectures trained on the same dataset. Future work is required to
451   determine the exact mechanism for the poor classification accuracy, and to improve on the initial
452   interpretable architecture proposed.
