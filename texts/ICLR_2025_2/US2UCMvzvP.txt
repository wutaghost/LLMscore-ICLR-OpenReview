009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Large language models (LLMs) excel in various tasks, but their performance in
012            non-English languages remains limited due to imbalanced training data. To ad-
013            dress this limitation, we explore how to transform chat LLMs to non-English.
014            Chat LLMs offer more advanced capabilities than base LLMs, such as multi-turn
015            conversation and alignment with human preferences. However, transforming chat
016            LLMs presents greater challenges than base LLMs. First, how can we effectively
017            transfer advanced capabilities without their supervised data in target languages?
018
               Second, how can we prevent the original capabilities from catastrophic forgetting
               without replaying their training procedure in English? We target these issues by
019
               introducing a simple framework called TransLLM. TransLLM divides the transfer
020
               problem into some common sub-tasks with the translation chain-of-thought, elim-
021            inating the need for complex training data. More importantly, TransLLM uses
022            two key strategies to prevent catastrophic forgetting: Low-rank adaptation, which
023            preserves the original LLM parameters during training, and recovery KD, which
024            utilizes data generated by the chat LLM itself to recover the original knowledge
025            from the frozen parameters. Experiments conducted across five languages and
026            three LLMs demonstrate the superiority of TransLLM. Notably, TransLLM out-
027            performs GPT-4 in Thai, demonstrating higher levels of helpfulness and safety,
028            using just 8B parameters and publicly accessible data. Our analysis demonstrates
029
               how recovery KD combined with LoRA helps mitigate catastrophic forgetting.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
      Large language models (LLMs), like GPT-4 (OpenAI, 2023) and Llama-3 (Dubey et al., 2024),
034
      have shown strong performance in various tasks, particularly in following instructions. However,
035
      most available data is primarily in English, hindering the performance of LLMs in non-English
036   languages. Even advanced LLMs, such as GPT-4, encounter safety challenges when tested with
037   non-English languages (Yong et al., 2023).
038
039
      Gathering extensive, high-quality data and performing intricate training from scratch in non-English
      languages is notably costly. Therefore, recent works attempt to transform off-the-shelf LLMs from
040
      English to non-English. Many studies concentrate on base LLMs that are only pre-trained (PT)
041
      on self-supervised corpora, utilizing knowledge distillation (KD) data produced by powerful LLMs
042   such as GPT-4 for transfer training and instruction tuning. For example, Chinese-Alpaca (Cui et al.,
043   2023) and Typhoon (Pipatanakul et al., 2023) initially continual pre-train base LLMs on target lan-
044   guages, followed by instruction tuning with English and translated KD data.
045
      Chat LLMs are enhanced beyond base LLMs through supervised fine-tuning (SFT) and reinforce-
046
      ment learning with human feedback (RLHF), making them more effective for advanced capabilities
047
      such as multi-turn conversations and aligning with human preferences (Ouyang et al., 2022). Unfor-
048   tunately, the SFT and RLHF data used for training the chat LLMs, such as Llama-2-chat (Touvron
049   et al., 2023), is expensive to obtain and usually not released to the public alongside model weights.
050   Consequently, transforming chat LLMs involves more challenges. Firstly, we need to transfer these
051   advanced capabilities without STF and RLHF data in target languages. Moreover, during continual
052   learning, LLMs face a substantial risk of forgetting original knowledge, which can be mitigated by
053   replaying previous training procedures (Mok et al., 2023). When transforming base LLMs, we can
      replay the PT process with widely available English corpora (Xu et al., 2023b). However, when

067   Recently, Huang et al. (2024b) proposed Chat Vector, which is derived by subtracting the weights of
068   a base LLM from its chat version, to equip the continual pre-trained base LLMs with chat capabilities
069   and achieve promising performance. However, the Chat Vector in English may not be well-suited
070
      for target languages, leading to sub-optimal transfer performance. Additionally, while Chat Vector
      helps mitigate catastrophic forgetting to a certain degree, it risks producing a high proportion of
071
      English responses (Huang et al., 2024b).
072
073   This paper addresses these issues by presenting a new framework named TransLLM. TransLLM
074   employs the strategy of translation chain-of-thought (TCOT) (Zhang et al., 2023), performing the
075   response generation in the target language as a sequence of sub-tasks step by step: It first translates
076
      the query to English; then responds to the query in English; and finally generates the non-English
      answer based on all the above context. However, we highlight that the original TCOT may not work
077
      due to the LLMs’ insufficient performance on these sub-tasks. Therefore, TransLLM enhances
078
      performance in target language modeling and translation via continual pre-training.
079
080   More importantly, TransLLM employs two key strategies to recover the original chat capabilities
081   after continual pre-training. Specifically, we employ the low-rank adaptation (LoRA) (Hu et al.,
082
      2021) for training to maintain the original LLM parameters. We introduce recovery KD, utilizing
      data in English generated by the chat LLM itself, to recover the original chat capabilities from the
083
      frozen parameters. The insight is that fitting the recovery KD data with the original parameters is
084
      more straightforward than optimizing the LoRA parameters for the same task. This enables the LLM
085   to learn a generalizable pattern that reduces the contribution of LoRA parameters when generating
086   English content, thereby mitigating catastrophic forgetting.
087
088
      We conduct comprehensive experiments across five languages (Thai, Arabic, Portuguese, Telugu,
      and Turkish) and three LLMs (Llama-2/3/3.1). We assess the effectiveness of TransLLM in terms
089
      of both helpfulness and safety. TransLLM outperforms strong open-source baselines and ChatGPT1
090
      on all settings. Notably, in Thai, TransLLM surpasses GPT-4 by 32.5% and 17.5% for the first and
091   second turns on the multi-turn conversation benchmark (MT-Bench). Additionally, it achieves an
092   8.65% gain over GPT-4 on the safety benchmark (AdvBenchmark).

123   2     M ETHOD
124
125   As shown in Figure 2, TransLLM consists of the following steps: (1) Model extension, (2) target
126   language pre-training, (3) translation pre-training, and (4) transfer fine-tuning on TCOT, recover
127   KD, and translation data.
128
129   2.1     M ODEL A RCHITECTURE
130
      Nowadays, popular LLMs use byte-level byte pair encoding (BBPE) tokenizer (Wang et al., 2020)
131
      following GPT-2 (Radford et al., 2019). However, the tokenizer is usually developed on the English-
132
      dominated dataset, therefore this tokenizer often tokenizes each non-English character to several
133   bytes resulting in a long sequence. Inspired by Cui et al. (2023) and Pipatanakul et al. (2023), we
134   extend the vocabulary using monolingual data of the target language to improve the model efficiency.
135
136
      LoRA is a parameter-efficient training method, which is another technique that has been widely used
      for transferring the LLM. However, in this work, we use LoRA not only for efficiency but also for
137
      preserving the original parameters. Considering a weight matrix W ∈ Rd×k of the target LLM,
138
      LoRA represents its update ∆W using two low rank matrices B ∈ Rd×r and A ∈ Rr×k as follows:
139
140                                         h̃ = W h, and ĥ = h̃ + ∆W h = h̃ + BAh,                                                            (1)
141   where r denotes the pre-determined rank, h denotes the input, h̃ denotes the output of the original
142   module, and ĥ denotes the output of the updated module. During training, the original W is frozen,
143   so that original knowledge can be recovered by the recovery KD.
144
145   2.2     T RAINING
146
147   2.2.1    TARGET L ANGUAGE P RE -T RAINING
148
      The chat LLMs are often insufficient on target language modeling due to the imbalanced training
149
      corpus. Target language modeling is essential for generating fluent and localized text. Furthermore,
150
      many works show that the monolingual pre-training can significantly improve the translation qual-
151   ity (Zheng et al., 2019; Xu et al., 2023a). To build a solid foundation for the target language, we
152   pre-train the TransLLM model on monolingual data of the target language using the pre-training
153   objective.
154
      We do not introduce any English task in this stage because of the following two reasons: first, the
155
      pre-training involves expensive computation, and it is also difficult to find a proper mixing ratio
156
      between the English and target language data; second, the English embeddings are rarely updated
157   on the target language data, therefore all the parameters of original LLM are almost unchanged.
158
159   2.2.2    T RANSLATION P RE -T RAINING
160
161   TCOT relies on translation to bridge the English and the target language. Therefore, we introduce
      translation pre-training to improve the bidirectional translation quality between English and the


                                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      target language. Inspired by mBART (Liu et al., 2020), we use the special language ID token to
163   specify the language of the text. Therefore, TransLLM is easy to extend for the multilingual setting
164   as readily as mBART. Considering we transform the LLM from language α to β, where α is typically
165   English in this paper, we formulate the parallel pair (sα , sβ ) as two instances: cat(sα , ⟨β⟩, sβ ) and
166   cat(sβ , ⟨α⟩, sα ), where cat(·) denotes the concatenate operation and ⟨·⟩ denotes the language id
167   token.
168
      The translation training could disturb the original English embeddings. Thus, we introduce English
169   monolingual data into the translation pre-training stage. Specifically, we randomly insert the trans-
170   lation instance between English monolingual data using line break “\n” as the separator. Based on
171   the first stage, we train the TransLLM model on the mixed data using the pre-training objective.
172
173   2.2.3    T RANSFER F INE -T UNING
174
175   The two-stage pre-training enables the TransLLM in target language modeling and cross-lingual
176
      translation. However, the TransLLM inevitably forgets the original knowledge. In this stage, we
      aim to recover the original knowledge and teach the TransLLM model how to perform TCOT and
177
      when to do translation.
178
179
      Recovery Knowledge Distillation Data. Previous works focus on transferring knowledge from
180
      base LLMs. To teach the base model how to follow human instructions, previous works perform
181   knowledge distillation with strong chat LLMs as the teacher by using the Alpaca dataset (Taori et al.,
182   2023). The Alpaca dataset generates queries using the self-instruct technique (Wang et al., 2022),
183   then responds using ChatGPT or GPT-4. Although the vanilla KD works well for base LLMs, we
184   argue that it is not helpful for chat LLMs as shown in Sec. 4.2. To address this problem, we introduce
185   the recovery KD that uses the target chat LLM to generate the responses. Although the recovery KD
186   data are often worse than GPT-4 KD data, it will help the model to recover the knowledge from
187   the original LLM parameters. We also introduce a special token ⟨RESPONSE⟩ in recovery KD
188   to direct the behavior of the TransLLM model. Considering a KD instance in English with query
189
      q α and answer aα , we formulate the query and label as x = q α and y = cat(⟨RESPONSE⟩, aα )
      respectively.
190
191
      TCOT Data. Based on the recovery KD data (q α , aα ), we use machine translation to ob-
192
      tain its translations (q β , aβ ). Finally, we can organize the TCOT data as x = q β and y =
193
      cat(⟨α⟩, q α , ⟨RESPONSE⟩, aα , ⟨β⟩, aβ ). That means when we input a query in β, the model should
194   first translate it into α as q α . Then the model should RESPONSE the English query as aα using
195   original knowledge as we teach in recovery KD. Finally, the TCOT outputs the response in β as aβ
196   based on all previous sequences. As discussed in Sec. 4.3, the previous sequences also contribute to
197   the final response. Different from Zhang et al. (2023), we use special tokens instead of natural lan-
198   guage to direct the model’s behavior. This is because the special tokens will not disturb the English
199   embeddings and make it convenient to extract results.
200
201   Translation Data. Due to the TCOT data, the model may be confused about the translation in-
202   struction in β without extra translation SFT. Therefore, we also construct bi-direction translation
203   data based on previous parallel pairs (q α , q β ) and (aα , aβ ). Taking the parallel pair (q α , q β ) as an
204
      example, we first wrap the source sentence using translation prompt templates as prompt(q α ).2 Then
      we can obtain x = prompt(q α ) and y = cat(⟨β⟩, q β ).
205
206   Finally, we randomly mix all the data mentioned above and fine-tune the TransLLM model by the
207   fine-tuning objective. Note: To maintain the original model parameters, we do not merge the
208   LoRA modules into the main backbone until the entire training process is finished.
209

      3.1     E XPERIMENT S ETUP
228   Settings. Our experiments involve five languages: Thai (TH), Arabic (AR), Portuguese (PT), Tel-
229   ugu (TE), and Turkish (TR). These languages differ in their resources, scripts, and language fam-
230   ilies. We establish three experimental tasks to comprehensively assess the TransLLM: (1) Trans-
231   form Llama-2-Chat-7B to TH. In this primary task, we evaluate performance across various bench-
232   marks, and all analyses are based on this task. (2) Transform Llama-3-Instruct-8B to AR. This task
233   examines the generalizability of TransLLM across different languages and LLMs. (3) Transform
234   Llama-3.1-Instruct-8B to all five languages. This task evaluates the flexibility of TransLLM in the
235   multilingual setting.
236
237   Models. We implement our pipeline using Chinese-Llama-Alpaca-23 and Llama-Factory 4
238
      projects for Llama-2 and Llama-3/3.1, respectively. We only expanding the vocabulary for Llama-2,
      as the vocabulary of Llama-3 is already optimized for multilingual use. Following Cui et al. (2023),
239
      we use SentencePiece (Kudo & Richardson, 2018) to learn the TH vocabulary on the monolingual
240
      TH data that we use in target language pre-training. After we merge the TH vocabulary with the
241   original vocabulary, the final vocabulary size (including 3 special tokens) is 43,012. The new em-
242   beddings are randomly initialized. For all LLMs, we apply LoRA on the weights of the attention
243   module and multi-layer perceptron blocks. The LoRA rank is set as r = 64. For a fair comparison,
244   we re-implement most of the baselines in TH by our setting following their papers. The details of
245   our model and baselines are in Appendix A.1.
246
247   Training Data. For target language pre-training, we use the monolingual TH and AR data from
248   mC4 (Xue et al., 2020). We first filter the monolingual data using the sensitive word list to reduce the
249   harmful text. Then, we use MinHashLSH5 to deduplicate documents following GPT-3 (Brown et al.,
250   2020). Finally, we have about 11 and 30 billion tokens of TH and AR data. Compared to the 2 trillion
251
      tokens English (EN) data used in Llama-2, the dataset is quite small. For translation pre-training,
      we collect the EN-TH and EN-AR parallel data from CCAligned (Chaudhary et al., 2019), Tatoeba
252
      Challenge Data (Tiedemann, 2020), and OpenSubtitles (Lison et al., 2018). To create an extremely
253
      low-resource scenario, we refrain from using any monolingual or parallel data for PT, TE, and TR.
254   We directly use the EN documents released in the Pile dataset which has been pre-processed (Gao
255   et al., 2020). We randomly sample 1 million parallel pairs and EN documents respectively for
256   translation pre-training. For the transfer fine-tuning, we use the query from the Alpaca dataset and
257   generate the response using the target chat LLMs for recovery KD. We further use Google Translate
258   to obtain TCOT and translation data based on recovery KD data. In our preliminary study, Google
259   Translate may translate the variable in code which is not desirable for the chat LLM. Thus, we use
260   GPT-4 to recognize the “do not translate” part. We use the same monolingual and translation data
261   for baselines, while we use the Alpaca-GPT-4 (Peng et al., 2023) as the SFT data following their
262
      setting. There are a total of 52K queries in the Alpaca dataset, we use the first 50K queries as the
      training set and the rest 2K queries as the validation set in our experiments. We provide the training
263
      details in Appendix A.2.
264

266
      instruction following benchmark AlpacaEval (Li et al., 2023), causal commonsense reasoning

      5   R ELATED W ORKS
498
499   Recently, there have been many works (Cui et al., 2023; Pipatanakul et al., 2023) that attempt
500   to transfer knowledge from English to non-English for LLMs. PloyLM (Wei et al., 2023) adopts
501
      multilingual pre-training based on the curriculum learning strategy that gradually exposes more
      low-resource corpus. Zhu et al. (2023) focus on building semantic alignment with cross-lingual in-
502
      struct tuning and translation training. ChatGPT (OpenAI, 2022) and GPT-4 (OpenAI, 2023) are also
503
      well-known multilingual LLMs. Chat Vector (Huang et al., 2024b) and CALM (Bansal et al., 2024)
504   transfer English knowledge through model integration. Some other works focus on transfer reason-
505   ing capabilities: Qin et al. (2023) introduce cross-lingual prompting to improve zero-shot chain-of-
506   thought reasoning across languages; She et al. (2024) propose multilingual alignment-as-preference
507   optimization to align reasoning across languages. The closest related work is PLUG (Zhang et al.,
508   2023), which directly fine-tunes base LLMs using TCOT data. In contrast to PLUG, we propose a
509   systematic framework for transforming chat LLMs. We emphasize that TCOT’s effectiveness largely
510   depends on the performance of its sub-tasks. More importantly, we introduce how to preserve the
511   original knowledge of the chat LLM. Concurrently, Xu et al. (2024) prompts the chat LLMs to gen-
512
      erate a large-scale SFT dataset named Magpie. However, Xu et al. (2024) focus on applying KD to
      the base LLM using Magpie, rather than the chat LLM itself. Besides, we explain how recovery KD
513
      combined with LoRA helps prevent catastrophic forgetting.
514
515
516   6   C ONCLUSION
517
518
      Chat LLMs have been specifically optimized for chat usage and therefore are helpful and safe in
519
      the dominant language. In this paper, we propose a framework for transforming an off-the-shelf
520
      chat LLM to other languages. In this framework, we utilize TCOT to transfer chat knowledge
521   and further enhance the TCOT’s sub-tasks using publicly available data. To recover the original
522   knowledge, we propose the recovery KD method supplemented with LoRA. The experiments across
523   different languages and LLMs show that we transfer desired capabilities to the target language and
524   outperform strong baselines in both helpfulness and safety. Overall, we hope this work can become
525   the foundation for developing safe LLMs in many languages other than English.
526
527
      Limitations and future works. Given our resource constraints, our experiments are focused
528   solely on LLMs with fewer than 8 billion parameters. For now, TransLLM is still highly dependent
529   on translation. Consequently, TransLLM can not handle the queries related to language features. A
530   straightforward approach is to train TransLLM to determine whether to respond using TCOT mode
531   or not. Due to the TCOT, the inference overhead of TransLLM is much longer than other base-
532   lines. Recently, Goyal et al. (2023) and Deng et al. (2023) show that the implicit chain-of-thought
533   achieves similar performance on reasoning tasks without additional inference overhead. We would
534   like to explore TransLLM with implicit TCOT in the future.
