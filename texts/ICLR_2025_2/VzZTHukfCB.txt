011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Current segmentation methods typically require many training images and precise
014            masks, while insufficient anomaly images hinder their application in industrial
015
               scenarios. To address such an issue, we explore producing diverse anomalies and
               accurate pixel-wise annotations. By observing the real production lines, we find
016
               that anomalies vary randomly in shape and appearance, whereas products hold
017
               globally consistent patterns with slight local variations. Such a characteristic in-
018            spires us to develop a Separation and Sharing Fine-tuning (SeaS) approach using
019            only a few abnormal and some normal images. Firstly, we propose the Unbalanced
020            Abnormal (UA) Text Prompt tailored to industrial anomaly generation, consisting
021            of one product token and several anomaly tokens. Then, for anomaly images, we
022            propose a Decoupled Anomaly Alignment (DA) loss to bind the attributes of the
023            anomalies to different anomaly tokens. Re-blending such attributes may produce
024            never-seen anomalies, achieving a high diversity of anomalies. For normal im-
025            ages, we propose a Normal-image Alignment (NA) loss to learn the products’ key
026
               features that are used to synthesize products with both global consistency and local
               variations. The two training processes are separated but conducted on a shared U-
027
               Net. Finally, SeaS produces high-fidelity annotations for the generated anomalies
028
               by fusing discriminative features of U-Net and high-resolution VAE features. The
029            extensive evaluations on the challenging MVTec AD and MVTec 3D AD dataset
030            (RGB images) demonstrate the effectiveness of our approach. For anomaly image
031            generation, on MVTec AD dataset, we achieve 1.88 on IS and 0.34 on IC-LPIPS,
032            while on the MVTec 3D AD dataset, we obtain 1.95 on IS and 0.30 on IC-LPIPS.
033            For the downstream task, by using our generated anomaly image-mask pairs, three
034            common segmentation methods achieve an average 11.17% improvement on IoU
035            on MVTec AD dataset, and a 15.49% enhancement in IoU on the MVTec 3D AD
036            dataset. The source code will be released publicly available.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
039

      Existing segmentation approaches require a large number of anomaly images with mask annotations,
041
      while the scarcity of anomaly images obstructs their application in industrial scenarios. To solve
042   this problem, generative methods for industrial scenarios have emerged to expand the training set of
043   segmentation models.
044
045
      To the best of our knowledge, generation approaches (Zavrtanik et al., 2021)in industrial scenarios
      can be broadly classified into two categories: Anomaly Generation (AG) and Anomaly Image
046
      Generation (AIG). AG methods (Li et al., 2021; Zavrtanik et al., 2021; Schlüter et al., 2022; Hu
047
      et al., 2024)generate anomalies only and merge them into the real normal images using different
048   strategies, e.g., CutPaste (Li et al., 2021)pastes a cropped normal region to normal images, which
049   simulates anomalies by misalignment. AnomalyDiffusion (Hu et al., 2024)generates anomalies by
050   a diffusion model, and edits anomalies onto the normal images guided by the anomaly masks, as
051   shown in Fig. 1(a). However, AG methods require anomaly masks as inputs, which easily suffer
052   from low fidelity and consistency in generation if these masks are unreasonably positioned. In
053   contrast to AG, as shown in Fig. 1(b), AIG approaches take a step further, generating anomalies
      and the industrial products that they lie in simultaneously. Therefore, AIG faces greater challenges

081   In real industrial manufacturing, the products in an individual production line are almost similar to
082   each other, while the anomalies are unforeseeable in shape and appearance. Such an observation
083   reveals a differentiated characteristic, i.e., the products satisfy global consistency with minor
084   variations in local details, while the anomalies hold randomness, which is rarely discussed in
085
      existing AG or AIG approaches. Motivated by such a characteristic, we propose a Separation and
      Sharing Fine-tuning method, short by SeaS, a controllable AIG method based on Stable Diffusion
086
      (Rombach et al., 2022). The key idea is to employ Unbalanced Abnormal (UA) Text Prompts con-
087
      taining a set of tokens that characterize products and anomalies separately, so that the anomaly
088   tokens align with the anomaly semantics for diverse generations, and a product token expresses a
089   globally consistent product surface. Specifically, to learn highly-diverse anomalies, we first pro-
090   pose a Decoupled Anomaly Alignment (DA) loss to bind the attributes of the anomalies to different
091   anomaly tokens. Recombining the decoupled attributes may produce anomalies that have never been
092   seen in the training dataset, therefore increasing the diversity of the generated anomalies. Secondly,
093   to learn globally-consistent patterns from products, we propose the Normal-image Alignment (NA)
094   loss. It enables the network to learn the key features of the product from normal images and fine-
095   tune a learnable embedding. Such an embedding ensures the preservation of global consistency
096
      amidst local detail variations. Thirdly, according to the experimental analysis, we find that ex-
      isting methods leverage the low-resolution features to predict the mask, which may introduce
097
      a large amount of boundary uncertainty. Thus, we propose a Refined Mask Prediction (RMP)
098
      branch to produce pixel-wise anomaly annotations for other downstream tasks. It combines the dis-
099   criminative U-Net features and high-resolution VAE features to generate accurate and crisp masks
100   in a progressive way. Extensive experiments on AIG and downstream anomaly segmentation tasks
101   show that SeaS outperforms the existing industrial anomaly generation methods. On MVTec AD
102   dataset, our model achieves 1.88 on IS metric and 0.34 on IC-LPIPS. Furthermore, training on the
103   image-mask pairs generated by SeaS, the downstream segmentation models achieve improvements
104   of average +5.53% AP and +11.17% IoU. On MVTec AD 3D dataset (RGB images), our method
105   attains 1.95 on IS metric and 0.30 on IC-LPIPS. Using the image-mask pairs generated by SeaS to
106   train the downstream segmentation models, we exhibit average improvements of +12.13% AP and
107
      +15.49% IoU.
      In summary, the key contribution of our approach lies in:

      Anomaly Image Generation. Early non-generative methods (DeVries & Taylor, 2017; Li et al.,
      2021; Zavrtanik et al., 2021) use data augmentation techniques to create anomaly images. Data aug-
122
      mentation techniques lack consistency in anomaly images, resulting in low fidelity. AG methods (Li
123
      et al., 2021; Zavrtanik et al., 2021; Schlüter et al., 2022; Hu et al., 2024; Gui et al., 2024) only gener-
124   ate anomalies and merge them into the real normal images. NSA (Schlüter et al., 2022) uses Poisson
125   Image Editing (Pérez et al., 2003) to facilitate the fusion of the cropped normal region. However,
126   AG methods require anomaly masks as inputs. If these masks are positioned in an unreasonable
127   manner, the generated images will have low fidelity and consistency. Previous AIG methods (Duan
128   et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2021a; Niu et al., 2020) are mainly based on GAN. Defect-GAN (Zhang
129   et al., 2021a) cannot generate masks. The masks produced by DFMGAN (Duan et al., 2023) often
130   do not align accurately with anomalies, limiting their utility in training segmentation models. We
131   propose a controllable AIG model based on Stable Diffusion to generate high-fidelity and diverse
132
      anomaly images with accurate masks.
133   Fine-tuning Diffusion Models. Fine-tuning is a potent strategy for enhancing specific capabilities
134   of pre-trained diffusion models (Gal et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2023b; Brooks et al., 2023). Person-
135   alized methods (Ruiz et al., 2023; Gal et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2024) utilize a small set of images
136   to fine-tune the diffusion model, thereby generating images of the same object. Several methods for
137   multi-concept image fine-tuning (Kumari et al., 2023; Xiao et al., 2023; Avrahami et al., 2023; Han
138
      et al., 2023; Jin et al., 2024) use cross-attention maps to align embeddings with individual concepts
      in the image. Nevertheless, they do not consider the diversity requirements between different con-
139
      cepts, which is important for industrial anomaly image generation. Thus, we propose a separation
140
      and sharing fine-tuning strategy for the different diversity needs of anomalies and products, which
141   independently learns products and anomalies on a shared U-Net.
142
143   Mask Prediction with Generation Method. Previous methods on mask prediction for generated
144
      images are mainly based on GANs (Zhang et al., 2021b; Li et al., 2022). However, these approaches
      do not guarantee the generation of accurate masks for exceedingly small datasets. Based on Sta-
145
      ble Diffusion (Rombach et al., 2022), some recent methods, i.e., DiffuMask (Wu et al., 2023b),
146
      DatasetDM (Wu et al., 2023a) and DatasetDiffusion (Nguyen et al., 2024), produce masks by ex-
147   ploiting the potential of the cross-attention maps. However, due to the low resolution of the cross-
148   attention maps, they are directly interpolated to a higher resolution to match the image size without
149   any auxiliary information, which leads to significant boundary uncertainty. We incorporate the high-
150   resolution features from the VAE decoder as auxiliary information for resolution retrieving, fusing
151   them with the discriminative features of U-Net decoder to generate accurate high-resolution masks.

302   3.3   R EFINED M ASK P REDICTION
303   High-fidelity pixel-wise annotations of anomalies play an important role in boosting segmentation
304   models. However, existing methods, such as DFMGAN (Duan et al., 2023) and AnomlayDiffusion
305   (Hu et al., 2024), produce anomaly masks that are not tightly matched with generated anomalies,
306   which is insufficient for training segmentation model. To address this issue, we design a cascaded
307   Refined Mask Prediction (RMP) branch, which is grafted onto the U-Net trained according to SeaS
308   (mentioned in Sec. 3.2). As shown in Fig. 4, RMP consists of two steps, firstly capturing discrimi-
309   native features from U-Net and secondly combining it with high-resolution features of VAE decoder
310
      to generate anomaly-matched masks.
311   Coarse Feature Extraction. The first step aims to extract a coarse but highly-discriminative feature
312   for anomalies from the U-Net decoder. Specifically, let F1 ∈ R32×32×1280 and F2 ∈ R64×64×640
313   denote the output feature of “up-2” and “up-3” layers of the decoder in U-Net, respectively. We
314   first leverage a 1 × 1 convolution block to compress the channel of F1 and F2 to F 1 ∈ R32×32×128
315   and F 2 ∈ R64×64×64 , respectively. Then, we upsample F 1 to 64 × 64 resolution and concatenate it
316
      with F 2 . Finally, four transformer layers are employed to fuse the concatenated features and obtain
317   a unified coarse feature F̂ ∈ R64×64×192 .
318   Mask Refinement Module. Directly upsampling the coarse feature F̂ to high resolution will re-
319   sult in a loss of anomaly details. Therefore, we design the Mask Refinement Module (MRM) to
320   refine the coarse feature F̂ in a progressive manner. As shown in Fig. 4, each MRM takes in two
321   features, i.e., the high-resolution features from VAE and the discriminative feature to be refined.
322   Firstly, the discriminative feature is upsampled to align with the high-resolution features of VAE. To
323   preserve the discriminative ability, the upsampled feature is processed through two chained convo-
      lution blocks for capturing multi-scale anomaly features and a 1 × 1 convolution for capturing local

367   3.4     I NFERENCE
368
369   During the generation of the abnormal image-mask pairs, aiming further to ensure the global con-
370   sistency of the abnormal image, we random select a normal image xob from Xob as input, and add
371   random noise to xob , which resulting in an initial noisy latent zˆ0 . Next, zˆ0 is input into the U-Net
372   for noise prediction, with the process guided by the conditioning vector edf (mentioned in Eq. 4).
373   In the final three denoising steps, the RMP branch (Sec. 3.3) leverages the features from the U-Net
374   decoder and VAE decoder to generate the final anomaly mask. Specifically, we average the refined
375   anomaly mask from these steps to obtain the refined mask M̂df′ ∈ R512×512×2 . Then we take the
376   threshold τ for the second channel of M̂df′ to segment the final anomaly mask Mdf ∈ R512×512×1 .
377   The effect of τ on the downstream segmentation models is shown in Tab. 29 in appendix A.8. In the
      last denoising step, the output of the generation model is used as the generated abnormal image.

400
      Implementation Details. We train SeaS by fine-tuning the pre-trained Stable Diffusion v1-4 (Rom-
401   bach et al., 2022). In AIG experiments, we use 60 normal images and 1/3 abnormal images with their
402   corresponding masks per anomaly type for training. During inference, we generate 1,000 abnormal
403   image-mask pairs for a single anomaly type. More details are given in appendix A.3.
404
      Datasets. We conduct experiments on MVTec AD dataset (Bergmann et al., 2019) and MVTec
405
      3D AD dataset (only RGB images) (Bergmann. et al., 2022). MVTec AD dataset contains 15
406
      product categories, each with up to 8 different anomalies, making it suitable for simulating real-
407   world industrial scenarios. MVTec 3D AD dataset includes 10 product categories, each with up to
408   4 different anomalies. It contains more challenges, i.e., lighting condition variations, product pose
409   variations. Due to the page limitation, results on MVTec 3D AD dataset are given in appendix A.4.
410
      Evaluation Metrics. For AIG, we leverage 2 metrics: the Inception Score (IS) and the Intra-cluster
411
      pairwise LPIPS distance (IC-LPIPS)(Ojha et al., 2021). The scarcity of abnormal images hampers
412
      the reliability of FID (Heusel et al., 2017) and KID (Bińkowski et al., 2018), as overfitted model
413   (Duan et al., 2023) achieves higher scores. For pixel-level anomaly segmentation and image-level
414   anomaly detection, we report 3 metrics: Area Under Receiver Operator Characteristic curve (AU-
415   ROC), Average Precision (AP) and F1 -score at optimal threshold (F1 -max). In addition, we report
416   Intersection over Union (IoU) for segmentation.

533   5     C ONCLUSION
534
535   In this paper, we propose a novel few-shot industrial anomaly image generation method named
536   SeaS. We explore an implicit characteristic that the anomalies exhibit randomness in shape and
537   appearance, while the products maintain global consistency with minor variations in local details.
538   We design a Separation and Sharing Fine-tuning strategy for industrial anomaly image generation,
539   and a Refined Mask Prediction branch to obtain a fine-grained mask. Our method surpasses existing
      methods on both AIG and downstream anomaly segmentation tasks.

790   A.2   A NALYSIS ON DECOUPLED A NOMALY ALIGNMENT LOSS AND MULTIPLE TOKENS
791
792   Here we give a more detailed analysis of the learning process of the DA loss. According to Eq. 3,
793   intuitively, the DA loss may pull the anomaly tokens similar to each other. However, the U-Net in
794   Stable Diffusion uses multi-head attention, which ensures different anomaly tokens cover different
795
      attributes of the anomalies. In Eq. 3, the cross-attention map is the multiply of the feature map of
      U-Net and the anomaly tokens. In the implementation of multi-head attention, both the learnable
796
      embedding of the anomaly token and the U-Net feature are decomposed into several groups along the
797
      channel dimension. E.g., the conditioning vector ea ∈ R1×C1 , which is corresponding to anomaly
798                                       C1

799
      token, is divided into {ea,i ∈ R1× q |i ∈ [1, q]}, and the image feature v ∈ Rr×r×C2 is divided
                     C2
800   into {vi ∈ R1× q |i ∈ [1, q]}, where q is the number of heads in the multi-head attention. Then the
801   corresponding groups are multiplied, and the outputs of all the heads are averaged. The attention
802   map A of ea is calculated by:
803                               q               ⊤
                               1X            Qi Ka,i
804                       A=         softmax( √ ), Qi = ϕq (vi ), Ka,i = ϕk (ea,i ).                    (8)
                               q i=1             d
805
806   Therefore, in the defect region, the DA loss only ensures the average of each head tends to 1, but
807   does not require the anomaly tokens to be the same with each other. In addition, each ea is different
808   from each other, and is combined by ea,i . The update direction of each ea,i is related to vi and
809   covers some features of the defect, it encompasses the attributes of anomalies from various
      perspectives, thereby providing diversified information.

      A.3   M ORE IMPLEMENTATION DETAILS
811
812
813   More training details. For the Unbalanced Abnormal Text Prompt, we set the number N of mul-
814   tiple <dfn > to 4 and the number N ′ of <ob> to 1, these parameters are fixed across all product
815   classes. For example, for the normal token <ob>, given the lookup U ∈ Rb×768 , where b is the num-
816
      ber of text embeddings stored by the pre-trained text encoder, we use a placeholder string "ob1"
      as the input. Firstly, "ob1" is converted to a token ID sob1 ∈ R1×1 in the tokenizer. Secondly,
817
      sob1 ∈ R1×1 is converted to a one-hot vector Sob1 ∈ R1×(b+1) . Thirdly, one learnable new embed-
818                                                                                     ′
      ding g ∈ R1×768 corresponding to sob1 is inserted to the lookup U, resulting in U ∈ R(b+1)×768 .
819
      Here, g ∈ R1×768 is the learnable embedding of <ob>. These embeddings and U-Net are learn-
820
      able during the fine-tuning process.
821
822   Training image generation model. For each product, we perform 800 × G steps for fine-tuning,
823   where G represents the number of anomaly categories of the product. The batch size of training
824
      image generation model is set to 4. During each step of our fine-tuning process, we sample 2 images
      from the abnormal training set Xdf , and 2 images from the normal training set Xob . We utilize the
825
      AdamW (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2018) optimizer with a learning rate of U-Net is 4 × 10−6 . The
826
      learning rate of the text embedding is 4 × 10−5 .
827
828   Training Refined Mask Prediction branch. We design a cascaded Refined Mask Prediction (RMP)
829   branch, which is grafted onto the U-Net trained according to SeaS. For each product, we perform
830
      800 × G steps for the RMP model, where G represents the number of anomaly types for the product.
      The batch size of training the RMP branch is set to 4. During each step of our fine-tuning process, we
831
      sample 2 images with their corresponding masks from the abnormal training set Xdf , and 2 images
832
      from the normal training set Xob . The masks used to suppress noise in normal images has each pixel
833   value set to 0. The learning rate of the RMP model is 5 × 10−4 .
834
835   Metrics. For anomaly image generation, we report 2 metrics: the Inception Score (IS) and Intra-
836
      cluster pairwise LPIPS Distance (IC-LPIPS). The Inception Score (IS), proposed in (Barratt &
      Sharma, 2018), serves as an independent metric to evaluate the fidelity and diversity of generated im-
837
      ages, by measuring the mutual information between input samples and their predicted classes. The
838
      IC-LPIPS (Ojha et al., 2021) is used to evaluate the diversity of generated images, which quantifies
839   the perceptual similarity between image patches in the same cluster. For pixel-level anomaly seg-
840   mentation and image-level anomaly detection, we report 3 metrics: Area Under Receiver Operator
841   Characteristic curve (AUROC), Average Precision (AP), and F1 -score at the optimal threshold (F1 -
842   max). All of these metric are calculated using the scikit-learn library. In addition, we calculate
843   the Intersection over Union (IoU) to more accurately evaluate the anomaly segmentation result.
844
      Resource requirement and time consumption. We conduct our training on NVIDIA Tesla A100
845   40G GPU. Specifically, we use a single A100 to train a generation model sequentially for each
846   product category, with each training process occupying about 20G of GPU memory. Since each
847   anomaly type requires isolate training, the training time depends on the total amount of anomaly
848   types across all products. For example, the product metal nut contains 4 anomaly types, and each
849   needs around 35 minutes. The generation model for metal nut spends 2 hours and 20 minutes on
850   training in total. For the RMP branch, each anomaly type needs around 25 minutes. Hence, it takes 1
851   hour and 40 minutes to train metal nut. More details are given in Tab. 6, where K is the total number
852   of anomaly types across all products. The comparison on time consumption is shown in Tab. 7. For
853
      the MVTecAD datasets with 73 anomaly types, our training takes 73 hours, which is shorter than
      the 249 hours required by AnomalyDiffusion and the 414 hours required by DFMGAN. In terms of
854
      inference time, SeaS costs 720 ms per image, which is shorter than the 3830 ms per image required
855
      by the Diffusion-based method AnomalyDiffusion. The inference time of the GAN-based method
856   DFMGAN is 48ms per image.

       A.7   M ORE DETAILS OF THE SEGMENTATION MODELS
1459
1460   As mentioned in the experiment part, we choose three segmentation models (BiSeNet V2 (Yu et al.,
1461   2021), UPerNet (Xiao et al., 2018), LFD (Zhou et al., 2024a)) to verify the validity of the generated
1462   image-mask pairs on the downstream anomaly segmentation as well as detection tasks. For BiSeNet
1463   V2 and UPerNet, we generally follow the implementation provided by MMsegmentation. For
1464   LFD, we also use the official implementation.
1465   Specifically, for BiSeNet V2, we choose a backbone structure of a detail branch of three stages with
1466   64, 64 and 128 channels and a semantic branch of four stages with 16, 32, 64 and 128 channels
1467   respectively, with a decode head and four auxiliary heads (corresponding to the number of stages in
1468   the semantic branch). As for UPerNet, we choose ResNet-50 as the backbone, with a decode head
1469   and an auxiliary head.
1470   In training segmentation models for downstream tasks, we adopt a training strategy of training
1471   a unified segmentation model for all classes of products, rather than training separate segmen-
1472   tation models for each class. Experimental results are shown in Tab. 19, which indicate that the
1473   performance of the unified segmentation model surpasses that of multiple individual segmentation
1474   models.

       Ablation on the Mask Refinement Module
1644   In the Refined Mask Prediction branch, the Mask Refinement Module (MRM) is utilized to generate
1645   refined masks. We devise different structures for MRM, as shown in Fig. 17, including Case a):
1646   those without conv blocks, Case b): with one conv blocks, and Case c): with chained conv blocks.
1647   As shown in Fig. 18, we find that using the conv blocks in Case b), which consists of two 1 × 1
1648   convolutions and one 3 × 3 convolution, helps the model learn the features of the defect area more
1649
       accurately, rather than focusing on the background area for using one convolution alone in Case a).
       Based on this observation, we further designed a chained conv blocks structure in Case c), and the
1650
       acquired features better reflect the defect area. This one-level-by-one level of residual learning helps
1651
       the model achieve better residual correction results for the defect area features. As shown in Tab. 28
1652   in the Appendix, Case c) improves the performance by + 0.28% on AUROC, + 2.29% on AP and +
1653   2.29% on F1 -max, + 0.32% on IoU compared with Case b). We substantiate the superiority of the
1654   MRM structures that we design, through the results of downstream segmentation experiments.
