009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Aligning Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) and Large Language Models (LLMs)
012            benefits in leveraging both textual and structural knowledge for Text-attributed
013            Graphs (TAGs) learning, which has attracted an increasing amount of attention
014            in the research community. Most existing literature assumes a uniformly iden-
015            tical level of learning difficulties across texts and structures in TAGs, however,
016
               we discover the text-structure imbalance problem in real-world TAGs, i.e., nodes
               exhibit various levels of difficulties when learning different textual and structural
017
               information. Existing works ignoring these different difficulties may result in
018
               under-optimized GNNs and LLMs with over-reliance on either simplistic text or
019            structure, thus failing to conduct node classifications that involve simultaneously
020            learning complex text and structural information for nodes in TAGs. To address
021            this problem, we propose a novel Curriculum GNN-LLM Alignment (CurGL)
022            method, which strategically balances the learning difficulties of textual and struc-
023            tural information on a node-by-node basis to enhance the alignment between GNNs
024            and LLMs. Specifically, we first propose a text-structure difficulty measurer to
025            estimate the learning difficulty of both text and structure in a node-wise manner.
026            Then, we propose a class-based node selection strategy to balance the training
027
               process via gradually scheduling more nodes. Finally, we propose the curriculum
               co-play alignment by iteratively promoting useful information from GNNs and
028
               LLMs, to progressively enhance both components with balanced textual and struc-
029
               tural information. Extensive experiments on real-world datasets demonstrate that
030            our proposed CurGL method is able to outperform state-of-the-art GraphLLM,
031            curriculum learning, as well as GNN baselines. To the best of our knowledge, this
032            is the first study of curriculum alignment on TAGs.

035   1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037   Text-attributed Graphs (TAGs) are ubiquitous in real-world scenarios (Li et al., 2024b), including
038   academic networks, social networks, website networks, etc. These graphs are characterized by
039
      nodes with rich textual attributes, providing valuable information for downstream tasks like node
      classification and link prediction. To leverage both structural and textual information on TAGs,
040
      aligning Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) and Large Language Models (LLMs), which excel in
041
      structural and textual modeling respectively, has attracted an increasing amount of attention in the
042   research community (Li et al., 2023c; Zhao et al., 2022a; Jin et al., 2023b).
043
044   However, we discover the text-structure imbalance problem in real-world TAGs, i.e., nodes exhibit
045
      various levels of difficulties when learning different textual and structural information, which is
      neglected in most existing literature. For illustration, we give two examples of varying difficulties
046
      across nodes in Figure 1, from the textual and structural perspectives respectively: 1) the learning
047
      difficulties of different nodes vary depending on their textural attributes. For instance, in the task
048   of predicting the paper (node)’s field (label) on academic networks, some nodes could be simply
049   predicted due to explicit inclusion of field information in their texts, while other nodes necessitate a
050   comprehensive understanding of the full text for accurate field prediction; 2) the learning difficulties
051   of different nodes vary depending on their topological structures (Wei et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2024).
052   For instance, the nodes near the class boundaries are more challenging to learn compared with those
053   located at the center of their corresponding classes, as boundary nodes interact more frequently with,
      and are more similar to nodes from other classes.

071   Existing works ignoring these different difficulties may result in under-optimized GNNs and LLMs,
072   failing to conduct node classifications that involve simultaneously learning complex text and structural
073   information for nodes in TAGs. Specifically, these models may either rely heavily on simplistic
074   textual attributes, overlooking complex yet informative structures for GNNs optimization, or depend
075   mostly on simple structures, ignoring detailed textual content for LLMs optimization. Therefore, the
076   ignorance of different learning difficulties for topologies and texts across nodes inevitably leads to
077
      suboptimal performance in TAG tasks that require a deep understanding of both textual and structural
      information simultaneously.
078
079   To address this problem, we propose to balance the text-structure learning difficulty for GNN-LLM
080   alignment in TAGs via employing curriculum learning, which remain unexplored in the existing
081   literature with following key challenges: 1) How to estimate the difficulty of different nodes in TAGs
082   with both structures and texts being taken into consideration; 2) How to make balance in the training
083
      process so that more nodes can be scheduled based on their textual and structural difficulties; 3) How
      to progressively enhance both GNNs and LLMs with balanced textual and structural information
084
      during alignment.
085
086   To tackle these challenges, we propose a Curriculum GNN-LLM Alignment (CurGL) for Text-
087   attributed Graphs. First, we propose a text-structure measurer that accounts for both textual attributes
088   and topological structures to measure the learning difficulty of each node. Particularly, we design a
089
      global center-boundary detection method to estimate the topological complexity of structure learning
      and integrate it with the loss from the LLMs or GNNs to determine the overall text-structure learning
090
      difficulty for each node. Second, we propose a class-based node selection strategy that balances the
091
      training process by gradually scheduling more nodes. This strategy selects nodes based on their class-
092   specific node’s difficulties, while preserving class balance of the selected subgraph. Third, we propose
093   a curriculum co-play alignment to progressively enhance both LLMs and GNNs with balanced textual
094   and structural information. In particular, we iteratively promote useful information obtained from
095   GNNs and LLMs, gradually involving more labeled nodes’ and confident pseudo-labeled nodes’
096   textual attributes and topological structures. Extensive experiments on five real-world datasets
097   demonstrate that our proposed CurGL method is able to outperform state-of-the-art GraphLLM,
098   curriculum learning, and GNN baselines significantly.
099   Our main contributions are summarized as follows:
100
101        • We propose a novel Curriculum GNN-LLM Alignment (CurGL) for TAGs, designed to
102          strategically balance the learning difficulties of textual attributes and topological structures on
103          a node-by-node basis, which is able to significantly improve GNN-LLM alignment and thus
104          enhance the performance. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first study of curriculum
105          alignment on TAGs.
106
107        • We propose a difficulty measurer for both textual attributes and topological structures to
             estimate the learning difficulty in a node-wise manner. Additionally, we introduce a class-based

121   In this section, we introduce the fundamental concepts of text-attributed graphs and the notation used
122   in this paper. We focus on the node classification task and describe the learning objectives for LLMs
123
      and GNNs on TAGs.
124   Text-attributed Graphs. A text-attributed graph, denoted as G = (V, A, S), consists of a node
125   set V = {v1 , v2 , . . . , vN } with N nodes, an adjacency matrix A ∈ {0, 1}N ×N , and a set of textual
126   attributes S = {sv }v∈V , where sv is the textual attribute of node v.
127   Node Classification Task. The node classification task aims to predict the labels of unlabeled
128   nodes within the same label space as the labeled nodes in a given graph. Formally, given a graph
129   G = (V, A, S) with a label set Y, the task is to predict the labels of the unlabeled nodes VU = V \ VL ,
130   where VL is the set of labeled nodes. The objective is to learn a function f : (A, S) → Y that maps
131   the node embeddings to the label space.
132
      Large Language Models for Node Classification. The node classification task on TAGs for LLMs
133   can be framed as a text classification task (Socher et al., 2013). LLMs aim to leverage the sentence
134   sv associated with each node v for label prediction. For a LLM f with parameters θ for node
135   classification task, the prediction process can be described as follows:
136
137

140   ŷv represents the predicted logits for node v. The training objective of the LLM fθ can be defined as

187   3.1    T EXT- STRUCTURE D IFFICULTY M EASURER
188
189   Previous curriculum graph learning methods primarily focused on designing difficulty measurers
190   for nodes or edges by considering the graph structure, such as class diversity among a node’s
191   neighbors (Wei et al., 2023). However, these methods lack a global structure difficulty measurement
192   and overlook difficulty of the textual attributes of the nodes, limiting their applicability to TAGs. To
193
      address this, we propose a text-structure difficulty measurer that accounts for both textual attributes
      and topological structures to estimate the learning difficulty of each node. Specifically, we design a
194
      global center-boundary detection to measure the difficulty of learning the topological structure from
195
      a global view, and combine this with the loss of the LLM or GNN to estimate the overall difficulty of
196   learning both the textual attributes and topological structures of each node.
197
198   Structure Difficulty. For structure difficulty, we introduce a global center-boundary detection method
199
      to measure the difficulty of learning the topological structure of each node. This method assumes that
      nodes located near class boundaries are more challenging to learn, while those at the center of a class
200
      should be prioritized for earlier learning. The global center-boundary detection method is defined as
201
      follows:

226

227
      node for LLM tuning, which can help to balance the learning of text and structure. Specifically, we
228
      define the whole difficulty measurer as follows:

254   To balance the learning difficulties of textual attributes and structures in TAGs, we need to gradually
255   involve more nodes in the training process, considering the varying difficulty levels across different

257   difficulty of nodes varies across different classes, and potentially leading to an imbalanced class
258   distribution within the subgraph.

      3.3   C URRICULUM C O - PLAY A LIGNMENT
294
295   To align the LLM and GNN in learning both complex textual attributes and topological structures
296   of nodes in TAGs, we iteratively promote useful information from LLM and GNN, progressively
297   enhancing both components with a balanced textual and structural information, i.e., ensuring that
298   the learning capabilities of the current LLM and GNN respectively correspond to the textual and
299   structural complexities of the nodes. Specifically, we propose a curriculum co-play alignment that
300   gradually incorporates more complex textual attributes and topological structures of nodes, iteratively
301   improving the learning of both text and structure via an easy-to-hard learning strategy. The GNN
302   primarily focuses on the progressively increasing topological complexity, while the LLM emphasizes
303
      the growing difficulty of textual information. Therefore, the parameter β is set to a larger value during
      LLM tuning and a smaller value during GNN training, as described in Section 3.1.
304
305   By employing a curriculum strategy, we prioritize simpler nodes at the beginning of the alignment
306   process. These simpler unlabeled nodes are more likely to be accurately predicted by the LLM
307   and GNN, making their pseudo-labels more reliable and suitable for training. Consequently, we
308   add a portion of the pseudo-labeled nodes to the candidate node set VC for further selection and
309
      training. This approach offers two benefits: 1) it encourages the GNN and LLM to mutually learn the
      textual and structural information of these unlabeled nodes; 2) adding the pseudo-labeled nodes to
310
      the candidate set VC enables a more accurate calculation of the topological complexity of the nodes,
311
      as the sampled subgraph from VC becomes more representative of the entire graph. We adopt the
312   output logits ŷ as the pseudo-label for each unlabeled node v in VU .
313
314   Specifically, we initially select α proportion nodes from the unlabeled set VU based on the output
315
      logits ŷ of the LLM and GNN, adding them to the pool of candidate nodes VC for further selection.
      Besides, to ensure the class balance of the subgraph, we also adopt a class-based strategy to select
316
      the unlabeled nodes from each class. The detailed algorithm to obtain the candidate nodes set
317
      VC from the labeled nodes VL and the unlabeled nodes VU is provided in the Algorithm 3 in the
318   Appendix A.4. We then select nodes from the candidate nodes set VC based on the text-structure
319   difficulty Dst of each node for training, as described in Section 3.2. For edges, we retain the edges
320   connecting these selected nodes VS to form a subgraph. Inspired by (Zhao et al., 2022a), we adopt a
321   variational EM algorithm to iteratively align the LLM and GNN through Curriculum Text-Structure
322   Learning. In the E-step, the GNN is fixed, and the LLM uses only the text information to predict the
323   labels (including both the labeled nodes and the pseudo-labeled nodes inferred by the GNN). In the
      M-step, the LLM is fixed, and the GNN uses the text embeddings from LLM to predict the labels.

495   5     R ELATED W ORKS
496
497   GNN-LLM Alignment for TAGs TAGs have gained increasing attention in the research community,
498   with GraphLLM (Jin et al., 2023d; Tang et al., 2024b; Xia et al., 2024; Kong et al., 2024; Li et al.,
499   2023b; Ren et al., 2024; Zou et al., 2023; Tang et al., 2024b; Huang et al., 2024a; Chen et al., 2024b;d;
500   Wei et al., 2024a; Guo et al., 2024) methods emerging as a promising approach to leverage the
501   strengths of both LLMs and GNNs. Among these GraphLLM methods, the alignment between
502   LLMs and GNNs (Li et al., 2023c; Jin et al., 2023a) has attracted an increasing amount of attention
503   by aligning them in the same vector space. For instance, GLEM (Zhao et al., 2022a) integrates
504
      GNNs and LLMs, within a variational Expectation-Maximization framework. It alternates updates
      between LLMs and GNNs, enhancing performance on downstream tasks. PATTON (Jin et al., 2023b)
505
      enhances GraphFormer Yang et al. (2021) by introducing two novel pre-training strategies tailored
506
      for text-attributed graphs: network-contextualized masked language modeling and masked node
507   prediction. However, exising GNN-LLM alignment methods ignore the varying learning difficulties
508   of textual attributes and structures across nodes in TAGs, leading to a suboptimal textual and structural
509   representation learning. In this paper, we focus on the balance of learning difficulties between textual
510   attributes and structures on a node-by-node basis to improve the alignment between GNNs and LLMs.
511
512
      Curriculum Learning on Graphs Graph Curriculum Learning (GCL) is different from traditional
513
      Curriculum Learning due to the inherent dependencies of graph data. As a result, curriculum learning
514
      methods (Gong et al., 2019; Zhou et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024a; 2023a) designed
515   for independent data cannot be directly applied to graphs. Researchers leverage graph structures
516   to measure difficulty through predefined or automated strategies. CLNode (Wei et al., 2023) is a
517   Curriculum Graph Learning method that measures local difficulty by considering the class diversity
518   among a node’s neighbors and uses global features to identify mislabeled nodes. RCL (Zhang et al.,
519   2023) is a Curriculum Graph Learning method that gradually integrates node relationships into the
520   training process, based on the complexity of those relationships. TSS (Wu et al., 2024) is a distinctive
521   perspective on employing curriculum learning methods specifically tailored for noisily labeled graphs.
522   However, existing GCL methods lack a measurer that accounts for global structural information to
523
      estimate the learning difficulty of nodes and overlook the varying difficulty across different classes,
      which can lead to subgraph imbalance. Additionally, these methods ignore textual attributes learning
524
      difficulty, limiting their applicability to TAGs. In this paper, we propose a Curriculum Learning
525
      approach for node selection that considers textual attributes, topological structures, and the varying
526   difficulty across different classes in TAGs.

529   6     C ONCLUSION
530
531
      In this paper, we propose a curriculum GNN-LLM alignment (CurGL) for TAGs, aimed at strate-
532
      gically balance the learning of textual attributes and topological structures of nodes, improving
533
      node representation learning. We introduce a text-structure difficulty measurer to estimate learn-
534   ing difficulty of textual and structural information of nodes. Then we propose a class-based node
535   selection strategy to balance the training process by gradually scheduling more nodes, considering
536   the varying class-specific node’s difficulties. Finally, a curriculum co-play alignment by iteratively
537   promoting useful information from LLM and GNN, progressively enhancing both components with
538   a balanced textual and structural information. Extensive experiments on real-world show that our
539   method outperforms existing GraphLLM approaches. In the future, we would like to extend our
      method to other graph-related tasks, such as link prediction and graph classification.

      A.3    PACING F UNCTION I MPLIMENTATION
865
866   We propose a class-based node selection strategy. This approach selects nodes based on their class-
867   specific difficulty levels while maintaining sampled subgraph class balance. Specifically, we divide
868   the candidate nodes VC into K classes, denoted as VC = [VC1 , VC2 , . . . , VCK ], where VCk represents
869   the candidate nodes in class k. We then select low-difficulty nodes from each class in a proportion to
870   λ, which is controlled by a PacingFunction. We utilize three distinct pacing functions: linear, root,
871   and geometric.
872
             • linear:
873
874                                              \lambda _{t} = \min (1, \lambda _{0} + (1-\lambda _{0})*\frac {t}{T})                   (14)

876

960                mechanism to understand the context of words in a sentence, enabling it to excel in
961                various natural language processing tasks.
962              – Sent-BERT. (Reimers & Gurevych, 2019) Sentence-BERT is an adaptation of the BERT
963                model specifically designed to generate sentence embeddings, allowing for effective
964
                   semantic similarity comparisons and improved performance on tasks like clustering and
                   information retrieval.

966
                 – RoBERTa. (Liu et al., 2019) RoBERTa is an enhanced version of BERT that modifies

967
                   sentence prediction objective to improve overall model performance.
968
969         • LLM as Enhancer
970              – GIANT. (Chien et al., 2022) GIANT introduces a method that utilizes XR-Transformers
971                Zhang et al. (2021) to perform neighborhood prediction, resulting in a large language
                   model (LLM) capable of generating feature vectors that surpass those produced by both

                    bag-of-words and standard BERT Kenton & Toutanova (2019) embeddings for node
973                 classification tasks.
974
                  – TAPE. (He et al., 2023) TAPE employs custom prompts to engage LLMs, producing
975
                    predictions and textual explanations for each node. These text explanations are then
976                 refined using DeBERTa He et al. (2020) to transform them into node embeddings
977                 suitable for GNNs. Consequently, GNNs leverage a blend of original text features,
978                 explanation-derived features, and prediction features to accurately predict node labels.
979               – OFA. (Liu et al., 2023a) OFA represents all nodes and edges using human-readable texts
980                 and encodes these elements from various domains into a unified space using LLMs. The
981                 framework is designed to be adaptable for different tasks by incorporating task-specific
982                 prompting substructures into the input graph.
983               – ENGINE. (Zhu et al., 2024b) ENGINE incorporates a tunable G-Ladder module into

985                 structural information. This configuration allows the output from each LLM layer,

994

995                 LLMs, such as the encoded node text. This integration allows the structural data from
996                 the GNN adapter to enhance the textual information from the LLMs, creating a cohesive

1003                ability.
1004              – LLaGA. (Chen et al., 2024a) LLaGA employs node-level templates to transform graph

1153   In this section, we provide a derivation of the loss functions for our proposed algorithm, which
1154   combines a LLM and a GNN within a variational EM framework following (Zhao et al., 2022a). We
1155   ensure that the final loss functions match those defined in Equations equation 9 and equation 10.
1156
1157   H.1                                P ROBLEM S ETUP
1158
1159   We consider a graph G = (V, E), where the node set V is partitioned into:
1160
1161

1162                                       • An unlabeled set VU with unknown labels yU .
1163
1164   Each node v ∈ V has:
1165
1166                                       • Text features sv .
1167                                       • An ego-graph Gv (for GNN input).
1168

1170   the graph structure.
