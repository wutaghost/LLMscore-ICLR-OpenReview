011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            We introduce CF-GISS, a novel framework for generative 3D indoor scene syn-
014            thesis that ensures collision-free scene layouts by incorporating an image-based
015            intermediate layout representation. In contrast to existing methods that directly
016
               construct the scene graph or object list, our approach facilitates substantially more
               effective prevention of collision artifacts as out-of-distribution (OOD) scenarios
017
               during generation. Furthermore, CF-GISS conditions layout generation on floor
018
               plans controllable via images or textual descriptions, enabling the production of
019            coherent, house-wide layouts that are robust to variations in geometric and seman-
020            tic structures. Our framework demonstrates state-of-the-art performance on the
021            3D-FRONT dataset, delivering high-quality, collision-free scene synthesis while
022            offering flexibility in accommodating a range of floor plan structures. Addition-
023            ally, we propose a novel dataset with significantly expanded coverage of house-
024            hold items and room configurations, as well as improved data quality.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Generative 3D indoor scene synthesis (Xu et al., 2002; Merrell et al., 2011; Yu et al., 2011; Fisher
030   et al., 2012; Qi et al., 2018; Zhang et al., 2018; Li et al., 2018a; Ritchie et al., 2019; Wang et al.,
031   2019; Yao et al., 2024; Min et al., 2024; Vaswani et al., 2017; Hu et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2020;
032   Paschalidou et al., 2021; Leimer et al., 2022; Tang et al., 2024; Lin & Mu, 2024) is essential not only
033
      for creative and technical workflows such as interior design, architectural planning, video game
      development, and virtual or augmented reality, but also for advancing embodied AI by providing
034
      simulated environments for training. This approach facilitates rapid prototyping, reduces manual
035
      labor, lowers deployment costs, and accelerates iteration. Despite advances in neural representations
036   such as NeRFs (Mildenhall et al., 2020) and Gaussian splatting (Kerbl et al., 2023), mesh-based
037   assets remain the predominant 3D representation in these applications due to their superior rendering
038   quality and explicit interactivity, particularly in video games, design, and simulation. Consequently,
039   most generative indoor scene synthesis methods (Zhang et al., 2018; Li et al., 2018a; Ritchie et al.,
040   2019; Wang et al., 2019; Paschalidou et al., 2021; Tang et al., 2024; Lin & Mu, 2024) follow a
041   procedural generation paradigm, constructing a scene graph or object list for the scene layout, with
042   each node containing detailed specifications for individual scene objects, which can be subsequently
043   retrieved from a CAD asset dataset and rendered by various graphics engines.
044   However, a fundamental limitation of directly constructing scene graphs or object lists is the inability
045   to prevent implausible collisions or overlaps between objects, such as a bed intersecting with cabi-
046   nets, during the generation process. While collision detection can be performed as a post-processing
047   step, it is computationally expensive, and resolving the detected collisions during post-processing
048   remains nontrivial.
049   In this paper, we propose CF-GISS, a novel framework aimed at resolving the issue of strictly pre-
050   venting collision artifacts in generative 3D indoor scene synthesis during the generation process.
051   Central to our approach is the synthesis of an RGB image representing the scene layout as an in-
052   termediate step in the procedural workflow, which is subsequently converted into an object list or
053   graph, rather than directly constructing the graph in a single step. Our key insight is that, compared
      to graph representations, which are inherently tabular, introducing an RGB image as an intermediate

054
      representation enables more effective detection of collision artifacts as out-of-distribution (OOD)
055   scenarios. Such collisions are characterized by a lower likelihood of occurrence during training,
056   leading to a large loss value, which ultimately facilitates strict avoidance of collisions during the
057   generation process without the need for post-processing. Our extensive evaluation of CF-GISS on
058   the 3D-FRONT dataset (Fu et al., 2021a) demonstrates its state-of-the-art performance both qual-
059   itatively and quantitatively, particularly in the near elimination of unreasonable object collisions, a
060   prevalent issue in existing methods.
061   Meanwhile, the floor plan structure serves as a crucial yet often overlooked constraint in recent
062   works on generative indoor scene synthesis, as room sizes, along with the placement of doors and
063   windows, substantially influence the logical arrangement of household layouts. Unlike methods
064   that focus on generating single-room layouts (Zhang et al., 2018; Li et al., 2018a; Ritchie et al.,
065   2019; Wang et al., 2019; Paschalidou et al., 2021; Tang et al., 2024; Lin & Mu, 2024), CF-GISS
066   is conditioned on the overall floor plan structure, which can be controlled via either images or text
067   prompts, to synthesize coherent, house-wide indoor scenes adaptable to floor plan variations in
068   geometric and semantic structures.
069   Finally, we introduce a large-scale dataset comprising 9,706 scenes with floor plans and scene lay-
070   outs, approximately 1.4 times larger than 3D-FRONT. Our dataset expands household item coverage
071   to 26 super-categories, including items from kitchens, bathrooms, and balconies, addressing gaps in
072   3D-FRONT, which lacks furnishings in these areas and occasionally leaves living rooms or bed-
073   rooms unfurnished. It also resolves common issues found in 3D-FRONT, such as misclassified
074   objects, unrealistic placements, and collisions, providing clean layouts without requiring extensive
075
      data cleaning. We demonstrate applications of this new dataset using CF-GISS.
076   Our contributions are summarized as: i) Novel framework - a novel framework for collision-free
077   generative 3D indoor scene synthesis with controllable floor plans via images or text prompts, op-
078   timized room layouts, and photorealistic rendering; ii) Performance - state-of-the-art performance
079   on the 3D-FRONT dataset, both qualitatively and quantitatively, especially the near elimination of
080   unreasonable object collisions; and iii) Dataset1 - a novel dataset of 9,706 scenes with floor plans
081
      and scene layouts, 1.4 times larger than 3D-FRONT, with improved item coverage and data quality.

083   2       R ELATED W ORK
084
085   Early work employed a rule-based constraint satisfaction formulation to generate 3D room layouts
086   for pre-specified sets of objects (Xu et al., 2002). Other approaches optimized cost functions based
087   on interior design principles (Merrell et al., 2011) and object-object statistical relationships (Yu et al.,
088   2011). The earliest data-driven approach modeled object co-occurrences using a Bayesian network
089   and Gaussian mixtures to capture pairwise spatial relations extracted from 3D scenes (Fisher et al.,
090
      2012). With the availability of large datasets of 3D environments, such as SUNCG (Song et al.,
      2017), 3D-FRONT (Fu et al., 2021a), SUN3D (Xiao et al., 2013), Matterport3D (Chang et al.,
091
      2017), InteriorNet (Li et al., 2018b), Structured3D (Zheng et al., 2020), and 3D-FURNITURE (Fu
092
      et al., 2021b), learning-based approaches have gained popularity. Various methods for indoor scene
093   synthesis have been proposed, including: human-centric probabilistic grammars (Qi et al., 2018),
094   Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) trained on matrix representations of scene objects (Zhang
095   et al., 2018), recursive neural networks for sampling 3D scene hierarchies (Li et al., 2018a), con-
096   volutional neural networks (CNNs) trained on top-down room images (Zhang et al., 2018; Ritchie
097   et al., 2019), spatial prior graph neural networks trained on labeled 3D spatial relationships (Wang
098   et al., 2019; Yao et al., 2024), and Variational Autoencoder (VAE) models trained on top-down func-
099   tional furniture group images (Min et al., 2024). Additionally, floor plan synthesis approaches have
100   been proposed using graph neural networks (Hu et al., 2020). With the development of transformer
101
      models (Vaswani et al., 2017), transformer-based approaches have become increasingly popular.
      These include floor plan-conditioned furniture synthesis and text-conditioned furniture synthesis
102
      using transformer autoregressive models (Wang et al., 2020; Paschalidou et al., 2021), as well as
103
      methods integrating expert knowledge in the form of differentiable scalar functions to guide the
104   generation of more ergonomic layouts (Leimer et al., 2022). Recently, diffusion models (Ho et al.,
105   2020) have demonstrated impressive visual quality in generative tasks, including indoor furniture
106   synthesis (Tang et al., 2024; Lin & Mu, 2024). However, these methods primarily synthesize layouts

141   3       M ETHOD
142
143   We propose a novel pipeline for 3D-aware indoor scene synthesis, as depicted in Figure 1. The
144   pipeline starts with a floor plan description—provided either as an image or text—and use a con-
145   ditional diffusion model to generate a corresponding 2D scene layout. The use of this 2D repre-
146   sentation enables us to leverage efficient image encoders for layout generation while effectively
147
      preventing object overlaps. Next, we employ automatic object detectors and segmentation maps to
      identify individual household items and extract a structured scene graph that hierarchically organizes
148
      spatial relationships and object attributes. Finally, the 3D scene objects are retrieved accordingly and
149
      rendered to produce photorealistic 3D-consistent images.

      3.1         D IFFUSION - BASED SCENE LAYOUT GENERATION
152
153
154
      Image-conditioned floor plans We leverage the recent success of image-based diffusion mod-
155
      els (Saharia et al., 2022a; Rombach et al., 2022; Amit et al., 2021) and frame the problem of gen-
156   erating diverse, realistic indoor scene layouts as a conditional image-to-image translation task, as
157   illustrated in Figure 1 (top-left). Unlike complex scene graphs or tabular formats, natural RGB im-
158   ages serve as a convenient intermediate representation for the layout, easily processed by existing
159   vision tools. Crucially, since RGB images are easy-to-interpret by an appropriate encoder, we can
160   construct a highly effective conditional generative model that accurately captures the data distri-
161   bution. In RGB images, collisions between objects are immediately visible and flagged as OOD
      samples, allowing the model to generate coherent, physically realistic layouts.

185   Text-conditioned floor plans Another convenient method to specify the floor plan is through
186
      natural language, particularly in practical scenarios where floor plan images are unavailable or in-
      compatible with the format accepted by our model. Given the success of text-to-image diffusion
187
      models (Ramesh et al., 2022; Saharia et al., 2022b) and our use of RGB images to represent filled
188
      layouts, text descriptions are a viable alternative within our framework. In this case, we generate a
189   fixed-size conditioning vector yc by passing the text input through a CLIP encoder (Radford et al.,
190   2021). Semi-structured text is particularly effective for such a task, for example, “This is a flat with
191   40 square meters, featuring 0 living rooms, 1 bedroom, 0 kitchens, and 1 bathroom”. The resulting
192   CLIP embedding serves as the conditional variable for the diffusion model, guiding the generation
193   of furniture layouts based on high-level semantic information encoded in the text description. This
194   allows for more intuitive control over the layout generation by leveraging natural language as an
195   additional input modality.
196   The text-based model is trained with the same procedure, using Equation 1 but the conditioning
197   variable is the CLIP embedding yc instead of the empty floor plan yi . Another minor difference is
198   that conditioning is introduced through a cross-attention layer (Vaswani, 2017) near the UNet bottle-
199   neck, as illustrated in Figure 1 (bottom-left). Although the CLIP was designed to handle both image
200   and text conditioning, we opt for operating directly in pixel space for image conditioning to ensure
201   higher-resolution image generation and preserve finer details, mitigating the compression artifacts
202   found in latent diffusion, albeit at a higher computational cost. Nevertheless, with separate encoders
203
      CF-GISS seamlessly handles both image and text inputs, catering to different user preferences.

242   3.3     R ENDERING
243
244
      Finally, we convert the structured scene graph into a 3D mesh. The wall and floor materials for
      each ei are procedurally sampled, while being aware of the rooms to which they belong. To main-
245
      tain uniform lighting and shadow consistency across the scene, an appropriately sized area light is
246
      placed at the center of each room. The UE engine (Epic Games) is subsequently utilized to generate
247   photorealistic renderings.
248
249   The key advantage of the multi-stage pipeline of CF-GISS (see Figure 1), which first generates a 2D
250
      layout rather than directly synthesizing an object scene graph (Tang et al., 2024; Lin & Mu, 2024),
      lies in its inherent ability to prevent object intersections. By leveraging the 2D RGB images as an
251
      intermediate representation, our approach ensures that the spatial relationships between household
252
      items are preserved, avoiding common issues such as object overlap and collision, which we validate
253   in Section 5.1.

      We collected a large-scale dataset, which we refer to as CF-dataset, of indoor scenes with floor
      plans and scene layouts, comprising a total of 9,706 different design schemes, approximately 1.4

266            • Rooms: Represented as enclosed loops of interior wall lines, defined by 2D coordinates.

469   6     L IMITATIONS , D ISCUSSIONS , AND C ONCLUSIONS
470
471   i) CF-dataset - While the CF-dataset includes extensive floor plans and scene layout data, the as-
472   sociated 3D models follow a uniform artistic style, lacking diversity in designs and corresponding
473   attributes, which are essential for training text-guided stylistic indoor scene synthesis. The inherent
474   subjective nature of design styles presents a challenge in gathering comprehensive dataset samples,
475   which we leave to future work. ii) CF-GISS - While the text conditioning of CF-GISS can theoret-
476   ically extend to various controls, such as spatial layouts (e.g., placing the sofa along the east wall)
477
      and stylistic features (e.g., a Bohemian sofa), without significantly altering the pipeline or model ar-
      chitectures, this requires obtaining additional annotated data for such attributes, yet not introducing
478
      substantial further novelty in methodology design. Hence, we focus on collision-free scene layout
479
      generation, which addresses a prevalent practical limitation in existing approaches, and leave the
480   incorporation of stylistic and spatial controls with texts to future work. Meanwhile, CF-GISS cur-
481   rently does not accommodate natural object overlaps, such as placing a lamp on a table. However,
482   this can be addressed by applying multiple layers of scene layouts, which also effectively prevents
483   vertical collisions, a feature we leave for future work. Another limitation of CF-GISS is its reliance
484   on YOLO (Jocher et al., 2023) for high-accuracy object detection, which struggles to detect the an-
485   gles of square targets (Ultralytics, 2024). This can lead to bounding box collisions despite the layout
      images being collision-free. We discuss additional failure cases in Appendix Figure 10.

      A.1    CF-GISS I NFERENCE E FFICIENCY
705
706   On a single RTX 8000 GPU, the diffusion model inference takes approximately 18 seconds; YOLO
707   object detection takes around 40 milliseconds; 3D model matching and scene construction take
708   about 100 milliseconds; and rendering, performed using the UE engine, takes approximately 30
709   seconds for a 2K image and around 120 seconds for a 4K image. While rendering is the most time-
710   consuming module, it is an independent component that can be flexibly replaced with any real-time
711   rasterization-based renderer when efficiency is a priority. We chose a ray-tracing-based renderer for
712   photorealistic quality.
713
714   A.2    A BLATION S TUDIES
715
      We additionally conducted an ablation experiment where the diffusion model generated 2D layouts
716
      as segmentation maps, i.e., quantized grayscale images with precision consistent with the number of
717
      household item categories, instead of RGB images. The diffusion model shares the same architecture
718   as described in Section 3.1. We present qualitative results in Figure 9, which indicate that the
719   diffusion model fails to capture meaningful 2D layouts when directly generating segmentation maps.
720   In Figure 9, for better visualization and comparison, we additionally converted each segmentation
721   map into an RGB-based image, consistent with the color scheme used in the main experiments.
722   Meanwhile, we conducted a quantitative evaluation, which yielded an FID of 13.34 and a KID
723   of 0.014 between the generated segmentation maps and the ground truth, as well as an FID of
724   328.20 and a KID of 0.52 between the RGB layouts converted from the generated segmentation
725   maps and the ground truth. All the metrics are consistently worse compared to CF-GISS as reported
726
      in Table 2 (Entire House). We believe this is due to the insufficient precision of the segmentation
      maps, which seriously compromises the robustness of the diffusion model. Compared to full-range
727
      RGB images, minor inaccuracies in generated segmentation maps can lead to significant errors. This
728
      further validates our current choice of the CF-GISS pipeline, where the use of RGB-based layouts
729   with 255 precision, combined with a YOLO detection step, improves the robustness of our approach.
