009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Recent advancements have highlighted the Mamba framework, a state-space mod-
012            els (SSMs) known for its efficiency in capturing long-range dependencies with
013            linear computational complexity. While Mamba has shown competitive perfor-
014            mance in medical image segmentation, it encounters difficulties in modeling local
015            features due to the sporadic nature of traditional location-based scanning methods
016
               and the complex, ambiguous boundaries often present in medical images. To over-
               come these challenges, we propose Uncertainty-Driven Mamba (UD-Mamba),
017
               which redefines the pixel-order scanning process by incorporating channel un-
018
               certainty into the scanning mechanism. UD-Mamba introduces two key scanning
019            techniques: sequential scanning, which prioritizes regions with high uncertainty
020            by scanning in a row-by-row fashion, and skip scanning, which processes columns
021            vertically, moving from high-to-low or low-to-high uncertainty at fixed intervals.
022            Sequential scanning efficiently clusters high-uncertainty regions, such as bound-
023            aries and foreground objects, to improve segmentation precision, while skip scan-
024            ning enhances the interaction between background and foreground regions, al-
025            lowing for timely integration of background information to support more accurate
026            foreground inference. Recognizing the advantages of scanning from certain to un-
027
               certain areas, we introduce four learnable parameters to balance the importance of
               features extracted from different scanning methods. Additionally, a cosine consis-
028
               tency loss is employed to mitigate the drawbacks of transitioning between uncer-
029
               tain and certain regions during the scanning process. Our method demonstrates
030            robust segmentation performance, validated across three distinct medical imaging
031            datasets involving pathology, dermatological lesions, and cardiac tasks.

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036
      Transformers have shown significant potential in image processing due to their ability to model long-
      range dependencies (Vaswani et al., 2017; Dosovitskiy et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2021; Bao et al., 2024;
037
      Zhang et al., 2024b). However, their quadratic computational complexity with respect to sequence
038
      length imposes substantial computational costs, particularly in high-resolution tasks like medical
039   image segmentation. Recently, state-space models (SSMs) have emerged as a more computationally
040   efficient alternative, offering linear complexity while preserving the ability to model long-range
041   dependencies (Gu et al., 2021). Among these, the Mamba architecture (Gu & Dao, 2023; Dao &
042   Gu, 2024) stands out, employing selective scanning techniques and hardware-optimized design to
043   achieve impressive results across various visual tasks (Liu et al., 2024b; Zhu et al., 2024; Li et al.,
044   2024; Hu et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024a).
045   In medical image segmentation, the primary objective is to accurately delineate regions that cor-
046   respond to target organs or pathological tissues, providing essential support for clinical diag-
047   noses (Ronneberger et al., 2015; Chen et al., 2024; Isensee et al., 2021; Hatamizadeh et al., 2022;
048   Li et al., 2018; Wang et al., 2021). Due to its capacity to capture long-range dependencies and
049   process high-resolution images efficiently, the Mamba framework has seen increasing application
050   in the medical imaging field (Yang et al., 2024; Xing et al., 2024). However, Mamba’s traditional
051   position-based sequential scanning method often leads to intermittent scanning of different seman-
052   tic regions (Figure 1(e)), which is particularly problematic when dealing with complex backgrounds
053
      and ambiguous boundaries in medical images. This hinders Mamba’s ability to accurately model
      local features essential for effective segmentation (Fan et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024a).

      To overcome this limitation, we propose Uncertainty-Driven Mamba (UD-
073   Mamba), which leverages channel uncertainty as a guiding metric to redefine
074   the pixel-wise scanning process. As illustrated in Figure 1(c), pixels with
075   higher median channel uncertainty are often associated with critical areas,
076   such as the foreground and boundaries. Conversely, regions with lower uncer-
077   tainty are typically related to the background. By calculating the uncertainty
078   map and ranking the pixels based on their uncertainty levels, as shown in Fig-
079   ure 1(d), we ensure that uncertain (and thus critical) regions are distinguished
080   from more certain regions (typically representing background information).
081   The proposed scanning strategy, as depicted in Figure 1(f), introduces two Figure 2: Ascend-
082   key methods: 1) Sequential scanning: This method processes pixels in ing vs. descending
083   strict order according to their uncertainty levels, effectively clustering high- uncertainty.
084   uncertainty regions such as boundaries and foreground areas. By focusing on
085   these critical regions, sequential scanning ensures that the model captures the
086   fine details in areas crucial for accurate segmentation. 2) Skip scanning: This technique moves
087
      vertically across the image at consistent uncertainty intervals, enhancing the interaction between
      background and foreground information. It supplements the model’s understanding of background
088
      regions while ensuring precise foreground segmentation. By combining sequential and skip scan-
089
      ning, UD-Mamba is able to focus on the fine structures of critical regions while maintaining an
090   understanding of the broader context. This dual-scanning approach enables a more balanced and
091   effective segmentation performance. Furthermore, as illustrated in Figure 2, scanning from regions
092   of low uncertainty to high uncertainty generally yields superior results compared to the reverse or-
093   der. To optimize this process, we introduce four learnable parameters that adjust the importance of
094   features gathered from different scanning techniques. Additionally, we apply a cosine consistency
095   loss to ensure that features derived from scanning uncertain-to-certain regions are aligned with those
096   from certain-to-uncertain regions, further enhancing segmentation accuracy.
097   Our contributions can be summarized as follows:
098
099          • We introduce a novel pixel-level selective scanning approach guided by channel uncer-
100            tainty, addressing the limitations of traditional position-based sequential scanning methods.
101
102          • We incorporate learnable parameters to balance feature importance across different scan-
103            ning directions and employ a cosine consistency loss to align forward and backward scan
104            results, improving feature consistency.
105
106          • Extensive experiments on three medical imaging datasets demonstrate that UD-Mamba
107            effectively identifies ambiguous regions, leading to more reliable segmentation outcomes
               compared to existing Mamba-based methods.

      2.1   M EDICAL I MAGE S EGMENTATION
111
112   In medical image segmentation, Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) and Transformers dom-
113   inate as leading frameworks. A significant advancement in CNN-based segmentation was intro-
114   duced by UNet (Ronneberger et al., 2015), which employs a symmetric encoder-decoder architec-
115   ture with skip connections. These skip connections effectively integrate local features from the
116   encoder with semantic information from the decoder, setting the foundation for many subsequent
117   improvements (Zhou et al., 2019; Oktay et al., 2018; Le & Saut, 2023). Despite its success, CNN-
118   based methods are limited by their local receptive fields, which hinder the capture of long-range
119
      dependencies essential for dense prediction tasks.
120   Inspired by the Vision Transformers (ViTs) (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2021), there has
121   been increasing interest in incorporating Transformers into medical image segmentation. Tran-
122   sUNet (Chen et al., 2024), one of the pioneering works, introduced a hybrid model that uses Trans-
123   formers in the encoder to model global context, while retaining the overall UNet structure. Swin-
124   UNet (Cao et al., 2022) further explored a fully Transformer-based framework for segmentation
125
      tasks. While Transformers are adept at modeling long-range dependencies, their self-attention mech-
      anism introduces quadratic complexity relative to input size, which poses scalability challenges,
126
      especially in pixel-level tasks like medical image segmentation.
127
128
129
      2.2   M AMBA - BASED M EDICAL I MAGE S EGMENTATION
130
      State Space Models (SSMs) have recently emerged as a powerful tool for visual tasks, with
131
      Mamba (Gu & Dao, 2023; Dao & Gu, 2024) showing promising results by efficiently modeling
132   global context with linear complexity. Mamba-based models have demonstrated their versatility
133   across a range of applications (Zhu et al., 2024; Ruan & Xiang, 2024; He et al., 2024; Zhang et al.,
134   2024a; Fan et al., 2024). U-Mamba (Ma et al., 2024) introduces a hybrid framework combining
135   CNNs and SSMs, effectively capturing both local and global features. Swin-UMamba (Liu et al.,
136   2024a) incorporates ImageNet-based pretraining into a Mamba-based UNet for enhanced medical
137   image segmentation performance. P-Mamba (Ye & Chen, 2024) combines Perona-Malik diffusion
138   with Mamba to improve echocardiographic left ventricular segmentation in pediatric cardiology.
139   Additionally, Wang et al. (Wang et al., 2024b) introduced LMa-UNet, a Mamba-based network with
140
      a large-window design for improved global context modeling.
141   Despite these advances, accurately segmenting complex medical images remains a challenge due to
142   the intricate background and ambiguous class boundaries. Moreover, traditional scanning mecha-
143   nisms, which intermittently scan different semantic regions, limit the model’s ability to consistently
144   capture the full range of contextual information within the images.

      3.4     U NCERTAINTY-D RIVEN S ELECTIVE S CANNING O PTIMIZATION S TRATEGY
275
276   As depicted in Figure 4 II, the UD-SSB applies four distinct scanning sequences: sequential and skip
277   scans from high-to-low uncertainty levels (y1 and y2 ), and sequential and skip scans from low-to-
278   high uncertainty levels (y3 and y4 ). Generally, regions with high uncertainty are more likely to cor-
279   respond to target areas and critical boundaries, while low-uncertainty regions are usually associated
280   with the background. In the Mamba framework, which operates as an autoregressive model, each
281   output depends on the hidden state derived from previous inputs. Scanning from low-uncertainty
282
      to high-uncertainty regions allows the model to first process simpler background information, ac-
      cumulating hidden state reserves before addressing more complex areas. As shown in Figure 2,
283
      this approach outperforms the reverse scanning order. Therefore, to capitalize on this property, we
284
      propose two optimization strategies to exploit the benefits of scanning from high-to-low uncertainty
285   while mitigating the inherent limitations of scanning from low-to-high uncertainty.
286
287   3.4.1    R EWEIGHTING OF DIFFERENT SCANNING SEQUENCES
288
289   To optimize the contribution of each scanning sequence, we introduce four learnable parameters (α1 ,
290   α2 , α3 , α4 ), each corresponding to one of the four scanning directions. These parameters serve to
291   enhance the advantages of scanning from high-to-low uncertainty while modulating the contribution
292
      of each individual scanning sequence. The reweighting mechanism is mathematically defined as:
293                                     yi′ = yi · αi   for   i = 1, 2, 3, 4                               (9)
294
295   This approach ensures that each scanning method contributes in proportion to its effectiveness in
296
      capturing critical image regions, with greater emphasis placed on scans that progress from more
      certain to less certain areas.
297
298
      3.4.2    C ONSISTENCY CONSTRAINTS BETWEEN BIDIRECTIONAL SCANS
299
300   To address the limitations associated with low-to-high uncertainty scans during the decoding phase
301   and improve overall segmentation performance, we introduce a cosine consistency constraint at the
302   end of the decoder. This constraint is applied between sequential and skip scans performed in both
303   directions (from high-to-low and low-to-high uncertainty). By aligning the results from low-to-high
304   uncertainty scans with those from high-to-low uncertainty scans, we ensure consistency in feature
305   representation across different scanning directions. To maintain positional consistency, all outputs
306
      yir are derived after the recovery operation is applied to yi . The cosine consistency loss is defined
      as:

      5    C ONCLUSION
514
515   In this paper, we introduce UD-Mamba, a novel architecture designed to address Mamba’s limita-
516   tions in local feature modeling. By integrating a pixel-level channel uncertainty-driven mechanism,
517   UD-Mamba effectively prioritizes pixels based on channel uncertainty, enabling comprehensive and
518   efficient feature extraction. Furthermore, as scanning from low-uncertainty to high-uncertainty vec-
519   tors typically yields greater benefits than the reverse process, we introduce four learnable parameters
520   to explore the impact of various scanning sequences on the autoregressive Mamba framework. Con-
521   currently, we enhance the efficacy of transitions from high-uncertainty to low-uncertainty regions
522   by constraining the cosine similarity loss between forward and backward scanning results. Exper-
523
      imental results on three medical imaging datasets demonstrate UD-Mamba’s superior performance
      in medical image segmentation tasks compared to traditional models.
524
525   Future work will focus on developing more precise and effective uncertainty estimation methods,
526   as model performance depends heavily on accurate channel uncertainty estimation. The use of
527   standard deviation to evaluate uncertainty may be inadequate for capturing more complex patterns
528   across diverse medical imaging tasks. Additionally, we aim to expand the application of UD-Mamba
529
      to a wider range of medical image segmentation challenges.
