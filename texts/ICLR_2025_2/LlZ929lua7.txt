009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Estimating uncertainty in text-to-image diffusion models is challenging due to
012            their large parameter counts (often exceeding 100 million) and operation in com-
013            plex, high-dimensional spaces with virtually infinite input possibilities. In this
014            paper, we propose Epistemic Mixture of Experts (EMoE), a novel framework for
015            efficiently estimating epistemic uncertainty in diffusion models. EMoE leverages
016            pre-trained networks without requiring additional training, enabling direct uncer-
017            tainty estimation from a prompt. We introduce a novel latent space within the
018
               diffusion process that captures model uncertainty better during the first denoising
               step than existing methods. Experimental results on the COCO dataset demon-
019
               strate EMoE’s effectiveness, showing a strong correlation between uncertainty
020
               and image quality. Additionally, EMoE identifies under-sampled languages and
021            regions with higher uncertainty, revealing hidden biases related to linguistic rep-
022            resentation. This capability demonstrates the relevance of EMoE as a tool for
023            addressing fairness and accountability in AI-generated content.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   In recent years, text-to-image diffusion models have made remarkable strides, enabling faster image
029   generation (Song et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2023; Yin et al., 2024), improved image quality (Dhariwal &
030   Nichol, 2021; Nichol et al., 2022; Rombach et al., 2022), and even extending into video generation
031   (Ho et al., 2022b; Khachatryan et al., 2023; Bar-Tal et al., 2024). Diffusion models operate through
032   a two-phase process: in the forward phase, noise is incrementally added to the data, while in the
033
      reverse phase, the model learns to denoise and reconstruct the image. However, despite their grow-
      ing popularity, these models often function as black boxes, providing little transparency into their
034
      decision-making processes or how they handle uncertainty (Berry et al., 2024; Chan et al., 2024). To
035
      address these limitations, we introduce Epistemic Mixture of Experts (EMoE), a novel framework
036   for capturing and quantifying epistemic uncertainty in text-conditioned mixture-of-experts diffusion
037   models, which are capable of generating high-resolution images (512 × 512 × 3). Epistemic un-
038   certainty, arising from a model’s lack of knowledge, can be reduced with additional data, whereas
039   aleatoric uncertainty, stemming from inherent randomness in the data, remains irreducible (Hora,
040   1996; Der Kiureghian & Ditlevsen, 2009; Hüllermeier & Waegeman, 2021).
041
      An example of our approach is illustrated in Figure 1. The top row contains images for the prompt,
042   “A white man holding the office of the US President” with low epistemic uncertainty (0.32), followed
043   by “A black man holding the office of the US President” with an uncertainty 0.34. The bottom row
044   displays images for the prompt, “A white woman holding the office of the US President” with an
045   uncertainty 0.43, followed by “A black woman holding the office of the US President” with high
046   epistemic uncertainty (0.60). This comparison highlights potential biases in the model’s handling of
047   demographic diversity across race and gender. To our knowledge, EMoE is the first framework to
048   effectively capture epistemic uncertainty in text-conditioned diffusion models for image generation.
049   The EMoE framework is built on two key components. First, it leverages pre-trained mixture-of-
050   experts (MoE) for zero-shot uncertainty estimation. Notably, the experts in the MoE were not trained
051   for uncertainty estimation but were independently trained on different datasets. Originally intro-
052   duced by Jacobs et al. (1991), Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) models form ensembles in sub-modules,
053   where each expert specializes in specific tasks, benefiting from a shared base model to ensure ef-
      ficiency while harnessing the collective power of multiple experts (Shazeer et al., 2017). Training

      The second key component of the EMoE framework is that it estimates uncertainty on a novel latent
      space identified by probing the intermediate activations of the diffusion model’s denoiser. This
085
      space enables the model to identify regions in the input space (i.e., prompts) where hallucinations
086
      or incorrect image generation are more likely. By disentangling the expert ensemble components
087   and measuring variance within this space, EMoE can detect high epistemic uncertainty early in the
088   denoising process and thereby offer a more proactive assessment than previous methods that evaluate
089   uncertainty after image generation (Song et al., 2024).
090
      By combining pre-trained experts with a novel latent space for uncertainty estimation, EMoE ad-
091
      dresses the challenge of quantifying epistemic uncertainty in text-conditioned diffusion models. We
092
      evaluate EMoE’s performance on the Common Objects in Context (COCO) dataset Lin et al. (2014),
093   and our contributions are as follows:
094
095          • We establish the EMoE framework for text-conditioned diffusion models, leveraging pre-
096            trained experts and introducing uncertainty estimation within a novel latent space in the
097            network (Section 3).
098          • We demonstrate the effectiveness of EMoE for image generation on the COCO dataset, a
099            widely used and challenging benchmark, and show that EMoE aligns with expectations of
100            epistemic uncertainty (Section 4.1).
101          • We further evaluate EMoE’s ability to detect novel data by assessing which languages the
102            model has previously encountered and examining the bias inherent in diffusion models.
103            This analysis is conducted across 25 different languages (Section 4.2 & Section 4.3).
104          • We justify our design choices by conducting a set of ablation studies (Section 4.4).
105
106   These contributions shed new light on the previously opaque area of epistemic uncertainty in text-
107   conditioned diffusion models, offering significant implications for risk assessment and decision-
      making processes in sensitive domains.

      2     BACKGROUND
109
110
      Diffusion models construct a Markov chain, where each step involves sampling from a Gaussian dis-
111   tribution. This setup is well-suited for uncertainty estimation, as probability distributions naturally
112   lend themselves to uncertainty reasoning (Hüllermeier & Waegeman, 2021). Furthermore, MoE
113   models are particularly effective at capturing epistemic uncertainty, as they leverage an ensemble of
114   experts, which can be viewed as a Bayesian approximation (Hoffmann & Elster, 2021).
115
116   2.1   D IFFUSION M ODELS
117
118   In the context of supervised learning, consider a tuple (x, y), where x represents an image of size
119   512 × 512 × 3 and y is the prompt associated with the image. The objective is to estimate the
120
      conditional distribution p(x|y), which is challenging due to its high-dimensional, continuous, and
      multi-modal nature. In this work, we use latent diffusion models (Rombach et al., 2022), a powerful
121
      model for arbitrary data distributions which reduces computational costs by operating in a latent
122
      space learned by an autoencoder. The autoencoder consists of an encoder E, which maps images to
123   their latent representation, and a decoder D, which does the opposite.
124
125   Diffusion models use a two-phase approach, consisting of a forward and a reverse process, to gen-
126
      erate realistic images. In the forward phase, an initial image x is encoded to z0 and then gradually
      corrupted by adding Gaussian noise over T steps, resulting in a sequence of noisy latent states
127
      z1 , z2 , . . . , zT . This process can be expressed as:

      2.3   S PARSE M IXTURE OF E XPERTS
185
186   MoE is a widely-used machine learning architecture designed to handle complex tasks by combining
187   the outputs of several specialized models, or “experts” (Jacobs et al., 1991; Shazeer et al., 2017). The
188   key intuition behind MoE is that different experts can excel at solving specific parts of a problem,
189   and by dynamically selecting or weighing their contributions, the overall model can perform more
190   effectively. MoE models are particularly useful in cases where the data is heterogeneous, involving
191   a variety of sub-tasks or domains that benefit from expert specialization.
192   MoE combines multiple expert models by forming an ensemble, utilizing cross-attention layers
193   and feed-forward networks embedded within the U-Net architecture. Let M denote the number of
194   experts, and let i denote the i-th expert. The cross-attention layer can then be expressed as:
195                       Qi = WQi ϕθ (zt ),      K i = WK i
                                                             τθ (y),     V i = WVi τθ (y).           (6)
196
      The matrices WQi , WK  i
                               , and WVi are learned projection matrices specific to each expert i, allowing
197
      each expert to attend to different aspects of the input information.
198
199   A similar process occurs within the feed-forward networks, where each expert processes the data
200   independently before their results are combined (Lepikhin et al., 2020). The ensemble created by
201   this mechanism leads to more robust predictions, as each expert is able to specialize and contribute
202
      uniquely to the final output. In addition to the ensemble created by the cross-attention and feed-
      forward layers, the MoE architecture includes a routing or gating network that dynamically selects
203
      which experts to activate. The gating network determines the top n ≤ M experts to use for a given
204
      input, and the final output is computed as a weighted sum of the selected experts’ outputs:

209   expert. This combination of expert specialization and dynamic routing allows MoE models to scale

      efficiently by being sparse and only selecting a subset of experts to pass through.

482   5   R ELATED W ORKS
483
484   Building ensembles of diffusion models for advanced image generation is challenging due to the
485   large number of parameters, often exceeding hundreds of millions (Saharia et al., 2022; Nichol
      et al., 2022; Ramesh et al., 2022). Despite this, methods like eDiff-I have emerged, using ensem-


                                                         9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      ble techniques to enhance image fidelity, though not for epistemic uncertainty estimation, requiring
487   approximately 2 million training iterations (Balaji et al., 2022). In contrast, DECU was specifically
488   developed for uncertainty estimation, with a training duration of 7 days (Berry et al., 2024), and fo-
489   cuses on estimating epistemic uncertainty for class label image generation. Our approach, however,
490   leverages pre-trained experts for epistemic uncertainty estimation, thereby reducing the computa-
491   tional burden to zero. Moreover, EMoE addresses a more complex challenge—estimating epistemic
492   uncertainty in text-based generation, rather than in a discrete input like a class label.
493   Previous research has addressed epistemic uncertainty estimation in neural networks, particularly
494   for image classification tasks, by employing Bayesian approximations (Gal et al., 2017; Kendall &
495   Gal, 2017; Kirsch et al., 2019). These works focus on discrete output spaces, which are significantly
496   simpler than image generation. However, another approach to estimating epistemic uncertainty is the
497   use of ensembles (Lakshminarayanan et al., 2017; Choi et al., 2018; Chua et al., 2018), commonly
498   applied in regression tasks (Depeweg et al., 2018; Postels et al., 2020; Berry & Meger, 2023a;b). For
499   example, Postels et al. (2020) and Berry & Meger (2023b) developed efficient ensemble generative
500   models based on Normalizing Flows (NF) to capture epistemic uncertainty. Berry & Meger (2023a)
501
      further advanced these methods by using Pairwise Difference Estimators to estimate uncertainty in
      a 257-dimensional output space with normalizing flows. Our work builds on this foundation by
502
      extending these techniques to higher-dimensional outputs (786,432 dimensions) in large diffusion
503
      models and considering the more complex input space of text.
504
505   With the rise of large generative models and the growing importance of uncertainty estimation, nu-
506   merous methods have been developed to estimate uncertainty in both image and text generation
507
      models (Malinin & Gales, 2020; Berry et al., 2024; Chan et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024). For instance,
      Chan et al. (2024) trained hyper-networks to estimate uncertainty in diffusion models for weather
508
      prediction. In contrast, EMoE generates uncertainty estimates from pre-trained expert networks,
509
      which are widely available online, such as on platforms like Hugging Face and Civit AI. Addition-
510   ally, some researchers have proposed using epistemic uncertainty to detect hallucinated responses
511   from large language models (Verma et al., 2023). In this context, EMoE could be employed for
512   hallucination detection in vision-language models, although the definition of hallucinated responses
513   varies across the literature (Xu et al., 2024; Duan et al., 2024; Sky et al., 2024). Further, while
514   previous methods have integrated uncertainty into model pipelines using MoE (Zheng et al., 2019;
515   Luttner, 2023; Zhang et al., 2024), these approaches neither address epistemic uncertainty nor con-
516   sider text-to-image generation tasks and are not applicable in a zero-shot manner.
517
518
519
      6   C ONCLUSIONS
520
521   In this paper, we introduced the Epistemic Mixture of Experts (EMoE) framework for estimating
522   uncertainty in text-to-image diffusion models. EMoE leverages pre-trained experts to provide com-
523   putationally efficient uncertainty estimates without the need for additional training. By incorporat-
524
      ing a novel latent space for uncertainty estimation within the diffusion process, EMoE can identify
      biases and regions of heightened uncertainty early in the image generation process.
525
526   Limitations. EMoE relies on the availability of pre-trained expert networks, which, although abun-
527   dant, may not always provide sufficient diversity for optimal uncertainty estimation in all scenarios.
528   The framework’s performance is closely linked to the quality and diversity of the pre-trained mod-
529   els it uses, which introduces potential unpredictability when handling novel or specialized inputs.
530
      Furthermore, while EMoE does not require additional training, it does require sufficient memory
      resources to load and run the ensemble of experts effectively.
531
532   Our experimental results show that EMoE not only improves the detection of epistemic uncertainty
533   but also sheds light on underrepresented linguistic biases in diffusion models. By utilizing readily
534   available pre-trained models, we demonstrated that EMoE scales efficiently while delivering reli-
535   able uncertainty estimates across a variety of input prompts. These capabilities have significant
536
      implications for fairness, accountability, and the robustness of AI-generated content.
537   As large generative models continue to expand in use, the ability to quantify and interpret uncertainty
538   will be increasingly important, particularly in applications like autonomous systems. Future work
539   may explore ways to address the limitations discussed and further optimize EMoE for more complex
      tasks and environments.

      Here is an intuitive explanation for our choice of estimator for epistemic uncertainty using the theory
837
      of Gaussian Processes. Each expert can be viewed as a sample from the posterior distribution of
838   functions given an input y, denoted as p(f (y)|y). By calculating the variance across these experts,
839   we obtain the variance σ 2 of p(f (y)|y), which serves as an estimate of epistemic uncertainty within
840   the Gaussian Process framework. In general, other works have used the difference among ensemble
841   components to denote epistemic uncertainty (Gal et al., 2017; Depeweg et al., 2018; Berry & Meger,
842   2023b).
843

844   equally. Therefore, let F = {fθi }N
                                        i=1 denote an ensemble of N neural networks, where each model
