                                                   A BSTRACT
010
011
               In the endeavor to make autonomous robots take actions, task planning is a major
012            challenge that requires translating high-level task descriptions into long-horizon
013            action sequences. Despite recent advances in language model agents, they remain
014            prone to planning errors and limited in their ability to plan ahead. To address these
015            limitations in robotic planning, we advocate a self-refining scheme that iteratively
016            refines a draft plan until an equilibrium is reached. Remarkably, this process can
017            be optimized end-to-end from an analytical perspective without the need to curate
018            additional verifiers or reward models, allowing us to train self-refining planners in
019            a simple supervised learning fashion. Meanwhile, a nested equilibrium sequence
020
               modeling procedure is devised for efficient closed-loop planning that incorporates
               useful feedback from the environment (or an internal world model). Our method
021
               is evaluated on the VirtualHome-Env benchmark, showing advanced performance
022
               with better scaling for inference computation. Code is available at https://
023            github.com/anonymous-iclr-2025/equilibrium-planner.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Recent advances in large language models (LLMs) have spurred tremendous progress in robotic
029   planning (Huang et al., 2022; Li et al., 2022; Singh et al., 2023; Driess et al., 2023; Ahn et al., 2023;
030   Huang et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2023; Hu et al., 2024). Based on their extensive world knowledge,
031   LLM agents seem close to autonomously performing robotic tasks, such as in household scenarios.
032   However, growing evidence shows that existing LLM agents struggle with task planning (Kaelbling
033
      & Lozano-Pérez, 2011) that decomposes a high-level task into mid-level actions. While this problem
      requires long-horizon planning as well as consideration of environmental feedback, LLMs are often
034
      limited by: (1) unidirectional dependency: due to autoregressive generation, previous tokens cannot
035
      attend to future tokens, resulting in limited ability to plan ahead (Wu et al., 2024a); (2) lack of error
036   correction for existing outputs, unless with a heavy system 2; (3) fixed forward process hindering the
037   allocation of more inference computation to further improve planning performance. These inherent
038   limitations of LLMs lead to inefficiency in the closed-loop long-horizon robotic planning.
039
      To address above challenges of LLM planners in closed-loop long-horizon planning, we advocate
040
      the approach of self-refinement (Welleck et al., 2023; Shinn et al., 2023; Kim et al., 2023; Madaan
041
      et al., 2023) that iteratively improves a previously generated plan. The reasons behind are threefold:
042   (1) bidirectional dependency: since the output is conditioned on a previous draft plan, it can attend
043   to all tokens in the plan (from an old version), thus improving its ability to plan ahead; (2) internal
044   error correction which allows implicit self-correction in a forward pass without an explicit, heavy
045   system 2; (3) dynamic computation allocation by iterating through a self-refinement process until
046   convergence. However, such a self-refining strategy imposes significant training difficulties because
047   it requires backpropagation through infinite self-refining steps (Werbos, 1990). This may be seen as
048   an extreme case that reflects some of the general challenges in teaching LLMs to plan and reason.
049   Existing solutions include curating process supervision (Uesato et al., 2022; Lightman et al., 2024)
050
      or applying reinforcement learning (Zelikman et al., 2024; OpenAI, 2024; Kumar et al., 2024), but
      they are considerably more complex than supervised training and difficult to implement.
051
052   This work proposes a simple learning framework for planning via self-refinement. Specifically, we
053   formulate the self-refining process as a fixed-point problem that recursively refines the plan until
      the equilibrium point, as illustrated in Fig. 1. While the forward process of this fixed-point problem

079   Another important cue for self-refinement in robotic tasks is closed-loop feedback from the environ-
080   ment. To efficiently incorporate environmental feedback, we devise a nested equilibrium sequence
081   modeling procedure consisting of inner and outer loops, where the inner loop iteratively refines a
082
      plan using previous feedback, while the outer loop updates the feedback by interacting with the envi-
      ronment. This enables closed-loop planning from even a few environmental interactions. Moreover,
083
      the nested equilibrium solving process is accelerated by reusing the previously derived equilibrium.
084
      We further implement the above design within an LLM agent framework, seamlessly integrating the
085   equilibrium model-based planner, an experience memory buffer containing past plans and feedback,
086   and a world model to estimate feedback in the absence of environmental interactions, thus allowing
087   the planning system to operate effectively in closed-loop long-horizon robot task planning scenarios.
088   The core contributions of our work are summarized as follows:
089
090   • We present equilibrium sequence modeling, a simple training approach for self-refining LLMs
091
        based on equilibrium models, allowing for end-to-end learning in a supervised manner.
092   • A nested equilibrium solving process is proposed to efficiently incorporate closed-loop feedback
093     into the equilibrium sequence modeling, reusing previous equilibrium solutions to alleviate infer-
094     ence computation. It is further implemented with a world model to improve practicality.
095   • Our method is evaluated on the VirtualHome-Env benchmark (Puig et al., 2018; Liao et al., 2019),
096     demonstrating its advantageous performance with better scaling w.r.t. inference computation.

      2   R ELATED W ORK
099
100
      LLMs for Planning. Scaling up inference computation to improve LLMs’ performance on planning
101
      and reasoning tasks has received increasing attention (Brown et al., 2024; Snell et al., 2024; Wu et al.,
102   2024b; OpenAI, 2024). Existing techniques involving chain-of-thought (Wei et al., 2022; Zelikman
103   et al., 2022; 2024), repeated sampling (Wang et al., 2023; Brown et al., 2024) and tree search (Yao
104   et al., 2023a; Zhao et al., 2023) showed preliminary results with handcraft system 2. Alternatively,
105   a method called self-refinement (Welleck et al., 2023; Shinn et al., 2023; Kim et al., 2023; Madaan
106   et al., 2023) suggests recursively refining the existing LLM output in an autonomous manner, but it
107   relies heavily on prompting or intricate training procedures. To fully exploit its potential, we propose
      an end-to-end optimization method for self-refinement through deep equilibrium models.

108
      Deep Equilibrium Models (Bai et al., 2019) are infinite-depth neural networks specified by fixed-
109   point problems x∗ = fθ (x∗ ), where fθ is an equilibrium layer. While their inference can take infinite
110   steps by the fixed-point iteration, their gradients are estimated using implicit differentiation (Krantz
111   & Parks, 2002) without backpropagating through all layers, thus enabling memory-efficient training.
112   They have been extensively applied to tasks such as visual understanding (Bai et al., 2020; 2022) and
113   image generation (Pokle et al., 2022; Geng et al., 2023; Bai & Melas-Kyriazi, 2024). In this paper,
114   we apply the fixed-point formulation of deep equilibrium models to the self-refinement process in
115   LLM planners, allowing for simple supervised training of LLMs to refine themselves. More detailed
116   introduction to deep equilibrium models is presented in Appendix A.

118   3     M ETHOD
119
120   We study the problem of robot task planning which aims to decompose a high-level task description
121   into long-horizon mid-level action sequences (Kaelbling & Lozano-Pérez, 2011). In the following,
122   we first discuss the limitations of LLM planners in self-refinement (Section 3.1) and address them
123   with a novel equilibrium sequence modeling scheme (Section 3.2), which is compatible with various
124   feedback from the designs in Section 3.3. Lastly, practical implementations are given in Section 3.4.
125
126   3.1   P RELIMINARIES ON S ELF -R EFINEMENT
127
      The prevailing LLMs are intrinsically limited in planning, as their unidirectional dependency results
128
      in limited capability to plan ahead (Wu et al., 2024a), and the lack of error correction hinders closed-
129   loop planning. These reasons call for alternative mechanisms to address robot task planning.
130
131   Recently, Welleck et al. (2023); Shinn et al. (2023); Kim et al. (2023); Madaan et al. (2023) proposed
132
      self-refinement, which uses an LLM fθ to iteratively refine the previous LLM output. This strategy
      naturally addresses the above limitations, since it introduces bidirectional token dependency and a
133
      dynamic error correction mechanism. Formally, let xt denote a draft plan and ct denote context (e.g.
134
      environmental feedback), then planning may be viewed as a self-refinement process as follows:
135
136                                              xt+1 = fθ (xt , ct ).                                     (1)
137   However, self-refinement via prompting (Shinn et al., 2023; Kim et al., 2023; Madaan et al., 2023)
138   has been found to be very limited by Huang et al. (2024). Alternative training-based methods require
139   careful curation of training sequences (Welleck et al., 2023; Havrilla et al., 2024) or reinforcement
140   learning (Qu et al., 2024; Kumar et al., 2024) and are therefore difficult to train. Overall, they remain
141
      deficient for robotic planning compared to system 2-based alternatives, as shown in Hu et al. (2024).
142
143   3.2   S ELF -R EFINEMENT AS A N E QUILIBRIUM M ODEL
144
      To address the training inefficiency of self-refinement approaches, this section proposes equilibrium
145
      sequence modeling, a simple supervised training scheme for teaching LLM planners to self-refine
146   through the lens of deep equilibrium models (Bai et al., 2019; Geng & Kolter, 2023).
147
148   Let us first consider a simplified scenario of self-refinement without environmental feedback, namely
149
      that the context ct is fixed, e.g. to a predefined system message c. Then, the self-refinement process
      in Eq. (1) reduces to a fixed-point problem concerning only the plan xt . Denote the initial plan by
150
      x0 = ∅ and the equilibrium plan, i.e. the endpoint, by x∗ , then its trajectory can be expressed as:
151
152                                 (x0 , c) → . . . → (xt , c) → . . . → (x∗ , c).                        (2)
153   Although its forward process is tractable with existing root-solving techniques, such as the classic
154   fixed-point iteration or alternative numerical methods (Broyden, 1965; Anderson, 1965), its training
155   requires recurrent backpropagation through multiple self-refining steps (Werbos, 1990). This results
156   in an extremely inefficient and unstable computational process where end-to-end training fails.
157   Instead, we approach it directly from an analytical perspective. Assuming access only to an outcome
158   supervision L(·, y) on the plan, e.g. its distance to the ground truth plan y, then self-refinement is
159   formulated as an optimization problem minimizing the loss function of the equilibrium plan:
160                                           min L(x∗ , y)
161                                             θ                                                          (3)
                                                s.t.   x∗ = fθ (x∗ , c).

203   This new formula represents a new equilibrium sequence modeling scheme that can be implemented
204   in two stages as in Fig. 2. In the first stage, we iterate over the fixed-point problem within Eq. (3) to

210   or reward models (Welleck et al., 2023; Havrilla et al., 2024; Qu et al., 2024; Kumar et al., 2024).

230   After the modification, the existing derivations only hold when we neglect the derivatives related to

232   the equilibrium planner can be trained in a similar supervised way as in Eq. (6) and Fig. 2, and after

234   iteratively interacting with the environment to obtain feedback is costly and may not be recoverable.

259   3.4   P RACTICAL I MPLEMENTATION
260
261   This section discusses the implementation of the proposed equilibrium planner. To enable effective
262   training while interacting with the environment, the following two modules are carefully devised to
263   complement the planner: an experience memory that caches all equilibrium plans and their feedback
264   during equilibrium solving, and a world model to estimate the closed-loop feedback in the absence
265   of environmental interactions. The planning framework is illustrated in Fig. 3.
266   Equilibrium Experience Memory. During the training process, our equilibrium model interacts
267   with the environment only when the inner loop reaches the equilibrium point. This results in a small
268   number of equilibrium points, which may not be sufficient for supervised training. To improve our
269   training efficiency and stability, we opt to cache all previously obtained equilibrium points, along
      with their environmental feedback, in an experience memory. Thereafter, these equilibrium points

      we alternate between using external and internal feedback in closed-loop robotic planning.

300
301   Benchmark. The VirtualHome-Env benhmark (Puig et al., 2018; Liao et al., 2019) is adopted during
302   the experiments. It contains 1360 long-horizon tasks with ground truth action sequence annotations
303   (average length 10.8) and provides updated scene graphs after each action, allowing simulation of
304   closed-loop feedback. To analyze the generalizability, we divided the dataset into a training set and
305   three test subsets, including the novel scene set, the novel task set, and the novel scene and task set.
306   More statistics and examples about VirtualHome-Env can be found in Appendix B.1.
307   Metrics. We use executability (Exec.), success rate (SR), goal conditions recall (GCR) following Hu
308   et al. (2024). Exec. evaluates whether the plan can be executed in given the environment, SR refers
309   to whether the goal is accomplished, and GCR measures the proportion of goal conditions achieved.
310   To examine the closed-loop planning capabilities, we study two test setups, without error correction
311   or with up to 10 corrections, the latter allowing for self-correction based on environmental feedback.
312   We also evaluate the computational efficiency by measuring TFLOPS at inference time.
313   Baselines. Our method is compared with Tree-Planner (Hu et al., 2024), SELF-REFINE (Madaan
314   et al., 2023), and a supervised finetuned planner. They are all reproduced using Llama 3 8B (Dubey
315   et al., 2024). In addition, we consider several baseline methods that call the GPT-3.5 API, including
316   ProgPrompt (Singh et al., 2023), Zero-shot Planner (Huang et al., 2022) and two self-refining plan-
317   ners, Local Replan (Raman et al., 2022; Guo et al., 2023) and Global Replan (Shinn et al., 2023).
318   Their results are presented for reference only. See Appendix B.2 for more details.
319   Implementation Details. Our implementation is consistent with the baseline methods by finetuning
320   from Llama 3 8B (Dubey et al., 2024) on the VirtualHome-Env training set (paired with the equi-
321   librium points). The number of finetuning epochs is set to 6, and the learning rate is set to 0.0002.
322   The world model is finetuned on all planner interactions for 5 epochs using the same learning rate.
323   A greedy LLM sampling strategy is used in later refinement steps until convergence. Moreover, we
      implement the KV cache to speed up inference. Further details are provided in Appendix B.3.

360   4.2   M AIN R ESULTS
361
      The experimental results in the two planning setups, without correction or with up to 10 corrections,
362
      are summarized in Tables 1 and 2. Overall, our method achieves the leading performance on the
363   majority of metrics. Specifically, the experimental results show that: (1) Even without error cor-
364   rection, our self-refining process still brings a significant improvement of 14% on SR in the novel
365   scene subset, with other metrics superior or comparable to the previous leading method. (2) By in-
366   corporating environmental feedback, our approach improves all metrics by more than 11% and up to
367   19%, showing clear advantages. (3) Similar to the existing finetuning-based methods, our generated
368   plans exhibit a high executability of over 90%, which can be improved to 99% by simply truncating
369   illegal overlength outputs. These results clearly confirm the advantages of our approach.
370   Figure 4 further compares our self-correction process with the baseline methods. As can be seen, our

476             5                               C ONCLUSION
477
478            This work proposes an equilibrium model-based LLM planner that is capable of self-refining plans
479            from external and internal feedback. Unlike existing self-refinement methods based on prompting
480            or sophisticated reinforcement learning, our proposed equilibrium sequence modeling allows simple
481            supervised training of the self-refining planners. Moreover, it also enables the planner to efficiently
482
               incorporate environmental feedback or a world model for closed-loop planning. We implement the
               proposed approach on the VirtualHome-Env benchmark, and the experimental results suggest that it
483
               can dynamically allocate inference computation to achieve state-of-the-art planning performance.

      A     BACKGROUND
757

      A.1   D EEP E QUILIBRIUM M ODELS
759
760   Traditional neural networks are constructed by explicitly stacking layers f (i) , which can be limited in
761   their expressiveness due to the fixed number of layers and predetermined forward process. Instead,
762   implicit models are defined by an underlying dynamic system to be solved, such as an ordinary
763   differential equation (Chen et al., 2018), a controlled differential equation (Kidger et al., 2020), a
764   stochastic differential equation (Kidger et al., 2021) or a fixed-point problem (Bai et al., 2019).
765
      Deep equilibrium models, first introduced in Bai et al. (2019), are a representative class of implicit
766   models characterized by fixed-point problems. Given an input c and a function fθ (x, c) such as a
767   Transformer block (Bai et al., 2019) or a Transformer (Geng et al., 2023), deep equilibrium models
768   define infinite-level stacking of this function xi+1 = fθ (xi , c) with i = 0, 1, . . . , L and L → ∞ by
769   solving the solution x∗ to the following fixed-point equation defined by fθ and c:
770
771                                               x∗ = fθ (x∗ , c)                                        (11)
772
      The forward pass of deep equilibrium models is root solving for the fixed-point problem. A common
773
      choice is the fixed-point iteration method, which starts from an initial guess x0 and iteratively applies
774   the transformation xt+1 = fθ (xt , c) until convergence. A sufficient condition for its convergence is
775   if fθ is a contraction mapping w.r.t. x, namely its Lipschitz constant is less than one (Banach, 1922),
776   which could be relaxed by the well-posedness condition in El Ghaoui et al. (2021). More advanced
777   root solvers include Broyden’s method (Broyden, 1965) or Anderson acceleration (Anderson, 1965).
778
779

782   1990) at high memory and computational cost, the gradient of deep equilibrium models is computed
783   analytically without differentiating over its forward pass. Given an equilibrium point x∗ = fθ (x∗ , c)
784   and a loss function L(x∗ , y), the loss gradient w.r.t. the model parameters θ is provided by the
785   implicit function theorem (Krantz & Parks, 2002; Bai et al., 2019) as follows:

                                             =          I−                .                            (12)
788                                      ∂θ      ∂x∗        ∂x∗       ∂θ

      Its proof is given in Appendix A.4. Due to the challenge of exactly computing the inverse Jacobian
790                     ∂fθ −1
      term A = (I − ∂x     ∗)   in the above gradient, existing work often approximate it via the damped
791
      fixed-point unrolling or the Neumann series (Geng et al., 2021b). Recently, Fung et al. (2022); Geng
792   et al. (2021a) propose to approximate the inverse Jacobian term by A ≈ I, with the former imposing
793   strong assumptions. In practice, dropping the inverse Jacobian/Hessian has been used extensively
794   in one-step gradient (Bolte et al., 2023; Luketina et al., 2016; Finn et al., 2017; Liu et al., 2019;

      A.3   T RANSFORMER - BASED D EEP E QUILIBRIUM M ODELS
798
799   Deep equilibrium models are initially proposed on Transformer architecture (Vaswani et al., 2017)
800   for language modeling tasks (Bai et al., 2019). This seminal work considers a Transformer block as
801   the basic unit fθ in the equilibrium model. Then, Geng et al. (2021a) investigates improvements over
802   the Transformer block by replacing self-attention with matrix decomposition. They also introduce
803   one-step gradient based on the approximation of A ≈ I for efficiency and stability, assuming that
804   the Lipschitz condition apply to a large number of matrix decomposition methods.
805   Recently, following the prevalence of Diffusion Transformers (Peebles & Xie, 2023), deep equilib-
806   rium models are extended to image generation tasks. Geng et al. (2023) propose generative equi-
807   librium Transformers consisting of two modules, one using Transformer as the basic unit fθ in the
808   equilibrium model. Their method yields advanced one-step image generation results. (Bai & Melas-
809   Kyriazi, 2024) replace most of the intermediate Transformer blocks with an equilibrium model, thus
      significantly reducing the number of parameters and memory usage for training and inference.

1022   B.3    I MPLEMENTATION DETAILS
1023
1024   Prompt. Our approach involves two LLMs, one LLM as the equilibrium planner and an additional
1025   LLM as an optional world model. The planner’s prompt is illustrated in Fig. 9. As can be seen, it
       consists of system prompt, task definition, environment description, history feedback, and draft plan.


                                                            19
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1026
       Notably, the system prompt is modified from Hu et al. (2024), and the environment section describes
1027   the initial environment sorted by rooms, including the object names with their IDs within each room.
1028   For the additional world model, we adopt a similar prompt, except that it receives more information
1029   about the initial environment, including edges in the scene graph that correspond to spatial relations
1030   The world model is then asked to predict the environmental feedback given a generated plan.
1031
       Finetuning. Both our equilibrium planner and the world model are finetuned in a supervised manner.
1032
       The equilibrium planner is finetuned for 6 iterations with a learning rate of 0.0002. At each iteration,
1033   we curate training data by pairing fixed points and ground truths, where the fixed points are sampled
1034   from an equilibrium memory of past iterations. To prevent overfitting to the history equilibrium, a
1035   decay ratio of 0.5 is used when sampling from the fixed points of previous iterations. Thereafter, we
1036   update the model parameters using gradient descent according to Eq. (6) for one epoch per iteration.
1037   For the world model, we collect all interacting experiences between the planner and the environment,
1038   including plans and feedback, and finetune it for 5 epochs using the same learning rate of 0.0002.
1039   Finetuning the world model takes about 30 hours due to its longer context (e.g. spatial relations).
1040   Inference. The inference procedure of our planner was described in Algorithm 1, which involves a
1041   nested equilibrium solving process. Given the environment and the task instruction, we initialize the
1042   draft plan x0 and the feedback c0 as null and iterate through a nested loop. Each inner loop reuses
1043   the feedback from the outer loop to self-refine the draft plan, and after it converges, we update
1044   the feedback by interacting with the environment or world model. This process continues until it
1045   reaches an upper bound on the outer loop, which we set to 10 to match Tree-Planner (Hu et al., 2024).
1046   We adopt greedy sampling for the LLM, except that the first refinement step uses top-k sampling
1047
       with k = 10 for higher diversity. Since most of the prompt remains unchanged during equilibrium
       solving, we implement KV cache to accelerate the inference process, which can be further improved
1048
       with parallel decoding techniques (Santilli et al., 2023; Cai et al., 2024; Kou et al., 2024).

1067   D    L IMITATIONS
1068
1069   While our equilibrium sequence modeling improves the planning capability of LLMs, we identify
1070   the following failure scenarios during the experiments: (1) hallucination of the equilibrium planner
1071
       and the world model as in vanilla LLMs; (2) lack of awareness of history context such as previously
       grabbed objects. The latter may be resolved with reasoning techniques as in Yao et al. (2023b).
1072
1073   In a broader sense, our method may be limited in generalizing to new domains because it requires
1074   the ground truth and environmental feedback during training. These procedures with the equilibrium
1075   solving process results in lower training efficiency. Also, the current formulation only considers the
1076   explicit output plan without implicit reasoning steps. Furthermore, our model has only text input and
1077
       no visual input, which limits its applicability in the real world. This can be resolved by introducing
       video-based planners (Du et al., 2024) and world models (Yang et al., 2024; Brooks et al., 2024).
