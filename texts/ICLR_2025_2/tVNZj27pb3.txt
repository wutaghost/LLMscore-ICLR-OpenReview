009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Due to the dynamic nature of tasks, how deep networks can transition from a
012            static structure, trained on previous tasks, to a dynamic structure that adapts
013            to continuously changing data inputs has garnered significant attention. This
014            involves learning new task knowledge while avoiding catastrophic forgetting of
015            previously acquired knowledge. Continual learning is a learning approach aimed
016            at addressing the problem of catastrophic forgetting, primarily by constraining or
017            isolating parameter changes to protect the knowledge of prior tasks. However, while
018
               existing methods offer good protection for old task knowledge, they often diminish
               the ability to learn new task knowledge. Given the sparsity of activation channels in
019
               a deep network, we introduce a novel misaligned fusion method within the context
020
               of continual learning. This approach allows for the adaptive allocation of available
021            pathways to protect crucial knowledge from previous tasks, replacing traditional
022            isolation techniques. Furthermore, when new tasks are introduced, the network can
023            undergo full parameter training, enabling a more comprehensive learning of new
024            tasks. This work conducts comparative tests of our method against other approaches
025            using deep network architectures of various sizes and popular benchmark datasets.
026            The performance demonstrates the effectiveness and superiority of our method.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Continual learning, a scenario that requires a model to handle a continuous stream of tasks while
032   preserving performance on all seen tasks, is pivotal for the advancement of artificial general intel-
033
      ligence (Masana et al., 2022; Liang & Li, 2024; Wang et al., 2024). The approach, mirroring the
      human learning process of acquiring and retaining diverse experiences about the real world, confronts
034
      a significant challenge: catastrophic forgetting (McCloskey & Cohen, 1989). This phenomenon
035
      results in the diminished proficiency of model in prior tasks after learning on new ones.
036
037   Various continual learning approaches have been proposed to mitigate the issue of catastrophic
038   forgetting, broadly categorized into three types. Regularization-based approaches entail adding
039
      regularization terms that leverage the weight information of previous tasks during the training of the
      current task. While this approach can mitigate catastrophic forgetting to some extent by constraining
040
      parameter shifts and ensuring protection of model parameters, it tends to result in relatively lower
041
      performance when confronted with significant variations in data characteristics. Rehearsal-based
042   approaches preserve data segments from previous tasks or use synthesized pseudo-data to retain
043   previous knowledge while learning new tasks, which can achieve a more unified output range for
044   the classification heads, leading to superior performance in scenarios of task agnostic. However,
045   from the perspective of data privacy protection (Agarwal et al., 2018), this approach does not suffice.
046   Architecture-based approaches focus on protecting parameters through techniques, achieving
047   performance that matches or exceeds that of previous network training. However, they have two
048   drawbacks, one is the requirement to know the task to which the identified object belongs in order to
049   achieve accurate recognition, and another is that this method leads to the isolation of tasks, hindering
050
      effective communication and information sharing among them.
051   We argue that existing architecture-based continual learning methods do not adequately leverage
052   the overall consideration of the sparsity of activation channels in deep networks. As illustrated
053   in Figure 1, We adopt a holistic perspective on the deep network, allocating distinct activation
      pathways for each task through pathway protection involves assigning unique pathways for data

082   Inspired by compensatory mechanisms observed in neuroscience and based on the sparsity of
083   activation channels in neural networks, we propose a novel method to maintain the overall stability

085   Our approach initially involves training a model for the first task. As new tasks emerge, a new
086   model is trained for each new task. Then, a matching procedure is employed to fuse the new and
087

088

089
      2020). Simple averaging may lead to interference and even cancellation of effective components, a
090   concern exacerbated during continual learning. Hence, in this paper, we align channels before model
091   fusion. In the shallow layers of the deep network, where tasks share more common features (Zhou
092   et al., 2022), we match the channels with high similarity to enhance mutual commonality. In contrast,
093   in deeper layers, where tasks exhibit more specific characteristics (Zhou et al., 2022), we match
094   channels with low similarity to facilitate the fusion of distinct task features while preserving their
095   distinctiveness, thus achieving pathway protection.

145   Continual learning. Deep networks exhibit a static structure, implying that once a task is learned,
146   network parameters need to remain fixed to prevent catastrophic forgetting (Wang et al., 2024).
147   However, continual learning addresses a more prevalent scenario in which tasks arrive as a continuous
148   data stream for the network to learn. In this context, strategies like regularization-based, rehearsal-
149   based, and dynamic architecture-based approaches are employed to mitigate catastrophic forgetting.
150   Regularization-based methods apply constraints to limit changes in weights or nodes from past
151   tasks, thereby reducing catastrophic forgetting. For instance, methods like EWC (Kirkpatrick et al.,
152   2017) incorporate the Fisher information matrix of previous task weights, while RWalk (Chaudhry
153
      et al., 2018) merges this matrix’s approximation with online path integration to gauge parameter
      importance. LwF method, on the other hand, employs output alignment to prevent the model weights
154
      from a large shift. SPG (Konishi et al., 2023) employs the Fisher information matrix to control the
155
      updates of each parameter, enabling more granular parameter protection. Rehearsal-based approaches
156   involve preserving portions of data from previous tasks or using some techniques to generate pseudo-
157   data (Shin et al., 2017). This data is then combined with the current dataset during the training
158   for the next task, alleviating catastrophic forgetting. For example, both approaches, LUCIR (Hou
159   et al., 2019) and iCaRL (Rebuffi et al., 2017), leverage the technique of preserving a portion of
160   previously acquired data along with knowledge distillation for incremental learning. Continual
161   Prototype Evolution (CoPE) (De Lange et al., 2021) combines the principles of the nearest-mean
      classifier with a reservoir-based sampling strategy. Dynamic architecture-based methods encompass

      expanding models and employing parameter isolation techniques to retain previous knowledge while
163   accommodating new knowledge expansion.
164
165   Parameter isolation-based continual learning. This approach aims to safeguard parameters to pre-
166
      serve knowledge acquired from previous tasks (Zhang et al., 2024b). The Piggyback method (Mallya
      et al., 2018) involves learning a series of masks over a post-pretrained model, corresponding to
167
      various tasks, resulting in a series of task-specific subnetworks. The PackNet method (Mallya &
168
      Lazebnik, 2018) uses pruning method to protect neurons, which are important to previous tasks.
169   CLNP method (Golkar et al., 2019) divides neurons in the deep network into active, inactive, and
170   interference parts, utilizing previously learned features and unused weights from the network to train
171   new tasks. Supsup method (Wortsman et al., 2020) employs masking to protect specific parameters
172   important for tasks. Chen et al. (2020) prunes the model to obtain the optimal subnetwork for the task,
173   thus preserving knowledge and achieves generalization for new tasks through re-growing. GPM (Saha
174   et al., 2021) utilizes gradient mapping to project the knowledge from previous tasks into mutually
175   orthogonal gradient subspaces, thereby enabling continual learning. The WSN algorithm (Kang et al.,
176   2022a), based on the lottery hypothesis, learns a compact subnetwork for each task while maintaining
177
      the weights chosen for previous tasks unchanged. SPU (Zhang et al., 2024a) employs causal tracking
      to select model parameters for updates, thereby facilitating knowledge protection. However, most
178
      of these methods involve pruning or masking based on network weights, leading to non-structured
179
      modifications that risk compromising the integrity of network. Our approach integrates the channel
180   properties of network. This allows different tasks to utilize distinct pathways for propagation and
181   flow, preserving the overall integrity of the deep network without causing disruption.
182
183
      The sparsity of deep network. According to the mechanisms observed in neuroscience, in the brains
      of healthy adults, the density of connections remains roughly constant. Despite learning more tasks,
184
      the capacity of neurons in the brain remains relatively unchanged. Meanwhile, within deep networks,
185
      this phenomenon also manifests. Upon completion of training, deep networks typically exhibit sparse
186   activation, with a small proportion of effectively activated neurons (Han et al., 2015; Liu et al., 2015;
187   Fan et al., 2020; Dai et al., 2021). Furthermore, as per the findings from Mao et al. (2017), there
188   exists an inverse correlation between the overall accuracy and granularity of deep networks. Under
189   comparable sparsity conditions, finer granularity tends to yield optimal accuracy. Concurrently, the
190   MEMO method (Zhou et al., 2022) highlights similarities in the shallow layers of different models
191   while showcasing differences in the deeper layers. Therefore, we hypothesized that within the coarser
192   granularity (shallow layers) of the deep network, a denser occupation of channels occurs, while in the
193   finer granularity (deeper layers), channel occupation tends to be sparser. We proceeded to validate
194
      this hypothesis through experimental verification.

236   3.2 G RAPH MATCHING FOR DEEP NETWORK FUSION .
237
      Recently, some studies have employed graph matching approaches for model fusion (Su et al.,
238
      2021). Graph matching bears resemblance to a quadratic assignment problem (QAP) (Loiola et al.,
239
      2007), with the objective of establishing correspondences between the nodes in an image and the
240   edges connecting these nodes. The activation distribution of deep network channels is not fixed
241   across training iterations, resulting in some neurons exhibiting high activation for one task, but low
242   activation for another. If a straightforward averaging fusion is performed, it may lead to interference
243   and blending of effective components within the deep network (Singh & Jaggi, 2020). Hence, aligning
244   the channels before fusion becomes a crucial step in the integration process.
245

246   problem. In our framework, a deep network is conceptualized as an image. This representation
247   enables the alignment of two deep networks through the application of a graph matching algorithm.

249   between adjacent layer channels as edges. It is noteworthy that, within deep networks, we assert
250   that matching occurs exclusively within each layer, as cross-layer matching holds no significant
251   relevance. This approach facilitates the effective application of graph matching methods in deep
252   networks, given their large-scale neuron configuration. The specific formula for graph matching is

300   3.3   T HE SPARSITY OF DEEP NETWORK .
301
302   The primary rationale behind this approach stems from the sparsity of deep networks. To accommo-
303   date future learning tasks, continual learners often utilize over-parameterized deep networks. The
304   reason is that continual learning frequently relies on over-parameterized deep deep networks to allow
305   flexibility for future tasks.
306   We believe that a deep network is composed of multiple layers, and we use ℓ to represent the index
307   of one layer of the deep network. Meanwhile, it has been observed in previous studies (Zhou et al.,
308   2022) that shallow layers across models of different tasks exhibit notable similarities, whereas deeper
309   layers demonstrate distinct characteristics. The deep network model can be decoupled into a classifier,
310   denoted as G(·), and a feature extractor, represented as F (·). The feature extractor further bifurcates
311   into a shallow-layer deep network S(·) and a deep-layer deep network D(·) in Eq.(4):
312
                                    M (x) = Gℓ (Fℓ (x)) = Gℓ (Dℓ (Sℓ (x)))) .                               (4)
313
314   The Lottery Ticket Hypothesis (Frankle & Carbin, 2018) posits that within deep networks, there exist
315   specific "winning tickets" generated during the training process. These winning tickets, it suggests,
316   enable comparable performance with the entire network in different tasks while employing fewer
317   parameters and requiring shorter training times. Regarding the sparsity aspect of deep networks,
318   the Lottery Ticket Hypothesis asserts that only a small subset of connections (weights) within the
319   network is crucial for learning and performance, while the remaining connections can be pruned (set
320
      to zero) without significantly affecting the performance of network.
321   According to previous studies, the sparsity of deep networks means it’s unnecessary to compute
322   information for every parameter, as much of it is ineffective. Theoretically, we can utilize the sparsity
323   of deep networks by allocating different channels for different tasks, achieving comparable or even
      superior performance, thereby tackling the catastrophic forgetting problem in continual learning.

      In this section, we delve into a comprehensive discussion of our proposed continual learning structure
327   based on graph matching, along with its specific implementation. Section 4.1 outlines the framework
328   of proposed method for continual learning based on graph matching. We fuse the model trained on a
329   new task with the one trained on previous tasks. Rather than merely averaging the parameters of the
330   two models, we conduct pathway alignment based on graph matching before fusion. To achieve the
331   protection and sharing of knowledge, we employ different similarity matrix across different layers of
332   the deep network. Section 4.2 provides a detailed account of the optimization process.

334   4.1   G RAPH - NOT-M ATCHING FOR C ONTINUAL L EARNING
335
336   Unlike previous architecture-based methods, which usually mask weights crucial for the previous
337
      task, our strategy stems from the holistic nature and sparsity of neural networks. We believe that
      safeguarding previous tasks through misaligned addition represents a matching approach. This
338
      method not only protects the overall integrity of the deep network, but also allocates different
339
      directional channels for various tasks.
340
341   Overview. The overall structure of our proposed LwI algorithm is shown in Figure 3. When a new
342   task arrives, we train a new model for it. We then employ graph matching for channel alignment before
343
      model fusion, by analogizing neural network nodes to nodes in graph matching and connections
      between deep network channels to edges in graph matching. The specific alignment operations, as
344
      illustrated in the left diagram, involve matching the channels with high similarity in shallow layers
345
      and low similarity in deep layers. Meanwhile, matching operations are not required in L1, and in
346   L4, only append operation is necessary. The overall process of our proposed method is illustrated in
347   Algorithm 3 in appendix.

      Model Fusion Process. The fusion process of the new and old models is illustrated in Algorithm 1.
      We use Euclidean Distance in Eq.(5) to compute distances between weights and subsequently employ
350
      specific graph matching algorithms:
351

      where K denoted the similarity matrix, eac and ebd denote the similarity relationship between
354
      the channels of adjacent layers in two models, specifically focusing on the edges (a, c) and (b, d),
355   respectively. We also utilize cosine similarity for measurement, and specific details can be found in the
356   ablation study and the appendix C.5. In the shallow layers, we need to maximize similarity matching
357   for protecting old knowledge and promoting the collaboration of different tasks. Hence, the similarity
358   matrix between two edges is itself. Conversely, in the deeper layers, we repeat a similar process but
359   utilize a minimizing similarity approach for protecting the individuality of different tasks, facilitating
360   misaligned fusion of channels. Therefore, the similarity matrix between two edges is represented by
361   its negation. The specific matching process can be found in Algorithm 2 in the appendix. This process
362   yields a permutation matrix, enabling us to perform matrix multiplication between the old model and

      the permutation matrix. Subsequently, the permutation matrix of similarity from the previous layer is

405   4.2      O PTIMIZATION
406
      Knowledge distillation aims to mitigate semantic discrepancies between the new and old models,
407
      otherwise, model fusion loses its significance. Additionally, in training a model for a new task,
408
      leveraging the universally applicable knowledge from the old task model, such as shallow-level
409   enhances the efficiency of learning through distillation. To leverage prior task knowledge, we
410   employed previous models as pre-trained models, integrating their parameters into the current model
411   for subsequent task training. Simultaneously, throughout the entire training process, the feature
412   extractor of the classifier undergoes continuous modifications. If there is a noticeable drift in
413   the feature space of the classifier, the knowledge memorized by the model may become outdated.
414   Consequently, it is imperative to maintain a relative consistency in the feature space of the classifier
415   during the training process. Further details can be found in the appendix C.6 and C.7.

530
531
      This paper proposes a framework for continual learning, LwI, achieving pathway protection between
      different tasks using model fusion approach. Inspired by the compensatory mechanisms in the human
532
      brain and considering deep network sparsity, we employ graph matching approach to achieve pathway
533
      protection for the cooperation and diversification between different tasks. our method facilitates
534   the collaboration and preservation of knowledge among different tasks, consequently leading to
535   enhanced performance. We validated our approach using two network structures of different sizes,
536   and further validation can be performed on larger models. Our method acknowledge some limitations,
537   notably the lack of validation of the proposed method using large models. Additionally, the graph
538   matching algorithm can be accelerated in future work by employing sparse matrix techniques, we
539   will investigate more effective and efficient matching processes in future work. We hope this work
      opens the new direction for future research, pathway protection.

921
922   In this paper, we employ two measures, task-agnostic and task-aware, to simultaneously evaluate
923   the performance of these methods in scenarios of known tasks (such as task incremental learning)
924   and unknown tasks (such as class incremental learning). Task-agnostic refers to appending all of the
925   classifier’s head to a given data and then taking the maximum value, where the label corresponding
926   to the maximum value is assigned to the category. Task-aware, on the other hand, involves already
927
      knowing the task associated with a given data and directly obtaining the maximum value from the
      corresponding classification head, where the label corresponding to the maximum value is assigned
928
      to the data’s category. Due to the lack of uniformity in the output of the classification head in our
929
      framework, the final performance of Task-agnostic is generally lower than that of Task-aware. Based
930   on the findings in Table 1 and the results below, it is evident that our approach has outperformed even
931   the exemplar-based methods iCaRL and LUCIR in the majority of task-agnostic scenarios.

1069   Through the comparison of results and analysis of the four experimental sets, we summarize the
1070

1071   Firstly, regarding the network model capacity, we posit that, under identical scenarios, the gradual
1072   increase in model capacity leads to a sparser channel occupancy. This sparsity constitutes a key
1073   aspect of our proposed methodology. Thus, the conclusions drawn from the experiments, particularly
1074   the higher performance gains achieved by ResNet18 over ResNet32 under task-agnostic conditions,
1075   validate the correctness of our proposed task diversification concept, as depicted in Figure 2.
1076   Secondly, with respect to the dataset, our observations indicate that under equivalent deep network
1077   architectures, the superiority of our method becomes more pronounced with increasing dataset
1078   complexity. This emphasizes the efficacy of our approach in handling intricate datasets. EWC
1079   and RWalk methods are designed to address issues arising from significant data variations, making
       it challenging for these regularizations to effectively constrain parameter shifts. LwF, primarily

       employed for training different tasks, experiences the blending of task knowledge, as illustrated in
1081   Figure 2. This blending is likely to result in outcomes inferior to our method. WSN requires a mask
1082   when dealing with various tasks, limiting its applicability to task-aware testing. Additionally, as
1083   the number of tasks increases, the reduction in learnable parameters diminishes its effectiveness.
1084   iCaRL and LUCIR methods benefit from partial datasets of all previous tasks during the training of
1085   subsequent tasks, offering advantages for task-agnostic testing.

       Thirdly, in the situation of task agnostic, our deep network exhibits lower performance compared to
1087   exemplar-based continual learning (CL) methods on the CIFAR-100 dataset. We posit that, while our

1210   C.5   E XPERIMENTS ON DIFFERENT SIMILARITY MEASUREMENT FORMULAS .
1211
1212   In this study, the Euclidean distance and cosine similarity were employed to measure the distance
1213
       between two model channels.
1214   The Euclidean distance primarily quantifies the distance between two vectors in space, with smaller
1215   absolute values indicating closer proximity. It is a commonly used distance measurement formula.
1216   On the other hand,
1217   Cosine similarity gauges the angle between two vectors within the same sphere, mainly reflecting
1218   directional differences. Larger numerical values denote smaller angle discrepancies, indicating closer
1219   proximity in space. It is a widely used formula for measuring similarity.
1220
       This section compared and validated the use of Euclidean distance and cosine similarity to measure
1221
       channel proximity and revealed that, in most cases, using distance measurement is preferable to using
1222
       cosine similarity.
1223

                                        Cosine Similarity =                                              (14)
