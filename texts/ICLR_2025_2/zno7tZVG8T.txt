009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Post-Training Quantization (PTQ) and Sparsification (PTS) are dominant meth-
012            ods in the compression of Large Language Models (LLMs) due to their minimal
013            resource usage and generalizability. It is a natural idea to integrate quantization
014            and sparsification in a unified framework, which however, often results in substan-
015            tial accuracy losses. Here we argue that, the key lies in optimization. This paper
016
               introduces a novel joint optimization strategy that concurrently mitigates errors
               induced by both sparsification and quantization. Unlike sequential approaches,
017
               our method employs learnable transformation matrices to simultaneously optimize
018
               errors across both dimensions, preventing the typical misalignments associated
019            with sequential optimizations. Furthermore, we present a reordering mechanism
020            within the learnable mask sparsification process to maintain consistent sparsity
021            ratios. This mechanism ensures the prioritization of the least important weights
022            during each update iteration, thus enhancing the stability of the compression pro-
023            cess. Our approach demonstrates considerable performance enhancements across
024            diverse models and datasets, with the most notable gains observed under condi-
025            tions of extremely low-bit quantization and high sparsity ratios. For example,
026            in the LLaMA2-13b model with weight quantization at 2 bit and a 75% sparsity
027
               configuration, our method surpasses the state-of-the-art (SOTA) by 9.03% in av-
               erage accuracy across five zero-shot tasks. Meanwhile, in the newest LLaMA3-8b
028
               model, with weight quantization at 3 bit and a 50% sparsity configuration, our
029
               method outperforms the SOTA by 4.58% (56.86% vs 52.28%) in zero-shot tasks
030            and achieves a perplexity reduction of 4.45 on the WikiText2 dataset (10.78 vs
031            15.23).

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   The remarkable capabilities of large language models (LLMs) (Touvron et al., 2023a;b; Brown et al.,
037   2020; Le Scao et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2022) have garnered considerable interest across a multi-
038   tude of disciplines. Yet, the escalating dimensions of these models present significant deployment
039
      challenges on consumer-grade graphics processing units (GPUs). As a result, the topics of model
      compression and acceleration have increasingly become focal points within the research community.
040
      Two prevalent techniques—model quantization and sparsification—are recognized for their effec-
041
      tiveness in both compressing and accelerating model inference processes. Quantization (Frantar
042   et al., 2023; Yuan et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2023; Xiao et al., 2023; Shao et al., 2023; Ma et al., 2024)
043   involves mapping high-precision floating-point parameters to lower-bit fixed-point representations.
044   This method not only reduces the model size by minimizing the bit requirements but also boosts
045   memory access efficiency, a crucial factor in speeding up computation. Especially on hardware plat-
046   forms equipped to accelerate bitwise operations, or on processors specifically optimized for low-bit
047   computations, quantized models can achieve notably faster processing speeds. Additionally, model
048   sparsification (Frantar & Alistarh, 2023; Sun et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2024) com-
049   presses models by transforming dense weight matrices into sparse formats through the application
050
      of sparse masks. This approach can significantly quicken computation on hardware platforms that
      are optimized for handling sparse matrix operations, thereby enhancing the overall execution speed
051
      of the models.
052
053   While model compression techniques such as quantization and sparsification accelerate computa-
      tional processes, they invariably entail some degree of performance degradation. The extensive

054
      fine-tuning required for large language models, which often comprise hundreds of millions of pa-
055   rameters, involves significant time and computational resources. As a result, compression methods
056   that bypass extensive training phases are gaining prominence. Among these, post-training quantiza-
057   tion, which necessitates no fine-tuning or only minor calibration, has been widely adopted from the
058   era of convolutional neural networks through to that of large language models. GPTQ (Frantar et al.,
059   2023) exemplifies advanced quantization techniques, implementing progressive quantization with
060   the Optimal Brain Surgeon (OBS) (Hassibi et al., 1993) algorithm to mitigate quantization errors
061   by revising high-precision parameters. Similarly, AWQ (Lin et al., 2023) performs input channel
062   scaling, utilizing activation statistics to achieve equivalent scaling of weight activations, effectively
063
      managing the impact of outliers on quantization. OmniQuant (Shao et al., 2023) pioneered the adap-
      tation of scaling and clipping parameters into learnable entities, facilitating block-wise optimization
064
      using small data batches. In the realm of large language models, non-training-dependent sparsifica-
065
      tion methods are increasingly favored over those requiring fine-tuning. For instance, Wanda (Sun
066   et al., 2023) posits that activations must be included in weight importance assessments, suggest-
067   ing that a singular weight statistic is insufficient to capture a weight’s true role within the model.
068   Dsnot (Zhang et al., 2023) introduces a training-free approach by iteratively updating sparse masks,
069   dynamically reducing the density of weights. Moreover, BESA (Xu et al., 2024) innovates by ap-
070   plying a learnable factor to a set of candidate sparsity rates, assigning variable sparsity to different
071   linear layers, and enabling dynamic mask optimization through adaptive sparsity.
072   The two compression techniques under consideration—model quantization and sparsification—do
073   not require a training phase, which allows for their combination to potentially enhance model com-
074   pression further. However, this amalgamation significantly impairs the model’s performance. Even
075   with advancements in cutting-edge compression algorithms, the performance of the model severely
076   deteriorates under configurations characterized by low bit-width and high compression ratios. A
077   primary contributor to this decline is the sequential implementation of the quantization and sparsity
078   algorithms. Our experiments indicate that initiating with quantization optimization followed by the
079   application of weight sparsity amplifies the quantization errors, consequently increasing the overall
080
      mean squared error loss.
081   This paper introduces a joint optimization strategy designed to concurrently minimize errors asso-
082   ciated with quantization and sparsity. More precisely, following each iteration that establishes a
083   sparsity mask, we calibrate the quantization process using small data batches, applying the sparsity
084   mask to the weights concurrently. Losses resulting from quantization and sparsity are optimized us-
085   ing a learnable transformation matrix, which adjusts dynamically. This joint optimization approach
086
      is designed to be orthogonal to a broad spectrum of existing quantization and sparsity algorithms,
      and it has demonstrated superior performance across a variety of algorithmic configurations. Fur-
087
      thermore, because the learnable transformation matrix modifies the distribution of weight data in
088
      each iteration, the learnable mask sparsity strategy must consistently ensure that weights with lower
089   importance factors are preferentially masked. To maintain this selection, we implement a reordering
090   of the weights based on sparsity metrics at every iteration, prioritizing the least important weights to
091   sparsity. This reordering strategy promotes stable convergence and maintains optimal performance
092   throughout the training process. Ultimately, our method excels in the combined compression of
093   large language models, particularly under conditions of low-bit and high-sparsity configurations. In
094   conclusion, the key contributions of this study are summarized as follows:
095
096          • We introduce a novel joint optimization strategy that integrates compression techniques to
097
               concurrently minimize errors associated with quantization and sparsity. This methodol-
               ogy is designed to be orthogonal to a wide spectrum of existing compression algorithms,
098
               demonstrating unparalleled performance across diverse algorithmic combinations.
099
100          • Furthermore, we have proposed a dynamic reordering method to enhance the efficacy of
101            learnable masks. By systematically reordering weight rows according to sparsity met-
102            rics at each iteration, this method ensures the consistent masking of the least significant
103
               weights. This process not only stabilizes the convergence of the optimization algorithm but
               also maximizes the effectiveness of learnable masking, contributing to more reliable model
104
               training outcomes.
105
106          • Our techniques have set new benchmarks in the field of combined compression for large
107            language models, particularly in configurations characterized by low bit-width and high
               sparsity. Notably, our strategy improved average performance by 9.03% over traditional

      2     R ELATED W ORK
117
118   The substantial parameter count of large language models presents considerable challenges for the
119
      application of quantization and sparsity methods, especially when fine-tuning is required. Given
      these challenges, this paper focuses on the efficient strategies of post-training quantization and spar-
120
      sity.
121
122   Post-training Quantization (PTQ). In the convolutional neural network (CNN) domain, several
123   post-training quantization methods, such as Adaround (Nagel et al., 2020), BRECQ (Li et al., 2021),
124   and QDROP (Wei et al., 2022a), employ adaptive rounding techniques for model weights. This
125
      involves reconstructing the model either layer-wise or block-wise to optimize the rounding parame-
      ters, thereby minimizing quantization errors. Specifically, in large language models, outliers signifi-
126
      cantly complicate the quantization process. GPTQ (Frantar et al., 2023) addresses this by correcting
127
      full-precision parameters using the OBS (Hassibi et al., 1993) algorithm. Furthermore, numerous
128   studies (Wei et al., 2022b; Lin et al., 2023; Xiao et al., 2023; Yuan et al., 2023; Shao et al., 2023;
129   Ma et al., 2024) have adopted equivalent transformation operations—including scaling, shifting,
130   rotating, and rearranging—to relocate the effects of activation outliers to the weights, effectively
131   diminishing their adverse impacts. Furthermore, the quantization method based on orthogonal ma-
132   trix (Ashkboos et al., 2024) transformation efficiently generates a quantized model without the need
133   for retraining. This orthogonally constrained sampling matrix effectively mitigates the impact of
134   outliers in activations on the quantization process.
135   Post-training Sparsity. Similarly, outliers in activations detrimentally influence sparsity perfor-
136   mance within large language models. Traditional pruning algorithms can severely degrade model
137   performance, typically necessitating subsequent fine-tuning for performance recovery. To address
138   this, a variety of post-training sparsity techniques (Frantar & Alistarh, 2023; Sun et al., 2023; Zhang
139   et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2024) have been developed to efficiently implement sparsity in large mod-
140   els. They range from employing single-dimensional to multi-dimensional importance metrics, from
141   those based on statistical characteristics to those relying on gradient-based optimization, and evolve
142
      from static to dynamic sparsity approaches. The evolution of post-training sparsity methods has sig-
      nificantly enhanced the adaptability of large language models across diverse computing platforms,
143
      promoting broader deployment and application.

146   3     M ETHODOLOGY
147
148   In this section, we initially introduce foundational concepts and establish notation conventions rele-
149   vant to quantization and sparsification. We then propose the application of a joint optimization com-
150   bination compression method in large language models. This method, in contrast to other combined
151   compression strategies, consistently achieves stable and low mean squared error losses throughout
152   the optimization process. Finally, to ensure effective sparsification of weights with low importance,
153   we introduce a reordering strategy that maintains the priority of non-critical weights, thus preserving
154   the model’s overall performance.

287   3.3   R EORDERING
288
289   To reduce the complexity of optimization, the learnable mask sparsification strategy typically es-
290
      tablishes a set of candidate sparsity rates for each row of the weight matrix W . Each candidate
      sparsity rate is multiplied by a learnable factor to ascertain the optimal sparsity rate for every row.
291
      Concurrently, the rows of W are organized according to a sparsity importance factor, and weights
292
      deemed less important are sparsified based on the aggregate sparsity rate derived from the candidate
293   rates. However, the learnable transformation matrix A, as specified in Eq. 7, alters the distribution of
294   weights with each iteration. Consequently, the importance of weights changes dynamically through-
295   out the optimization process. We propose that at the beginning of each optimization iteration, the
296   quantized weights Q(AW ) are reordered based on the following importance metric:
297
298                                                          2
                                             γi,j = ∥X:,i ∥F · |Wi,j | .                                  (9)
299
300   By employing a reordering strategy, the dynamic mask sparsification method consistently and accu-
301
      rately targets weights with lower importance in each iteration. This approach significantly enhances
      the generalizability and effectiveness of the joint optimization method, thereby ensuring consistently
302
      superior performance across various models and datasets.

517   5    C ONCLUSION
518
519
      Post-training quantization and sparsification methods have shown considerable promise in the com-
520   pression of large language models. However, previous combined compression approaches have
521   relied on sequential optimization, separately minimizing quantization and sparsification errors. This
522   practice has led to a significant increase in mean squared error (MSE) loss, especially in high com-
523   pression rate configurations. Our combined compression approach mitigates this issue by simulta-
524   neously optimizing both quantization and sparsification errors. Additionally, learnable sparsifica-
525   tion masks often fail to appropriately sparsify less important weights when dealing with dynamic
526   weights. To overcome this, we propose a reordering method that prioritizes the sparsification of
527   lower-importance weights in each iteration. This approach ensures stability and convergence in
528
      the optimization process, further reducing the MSE of the objective function. Our method con-
      sistently improves performance across various compression configurations and models. Notably,
529
      the combined compression method exhibits substantial potential for enhancing model performance,
530
      particularly in high compression rate scenarios. Future research could focus on developing more
531   effective learning strategies for joint optimization to further advance this field.
