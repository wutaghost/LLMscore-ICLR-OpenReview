009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Co-speech gestures are essential to non-verbal communication, enhancing both
012            the naturalness and effectiveness of human interaction. Although recent methods
013            have made progress in generating co-speech gesture videos, many rely on explicit
014            visual controls, such as pose images or TPS keypoint movements, which often
015            lead to artifacts like inconsistent backgrounds, blurry hands, and distorted fingers.
016            In response to these challenges, we present the Implicit Motion-Audio Coupling
017            (IMAC) method for co-speech gesture video generation. IMAC strengthens audio
018
               control by coupling implicit motion parameters, including pose and expression,
               with audio inputs. Our method utilizes a two-branch framework that combines an
019
               audio-to-motion generation branch with a video diffusion branch, enabling real-
020
               istic gesture generation without requiring additional inputs during inference. To
021            improve training efficiency, we propose a two-stage slow-fast training strategy that
022            balances memory constraints while facilitating the learning of meaningful gestures
023            from long frame sequences. Furthermore, we introduce a large-scale dataset de-
024            signed for co-speech gesture video generation and demonstrate that our method
025            achieves state-of-the-art performance on this benchmark.

028   1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Co-speech gestures are essential to non-verbal communication, playing a key role in how humans
031   convey meaning. As virtual agents become more interactive, generating gestures synchronized with
032   speech is crucial for human-computer interaction. While methods exist for co-speech gesture gener-
033
      ation (Liu et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024) and talking head videos (Tian et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2024a),
      generating realistic co-speech gesture videos remains challenging, especially for half-body anima-
034
      tions that mimic natural human communication. Addressing this is critical for improving virtual
035
      agents in content creation, entertainment, and education.
036
037   Several methods have been proposed to address the co-speech gesture video generation task. For
038   instance, MYA (Huang et al., 2024) and Vlogger (Corona et al., 2024) utilize motion generation
039
      models to create motion data, which is then rendered as pose image sequences to serve as explicit
      visual control. These pose image sequences, along with a reference image, are then used as input to
040
      a diffusion model to generate the final videos. Similarly, S2G (He et al., 2024) uses TPS keypoint
041
      movements as explicit visual control for gesture video generation. However, these approaches often
042   struggle with issues such as inconsistent backgrounds, blurry hands, and distorted hands. The root
043   cause of these artifacts lies in the explicit visual control, which edits the reference image. When this
044   manipulation is translated into pixel space, the resulting distortions introduce visual artifacts that
045   degrade the overall quality of the generated videos.
046
      A straightforward solution to the above issues might be directly using audio information as the
047
      condition for video diffusion generation, similar to prior works in talking head generation (Tian
048   et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2024a). However, unlike talking head generation, co-speech gesture video
049   generation involves producing upper-body gestures. The many-to-many mapping between audio
050   and gestures complicates this approach, as the correlation between audio and gestures is relatively
051   weak. This makes it difficult for the weak audio signal alone to serve as an effective condition
052   for generating co-speech gesture videos. To this end, we propose our Implicit Motion-Audio Cou-
053   pling (IMAC) method to address the co-speech gesture video generation task. Inspired by Handif-
      fuser (Narasimhaswamy et al., 2024), we couple implicit motion parameters (pose and expression)

092   2   R ELATED W ORK
093
094   Co-speech Gesture Video Generation. A common approach to co-speech gesture video genera-
095   tion splits the process into two stages: audio-to-motion generation and motion-to-video synthesis.
096   Speech2Gesture (Ginosar et al., 2019) uses a generative adversarial networks (GAN) (Goodfellow
097   et al., 2020) to generate 2D skeleton movements, followed by another GAN for video synthesis.
098   Speech-Drives-Templates (Qian et al., 2021) applies a variational autoencoders (VAE) (Kingma,
099
      2013) in the first stage and image warping in the second. Vlogger (Corona et al., 2024) utilizes two
      diffusion models to generate pose images as explicit visual controls and the corresponding human
100
      videos. MYA (Huang et al., 2024) is originally designed as a pose image guided video genera-
101
      tion framework but can be easily adapted for co-speech gesture video generation by integrating an
102   audio-to-motion technique. S2G (He et al., 2024) employs a diffusion model to map audio to key-
103   point movements, using a nonlinear thin-plate spline (TPS) transformation to decouple latent motion
104   from video. However, these methods often suffer from artifacts such as inconsistent backgrounds,
105   and blurry, or distorted hands. These issues arise from the explicit visual control, which modifies the
106   reference image and introduces distortions when translated into pixel space. In contrast, our method
107   couples implicit motion parameters with audio information, enabling the generation of high-quality
      gesture videos without such artifacts.

      Co-speech Gesture Generation. Co-speech gesture generation aims to produce lifelike human
109   gestures synchronized with given audio inputs. Due to the complex, many-to-many relationship
110   between audio and gestures, generative models have proven more effective than deterministic mod-
111   els. Researchers have applied various generative approaches to this task. For example, GAN-based
112   models have been used to predict skeleton movements, enhancing gesture diversity (Ginosar et al.,
113   2019). Other methods have employed VAE (Yi et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024) and flow-based models
114   (Ye et al., 2022) to capture the intricate relationship between audio and gestures. Recently, diffusion-
115   based generative models have gained attention, with several studies exploring their use in gesture
116   generation (Zhu et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2023a; Chen et al., 2024). However, these approaches typ-
117
      ically generate only motions, which are later rendered as pose images rather than gesture videos. In
      contrast, our work introduces an audio-to-motion generation branch that directly generates implicit
118
      motion parameters without rendering, using them to enhance the model’s audio control capabilities
119
      for co-speech gesture video generation.
120
121   Talking Head Video Generation. A related area of research is talking head video generation,
122   where key challenges include achieving precise synchronization between lip movements and audio
123
      while maintaining the subject’s visual realism. Researchers have tackled these challenges using
      various innovative techniques. Recent advancements have utilized large-scale pre-trained diffusion
124
      models, integrating them with specialized audio control modules to achieve impressive results in
125
      both visual fidelity and audio-visual synchronization (Tian et al., 2024; Stypułkowski et al., 2024;
126   Xu et al., 2024a). Inspired by these works, we also employ audio as a direct control signal for
127   co-speech gesture video generation. However, since audio alone is often too weak to effectively
128   drive gesture video generation, we couple it with implicit motion parameters to enhance the model’s
129   control capabilities.

134   3.1   P RELIMINARY: L ATENT D IFFUSION M ODELS (LDM S )
135
136   Latent Diffusion Models (LDMs) (Rombach et al., 2022; Blattmann et al., 2023b) are effective
137   and efficient approaches for generating high-quality images or videos by performing diffusion and
138   denoising in the latent space. Compared to pixel-level diffusion models (Ramesh et al., 2022; Song
139   et al., 2021; Ho et al., 2022), LDMs significantly reduce computational complexity by operating in
140
      a compressed latent space derived from a pre-trained VAE.
141   Given an input video x ∈ RF ×H×W ×3 , a VAE encoder E compresses it into a latent representation
142   z = E(x), where z ∈ RF ×h×w×c , with h < H and w < W denoting the spatially downsampled
143   dimensions and c being the number of latent channels. To recover the original video, a VAE decoder
144   D reconstructs the latent representations back into the pixel space x̄ = D(z).
145   The LDM framework consists of two primary processes: diffusion and denoising. During the dif-
146   fusion phase, noise is progressively added to the latent variable z, resulting in a noisy latent repre-
147   sentation zt , where t ∈ {1, . . . , T } with T being the total number of diffusion steps. The denoising
148   phase applies a learned denoising function ϵθ (zt , t, c) to iteratively remove the noise and recover
149   the clean latent representation z0 . The denoising model is typically optimized using the following
150   objective:
151
152
                                  LLDM = Ez,ϵ∼N (0,1),t ∥ϵ − ϵθ (zt , t, c)∥22 ,

153                                                                                                       (1)
154
155   where ϵ represents the added noise, and c represents any conditional guidance, such as text prompts
156   or initial frames. The model typically adopts a 3D U-Net architecture (Wang et al., 2023b;a) for
157   improved temporal modeling, particularly when dealing with video data.
158   In this work, we build our model on the open-sourced Image-to-Video (I2V) diffusion model,
159   I2VGen-XL (Zhang et al., 2023b), which is capable of generating complex motions from a single
160   input image (Xing et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2024). While our model primarily utilizes I2VGen-XL for
161   dynamic video generation, it remains flexible and can also integrate other video diffusion models,
      such as SVD (Blattmann et al., 2023a).

190   3.2     P ROBLEM D EFINITION
191
192   Given a human reference image Iref and an audio sequence A ∈ RF , where F denotes the number
193   of frames, co-speech gesture video generation seeks to produce a video that faithfully retains the
194   appearance of the individual in Iref while synchronizing fluidly with the audio sequence A ∈ RF .
195   The goal is to generate a video that not only preserves the visual identity of the reference image
196
      but also captures the nuances of the speaker’s style in the audio. This results in natural, expressive
      gestures closely tied to the audio cues, creating a cohesive and lifelike video.

201   3.3.1    R EFERENCE E NCODER
202
      In human-centered video generation, reference control is essential for preserving fine-grained ap-
203
      pearance details across frames, such as facial identity, clothing textures, and background elements.
204   This ensures consistency between the reference image and the generated video. Previous methods
205   like ControlNet (Zhang et al., 2023a) fall short because they rely on control features (e.g., depth
206   and edge maps) that are spatially aligned with the target image. Such reliance does not accommo-
207   date the spatial misalignment between reference and target images inherent in our task. Similarly,
208   methods like ReferenceNet (Hu, 2024; Chang et al., 2023) introduce computational inefficiencies by
209   employing complex attention mechanisms to handle reference features, which significantly increase
210   the computational load.
211   In co-speech gesture generation, we focus on a few identities since learning gestures for each identity
212   requires substantial data, and scaling to more identities significantly increases data needs. There-
213   fore, using a heavy encoder is unnecessary for this setting. To address this, we utilize a lightweight
214   reference encoder composed of a series of residual-connected convolutional modules to extract ref-
215   erence features. More importantly, the reference encoder adjusts the feature dimensions to match
      those of the noise latent, allowing the features to be added together for further learning. In addi-

231   3.3.2    AUDIO - TO -M OTION G ENERATION
232
233   Current diffusion-based methods often rely on explicit visual control for generating co-speech
234   gesture videos, typically using an audio-to-motion framework that first generates pose image se-
235   quences (Huang et al., 2024) or TPS keypoint movements (He et al., 2024). This introduces system
236   complexity and results in suboptimal output quality. A more straightforward approach would be to
237
      directly condition the diffusion model on audio information. However, relying solely on audio is
      insufficient due to the weak correlation between audio and gestures. To address this, we propose
238
      enhancing the audio input with implicit motion information. Specifically, we incorporate SMPL-X
239
      (Pavlakos et al., 2019) motion parameters, comprising poses and expressions, into our training pro-
240   cess, and subsequently generate the corresponding motion parameters during inference. Formally,
241   given an audio sequence A ∈ RF , the model generates a motion parameter sequence, including
242   a pose vector β ∈ RF ×3J for joint rotations, capturing finger articulation across J joints, and an
243   expression vector θ ∈ RF ×100 for facial expressions.
244
      Audio Encoding
245
246   Given an audio sequence A ∈ RF , we extract the MFCC feature and process it with one temporal
247   self-attention layer. Concurrently, we use a pre-trained HuBERT (Hsu et al., 2021) model to extract
248   the semantic feature. Afterward, two types of features are concatenated and fused by a linear layer.
249   Motion Parameter Generation
250
      Given the extracted audio features, a conventional approach is to use a cross-attention layer to condi-
251
      tion motion parameter generation on these audio features. However, our primary objective is video
252
      generation, and relying on cross-attention diminishes the strength of the audio information in the
253   subsequent video generation process. To address this limitation, we propose concatenating the au-
254   dio features with noisy motion parameters during training to retain the strength of the audio signal.
255
256
      For processing pose and expression information, we employ two transformer encoders, each com-
      prising a series of temporal self-attention layers to handle the respective features. Once the motion
257
      features are encoded, the resulting representation is passed to the video generation branch via cross-
258
      attention, ensuring effective integration of the motion data into the video synthesis.
259
260   It is important to note that we do not use classifier-free guidance (Ho & Salimans, 2022) in our
261   framework, as our goal is to enhance the audio information using motion parameters, not to intro-
262
      duce variability in motion generation. Moreover, the video diffusion branch already incorporates
      diversity, and adding diversity to the audio-to-motion branch would significantly complicate train-
263
      ing.

289   3.4.2       I NFERENCE
290
      During inference, we employ a strategy similar to that used in training. Given an audio sequence,
291
      we divide it into longer frame sequences (F = 80) and sequentially pass shorter chunks (F =
292
      16) to the video diffusion model. For long video generation, previous methods often use complex
293   schemes, such as overlap denoising (Huang et al., 2024) or optimal motion selection (He et al.,
294   2024). However, in our experiments, we find that simply using the last frame of the previously
295   generated clip as a reference image is sufficient.

309   4.1.1       DATA P ROCESSING
310
311   Video Filtering. Some of the collected video candidates may not meet the high-quality standards
312
      required for co-speech gesture video generation. For instance, certain videos may feature multiple
      individuals or exhibit significant scene changes across frames. To handle this, we first segment the
313
      videos into clips using SceneDetect2 , ensuring that videos with different scenes are separated. Next,
314
      we filter out multi-person videos by employing TalkNet (Tao et al., 2021), a speaker diarization
315   model that detects and distinguishes different speakers in a video. By using TalkNet, we ensure
316   that the remaining videos contain only single-person scenes. Finally, we use MediaPipe (Lugaresi
317   et al., 2019) to detect human faces and discard videos with low face detection confidence, which are
318   typically videos featuring side views of faces. Additionally, we retain only video clips longer than 3
319   seconds, as shorter clips are unlikely to contain meaningful gestures.
320
      Data Annotation. After obtaining the video clips from the previous stage, we annotate our data as
321   follows. First, we extract the audio from the videos using ffmpeg. Next, SHOW (Yi et al., 2023)

346   4.2   M ETRICS
347
348   For better comparison with existing works, we follow the previous work (He et al., 2024) to de-
349   sign our evaluation metrics. We assess the quality, diversity, and synchronization between gestures
350
      and speech using four key metrics: Fréchet Gesture Distance (FGD) (Qian et al., 2021), Diversity
      (Div.) (Liu et al., 2022), Beat Alignment Score (BAS) (Li et al., 2021), and Fréchet Video Distance
351
      (FVD) (Unterthiner et al., 2018).
352
353   FGD quantifies the distributional discrepancy between real and synthesized gestures within the fea-
354   ture space. Diversity measures the average feature distance among generated gestures, indicating
355   their variability. To compute both FGD and Diversity, we first extract 2D human skeletons using the
356
      DWPose (Yang et al., 2023b) framework, a readily available pose estimation tool, and train an auto-
      encoder using skeleton data from our training dataset. BAS represents the mean distance between
357
      the nearest speech beats and corresponding gesture beats, ensuring temporal coherence between
358
      speech and gestures. For this metric, skeletons are also extracted from the test set to identify the
359   gesture beats. FVD assesses the overall fidelity of the gesture videos by leveraging the I3D (Wang
360   et al., 2019) classifier, which is pre-trained on the Kinetics-400 (Kay et al., 2017) dataset, to compute
361   FVD within the feature space.

      distortion and maintains a consistent background. In contrast, S2G and MYA struggle with incon-
      sistent backgrounds and suffer from blurry hands and distorted fingers. It is important to note that
412
      the blur produced by our method is natural motion blur, whereas the blur in S2G and MYA is caused
413
      by their explicit visual TPS keypoint or pose image control. When this control is translated into
414   pixel space, it results in blurred outputs. These flaws are even more pronounced in the videos, and
415   we encourage readers to review the video comparisons available in the supplementary material. Ad-
416   ditionally, MYA often memorizes appearance features during training. This causes the generated
417   videos to replicate the memorized appearance instead of using the reference image, resulting in
418   inconsistencies, as shown in Fig. 5.

      The quantitative results are presented in Table. 2. As seen in Table. 2, our model achieves state-
420   of-the-art performance across three objective metrics. In particular, the superior results on FGD
421   and Diversity demonstrate our model’s effectiveness in generating natural and diverse gestures. In
422   addition, our model achieves the best FVD performance, indicating its ability to generate higher-
423   quality videos compared to previous works. The lower BAS performance of our method compared
424   to MYA may be due to the audio-to-motion generation stage we trained for MYA. While it does not
425   produce ideal gestures, it effectively captures the alignment between gesture peaks and audio peaks,

      In this paper, we introduce the Implicit Motion-Audio Coupling (IMAC) method, designed to tackle
532
      key challenges in co-speech gesture video generation by integrating implicit motion parameters with
533
      audio information. Our innovative two-branch framework, which combines an audio-to-motion gen-
534   eration branch with a video diffusion branch, facilitates realistic gesture generation without requiring
535   additional inputs during inference. To optimize the training process, we propose a two-stage slow-
536   fast training strategy, allowing the model to learn meaningful gestures efficiently while addressing
537   memory constraints. Additionally, we develop a large-scale dataset tailored for co-speech gesture
538   video generation and demonstrate the state-of-the-art performance of our method on this dataset.
539   Extensive experiments and analysis confirm that our approach generates realistic, natural co-speech
      gesture videos that align seamlessly with the audio.

706   The training process is divided into two stages. In the first stage, we train only the audio-to-motion
707   branch using audio and motion parameters. This stage runs for 3,000 epochs with a learning rate
708   of 1e-4. The first stage is trained on 8 RTX 8000 GPUs for 1 day with a batch size of 256. In the
709   second stage, we train the entire network for 200 epochs with a learning rate of 1e-5. This stage is
710   trained on 4 A100 GPUs for 4 days with a batch size of 1. For the audio-to-motion branch, the frame
711   sequence length is 80, while for the video diffusion branch, it is 16. The input image resolution is
712
      512x512. The classifier-free guidance (CFG) scale is set to 3.5 for the video diffusion branch.
