009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011
               Recent work has suggested that certain neural network architectures—particularly
012
               recurrent neural networks (RNNs) and implicit neural networks (INNs)— are ca-
013
               pable of logical extrapolation. That is, one may train such a network on easy
014            instances of a specific task and then apply it successfully to more difficult in-
015            stances of the same task. In this paper, we revisit this idea and show that (i) The
016            capacity for extrapolation is less robust than previously suggested. Specifically, in
017            the context of a maze-solving task, we show that while INNs (and some RNNs)
018            are capable of generalizing to larger maze instances, they fail to generalize along
019            axes of difficulty other than maze size. (ii) Models that are explicitly trained to
020            converge to a fixed point (e.g. the INN we test) are likely to do so when extrapo-
021            lating, while models that are not (e.g. the RNN we test) may exhibit more exotic
022
               limiting behaviour such as limit cycles, even when they correctly solve the prob-
               lem. Our results suggest that (i) further study into why such networks extrapolate
023
               easily along certain axes of difficulty yet struggle with others is necessary, and (ii)
024
               analyzing the dynamics of extrapolation may yield insights into designing more
025            efficient and interpretable logical extrapolators.

054
      In this work we revisit prior results on logical extrapolation with both RNNs and INNs in the context
055   of a single task: maze-solving (Bansal et al., 2022; Anil et al., 2022). While previous studies on
056   logical extrapolation in maze-solving have characterized difficulty as simply a function of maze size,
057   we introduce two new ways in which to vary the difficulty: (i) a binary variable deadend start ∈
058   {True, False} which, when set to True, constrains the start point to have exactly degree 1, (ii)
059   a percolation constant p ∈ [0, 1] which relates to the likelihood of a maze containing loops. Only
060   for p = 0 are mazes are guaranteed to be acyclic and thus have unique solutions. We note that in
061   both prior works examining logical extrapolation in maze-solving, deadend start = True and
062   p = 0. We show that the models introduced in prior work (Bansal et al., 2022; Anil et al., 2022)
063
      do not generalize when deadend start and p are varied, even though they generalize well as
      maze size is varied. More detail on the maze solving task and the modifications we make is given in
064
      Section 3.2.
065
066   As our second major contribution, we investigate how RNNs and INNs generalize. Initially, it
067   was observed that with proper training RNNs converge to a fixed point, even for more difficult
068   task instances such as larger maze sizes (Bansal et al., 2022). However, Anil et al. (2022) hints
069
      at more complex behaviour, as they find evidence of periodicity in the dynamics of INNs when
      applied to larger mazes. We quantify this phenomenon using tools from Topological Data Analysis
070
      (TDA)(De Silva et al., 2012; Perea & Harer, 2015; Tralie & Perea, 2018). We find that, while the
071
      INN we consider (Anil et al., 2022) consistently converges to a fixed point, regardless of maze size,
072   the RNN we consider (Bansal et al., 2022) exhibits more complex limiting behaviour. Specifically,
073   for most larger mazes, this RNN converges to either a two-point cycle or two-loop cycle. In order to
074   streamline TDA on INN/RNN architectures, this work also contributes a PyTorch-based (Paszke
075   et al., 2019) wrapper to Ripser (Bauer, 2021; Tralie et al., 2018), a fast Python library for TDA
076   (see Subsection 2.3 and Appendix D for more details).
077
      Our results suggest that a network’s ability to extrapolate may depend on the axis along which
078   difficulty is increased; thus, greater caution is needed when using neural networks for extrapolation.
079   We conclude by discussing how the tools introduced for studying periodicity may also be useful in
080   other deep learning contexts.

      2.1   R ECURRENT N EURAL N ETWORKS
085
086   A special class of RNNs, namely, weight-tied input-injected networks (or simply weight-tied
087   RNNs), are used in logical extrapolation (Schwarzschild et al., 2021b; Bansal et al., 2022). For
088   a K-layer weight-tied RNN NΘ , the output is given by
089
090                    NΘ (d) = PΘ2 (uK ) where uj = TΘ1 (uj−1 , d) for j = 1, . . . , K.                 (1)
091
092   Here, Θ1 and Θ2 are the parameters of the networks TΘ1 and PΘ2 respectively, and Θ := {Θ1 , Θ2 },
093   while d denotes the input features. These networks represent a unique class of architectures that
094   leverage weight sharing across layers to reduce the number of parameters. The input injection at
095
      each layer ensures the network does not ‘forget’ the initial data (Bansal et al., 2022). In (Bansal
      et al., 2022), it is empirically observed that a certain weight-tied RNN extrapolates to larger mazes
096
      when applying more iterations. The authors speculate that the reason for this success is that the
097
      model has learned to converge to fixed points within its latent space (Section 5, Bansal et al. (2022)).
098
099
      2.2   I MPLICIT N ETWORKS
100
101   Drawing motivation from (Bansal et al., 2022), (Anil et al., 2022) propose to use implicit neural
102   networks (INNs) for logical extrapolation tasks. INNs are a broad class of architectures whose
103   outputs are the fixed points of an operator parameterized by a neural network. That is,
104
105                             NΘ (d) = PΘ2 (u⋆ )     where     u⋆ = TΘ1 (u⋆ , d).                       (2)
106
107   Here again Θ = {Θ1 , Θ2 } refers collectively to the parameters of the networks TΘ1 and PΘ2 and d
      is the input feature, while u⋆ represents a fixed point of TΘ . These networks can be interpreted as


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      infinite-depth weight-tied input-injected neural networks (El Ghaoui et al., 2021; Bai et al., 2019;
109   Winston & Kolter, 2020).
110
111   Unlike traditional networks, INN outputs are not defined by a fixed number of computations but
112
      rather by an implicit condition. INNs have been applied to domains as diverse as image classification
      (Bai et al., 2020), inverse problems (Gilton et al., 2021; Yin et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2022; Heaton
113
      et al., 2021; Heaton & Wu Fung, 2023), optical flow estimation (Bai et al., 2022), game theory
114
      (McKenzie et al., 2024a), and decision-focused learning (McKenzie et al., 2024b). In principle,
115   INNs are naturally suited for logical extrapolation, as they are not defined via an explicit cascade of
116   layers, but rather by an implicit, fixed-point, condition. This condition can be viewed as specifying
117   when the problem is considered solved. Key to logical extrapolation is that this characterization of
118   “solving a problem” is always the same, regardless of the difficulty of the problem at hand.
119
120   2.3     T OPOLOGICAL DATA A NALYSIS IN THE L ATENT S PACE
121
122   For both RNNs and INNs, we call {uj }K              n
                                                 j=1 ⊂ R the latent iterates, and n the latent dimension.
123   Note that n can be, and often is, larger than the dimension of the input feature d or network output
124   NΘ (d). To characterize the limiting behaviour of the sequence of latent iterates we study its shape.
125   Intuitively, if this sequence exhibits periodic behaviour, it should trace out a loop. More precisely,
126
      the sequence should appear as though it was sampled from the topological equivalent of a circle
      embedded in Rn . Topological data analysis (TDA) provides a set of tools for analysing the shape of
127
      point clouds, and has been previously applied in other contexts to study periodicity (De Silva et al.,
128
      2012; Perea & Harer, 2015; Tralie & Perea, 2018).
129
130   As stated in Section 1, we construct a PyTorch wrapper to Ripser (Bauer, 2021; Tralie et al.,
131   2018), a fast Python library for TDA (see Appendix D for further details). Ripser computes
132
      (persistent) homology groups to identify the most significant topological features of the point cloud.
      The relevant quantities are the zeroth and first (persistent) Betti numbers, which we denote as B0 and
133
      B1 respectively. These are the “dimensions”1 of the zeroth and first homology groups, and count the
134
      respective number of connected components and loops in the data. We identify and interpret three
135   common values of the tuple [B0 , B1 ].
136
137           1. Convergence to a point ([B0 , B1 ] = [1, 0]). The sequence is clustered around a single
138              point. No loops are present.
139           2. Two-point cycle ([B0 , B1 ] = [2, 0]) The sequence is clustered around two points, and
140              alternates between them. No loops are present.
141
              3. Two-loop cycle ([B0 , B1 ] = [2, 2]) The sequence lies along two well-separated, thickened
142
                 loops, and alternates between them.
143
144   We emphasize that, while 1. represents the expected convergent behaviour (see Sections 2.1, 2.2) 2.
145   and 3. represent novel, previously undetected limiting behaviour. A more precise overview of TDA
146   is presented in Appendix B.

      3.1   T HE MAZE - SOLVING TASK
194
195   In this and previous work (Schwarzschild et al., 2021a;b; Bansal et al., 2022; Anil et al., 2022),
196   maze solving problems are encoded as raster images ( Figure 2). Given this RGB input image, the
197   task is to return a black and white image representing the unique path from start (indicated by a
198   green tile) to end (indicated by a red tile). In this work, we consider “accuracy” on a single maze
199   to be 1 if the solution is exactly correct and 0 otherwise. However, instead of using the original
200   “easy to hard” dataset (Schwarzschild et al., 2021a), we use the maze-dataset Python package
201   (Ivanitskiy et al., 2023). maze-dataset can provide maze-solution pairs in the same format and
202
      from the same distribution, but allows modifications to the distribution if desired. The original “easy
      to hard” dataset only contains acyclic (i.e. percolation parameter p = 0, any path between two nodes
203
      is unique) mazes generated via randomized depth-first search (RDFS) and with start positions having
204
      exactly degree 1 (i.e., deadend start=True). We remove these restrictions to investigate the
205   behavior of the selected models on out-of-distribution mazes in Subsection 3.2. More details on our
206   usage of maze-dataset are given in Appendix A.
207

      3.2   E XTRAPOLATION
209
210   Usage of the maze-dataset package allows us to explore the behavior of DT-Net and PI-Net
211   outside of the training distribution in a direction other than simply maze size. Specifically, in ad-
212   dition to being able to create mazes of any size (Figure 2), we investigate mazes whose start is
213   not restricted to nodes of degree 1 (Figure 3). Furthermore, by setting the percolation parameter
214   p to values > 0, we can create mazes that may contain cycles, which means that the uniqueness
215   of valid paths or even shortest-path solutions is no longer guaranteed (Figure 3). More detail on
      maze-dataset and our usage of it is given in Appendix A of the appendix and Subsection 3.1.

      The results of Subsection 3.2 suggest two points warranting further discussion. First, we highlight
      that seemingly mild distribution shifts (e.g. that induced by toggling deadend start) can have a
364
      large negative effect on model performance, while performance can be unchanged under a seemingly
365
      larger distribution shift (e.g. increasing maze size). Secondly, other distribution shifts (e.g. using a
366   nonzero percolation parameter and thus allowing for maze cycles) may make the task, as framed by
367   the training data given to the model, ill-posed. More specifically, both DT-net and PI-net are
368   trained to find the unique path from start to end, but when the maze has even a single loop, there
369   is no longer a unique path. When presented with a maze that does not have a unique solution path,
370   both models fail (see Figure 3.2 and Appendix Subsection C.3), whereas a human might reasonably
371   reinterpret the task (e.g. “find the shortest path”, or even “find a path”) and solve it.
372   The results of Subsection 3.3 suggest that the dynamics of RNNs are richer than previously thought,
373   particularly when the latent space is high-dimensional. While Section 4 clearly shows that models
374   trained with path independence (Bansal et al., 2022) converge to fixed points more frequently, it is
375   unclear how this correlates with (i) overall model accuracy and (ii) robustness towards distributional
376   shifts. If allowing more exotic limiting behaviour (e.g. limit cycles, not just fixed points) is benign,
377   or even desirable, various theoretical results on the convergence and backpropagation of RNNs
      and INNs (Liao et al., 2018; Wu Fung et al., 2022; Ramzi et al., 2022; Geng et al.; Bolte et al.,

423
424   Using a maze-solving task with out-of-distribution test datasets constructed along different axes
425   (maze size, deadend start, and percolation), we demonstrate that the ability of RNNs or INNs to
426   extrapolate can depend on the type of out-of-distribution shift considered. Specifically, we find that
427   a trained RNN and INN (DT-Net and PI-Net, respectively), can successfully extrapolate maze-
428   solving to larger mazes but are less successful in extrapolating maze-solving to mazes with start
429   positions with multiple neighbors and mazes with loops.

      A        A DDITIONAL M AZE DATASET D ETAILS
595
596   Both DT-Net and PI-Net were trained on the same maze dataset (Schwarzschild et al., 2021a)
597   containing 50, 000 mazes of size 9 × 9, meaning the mazes are subgraphs of the 5 × 5 lattice.
598   Training mazes were generated via RDFS without percolation, and with the start position being
599   constrained to being at a dead end (exactly 1 neighbor). We mimic this training distribution and add
600   out-of-distribution shifts using the maze-dataset Python package (Ivanitskiy et al., 2023).
601
      We first note that for controlling maze size, maze-dataset.MazeDatasetConfig takes a
602   parameter grid n which denotes the size of the lattice which the maze is a subgraph of. By contrast,
603   the “easy to hard” (Schwarzschild et al., 2021a) dataset considers an n × n mean that many blocks in
604   its raster representation. To convert between these two notions of maze size, n = 2 · (grid n) − 1.
605
      We can modify the start position constraint in maze-dataset by setting
606
      deadend start=False in endpoint kwargs.                        When False, the start position is
607
      sampled uniformly at random from all valid nodes, while when True the start position is samples
608   uniformly at random from all valid nodes with degree one. Valid nodes are, by default, those not
609   directly matching the end position or directly adjacent to it.
610
611
      Randomized depth-first search (RDFS) is a standard algorithm for generating mazes, and produces
      mazes which are spanning trees of the underlying lattice, and thus do not contain cycles. For any
612
      acyclic graph with a spanning connected component, there is a unique (non-backtracking) path be-
613
      tween any pair of points, and thus solutions are guaranteed to be unique. In our work, we select
614   LatticeMazeGenerators.gen dfs percolation as the maze ctor parameter. This
615   function takes an additional variable p in maze ctor kwargs, which controls the percolation
616   parameter. This percolation parameter, denoted p in our work, means that the final maze is the result
617   of a logical OR operation on the presence of all possible edges in the maze between an initial maze
618   generated via RDFS and a maze generated via percolation, where each edge is set to exist with prob-
619   ability p. This is equivalent to first generating a maze with RDFS and then setting each wall to an
620   edge with proability p. Since the initial RDFS maze is a spanning tree, adding any edge will cause
621   the creation of a cycle, thus giving our desired out-of-distribution mazes.

734   C.1   A DDITIONAL D ETAILS ON PI-net
735
736   During our experiments we determined that there was another model parameter, beyond the num-
737   ber of iterations, that had a strong impact on the accuracy of PI-Net. Specifically, there is a
738   threshold parameter within the forward solver, Broyden’s method, that controls the maximum
739   rank of the inverse Jacobian approximation. Based on code included in the supplementary material
740   for Anil et al. (2022), it appears the threshold parameter was originally set at 40. However,
741
      with this setting PI-Net performed very poorly; it failed on all mazes of size 49 × 49. Increas-
      ing threshold increased the accuracy of PI-Net, but also increases memory costs because it
742
      requires storing a number of high-dimensional latent iterates equal to threshold. For our experi-
743
      ments, we used threshold = 1,000 in order to achieve strong accuracy without unreasonable
744   memory requirements.

796
797   A number of optimizations were applied to reduce the memory and compute time of TDA. Instead
798   of providing a sequence of high-dimensional latent iterates directly to Ripser, we use a smaller
799   distance matrix containing pairwise distances between the iterates. This matrix is computed using an
800   optimization from Tralie & Perea (2018), where the iterates are first compressed with singular value
801   decomposition (SVD) to reduce memory costs. The SVD computation is performed in PyTorch
802
      to leverage GPU acceleration. Initially, we also used a diagonal convolution optimization, also from
      Tralie & Perea (2018), to avoid redundant computations when constructing the distance matrix for
803
      the sliding window embedding. However, this second optimization was not used in our final TDA
804
      experiments, as we dropped the sliding window embedding.
