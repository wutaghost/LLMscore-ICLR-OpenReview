009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               Kolmogorov-Arnold Networks (KANs) have seen great success in scientific do-
012
               mains thanks to spline activation functions, becoming an alternative to Multi-
013
               Layer Perceptrons (MLPs). However, spline functions may not respect symmetry
014            in tasks, which is crucial prior knowledge in machine learning. Previously, equiv-
015            ariant networks embed symmetry into their architectures, achieving better per-
016            formance in specific applications. Among these, Equivariant Multi-Layer Percep-
017            trons (EMLP) introduce arbitrary matrix group equivariance into MLPs, providing
018            a general framework for constructing equivariant networks layer by layer. In this
019            paper, we propose Equivariant Kolmogorov-Arnold Networks (EKAN), a method
020            for incorporating matrix group equivariance into KANs, aiming to broaden their
021            applicability to more fields. First, we construct gated spline basis functions, which
022
               form the EKAN layer together with equivariant linear weights. We then de-
               fine a lift layer to align the input space of EKAN with the feature space of the
023
               dataset, thereby building the entire EKAN architecture. Compared with baseline
024
               models, EKAN achieves higher accuracy with smaller datasets or fewer param-
025            eters on symmetry-related tasks, such as particle scattering and the three-body
026            problem, often reducing test MSE by several orders of magnitude. Even in non-
027            symbolic formula scenarios, such as top quark tagging with three jet constituents,
028            EKAN achieves comparable results with EMLP using only 26% of the parameters,
029            while KANs do not outperform MLPs as expected.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Kolmogorov-Arnold Networks (KANs) (Liu et al., 2024b;a) are a novel type of neural network
036   inspired by the Kolmogorov-Arnold representation theorem (Tikhomirov, 1991; Braun & Griebel,
037   2009), which offers an alternative to Multi-Layer Perceptrons (MLPs) (Haykin, 1998; Cybenko,
038   1989; Hornik et al., 1989). Unlike MLPs, which utilize fixed activation functions on nodes, KANs
039
      employ learnable activation functions on edges, replacing the linear weight parameters entirely with
      univariate functions parameterized as splines (De Boor & De Boor, 1978). On the other hand, each
040
      layer of KANs can be viewed as spline basis functions followed by a linear layer (Dhiman, 2024).
041
      This architecture allows KANs to achieve better accuracy in symbolic formula representation tasks
042   compared with MLPs, particularly in function fitting and scientific applications. Subsequent works
043   based on KANs have demonstrated superior performance in other areas, such as sequential data
044   (Vaca-Rubio et al., 2024; Genet & Inzirillo, 2024b;a; Xu et al., 2024), graph data (Bresson et al.,
045   2024; De Carlo et al., 2024; Kiamari et al., 2024; Zhang & Zhang, 2024), image data (Cheon,
046   2024b;a; Azam & Akhtar, 2024; Li et al., 2024a; Seydi, 2024; Bodner et al., 2024), and so on.
047
      However, KANs themselves perform poorly on non-symbolic formula representation tasks (Yu et al.,
048   2024). One reason for this is that splines struggle to respect data type and symmetry, both of which
049   play important roles in machine learning. Many recent works utilize symmetry in data to design
050   network architectures, achieving better efficiency and generalization on specific tasks. For example,
051   Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) (LeCun et al., 1989) and Group equivariant Convolutional
052   Neural Networks (GCNNs) (Cohen & Welling, 2016a) leverage translational and rotational symme-
053   tries in image data, while DeepSets (Zaheer et al., 2017) and equivariant graph networks (Maron
      et al., 2018) exploit the permutation symmetry in set and graph data.

      Equivariant Multi-Layer Perceptrons (EMLP) (Finzi et al., 2021) propose a general method that
055   allows MLPs to be equivariant with respect to arbitrary matrix groups for specific data types, thereby
056   unifying the aforementioned specialized network architectures. They construct the equivariance
057   constraint using discrete and infinitesimal generators of the matrix group, and then obtain equivariant
058   linear weights by solving this constraint. Next, they apply gated nonlinearities (Weiler et al., 2018a),
059   which use the additional output of the previous layer as gate scalars. Due to the generality of matrix
060   groups, EMLP outperforms non-equivariant baselines on several datasets with complex symmetries.
061   Inspired by EMLP, we propose Equivariant Kolmogorov-Arnold Networks (EKAN), which embed
062   matrix group equivariance into KANs. By specifying the data type and symmetry, EKAN can serve
063   as a general framework for applying KANs to various areas. In Section 2, we introduce the pre-
064   liminary knowledge of group theory. In Section 3, we summarize related works. In Section 4, we
065   construct a layer of EKAN. We add gate scalars to the input and output space of each layer, and de-
066   fine gated spline basis functions between the input and post-activation space. To ensure equivariance
067   when linearly combining gated basis functions, we construct the equivariant constraint and solve for
068   the equivariant linear weights similar to EMLP. In Section 5, we build the entire EKAN architecture.
069
      We insert a lift layer before the first layer and discard the gate scalars from the output of the final
      layer, so that the input and output space of EKAN can be consistent with the original dataset. In
070
      Section 6, we evaluate EKAN on tasks with known symmetries. We show that EKAN can achieve
071
      higher accuracy than baseline models with smaller datasets or fewer parameters. In Section 7, we
072   conclude this work. In Figure 1, we compare the architectures of MLPs, EMLP, KANs, and EKAN.

090           • We propose EKAN, an architecture that makes KANs equivariant to matrix groups. To our

      Equivariant networks. Equivariant networks have gained significant attention in recent years due
165
      to their ability to respect and leverage symmetries in data. GCNNs (Cohen & Welling, 2016a) embed
166   discrete group equivariance into traditional CNNs through group convolutions. Steerable CNNs
167   (Cohen & Welling, 2016b) introduce steerable filters, which provide a more flexible and efficient
168   way to achieve equivariance compared with GCNNs. Subsequently, SFCNNs (Weiler et al., 2018b)
169   and E(2)-equivariant steerable CNNs (Weiler & Cesa, 2019) extend GCNNs and steerable CNNs to
170   continuous group equivariance, while 3D Steerable CNNs (Weiler et al., 2018a) extend these models
171   to 3D volumetric data. On the other hand, some works use partial differential operators (PDOs) to
172   construct equivariant networks (Shen et al., 2020; 2021; 2022; He et al., 2022; Li et al., 2024b).
173   Based on these theoretical frameworks, equivariant networks are widely applied in various fields,
174
      such as mathematics (Zhao et al., 2022), physics (Wang et al., 2020), biochemistry (Bekkers et al.,
      2018; Winkels & Cohen, 2019; Graham et al., 2020), and others.

366   5     EKAN ARCHITECTURE
367
368   In this section, we construct the entire EKAN architecture. The main body of EKAN is composed
369   of stacked EKAN layers. The output space of the l-th layer serves as the input space of the (l + 1)-th
370   layer, which we refer to as the latent space Ul . For the dataset, we usually know its data type, or in
371   other words, how group elements act on it. To embed this prior knowledge into EKAN, we set the
372   input space of the first layer as the feature space of the dataset Ui and the output space of the final
373   layer as the label space of the dataset Uo .
374   However, the actual input/output features of the EKAN layer stack lie in Ugi /Ugo . Therefore, we
375   need to add extensions to align the network with the dataset. First, the gate scalars of the actual
376   output feature are directly dropped to obtain the final output label of EKAN, which resides in Uo .
377   Then, we add a lift layer before the first layer to preprocess the raw input feature of EKAN, which
      is essentially an equivariant linear layer between Ui and Ugi (see Section 4.3 for more details).

409   6.1   PARTICLE SCATTERING
410
411   In electron-muon scattering, we can observe the four-momenta of the incoming electron, incoming
412
      muon, outgoing electron, and outgoing muon, denoted as q µ , pµ , q̃ µ , p̃µ ∈ R4 , respectively. We aim
      to predict the matrix element, which is proportional to the cross-section (Finzi et al., 2021):
413
414                          |M|2 ∝ [pµ p̃ν − (pα p̃α − pα pα )g µν ][qµ q̃ν − (q α q̃α − q α qα )gµν ].                         (23)
415   According to Einstein’s summation convention, in a monomial, if an index appears once as a super-
416   script and once as a subscript, it indicates summation over that index. The metric tensor is given by
417   gµν = g µν = diag(1, −1, −1, −1), and aµ = gµν aν = (a0 , −a1 , −a2 , −a3 ). The matrix element
418   is invariant under Lorentz transformations. In other words, this task exhibits O(1, 3) invariance (see
419   Appendix A.2 for more details), with the feature space Ui = 4T1 and the label space Uo = T0 .
420   We embed the group O(1, 3) and its subgroups SO+ (1, 3) and SO(1, 3) equivariance into EKAN.
421   Models are evaluated on synthetic datasets with different training set sizes, which are generated
422   by sampling q µ , pµ , q̃ µ , p̃µ ∼ N (0, 412 ). Both EKAN and KAN have the depth of L = 2, the
423   spline order of k = 3, and grid intervals of G = 3. Although the lift layer increases the parameter
424   overhead, we set the width of the middle layer in EKAN to n1 = 1000 (shape as [16, 1000, 1],
425   and the software will automatically calculate the appropriate feature space structure based on the
426   user-specified dimension), and set the width of the middle layer in KAN to n1 = 3840 (shape as
427   [16, 3840, 1]) to keep the parameter count similar. Both EMLP and MLP have the depth of L = 4
428
      and the middle layer width of n1 = n2 = n3 = 384 (shape as [16, 384, 384, 384, 1]). In these

      7      C ONCLUSION
528
529
      To our knowledge, this work is the first attempt to combine equivariance and KANs. We view
      the KAN layer as a combination of spline functions and linear weights, and accordingly define the
530
      (gated) input space, post-activation space, and (gated) output space of the EKAN layer. Gated ba-
531
      sis functions ensure the equivariance between the gated input space and the post-activation space,
532   while equivariant linear weights guarantee the equivariance between the post-activation space and
533   the gated output space. The prior work has demonstrated that “EMLP > MLP” on tasks with sym-
534   metries and “KAN > MLP” on symbolic formula representation tasks. Our experimental results
535   further indicate that on symbolic formula representation tasks with symmetries, “EKAN > EMLP”
536   and “EKAN > KAN”. Moreover, on non-symbolic formula representation tasks with symmetries,
537   although it may be that “KAN < MLP”, we show that “EKAN > EMLP”. We expect that EKAN can
538   become a general framework for applying KANs to more fields, such as computer vision and natural
539   language processing, just as EMLP unifies classic works like CNNs and DeepSets.

808   number of epochs to 15000, which is sufficient for the MSE loss to converge to the minimum. We
809   perform this experiment on a single-core NVIDIA GeForce RTX 3090 GPU with available memory
