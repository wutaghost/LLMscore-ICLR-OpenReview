009                                                       A BSTRACT
010
011              Image generation is a prevailing technique for clinical data augmentation for ad-
012              vancing diagnostic accuracy and reducing healthcare disparities. Diffusion Model
013              (DM) has become a leading method in generating synthetic medical images, but
014              it suffers from a critical twofold bias: (1) The quality of images generated for
015              Caucasian individuals is significantly higher, as measured by the Fréchet Incep-
016              tion Distance (FID). (2) The ability of the downstream-task learner to learn critical
017              features from disease images varies across different skin tones. These biases pose
018
                 significant risks, particularly in skin disease detection, where underrepresentation
                 of certain skin tones can lead to misdiagnosis or neglect of specific conditions.
019
                 To address these challenges, we propose FairSkin, a novel DM framework
020
                 that mitigates these biases through a three-level resampling mechanism, ensuring
021              fairer representation across racial and disease categories. Our approach signif-
022              icantly improves the diversity and quality of generated images, contributing to
023              more equitable skin disease detection in clinical settings.

      2.1   I MAGE G ENERATION FOR M EDICAL D ISEASES
076
077   Image generation for medical diseases is a burgeoning field that leverages generative models, such
078   as Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) (Ma et al., 2021), Variational Autoencoders (VAEs)
079   (Volokitin et al., 2020), and Diffusion Models (DMs) (Akrout et al., 2023), to create synthetic med-
080   ical images. This technology facilitates addressing critical challenges in the medical domain (Kaze-
081   rouni et al., 2023), such as the scarcity of labeled data, privacy concerns, and the lack of experts.
082   Compared to GAN-based and VAE-based methods (Frid-Adar et al., 2018; Rais et al., 2024; Cetin
083   et al., 2023), DM-based strategies have been widely utilized for data augmentation in the medical
084   domain. For instance, Chen (2023) utilized DM for data augmentation for image classification of
085
      the Cell Cycle Phase, indicating a high potential in addressing issues related to insufficient data or
      unbalanced sample sizes. Recently, conditional diffusion models (Zhang et al., 2023) have gained
086
      significant attention in medical image generation for their flexibility and performance, achieving
087
      state-of-the-art results in tasks such as MRI (Dorjsembe et al., 2024), X-ray (Hung et al., 2023),
088   and skin disease generation (Akrout et al., 2023; Borghesi & Calegari, 2024), and demonstrating
089   comparable classification performance even with fully synthetic data. All the works have proved the
090   effectiveness of DM in medical image generation. However, all the above approaches only consider
091   generation quality, ignoring the bias existing in the generation process, which may limit the fairness
092   for specific tasks.
093

      3.2   T RAINING DATA R ESAMPLING
163
164   To address the imbalance in the dataset, we employ two resampling strategies: Class Balanced
165   Random Sampling (CBRS) and Square Root Random Sampling (SQRS). These methods aim to
166   ensure that the training data fed into the diffusion model maintains a balanced representation across
167   both race and disease classes.
168
169   Class Balanced Random Sampling. CBRS ensures that each class within the dataset is equally
170   represented during the training process. To mitigate class imbalance, we introduce a reweighting
171
      technique where each sample is assigned a weight inversely proportional to its class frequency.
      Formally, for each (r, d) pair, the weight wr,d is defined as: wr,d = N1r,d , where Nr,d is the number
172
173
      of samples belonging to race r and disease d. During training, these weights are incorporated into
      the loss function to ensure that minority classes contribute proportionally more to the overall loss,
174
      thereby encouraging the model to learn equally from all classes.
175
176   Importantly, we utilize all available samples without restricting the number of samples per class
177   based on the minimum class size. This approach allows the model to leverage the full diversity of
178   the dataset while addressing imbalance through reweighting.
179
180   Square Root Random Sampling. SQRS is designed to balance the representation of classes by
181
      assigning weights based on the square root of their class frequencies. Specifically, for each (r, d)
      pair, the weight wr,d is set to: wr,d = √ 1 , where Nr,d is the number of samples belonging to
182                                               Nr,d
183   race r and disease d. This weighting scheme reduces the dominance of majority classes more gently
184   compared to CBRS, allowing for a more nuanced balance between oversampling minority classes
185   and retaining information from majority classes.

189
      ances into the generative process. This subsection outlines the original diffusion model training and
190   promoting class diversity loss.
191
192   Original Diffusion Model Training. The original diffusion model training adheres to the standard
193   methodology, wherein the model learns to reverse a predefined noise diffusion process to generate
194   high-fidelity images. The training objective is to minimize the discrepancy between the generated
195   images and the real images in the dataset. Mathematically, the loss function commonly used is the
196   Variational Lower Bound (VLB), defined as:
197                                    LDM = Ex,ϵ,t ∥ϵ − ϵθ (xt , t)∥2 ,

198

199
      in the diffusion process, xt is the noised version of x at timestep t, and ϵθ is the neural network
200
      parameterized by θ that predicts the noise. The objective is to train ϵθ such that it accurately predicts
201   the noise ϵ added to x, thereby enabling the model to reconstruct the original image from the noised
202   version during the reverse diffusion process.
203
204   Promoting Class Diversity Loss. To counteract potential mode collapse and ensure that the dif-
205   fusion model generates a diverse set of images across all race and disease classes, we introduce a
206   Class-Balancing Diffusion Model (CBDM) (Qin et al., 2023). CBDM incorporates a class diversity
207   loss component Lr designed to enforce equitable representation of each (r, d) class during the image
208   generation process.
209   The regularization term Lr is formulated to promote class diversity by penalizing discrepancies in
210   noise predictions across different classes. Specifically, Lr penalizes the model if the noise estimated
211   under the true class label y significantly differs from the noise estimated under a randomly sampled
212   class label y ′ . This mechanism discourages the model from becoming biased towards majority
213   classes and encourages it to maintain consistency across all classes. The regularization term Lr is

      3.4   D OWNSTREAM BALANCING
224
225   After training the diffusion model, it is essential to ensure that the classification performance with
226   generated data augmentation remains balanced across all race and disease classes. The FairSkin
227   framework incorporates two downstream balancing techniques: Imbalance-aware Augmentation and
228   Dynamic Reweighting. These strategies enhance the classifier’s ability to perform equitably across
229   different race and disease classes.
230
231   Imbalance-aware Augmentation. Imbalance-aware Augmentation leverages the trained diffu-
232   sion model to generate synthetic samples for underrepresented (r, d) classes, thereby augmenting
233   the training dataset to achieve a more balanced distribution. This process involves generating a
234
      specified number of synthetic images conditioned on each minority class to compensate for their
      scarcity in the original dataset. Formally, for each minority (r, d) pair, the diffusion model gener-
235
      ates mr,d synthetic images x̂i as : x̂i = DM(zi ; r, d), ∀i ∈ {1, . . . , mr,d }, where zi represents the
236
      random noise input to the diffusion model. The generated synthetic samples are then combined with
237   the original dataset to form an augmented dataset Saug :
238
239                                         Saug = S ∪ {(x̂i , r, d)}M
                                                                     i=1 ,
240                 P
      where M = r,d mr,d . The number of synthetic samples mr,d for each (r, d) pair is determined
241
      based on the desired class distribution, typically aiming to balance the number of samples across all
242   classes or to achieve a predefined ratio that mitigates the original imbalance.
243
244   This augmentation strategy enriches the training data with diverse examples from minority classes,
245
      enhancing the classifier’s ability to generalize and perform uniformly across all classes. By intro-
      ducing synthetic variability, the model gains exposure to a broader range of features representative
246
      of each class, thereby improving its fairness in classification tasks.
247
248
      Dynamic Reweighting. Dynamic Reweighting dynamically adjusts the class weights during the
249   training of the classification model based on validation performance metrics, considering we have
250   train, validation, and test split. This technique ensures that classes with poorer performance receive
251   higher emphasis, promoting balanced learning across all classes. As training progresses, after each
252   epoch, the disease validation accuracy Ar for each racial class is computed. The weights are then
253   updated based on the inverse of the validation accuracy: wr = A1r , This update rule ensures that
254   classes with lower validation accuracy Ar receive higher weights, thereby increasing their influ-
255   ence on the loss function during subsequent training iterations. Such dynamic reweighting strategy
256   ensures that the classifier focuses more on classes that are underperforming, promoting balanced
257   performance across all race and disease classes.

263   In this work, we evaluate the proposed approach based on fitzpatrick17k datasets, which contain
264   16,577 clinical images with Fitzpatrick skin type labels. We selected the top 5 disease classes from
265   the Fitzpatrick17k dataset (Groh et al., 2021) and further divided them into 15 sub-classes based on
266
      race labels with at least 12 images and a maximum of 412 images per class as shown in Table 4 in
      Appendix A. We split each subcategory into training, validation, and test sets in an 8:1:1 ratio. We
267
      evaluate FairSkin using two sets of metrics: generation quality and fairness.
268
269   Fréchet Inception Distance (FID): We use FID (Heusel et al., 2017) to evaluate the similarity
      between generated images and real images in the feature space. By using a pre-trained Inception

      network to extract features from both real and generated images, a lower FID score indicates better
271   image generation quality.
272

274
      computed the variance, resulting in the FID Variance Score, which is used to indicate the fairness of
      the generated image quality.
275
276   Inception Score (IS score): IS is a commonly used metric for evaluating the quality of images
277   generated by models such as GANs. It assesses the model’s performance by measuring both the
278   quality and diversity of the generated images.
279   Demographic Parity (DP): DP is a widely used fairness metric in classification tasks that evalu-
280   ates whether the proportion of positive outcomes is the same across different demographic groups.
281                                        P
      Formally, DP is expressed as DP = z∈Z p(Ŷ = 1) − p(Ŷ = 1 | Z = z) , where p(Ŷ = 1) rep-

285
      of the absolute differences across all demographic groups Z reflects the level of demographic par-
      ity, with smaller values indicating greater fairness. This approach to ensuring fairness aligns with
286
      the framework described in (Agarwal et al., 2018), which proposes a reduction method to address
287
      fairness in classification tasks by reducing the problem to a sequence of cost-sensitive classification

      5   C ONCLUSION
487
488
      In this paper, we address the challenge of balancing fairness and performance in image generation
489   and downstream classifier tasks when dealing with long-tailed datasets through FairSkin. We
490   introduce a novel framework and propose strategies at three levels: Training Data Resampling, Bal-
491   anced DM Training, and Downstream Balancing. Our approach effectively enhances the overall
492   quality of generated images while reducing disparities in image generation quality across different
493   classes. Furthermore, it improves the fairness of downstream classifiers when using generated im-
494   ages for data augmentation. Our comprehensive experiments demonstrate significant performance
495   improvements across various tasks, highlighting the practicality and effectiveness of our methods in
496   real-world applications.
497
498   6   B ROADER I MPACT AND F UTURE W ORK
499
500   The FairSkin framework has the potential to significantly impact the development of AI-driven
501   medical diagnostic tools by addressing racial bias in medical image generation. By ensuring fair
502   representation and improved image quality across diverse skin tones, this work can help reduce
503   misdiagnoses and healthcare disparities, particularly in dermatology. The approach presented here
504   contributes to broader efforts in making AI technologies more inclusive and equitable, promoting
505
      better healthcare outcomes for underrepresented groups.
506   In terms of future work, we aim to extend FairSkin to other domains of medical imaging beyond
507   dermatology, such as radiology and ophthalmology, where racial and ethnic disparities in diagnostic
508   performance have also been observed. Additionally, we plan to refine the resampling techniques
509   and class diversity loss functions to further enhance fairness and representation. Expanding the
510   framework to include real-world clinical validation will also be a key focus to ensure its effectiveness
511
      in practical healthcare settings.

524   Our research focuses on generating fair and diverse skin disease images to address disparities in
525   medical diagnostics for underrepresented populations. We ensure that various skin tones, particu-
526   larly those underrepresented in existing datasets, are adequately reflected, thereby mitigating diag-
527   nostic bias. Our synthetic images are created without using personal data, ensuring compliance with

      privacy standards such as the Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act (HIPAA) of 1996
      and the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) (Regulation (EU) 2016/679). Furthermore, any

      any potential privacy concerns. The methodology, including model architecture and data handling,
531   is made transparent and reproducible. No real human subjects are involved, eliminating any risk of

533   responsible and ethical application in healthcare, ensuring that it is only applied in ways that en-
