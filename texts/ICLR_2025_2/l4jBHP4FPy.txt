                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
010
               Despite the remarkable success achieved by neural networks, particularly those
               represented by MLP and Transformer, we reveal that they exhibit potential flaws
011
               in the modeling and reasoning of periodicity, i.e., they exhibit satisfactory perfor-
012
               mance within the domain of training period, but struggle to generalize to out of
013            the domain (OOD). The inherent cause lies in the way that they tend to memo-
014            rize the periodic data rather than genuinely understand the underlying principles
015            of periodicity. In fact, periodicity is essential to various forms of reasoning and
016            generalization, underpinning predictability across natural and engineered systems
017            through recurring patterns in observations. In this paper, we propose FAN, a novel
018            network architecture based on Fourier Analysis, which empowers the ability to
019            efficiently model and reason about periodic phenomena, meanwhile maintaining
020            general-purpose ability. By introducing Fourier Series, periodicity is naturally
021
               integrated into the structure and computational processes of FAN. On this basis,
               FAN is defined following two core principles: 1) its periodicity modeling capabil-
022
               ity scales with network depth and 2) the periodicity modeling available throughout
023
               the network, thus achieving more effective expression and prediction of periodic
024            patterns. FAN can seamlessly replace MLP in various model architectures with
025            fewer parameters and FLOPs, becoming a promising substitute to traditional MLP.
026            Through extensive experiments, we demonstrate the superiority of FAN in peri-
027            odicity modeling tasks, and the effectiveness and generalizability of FAN across a
028            range of real-world tasks, including symbolic formula representation, time series
029            forecasting, language modeling, and image recognition.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   The flourishing of modern machine learning and artificial intelligence is inextricably linked to the
035   revolutionary advancements in the foundational architecture of neural networks. For instance, multi-
036   layer perceptron (MLP) (Rosenblatt, 1958; Haykin, 1998) plays a pivotal role in laying the ground-
037   work for current deep learning models, with its expressive power guaranteed by the universal ap-
038   proximation theorem (Hornik et al., 1989). Recent claims about the impressive performance of large
039
      models on various tasks are typically supported by Transformer architecture (Vaswani et al., 2017;
      Touvron et al., 2023; OpenAI, 2023). In this context, the community’s enthusiasm for research on
040
      neural networks has never diminished. Some emerged neural networks demonstrate notable capa-
041
      bilities in specific fields (Gu & Dao, 2023; Liu et al., 2024), sparking widespread discussion within
042   the community.
043
044   Beneath the surface of apparent prosperity, we uncover a critical issue that remains in existing neu-
045
      ral networks: they struggle to model the periodicity from data, especially in OOD scenarios. We
      showcase this issue through an empirical study as illustrated in Figure 1. The results indicate that
046
      existing neural networks, including MLP (Rosenblatt, 1958), KAN (Liu et al., 2024), and Trans-
047
      former (Vaswani et al., 2017), face difficulties in fitting periodic functions, even on a simple sine
048   function. Although they demonstrate proficiency in interpolation within the domain of training data,
049   they tend to falter when faced with extrapolation challenges of test data, especially in OOD scenar-
050   ios. Therefore, their generalization capacity is primarily dictated by the scale and diversity of the
051   training data, rather than by the learned principles of periodicity to perform reasoning. We argue
052   that periodicity is an essential characteristic in various forms of reasoning and generalization, as it
053   provides a basis for predictability in many natural and engineered systems by leveraging recurring
      patterns in observations. In fact, real-world tasks inherently contain many periodic and non-periodic

090   employed. We believe FAN is promising to be an important part of the fundamental model backbone.

305
      compare the models’ performance in learning the underlying principles of periodicity. Specifically,
306   we generate data from periodic functions over a large domain, using a portion of this domain as
307   training data and the entire domain as test data, i.e., a part of test data would be out of the domain

      4.3     T IME S ERIES F ORECASTING
379
380   Setup. Time series forecasting plays a critical role in various real-world applications. In our ex-
381   periments, we employ four public datasets of this task to assess the model performance on time
382   series forecasting, including Weather (Wu et al., 2021a), Exchange (Lai et al., 2018), Traffic (Wu
383   et al., 2021a), and ETTh (Zhou et al., 2021) datasets. For each dataset, we input 96 previous time
384   steps and forecast the subsequent time steps of {96, 192, 336, 720}. In this task, we choose the
385   sequence models as baselines, including LSTM, Mamba, Transformer, Transformer with FAN , and
386
      Transformer with FAN (Gated).
387
388   Results. As presented in Table 2, we compare the performance of Transformer with FAN and
389   other sequence models for time series forecasting tasks on four public datasets. In most cases,
390
      Transformer with FAN and its gated version achieves the best performance on these tasks, compared
      to LSTM, Mamba, and the standard Transformer. The improvements of Transformer with FAN and
391
      FAN (Gated) over the standard Transformer are notable, with the average relative improvements
392
      ranging from 14.3% to 15.0% for MSE and from 7.6% to 7.9% for MAE. These results suggest
393   that incorporating explicit periodic pattern encoding within neural networks improves time series
394   forecasting performance in real-world applications.

476   5     R ELATED W ORK
477
478   In this section, we outline the two most relevant directions and associated papers of this work.
479
480   Learning Periodicity with Neural Networks. Periodic functions are one of the most basic func-
481   tions of importance to human society and natural science (Newton, 1687; Osborn & Sensier, 2002;
482   Kwasnicki, 2008; De Groot & Franses, 2012; Zhang et al., 2017). However, commonly used neural
483   networks, such as MLPs and transformers, struggle with modeling periodicity. This limitation is
484   attributed to the lack of inherent “periodicity” in their inductive biases. Some previous works (Sil-
485   vescu, 1999; Liu, 2013; Parascandolo et al., 2016; Uteuliyeva et al., 2020) proposed merely using
      standard periodic functions themselves or their linear combinations as activation functions, which


                                                        9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      only work well on some shallow and simple models. On this basis, work (Liu et al., 2020) intro-
487   duced the Snake function, i.e., x + sin2 (x), as the activation function. However, we observed that it
488   can fit periodic functions to a certain extent, but its effect is limited especially for OOD scenarios,
489   as demonstrated in Appendix D. Therefore, although some previous studies have attempted to inte-
490   grate periodic information into neural networks, their actual performance and range of applications
491   remain heavily constrained.
492
493   Fourier-based Neural Network. Previous studies have explored Fourier-based neural networks
494   to enhance the computational tasks (Zuo & Cai, 2005; Tan, 2006; Zuo et al., 2008; Li et al., 2021b;
495   Chen et al., 2022). Fourier Neural Networks (Silvescu, 1999; Ngom & Marin, 2021) are shallow
496   feedforward networks that employ cosine activation functions to map inputs to their Fourier decom-
497   positions. Work (Lee et al., 2021) directly utilized the Fourier Series constructed by a shallow neural
498   network for generating periodic signals. In addition, work (Jiang et al., 2022) introduces Fourier Se-
499
      ries at the end of models to embed periodic components within the network. These approaches
      generally possess a similar principle as Eq. (3), using a neural network to simulate the formula of
500
      Fourier Series. However, this leads to the same problem as in Eq. (5), i.e., they are hard to serve as
501
      building blocks for deep neural networks, which limits these approaches’ capabilities.
502
503   In this paper, we design FAN to address these challenges, which performs exceptionally well on
504   periodicity modeling and a range of real-world tasks.
505
506   6   D ISCUSSION
507
508
      In this section, we mainly discuss the expressive power and application scope of FAN as follows.
509
510   First, FAN theoretically possesses the same expressive power as MLP as it also adheres to the uni-
511   versal approximation theorem, which ensures its capacity for functional approximation (refer to
512
      Appendix E for the detailed explanation). Moreover, FAN introduces an important enhancement
      by explicitly incorporating periodicity, a feature absent in traditional MLPs. Through this design,
513
      FAN not only retains the capabilities of MLP but also enhances its ability to capture periodic char-
514
      acteristics in data. For periodic tasks and some non-periodic tasks that are partially periodic, FAN
515   leverages its effective periodicity modeling ability to yield better results. Therefore, FAN can be
516   seen as a promising alternative to MLP.
517
518
      Second, beyond tasks that explicitly require periodicity modeling, FAN also has utility in a broader
      range of applications, which has been evidenced by our extensive experiments on real-world tasks,
519
      such as symbolic formula representation, time series forecasting, language modeling, and image
520
      recognition, where FAN achieve competitive or superior performance than MLP and other base-
521   lines. In fact, many machine learning tasks may harbor hidden forms of periodicity, even without
522   explicitly including periodicity, such as mathematical operations and logic reasoning. If the neural
523   network lacks the ability to model periodicity, it could impair its learning efficiency. From a deeper
524   perspective, periodicity is not just a data feature but reflects a form of structural knowledge—one
525   that allows for the transfer and reuse of abstract rules and principles across different contexts.
526
527
528
      7   C ONCLUSION
529
530   In this paper, we have proposed Fourier Analysis Network (FAN), a novel neural network archi-
531
      tecture for tackling the problem of periodicity modeling, which utilizes Fourier Series to facilitate
      capturing the underlying principles within data and reasoning. Experimental results demonstrate
532
      that FAN can successfully fit a variety of both basic and complex periodic functions, whereas other
533
      approaches failed. Moreover, FAN and its combination with Transformer also exhibit superior per-
534   formance in multiple real-world tasks, including symbolic formula representation, time series fore-
535   casting, language modeling, and image recognition tasks, outperforming existing neural networks
536   such as MLP, KAN, Transformer, CNN, LSTM, and Mamba. These promising results, especially
537   the stronger performance and the fewer parameters and FLOPs compared to MLP, suggest its po-
538   tential to become a key component of foundational models. In the future, we aim to further increase
539   the scale of FAN and expand its scope of application, reinforcing its role as a versatile and powerful
      building block in the machine learning landscape.

       E    H OW FAN C OMPLY WITH U NIVERSAL A PPROXIMATION T HEOREM
973
974
       The Universal Approximation Theorem states that a feed-forward network with a single hidden layer
975    containing a finite number of neurons can approximate continuous functions on compact subsets of
976    Rn , under mild assumptions on the activation function, which needs to be a non-constant, no-linear,
977    and continuous function. FAN Layer is defined as ϕ(x) = [cos(Wp x)|| sin(Wp x)||σ(Bp̄ + Wp̄ x)],
978    where || denotes concatenation and σ denotes the standard activation function, such as ReLU and
979    GELU. Since sin and cos functions also satisfy the required conditions of being non-constant, con-
980    tinuous, and non-linear activation functions, the FAN layer adheres to the Universal Approximation
981    Theorem.

983    F   M ORE D ETAILS OF BASELINES

985    In our experiments, we mainly compare FAN with the following baselines. 1) MLP (Rosenblatt,
986    1958): the most classic model, which is widely used in the backbone of various models. 2) Trans-
987    former (Vaswani et al., 2017): a prevalent model known for its self-attention mechanism, which
988    achieves outstanding performance on various tasks. 3) KAN (Liu et al., 2024): an emerged model
989    specialized for symbolic formula representation, which uses the b-spline functions instead of fixed
990    activation functions. 4) LSTM (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997): a well-known recurrent neu-
991    ral network (RNN) that can capture long-term dependencies on sequential data. 5) Mamba (Gu &
992
       Dao, 2023): an emerged selective state space model (SSM) that achieves competitive performance
       on some tasks with sequential inputs. 6) CNN (LeCun et al., 1998): convolutional neural network
993
       contains the convolutional layers, which are effective in processing image data.
