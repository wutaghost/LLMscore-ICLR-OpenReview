009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               Implicit Neural Representations (INRs) represent data as continuous functions us-
012
               ing the parameters of a neural network, where data information is encoded in
013
               the parameter space. Therefore, modeling the distribution of such parameters is
014            crucial for building generalizable INRs. Existing approaches learn a joint dis-
015            tribution of these parameters via a latent vector to generate new data, but such
016            a flat latent often fails to capture the inherent hierarchical structure of the pa-
017            rameter space, leading to entangled data semantics and limited control over the
018            generation process. Here, we propose a Controllable Hierarchical Implicit Neural
019            Representation (CHINR) framework, which explicitly models conditional depen-
020            dencies across layers in the parameter space. Our method consists of two stages:
021            In Stage-1, we construct a Layers-of-Experts (LoE) network, where each layer
022
               modulates distinct semantics through a unique latent vector, enabling disentangled
               and expressive representations. In Stage-2, we introduce a Hierarchical Control-
023
               lable Diffusion Model (HCDM) to capture conditional dependencies across lay-
024
               ers, allowing for controllable and hierarchical data generation at various semantic
025            granularities. Extensive experiments on CelebA-HQ, ShapeNet, SRN-Cars, and
026            AMASS datasets demonstrate that CHINR improves generalizability and offers
027            flexible hierarchical control over the generated content.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Implicit Neural Representations (INRs) are powerful tools to represent complex data as continu-
034   ous functions with neural network parameters (Tancik et al., 2020; Mildenhall et al., 2021; Dupont
035   et al., 2022a; You et al., 2024). This functional representation is agnostic to the underlying data
036   modality, making INRs a universal way to represent diverse data types, including audio, images,
037   and 3D volumes. Unlike conventional discrete data structures with explicit formats, INRs implicitly
038   encode data in the parameter space. This allows for more detailed, compact, and flexible repre-
039
      sentations. Recent advancements in the quality and efficiency of INRs greatly enhanced their per-
      formance across various applications, such as image fitting (Sitzmann et al., 2020; Dupont et al.,
040
      2021a), video compression (Chen et al., 2021a; 2023; Li et al., 2022), shape modeling (Zhao et al.,
041
      2022; Michalkiewicz et al., 2019), novel view synthesis (Mildenhall et al., 2021), and beyond.
042
043   The generalization ability of INRs, however, remains limited, since INRs are typically trained to
044   overfit individual data instances. Keeping in mind that all data information is embedded in INR’s
045
      parameters, the parameter space acts as a hidden manifold that captures the structures and variations
      of data. Building on this concept, a recent line of work (Perez et al., 2018; Chan et al., 2021; Dupont
046
      et al., 2022a; You et al., 2024) has focused on modeling the parameter distribution to generalize
047
      INRs. A common approach is to adopt a two-stage framework. First, a collection of INRs is fitted to
048   each data instance. Given a collection of data {(x, f )}, each INR fθ : X → F is learned by finding
049   weight θ to minimize the distance between fθ (x) and f ∈ F. x ∈ X denotes coordinates (e.g. pixel
050   locations) and f ∈ F denotes signals (e.g. RGB values). Second, the weight distribution p(θ) is
051   learned through generative models. Since the raw INR parameters are intractably high-dimensional,
052   a condensed latent vector h is often used to represent each INR via modulation (Dupont et al.,
053   2022a). This simplifies the task of learning p(θ) by transforming it into the surrogate task of learning
      p(h), which exists in a more manageable lower-dimensional space.

      Despite the various modeling approaches, these methods learn the joint distribution of all parame-
071
      ters of an INR, treating each INR’s parameters indiscriminately via a flattened latent. This approach
072
      overlooks the inherent hierarchical structure of INRs, particularly in multilayer perceptron (MLP)-
073   based architectures such as SIREN (Sitzmann et al., 2020), where each layer’s representation ca-
074   pacity depends conditionally on the previous layers. As a result, these methods fail to capture the
075   hierarchical patterns in the data. Taking the facial image as an example, hierarchical semantics refer
076   to progressively detailed facial characteristics, such as overall facial shape, expression, and shape of
077   eyes. Meanwhile, the parameters of INRs exhibit layer-wise expanded representation capacity (Yüce
078   et al., 2022), determined by its hierarchical structure. However, learning the joint distribution with
079   flattened parameters ignores the correspondences between the INRs’ expanded representation capac-
080   ity across layers and the hierarchical patterns in the data. This misalignment brings two challenges:
081
      (1) The generalizability of INRs to unseen data is impaired. The joint distribution learning encodes
      the entangled semantics together into one flat latent, where the co-occurrence of certain semantics
082
      is inevitable. Therefore, the diversity of the generated data is limited. (2) Control over the generated
083
      content is limited. While generative models, such as latent diffusion, are employed with INRs to
084   generate new data, they cannot link the sampled noise to the expected semantics in the output.
085
086   To address these challenges, we propose a novel approach that models the hierarchical structure
087
      of INRs, promoting layer-wise control in the generation process. Our method starts by training a
      collection of INRs on a dataset. Each layer of an INR is parameterized as a Mixture-of-Experts
088
      (MoE) layer to increase expressivity, where a set of expert weights and a latent vector are learned.
089
      The experts at each layer are shared across the dataset, while the latents are data-specific, routing
090   the data flow and modulating the contribution of experts. Layers of MoE are cascaded to form an
091   INR, which we call a Layers-of-Experts (LoE) network. Consequently, a LoE with L layers will
092   have L latents adapted to the fitted data, effectively capturing and relating its complex patterns with
093   layers of latents. By modeling the conditional dependencies of these layer-wise latents with our
094   proposed hierarchical controllable diffusion model (HCDM), we maintain the hierarchical structure
095   of INRs. This unlocks a controllable generation process, aligning the layers in INRs with the hier-
096   archies of data semantics for the first time. As illustrated in Fig. 1, the data flow for a generation
097   process resembles a tree-like structure: the routing in the next layer is constrained by the paths in
098
      previous layers, allowing full control of where a different routing strategy should be explored. Early
      deviations in routing lead to significant semantic differences in the generated content, while a later
099
      deviation results in minor differences in details.
100
101   The contributions of our paper are summarized as follows:
102
103   • We model the INR as a LoE framework, where each layer includes multiple experts to improve
104
        expressivity. Additionally, the layer-wise latent structure aligns the inherent hierarchy of data
        semantics with INR’s expanded representation capacity.
105
106   • We are the first to model the hierarchical and conditional dependencies within INR parameters
107     with the proposed HCDM, which enables hierarchical control over the generated data semantics
        at different levels of granularity.

113   2     BACKGROUND
114
115   In this section, we introduce INRs and their generalization ability, while highlighting their connec-
116   tions to our proposed approach. We also analyze the inherent hierarchy in INR’s parameters.
117
118   2.1   I MPLICIT N EURAL R EPRESENTATION AND G ENERATIVE INR S
119
120   Implicit Neural Representations (INRs) parameterize data such as audio, images, video, and 3D vox-
121
      els as mappings from coordinates to signals, enabling a unified framework for various data modal-
      ities (Genova et al., 2019a;b; Xie et al., 2022). Remarkable progress has been made to enhance
122
      the representation quality, efficiency and compactness for audio (Zuiderveld et al., 2021; Luo et al.,
123
      2022; Su et al., 2022; Lanzendörfer & Wattenhofer, 2023), images (Sitzmann et al., 2020; Fathony
124   et al., 2020; Chen et al., 2021b; Xu et al., 2022; Saragadam et al., 2023), 3D contents (Mildenhall
125   et al., 2021; Barron et al., 2021; Tiwari et al., 2022; Ortiz et al., 2022; Zhao et al., 2022; Ruan et al.,
126   2024), and videos (Chen et al., 2021a; Li et al., 2022; Yan et al., 2024). Despite performing well
127   on different modalities, INRs struggle to generalize to multiple and unseen data, as each instance is
128   typically overfitted with a separate MLP. To address this, two key strategies have emerged: (1) learn-
129   ing content-specific input features (Yu et al., 2021; Hu et al., 2023; Lazova et al., 2023)(Xu et al.,
130   2024) and (2) modulating or customizing network parameters with latents or hypernetworks (Mehta
131   et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2022; Dupont et al., 2022b; Kim et al., 2023)(Xu et al., 2024). Generative
132
      models (Goodfellow et al., 2014; Ho et al., 2020) further extend INR’s capability to generate new
      data. GRAF (Schwarz et al., 2020) and GIRAFFE (Niemeyer & Geiger, 2021) generate shape and
133
      appearance codes from noise, which are combined with coordinates to construct scenes. Erkoç et al.
134
      (2023) use a diffusion model to generate INR weights. Dupont et al. (2021b); Du et al. (2021);
135   Koyuncu et al. (2023) train hyper-networks to generate INR parameters. Dupont et al. (2022a);
136   Bauer et al. (2023); Park et al. (2024) employ a two-stage framework to learn the distribution of
137   latents that map to or modulate INRs, and generate new content by sampling in the latent space.
138   mNIF (You et al., 2024) further enhances the expressivity of INR via model averaging. These meth-
139   ods essentially model the distribution of INR parameters p(θ) by learning latent distributions p(h),
140   but fail to capture the layer-wise hierarchical structure of INR parameters, limiting their ability to
141   accurately model distributions and control generation, which will be discussed in Sec. 2.2. Build-
142   ing on the latent modulation approach, we introduce a hierarchical controllable diffusion model,
143
      capturing dependencies between layer-wise latents for improved generalization and control.

187   3           P ROPOSED M ETHOD
188
189   Our method uses a two-stage framework to align the hierarchy of data semantic and INR’s expanded
190   representation ability, as shown in Fig. 2. In the first stage, we train individual INRs to fit a target
191   dataset. In the second stage, we use generative models to learn the weight distribution for generating
192   new data. Directly modeling the INR weight distribution brings three challenges: (1) independently
193   trained INRs make it hard to extract shared information for distribution learning, (2) the high di-
194
      mensionality of raw weights makes distribution modeling highly challenging, and (3) it ignores the
      hierarchical structure of INRs. To address these issues, we configure the INR as a Layers-of-Experts
195
      (LoE) network, where each layer contains a set of shared expert weights and an instance-specific
196
      latent vector. As shown in the left of Fig. 2, the inference process builds each INR layer by layer,
197   combining the experts with the corresponding latent vector. This structure captures shared informa-
198   tion through the experts, simplifies distribution learning by focusing on the latents, and explicitly
199   models the conditional dependencies within the hierarchical structure of INRs. In the following
200   sections, we first define the LoE structure and the learning task, followed by detailed explanations
201   of the two stages.
202

226   3.2     S TAGE -1: L EARNING A DATASET OF L O E INR S
227
      Similar to Functa (Dupont et al., 2022a) and mNIF (You et al., 2024), we use meta-learning and
228
      auto-decoding to train both the data-specific latents h and the shared parameters θ for the LoE
229
      INR during Stage-1. For meta-learning, we adopt an interleaved training procedure inspired by
230   CAVIA (Zintgraf et al., 2019), where the experts and latents are updated alternately in separate
231   training loops. In the inner loop, we fix θ and adapt the latents h to data samples. Within each inner
232   loop, h is first randomly initialized around zero and then updated for a few steps. In the outer loop,
233   θ is optimized based on the updated h. This ensures each data-specific latent can be effectively
234   learned within a few iterations, encouraging faster convergence and adaptation to new data, which is
235   essential for distribution modeling and generalization in Stage-2. In the case of auto-decoding, we
236   jointly optimize all parameters, maintaining a latent bank for the dataset and updating the sampled
237   batch of latents in each iteration. Unlike meta-learning, auto-decoding does not require second-order
238
      derivatives, making it more computationally efficient. Due to this efficiency, we apply auto-decoding
      specifically for NeRF training.
239
240   In both approaches, each data-specific latent h consists of L components separately modulating
241   the L layers in the LoE INR. This setup facilitates conditional distribution modeling for Stage-
242   2, as opposed to learning their joint distribution like mNIF and Functa, enabling hierarchical and
243   controllable generation.

320
321   In this section, we begin by introducing the datasets and evaluation criteria. Then, we provide
322   quantitative and qualitative results to demonstrate the efficacy of our proposed framework. Next,
323   we highlight the controllability of the INR generation process, enabled by conditional dependency
      modeling and hierarchical sampling. Furthermore, we analyze the latent space and illustrate how

      data semantics are embedded within INR’s weight space hierarchically. We show that this structure
325   grants the INR a compositional property across layers. Finally, we conduct ablation studies to show

      the proposed HCDM successfully models the conditional dependencies of the modulation latents,
364

365
      different granularities in the generated contents. Specifically, to sample a latent h = [h1 , · · · , h5 ],

373   as in the second column and conditionally sample the rest. This progressive fixation allows us to

      control the finer details of the generated output.

      5     C ONCLUSION
526
527
      In this work, we proposed the Controllable Hierarchical Implicit Neural Representation (CHINR)
528   framework, addressing the limitations of existing generative INRs that learn joint parameter distri-
529   butions while ignoring the hierarchical structure of parameters and data semantics. By structuring
530   the INR as a Layers-of-Experts (LoE) network and leveraging a Hierarchical Controllable Diffusion
531   Model (HCDM), our approach captures conditional dependencies across layers, improving general-
532   izability and enabling controllable data generation.
533
      One limitation is scalability to larger datasets, as the shared network may struggle to capture com-
534   plex and diverse data patterns. A possible solution is to incorporate local information using patch-
535   wise modulation (Mehta et al., 2021; Bauer et al., 2023) or INRs with localized nonlinearity (e.g.,
536   WIRE (Saragadam et al., 2023)). Future directions include exploring sparse gatings, as in Mixture
537   of Experts (MoE) methods Wang et al. (2022), to promote expert diversity and specialization. Addi-
538   tionally, the learning of layer-wise semantic hierarchy in Stage-1 can be guided through predefined
539   attributes or deep clustering. This would allow the model to develop more interpretable and distinct
      semantics across layers, improving control over fine-grained details or desired characteristics.

      Implementation details. The LoE structure can be configured with the number of layers L,
759
      the number of experts at each layer K, the channel dimension of each expert C, and the di-
760
      mension of the latent at each layer H, denoted as a tuple (L, K, C, H). We train LoEs of
761   (7, 384, 128, 128), (5, 256, 64, 256), (6, 256, 64, 64), and (5, 64, 64, 64) in CelebA-HQ (Karras,
762   2017), ShapeNet (Chang et al., 2015), SRN-Cars (Sitzmann et al., 2019), and AMASS (Mahmood
763   et al., 2019) datasets, respectively. We follow mNIF (You et al., 2024) on the data processing pro-
764   tocols for CelebA-HQ, ShapeNet, and SRN-Cars datasets. Details about the AMASS dataset are
765   provided in Sec. B.3.
766
      Training details. In Stage-1, we train LoEs via meta-learning on CelebA-HQ, ShapeNet, and
767   AMASS, and with auto-decoding on SRN-Cars. We use a batch size of 32, an outer learning rate of
768   1e−4, an inner learning rate of 1 with 3 steps, and train the LoE for 800 epochs in the meta-learning
769   setting. For auto-decoding experiments on SRN-Cars, we use a batch size of 8, a learning rate of
770   1e−4, and train the LoE for 1000 epochs. In both settings, we use the AdamW (Loshchilov, 2017)
771   optimizer without weight decay. In Stage-2, we set the training batch size to be 32, learning rate
772   1e−4, and cosine scheduler with minimum learning rate 0.0. We train the HCDM for 1000 epochs
773   with the AdamW optimizer.

      B.5   L ATENT- BASED R ETRIEVAL
903   We show an application of data retrieval by latents, since they already embed rich semantic mean-
904   ings. We first obtain the latents for the target data by fitting it to the LoE through a few gradient
905   steps. Once the latents are optimized, they can be used to retrieve similar data by comparing their
906   latent representations to the searched set, allowing us to search for semantically similar examples
907   within the latent space. Fig. 14 shows this process by using images from the test-split of CelebA-
908   HQ as the targets, and train-split images as the searched set. We demonstrate two approaches for
909   retrieval: (1) using the flattened h for all layers, and (2) layer-wise retrieval using each layer’s latent
910   hl . As shown in Fig. 14, retrieval by the flattened h will retrieve samples that are broadly similar,
911
      while layer-wise retrieval retrieves samples with specific semantic similarities. For example, h2 re-
      trieves faces with similar orientations, while h3 retrieves faces with similar facial features such as
912
      eye shape and expressions.
