                                                  A BSTRACT
013
014
015            Complex nonlinear systems permeate various scientific and engineering domains,
016            presenting significant challenges in accurate modeling and analysis. This paper in-
017            troduces the Koopman Universal Neural Dynamic Operator (KUNDO), a ground-
018            breaking framework that bridges the gap between data-driven machine learning
019
               approaches and traditional mathematical modeling. KUNDO uniquely combines
               neural networks, Koopman operator theory, and the universal approximation the-
020
               orem to achieve fully explicit expression identification for complex nonlinear sys-
021
               tems. Our framework demonstrates remarkable efficiency in small sample sce-
022            narios, overcoming limitations of both classical physical models and black-box
023            machine learning techniques. By learning Koopman-compatible basis functions
024            through neural networks, KUNDO transforms strongly nonlinear dynamics into
025            interpretable mathematical forms, greatly decreasing the limitations of human se-
026            lection of basis functions without sacrificing predictive power. We present theoret-
027            ical analyses of KUNDO’s mathematical properties and validate its performance
028            across diverse nonlinear systems. The results showcase KUNDO’s potential to
029            revolutionize system identification, offering new avenues for scientific discovery
030
               and engineering applications in fields such as climate science, financial modeling,
               and advanced robotics. This work presents a significant advance towards inter-
031
               pretable AI and data-driven modeling in systems analysis.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
055
056
057
058
059   In contemporary science and engineering, accurately capturing the dynamic behavior of complex
060   nonlinear systems while providing explicit mathematical expressions for them is an extremely chal-
061   lenging problem. Complex nonlinear systems are ubiquitous in various aspects of our lives, from
062
      climate change models to dynamic fluctuations in financial markets, from intricate interactions in
      biological systems to the control of advanced robotics. The behavior of these systems shapes our
063
      world, making the ability to understand and model their complex dynamics crucial for advancing
064
      fundamental science and solving practical engineering problems.
065
066   Traditional modeling methods often face numerous limitations when dealing with these complex
067   nonlinear systems. Classical physical models and dynamical systems theory, while providing rigor-
068
      ous mathematical frameworks, struggle with strongly nonlinear systems. These methods typically
      rely on idealized assumptions, making it difficult to address the dynamic characteristics of complex
069
      real-world systems and thus limiting their applicability. Meanwhile, machine learning, especially
070
      deep learning techniques, has made significant progress in modeling complex phenomena in re-
071   cent years. Through large-scale data, deep learning can capture the intricate dynamics of systems,
072   demonstrating powerful predictive capabilities. However, these models are often viewed as ”black
073   boxes,” lacking explicit mathematical explanations and interpretability. This ”black box” nature not
074   only diminishes the application value of the models in scientific and engineering fields but also hin-
075   ders our in-depth understanding of the internal mechanisms of systems, impeding comprehensive
076   analysis of system behavior.
077   The main challenge facing the field of modern system identification is: how can we design a method
078   that captures the dynamic behavior of complex nonlinear systems while providing models with fully
079   explicit mathematical expressions? Such a method must not only possess high predictive power but
080   also achieve breakthroughs in interpretability and analyzability, especially for systems with higher
081   dimensions and stronger nonlinearities.
082
      This work proposes the Koopman Universal Neural Dynamic Operator (KUNDO) framework, aim-
083   ing to address this challenge and provide a revolutionary modeling method for nonlinear systems.
084   The core innovation of KUNDO lies in its ability to achieve fully explicit expression identification
085   for complex nonlinear systems while demonstrating efficient performance in small sample scenar-
086   ios. This breakthrough is achieved through the fusion of the powerful expressive capability of neural
087   networks, the rigorous mathematical foundation of Koopman operator theory, and the universal ap-
088   proximation theorem.
089   The working principle of KUNDO is to use neural networks to learn a set of special basis functions
090   that are compatible with Koopman operator theory. Leveraging the universal approximation theo-
091   rem, these neural networks can approximate any continuous nonlinear function, enabling KUNDO
092   to transform complex nonlinear dynamics into fully explicit mathematical expressions. This trans-
093   formation preserves the essential characteristics of the system and provides unprecedented inter-
094   pretability and analytical capabilities, especially for systems with higher dimensions and stronger
095   nonlinearities. Moreover, KUNDO offers a new approach to understanding complex systems, capa-
096   ble of efficient learning and generalization even in scenarios with limited data.
097   The main contributions of this study include:
098
      1. Innovative Neural Network Architecture Design: We propose a novel neural network archi-
099
      tecture specifically designed to learn Koopman-compatible basis functions. This architecture can
100
      capture complex nonlinear dynamics while ensuring the learned expressions are interpretable.
101
102   2. Integration with Koopman Operator Theory: We develop a method that combines the learned
103   basis functions with Koopman operator techniques to construct fully explicit system models. These
104
      models not only possess predictive capabilities but also provide mathematical analyzability for the
      system.
105
106   3. Mathematical Property Analysis of the KUNDO Framework: We conduct elaborate theoreti-
107   cal analyses of the mathematical properties of KUNDO, proving its effectiveness and robustness in
      handling complex nonlinear systems.


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      4. Validation on Diverse Nonlinear Systems: We validate the effectiveness of KUNDO on a series
109   of challenging complex nonlinear systems, demonstrating its superiority in identifying fully explicit
110   expressions and its efficient performance in small sample scenarios.
111
112
      By integrating deep learning, dynamical systems theory, and the universal approximation theorem,
      KUNDO represents a new paradigm in machine learning. It not only enhances our understanding
113
      and predictive capabilities of complex nonlinear systems but also paves the way for interpretable AI
114
      and data-driven scientific discovery. We believe that this approach of combining machine learning
115   with traditional scientific theories will play a crucial role in future AI systems, driving innovation in
116   scientific discovery and engineering applications.

120   2   R ELATED W ORK
121
122
123
      This section reviews relevant research in system identification and modeling for complex nonlin-
      ear systems, covering traditional methods, Koopman operator theory applications, neural net-
124
      works, and hybrid approaches.
125
126   Traditional methods rely on physics-based mathematical models and classical dynamical systems
127   theory (Ljung & Söderström, 1983; Ljung, 1998; Söderström & Stoica, 2002), including linear
128   regression (Draper, 1998), autoregressive models (Box et al., 2015), and state-space representa-
129
      tions (Durbin & Koopman, 2012). While effective for simpler systems, they struggle with high-
      dimensional, strongly nonlinear complex systems (Nelles & Nelles, 2020; Billings, 2013), often
130
      assuming linear or weakly nonlinear structures (Aguirre & Billings, 1995; Juang, 1994).
131
132   Koopman operator theory transforms nonlinear systems into linear operators in a high-
133   dimensional space of observables (Koopman, 1931; Mezić, 2005). Dynamic Mode Decomposition
134   (DMD) applies this theory to data-driven system identification (Schmid, 2010; Kutz et al., 2016).
135
      Extended methods like EDMD introduce nonlinear basis functions (Williams et al., 2015), while
      recent advancements include Hankel-DMD (Arbabi & Mezic, 2017) and time-delay embeddings
136
      (Brunton et al., 2017).
137
138   Neural networks have demonstrated remarkable capabilities in modeling nonlinear systems (LeCun
139   et al., 2015), from RNNs (Elman, 1990) to LSTM (Hochreiter, 1997) and GRU (Cho, 2014). Recent
140   innovations include Neural ODEs (Chen et al., 2018) and Graph Neural Networks (Scarselli et al.,
141
      2008; Battaglia et al., 2016; Gilmer et al., 2017). However, their ”black-box” nature often limits
      interpretability (Schmidt & Lipson, 2009).
142
143   Hybrid methods combining Koopman theory and neural networks aim to leverage both approaches’
144   strengths. Examples include DeepKoopman (Lusch et al., 2018), Koopman Autoencoders (Otto &
145   Rowley, 2019), and EDMD with dictionary learning (Li et al., 2017). Despite improved modeling
146   capabilities, these approaches face challenges in interpretability (Brunton et al., 2016a), stability for
147
      chaotic systems (Takeishi et al., 2017), and performance with limited data (Kaiser et al., 2021).
148   Recent research focuses on enhancing model transparency and interpretability in dynamical sys-
149   tem modeling (Rudin, 2019). Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs) incorporate physical
150   constraints into neural network training (Raissi et al., 2019), while other approaches explore inter-
151   pretable structures or regularization terms (Adadi & Berrada, 2018). However, a systematic frame-
152   work deeply integrating traditional physical theories with machine learning methods is still lacking.

      3   F RAMEWORK : KOOPMAN U NIVERSAL N EURAL DYNAMIC O PERATOR
156
157
158   This section provides a detailed description of the methodology of the Neural Network KUNDO
159   (Koopman Universal Neural Dynamic Operator). KUNDO integrates Koopman operator theory, the
160   universal approximation theorem, and deep learning techniques to achieve efficient modeling and
161   prediction of complex nonlinear dynamical systems through two-layer Koopman-like mappings and
      system identification.

      3.1   M ETHODOLOGICAL F RAMEWORK
163
164   The KUNDO method begins by concatenating the original state vector x ∈ Rn and control input
165   u ∈ Rm to form the input vector xu = [x, u] ∈ Rn+m .
166
      A neural network Φ : Rn+m → Rd is designed such that each neuron in the output layer corresponds
167
      to a distinct nonlinear basis function, explicitly outputting a single basis function value:
168
169
                               Φ(x, u) = [ϕ1 (x, u), ϕ2 (x, u), . . . , ϕd (x, u)]T ,               (1)
170   where d is the number of basis functions. This network can encompass various architectures, such
171   as feedforward neural networks, convolutional neural networks (CNN), recurrent neural networks
172
      (RNN), graph neural networks (GNN), or combinations thereof.
173   Using these learned basis functions, a system dynamics model is constructed through a linear map-
174   ping akin to the Extended Dynamic Mode Decomposition (EDMD) framework:

217
218   Based on the identified explicit system model, we construct a continuous-time dynamic equation:

      Using this model, we can predict the future state of the system. Given an initial state x0 and a
224                                    −1

225   methods, such as the Euler method:

228   Through recursive calculations, we obtain state predictions for the system at future time points. This
229   method combines the advantages of data-driven modeling and analytical expression, achieving an
230   in-depth understanding and effective prediction of nonlinear systems. It represents an important
231   development in modern system identification and prediction.

233   3.4   T HEORETICAL A NALYSIS
234
235   The KUNDO method introduces a dynamic embedding space defined by the neural network
236
      Φ(·), achieving adaptive learning of state representation. This process can be viewed as a finite-
      dimensional approximation of the generalized Koopman operator:
237
238                                         Φ(f (x, u)) ≈ ΓΦ(x, u),                                      (9)
239
      where Γ captures the evolution of the system in the embedding space. This approach not only
240
      extends traditional Koopman theory but also aligns with the EDMD framework, providing a new
241   perspective for spectral analysis of nonlinear dynamical systems. Through the learned embed-
242   ding and dynamic parameters, we can analyze characteristic structures of the system, such as
243   invariant subspaces and periodic orbits, offering new tools for the qualitative analysis of complex
244   systems.
245
      Although neural networks may map states to high-dimensional spaces, the entire KUNDO frame-
246
      work acts as a form of implicit regularization. By learning an effective embedding, the method
247   automatically identifies and retains the most relevant dynamic features, achieving data-driven di-
248   mension reduction. This ensures powerful modeling capabilities for complex nonlinear systems
249   while controlling model complexity through the parameter matrix Γ, effectively preventing overfit-
250   ting.
251
      Overall, the KUNDO method represents a modern approach that combines theoretical guidance
252
      with data-driven techniques, opening new possibilities for the analysis and prediction of complex
253   dynamical systems. It provides powerful tools to understand and interpret the intrinsic dynamic
254   structures of these systems. More detailed analysis and mathematical properties can be found in
255   Appendix A.1.

      4.2     S ELECTION OF BASELINE M ETHODS AND R ATIONALE
330
331   To ensure fairness and comprehensiveness of comparison, we selected multiple representative base-
332   line methods in the field of system identification. These methods include SINDy(Brunton et al.,
333   2016b), Latent Neural ODE (Chen et al., 2018), LSTM(Hochreiter, 1997), NTM(Graves, 2014),
334   GPR(Williams & Rasmussen, 2006). These methods cover a range from sparse models to deep
335   learning, from linearization methods to memory-based models, as well as the ability to handle noise
336   and uncertainty.
337   SINDy identifies explicit dynamic equations of the system through sparse regression, suitable for
338   systems with clear physical mechanisms and offering good interpretability. Latent Neural ODE
339   (NODE) as an implicit model can effectively capture complex nonlinear relationships but lacks
340   interpretability. LSTM has strong expressive power, suitable for processing time series data, but
341   similarly lacks physical interpretation of the model. NTM combines the capabilities of neural net-
342   works and Turing machines, possessing memory and complex computational characteristics, suit-
343
      able for handling high-complexity dynamic systems. Gaussian Process Regression (GPR), based
      on Bayesian theory, can provide uncertainty estimates for predictions, suitable for small sample and
344
      noisy data environments, with advantages in uncertainty assessment.

349
350   For each system, we train KUNDO and each baseline method separately. KUNDO’s neural ba-
351   sis function component is implemented using the Neural ODE framework, where the initial step
352   involves mapping the input vector to a latent space with dimensions equal to the number of basis
353   functions.
354   Other baseline methods also adopt their respective suitable training steps. The SINDy method in-
355   cludes two main steps: feature library construction and sparse regression; Latent Neural ODE in-
356   volves latent space neural network design and integration of ODE solvers; LSTM and NTM include
357   network structure design and optimization; GPR includes kernel function selection and Bayesian
358   model training.
359
      To ensure fairness in comparison, we strive to maintain consistency in the model complexity (such
360   as number of network layers and parameters) across methods, avoiding performance bias due to
361   differences in model capacity. For KUNDO, the default settings are a two-layer neural network with
362   256 neurons per layer, learning rate of 1 × 10−3 , 1000 epochs, and 11 basis functions.

522
      KUNDO uniquely combines Koopman operator theory with neural networks, achieving fully ex-
523   plicit expression identification for complex nonlinear systems. It outperforms mainstream methods
524   in accuracy, extrapolation, noise robustness, and small sample learning. By learning Koopman-
525   compatible basis functions, KUNDO transforms nonlinear dynamics into interpretable forms, re-
526   ducing reliance on human expertise without sacrificing predictive power. The method demonstrates
527   stable performance across various hyperparameter settings, showcasing its robustness and tunability.
528   However, KUNDO’s integration of EDMD and other optimization techniques increases computa-
529   tional complexity, resulting in longer training times compared to simpler models. Current imple-
530   mentation may not fully utilize GPU parallelization, potentially limiting scalability for large-scale
531
      or real-time applications.
532   Future work will focus on optimizing computational efficiency, particularly GPU utilization, to en-
533   hance KUNDO’s applicability in real-time and large-scale systems. Exploring its potential in higher-
534   dimensional systems and more complex dynamical tasks remains crucial. These developments will
535   further establish KUNDO’s role in advancing interpretable AI and data-driven modeling across sci-
536   entific and engineering domains.

769

771   dimensional approximation of the generalized Koopman operator.

773   Nonlinear Dynamical Systems and Koopman Operator Consider a discrete-time nonlinear dy-
774   namical system described in state space as:
775
776                                             xt+1 = f (xt , ut ),                                  (13)
777   where xt ∈ Rn is the system state at time t, ut ∈ Rm is the control input, and f : Rn+m → Rn is a
778   nonlinear mapping.

      erator K is defined as
781
782                           Kϕ(xt , ut ) = ϕ(xt+1 , ut+1 ) = ϕ(f (xt , ut ), ut+1 ).                (14)

787   the space of observation functions, i.e., for any observation functions ϕ1 , ϕ2 and scalars α, β ∈ C,
788   we have
789                                   K(αϕ1 + βϕ2 ) = αKϕ1 + βKϕ2 .                                    (15)

791   Finite-Dimensional Approximation of Generalized Koopman Operator Traditional Koopman
792   operators act on infinite-dimensional observation function spaces. To utilize Koopman theory in
793   practical applications, we need to perform finite-dimensional approximations. The KUNDO method
794

      nonlinear dynamical systems to be approximated as linear mappings in this space.

797   defined by a neural network. The finite-dimensional approximation of the generalized Koopman
