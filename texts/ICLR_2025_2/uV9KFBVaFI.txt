013                                               A BSTRACT
014
015            Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) have significantly advanced visual
016            tasks by integrating visual representations into large language models (LLMs).
017            The textual modality, inherited from LLMs, equips MLLMs with abilities like
018            instruction following and in-context learning. In contrast, the visual modality
019            enhances performance in downstream tasks by leveraging rich semantic content,
020
               spatial information, and grounding capabilities. These intrinsic modalities work
               synergistically across various visual tasks. Our research initially reveals a persis-
021
               tent imbalance between these modalities, with text often dominating output gener-
022
               ation during visual instruction tuning. This imbalance occurs when using both full
023            fine-tuning and parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) methods. We then found
024            that re-balancing these modalities can significantly reduce the number of trainable
025            parameters required, inspiring a direction for further optimizing visual instruc-
026            tion tuning. Hence, in this paper, we introduce Modality Linear Representation-
027            Steering (MoReS) to achieve the goal. MoReS effectively re-balances the intrinsic
028            modalities throughout the model, where the key idea is to steer visual represen-
029            tations through linear transformations in the visual subspace across each model
030            layer. To validate our solution, we composed LLaVA Steering, a suite of mod-
031
               els integrated with the proposed MoReS method. Evaluation results show that
               the composed LLaVA Steering models require, on average, 500 times fewer train-
032
               able parameters than LoRA needs while still achieving comparable performance
033
               across three visual benchmarks and eight visual question-answering tasks. Last,
034            we present the LLaVA Steering Factory, an in-house developed platform that en-
035            ables researchers to quickly customize various MLLMs with component-based
036            architecture for seamlessly integrating state-of-the-art models, and evaluate their
037            intrinsic modality imbalance. This open-source project enriches the research com-
038            munity to gain a deeper understanding of MLLMs.

043
044   Recent advancements in Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) (Liu et al., 2024b; Xue
045   et al., 2024; Zhou et al., 2024a; Chen et al., 2023) have demonstrated impressive capabilities across
046   a variety of visual downstream tasks. These models integrate visual representations from pretrained
047   vision encoders via various connectors (Liu et al., 2024a; Li et al., 2023a; Alayrac et al., 2022)
048   into LLMs, leveraging the latter’s sophisticated reasoning abilities (Zhang et al., 2024; Abdin et al.,
049   2024; Zheng et al., 2023a).
050   To better integrate visual representations into LLMs, the most popular MLLMs adopt a two-stage
051   training paradigm: pretraining followed by visual instruction tuning. In the pretraining stage, a
052   connector is employed to project visual representations into the textual representation space. We
053   define these two modalities—text and vision—as intrinsic to MLLMs, each carrying rich semantic
      information that serves as the foundation for further visual instruction tuning on downstream tasks

079   In the visual instruction tuning stage, due to its high computational cost, researchers have pur-

086   large-scale MLLMs remain prohibitively expensive to fine-tuning.
087   This raises a critical question: is there any further possibility to reduce more trainable parameters
088   so that the visual instruction tuning can be further improved? Our research offers a novel viewpoint
089   by focusing on the intrinsic modality imbalance within MLLMs. A closer analysis uncovers an
090   imbalance in output attention computation (Chen et al., 2024a), where textual information tends
091   to dominate the attention distribution during output generation. Specifically, we investigate this
092   issue by analyzing attention score distributions, which evaluates the balance between text and visual
093
      modalities. As shown in Figure 1, visual representations are significantly underutilized during visual
      instruction tuning. More importantly, our analysis reveals that achieving a better balance between
094
      these modalities can substantially reduce the number of trainable parameters required for fine-tuning.
095
      Hereby we suppose that intrinsic modality rebalance is the Midas touch to unlock further reductions
096   in the number of trainable parameters.
097
098   To address this challenge, we introduce Modality Linear Representation-Steering (MoReS) to op-
099
      timize visual instruction tuning, significantly reducing the number of trainable parameters while
      maintaining equivalent performance. Unlike full fine-tuning, which modifies the entire model, or
100
      other popular PEFT methods such as LoRA (Hu et al., 2021), OFT (Qiu et al., 2023), Adapter
101
      (Houlsby et al., 2019), and IA3 (Liu et al., 2022), MoReS focuses solely on steering the visual
102   representations. Specifically, our approach freezes the entire LLM during visual instruction tuning
103   to preserve its capabilities in the textual modality. Instead of fine-tuning the full model, we intro-
104   duce a simple linear transformation to steer visual representations in each layer. This transformation
105   operates within a subspace after downsampling, where visual representations encode rich semantic
106   information in a compressed linear subspace (Zhu et al., 2024; Shimomoto et al., 2022; Yao et al.,
107   2015). By continuously steering visual representations across layers, MoReS effectively controls
      the output generation process, yielding greater attention inclined to visual modality.

      To validate the efficacy of our proposed MoReS method, we integrated it into MLLMs of varying

113   training setup.
114
      In our experiments, we observed the need for a comprehensive framework to systematically analyze
115   and compare various model architectures and training strategies in MLLMs. The wide range of de-
116   sign choices and techniques makes it difficult to standardize and understand the interplay between
117   these components. Evaluating each method across different open-source models is time-consuming
118   and lacks consistency due to implementation differences, requiring extensive data preprocessing and
119   careful alignment between architectures and training recipes. To address this issue, we developed the
120   LLaVA Steering Factory, a flexible framework that reimplements mainstream vision encoders, multi-
121   scale LLMs, and diverse connectors, while offering customizable training configurations across a
122   variety of downstream tasks. This framework simplifies pretraining and visual instruction tuning,
123
      minimizing the coding effort. Additionally, we have integrated our attention score distribution analy-
      sis into the LLaVA Steering Factory, providing a valuable tool to the research community for further
124
      studying intrinsic modality imbalance in MLLMs.
125

127

129            that addresses intrinsic modality imbalance in MLLMs by steering visual representations

               through linear transformations within the visual subspace, effectively mitigating the issue

141            attention score analysis, facilitating systematic comparisons and providing deeper insights

      Integrating Visual Representation into LLMs: To leverage pre-trained large language models
      (LLMs) for understanding visual instructions and generating responses, researchers have introduced
148
      cross-attention mechanisms to integrate image information into the language model. Notable exam-
149
      ples include models such as LLaMA 3-V (Dubey et al., 2024), IDEFICS (Laurençon et al., 2023),
150   and Flamingo (Awadalla et al., 2023; Alayrac et al., 2022). These models typically follow a two-
151   stage training process: pretraining on large-scale image-text datasets, followed by supervised fine-
152   tuning (SFT) with carefully curated high-quality data. During this process, the self-attention layers
153   in the LLM decoder are kept frozen, with only the cross-attention and perceiver layers updated,
154   ensuring that the text-only performance remains intact.
155
      Another prominent approach employs a decoder-only architecture, as seen in models like the LLaVA
156   family (Liu et al., 2024b;a; 2023), BLIP (Xue et al., 2024; Li et al., 2023a), and Qwen-VL (team,
157   2024; Bai et al., 2023). These models also follow the pretraining and visual instruction tuning
158   paradigm. In the pretraining stage, a randomly initialized connector is trained while keeping the
159   LLM frozen. However, recent studies (Bai et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023) have demonstrated sce-
160   narios where both the projector and vision encoder are jointly trained during pretraining. Given the
161   limited capacity of adapter modules, it is common to unfreeze the LLM during visual instruction
      tuning, while keeping the vision encoder frozen.


                                                        3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      NVLM (Dai et al., 2024) represents a hybrid approach, combining elements of both the cross-
163   attention and decoder-only architectures. In contrast, vision-encoder-free methods, as explored by
164   models like Fuyu (Bavishi et al., 2023), SOLO (Chen et al., 2024b), and EVE (Diao et al., 2024), di-
165   rectly integrate visual information into LLMs at the pixel level, foregoing traditional vision encoders
166   altogether.
167
      While these approaches have advanced the integration of visual representations into LLMs, they still
168
      face significant challenges in the computational demands of visual instruction tuning, motivating
169   further exploration into more efficient methods.
170
171   Visual Instruction Tuning: Fine tuning of multimodal large language models (MLLMs) for down-
172
      stream tasks has gained considerable attention, but remains computationally expensive due to large-
      scale visual instruction datasets and model sizes (Wang et al., 2022). To tackle this challenge, re-
173
      cent advancements have introduced parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) methods (Houlsby et al.,
174
      2019; Li & Liang, 2021), such as LoRA (Hu et al., 2021), enabling more efficient visual instruction
175   tuning.
176
177   However, many of these PEFT methods primarily focus on optimizing weights but ignore the intrin-
178
      sic representation imbalance during visual instruction tuning, thus cannot further reduce the required
      trainable parameters. This means to look for other novel approaches that can improve the efficiency
179
      and effectiveness of visual instruction tuning.
180
181   Representation Steering: Recent studies (Singh et al., 2024; Avitan et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024;
182   Subramani et al., 2022) have demonstrated that the representations induced by pre-trained language
183   models (LMs) encode rich semantic structures. Steering operations within this representation space
184
      have shown to be effective in controlling model behavior. Unlike neuron-based or circuit-based
      approaches, representation steering manipulates the representations themselves, providing a clearer
185
      mechanism for understanding and controlling the behavior of MLLMs and LLMs. For example,
186
      (Zou et al., 2023) explores representation engineering to modify neural network behavior, shifting
187   the focus from neuron-level adjustments to transformations within the representation space. Simi-
188   larly, (Wu et al., 2024a) applies scaling and biasing operations to alter intermediate representations.
189   Furthermore, (Wu et al., 2024b) introduces a family of representation-tuning methods that allows
190   for interpretable interventions within linear subspaces.
191
      In this work, we leverage the concept of representation steering to introduce a novel approach,
192
      MoReS, which enhances attention to visual representations, thereby demonstrating superior param-
193   eter efficiency compared to baseline PEFT methods (Hu et al., 2021; Houlsby et al., 2019; Liu et al.,
194   2022; Qiu et al., 2023).

260   4   M O R E S M ETHOD
261
262   Based on insights gained from intrinsic modality imbalance, we introduce Modality Linear
263   Representation-Steering (MoReS) as a novel method for visual instruction tuning which can rebal-
264   ance visual and textual representations and achieve comparable performance with fewer trainable
265   parameters.
266   Our approach is grounded in the linear subspace hypothesis, originally proposed by Bolukbasi et al.
267   (2016), which suggests that information pertaining to a specific concept is encoded within a linear
268   subspace in a model’s representation space. This hypothesis has been rigorously validated across
269   numerous domains, including language understanding and interpretability (Lasri et al., 2022; Nanda
      et al., 2023; Amini et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2024c).

273   that the language model’s existing capabilities are preserved, while continuously guiding the MLLM

      5.4     M ULTI - SCALE DATA F INE - TUNING
458
459   During visual instruction tuning, the scale of specific task datasets can vary significantly. To gain a
460   comprehensive understanding of our method compared to other training approaches, we follow the
461   methodology of (Chen et al., 2022) and randomly sample 1K, 5K, and 10K data points from each
462   dataset, defining these as small-scale, medium-scale, and large-scale tasks, respectively. Given the
463   limited resources available, we choose MoReS-L for fine-tuning.
464   Table 3 demonstrates that MoReS exhibits strong capabilities across all scales. Notably, in small-
465   scale tasks, MoReS outperforms full fine-tuning performance while using only 575 times fewer
466   parameters than LoRA and 8,475 fewer than full fine-tuning. In contrast, methods like OFT and
467   IA3 fail to surpass full fine-tuning despite utilizing significantly more parameters. This result under-
468   scores the practicality of MoReS in real-world scenarios where data collection can be challenging,
469   suggesting that MoReS is suitable for multi-scale visual instruction tuning.

      To gain deeper insights into our MoReS method, we conduct ablation studies focusing on its sub-
      space choice and steered visual token ratio. We use LLaVA Steering-3B model as our baseline for
474
      comparison. Table 4 summarizes the results of two types of ablations.
475
476   First, concerning the choice of subspace rank, we found that a rank of 1 achieves the highest average
477   performance of 81.8 across four visual tasks while also requiring the fewest parameters, specifically
478   0.164M. Second, regarding the steered visual token ratio, we varied this parameter from 100%
479
      (dense steering) to 1% (sparse steering). The results indicate that a ratio of 1% is optimal, yielding
      the best or near-optimal performance on four benchmarks while also significantly reducing inference

      overhead due to its sparse steering approach.

526   7    C ONCLUSION
527
528   This paper introduces Modality Linear Representation-Steering (MoReS), a novel method to sig-
529   nificantly reduce the required number of trainable parameters during visual instruction tuning. The
530   key idea behind MoReS is to re-balance visual and textual representations while still maintaining
531   strong performance across a variety of downstream tasks. By integrating MoReS into LLaVA family
532   models, comprehensive evaluation results confirm the effectiveness of the proposed solution. Hence,
533   it further confirms our assertion that intrinsic modality rebalance would represent a promising new
534   approach to optimizing visual instruction tuning.
535   To facilitate future research in the community, we also present the LLaVA Steering Factory, a versa-
536   tile framework designed to enhance the development and evaluation of MLLMs with minimal coding
537   effort. This framework enables customizable training configurations for various tasks and integrates
538   analytical tools, such as attention score distribution analysis. This facilitates systematic comparisons
539   among different methods and offers deeper insights into the intrinsic modality imbalance, ultimately
      contributing to more effective visual instruction tuning.
