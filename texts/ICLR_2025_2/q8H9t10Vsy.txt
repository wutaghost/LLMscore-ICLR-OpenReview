011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Fine-tuning large language models (LLMs) for specific downstream tasks has tradi-
014            tionally relied on memory-intensive optimizers using classical backpropagation,
015
               which demands substantial memory to store model states for gradient compu-
               tation, motivating the development of memory-efficient zeroth-order optimizers
016
               that operate in a forward-only manner. However, the slower convergence of the
017
               zeroth-order optimizer remains a challenge, which recent research addresses by
018            incorporating Hessian information to accelerate training, although storing even
019            the diagonal Hessian requires memory equivalent to that of the model weights,
020            leading to significant memory usage. To mitigate this problem, we propose a
021            novel approach that integrates the block coordinate descent (BCD) method with
022            a Hessian-informed zeroth-order optimizer, allowing us to treat model layers as
023            separate blocks and update only a subset of layers per training iteration, thereby
024            reducing memory requirements and accelerating convergence. Specifically, at
025            each iteration, an active block of layers is selected according to the chosen BCD
026
               rule, such as ascending order, and their weights are updated while the other layers
               remain fixed, with diagonal Hessian information stored and updated exclusively
027
               for the active layers. For fine-tuning foundation models of medium size (OPT-1.3B
028
               and LLaMA-2-7B), our method achieves up to 39% memory reduction compared
029            to existing Hessian-informed zeroth-order methods, while preserving baseline
030            accuracy and memory usage to zeroth-order methods across various tasks, offer-
031            ing a memory-efficient alternative method for LLMs fine-tuning, especially on
032            memory-constrained devices.

035   1   INTRODUCTION
036
037   Fine-tuning transformer-based large models is an essential step in adapting pre-trained models to
038   specific downstream tasks and further improving performance (Raffel et al., 2020). This process also
039
      allows the model to continue training on lower-end devices compared to those used for pre-training,
      thus improving accessibility and reducing the training cost. For this intent, parameter-efficient fine-
040
      tuning (PEFT) techniques, such as LoRA (Hu et al., 2021), have been proposed to enable fine-tuning
041
      on consumer-level GPUs or even edge devices, providing significant economic and practical benefits.
042   Typically, fine-tuning employs traditional optimizers like SGD or Adam (Kingma, 2014), which
043   use backpropagation to update model weights. This process requires storing parameters, gradients,
044   activations, and possibly other optimizer states, significantly increasing memory requirements (Lv
045   et al., 2023b;a; Rajbhandari et al., 2020). As model sizes have increased and larger batch sizes are
046   employed for training, the memory demands of traditional optimizers have become a significant
047   bottleneck for devices with limited memory resources, even when using existing PEFT methods (Cai
048   et al., 2020). Our work aims to address this challenge by exploring memory-efficient techniques
049   further to reduce the memory overhead during fine-tuning on low-end devices.
050   To tackle the memory inefficiency issue, recent advancements have explored the use of zeroth-
051   order optimizers such as MeZO (Malladi et al., 2023) that estimate the gradients with only forward
052   passes, which eliminates the need for backpropagation, thereby significantly reducing memory
053   consumption by avoiding the storage of intermediate optimizer states. Though memory-efficient, the
      slower convergence rates of zeroth-order optimizers have limited their practical utility. To accelerate

076   To efficiently and effectively utilize second-order information, we consider the traditional block
077   coordinate descent (BCD) method, which solves optimization problems successively along coordinate
078   directions, and propose a block coordinate descent Newton method (BCD-Newton) to tackle our
079   challenge. Inspired by recent advances in applying BCD with Adam (Kingma, 2014) and AdamW

083
      block-wise importance sampling, to achieve optimal training performance. This approach significantly
084
      reduces the memory required to store Hessian information. In our optimization step, beyond the basic
085   zeroth-order method with two forward passes, a three-step forward pass is employed to incorporate
086   second-order updates, thereby facilitating faster convergence. The second-order term is stored as a
087   diagonal Hessian estimate matrix, sized according to the active block of selected transformer layers,
088   and is updated throughout the training process. Thus, we propose a novel optimizer that addresses the
089   memory-convergence trade-off inherent in Hessian-informed zeroth-order optimization by integrating
090   BCD techniques, while simultaneously improving convergence rates.
091   Through extensive experiments on a single RTX 4090 or RTX A6000 GPU, we demonstrate that our
092   method enhances training efficiency and memory management while fine-tuning foundation models,
093   including OPT-1.3B (Zhang et al., 2022b) and LLaMA-2-7B (Touvron et al., 2023). As a BCD zeroth-
094   order Newton method, it empirically delivers superior convergence speed and accuracy compared
095   to MeZO. Additionally, compared to the HiZOO baseline, our approach achieves approximately
096   a 50% speedup and a 40% reduction in memory usage with comparable baseline accuracy across
097   multiple GLUE (Wang, 2018) and SuperGLUE (Wang et al., 2019) tasks. These improvements make
098   our method particularly well-suited for fine-tuning large models on devices with limited memory,
099
      expanding the accessibility of large language models in real-world applications.
100   In summary, our main contributions are three-fold:
101
102          • We propose a novel block coordinate descent fine-tuning pipeline that integrates the previous
103            Hessian-informed zeroth-order optimizer, reducing the memory overhead to make the
104            method a practical and convergence-enhanced alternative to MeZO.
105          • We design improved block coordinate descent schemes that reduce the compute and memory
106            cost of the Hessian-informed forward-only optimizer. By adaptively updating weights across
107            block coordinates of the model layers, this method manages blockwise updates efficiently
               and reduces memory and computational costs.

114
115   First and second order Optimization for LLMs. Traditional first-order optimizers, such as SGD,
116   AdaGrad (Duchi et al., 2011), and RMSProp (Tieleman et al., 2012), are foundational tools in deep
117   learning. Adam (Kingma, 2014), with its adaptive moment estimates for faster convergence, and its
118
      variant AdamW (Loshchilov, 2017), which modifies the weight decay term to improve generalization,
      have become the dominant optimizers for fine-tuning large language models (LLMs). Second-order
119
      optimization methods incorporating Hessian information, such as K-FAC (Martens & Grosse, 2015),
120
      EVA (Zhang et al., 2022a), Adahessian (Yao et al., 2021), and Sophia (Liu et al., 2023), have been
121   explored to further accelerate convergence. However, estimating the Hessian is computationally and
122   memory intensive, particularly with the growing size of LLMs, which makes these second-order
123   methods less practical for fine-tuning on devices with limited memory resources.
124
      Zeroth-order (ZO) Optimization. A classical zeroth-order optimization method is SPSA (Spall,
125
      1992), along with its corresponding SGD variant, ZO-SGD, which estimates the gradient using
126
      two forward passes before and after parameter perturbation. Recently, MeZO (Malladi et al., 2023)
127   adapted ZO-SGD by incorporating the random number generator, enabling an in-place implementation
128   that significantly reduces memory usage for storing random vectors during training. Based on MeZO,
129   recent work explores its variants like incorporated sparsity for memory efficiency (Guo et al., 2024).
130   Additionally, Zhang et al. (2024) conducted a benchmark study to analyze and enhance zeroth-order
131   fine-tuning methods. However, the convergence performance of ZO methods often falls behind that
132   of first-order methods. To improve convergence, HiZOO (Zhao et al., 2024b) proposed to utilize
133   Hessian information through diagonal Hessian estimation. Beyond these approaches, several other
134   gradient-free methods have been proposed, such as using evolutionary algorithms for gradient-free
135
      optimization (Sun et al., 2022b;a).
136   Memory-efficient Fine-tuning for LLMs. Numerous algorithms have been developed to reduce
137   memory costs for training LLMs. Based on backpropagation, practical techniques such as gradient
138   checkpointing (Chen et al., 2016) recompute gradients, FlashAttention (Dao et al., 2022) employs
139   tiling and recomputation to leverage cache for improved efficiency, and the ZeRO optimizers (Ra-
140   jbhandari et al., 2020; Ren et al., 2021) enable offloading to manage memory usage effectively.
141
      Additionally, researchers have utilized compression and quantization methods to approximate gra-
      dients, activations, and other optimizer states, enhancing training performance (Jiang et al., 2022;
142
      Li et al., 2024). On another front, methods like LOMO (Lv et al., 2023b;a) fuse gradient updates to
143
      accelerate training. One notable approach to fine-tuning is parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT)
144   methods, which includes techniques such as Adapters (LoRA) (Hu et al., 2021; Houlsby et al., 2019),
145   prompt tuning (Lester et al., 2021), and selective methods like bias-only fine-tuning (Zaken et al.,
146   2021) and layer-wise freezing (Brock et al., 2017). In addition, Zhao et al. (2024a) recently introduced
147   GaLore which reduces memory costs by projecting gradients into a low-rank compact space.
148
      Block Coordinate Descent (BCD) methods for LLM Optimization. In BCD, the optimization
149
      objective is minimized successively along coordinate directions. When applied to LLM fine-tuning,
150   this approach can be seen as a branch of selective methods in parameter-efficient fine-tuning. The re-
151   cently proposed BAdam (Luo et al., 2024) showcases the effectiveness of combining block coordinate
152   descent with Adam. Similarly, LiSA (Pan et al., 2024) improves performance by selectively updating
153   transformer layers with AdamW optimizer, outperforming LoRA across tasks on LLaMA-2-70B.
154
      Overall, our method offers a complementary optimizer-based solution that can be combined with
155
      techniques like compression and system-level approaches to improve memory efficiency. Amid the
156   rapid advancements in efficient training for LLMs and other foundation models, the most closely
157   related works to ours are HiZOO and BAdam. However, our approach distinguishes itself by
158   addressing the memory overhead of these methods in two key ways: first, by eliminating the need for
159   backpropagation through zeroth-order optimization, and second, by reducing the memory cost of
160   Hessian-informed methods through block coordinate descent. Furthermore, unlike PEFT methods,
161   our approach enables full parameter fine-tuning, which has been demonstrated to yield superior
      performance in various tasks (Ding et al., 2022).

      3.2     R EVISITING M EMORY C ONSUMPTION FROM BCD P ERSPECTIVE
248
249   Who consumed my memory? Second-order methods incorporate full or diagonal Hessian matrix, or
250   its estimation, as a preconditioner to accelerate convergence, but this introduces a significant memory
251   cost of O(d). For large models such as LLaMA-2-7B (Touvron et al., 2023) with d = 7 billion
252   parameters, this requires 2d memory in FP16 precision, resulting in approximately over 14GB of
253   memory storage. When combined with the memory required for model parameters, this easily exceeds
254   the capacity of consumer-level devices, undermining MeZO’s original goal of achieving memory
255
      efficiency. Our experiments further demonstrate that directly applying Hessian-based optimization
      steps significantly increases memory usage, as shown in Table 1. Even though approaches such as
256
      HiZOO offer performance improvements, the considerable memory overhead from storing Hessian
257
      information becomes a bottleneck, particularly when fine-tuning large models. This dilemma leads
258   to a situation where the benefits of second-order methods are outweighed by their heavy memory
259   consumption, limiting their practicality in memory-constrained environments. Furthermore, the
260   memory consumption increases with batch size for both first-order and Hessian-based methods,
261   intensifying the memory overhead, as illustrated in Figure 2.
262
      How to reduce Hessian memory consumption? To address this memory-convergence trade-off,
263
      we propose integrating block coordinate descent (BCD) into the zeroth-order Newton optimization.
264   BCD allows us to partition the model into blocks, optimizing only a subset of layers at each iteration
265   while keeping the rest fixed. This approach dramatically reduces the memory required for storing
266   Hessian information, as it is only computed for the active blocks. For instance, by partitioning the
267   aforementioned LLaMA-2-7B model into D = 32 blocks, corresponding to its 32 transformer layers,
268   we reduce the additional memory cost associated with Hessian storage to 2d  D , bringing it to under
269   1GB of memory. This significantly improves memory efficiency while preserving the advantages of
      second-order optimization.

308   Motivated by the revisiting of the second-order Hessian memory consumption, we identified a signifi-
309
      cant bottleneck caused by the storage of diagonal Hessian estimation, which introduced substantial
      memory overhead, particularly for large models. Ultimately, to address this memory-convergence
310
      trade-off, we propose a new method that integrates block coordinate descent (BCD) with a zeroth-
311
      order Newton optimizer, termed Block-wise diagonal-Hessian Preconditioned Coordinate Descent
312   Forward-only optimizer (B-PDF). Recognizing the layerwise structure of the transformer model, we
313   treat each layer as a block for Hessian-informed zeroth-order optimization. By partitioning the model
314   into blocks and updating only a subset of layers at each iteration, we reduce the Hessian storage
315   requirement while maintaining the convergence benefits of second-order methods. Additionally, we
316   update the embedding and language model (LM) head layers solely through ZO optimization to
317   mitigate the instability and overhead typically associated with second-order methods, resulting in a
318   more memory-efficient and scalable approach for fine-tuning large models.

320   be employed to determine the active block θπi . Possible strategies include using ascending order,
321   a layerwise importance sampling scheme based on the mean weight norms of each block πi , the
322   Gaussian-Southwell-Diagonal rule (Nutini et al., 2017), or dynamically updated probabilistic lists
323   employing a bandit method. Formally, for the current step T , the parameter block θπb to update can
      be selected using several types of BCD algorithms:

      Remark 1. The optimization objective is to minimize the loss function L(θ) by leveraging diago-
364
      nal Hessian preconditioning within the memory-efficient framework. During each iteration, after
365
      selecting the active blocks for updates, zeroth-order optimization with a diagonal Hessian precondi-
366   tioner is performed for the chosen layers. The diagonal Hessian estimate will be reinitialized for a
367   newly selected block, and updates for that block will occur over several subsequent iterations. The
368   algorithm applies in-place perturbations to the parameters in three steps with the perturbation scale
369   corresponding to µi = 0, +µ, −2µ, sampling a normally distributed random vector z to perturb the
370   selected block θπb . For each perturbation, the loss function L(θ) is computed to estimate the gradient
371   information. Afterwards, the diagonal Hessian is updated based on the difference in the computed
372   losses from the perturbed parameters. The gradient for the selected block is then projected using the
373   updated Hessian, and the weights of the active block are updated accordingly.
374   Remark 2. The proposed algorithm efficiently combines BCD with a zeroth-order Newton method
375   by updating only a subset of model layers per iteration. This approach reduces memory usage by
376   eliminating backpropagation and utilizing block-wise gradient updates, while maintaining conver-
377   gence speed through the use of diagonal Hessian approximations. The consistent use of random
      vectors and selective parameter perturbation further enhance the method’s memory efficiency.

526
527   In this paper, we propose a novel memory-efficient zeroth-order Newton method that integrates block
528   coordinate descent (BCD) with a diagonal Hessian-preconditioned zeroth-order optimizer for fine-
529   tuning large language models (LLMs). Our approach effectively mitigates the substantial memory
530   overhead commonly associated with second-order methods by employing selective block-wise
531
      updates. By combining the BCD technique with the Hessian preconditioner, we achieve significant
      reductions in memory consumption while preserving competitive accuracy and convergence speed
532
      performance. Our extensive experiments on OPT-1.3B and LLaMA-2-7B demonstrate that our
533
      method can reduce memory usage by up to 39% compared to existing second-order optimizers
534   while maintaining baseline accuracy across various downstream tasks. Furthermore, our approach
535   exhibits faster wall-clock convergence than conventional zeroth-order methods, making it a practical
536   and scalable solution for fine-tuning large models on resource-constrained devices. Future work
537   will aim to extend this methodology to larger models and more complex tasks, as well as refine
538   the block selection strategies to further enhance both efficiency and performance. In summary, our
539   method provides a promising direction for memory-efficient fine-tuning of LLMs, offering practical
      advantages, particularly in memory-limited environments.

      A    I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
703
704
      The implementation of the B-PDF function is designed to enhance zero-order optimization (ZOO) by
705   incorporating selective layer-wise updates based on Hessian-informed perturbations.
706
707   In the zo_Hessian_step_update function (Zhao et al., 2024b), the Hessian matrix is initialized
708
      if it does not already exist. This matrix is created by iterating over each trainable parameter of the
      model and initializing a tensor of ones with the same dimensions as the respective parameter.
709
      The estimate Hessian matrix serves as a second-order approximation that is updated during the
710
      optimization process.
711
712   In our framework, we introduce a layer-specific update mechanism within the optimization function
713   hizoo_step_update, which implements a periodic selection of layers, referred to as "hizoo
714
      layers." These layers are chosen iteratively every fixed number of steps, with the cycle determined by
      a step counter.
715
716   The layers can be selected either sequentially, in an ordered manner, or via other rules such as
717   Gauss-Southwell quadratic diagonal selection (GSQ) (Nutini et al., 2017), which prioritizes layers
718   based on previous scores.
719   Following MeZO (Malladi et al., 2023) and HiZOO (Zhao et al., 2024b), we apply a noise-based
720   perturbation to the selected Hessian-informed layers during each iteration using Gaussian noise. The
721   noise is scaled by the square root of the corresponding Hessian matrix and a random vector sampled
722   from a normal distribution. This approach allows the optimization process to focus on specific layers
723   while updating their parameters iteratively.
724   By controlling the frequency and scope of these updates, we distribute optimization efforts across
725   different parts of the network over time. This can ensure that updates are not applied uniformly
726   but are instead targeted based on layer importance, thereby improving the overall efficiency of the
727   training process.
728
      Additionally, memory management is considered throughout the implementation, as the Hessian
729
      matrix is periodically cleared, and GPU memory is freed using torch.cuda.empty_cache(),
730   ensuring that the training process remains efficient, even in memory-constrained environments.
731
732   In addition, we use torch.clamp API to clamp the intermediate results to meet the precision
733
      requirements and reduce the instability of second-order methods.
734   Overall, the B-PDF implementation introduces a structured and targeted optimization approach that
735   leverages layer-wise perturbations to enhance the zero-order optimization process effectively.
