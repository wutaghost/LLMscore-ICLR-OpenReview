011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            The study of learning causal structure with latent variables has advanced the un-
014            derstanding of the world by uncovering causal relationships and latent factors.
015
               However, in real-world scenarios, such as those in climate systems, causal rela-
               tionships are often nonparametric, dynamic, and exist among both observed vari-
016
               ables and latent variables. These challenges motivate us to consider a general
017
               setting in which causal relations are nonparametric and unrestricted in their occur-
018            rence, which is unconventional to current methods. To solve this problem, with
019            the aid of 3-measurement in temporal structure, we theoretically show that both
020            latent variables and processes can be identified up to minor indeterminacy under
021            mild assumptions. Furthermore, we demonstrate that the observed causal structure
022            is identifiable if there is generation variability, roughly speaking, the latent vari-
023            ables induce sufficient variations in generating the noise terms, by the established
024            functional equivalence. The primary idea of this framework is to learn causal
025            representations from causally-related observations, and subsequently address this
026
               problem as a task of general nonlinear causal discovery. Based on these theo-
               retical insights, we develop an estimation approach simultaneously learning both
027
               the observed causal structure, latent representation, and latent Markov network.
028
               Experimental results in simulation studies validate the theoretical foundations and
029            demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed methodology. In the climate data
030            experiments, we show that it offers a powerful and in-depth understanding of the
031            climate system.

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   In real-world observations, such as video data, temperature distributions, and economic investiga-
037   tions, are often partially observed. Estimating latent variable causal graphs from these observations
038   is particularly challenging, as the latent variables are in general not identifiable or unique due to the
039
      possibility of undergoing nontrivial transformations (Hyvärinen & Pajunen, 1999), even when the
      independent factors of variation are known (Locatello et al., 2019). Several studies have aimed to
040
      uncover causally related latent variables in specific cases. For instance, (Silva et al., 2006) identify
041
      latent variables in linear-Gaussian models using Tetrad conditions (Spearman, 1928), while the gen-
042   eralized independent noise (GIN) condition (Xie et al., 2020) has been proposed to estimate linear,
043   non-Gaussian latent variable causal graphs. Recent work employs rank constraints to identify hier-
044   archical latent structures (Huang et al., 2022). However, these approaches are constrained by linear
045   relations and require specific types of structural assumptions.
046
      Furthermore, nonlinear Independent Component Analysis (ICA) has established identifiability re-
047
      sults using auxiliary variables (Hyvarinen & Morioka, 2016; 2017; Hyvärinen et al., 2023), showing
048   that independent factors can be recovered up to a certain transformation of the underlying latent
049   variables under appropriate assumptions. In contrast, (Zheng et al., 2022; Zheng & Zhang, 2023)
050   develop identifiability results without auxiliary variables, relying instead on a sparse structure in
051   the generating process, while Gresele et al. (2021) impose restrictions on the function classes of
052   generating process. The framework of nonlinear ICA has further been extended to incorporate tem-
053   poral structures (Hyvarinen & Morioka, 2017; Yao et al., 2022; Lachapelle et al., 2024), utilizing
      time-lagged dependencies in temporal data to model dynamic causal mechanisms. Consequently,


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      identifying latent structures has become a prominent research focus (Schölkopf et al., 2021). For
055   instance, (Lippe et al., 2022) propose methods for identifying latent variables in both time-lagged
056   and instantaneous contexts, (Yao et al., 2023) address settings with partially observed variables,
057   (Buchholz et al., 2024) explore interventional data under linear mixing assumptions, and (Zhang
058   et al., 2024) develop a general framework for identifying latent structures using multiple distribu-
059   tions. However, most prior work assumes that the generating processes from sources to observations
060   are deterministic, except for a few studies that consider linear additive noise (Khemakhem et al.,
061   2020; Hälvä et al., 2021; Gassiat et al., 2020)—let alone scenarios where causal relations exist
062   among observations. Moreover, all of the aforementioned work is constrained by the assumption
063
      of an invertible and deterministic generating function, which is often considered infeasible in many
      real-world scenarios.
064
065   A typical setting where such methodological assumptions are too restrictive is the climate sys-
066   tem (Rolnick et al., 2022; Lucarini et al., 2014). With high-level latent variables dynamically chang-
067   ing and influencing observations (e.g., temperature, humidity) and causal structure (e.g., wind sys-
068   tem), this presents a challenging problem that necessitates developing a solution to uncover these
069
      complex relationships. To address it, we focus on a temporal causal structure involving dynamic
      latent variables driving a latent causal process and causally-related observed variables, with all con-
070
      nections represented through general nonlinear functions. The final objective is to identify these
071
      factors under minimal indeterminacy. For this challenging task, we address three fundamental ques-
072   tions: (i) What unique attributes of latent variables can be recovered from the causally-related ob-
073   served variables with the aid of temporal structures? (ii) How to do causal discovery in the presence
074   of latent variables, without relying on conventional restrictions? (iii) How can empirically identify
075   both the latent processes and the observed causal structure simultaneously? Begging these questions,
076   our main contributions are mainly three-fold:
077
078         1. We theoretically establish the conditions required for achieving the identifiability of latent
079            variables from causally-related observations in 3-measurements model.
080         2. We establish functional equivalence between a nonlinear Structural Equation Model (SEM)
081            and a nonlinear ICA model, and provide identifiability guarantees of observed causal Di-
082            rected Acyclic Graph (DAG) in favor of it.
083         3. Building on these theoretical foundations, we present a comprehensive estimation frame-
084            work that, to the best of our knowledge, is the first to simultaneously tackle causal repre-
085            sentation learning and causal discovery. Extensive experiments on diverse synthetic and
086            real-world datasets validate the effectiveness of our theory and methodology.

532
533         We establish identifiability results for uncovering latent causal variables, latent Markov network, and
534         observed causal DAG, especially in complex nonlinear systems such as climate science. Simulated
535         experiments validate our theoretical findings, and real-world experiments offer causal insights for
536
            climate science.
537         For future work, we aim to address the issue of performance degradation in data with increasing
538         dimensionality. A possible approach to tackling this challenge is to resort to the divide-and-conquer
539         strategy, which partitions the high-dimensional problem into a set of overlapping subsets of variables
            with lower dimensionality, leveraging the prior knowledge of geographical information.
