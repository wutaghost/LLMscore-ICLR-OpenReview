011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Structured pruning is one of the most popular approaches to effectively compress
014            the heavy deep neural networks (DNNs) into compact sub-networks while retain-
015
               ing the original network performance. The existing methods suffer from multi-
               stage procedures along with significant engineering efforts and human expertise.
016
               The Only-Train-Once series (OTOv1-v3) has been recently proposed to resolve
017
               the many pain points by streamlining the workflow. However, the built-in sparse
018            optimizers in the OTO series, i.e., the Half-Space Projected Gradient (HSPG) fam-
019            ily, have limitations that require hyper-parameter tuning and the implicit controls
020            of the sparsity exploration, consequently requires intervening by human expertise.
021            To further address such limitations, we propose a novel Hybrid Efficient Struc-
022            tured Sparse Optimizer (HESSO). HESSO could automatically and efficiently
023            train a DNN within a single run to produce a high-performing sub-network. Mean-
024            while, it is almost tuning-free and enjoys user-friendly integration for generic
025            training applications. To address another common issue of irreversible pruning
026
               performance collapse observed in some DNNs, we further propose a novel Cor-
               rective Redundant Identification Cycle (CRIC) to plug into HESSO for reliably
027
               identifying indispensable structures. We numerically demonstrate the efficacy of
028
               HESSO and its enhanced version HESSO-CRIC on a variety of applications rang-
029            ing from computer vision to natural language processing, including large language
030            model. The numerical results showcase that HESSO can achieve competitive per-
031            formance to varying state-of-the-art benchmarks and support most DNN archi-
032            tectures. Meanwhile, CRIC can effectively prevent the irreversible performance
033            collapse and further enhance the performance of HESSO on certain applications.

038   Large deep neural networks (DNNs) have successfully powered a variety of applications (Ji and
039
      Chen, 2019; Zhou et al., 2024; Zhu et al., 2023). However, their typical significant time and space
      complexities make inference expensive and restrict deployment in resource-constrained environ-
040
      ments. Consequently, how to compress the full DNN to the greatest extend while preserving the
041
      performance becomes essential in the many industrial and academic AI deployment pipelines. There
042   are various model compression techniques including but not limited to pruning (Chen et al., 2021b;
043   2023c; Fang et al., 2023), knowledge distillation (Ko et al., 2024) and quantization (Han et al.,
044   2015), which have been well developed in the past decades.
045

046
      from a pre-defined full DNN by identifying and removing redundant structures (Gale et al., 2019;
047
      Han et al., 2015; Chen et al., 2021b; 2023c; Fang et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2024).
048   Classical pruning methods focus on conducting a multi-stage procedure, requiring significant engi-
049   neering efforts and expertise to manually build pruning search space, identify redundant structures,
050   construct sub-network, and fine-tune to recover lost knowledge. To alleviate the human engineering
051   burden, recent works (Chen et al., 2023c;b; Fang et al., 2023) have proposed pruning dependency
052   graph to automate the pruning search space and sub-network construction. OTOv1-v2 (Chen et al.,
053   2021b; 2023c) further unify these multi-stage components together, requiring only a single train-
      ing run to directly get a compact sub-network without the need of further fine-tuning. Specifically,

      In this section, we provide a brief literature review on automatic structured pruning, while additional
125   reviews on knowledge transfer and DNN architecture optimization can be found in Appendix A.
126
127
      General Pruning Procedures. Structured pruning aims to compress DNNs by removing unnec-
128   essary structures while maintaining performance (Han et al., 2015; Wen et al., 2016). The general
129   procedure typically involves: (i) training a full model; (ii) identifying and removing redundant struc-
130   tures to construct a slimmer DNN based on various criteria (Lin et al., 2019; He et al., 2018a; Wen
131   et al., 2016; Li et al., 2020b; Zhuang et al., 2020; Chen et al., 2017; 2018; 2021a; 2020b; Gao et al.,
132   2020; Zhuang et al., 2020; Meng et al., 2020; Yang et al., 2019; Zhou et al., 2019; van Baalen et al.,
133   2020; Frankle and Carbin, 2018); and (iii) retraining the pruned model to recover any accuracy lost
134   during pruning. These methods often require a complex and time-consuming process, involving
135   multiple training iterations and significant domain knowledge to manually handle each step.
136
137   Automated Pruning Given Pre-defined Search Space. To resolve the pain-points of human in-
138   terventions, automated pruning is raising interests from different perspectives. Given a predefined
139
      search space, AMC (He et al., 2018b) employs reinforcement learning agents to automatically de-
      termine the optimal pruning ratio. EagleEye (Li et al., 2020a) further introduces a sub-network eval-
140
      uation scheme based on adaptive batch normalization, which can be integrated into AMC. OFA (Cai
141
      et al., 2019) automates the generation of sub-networks for different hardware platforms in a sin-
142   gle process. While these approaches yield impressive performance, their application is limited to
143   predefined search spaces. Moreover, AMC incurs additional training costs for its reinforcement
144   learning agent. OFA’s training procedure is complex and heavy to adopt all sub-networks. It also
145   requires knowing the optimal training procedure for the largest super-network in advance to ensure
146   the performance, which makes practical adoption less convenient.
147
148   Automated Pruning Over Any DNNs. On the other hand, automatically pruning arbitrary mod-
149   els without prior knowledge of the search space remained a significant challenge. Recent methods,
150   such as OTO (Chen et al., 2021b; 2023c;b) and DepGraph (Fang et al., 2023), have made progress
151   in automating the structured pruning process for general DNNs via dependency graph analysis.
152   Subsequent works like (Wang et al., 2024) and (Ren et al., 2024) automates pruning over ONNX
153
      models. ATO (Wu et al., 2024) introduces ControlNet upon OTOv2. Among these, OTO offers a
      one-shot joint training and pruning framework that can seamlessly integrate into various training
154
      processes to produce high-performing sub-networks in a single run. While these automated ap-
155
      proaches have significantly improved user convenience, end-users still face significant challenges
156   with hyper-parameter tuning and the sparse optimization expertise required to calibrate OTO’s built-
157   in HSPG family (Chen et al., 2020c; Dai et al., 2023). Furthermore, some DNNs contain indis-
158   pensable structures, the pruning of which leads to irreversible performance degradation. Identifying
159   these critical structures remains an open problem that is often handled manually on a case-by-case
160   basis, complicating practical use. In this work, we tackle these pain points to propose an efficient,
161   tuning-free, and user-friendly joint training and pruning optimizer HESSO along with its enhanced
      version HESSO-CRIC to reliably identify indispensable structures to ensure the performance.

233   3.2   H YBRID T RAINING IN HESSO
234
235   After identifying the redundant groups in Section 3.1, the next step is to project these groups onto
236   zero and transfer their knowledge to the important groups, ensuring the pruned model maintains its
237   performance. This is achieved through a hybrid training schema.
238   For the redundant groups GR , we progressively and uniformly push their parameters towards zero.
239   This process is detailed in line 7-8 in Algorithm 1 and decipted in Figure 2. The goal is to ensure
240   that the parameters in the redundant groups become zero after Tp steps. During this penalization
241   process, there is a risk of forgetting the knowledge contained in the redundant groups, which may
242   manifest as a degradation in the objective function’s value. To mitigate this, we employ a standard
243   optimization method, such as vanilla SGD or its variants like Adam, on the important groups GI .
244   This step aims to continue optimizing the objective function f and preserve the model’s performance
245
      despite the pruning of redundant groups. By maintaining the optimization of the important groups,
      the knowledge lost from the redundant groups can be transferred and compensated for, ensuring that
246
      the pruned model remains effective.

248   Next, we provide brief intuitive comparisons of HESSO against two popular pruning algorithms.
249
250   Minimize tuning efforts compared to DHSPG. DHSPG in OTOv2 involves significant hyper-
251   parameter tuning to adjust parameters for sparsity exploration. This tuning often requires domain-
252   specific knowledge, as the appropriate settings can vary depending on the particular application or
253
      dataset. This requirement can make DHSPG more complex and less accessible, particularly for
      practitioners without extensive expertise in hyper-parameter and sparse optimization. Contrarily,
254
      HESSO offers more explicit control over sparsity exploration. The pruning process in HESSO is
255
      regulated by the pruning periods P and the period length TP , which determine the pace and extent of
256   the pruning procedure. This structured approach simplifies the process, making it easier to manage.
257
258   Architecture-agnostic computational invariance compared to ResRep and SliceGPT.
259   ResRep (Ding et al., 2021b) and SliceGPT (Ashkboos et al., 2024) are proposed to preserve com-
260   putational invariance, i.e., making pruned and full models produce similar outputs, for CNNs and
261   transformers, respectively. However, they are architecture specific, requires additional efforts, such
262   as injecting additional layers in SliceGPT and computing reset gradients in ResRep. The knowledge
263   transfer in HESSO similarly seeks to maintain computational invariance but does so by preserving
264   objective function levels. In addition, HESSO is architecture-agnostic, efficient and user-friendly,
265   demonstrating both scalability and versatility compared with ResRep and SliceGPT.
266   As a result, HESSO is generally easier to use and more adaptable to various applications, as it
267   significantly reduces the need for extensive tuning and specialized knowledge. The design of hy-
268   brid training for knowledge transfer effectively promotes the performance of pruned model. They
269   make HESSO a more user-friendly and efficient option for achieving structured sparsity in models,
      allowing for more straightforward and consistent application across different tasks and domains.

      3.3   A PPROXIMATION E RRORS OF S ALIENCE S CORES
271
272   Although HESSO could tackle most DNNs and applications, it may sometimes yield unsatisfactory
273   results when the target DNN possesses certain indispensable structures, defined as follows.
274   Definition 3.1 (Indispensable structure). Given a deep neural network M, a minimally removal
275   structure is called indispensable if removing it from M would cause significant performance degra-
276   dation, which can not be recovered given user resources. In particular, we say a minimally removal
277   structure as ϵ-indispensable associated with an objective f if pruning the variables [x]g → 0 dete-
278   riorates f at least ϵ, i.e., f (x∣[x]g → 0) ≥ f (x) + ϵ for a minimization optimization problem. The
279   degradation ϵ can not be recovered by (i) keeping training M, (ii) the training cost such as GPU days
280
      exceeding user budget, or (iii) the training receipt for M is black-box and hard to be reproduced.
281   The origin of indispensable structures varies. One reason may be due to architectural design issues
282   where certain layers in M play more critical roles than others and are very sensitive to any mod-
283   ifications, as exemplified by a low-level vision benchmark in Section 4.2. Another reason could
284   be the learning strategy. For instance, in large language models (LLMs), it has been observed that
285   knowledge is unevenly distributed across different layers (Chen et al., 2023a). Removing any of
286   these structures could result in an irreversible collapse of the DNN’s performance.
287
288   Salience score approximation errors. The existing saliency scores might fail to identify these
289   indispensable components accurately. As described in Appendix D, they are typically designed to
290
      approximate the impact of projecting groups of variables to zero over the objective function. Such
      approximations, for example, perhaps the most commonly used Taylor importance scores, are more
291
      accurate when the iterate is close enough to the origin point.

303   3.4   C ORRECTIVE R EDUNDANCY I DENTIFICATION C IRCLE
304
305   To address the limitations discussed in Section 3.3, we propose a novel Corrective Redundant Iden-
306   tification Cycle (CRIC). This method aims to more reliably identify redundant structures within the
307   target DNN, even when indispensable structures are present. The CRIC mechanism can be seam-
308
      lessly integrated into HESSO, enhancing its ability to accurately discern which parts of the model
      can be pruned without compromising performance.
309
310   To mitigate the issue of false positive redundant predictions caused by the approximation error, such
311   as Taylor expansion, CRIC measures the saliency score of redundant group candidates multiple
312   times along the projection to the origin. Unlike the greedy approach in HESSO, CRIC incorporates
313   a corrective cycle mechanism. This mechanism iteratively promotes groups as redundant and tracks
      the outlier groups. The cycle terminates when the redundancy prediction is deemed reliable, i.e., no
      outlier appearance is detected. The final output is a set of redundant groups GR with the bottom-K
314
315
      overall saliency scores. This approach significantly reduces false positive redundant identifications
316
      and addresses the failure cases of HESSO, as demonstrated numerically in Section 4.
317

517   We observed that without conducting a knowledge distribution analysis and manually skipping cer-
518   tain layers from pruning, as LoRAShear (Chen et al., 2023a) did, HESSO often led to an irreversible
519   performance collapse. This is because knowledge in LLMs is unevenly distributed across layers
520   due to the learning strategy. The saliency scores calculated upon the pretraining weights may fail to
521   identify essential structures, making it difficult to differentiate between indispensable components
522   and those that could be pruned. As a result, pruning such critical structures severely degrades the
523   model’s performance, making recovery with limited resources nearly impossible.
524   HESSO-CRIC was able to automatically bypass these crucial structures, enabling effective and suc-
525   cessful pruning. We then compared with SliceGPT (Ashkboos et al., 2024), LLM-Pruner (Ma et al.,
526   2023), LoraShear (Chen et al., 2023a) and LoraPrune (Zhang et al., 2023) across several popular
527   benchmarks. Our findings indicate that HESSO-CRIC consistently outperforms them at varying
528   pruning ratios, with performance improvements becoming more pronounced as the pruning ratio
529   increases. This is because LLM-Pruner, LoRA-Prune, and LoRAShear are LoRA-based techniques.
530   Lora primarily focuses on fine-tuning well-trained models and is less effective in capturing knowl-
531
      edge for underfitted models, such as pruned LLMs.

533   5     C ONCLUSION
534
535   In this work, we introduced HESSO-(CRIC), a novel Hybrid Efficient Structured Sparse Optimizer
536   tailored for pruning deep neural networks while preserving performance. By combining a hybrid
537   training strategy with explicit, progressive pruning control, and the Corrective Redundant Identi-
538   fication Cycle (CRIC), HESSO-(CRIC) effectively tackles challenges such as tuning efforts, user
539   difficulty, and irreversible performance degradation. Our experiments across diverse domains show
      that it not only competes with but often surpasses state-of-the-art methods.

759   Knowledge Transfer. To retain the performance of the pruned sub-network, HESSO-(CRIC) in-
760
      corporates a knowledge transfer mechanism through a hybrid training schema. This approach differs
      from prior methods, which explicitly use knowledge distillation from unpruned models to preserve
761
      information in pruned models. Existing techniques typically require expensive computations that
762
      involve both pruned and unpruned models, whether by processing logits (Lagunas et al., 2021)
763   or the hidden activations of intermediate layers (Xia et al., 2022; Ko et al., 2023). In contrast,
764   our approach preserves knowledge without incurring such computational costs. Another related
765   works, ResRep (Ding et al., 2021b) and SliceGPT (Ashkboos et al., 2024), also aim to preserve
766   computational invariance. The knowledge transfer in HESSO-(CRIC) similarly seeks to maintain
767   computational invariance but does so by focusing on preserving objective function levels. How-
768   ever, SliceGPT is restricted to the transformer architectures and requires manually injecting ad-
769   ditional layers. ResRep is restricted to CNN architectures and require conducting structurally re-
770   parametrization via computing resetting gradients. HESSO-(CRIC) is architecture-agnostic, effi-
771
      cient and user-friendly, demonstrating both scalability and versatility.
772
773   Neural Architecture Optimization. Another related realm is the optimization over pre-specified
774   neural architecture. NAO (Luo et al., 2018) encodes the DNN architecture into a latent representa-
775   tion, search over the latent space, then decodes back to a revised architecture. NAT (Guo et al., 2019)
776   performs operator transformation upon the given DNN to produce more accurate network. These
777   approaches transform and improve the existing DNNs, yet not search an optimal sub-network. As
778   a result, their produced networks are typically not significantly compact compared to the baseline
779   models. Contrarily, our approach focuses on automatically and effectively discovering compact
780
      sub-networks given pre-specified DNNs via structured pruning.
