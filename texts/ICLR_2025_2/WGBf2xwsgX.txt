009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011               Millions of abandoned oil and gas wells are scattered across the world, leaching
012               methane into the atmosphere and toxic compounds into the groundwater. Many
013               of these locations are unknown, preventing the wells from being plugged and
014               their polluting effects averted. Remote sensing is a relatively unexplored tool for
015               pinpointing abandoned wells at scale. We introduce the first large-scale bench-
016               mark dataset1 for this problem, leveraging high-resolution multi-spectral satellite
017
                  imagery from Planet Labs. Our curated dataset comprises over 213,000 wells
                  (abandoned, suspended, and active) from Alberta, a region with especially high
018
                  well density, sourced from the Alberta Energy Regulator and verified by domain
019
                  experts. We evaluate baseline algorithms for well detection and segmentation,
020               showing the promise of computer vision approaches but also significant room for
021               improvement.

024   1       I NTRODUCTION
025
026   Across the world, there are millions of abandoned oil and gas wells left to degrade by the compa-
027   nies or individuals that built them. No longer producing usable fossil fuels, these wells nonetheless
028
      have a significant impact on the environment, with many of them leaking significant quantities of
      methane, a powerful greenhouse gas, into the atmosphere. In Canada, an estimated 370,000 aban-
029
      doned wells produce methane equivalent to half a million metric tons of CO2 annually (Williams
030
      et al., 2020; ECCC, 2024), while in the U.S. there are an estimated 4 million abandoned wells
031   (Williams et al., 2020), releasing over five million metric tons of CO2 equivalent emissions per year.
032   Abandoned wells also pose health and safety concerns, in particular by leaching toxic chemicals
033   into the groundwater of surrounding communities (Cahill et al., 2019).
034
      It is possible to plug abandoned wells to mitigate the harms associated with them (with so-called
035
      “super-emitter” wells an especially high priority (Riddick et al., 2024; Kang et al., 2016)). However,
036
      a significant fraction of abandoned wells remain unknown. In Pennsylvania, as much as 90% of
037   abandoned wells are estimated to be unrecorded (Kang et al., 2016). In Canada, abandoned wells
038   have been described as the most uncertain source of methane emissions nationally due to the poor
039   quality of data surrounding them (Williams et al., 2020).
040
      With the advent of large-scale remote sensing datasets and powerful machine learning tools to pro-
041
      cess them, it has become possible to label and monitor the built environment as never before (Rolf
042
      et al., 2024). Many such works have focused on opportunities to use remote sensing to accelerate
043   climate action and environmental protection, and oil and gas infrastructure has increasingly been an
044   object of scrutiny (see e.g. (Yang et al., 2013; Sheng et al., 2020)).
045
046
      In this paper, we present the first large-scale machine-learning dataset for pinpointing onshore oil
      and gas wells, encompassing abandoned, suspended, and active wells. Our main contributions are
047
      as follows:
048
049             • We introduce the Alberta Wells Dataset, which includes information on over 200k aban-
050               doned, suspended, and active onshore wells with high-resolution satellite imagery.
051             • We frame the problem of identification of wells as a challenge for object detection and
052               binary segmentation.

      2   P REVIOUS W ORK
062
      Hundreds of satellites continuously monitor the Earth’s surface, generating petabyte-scale remote
063
      sensing datasets (Rolf et al., 2024). With advancements in hardware, the quality of remote sensing
064
      images has significantly improved in terms of spatial and temporal resolution. High-quality remote
065   sensing data are available through state-funded projects like Sentinel and Landsat, and more recently
066   through private enterprises such as Planet Labs (PBC, 2024). Increasingly, machine learning has
067   been used to parse such raw data, including in a wide range of applications for tackling climate
068   change (Yang et al., 2013). Benchmark datasets in this area have included tasks in land use and land
069   cover (LULC) estimation (Sumbul et al., 2019), crop classification (Sykas et al., 2022; Tseng et al.,
070   2021), species distribution modeling (Teng et al., 2023), and forest monitoring (Ioannis Bountos
071   et al., 2023).Some datasets like SpaceNet 7 Etten et al. (2021) include a few cases of study sites
072   with oil wells, although the dataset was developed for multi-temporal urban monitoring.
073   Within this area of research, an increasing body of work has considered the problem of detecting
074   artifacts associated with oil and gas operations. The detection of oil spills using a combination of re-
075   mote sensing and machine learning has been widely explored (Chen et al., 2017; Wang et al., 2023a;
076   Yang et al., 2022). Recently, the detection of oil and gas infrastructure has also been investigated
077   (Sheng et al., 2020; Prajapati et al., 2022), with some studies focusing on the goal of estimating
078   methane emissions (Zhu et al., 2022; Omara et al., 2023). The dataset by (Sheng et al., 2020) in-
079
      cludes 7,066 aerial images, with 149 images of oil refineries. The METER-ML dataset (Zhu et al.,
      2022) comprises 86,599 georeferenced images in the U.S. labeled for methane sources. The OGIM
080
      v1 dataset (Omara et al., 2023) includes 2.6 million point locations of major facilities. A dataset by
081
      (Chang et al., 2023) features 1,388 images of pipelines in the Arctic, while a dataset by (Wang et al.,
082   2023b) includes 3,266 images of heavy-polluting enterprises with 0.25 m resolution.
083
084   The problem of detection of oil and gas wells has also been proposed by a number of authors. Ex-
085
      isting datasets, however, are quite small (500-10,000 samples) and typically are limited to a small
      region and contain only active wells, limiting their applicability in the context of identifying aban-
086
      doned or suspended wells as summarized in Table 1. Most of these studies have primarily focused
087
      on basic machine learning algorithms for well detection due to the limited sample size.

121   3     A LBERTA W ELLS DATASET
122
123   In this paper, we introduce the Alberta Wells Dataset for oil and gas well detection. The dataset is
124   drawn from the province of Alberta, Canada, a region with the third-largest oil reserves in the world
125   and a substantial number of oil and gas wells, many of which have been present for over a century.
126
      The entire province of Alberta (an area larger than the UK and Germany combined) encompasses a
      diverse range of geographical zones and is highly diverse for a landlocked region, including prairies,
127
      lakes, forests, and mountains. The dataset contains over 94,000 patches of satellite imagery acquired
128
      from Planet Labs (PBC, 2024), covering more than 213,000 individual wells. Each patch is anno-
129   tated with labels for both segmentation and bounding box localization. The annotations are based
130   on data from the Alberta Energy Regulator, quality-controlled by domain experts.
131
132
      Our dataset attempts to maximize the amount of data available for learning by including a mixture
      of active and suspended wells alongside abandoned wells. These types of wells appear overall
133
      similar in satellite imagery. In contrast to abandoned wells, “suspended” refers to wells that have
134
      merely paused operations temporarily, though this designation can be inaccurate, and some wells
135   are classified as suspended for long enough that they are truly abandoned. Active wells are those
136   that are currently in operation.
137
138
      To simulate real-world conditions, we ensure a varied density of wells per image, as highlighted in
      Figure 1. We also include satellite imagery patches with no wells present from areas nearby to areas
139
      with wells, ensuring no overlap between the samples. This balanced dataset maintains an equal
140
      distribution of well and non-well images. Table 2 details the total sample count in each dataset split,
141   alongside the number of well and non-well patches.

144   3.1   W ELL DATA C OLLECTION , Q UALITY C ONTROL & PATCH C REATION
145
      The Alberta Energy Regulator (AER) oversees the energy industry in the province, ensuring compa-
146
      nies adhere to regulations as they develop oil and gas resources. AER publishes AER ST37 (AER,
147   2024), a monthly list of all wells reported in Alberta, detailing their geographic location, mode of
148   operation, license status, and type of product being extracted, among other attributes. This data pro-

150   point per site but often contains duplicates. However, this data cannot be used directly because the
151   license status or mode of operation does not always correlate with the actual status of the well and
152   often contains duplicates. Therefore, we work with domain experts to perform quality control on
153   the dataset as illustrated in Figure 2.

310   3.3   S ATELLITE I MAGERY ACQUISITION & L ABEL C REATION
311
312   We used PlanetScope-4-Band imagery (PBC, 2024) featuring RGB and Near Infrared bands to repre-
313   sent satellite images of the region with a medium resolution of about 3 meters per pixel. PlanetScope,
314   a product of Planet Labs, consists of approximately 130 satellites that can image the entire Earth’s
315   land surface daily, collecting up to 200 million sq. km of data each day. We obtained Surface Re-
316   flectance imagery, which is offset-corrected, flat-field-corrected, ortho-rectified, visually processed,
317   and radio-metrically corrected. These processes ensure consistency across varying atmospheric con-
318   ditions and minimize uncertainty in spectral response over time and location, making the data ideal
319   for temporal analysis and monitoring applications.
320   We choose Planet Labs data over other alternatives since it is updated daily, making it possible to
321   pick a consistent time for all the images, which is important for training dataset consistency. It
322   also provides multispectral imagery (4-band: RGB+Near Infrared), and the Near Infrared band is
323   a useful addition since certain features, like ground depressions indicating well sites, may be more
      detectable in this band. Lastly, while other alternatives may be limited in remote regions, Planet’s


                                                         6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      global satellite constellation ensures more consistent coverage. All the imagery we use is made
325   publicly available in the dataset.
326
327   To ensure the highest quality, we selected images with no cloud cover. The images were acquired
328
      by Planet satellites within a timeframe that aligns with the well-location data from AER. We ob-
      tained satellite images for each sample based on geographical coordinates, ensuring an intersection
329
      between the actual area of interest and the acquired imagery.
330
331   We frame the task of identifying wells as both an object detection and segmentation task since
332   related remote sensing tasks have found both framings to be constructive. For each image patch, as
333   shown in Figure 4, we generated corresponding segmentation maps and object detection annotations
334
      for all known wells in the image based on the point labels provided in the AER data. For binary
      segmentation, we annotated each well site with a circle to match the teardrop shape typical for well
335
      sites. We standardized the diameter of a well site to a value of 90 meters (such sites typically range
336
      from 70 to 120 meters in diameter). We used the same scale to define bounding boxes in the object
337   detection task, following the COCO (Lin et al., 2014) format for annotations. The overlap in ground
338   truth bounding boxes for some of the wells in Figure 4 and Figure 9 reflects the clustering of multiple
339   wells in densely developed oil and gas sites, where the spatial overlap of wells and infrastructure
340   is common. (Note that this is a characteristic of the data, not a limitation of our quality control
341   strategy.) Additionally, we created multi-class segmentation maps, where each class represents a
342   different state of the well (active, suspended, or abandoned), and included this information in the
343   object detection annotations. (We do not perform multi-class segmentation experiments here, but it
344   is possible that future researchers may find this task useful.)

346   4     B ENCHMARK E XPERIMENTS
347
348
      We train benchmark deep learning models for binary segmentation and object detection tasks. Our
349   focus includes all oil and gas wells, regardless of their operational status, since they exhibit similar
350   footprints and consistent features, making them detectable in satellite imagery.
351
352
      For both tasks, all models were trained using RGB and Near-Infrared (NIR) channels of the multi-
      spectral satellite imagery. We augment images by randomly resizing images to 256×256, ensuring
353
      all bounding boxes remain intact for object detection. We then apply horizontal and vertical flipping
354
      with a probability of 0.25 each, followed by normalization using channel-wise mean and standard
355   deviation calculated from the training split of the dataset. The hyperparameters we use in these
356   various models represent standard performant settings and are not intended to represent the outcome
357   of hyperparameter optimization.

      4.1   B INARY S EGMENTATION
360
361   We selected well-known baseline models for binary segmentation, encompassing the deep CNN-
362   based approaches U-Net (Ronneberger et al., 2015), PAN(Li et al., 2018), and DeepLabV3+ (Chen
363   et al., 2018) as well as the Transformer-based architectures Segformer (Xie et al., 2021) and UperNet
364   (Xiao et al., 2018).
365
      U-Net (Ronneberger et al., 2015) was chosen for its widespread use as a baseline, offering an effec-
366   tive encoder-decoder architecture for multi-scale feature extraction. PAN (Li et al., 2018) improves
367   multi-scale context with pyramid pooling and attention mechanisms. DeepLabV3+(Chen et al.,
368   2018) was selected for its popularity in remote sensing tasks with its Atrous Convolution and ASPP
369   module for capturing contextual information at various scales. SegFormer (Xie et al., 2021) is a
370   transformer-based architecture designed for semantic segmentation, utilizing self-attention mecha-
371   nisms for capturing long-range dependencies. UperNet (Xiao et al., 2018) combines UNet (Ron-
372   neberger et al., 2015) and PSPNet (Zhao et al., 2016) architectures, featuring a UNet-like structure
373   for multi-scale feature fusion and PSPNet’s pyramid pooling module integrated with a Swin Trans-
374
      former (Liu et al., 2021) backbone for efficient multi-scale processing.
375   We train all CNN-based models using a ResNet50 (He et al., 2015) backbone, a batch size of 128,
376   and the BCELogits loss function. To fine-tune the model, a cosine annealing scheduler (Loshchilov
377   & Hutter, 2016) is used, which adjusts the learning rate smoothly in a cyclical manner by gradually
      decreasing it. To evaluate the impact of backbones with larger receptive fields and attention mech-


                                                         7
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


378
      anisms, we also experimented with additional backbones with U-Net. This included ResNeXt50
379   (Xie et al., 2016), which enhances feature learning through grouped convolutions; SE-ResNet50
380   (Hu et al., 2017), which introduces channel-wise attention with Squeeze-and-Excitation blocks; and
381   EfficientNetB6 (Tan & Le, 2019), known for its balanced scaling. For transformer-based models,
382   while both Segformer and UperNet use a Dice loss function and a polynomial learning rate sched-
383   uler, Segformer utilizes a mit-b0-ade (Xie et al., 2021) backbone with a batch size of 128, and
384   UperNet employs a Swin Small Transformer with a batch size of 64. All models are optimized
385   using AdamW (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2017) for 50 epochs.
386   We evaluate the binary segmentation task with respect to IoU, Precision, Recall, and F1-Score. High
387   Precision corresponds to reducing false positives, while high Recall corresponds to reducing false
388   negatives. IoU measures the overlap between predicted and ground truth masks, offering further
389   insight into segmentation accuracy. F1-Score, the harmonic mean of precision and recall, provides
390   a balanced measure considering both false positives and false negatives.

393   4.2   O BJECT D ETECTION
394
395   For binary object detection, we consider both single-stage, i.e., RetinaNet (Lin et al., 2017), FCOS
396
      and SSD, and two-stage CNN-based architectures, i.e. Faster R-CNN (Ren et al., 2015).
397   RetinaNet (Lin et al., 2017) is a one-stage architecture trained using focal loss, which helps to ad-
398   dress class imbalance. It uses a Feature Pyramid Network (FPN) for multi-scale feature extraction
399   and efficient object detection across different scales. Faster R-CNN (Ren et al., 2015) is a two-stage
400   model recognized for its high accuracy. It employs a Region Proposal Network (RPN) for generat-
401   ing region proposals and a separate network for predicting class labels and refining bounding box
402
      coordinates. FCOS (Fully Convolutional One-Stage Object Detection) (Tian et al., 2019) directly
      predicts object locations and categories from feature maps, which is effective for small object detec-
403
      tion. SSD (Single Shot MultiBox Detector) (Liu et al., 2015) uses multiple feature maps at different
404
      scales, enhancing its accuracy for small objects.
405
406   All object detection models are trained using a ResNet50 backbone, except for SSD Lite, which
407   is trained with a MobileNet backbone. The batch size is set to 256 for Faster R-CNN and FCOS
408
      and 512 for RetinaNet and SSD Lite. We used a cosine annealing scheduler (Loshchilov & Hutter,
      2016) and trained all models for 120 epochs. All models are optimized using the AdamW optimizer
409
      (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2017).
410
411   For binary object detection model evaluation, we calculate Intersection over Union (IoU) at various
412   thresholds (e.g., IoU0.1 , IoU0.3 , IoU0.5 ), which measures how well predicted bounding boxes align
413   with ground truth. IoU is computed by dividing the area of overlap by the area of their union, with
414
      higher values indicating better alignment. IoU thresholds define the minimum overlap required for
      a predicted box to match a ground truth box. (For example, an IoU0.5 threshold means a predicted
415
      box must have at least 50% overlap with a ground truth box to be considered a correct detection.)
416
417   We also assess Mean Average Precision (mAP), including mAP50 and mAP50:95 , measuring the
418   model’s precision-recall trade-off and detection accuracy at various IoU thresholds. mAP50 mea-
419   sures precision at an IoU threshold of 0.5, while mAP50:95 averages precision across IoU thresholds
420
      from 0.5 to 0.95. Higher mAP scores reflect better detection accuracy and precision.While higher
      IoU values indicate better accuracy for individual predictions, mAP provides a broader measure of
421
      detection performance by capturing precision across different IoU criteria.

490   5   L IMITATIONS
491
492   We do not envision any significant negative uses of our work. Localization of wells is primarily
493   of interest to the climate change mitigation community and is not, for example, a primary means
494   whereby fossil fuel companies select new locations for drilling. Therefore, we do not believe this
495   dataset is susceptible to dual use risks.
496   One potential limitation of our work is that we rely on well locations listed by the Alberta Energy
497   Regulator. It is likely that some true well locations are missing in this data, leading to the potential
498   for false negatives in the ground-truth data for this problem. However, it is to be expected that this
499   will not significantly affect the training of algorithms since these labels represent a small fraction
500   of the negative locations in the dataset, and deep learning algorithms are known to be robust to
501   moderate amounts of label noise (see e.g. (Rolnick et al., 2017)). Instead the effect may simply be
502
      that the reported test accuracy is actually lower than the true value (due to certain correctly predicted
      well locations being evaluated as false). We hope to investigate such effects further in future work.
503
504   Our dataset is focused on Alberta, because (1) it is a very large region with a significant amount of
505   high-quality labeled data available, (2) it is one of the world’s most important locations for oil and
506   gas production, so identifying wells in Alberta is of immediate impact. Future works may wish to
507   assess the capacity for few- or zero-shot transfer learning from Alberta to other regions with a high
508
      expected concentration of abandoned wells, including the Appalachian and Mountain West regions
      of the United States, as well as a number of former Soviet states.
509
510
511   6   C ONCLUSION
512
513   In this paper, we present the first large-scale benchmark dataset aimed at identifying oil and gas
514   wells, with a focus on abandoned and suspended wells, which are a significant source of greenhouse
515   gases and other pollutants. We combine high-resolution imagery, an extensive database of well loca-
516   tions, and expert verification to create the Alberta Wells Dataset. We frame well identification both
517   in terms of object detection and binary segmentation and evaluate the performance of a wide range
518   of popular deep learning methods on these tasks. We find that the UNet model (with a Efficient-
519
      NetB6 backbone), in particular, represents the most promising baseline for the binary segmentation
      task, while for object detection, all models demonstrate more mixed results, with Single Stage Mod-
520
      els (such as FCOS and SSD) providing a relatively promising baseline. These results show that the
521
      Alberta Wells Dataset represents both a challenging as well as a societally impactful set of tasks.
522
523   The value added by the dataset is twofold. First, most global fossil fuel-producing regions do not
524   have databases of well locations comparable to that provided by AER. Alberta’s varied landscape
525
      provides an ideal setting for developing algorithms for the detection of wells, which can then be
      used directly in other locations or fine-tuned. Second, even in Alberta, the list developed by AER
526
      is not comprehensive, and many abandoned wells are believed to be missing. Our work can provide
527
      candidate locations for domain experts to examine so as to determine the true number and locations
528   of abandoned wells in Alberta.
529
530   We hope that our work may be of use to policymakers and other stakeholders involved in climate
531
      action and environmental protection according to the following envisioned steps:
532
             • Use the Alberta Wells Dataset to train algorithms for pinpointing well locations.
533
534          • Run these algorithms at scale across a broader region of interest, comparing against any
535            existing databases to identify those wells that may be undocumented.
536          • Flag abandoned wells for plugging, prioritizing those identified as super-emitters.
537
538   We believe that the scalability of machine learning tools for remote sensing will make them an
539   invaluable tool in pinpointing and mitigating the global environmental impact of abandoned oil and
      gas wells.

      The results in Table 9 emphasize the necessity of including all well types (active, suspended,

829

834   groundwater contamination. Precision for active-only training (0.998) is higher than model trained
835   on all well types (0.913), but its inability to detect all wells in a image (due to low recall) lim-
836   its its applicability. Therefore, incorporating all well types in training ensures balanced, reliable
837   performance, allowing for accurate detection and classification in diverse and realistic contexts.

896   D     DATASET I NFORMATION
897
898   The purpose of this dataset is to assist in training deep learning systems to identify oil and gas
899   wells, including abandoned, suspended, and active ones. This will enable the detection of wells in
900   a specific area, allowing comparison with government records. If discrepancies are found, experts
901   can conduct further investigations, which can possibly lead to the discovery of an abandoned or
902   suspended device that might not be present in government records.
903
904   D.1   DATASET S TRUCTURE
905
906   We provide training, validation, and testing sets, split using our proposed algorithm (as described in
907   Section 3.2 of the main paper) to create a well-distributed dataset.
908
      The proposed method aims to create smaller regions of well concentration by clustering the centroids
909
      of patches. These regions are designed to be (a) mutually non-intersecting, (b) part of a larger
910
      geographic region by clustering the centroids of the initial clusters, and (c) containing a similar
911   distribution of non-well patches within the same region.
912
913   This approach ensures that the training, validation, and test sets include representations from all
914
      geographic regions, providing a diverse and comprehensive evaluation. Thus, the dataset represents
      various geographical regions and offers a diverse benchmark for evaluation and testing.
915
916   Each dataset split is saved in an HDF5 format file, structured as described in the following sections,
917   and then compressed into a .tar.gz file for faster transfer. Details on the number of samples in each
      set and the size of the dataset, both original and compressed, are presented in Table 3.

1218   F    C HALLENGES FOR ML C OMMUNITY
1219
1220   The Alberta Wells Dataset presents several intriguing challenges for machine learning. Key issues
1221   include an imbalanced data distribution, with fewer instances of areas with multiple wells compared
1222
       to those with single or two wells, and the visual similarity among active, suspended, and abandoned
       wells, which can confuse standard models. Additionally, varying spatial relationships in the im-
1223
       agery due to varying geography create difficulties for off-the-shelf models. Noise in annotations,
1224
       even after data quality control and cleaning—such as misclassified wells—further complicates the
1225   task. Despite these challenges, the dataset’s large scale and geographical diversity, covering over
1226   213,000 wells, offer significant opportunities for developing robust and generalizable ML models
1227   for monitoring oil and gas infrastructure.
