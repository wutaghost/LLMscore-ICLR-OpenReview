009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Modern wearable devices can conveniently and continuously record various biosig-
012            nals in the many different environments of daily living, ultimately enabling a rich
013            view of individual health. However, not all biosignals are the same: high-fidelity
014            measurements, such as photoplethysmography (PPG), contain more physiological
015            information, but require optical sensors with a high power footprint. In a resource-
016            constrained setting, such high-fidelity biosignals may be unavailable. Alternatively,
017            a lower-fidelity biosignal, such as those from an accelerometer, has a significantly
018
               smaller power footprint and is available in almost any wearable device. While
               multi-modal modeling and cross-modal reconstruction of biosignals have been done
019
               before, here, we demonstrate that we can distill representational knowledge across
020
               biosignals with different levels of fidelity, i.e., from PPG to accelerometer, using 20
021            million minutes of unlabeled data collected from ∼172K participants in the Apple
022            Heart and Movement Study under informed consent. Our knowledge distillation
023            framework does not require labels; we pre-train PPG encoders via self-supervised
024            learning, and then distill the representational knowledge from the PPG encoders
025            to accelerometer encoders. We first demonstrate strong cross-modal alignment
026            on unseen data, e.g., 99.2% top-1 accuracy for retrieving PPG embeddings from
027            accelerometer embeddings. We show that distilled accelerometer encoders have
028            significantly more informative representations compared to self-supervised or su-
029
               pervised encoders trained on accelerometer data for downstream targets, observed
               by at least 23%-49% improved performance for predicting heart rate and heart rate
030
               variability, and are readily predictive of a wide array of downstream targets in-
031
               cluding demographic variables, health conditions, use of medications, and lifestyle
032            habits. We also demonstrate that our framework can be applied to different encoder
033            architectures with different pre-training strategies of the strong encoder, and can
034            be used to simultaneously do cross-modality distillation and model compression.
035            Additionally, we perform various ablations for augmentations, hyperparameters and
036            multi-modal training. We believe our proposed representational knowledge distilla-
037            tion framework may unlock new opportunities for developing digital biomarkers
038            from any wearable device with lower-fidelity biosignals, and help individuals track
039            their health more frequently and conveniently.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
043
044   The recent growth of wearable devices has empowered individuals to track their health more con-
045
      veniently and frequently. Wearable devices can record various biosignals and provide individuals
      with a continuous view of their health metrics in daily environments, a view which is hard to achieve
046
      in clinical settings. While this unlocks new exciting opportunities for wearable devices, challenges
047
      remain. High-fidelity biosignals that have substantial physiological information content tend to
048   require specific hardware and sensors for recording, therefore making them unavailable in some
049   wearable devices. Even when available, they may be difficult to collect frequently due to power
050   constraints of small wearable devices, or they may require active user engagement. An example of
051   such is photoplethysmography (PPG), which measures volumetric changes in arterial blood flow
052   and contains diverse physiological information but requires power-consuming optical sensors for
053   recording. On the other hand, lower-fidelity biosignals, such as those from an accelerometer, while
      capable of detecting certain minute physiological movements (Inan et al., 2015; Kim et al., 2016), are


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      known to be more susceptible to motion artifacts and thus more challenging to extract physiological
055   information from, compared to PPG. However, they are available on almost all wearable devices
056   and have a significantly smaller power footprint. Independently, another challenge for developing
057   digital biomarkers for wearable devices is the lack of large labeled medical datasets because they are
058   much more expensive and time-consuming to collect unlike in other domains. Given these challenges,
059   the question that remains is whether we can train powerful deep neural networks for lower-fidelity
060   biosignals, such as the accelerometer, without requiring labels and by leveraging information in
061   high-fidelity biosignals, such as PPG. In this work, we aim to address this question.
062   Unsupervised representation learning techniques, such as self-supervised learning, have been proven
063   successful in training deep encoders without requiring any explicit labels during training in various
064   domains of deep learning such as natural language processing (Devlin et al., 2019; OpenAI, 2023),
065   computer vision (Chen et al., 2020; 2021; Oquab et al., 2023), and speech recognition (Baevski
066   et al., 2020; 2022). In addition, there has been several works for training encoders for health and
067   wellness applications using biosignals (Cheng et al., 2020; Kostas et al., 2021; Sarkar & Etemad,
068   2022; Mohsenvand et al., 2020; Gopal et al., 2021; Kiyasseh et al., 2021; Mehari & Strodthoff,
069
      2022; Wu et al., 2020; Spathis et al., 2021; Tang et al., 2021; Yuan et al., 2023; Lai et al., 2023;
      Abbaspourazad et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024). Independent to representation learning, knowledge
070
      distillation has been extensively used for transferring knowledge from a neural network (teacher) to
071
      another neural network (student) (Hinton et al., 2015), which can also unlock cross-modal distillation
072   (Gupta et al., 2015; Aytar et al., 2016; Tian et al., 2022). While knowledge distillation has been
073   traditionally often used in supervised settings (Hinton et al., 2015; Gupta et al., 2015; Aytar et al.,
074   2016; Tian et al., 2022), there is a growing interest in combining it with self-supervised learning via
075   self-distillation (Xie et al., 2020; Fang et al., 2021; Caron et al., 2021) or distillation from multiple
076   foundation models into one to improve and agglomerate their representations (Wei et al., 2022; Wang
077   et al., 2024; Ranzinger et al., 2024). See Section 2 for how our work relates to the existing work.
078   In this paper, using unlabeled sensor data collected under informed consent from the large longitudinal
079   Apple Heart and Movement Study (AHMS) (MacRae, 2021; Truslow et al., 2024), we demonstrate
080   cross-modal representational knowledge distillation to train accelerometer encoders distilled from
081   PPG teacher encoders. Our contributions are: 1) Representational knowledge distillation across
082   wearable biosignals in a large-scale dataset: While multi-modal modeling and cross-modal recon-
083   struction of biosignals have been done before, we study representational knowledge distillation across
084   wearable biosignals using a dataset at such large scale with 20M minutes of multi-modal sensor
085   data from ∼172K participants. 2) Fully unsupervised representational knowledge distillation
086
      framework: We combine and adopt techniques inspired by uni-modal and multi-modal pre-training
      frameworks from other domains of deep learning to create a fully unsupervised distillation framework
087
      for biosignal time-series, which is particularly crucial for health applications where labeled data is
088
      limited. 3) Studying representational alignment: We study representational alignment of PPG and
089   accelerometer encoders, by doing retrieval analysis. 4) Studying representational information:
090   We study the representational power of PPG and accelerometer encoders for critical health targets
091   such as heart rate and heart rate variability, across different available labeled data regimes, as well
092   as for demographic variables and 46 health targets including health conditions, use of medications
093   and lifestyle habits from AHMS survey questions. In addition to these contributions, to support
094   the robustness of the findings, we perform ablation studies to evaluate the efficacy of the encoder
095   architecture (Transformer and EfficientNet), the pre-training strategy of the PPG teacher model
096   (masked autoencoding and contrastive learning), the augmentations, and other training choices.

098   2    R ELATED WORK
099
100
      Uni-modal and multi-modal representation learning: Unsupervised representation learning tech-
101
      niques, e.g., self-supervised learning, have been proven successful in training generalist models,
102   also known as foundation models, without requiring any explicit labels during training in various
103   domains of deep learning such as natural language processing (Devlin et al., 2019; OpenAI, 2023),
104   computer vision (Chen et al., 2020; 2021; He et al., 2021; Oquab et al., 2023), speech recognition
105   (Baevski et al., 2020; 2022), and health (Cheng et al., 2020; Kostas et al., 2021; Sarkar & Etemad,
106   2022; Mohsenvand et al., 2020; Gopal et al., 2021; Kiyasseh et al., 2021; Mehari & Strodthoff,
107   2022; Wu et al., 2020; Spathis et al., 2021; Tang et al., 2021; Yuan et al., 2023; Lai et al., 2023;
      Abbaspourazad et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024). While most of these works have been primarily on


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      training uni-modal foundation models, there has been a recent shift in training multi-modal foundation
109   models to allow for leveraging information from multiple modalities, either to train a model that
110   simultaneously processes multiple modalities (Mizrahi et al., 2023; Meta, 2024), or to train and bind
111   multiple modality-specific foundation models (Radford et al., 2021; Girdhar et al., 2023; Thapa et al.,
112   2024), particularly with a contrastive objective. Similarly for health applications, there has been a
113   growing interest in cross-modal reconstruction of biosignals (Sarkar & Etemad, 2020), or modeling
114   and pre-training multiple biosignal modalities simultaneously (Deldari et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2024;
115   Deldari et al., 2024; Thapa et al., 2024).
116   Knowledge distillation: Knowledge distillation has been extensively used for transferring knowledge
117   from a neural network (teacher) to another neural network (student) in other domains (Hinton et al.,
118   2015; Tian et al., 2022), traditionally often in supervised settings to transfer knowledge from a large
119   neural network to a small neural network (Hinton et al., 2015; Tian et al., 2022) or from a high-fidelity
120   modality to a low-fidelity modality (Gupta et al., 2015; Aytar et al., 2016; Tian et al., 2022), or from
121   an ensemble network into a single one (Tian et al., 2022), by using the teacher’s output logits as
122   soft labels of the student model. Alternatively, knowledge distillation can also be performed using
123
      intermediate representations. With the recent emergence of foundation models, there has been several
      works for combining self-supervised learning and knowledge distillation via self-distillation (Xie
124
      et al., 2020; Fang et al., 2021; Caron et al., 2021), or distilling one or several existing foundation
125
      models to a single foundation model to improve and agglomerate their representations (Wei et al.,
126   2022; Wang et al., 2024; Ranzinger et al., 2024).
127
128   In this work, we adopt ideas from different related work in uni-modal and multi-modal representation
129
      learning and knowledge distillation, for biosignal time-series. Similar to uni-modal foundation models,
      we train our PPG teacher encoder using masked autoencoding (He et al., 2021) and contrastive learning
130
      (Chen et al., 2020), with our own variation of these frameworks. We then transfer knowledge from
131
      our PPG teacher encoders to accelerometer student encoders via a cross-modal knowledge distillation
132   framework (Tian et al., 2022) that bears similarity to multi-modal contrastive learning (Radford et al.,
133   2021; Girdhar et al., 2023) and self-distillation for model compression (Fang et al., 2021). There
134   has been prior work on training uni-modal foundation models for PPG and accelerometer (Spathis
135   et al., 2021; Yuan et al., 2023; Abbaspourazad et al., 2024) or multi-modal foundation models for
136   other biosignals (Deldari et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2024; Deldari et al., 2024; Thapa et al., 2024). These
137   multi-modal biosignal foundation models either take multiple modalities as input (Liu et al., 2024;
138   Deldari et al., 2024); or they “bind” multi-modality embeddings into a shared subspace (Deldari et al.,
139   2022; Thapa et al., 2024) similar to (Radford et al., 2021) from randomly-initialized encoders, which
140
      is primarily different for our motivation of cross-modal representational knowledge distillation and
      model compression from an existing high-fidelity teacher encoder. In fact, we show that training
141
      both encoders of high-fidelity and low-fidelity modalities together degrades the quality of low-fidelity
142
      embeddings (see Section 5.5). In line with our motivation, there has been prior work on leveraging
143   asymmetric information in biosignals, by cross-modal reconstruction of electrocardiogram (ECG)
144   from PPG, for a more accurate estimation of heart rate (Sarkar & Etemad, 2020). Also, our work
145   focuses on accelerometer during low-motion periods where it captures minute cardiovascular signals
146   such as the ballistocardiogram, which is distinct from modeling slower changes in accelerometer
147   during gross motion for health and fitness (Hallgrı́msson et al., 2018; Ni et al., 2019; Spathis et al.,
148   2021). All in all, while multi-modal modeling and cross-modal reconstruction of biosignals have been
149   done before, our work studies unsupervised representational knowledge distillation across wearable
150   biosignals using large-scale data collected from wearable consumer devices, to develop a single
151
      encoder for accelerometer that is readily predictive of a wide array of downstream health targets.

191   framework is agnostic to the pre-training of the PPG teacher encoder and study its efficacy, we
192   investigated two popular pre-training strategies: masked autoencoding (MAE) as the main framework
193   and contrastive learning (CL) as an ablation, which we explain in detail below.
194   Masked autoencoding: Our masked autoencoding pre-training framework is adopted from the prior
195   work on images (He et al., 2021), for time-series. We turn the multi-channel PPG input (4-channels
196   and 60s-long, as explained in Section 4.1) into patches using non-overlapping fixed-length windows,
197   and then project the patches with a learnable linear tokenizer into tokens, which results in 192 256-D
198   tokens. Sinusoidal positional embeddings are added to the input tokens, then 80% of input tokens
199   are randomly dropped and the 38 kept tokens are passed through the encoder Transformer to get
200   encoder output tokens. Learnable mask tokens, initialized with a 256-D token drawn from a uniform
201
      distribution ∼ U (0, 1), are added back to the encoder output tokens at positions where input tokens
      where dropped, followed by adding sinusoidal positional embeddings. These 192 new tokens are
202
      then processed by the decoder Transformer to generate the decoder output tokens. Finally, with a
203
      linear projection, the decoder output tokens are projected to the multi-channel PPG output, with the
204   objective of reconstructing PPG “pixel” values in those indices whose patches/tokens were dropped
205   in the encoder’s input. For maximum learning rate, we used 2e-4 and batch size was set to 512.
206   A complete list of other architectural and training hyperparameters that are shared across methods
207   using Transformers can be found in Appendix Table 4. As a baseline for our distilled accelerometer
208   encoders, we also train masked autoencoders for accelerometer in the same way explained above.
209
      Contrastive learning: While our main pre-training framework for the teacher encoder is masked
210   autoencoding, we perform ablation in regards to the teacher pre-training method and architecture via
211   contrastive learning with EfficientNets. We would like to emphasize that contrastive learning can also
212   be done with Transformer models without any meaningful difference (Appendix Table 13), but we
213   choose EfficientNets for the main results to simultaneously demonstrate an ablation on changing the
214   teacher pre-training strategy and student/teacher architecture. Our contrastive pre-training framework
215   closely follows a prior work for PPG signals (Abbaspourazad et al., 2024), with the major difference
      that the positive pairs are selected as two augmented views of the same sample. This choice is made to

216
      enforce the encoders to contain more segment-level information necessary for the main downstream
217   targets used in this study, as opposed to participant-level positive pair selection which was investigated
218   in the prior work (Appendix Table 14). Our stochastic augmentation module consists of a stochastic
219   cascade of several standard individual augmentations (Iwana & Uchida, 2021) including {cut out: 0.4,
220   magnitude warp: 0.25, add Gaussian noise: 0.25, channel permute: 0.25, time warp: 0.15}, where
221   the values are the assigned probabilities of whether the augmentation is applied for each segment
222   or not. During pre-training, we augment each sample twice; the two augmented views are then
223   passed through a joint-embedding architecture, where one encoder is an exponential moving average
224   (m = 0.99 for momentum updates) of the other encoder that is updated via backpropagation. The
225
      256-D embeddings of each encoder are then projected to a lower dimensional subspace (128-D) via
      multi-layer perceptron projection heads. The training objective is maximizing the mutual information
226
      of the down-projected embeddings of the two views of the same PPG segment, and minimizing that for
227
      different PPG segments, implemented by a regularized InfoNCE loss. We use Kozachenko-Leonenko
228   (KoLeo) differential entropy estimator (Sablayrolles et al., 2019; Oquab et al., 2023) with the weight
229   of 0.1 for regularization, and temperature of 0.04 for scaling similarity scores in the InfoNCE loss.
230   For the maximum learning rate, we used 1e-3, while batch size was set to 256. Other common
231   hyperparameters for training EfficientNets is available in Appendix Table 5. As a baseline for our
232   distilled accelerometer encoders, we also train contrastive learned encoders for accelerometer in the
233   same way explained above.

      After pre-training the PPG encoder in Section 3.1, we distill its representational knowledge to an
      accelerometer encoder in a dataset of paired PPG-accelerometer segments (Section 4.1). This second
238
      stage is also fully unsupervised without requiring any explicit labels. We perform representational
239
      knowledge distillation via multi-modal contrastive learning similar to a technique used previously
240   to weakly supervise an image encoder with text (CLIP) (Radford et al., 2021), but here we use
241   it to transfer knowledge. To do this, unlike standard approaches (Radford et al., 2021), we first
242   perform augmentations on both modalities, PPG and accelerometer, using our stochastic augmentation
243   (see Section 3.1), where the augmentations are independently drawn for each modality in a PPG-
244   accelerometer pair. We found that augmentations were crucial for the quality of the embeddings (see
245   Ablation 5.5). The augmented PPG and accelerometer signals are then processed by the PPG teacher
246   encoder (frozen) and accelerometer student encoder, respectively, to get 256-D output embeddings.
247   To calculate the objective, we first down-project the embeddings to 128-D with trainable multi-layer
248

249
      collapse. The student encoder is trained to generate embeddings similar to the teacher encoder, where
250

281   4.1   DATASETS
282
283   We used the PPG and accelerometer signals recorded on Apple Watch from participants in the Apple
284   Heart and Movement Study (AHMS) (MacRae, 2021; Truslow et al., 2024). AHMS is an ongoing
285   research study designed to explore the links between physical activity and cardiovascular health,
286   which is sponsored by Apple and conducted in partnership with the American Heart Association and
287   Brigham and Women’s Hospital. To be eligible for the Study, participants must be of legal age to
288
      provide informed consent (18, 19, or 21 based on location), reside in the United States, have access
      to an iPhone with the Research app, have an Apple Watch, and provide informed consent within the
289
      Research app to participate (Shapiro et al., 2023).
290
291   We created a paired PPG-accelerometer pre-training dataset that we used for pre-training uni-modal
292   and distilled models. Apple Watch intermittently and passively records simultaneous green PPG and
293   accelerometer signals during low-motion periods multiple times per day. PPG and accelerometer
294
      signals are recorded simultaneously at a 256Hz or 64Hz sampling rate and are 60 seconds in duration.
      PPG signals consist of four optical channels corresponding to different combinations of transmitting
295
      and receiving diodes, and accelerometer signals consist of 3 channels corresponding to 3 spatial
296
      dimensions (see Figure 1 for signal examples). We curated a pre-training dataset of paired PPG-
297   accelerometer segments from ∼172K participants, where ∼20M paired segments were randomly
298   drawn from the full dataset given two conditions: 1) each participant had at least four segments in
299   the pre-training dataset, and 2) the number of segments per participant was as uniform as possible.
300   PPG segments were pre-processed using dark subtraction (to reject signals introduced by ambient
301   light). Both PPG and accelerometer segments were further pre-processed by bandpass filtering,
302   down-sampling to 64Hz if needed, and temporal channel-wise z-scoring for each segment. For PPG
303   teacher pre-training, we use only the PPG segments of the same dataset. Brief statistics of our curated
304   dataset are in Table 6, and AHMS demographics can be found in prior publications (Shapiro et al.,
305
      2023; Abbaspourazad et al., 2024; Truslow et al., 2024). The training and evaluation splits were
      stratified based on participants such that there were no overlapping participants in these two splits.

308   4.2   E VALUATION METRICS
309
      PPG-accelerometer pair embeddings retrieval: To assess the quality of the PPG-distilled ac-
310
      celerometer embeddings (256-D representations after the encoder), we perform a retrieval experiment
311
      to see how well the accelerometer embeddings can retrieve their corresponding matched PPG seg-
312   ment on unseen test data. For a batch of paired PPG and accelerometer segments on held-out test
313   participants, we compute the cosine similarity between each distilled accelerometer embedding and
314   PPG embedding, which generates a ranked list that can be used for retrieval. We evaluate retrieval
315   quality using top-K accuracy for K = 1, 3, 5 (i.e., for a given query accelerometer embedding, how
316   often is its paired PPG embedding in the top K most similar embeddings). We also report the mean
317   rank, i.e., the mean position of the true paired PPG segment in the rankings; smaller values are better,
318   with 1 indicating perfect retrieval as the correct segment is always ranked first. As an ablation, we
319   repeated the same retrieval analysis from PPG to accelerometer embeddings.

321   predicting heart rate (HR), and two popular measures of heart rate variability: standard deviation
322   of normal-to-normal intervals (SDNN) and root mean square of successive differences (RMSSD)
323   (Natarajan et al., 2020; Shaffer & Ginsberg, 2017). In addition, we perform linear probing for
      predicting self-reported age, body mass index (BMI), biological sex, and 46 health targets including

      5.5   A DDITIONAL ABLATION STUDIES
467
      Unless otherwise specified, we performed the following ablation studies with “Accel-KD via PPG-
468   MAE” as it is our main method (Figure 1), without loss of generalization given similar qualitative
469   conclusions to “Accel-KD via PPG-CL” (Section 5.2 and Appendix Table 13). We also performed
470   several other ablations presented in Appendix A.3.
471
      Augmentations: During our cross-modal knowledge distillation, both pairs of signals, PPG and
472
      accelerometer, are augmented with our stochastic cascade augmentation module (Section 3.2). While
473
      prior works have investigated augmentations for uni-modal contrastive learning of biosignals, the
474   effect of augmentations for multi-modal knowledge distillation of biosignals remains unknown.
475   Therefore, we investigated the importance of augmentations and observed that they were critical for
476   our cross-modal knowledge distillation as shown in Appendix Table 17. For instance, we observed
477   45% higher mean absolute error for heart rate prediction when the augmentations were absent during
478   the distillation stage. In addition, we investigated the importance of individual augmentation functions
479   during knowledge distillation (Appendix A.3 and Appendix Table 17).

481   significantly reduced performance: As discussed in Section 2, there are prior works for multi-
482   modal pre-training of biosignals where they bind different modality embeddings via multi-modal
483   contrastive learning (Deldari et al., 2022; Thapa et al., 2024), which does not involve pre-training
484   a uni-modal teacher encoder (stage 1 in Figure 1). While, our motivation here is different for we
485   use multi-modal contrastive learning to enable unsupervised representational knowledge distillation,
      we questioned whether we get the same improvement for the accelerometer encoders when binding

511   6    D ISCUSSION , LIMITATIONS AND FUTURE WORK
512
513   Here, we introduced a fully unsupervised representational knowledge distillation framework to
514   transfer knowledge from high-fidelity to low-fidelity biosignals. We showed that our knowledge
515   distillation framework is not unique to the teacher/student architecture, or teacher pre-training method,
516   and it results in near-perfect alignment of PPG and accelerometer embeddings as quantified by our
517   retrieval analysis. In addition, it improves the quality of the representations in the low-fidelity
518   biosignal compared to self-supervised and supervised baselines, while maintaining label efficiency
519   for downstream targets. We demonstrated that a single accelerometer encoder is predictive of heart
520
      rate, heart rate variability, demographic variables and a wide array of downstream health targets. We
      also showed that it can be used to simultaneously compress the student model while transferring
521
      knowledge across modalities. We also performed ablations with respect to several training choices.
522
      Our work primarily focused on accelerometer signals during low-motion and sedentary periods
523   (Section 4.1), where accelerometer captures ballistocardiogram and therefore minute cardiovascular-
524   related information (Inan et al., 2015; Kim et al., 2016). Future work can investigate models that
525   take slower-scale activity metrics on wearable devices such as steps, speed, sleep, and slow changes
526   in accelerometer, as well as minute changes in accelerometer at sedentary settings to improve the
527   performance for downstream targets (Hallgrı́msson et al., 2018; Ni et al., 2019; Spathis et al., 2021).
528   In addition, another interesting area of investigation for future work could be experimenting with
529   modality specific augmentations (Qian et al., 2022; Demirel & Holz, 2023). One caveat of our work
530   is that it currently supports two modalities, while future work can consider statistical objectives
531
      for mutual information maximization, when there are more than one teacher or student modalities
      (Shidani et al., 2024), or by binding all student modalities to a single teacher modality (Girdhar
532
      et al., 2023). Another caveat is that while this work can be used for knowledge transfer or retrieval
533
      of the high-fidelity modality embeddings, it does not provide a generative model across modalities
534   (Sarkar & Etemad, 2020); future work can consider recent techniques to incorporate generative
535   capabilities using unified encoder and decoder Transformers (Mizrahi et al., 2023; Meta, 2024).
536   Ideally, future work can also consider modeling other modalities such as text, images and videos to
537   leverage information from these other input sources and weakly supervise biosignal representations,
538   similar to prior work for modeling accelerometer during motion and other modalities such as video
539   and text (Moon et al., 2022; Tan et al., 2023).

      A.2    DATASET AND EVALUATIONS
876
877   Dataset statistics: Brief statistics for our curated PPG-accelerometer dataset from AHMS is available
878   in Table 6.
879   Linear probing for heart rate and heart rate variability: We perform linear probing for predicting
880   heart rate (HR), and two popular measures of heart rate variability: standard deviation of normal-to-
881   normal intervals (SDNN) and root mean square of successive differences (RMSSD). These targets
882   are from Apple Watch’s generated values during low-motion periods where PPG peaks are reliably
883   detected, resulting in accurate prediction of heart rate and heart rate variability. These targets are
884   chosen because they are widely used in wearable devices (Natarajan et al., 2020) and are indicative
885   of health status (Shaffer & Ginsberg, 2017), training load in athletes (Plews et al., 2013) and stress
886
      levels (Kim et al., 2018). Being able to predict them via low-fidelity biosignals will enable a more
      frequent prediction of such targets, giving the users a broader view of their health-related changes in
887
      different scenarios of their daily life. We formulate this problem as a regression task, where we use
888
      ridge regression to predict the continuous value of these targets and we use mean absolute error to
889   quantify performance. For heart rate, we report error in beats per minute (BPM), and for SDNN and
890   RMSSD, we report error in milliseconds (ms). For these targets given that they change from segment
891   to segment, we perform the linear probing at segment granularity: each segment contributes one and
892   only one sample in the downstream training/evaluation. However, the downstream training/evaluation
893   splits are stratified based on participants such that the evaluation split’s participants have no overlap
894   with those in the training split of the linear probing or pre-training.
895   Linear probing for age, BMI, biological sex, and health targets from AHMS survey questions:
896   We perform linear probing for predicting self-reported age, body mass index (BMI), biological sex
897   (sex assigned at birth), and health targets from survey questions for participants in AHMS. During
898   AHMS, participants fill out multiple questionnaires containing various questions regarding their
899   historical health record and demographics (Truslow et al., 2024; Abbaspourazad et al., 2024). The
900   response to these questions are usually in form of ‘yes’ or ‘no’ for whether the participant has had
901   a history of a health condition (e.g., asthma), or whether they take specific medications (e.g., anti-
902   depressants), or regarding their lifestyle habits (e.g., smoking). For the classification tasks, we use
903
      ridge regression to predict scores for binarized targets (0/1) and we quantify the performance with area
      under curve of receiver’s operating curve (AUC). For biological sex, we classify male versus female,
904
      and for health targets we classify ‘yes’ versus ‘no’. For regression tasks (age and BMI), we use ridge
905
      regression to predict continuous targets and we use mean absolute error to quantify performance.
906   Age is reported in years and BMI is reported in kg/m2 . In all these subject-related targets that do
907   not vary from segment to segment, we perform the linear probing at participant granularity: we
908   mean-aggregate all the embeddings associated to each participant so that each participant contributes
909   one and only one sample in the downstream training/evaluation. Similar to the heart rate and heart
910   rate variability linear probing, the downstream training/evaluation splits for these targets are stratified
911   based on participants such that the evaluation split’s participants have no overlap with those in the
912   training split of the linear probing or pre-training.
913   AHMS survey is formed of multiple questionnaires which participants fill out over the course of their
914   participation in the study. Tables 20 and 21 contain AHMS survey questions about medical conditions
915   and medications, respectively, in addition to the corresponding target labels used in Appendix Table
916   12. Table 22 includes AHMS survey questions about drinking and smoking habits, and Table 23
917   defines our logic to summarize these questions into binary labels for the related targets used in
      Appendix Table 12.

931   Ablation on choice of positive pairs in teacher pre-training with contrastive learning: We
932   selected the positive pairs as two augmented views of the same sample, as opposed to two different
933
      segments of the same participants proposed in (Abbaspourazad et al., 2024). This choice was made to
      enforce the encoders to contain more segment-level information necessary for the main downstream
934
      targets used in this study (heart rate and heart rate variability). Appendix Table 14 demonstrates the
935

936   pair selection strategies.
937
938   Ablation on number of PPG channels in teacher pre-training: We performed an ablation about
939
      the effect of the number of PPG channels in teacher pre-training on downstream evaluations in Table
      15, where we only kept one of the PPG channels for modeling and observed a drop in performance.
940
941   Ablation on larger model sizes for teacher pre-training: We made several optimizations to keep
942   our model sizes small for feasibility on running wearable devices with power and resource constraints.
943   Interestingly, we observed signs of overfitting as we increased our encoder size, which is why our
944   encoder sizes are not larger. This could be due to the fact that one needs to scale the model and data
945
      size simultaneously to gain benefits of scaling laws (Kaplan et al., 2020; Zhai et al., 2022). As an
      example, when we increased the encoder size in “PPG-MAE” (from 6.3M to 12.7M) by increasing
946
      the number of layers from 8 to 16, we observed initial signs of overfitting as shown in Table 16. We
947
      believe future work can investigate the scaling laws for encoder models of wearable biosignals by
948   growing the encoder and data size simultaneously (Kaplan et al., 2020; Zhai et al., 2022).
949
950   Ablation on augmentations: In addition to comparing knowledge distillation with and without aug-
951
      mentations (Section 5.5), we studied the importance of individual augmentations during knowledge
      distillation. To this end, we performed the knowledge distillation from PPG to accelerometer, where
952
      we only kept one of the augmentation functions during distillation (applied in every forward pass),
953
      one at a time. This was done while maintaining all other training choices the same to control for
954   the effect of augmentations. We observed that: 1) our stochastic augmentation module achieved the
955   highest accuracy (Appendix Table 17), likely because it captures more diverse range of distortions
956   during training, 2) among the individual augmentations, ”add Gaussian noise” and ”Cut out” had the
957   highest importance, while ”Time warp” had the least importance.
958
      In general, our hypothesis for why augmentations are important for knowledge distillation across
959
      PPG and accelerometer is that given the relationship between arterial blood flow present in PPG and
960   ballistocardiogram in accelerometer (Inan et al., 2015; Kim et al., 2016), particularly for aligned
961   PPG-accelerometer segments during low-motion periods which is the focus of our work, knowl-
962   edge distillation without augmentations is a relatively easier pre-training task compared to that with
963   augmentations. Therefore, we think distillation without augmentations, and even very simple augmen-
964   tations as shown by individual augmentations results in Appendix Table 17, leads to capturing less
965   minute information, which is relatively similar to why and how the amount and type of augmentations
966   in uni-modal contrastive learning is critical as demonstrated in prior work (Chen et al., 2020).
967   Ablation on impact of λ in the multi-modal contrastive objective λ: We studied the impact
968   of λ in Equation 2 for our representational knowledge distillation. We observed that while the
969   improvements of accelerometer embeddings via distillation were robust to λ, higher values of λ
970   (λ = {0.75, 1}) were the most optimal (Appendix Table 18), indicating that keeping PPG embeddings
971   as anchor embeddings provided the most knowledge transfer, perhaps due to the fact that PPG is the
      higher-fidelity modality.

996    A.4   D ISCUSSION
997
998    Discussion for why in uni-modal pre-training with contrastive learning was better for accelerom-
999    eter, and with masked autoencoding was better for PPG: Our hypothesis for this is that given that
1000   accelerometer is noisier than PPG (see Figure 1 for examples), reconstructing accelerometer in the
1001
       output space via MAE is a rather difficult pre-training method that bottlenecks the quality of extracted
       representations, as opposed to that for a signal such as PPG which is more structured and less noisier
1002
       to reconstruct. Therefore, we think MAE pre-training may be more suitable for less noisy biosignals,
1003
       and CL pre-training is more suitable for noisier biosignals. This, in fact, is a major motivation and
1004   difference for pre-training strategies that reconstruct in the representation space versus output space
1005   (Littwin et al., 2024). All in all, for both pre-training frameworks and Transformer/EfficientNet
1006   architectures, our representational knowledge distillation framework robustly distills the information
1007   from PPG to accelerometer, and improves the information in the accelerometer embeddings.
