011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            We give a complete characterization of optimization problems that can be solved
014            by gradient descent with a linear convergence rate. We show that the well-known
015            Polyak-Łojasiewicz inequality is necessary and sufficient for linear convergence
016
               with respect to function values to the minimum, while a property that we call
               “weak-quasi-strong-convexity”, or WQSC, is necessary and sufficient for linear
017
               convergence with respect to distances of the iterates to an optimum.
018

020   1   I NTRODUCTION AND BACKGROUND
021
022   Gradient descent is the most popular algorithm for optimizing a lower bounded and smooth function
023   f : Rn → R. It starts from an initial guess and it updates in the direction opposite of the gradient:
024                                              x̃ = x − η∇f (x),                                       (1)
025
      where η is the step size, also called the learning rate.
026
027   For many modern applications, gradient descent is overly simplistic. It forms, however, the basis for
028   many practical algorithms like stochastic, distributed or online gradient descent. While favourable
029   convergence guarantees for gradient descent have been long associated with convexity of the objec-
030
      tive function f , Polyak (1963) showed that a much more general property is sufficient to guarantee
      linear convergence with respect to the values of f to the minimum f ∗ . The usual result that can be
031
      found in the literate is for step size η = L1 . Below, we present a slightly more general result for any
032
      η ≤ L2 .

      As the condition of Lipschitz continuity of the gradient (L-smoothness) is standard and satisfied by
055   most problems in practice, the second condition
056
057                                        ∥∇f (x)∥2 ≥ 2µ(f (x) − f ∗ )
058   became known as Polyak–Łojasiewicz (PL) (as at the same time with Boris Polyak, Stanisław
059   Łojasiewicz studied more general versions of this condition). The PL condition with constant µ
060   is weaker than µ-strong-convexity and does not require the existence of a unique minimizer. It im-
061   plies however that all critical points of f must be global minima, thus also that all local minima are
062   global.
063   More recently, another generalization of the notion of strong convexity which relates to convergence
064   with respect to distances, has been studied (see Bu & Mesbahi (2020); Necoara et al. (2019) for
065   similar versions). We define it below:

      Contribution: Our main contribution is to go backwards in both the cases of PL and WQSC
156   conditions. That is, we show that PL is a necessary condition for linear convergence of GD for the
157   values of the objective function to the minimum (Theorem 5), while WQSC is necessary for linear
158   convergence of GD with respect to the distances of its iterates to the set of optima X ∗ (Theorem 9).
159   We believe that our results is a solid step towards understanding tractability in optimization. They
160   also imply many interesting corollaries, of which two are presented in the appendix. The reason that
161   we present such corollaries in the appendix is in order to keep the main text as simple and elementary
      as possible. The interested reader though is definitely recommended to take a look at them.

      2   R ELATED WORK
163
164
165   While the necessity of the aforementioned convexity-like properties with respect to the convergence
166
      guarantees of gradient descent is a natural question, there is not much in the literature dedicated
      to it. This is surprising to us, as characterizing solvable optimization problems can save future
167
      theoreticians and practitioners much unnecessary effort.
168
169   The popular paper by Karimi et al. (2016) shows (in Appendix A) that among all the properties
170   proposed in the literature that guarantee linear convergence for gradient descent, the PL condition is
171   the weakest. Such properties include error bounds Luo & Tseng (1993), essential strong convexity
172
      Liu et al. (2014), quadratic growth Anitescu (2000) and the, more general, Kurdyka-Łojasiewicz
      inequality Attouch et al. (2013); Bolte et al. (2010). Various relationships between these notions
173
      have been investigated, see for instance Karimi et al. (2016) (Appendix A), Bolte et al. (2017) and
174
      Rebjock & Boumal (2024). Our results close a chapter with respect to the search of new relaxations
175   of strong convexity, at least as far as the widespread notion of linear convergence is considered.
176   If one considers different convergence guarantees, there should be perhaps different convexity-like
177   properties associated with them.
178
      The paper closest to the result of Theorem 5 is Abbaszadehpeivasti et al. (2023) (Theorem 5), which
179
      shows essentially the same result, but instead of using the standard L-smoothness assumption, it uses
180
      a lower bounded curvature assumption (equation (3) in that paper). This lower bounded curvature
181   assumption makes the proof almost direct, but it also removes a lot of the generality of the result.
182   One can easily see a duality here: for showing a linear convergence rate starting from a PL inequality
183   one assumes an upper bound for the curvature of the function; for showing a PL inequality starting
184   from a linear convergence rate, ones assumes a lower bound for the curvature of the function. Our
185   Theorem 5 shows that for passing from a linear convergence rate to a PL condition, L-smoothness
186   is all that is needed in the case of gradient descent and unnatural assumptions like equation (3)
187   ofAbbaszadehpeivasti et al. (2023) are not required. In Section 3, we give in addition an example of
188   a function that is L-smooth, but does not have a lower bounded curvature. Even if a function does
189
      have lower bounded curvature, such a lower bound governs the quality of the derived PL condition
      in Theorem 5 of Abbaszadehpeivasti et al. (2023), that is, if such lower bound is poor, the derived PL
190
      inequality is also poor. The same happens with our Theorem 5, but with respect to L-smoothness. If
191
      L is too large, our derived PL inequality behaves poorly. However, this is to be expected, as a poor
192   smoothness constant would yield a poor convergence result in Proposition 1. Similarly, complete
193   lack of L-smoothness (L = ∞) would yield to a failure for our Theorem 5, but also for Proposition 1.
194
195
      The papers closest to the result of Theorem 9 is Alimisis (2024a) and Necoara et al. (2019). Theorem
      5 in Alimisis (2024a) shows essentially the same result with our Theorem 9 but assuming that the
196
      optimization problem has a unique optimum. It turns out that this assumption can be substituted by
197
      the more general one of the set of optima X ∗ being convex. An interesting class of functions with
198   potentially multiple optima, which indeed form a convex set, is quasi-convex functions. Theorem 5
199   of Necoara et al. (2019) shows that a convex optimization problem for which GD converges linearly
200   for distances of the iterates to an optimum must satisfy a quadratic growth condition.
201
      Necoara et al. (2019) also present a property they call “quasi-strong-convexity” (Definition 1), which
202
      is a slightly stronger version of our property (Definition 2). The latter turns out to be a valuable
203
      relaxation of the property presented in Necoara et al. (2019) (and in other works). A first hint on that
204   are the results of Alimisis & Vandereycken (2024b) on the geodesic “weak-strong-convexity” of the
205   symmetric eigenvalue problem. There, the authors show that the symmetric eigenvalue problem is
206   WQSC on the Grassmann manifold in the sense defined in Definition 2. A second hint is given by
207   Alimisis (2024a) and the current work: while both the property of Definition 2 and the quasi-strong-
208   convexity of Necoara et al. (2019) are sufficient to guarantee linear convergence of 1 with respect to
209   distances, only the first can be shown to be also necessary. The WQSC property defined in this work
210   is essentially a generalization of the notion appeared in Bu & Mesbahi (2020) and Alimisis (2024a)
211   to the case of an optimization problem with multiple optima that form a convex set.
212   It is interesting to note that WQSC can be used to analyze even Nesterov’s accelerated gradient
213   descent Bu & Mesbahi (2020); Alimisis et al. (2024c). Similarly, the PL condition can be used
214   to analyse many different algorithms (see for instance Radhakrishnan et al. (2020); Bassily et al.
215   (2018); Li & Li (2018)). Since in this paper we deal only with the case of gradient descent, a natural
      question is whether some of our results can be extended to other optimization algorithms.

441
442   We end with some possible directions for future work.
443
444          • What form do our results take if gradient descent is substituted by some other algorithm,
445            possibly gradient-related? For instance, what about stochastic gradient descent and conver-
446
               gence in expectation?
447          • What form do our results take if the linear convergence guarantees are substituted by some
448            other convergence guarantees? For instance, what would be the connection between weak-
449            quasi-convexity Guminov et al. (2023) and an algebraic convergence rate? We conjecture
450
               that the most promising way to phrase such results is through an assumption on the be-
               haviour of some energy functional which describes the optimization problem.
451
452          • What is the deeper intuitive meaning of PL and WQSC inequalities that makes them useful
453            for optimization?
454          • Can we find PL or WQSC models in deep learning? There is already some work on that
455            Soltanolkotabi et al. (2018) (Lemma 7.12), but general understanding is largely missing.
456
457   We believe that a thorough exploration of the fundamentals of optimization has a lot to offer in the
458   optimization practice. We hope that our work answers a few meaningful questions in this direction
459   and also creates enough stimulus for future work.
