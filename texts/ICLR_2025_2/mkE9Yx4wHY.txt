009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            In this work, we introduce a methodology for alignment designed to enhance
012            the ability of large language models (LLMs) to articulate their reasoning—self-
013            explanation—even in the absence of annotated rationale explanations. Our align-
014            ment methodology comprises three key components: explanation quality assess-
015            ment, self-preference dataset generation, and model alignment. Additionally, we
016            present a novel technique called Alignment with Anchor Preference Pairs, which
017
               improves the selection of preference pairs by categorizing model outputs into
               three groups: consistently correct, consistently incorrect, and variable. By ap-
018
               plying tailored strategies to each category, we enhance the effectiveness of Direct
019
               Preference Optimization (DPO). Our experimental results demonstrate that this
020            approach significantly improves explanation quality while maintaining accuracy
021            compared to other fine-tuning strategies.

024   1   I NTRODUCTION
025
026   Large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated remarkable capabilities across various tasks.
027   However, fine-tuning these models for specific applications often leads to a critical trade-off: im-
028   provements in one area may compromise the model’s generalization capabilities (Yang et al., 2024;
029   Kirk et al., 2024). In our study, we aim to enhance a secondary task—specifically, the model’s
030
      ability to articulate reasoning processes in natural language, a skill known as self-explanation (Mad-
      sen et al., 2024a)—in parallel with the primary task, despite the constraint of not having human-
031
      annotated rationales.
032
033   The lack of annotated data of both high- and low-quality explanations can be framed in the context
034   of model aligning without human preference data. Recent research has explored ways to align LLMs
035   without direct human input. Some approaches generate self-instruct data to fine-tune models (Wang
036
      et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023; Gulcehre et al., 2023), while others, like Bai et al. (2022); Yuan
      et al. (2024); Wu et al. (2024), use LLM-generated feedback to train reward models. Building on
037
      these advancements, we propose an end-to-end approach to align LLMs on classification tasks while
038
      also ensuring the generation of high-quality self-explanations, even without annotated data for this
039   secondary task. Our approach integrates three core components: evaluating generated explanations,
040   creating self-preference datasets, and aligning the model. Additionally, we introduce Alignment
041   with Anchor Preference Pairs, a method that improves preference pair selection by categorizing
042   model responses into three groups: consistently correct, consistently incorrect, and variable. For
043   each category, we apply tailored strategies to construct preference pairs, which are then used in the
044   Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) phase (Rafailov et al., 2023). Our results demonstrate that
045   this method consistently improves explanation quality, mitigating the degradation caused by SFT.
046   Moreover, we show that using anchor preference pairs outperforms self-alignment strategies that
047
      rely solely on judge-based evaluations for preference pair selection.
048   Our contributions are summarized as follows:
049
050     (i). We introduce a framework for the qualitative assessment of self-explanations, designed to
051          evaluate how effectively the model conveys its reasoning.
052    (ii). We analyze how supervised fine-tuning for classification tasks affects the quality of self-
053          explanations. Our findings demonstrate that while SFT improves classification accuracy, it
             often reduces explanation quality, underscoring the need for improved alignment strategies.


                                                        1

054
      (iii). We propose a novel method, Alignment with Anchor Preference Pairs, for constructing high-
055          quality preference pairs when building self-preference datasets. This method uses the model’s
056          behavior on each input prompt to apply specific strategies while creating preference pairs. Our
057          approach consistently outperforms other methods that rely solely on judge-based evaluations
058          for selecting preference pairs.
059
      (iv). We develop an end-to-end methodology for aligning LLMs to downstream classification tasks
060         while maintaining the quality of their self-explanations, even in the absence of explanation-
061         rich datasets.

066   2.1   Q UALITY C RITERIA FOR E FFECTIVE S ELF -E XPLANATIONS
067
      We focus on the model’s ability to provide a holistic explanation, which we define as one that excels
068
      across multiple criteria, collectively determining what constitutes a good explanation. This approach
069
      contrasts with previous work that emphasized specific trustworthiness metrics, such as faithfulness
070   (Madsen et al., 2024b;a; Lanham et al., 2023; Lyu et al., 2023; Turpin et al., 2023; Parcalabescu
071   & Frank, 2024) and truthfulness (Zhang et al., 2024; Sharma et al., 2023; Burns et al., 2022; Joshi
072   et al., 2024). A high-quality, holistic explanation may be unfaithful in the sense that it does not
073   accurately represent the model’s internal reasoning, or conversely, a faithful explanation that truly
074   reflects the model’s reasoning may suffer from a lack of clarity, reducing its quality. While aiming
075   for explanations that fulfill a holistic explanation framework might incidentally enhance faithfulness,
076   it is not a strict requirement.
077   We evaluate self-explanations based on the following criteria:
078
079         1. Logical coherence: The explanation should follow a clear and logical reasoning process,
080            with all components cohesively connected to form a unified, non-contradictory narrative.
081
            2. Clarity: The explanation must present ideas clearly and precisely, using appropriate termi-
082            nology to effectively communicate complex concepts without unnecessary complexity.
083
084
            3. Relevance: The explanation should comprehensively address the task at hand, directly
               answering the specific context or requirements without omitting critical information.
085
086         4. Depth of argumentation: The explanation must provide strong reasoning and credible
087            evidence to support its conclusions, reflecting a deep understanding of the task.
088         5. Factual accuracy: This criterion assesses the correctness of individual claims within the
089            explanation. While related to truthfulness, factual accuracy focuses on whether specific
090            statements align with established knowledge.
091
092   2.2   S ELF -E XPLANATIONS E VALUATION M ETHODOLOGY
093
094   Let M represent a large language model tasked with generating responses for a classification prob-
095   lem. Each response consists of two components: a self-explanation, denoted as εi , and a predicted
096   classification label, ŷi , corresponding to an input prompt xi . The self-explanation εi is produced
097   by prompting the model to articulate its reasoning before providing a final prediction, following the
098
      Chain-of-Thought prompting strategy (Wei et al., 2022).
099   Our methodology is inspired by recent approaches that utilize LLMs as evaluators of other models’
100   outputs (Dubois et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024; Fernandes et al., 2023; Bai et al., 2023; Saha et al., 2024).
101   This approach has shown versatility, extending beyond simple evaluation to various applications in
102   model improvement and self-alignment strategies. For instance, researchers have employed this
103   framework to generate self-instruct data for fine-tuning models (Wang et al., 2023; Chen et al.,
104
      2023; Gulcehre et al., 2023) and to create feedback for training reward models (Bai et al., 2022;
      Yuan et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2024).
105
106   For our evaluation, we employ a more capable model, MJudge , to assess the quality of self-
107   explanations εi based on predefined criteria (detailed in Section 2.1). The evaluation process pro-

119   3     S ELF -E XPLANATION A LIGNMENT WITH A NCHOR P REFERENCE PAIRS
120
121   In this section, we introduce a methodology for alignment designed to enhance the ability of large
122   language models (LLMs) to articulate their reasoning—self-explanation—even in the absence of
123   annotated rationale explanations. However, we assume access to human-annotated data in the form
124   of classification datasets for domain-specific adaptation, reflecting a common constraint in real-
125   world applications, where comprehensive explanation data is often scarce or prohibitively expensive
126   compared to classification datasets.
127   Building on prior work (Bai et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023; Yuan et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2024), our
128   alignment methodology incorporates familiar components such as self-preference dataset genera-
129   tion, human-free evaluation of candidate responses using LLM-as-Judge, preference pair selection,
130   and model alignment.
131   However, our approach differs from previous methods in two key ways: First, for the assessment
132   of candidate responses, we use the evaluation explanation quality framework introduced in Sections
133   2.1 and 2.2. Second, we propose a novel technique, Alignment with Anchor Preference Pairs, which
134   improves preference pair selection by categorizing model outputs into three groups: consistently
135   correct, consistently incorrect, and variable. By applying tailored strategies to each category, we
136   enhance the effectiveness of DPO.
137   The steps of the methodology are as follows:
138
139         1. Supervised fine-tuning of the base model MBase specifically on a target classification task,
140            resulting in MSFT .
141
            2. Instruct MSFT to generate multiple explanation-prediction pairs for each prompt, and eval-
142            uate the quality of these self-explanations using the methodology outlined in Sections 2.1
143            and 2.2. During alignment, the base model MBase acts as the judge MJudge , ensuring the
144            process remains self-contained.
145
            3. Construct an alignment dataset by selecting preference pairs using an anchor-based strategy
146            (see Section 3.3).
147
148
            4. Align MSFT via DPO with the dataset created in the third step, producing the aligned model
               MAnchor .

157   full text of the selected option to provide richer context and preserve the model’s text generation
158   capabilities. Details on datasets and training setups are provided in Section 4.1.

255   ARC-Challenge (Clark et al., 2018), LogiQA (Liu et al., 2020), and OpenbookQA (Mihaylov
256   et al., 2018). These datasets are established benchmarks for reasoning tasks, requiring a challenging
257   reasoning process, which makes them an ideal fit for evaluating the quality of self-explanations. A
258   key factor in their selection was the size of their training sets, which provided a sufficient number of
259   input prompts to support the creation of the self-preferenceion dataset. In the case of AQuA-Rat,
260
      we sampled 5,000 examples due to computational constraints. For evaluation, we used the test split
      of each dataset. For detailed dataset descriptions, see Appendix D.
261
262   SFT Training Details: For MSFT , we used the AdamW optimizer with a learning rate of 5 × 10−5
263   for one epoch, following a cosine schedule with 10% warmup steps. Gradient clipping was set to 0.3,

266
      rank r = 256) to all linear layers. LoRA adapters were used to accelerate training and to act
      as a regularization method (Biderman et al., 2024), addressing the overfitting tendencies of DPO
267
      (Thakkar et al., 2024), which is applied during the later alignment phase.

310   4.1.1   I MPACT OF S UPERVISED F INE -T UNING ON S ELF -E XPLANATIONS
311
312
313   We observed a significant trade-off in evaluation results before and after applying supervised fine-
314
      tuning on a classification task (see Table 1). While SFT notably improved classification accuracy, it
      resulted in a substantial decline in the quality of self-explanations compared to the base model. The
315
      average decrement in MSFT from the equilibrium point, as measured across judges, ranged from
316
      6.5% to 13.3% across benchmarks.
317
318   Building on evidence that supervised fine-tuning can improve performance on specific tasks at the
319   expense of a model’s generalization abilities (Yang et al., 2024; Kirk et al., 2024), we hypothesize
320
      that this decline occurs because the task of selecting predefined answers does not inherently encour-
      age the model to articulate its reasoning, leading to a specialization that diminishes the quality of
321
      the generated explanations.
322
323   These findings highlight the necessity for alignment techniques that can preserve high-quality ex-
      planations in situations where datasets with annotated explanations are unavailable for fine-tuning.

434      We define λ as the proportion of the self-preference dataset used to align MAnchor , corresponding

436      insight into how the composition of the self-preference dataset for MAnchor differs from that of
437      MRank .
438      The (CI) and (V) strategies ensure—assuming the self-explanation is faithful—that the winning
439      explanation εw
                      i supports the ground-truth label yi . For the (V) strategy, this is achieved by sampling
440      εw
          i from  the set Bw       n     n
                           i = {εi : ŷi = yi }. In the case of the (CI) strategy, MDebater is employed to
441      provide arguments that explicitly support yi .
442

443      based on scores assigned by judges during its creation. As a result, it does not guarantee that the
444      winning explanations, εwi , will support the ground-truth label yi . The likelihood of selecting cases
445      in the preference dataset where εw i aligns with an outcome different from yi increases, particularly
446      as λ grows.

471
472
         LLM-as-Evaluator: This concept refers to the ability of large language models (LLMs) to evaluate
473
         the outputs of other LLMs, a technique commonly referred to as LLM-as-a-Judge. This approach
474      has gained considerable traction in recent years (Dubois et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024; Fernandes et al.,
475      2023; Bai et al., 2023) and is frequently used to assess LLM performance across various downstream
476      tasks (Zheng et al., 2023). It has proven particularly effective in automating evaluations, as demon-
477      strated on platforms like LMSys Chatbot Arena. Key implementations include direct scoring based
478      on specific criteria, pairwise comparisons (Liu et al., 2024), reference-based evaluations, and ensem-
479      ble methods (Verga et al., 2024). While LLM-as-a-Judge offers scalability and consistency, it can
480      also inherit biases from the evaluation model, potentially amplifying problematic outputs (Huang
481      et al., 2024). Despite these challenges, it remains a valuable tool due to its efficiency and cost-
482
         effectiveness in evaluating LLM systems. In our work, we introduce a framework for the qualitative
         assessment of self-explanations using the LLM-as-a-Judge technique, designed to evaluate how ef-
483
         fectively a model conveys its reasoning.
484
485      Self-Alignment: Several approaches have been developed to improve LLMs without requiring
         human-annotated feedback. One method involves fine-tuning models using high-quality, self-


                                                                                 9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      generated input-output pairs (Wang et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023; Gulcehre et al., 2023), though
487   this can perpetuate biases in example selection without a clear mechanism for improving selection
488   quality. Another influential approach is Constitutional AI (Bai et al., 2022), where an LLM pro-
489   vides feedback and refines responses, which are then used to train a separate, static reward model.
490   Building on this concept, Yuan et al. (2024) and Wu et al. (2024) proposed using the LLM itself
491   as a dynamic reward model, eliminating the need for a static one. This allows for continuous im-
492   provement in both generation and evaluation capabilities through iterative training processes. In
493   our work, we introduce a novel method for creating a self-preference dataset. Our approach, called
494   Alignment with Anchor Preference Pairs, enhances preference pair selection by categorizing model
495
      behavior in response to each input prompt and applying tailored strategies for each category. To eval-
      uate a model’s consistency for a given input prompt, an anchor—i.e., a ground-truth reference—is
496
      required, which we derive from a classification task used as a probing mechanism.
497
498   LLM-as-a-Debater: This adversarial approach aims to improve model performance through argu-
499   mentation. In Perez et al. (2019), debaters are limited to extracting relevant statements from a source
500   text, rather than generating original arguments. Du et al. (2023) extended this concept by involving
501
      multiple LLM instances to debate their individual responses over several rounds, eventually converg-
      ing on a shared final answer. Khan et al. (2024) further developed this approach by using debate-like
502
      scenarios to challenge and refine model outputs through simulated arguments. In our work, we adopt
503
      the LLM-as-a-Debater approach in the role of a consultant, specifically following Khan et al. (2024),
504   for cases where the model’s response to certain input prompts is consistently incorrect. This strategy
505   enables the creation of self-preference examples that avoid reinforcing problematic behavior.
506
507
508
      6   L IMITATIONS
509
510
511   We acknowledge some limitations in our approach. First, evaluating the model’s consistency on
512   a given input prompt requires a anchor—ground truth reference. Consequently, the selection of
513
      preference pairs via the anchor strategy relies on a classification task as the probing mechanism,
      which restricts its applicability. Second, when ranking the quality of self-explanations, we assign
514
      equal weights across all evaluation dimensions. This uniform weighting may not accurately reflect
515
      the varying significance of different aspects of explanation quality, which can differ depending on
516   the user or specific application. Moreover, this approach may overlook instances where individual
517   explanations degrade in separate criteria, potentially leading to preference pairs where score differ-
518   ences arise from unrelated factors.
519
      Finally, using the base model as the judge during the creation of the self-preference dataset ad-
520
      dresses the issue that MSTF exhibits a decline in explanation quality (see Section 4.1.1) and reduced
521
      agreement with the evaluation judge (see Appendix C). However, this approach could become a bot-
522   tleneck in scenarios where multiple iterations of alignment are performed, as the base model remains
523   static and may fail to capture important evaluation nuances over time.
524

      7   C ONCLUSION
527
528
529   In this work, we introduced a methodology for alignment that enhances LLMs’ ability to generate
530   high-quality self-explanations, even in the absence of annotated rationale explanations. Our ap-
531
      proach provides an end-to-end solution for aligning LLMs on classification tasks, ensuring that they
      not only produce accurate predictions but also articulate coherent explanations for their decisions.
532
      This is achieved through three core components: evaluating the quality of generated explanations,
533
      creating self-preference datasets, and aligning the model. Central to our approach is Alignment
534   with Anchor Preference Pairs, a novel method that refines preference pair selection by categorizing
535   model outputs into three groups: consistently correct, consistently incorrect, and variable. For each
536   category, we apply tailored strategies to construct preference pairs, which are then used in DPO. Our
537   empirical results demonstrate that this method consistently improves explanation quality, reducing
538   the degradation caused by task specialization. Furthermore, we show that using anchor preference
539   pairs outperforms self-alignment methods that rely solely on judge-based evaluations for preference
      pair selection.

      B.4    D ISCUSSION
964
965   Among the evaluated models, largest judge, Mistral-Large-Instruct-2407 showed the
966   strongest alignment with human judgments, achieving moderate agreement across most crite-
967   ria. Other models, LLama3-70B-Instruct and Qwen2.5-72B-Instruct exhibited lower
968
      alignment, with Kappa values indicating slight to fair agreement and weaker correlations overall.
969   We emphasize correlation over full alignment due to inherent subjectivity in both human and model
970   assessments. When evaluating complex criteria like explanation quality, raters naturally vary in their
971   interpretations and standards. Thus, achieving absolute agreement becomes not only challenging but
      potentially less meaningful as an alignment measure.


                                                       18
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


972
       Correlation metrics better capture how LLMs and human raters rank explanations relative to each
973    other. Strong correlations demonstrate that both groups identify similar patterns in quality, even if
974    their absolute scores differ. This relative agreement is crucial for applications like ours that aim
975    to differentiate between stronger and weaker outputs, rather than establish absolute quality bench-
976    marks.

       B.5   L IMITATIONS
987
988
989
990    Despite providing valuable insights, our human evaluation has several limitations that should be
991    acknowledged. Firstly, the sample size was relatively small (30 explanations from 10 questions
992    sourced exclusively from the LogiQA dataset) which may limit the generalizability of our findings
993    to other datasets or domains. Secondly, the inter-annotator reliability among human raters was only
994    fair, with α values ranging from 0.16 to 0.47, indicating variability and inconsistency in human
995    judgments. This variability highlights the third limitation: the inherent subjectivity in evaluating
996    complex criteria such as explanation quality, logical coherence, and depth of argumentation. Differ-
997    ent raters may apply diverse standards and interpretations, leading to discrepancies in assessments
998
       and underscoring the challenges in achieving high agreement in subjective evaluations.

1057
1058   All datasets used in our experiments are established reasoning benchmarks. The questions, along
1059   with related context and answer options, were inserted into our template (provided in 11) and used
1060   as input prompts for the model.
1061
1062   D.1   AQ UA-R AT
1063
1064   AQuA-Rat (Ling et al., 2017) contains approximately 100,000 algebraic word problems, each ac-
1065   companied by a natural language rationale explaining the solution steps. Each problem includes a
1066   question and five answer options. Due to computational constraints, we sampled 5,000 examples
1067   from the training set and used 254 test samples for our experiments. We utilized only the questions
1068   and answer options and excluded the provided rationales since our study aims to enhance the model’s
1069   ability to generate self-explanations in natural language while performing the primary classification
1070   task, without relying on human-annotated reasoning patterns.
1071
1072   D.2   ARC-C HALLENGE
1073
1074   The ARC-Challenge dataset (Clark et al., 2018) is a subset of the ARC dataset containing grade-
1075   school level, multiple-choice science questions. We used 1,119 training samples and 1,172 test
1076   samples from ARC-Challenge dataset. These samples are selected for being challenging, as they
1077   could not be answered correctly by either retrieval-based or word co-occurrence algorithms. In
1078   our experiments, we incorporated the questions and their corresponding four answer options. We
1079   omitted the associated corpus of sentences to focus purely on the model’s reasoning capabilities
       rather than external knowledge retrieval.

1085   ical reasoning. In our experiments, we utilized English versions of 7,376 training samples and 651
