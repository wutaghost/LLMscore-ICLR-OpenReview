011                                                 A BSTRACT
012
013            Supervised and unsupervised learning methods experience a decline in perfor-
014            mance when applied to incomplete, corrupted, or noisy datasets. Matrix comple-
015            tion is a common task to impute the missing values in sparsely observed ma-
016
               trices. Given a matrix X ∈ Rm×n , low-rank matrix completion computes a
               rank-r approximation of X, where r ≪ min{m, n}, by only observing a few
017
               random entries of X. It is commonly applied for recommender systems, image
018
               processing, and multi-output collaborative modeling. Existing matrix completion
019            methods suffer either from slow convergence or failure under significant miss-
020            ing data levels. This paper proposes a novel approach, the Sparse Reverse of
021            Principal Component Analysis (SRPCA), that reformulates matrix factorization
022            based low-rank completion (minU,V ∥PΩ (X − UVT )∥2F ) to iteratively learn a
023            single low-rank subspace representation by solving the convex optimization prob-
024            lem minV ∥PΩ (X−PVT )∥2F under the principal component analysis framework,
025            resulting in a significant convergence acceleration. SRPCA converges iteratively
026            and is computationally tractable with a proven controllable upper bound on the
027
               number of iterations until convergence. Unlike existing matrix completion algo-
               rithms, the proposed SRPCA applies iterative pre-processing resets that maintain
028
               smoothness across the reconstructed matrix, which results in a performance boost
029
               for smooth matrices. The performance of the proposed technique is validated on
030            case studies for image processing, multivariate time-series imputation, and col-
031            laborative filtering. SRPCA is also compared with state-of-the-art benchmarks for
032            matrix completion.

035   1    I NTRODUCTION
036
037   Matrix completion is a common task for recovering missing or corrupted data in matrices (Wang
038   & Fan, 2024). It has constantly received tremendous attention from many research fields such as
039   collaborative filtering (e.g., recommender systems) (Yu et al., 2009; Chen & Wang, 2022), link
040   analysis (Gleich & Lim, 2011), distance embedding (Candès & Recht, 2009), computer vision (Chen
041   & Suter, 2004; Li et al., 2012), image processing (Ji et al., 2010; Jia et al., 2022), and so forth. In
042   any field, missing data in high volumes has a negative impact on various data analysis processes, as
043   many supervised and unsupervised learning methods cannot be applied directly to incomplete data
044
      (Audigier et al., 2016). Consequently, scalable and novel algorithms for matrix completion are still
      in constant demand, especially for applications with high levels of missing data.
045
046   The low-rank matrix has a key characteristic where the important information it contains, expressed
047   in terms of degree of freedom, is significantly smaller than the total number of entries. This means
048   that even if only a few entries are observed, there is still a good possibility of being able to reconstruct
049   the entire matrix (Nguyen et al., 2019). Many data matrices analyzed are low-rank or approximately
050
      low-rank structured (Candès & Recht, 2009). Taking a movie recommender system as an example,
      there are only few factors that may contribute to users’ preferences, suggesting that the data matrix
051
      recording users’ rating scores is actually low-rank structured.
052
053   Most approaches that solve low-rank matrix completion problems can be mainly divided into two
      categories, nuclear norm based and matrix factorization based (Sun & Luo, 2016). In the first cate-


                                                           1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      gory, the objective of matrix rank minimization is approximated by nuclear norm minimization. This
055   category’s methods include interior-point-based Semi-Definite Programming (SDP) solver (Candès
056   & Recht, 2009), conjugate gradient method (Blanchard et al., 2015), Singular Value Threshold
057   (SVT) algorithm (Cai et al., 2010), Augmented Lagrange Multiplier (ALM) algorithm (Lin et al.,
058   2010), and robust principal component analysis (Zhang et al., 2012), etc. In the second category,
059   the original matrix is compactly represented as the product of two low-rank matrices. The two
060   low-rank matrices are usually iteratively updated through various algorithms such as Alternating
061   Least Squares (ALS) (Jain et al., 2013; Gu et al., 2024) and Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD)
062   (Gemulla et al., 2011; Qin et al., 2024). Nuclear norm based algorithms are known to be more
063
      time-consuming as matrix dimension increases, while matrix factorization algorithms, which are
      non-convex heuristics, scale badly with high levels of missing data Gu et al. (2024). There is a very
064
      important requirement for a realistically and practically good matrix completion, which is the local
065
      and global smoothness in the reconstructed matrix. For example, in image processing, it is critical
066   to obtain smoothness over the image. Often, this requirement comes at the cost of moderately up-
067   dating the observed values, e.g., Gaussian filters and Variational Bayesian techniques (Kawasumi &
068   Takeda, 2018; Paliwal et al., 2022).
069
      To address this limitation, we propose the Sparse Reverse of the Principle Component Analysis
070
      (SRPCA) to complete matrices in their original space. The proposed approach maintains a high level
071
      of smoothness by iteratively finding the principal components of the matrix based on the predicted
072   values of both the missing and the observed parts of the matrix, while guaranteeing that the principal
073   components are capable of reconstructing the observed part of the matrix with minimal differences.
074   The main contributions of the paper can be summarized in the following:
075
076          • A novel low-rank matrix completion method called SRPCA is proposed. It leverages both
077            the missing and the observed part of the matrix-to-recover to iteratively learn the principle
078
               components that adequately represent the underlying low-rank matrix. SRPCA maintains
               smoothness across the reconstructed matric by applying an iterative pre-processing step.
079
080          • SRPCA is a matrix factorization based algorithm that modifies the non-convex problem to a
081
               convex one where it iteratively learns a single low-rank subspace representation, instead of
               two, by leveraging the principle component analysis framework. This aids in accelerating
082
               convergence.
083
084          • SRPCA is proved to improve in performance iteratively until convergence with a control-
085
               lable upper bound on the number of iterations.
086          • An extension of SRPCA, called FastSRPCA, is proposed to offer a faster convergence in
087            matrix completion applications where convergence rate is valued more than further improv-
088
               ing a sub-optimal matrix recovery.
089          • SRPCA is evaluated on three diverse low-rank matrix completion based case studies and is
090            shown to improve on other state-of-the-art benchmarks in terms of computational time and
091            matrix recovery.

095   2.1   M ATRIX C OMPLETION
096
097   If we assume that the data matrix to be recovered has a low-rank structure, the matrix completion
098
      problem should be defined as follows (Candès & Recht, 2009):
099                              min rank(M ),     s.t. Xi,j = Mi,j , ∀(i, j) ∈ Ω                        (1)
                                 M
100
101
      where X ∈ Rm×n is the sparse observed matrix, M ∈ Rm×n the reconstructed matrix of X, and
      Ω represents the observed entries of X.
102
103   This problem is a simple explanation of the low-rank matrix completion problem. Unfortunately, the
104   rank minimization is NP-hard and has led researchers to propose different relaxations to solve the
105   problem. Specifically, a commonly used convex relaxation for the rank is the nuclear norm, ∥M ∥∗ ,
106   which approximates problem (1) as (Candès & Recht, 2009; Sun & Luo, 2016; Hardt & Wootters,
107
      2014):
                                 min ∥M ∥∗ , s.t. Xi,j = Mi,j , ∀(i, j) ∈ Ω                          (2)
                                   M

      Or:
109                                                  1        2
110                                     min τ ∥M ∥∗ + ∥X − M ∥F                                          (3)
                                        M            2
111             2

      where ∥·∥F is the Frobenius norm and τ is apositive penalty parameter. Equations (2) & (3) can be
      conveniently optimized through some interior-point-method-based SDP solvers (Candès & Recht,
113
      2009), like SDPT3 and SeDuMi. Cai et al. (2010) further proposed an SVT algorithm to solve
114
      (2) & (3). In the SVT, the estimate M converges to a unique solution of (3) through an iterative
115   algorithm.Unfortunately, both SDP solvers and the SVT algorithm are problematic when applied to
116   a large-size data set (Sun & Luo, 2016). Especially for the SVT, SVD computation is required at
117   each iteration, which is time-consuming.
118
      Another common approach to relax the rank is via matrix factorization, in which the unknown data
119
      matrix is expressed as the product of two low-rank matrices, U and V Sun & Luo (2016). In this
120
      case, the low-rank condition is satisfied automatically, and problem (1) can be transformed to:
121
                                                            2
122                              min PΩ (X − U V T ) F ,        s.t. M = U V T                           (4)
                                 U ,V
123
124   Problem 4 is clearly a non-convex optimization problem. Compared with the nuclear norm based
125
      approach, the matrix factorization based approach performs much better on computation time. ALS
      is one of the popular matrix factorization-based methods, which originates from the power factoriza-
126
      tion method (Haldar & Hernando, 2009). In the ALS algorithm, the observed entries are randomly
127
      partitioned into a number of subsets at first. Then, U and V are initialized through the SVD of
128   the first subset of the observed matrix. Next, at each iteration when moving to the next subset, U
129   and V are alternatively updated to minimize the difference between U V T and the observed entries
130   of that subset. ALS decreases the computational time because it does not apply SVD at each iter-
131   ation. However, it may lead to high inaccuracies at high levels of missing data, and it ignores the
132   smoothness of the data set due to the random partitioning of the original matrix. A recent matrix fac-
133   torization based method is the Gauss-Newton Matrix Recovery (GNMR) (Zilber & Nadler, 2022),
134   which utilizes a Gauss-Newton method to solve for the two factor matrices every iteration. Another
135   method we use for benchmarking is Low-Rank Gaussian Copula (LRGC) (Zhao & Udell, 2020)
136
      which is a semiparametric algorithm for data imputation that also offers uncertainty quantification.
      For more comprehensive surveys, we refer readers Nguyen et al. (2019).

      2.2   P RINCIPLE C OMPONENT A NALYSIS (PCA)
140
      PCA is one of the most widely used statistical tools for data analysis and dimensionality reduction
141   (Candès et al. (2011)). It has been applied in many different areas, such as quantitative finance
142   (Han et al., 2023; Chin et al., 2023), neuroscience (Lawrence et al., 2023), and image processing
143   (Mishra et al., 2024). PCA provides a roadmap for transforming the original data set to a new basis
144   with a lower dimension, thus filtering out the noise and revealing the hidden simplified dynamics.
145   Therefore, with PCA, it is possible to extract critically important information from original data,
146   thus simplifying the data structure.
147   Suppose we have a data matrix M . The goal of PCA is to find an orthonormal matrix where
148   P = M V , such that the covariance matrix of P is diagonalized and expressed as:

      3.1   P ROBLEM F ORMULATION
165
166   Based on the general matrix completion problem, the goal is to construct a matrix M ∈ Rm×n that
167   estimates the missing part of matrix X ∈ Rm×n . Let Ω = {(i, j) : Xi,j is observed}, PΩ (X) ∈
168   Rm×n to be the matrix that preserves the entities in Ω and replaces the remaining entities by 0,
169   and Ω⊥ to be the complement of Ω. Contrary to the matrix factorization approach in 4, where the
170   purpose is to consectively minimize for each of the two factor matrices, we minimize the problem
171
      to a convex problem, where we only solve for one of them as per the following with M = P V T :
172                                                                       2
                                            min PΩ (X − P V T ) F                                         (8)
173                                          P
174   where P is the principal component matrix, and V is the eigenvector matrix of M T M .

502   5   C ONCLUSION
503
504   Missing or corrupted data is pervasive across various fields, thus, affecting downstream learning
505   tasks. Low-rank matrix completion is a common task to recover the missing entries of a partially
506   observed matrix. Existing methods either fail under significant missing data levels or suffer from
507   slow convergence. Th paper proposes a novel low-rank matrix completion method for incomplete
508
      datasets, called the Sparse Reverse of the Principle Component Analysis (SRPCA). The contribu-
      tions are multi-fold: (i) this approach maintains a certain level of smoothness across the matrix; (ii)
509
      it modifies the non-convex matrix factorization problem into a convex optimization problem with a
510
      closed for solution, leveraging subspace representation in terms of principal components; (iii) it con-
511   verges iteratively, which is critical for scenarios that stop the algorithm after reaching the maximum
512   number of iterations or due to external reasons; and (iv) it is also computationally tractable, with a
513   controlled upper bound on the number of iterations until convergence. In addition, a faster extension
514   – fastSRPCA – is provided to improve on convergence rate at the expense of marginal missing data
515   recovery. The efficacy of the SRPCA algorithm is validated on a natural image, multisensor dataset,
516   and movie ratings dataset.
517   Limitations. (i) The first obvious limitation of SRPCA is that it requires the knowledge of the rank
518   as an input. In most cases, this information is not readily available. However, there are low-rank
519   matrix completion methods in literature (Nguyen et al., 2019) that we can influence from to make
520   the rank selection adaptive. (ii) SRPCA suffers from singularity results in random predicitons for a
521   column that has all its entries missing, which happens scarcely or for extremely high missing levels
522   of data ( 90%). (iii) SRPCA is based on an assumption that the matrix completion environment is
523   based on Missing-Completely-At-Random (MCAR). It is interesting for future studies to see how to
524   develop it for Missing-At-Random (MAR) and Missing-Not-At-Random (MNAR) environments.
