009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Recent advances in Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) and Graph Transformers
012            (GTs) have been driven by innovations in architectures and Positional Encodings
013            (PEs), which are critical for augmenting node features and capturing graph topol-
014            ogy. PEs are essential for GTs, where topological information would otherwise be
015            lost without message-passing. However, PEs are often tested alongside novel ar-
016
               chitectures, making it difficult to isolate their effect on established models. To ad-
               dress this, we present a comprehensive benchmark of PEs in a unified framework
017
               that includes both message-passing GNNs and GTs. We also establish theoretical
018
               connections between MPNNs and GTs and introduce a sparsified GRIT attention
019            mechanism to examine the influence of global connectivity. Our findings demon-
020            strate that previously untested combinations of GNN architectures and PEs can
021            outperform existing methods, offering a more comprehensive picture of the state-
022            of-the-art. To support future research and experimentation in our framework, we
023            make the code publicly available.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      Graph machine learning has traditionally relied on message-passing neural networks (MPNNs),
      which work through iterative rounds of neighborhood aggregation (Kipf & Welling, 2016). In each
029
      round, nodes update their states by incorporating information from their neighbors along with their
030
      own current states. While effective in capturing local graph structures, this approach can strug-
031   gle with modeling long-range dependencies. Graph Transformer (GT) architectures utilize full at-
032   tention mechanisms to circumvent this, but necessitate new methods to integrate graph topology
033   information (Dwivedi & Bresson, 2020). This is similar to how positional encodings (PEs) in Nat-
034   ural Language Processing (NLP) represent token positions within sequences (Vaswani et al., 2017).
035   However, encoding positional information in graphs is more complex than in sequences. Ideally,
036   positional encodings should allow the reconstruction of the graph’s topology from node features and
037   provide useful inductive biases to improve performance (Black et al., 2024). Despite the growing
038   number of new graph transformer architectures and positional encodings, there has been a lack of
039
      systematic evaluation comparing these encodings across different GT architectures. This makes it
      difficult to determine whether observed performance improvements are due to novel encodings or
040
      architectural innovations.
041
042   In this paper, we conduct a comprehensive evaluation of various positional encodings for both
043   message-passing and transformer frameworks. Our goal is to understand the impact of positional
044   encodings on model performance and identify the best combinations of encodings and architectures.
045
      By benchmarking state-of-the-art graph transformers with a variety of positional encodings, we
      provide a clear picture of the current state of the field and offer guidance for future research. Addi-
046
      tionally, we further strengthen the theoretical connection between MPNNs and GTs. Although GTs
047
      are generally considered fundamentally different due to their use of attention mechanisms, we show
048   that under certain conditions, MPNNs and GTs can be equally expressive, with additional results
049   that extend the scope of previous analyses (Veličković, 2023; Müller & Morris, 2024). Specifically,
050   MPNNs can be applied to fully-connected graphs and operate like a GT, while attention mech-
051   anisms can also be adapted for local message-passing. Our theoretical analysis demonstrates that
052   both MPNNs and GTs can have the same expressiveness when the underlying topology of the MPNN
053   is fully connected. Based on these insights, we extend our evaluation to include MPNNs with po-
      sitional encodings on fully-connected graphs and modify state-of-the-art attention mechanisms for

054
      localized graph convolutions. Our results indicate that by combining existing positional encodings
055   and architectures, state-of-the-art performance can be achieved on several benchmark datasets.
056
057   Our contributions can be summarized as follows:
058
               1. We conduct an empirical evaluation of various positional encodings across message-
059
                  passing neural networks and Graph Transformers to consolidate the current state-of-the-art
060               and find new combinations that surpass the previous best models.
061
062
               2. We provide theoretical insights into the relationship between MPNNs and GTs, showing
                  conditions where they share similar expressiveness. Based on these observations, we intro-
063
                  duce a sparsified version of GRIT attention for localized graph convolutions, which proves
064
                  effective across multiple datasets.
065
066
               3. We provide a unified evaluation framework implementing all used architectures and posi-
                  tional encodings in one codebase to facilitate the testing of new positional encodings and
067
                  models. The code is made publicly available.1

070   2       R ELATED W ORK
071
072   Message-Passing Neural Networks (MPNNs). Earlier graph neural networks (GNNs), including
073   models like GCN (Kipf & Welling, 2016), GAT (Veličković et al., 2017), GraphSAGE (Hamilton
074   et al., 2017), and GIN (Xu et al., 2018), have paved the way for various advancements. Some con-
075   volutional filtering variants incorporate edge attributes into their architecture. GatedGCN (Bresson
076
      & Laurent, 2017) employs gates as sparse attention mechanisms, while GINE (Hu et al., 2019)
      augments features with local edges. Recent efforts aim to enhance the expressive power of GNNs,
077
      addressing the limitations imposed by the 1-WL test. For instance, Principal Neighborhood Ag-
078
      gregation (PNA) (Corso et al., 2020) combines different aggregators with degree-scalers to tackle
079   isomorphism tasks in continuous feature spaces. Higher-order GNNs, like k-GNN (Morris et al.,
080   2019; Maron et al., 2019), build on the k-WL algorithm, a more generalized version of the WL test,
081   offering increased expressive power. Other approaches, such as GSN (Bouritsas et al., 2022) and
082   GIN-AK+ (Zhao et al., 2021), utilize substructures (subgraphs) for message passing, while methods
083   like CIN (Bodnar et al., 2021) operate on regular cell complexes, although they remain less pow-
084   erful than the 3-WL test. Importantly, these models serve as baselines in some graph transformers,
085   demonstrating comparable performance with certain GTs, as cited in GraphGPS (Rampášek et al.,
086   2022), GRIT (Ma et al., 2023), and Exphormer (Shirzad et al., 2023).
087
088   Graph Transformers (GTs). Graph Transformers (GT) were popularized in recent years
089
      (Rampášek et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2023; Mao et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2023). Modules includ-
      ing positional or structural encodings, global attention, and local message passing are considered as
090
      mainstream design components for a standard graph transformer model, which successfully solved
091
      the problem of in-scalability (Rampášek et al., 2022; Shirzad et al., 2023) in large graphs, lack of
092   graph inductive bias (Ma et al., 2023), and over-smoothing problems (Chen et al., 2022b). Apart
093   from its maturity in some machine learning fields such as natural language processing, computer
094   vision, or bioinformatics that many previous GT papers have mentioned, GTs have also demon-
095   strated their strength by extending their application to scientific domains such as differential equa-
096   tions (Bryutkin et al., 2024; Choromanski et al., 2022), quantum physics (Wang et al., 2022a), and
097   symbolic regression (Zhong & Meidani). Some recent works are theoretical analysis in graph trans-
098   formers regarding the theoretical expressive power of GT (Zhou et al., 2024), and the analytical
099   relationship between positional encodings in GT (Keriven & Vaiter, 2024; Black et al., 2024). How-
100
      ever, there is currently a lack of a practical benchmark that compares different types of positional
      encodings. MPNNs and GTs have been compared extensively in the literature, with early work ob-
101
      serving that these models can simulate one another (Veličković, 2023). A more rigorous theoretical
102
      analysis has demonstrated that GTs can be related to MPNNs when a virtual node is employed (Cai
103   et al., 2023). Furthermore, it has been established that GTs can simulate MPNNs, provided that the
104   positional encodings are sufficiently strong (Müller & Morris, 2024). In contrast, our findings show
105   conditions under which MPNNs operating on fully connected graphs can achieve equal expressive-
106   ness to that of GTs, without requiring additional positional encodings or architectural modifications.

      3.3   G RAPH T RANSFORMERS
155
      Transformer models have been widely used in modeling sequence-to-sequence data in different do-
156   mains (Vaswani et al., 2017). Although the attention mechanism has commonly been used to learn
157   on graph-structured data (Veličković et al., 2017), the use of transformers is relatively recent. A
158   GT layer relies on a self-attention module that lets nodes attend to a set of “neighbors”, effectively
159   resulting in a dependency graph G of nodes that can attend to each other. We will refer to the nodes
160   that a node u can attend to simply as its neighborhood N (u). Many architectures use “full attention”
161   on the graph (as opposed to “sparse attention”), meaning that all nodes can attend to all other nodes
      in the graph, i.e., the underlying dependency graph for attention is fully connected. Based on the

      3.4   B RIDGING GT S AND WL
185
186   In the literature, various attempts have been made to bridge the gap between Graph Transformers
187   (GTs) and the WL test (Müller & Morris, 2024; Cai et al., 2023). This is usually done by defining
188   new variants of the WL algorithm that apply to the GT of interest (Kim et al., 2022). However, we
189   argue that such extensions are not necessary. Instead, we can interpret the execution of a GT on G
190   as an MPNN on a new topology G′ = (V, E ′ ) corresponding to the dependency graph, representing
191   the information flow in the attention layer (Veličković, 2023). For example, a GT with full attention
192   can be seen as an MPNN on the fully connected graph, with E ′ = V × V . Relative positional
193
      encodings can be added to the MPNN as edge labels. This means we can use the (edge-augmented)
      1-WL algorithm on G′ to upper bound the expressive power of a GT on G. While it is perhaps not
194
      surprising that GT expressivity can be upper bounded in this way, we also show that GTs can attain
195
      this upper bound under some reasonable assumptions. To facilitate this proof, we use the same idea
196   as Xu et al. (2018) to show the equivalence between the GIN architecture and 1-WL.
197

      3.5   S PARSE GRIT M ESSAGE -PASSING C ONVOLUTION
234
235   GRIT introduces two main innovations: (1) A new attention mechanism that updates edge labels
236   on a fully connected graph and (2) RRWP as a positional encoding. While it is relatively easy to
237   use RRWP with both other message-passing and Graph Transformer architectures, we need some
238   adaptions to use the GRIT attention mechanism with message-passing GNNs on sparse graphs.
239   As motivated earlier in Section 3.4, a Graph Transformer can be seen as message-passing on a
240   fully-connected graph. Therefore, we generalize the GRIT attention mechanism designed for fully-
241   connected graphs to a message-passing convolution that works with any neighborhood. We call the
242
      resulting convolution Sparse GRIT, as it can attend to local neighborhoods on sparse graphs and does
      not suffer from the quadratic computational overhead that the original GRIT mechanism has. This
243
      makes sparse GRIT more efficient and scalable, as we further underline in our empirical evaluation
244
      in Section 5.2.
245
246   Sparse GRIT utilizes the same updating edge labels êi,j as the original, but only for edges that exist
247   in the original graph. This further distinguishes the convolution from other popular local attention
248
      mechanisms like GAT. The main difference to GRIT lies in the update function x̂i for nodes, which
      now attend to their local neighborhood instead of all nodes in the graph. It becomes:
249
250                                   X          ewj ·êi,j
251                          x̂i =             P         wk ·êi,k

450   5.3     RUNNING T IME AND M EMORY C OMPLEXITY FOR PE S
451
452   The computational cost of positional encodings (PEs) is a critical consideration, particularly for
453   large graphs where methods with high complexity quickly become infeasible. We evaluated the
454   running time and memory usage for various PEs, and the full results are presented in Appendix A.8.
455   RRWP is the most memory-intensive PE, but maintains reasonable running times. RWSE and
456   RWDIFF, on the other hand, tend to have significantly longer running times but are relatively more
457   memory-efficient. Laplacian-based methods, such as LapPE and ESLapPE, offer a good balance be-
458   tween computational speed and memory usage, making them practical even for larger datasets. PPR
459   and GCKN come with high computational demands in both time and memory, making them less
460   suited for large-scale graphs. In contrast, Laplacian-based encodings like ESLapPE strike a better
461
      trade-off, making them practical for a broader range of graph sizes while still offering competitive
      performance.
462
463
464   5.4     G UIDELINES FOR P RACTICIONERS
465
466   For superpixel graph datasets, such as PascalVOC-SP, COCO-SP, MNIST, and CIFAR10, we found
467   that the inclusion of positional encodings generally does not result in substantial performance im-
468   provements. In particular, larger superpixel graphs like PascalVOC-SP and COCO-SP showed mini-
469   mal to no gains from adding PEs, while MNIST similarly exhibited negligible benefits. An exception
470   to this trend is CIFAR10, where RRWP demonstrated potential for enhancing model performance.
471
      This suggests that while superpixel graphs may not typically benefit from positional encodings,
      RRWP could be considered as a candidate for improvement. However, the gains observed may not
472
      always justify the increased computational complexity associated with RRWP for such datasets.
473
474   In contrast, molecular datasets, such as ZINC and the Peptides variations, displayed a strong de-
475   pendency on the choice of positional encoding, with significant variations in model performance
476   based on the PE used. For instance, ZINC consistently showed the best results with PPR. On the
477
      other hand, the Peptides datasets revealed task-specific preferences: Peptides-func benefited the
      most from RRWP, while Peptides-struct achieved optimal performance with WLPE. Interestingly,
478
      despite using identical graph structures, the two Peptides tasks favored different PEs, which indi-
479
      cates that the nature of the prediction target (functional vs. structural) plays a significant role. Thus,
480   when dealing with molecular datasets, practitioners are advised to experiment with various PEs, as
481   the optimal choice may depend more on the specific task than on the graph structure itself. Still,
482   the optimal PE for a given dataset is generally consistent across different models, which, in com-
483   bination with the fact that we test on commonly used datasets provides practitioners with a strong
484   starting point for their experiments. This distinction is further highlighted when comparing random-
485   walk-based encodings with Laplacian encodings, where one typically emerges as the clear winner
      depending on the dataset and task.

      6   C ONCLUSIONS
487
488
      This study underscores the critical role of positional encodings in enhancing the performance of
489   Graph Neural Networks (GNNs), particularly within Graph Transformer architectures. We con-
490   ducted a thorough comparison of various positional encodings across a wide array of state-of-the-art
491   models and identify the optimal configurations for diverse datasets, as well as offer valuable insights
492   into the relationship between positional encodings and model performance. While we consolidated
493   much of the current state-of-the-art, we also identified new configurations that surpass the perfor-
494   mance of existing best models, such as Exphormer on PCQM-Contact. This underscores the neces-
495   sity of in-depth comparisons to provide a fair and accurate ranking. Our theoretical considerations
496   have led to the development of the SparseGRIT model. This model shows competitive performance
497
      across multiple benchmarks, while maintaining scalability to larger graphs. It shows that sparse
      message-passing together with the right positional encodings is a viable option on many datasets.
498
499   Furthermore, we provide a comprehensive overview of the current state-of-the-art in graph learn-
500   ing and highlight the importance of selecting appropriate positional encodings to achieve optimal
501   results. Our unified codebase includes implementations of all tested models and encodings, which
502   serves as a valuable resource for future research. The framework ensures reproducible results and
503
      supports the integration of new datasets, models, and positional encodings, thereby facilitating fur-
      ther experimentation.
504
505
      Limitations. Due to computational constraints, we could not explore all possible hyperparameter
506
      configurations and there might be slightly better performing ones that we did not catch. Additionally,
507
      although we tested a wide range of models and encodings, it is infeasible to test every model and PE.
508   This is why we focused on current state-of-the-art for both. Further, our evaluations are based on a
509   specific set of benchmark datasets, which may not fully represent the diversity of real-world graph
510   structures. Thus, performance on these benchmarks may not generalize to all types of graph data.
511   Nevertheless, our unified codebase serves as a robust foundation for further testing and development,
512   and enables researchers to overcome these limitations by facilitating the inclusion of new datasets,
513   models, and positional encodings.

813
814   Positional Encodings for Graphs. Positional encodings are traditionally used in natural language
815   processing to capture the absolute position of a token within a sentence (Vaswani et al., 2017) or the
816   relative distance between pairs of tokens (Shaw et al., 2018; Ke et al., 2020; Chen, 2021). Similarly,
817   positional encoding in graphs aims to learn both local topology and global structural information
818   of nodes efficiently. This approach has been successfully implemented with the introduction of
819   the graph transformer (Dwivedi & Bresson, 2020). With the advent of graph transformers in the
820   field of graph representation learning, many traditional graph theory methods have been revitalized.
821
      Graph signal processing techniques have been employed such as Laplacian decomposition and fi-
      nite hop random walks (Rampášek et al., 2022; Dwivedi et al., 2023; Ma et al., 2023; Beaini et al.,
822
      2021; Dwivedi & Bresson, 2020; Kreuzer et al., 2021; Dwivedi et al., 2021; Lim et al., 2022; Wang
823
      et al., 2022b) as absolute or relative positional encoding. Node properties such as degree central-
824   ity (Ying et al., 2021) and personalized PageRank (PPR) (Gasteiger et al., 2018; Fu et al., 2024)
825   could be mapped and expanded into higher dimensions for absolute positional encoding, while the
826   shortest distance between nodes could be used for relative positional encoding (Li et al., 2020; Ying
827   et al., 2021). Recent studies have focused on developing learnable positional encodings for graphs
828   (Ying et al., 2021) and exploring their expressiveness and stability as well (Wang et al., 2022b; Ma
829   et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2023). Additionally, graph rewiring combined with layout optimization to
830   coarsen graphs has been proposed as a form of positional encoding (Grötschla et al., 2024).
831
832   GNN Benchmarking. One of the first GNN benchmarking papers compared architectures with
833   and without positional encodings (PEs) (Dwivedi et al., 2023), where their PE mainly refers to Lapla-
834   cian positional encoding (LapPE). Their study was limited to the GatedGCN model and discussed
835   the expressive power, robustness, and efficiency of state-of-the-art message-passing methods. Addi-
836
      tionally, several surveys have benchmarked the complexity, specific tasks, unified message-passing
      frameworks (Wu et al., 2020; Zhou et al., 2020), robustness, and privacy (Ju et al., 2024). The LRGB
837
      dataset (Dwivedi et al., 2022) has been tested in both GNNs and transformers to demonstrate the su-
838
      periority of Graph Transformers (GTs) over Message Passing Neural Networks (MPNNs). Many
839   state-of-the-art GTs have included this benchmark in their experiments (Rampášek et al., 2022;
840   Shirzad et al., 2023; Ma et al., 2023). One limitation of the LRGB benchmark is that LRGB only
841   considers LapPE and random walk structural encodings (RWSE). One notable work benchmarked
842   using LRGB by fine-tuning the architectures of GraphGPS and pre-processing (Tönshoff et al.,
843   2023). We adopt their settings but place greater emphasis on the effect of positional encodings.
