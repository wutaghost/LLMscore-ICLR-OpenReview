                                                 A BSTRACT
008
009
               We present a comprehensive empirical study on how backdoor attacks affect CLIP
010
               by analyzing the representations of backdoor images. Specifically, based on the
011            methodology of representation decomposing, image representations can be de-
012            composed into a sum of representations across individual image patches, attention
013            heads (AHs), and multi-layer perceptrons (MLPs) in different model layers. By
014            examining the effect of backdoor attacks on model components, we have the fol-
015            lowing empirical findings. (1) Different backdoor attacks would infect different
016            model components, i.e., local patch-based backdoor attacks mainly affect AHs,
017            while global noise-based backdoor attacks mainly affect MLPs. (2) Infected AHs
018            are centered on the last layer, while infected MLPs are decentralized on several
019
               late layers. (3) Some AHs are not greatly infected by backdoor attacks, and even
               infected AHs could still maintain the original functionality. These observations
020
               motivate us to defend against backdoor attacks by detecting infected AHs, re-
021
               pairing their representations or filtering backdoor samples with too many infected
022            AHs, in the inference stage. Experimental results validate our empirical findings
023            and demonstrate the effectiveness of the defense methods.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Recently, Contrastive Language-Image Pretraining (CLIP) (Radford et al., 2021) has received much
029   attention due to its powerful visual representations learned from natural language supervision (Xu
030   et al., 2021; Wu et al., 2023). Recent research (Carlini & Terzis, 2022; Carlini et al., 2023; Bansal
031   et al., 2023) has disclosed the vulnerability of CLIP against backdoor attacks. Specifically, a ma-
032   licious adversary can poison a small proportion of backdoor image-text pairs into the pre-training
033
      data, which would result in a backdoored CLIP after multimodal contrastive learning. In the infer-
      ence stage, the backdoored CLIP would produce tampered image representations when the trigger
034
      is attached to the images, close to the text representation of the target attack class. This situation
035
      exposes a serious security risk of deploying CLIP in practical applications.
036
037   To defend against backdoor attacks on CLIP, recent research has proposed a few backdoor defense
038   methods, e.g, robust multimodal contrastive learning in the pretraining stage (Yang et al., 2023a),
039
      fine-tuning the backdoored CLIP (Bansal et al., 2023), reverse-engineering the trigger (Sur et al.,
      2023), and detecting backdoor samples in the inference stage (Niu et al., 2024). However, there
040
      still remains a limited systematic understanding of how backdoor attacks affect CLIP. To fill this
041
      gap, we conduct a comprehensive empirical study to investigate how backdoor attacks affect CLIP
042   by analyzing the representations of backdoor images. Specifically, following the methodology of
043   representation decomposing (Gandelsman et al., 2024), we decouple the image representation as
044   a sum of representations across individual image patches, attention heads (AHs), and multi-layer
045   perceptrons (MLPs). Furthermore, we use mean-ablation (Gandelsman et al., 2024), i.e., replacing
046   representations of backdoor images on AHs or MLPs with mean representations of clean images
047   on the same components. In this way, we can examine the effect of backdoor attacks on these
048   components by comparing the attack success rate (ASR) and the clean accuracy (CACC). Our key
049   findings are summarized as follows.
050   (1) Different backdoor attacks would infect different model components, i.e., local patch-based
051   backdoor attacks mainly affect AHs, while global noise-based backdoor attacks mainly affect
052   MLPs. First of all, we directly mean-ablate all AHs or MLPs. The results are shown in Figure
053   1 (a)-1 and (a)-2. We can see that mean-ablating all MLPs has little effect on the ASR of BadNet
      (Gu et al., 2017) and BadCLIP (Liang et al., 2023) but dramatically decreases the ASR of Blended

      (3) Some AHs in the last layer are not greatly infected by backdoor attacks, and even infected AHs
      could still maintain the original functionality. We further explore the characteristics of infected
094
      AHs and MLPs. By visualizing head-specific attention maps as shown in Figure 2, we found that
095
      some AHs do not catch the triggers. Moreover, based on the algorithm TEXTSPAN (Gandelsman
096   et al., 2024), we characterize the functionality change of infected AHs or MLPs by CLIP’ text
097   representations. The results are shown in Figure 2 and Figure 3. We can see that certain descriptive
098   texts of infected AHs have no significant change in semantics, e.g., the 4th AH (“color”) and the 10th
099   AH (“location”). The potential reason lies in that the triggers inherently have visual information
100   related to “color” and “location” that is consistently captured by these AHs. This finding reveals the
101   different effects of backdoor attacks on the functionality of infected components.
102   These observations motivate us to defend against backdoor attacks by repairing representations of in-
103   fected model components or filtering backdoor samples. Specifically, we directly mean-ablate MLPs
104   in the last five layers for global noise-based attacks due to the decentralization of infected MLPs. For
105   local patch-based attacks, instead of removing all AHs in the last layer, we selectively mean-ablate
106   AHs which are much affected by backdoor attacks. To this end, we construct head-specific proto-
107   types by averaging head-specific representations from a small proportion of clean validation data.
      Based on these head prototypes, we select the AHs with lower cosine similarity between their repre-

181   3    A C LOSER L OOK AT BACKDOOR ATTACKS ON CLIP
182
183   In this section, we conduct preliminary experiments to investigate how backdoor attacks affect CLIP.
184   Specifically, we consider four backdoor attacks (i.e., BadNet (Gu et al., 2017), Blended (Chen et al.,
185   2017), ISSBA (Li et al., 2021), and BadCLIP (Liang et al., 2023)) to poison CLIP (Bansal et al.,
186   2023; Carlini & Terzis, 2022), thereby producing four types of backdoored CLIPs respectively. The
187   details of backdoor attacks are shown in Appendix E.1. To explore the effect of backdoor attacks
188   on each model component, we use mean-ablation (Gandelsman et al., 2024) that replaces represen-
189
      tations of potentially infected components with mean representations of corresponding components
      from clean validation images. In this way, we can validate the effect of backdoor attacks on the
190
      component by comparing attack success rates (ASR) and clean accuracy (CACC). We conduct this
191
      experiment on the ImageNet-1K validation dataset, using 20% of the images as the clean validation
192   data. We mainly explore the effect of backdoor attacks on attention heads (AHs) and multi-layer
193   perceptions (MLPs). The key findings are summarized as follows.
194
195
      Finding 1: different backdoor attacks would infect different model components, i.e., local patch-
      based backdoor attacks mainly affect AHs, while global noise-based backdoor attacks mainly
196
      affect MLPs. First of all, we directly mean-ablate all AHs or MLPs. From the results in Figure 1
197
      (a)-1 and (a)-2, we can see that after mean-ablating all MLPs, ASR of BadNet and BadCLIP have
198   little effect compared with their baseline ASR (dash lines), while ASR of Blended and ISSBA dra-
199   matically decreases nearly to zero. Conversely, when mean-ablating all AHs, the ASR of BadNet
200   and BadCLIP become almost zero, while the ASR of Blended and ISSBA remain unchanged. This
201   observation indicates that BadNet and BadCLIP mainly affect AHs, while Blended and ISSBA pri-
202   marily affect MLPs. Besides, mean-abating all MLPs has little effect on the CACC (nearly reduced
203   by 6%∼7%), while mean-ablating all AHs greatly decreases the CACC to reach almost zero. This
204   observation is consistent with the finding in (Gandelsman et al., 2024) that MLPs have a negligible
205   effect on generalization, while AHs capture useful information for generalization.
206   Explanation for the finding 1. The potential reason for this observation lies in the characteristics
207   of their triggers. Specifically, the triggers of BadNet and BadCLIP are local patches located in a
208   small area of the image, while the triggers of Blended and ISSBA are noise pixels embedded into
209   the entire image. Considering the multi-head self-attention mechanism in ViTs that can encode
210   contextual cues of a sequence of image patches, the information of local patch triggers is easier to
211   encode into AHs than that of global noise pixels. Conversely, MLPs mainly focus on aggregating
212
      representation information from AHs, which attends to global noise pixels (Gu et al., 2022).
213   Finding 2: infected AHs are centered on the last layer, while infected MLPs are decentralized on
214   several late layers. Here, we further explore the effect of backdoor attacks on AHs or MLPs in
215   various model layers. Specifically, we use three types of mean-ablation schemes, i.e., forward/back-
      ward/separate ablation. Forward ablation means that we ablate AHs or MLPs in sequence up to a

458   4.2     E XPERIMENTAL R ESULTS
459
460   The experimental results of repairing representations and detecting backdoor samples are shown in
461   Table 1 on ImageNet-1K, Table 5 on Caltech-101 and Oxford Pets in Appendix D.2, and Table 2.
462   From these tables, we can conclude the following points.
463   • Basic representation decomposing has little defense effect. We can see that using the original
464     representation decomposing can not significantly decrease the ASR of backdoor attacks, and even
465     increase them in some cases (e.g., BadNet on ImageNet-1K). This observation implies backdoor
466     attacks have little indirect effect on model components since representation decomposing only
467     considers the direct effects of model components and neglects all indirect effects. Meanwhile, us-
468
        ing representation decomposing decreases CACC slightly (i.e., CACC drops by 2%∼3%), which
        implies indirect effects of decomposing have little effect on generalization.
469
470   • Decomp-Rep achieves strong defense performance. Based on the basic representation decom-
471     posing, Decomp-Rep further mean-ablates representations of heavily infected attention heads
472     (AHs), which greatly decreases the ASR of backdoor attacks and maintains the CACC. Specifi-
473
        cally, Decomp-Rep reduces the ASR of BadCLIP, a state-of-the-art backdoor attack, to near zero
        while maintaining the CACC, which verifies the superiority of Decomp-Rep.
474
475   • Decomp-Rep can further improve the defense performance of CleanCLIP. When using the fine-
476     tuned CLIP by CleanCLIP, Decomp-Rep can further reduce the ASR of backdoor attacks. This
477     observation validates the scalability of Decomp-Rep to existing defense methods (Decomp-Rep
478
        is plug-and-play to these defense methods).
479   • Decomp-Det achieves superior detection performance. We can see that Decomp-Det achieves
480     superior performance in all cases by a significant margin. Specifically, the average AUROC
481     performance of our method exceeds STRIP, SCALE-UP, and TeCo by 0.118, 0.220, and 0.187
482     respectively, which validates the superiority of Decomp-Det. Specifically, we found that Decomp-
483
        Det can achieve better detection performance against powerful backdoor attacks, e.g., BadCLIP.

532   5     C ONCLUSION
533
534   In this paper, we present a comprehensive empirical study of how backdoor attacks affect CLIP.
535   Our empirical findings reveal the attack preference of backdoor attacks on model components, the
536   difference in the locations of infected components, and the different effects of backdoor attacks
537   on the functionality of infected components. Inspired by these findings, we propose to repair rep-
538   resentations of infected components or filter backdoor samples. Experimental results validate the
539   empirical findings and the effectiveness of our methods. We hope that our findings can motivate
      more researchers to design effective defense methods against backdoor attacks on CLIP.

      A     R ELATED W ORKS
757
758
      Backdoor attacks and defenses on supervised learning. Backdoor attacks are serious security
759
      threats to machine learning systems (Li et al., 2022; Carlini & Terzis, 2022; Xu et al., 2022; Chen
760
      et al., 2021). Early research on backdoor attacks focused on designing a variety of triggers that
761   satisfy the practical application scenarios, mainly including invisible stealthy triggers (Chen et al.,
762   2017; Turner et al., 2019; Li et al., 2021; Doan et al., 2021; Nguyen & Tran, 2021; Gao et al., 2023;
763   Souri et al., 2022) and physical triggers (Chen et al., 2017; Wenger et al., 2021). To defend against
764   these attacks, researchers proposed a series of defense methods at different stages of developing
765   models, i.e., data cleaning in the pre-processing stage (Tran et al., 2018; Zeng et al., 2023; Liu et al.,
766   2023a; Qi et al., 2023), robust anti-backdoor training (Chen et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2022; Huang
767   et al., 2023), mitigation in the post-training stage (Min et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024; Zhu et al.,
768   2024b; Min et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2023; Xiang et al., 2022), and test-time detection in the infer-
769
      ence stage (Shi et al., 2023; Mo et al., 2024; Guo et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023b; Feng et al., 2023).
      Recently, researchers have paid much attention to the backdoor security of vision transformers and
770
      proposed customized backdoor attack and defense methods based on the characteristics of vision
771
      transformers (Yuan et al., 2023; Doan et al., 2023; Subramanya et al., 2024; Zheng et al., 2023).
772
773   Backdoor attacks and defenses on CLIP. As multimodal models achieve significant develop-
774   ment, researchers have paid much attention to the backdoor security on multimodal models (Walmer
775
      et al., 2022; Han et al., 2024; Liang et al., 2024; Zhu et al., 2024a; Yang et al., 2023b; Zhu et al.,
      2024c). Pioneer (Carlini & Terzis, 2022) disclosed that multimodal contrastive learning is suscep-
776
      tible to backdoor attacks. Furthermore, BadCLIP (Liang et al., 2023) designed a dual-embedding
777
      framework for backdoor attacks on CLIP by making visual trigger patterns approximate the textual
778   target semantics in the embedding space. To defend against backdoor attacks, RoCLIP (Yang et al.,
779   2023a) proposed robust multimodal contrastive learning during the pertaining stage by modifying
780   images’ captions. CleanCLIP (Bansal et al., 2023) aimed to fine-tune the backdoored CLIP by using
781   additional unimodal self-supervised loss. TIJO (Sur et al., 2023) focused on trigger inversion to
782   reverse-engineer the triggers in both modalities.
783
      Interpreting CLIP’s image representations. Although CLIP’s powerful visual representation
784   ability has achieved impressive performance on many downstream tasks, there is still a limited un-
785   derstanding of what information is encoded in the CLIP’s representations. To better understand
786   CLIP, there were a few works that attempt to interpret visual contents by text representations, such
787   as providing text descriptions for image regions in which a neuron is active (Hernandez et al., 2022),
788   projecting model features into a bank of text-based concepts (Yuksekgonul et al., 2023), and study-
789   ing entanglement in CLIP between images of words and natural images (Materzyńska et al., 2022).
790   Specifically, recent work (Gandelsman et al., 2024) had a further exploration of CLIP’s image rep-
791   resentations by decomposing them into text-explainable directions that are attributed to specific
792
      attention heads and image locations. Similarly, INViTE (Chen et al., 2024) presented a framework
      for interpreting ViT’s latent tokens with text explanations.

960   E.1      D ETAILED SETTINGS OF BACKDOOR ATTACKS
961
962   In the experiment, we use five backdoor attacks: BadNet (Gu et al., 2017), Blended (Chen et al.,
963   2017), Label Consistent (Turner et al., 2019), ISSBA (Li et al., 2021), and BadCLIP (Liang et al.,
964
      2023). Here, we introduce these methods in detail.
965   • BadNet3 is a seminal work on backdoor attacks in deep learning, generating poisoned examples
966     by stamping a small patch randomly into images and altering their labels to the target class. We
967     set the patch size to 16 pixels.
968   • Blended enhances the stealthiness of backdoor attacks from the perspective of the trigger. It
969     implements an invisible backdoor attack by blending the trigger with the original images linearly,
970     thus evading human detection. The blending ratio for the trigger is 0.2.
971
          3
              https://github.com/THUYimingLi/BackdoorBox

972
       • Label Consistent enhances the stealthiness of backdoor attack from the perspective of the label.
973      It employs generative models or adversarial perturbations to selectively poison images associated
974      with the target class.
975
976
       • ISSBA4 introduces an invisible attack that creates sample-specific triggers by encoding an
         attacker-specified string into benign images using an encoder-decoder network.
977
978    • BadCLIP5 proposes a backdoor attack on CLIP, which optimizes visual trigger patterns in a dual-
979      embedding guided framework to make the attack undetectable. For BadCLIP, we employ the
980      same parameter settings specified in the original paper.
981    For these backdoor attacks, we utilize the AdamW optimizer with an initial learning rate of 1e-5,
982    applying cosine scheduling over a total of five epochs with a batch size of 128.

       E.2     D ETAILED SETTINGS OF C LEAN CLIP
985
986    CleanCLIP6 (Bansal et al., 2023) defends against backdoor attacks in multimodal contrastive learn-
987    ing by optimizing the integration of multimodal contrastive and unimodal self-supervised losses
988    using a limited amount of clean data. Note that the backbone of the visual encoder in CleanCLIP
989    is ResNet-50. In this paper, we use the vision transformer (ViT-B/32) as the visual encoder. We
990    adapted the parameters used in the original paper to our case. Specifically, we randomly selected
991    10,0000 image-text pairs from CC3M as the fine-tuning data. The learning rates were set to 5e-6 for
992    BadNet, Blended, and BadCLIP, and 3e-6 for Blended and ISSBA on ImageNet-1K. The batch size
993
       was 64. The fine-tuning epoch was 10. Note that we did not blindly reduce attack success rates by
       adjusting the learning rates, but maintained clean accuracy of the fine-tuned model.

996    E.3     D ETAILED SETTINGS OF DETECTION METHODS
997
       In the experiment, we compare three backdoor detection methods: STRIP (Gao et al., 2019),
998
       SCALE-UP (Guo et al., 2023), and TeCo (Liu et al., 2023b). Here, we introduce these methods
999
       in detail.
1000
1001   • STRIP7 is the first black-box TTSD method that overlays various image patterns and observes the
1002     randomness of the predicted classes of the perturbed input to identify poisoned samples. In our
1003     experiments, for each input image, we use 64 clean images from the test data for superimposition.
1004   • SCALE-UP8 is also a method for black-box input-level backdoor detection that assesses the ma-
1005     liciousness of inputs by measuring the scaled prediction consistency (SPC) of labels under ampli-
1006     fied conditions, offering effective defense in scenarios with limited data or no prior information
1007     about the attack.
1008   • TeCo9 modifies input images with common corruptions and assesses their robustness through
1009     hard-label outputs, ultimately determining the presence of backdoor triggers based on a de-
1010     viation measurement of the results. In our experiments, considering concerns about runtime,
1011     we selected “elastic transform”, “gaussian noise”, “shot noise”, “impulse noise”, “motion blur”,
1012     “snow”, “frost”, “fog”, “brightness”, “contrast”, “pixelate”, and “jpeg compression” as methods
1013     for corrupting images. The maximum corruption severity was set to 6.
