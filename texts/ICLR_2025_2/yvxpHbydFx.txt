009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            This work addresses the critical question of why and when diffusion models, de-
012            spite their generative design, are capable of learning high-quality representations
013            in a self-supervised manner. We hypothesize that diffusion models excel in repre-
014            sentation learning due to their ability to learn the low-dimensional distributions of
015            image datasets via optimizing a noise-controlled denoising objective. Our empir-
016            ical results support this hypothesis, indicating that variations in the representation
017            learning performance of diffusion models across noise levels are closely linked to
018
               the quality of the corresponding posterior estimation. Grounded on this observa-
               tion, we offer theoretical insights into the unimodal representation dynamics of
019
               diffusion models as noise scales vary, demonstrating how they effectively learn
020
               meaningful representations through the denoising process. We also highlight the
021            impact of the inherent parameter-sharing mechanism in diffusion models, which
022            accounts for their advantages over traditional denoising auto-encoders in repre-
023            sentation learning.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Diffusion models, a new family of likelihood-based generative models, have demonstrated superior
029   performance among many generative tasks, including image generation (Alkhouri et al., 2024; Ho
030   et al., 2020; Rombach et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2024), video generation (Bar-Tal et al., 2024; Ho
031   et al., 2022), speech and audio synthesis (Kong et al., 2020; 2021), semantic editing (Roich et al.,
032   2022; Ruiz et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024a) and solving inverse problem (Chung et al., 2022; Song
033
      et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024; Alkhouri et al., 2023). At its core, diffusion models are learning a
      data distribution from training samples by imitating the non-equilibrium thermodynamic diffusion
034
      process (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015; Ho et al., 2020; Song et al., 2021). In the forward process,
035
      training samples are gradually combined with increasing Gaussian noise until the data structure is
036   completely destroyed while in the backward process, a model is trained to restore the structure from
037   the noised data (Hyvärinen & Dayan, 2005; Song et al., 2021).
038
039
      In addition to their impressive generative capabilities, recent studies (Baranchuk et al., 2021; Xiang
      et al., 2023; Mukhopadhyay et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024b; Tang et al., 2023) have highlighted the
040
      exceptional representation power of diffusion models, suggesting that they could serve as a unified
041
      foundation model for both generative and discriminative vision tasks. Specifically, recent evalua-
042   tions across various applications, including classification (Xiang et al., 2023; Mukhopadhyay et al.,
043   2023), semantic segmentation (Baranchuk et al., 2021), and image alignment (Tang et al., 2023),
044   show that diffusion models are capable of learning high-quality representations, often matching or
045   even surpassing the performance of previous state-of-the-art methods. However, it remains unclear
046   whether the representation capabilities of diffusion models stem from the diffusion process or the
047   denoising mechanism (Fuest et al., 2024). More fundamentally, given their generative design, when
048   and why diffusion models can learn high-quality representations in a self-supervised manner?
049   This work aims to address this question through a comprehensive investigation, both empirically
050   and theoretically, grounded in the formulation of denoising auto-encoders (DAEs) for learning dif-
051   fusion models (Vincent et al., 2008; 2010; Vincent, 2011). We hypothesize that diffusion models
052   can learn high-quality representations without supervision due to their superior ability to approx-
053   imate the low-dimensional distributions of image datasets, as supported by recent findings (Wang
      et al., 2024). Although image dataset can be very high-dimensional, recent results (Pope et al., 2021;

      are much lower than the ambient dimension, and it has shown that the number of samples to learn the
077

081   On the empirical side, we support our claim by reconciling several intriguing phenomena related
082
      to the quality of learned representations in diffusion models. Recent studies Zhang et al. (2023)
      reveal that diffusion models operate in two regimes: memorization and generalization, depending
083
      on training data size. In the memorization regime with limited samples, the model captures only the
084
      empirical distribution of training data without the ability to generate new samples. In contrast, in
085   the generalization regime, diffusion models are able to learn the underlying distribution. Our exper-
086   iments in Figure 2 confirm that high-quality representations are only learned in the generalization
087   regime with sufficient samples due to its ability of learning the underlying distribution. More impor-
088   tantly, in the generalization regime, we show that the quality of hidden representations in diffusion
089   models/DAEs follows a uni-modal curve (see Figure 1 and Figure 7): high-quality representations
090   are learned at an intermediate step close to the clean image, whereas the representation quality de-
091   grades as it approaches either pure noise or the clean image.
092   Building on these empirical observations, we provide theoretical insights using a noisy mixture of
093   low-rank Gaussian distributions. Our assumption captures the inherent low-dimensionality of the
094   image data distribution (Pope et al., 2021; Gong et al., 2019; Stanczuk et al., 2022), where the data
095   lies on a union of low-dimensional subspaces. We analyze the unimodal trend in representation
096   performance by relating it to the Class-specific Signal-to-Noise Ratio (CSNR). Specifically, we
097   consider the optimal posterior estimation function under our data assumption and show that the
098   CSNR is determined by the interplay between data “denoising” and class confidence rate as the
099
      noise scale increases. Additionally, our study reveals an implicit weight-sharing mechanism inherent
      in diffusion models, which helps explain their strengths compared to traditional one-step DAEs,
100
      particularly in the small noise regions.
101
102   Contribution of this work. In summary, our findings can be highlighted as follows:
103   • Linking posterior estimation ability of diffusion models to representation learning. Our em-
104     pirical results reveal that, much like the dynamics of diffusion representation learning, poste-
105     rior estimation quality across noise levels follows a similar unimodal curve. This indicates that
106     changes in representation quality are a direct reflection of changes in posterior estimation quality,
107     prompting us to explore representation learning through the more fundamental lens of posterior
        recovery.

      2     R EPRESENTATION L EARNING VIA DIFFUSION MODELS
140   In this section, we first review the fundamentals of diffusion models and outline the feature extraction
141   method used in this work. Following this, we illustrate the connection between diffusion posterior
142   estimation and representation learning, which serves as the foundation for the subsequent analysis
143   in Section 3.
144   2.1   P RELIMINARIES ON DENOISING DIFFUSION MODELS
145

146
      noising process by mapping an underlying data distribution, pdata , to a Gaussian distribution.

148

149

151   st and st σt represent the scaling of the signal and noise, respectively.
152   The reverse process. Noise is gradually removed from x1 following the reverse-time SDE:

182   2.2    E XTRACTING REPRESENTATIONS FROM DIFFUSION MODEL
183
184   In this work, we always refer representation quality to the quantitative metrics used in downstream
185
      tasks—such as accuracy in classification and adopt the following feature extraction setups to lever-
      age diffusion models for representation learning:
186
187   Use clean images as network inputs. First, we use the clean image x0 as input to the network in
188   contrast to conventional approaches that use the noisy image xt (Xiang et al., 2023; Baranchuk et al.,
189   2021; Tang et al., 2023). This setup aligns with the goal of representation learning: when training
190
      neural networks for classical representation tasks, whether in a supervised or self-supervised manner,
      it is standard practice to apply some kind of data augmentations or corruptions—such as cropping,
191
      color jittering, or masking. These augmentations improve the robustness of the trained model and
192
      enhance performance. However, during inference, clean, unaugmented images are typically used as
193   inputs. Similarly, in diffusion models, since our focus is on their role in representation learning, ad-
194   ditive Gaussian noise serves as a form of data augmentation, necessary only during training. During
195   inference, using the clean image x0 as input is sufficient. As demonstrated in Figure 1(c)-(d), this
196   approach preserves the overall unimodal representation dynamic while achieving better performance
197   at higher noise levels. As such, throughout the remainder of this paper, we use the clean data x0 as
198   input to the diffusion model, i.e., we always consider xθ (x0 , t) where t serves solely as an indicator
199   of the noise level for diffusion model to adopt during feature extraction.
200   Layer selection for representations. Second, we extract features only from the bottleneck layer
201   of the U-Net architecture (Ronneberger et al., 2015),1 following the protocols used in (Kwon et al.,
202   2022; Park et al., 2023).2 Unlike prior methods (Xiang et al., 2023; Baranchuk et al., 2021), we do
203
      not conduct a grid search for the optimal layer, as our focus is on understanding the process rather
      than achieving state-of-the-art results.

248   Unimodal curve of representation quality. Previous studies (Xiang et al., 2023; Baranchuk et al.,
249   2021; Tang et al., 2023) have empirically shown that the representation dynamics of diffusion models
250   follow a unimodal curve as the noise scale increases, across various tasks such as classification, seg-
251   mentation, and image correspondence. Our findings corroborate this observation, as demonstrated
252   in Figure 1(a), where the representation quality consistently exhibits a unimodal trend, regardless
253   of the specific network architecture or dataset used (see Figure 1(c)-(d)). In the following analysis,
254
      we argue that this unimodal behavior arises from subtle differences between the requirements of
      representation learning and the generative nature of diffusion models.
255
256   High-fidelity image generation demands that diffusion models capture every aspect of the data dis-
257   tribution—from coarse structures to fine details. In contrast, representation learning, particularly
258   for high-level tasks such as classification (Allen-Zhu & Li, 2022), prefers an abstract representa-
259   tion, where finer image details may even act as ‘noise’ that hinders performance. As shown in
260
      Figure 1(b), as the noise level increases, the predicted posteriors for clean input x0 transition from
      ‘fine’ to ‘coarse’ (Wang & Vastola, 2023; Choi et al., 2022), gradually removing fine-grained details.
261
      For the classification task in the plot, the best performance is achieved when the posterior estima-
262
      tion retains the essential information while discarding some class-irrelevant details. These findings
263   indicate a trade-off between generative quality and representation performance (Chen et al., 2024b),
264   prompting us to attribute variations in feature quality across noise levels to differences in posterior
265   prediction.

      3.1 A SSUMPTIONS OF L OW-D IMENSIONAL DATA D ISTRIBUTION
271
      Although real-world image datasets are high-dimensional in terms of pixel count and data volume,
272
      extensive empirical studies Gong et al. (2019); Pope et al. (2021); Stanczuk et al. (2022) suggest that
273
      their intrinsic dimensionality is considerably lower. Moreover, state-of-the-art large-scale diffusion
274   models (Peebles & Xie, 2023; Podell et al., 2023) commonly employ auto-encoders (Kingma, 2013)
275   to map images to a low-dimensional latent space (Rombach et al., 2022) for better training efficiency.
276   Consequently, image datasets often reside on a union of low-dimensional manifolds.
277
      In light of this, many recent studies of diffusion models have been focused on approximating low-
278
      dimensional distributions (Wang et al., 2024). Moreover, as union of low-dimensional manifolds
279
      can be locally approximated by a union of linear subspaces, it motivates us to model the underlying
280   data distribution as a mixture of low-rank Gaussians (MoLRG) (Wang et al., 2024). The data points
281   generated by MoLRG lie on a union of subspaces. Within each subspace, the data follows a Gaussian
282   distribution with a low-rank covariance matrix that represents the subspace basis. Formally, we
283   introduce a noisy version of the MoLRG distribution as follows:

399   the diffusion process helps counteract the intrinsic data noise δ. The noise ratio (σt /δ) can be
400   interpreted as the “denoising” rate, where a larger ratio indicates more data noise being canceled
401   out and vice versa. Meanwhile, h(ŵk , δ) represents the class confidence rate, with lower values
402   meaning less class-specific information is captured by the model. With σt increases from 0 to
403   ∞, the “denoising rate” rises accordingly, while the class confidence rate decreases monotonically.
404   Thus, from Theorem 1, we can derive the rationale behind the unimodal behavior of CSNR.
405   • The unimodal curve of CSNR. The unimodal curve is decided by the interplay between the
406     “denoising rate” and the class confidence rate as noise increases. As observed in Figure 4(c), the
407     “denoising rate” (σt2 /δ 2 ) increases monotonically with σt while the class confidence rate h(ŵk , δ)
408     monotonically declines. Initially, as σt increases, the class confidence rate remains relatively sta-
409
        ble due to its flat slope (as seen in Figure 4(c)), and an increasing “denoising rate” enhances
        the CSNR, resulting in improved posterior estimation. However, as indicated by (7), when σt
410
        becomes too large, h(ŵk , δ) approaches h(ŵl , δ), leading to a drop in CSNR, which limits the
411
        model’s ability to project x0 onto the correct signal space and ultimately impairs posterior esti-
412     mation. This interpretation is validated by the visualization in Figure 3. In the plot, each class
413     is represented by a colored straight line, while deviations from these lines correspond to the δ-
414     related noise term. Initially, increasing the noise scale effectively cancels out the δ-related data
415     noise, resulting in a cleaner posterior estimation and improved probing accuracy. However, as the
416     noise continues to increase, the class confidence rate drops, leading to an overlap between classes,
417     which ultimately degrades the feature quality and probing performance.
418
419
      Back to our real-world analogy, the proportion of data associated with δ represents class-irrelevant
      attributes or finer image details. The unimodal representation learning dynamic thus captures a “fine-
420
      to-coarse” shift (Choi et al., 2022; Wang & Vastola, 2023), where these details are progressively
421
      stripped away. During this process, peak representation performance is achieved at a balance point
422   where class-irrelevant attributes are eliminated, while class-essential information is preserved.

460   In Section 3, we analyzed diffusion representation dynamics with a focus on the denoising process,
461   assuming sufficient training data for learning the underlying distribution. In this section, we ex-

463   diffusion models’ representation learning dynamics.
464
      4.1 W EIGHT SHARING IN DIFFUSION MODELS HELPS REPRESENTATION LEARNING
465
466
      In this subsection, we demonstrate how the inherent weight-sharing mechanism in diffusion models,
      stemming from their loss design, enhances representation learning performances compared with
467
      traditional DAEs.
468
469   Previously, in Section 3, we analyzed the optimal posterior function by treating each noise level
470   independently. However, the training objective for diffusion models in (3) involves minimizing
471   the loss across all noise levels simultaneously, which results in interactions and parameter sharing
472
      among denoising subcomponents at different noise levels. We hypothesize that these interactions
      and parameter sharing create greater feature similarity across noise scales, effectively functioning as
473
      an implicit “ensemble” mechanism that enhances the performance of diffusion models compared to
474
      individual DAEs (Chen et al., 2024b), which accounts for the significant performance gap between
475   DAEs and diffusion models, as shown in Figure 4(a) and Figure 5(a).
476
      To test this hypothesis, we trained 10 individual DAEs, each at a different noise level, as well as a
477   single DDPM-based diffusion model on CIFAR10 and CIFAR100 datasets. We then conducted lin-
478   ear probing on the features extracted from both setups. To evaluate feature similarity, we calculated
479   the sliced Wasserstein distance (SWD) (Doan et al., 2024) between features for both diffusion and
480   DAE models at various noise levels and their corresponding features at σt = 0.06, which achieves
481   near-optimal accuracy for all scenarios.
482   As shown in Figure 6, diffusion models consistently outperform individual DAEs, particularly at
483   lower noise levels, where the performance gap is most pronounced. In these low-noise regions, due
484   to the almost negligible additive noise, individual DAEs are more likely to be trained as identity
485   functions, leading to trivial representations. In contrast, the parameter sharing in diffusion models
      alleviates this issue significantly. The SWD curve demonstrates an inverse correlation with the test

      4.2 T HE INFLUENCE OF DATA COMPLEXITY IN DIFFUSION REPRESENTATION LEARNING
514
515
      So far, our analyses are based on the assumption that the training dataset contains sufficient samples
      for the diffusion model to learn the underlying distribution. Interestingly, if this assumption is
516
      violated by training the model on insufficient data, the unimodal representation learning dynamic
517
      disappears and the probing accuracy also drops severely.
518
      As illustrated in Figure 7, we train 2 different UNets following the EDM (Karras et al., 2022)
519
      configuration with training dataset size ranging from 25 to 215 . The unimodal curve emerges only
520   when the dataset size exceeds 212 , whereas smaller datasets produce flat curves.
521
      The underlying reason for this observation is that, when training data is limited, diffusion models
522   memorize all individual data points rather than learn the true underlying data structure (Wang et al.,
523   2024). In this scenario, the model memorizes an empirical distribution that lacks meaningful low-
524   dimensional structures and thus deviates from the setting in our theory, leading to the loss of the
525   unimodal representation dynamic. To confirm this, we calculated the generalization score, which
526   measures the percentage of generated data that does not belong to the training dataset, as defined in
527   (Zhang et al., 2023). As shown in Figure 2, representation learning only achieves strong accuracy
528   and displays the unimodal dynamic when the generalization score approaches 1, aligning with our
529   theoretical assumptions.
530
531   5   C ONCLUSION
532
533
      In this work, we establish a link between distribution recovery, posterior estimation, and repre-
534   sentation learning, providing the first theoretical study of diffusion-based representation learning
535   dynamics across varying noise scales. Using a low-dimensional mixture of low-rank Gaussians,
536   we show that the unimodal representation learning dynamic arises from the interplay between data
537   denoising and class specification. Additionally, our analysis highlights the inherent weight-sharing
538   mechanism in diffusion models, demonstrating its benefits for peak representation performance as
539   well as its limitations in optimizing high-noise regions due to increased complexity. Experiments on
      both synthetic and real datasets validate our findings.

763   A.1   R ELATED W ORKS
764
765   Denoising auto-encoders. Denoising autoencoders (DAEs) are trained to reconstruct corrupted
766   images to extract semantically meaningful information, which can be applied to various vision (Vin-
767   cent et al., 2008; 2010) and language downstream tasks (Lewis, 2019). Related to our analysis of the
768   weight-sharing mechanism, several studies have shown that training with a noise scheduler can en-
769   hance downstream performance (Chandra & Sharma, 2014; Geras & Sutton, 2014; Zhang & Zhang,
770
      2018). On the theoretical side, prior works have studied the learning dynamics (Pretorius et al.,
      2018; Steck, 2020) and optimization landscape (Kunin et al., 2019) through the simplified linear
771
      DAE models.
772
773
      Diffusion-based representation learning. Diffusion-based representation learning Fuest et al.
774
      (2024) has demonstrated significant success in various downstream tasks, including image classifi-
775   cation (Xiang et al., 2023; Mukhopadhyay et al., 2023; Deja et al., 2023), segmentation (Baranchuk
776   et al., 2021), correspondence (Tang et al., 2023), and image editing (Shi et al., 2024). To further en-
777   hance the utility of diffusion features, knowledge distillation (Yang & Wang, 2023; Li et al., 2023)
778   methods have been proposed, aiming to bypass the computationally expensive grid search for the
779   optimal t in feature extraction and improving downstream performance. Beyond directly using in-
780   termediate features from pre-trained diffusion models, research efforts has also explored novel loss
781   functions (Abstreiter et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2023) and network modifications (Hudson et al.,
782   2024; Preechakul et al., 2022) to develop more unified generative and representation learning ca-
783
      pabilities within diffusion models. Unlike the aforementioned efforts, our work focuses more on
      understanding the representation learning capabilities of diffusion models.
