009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Fine-tuning techniques such as Reinforcement Learning with Human Feedback
012            (RLHF) and Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) allow us to steer Large Lan-
013            guage Models (LLMs) to be align better with human preferences. Alignment is
014            equally important in text-to-image generation. Recent adoption of DPO, specif-
015            ically Diffusion-DPO, for Text-to-Image (T2I) diffusion models has proven to
016            work effectively in improving visual appeal and prompt-image alignment. The
017            mentioned works fine-tune on Pick-a-Pic dataset, consisting of approximately one
018
               million image preference pairs, collected via crowdsourcing at scale. However, do
               all preference pairs contribute equally to alignment fine-tuning? Preferences can
019
               be subjective at times and may not always translate into effectively aligning the
020
               model. In this work, we investigate the above-mentioned question. We develop
021            a quality metric to rank image preference pairs and achieve effective Diffusion-
022            DPO-based alignment fine-tuning.We show that the SD-1.5 and SDXL models
023            fine-tuned using the top 5.33% of the data perform better both quantitatively and
024            qualitatively than the models fine-tuned on the full dataset. The code is available
025            at this link.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Currently, diffusion-based Text-to-Image (T2I) (Rombach et al., 2021; Podell et al., 2023; Chen
031   et al., 2023; 2024) models are state-of-the-art in image generation. These models are trained in
032   a single stage on a large-scale dataset of images scraped from the internet, enabling them to have
033
      huge knowledge. However, their outputs often fail to align with human preferences, as they are
      not explicitly optimized for this purpose. In contrast, Large Language Models (LLMs) undergo
034
      training in two distinct stages: the first stage involves pre-training on large web-scale datasets, while
035
      the second stage uses Supervised Fine-tuning (SFT) and Reinforcement Learning based on Human
036   Feedback (RLHF) to align outputs with human preferences. While significant progress has been
037   made in alignment fine-tuning for LLMs, aligning T2I outputs with human preferences remains a
038   difficult challenge.
039
      Recent works have begun exploring how to better align T2I models with human preferences.
040
      These approaches can be broadly classified into two broad categories – they either use a reward
041
      model trained on human preference data to guide the T2I model, or they directly fine-tune the T2I
042   model on pairwise preference data. Reinforcement Learning (RL) based approaches like Align-
043   prop(Prabhudesai et al., 2023), ImageReward(Xu et al., 2023), DDPO(Black et al., 2023) do not
044   scale well to large datasets and are highly prone to problems like overfitting and mode collapse.
045   Additionally, training good reward models and using them to fine-tune diffusion models introduces
046   significant operational challenges, as it adds a lot of computational overhead.
047
      To address this gap in diffusion model alignment, approaches like Diffusion-DPO(Wallace et al.,
048   2023a) have emerged, reformulating the loss function to completely remove the reward model and
049   directly fine-tune on pairwise image preference data, which solves the problems of traditional RL-
050   based approaches. In recent works, more preference alignment approaches like Diffusion-KTO(Li
051   et al., 2024) and IPO(Azar et al., 2023) have emerged, building on Diffusion-DPO to further improve
052   diffusion model alignment. However, all of these approaches share a common drawback: they either
053   require pairwise preference data or a label of “good” or “bad” for each image. These labels, collected
      from human based annotators, can be noisy as preference is subjective. Additionally, these labels do

      To address these shortcomings, we propose our novel approach — Effective Text-to-Image Align-
114
      ment with Quality Aware Pair Ranking. Specifically, we introduce a quality metric to assess the
115   quality of a pair of images and the corresponding prompt as a fine-tuning sample. We use a carefully
116   devised metric based on AI reward model score to rank all samples from the alignment fine-tuning

132
133   The alignment of diffusion models with human preferences has become a critical area of research,
134   especially as these models are being used increasingly to generate content with specific objectives.
135   Alignment of diffusion models to human preferences can largely be categorized into two broad
136   categories - with a reward model and without a reward model. Approaches like DRAFT(Clark
137   et al., 2024), AlignProp(Prabhudesai et al., 2023), ReFl(Xu et al., 2023), and ImageReward(Xu
138   et al., 2023) directly backpropagate the gradients from a differentiable reward model to fine-tune
139   the diffusion model. These approaches work for a finite vocabulary set, but do not generalize well
140   to an open vocabulary set and struggle to optimize for complex reward functions like CLIP score
141
      Radford et al. (2021). Different reward models are used to fine-tune the diffusion model based on the
      end-user task. DPOK(Fan et al., 2023) and DDPO(Black et al., 2023) are Reinforcement Learning
142
      based approaches that maximize the score from the reward model over a set of limited prompts which
143
      limits the performance of these methods as the number of prompts increases. DOODL(Wallace et al.,
144   2023b) attempts to generate more aesthetically pleasing images by doing iterative improvements to
145   the generation at run-time.
146
147
      The other set of approaches which do not use an explicit reward model are inspired from the suc-
      cess of direct preference optimization. The recent work of Diffusion-DPO(Wallace et al., 2023a)
148
      is able to fine-tune a diffusion model on a dataset of prompts and image pairs by reformulating the
149
      loss function. Diffusion-KTO(Li et al., 2024) builds on top of Diffusion-DPO and does not require
150   pairwise preference data, allowing fine-tuning of diffusion models on single image feedback. Ad-
151   ditionally, D3PO(Yang et al., 2023) suggest creating its own image pairs from a set of prompts and
152   then using a reward model to identify preferred images. Despite all these advances, these approaches
153   still suffer from noisy pairwise preference datasets and over-optimization.
154
      Most diffusion models (Rombach et al., 2021; Podell et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023; 2024; Esser
155
      et al., 2024) are sometimes trained in two stages, where the first stage involves training on a broad
156   dataset followed by fine-tuning on carefully selected good dataset that is more preferred by humans.
157   These models do full fine-tuning of the diffusion model on a subset of ’good’ images which are
158   selected via an AI reward model, usually an aesthetic classifier. Parrot(Lee et al., 2024) uses Pareto-
159   optimal sorting to rank images on multiple reward scores to select the optimal subset. Models like
160   DALLE-3(Shi et al., 2020), SD3(Esser et al., 2024), and CogView(Ding et al., 2021) re-caption
161   existing web-scraped datasets to improve text fidelity. However, these approaches require large
      amount of resources to caption millions of Images.

165   3.1   BACKGROUND
166
167   Diffusion-DPO (Wallace et al., 2023a) considers a setting with a fixed dataset D = {(c, xw , xl )}
168   where each samples consists of a prompt or caption c, a winning image xw , and a losing image xl .
169   The aim is to train a new model pθ on these preference pairs, which is more aligned with human
170   preferences compared to the reference model pref . Diffusion-DPO achieves this by completely
171   removing the reward model and reformulating the loss as a function to encourages more denoising
172
      at xw than xl .
173   However, as we can observe from Figure 1a, human preference is subjective, and this sometimes
174   results in noisy labels. Existing approaches do not try to identify these noisy labels and use the
175   entire dataset for fine-tuning as is. For instance, Diffusion-DPO selects all image preference pairs,
176   excluding only those with ties, without any validation of the preferences.
177
178
179   3.2   Q UALITY METRIC FOR RANKING PREFERENCE PAIRS
180
181   We are now able to capture human preferences from online forums. While all the preferences are
182   made by humans, various factors can affect their judgement. Since they are not fully vetted, the
183   reviewer might have malicious intent, different creative, domain and technical knowledge. Most
184   importantly, preferences are highly subjective. Therefore, we look for pairs that are more aligned
185
      with overall preference.
186   Diffusion-KTO (Li et al., 2024) selects samples where win rate of an image w.r.t all the images it was
187   compared with i.e it selects a pair if the winning image won in all the comparisons of the image and
188   losing image lost in all the comparisons made with it. Though this might be a theoretically sound
189   approach, considering the Pick-a-Pic(Kirstain et al., 2023) dataset, less than 5% of the images were
190   compared more than five times and only 25% of the images were actually compared more than once.
191
      These low numbers, combined with the fact that the comparisons were made by random individuals,
      make this an unreliable metric.
192
193   We propose a quality metric for each sample, where a higher score indicates a greater likelihood of
194   the pair being correctly labeled. Through experiments, we demonstrate that fine-tuning with higher-
195   quality pairs leads to improved model performance. However, as lower-quality pairs are introduced,
196   performance begins to decline, supporting the importance of ranking image preference pairs.
197   Consider any paired preference dataset D = {(c1 , x1w , x1l ), (c2 , x2w , x2l ), ...., (cn , xnw , xnl )}, where
198   each sample consists of a caption (c), a winning image (xw ), and a losing image(xl ). We use an
199   AI reward model trained to model human preferences to get the probability of the winning image
200   to be winning and the losing image to be losing. We use the HPSv2(Wu et al., 2023) model that is
201   trained on a expert-reviewed dataset for human preference to output preference for image given the
202   prompt. This preference value will ranges from 0-1, allowing us to interpret them as the probability
203
      of the image being preferred. We refer to this model as ψ. Now quality Q of each sample pair can
      be written as
204
205
206
207                                   Q(c, xw , xl ) = ψ(xw /c) ∗ (1 − ψ(xl /c))                                   (1)
208
209
210   This can be viewed as probability of pair being correct i.e. probability of the winning image being
211   the winning image and the losing image being the losing image.
212   In Figure 1b, we see a sharp decrease in quality score for the initial 100k pairs, followed by a gradual
213   decline for the majority of the dataset, and finally, another sharp drop towards the end, where the
214   samples are of the poorest quality. This plot illustrates that the dataset has good samples where the
215   winning image is clearly better, average samples where the preference is more subjective and bad
      samples where the reward model does not agree with human labels.

      4.1   DATASET
219
220   We demonstrate the efficacy of our model on the Pick-a-Pic v2 dataset (Kirstain et al., 2023), which
221   is a crowd sourced dataset. A human reviewer is presented with a caption and a pair of images
222   generated by T2I models like Stable Diffusion 2.1 (Rombach et al., 2021), Dreamlike Photoreal
223   2.05, and Stable Diffusion XL (Podell et al., 2023) variants. The reviewer selects one of the two
224   presented images as more preferred or marks it as a tie. The dataset contains 1 million rows split
225   into 959.5k rows, 20.5k rows, 20.5k rows of train, validation and test sets respectively. The training
226
      set contains approximately 58k distinct captions.
227
228   4.2   H YPER - PARAMETERS
229
      We run experiments on SD1.5(Rombach et al., 2021) and SDXL(Podell et al., 2023) models.
230
      For pairwise preference fine-tuning we use the fine-tuning approach as highlighted in Diffusion-
231   DPO(Wallace et al., 2023a). For both set of experiments we use the ADAMW optimizer. For all
232   SD1.5 and SDXL experiments we use a batch size of 128. All experiments are run on a cluster
233   of 8 NVIDIA 80 GB A100 GPUs. We train at fixed square resolution of 512x512 for SD1.5 and
234   1024x1024 for SDXL. We train for 1 epoch with a learning rate of 1e−4 for SD1.5 and 1e−5 for
235   SDXL. In line with the Diffusion-DPO paper, we use a Beta value of 2000 for SD1.5 and 5000 for
236   SDXL. We do not use any dataset augmentations and keep learning rate constant with no warm-up.
237   For all our experiments we fine-tune using the LoRA approach and use a rank of 64 for both SD1.5
238   and SDXL.
239
240   4.3   E VALUATION
241
      To verify the effectiveness of our approach we compare against the state-of-the-art human prefer-
242
      ence learning approaches like Diffusion-DPO (Wallace et al., 2023a) fine-tuned on the entire training
243
      dataset. As we use the LoRA technique, we also fine-tune LoRAs for the state-of-the-art approaches
244   and compare against them. We evaluate all checkpoints on the Pick-a-Pic validation set (Kirstain
245   et al., 2023), which consists of 500 unique prompts. We choose four AI reward models: ImageRe-
246   ward (Xu et al., 2023), Pickscore, HPS-v2 Wu et al. (2023) and Laion aesthetics classifier. ImageRe-
247   ward is the first general-purpose text-to-image human preference reward model, which is trained on
248   a total of 137k pairs of expert comparisons. PickScore is a CLIP-based scoring model with a variant
249   of InstructGPT’s (Ouyang et al., 2022) reward model objective. Laion aesthetics classifier is also a
250   CLIP based model with a pretrained MLP that is used to measure the aesthetic quality of an image.
251   We also present scores from HPS-v2 scoring model on the HPS-v2 test set, which consists of 3200
252
      prompts. HPSv2 is a preference prediction model trained on the HPD-v2 dataset. HPS-v2 can be
      used to compare images generated with the same prompt. Additionally, we perform a user study to
253
      compare our approach to the state-of-the-art Diffusion-DPO. Similar to Diffusion-DPO, we employ
254
      reviewers to select the preferred generation under three different criteria: Q1 General Preference
255   (Which image do you prefer given the prompt?), Q2 Visual Appeal (prompt not considered) (Which
256   image is more visually appealing?) Q3 Prompt Alignment (Which image better fits the text descrip-
257   tion?). Five responses are collected for each comparison with majority vote (3+) being considered
258   the collective decision. For the user study, we randomly sample 25 prompts from each of the four
259   sub-sections of the HPS-v2 test set: photos, anime, paintings and concept-art.

503   6   L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
504
505   In this work, our focus was primarily on training a better preference optimized model using a min-
506   imal fraction of the dataset. However, we acknowledge that there are limitations to our approach.
507
      Our approach relies on a quality metric for selecting training pairs, which could potentially overlook
      specific edge cases. While we discard the ambiguous preference pairs, future works could find a
508
      way to leverage this data as well without any performance loss. Future works could also explore the
509
      use of the quality metric as a dynamic threshold, not just for ranking pairs but also for constructing
510   pairwise preference datasets that balance high-quality and nuanced samples.

513   7   C ONCLUSION
514
515   In this paper, we address the problem of optimal fine-tuning of diffusion models to better align
516   them with human preferences. Unlike previous approaches, we solve this problem by introducing a
517   quality metric that prioritizes high-quality preference pairs and fine-tune in a sorted fashion on this
518
      dataset. We demonstrate that our data ranking strategy significantly enhances diffusion model align-
      ment, achieving superior results across multiple AI-based metrics and human evaluators. Our exper-
519
      iments show that models fine-tuned with less than top 10% of the Pick-a-Pick v2 dataset outperform
520
      baseline models in both quantitative metrics and human preference evaluations. We run multiple
521   ablations to showcase the effectiveness of our data ranking approach across multiple methods. We
522   validate our initial hypothesis that not all preference pairs contribute equally, and fine-tuning on the
523   entire dataset can be detrimental. By applying our fine-tuning strategy alongside early stopping, one
524   can significantly enhance training efficiency, leading to a more robust and powerful model.
