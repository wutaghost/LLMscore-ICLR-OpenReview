009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Diffusion models have achieved remarkable success in Text-to-Image generation
012            tasks, leading to the development of many commercial models. However, recent
013            studies have reported that diffusion models often repeatedly generate memorized
014            images in train data when triggered by specific prompts, potentially raising social
015            issues ranging from copyright to privacy concerns. To sidestep the memorization,
016            there have been recent studies for developing memorization mitigation methods
017            for diffusion models. Nevertheless, the lack of benchmarks hinders the assessment
018
               of the true effectiveness of these methods. In this work, we present MemBench,
               the first benchmark for evaluating image memorization mitigation methods. Our
019
               benchmark includes a large number of memorized image trigger prompts in var-
020
               ious Text-to-Image diffusion models. Furthermore, in contrast to the prior work
021            evaluating mitigation performance only on trigger prompts, we present metrics
022            evaluating on both trigger prompts and general prompts, so that we can see whether
023            mitigation methods address the memorization issue while maintaining performance
024            for general prompts. Through our MemBench evaluation, we revealed that existing
025            memorization mitigation methods notably degrade overall performance of diffusion
026            models and need to be further developed.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Text-to-Image (T2I) generation has shown significant advancements and successes with the advance
032   of diffusion models. Compared to previous generative models, text-conditional diffusion models
033
      excel in generating diverse and high quality images from user-desired text prompts, which has led
      to the vast release of commercial models such as MidJourney. However, recent studies (Somepalli
034
      et al., 2023a;b; Carlini et al., 2023) have revealed that certain text prompts tend to keep replicating
035
      images in the train dataset which can cause private data leakage leading to potentially serious privacy
036   issues. This issue has already triggered controversy in the real world: specific prompts containing the
037   term “Afghan” have been known to reproduce copyrighted images of the Afghan girl when using
038   MidJourney (Wen et al., 2024b). One of the major issues with such prompts is that, regardless of
039   initial random noise leveraged in the reverse process of the diffusion model, they always invoke
040   almost or exactly same memorized images (Wen et al., 2024b; Carlini et al., 2023; Webster, 2023).
041
      To address this matter, Wen et al. (2024b) and Somepalli et al. (2023b) have proposed mitigation
042   methods to prevent the regeneration of identical images in the train dataset invoked from certain text
043   prompts. However, the evaluation of these memorization mitigation methods has lacked rigor and
044   comprehensiveness due to the absence of benchmarks. As an adhoc assessment method, the current
045   studies (Wen et al., 2024b; Somepalli et al., 2023b) have adopted the following workaround: 1)
046   simulating memorization by fine-tuning T2I diffusion models for overfitting on a separate small and
047   specific dataset of {image, prompt} pairs, and 2) assessing whether the images used in the fine-tuning
048   are reproduced from the query prompts after applying mitigation methods. However, it remains
049   unclear whether such results can be extended to practical scenarios with the existing large-scale
050
      pre-trained diffusion models and can represent the effectiveness for resolving memorization.
051   In this work, we present MemBench, the first benchmark for evaluating image memorization
052   mitigation methods for diffusion models. Our MemBench includes the following key features to
053   ensure effective evaluation: (1) MemBench provides 3,000, 1,500, 309, and 1,352 memorized image
      trigger prompts for Stable Diffusion 1, 2, DeepFloydIF (Shonenkov et al., 2023), and Realistic


                                                        1

054
      Vision (CivitAI, 2023), respectively. In contrast, previous work (Webster, 2023) only provided 325,
055   210, 162, and 354 prompts. By increasing the number of prompts, we enhance the reliability of
056   the evaluation. (2) We take into account a general prompt scenario to assess the side-effects of
057   mitigation methods, which has been overlooked in prior work. The prior mitigation methods (Wen
058   et al., 2024b; Ren et al., 2024; Somepalli et al., 2023b) have been evaluated solely on memorized
059   image trigger prompts, but has often exposed the side-effect of performance degrading. Ideally, the
060   performance on general prompts should be maintained even after mitigation methods are deployed.
061   (3) We suggest to use multiple metrics. As previous mitigation works (Wen et al., 2024b; Somepalli
062   et al., 2023b) have measured, MemBench includes SSCD (Pizzi et al., 2022), which measures the
063
      similarity between memorized and generated images, and CLIP Score (Hessel et al., 2021), which
      measures Text-Image alignment. Additionally, MemBench involves Aesthetic Score (Schuhmann
064
      et al., 2022) to assess image quality, which has been overlooked by prior work and allows to penalize
065
      unuseful trivial solutions. (4) We propose the reference performance that mitigation methods should
066   achieve to be considered effective. In previous works (Ren et al., 2024; Wen et al., 2024b; Somepalli
067   et al., 2023b), the effectiveness of mitigation methods has been demonstrated by measuring the
068   decrease in SSCD and the extent to which the CLIP Score is maintained before and after applying
069   the mitigation method. However, this does not necessarily confirm whether image memorization has
070   been adequately mitigated. Therefore, we provide guidelines on the target values.
071
      Through applying mitigation methods in MemBench, we observe the following: When these methods
072   are applied to the image generation of memorized prompts, both Text-Image Alignment and image
073   quality decrease. Additionally, We observe a significant increase in the standard deviation of the
074   Aesthetic Score, which highlights the generation of very low-quality images. In the general prompt
075   scenario, mitigation methods degrade generation performance, making practical application difficult.
076
      Our additional contribution lies in offering an effective algorithm to search for memorized image
077
      trigger prompts. The absence of such benchmarks originates from the significant challenge of
078   collecting prompts that induce memorized images. Existing searching methods (Carlini et al., 2023;
079   Webster et al., 2023) require extensive computational resources, large system memory, and access to
080   the diffusion model’s training data to function. Furthermore, with the LAION dataset now private1 ,
081   these methods have become unusable. In contrast, our proposed searching algorithm, based on
082   Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC), offers a more efficient approach to searching for problematic
083   prompts directly within an open token space, without relying on any dataset. Notably, our method is
084   currently the only available approach that can operate under these constraints.

      2       R ELATED W ORK
088
089
      Memorization Mitigation Methods. Memorization mitigation methods are divided into two cate-
      gories: the inference time methods and the training time methods. The inference time methods aim to
090
      prevent the generation of images that are already memorized in pretrained diffusion models during
091
      the generation process. Somepalli et al. (2023b) propose a rule-based text embedding augmentation
092   to mitigate memorization. This includes adding Gaussian noise to text embeddings or inserting
093   random tokens in the prompt. Wen et al. (2024b) propose a loss that predicts if a prompt will induce
094   a memorized image, and present a mitigation strategy that applies adversarial attacks on this loss
095   to modify the text embeddings of trigger prompts. Both of these works evaluate their methods
096   by intentionally overfitting the diffusion model on specific small {image, text} pairs to induce the
097   memorization effect, and then checking whether the images are regenerated from the corresponding
098   prompts when their methods are applied. Ren et al. (2024) analyze the impact of trigger prompts on
099   the cross-attention layer of diffusion models and propose a corresponding mitigation method.
100   Train time methods aim to prevent diffusion models from memorizing training data during model
101   training by employing specific training techniques. Although several methods (Daras et al., 2024; Liu
102   et al., 2024) have been proposed, experiments have been conducted only on small models and datasets
103   such as CIFAR-10 and CelebHQ. While some experiments (Ren et al., 2024; Wen et al., 2024b) have
104   been conducted on large models such as Stable Diffusion, they only assess whether the fine-tuning
105   dataset is memorized when fine-tuning the model. To date, no train time mitigation method has been
106
      tested by training large-scale diffusion models from scratch to evaluate its effectiveness.

      In this work, we focus on the inference time methods, considering the practical scenarios of utilizing
109   existing large-scale pre-trained diffusion models, such as Stable Diffusion. To effectively evaluate
110   these methods, we introduce MemBench, which provides sufficient test data and appropriate metrics

112
      Training Data Extraction Attack. Our MemBench is constructed by our proposed computational
113
      method that shares a similar vein with the following attack methods. Carlini et al. (2023) propose a
114
      method to search for memorized image trigger prompts in Stable Diffusion. In the pre-processing
115   stage, they embed the entire training set of Stable Diffusion into the CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) feature
116   space and cluster these embeddings to identify the most repeated images. In the post-processing stage,
117   Stable Diffusion is used to generate 500 images for each prompt corresponding to these clustered
118   images. The similarity among these 500 generated images is measured, and only those prompts that
119   produce highly similar images are sampled. Finally, image retrieval is performed on the training
120   data using generated images from these selected prompts to verify if the generated images match
121   the training data images. The pre-processing involves CLIP embedding and clustering of 160M
122   images, while the post-processing involves generating 175M images, i.e., computationally demanding.
123
      Webster (2023) propose an advanced searching algorithm. In the pre-processing stage, an encoder is
      trained to compress CLIP embeddings. Then, 2B CLIP embeddings are compressed and clustered
124
      using KNN (Webster et al., 2023). In the post-processing stage, Webster introduces an effective
125
      method that performs a few inferences of the diffusion model to predict whether a prompt will induce
126   memorized images. This method is applied to 20M prompts acquired from the pre-processing stage.
127
128   Both methods share common bottlenecks: they are memory inefficient and require extremely high
129
      computational costs. Moreover, the most fundamental problem is their reliance on training data as
      candidate trigger prompts. With LAION becoming inaccessible2 , these methods can no longer be
130
      reproducible and utilized. However, our method can search more for trigger prompts efficiently than
131
      those methods even without any pre-processing steps and any dataset.
132
133   In another line of research, Chen et al. (2024) propose a method for extracting training data from
134   unconditional diffusion models. In contrast, several studies (Somepalli et al., 2023b; Gu et al., 2023)
135
      indicate that conditioning plays a critical role in memorization, with unconditional models being less
      susceptible to it. Furthermore, since T2I diffusion models are the ones widely applied in real-world
136
      scenarios, our work focuses on constructing a memorization benchmark for T2I diffusion models.
137
138   Note that, while relevant, our computational method is proposed to construct a benchmark dataset for
139   specific target diffusion models, not for applying our method to actually attack models.
140   Benchmark Dataset. Since the only existing dataset that can be used for evaluating mitigation
141   methods is the small dataset released by Webster (2023), Ren et al. (2024) evaluate their method on the
142   Webster dataset, while it is not originally purposed as a benchmark dataset. The dataset is constructed
143   by the training data extraction attack method proposed by Webster, which is not scalable; thus, the
144   dataset remains a small scale. Also, Ren et al. did not measure the loss of semantic preservation after
145   mitigation, which is an important criterion but overlooked. Our benchmark is the first benchmark for
146   evaluating those mitigation methods with carefully designed metrics and sufficient test data.

148   3       S EARCHING M EMORIZED I MAGE T RIGGER P ROMPT WITH MCMC
149
150   We present our proposed scalable computational method to construct our MemBench dataset. Given
151   a pre-trained diffusion model, we computationally search memorized image trigger text prompt. In
152   this section, we first brief the preliminaries, formulate the search as an optimization problem, and
153   propose a Markov Chain Monte Carlo algorithm.

213   Our objective is to construct a memorized image trigger prompts dataset and verify corresponding
214   memorized images, i.e. to construct Dmem = {p | E[M(x(ϵθ , p), Dtrain )] > κ, p ∈ T } where
215   κ is the threshold and T is space of all possible prompts. As mentioned in Section 2, the prior
      works (Carlini et al., 2023; Webster, 2023) utilized Dtrain to search for candidate prompts that could

      become Dmem . They then generated images from these candidate prompts and conducted image
217   retrieval to find memorized images within Dtrain which is expensive. Moreover, since the training
218   dataset, LAION, is no longer accessible, this approach becomes infeasible. Thus, we approach the
219   problem from a different perspective. We search for candidate prompts that could become Dmem
220   without using Dtrain . Then, we generate images from these candidate prompts and use a Reverse
221   Image Search API3 to find images on the web akin to generated ones by regarding the web as the
222   training set. Finally, we perform a human verification process.

226   Given a finite set W containing all possible words (tokens), where |W| = m, we model a sentence p
227   with n words as an ordered tuple drawn from the Cartesian product of W, represented as P = W n .
228   To solve the optimization problem, we treat Dθ (·) as a negative energy function and model the target
229   Boltzmann distribution π such that higher values of Dθ (·) correspond to higher probabilities as

295   3.4    DATASET C ONSTRUCTION BY L EVERAGING MCMC
296
297   We conduct dataset construction in two stages: 1) using a masked sentence as the prior and employing
298
      MCMC to find memorized image trigger prompts, and 2) using the memorized image trigger prompts
      as the prior for augmentation through MCMC.
299
300   Using Masked Sentence as Prior. This stage aims to discover new memorized images. The
301   sentence is initialized with sentence of length n [MASK] token, i.e. p0 = {[MASK], [MASK], ... ,
302   [MASK]}. We then employ Algorithm 1 initialized with p0 to obtain the candidate prompt. Similar to
303   the conventional approach (Carlini et al., 2023) to extract train images, we then generate 100 images
304   for this prompt and leverage DBSCAN (Ester et al., 1996) clustering algorithm with SSCD (Pizzi
305   et al., 2022) to extract images forming at least 20 nodes. Those images are employed to Reverse
306
      Image Search API to find train image sources and human verification is conducted.
307   Using Found Trigger Prompts as Prior. This stage aims to augment memorized image trigger
308   prompts. We leverage the prompts found in the previous stage or those provided by Webster (2023) as
309   the prior, π0 . In this process, we employ a slightly modified algorithm to enhance diversity. Instead of
310   running a single chain for one prompt, we run n separate chains for each word position in an n-length
311   sentence, treating each position as the first updating index in Gibbs sampling. This method ensures a
312   varied exploration of the prompt space. We then save the top 100 prompts with the highest Dθ (·). We
313   retained all prompts generated during the MCMC sampling process and then selected 20 augmented
314   prompts per original prompt, considering diversity. The detailed process is provided in Appendix C.

      4.2   E FFICACY OF M EMORIZED I MAGE T RIGGER P ROMPT S EARCHING
352
353
      In this section, we validate the efficiency of our method in discovering memorized images without
354
      access to Dtrain . The task of finding memorized image trigger prompts without Dtrain is defined
355   as follows: without any prior information, the method must automatically find trigger prompts that
356   induce memorized images. This involves: 1) selecting candidate prompts, 2) generating 100 images
357   for each candidate prompt, 3) applying DBSCAN (Ester et al., 1996) clustering with SSCD to get
358   candidate images and using a Reverse Image Search API to verify those images’ presence on the web.
359   To the best of our knowledge, this task is novel, so we provide naive baselines. As the first baseline,
360   we perform a greedy search by measuring Dθ for all prompts in the prompt dataset and selecting the
361   top 200 prompts as candidate prompts. For the prompt dataset, we leveraged DiffusionDB, which
362   contains 13M prompts collected from diffusion model users. Additionally, we provide three other
363
      baselines, all of which are algorithms that solve optimization problems in the prompt space. For two
      of these baselines, we adapt ZeroCap (Tewel et al., 2022) and ConZIC (Zeng et al., 2023), methods
364
      designed to maximize the CLIP Score for zero-shot image captioning, by replacing their objective
365
      function with Dθ . Similarly, we also adapt PEZ (Wen et al., 2024a) by substituting its objective
366   function with Dθ to serve as another baseline. For each of these three methods, we conducted 200
367   iterations and obtained prompts. For our method, we performed 200 MCMC runs with 150 iterations
368   each, and selected the resulting prompts as candidate prompts. For more detailed implementation,
369   please refer to Appendix G.
370
      The results are shown in Table 2. Our method significantly outperforms other methods. The results in
371
      Table 2 demonstrate that our method significantly outperforms others. To generate 200 candidate
372   prompts, ZeroCap and PEZ required 44 and 33 hours, respectively, on an A100 GPU but failed to
373   identify any memorized image trigger prompts. ZeroCap’s sequential prediction hindered the prompt
374   being optimized to have higher Dθ values than general prompts. For PEZ, we observed that the
375   prompts were optimized to produce images with a specific color (e.g., sunflower fields, grassy fields),
376   and the prompts themselves were very unnatural. ConZIC identified 6 memorized images in 24 hours
377   but struggled with local minima and lacked diversity in its optimization process, resulting in lower
      efficiency compared to our method.

393   5       M EM B ENCH : M ETRICS , S CENARIOS AND R EFERENCE P ERFORMANCE
394
395
396   Metrics. We present rigorous metrics for correctly evaluating mitigation methods, which include
397
      similarity score, Text-Image alignment score, and quality score. Following previous works, we adopt
      SSCD (Pizzi et al., 2022) as the similarity score and measure max SSCD between a generated image
398
      using trigger prompt and memorized images. In detail, if a prompt p∗ triggers images {x∗1 , ..., x∗k }
399
      included in Dtrain , we measure maxx∈{x∗1 ,...,x∗k } SSCD(x(p∗ , ϵθ ), x). Secondly, we adopt CLIP
400
      Score (Hessel et al., 2021) to measure Text-Image alignment between prompt and generated images.
401   Lastly, We adopt an Aesthetic Score (Schuhmann et al., 2022) as the image quality score. While
402   previous works did not measure image quality scores, we observed issues shown in Figure 2. When
403   memorization mitigation methods are applied, we observed that image quality degrades, the rich
404   context generated by the diffusion model is destroyed, or distorted images are formed. To further
405   investigate, we have quantified this by calculating the standard deviation of Aesthetic Score. An
406   ideal memorization mitigation method should be able to preserve the generation capabilities of the
407   diffusion model.
408
      Scenarios. To ensure that memorization mitigation methods can be generally applied to diffusion
409
      models, we provide two scenarios: the memorized image trigger prompt scenario and the general
410   prompt scenario. First, the memorized image trigger prompt scenario evaluates whether mitigation
411   methods can effectively prevent the generation of memorized images. This scenario uses the mem-
412   orized image trigger prompts we identified in Section 3. We generate 10 images for each trigger
413   prompt and measure the Top-1 SSCD and the mean values of the Top-3 SSCD. We also measure the
414   proportion of images with SSCD exceeding 0.5. For CLIP Score and Aesthetic Score, we calculate
415   the average value across all generated images. Second, the general prompt scenario ensures that the
416   performance of the diffusion model does not degrade when using prompts other than trigger prompts.
417   We leverage the COCO (Lin et al., 2014) validation set as general prompts. In this scenario, images
418
      are generated once per prompt, and the average CLIP Score and Aesthetic Score are measured.
419   Reference Performance. We propose a reference performance for interpreting the performance of
420   mitigation methods. An effective mitigation method should be able to reduce SSCD while maintaining
421   CLIP Score. However, although SSCD is a metric designed to compare the structural similarity of
422   images for copy detection tasks, it inevitably includes semantic meaning due to the self-supervised
423   nature of the trained neural network. On the other hand, the semantic meaning of the trigger prompt
424   should still be reflected in the generated image to maintain CLIP Score even when a mitigation
425   method is applied. Therefore, it is uncertain how much the SSCD between memorized images and
426   generated images can be reduced while maintaining the CLIP Score between trigger prompts and
427
      generated images. In this regard, we provide a reference performance to indicate how much SSCD
      can be reduced while maintaining a high CLIP Score. We assume querying images with trigger
428
      prompts via the Google Image API4 as a strong proxy model for the generative model and provide
429
      the reference performance based on this approach. Please refer to Appendix D for details.

464
465
      Secondly, we explore the cause of image memorization in Stable Diffusion 2, trained on LAION-5B,
466   whose duplicates are removed. Previous works (Somepalli et al., 2023b; Gu et al., 2023) suggested
467   that image memorization issues arise from duplicate images in the training data. Webster et al.
468   (2023) confirmed that the LAION-2B dataset contains many duplicate images likely to be memorized.
469   However, Stable Diffusion 2 still exhibits image memorization issues while reduced. We hypothesize
470   that this memorization arises due to layout duplication. Figure 3 shows the images found by Reverse
471   Image Search API that are memorized by Stable Diffusion. We found that there are often over
472   100 images on the web with the same layout but different color structures. LAION-5B underwent
473   deduplication based on URLs5 , but this process may not have removed these images. These layout
474
      memorizations are also obviously subject to copyright, posing potential social issues. Additional
      examples are provided in Appendix H.

478
479   In this section, we evaluate image memorization mitigation methods on our MemBench in Stable
480   Diffusion 1. For results of Stable Diffusion 2, please refer to Appendix F.2.
481
      Baselines. We use Stable Diffusion 1.4 as the base model. The image memorization mitigation
482
      methods evaluated include: 1) RTA (Somepalli et al., 2023b), which applies random token insertion
483   to the prompt, 2) RNA (Somepalli et al., 2023b), which inserts a random number between [0, 106 ]
484   into the prompt, 3) method proposed by Wen et al. (2024b) that applies adversarial attacks to text

      embeddings, and 4) method proposed by Ren et al. (2024) that rescales cross-attention. Image
487   generation is performed using the DDIM (Song et al., 2021a) Scheduler with a guidance scale of 7.5
488   and 50 inference steps.
489
490   Results. We present the experimental results in Table 3. As shown in Table 3, for all methods,
491   lowering the SSCD significantly reduces both the CLIP Score and the Aesthetic Score. This indicates
492   a degradation in text-image alignment and image quality. In particular, upon examining images with
493
      low Aesthetic Scores, we observe that issues in Figure 2 occur across all methods. While Ren et al.
      (2024) measured FID, they reported that FID decreases when their method is applied. They attribute
494
      this phenomenon to the mitigation method preventing memorized images from being generated,
495
      thereby increasing the diversity of generated images. As a result, FID does not effectively measure
496   image quality. We provide FID values in Appendix F.1. However, the Aesthetic Score offers a more
497   straightforward way to evaluate individual image quality and better highlight image quality issues.
498   Moreover, when hyper-parameters are set as high values for mitigation methods, it leads not only to
499   a lower Aesthetic Score but also to a much larger standard deviation. This indicates that diffusion
500   model outputs become unreliable. As reported by Wen et al. (2024b), all methods exhibit a trade-off
501   between SSCD and CLIP Score. Regarding the reference performance obtained via API search, it
502   can be observed that the SSCD can be reduced to 0.088 while maintaining a high CLIP Score. Due to
503   the inherent limitations of the Stable Diffusion baseline model, the CLIP Score cannot exceed 0.273
504
      when mitigation methods are applied. However, mitigation methods should aim to reduce the Top-1
      SSCD to around 0.088 while maintaining at least this level of CLIP Score.
505
506   To provide a more detailed analysis of each method, we observe that the approach proposed by Wen et
507   al. achieves the best performance in the trade-off between SSCD and CLIP Score. However, to
508   reduce the proportion of images with SSCD exceeding 0.5—indicative of image memorization—to
509   nearly zero, their method still requires a reduction in CLIP Score by 0.025. Given the scale of the
510
      CLIP Score, this drop suggests that the generated images may be only marginally related to the given
      prompts. Moreover, a significant decrease in the Aesthetic Score is also observed. On the other hand,
511
      the method proposed by Wen et al. has an additional advantage: it does not result in any performance
512
      drop in the general prompt scenario on the COCO dataset, making it the most suitable option for
513   practical applications as of now.
514
515   The most recent method proposed by Ren et al. (2024) shows a considerable reduction in the CLIP
516
      Score. Even at the lowest hyper-parameter setting (c = 1.0), the reductions in both CLIP Score and
      Aesthetic Score are substantial, limiting its general applicability to diffusion models. The most basic
517
      approaches, RNA and RTA, show a decrease in CLIP Score by 0.015 at the hyper-parameter setting
518
      (n = 6) that lowers the proportion of images with SSCD exceeding 0.5 to 0.05. This is expected,
519   given the nature of these methods: both attempt to prevent image memorization by adding irrelevant
520   tokens to the prompts. As a result, RNA and RTA are unreliable for application to diffusion models.

      8    C ONCLUSION
525
526   We have presented MemBench, the first benchmark for evaluating image memorization mitigation
527   methods in diffusion models. MemBench includes various memorized image trigger prompts,
528   appropriate metrics, and a practical scenario to ensure that mitigation methods can be effectively
529   applied in practice. We have provided the reference performance that mitigation methods should aim
530   to achieve. Through MemBench, we have confirmed that existing image memorization mitigation
531   methods are still insufficient for application to diffusion models in practical scenarios. The lack of a
532   benchmark may have previously hindered the research of effective mitigation methods. However, we
533
      believe that our benchmark will facilitate significant advancements in this field.
534   Limitations and Future Work. Another contribution of our work is providing an algorithm for
535   efficiently searching memorized image trigger prompts based on MCMC. Our approach is faster
536   than other searching algorithms we have tried, yet it does not exhibit exceptionally high speed.
537   Consequently, due to time constraints, we were unable to provide a larger number of memorized
538   images. However, our method allows for the continuous search of more memorized images and their
539   corresponding trigger prompts, and we plan to update the dataset regularly. Additionally, we aim to
      enhance the efficiency of our memorized image trigger prompt searching algorithm in the future.

872   Section 3.4. We explain 1) how memorized image trigger prompts and corresponding memorized
873   images for Stable Diffusion 1 and 2 in Table 1 were found, 2) implementation details of the data
874   augmentation algorithm using MCMC, and 3) its efficiency.
875   For Stable Diffusion 1 and Realistic Vision, we initialized sentences with n-length mask tokens
876   and implemented Algorithm 1 to find new memorized image trigger prompts and corresponding
877   memorized images (please refer to Section 3.4 “Using Masked Sentence as Prior”). We then
878   perform the MCMC process with p0 initialized by trigger prompts to perform the augmentation
879   (please refer to Section 3.4 “Using Found Trigger Prompts as Prior”). For Stable Diffusion
880   2 and DeepFloydIF, the process of finding trigger prompts using masked sentences was omitted.
881   This was due to two reasons: firstly, the prediction accuracy of Dθ for memorized image trigger
882   prompts is lower for these models. Secondly, as Stable Diffusion 2 is trained on the deduplicated
883
      LAION-5B and LAION-A, the memorized image trigger prompts are sparser, making optimization
      from a masked sentence initialization difficult. Therefore, for Stable Diffusion 2 and DeepFloydIF,
884
      only the trigger prompt augmentation algorithm was leveraged. The prompts were initialized in two
885
      ways before undergoing the data augmentation process: 1) using trigger prompts found from Stable
886   Diffusion 1, and 2) using trigger prompts provided by Webster (2023). We further elaborate on the

891
      Trigger prompt augmentation was carried out using a different approach from trigger prompt searching
892
      (Algorithm 1). The process of generating candidate trigger prompts through prompt augmentation is
893   detailed in Algorithm 2. We initialized p0 with the trigger prompt itself and then performed MCMC.
894   As explained in Section 3.4, in trigger prompt augmentation, we run n separate chains for each word
895   position in an n-length sentence, treating each position as the first updating index in Gibbs sampling.
896   During the MCMC process, all prompts with calculated Dθ values were stored in the prompt bank.
897   Additionally, we adopted an early stop counter. The prompts returned by Algorithm 2 tend to have
898   low diversity due to the nature of Gibbs Sampling. Therefore, Algorithm 3 is applied to all returned
899   prompts to create a smaller, more diverse subset of prompts. Afterward, these prompts undergo an

994    In Section 5, we posited that the Google Image Search API6 serves as a strong proxy model for
995    the generative model and presented the measured reference performance. We provide the details
996    of this approach in this section. Before explaining our use of the Google Image Search API, it
997
       is important to reiterate our goal in presenting reference performance: to determine the extent to
       which the SSCD (Pizzi et al., 2022) between the memorized image and the generated image can be
998
       minimized while maintaining the semantic content of the trigger prompt. To address this question, we
999
       utilized the Google Image Search API to measure reference performance as follows: 1) Query 100
1000   images using the memorized image trigger prompt via the API. 2) Measure the CLIP Score (Hessel
1001   et al., 2021) between the 100 images and the trigger prompt. 3) Retain only the image with the
1002   Top-1 CLIP Score. 4) Measure the SSCD between this retained image and the memorized image
1003   triggered by the prompt in Stable Diffusion. 5) Repeat steps 1-4 for all memorized image trigger
1004   prompts in MemBench. After completing these steps, we reported the average Top-1 CLIP Score
1005   and the average SSCD of images with the Top-1 CLIP Score in Section 7. Our findings show that
1006   the SSCD can be reduced to 0.200 while maintaining a CLIP Score of 0.329. This indicates that the
1007   minimum achievable SSCD with maintaining CLIP Score is 0.210. Therefore, we should strive to
1008
       develop mitigation methods that achieve this or better. Please note that we did not measure Aesthetic
       Score (Schuhmann et al., 2022) and evaluate the reference performance in COCO (Lin et al., 2014)
1009
       settings, since comparing them with the mitigation method is not meaningful.

             S EARCHING E XPERIMENT
1084
1085   In this section, we provide implementation details of other baselines that we have tried to search
1086   the memorized image trigger prompts, presented in Section 4.2. All three algorithms (Wen et al.,
1087   2024a; Zeng et al., 2023; Tewel et al., 2022) that we provide are originally intended to solve various
1088   optimization problems in the text space, using specific objective functions. For our experiments to
1089   search for memorized image trigger prompts, we replaced each method’s objective function with Dθ .
1090
       ZeroCap (Tewel et al., 2022). ZeroCap is an optimization method developed for zero-shot image
1091
       captioning tasks. This method leverages a pre-trained CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) to measure the
1092   CLIP similarity between an image and the current caption, and manipulate the prompt to maximize
1093   this CLIP Score, searching the best caption that describes the image. ZeroCap predicts the next word
1094   using a large language model (LLM) and sequentially adds tokens to the prompt in a manner that
1095   maximizes CLIP similarity. Additionally, a Context Cache is introduced for gradient descent, where
1096   the Context Cache is a set of key-value pairs derived when the current prompt is embedded into the
1097   LLM. The optimization function is consistute of 1) CLIP similarity loss between the image and the
1098   prompt, and 2) the cross-entropy (CE) loss between the distribution of the predicted token of the
1099   original Context Cache and that of the updated Context Cache. ZeroCap performs gradient descent
1100
       on the optimization function to update the Context Cache five times, after which the token predicted
       by this Context Cache is designated as the next token to continue the sentence. Furthermore, beam
1101
       search is utilized in this process. In our experiments, we replaced the CLIP similarity loss with Dθ
1102
       and implemented the algorithm accordingly. All hyper-parameters were set to match those in the
1103   original paper.
1104
1105   To generate 200 candidate prompts using ZeroCap, it took approximately 44 hours on an A100 GPU,
1106
       yet not a single memorized image trigger prompt was found. While Dθ values were higher compared
       to those of general prompts (captions from COCO validation set), they were still lower than the values
1107
       for actual trigger prompts. This suggests an inherent issue with ZeroCap’s sequential prediction
1108
       method.
1109
1110   PEZ (Wen et al., 2024a). The PEZ algorithm is an optimization technique designed to find prompts
1111   that will induce a diffusion model to generate a specific desired image. The algorithm operates in two
1112   main steps for each iteration: 1) perform gradient descent on the prompt in the continuous space with
1113   respect to the diffusion model’s CLIP model, and 2) project the updated prompt back into the discrete
1114   space of the CLIP’s embedding space. In our adaptation of this algorithm, we utilized Dθ (p) as the
1115
       objective function for calculating the gradient. All hyper-parameters were set to match those in the
       original paper.
1116
1117   To generate 200 candidate prompts using PEZ, it took approximately 33 hours on an A100 GPU.
1118   However, similar to ZeroCap, not a single memorized image trigger prompt was found. Although
1119   the Dθ values were comparable to those of actual trigger prompts, memorized images were not
1120   discovered. Upon inspection, we observed that the prompts were optimized to produce images with a
1121
       specific color (e.g., sunflower fields, grassy fields), and the prompts themselves were very unnatural.
       This suggests that the optimization process did not result in the desired memorized image trigger
1122
       prompts.
1123
1124   ConZIC (Zeng et al., 2023). ConZIC, like ZeroCap, is a technique designed to optimize the CLIP
1125   Score for zero-shot image captioning tasks. Similar to our approach, ConZIC selects a single word
1126   within the sentence, predicts the word using BERT, and then replace it with the word which shows
1127   the highest value of objective function. The objective function here is a sum of the CLIP similarity
1128   and the conditional probability distribution from BERT. In our experiments, we substituted the CLIP
1129   similarity with Dθ as the objective function.
1130   To generate 200 candidate prompts using ConZIC, it took approximately 24 hours on an A100 GPU.
1131   Unlike the other methods, ConZIC successfully identified 6 memorized images. However, ConZIC’s
1132   optimization process is designed to consistently update the prompt to maximize the objective function,
1133   which tends to result in getting stuck in local minima and the lack of diversity. These lead to less
       efficiency compared to our method.
