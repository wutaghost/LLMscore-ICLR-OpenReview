009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Gaussian process (GP) regression is widely used for its flexible mean predictions
012            and inherent uncertainty quantification. However, its scalability is limited by cu-
013            bic time complexity, O(n3 ), and quadratic space complexity, O(n2 ), making it
014            infeasible for large-scale datasets. Although recent advances have introduced ap-
015            proximate methods with time complexity O(nm2 ), where m ≪ n is a tuning
016
               parameter, these methods each have their own bottlenecks, such as requiring a
               relatively large m or involving expensive preprocessing steps. Moreover, for ex-
017
               tremely large datasets with millions of samples, the space complexity O(n2 ) be-
018
               comes another significant bottleneck. In this paper, we present a novel method
019            based on the Hilbert-Schmidt singular value decomposition that obtains a low-
020            rank decomposition “for free”, reducing both time complexity to O(nm2 ) and
021            space complexity to O(nm), with no preprocessing overhead. We used simulated
022            large-scale datasets to demonstrate the performance of our method compared to
023            state-of-the-art approaches.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
      Gaussian Process Regression (GPR) has become a cornerstone for nonparametric regression, largely
028
      due to its flexibility in modeling complex data and its inherent ability to provide uncertainty quan-
029
      tification (Cressie, 2015). GPR is widely used across numerous fields, including spatial and spa-
030   tiotemporal modeling (Banerjee et al., 2003), epidemiology (Lawson et al., 2016), and machine
031   learning (Rasmussen & Williams, 2006). Its ability to estimate both the conditional mean and co-
032   variance functions has made it indispensable for applications such as spatiotemporal data analysis,
033   surrogate modeling for complex physical simulations, and Bayesian optimization.
034
      However, a major challenge with GPR is its poor scalability. As size increases, GPR suffers from
035
      cubic time complexity, O(n3 ), due to the inversion of the kernel matrix, and quadratic space space
036   complexity, O(n2 ), due to the need to store the kernel matrix, where n is the number of data points.
037   These computational constraints render GPR impractical for large-scale datasets, such as those found
038   in forestry, geospatial applications, climate science, and single-cell RNA sequencing, where the
039   sample size can reach millions or even tens of millions.
040
      As a result, over the past two decades, significant progress has been made in scaling GPR for large
041
      datasets, with a primary focus on reducing computational complexity to O(nm2 ), where m ≪
042   n is a tuning parameter. These approaches can be broadly categorized into four groups: sparse
043   approximations, low-rank approximations, probabilistic methods, and structured kernels.
044
045
      Sparse approximations reduce the effective number of data points by using a subset of points. Ex-
      amples of these methods include Sparse GPR (SGPR, Titsias, 2009, Nearest Neighbor Gaussian
046
      Process (NNGP, Finley et al., 2020), and approaches based on inducing points. Low-rank approx-
047
      imations aim to approximate the full kernel matrix using lower-dimensional representations. Com-
048   mon methods include the Nystrom approximation (Williams & Seeger, 2000) and random Fourier
049   features (Rahimi & Recht, 2007). Additionally, methods like Deep Kernel Learning (DKL, Wil-
050   son et al., 2016) combine GPs with neural networks to learn effective representations from high-
051   dimensional data, thereby reducing the dimensionality of the kernel. Probabilistic methods take
052   a different route by optimizing the GP model through variational inference, allowing for effi-
053   cient approximation of the posterior distribution. Examples include Variational Nearest Neighbor
      (VNN, Wu et al., 2022) and Sparse Variational Gaussian Process (SVGP, Hensman et al., 2015).

054
      Variants like SVGP with Contour Integral Quadrature (SVGP-CIQ, Pleiss et al., 2020) further refine
055   this approach by employing advanced numerical methods to enhance efficiency. These methods are
056   often combined with Natural Gradient Descent (NGD, Salimbeni et al., 2018; Hensman et al., 2012)
057   to further improve the computational efficiency of updating the variational parameters. Structured
058   kernel approximations take advantage of specific structures in the covariance matrix to enable effi-
059   cient computations. Methods like Structured Kernel Interpolation (SKI, Wilson & Nickisch, 2015)
060   and KISS-GP (Wilson & Nickisch, 2015) belong to this category, exploiting grid-based or interpo-
061   lation methods to approximate the kernel matrix more efficiently. Additionally, Krylov subspace
062   methods, such as Lanczos Variance Estimates (LOVE, Pleiss et al., 2018), can approximate the
063
      kernel matrix with reduced complexity while maintaining high accuracy.
064   These various scalable GPR methods have significantly expanded the range of applications that GPs
065   can handle in the context of big data. To streamline the use of these approaches, GPyTorch (Gardner
066   et al., 2018) provides a powerful and efficient framework that integrates most of these methods into a
067   single toolbox. By offering flexible options for scaling GPs, GPyTorch allows users to efficiently ap-
068   ply these techniques to large-scale datasets while maintaining robust predictive performance, making
069
      it an invaluable resource for researchers and practitioners alike.
070   Despite their advancements, these methods each have their own bottlenecks. For instance, some
071   approaches require a relatively large m, reducing their efficiency, while others involve complex pre-
072   processing steps or have tuning parameters that are difficult to optimize. Additionally, for extremely
073   large datasets, with millions or tens of millions of samples, the space complexity—which has been
074   relatively less addressed by many of these methods—becomes a significant bottleneck. Moreover,
075
      methods like DKL and some variants of SVGP often require GPUs to manage the computational
      load, which may not be readily available to all practitioners. This highlights the need for a method
076
      that is both time- and space-efficient, requires minimal tuning or preprocessing, and does not depend
077
      on GPU resources—though it should still be able to benefit from GPU acceleration when available.

184   3     M ETHOD
185
186   As discussed in Section 2.1, the primary computational bottlenecks in GPR arise from the inversion
187   of the kernel matrix KXX , which incurs a time complexity of O(n3 ) and a space complexity of
188   O(n2 ). Current scalable methods typically approach these problems using two common strategies:
189
      Low-rank approximation: These methods approximate KXX with a low-rank representation to make
190   computations more tractable. However, the cost of computing this low-rank representation is often
191   significant, and in practice, it may introduce numerical errors that compromise the model’s perfor-
192   mance.
193
      Sparse approximation: This approach reduces the size of KXX by using a subset of inducing points
194
      to approximate the dataset. The challenge with this method lies in choosing the number of inducing
195
      points: too few points compromise accuracy, while too many reintroduce the computational burden
196   of a large matrix.
197
198   In this section, we propose a novel framework that addresses both size and stability challenges simul-
199
      taneously. Our method leverages a low-rank decomposition of the kernel matrix without incurring
      the high computational cost of explicitly calculating this decomposition. We achieve these goals by
200
      using the HS-SVD introduced in Section 2.2, which is based on the Mercer decomposition of the
201
      kernel. This allows us to bypass the direct computation of the low-rank form while still retaining the
202   advantages of the decomposition.

515   5     D ISCUSSION
516
517   In this paper, we introduced the HS-SVD method for obtaining low-rank approximations of kernel
518   matrices. We discussed the numerical advantages of this approach, particularly in reducing both
519
      computational and memory cost, while also enhancing numerical stability. As an illustrative ex-
      ample, we constructed the compact Matérn kernel for arbitrary dimensions, and provided insights
520
      into its derivation, highlighting its connection to the widely used Matérn Kernel. Through empirical
521
      comparisons with a variety of SOTA scalable GP methods, we demonstrated that our method signif-
522   icantly reduces computational time and memory requirements while achieving the best or near-best
523   prediction MSE, in various cases.
524
525
      While the HS-SVD method offers significant computational benefits, several challenges and oppor-
      tunities for improvement remain. First, although our method scales exceptionally well with sample
526
      size, it does not fully overcome the curse of dimensionality. Specially, the truncation length m grows
527
      exponentially with dimension r, which limits its efficiency in regression with high-dimensional fea-
528   tures. One potential solution to this is integrating HS-SVD with DKL, which utilizes neural net-
529   works to learn low-dimensional feature representations of high-dimensional data, thus mitigating
530   the curse of dimensionality. Second, our method requires the Mercer decomposition for the cho-
531   sen kernel, which may not always be available. However, an approximate Mercer decomposition
532   might be computed in such cases. Importantly, this approximation only needs to be computed once
533   and can be reused across multiple applications, making it a manageable limitation. Third, currently
534   implemented on a single CPU core, our method would benefit from a GPU-based implementa-
535   tion, allowing for further speedups. Fourth, our method can be combined with existing methods
536
      such as variational approaches for even greater scalability. Finally, beyond its application in GP,
      the HS-SVD method can be extended to other problems that involve using kernel matrices, such
537
      as smoothing splines and Radial Basis Function (RBF) approximations. This broader applicability
538
      opens up numerous avenues for further research, potentially expanding the impact of this method
539   across multiple domains.
