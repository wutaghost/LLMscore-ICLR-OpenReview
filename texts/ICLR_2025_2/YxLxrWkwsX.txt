009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Neural Radiance Fields (NeRF) have significantly advanced the field of novel
012            view synthesis, yet their generalization across diverse scenes and conditions re-
013            mains challenging. Addressing this, we propose the integration of a novel brain-
014            inspired normalization technique Neural Generalization (NeuGen) into leading
015            NeRF architectures which include MVSNeRF and GeoNeRF. NeuGen extracts
016            the domain-invariant features, thereby enhancing the models’ generalization ca-
017
               pabilities. It can be seamlessly integrated into NeRF architectures and cultivates a
               comprehensive feature set that significantly improves accuracy and robustness in
018
               image rendering. Through this integration, NeuGen shows improved performance
019
               on benchmarks on diverse datasets across state-of-the-art NeRF architectures, en-
020            abling them to generalize better across varied scenes. Our comprehensive evalu-
021            ations, both quantitative and qualitative, confirm that our approach not only sur-
022            passes existing models in generalizability but also markedly improves rendering
023            quality. Our work exemplifies the potential of merging neuroscientific principles
024            with deep learning frameworks, setting a new precedent for enhanced generaliz-
025            ability and efficiency in novel view synthesis. A demo of our study is available at
026            https://neugennerf.github.io.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
030
      The problem of novel view synthesis in computer vision and graphics has caught the attention of
031
      researchers in recent years. The ability to generate previously unseen perspectives of an object or
032
      scene is not just a technical challenge but a gateway to transformative applications in virtual reality,
033   3D modeling, and beyond. While the potential is vast, the problem is intricate: Given a set of images,
034   along with their camera pose, the goal is to render photo-realistic images of the scene through
035   novel viewpoints that accurately represent the actual scene. Recently, differential neural rendering
036   methods, such as Neural Radiance Fields (NeRF) (35) and others (57; 31; 29; 27) have showcased
037   the potential of deep learning techniques in producing high-fidelity reconstructions from input views.
038   By representing scenes as continuous volumetric scene functions, methodologies like NeRF have set
039   new benchmarks in the field, enabling the synthesis of intricate scenes with remarkable accuracy.
040   However, as with many cutting-edge solutions, there are nuances to consider. The bottleneck of long
041
      per-scene optimization limits the practicality of these methods. Many works (55; 5; 16) propose
      different architectures to overcome the issue, but we believe that data representation techniques can
042
      be adopted to optimize the generalizability of NeRFs. Specifically, we take inspiration from the
043
      mammalian brain on how it perceives different visual stimuli. Much work has been done on how
044   the mammalian visual cortex encodes visual data (20; 22). Building on the foundation of brain-
045   inspired computational models and the challenges inherent in novel view synthesis, in this work, we
046   present two major contributions to the field of computer vision and novel view synthesis, described
047   as follows:
048
049         1. Introducing NeuGen (Neural Generalization), a neuro-inspired layer, that draws on the
050            neural signal regulation mechanisms found in the mammalian visual cortex, tailored for
051            enhancing domain generalization in NeRFs.
052         2. Through NeuGen, we offer a novel data representation approach that captures high-
053            frequency, domain-invariant features, significantly enhancing the view synthesis capabili-
               ties of NeRFs.

084    2          R ELATED W ORK
085
086    Neural scene representations: Representing the geometry and appearance of a 3D scene from
087    2D images has been a promising direction for researchers in recent years. Earlier methods
088    (1; 12; 15; 37; 38) demonstrated Multi-Layered Perceptrons’ (MLP) ability to implicitly rep-
089    resent shapes, where the weight of the network maps continuous spatial coordinates to either
090    occupancy or signed distance values. Improvements in differentiable rendering methods paved
091    the way for advancements in neural scene representations where multi-view observations were
092    used to learn the geometric and appearance details. One method that drove more innovations
093
       in this domain was NeRF (35). The NeRF method is proposed to optimize a continuous 5D
       neural radiance field. It combines MLPs with differentiable volume rendering and achieves
094
       photo-realistic view synthesis. NeRF opened up many research frontiers in neural rendering such
095
       as dynamic view synthesis (26; 39), relighting (8; 2), real-time rendering (54), pose estimation
096    (32), and editing (52; 6). However, a significant constraint with NeRF and the following methods
097    is that it needs to be optimized for each scene, which requires extensive time to produce good results.
098
099
       Domain generalization in NeRFs: Researchers have focused on proposing novel architectures
100
       to tackle the problem of per-scene optimization. GRF (46), pixelNeRF (55), and MINE (23)
101
       tried to render novel views with a limited number of source views, but their ability to adapt to
102    unseen challenging scenes remained limited. Later, IBRNet (49) was introduced, which draws
103    inspiration from both Image-based rendering (IBR) and NeRF, along with novel ray transformers
104    that aggregate and blend information from multiple views and incorporate more long-range context
105    along the ray. More recently, MuRF (53) proposed a multi-baseline radiance field approach that
106    handles both small and large baseline settings through a target view frustum volume representation
107    and CNN-based decoder. Apart from this, MVSNeRF (5) and GeoNeRF (16) methods have been
       proposed that build cost volumes from 2D image features, which are eventually used to construct

      radiance fields. MVSNeRF, more specifically, constructs a neural encoding volume from a low-cost
109   volume that helps achieve better generalised results. GeoNeRF constructs feature volumes at
110   multiple resolutions to capture and use more refined features. While these methods are currently
111   state-of-the-art, they struggle with homogenous and shiny geometry and thin structures in the scene.
112
113
      Biologically-inspired techniques: The field of computer vision has been profoundly shaped
114
      by insights drawn from the brain’s complex mechanisms. Hierarchical models, mirroring the
115   layered processing of the visual cortex, have unlocked new perspectives in object recognition (44).
116   With its foundation in neural network architectures inspired by neural processes, deep learning
117   has catalyzed significant computational vision breakthroughs (24; 20; 48). Techniques such as
118   residual learning and deeper convolutions, though not exact replicas of neural processes are guided
119   by the fundamental principles of neural functioning. This ongoing integration of brain-inspired
120   methodologies has been pivotal across the computational vision landscape. Notable efforts include
121   the computational modeling of the brain’s central visual system (11) and investigations into the
122   recurrent connections of the visual cortex (20) among others (44; 25). These efforts have signifi-
123
      cantly enhanced computational tasks and deepened our comprehension of the relationship between
      biological vision and computational methodologies (3; 22; 40; 42; 17). Despite their innovative
124
      design and theoretical promise, these models often encounter challenges when benchmarked against
125
      real-world datasets.
126
127
128   Our proposed approach is data-centric; instead of changing the architecture of the existing methods
129
      that use spatial features as a core part of their methodology, we present a brain-inspired feature-
      enhancing technique that captures high-frequency domain-invariant features from input source im-
130
      ages and ultimately improves the performance of generalized NeRF models on challenging scenes.

133   3   N EU G EN : N EURAL G ENERALIZATION
134
135   We introduce a novel neuro-inspired layer called Neural Generalization (NeuGen), designed to cap-
136   ture the neural signal regulation characteristics of certain excitatory neurons in the mammalian visual
137
      cortex (56; 4; 41; 9). These neurons are distinguished by their ability to intricately encode con-
      trasting elements and structural nuances of visual stimuli, with variable firing sequences that align
138
      closely with variations in visual contrast. By emulating this capability, NeuGen produces refined
139
      and contextually aware representations, which are particularly beneficial for scene reconstruction
140   tasks requiring adaptability and a nuanced understanding of visual inputs. NeuGen implements
141   three key mechanisms inspired by the visual cortex’s contrast processing: patch-based processing
142   analogous to neural receptive fields, local contrast normalization reflecting neural adaptation, and
143   multi-scale feature integration similar to hierarchical visual processing. This biologically inspired
144   design enables robust feature extraction that remains consistent across varying domains and viewing
145   conditions. We integrate this process into deep learning models as a preprocessing layer using a
146   high-level mathematical formulation that mimics these biological mechanisms.

149   independent representation, denoted as I G . The image I is segmented into patches of size s, repre-
150   sented as P = {ps1 , ps2 , . . . , psn }, where each patch p of size s encircles a pixel k at coordinates
151   i, j including all channels. For each patch, its mean µps and standard deviation σps are calculated
152   to construct I G :

       To demonstrate how NeuGen boosts the performance of generalized NeRFs, we choose two state-
208
       of-the-art methods: MVSNeRF (5) and GeoNeRF (16). Both methods follow the classical volume

211    rationale firmly establishes them as prime candidates for our experimental analysis.

      MVSNeRF (5) divides its architecture into three essential components. First, the image features
      are extracted using a stack of 2D CNN layers. In the second step, the cost volume generation
228
      component takes the extracted feature maps as input and generates a plane-swept cost volume. This
229
      cost volume contains important information about the depth as well as the appearance of the scene.
230   Although this cost volume can be used to further perform view synthesis, MVSNeRF introduces
231   another component called Neural Encoding Volume that further refines the cost volume. A 3D CNN
232   then extracts meaningful encoding from the volume itself which ultimately leads to better view
233   synthesis results. For the final step, which is neural radiance field construction, the conventional
234   volumetric rendering from (35) is used.
235
236   GeoNeRF (16) uses MVSNeRF as a baseline and proposes a number of improvements in
237   the architecture. First and foremost, MVSNeRF uses a low-resolution 3D cost volume which
238
      makes it challenging to render good quality detailed images. To counter this constraint, GeoNeRF
      proposes a geometry reasoner; it constructs a hierarchy of cost volumes, inspired by CasMVSNet
239
      (13), for each nearby source view, which is then used to infer the geometry and appearance
240
      of the unseen scene. As opposed to MVSNeRF, cascade cost volumes are constructed at three
241   different resolutions which ultimately create a more dense feature volume. They also propose
242   an improvement in the neural field construction wherein the sampled points along the ray are
243   processed through an attention-based mechanism (47; 10) to infer geometry and appearance from
244   the cost volumes. This attention mechanism allows GeoNeRF to learn long-range dependencies and
245   aggregate information from multiple source views.

247   5   M ETHODOLOGY
248
249   As explained in section 4, both MVSNeRF and GeoNeRF create a feature volume by first extracting
250   2D image features and finally warping these to a plane sweep volume. To reiterate, MVSNeRF
251   creates a low-resolution feature volume while GeoNeRF creates a high-resolution cascaded volume
252   using multi-scale feature volumes. The quality of the extracted 2D features determines the quality
253   of feature volumes which ultimately influences the quality of volumetric rendering when using less
254   number of source images for generalized models.
255   While the research community has come up with many novel architectures that have achieved rea-
256   sonable performance on generalization tasks, there is still room for improvement. The current state-
257   of-the-art methods (5; 16) struggle against challenging geometry where finer details are not rendered
258   well. Moreover, these methods also struggle against reflective and homogeneous surfaces. These
259   limitations can be seen in Figures 3, 4 and 5. Instead of a new architectural change, our proposed
260   approach (NeuGen) is focused more on introducing a new data representation that is capable of high-
261   frequency domain-invariant features. When this data representation is merged with the original data,
262   we get a features-enhanced form of data. This is illustrated in Figure 1.

299
300
      First, we conduct a two-part experiment to assess NeuGen’s impact on image quality and feature
301   extraction. The primary quantitative analysis, shown in Figure 2 (B, C, D), involved calculating
302   and averaging Structural Similarity Index (SSIM) (50) scores across each class by comparing the
303   first image with all others in both NeuGen I G , and original I versions. This provided insights into
304   NeuGen’s consistent emphasis on features. Additionally, Figure 2 (A) presents a qualitative com-
305   parison, where NeuGen images are visually contrasted with their originals, alongside their SSIM
306   scores and Scale-Invariant Feature Transform (SIFT) (30) matches. This part of the experiment
307   highlights NeuGen’s effectiveness in improving image structure and feature robustness. NeuGen’s
308   enhancement of SSIM scores demonstrates its proficiency in preserving the structural details of im-
309
      ages, with SSIM being an established metric for assessing image quality through local pixel patterns.
      Elevated SSIM values reflect the alignment of structural content, brightness, and texture fidelity in
310
      images. NeuGen’s design to accentuate contrasting features ensures the structural consistency of
311
      images, contributing to superior reconstructions. Moreover, the higher number of SIFT matches
312   with NeuGen usage highlights its effectiveness in extracting and defining image features.
313
314   This improvement indicates that NeuGen reinforces the distinctiveness and resilience of SIFT-
315
      generated features, thus improving image correspondence across various transformations. The in-
      troduction of NeuGen introduces a novel data representation for NeRF models, optimizing the way
316
      visual information is processed and utilized. This representation’s usefulness is evidenced by our
317
      experiments, where NeuGen’s ability to preserve structural integrity and enhance distinctive high-
318   frequency features is showcased. The efficacy of this new data representation is apparent in both the
319   qualitative and quantitative improvements visible in Figure 2 and supported by our results. Such
320   representations are helpful for NeRF models, primarily when tasked with the complex challenge of
321   novel view synthesis, as they rely heavily on accurate and robust feature detection for successful
322   execution. With NeuGen features incorporated, state-of-the-art methods for NeRF domain general-
323   ization, such as MVSNeRF and GeoNeRF, can gain major improvements in the results, as shown by
      our quantitative as well as qualitative results.

338   To demonstrate the effectiveness of NeuGen, we conduct experiments with two leading NeRF mod-

342   els. This suggests that NeuGen enhances the model’s ability to optimize scenes effectively, leading
343
      to superior results. The per-scene optimized MVSNeRF model shows notable enhancements in im-
      age quality and structural consistency, indicating that NeuGen plays a crucial role in refining the
344
      feature extraction and rendering processes. The improved performance in terms of PSNR, SSIM,
345
      and LPIPS metrics underscores the impact of NeuGen in facilitating better scene understanding and
346   reconstruction. Similarly, for GeoNeRF, training from scratch with NeuGen for the same duration
347   demonstrates significant performance improvements across all metrics, even without the need for
348   subsequent fine-tuning. This highlights NeuGen’s capability to boost performance from the out-
349   set, making the model more robust and effective in handling complex and diverse scenes. The
350   results show that NeuGen-enhanced GeoNeRF models exhibit higher fidelity in rendering and better
351   generalization across different datasets. These experiments showcase NeuGen’s ability to enhance
352   generalization in NeRF models. The integration of NeuGen boosts the performance metrics and also
353   ensures that the models can handle a wider range of scenes and conditions with greater accuracy.

      By leveraging the biologically inspired normalization techniques, NeuGen provides a significant

      NeRF architectures.

477   7      D ISCUSSION
478
479
      It is crucial to contextualize our results within the broader landscape of generalizable NeRF re-
480   search. The improvements demonstrated by our NeuGen approach, while numerically modest, rep-
481   resent a significant step forward in this challenging domain. In the field of generalizable NeRFs,
482   incremental yet consistent improvements signify meaningful progress. Our method exhibits such
483   consistency across multiple datasets, suggesting a robust trend rather than statistical variance. This
484   pattern aligns with recent advancements in the field, where modest quantitative gains frequently
485   accompany substantial qualitative enhancements. For instance, the work of (45) reports moder-
      ate improvements without conclusive results, noting that GNT (Generalizable NeRF Transformer)


                                                              9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      does not consistently outperform other methods across all metrics and datasets. Similarly, the work
487   of (7) on efficient 3D Gaussian Splatting reported improvements of 0.11 in PSNR and 0.004 in
488   SSIM—comparable to our results—while achieving notable visual quality improvements. Here, it
489   is imperative to acknowledge the limitations of traditional metrics like PSNR and SSIM in fully
490   capturing perceptual quality, particularly for fine-grained details where our method excels. This
491   phenomenon has been well-documented in the literature (21), highlighting that pixel-wise metrics
492   often fail to reflect improvements in texture and intricate visual features that are readily apparent to
493   human observers.
494   While the quantitative metrics provide a standardized basis for comparison, the qualitative improve-
495   ments, offer crucial insights into the visual enhancements achieved by NeuGen. These improve-
496   ments are particularly evident in challenging scenarios such as homogeneous surfaces, shiny mate-
497   rials, and intricate geometric structures—areas where generalized NeRF models traditionally strug-
498   gle. Given these considerations, the true value of our contribution lies in the consistent quantitative
499   improvements coupled with the significant qualitative enhancements across diverse datasets and
500   challenging scenarios.
501   Finally, it is worth noting that our approach is draws inspiration from the mammalian visual cortex,
502   which excels at detecting high-frequency components in visual stimuli. NeuGen, as a neuro-inspired
503   layer, encapsulates the response normalization property observed in a specific group of excitatory
504   neurons in the visual cortex, known for their encoding of both structure and contrast (43; 18). This
505   biological mechanism has been implemented through a high-level mathematical formulation in Neu-
506   Gen, as explained in section 3, allowing it to enhance domain-invariant features. NeuGen-enhanced
507
      images emphasize high-frequency details, improving the robustness of NeRF models across diverse
      scenes. Similar bio-inspired approaches (19; 36) have demonstrated that brain-like representations
508
      enhance model robustness, while recent works (51; 58) further emphasize the importance of high-
509
      frequency features for generalization in visual models.

512   8   C ONCLUSION
513
514   In this study, we introduce a novel neuro-inspired layer, NeuGen (Neural Generalization), into Neu-
515   ral Radiance Fields (NeRF) models, with a specific focus on enhancing domain generalization (per-
516   scene optimization). By drawing inspiration from the neural signal regulation characteristics ob-
517   served in the mammalian visual cortex, NeuGen significantly improves the performance of NeRF
518   models such as MVSNeRF and GeoNeRF, particularly in challenging scans involving complex ge-
519
      ometries and textures. Our experiments demonstrate that NeuGen enhances the models’ ability
      to generalize across diverse scenes by emphasizing high-frequency, domain-invariant features. By
520
      processing the input images to create NeuGen-enhanced images, we ensure better feature extrac-
521
      tion, which translates to improved structural similarity and reduced artifacts in the rendered scenes.
522   The integration of NeuGen into NeRF architectures, both during the initial training and fine-tuning
523   phases, has resulted in noticeable improvements in rendering quality and robustness, making it a
524   valuable addition to advanced NeRF architectures. The quantitative and qualitative evaluations con-
525   firm that NeuGen improves generalizability and also markedly improves the rendering quality of
526   NeRFs across different datasets, including Realistic Synthetic, LLFF, and DTU. These improve-
527   ments highlight NeuGen’s potential to address the limitations of current NeRF models, particularly
528   their struggle with homogeneous and reflective surfaces, as well as thin structures.
529   Despite the promising results, further testing on additional NeRF models is necessary to fully vali-
530   date NeuGen’s effectiveness across various architectures. Expanding the scope of NeuGen’s applica-
531   bility to other NeRF variants and more complex real-world datasets will help confirm its robustness
532   and establish its role as a versatile enhancement across different NeRF-based approaches. Future
533   work will involve testing NeuGen on emerging methods, such as Gaussian Splatting to assess its
534   impact in settings that leverage sparse views and neural point representations. Ablation study, im-
535   plementation details and analysis of LPIPS results can be found in the Appendix.

      Finding optimal value for NeuGen integration: In our study, we conducted a comprehensive
      sweep to evaluate the impact of varying the weight of NeuGen on the performance of Neural Radi-
762
      ance Fields (NeRF) models. This sweep was crucial in determining the optimal setting for NeuGen,
763
      a key component in our approach to enhance the domain generalization capabilities of NeRF mod-
764   els. The methodology involved systematically adjusting the weight of NeuGen across a range of
765   values from 0.5 to 3.0, observing the corresponding changes in the models’ performance metrics.
766   This process explored how different weightings of NeuGen influence the model’s ability to process
767   and render complex visual scenes. The primary focus was on understanding the balance between
768   preserving the original image characteristics and incorporating the enhanced features provided by
769   NeuGen. Some top-performing results from this extensive sweep can be seen in Supplementary
770   Table 1. During this extensive sweep, we discovered that a NeuGen weight of 0.5 yielded the most
771   promising results. This specific weighting provided an optimal balance, enhancing the model’s per-
772
      formance, particularly in rendering scenes with intricate details and challenging textures. It allowed
      the models to leverage NeuGen’s strengths effectively without overwhelming the original image
773
      data. Equation 2 of the main manuscript now becomes a representation of the optimal setting dis-
774
      covered in our NeuGen sweep. In this equation, Ii(e) denotes the enhanced image, while Ii and
775   Ii(n) represent the original image and the NeuGen-processed image, respectively. This formulation
776   demonstrates the combination of the original image with the NeuGen-enhanced image, where the
777   weight of NeuGen is set to 0.5:
778
779
                                  IiE = Ii ⊕ (IiG ∗ 0.5)    for i = 1, 2, . . . , N                      (5)

      This weight effectively balances the original and enhanced features, ensuring that the final image
782   retains essential characteristics while benefiting from the improved feature representation offered
783   by NeuGen. The success of this setting underscores the potential of NeuGen in enhancing the

867   Integerating NeuGen with features fusion: To find out the best way to utilize the features-rich
868
      NeuGen images, we experimented with a features fusion method. As mentioned in the main
      manuscript, MVSNeRF first extracts the 2D features from images before creating a 3D cost volume.
869
      We tried to extract the features from the original and NeuGen images separately before “fusing”
870
      them into a single feature set, which we believed could be more efficient.
871
872   More specifically, MVSNeRF uses a feature pyramid network (FPN) to extract hierarchical features
873   from images. With our approach, initial convolutional layers conv0, conv1, and conv2 extract fea-
874
      tures from both sets of images (original and NeuGen) independently in a bottom-up pathway. Each
      layer doubles the number of channels and downsamples the spatial dimensions by 2x; this results
875
      in 32 feature maps. These features from each image set are then concatenated along the channel
876
      dimension, resulting in the features from both streams into a unified set of features with 64 chan-
877   nels. The 64-channel feature map then goes through a set of ”fusion” convolutional layers Fusion1
878   and Fusion2. Fusion1 takes as input the concatenated features, which is a tensor of shape (B, 64,
879   H/4, W/4), where B is the batch size, H is the height and W is the width. It then applies a convo-
880   lution with kernel size 3x3 and padding size 1. So it will convolve over the spatial dimensions H/4
881   and W/4. Notably, Fusion1 outputs 32 feature maps. This fuses the information from both streams
882   and projects it down to a 32-dimensional representation. Fusion2 takes this 32-channel fused repre-
883   sentation from Fusion1 as input and applies another 3x3 convolution, further integrating the fused
884   features into a shared embedding.
885   Ultimately, we wanted to design an architecture that takes features from both sets of images and
886   utilizes the best features from both sets in a learnable manner. We trained MVSNeRF from scratch
887   with the features fusion incorporated. We observed that fusing features did not produce better results
888   as shown in Supplementary Table 2.

902   The DTU, Realistic Synthetic, and LLFF datasets provide a comprehensive suite for assessing the
903
      performance of view synthesis methodologies like NeRF. The DTU dataset features 80 scenes
      from fixed camera positions with high-resolution structured light scans, ideal for testing multi-view
904
      stereopsis [4]. The Realistic Synthetic dataset, optimized for continuous volumetric scene function
905
      representation, contains scenes such as “lego”, “drums”, “ficus”, “mic”, “hotdog”, “materials”,
906   “chair”, and “ship”, which are instrumental for NeRF’s view synthesis [3]. Lastly, the LLFF dataset,
907   with its real-world forward-facing images, challenges algorithms to handle varying lighting and
908   complex dynamics, offering scenes like “leaves”, “flowers”, “ferns”, “orchids”, “room”, “trex”,
909   “horns”, and “fortress” for rigorous real-world testing [5]. Together, these datasets benchmark the
910   accuracy of algorithms like NeRF and their robustness in diverse and complex environments.
911
912   DTU dataset: The DTU dataset, introduced by Jensen et al., serves as a comprehensive benchmark
913   for multi-view stereopsis methodologies [4]. Comprising 80 diverse scenes, the dataset offers
914   various environments captured from 49 or 64 precise camera positions. Each scene is further
915   enriched with reference structured light scans, meticulously acquired using a 6-axis industrial robot.
916   This dataset’s granularity and diversity make it an invaluable resource for evaluating the robustness
917   and accuracy of algorithms like NeRF. Given NeRF’s emphasis on synthesizing novel views from
      sparse inputs, the DTU dataset, with its varied camera positions and detailed scans, provides a

      representation is optimized using a limited set of input views, emphasizing NeRF’s capability to
923
      interpolate and extrapolate scene information. The dataset contains scenes that, when processed
924
      through NeRF, can be reconstructed with remarkable accuracy. This high-fidelity reconstruction is

944   metric that quantifies image similarity in terms of visual perception, offering a valuable perspective

946
      The Supplementary Tables 3, 4, and 5, expand on the PSNR and SSIM results from Tables 1, 2,
947
      and 3 in the main manuscript, providing an in-depth analysis using the Learned Perceptual Image
948   Patch Similarity (LPIPS) metric. These tables elucidate the impact of the NeuGen enhancement
949   on the perceptual image quality of MVSNeRF and GeoNeRF across multiple datasets. The LPIPS
950   scores, which reflect a perceptually higher image quality, generally show an improvement with the
951   integration of NeuGen, as evidenced by the consistently lower scores for MVSNeRF with NeuGen
952   in Supplementary Table 3 for the Realistic Synthetic dataset [3]. Similarly, Supplementary Ta-
953   ble 4 demonstrates that GeoNeRF with NeuGen improves on the LLFF dataset [5]. However, the
954   enhancements in the DTU dataset [4] are modest (Supplementary Table 5). These supplemen-
955   tary insights underscore the general efficacy of NeuGen in refining the visual perception of images,
956
      crucial for applications where detail quality is of utmost importance.
957   The decision to reserve the LPIPS analysis for the supplementary material was guided by the desire
958   to maintain a clear and straightforward narrative in the main manuscript. Since PSNR and SSIM are
959   conventionally higher-is-better metrics, their inclusion in the main results allows for a more direct
960   comparison with previous works and a more immediate comprehension of the model’s performance.
961   In contrast, due to its inverted scale, the LPIPS metric’s lower-is-better nature could complicate the
962
      direct comparison narrative. Including it in the supplementary material allows a comprehensive view
      of the model’s performance without over-complicating the main discussion.
