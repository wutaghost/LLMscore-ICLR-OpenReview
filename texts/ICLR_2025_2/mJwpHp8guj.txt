011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Predictive control enables the operation of physical systems along an optimal tra-
014            jectory based on forecasts and dynamic simulations. However, the complexity
015
               of system dynamics and high computational cost of optimization typically re-
               strict the optimization window to short horizons. Thus, any potential benefits
016
               from mid- and long-term rewards are withdrawn. This is particularly relevant for
017
               optimization of district energy systems using various low-environmental-impact
018            sources. To address this, we present an end-to-end methodological framework
019            for learning state-space representations of such systems that significantly reduce
020            computational load. The proposed approach leverages the implicit graph structure
021            of such systems to develop and train a physics-informed spatio-temporal graph
022            neural network. This methodology is evaluated on a real-world district heating
023            system incorporating thermal solar panels, storage, biomass and natural gas boil-
024            ers. Through historical time-series data augmentation and hyperparameter opti-
025            mization, the learned model demonstrates strong generalization ability and high
026
               accuracy in predicting system dynamics. Our method reduces simulation time by
               four orders of magnitude, cutting optimization time from several days to mere
027
               minutes, while also lowering operational costs by up to 25%.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Mitigating climate change requires substantial reduction in greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions (Port-
033   ner et al., 2022). To do so, the international energy agency (IEA) outlines the need to deploy large
034   energy networks with multiple low-carbon-footprint energy sources to reach net-zero emissions by
035   2050 (IEA, 2023). District heating networks are an example of such large energy networks infras-
036   tructure (Angelidis et al., 2023). They can use simultaneously various renewable energy sources
037   such as biomass, geothermal, solar thermal, heat pumps in addition to thermal energy storage. In-
038   corporating an increasing number of energy sources requires rethinking smart control strategies to
039
      ensure efficient system deployment and achieve sustainable objectives. Nevertheless, the different
      underlying dynamics (non-linearities, response time, intermittence, discharge rate etc.) brings new
040
      complexity to numerical simulation which then makes the optimization of such systems prohibitively
041
      time-consuming (Dorotić et al., 2019; Delubac et al., 2021). To tackle this and to reduce the compu-
042   tational load, several approaches have been adopted in literature such as linearization technics (Rojer
043   et al., 2024; Wirtz et al., 2021) and reduced order models (Falay et al., 2020). These approaches of-
044   ten lead to a simplification of the system dynamics and require considerable engineering efforts for
045   each new input variable to the system.
046
      The acceleration of complex and traditional simulations is one of the fields where deep learning
047
      models offer an appealing alternative, technically called surrogate models (SM). Neural networks
048   are in general the backbone of these data-based models, thanks to their capacity to capture complex
049   patterns and to handle various data structures (grids, graphs etc.) (Bronstein et al., 2021). This
050   technic was applied to diverse type of dynamical systems such as climate forecasting (Verma et al.,
051   2024), thermal and electrical load forecasting (Wang et al., 2023; Chitalia et al., 2020) and chemical
052   reactors (Ren et al., 2022), among others. More recent theoretical works consider either refining
053   predictions’ accuracy (Hua et al., 2023; Beintema et al., 2023) or propose enhanced training proce-
      dures to reduce computational resources (Meyer et al., 2023; Fan et al., 2023). Finally, some studies

083   In this work, we propose an end-to-end predictive control methodology for large real-world district
084
      energy systems. The proposed approach, schematized in Figure 1, takes advantage of the graph
      representation of such systems to develop an appropriate physics-informed spatio-temporal graph
085
      neural network (PI-STGCN). In contrast to previous works, our proposition is system-agnostic and
086
      enables handling various energy sources at different locations along with multiple consumer nodes to
087   learn a state-space representation between these entities. The surrogate model development pipeline,
088   shown in the right block of Figure 1, relies on hyperparameters optimization and historical time-
089   series augmentation used in the learning phase. We demonstrate that, in addition to expanding the
090   dataset size, the latter technique enables the incorporation of physically plausible scenarios into the
091   training set. Our methodology extends beyond the train-validation-test paradigm by further assess-
092   ing the learned model on unseen data patterns. This extension serves as an additional validation
093   step before using the model with an evolutionary optimization algorithm. The effectiveness of our
094   proposal is demonstrated through its application to a real-world system combining a slow inertial
095
      energy source (biomass) with an intermittent source (solar panels).
096   The primary contributions are summarized as follows:
097

099              district energy networks. It enables the modeling of diverse producer and consumer types,
100              accelerating the simulation of these networks. It effectively captures both the fast and slow
101
                 dynamics of such multi-source systems.
102            • We propose an adaptation of Gaussian jittering to augment time-series data, exposing the
103              model to plausible training scenarios. The incorporation of first-principle conservation
104              equations allows for more confident predictions. In addition, systematic hyperparameters’
105
                 optimization is carried out to further enhance the model performance.
106            • The proposed end-to-end framework bridges the gap between forecasting tasks and pre-
107              dictive control optimization using a state-space surrogate model. This approach aims to
                 accelerate the deployment and management of multi-source energy networks utilizing re-

123   variables (Blaud et al., 2023). An optimal control problem is mathematically formulated for time
124   t ∈ [0, tf ] as follows:

      DT models are more common and easier to construct as data is represented via discrete elements
137

138
139   In the field of district energy systems, a number of studies proposed surrogate DT models (Ow-
140   erko et al., 2020; de Jongh et al., 2021; Saloux et al., 2023; Boussaid et al., 2024; de Giuli et al.,
141   2024). For example, de Giuli et al. (2024) proposed to associate a recurrent neural network (RNN)
142
      to each consumer node in a district heating network (DHN). However, they only considered a sin-
      gle producer network, and the surrogate model conception relies on creating and connecting RNN
143
      cells, meaning that GNN could have been used instead. The use of GNN allows encoding topo-
144
      logical features of data as inductive bias in the model as in Boussaid et al. (2024). The authors
145   employed a spatio-temporal graph convolution network (GCN) in addition to graph attention (GAT)
146   and proposed a surrogate model to accelerate dynamic simulations by one to two orders of mag-
147   nitude. However, no physical constraints were incorporated, and similarly, only a single producer
148   networks were presented. Finally, other studies (Huang et al., 2023; Saloux et al., 2023) have pro-
149   posed control strategies for district energy systems in which heat load forecasts (represented by the
150   blue rectangle in the left block of Figure 1) are generated by data-driven models, while the physical
151   system still used a numerical simulation. Another approach for control of energy systems employs
152   deep reinforcement learning and showcased interesting results (Yeh et al., 2023), but such methods
153
      need considerable training times (ranging from several hours to days). The frugality of training our
      surrogate model is a key advantage and a surrogate model is a highly modular tool, meaning that it
154
      can be used either as a predictive model and/or for optimal control. Our work completes the related
155
      studies by employing a versatile neural network architecture that is application-agnostic, making it
156   suitable for district energy systems. The model is based on a spatio-temporal graph neural network
157   (Ji et al., 2023) and benefits from recent demonstrations showing that ’time-then-space’ models have
158   an expressivity advantage over ’time-and-space’ representations (Gao & Ribeiro, 2022). To further
159   enhance generalizability, a physics-informed approach is used in training (Raissi et al., 2019), where
160   a first-principles mass balance constraint, applicable to all district energy systems, is incorporated
161   into the loss function (Guelpa et al., 2019). Finally, the learned model is combined with a genetic
      algorithm (Deb, 2001) for optimal control of district energy systems.

178   3.1   P HYSICAL SYSTEM DESCRIPTION
179
180   District heating networks consist of several producers delivering heat to consumers via a network
181   of pipes and control valves (equivalent to Steiner points). Therefore, such systems can be suitably
182   represented as a graph G = (V, E), where V is the set of nodes (producer, consumer or valve), and
183
      E the set of edges (i.e., pipes). Section 3.2 will later show that each graph entity holds multiple in-
      terconnected physical features. Recent generation of these systems are characterized by employing
184
      different producer types at different locations of the network (Pakere et al., 2023). Various types of
185
      heat generators can be integrated into district heating networks, including biomass boilers, geother-
186   mal sources, natural gas boilers, solar panels, and heat pumps. Thermal energy storage plays a
187   critical role by enabling asynchronous production and peak load shaving. In this work, we consider
188   a real-world DHN featuring three producers: a biomass boiler, a natural gas boiler, and a solar ther-
189   mal panel field connected to thermal storage. Numerous valves can be seen in Figure 2 between the
190   solar field (Psol ) and the storage (T ES), they allow different cycles: Charging or discharging the
191   storage, or direct injection from the solar field to the network. A graph representation of the sys-
192   tem is schematized in Figure 2. The considered network incorporates different type of constraints
193   making it a well representative case that requires complex control strategies (Veyron et al., 2022):
194
             • When the biomass boiler is turned on, it must remain so for a minimum time-on τmin,on and
195            similarly when it is shut down for a minimum time-off τmin,off . Besides, power variations’
196            are limited with ramp constraints.
197
             • Solar energy must be used when available to avoid overheating and to increase the contri-
198
               bution of renewables in the production portfolio. Simultaneously charging and discharging
199            the TES is prohibited.
200
             • Finally, the producers must provide enough heat energy to meet the heat demand of the two
201
               clusters while temperature levels must remain above a fixed threshold.
202
203   Additional constraints and a detailed mathematical formulation of the physical system are given in
204   appendix A.1. Besides these constraints, network operators depend on external disturbances: solar
205   irradiance Girr and external temperature Text , both impact the production of solar energy, and finally
206   the heat demand of the two clusters, Q̇n (north) and Q̇s (south). The aim of the predictive control is
207   to optimize the usage of the energy sources (when to switch a source on or off, the power levels of
208   each source, the flow rates, etc.). To do so, network operators dispose of control variables which are
209   the mass flow rates sent to each of the clusters. This updates equation 1 as follows:

279   In equation 5, the loss term is weighted (via λ) with a physical constraint term represented by Fm .

284   Once the surrogate model is trained and considered valid, it is used as the predictive model inside the
285   control loop (Figure 1). The optimization horizon, Hopt , is selected based on multiple constraints.
286   First, it is well-established that forecast accuracy for disturbances, such as weather and heat demand,
287   decreases over extended prediction periods. Conversely, setting a shorter optimization horizon may
288   result in the loss of long- and medium-term rewards. In the literature, many related studies have
289   limited their optimization horizons to short periods (ranging from a few hours to a single day) due to
290   the aforementioned reasons, as well as the rising computational costs (Jansen et al., 2024; de Giuli
291   et al., 2024; Jäkle et al., 2023; Wirtz et al., 2021; Quaggiotto et al., 2021). In this work a compromise
292

338   tion T 1 ), where new control and disturbances are generated by using random multipliers ω and then
339   simulate the system using a high fidelity numerical model. A set of times-series ω with values in
340   r = [0.9, 1.1] is generated via the normal distribution N (1, σaug ) where σaug = (rmax − rmin )/6.

      The range r is chosen because the control variables, representing mass flow rates, are constrained

342

343

507
      learning a state-space constrained model, the optimal control problem becomes an unconstrained
508   problem. In fact, the numerical simulation model already incorporates the different constraints
509   presented in section 3.1, meaning that the outputs of the surrogate model are implicitly constrained.
510   Once the genetic algorithm reaches convergence, the optimal solution found by the surrogate model
511   is sent to the system, i.e. the high fidelity numerical model to confirm its feasibility and optimal
512   cost. The GA algorithm is executed with a population of 100 individuals (one-week time series for
513   each control variable) evolving over 200 generations. The results, summarized in Table 3, indicate
514   significant cost reductions compared to real system operations. The highest reduction, 25%, occurs
515   in week 3, where optimal use of solar power and storage minimizes costs. In contrast, for week 1,
516
      which falls in winter with the highest heat demand, operational costs are reduced by 8%. Due to low
      solar availability and demand constraints, high mass flow rates are necessary, resulting in biomass
517
      and gad boilers operating at high load. An intermediate cost reduction of 13% is observed for the
518
      median week.
519
520   One of the most notable results is the significant reduction in computation time. Using the GA op-
521   timizer with the Dymola software requires several days of calculation, rendering it infeasible for
522
      weeks 2 and 3, where the optimization calculation time exceeds its predictive horizon. By contrast,
      the inference model reduces the computational time to just a few minutes. The variation in computa-
523
      tion times across weeks is attributed to the complexity of the system’s dynamics. Week 3, in summer,
524
      requires more time due to the involvement of thermal storage, with multiple cycles (charging, direct
525   injection, discharging, etc.). This drastic improvement enables real-time use of the optimal control
526   framework and highlights the clear advantages of deep learning models in promoting, optimizing
527   and deploying district energy systems effectively.

531
      In this work, we presented a streamlined methodology for deep learning-based optimal control
532
      for computationally intensive multi-source district energy networks simulations. A comprehensive
533
      framework for training and validating the physically-informed surrogate model is provided. It re-
534   lies on historical data augmentation through Gaussian jittering and hyperparameters optimization.
535   The learned model is then used by a genetic algorithm optimizer to provide accurate estimates to
536   optimize a given objective function. The results demonstrate the effectiveness of our approach in
537   optimizing costs (up to 25%) and reducing computational time (from several days to few minutes).
538   This work opens up new perspectives for the optimized deployment and control of multi-source
539   district energy systems, thus contributing to the decarbonization of energy systems to meet environ-
      mental objectives.
