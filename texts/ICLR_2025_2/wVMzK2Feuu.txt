009                                                    A BSTRACT
010
011
               Long-tailed distribution datasets are prevalent in many machine learning tasks, yet exist-
012
               ing neural network models still face significant challenges when handling such data. This
013            paper proposes a novel adaptive pruning strategy, LTAP (Long-Tailed Adaptive Pruner),
014            aimed at balancing model efficiency and performance to better address the challenges
015            posed by long-tailed data distributions. LTAP introduces multi-dimensional importance
016            scoring criteria and designs a dynamic weight adjustment mechanism to adaptively deter-
017            mine the pruning priority of parameters for different classes. By focusing on protecting
018            parameters critical for tail classes, LTAP significantly enhances computational efficiency
019            while maintaining model performance. This method combines the strengths of long-tailed
020            learning and neural network pruning, overcoming the limitations of existing approaches
021
               in handling imbalanced data. Extensive experiments demonstrate that LTAP outperforms
               existing methods on various long-tailed datasets, achieving a good balance between model
022
               compression rate, computational efficiency, and classification accuracy. This research pro-
023
               vides new insights into solving model optimization problems in long-tailed learning and
024            is significant for improving the performance of neural networks on imbalanced datasets.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Long-tailed learning aims to address the problem of highly imbalanced class distributions, where most
033
      classes (i.e., tail classes) have scarce samples, while few classes (i.e., head classes) have abundant samples
      Liu et al. (2019); Wang et al. (2017); Tan et al. (2020); Li et al. (2020). This data distribution is prevalent in
034
      real-world applications, such as e-commerce product classification, speech recognition, and natural language
035
      processing Ouyang et al. (2016); Yang & Xu (2020). Although deep learning models perform excellently on
036   head classes, their performance on tail classes remains limited, mainly because models tend to overfit head
037   classes and neglect feature learning for tail classes, leading to insufficient overall model generalization Kang
038   et al. (2019).
039
      To improve long-tailed learning performance, researchers have proposed various methods, including multi-
040
      expert systems and modular designs Wang et al. (2020); Liu et al. (2019). However, these traditional methods
041
      face numerous challenges in practical applications. For instance, multi-expert systems often require train-
042   ing and maintaining multiple independent sub-models, resulting in enormous computational and storage
043   resource consumption Xiang et al. (2020). Modular designs rely on predefined module structures, lacking
044   dynamic adaptability and struggling to cope with constantly changing data distributions Ren et al. (2020).
045   Moreover, these methods often struggle to efficiently utilize parameters when dealing with tail classes, lim-
046   iting model performance on scarce data Zhang et al. (2021a).

047
      As a model compression and optimization technique, pruning optimizes model structure and improves com-
048
      putational efficiency by removing redundant or unimportant neurons or connections Han et al. (2015); Liu
049   et al. (2018); Zhu & Gupta (2017). In recent years, pruning methods have shown significant advantages
050   in improving model performance, reducing parameter counts, and accelerating inference Frankle & Carbin
051   (2018); Blalock et al. (2020). However, ordinary pruning methods face special challenges when applied to
052   long-tailed learning:
053
054       • Pruning bias due to class imbalance: conventional pruning methods, blind to class-specific contri-
055         butions, risk exacerbating the very imbalance they aim to address by inadvertently removing neurons
056         crucial for tail class recognition He et al. (2021a).
057
          • Difficulty in dynamic adjustment: the static nature of traditional pruning methods conflicts with the dy-
058         namic evolution of the association between parameters and data distribution during training, potentially
059         leading to suboptimal or even harmful pruning decisions Molchanov et al. (2019).
060
061       • Single evaluation criterion: the reliance on simplistic pruning criteria fails to capture the nuanced impor-
062         tance of neurons in the complex landscape of long-tail distributions, potentially misleading the pruning
063
            process Frankle et al. (2020).
064
      To address these challenges, this paper proposes a novel pruning strategy called Long-Tailed Adaptive
065
      Pruner (LTAP), specifically optimized for long-tailed learning environments. LTAP is rooted in the under-
066
      standing that effective long-tailed learning requires a fundamental rethinking of how we allocate and utilize
067   model capacity Kang et al. (2019). Our method makes innovative contributions in the following aspects:
068
069       • New LT-Vote mechanism: Through the LT-Vote (Long-Tailed Voting) mechanism, we dynamically ad-
070         just the weights of different pruning criteria based on the classification accuracy of different classes,
071         enabling the pruning process to more specifically optimize the learning performance of tail classes,
072         enhancing model robustness on long-tail distribution data.
073
          • Multi-stage dynamic pruning: Our method divides the pruning process into multiple stages, gradually
074
            removing redundant parameters, and dynamically adjusts the pruning strategy at each stage based on
075
            current model performance, ensuring continuous performance optimization during the pruning process.
076
077       • Efficient resource utilization: By reducing model parameter count and computational requirements
078         through pruning, we improve model operational efficiency in resource-constrained environments while
079         maintaining or even improving classification accuracy on tail classes.
080
081
      Experimental results show that our proposed LTAP method significantly outperforms traditional pruning
      methods and other long-tailed learning methods on multiple long-tailed distribution datasets, validating its
082
      effectiveness in enhancing tail class recognition ability, optimizing model structure, and improving compu-
083
      tational efficiency.

320   Implementation Details. We use the knowledge generated from the long-tailed recognition task to guide the
321
      pruning of the backbone network. Specifically, for each parameter in the model, we calculate scores using
      ‘magnitude’, ‘avg magnitude’, ‘cosine similarity’, ‘taylor first order’, and ‘taylor second order’ during the
322
      gradient descent process. These scores are then weighted based on the cumulative change in accuracy for
323
      each class on the validation set. The weighted sum of the scores is used to determine whether to prune a
324   parameter. We start the continuous pruning process after the 100th epoch, and the final model retains 30%
325   of the original parameters. For the final evaluation phase, we use the same settings as DODA Wang et al.
326   (2024) for all baseline methods and our method. For the CIFAR-100-LT dataset, we follow the general
327   experimental settings of Cao et al. (2019) and use ResNet-32 (proposed by He et al. (2016)) as the backbone
328   network. The network is trained for 200 epochs using the GD optimizer with an initial learning rate of 10-̂4,

450   5                         C ONCLUSION
451
452   We have presented LTAP, a dynamic pruning strategy designed to enhance model efficiency and perfor-
453   mance on long-tailed datasets. By dynamically adjusting pruning criteria based on class-specific perfor-
454   mance, LTAP addresses the inherent pruning bias in conventional methods, particularly for tail classes.
455
      Our theoretical analysis establishes that tail classes benefit more from model overparameterization, which
      informs our tail-biased pruning approach. Extensive experiments on benchmark long-tailed datasets, in-
456
      cluding CIFAR-100-LT, ImageNet-LT, and iNaturalist 2018, demonstrate that LTAP consistently improves
457
      classification accuracy, particularly for tail classes, while significantly reducing the model’s computational
458   overhead. By offering a balanced trade-off between model compression and accuracy, LTAP provides a
459   robust solution to the challenges of long-tailed learning and opens new possibilities for optimizing neural
460   networks in imbalanced and resource-constrained environments.

782   B    R ELATED W ORK
783
784   Long-tailed Learning. Long-tailed learning, which aims to address the problem of severely imbalanced
785
      class distributions, has become an important research direction in machine learning in recent years. Existing
      long-tailed learning methods mainly include resampling and reweighting, transfer learning and knowledge
786
      distillation, as well as multi-expert systems and modular designs.
787
788   Resampling methods Chawla et al. (2002); He et al. (2008) and reweighting techniques Cui et al. (2019);
789   Cao et al. (2019) balance data distribution and learning processes by adjusting sample sampling probabilities
790   or loss weights. However, these methods may lead to information loss or introduce noise, and struggle to
791
      adapt to dynamically changing data distributions. Transfer learning Yin et al. (2019); Liu et al. (2019) and
      knowledge distillation Xiang et al. (2020); He et al. (2021b) techniques attempt to transfer knowledge from
792
      head classes to tail classes, or extract knowledge from large pre-trained models. However, these methods
793
      often rely on additional pre-trained models or complex training strategies, increasing computational com-
794   plexity and model dependencies. Multi-expert systems Wang et al. (2017); Xiang et al. (2020) and modular
795   designs Zhang et al. (2021b); Liu et al. (2021b) design specialized sub-models or modules for different data
796   subsets. While these methods perform well in certain scenarios, they often lead to a significant increase in
797   model parameters, raising the risk of overfitting, and their fixed structural design limits the ability to adapt
798   to dynamically changing data distributions.


                                                                17
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


799
      Although the above methods have made some progress in addressing long-tailed problems, they still face
800
      challenges such as insufficient flexibility, low computational efficiency, and difficulty in adapting to dynamic
801   environments.
802
803   Neural Network Pruning. Neural network pruning, as an important model compression and optimization
804
      technique, has received widespread attention in recent years. Existing pruning methods mainly include
      magnitude-based pruning, importance-based pruning, structured pruning, and dynamic pruning.
805
806   Magnitude-based pruning methods Han et al. (2015); Li et al. (2016) compress networks by removing con-
807   nections or neurons with small weight magnitudes. These methods are simple and intuitive but may over-
808   look parameters that are small in value but functionally important. Importance-based pruning methods
809   Molchanov et al. (2016; 2019) evaluate the importance of parameters by calculating their impact on model
810
      output, but typically rely on a single scoring criterion, making it difficult to comprehensively capture pa-
      rameter importance in complex tasks. Structured pruning methods Li et al. (2016); Liu et al. (2017) aim to
811
      remove entire convolution kernels or neurons to achieve higher hardware acceleration effects. While these
812
      methods can significantly reduce model size and computation, they may lead to severe loss of expressive
813   power. Recent dynamic pruning strategies Lin et al. (2020); Liu et al. (2021a) allow dynamic adjustment of
814   network structure during inference, providing greater flexibility, but mainly focusing on improving compu-
815   tational efficiency.
816
      Although existing pruning methods have achieved significant results in model compression and acceleration,
817
      they still have notable shortcomings in addressing long-tailed learning problems: (i) these methods typically
818
      assume uniform data distributions, ignoring the special characteristics of long-tailed data. (ii) they adopt
819   single importance evaluation criteria, making it difficult to comprehensively capture the role of parame-
820   ters in different classes. (iii) they lack dynamic adjustment mechanisms tailored to long-tailed distribution
821   characteristics, limiting their applicability in complex scenarios.
822
      Based on the above analysis, we believe it is necessary to develop a pruning method specifically for long-
823
      tailed learning, which can both fully leverage the advantages of pruning techniques and effectively address
824
      the special challenges posed by long-tailed distributions. This is the motivation behind the LT-Vote-based
825   pruning strategy proposed in this paper.
