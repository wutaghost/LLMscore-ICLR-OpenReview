009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Deep learning has become the de facto approach in nearly all learning tasks. It
012            has been observed that deep models tend to memorize and sometimes overfit data,
013            which can lead to compromises in performance, privacy, and other critical metrics.
014            In this paper, we explore the theoretical foundations that connect memorization to
015            various orders of sample loss, i.e., sample loss, sample loss gradient, and sam-
016
               ple loss curvature, focusing on learning dynamics to understand what and how
               these models memorize. To this end, we introduce two proxies for memoriza-
017
               tion: Cumulative Sample Loss (CSL) and Cumulative Sample Gradient (CSG).
018
               CSL represents the accumulated loss of a sample throughout training, while CSG
019            is the gradient with respect to the input, aggregated over the training process.
020            CSL and CSG exhibit remarkable similarity to stability-based memorization, as
021            evidenced by considerably high cosine similarity scores. We delve into the the-
022            ory behind these results, demonstrating that CSL and CSG represent the bounds
023            for stability-based memorization and learning time. Additionally, we extend this
024            framework to include sample loss curvature and connect the three orders, namely,
025            sample loss, sample loss gradient, and sample loss curvature, to learning time and
026            memorization. The proposed proxy, CSL, is four orders of magnitude less com-
027
               putationally expensive than the stability-based method and can be obtained with
               zero additional overhead during training. We demonstrate the practical utility of
028
               the proposed proxies in identifying mislabeled samples and detecting duplicates
029
               where our metric achieves state-of-the-art performance. Thus, this paper provides
030            a new tool for analyzing data as it scales in size, making it an important resource
031            in practical applications.

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Deep learning has become the de facto standard for almost all machine learning tasks from image
037   (Ho et al., 2020) and text generation (Radford et al., 2019) to classification (Krizhevsky et al., 2009;
038   Soufleri et al., 2024a) and reinforcement learning (Shakya et al., 2023). While they have been ex-
039
      tremely successful, they tend to memorize and overfit to the training data. While some memorization
      is indeed needed to obtain generalization (Feldman, 2020), these deep models can also memorize
040
      totally random images (Zhang et al., 2017). Thus to understand memorization, researchers have put
041
      in significant effort (Zhang et al., 2017; Arpit et al., 2017; Carlini et al., 2019a; Feldman & Vondrak,
042   2019; Feldman & Zhang, 2020; Feldman, 2020). Such focus is crucial due to the broad implications
043   of memorization for multiple connected areas, including generalization (Zhang et al., 2021; Brown
044   et al., 2021), noisy learning (Liu et al., 2020), identifying mislabeled examples (Maini et al., 2022),
045   recognizing rare and challenging instances (Carlini et al., 2019a), ensuring privacy (Feldman, 2020),
046   and addressing risks from membership inference attacks (Shokri et al., 2017; Carlini et al., 2022;
047   Ravikumar et al., 2024b).
048   Many approaches to study memorization have been proposed (Carlini et al., 2019a; Jiang et al.,
049   2021; Feldman, 2020). Notably, the stability-based metric proposed by Feldman (2020) measures
050   the change in expected output probability when the sample under investigation is removed from the
051   training dataset. This metric offers a robust theoretical framework for understanding memorization,
052   which was subsequently validated empirically for deep neural networks (Feldman & Zhang, 2020).
053   However, this approach is impractical for most applications due to its high computational cost.
      Recent literature has introduced other proxies for memorization, such as learning time (Jiang et al.,

      Memorization in deep neural networks has gained attention, with recent works improving our un-
150   derstanding of its mechanisms and implications (Zhang et al., 2017; Arpit et al., 2017; Carlini et al.,

161   introduced split learning and forgetting times, while Carlini et al. (2019a) combined metrics to study
      memorization. Jiang et al. (2021) proposed the C-score, a computationally efficient memorization

186   To build intuition, let us explore the loss progression of two samples, namely, an “easy” and a “hard”
187   example, both from the same class (peacock) in the ImageNet dataset (Russakovsky et al., 2015), as
188   illustrated in Figure 2. In this context, loss refers to the per-sample cross-entropy loss, which tracks
189   how well the model predicts a specific example at each stage of training.
190   For the easy sample, learned early in the process, the loss follows a simple pattern: it starts high,
191   quickly drops, and stays low for the rest of the training. The hard sample, on the other hand, behaves
192   differently. Its loss remains high for a much longer period before eventually dropping, indicating that
193   it is learned much later. This contrast in loss dynamics’ patterns evidently shows how the cumulative
194   per-sample loss throughout training can distinguish between easy and hard examples with precision.
195   Additionally, the figure demonstrates traditional metrics like learning time and forgetting time,
196   which rely on thresholds to determine when a sample is learned, may fall short of distinguishing be-
197   tween easy and hard examples. As we observe in Figure 2, a sample might be learned in one epoch,
198   unlearned in the next, and then relearned showing that learning is a noisy process. To overcome this
199   noise, we propose using cumulative sample loss, CSL (or mean sample loss) and cumulative sample
200   gradient, CSG (or mean sample gradient) as more reliable metrics. These metrics smooth out the
201   noise from fluctuations in learning. As we will demonstrate, hard examples tend to be memorized by
202
      the model, while easy examples are generalized; the proposed cumulative metrics, tracked through-
      out the training process, are key to capturing this correlation. The two proposed metrics CSL and

347   shows that the cumulative gradient is bounded by the cumulative curvature, thus bounding the total
348   learning time. The full proof in available in Appendix E.5.
349   Interpreting Theory. This theorem establishes a link between learning time, CSG, and cumulative
350   sample curvature. It shows that learning time is bounded by CSG, which in turn is bounded by
351   cumulative sample curvature. The theorem implies that CSG provides a tighter bound on learning
352   time than sample loss curvature. This is supported by the mislabel detection performance of CSG
353   compared to cumulative sample curvature, as demonstrated in the Experiment Section 5.3.
354
      Remark on Assumptions. We briefly and qualitatively evaluate the practicality of our assump-
355   tions. Prior work (Hardt et al., 2016) has demonstrated that models trained using stochastic gradient
356   methods, such as stochastic gradient descent, exhibit low generalization error. Furthermore, it has
357   been established that these methods are uniformly stable (Hardt et al., 2016), supporting the plau-
358   sibility of our assumptions on stability (Equation 1) and generalization (Equation 14). Model bias
359   is an intrinsic characteristic of the model itself, and it is reasonable to assume a uniform bound
360   across different datasets. Virmaux & Scaman (2018) have provided a general upper bound for the
361   Lipschitz constant of any differentiable deep learning model, validating the Lipschitz continuity as-
362   sumption in the context of deep models. The assumptions of an unbiased gradient estimator, along
363
      with bounded gradient norm and variance, are widely used in the optimization literature (Lian et al.,
      2017; Aketi et al., 2024), making these assumptions reasonable. In practice, loss functions are often
364
      upper-bounded, supporting the validity of the bounded loss assumption. Finally, α-adjacent dataset
365
      can be guaranteed by design. However, in practice, this may not be necessary since the size of the
366   ball Bp (α) is unrestricted. Therefore, two samples from the same class that are ‘similar’ might
367   suffice to meet this criterion.
368
369
      Key Theory Takeaways. (1) Learning time exhibits a linear relationship with all three metrics (i.e.,
      orders of loss). (2) Stability-based memorization also follows this linear relationship with the three
370
      metrics. (3) Additionally, learning time and memorization are linearly related. (4) Loss serves as
371
      the most compute-efficient proxy among the proxies considered for measuring memorization.

531
      This paper provides a comprehensive theoretical framework that connects memorization proxies,
      such as input loss curvature, cumulative sample loss, and cumulative sample gradient, to learning
532
      dynamics and stability-based memorization. Our results demonstrate that these proxies are not only
533
      highly effective in capturing memorization behavior but also computationally efficient, being four
534   orders of magnitude faster than existing stability-based metrics. We validate our framework through
535   extensive experiments and show its practical applications in identifying mislabeled examples, bias,
536   and duplicates in datasets. The proposed metrics achieve state-of-the-art performance in identifying
537   duplicate and mislabeled examples. By offering efficient tools to understand memorization, our
538   framework can lead to more interpretable models across a wide range of machine learning tasks.
539   Ultimately, this work paves the way for more scalable, accurate, and data-centric approaches in the
      deployment of deep learning models in real-world applications.

846
847   In this section, we provide a detailed analysis of the computational cost of the proposed proxies in
848   comparison to other techniques. We assume the cost of one forward pass through a neural network
849
      is F , and consequently, the cost of a backpropagation step is 2F , making the total cost for one
      forward-backward pass 3F . Using previously defined notation, let m represent the dataset size and
850
      T the total number of training epochs.
851
852   Stability-Based Memorization. Feldman & Zhang (2020) trained between 2,000 and 10,000
853   models to compute the stability-based memorization score. Thus, the total computational cost is
854   10, 000 · 3F · T · m.
855   Cumulative Sample Curvature. Garg et al. (2024) trained a single model and proposed using
856   sample curvature averaged over training to estimate memorization. Hutchinson’s trace estimator was
857   employed to calculate curvature, which requires 2 forward passes and 1 backward pass, repeated n
858   times over the entire dataset for each epoch. While their results show that n ranges from 2 to 10,
859   we use n = 2 to provide the computational advantage in their favor, even though n = 10 produces
860   better results. Thus, the total cost consists of the training and curvature computation.
861                                   Cost = 3F · T · m + 4F · T · m · n
862                                        = 3F · T · m + 4F · T · m · 2
863
                                           = 11F · T · m

868
