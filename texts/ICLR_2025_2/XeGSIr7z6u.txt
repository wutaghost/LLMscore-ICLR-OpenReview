009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            As the training set size increases, diffusion models have been observed to transi-
012            tion from memorizing the training dataset to generalizing to the underlying data
013            distribution. To study this phenomenon more closely, here, we first present a
014            mathematically principled definition of this transition: the model is said to be in
015            the generalization regime if the generated distribution is closer to the sampling
016            distribution compared to the probability distribution associated with a Gaussian
017            kernel approximation to the training dataset. Then, we develop an analytically
018            tractable diffusion model that features this transition when the training data is
019
               sampled from an isotropic Gaussian distribution. Our study reveals that this tran-
               sition occurs when the distance between the generated and underlying sampling
020
               distribution begins to decrease rapidly with the addition of more training samples.
021
               This is to be contrasted with an alternative scenario, where the model’s memoriza-
022            tion performance degrades, but generalization performance doesn’t improve. We
023            also provide empirical evidence indicating that realistic diffusion models exhibit
024            the same alignment of scales.

027   1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      In recent years, generative artificial intelligence has made tremendous advancements—be it image,
030   audio, video, or text domains—on an unprecedented scale. Diffusion models (Sohl-Dickstein et al.,
031   2015; Song & Ermon, 2019; Ho et al., 2020) are among the most successful frameworks, serving
032   as the foundation for prominent content generation tools such as DALL-E (Ramesh et al., 2022),
033   Stable Diffusion (Rombach et al., 2022), Imagen (Saharia et al., 2022), Sora (OpenAI, 2024) and
034   numerous others. However, the factors that contribute to the strong generalization capabilities of
035   diffusion models, as well as the conditions under which they perform optimally, remain open.
036   In this paper, we will focus on a particular generalization behavior that diffusion models exhibit. Em-
037   pirical observations show that for small number of training samples, diffusion models memorize the
038   training data (Somepalli et al., 2022; Carlini et al., 2023). As the training dataset size increases, they
039   transition from memorizing data to a regime where it can generate new samples from the underlying
040   distribution (Kadkhodaie et al., 2023). This has been termed the memorization-to-generalization
041   transition. What is the nature of this transition?
042   In this paper, we take steps towards better understanding of this phenomenon. Specifically, we
043   provide a mathematically precise definition of the memorization-to-generalization transition in terms
044   of distances between the training and generated distribution, denoted as ET G , and the distance
045   between the original underlying distribution and generated distribution, denoted as EOG . We say
046   that the diffusion model is starting to generalize if the probability of ∆ = ET G − EOG > 0 is
047   very close to unity implying that the generated distribution exhibits greater proximity to the original
048   underlying distribution relative to the training dataset.
049   On the onset of memorization to generalization transition individually ET G , EOG might show very

051   is failing to memorize because of faster increase in ET G compared to EOG with the increase of
052   train dataset size n. However in this case the model does not learn to generalize well because EOG
053   remains almost constant during the transition. The scenarios in (b) and (c) present other possibilities.
      Empirically, we rule out the possibility (b) in a realistic diffusion model. In this paper we show that

075   1.1   O UR CONTRIBUTIONS
076
      Our main contributions in this paper are as follows:
077
078         1. Given a finite number of samples from a probability distribution, the true distribution can
079            be approximated by a L2 -distance optimal Gaussian kernel. We observe that in the context
080            of higher dimensional statistics, the variance of the kernel coincides with that mixing time
081            for the samples under Ornstein-Uhlenbeck forward diffusion process (Biroli et al., 2024).
082         2. Using the observation above, we formulate a new, mathematically rigorous metric, namely
083            probability of ∆ > 0, for characterizing the memorization to generalization transition.
084
            3. We present an analytically tractable diffusion model that features a transition from memo-
085            rization to generalization, as measured by the aforementioned metric.
086
087
            4. We show that as the size of the training dataset increases, the memorization to generaliza-
               tion transition in the model introduced above occurs at the same scale as the onset of a rapid
088
               fall phase in the generalization error EOG .
089
090         5. We hypothesize that the alignment of memorization-to-generalization transition and onset
091
               of rapid convergence is a generic property of diffusion models. We test this hypothesis on
               a realistic U-Net based non-linear diffusion model.
092
093   Overall, our study shows that memorization-to-generalization transition in diffusion models occurs
094   on onset of fast convergence of the generated distribution to the sampling distribution of the training
095   data.

097   1.2   R ELATED WORKS
098
099   The idea of diffusion based generative models originated in the pioneering work of Sohl-Dickstein
100   et al. (2015). Subsequently, diffusion models are made scalable for real world image generation
101   (Song & Ermon, 2019; Ho et al., 2020; Kadkhodaie & Simoncelli, 2020; Song et al., 2021a). The
102   quality of the generated distribution was further enhanced through guided diffusion at the cost of
103   reduced diversity (Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021; Ho & Salimans, 2022; Wu et al., 2024; Bradley &
104
      Nakkiran, 2024; Chidambaram et al., 2024).
105   In a compelling study, Kadkhodaie et al. (2023) has demonstrated that as the train dataset size
106   increases diffusion models make a transition from memorizing the train dataset of facial images to
107   a generalization regime where two diffusion models of identical architecture can produce similar
      looking new faces even when trained on disjoint sampling sets. Yoon et al. (2023) has given a


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      precise definition of memorization capacity of a diffusion model. However when the model is not
109   memorizing, to what extent it is actually generalizing and sampling from the underlying distribution
110   is an open question that we study in this paper.
111
112
      Recent advancements toward achieving that objective have been made in (Shah et al., 2023; Cui
      et al., 2024; Biroli et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024). Biroli et al. (2024) has studied the evolution of
113
      Gaussian mixture model in forward diffusion process from the point of view of the random energy
114
      model and analytically established various important time scales in asymptotically large dimensions
115   d with exponentially large number of samples n = O(ed ). On the other hand, Cui et al. (2024) has
116   studied the reverse diffusion process of finite number of samples n = O(d0 ). In this limit it is shown
117   that the mean of the underlying distribution can be recovered with a trained two-layer denoiser with
118   one hidden neuron and a skip connection up to square error that scales as d/n. Similar upper bound
119   on the square error is noted previously by Shah et al. (2023). p Very recently Wang et al. (2024)
120   has provided a probabilistic upper bound of order d/(1 − d/n)2 on the square Frobenius error
121   in recovering the variance of the underlying distribution of a diffusion model with strong inductive
122   bias for sufficiently large train dataset n = O(d). It is not known how tight the upper bound is in
123   the regime of memorization/generalization or when the bound is saturated.

372            From Fig. 2 we see that near n ∼ d, we enter at the regime of fast convergence where the
373            Hellinger distance between the original and generated distribution H 2 decreases rapidly.
374            This shows the initiation of generalization (i.e., P (∆ > 0) ∼ 1) takes place on the onset of
375
               fast convergence.
376          • N ∼ n ≫ d: In this domain P (∆ > 0) decreases and eventually reaches the value 0.5. See
377            Fig. 3 for further details. This is because the distinction between the train and the original
               dataset disappears in this limit.

427
428   In this paper we have defined a mathematically precise metric for memorization to generalization
429   transition. We have further constructed a linear diffusion model which shows the memorization to
430   generalization transition, based on the aforementioned metric, aligns with the on-set of fast conver-
431   gence of generalization error. Finally we have shown that this alignment phenomena is generic and
      occurs in realistic non-linear diffusion models.

459   Our work generates the possibility of further analytical study on diffusion models. For instance,
460   instead of a linear diffusion model, a (stack of) wide neural network in the kernel approximation
461   regime (Jacot et al., 2018; Lee et al., 2019; Bordelon et al., 2021; Canatar et al., 2021; Atanasov
462   et al., 2023) or in mean field regime (Mei et al., 2018; Yang & Hu, 2021; Bordelon & Pehlevan,

464   to generalization transition might be employed to systematically select optimal hyperparameters for
465   model training. Specifically, for a given training set, one can plot the curve of P (∆ > 0) versus
466   n across various hyperparameter configurations. The model that transitions to generalization at a
467
      smaller dataset size may be regarded as the more efficient one. We leave a thorough investigation of
      these questions to future work.
