011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            The Multi-Modal Hashing (MMH) method based on complete modalities cannot
014            effectively handle incomplete multi-modal samples, thus requiring the comple-
015
               tion of missing modalities. Existing completion methods typically use complete
               modality samples with the same label to generate completion information. On one
016
               hand, they cannot fully utilize the different information between samples with dif-
017
               ferent labels; on the other hand, they cannot effectively extract the global structural
018            information of multi-modal samples. Therefore, we propose the autoencoder and
019            classifier based joint-guided completion for partial multi-modal hashing (JCPMH)
020            method that integrates autoencoders and classifiers. First, to fully utilize the dif-
021            ferent information between samples with different labels, we design a multi-modal
022            classification module composed of multiple classifiers to learn different informa-
023            tion. Second, we concatenate the multi-modal data into a whole and extract cross-
024            modal global structural information through an autoencoder. Finally, based on the
025            hashing module, multi-modal classification module and autoencoder module, we
026
               design a loss function to guide the generator to generate more accurate completion
               information for learning hash codes. JCPMH can utilize partial multi-modal sam-
027
               ples for offline training and handle incomplete multi-modal samples during online
028
               retrieval. Additionally, we conducted extensive experiments to demonstrate the
029            effectiveness of this model.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   With the rapid growth of internet data, multi-modal hashing (MMH)Shen et al. (2015b); Lu et al.
035   (2019; 2020); Zhu et al. (2020) has been widely applied to multi-modal retrieval tasks due to its
036   low storage overhead and fast retrieval capabilities. However, most MMH works assume that the
037   multi-modal samples being processed is complete, meaning that data from all corresponding modal-
038   ities are present. This assumption limits the application of these methods. To address the issue of
039
      missing modalities, there are two approaches: one approach is to ignore the completion process for
      missing modalities and directly use partial modality dataZheng et al. (2021). However, this often
040
      leads to significant loss of multi-modal semantic information, especially when the partial data ratio
041
      (PDR)Zheng et al. (2021) is high. Therefore, we need to adopt the second approach, which involves
042   completing the data before using the partial multi-modal samplesa, and then using the completed
043   samples to learn hash codes.
044
045
      Among the completion methods, NCHTan et al. (2023) uses a deep learning strategy, introducing
      neighbor information to dynamically generate completion information from complete multi-modal
046
      samples with the same label. On one hand, it cannot utilize information from partial modality data,
047
      resulting in low data utilization, and on the other hand, it ignores the discriminative information
048   between samples of different categories. Additionally, existing methods often process data from
049   multiple modalities separately to extract multi-modal semantic information, leading to the loss of
050   global structural information of multi-modal samples. To solve these problems and effectively com-
051   plete missing modalities, we propose a autoencoder and classifier based joint-guided completion for
052   partial multi-modal hashing (JCPMH) method that integrates autoencoderHinton & Zemel (1993)
053   and classifiers. Specifically, we concatenate data from multiple corresponding modalities into a
      whole and use an autoencoder to fully exploit the global structural information of multi-modal sam-

054
      ples. Additionally, we design a multi-modal classification module composed of multiple classifiers
055   to extract category-level discriminative information using label supervision. Unlike NCH, this clas-
056   sification module can extract information from partial modality data, improving data utilization and
057   extracting richer information. We then use these two types of information to jointly guide the gener-
058   ation of completion information, feeding the completed and complete multi-modal samples into the
059   subsequent hashing network to learn more accurate and discriminative hash codes. Both NCH and
060   our JCPMH can handle incomplete multi-modal samples during offline training and online query
061   stages. Our main contributions are as follows:

076   2.1   H ASHING -BASED M ULTI -M ODAL R ETRIEVAL
077
078   Different from single-modal hashingGionis et al. (1999); Gong & Lazebnik (2011); Shen et al.
079   (2015a); Wang et al. (2018); Chen & Lu (2020); Zheng et al. (2020); Yu et al. (2022) and cross-modal
080   hashingXu et al. (2017); Jiang & Li (2017), multi-modal hashing requires the fusion of multi-modal
081   samples to learn compact binary codes. Considering the cost of manual annotation and the generality
082
      of methods, some unsupervised works Song et al. (2013); Liu et al. (2015); Shen et al. (2022; 2018)
      have been proposed. They do not require label information. Most of these unsupervised methods are
083
      based on shallow learning methods. They typically use graphs or matrices to construct relationships
084
      within or between modalities. Considering that the learned hash codes are discrete binary codes,
085   they also introduce some discrete optimization strategies. Multiple Feature Hashing (MFH)Song
086   et al. (2013) constructs affinity matrices for each modality internally and considers all these local
087   structures in the subsequent optimization process to learn fused multi-modal representations. Multi-
088   view Alignment Hashing (MAH)Liu et al. (2015) formulates regularized kernel non-negative matrix
089   factorization to learn hash codes. It explores the semantic information and joint probability distri-
090   bution of multi-modal data. Multi-view Discrete Hashing (MvDH)Shen et al. (2018) learns labels
091   through spectral clustering and uses this supervised information to enhance the discrimination of
092   the learned hash codes. Unsupervised multi-view distributed hashing (UMvDisH)Shen et al. (2022)
093
      proposes an unsupervised multi-modal distributed learning method that directly learns hash codes
      on multi-modal distributed data. It also introduces the alternating direction method of multipliers
094
      (ADMM)Boyd et al. (2011) to solve the decentralized sub-optimization problem.
095
096   Due to the lack of guidance from manually annotated labels, these unsupervised methods have lim-
097   ited ability to explore semantic information between samples. Supervised methodsLu et al. (2019;
098   2020); Zhu et al. (2020); Yang et al. (2017); Liu et al. (2020) focus on exploring semantic informa-
099
      tion in labels and use labels to supervise the learning of hash codes. Flexible Discrete Multi-view
      Hashing (FDMH)Liu et al. (2020) proposes a collaborative learning strategy that encodes visual and
100
      semantic embeddings into a consistent Hamming space. With the development of deep learning,
101
      some works have applied deep learning methods to supervised MMH tasks. Deep Collaborative
102   Multi-View Hashing (DCMVH)Zhu et al. (2020) is the first to propose a deep MMH model. It de-
103   signs a fusion network that deeply integrates multi-modal features and learns representations that
104   include low-level multi-view feature distributions and high-level semantics. Inspired by the suc-
105   cess of graph convolutional networks (GCNs)Kipf & Welling (2016), Flexible Graph Convolutional
106   Multi-modal Hashing (FGCMH)Lu et al. (2021a) constructs a graph structure for each modality.
107   Through intra-modal GCNs, it extracts intra-modal structural information. Additionally, hash GCNs
      and semantic GCNs process the fused graph after modality fusion.

      2.2   PARTIAL M ULTI -M ODAL H ASHING
109
110
      To address the issue of incomplete modality data in practice, several attempts have been made. For
111
      cross-modal scenarios, Partial Multi-Modal Hashing (PM2 H) Wang et al. (2015) was the first to
112   attempt partial cross-modal hashing. It utilizes complete modalities to explore cross-modal con-
113   sistency and enhances the representation of hash codes through orthogonal rotation. Semi-Paired
114   Discrete Hashing (SPDH) Shen et al. (2017) uses fully paired anchors to leverage partial samples,
115   constructing a common subspace for both complete and incomplete samples. The Collective Affin-
116   ity Learning Method (CALM) Guo & Zhu (2020) is an unsupervised method that uses collective
117   affinity reconstruction to learn anchor graphs for each modality. It also proposes adaptive affine
118   fusion to adaptively fuse adjacency information from different modalities. Incomplete Cross-Modal
119   Retrieval with Deep Correlation Transfer (ICMR-DCT) Shi et al. (2024) leverages available modal-
120
      ities and neighboring relationships in partial multi-modal samples. It uses a graph attention network
      (GAT)Veličković et al. (2018)-based encoder to generate missing modalities and compress multi-
121
      modal features into a subspace.
122
123   These cross-modal retrieval methods for handling missing modalities are inspiring, but they cannot
124   be directly applied to partial MMH tasks. FOMH Lu et al. (2019) and FGCMH Lu et al. (2021b)
125   propose flexible MMH models that can handle missing modalities during the query phase of multi-
126
      modal retrieval but cannot utilize partial multi-modal samples during the training phase. SAPMH
      Zheng et al. (2021), GCIMH Shen et al. (2023), and NCH Tan et al. (2023) can handle partial
127
      multi-modal samples during both training and query phases. SAPMH proposes a shallow hashing
128
      method to construct latent representations for each available modality; however, shallow methods
129   lack semantic representation capabilities compared to deep learning frameworks. GCIMH designs
130   a teacher-student structure with three modules. It first uses mean imputation to complete miss-
131   ing modalities, then inputs the completed data into two teacher networks to extract information.
132   However, simply using fixed values for imputation may introduce erroneous information, mislead-
133   ing subsequent processing. NCH adopts a Transformer Encoder Vaswani et al. (2017) structure
134   to generate missing modality data through neighboring samples with the same label, ignoring the
135   discriminative information between samples of different tcategories. Additionally, NCH can only
136   use fully paired anchors to generate missing modality data. Considering these issues, we propose a
137
      method named JCPMH to effectively complete missing modalities and generate robust hash codes.

      4.2   E XPERIMENTAL S ETTING AND E VALUATION M ETRIC
329
330   Our experiment consists of five parts. The first part is conducted on fully-paired samples, which can
331   be considered a traditional multimodal hashing task. We will compare our method with the following
332   multimodal hashing models: DMVH, SDMH, FOMH, FDMH, DCMVH, SAPMH, FGCMH, NCH,
333   GCIMH. The second part, which is also crucial, involves conducting experiments in a scenario with
334   missing modalities. In this context, only the SAPMH, NCH, and GCIMH models mentioned above
335
      are capable of handling missing modalities during both the training and query stages.
336   Additionally, we conducted a series of experiments to evaluate the effectiveness of missing data
337   completion in subsection 4.5. In subsection 4.6 and subsection 4.7, we conducted a detailed
338   analysis of the results from ablation studies and parameter sensitivity analysis.
339   For evaluation metrics, mAP (mean Average Precision) serves as our main metric. It is widely used
340   in image retrieval and multi-modal retrieval tasks. mAP requires the participation of all samples
341   from the database in its calculation, making it an effective reflection of the retrieval capability of
342   hash codes.
343
      We run the model on a 16GB VRAM V100 GPU. For the classification module, both the image and
344
      text classifiers are MLPs. The hidden layers have dimensions of 2048 and 512, respectively. The
345   output layer length corresponds to the number of categories: 24 for the Flickr dataset and 21 for the
346   NUS-WIDE dataset. For the autoencoder, its encoder consists of two layers of Graph Convolutional
347   Networks (GCN) with an intermediate hidden layer of 4096 dimensions. The decoder comprises two
348   linear layers that separately output reconstructed image and text data, using ReLU as the activation
349   function. For the hashing network, we designed two MLPs as generators. Each takes one modality
350   (either image or text) as input and generates the other modality. The hidden layer dimensions are
351   2048. Similarly, the hashing networks for both image and text are also MLPs, transforming the
352   incomplete text and image features into a unified 1024-dimensional representation. Finally, the
353
      fusion model is a linear layer, and its output dimension corresponds to the final hash code length.
