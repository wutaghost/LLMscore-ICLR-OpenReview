009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            The Superficial Alignment Hypothesis posits that almost all of a language model’s
012            abilities and knowledge are learned during pre-training, while post-training is
013            about giving a model the right style and format. We re-examine these claims
014            by empirically studying the scaling behavior of post-training with increasing fine-
015            tuning examples and evaluating them using objective task-specific standardized
016            benchmarks. Through experiments with the Llama-3, Mistral, and Llama-2 model
017
               families of multiple sizes, we observe that, similar to the pre-training scaling
               laws, post-training task performance scales as a power law against the number
018
               of finetuning examples. This power law relationship holds across a broad array
019
               of capabilities, including mathematical reasoning, coding, instruction following,
020            and multihop-reasoning. In addition, for tasks like math and multihop reason-
021            ing, we observe that a handful of examples merely align the model stylistically
022            but do not saturate performance on the benchmarks. Model performance is in-
023            stead correlated with its reasoning ability and it improves significantly with more
024            examples, illustrating the need for holistic evaluation programs leveraging objec-
025            tive benchmarks in addition to measurement of alignment to human preferences.
026            We also observe that language models are not necessarily limited to using knowl-
027            edge learned during pre-training. With appropriate post-training, a model’s ability
028
               to integrate new knowledge greatly improves on downstream tasks like multihop
               question-answering. Taken together, these results shed new light on the Superfi-
029
               cial Alignment Hypothesis, suggesting that it is, at best, an over-simplification.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Large Language Models (LLMs) based on the Transformer architecture have achieved state-of-
035   the-art performance on tasks that involve instruction following, problem-solving, and reasoning
036   (Vaswani et al., 2017; Achiam et al., 2023; Dubey et al., 2024). The standard pipeline for build-
037   ing LLMs powered applications involves unsupervised training of a model on a giant corpus of data
038   to gain general language understanding capability, referred to as pre-training (Radford et al., 2019;
039   Brown et al., 2020). The model is further improved using post-training, which involves finetuning
040
      it to excel at a particular domain or behave like a helpful chatbot. This process is also referred
      to as alignment. The predominant way to do this is through Supervised Finetuning (SFT) where
041
      the language model is provided with a prompt, and the model is finetuned to respond to the task
042
      (Wei et al., 2022). An additional step is Reinforcement Learning through Human Feedback (RLHF)
043   where a model is trained using reinforcement learning to generate human-preferred responses, by
044   being rewarded for good responses and penalized for bad responses (Ouyang et al., 2022).
045
046
      To achieve the post-training goal of responding appropriately to various user queries, LLMs need
      to develop several task-specific capabilities, like mathematics, reasoning, utilizing knowledge, and
047
      tool use. To teach a model these capabilities, model builders collect human-annotated or syntheti-
048
      cally generated data and finetune the model to obtain the desired behavior. Since data collection at
049   scale is labor and cost-intensive, it is essential to understand the qualitative and quantitative value
050   of obtaining additional finetuning data. Studies like LIMA by Zhou et al. (2024) have hypothe-
051   sized that post-training alignment is all about learning the style and format of the desired behavior.
052   Specifically, it puts forward the Superficial Alignment Hypothesis, whose claims are:
053
             • C1: A model’s knowledge is learned entirely during pre-training.


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
             • C2: Post-training is largely about style and doesn’t does not teach a model new capabilities.
055
056          • C3: A small number of examples can saturate a model’s performance for a given task.
057
058   However, the experiments from LIMA and follow-up works by Lin et al. (2023) primarily evalu-
059
      ate chatbot style interaction capabilities - tasks that require mostly cosmetic changes to a model’s
      response style. It is unclear how these models improve on task-specific reasoning capabilities dur-
060
      ing post-training. They are also evaluated using a subjective win-rate comparison, over open-ended
061
      prompts. This doesn’t provide an objective pattern to analyze model behavior and thus fails to pro-
062   vide useful information about the nature of model performance and dataset size. For researchers
063   and practitioners who finetune LLMs to perform specific tasks, understanding scaling behavior with
064   more data is crucial in aiding data collection and annotation efforts. There is also a need to study if
065   these LLMs are limited to the knowledge acquired during pre-training, or if we can introduce new
066   knowledge and show how to utilize it effectively. So, we design three research questions to better
067   investigate these claims:
068
069         1. How does post-training model performance scale with finetuning dataset size?
070         2. Is the model significantly improving on task-related capabilities or just learning the re-
071            sponse style?
072
            3. Can a model integrate new knowledge from beyond the pertaining knowledge cutoff?
073
074
      In the following section, we summarise the key results from the study, followed by sections that
075   detail the experimental setup, results, and conclusions for each of the research questions outlined
076   above.

079   2   K EY TAKEAWAYS
080
081
             • Post-training performance on a task has a power law relationship of the form P ∝ D1/b
               with the number of finetuning samples, across models of multiple families and sizes, similar
082
               to scaling laws established for pretraining and inference(Kaplan et al., 2020; Brown et al.,
083
               2024). (Section 3)
084
085          • Evaluating alignment models using win-rates as shown in Zhou et al. (2024) could be mis-
086
               leading for reasoning-based tasks. For instance, LLM-based judges can prefer model gen-
               erations that exhibit a chatbot-style answer for mathematical questions, even though the
087
               model might be poor at mathematical abilities as observed on math benchmarks. (Section
088
               3.3)
089
090          • Through extensive error analysis on tasks like math and multihop reasoning, we see that
091            when a model is finetuned for a task, the improvements in task-specific style and formatting
092
               saturate in just 100 examples, as hypothesized by the Superficial Alignment Hypothesis.
               However, the model’s performance on the task is directly correlated with its improvements
093
               in reasoning ability, which improves notably during post-training with more finetuning
094
               examples. (Section 4)
095
096          • Post-training a model for reasoning can also help a model integrate knowledge beyond its
097
               pre-training knowledge cutoff. Compared to pre-trained models, finetuned models learn
               and use new knowledge on downstream tasks effectively. (Section 5)
098
099
      These experiments help frame the Superficial Alignment Hypothesis in a new light. Most impor-
100
      tantly, the focus on post-training should not be entirely on stylistic alignment, but also on measuring
101
      downstream task metrics. When seen through this lens, we see that after finetuning on a few high-
102   quality examples, LLMs behave in the right style and format, especially when evaluated through
103   subjective techniques like win rate. However, this doesn’t necessarily warrant the conclusion that the
104   model has been aligned for the task. When evaluated for their objective task-specific performance,
105   we see that the models do improve significantly with additional data on many tasks during post-
106   training over their pre-trained counterparts. In addition, these improvements are primarily driven by
107   improvements in reasoning and analytical abilities during finetuning. Good post-training is also an
      effective way for LLMs to learn and integrate new knowledge from beyond their knowledge cut-off.

118   3       P OST- TRAINING DATA S CALING
119
120   How does post-training model performance scale with finetuning dataset size?
121
      Following prior art (Zhou et al., 2024; Lin et al., 2023), we interpret pre-training as training a
122
      model using a large corpus of text. The primary objective is to gain general-purpose language
123   understanding and is mainly optimized using negative log-likelihood next token prediction loss on a
124   large corpus. In contrast, post-training is the process of finetuning a language model to improve on
125   specific capabilities that are important and relevant to humans - instruction following, math, coding,
126   etc. These are primarily optimized by evaluating through task-specific benchmarks. Although this
127   distinction is not always clear because of intermediate training stages, the simplified setting allows us
128   to thoroughly evaluate the Superficial Alignment Hypothesis and understand the value of finetuning
129   data without many confounding variables.
130
131   The primary implication of the Superficial Alignment Hypothesis is that pre-training is all that mat-
132   ters, and with a rather small set of examples, we can align a model during post-training. How-
133   ever, this is a broad claim that is supported by a limited set of chatbot-style experiments. Post-
134   training a model involves finetuning for instruction following, problem-solving, and coding, and
135   unlike chatbot-style dialogue whose evaluation is subjective and comparative, these capabilities can
136
      be judged using standardized benchmarks. For researchers and model builders who aim to improve
      performance on such tasks, it is important to understand performance scaling on such benchmarks
137
      with increasing finetuning data.
138
139
      3.1       E XPERIMENT D ESIGN
140
141   We look at four tasks (training datasets used are in parenthesis) - mathematical problem solving
142   (GSM8k (Cobbe et al., 2021)), instruction following ((Sun et al., 2024; Conover et al., 2023)), cod-
143   ing (StarCoder Self-Align 1 ) and multihop question answering (SubQA (Tang et al., 2021)). Start-
144   ing from the base model and finetuning with increasing dataset size, we observe how performance
145   scales during finetuning. For each task, we ran evaluations using a standard framework where avail-
146   able (Gao et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2023). More details about the training data are
147   available in Table 1 and additional dataset construction details can be found in Appendix A.3.
148   For our experiments, we finetune Llama-3, Llama-2 and Mistral model families on these tasks
149   (Dubey et al., 2024; Touvron et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2023). We chose base models because it
150   is likely that instruct models are already extensively finetuned for these tasks.
151
      We first finetuned the smallest (sub-10 Billion parameter) models from these model families, with
152
      the dataset splits of 0, 100, 500, 1000, 5000, and 10000 examples until the training dataset was
153   exhausted. To study the effects of model parameter size, we also scale up the models in the Llama-2
154   and Llama-3 families from 7B to 70B parameters - a 10X increase. Since full-parameter fine-tuning
155   of 70B models on a multi-node GPU cluster is resource-intensive, we limited the number of training
156   runs for the 70B model sizes.
157
      To ensure fairness, every model in a model family was trained with the same set of hyperparameters
158
      for a given task and dataset size, for 3 epochs over the base model with the default chat template
159   from HuggingFace. More details about the hyperparameters for training and inference can be found
160   in A.7.

      3.3    G ENERAL P URPOSE A LIGNMENT VS TASK S PECIFIC F INETUNING
225
226   The LIMA paper also introduced the LIMA dataset, a collection of 1,000 carefully curated prompts
227   that was intended to align a pre-trained model to be on par with state-of-the-art post-trained models.
228   It is based on the hypothesis that the model has inherently learned most of its capabilities during
229   pre-training and thus, the model just needs to adopt a stylistic format to answer questions.
230
      In this section, we finetune the Llama-3 8b model with the LIMA dataset, using the same train-
231
      ing setup as the rest of the datasets. We call this model LIMA-1k. We also finetune a pre-trained
232   model with 1,000 examples specific to a task - GSM8k for math and SubQA for multihop reasoning.
233   This ensures that the LIMA and task-specific models are trained with a similar data “budget”. We
234   call models finetuned with task-specific datasets Task-1k. We then evaluate the performance of the
235   chat-bot style aligned LIMA-1k against task-specific fine-tuned Task-1k model on the task-specific
236   benchmarks.
237

      5.2     I NTRODUCING NEW KNOWLEDGE
379
380
      There are two primary ways to introduce new knowledge to a model - finetuning on the new events
381
      or during inference as part of the prompt. The latter is a simplified Retrieval Augmented Generation
382   (RAG) setup Lewis et al. (2020). We investigate the role of post-training in both of these cases.
383
384   Event SFT: To train the model to learn this new information, we finetune a model on the Direct
385
      Question as the prompt and the Event + Answer as the response. The format is the same as the
      SubQARecall to keep the data in-distribution.
386
387   Event RAG-Oracle: In this method, we simulate a RAG setup in which knowledge is added in the
388   prompt during inference instead of training on it. To isolate the errors introduced by the retriever
389   component of RAG, we directly add the corresponding event in the prompt, emulating a perfect
390   retriever i.e. an Oracle.
391

404
      Post-training a model for reasoning helps models learn and integrate new knowledge better.
405
      As seen in Table 4, models post-trained for reasoning are significantly better at learning new knowl-
406
      edge (Direct Question) as well as integrating the new knowledge (Multihop Reasoning Question).
407   However, SFT or RAG on the pre-trained model fails to show the same improvement on the harder
408   multi-hop questions. Note that this answer is just one hop from the answer to the direct question
409   but requires it to reason through the steps. This shows that the model can’t correctly use this new
410   information in the right way on reasoning tasks.
411
      However, if the model is first post-trained to do reasoning, it gets better at absorbing new infor-
412
      mation and using it in multihop reasoning tasks. This post-training was done on data from before
413   the knowledge cutoff. Such post-training led to a marked difference in both SFT and RAG-based
414   methods for introducing new knowledge.
415
416
417   Models hallucination is mitigated by post-training for reasoning, but is not eliminated. Sev-
418   eral studies have shown that LLMs hallucinate when introduced to new knowledge. Since all of
419   the models in our experiments are finetuned to recall the event first and generate the answer based
420   on it, we can easily check for hallucination in the recalled event. We also analyze the subsequent
421   reasoning chain for reasoning errors. Both of these are done using GPT-4o and the prompt used is
422
      shown in the Appendix A.3.4.
423   From Figure 5, we see that hallucinations about the event are a major factor in erroneous model
424   responses. Even after introducing new knowledge, base models hallucinate significantly. This is
425   in line with other works that demonstrate that finetuning models with new knowledge can lead to
426   hallucinations (Gekhman et al., 2024).
427   However, models post-trained for reasoning make a marked improvement in both hallucination and
428   reasoning errors. Although hallucination is not completely mitigated, the true value of finetuning
429   is in preparing the model to reason with the new knowledge it receives. In addition, RAG-based
430   methods are better than SFT for introducing new knowledge because the relevant information being
431   in the context is a lot more useful. We also do a variety of ablation studies to compare against
      continual pre-training and LIMA-based finetuning, which are detailed in the Appendix A.2.

454   6   R ELATED W ORK
455
456   Language Model Alignment: LLMs are first pretrained to gain general language understanding
457   and world modeling, which is then followed by an alignment phase that involves SFT and RLHF to
458   appropriately respond to user’s prompts (Ouyang et al., 2022; Wei et al., 2021; Dubey et al., 2024).
459   Several recent works laid out their hypothesis on whether alignment is more about learning style and
460   might not even be necessary because of In-Context Learning (Zhou et al., 2024; Lin et al., 2023).
461   More recent works by Zhao et al. (2024) show that instruction tuning on better base models can
462   outperform In Context Learning.
463   Scaling Laws: Several works like Hoffmann et al. (2022); Kaplan et al. (2020); Bahri et al. (2024);
464   Michaud et al. (2024) have studied and developed scaling laws for LLM Pretraining in terms of the
465   dataset token count and the model parameter count against the cross-entropy loss. However, Schaef-
466   fer et al. (2024) show that it is hard to predict scaling laws for downstream task performance because
467   of constraints in the action space. Isik et al. (2024); Hernandez et al. (2021) derive scaling laws for
468   finetuning as a function of pretraining data and transfer learning respectively, and not post-training
469
      or instruction following. However, researchers and model builders improve model performance by
      collecting individual prompt-response examples rather than collective dataset tokens (Dubey et al.,
470
      2024). Other works like Jiang et al. (2024); Li et al. (2024) also shed light on the performance of
471
      models with varying finetuning dataset sizes in specific domains and models.
472
473
474   7   C ONCLUSIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
475
476
477
      Conclusions: LLM post-training is a complex endeavor that involves improvements to instruction
      following, stylistic formatting, reasoning abilities, and general alignment to human preferences.
478
      LLMs can imitate the required style with “superficial” finetuning using a handful of examples, lead-
479
      ing to the Superficial Alignment Hypothesis. However, a solely stylistic evaluation fails to charac-
480   terize the many aspects of reasoning and task-specific capabilities that are key goals of finetuning.
481   In fact, task-specific skills & reasoning significantly improve after post-training with more examples
482   compared to the pre-trained model. These improvements closely follow a power law in our exper-
483   iments with the number of finetuning examples across multiple model families and sizes. We also
484   see that these improvements are driven by the model’s reasoning ability during generation, and are
485   not limited to the model’s alignment to formatting or style. In addition, we see that the win rate
      against other models can be a misleading metric to measure tasks that require complex reasoning,


                                                        9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      signaling the need for holistic evaluation programs leveraging standardized, objective benchmarks,
487   in addition to measurement of alignment to human preferences.
488
489   We also observe that good post-training can help LLMs overcome problems associated with knowl-
490
      edge cutoff, by enabling them to better utilize knowledge from beyond the pre-training corpus either
      via further finetuning or RAG. These results put together highlight the qualitative and quantitative
491
      characteristics of post-training, and the role of data scaling in this.
492
493   Limitations and future work: In this work we showed the performance improvement of a model
494   on a task when it is finetuned with increasing task-specific data. However, frontier LLMs are trained
495   to excel at multiple tasks, and we don’t thoroughly understand how finetuning for one task or domain
496
      affects the performance on others. A big open question would be investigating how to take advantage
      of this scaling behavior while preventing model degradation on existing capabilities. Similarly, we
497
      showed how models can learn new knowledge beyond their pre-training data cutoff, but the issue of
498
      hallucination isn’t solved. Further research in effectively introducing new knowledge, like continual
499   learning methods during post-training can shed light on this.
500
501   In this work, we also limited the scope to supervised finetuning, on tasks that involve text generation.
502
      However, the implications from this opens up several interesting directions to explore further. Most
      notably, LLM post-training involves RLHF after supervised finetuning, and it would be interesting
503
      to see how RLHF can contribute to these improvements and how it scales with more data.
