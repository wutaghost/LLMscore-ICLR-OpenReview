009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Neural Architecture Search (NAS) relies heavily on labeled data, which is labor-
012            intensive and time-consuming to obtain. In this paper, we propose a novel NAS
013            method based on an unsupervised paradigm, specifically Masked Autoencoders
014            (MAE), thereby eliminating the need for labeled data during the searching pro-
015            cess. By replacing the supervised learning objective with an image reconstruction
016            task, our approach enables the robust discovery of network architectures without
017            compromising performance and generalization ability. Additionally, we address
018
               the problem of performance collapse encountered in the widely-used Differen-
               tiable Architecture Search (DARTS) in the unsupervised setting by designing a
019
               hierarchical decoder. Through extensive experiments conducted across various
020
               search spaces and datasets, we demonstrate the effectiveness and robustness of our
021            method, offering empirical evidence of its superiority over baseline approaches.

024   1   I NTRODUCTION
025
026   In recent years, there has been a significant surge of interest in Neural Architecture Search (NAS)
027   within the machine learning field Zela et al. (2020); Liang et al. (2019); Ramachandran et al. (2018);
028   Liu et al. (2019a). NAS algorithms have emerged as a powerful tool for automatically discovering
029   superior network architectures, potentially saving valuable time and effort for human experts. These
030   algorithms have demonstrated remarkable success in various tasks, including but not limited to im-
031   age classification and object detection, by discovering neural architectures that achieve state-of-the-
032   art performance.
033   Existing NAS methods focus on learning from labeled data, leveraging the power of supervised
034   learning to guide the search for the optimal architectures. By utilizing labeled data, consisting of
035   samples paired with their corresponding ground truth labels, NAS algorithms aim to find competitive
036   models that can perform effectively across a variety of tasks and scenarios. However, obtaining
037   substantial quantities of human-annotated data proves to be costly and time-consuming. A portion
038   of the research Liu et al. (2020); Yan et al. (2020); Zhang et al. (2021) has shifted its attention
039
      towards exploring methods to minimize the reliance on annotated data.
040   In this study, we present a novel NAS method called MAE-NAS based on Masked Autoencoders He
041   et al. (2022). To the best of our knowledge, this area has received limited explicit exploration in
042   previous research. Specifically, we apply the unsupervised paradigm of mask image modeling Xie
043   et al. (2022); He et al. (2022) to the widely adopted DARTS method Liu et al. (2019b). Instead
044   of relying on the supervised classification objective employed in DARTS, we replace it with the
045
      image reconstruction task, thereby preventing the need for labeled data during the search process.
      Our approach is shown in Figure 1, where the randomly masked images are fed into the encoder
046
      and passed through the decoder to produce a reconstructed image. The encoder section covers the
047
      search space for NAS, aimed at selecting a superior model to enhance the quality of the reconstructed
048   image. Based on MAE-NAS, we further investigate the issue of performance collapse of DARTS in
049   an unsupervised setting. The results indicate that the occurrence of collapse is highly correlated with
050   the size of the mask ratio. Notably, a higher mask ratio (i.e., greater than 0.5) effectively enables
051   DARTS to robustly overcome performance collapse. Motivated by this, we propose a hierarchical
052   decoder to stabilize the search process, fundamentally solving the issue of performance collapse in
053   DARTS. Specifically, the decoder takes hierarchical features as input, which encodes both fine and
      coarse-grained information of the input image, and its output is the reconstructed image.

094

      Supervised neural architecture search. It has emerged as a prominent paradigm in NAS research.
096   Initially, NAS methods involved training candidate architectures from scratch and iteratively up-
097   dating the controller based on performance feedback. However, this approach needs a substantial
098   computational cost, as exemplified by NAS-Net Zoph et al. (2018b), which requires approximately
099   1350-1800 GPU days. To address this challenge and enhance the efficiency of NAS, weight-sharing
100   mechanisms have been widely adopted in various studies. These approaches can also be classified
101   into two main categories: one-shot methods Bender et al. (2018); Dong & Yang (2019a;a); Li et al.
102   (2020c); Chu et al. (2021b; 2023) and gradient-based methods Liu et al. (2019b); Chu et al. (2021a;
103   2020); Li et al. (2020a).
104   One-shot methods Bender et al. (2018); Cai et al. (2019); Chen et al. (2019b) entail training an
105   over-parameterized supernet using diverse sampling strategies. Once the supernet is effectively
106   trained, multiple child models are evaluated as potential alternatives, and those exhibiting superior
107   performance are selected. In contrast, gradient-based algorithms optimize both the network weights
      and architecture parameters simultaneously through back-propagation. The selection of operators is


                                                            2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      based on the magnitudes of the architecture parameters. These approaches aim to reduce the com-
109   putational cost of NAS while still achieving commendable performance. Through the utilization of
110   weight-sharing mechanisms and the adoption of different optimization strategies, researchers have
111   made significant progress in enhancing the efficiency and practicality of NAS. Given the differen-
112   tiable and end-to-end characteristics of the DARTS paradigm, our study adopts it to investigate the
113   unsupervised NAS.
114
      Unsupervised neural architecture search. Recently, there has been a growing emphasis on the
115   application of unsupervised learning, including the field of NAS. This unsupervised paradigm has
116   gained attention due to its potential to alleviate the reliance on labeled data. Notably, UnNAS Liu
117   et al. (2020) provides a comprehensive analysis of the impact of labeled data on NAS performance.
118   Their findings challenge the conventional belief that labeled data is indispensable for NAS. Building
119   upon this, RLNAS Zhang et al. (2021) leverages random labels instead of true labels. Surprisingly,
120   their research demonstrates that neural architectures discovered using random labels can achieve
121   comparable or even superior performance over supervised NAS methods. MIM-DARTSGuo et al.
122   (2022) introduces the MAE objective as an auxiliary loss on top of the original supervised loss to
123
      address the issue of performance collapse of DARTS. However, no studies have explored masked
      autoencoders for fully unsupervised NAS.

129
130
      Let Ltrain and Lval represent the training and validation loss, respectively. For DARTS Liu
131
      et al. (2019b), its goal is to find α∗ that minimizes the validation loss Lval (w∗ , α∗ ), where the
132   weights w∗ associated with the architecture are obtained by minimizing the training loss w∗ =
133   arg minw Ltrain (w, α∗ ). This formulation leads to a two-level optimization problem:
134
135
136                                              \begin {aligned} \mathop {\min }_{\alpha } \quad &\mathcal {L}_{val}(w^{*}(\alpha ), \alpha ) \\ s.t. \quad & w^{*}(\alpha ) = \arg \min _{w} \mathcal {L}_{train}(w, \alpha ) \end {aligned} \label {darts_equation}
137                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                              (1)
138
139
140   Our approach, MAE-NAS, is grounded in a crucial observation: supervised neural architecture
141   search often yields final models that overfit the training data. In other words, regardless of how
142   we optimize α and w in Equation 1, these models consistently achieve near-zero training errors.
143   However, the ultimate goal of the search process is to identify architectures that exhibit strong gen-
144
      eralization performance on the validation set. This presents an inherent contradiction in supervised
      learning. With this perspective in mind, we propose leveraging the widely-used SimMIM Xie et al.
145
      (2022) as a proxy task for NAS. In this way, we seek to discover models with enhanced general-
146
      ization capabilities in the unsupervised paradigm. Building upon DARTS, the new optimization
147   objective is formulated as follows:
148
149
150                                 \begin {aligned} \mathop {\min }_{\alpha } \quad &\mathcal {L}_{val}^{M}(w^{*}(\alpha ), \alpha , M) \\ s.t. \quad & w^{*}(\alpha ) = \arg \min _{w} \mathcal {L}_{train}^{M}(w, \alpha ^*, M) \end {aligned} \label {darts_mea_equation}
151                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                              (2)
152
153
154
      where M denotes the set of masked pixels, and LM              M
                                                          val and Ltrain represent the image reconstruction
155
      loss, which is identical to SimMIM (more details in appendix A.1.1). And the SSL-style objective
156   is the sole loss function and doesn’t bring in extra training cost. As shown in Figure 1, our method
157   comprises an encoder that transforms the input into a latent representation, as well as a decoder that
158   reconstructs the original input. Specifically, the same supernet as DARTS serves as the backbone
159   of the encoder. In this way, the masked autoencoder becomes robust NAS learners, seeking to
160   learn promising encoder architectures from the DARTS search space, resulting in the minimal image
161   reconstruction loss. Note applying MAE on convolution networks is non-trivial, we generally follow
      SparK Tian et al. (2023) for this purpose and give the details in Appendix A.1.1.

      3.1.1   E SCAPING F ROM P ERFORMANCE C OLLAPSE .
163
164   DARTS exhibits a significant decline in performance when skip connections become dominant in
165   a supervised setting. Numerous studies Chu et al. (2021a); Xu et al. (2020) have shed light on
166   the underlying cause of this behavior. It is attributed to unfair competition between skip connec-
167   tions and other operations, resulting in unstable training of the supernet. Consequently, several
168
      approaches Chu et al. (2021a); Zela et al. (2020) have been proposed to address this issue by intro-
      ducing various types of regularization to facilitate DARTS in escaping local optima and achieving
169
      better generalization properties. For example, R-DARTS Zela et al. (2020) seeks to add L2 or Sched-
170
      uledDropPath regularization to the optimization objective. We cannot help but ask a question: does
171   the same issue also exist within the unsupervised paradigm?
172
173   In response to the above question, we conduct multiple independent repeated experiments based on
174
      MAE-NAS. In an unsupervised setting, we observe an interesting phenomenon where the occurrence
      of performance collapse is highly correlated with the size of the mask ratio. Specifically, when the
175
      mask ratio is less than 0.5, the probability of collapse is significantly high, whereas when the mask
176
      ratio exceeds 0.5, the collapse phenomenon no longer exists.
177
178   Next, we attempt to explain the phenomena. Image reconstruction in MAE is a more difficult proxy
179   task than the supervised classification originally adopted in DARTS. And the larger the mask ratio,
180
      the more challenging it is to reconstruct the image. On the other hand, the encoder is designed to
      identify high-performance architectures, which have greater capability to better restore the masked
181
      image. When a large mask ratio makes image reconstruction difficult, the encoder naturally learns
182
      a more powerful architecture in order to better fulfill the optimization objective. This effectively
183   prevents the encoder from converging to some poor architectures, which helps escape from perfor-
184   mance collapse. Additionally, the unsupervised proxy can be also viewed as a powerful regulariza-
185   tion where the mask ratio controls the strength of the regularization. Remarkably, the finding aligns
186   with the conclusion of collapse in a supervised setting.
187
188   3.1.2   H IERARCHICAL D ECODER .
189
190
      At a glimpse, adjusting the mask ratio seems to be a solution. However, the encoder may inad-
      vertently discard promising architectures if the thresholds are set inappropriately. As previously
191
      mentioned, the collapse issue is largely due to the DARTS method. The unfair competition be-
192
      tween skip connections and other operations leads to highly unstable training in the searching stage
193   Chu et al. (2020). To stabilize the training of DARTS and fundamentally address the problem of
194   performance collapse, we present a more elegant solution called hierarchical decoding.
195
196
      The original decoder in SimMIM takes the tokens derived from the encoder as inputs and subse-
      quently processes them by a series of transformer blocks to reconstruct the image. In contrast, our
197
      encoder (i.e. DARTS) is designed to extract hierarchical features of different resolutions, denoted
198
      as F1 , F2 , and F3 , which encode fine-grained and coarse-grained information of the image. To fully
199   supervise such features at different levels, we reconstruct the image separately from F1 , F2 , and F3 .
200   Subsequently, such multi-grained outputs are aligned to the same scale by upsampling and combined
201   with summation and linear operations, ultimately producing the reconstructed image. This process
202   can be mathematically represented as follows:
203
                              \begin {split} & I_{rec} = Linear(Conv(F_1) + \\ & Upsample(Conv(F_2), 2) + Upsample(Conv(F_3), 4)) \end {split}
204                                                                                                                                               (3)
205
206   where Irec represents the reconstruction image. In detail, we only focus on the part of masked
207   patches when computing the loss, disregarding other regions of the input image. Compared to
208   supervised NAS methods like DARTS, our approach only introduces the hierarchical decoder for
209   image reconstruction. Such a decoder is extremely lightweight, as light as a convolution layer,
210   which brings negligible computational cost.
211   The motivation behind this design encompasses two aspects: firstly, it can accelerate gradient back-
212   propagation greatly and improve training stability effectively. The experimental results (Appendix
213   A.2.2) show that the hierarchical decoder leads to the smoother convergence curve and the lower
214   training loss. Secondly, it allows to learn more robust hierarchical features, enabling us to discover
215   stronger vision backbones. Specifically, we think that single-scale algorithm cannot learn multi-
      scale features well. The multi-scale structure has been a universal paradigm in computer vision.

241   3.1.3    R ELATIONSHIP TO PRIOR WORKS .
242
      Label-free NAS is not new in the NAS field. Previous literature such as UnNAS Liu et al. (2020)
243   and RLNAS Zhang et al. (2021) have demonstrated that label-free NAS can make NAS work as
244   well as supervised NAS. To our best knowledge, we are the first to explore the MAE paradigm in
245   the unsupervised setting, and it’s not straightforward to make it work. Directly applying it suffers
246   from the performance collapse issue like DARTS. We couple the masked autoencoder’s objective
247   with our proposed Hierarchical Decoder to address the collapse issue in DARTS and its variants.
248   We conduct comprehensive experiments across various datasets and tasks to verify the robustness
249   and generalization, which demonstrates MAE serves as an almost perfect proxy task for NAS. In
250   contrast, other unsupervised proxy metrics each have their limitations and constraints in application
251
      scenarios. For example, the angle metric adopted by RLNAS does not apply to the architectures
      with non-parametric operators (different activation functions, max pooling, and average pooling).
252
      UnNAS seeks to introduce several unsupervised proxies (rotation, color, and jigsaw tasks) for NAS,
253
      but the experiments Liu et al. (2020) have shown that the performance of these proxy tasks is not
254   consistent across different datasets. This has impacted its use cases and application scopes.

411   4.7    S ENSITIVITY A NALYSIS OF M ASK R ATIO AND PATCH S IZE
412
      In MAE, mask ratio and patch size are two important parameters, which greatly affect the modeling
413
      performance. Mask ratio refers to the proportion of pixels in an image that are randomly masked
414
      or hidden during the training process. The masking operation helps the model learn robust repre-
415   sentations by forcing it to reconstruct the original image from an incomplete or corrupted input.
416   Patch size refers to the size of the masked patches in an image. These patches are randomly selected
417   and masked during training, and the model is trained to reconstruct the original image based on the
418   remaining unmasked pixels. Thus the patch size determines the spatial extent of the masked regions
419   in an image.

518   In Section 3.1, we explain from the view of optimization why HD is able to solve the issue of per-
519   formance collapse: it accelerates gradient back-propagation greatly and improves training stability
520   effectively. We also draw the training loss during the search phase in Appendix A.2.2, and find that
521   HD leads to the smoother convergence curve and the lower training loss than the one without it.
522   We rethink this question from the view of image reconstruction. As shown in Figure 3, MAE-NAS
523   achieves superior image reconstruction quality over its counterpart without HD. Conceptually, the
524   encoder is expected to be equipped with high-performance architectures, which have stronger ability
525   to better restore the masked image. If the reconstruction image has higher quality, in order to achieve
526   the goal, the encoder naturally learns a more powerful architecture. Such mechanism prevents the
527   encoder from converging to a poor architecture, thus helps MAE-NAS escape from performance
528
      collapse.

      Obtaining labeled data is time-consuming, making unsupervised NAS methods attractive. We pro-
533
      pose MAE-NAS based on Masked Autoencoders that eliminates the need for labeled data. Our
534   approach replaces the supervised learning objective with a reconstruction loss, enabling the discov-
535   ery of network architectures with stronger representation and improved generalization. Experimen-
536   tal results on seven search spaces and three datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of MAE-NAS,
537   achieving comparable performance with its counterparts under the same complexity constraint. Such
538   experiments primarily covers image understanding tasks such as classification and object detection.
539   However, it’s nontrivial to apply our method to image generation tasks, and we’ll explore this in the
      future work.

731   as their NAS proxy for further improvements. All experiments are conducted on ImageNet, whose
732   training set is split into two parts: 50,000 images for validation and the rest for training.
733   P-DARTS The motivation behind P-DARTS Chen et al. (2019a) is to close the depth gap between
734   searching and training by presenting a progressive search strategy. The method starts with a small
735   network and progressively increases its size and complexity over multiple stages. Meanwhile, some
736   prior knowledge is introduced for search space regularization, to avoid the issue of performance
737   collapse. For example, they apply dropout after each skip-connect operation. Besides, they control
738   the number of preserved skip-connects manually. The aforementioned strategies, to some extent,
739   compromise the fairness of the comparison. To this end, we remove these artificial limitations for
740
      fair comparison. We run P-DARTS without handcrafted tricks and our approach each three times to
      get an average result. As shown in Table 9, our approach achieves 97.16% Top-1 accuracy, which
741
      is 0.68% higher than P-DARTS. In conclusion, our method effectively addresses the problem of
742
      performance collapse for P-DARTS without human prior.
743
744   PC-DARTS The motivation behind PC-DARTS Xu et al. (2020) is to deal with the challenge of
745   memory and computational efficiency of NAS. Traditional methods for architecture search require a
746
      large number of parameters and operations, making them computationally expensive and memory-
      intensive. To this end, PC-DARTS proposes using partial channel connections and allows for param-
747
      eter sharing across different channels in a convolutional neural network, which effectively reduces
748
      the number of parameters and the computational cost.
749
750   To verify the effectiveness of masked autoencoders as a NAS proxy in the PC-DARTS setting, we
751   compare the performance of PC-DARTS with its combination with ours. To ensure the reproduction
752
      of our results, we utilize their source code and conduct multiple repeated experiments with different
      random seeds under the same settings. As shown in Table 10, MAE-NAS achieves a 0.18% top-1
753
      accuracy increase with the lower search cost compared to PC-DARTS.
754
755   Overall, the above results demonstrate the potential of MAE-NAS to enhance the performance of
      existing NAS algorithms, even under suboptimal configurations. We believe that our approach can
