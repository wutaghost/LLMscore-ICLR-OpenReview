011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            While prior work has explored whether large language models (LLMs) possess
014            a “theory of mind” (ToM) - the ability to attribute mental states to oneself and
015
               others - there has been little work testing whether LLMs can implicitly apply such
               knowledge to predict behavior, or to judge whether an observed behavior is ra-
016
               tional. Such skills are critical for appropriate interaction in social environments.
017
               Our approach to study such capabilities is to create a new dataset, called Sim-
018            pleToM, containing concise, diverse stories (e.g., “The can of Pringles has moldy
019            chips in it. Mary picks up the can in the supermarket and walks to the cashier.”),
020            each with three questions that test different degrees of ToM reasoning, asking
021            models to predict (a) mental state (“Is Mary aware of the mold?”), (b) behavior
022            (“Will Mary pay for the chips or report the mold?”), and (c) judgment (“Mary
023            paid for the chips. Was that reasonable?”). To our knowledge, SimpleToM is the
024            first dataset to systematically explore downstream reasoning requiring knowledge
025            of mental states in realistic scenarios. Our experimental results are intriguing:
026
               While most models can reliably predict mental state on our dataset (a), they often
               fail to correctly predict the behavior (b), and fare even worse at judging whether
027
               given behaviors are reasonable (c), despite being correctly aware of the protago-
028
               nist’s mental state should make such secondary predictions obvious. We further
029            show that we can help models do better at (b) and (c) via interventions such as
030            reminding the model of its earlier mental state answer and mental-state-specific
031            chain-of-thought prompting, raising the action prediction accuracies (e.g., from
032            49.5% to 93.5% for GPT-4o) and judgment accuracies (e.g., from 15.3% to 94.7%
033            in GPT-4o). However, while this shows that models can be coaxed to perform
034            well, it requires task-specific interventions, and the natural model performances
035            remain low, a cautionary tale for LLM deployment. SimpleToM thus breaks new
036            ground in probing real-world ToM reasoning, and reveals surprising, new insights
037
               about current model capabilities. We hope the dataset enables further exploration
               by the community into this critical area of model behavior.

040   1   I NTRODUCTION
041
042   People infer what others know, anticipate their actions, and expect them to choose cost-minimizing
043   behaviors (Gergely & Csibra, 2003; Liu & Spelke, 2017). Performing such social reasoning in-
044   volves attributing mental states to oneself and others, an ability widely known as Theory of Mind
045   (ToM) (Premack & Woodruff, 1978). ToM has been extensively studied in psychology in a range of
046   scenarios, for instance, studies of manipulation, secrecy, deception, lying, and misleading behavior
047   (Doherty, 2008). Examples of classical tests in developmental psychology include the unexpected
048   transfer false belief task, e.g., the Sally-Anne task (Baron-Cohen et al., 1985), and the unexpected
049   contents false belief task, e.g., the Smarties task (Perner et al., 1987). Given the increasing use
050
      of LLMs in human interactions and as decision-making agents within complex, human-centered
      environments, it is crucial to assess their ToM capabilities.
051
052   Current datasets for assessing ToM in LLMs are limited by their reliance on the classical Sally-
053   Anne task or templated variants of it (Le et al., 2019; Nematzadeh et al., 2018; Wu et al., 2023; Xu
      et al., 2024). These datasets have significant shortcomings: (i) limited diversity in how information

142   2     S IMPLE T O M DESIGN
143
144   We design the stories in SimpleToM to contain diverse types of information asymmetry, using a
145   concise format and associated with specific question types testing explicit and applied ToM.
146
147   2.1     D IFFERENT TYPES OF INFORMATION ASYMMETRY
148
149   To expand beyond the classical false belief task, we seed the creation of SimpleToM with ten di-
150   verse scenarios where information asymmetry occurs naturally (Table 1). This is inspired by social
151
      psychology literature to cover asymmetries like manipulation, deception, secrecy, lying, and mis-
      leading behavior (Doherty, 2008), seen in real-world contexts like sales of “lemon” products, where
152
      items with hidden flaws are purchased due to a lack of information (Akerlof, 1978). These are
153
      under-examined in existing ToM tests. We further describe the scenarios with examples in Table 7
154   (Appendix F.1).
155

194   2.3    Q UESTIONS TESTING EXPLICIT AND APPLIED T O M
195
196   We use three types of questions (Figure 1) to probe a model’s grasp of each story, covering both
197   explicit theory of mind (conceptual knowledge about others’ mental states; i.e., via (a) mental state
198   question about information awareness) and applied theory of mind (the ability to use theory of mind
199   in real-life contexts; i.e., via (b) behavior and (c) judgment questions) (Lee et al., 2024).
200   Mental state (MS) question about information awareness: We test ability of models to infer men-
201   tal states, specifically information awareness, through a simple yes/no question (Is <Person X>
202   likely to be aware that "<key information>"?) To infer whether a character is
203   aware of something in SimpleToM stories, a model has to make implicit commonsense inferences
204   about what the character can perceive or know in the given situation (including commonsense rea-
205   soning about physical objects, space, intent, goals of others, and so on).
206   Behavior prediction question: This question asks which of two possible actions the main subject
207   (Person X) is likely to perform next. For instance, beyond answering that a person shopping for
208   chips in the supermarket is unlikely to know that “the can of Pringles has moldy chips in it”, a
209   model that successfully applies this inference for behavior prediction should also infer that a person
210   who picked up such a can in the supermarket would likely “pay for the chips” rather than “report
211   the moldy chips.” To answer these questions correctly, models need to implicitly reason over the
212
      situation to infer the mental state of character, and realize how the character’s lack of awareness of
      the key information would impact their likely next action.
213
214   Judgment question: The judgment question specifies that the “correct” action was taken (rather
215   than the incorrect one) and asks if this was a reasonable choice. As the inference graph in Figure 1
      illustrates, the judgment question goes beyond behavior prediction as it requires two levels of im-


                                                                      4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      plicit reasoning, first implicitly predicting the behavior of Person X, which itself relies on implicitly
217   understanding their mental state. People’s mental states are an important factor to consider in mak-
218   ing appropriate judgments of their behavior (Jara-Ettinger et al., 2016; Schein & Gray, 2018). For
219   instance, buying a can of Pringles that has moldy chips in it is not a reasonable action if the person
220   knows about the moldy chips. However, it is a perfectly reasonable (and expected) behavior if this
221   piece of key information is not a part of the person’s mental state.

      3       S IMPLE T O M CREATION
224
225   3.1     G ENERATING DIVERSE STORIES
226
227   Specifically, the construction of SimpleToM consists of the following steps:
228
229   Step 1: Manually create one example seed story for each scenario.
230   Step 2: For each scenario, using the seed story as example, prompt the LLM to suggest 10 diverse
231           sets of entities compatible with an information asymmetry. (See prompt in F.5.)
232   Step 3: For each set of suggested entities, along with the seed story, prompt the LLM to write
233           three new stories at different levels of “severity.” With each story, also generate likely next
234
              “unaware” and “aware” behaviors (see Section 2.2). Appendix F.4 provides further details.
235   We went through two rounds of this process. First, we used GPT-4 and Claude-3-Opus1 to generate
236   a total of 1200 stories.2 After annotating and filtering this initial set (Section 3.2), we picked a
237   new set of top-scoring seed stories and sourced 10 additional sets of entities from each of GPT-
238   4o and Claude-3.5-Sonnet. We used these two newer models to generate stories for all 40 sets
239   of entities, for a total of 2400 more stories. By using several generator models, varied entities
240   and different seed stories, the resulting stories in SimpleToM have a wide range of information
241   asymmetries instantiated in different real-world situations, effectively broadening neural ToM tests
242   beyond traditional settings (Section 6). These contexts also allow for nuanced and implicit traits
243
      (e.g., buyers would avoid products with defects if they know about them).
244
245   3.2     S TRICT QUALITY CONTROL ON STORIES THAT GOES INTO S IMPLE T O M
246
      We gather human annotations on each story (and unaware/aware next actions). We asked annotators
247   four questions for each story, summarized in Figure 2. This process verifies that the key information
248   in each story is something that Person X has false belief about. We also carefully verify that the next
249   likely “unaware action” is appropriate if and only if Person X is unaware of the key information.
250   We similarly verify the “aware action,” for the counterfactual situation where Person X is some-
251   how aware of the key information. Appendix E provides further details about the crowdsourcing
252   procedure, with instructions, examples and question templates.
253   Our annotators passed a rigorous qualification test (Appendix E.2) and met other high-standard
254   requirements (Appendix E.3). Only stories for which all crowdworkers (3) judged all aspects to be
255   valid were included in SimpleToM.3 This results in 1147 stories (out of the original 3600) in the final
256   SimpleToM dataset. Table 8 (Appendix F.2) provides statistics and further details for SimpleToM.

300
      type of question below. Note that these are binary questions where random performance is 50%.
301
302   Mental state (MS) question about information awareness: Our results (Table 2, “mental state”
303   column) show that reasoning over implicit information in given situations to infer mental states is
304   still challenging for models like GPT-3.5 (36.5% accuracy), while newer and/or bigger models like
305
      Claude-3-Haiku, o1-mini, and Llama-3.1-8B perform reasonably well (around 88%). In fact, all
      recent frontier models are proficient at inferring characters’ awareness in our dataset – GPT-4o,
306

308   states in SimpleToM stories are implicit but reasonably easy to infer, as designed.
309
310   Behavior prediction: On behavior prediction questions (Table 2, “behavior” column), smaller and
311
      older models perform extremely poorly (with GPT-3.5 achieving only 7.6% accuracy and several

312
      Sonnet, and GPT-4o, performance on the behavior prediction task is much worse than on the mental
313   state task with at least a 30% performance drop. This large inconsistency suggests that while fron-

315   directly asked, they struggle to apply this knowledge in real-life contexts to make predictions about

      6   R ELATED W ORK
487
488
      Theory of Mind has been extensively studied in psychology in a range of scenarios (see Appendix
489   C). ToM reasoning, and broadly social commonsense, has also been shown to be important by the
490   different parts of the AI community including in conversations (Kim et al., 2023b;a), games (Zhou
491   et al., 2023b; Liu et al., 2024b), and even multi-modal setups (Jin et al., 2024), with most popular
492   ToM tests using stories to probe LLMs. Relying on stories from small test sets in cognitive science
493   studies to benchmark ToM abilities in LLMs (Bubeck et al., 2023; Kosinski, 2024) could produce
494   results that differ given minor alterations (Ullman, 2023) and would be more robust if tested on larger
495   samples. Yet expert-crafted or naturally occurring self-contained stories that can serve as targeted
496   tests of ToM are scarce and human story-writing is expensive, leading to the use of automatically
497
      generated datasets for studying ToM behavior in LLMs (Jung et al., 2024; Wilf et al., 2023; Sap et al.,
      2022; Shapira et al., 2023; Sclar et al., 2023). Existing generated datasets allow studies of ToM
498
      to be carried out at scale, but templated stories often limit settings where information asymmetry
499
      arises. For example, the entire dataset might only contain stories about some object being moved
500   (over-reliance on classical Sally-Anne task, e.g., in ToMi (Le et al., 2019), ToM-bAbI (Nematzadeh
501   et al., 2018), Hi-ToM (Wu et al., 2023), OpenToM (Xu et al., 2024)). Or the dataset might focus
502   on whether some character has witnessed a sudden external event (BigToM (Gandhi et al., 2024)).
503   These stories are often systematically generated with the explicit use of mentalizing words to convey
504   percepts and beliefs, e.g., “unknown to Amy” and “Amy thinks that” in Xu et al. (2024) or “Noor
505   sees” and “Mei does not notice” in Gandhi et al. (2024). However, the explicit use of mentalizing
506   words also makes the stories (i) unnaturally simplistic, having removed the need for commonsense
507   inferences about percepts or beliefs, and (ii) sometimes unrealistic, with combinations like “Cheng
508
      does not notice the power outage” when he “use[s] a projector to show a documentary”(Gandhi et al.,
      2024). Other existing datasets could be improved by addressing issues such as lacking exploration
509
      of applied ToM beyond action prediction (Zhou et al., 2023a; Gandhi et al., 2024), confounding
510
      factors like memory loads or tracking requirements (Le et al., 2019; Xu et al., 2024), and violating
511   Quesque & Rossetti (2020)’s criteria (see Appendix C) for validating ToM (Chen et al., 2024). Our
512   work extends existing datasets by following Tian et al. (2024) in combining the generative strength
513   of LLMs and the verification ability of human annotators, and extends the existing efforts toward
514   robust, generalizable evaluation (Kiela et al., 2021; Srivastava et al., 2024), avoiding known pitfalls
515   while preserving the systematic and scalable nature of the dataset creation process.

      7   C ONCLUSION
518
519
      SimpleToM is the first dataset of its kind testing both explicit and applied ToM using a large set
520
      of diverse, concise, simple stories, covering different ways in which information asymmetry may
521
      arise. The dataset opens up new opportunities for evaluating and debugging ToM abilities of LLMs,
522   especially in the relatively under-studied area of applied ToM. Our analyses reveal a jarring gap
523   between explicit and applied ToM capabilities in current frontier LLMs. Thus, if our goal is LLM
524   agents capable of applying ToM in complex, human-centered environments, we need to look beyond
525   testing LLMs with psychology-inspired ToM questions, and also start testing them more rigorously
526   on applied ToM (e.g., behavioral prediction and judgment) in different situations.
527
      We show that this gap can be largely closed in the best models by well-designed interventions at
528   inference time, like reminding the model of its answers to important questions or guiding it with
529   a custom chain-of-thought prompt. We argue that a robust LLM should perform well on Simple-
530   ToM without such interventions, so it can independently and flexibly apply ToM-related reasoning
531   whenever required within potentially complex and multi-faceted environments. Model developers
532   interested in real-world deployment of their models, should be alert to closing this performance gap
533   so the models can interact with society appropriately (see example applications in Appendix A),
534   ideally without the higher inference costs associated with explicit chain-of-thought reasoning or
535   implicit o1-preview reasoning tokens (see further discussion in Appendix I).
536   SimpleToM can also facilitate the community in pursuing various exciting directions for future work,
537   including studying how ToM performance may differ with stories that involve different levels of
538   harmfulness and unethicality (see Appendix F.3), injecting different persona (Appendix M) and
539   innovative modeling approaches that can help close the gap between explicit and applied ToM in AI
      models.

      A    I MPORTANCE OF APPLIED T O M
865
866   To emphasize the importance of the capabilities tested in SimpleToM, we provide examples of ap-
867   plications where failing on applied ToM would be problematic:
868
869
      The case of a bad personal AI assistant - failing to implicitly reason over other’s mental states to
      predict behavior:
870
871   Matt is a professional athlete. A growth hormone got into the supply chain for Bob’s Burgers,
872   where Matt regularly had dinner. Despite this, the owners decided to continue to sell their burgers
873   to save costs. Imagine a personal AI assistant, having read complaints about the growth hormone
874   contamination at Bob’s Burgers but failing to apply the understanding that others’ mental states may
875
      be different from their own (ToM), could reason that Matt, like the AI assistant, is also aware of
      this (awareness) and then incorrectly predicts that Matt will “refuse the burger due to the growth
876
      hormone” (behavior).
877
878   This could then lead to undesirable consequences such as not being able to warn Matt in time to stop
879   him from consuming the contaminated burger. In the case of an unannounced blood test, Matt could
880   then show up with positive traces of this illegal growth hormone and be accused of doping. In this
881
      case, such an AI assistant, lacking a nuanced understanding of human awareness and motivations,
      might also falsely assume Matt’s intentional wrongdoing. This highlights a critical limitation: with-
882
      out robust ToM, the AI fails to grasp that Matt’s actions could stem from unawareness rather than
883
      culpability, leading to flawed judgments that could unjustly tarnish his reputation or career.
884
885   The case of a bad AI judge - failing to make appropriate judgments of behavior:
886   Alice visited the supermarket to purchase some carrots to pack lunch for her husband Bob. After
887   consuming the lunch Alice packed, Bob succumbed to a severe E. coli infection. It turned out the
888   supermarket’s carrots were contaminated with E. coli and were subsequently recalled. Imagine an
889   AI judge is tasked with evaluating this case to decide whether Alice should be held responsible and
890   imprisoned for Bob’s death. A bad AI judge, failing to apply the understanding that others’ mental
891   states may be different from their own (ToM), could incorrectly assume that Alice was aware of the
892   E. coli and judge that Alice’s act of packing the contaminated carrots for her husband was wrong
893
      (judgment).
894   This could then lead to undesirable consequences such as severely punishing the innocent Alice
895   who didn’t know feeding Bob the carrots would kill him. In common law jurisdictions, whether a
896   defendant is found guilty is often decided taking into account both mens rea (“guilty mind”) and
897   actus reus (“guilty act”). Therefore, the ability to apply ToM is important for potential AI judges to
898   appropriately assess whether an individual has a “guilty mind” when making key judgments, such
899
      as determining whether to convict someone.
900   We are excited about the possibility of future generations of models to improve on applied ToM in
901   our dataset. This could pave the way for models to effectively interact with humans – for instance,
902   serving as reliable personal AI assistants as well as trustworthy AI judges. We hope SimpleToM, as
903   the first resource of its kind to measure LLMs’ capability on such diverse applied ToM scenarios,
904   will help facilitate the community in pursuing exciting directions that bring us there.

906   B    FAQ S
907
908        Q: How is SimpleToM different from existing datasets?
909           SimpleToM addresses limitations in previous efforts to examine Theory-of-Mind (ToM)
910           reasoning in LLMs, by (1) having diverse false belief setups (e.g., beyond those in Sally-
911           Anne task where some object is moved when a character is not present), (2) requiring
912           LLMs to make commonsense inferences in situations rather explicit use of mentalizing
913
              words to convey what characters perceive or believe, and (3) going beyond explicit ToM to
              test models’ ability to apply inferred knowledge in follow-up applied ToM questions (such
914
              as behavior prediction and judgment of behavior).
915
916
           Q: What new insights does SimpleToM help uncover about models’ ToM capabilities?
917           Our analysis reveals novel insights on how frontier models are generally proficient in ex-
              plicit awareness inference questions but this success does not transfer to applied ToM


                                                       17
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


918
               (applying this knowledge is applied to “behavior” and “judgment” questions). We show
919            that these capabilities are decoupled in LLMs: inferring characters’ awareness and apply-
920            ing them in downstream reasoning. Although models seem to answer awareness ques-
921            tions correctly, they have not yet learned to perform ToM-based reasoning for downstream
922            questions. As a result, we argue that achieving ToM in LLMs is not just about getting
923            psychology-inspired ToM questions correct (stopping at the mental state question), but they
924            have to be able to apply them (which is precisely what SimpleToM extends to examine).
925            Analysis by scenarios further highlights the need to test on different scenarios, and ones
926            that are varied and different from those in classical ToM tests to ensure that we are effec-
927
               tively testing the ToM reasoning abilities of models (rather than models’ ability to match
               similar situations in training data).
928
929
           Q: What do the different inference interventions reveal about models’ ToM capabilities?
930
931            Patch, guide, think aloud combined method results show belief-to-judgment inference ro-
932            bustness. When reminded of their answer to mental state questions about information
933            awareness and using ToM-specific CoT, models’ belief-to-judgment inference is pretty ro-
934            bust in existing LLMs. The models have some knowledge linking irrationality and un-
935            awareness (e.g., unawareness should be accounted for seemingly irrational behavior). So
936            arriving at the “correct belief” will be crucial part of ToM-based reasoning, but this is not
937            done implicitly in current frontier models’ reasoning.
938
939        Q: Are the poor performance on the applied ToM questions a reflection of fundamental
940
              flaws in ToM capabilities of models or specific question-wording?
941
               We illustrate in Appendix G some prompt variations that we have experimented with for the
942
               judgment question. Across Llama-3.1-405B, Claude-3.5-Sonnet and GPT-4o, the scores
943            using different variants were all consistently below random (never exceeding 30% accu-
944            racy), indicating that the low scores on the judgment questions come more from funda-
945            mental flaws in the applied ToM capabilities of models rather than an effect of specific
946            formatting/wording.

949   C    S TUDIES OF T O M IN PSYCHOLOGY
950
951
      Theory of Mind has been extensively studied in psychology in a range of scenarios, for instance,
952
      studies of manipulation, secrecy (Peskin & Ardino, 2003), deception, lying (Lewis et al., 1989;
953
      Perner, 1993; Peskin, 1992), misleading behavior (Chandler et al., 1989; Wimmer & Perner, 1983;
954   Doherty, 2008), autism (Frith & Happé, 1994), and analysis of rational behavior (Gergely & Csibra,
955   2003; Liu & Spelke, 2017). Classical tests of ToM in developmental psychology include testing the
956   development of this ability in children via false belief prediction – using the unexpected transfer
957   false belief task, the Sally-Anne task (Baron-Cohen et al., 1985), or the unexpected contents false
958   belief task, the Smarties task (Perner et al., 1987). Quesque & Rossetti (2020) review classic tests of
959   ToM and outline two important criteria for tasks that validate ToM: (1) The task must indicate that
960   the respondents can differentiate between the other’s mental state and their own. (2) Lower-level
961   processes, like associative learning, should be ruled out as explanations for achieving successful
962
      performance. Given the wide applicability of ToM reasoning in various real-life situations such as
      analyzing people’s behavior (Liu et al., 2024a; Jara-Ettinger et al., 2016) and making judgments
963
      (Schein & Gray, 2018; Young et al., 2007), there has also been increasing interest in assessing ToM
964
      capabilities in AI models (Le et al., 2019; Ullman, 2023; Kosinski, 2024; Jin et al., 2024; Trott et al.,
965   2023).

1446
       This overhead is not present when just updating the system prompt (just a fixed cost from the longer

1451   without any chain-of-thought prompting, reports an average of 536 output tokens on the behavior
1452
       prediction questions (vs 486 tokens on mental state questions and 605 tokens on judgment ques-
       tions).
