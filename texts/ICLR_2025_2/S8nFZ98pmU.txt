009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Recent advancements in deep learning have significantly impacted the study of
012            dynamical systems. Traditional approaches predominantly rely on supervised
013            learning paradigms, limiting their scope to large scale problems and adaptabil-
014            ity to new systems. This paper introduces a novel meta learning framework tai-
015            lored for dynamical system forecasting, hinging on the concept of mapping the
016            observed trajectories to a system-specific embedding space which encapsulates
017            the inter-system characteristics and enriches the feature set for downstream pre-
018            diction tasks. Central to our framework is the use of contrastive learning for tra-
019
               jectory data coupled with a series of neural network architecture designs to extract
               the features as augmented embedding for modeling system behavior. We present
020
               the application of zero-shot meta-learning to dynamical systems, demonstrating
021
               a substantial enhancement in performance metrics compared to existing baseline
022            models. A notable byproduct of our methodology is the improved interpretability
023            of the embeddings, which now carries explicit physical significance. Our results
024            not only set a new benchmark in the field but also pave the way for enhanced inter-
025            pretability and deeper understanding of complex dynamical systems, potentially
026            opening new directions for how we approach system analysis and prediction.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   In the field of dynamical systems, the application of deep learning represents a significant develop-
032   ment, introducing new perspectives and methods for analyzing complex temporal behaviors. His-
033   torically, the analysis and prediction of these systems have largely depended on supervised learning
034   techniques (Kumpati et al., 1990; Ljung, 1998; Hefny et al., 2015; Brunton et al., 2016). Although
035   these techniques have proven useful in specific situations, they have certain drawbacks, particularly
036   in terms of applying acquired knowledge to novel or changing systems. Supervised learning mod-
037   els typically necessitate accurately labeled datasets tailored to each individual system, resulting in
038   a lack of applicability to scenarios involving extensive volumes of unlabeled data and flexibility to
039
      transfer knowledge to new systems. This specific adaptation to particular datasets poses generaliza-
      tion issues (Kirchmeyer et al., 2022), as the models face difficulties in adjusting to the new systems
040
      with varying dynamics or conditions. Moreover, the reliance on substantial volumes of labeled data
041
      renders these models impractical in situations where data is costly or less feasible to acquire, or in
042   instances where systems evolve beyond the distribution of the initial training data.
043
044   Meta-learning (Huisman et al., 2021; Hospedales et al., 2021) and multitask learning (Zhang &
045
      Yang, 2018; Caruana, 1997) approaches have gained traction for their ability to handle multiple
      tasks and adapt to new scenarios. These approaches leverage “comparison” as a fundamental con-
046
      cept (Tian et al., 2020b), utilizing it to derive embeddings that uniquely identify objects across
047
      different categories. This methodology is exemplified by innovations such as the siamese network
048   (Koch et al., 2015) and triplet loss (Schroff et al., 2015), which paved the way for the emergence of
049   “contrastive learning”. This branch of machine learning, together with the pre-train and fine-tune
050   strategies, facilitates few-shot or zero-shot learning, enabling models to apply knowledge from ex-
051   tensive datasets to previously unseen data. Despite their successes, applying these methodologies
052   directly to dynamical systems poses substantial hurdles (Nagabandi et al., 2018; Wang et al., 2022),
053   due to the unique patterns of such systems. Unlike traditional meta-learning where the new tasks or
      unseen data are typically defined in categorical terms, the concept of embedding in dynamical sys-


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      tems lacks the notion of distinct classes. This absence of clear-cut categories presents a challenge in
055   adapting existing contrastive learning methods to the field of dynamical systems.
056
057   Motivated by this challenge, our paper introduces a specialized meta-learning framework tailored
058
      for dynamical systems. This framework encompasses a dual-phase process, firstly focusing on the
      discovery of unique system embeddings by contrastive learning and subsequently employing these
059
      embeddings for forecasting tasks. Our contributions are summarized as follows:
060
061         1. We introduce a novel perspective contrastive learning applied to dynamical system identifi-
062            cation problems, by comparing truncated trajectories sampled both within a single system
063            (intra-system) and across different systems (inter-system) to learn an effective represen-
064            tation of the system dynamics. To our knowledge, this study is the first exploration of
065            zero-shot meta-learning techniques for dynamical systems that does not require adaptation
066            to new systems or explicit labeling of system-specific coefficients.
067         2. We systematically developed a learning framework for meta dynamical system learning,
068            incorporating distinctively designed modules. These include the “Local Linear Least
069            Square” feature extractor for vector-based systems and “Spatial Adaptive LinEar Modu-
070            lation (SALEM)” for grid-based system. Furthermore, we also proposed a dimensional
071            square ratio contrastive loss function, uniquely tailored for trajectory contrastive learning
072            in dynamical systems.
073         3. Through the synthetic experiments, we offer qualitative evidence of the efficacy of con-
074            trastive learning in the embedding space. We also quantitatively demonstrate that the fore-
075            casting errors with learned embedding are significantly lower compared to those of baseline
076            neural networks in dynamics prediction.

078   2     BACKGROUND AND R ELATED W ORK
079
080   2.1   D EEP L EARNING BASED DYNAMICAL S YSTEM L EARNING
081
082   In the context of identifying and understanding dynamical systems, deep learning has emerged as
083   a popular and powerful tool at handling the temporal dependencies and nonlinear dynamics char-
084   acteristic of these systems. Earlier models such as recurrent neural networks (RNNs) (Bailer-Jones
085   et al., 1998) excel in capturing these intricate temporal patterns, enabling predictions and analyses of
086
      discrete time system behaviors. Following the setup of continuous-time dynamical systems, the con-
      cept of Neural Ordinary Differential Equations (Neural ODEs) has been introduced by Chen et al.
087
      (2018). For predicting complex discrete time system such as Partial differential equations (PDEs),
088
      ResNet (He et al., 2016) is commonly used as backbone model for forecasting the future dynamics
089   (Long et al., 2018; 2019; Xu et al., 2019).
090
091
      2.2   M ETA DYNAMICAL S YSTEM L EARNING
092
093   Meta dynamical system learning, being a merging branch of dynamical system learning, focuses on
094   developing models that can adapt and generalize across various physical systems and environments.
095   Yin et al. (2021) presents LEADS, a new framework for modeling multiple dynamical systems, by
096   capturing common dynamics within a shared model while also accounting for environment-specific
097   model. Following the work, Kirchmeyer et al. (2022) introduces a hypernetwork learned jointly with
098   a context vector from observed data, aims for fast adaptation and enhanced generalization across en-
099
      vironments with minimal data samples. Wang et al. (2022) proposed DyAd, a meta-learning frame-
      work comprising an encoder that deduces time-invariant hidden features of the task with limited
100
      supervision, and a forecaster that generalizes the dynamics of the entire domain. More recently,
101
      Blanke & Lelarge (2023) proposes CAMEL, parameterizing an affine structure to accommodate
102   the new task by few shot learning. Nevertheless, these earlier meta-learning models for dynamical
103   systems depended on supervised training on labeled system coefficients or few shot adaptation to
104   new systems, limiting their usability in complex situations where environments rapidly change, or
105   prior knowledge about the new systems is unavailable. Our approach, on the other hand, employs
106   contrastive learning to automatically identify system coefficients without relying on labeled data.
107   By leveraging unsupervised learning techniques and zero-shot forecasting, our method can operate
      effectively even in the absence of prior knowledge about new systems.

      2.3   C ONTRASTIVE L EARNING
109
110   Contrastive Learning, diverging from traditional supervised learning methods that directly map data,
111   focuses on implicitly deriving data representations by comparing examples. Its inception dates
112   back to the early 1990s, as evidenced by foundational works such as Bromley et al. (1993). The
113   method has been extensively applied across various domains, significantly impacting metric learning
114   (Chopra et al., 2005; Sohn, 2016), a field closely related to our work.
115   In the community of unsupervised and semi-supervised learning, contrastive learning has gained
116   prominence, especially in self-supervised learning (SSL) tasks. Its effectiveness is well-established
117   in areas such as computer vision and natural language processing, as demonstrated in research by
118   Chen et al. (2020a); He et al. (2020); Tian et al. (2020a) for computer vision, and Wu et al. (2020);
119   Gao et al. for natural language processing. There has been notable advancements in the design of
120   contrastive loss, with significant contributions from Oord et al. (2018a); Chen et al. (2020a;b).
121   In recent years, its application to time series data has gained attention due to the unique challenges
122   posed by the temporal nature of the data (Pöppelbaum et al., 2022; Yue et al., 2022), enabling tasks
123   such as anomaly detection, clustering, and classification. Despite the promising results in the time
124   series domain, which is closely related to dynamical systems, the application of contrastive learning
125   to the latter remains largely unexplored. The major challenges include the requirement for high
126   interpretability in dynamical systems, as they are often governed by physical laws and principles,
127   and the need for learned representations to align with these underlying physical mechanisms. Addi-
128   tionally, the diverse forms of state representation in dynamical systems, such as grid-based systems
129
      (to be introduced later), pose difficulties in integrating learned embedding into downstream tasks,
      requiring careful consideration of how the representations can be effectively utilized.

      3.1   R EPRESENTATION L EARNING OF M ULTIPLE DYNAMICAL S YSTEMS
135
136   In this paper, we consider two types of common settings of autonomous dynamical systems: con-
137   tinuous time and discrete time systems. In the former, we consider the unknown system equation
138   in the following form: ẋ = dx(t)/dt = f (x), where x ∈ X is the system state and ẋ is its time
139   derivative to time index t, and the future state depends solely on the current state if function f (·) is
140   fixed. In the discrete time step setting, we consider the autonomous system equation in the form of
141   xt+1 = f (xt )1 . f ∈ F : X → T X or X denotes the system function that maps system state space
142   X ∈ Rn to its temporal derivative space T X ∈ Rn or future state space X .
143   F is the functional space that f (·) belongs to, and we consider that all functions f (·) share certain
144   characteristics but may vary in some aspects. The difference can be represented by a set of param-
145   eters ϕ (e.g., physics constants, material properties), that is fϕ (·) ∈ F, where ϕ ∈ Φ and Φ is the
146   coefficient space. The main objective is to develop methodologies to establish an encoder from a
147   trajectory observation to an informative embedding corresponding to ϕ without prior knowledge of
148   it. By doing so, we can accurately parameterize fϕ (·) and employ it to forecast the evolution of
149
      future trajectories.

181   This section presents our two-step meta-learning framework tailored for dynamical systems, as
182   shown in Figure 1. The first step involves employing contrastive learning to derive embeddings
183   for each trajectory, capturing the coefficients or the physics properties of the dynamical system. In
184   the second step, we apply the embedding model to the initial segment of the trajectory to deduce the
185
      embedding. These embeddings, representing inferred coefficients, serves as inputs for dynamical
      system model to predict the future trajectories.
186

188   continuous-time vector-based systems with fully observable states and interpretable physics coef-

190   each state) in discrete time steps. The remaining sections are organized as follows: Section 4.1 in-
191
      troduces the contrastive loss design, Section 4.2 and Section 4.3 discuss the design of the “encoder”
      and dynamical system learning for continuous-time vector-based systems, and Section 4.4 describes
192
      the “decoder” design to incorporate trajectory knowledge for grid-based discrete time dynamical
193
      systems.

502   6.2     C OMPARISON WITH P RIOR W ORKS
503
504   Recently, the field of meta-learning for dynamical systems has begun to gain attention, with a small
505   but growing number of studies endeavoring to tackle this complex issue. Our approach, from the
506   formulation of the problem to the learning techniques used, significantly distinguishes from previous
507   methods.
508
      Our method assumes no provided label information regarding the system coefficients, thus not re-
509   quiring the knowledge of new system coefficients for either supervised system embedding learning
510   or in predicting a new system. This is the major difference between our method and prior works.
511   Wang et al. (2022) needs the system coefficients for weak supervised learning. Yin et al. (2021);
512   Kirchmeyer et al. (2022) assume the linear decomposition of the shared dynamics and environment-
513   specific dynamics, where the latter requires unique model parameters fitted separately for each en-
514   vironment. These strong conditions facilitate the learning process as they acquires knowledge about
515   knowledge for sub-systems. However, in many instances, especially in forecasting scenarios, this
516   kind of information is often not available.
517   Once the embedding is learned, our approach is compatible with other meta-learning method Yin
518   et al. (2021); Kirchmeyer et al. (2022) to quickly adapt to new vector-based systems. For the grid-
519   based systems, similar to Wang et al. (2022), we generalize the environment-specific embedding
520   from the input and utilize this mapping for forecasting tasks of new systems, while our approach
521   does not require explicit knowledge for this embedding learning.
522
523
524   7      C ONCLUSION AND L IMITATIONS
525
526
      In this paper, we present a novel meta-learning framework for dynamical systems, leveraging con-
527
      trastive learning coupled with a tailored feature extractor and a custom loss function. This architec-
528   ture facilitates the extraction of embeddings that capture the underlying physical parameters of the
529   system. We further enhance this model with an innovatively designed forecasting module, which uti-
530   lizes the model embeddings for more accurate prediction. Our approach not only achieves superior
531   forecasting accuracy but also enhances model interpretability and physical relevance.
532
      As we aim to build a contrastive learning framework for general dynamical systems, we acknowl-
533
      edge several limitations in the current study. Firstly, the complexity of the local linear extractor
534   grows quadratically with the system dimension, and we have yet explored its application for high-
535   dimensional vector-based systems. Furthermore, the embedding space utilized in this paper is also
536   of a low dimensionality. The potential of high-dimensional embedding spaces remains unexplored,
537   where we anticipate that the requisite data size could increase exponentially with the dimensional-
538   ity of the embedding space. We hope our work can inspire future researchers on more universally
539   applicable unsupervised learning methods that can handle high-dimensional continuous embedding
      space.

844   C.1     C ONTRASTIVE L EARNING L OSS F UNCTION C HOICE AND A BLATION S TUDIES
845
846   In classical applications of contrastive learning such as computer vision, the target of learning pro-
847   cess is to form discrete clusters in the embedding space, where each cluster represents a specific
848
      category or class. This learning objective is a direct result of the inherent categorization of the data
      and is explicitly influenced by the design of contrastive loss functions (Oord et al., 2018a; Chen et al.,
849
      2020a;b). These loss functions, often in conjunction with softmax or exponential functions, work by
850
      assigning probabilities to pairs of samples, quantifying how likely it is that a pair of embeddings be-
851   longs to the same class. This probabilistic approach allows the model to effectively determine how
852   closely an anchor point (a reference data point) is related to its positive pairs, compared to various
853   negative pairs, facilitating the formation of distinct, class-based clusters in the embedding space.
854
      In contrast, when applied to dynamical systems, contrastive learning results in a more continuous
855
      distribution of embeddings rather than distinct clusters. This difference stems from the continuous
856
      nature of dynamical systems, where the coefficients of the sub-systems are smoothly interconnected.
857
858   To assess the effectiveness of various embedding learning modules, we systematically replaced each
859   module and analyzed the resultant embeddings. Figure 4 presents the outcomes of these ablation
860
      studies. In the top-left figure, the absence of covariance loss leads to embeddings that tend to align
      along a line, suggesting a linear correlation between latent dimensions and resulting in dimension
861
      collapse due to this linear dependency.
862

      (2023), where a similar linear arrangement of embeddings is observed. However, the rescaling of
