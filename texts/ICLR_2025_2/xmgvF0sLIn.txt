009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011               Recent years have seen significant progress in Text-To-Audio (TTA) synthesis, en-
012               abling users to enrich their creative workflows with synthetic audio generated from
013               natural language prompts. Despite this progress, the effects of data, model archi-
014               tecture, training objective functions, and sampling strategies on target benchmarks
015               are not well understood. With the purpose of providing a holistic understanding
016               of the design space of TTA models, we setup a large-scale empirical experiment
017
                  focused on diffusion and flow matching models. Our contributions include: 1)
                  AF-Synthetic, a large dataset of high quality synthetic captions obtained from an
018
                  audio understanding model; 2) a systematic comparison of different architectural,
019
                  training, and inference design choices for TTA models; 3) an analysis of sampling
020               methods and their Pareto curves with respect to generation quality and inference
021               speed. We leverage the knowledge obtained from this extensive analysis to pro-
022               pose our best model dubbed Elucidated Text-To-Audio (ETTA). When evaluated
023               on AudioCaps and MusicCaps, ETTA provides improvements over the baselines
024               trained on publicly available data, while being competitive with models trained on
025               proprietary data. Finally, we show ETTA’s improved ability to generate creative au-
026               dio following complex and imaginative captions — a task that is more challenging
027               than current benchmarks.

      1       I NTRODUCTION
030
031
      The design space of text-to-audio (TTA) models is complex, including a myriad of correlated factors.
032
      While our research community has attempted to understand this design space and the contribution of
033   each factor, drawing conclusions between experiments is challenging. Our goal in this work is not to
034   explore novel model designs or methodologies. Instead, we aim to provide a holistic understanding of
035   existing paradigms for building TTA models, to identify important aspects that allow for improving
036   results, and to assess scalability with respect to data and model size.
037
      In this paper, we aim to elucidate the design space of TTA model with respect to training data,
038
      model architecture, implementation, capacity, objective functions during training, and sampling
039   methods during inference. In a controlled scenario and with a vast sweep over factors, we offer
040   insights on the contribution of each factor. In addition to elucidating the design space of TTA
041   models, our best configuration produces a model – namely Elucidated Text-to-Audio (ETTA) –
042   that significantly improves over open-sourced baselines on both AudioCaps (Kim et al., 2019) and
043   MusicCaps (Agostinelli et al., 2023) benchmarks with a single model.
044
      Recent research has shown that scaling dataset size, combined with a careful data filtering strategy,
045   can yield sizeable improvements on benchmarks in other domains (Radford et al., 2019; Betker et al.,
046   2023). Comparatively, the datasets used in TTA are generally much smaller, and their captions of
047   varying quality, thus posing a challenge to scaling datasets (Liu et al., 2023b; Huang et al., 2023c). In
048   order to circumvent these challenges, we introduce a large-scale and high-quality dataset of synthetic
049   captions, and show that it is possible to leverage synthetic captions to obtain significant improvements.
050
      While Transformers (Vaswani, 2017) have become the de facto architecture choice in many domains,
051   sometimes their efficiency and stability, specially in larger models, are severely impaired by imple-
052   mentation details related to numerical precision and weight initialization. 1 We improve on several

087   2    R ELATED W ORKS
088
089   Diffusion and Flow Matching Based Models Diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020; Song et al., 2021;
090   Kong et al., 2021; Kingma et al., 2021; Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021) are a type of deep generative
091   models that learn the data distribution with optional conditions (e.g. text-to-X generation). They learn
092   a reverse stochastic process that gradually transforms the Gaussian noise into clean data. The training
093
      objective of diffusion models is to predict the score function, i.e. the gradient of the log-likelihood
      with respect to data, via a neural network. Alternatively, some flow matching models predict the
094
      vector field related to the optimal transport between distributions (Lipman et al., 2022; Tong et al.,
095
      2023). These models can also be trained in the latent space (Rombach et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2023b)
096   for better efficiency, scalability, and quality. Appendix B includes the mathematical details.
097
098   Text-to-Audio Models There are two main streams of text-to-audio (TTA) models (including
099
      both audio and music generation) in the research community. One line of work uses diffusion and
      flow matching-based models. These works proposed numerous architectural and training designs for
100
      audio generation (Liu et al., 2023b; Ghosal et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2023c;a; Liu et al., 2024; Kong
101
      et al., 2024b; Xue et al., 2024; Haji-Ali et al., 2024; Hai et al., 2024; Vyas et al., 2023) and music
102   generation (Melechovsky et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2023b; Evans et al.; 2024a;b; Lam et al., 2024;
103   Schneider et al., 2024; Lan et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024b;a; Fei et al., 2024). However, there is no
104   systematic study on their design choices, and a main challenge is that the design space has too many
105   variables to investigate. Our work falls in this category and aims at conducting the first systematic
106   study on the design space of diffusion and flow matching based TTA models, and we choose to use
107   the latest Stable Audio Open (Evans et al., 2024b) as our base model to investigate. Another line of
      research focuses on the language model approach and uses next token prediction to train a language

108
      model on discrete token representation of audio (Kreuk et al., 2022; Borsos et al., 2023; Agostinelli
109   et al., 2023; Copet et al., 2024). These works are orthogonal to our study.
110
111   Audio-Caption Datasets AudioSet (Gemmeke et al., 2017) pioneered large-scale audio-text dataset
112
      with labels for about 2M audio segments. AudioCaps (Kim et al., 2019) and MusicCaps (Agostinelli
      et al., 2023) are subsets of AudioSet with high-quality human-annotated captions. They are among
113
      the most common benchmarks for text-to-audio and text-to-music generation. With the rapid progress
114
      in large language models (LLMs) in recent years, LLM-enhanced audio-caption datasets such as
115   WavCaps (Mei et al., 2024) and Laion-630K (Wu et al., 2023) were proposed, enabling large-scale
116   audio-language models including TTA and other tasks. However, the captions can be noisy as the
117   caption generation process does not depend on the audio signals. In the domain of TTA, recent works
118   have used different collections of audio-caption pairs (mostly by combining existing datasets) in
119   order to train powerful TTA models. Examples include TangoPromptBank (Ghosal et al., 2023),
120   AudioLDM (Liu et al., 2024), and Make-an-Audio (Huang et al., 2023c). However, these works
121   mostly constitute combination and/or augmentation of existing data.
122   Synthetic Data for Improved TTA Very recently, several concurrent works have studied using
123   audio captioning models to generate synthetic captions of unlabeled audio. This leads to more
124   accurate audio-caption pairs that could be used to train better TTA models. In detail, Sound-VECaps
125   (Yuan et al., 2024) uses CogVLM (Wang et al., 2023) to generate visual descriptions and EnClap
126   (Kim et al., 2024) to generate sound descriptions, and then use ChatGPT to condense into captions.
127   This approach does not apply to audio data without video, and the captions may contain excessive
128   visual information that does not exist in audio. GenAU (Haji-Ali et al., 2024) is trained on captions
129
      generated with AutoCap (Haji-Ali et al., 2024). However, this dataset is not open-sourced, and
      so we could not evaluate its quality. Tango-AF is trained on AF-AudioSet (Kong et al., 2024b)
130
      generated with Audio Flamingo (Kong et al., 2024a). It has very high quality, but is very small in
131
      scale. All these studies demonstrate synthetic captions could lead to significant improvement of TTA
132   generation quality. Inspired by these pioneering studies, we propose a larger synthetic dataset of
133   captions leveraging an audio language model followed by filtering that ensures high quality captions.

144   3.1   AF-S YNTHETIC
145
146   Inspired by the recent success of synthetic captions in the text-to-image domain (Betker et al., 2023;
147
      Nguyen et al., 2024), we aim to build a large-scale and high-quality synthetic captions dataset for
      better text-to-audio models. While there are several in-the-wild datasets with paired text and audio
148
      data, they have certain limitations that we aim to overcome. Captions in WavCaps (Mei et al., 2024)
149
      and Laion-630K (Wu et al., 2023) are noisy because they are produced from text metadata only, not
150   considering the actual audio. Sound-VECaps does not apply to audio data without video, and the
151   captions may contain excessive visual information that does not exist in audio. AutoCap (Haji-Ali
152   et al., 2024) and AF-AudioSet (Kong et al., 2024b) are closest to our approach; however, AutoCap is
153   not open-sourced, and AF-AudioSet is small in scale.
154
      We follow and improve the caption synthesis pipeline from AF-AudioSet. We use Audio Flamingo
155
      (Kong et al., 2024a) to generate ten captions for each audio sample and store the caption c with the
156   highest CLAP similarity cos(CLAPaudio (a), CLAPtext (c)) to the audio a (Wu et al., 2023). We
157   discard the caption if the similarity is below 0.45, the optimal threshold according to AF-AudioSet
158   (Kong et al., 2024b). In addition, there are challenges when applying this pipeline to larger-scale
159   synthesis (beyond AudioSet), such as extremely long, homogeneous, or low-quality audio. To address
160   these challenges, we caption each non-overlapping ten-second segment to obtain as many captions
161   as possible. We then use keywords, e.g. “noisy”, “low quality”, or “unknown sounds”, to detect
      low-quality audio. Finally, we also sub-sample long audio segments except for music and speech.

      3.2         ETTA
210
211   Our TTA model, dubbed Elucidated Text-To-Audio (ETTA), is built upon the LDM (Rombach et al.,
212   2022) paradigm and its application to audio generation. First, a variational autoencoder (VAE)
213   (Kingma & Welling, 2014) is trained to compress waveform into a compact latent space. Once the
214   VAE is trained, we freeze it and train a latent generative model in the VAE latent space. See Appendix
215

      B for mathematical details. We conduct our experiments based on the stable-audio-tools
217   library,3 which provides the most recent practices in building TTA models.
218
219   ETTA-VAE For training the VAE, we adopt a 44kHz stereo Audio-VAE with 156M parameters
220
      using the same default configuration used in stable-audio-tools with a latent frame rate of
      21.5Hz. We refer to (Evans et al., 2024b) and Appendix B for details. The Audio-VAE is trained
221
      from scratch on our large-scale collection of publicly available datasets (see Table 14). In terms of
222
      quality, our Audio-VAE matches or exceeds Stable Audio Open, as shown in Table 24 and Table 25. 4
223
224   ETTA-LDM Next, we train a text-conditional latent generative model for TTA synthesis. The
225   latent model can be either a diffusion model (Ho et al., 2020; Song et al., 2021; Salimans & Ho, 2022)
226
      or a flow matching model (Lipman et al., 2022; Tong et al., 2023). We parameterize our model using
      the Diffusion Transformer (DiT) (Peebles & Xie, 2023) architecture based on Evans et al. (2024b)
227
      and Lan et al. (2024), with 24 layers, 24 heads, and a width of 1536 as the default choices. We
228
      condition our model on the outputs of the T5-base (Raffel et al., 2020) text encoder, which outputs
229   embeddings for variable-length text. In our experiments, we also explore other common choices and
230   combinations of different text encoders – including T5-based (Raffel et al., 2020; Chung et al., 2024)
231   and CLAP models (Wu et al., 2023) – to study the effect of this component.
232
      ETTA-DiT Finally, we provide several key improvements to the DiT implementation in Evans
233
      et al. (2024b), and call our implementation ETTA-DiT. Through experiments, we find that solely
234
      replacing their architecture with ETTA-DiT leads to improved training losses and evaluation results.
235   Our improvements include:
236
237   1) Adaptive layer normalization (AdaLN):5 We switch from prepending to AdaLN timestep embed-
238
      ding and apply AdaLN to self-attention, cross-attention, and feed-forward layer inputs. The AdaLN
      parameters are initialized with scale = 1 and bias = 0 so that AdaLN does not modulate the feature
239
      at initialization. When applying AdaLN, we enforce FP32, use torch.autocast for numerical
240
      precision, use a bias term for the linear layer, and use unbounded gating (i.e. no sigmoid).
241
242   2) Final layers: We initialize the final projection layer of DiT to output zeros. This matches the mean
243   of the VAE latent distribution, and therefore leads to improved stability and convergence rate. We
244
      also use AdaLN in the final projection layer.
245   3) Other changes: We use the tanh approximation mode of the GELU activation (Hendrycks &
246   Gimpel, 2016). We use rotary position embedding (RoPE) (Su et al., 2024) in the self-attention
247   layer, with rope base = 16384 to inject relative positional information. We apply dropout with
248   pdropout = 0.1 for all modules to enhance robustness in parameter estimation.

251   3.3    T RAINING OBJECTIVE AND S AMPLING
252
253   Training For the diffusion model training objective, we use the v-prediction loss function (Salimans
254   & Ho, 2022). For the flow matching training objective, we use the optimal transport conditional flow
255   matching (OT-CFM) loss function (Lipman et al., 2022; Tong et al., 2023). We refer to Appendix B
256   for details of these methods. Prior works also found sampling t more often on intermediate steps
257   leads to better results (Esser et al., 2024; Lan et al., 2024). We follow their approach and sample t
258
      from a logit-normal distribution, in practice t ∼ σ(N (0, 1)), when training ETTA with OT-CFM.
259   Sampling We consider Euler and 2nd -order Heun (Karras et al., 2022) methods for solving the
260   ODE parameterized by ETTA. We conduct an extensive sweep over hyperparameters focusing on
261   two major design choices: the number of function evaluations (NFE) and the classifier-free guidance
262   (CFG) (Ho & Salimans, 2022) scale wcfg . We draw Pareto curves across benchmark datasets and
263   metrics to discover the optimal choice for ETTA. In addition, we also explore the effectiveness of a
264
      recently proposed guidance method, autoguidance (Karras et al., 2024), in TTA applications.

      4     E XPERIMENTS
271
272
      Our experiments thoroughly evaluate our framework ETTA on benchmark datasets (AudioCaps and
273   MusicCaps). We start with a systematic comparison to elucidate the design space of TTA in four
274   major aspects: 1) training data, 2) training objectives, 3) architectural design and model sizes, and
275   4) sampling methods. Furthermore, we show ETTA’s improved ability to generate creative audio
276   following complex and imaginative captions, a task that is more challenging than current benchmarks.
277   In our commitment to fully elucidate all aspects of our investigation, we also document the additional
278   directions we explored, including numerous additional ablations (in Appendix D) and mixed or
279   negative results (in Appendix E). We train all models using 8 A100 GPUs.

281   4.1   T RAINING DATA
282
      We train ETTA on four different training datasets to assess TTA quality: AudioCaps (50K captions),
283
      AF-AudioSet (161K captions), TangoPromptBank (1.21M captions), and our AF-Synthetic (1.35M
284
      captions). We fix audio length to 10 seconds and sampling rate to 44.1kHz in all these datasets.

286
      4.2   T RAINING O BJECTIVE AND S AMPLING
287
288   Audio VAE We train a 44.1kHz stereo Audio-VAE based on stable-audio-tools with our
289   collection of unlabeled and public audio datasets (Table 14). We train the Audio-VAE for 2.8M steps,
290   with a total batch size of 64 with 1.5 seconds per sample. We train with full precision (FP32) to
291   make the waveform compression model as accurate as possible. The latent dimension is 64 and the
292   frame rate is 21.5 Hz.
293   Training Objective and Architecture We train ETTA-LDM with ETTA-DiT as the backbone.
294   We use the T5-base text embedding with max length=512 truncation to accommodate longer
295   captions in AF-Synthetic.6 We train with both v-diffusion and OT-CFM objectives, where we
296   additionally apply logit-normal t-sampling for OT-CFM (see Section 3.3). Our final model is trained
297   for 1M steps with a learning rate of 10−4 and total batch size of 128 with 10 seconds per sample.
298   For ablation studies, we train each model for 250k steps unless otherwise stated. We use BF16
299   mixed-precision training (Micikevicius et al., 2017) and flash-attention 2 (Dao et al., 2022)
300   to maximize training throughput.
301   Sampling For diffusion models, following (Evans et al., 2024b) we use the dpmpp-3m-sde
302   sampler 7 and CFG scale wcfg = 7. For OT-CFM models, we compare between Euler and 2nd -order
303   Heun samplers and draw Pareto curves for each method with respect to the number of function
304   evaluations (NFEs) and CFG scale. After this extensive sweep, we choose Euler sampling with NFE
305   = 100, wcfg = 3.5 for main results, and wcfg = 3 for ablation studies unless otherwise stated.

307   4.3   R ESULTS
308
      Metrics We use a collection of established objective metrics for systematic evaluation. 1) Fréchet
309
      distance (FD) measures the distributional gap between generated and ground truth audios using
310
      features extracted from an audio classifier. We consider three classifiers: VGGish (FDV ), commonly
311   referred to as Fréchet Audio Distance (FAD) (Kilgour et al., 2018)), Openl3 (Cramer et al., 2019)
312   (FDO ), and PANNs (Kong et al., 2020) (FDP ). 2) Kullback–Leibler divergence (KL) is an instance-
313   level metric that measures the difference between the posterior distributions of audio events for the
314   ground truth and generated audio samples. This metric helps assess how close the generated audio
315   aligns with the ground truth on the single-sample level. We report KL using PaSST (Koutini et al.,
316   2022) (KLS ) and PANNs (KLP ). 3) Inception Score (IS) evaluates the diversity and specificity of
317   the generated samples without requiring ground truth. IS is calculated from the entropy of instance
318   posteriors and the entropy of marginal posteriors, where a higher score reflects both better diversity
319
      and sharper class predictions. We use PANNs for IS (ISP ). 4) Finally, CLAP score measures the
      cosine similarity between text and audio embeddings, which indicates the correlation between the
320
      generated sample and the given prompt. For extensive evaluation, we use two CLAP models: CLL

466   Choice of Sampler and its Impact on Metrics Figure 3 and Figure 4 in the Appendix present
467   a comprehensive analysis of the impact of sampler choices. The results reveal several key insights:
468   1) All metrics improve as the number of function evaluations (NFE) increases, as expected. 2) At
469   lower NFE, the Heun sampler is noticeably better than Euler; as NFE increases, they converge to
470   similar results. 3) FD behaves like a convex function with respect to the CFG scale, indicating that
471   FD penalizes low diversity caused by CFG’s over-emphasis on text condition. 4) Metrics such as
472   KL, IS, and CL show continuous improvement with higher CFG scales, suggesting their preference
473   for accuracy over diversity. Therefore, one should be cautious when selecting the CFG scale, as
474
      optimizing for these metrics alone may lead to a trade-off between diversity and accuracy. Detailed
      results on the choices of sampler and NFE are provided in Table 18 in Appendix D. In summary:
475
476    Heun’s sampler is better than Euler at lower NFE. wcfg = 3.5 provides the best overall metrics,
477    and one should be cautious that a higher CFG scale potentially leads to lower diversity.

510   While this is encouraging, we find potential mode-collapse when fine-tuning ETTA on MusicCaps

514
      optimizing for a single metric such as FD does not represent the overall quality and therefore should
515   be discouraged. We believe that it is important to evaluate TTA models by incorporating as many
516   objective metrics and datasets as possible to have a more comprehensive understanding of the model.
517   We also believe it is necessary to develop novel evaluation metrics for defects like mode collapse.

519    A potentially mode-collapsed model could still yield nice-looking evaluation numbers. Therefore,

522   Future work While this work aims to elucidate the design space of TTA with large-scale experi-
523   ments, there are still several unexplored problems. We plan to study these in our future work.
524   1) Data Augmentation To isolate the effect of data scaling, we use AF-Synthetic and other datasets
525   without any data augmentation. Because of this, the diversity of text captions and audio may be
526   limited. Previous works (Melechovsky et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023b; 2024; Huang et al., 2023c;a)
527   reported improvements with data augmentation, such as caption rephrasing and audio re-mixing. We
528   plan to design scalable methods for a systematical study on the effect of data augmentation.
529   2) Challenges in Evaluating TTA The research community has yet to reach a consensus on
530   which attributes are effectively captured by current evaluation metrics for TTA models. Evaluating
531   TTA is particularly challenging due to confounding factors like differences in model accuracy,
532   diversity, choice of waveform decoder, and more. For example, we empirically find that the CFG
533   scale that yields the best quantitative metrics may not align with human preferences. Designing
534   evaluation methods that accurately reflect both the accuracy and diversity of TTA models, in a
535   way that corresponds with human perception, remains a difficult problem. We hope that our work
536   will stimulate discussion and serve as a reference point for developing a standardized approach to
537
      evaluating TTA models.

816   B     M ATHEMATICAL BACKGROUND
817
818   Let pdata be the data distribution and X ∼ pdata be N i.i.d. training samples drawn from the data
819   distribution. In (unconditional) audio synthesis, we assume pdata is on [−1, 1]L where L = 441000
820   is a fixed length for 10 seconds of audio at 44.1kHz sampling rate. A generative model on X aims to
821   model pθ (x) ≈ pdata (x) and draw samples from it. In text-to-audio synthesis, each sample x = (a, c)
822   is composed of an audio a ∈ [−1, 1]L and a corresponding caption c in the natural language space.
823   In this case, we aim to model pθ (a|c) and draw samples conditioned on a given caption c. For
824
      conciseness, we introduce all the mathematical background in the unconditional setting, and these
      can be translated into the conditional setting by conditioning all distributions on c.
825

827   B.1   VARIATIONAL AUTO E NCODERS
828
829
      Variational auto encoders (VAEs) (Kingma & Welling, 2014) include an encoder E and a decoder D.
      E aims to encode a sample x into a lower-dimensional space, and D aims to reconstruct E(x) to the
830
      original space with minimal information loss. The training loss is
831
832                      LVAE = Ex∼X [R(D(E(x)), x) + KL(qE (z|x) ∥ N (0, I))],
833
834   where R is a reconstruction loss that measures the distance between the original sample x and
835   the reconstructed sample D(E(x)). qE (z|x) is the approximate posterior distribution of the latent
836   variable z given x using E, and the KL divergence loss measures how close the posterior distribution
837
      is to the prior N (0, I).

865
866   Diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020; Song et al., 2021) include two processes:
867
868         1. A fixed Markov chain diffusion process
869
870                                                dx = f (x, t)dt + g(t)dw,
871
               where x represents data, t ∈ [0, 1] represents time, f is the drift term, g is the diffusion term,
872

874         2. A learned Markov chain reverse process
875
876
                                      dx = [f (x, t) − g(t)2 ∇x log pt (x)]dt + g(t)dw̄,
877

      B.4   L ATENT D IFFUSION M ODELS
902
      Latent diffusion models (LDMs) (Rombach et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2023b) combine VAE with
903
      diffusion models, training the diffusion models within the latent space of the VAE. In this approach,
904   the VAE’s latent variable z serves as the target for generation. Rather than directly modeling pdata ,
905   LDMs model the pushforward distribution E# pdata , utilizing the frozen encoder and decoder from
906   the VAE to transition between the original data space and the latent space.

      B.5   C LASSIFIER -F REE G UIDANCE
909
910   Classifier-Free Guidance (CFG) (Ho & Salimans, 2022) adjusts the balance between diversity
911   and quality in generative models by over-emphasizing conditioning. The model is trained both
912   conditionally and unconditionally by randomly replacing the condition c with a null embedding ∅.
913   During sampling, the guided output is given by:
914
915
916                         vθ (xt , t|c) = vθ (xt , t) + wcfg · (vθ (xt , t|c) − vθ (xt , t)),
917
      where wcfg is a guidance scale. wcfg = 1 disables guidance and wcfg > 1 amplifies the conditioning.

1235   2023a) that presented improvement of various speech tasks (e.g., Text-to-Speech (TTS)) by pretraining

1238   noisy input. Note that we do not feed the masked feature to cross-attention input, so the cross-attention

1240   700k steps. Then, we reset the first input projection layer of DiT and optimizer, and switch to the
1241   main TTA task starting from the pretrained weight. We observe that the training loss starts much
       lower for the pretrained model.

1271   text encoders. We consider T5-base, T5-large, FLAN-T5-base, and FLAN-T5-large. In
1272   addition, we experiment with a dual text encoder setup (Huang et al., 2023a; Liu et al., 2024; Haji-Ali
1273   et al., 2024) by using CLAP as additional global text embedding. We use a different CLAP checkpoint
1274   (LAION’s music audioset epoch 15 esc 90.14) to the benchmark CLAP models (CLL and
1275   CLM ) to rule out a possibility of inflated result from the same representation. In this experiment, we
1276
       start training with a pretrained weight from the inpainting task for 700k training steps, 13 and trained
       each model with different text encoder for 300k steps.
1277
1278   Table 21 summarizes the result on different text encoder choices evaluated on AudioCaps. Unfor-
1279   tunately, we were not able to discover noticeably better choice compared to others. Nevertheless,
1280   we find interesting observations: 1) FLAN-T5-base scores relatively better than T5-base for
1281   FDV and FDO , but the opposite can be observed for other metrics such as KLS . 2) for our setup, we
1282
       have not found strong evidence that dual text encoder with CLAP is better; it worsened most metrics
       except for FLAN-T5-large. 3) larger T5 encoder may not necessarily be better in improving
1283
       results, where base model generally scored better metrics than large model. T5-large showed
1284
       surprisingly worse result compared to others for two independent training runs (with or without
1285   CLAP). While this seems counter-intuitive, it also suggests that the optimal choice of text encoder
1286   would depend on other factors such as training dataset and the main TTA model capacity at hand.
1287
1288    No single text encoder consistently outperformed others. The effectiveness of text encoders seems
1289    to depend on specific metrics and setup. Larger text encoders do not always lead to better results.

1317   Autoguidance Recently, (Karras et al., 2024) showed that the improvement in perceptual quality of
1318   CFG stems from its ability to eliminate unlikely outlier samples, but it may reduce diversity from
1319   over-emphasis. They proposed a new way of guiding the model, called autoguidance, that uses a
1320   bad version of the same model (either by under-training and/or with smaller model) that increases
1321   diversity while ensuring high-quality output as follows (omitting the condition c for brevity):
1322
1323                          vθ (xt , t) = vθag (xt , t) + wag · (vθ (xt , t) − vθag (xt , t)),
1324
1325   where θag denotes a bad model and wag is the scale for autoguidance. Same as CFG, wag = 1
1326   disables the guidance and wag > 1 amplifies the main model’s prediction.
1327   We conducted experiments applying autoguidance to evaluate its effectiveness to our TTA setup. The
1328   results are in Tables 22 and 23. From our grid search of wag from 1 to 2.5 with 0.25 interval, wag = 2
1329   provided the best possible metrics.
1330
       Subjectively, we observed that while autoguidance could produce more diverse audio samples
1331
       corroborating (Karras et al., 2024), but these samples sometimes lacked realism. We find that the
1332   method is sensitive to the choice of the bad model and its guidance scale wag . In terms of improving
1333   benchmark results, despite our best efforts and various combinations including different bad models
1334   (either under-trained versions or smaller models) and guidance scales, we were unable to identify a
1335   setup that clearly outperforms plain CFG with wcfg = 3. Similar benchmark metrics could only be
1336   achieved by combining both CFG and autoguidance, but at an increased cost with 2x NFE.
1337
       We conjecture that our search space may have been incomplete. However, we do observe noticeable
1338   increase in diversity from autoguidnace where the same ETTA checkpoint can sometimes generate
1339   even more “interesting” samples, so we believe autoguidance holds its potential towards creativity.
1340   We leave exploring recently proposed methods for sampling from the model for better TTA results in
1341   future work.
