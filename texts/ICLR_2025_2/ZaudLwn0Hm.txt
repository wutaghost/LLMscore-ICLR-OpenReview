009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Vision-Language Models (e.g., CLIP), with their immense capacity and exten-
012            sive exposure to vast data during pre-training, have demonstrated a strong abil-
013            ity to capture real-world concepts. When fast adapted to downstream tasks
014            with only a few labeled samples, parameter-efficient methods, such as prompt-
015            based and adapter-based approaches, which adjust only a small portion of the
016            parameters, have proven effective in reducing the escalating costs in large vision-
017            language models. However, conventional efficient fine-tuning techniques, using
018
               task-specific objectives like cross-entropy loss, often lead to overfitting the down-
               stream data distributions. This overfitting diminishes the model’s ability to retain
019
               its original generalization capacity, especially on out-of-distribution (OOD) sam-
020
               ples. Unlike the pretraining stage, where rich textual descriptions are available,
021            fine-tuning is typically constrained to using only class names. This creates subop-
022            timal text-image alignment in the shared feature space, as it may exacerbate image
023            feature variance within the same class. To address this issue, we propose Proto-
024            typical Evolutionary Adaptation (PEA), leveraging off-the-shelf image centroids
025            as prototypes to regulate image feature variance, mitigating the excessive feature
026            variance within the same class caused by selective bias. Additionally, we intro-
027            duce learnable shift vectors to capture the dynamics of class prototypes, ensuring
028            that they remain compact and informative. Experiments across diverse datasets
029
               and model architectures in few-shot learning demonstrate that our approach con-
               sistently outperforms existing methods while maintaining robust generalization
030
               under varying distribution shifts.

      Vision-Language Models (VLMs) like CLIP have demonstrated impressive zero-shot classification
036   abilities by learning a shared semantic space between visual and textual modalities. This success
037   is driven by the model’s ability to leverage vast datasets of web-scale image-text pairs during pre-
038   training, allowing it to classify images into various categories using only prompts, such as “a photo
039   of a [class]”, without any additional training. While CLIP excels in these zero-shot tasks, its per-
040   formance can be further enhanced in downstream tasks with limited labeled data. To address this,
041   recent research has focused on developing parameter-efficient fine-tuning methods that reduce the
042   number of trainable parameters while improving performance on few-shot learning tasks.
043   Parameter-efficient methods, such as prompt-based approaches like CoOp (Zhou et al., 2022c) and
044   adapter-based (Gao et al., 2024) approaches like CLIP-Adapter, have made significant strides in
045   adapting CLIP to few-shot learning tasks. These approaches introduce minimal additional param-
046   eters while achieving considerable performance improvements. However, despite their efficiency,
047   these methods often suffer from overfitting on limited downstream data, particularly when relying
048   solely on class names for fine-tuning, leading to a reduction in generalization performance, espe-
049   cially on out-of-distribution (OOD) samples(Kumar et al., 2022).
050   To address these limitations, we propose Prototypical Evolutionary Adaptation (PEA), a novel ap-
051   proach that builds upon the class prototype methodology. While conventional class-prototype meth-
052   ods such as Nearest Mean Classifier (NMC) use static prototypes based on feature averages, these
053   prototypes can be biased and insufficient in capturing the true distribution of class features. Our
      method introduces dynamic prototypes that evolve throughout the fine-tuning process, leveraging

089   2   R ELATED WORKS
090
091   Vision Language Models (VLM S) In recent years, VLM S have attracted significant attention
092   from researchers, emerging as a promising paradigm and have been successfully applied to numer-
093   ous visual tasks. A notable example is CLIP (Radford et al., 2021), which leverages weak super-
094   vision by using the linguistic description of each image as a training signal. It underwent training
095
      on a vast corpus of 400 million web-crawled images and texts, achieving results competitive with
      supervised baseline. Then a crops of works (Goel et al., 2022; Li et al., 2022; Zhai et al., 2023)
096
      explored vision-language pretraining to obtain versatile applicable representations. Although, these
097
      pretrained VLM S have learned transferable representations for both vision and languages, adapt-
098   ing to downstream tasks remains a challenging research problem. There have been many tailored
099   methods proposed to adapt VLM S for few-shot classification (Zhou et al., 2022c;a), semantic seg-
100   mentation (Lin et al., 2023; He et al., 2023) and object dection (Mao et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2023).
101
102   Efficient transfer leaning. Given the large size of pre-trained VLMs like CLIP (Radford et al.,
103   2021), efficiently fine-tuning these models for downstream tasks has become a central focus of re-
104   cent research. The goal of parameter-efficient transfer learning is to achieve optimal performance
105   with minimal modifications to the pre-trained model, which is particularly important in few-shot
106   learning scenarios where labeled data is scarce. One prominent approach is prompt tuning, which
107   optimizes only the input prompts while keeping the backbone of the model frozen. Methods like
      CoOp (Zhou et al., 2022c) introduced learnable textual prompts that adapt to downstream tasks


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      through back-propagation, allowing the model to leverage the rich knowledge embedded in the
109   pre-trained weights. By tuning just the prompts, this approach minimizes the need to modify the
110   model’s core parameters, making it both efficient and effective for few-shot learning. However,
111   despite strong performance gains, prompt tuning has been shown to face limitations in generaliza-
112   tion, particularly when dealing with unseen classes. To address these challenges, CoCoOp (Zhou
113   et al., 2022a) extends CoOp by incorporating visual features into the prompt generation process,
114   enhancing the model’s ability to generalize from base classes to novel ones. Another key strategy
115   in parameter-efficient fine-tuning is adapter-based methods. Instead of fine-tuning the entire model,
116   these methods introduce lightweight adapter modules that adjust the visual and textual representa-
117
      tions of CLIP. CLIP-Adapter (Gao et al., 2024) refines the original vision and language embeddings
      by training task-specific adapters, which are inserted into pre-trained layers. This approach retains
118
      the efficiency of the model by limiting the number of trainable parameters while still improving
119
      task-specific performance. However, despite their efficiency, adapter-based methods still require
120   additional computational cost during inference stage.
121
122
      Few-shot learning. Few-shot learning approaches are typically divided into two main categories:
123
      metric-based methods and optimization-based methods. Metric-based methods aim to map sam-
124   ples into an embedding space where classification is performed based on the distance between
125   the query samples and class prototypes. These methods rely on predefined, task-agnostic distance
126   metrics to measure similarity between the samples and the class representatives. Commonly used
127   metrics include cosine similarity, which calculates the cosine of the angle between two vectors in
128   the embedding space, and Euclidean distance, which measures the straight-line distance between
129   two points. One of the most well-known metric-based methods is Prototypical Networks (Snell
130   et al., 2017), which computes a single prototype for each class and classifies new samples based on
131   their proximity to these prototypes. While these methods are efficient, they may struggle to adapt
132
      to more complex tasks where a single prototype per class does not capture intra-class variations.
      Optimization-based methods, on the other hand, aim to learn optimal initial model parameters that
133
      can be quickly fine-tuned for new tasks using only a few labeled examples. Model-Agnostic Meta-
134
      Learning (MAML) (Finn et al., 2017) is a prominent example of this approach. In MAML, the
135   model is trained to be sensitive to changes in task-specific data, allowing it to adapt rapidly with
136   minimal updates. During the meta-training phase, MAML optimizes the model parameters on a set
137   of base tasks so that it can quickly adapt to novel tasks with only a few gradient steps. In this paper,
138   we utilize the limited supervision signal to better calibrate the biased mean estimation of frozen
139   visual features rather than learning the metric.

      4.1    M OTIVATION
211
212   In real-world scenarios, objects that share the same label can exhibit vastly different characteristics,
213   as their appearances vary dramatically in terms of color, texture, shape, background, and style. These
214   differences, ranging from subtle to significant, could be further amplified in the feature space after
215   extraction by VLM S. As illustrated in Figure 2, the extracted visual features are highly diverse,
      and some have low similarity scores with their ground-truth class names. This rich visual diversity


                                                                                    4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      challenges the effectiveness of simple prompt templates like ’a photo of a [class]’, as such prompts
217   may not sufficiently capture the detailed variations present in these images.
218
219   With the widespread use of GPT-3 (Brown, 2020) to generate descriptions, Menon & Vondrick
220
      (2023); Pratt et al. (2023); Roth et al. (2023) circumvented the challenges posed by rich visual di-
      versity and leverage the knowledge embedded in Large Language Models(LLMs) for the automatic
221
      generation of class-specific descriptions. These descriptions aim to enhance the diversity of tex-
222
      tual representations by focusing on the discriminative features of image categories, which are then
223   aligned with the query images. However, in Figure 2, the detailed textual descriptions generated by
224   LLMs may still exhibit low similarity scores with the features extracted from the query images. Fur-
225   thermore, as pointed in Zhou et al. (2022b), even minor modifications to the prompt, e.g., changing
226   the prompt ’a photo of [class]’ to ’a photo of a [class]’, can give rise to a performance improvement
227   of up to 6%. This sensitivity to specific wording suggests that overly detailed descriptions may
228   actually degrade downstream performance due to the nuanced nature of language.
229   Since broad and generic class templates can be considered as the class centroids of detailed class-
230   specific descriptions within the textual feature space, it is natural to extend this concept to the visual
231   domain to account for rich visual diversity. A straightforward method to tackle this issue is to align
232   the textual class prototypes with the visual class prototypes. However, this approach may suffer from
233   selection bias in that the support dataset is randomly divided, and the informative class centroid is
234   also affected by the number of shots (K-shot). Specifically, a larger K leads to a more accurate
235   estimation of the class centroid. Motivated by these challenges, we propose PEA to address the
236   issues for accurate class centroid estimation.

292   Connection to other parameter-efficient fine-tuning methods. As discussed in (Kumar et al.,
293   2022; Mukhoti et al., 2023), full fine-tuning can distort pretrained features and degrade perfor-
294   mance, especially under mild distribution shifts. LP leverages the advantage of inheriting frozen
295   pretrained features, achieving good performance under distribution shifts; however, it often results
296
      in unsatisfactory downstream performance.
297   A simple yet efficient remedy proposed in Wortsman et al. (2022); Ilharco et al. (2022); Kim et al.
298   (2024) involves patching pretrained models by linearly interpolating weights between zero-shot
299   models and fine-tuned models. This method implicitly edits the frozen representations in the weight
300   space. Another line of work (Zhou et al., 2022c;a) aims to learn soft prompts by optimizing a con-
301   tinuous set of prompt vectors, which interferes with the frozen representations through the input
302
      space.
303   The most relevant works to ours are Yu et al. (2023); Sui et al. (2024), which steer the frozen
304   features directly within the embedding space. Both methods focus on the textual feature space, and
305   the experiments show that they yield only marginal improvements when applied to the visual feature
306   space. In contrast, we exploit the intrinsic properties of the visual feature space. By only calibrating
307   the biased prototypes, we further enhance few-shot learning with altering the frozen representations.

369   experiments. Using ImageNet (Deng et al., 2009) as the source domain for adaptation, we evaluated
370   our method on four of its variants as target domains: ImageNetV2 (Recht et al., 2019), ImageNet-
371   Sketch (Wang et al., 2019), ImageNet-A (Hendrycks et al., 2021b), ImageNet-R (Hendrycks et al.,
372   2021a). In this scenario, the model was trained using only a few labeled samples from the source do-
373   main (ImageNet), and the target datasets were exclusively used for testing. This setup allowed us to
374
      evaluate the model’s domain generalization capabilities without any exposure to the target domains
      during training.

377   Training details. In our experiments, we leveraged pre-trained features from CLIP (Radford et al.,
      2021) using two primary backbone architectures: ResNet-50 (He et al., 2016) and ViT-B/16 (Doso-


                                                                                                                       7
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


378
      vitskiy et al., 2021). The main experiments were conducted with both ResNet-50 and ViT-B/16,
379   while the ablation studies specifically utilized ResNet-50 as the backbone. To make full use of the
380   frozen features to accelerate the training process, so we extracted all the pre-trained features from the
381   support sets and performed adaptation experiments based on these features. Following the method-
382   ology in Yu et al. (2023); Zhou et al. (2022b), we applied data augmentation during the feature
383   extraction stage, including random zooms, crops, and flips. Each support sample was augmented
384   20 times to enhance the diversity of the training data. We employed the same text prompts for each
385   dataset as specified in Yu et al. (2023); Zhou et al. (2022c). Training was carried out over 200 epochs
386   using the SGD optimizer with a momentum of 0.9, inspired by the training strategies in Yu et al.
387
      (2023). We set the default initial learning rate to 2 × 10−3 to prevent underfitting on the support
      sets. The learning rate was scheduled to decrease during training following a cosine decay pattern.
388
      All experiments are conducted on a single NVIDIA GeForce RTX 4090. To ensure robustness, all
389
      experiments were run with three different random seeds, and the results were averaged across these
390   runs. Our method introduces a calibration strength parameter, denoted as α, which adjusts the in-
391   fluence of the prototypes during adaptation. By default, α is set to 0.5 for all datasets, providing
392   a balance between the biased and evolved prototypes. We also explored the impact of varying α
393   values in our ablation studies.
394
395
396   Baselines. To evaluate the effectiveness of our proposed method, we compare it against several
397
      baseline approaches, which we organize into four distinct groups based on their methodologies and
      how they interact with pre-trained models. (1) Zero-shot and random LP (Radford et al., 2021):
398
      This group serves as a basic benchmark. It includes the zero-shot CLIP model, which uses prompts
399
      like “a photo of a [class]” without any additional training. Additionally, a linear classifier with
400   random initialization is trained on top of the frozen pre-trained CLIP visual encoder’s features. (2)
401   Improved LP Methods (Wortsman et al., 2022; Silva-Rodriguez et al., 2024): These methods en-
402   hance standard linear probing by leveraging prior knowledge from textual embeddings. Classifier
403   weights are initialized using class name prototypes derived from textual features, providing a better
404   starting point for learning. They also introduce additional constraint terms during training to more
405   effectively capture class-specific characteristics. (3) Prompt Tuning Methods (Implicit Represen-
406   tation Editing via Input Space) (Zhou et al., 2022c; Chen et al., 2023): Techniques like Context
407   Optimization (CoOp) learn continuous prompt vectors through back-propagation. (4) Methods Di-
408
      rectly Altering the Feature Space (Yu et al., 2023): This group includes approaches like TaskRes,
      which directly steers the frozen features in the textual embedding space using a task-specific residual
409
      connection.

414   Few-shot results. We compare our proposed method, PEA, with several baseline methods in the
415   few-shot learning setting, as summarized in Table 1. Across 12 datasets, PEA consistently demon-
416   strates superior performance, achieving the highest average accuracy of 75.15%. Notably, it excels
417   on datasets such as Oxford Pets (88.99%), Flowers102 (96.06%), and UCF101 (79.41%). Further-
418   more, as shown in Figure 3, we observe that as the number of images per class increases, the more
419   informative class centroids lead to significant performance improvements.

422   6     C ONCLUSION
423
424
      In this paper, we revisit classic prototype-based methods in Vision-Language Models (VLMs) and
425
      propose a novel approach called Prototypical Evolutionary Adaptation (PEA). PEA refines the pro-
426   cess of obtaining accurate class prototypes within the visual feature space by dynamically calibrat-
427   ing them throughout the fine-tuning process. This accurate class propotype will benefit the linear
428   probing in the context of few-shot leanring. We conduct extensive experiments to evaluate the
429   effectiveness of PEA on CLIP few-shot classification tasks and out-of-distribution generalization.
430   Our method consistently outperforms state-of-the-art adapter-based and prompt-based approaches,
431   demonstrating its superior performance. In future work, we aim to explore the application of PEA
      in other tasks and scenarios, such as test-time adaptation.
