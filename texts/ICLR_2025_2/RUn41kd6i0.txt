011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            While in-context learning with large language models (LLMs) has shown impres-
014            sive performance, we have discovered a unique miscalibration behavior where
015            both correct and incorrect predictions are assigned the same level of confidence.
016
               We refer to this phenomenon as indiscriminate miscalibration. We found that
               traditional calibration metrics, such as Expected Calibrated Errors (ECEs), are
017
               unable to capture this behavior effectively. To address this issue, we propose new
018
               metrics to measure the severity of indiscriminate miscalibration. Additionally, we
019            develop a novel in-context comparative inference method to alleviate miscalibra-
020            tions and improve classification performance. Through extensive experiments on
021            five datasets, we demonstrate that our proposed method can achieve more accurate
022            and calibrated predictions compared to regular zero-shot and few-shot prompting.

025   1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027   LLMs have exhibited emergent capabilities such as advanced creative writing, summarization, trans-
028   lation, arithmetic and commonsense reasoning, etc (Wei et al., 2022; Brown et al., 2020). One of the
029   most fascinating aspects of LLMs is their in-context learning capabilities. In particular, this involves
030   adding pairs of demonstration examples to the prompt, and has been shown to significantly enhance
031   the performance of LLMs (Brown et al., 2020). This capability offers users the significant advan-
032   tage of utilizing LLMs without the need to train or fine-tune their own models. As a result, there is
033
      a growing need for research into calibration techniques (Zhou et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2021; Jiang
      et al., 2023a; 2021) to ensure the reliability of model outputs as well and improve performance.
034
035   The concept of calibration in modern deep learning models was first introduced in (Guo et al., 2017)
036   where the authors also proposed metrics (such as Expected Calibration Errors, reliability diagrams,
037   etc) and methods (such as temperature scaling) for characterizing and mitigating miscalibration
038   issues. The miscalibration of LLMs has also been studied in recent works (Xiong et al., 2023; Tian
039
      et al., 2023; Liusie et al., 2024; Zhao et al., 2023; Geng et al., 2023; Kamath et al., 2020). In this
      work, we show that LLMs with zero-shot and few-shot prompting exhibit a unique miscalibration
040
      issue on classification tasks, which we refer to as indiscriminate miscalibration. This phenomenon
041
      occurs when models assign equal confidence to correct and incorrect predictions. We show that
042   this phenomenon cannot be quantitatively measured by Expected Calibration Errors (ECE). One
043   alternative metric that can help catch this phenomenon is using Macro-average Calibration Error
044   (MacroCE) proposed in Si et al. (2022). However, it may not depict the phenomenon thoroughly
045   as it only computes the means of the distributions. We further propose a metric to help describe
046   the phenomenon in more details. We hypothesize that this indiscriminate miscalibration occurs
047   because the model treats each sample independently and has not been trained on the corresponding
048   dataset. As a result, the predicted probabilities are not comparable across samples, which can further
049   negatively impact prediction accuracy.
050   To this end, we propose a label-free in-context comparative inference method that adds unlabeled
051   samples to the prompt. This encourages the model to adjust and calibrate its predictions without
052   requiring labels for the demonstration examples. The added examples serve as a proxy to help the
053   model better understand the test examples and the corresponding task. We delve deeper into the
      principles of our method and develop an aggregation step for improved calibration and a post-hoc

084
      Confidence calibration on modern neural networks has been discussed in (Guo et al., 2017) where
085
      the authors showed that large vision models are poorly calibrated. and proposed the Expected Cal-
086
      ibration Errors (ECEs) that have been widely used in the literature. A more recent paper reviews
087   some drawbacks of ECEs and proposed Instance-level Calibration Error (ICE) and Macro-average
088   Calibration Error (MacroCE) (Si et al., 2022). Recent studies have shown great interests of model
089   uncertainty and calibration in language models as well(Xiong et al., 2023; Tian et al., 2023; Liusie
090   et al., 2024; Zhao et al., 2023; Geng et al., 2023; Kamath et al., 2020). As one of the most popu-
091   lar method for prompt engineering with LLM, in-context few-shot prompting (Brown et al., 2020)
092   has the output instability issue, which was revealed by Zhao et al. (2021). They proposed a simple
093   method to estimate and adjust majority label bias, recency bias, and common token biases intro-
094   duced by in-context learning. Jiang et al. (2021) also showed that LLMs can be overconfident about
095
      their answers and calibration purposed fine-tuning or post-hoc calibration method can be used to
      improve the performance. Other similar methods such as batch calibration (Zhou et al., 2023) has
096
      been proposed as well. More recent studies show that few-shot prompting, finetuning or chain-of-
097
      thoughts can all suffer miscalibration issues (Zhang et al., 2023). In a low shot setting (e.g. < 4
098   demonstration examples), model prediction accuracy and calibration error can both increase and the
099   trade-off can be improved with larger models or more shots (e.g. > 8 shots). Moreover, instruction
100   fine-tuned LLMs exhibit the same miscalibration issue(Jiang et al., 2023a) as well.

151   3.1                             N OTATIONS

153   Denote a classifier as fθ (X) which is parameterized by θ and takes input X ∈ X . fθ (X) is opti-
154   mized on the training data Dtrain = {x1 , y1 , ..., xN , yN } with label Y ∈ Y = {1, 2, ..., K}. We take
155   a calibration point of view and assess whether the label probability generated by a language model
156   can accurately express its confidence level such that the probability estimate P̂ (Y |X) can reflect the
157   actual probability of the prediction being correct (Guo et al., 2017; Jiang et al., 2021; Liusie et al.,
158   2024). Here we use P̂ to represents the probability estimate, which is a vector of probabilities over
159   K labels. The label estimate is derived by taking the argmax such that Ŷ = argmaxY P̂ (Y |X) (Guo
160   et al., 2017; Liusie et al., 2024). For simplicity, we further define p∗ = maxY P̂ (Y |X) = P̂ (Ŷ |X)
161   to represent the probability estimate of the predicted label which is used to represent the confidence
      (Guo et al., 2017) of the label estimate.

       3.2              C ONFIDENCE E STIMATE FOR LLM S
163
164   For an LLM, we extract the token probability for each class Y ∈ Y{1, 2, ..., K} as the probability
165   estimates P̂ (Y |X). For example, in a binary classification setting, we have the label domain to be
166   Y = {0, 1} where 1 indicates ‘Yes’, and 0 indicates‘No’. The probability estimate P̂ (Y = 1|X) is
167   extracted by computing the likelihood of generating label ‘Yes’. More specifically, if the generated
168   sentence is ‘The answer is: Yes.’ and the word ‘Yes’ is tokenized as ‘Yes’. Then we use the
169   probability of token ‘Yes’ after ‘The answer is:’. In the case where a label word has multiple ways
170   of being tokenized, we sum over all possible tokens. 1
171   Now that we have extracted label probability estimate, we then compute the Expected Calibrated
172   Error (ECE) (Guo et al., 2017), which has been widely used to to characterize the calibration level
173   of a model’s confidence (Geng et al., 2023). It is defined as the following,
174                                                               ECE = Ep̂ [|P(Ŷ = Y |P̂ = p) − p|].
175
176   The lower the ECE, the more calibrated the model is. Similarly to many other observations (Xiong
177   et al., 2023; Tian et al., 2023; Liusie et al., 2024; Zhao et al., 2023; Geng et al., 2023; Kamath
178
      et al., 2020), we found that the LLMs’ probability estimates can be miscalibrated (Figure 2, Table
      1). However, while ECE can be generally used to measure whether a model is calibrated, it fails
179
      to distinguish different miscalibration behaviors. For example in Figure 1, two reliability diagrams
180
      have same ECE but have clearly different miscalibration behaviors which we will elaborate more in
181   the next section.

201    3.3              I NDISCRIMINATE M ISCALIBRATION FOR I N -C ONTEXT L EARNING
202
203   In contrast to other models’ miscalibration behavior revealed in Guo et al. (2017), LLM’s miscali-
204   bration can be special such that it’s overconfident and the phenomenon has been reported in several
205   previous studies (Jiang et al., 2021; Tian et al., 2023). In our experiments, we found that regardless
206
      whether the label estimate is correct or not, LLM tends to give equal confidence (e.g.probability esti-
      mate) to its predictions when LM is not trained on the task (e.g. in zero-shot setting). The confidence
207
      is not necessarily high (e.g. overconfident). In certain scenarios, LM can also be underconfident (e.g.
208
      low confidence with high accuracy). More specifically, as shown in Figure 1 (a), the model gives
209   same accuracy for all confidence levels, this is also discussed in Jiang et al. (2021). We report this
210   phenomenon using several classification tasks as an example, shown in Figure 2.
211
212
      We refer to such behavior as indiscriminate miscalibration, a special case of miscalibration , that
      can’t be captured by ECEs. In conventional miscalibration scenario such as the simulated scenario

275   4.1     C OMPARATIVE I NFERENCE
276
277   Considering three samples xi , xj , xk , we are interested in performing a comparison to derive the
278   labels probability estimate P̂ (yi , yj , yk |xi , xj , xk ; C), where C represents a prompting instruction
279   asking for a comparison. The following template is a prompt example,
280
281     (Task Definition) A news description topic can be one of the
282     following four types. ###Types: World, Sports, Business, Sci/Tech.
283
284     (Inputs Samples) For the following 3 news descriptions: ###News
285     Description 1: XXXX. ###News Description 2: XXXX. ###News
286     Description 3: XXXX.
287
288     (Comparison prompt) By comparing them, we know the most suitable
289
        types for each of these 3 news descriptions, respectively, are:
290
291   Note that even though we use words like ‘comparing’, the goal is not to ask LLMs to say if sample
292
      1 is ‘more positive’ than sample 2 or sample 3 as that may change the original task meaning. It’s
      more important to present multiple unlabeled samples in the context and ask LLMs to predict labels
293
      for each of them jointly.

      4.1.1    A SYMMETRIC P ROBABILITIES
296
297   The first thing to notice is that, due to the auto-regressive nature of large language models, we have
298   P̂ (yi , yj |xi , xj ; C) ̸= P̂ (yj , yi |xj , xi ; C). More specifically, if xi appears before xj in the prompt,
299   one would expect yˆi to be generated before yˆj , which means the yˆj is generated conditioned on yˆi .
300   And it can be shown with the following,
301
302                          P̂ (yi , yj |xi , xj ; C) = P̂ (yi |xi , xj ; C)P̂ (yj |xi , xj , yˆi ; C).           (2)
303
      This could lead to bias toward samples generated in later orders. Indeed, ordering of words in
304
      the prompts can impact performance significantly (Lu et al., 2021; Min et al., 2022). In our
305
      experiments, we observed a degradation in inference performance for samples appearing later in
306   the input (Figure 4). Besides, the post-processing and probabilities extractions of inputs after the
307   first prediction can be tricky. Hence, we focus on the prediction of the first input throughout the
308   paper, i.e. P̂ (yi |xi , xj , ...; C) and always put the sample of interest as the first sample to predict.
309   The rest input samples only serve as a reference comparison inputs. Listing the input of interest at
310   the beginning also have a benefit such that if users are only interested in the input label, they don’t
311   need to ask LLMs to generate all rest tokens which can be more efficient.

356   4.2              P OST- HOC C ALIBRATION
357
358   There exist many ways in the literature for calibrating probabilities such as Histogram binning
359   (Zadrozny & Elkan, 2001), Isotonic regression(Zadrozny & Elkan, 2002), Platt scaling (Platt et al.,
360   1999), etc. One simplest approach is temperature scaling (Guo et al., 2017) where we apply weighted
361   softmax on logits or probabilities to recalibrate probabilities. While temperature scaling can de-
362   crease ECE, it does not help with improving model’s classification performance (Guo et al., 2017).
363
      Extensions of Platt scaling towards multi-class classification is referred as vector scaling and matrix
      scaling, where one would optimize the following linear layers on top of the probability estimates,
364
365                                                            P̃ (yi |xi ) = softmax(W P̂ (yi |xi ) + b),
366
367
      where P̃ (yi |xi ) is the calibrated probabilities. In the vector scaling case, W is restricted to a diagonal
      matrix. And W and b are optimized using a validation dataset. Inspired by these methods, we
368
      propose to further calibrate our probability estimates by applying such affine transformations but on
369
      top of different comparison references. More specifically, we retrieve the recalibrated probabilities
370   by,
371
                                                                        J
372                                                                     X
373
                                         P̃ (yi |xi , Dj ; C) = softmax{ {Wj P̂ (yi |xi , {x}j ; C)) + bj },                                                                                                                   (5)
                                                                                             j=1
374
375    where Wj , bj can be estimated via argmax{−yk logP ∗ (yk |xk , {x}j ; C))} with a set of {xk , yk }.
376   This is similar to the principle of contextual calibration used in Guo et al. (2017); Zhao et al. (2021).
377   The difference is that previous studies focused calibrating biases introduced from labels, or other
      prompt contextual information whereas we are focusing on the comparison samples. Note that both

      shown by Figure 2, Figure 3 and also by ECEs in Table 1. Aggregation can also improve F1 and

470   Llama2-13b. We can also expect combining ICL, aggregation and our methods can further enhance
471   performances (Table 1). However, this will require more tailored labels, and inference costs.

      5.4   LLM S C AN C OMPARE A ND C ONTRAST S AMPLES FOR A DJUSTING C ONFIDENCES
474
475
      Previous sections have shown that adding unlabeled input samples in the prompt and asking LLMs
476   to compare them can lead to improved accuracy and calibration. We hypothesize that this is be-
477   cause, in the baseline (e.g., zero-shot) approach, LLMs struggle to differentiate between correct and
478   incorrect predictions due to treating each sample independently during inference. By providing the
479   comparison samples, it enforces model to compare and contrast across multiple variations, which
480   ’regularizes’ and calibrates its confidences. To better verify this, we compare the 0-shot baseline
481   with 0-shot-LF as an ablation experiment and plot the histogram of bootstrapped 2 probabilities of
482   correct prediction and incorrect predictions in Figure 5b. The results show that our comparison-
483   based approach yields more pronounced differences.
484
          2
485         We bootstrapped probabilities from correct prediction and incorrect distribution with the same amount to
      better visualize the histogram as these two categories can be imbalanced.


                                                              9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      Moreover, our findings suggest that the model is indeed comparing and contrasting across different
487   samples. This is particularly evident when looking into the financial dataset, where the contrast
488   result is more pronounced. We attribute this to the fact that the financial task is an ordinal task with
489   ’positive’ and ’negative’ labels, where contrast and comparison naturally becomes more effective
490   (in the sense that it amplifies the differences between prediction probabilities but doesn’t necessarily
491   lead to higher accuracy). With this setup, our approach amplifies the probability differences between
492   correct and incorrect predictions, leading to more outstanding differentiation.
493
494   5.5   C ONSIDERATIONS AND B EST P RACTICES FOR E FFECTIVE C OMPARATIVE I NFERENCE
495
496   First observation is that we can combine LF method with few-shot to further improve performances.
497
      We found performances enhanced across the board except for the Llama2-7b based on averged
      F1 score (Table 1). We hypothesize this is because Llama2-7b is not capable to leverage both
498
      comparative inference and ICL at the same time.
499
500   Secondly, we found that LF comparative inference with ICL are mostly effective with 10-shots
501   setting whereas the independent method perform well with 3-shot for smaller models but better
502   with 10-shot for larger or more capable models (e.g. Llama3-8b or 70b models). For examples,
503
      10-shot comparative inference setting is significantly better than other baselines for Llama2-7b and
      Llama2-13b models on the TREC dataset.
504
505
506   5.6   P OST- HOC C ALIBRATION R ESULTS
507
      As described in section 4.2, vector scaling has K parameters while our post-hoc method will have
508
      J × K parameters. In our experiments, K is generally < 10 and we restrict J = 10, which
509   will restricted the total number of trainable parameters to less than hundreds for vector scaling and
510   thousands for matrix scaling. In addition, the optimizations of W and b are conducted using 200
511   validation samples for all datasets. Similarly, we reported F1 scores and ECE scores averaged across
512   5 datasets for each model in Table 2. We show the breakdowns in the Appendix.
513
      We found that comparative inference with post-hoc calibration is consistently better than baseline
514
      post-hoc calibration methods or the baselines with few-shot prompting for Llama2 family in terms of
515   F1 scores. For Llama3, baseline with few-shot prompting is better, which is likely due to the fact that
516   Llama3 models have strong capability on few-shot prompting. We further show that comparative
517   inference with post-hoc calibration can be combined with few-shot prompting and this will further
518   boost the performance and is consistently better than all other methods in terms of F1 scores. ECEs
519   on average are improved compared to baselines but for many settings are not better than certain
520   methods in the previous setction. As indeed, post-hoc calibration has been recently used to improve
521   classification performances (Zhao et al., 2021; Zhou et al., 2023) instead of focusing on conventional
522   calibration issues.
523
524   6     D ISCUSSION
525
526   In this paper, we study a special miscalibration behavior of large language models when being
527   used for zero-shot and few-shot prompting, which we refer to as indiscriminate miscalibration. We
528   propose metrics to quantify the severity of this issue and develop a label-free in-context comparative
529   inference method to mitigate it. We show our label-free method can achieve better classification
530   performance as well as more calibrated predictions on multiple datasets.
531
532   7     L IMITATIONS
533
534   The primary limitation is that this study only considered classification tasks. Secondly, we consid-
535   ered 5 models but are all from Llama families. While these 5 models are widely recognized for
536   their performance in natural language processing tasks, the inclusion may introduce biases inherent
537   to this particular model family. Future research can benefit from exploring a broader range of tasks
538   and considering models from other families to provide a more comprehensive understanding of their
539   capabilities and limitations. Finally, we did not explore different strategies for selecting comparison
      samples or utilize fine-tuned models, which could be a promising direction for future research.
