027                                                    A BSTRACT
028
029              Generating diverse responses from large language models (LLMs) is crucial for
030              applications such as planning/search and synthetic data generation, where diversity
031              provides distinct answers across generations. Prior approaches rely on increasing
032              temperature to increase diversity. However, contrary to popular belief, we show not
033              only does this approach produce lower quality individual generations as temperature
034
                 increases, but it depends on model’s next-token probabilities being similar to the
                 true distribution of answers. We propose SimpleStrat, an alternative approach
035
                 that uses the language model itself to partition the space into strata. At inference,
036
                 a random stratum is selected and a sample drawn from within the strata. To
037              measure diversity, we introduce CoverageQA, a dataset of underspecified questions
038              with multiple equally plausible answers, and assess diversity by measuring KL
039              Divergence between the sampling distribution and uniform distribution over valid
040              ground truth answers. As computing a posterior probability for proprietary models
041              is infeasible, we measure recall on ground truth solutions. Our evaluation show
042              using SimpleStrat achieves higher recall by 0.05 compared to GPT-4o and 0.36
043              average reduction in KL Divergence compared to Llama 3.

046   1     I NTRODUCTION .
047
048   Large language models (LLMs) are routinely resampled in order to get a wide set of plausible
049   generations. Three key settings where this is important are: 1) improving downstream accuracy with
050   planning or search for agentic tasks (i.e. Tree-of-thought (Yao et al., 2024), AgentQ (Putta et al.,
051   2024)), 2) estimating prediction uncertainty (Aichberger et al., 2024), and 3) generating diverse
052   datasets for post-training (Dubey et al., 2024) and fine-tuning (Dai et al., 2023). All these use cases
053   rely on the model generating multiple plausible generations for the same prompt when multiple
      answers exists.

      Naively, increasing temperature, a parameter that controllably flattens an LLM’s softmax, can
      improve an LLM’s generation diversity. However, temperature introduces two problems. First, higher
070
      temperatures degrades generation quality. Recent evidence suggests removing temperature scaling
071
      is desirable for multi-step reasoning to reduce errors compounding (Zhang et al., 2024). This is
072   especially critical in syntax sensitive settings like code generation where low temperatures (≤ 0.15)
073   are often used. Second, controlling for temperature does not necessarily improve diversity in the
074   answer space. In Figure 1, we illustrate increasing temperature doesn’t lead to meaningful increase
075   in diversity if the model is excessively confident and suffers from mode collapse. When asked to
076   "Name a US State," the model heavily skews towards answering "California", high temperature only
077   marginally softens the skew while surfacing incorrect answers and hurting instruction following.
078   Our goal is to improve diversity when resampling LLMs, even in cases of severe mode collapse
079   in next-token probabilities without manual intervention. Our analysis reveals that GPT-4 assigns
080   87% of its logit weight to "California" when prompted to name a US state. This observed bias
081   can be attributed to the worsening of calibration due to post-training as reported in the GPT-4 tech
082   report (OpenAI et al., 2024). This stark bias mirrors human cognitive bias, exemplified by the
083   blue-seven phenomenon—where individuals disproportionately select blue and seven when asked
084   to choose a random color and number. To counteract similar biases in human populations, social
085   scientists, particularly in political polling, employ stratified sampling techniques (Simpson, 1951;
086
      Howell, 1992; Morris, 2022). We propose adapting this method to address mode collapse in LLMs.
087   We propose SimpleStrat, a training-free sampling approach to increase diversity. SimpleStrat improves
088   LLM generation diversity without degradation to generation quality while ensuring that an LLM’s
089   outputs are aligned with the true distribution of answers. SimpleStrat consist of three stages: auto-
090   stratification, heuristic estimation, and probabilistic prompting. Even if a language model cannot
091   generate diverse solutions, we find that it can be prompted to identify useful partitions of the solution
092
      space based on the user request. We call this process auto-stratification. In Fig. 1, SimpleStrat
      identifies two semantically significant strata from user request, "Name a US State": "(East/West) of
093
      the Mississippi River" and "(North/South) of the Missouri Compromise Line."
094
095   Next, the heuristic estimation computes the joint probabilities across all strata. Back to Fig. 1,
096   SimpleStrat then outputs the probability for all four possible regions in US. Finally, SimpleStrat
097   samples from the joint probability distribution to augment the original user prompt with the selected
098
      stratas. We note that this approach to diversity is orthogonal to increasing temperature and hence
      does not affect generation quality.
099
100   We evaluate SimpleStrat on underspecified questions, specifically questions that have more than one
101   plausible answer. However, unlike ambiguous questions more widely, an answer to an underspecified
102   question can be easily verified to be a valid without additional context. These questions capture
103   settings where the user is indifferent to the particular answer as long as it’s valid or in settings where
104
      we wish to resample to get a set of candidates solutions. We introduce CoverageQA, a benchmark of
      underspecified questions with on average 28.7 equally plausible answers.
105
106   We measure diversity by computing the Kullback-Leibler (KL) Divergence from the Maximum a
107   Posterior(MAP) distribution of answers to a uniform distribution over all valid answers. By computing
      the MAP using next-token probabilities, we show SimpleStrat samples from a less biased distribution.

      2    R ELATED WORK .
125
126   Temperature Scaling. Going back as far as Platt scaling (Platt, 2000) and later applied to neural
127   networks (Hinton, 2015; Guo et al., 2017), temperature scaling controls the randomness of probability
128   distributions1 . For dataset generation with LLMs, Chung et al. (2023) extends temperature-based
129   diversity by additionally downsampling previously sampled tokens. To address the decrease in quality,
130   they advocate for human intervention to manually filter out irrelevant diversity and manually fixing
131   wrong answers in QA tasks. We show in our work temperature scaling leaves much to be desired.
132   Improving Language Model Diversity with Search. In autoregressive generation, choices over
133   early tokens tend to have more impact on the eventual completion. Beam search ameliorates this
134   bias by allowing for multiple candidates in searching for the probability maximizing completion,
135   Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) Lowerre & Reddy (1976). At the end of the search, beam search will
136   have multiple candidate solutions encountered during search. Diverse Beam Search (DBS) proposes
137   introducing an auxiliary dissimilarity objective quantifying the diversity among candidates in the
138   beam (Vijayakumar et al., 2016). Especially on the task of image captioning, DBS shows improve-
139   ment for discovering higher probability completions and discovering diverse continuations. Our
140
      improvements are orthogonal to beam search and our in-context approach corrects for inaccuracies in
      the modeled likelihoods of candidate solutions.
141
142   Other approaches (Samvelyan et al., 2024; Bradley et al., 2023) based on MAP-Elites (Mouret &
143   Clune, 2015) require manual determined dimensions of relevant diversity and discretization of the
144   solution space into equally-sized bins. Diversity is then achieved by mutations and evolutionary
145   methods to cover adjacent bins. This search is potentially slow if the seed set of solutions do not
146
      already provide coverage over the solutions space. Our approach does not need seed solutions and
      avoids manually identifying dimensions of diversity. Instead, we rely solely on capabilities within the
147
      model.
148
149   In-context Methods to Increase Diversity. When LLMs were first introduced, LMs were used to
150   augment existing datasets with more diversity (Wei & Zou, 2019; Ng et al., 2020; Dai et al., 2023).
151   As natural language is difficult to guarantee correctness, the space of augmentations is conservatively
152
      limited to thesaurus based synonym replacement. More recently, Language Model Crossover proposes
      presenting a random subset of existing data points to an LLM and ask it to hallucinate more data
153
      points that likely came from the same distribution Meyerson et al. (2023). This is limited combining
154
      aspects of existing data points into new generations. Although these methods address the limitations
155   of using the model’s token probabilities by in-context learning, they are ineffective at generating
156   meaningful diversity. They are limited to either a human identified domains of interest or trivial
157   variations sourced from synonyms or minicking random subsets of the existing dataset.
158
      Applications of Diversity. As shown by Raventós et al. (2024), dataset diversity is crucial for model
159
      generalization. Below sufficient coverage of the desired task, the model will resort to memorization,
160

163   used for generating synthetic data (Dubey et al., 2024), methods for diversity will be critical. This
164   insight follows from extensive work demonstrating the benefits of data augmentation for bias mit-

      In code and math applications, checking validity efficiently enables more aggressive augmentations.

      One such augmentation for diversifying the languages supported by the model, data is translated to
169   different natural or programming language (Chen et al., 2023; Cassano et al., 2023). In other domains
170   such as images, text-to-image models have been used to do diversify data into uncommon settings. In
171   the setting of diversifying an accumulating dataset, these methods can take advantage of an existing
172   source of variance (for translation) or set of previously generated data points. Our primary focus is
173   on settings where SimpleStrat is unaware of past data samples to support a wider set of applications.
174
      Ambiguous or Underspecified Datasets. ClariQ (Aliannejadi et al., 2020), CLAQUA (Xu et al.,
175   2019), and AmbigQA (Min et al., 2020) focus on assessing LM’s ability to formulate clarifying
176   questions. These question tend to have only 2 candidate solutions, as there exists a ground truth
177   clarifying question whose answer fully specifies the question. Ambiguous Trivia QA (Kuhn et al.,
178   2022) also looks at under-specified questions, but assume a user has contextual information that’s
179   hidden. For instance, "Where in England was she born?" or "Who was the first woman to make a
180   solo flight across this ocean?". We distinguish our underspecified question setting in this paper as
181   one where the user is indifferent. In this setting, the given an answer it should be easy to verify the
182   answer is correct without additional hidden context.
183   Coding datasets like Description2Code (Caballero et al., 2016), Wiki2SQL (Zhong et al., 2017),
184   SPIDER (Yu et al., 2019), code-contest (Li et al., 2022), Apps (Hendrycks et al., 2021), and Leetcode
185   Hard Shinn et al. (2023) admit multiple valid answers. However, the space of valid implementations
186   is infinite, making diversity difficult to measure, and good coding practices enforce preferences
187   among valid implementations. We additionally construct CoverageQA to have an exhaustive list of
188   ground-truth answers in order to measure the impact of diversity on coverage.

194   3.1   W ORKFLOW OVERVIEW
195
196   As illustrated in 2, SimpleStrat consist of three stages, 1) auto-stratification, 2) heuristic estimation,
197   and 3) probabilistic prompting. For each unique user prompt, the outputs of the first two stages can
198   be cached to avoid recomputing feed-forwards.
199
200
201   3.2   AUTO -S TRATIFICATION
202
203
      For a given user request, ruser , we call S, the space of valid solutions. In many settings, the space of
204   potential solutions, S may be naturally partitioned based on geography, parity, or demographics. The
205   partition function, P : S → L, assigns any solution s from S to a partition label lj in L the set of parti-
206   tion labels. Partition functions are most useful if they’re as balanced as possible. A balanced partition
207   function minimizes imbalance(P, L) = maxl∈L (|{s | P (s) = l}|) − minl∈L (|{s | P (s) = l}|).
208   The goal of auto-stratification is to search for a set of partition functions P = {P1 , P2 , ..., Pn }, that
209   are balanced. Traditionally, in settings where there are oft-overlooked or a large or infinite number
210   of valid solutions, stratified sampling can ensure our limited budget of samples covers the space of
211   solutions evenly.
212   Based on this insight, we prompt the language model to identify promising dimensions of diversity.
213   Concretely, the language model proposes good clarifying questions that will potentially eliminate
214   half of the potential solutions based on the user request. These clarifying questions tend to align with
215   semantically significant differences. In the running example, when asked, "Name a US State," the
      states can be partitioned based on East or West of the Mississippi River. See App. C for full prompt.

219   used in forecasting to estimate well-calibrated probabilities of events that have not yet occured. For

278   4.2   C OVERAGE QA-W IKIPEDIA A PPROACH
279
280   To generate CoverageQA-Wikipedia, we leverage the Wikidata knowledge base which contains all
281
      relational mappings between entities and properties in Wikipedia. Our generation process starts with
      an initial item-property pairing and a constraint on the number of correct answers. We then perform a
282
      recursive search through Wikidata to find all sets of item-property constraints and their corresponding
283
      answers that meet our criteria. These constraints are subsequently transformed into natural language
284   questions using GPT-4.
285
286   Consider an initial pairing of the Wikidata item "country" with the property "instance of". We might
287
      specify that we want between 20 and 40 valid answers. Our search would then yield a set of all
      constraints from the knowledge base that fit the initial conditions, such as "instance of country, located
288
      in Europe, uses Euro as currency". GPT-4 would convert this into a natural language question like
289
      "Name a country located in Europe that uses the Euro as its currency."
290
291   This approach has several advantages: 1) it allows us to create a diverse and extensive benchmark
292   that can be easily updated with weekly updates to Wikidata, 2) it allows us to arbitrarily specify the
293
      size of the solution space as constraints can be added or removed to form; and 3) this process in
      principle can curate a large dataset with little manual effort or supervision. In the initial instantiation
294
      of CoverageQA dataset, we publish 105 questions across 4 domains, each corresponding to a different
295
      initial seed item-property pair. To ensure quality, we employ both automatic filters (e.g., excluding
296   certain generic properties) and manual curation to remove redundant or unsuitable questions. This
297   dataset can be substantially expanded as we only used 4 domains, but we leave this for future work.
298   For a details on the dataset breakdown and details on the question generation process, please refer to
299   Appendix A.1.

314   5.2   M EASURING D IVERSITY
315
316   We consider two measures of diversity. For models with accessible softmax next-token probabilities,
317   we compute the a Posterior probability of each solution in the solution space. We then define
318   distributional diversity as the distributional distance between the a Posterior sampling distribution
319   and the ground-truth distribution derived from these probabilities. For CoverageQA, the ground-truth
320
      distribution is uniform over valid solutions and zero elsewhere. In general, it can be more complex.
321   In setting where we do not have access to the next-token distribution, we evaluate diversity by
322   resampling responses to CoverageQA 100 times per question. This allows us to empirically observe
323   the diversity in the form of coverage. We call this coverage diversity. To measure coverage, we report
      the recall (unique valid solutions/total unique valid solutions) on the reference solutions. Note

487
488
      Although SimpleStrat shows improvement empirically, it is sensitive to the model selecting good
489   meaningful axis in auto-stratification and correct joint probabilities in heuristic estimation. As work
490   on LLMs for forecasting improve, we expect LLMs to produce better estimates especially when
491   given access to external data and data analysis tools. For our prototype, we restricted to studying the
492   model’s intrinsic capabilities. Further, the model may have biases concerning race and gender that
493   may be reflected also in the auto-stratification and heuristic estimation. As such, it is recommended
494   the probabilistic prompt distribution of SimpleStrat is carefully inspected for critical applications.
495   Finally, CoverageQA is a dataset of short responses. Although this make evaluation more practical,
496   we believe SimpleStrat will be especially impactful in settings that require low temperature especially
497
      multi-step reasoning as identified by Zhang et al. (2024).

499   7   C ONCLUSION
500
501   In this paper, we propose SimpleStrat which offers an innovative alternative by leveraging the LLM
502   itself to partition the solution space into distinct strata. This process we call auto-stratification. At
503   inference time, a random stratum is selected, and a sample is drawn from within that stratum. This
504   approach achieves better diversity without sacrificing quality as increasing temperature would.
505   To quantitatively measure diversity, we introduced the CoverageQA dataset, which consists of
506   underspecified questions with multiple equally valid answers. We measure diversity with two metrics:
507   for open-source models, we measure distributional difference with KL Divergence and for proprietary
508   models, we measure coverage over the set of ground-truth solutions. Our rigorous evaluation on
509   both proprietary and open-source LLMs demonstrated that SimpleStrat achieves significantly higher
510   recall and produces answer distributions closer to uniform compared to traditional temperature-based
511
      sampling methods.

706   Generation Procedure
707
708   To generate the questions, we manually came up with initial item and property pairings to run the
709
      recursive search. We constrain the recursive search to yield between 20-40 possible answers to keep
      the questions within common and relevant categories. We found that with fewer than 20 answers,
710
      the questions become too obvious, while with more than 40, they tend to get too specific and stray
711
      from general knowledge. The recursive search first finds all items that satisfy the initial conditions,
712   then iteratively adds properties in steps until either the maximum depth (number of constraints) is
713   reached or the number of answers falls outside the desired range. We blacklist properties that are
714   detrimental to high-quality question generation, such as an item’s presence in a specific database,
715   numeric properties like population, and properties that introduce high ambiguity. We then manually
716   evaluate the generated conditions and answers to ensure they meet our criteria. With an appropriate
717   initial condition, one query can generate hundreds of valid constraints that can later be turned into
718   questions. Finally, we use GPT-4 to convert these constraints into natural language.
