009                                                        Abstract
010
011
                   In the field of multi-modal language models, the majority of methods are built on an archi-
012                tecture similar to LLaVA. These models use a single-layer ViT feature as a visual prompt,
013                directly feeding it into the language models alongside textual tokens. However, when dealing
014                with long sequences of visual signals or inputs such as videos, the self-attention mechanism
015                of language models can lead to significant computational overhead. Additionally, using
016                single-layer ViT features makes it challenging for large language models to perceive visual
017                signals fully. This paper proposes an efficient multi-modal language model to minimize
018                computational costs while enabling the model to perceive visual signals as comprehensively
019                as possible. Our method primarily includes: (1) employing cross-attention to image-text
020
                   interaction similar to Flamingo. (2) utilize hierarchical ViT features. (3) introduce the
                   Mixture of Experts (MoE) mechanism to enhance model effectiveness. Our model achieves
021
                   competitive scores on public multi-modal benchmarks and performs well in tasks such as
022
                   image captioning and video captioning.

026   1     Introduction
027
028   Recently, both academia and industry have seen the emergence of numerous outstanding large language
029   models (Brown et al., 2020; Achiam et al., 2023; Anil et al., 2023; Gao et al., 2023; Team et al., 2023; Bai
030   et al., 2023; Team, 2023; Zeng et al., 2022; Young et al., 2024), these models not only reduce the gap to
031   the performance of GPT-4 but also excel across significant benchmarks. These powerful language models
032
      have fostered the development of vision-language models. Today’s vision-language models can handle
      various visual tasks, including object recognition, object localization, OCR (optical character recognition),
033
      and document analysis. These advancements have significantly improved the model’s ability to process
034
      complex visual information and generate accurate textual descriptions.
035
036   Researchers have explored many methods to enhance the perceptual capabilities of vision-language models
037   for details. Some studies (Lin et al., 2023; Jain et al., 2024; Hong et al., 2024; Lee et al., 2024) focus on using
038   multiple visual encoders to enhance the encoding of visual signals, while others (Liu et al., 2024b; Li et al.,
039
      2024b; Liu et al., 2024a; Chen et al., 2024; Dong et al., 2024) aim to improve the model’s perception of small
      objects by increasing image resolution or slicing high-resolution images. These methods have notably boosted
040
      the performance of vision-language models in detail recognition, particularly in tasks such as OCR and
041
      document analysis. Once visual signals are encoded into features, the resulting features often require long
042   token lengths. Attempts to use multiple encoders to extract visual features, increase input image resolution, or
043   slice images, especially when dealing with video data or multiple image inputs, these operations significantly
044   increase the length of visual tokens. In models like LLaVA (Liu et al., 2023a), directly feeding excessively
045   long visual tokens into language models will result in substantial computational overhead. On the other
046   hand, adopting approaches akin to Q-former Li et al. (2023a) for compressing visual features may lead to
047   loss of visual information. Therefore, choosing appropriate strategies to balance computational efficiency
048   and information richness is crucial in designing efficient vision-language models.
049   This paper proposes an efficient visual-language model that adopts a cross-attention mechanism similar to
050   Flamingo (Alayrac et al., 2022) for interaction between visual and textual inputs. Adopting cross-attention
051   ensures that even with long visual tokens, controlling the feature dimensions in cross-attention does not lead
052   to excessive computational overhead. To feed sufficient visual features into the language model, hierarchical

      2       Model Architecture
093
094   Our model architecture is based on Flamingo (Alayrac et al., 2022), primarily consisting of a visual encoder,
095   a large language model, and a Gated Cross Attention Layer. To enable the multi-modal model to capture
096
      more fine-grained visual signals, we extracted hierarchical visual features from different layers of the visual
      encoder and increased the length of Flamingo’s media tokens. Fig. 2 is our model framework diagram.
097
098   Visual Encoder: To enhance our multi-modal model’s visual perception capability, we utilized the 4.4B
099   EVA2-CLIP-E-Plus (Sun et al., 2023) model. In practice, we removed the norm and head layers after the last
100   transformer block. To extract hierarchical visual features, we uniformly sampled 8 feature sequences from
101   the last 40 layers of the transformer and sequentially fed these 8 feature sequences into different Gated Cross
102   Attention layers of Flamingo.
103   Gated Cross-Attetion Layer: Similar to Flamingo, we use gated cross-attention to interact between vision
104   and text. Unlike Flamingo, we replace the media token <image> with a set of learnable tokens of sequence
105   length 16, hoping these learnable tokens can carry visual features similar to Qformer. Because not all text
106   sequences are necessarily related to visual features, we pad a set of all-zero vectors on the visual feature
107   sequence. The attention mask for learnable tokens, text sequences, and visual features is shown in Fig. 2,
      where each set of learnable tokens can only interact with the corresponding image, and text sequences can

181   3     Training
182
183   Our training process consists of three stages: multi-modal pre-training, multi-task continual pre-training,
184   and multi-modal instruction fine-tuning.
185
186
      3.1   Multi-modal Pre-training
187
188
      Our multi-modal pre-training aims primarily at two objectives: 1) Cross-modal alignment of images and
189   text, and 2) Modeling the intrinsic relationships within multi-modal data. We collected a large-scale dataset
190   of image-text captions and web-type multi-modal data based on these objectives. For the image-text caption
191   data, we implemented a data cleaning process to filter out anomalies such as images with unusual aspect
192   ratios and text with repetitive words and to ensure relevance between images and text. We applied relevance
193   filtering similar to MMC4 (Zhu et al., 2024) for web-type multi-modal data to retain highly correlated images.
194   The detailed data processing procedures are documented in the appendix A.1. The Table 1 illustrates the
195   distribution of our pre-training data. We obtained 2.5 billion image-text caption data and 50 million web-type
196   multi-modal data. It is worth noting that 60% of this data consists of Chinese, including a significant amount
197
      of self-built Chinese caption data. This was done to enhance the fine-grained alignment capability of our
      multi-modal model, covering specific visual concepts such as celebrity, landmark building, and dish.
198
199   During model training, we concatenated the caption and multi-modal web-type data separately to ensure
200   each sample had up to 64 images and a sequence length of 2048, resulting in a total of 60 million training
201   samples. In the first 25% phase of training, only the parameters of the Gated Cross Attention Layer were
202   trained. In the subsequent 75% phase, we unfrozen the parameters of the latter half of the Visual Encoder
203   for training. The input image size was 224 × 224 during this phase. The training objective was to minimize
204
      the cross-entropy of the text tokens. We employed a cosine learning rate strategy with a maximum learning
      rate of 6.4e−4 . We completed training on the entire set of 60 million training samples. The detailed training
205
      hyperparameter settings are documented in the appendix B.
206

210   We introduce the multi-task continual pre-training stage between the multi-modal pre-training and instruction
211

212
      stage is still about acquiring ability, not activating ability.
213

      data which are sampled from the first pre-training stage to prevent catastrophic forgetting. The VQA data

      3.3.2     Scaling via Mixture-of-Experts
271
272   In order to achieve better performance, we get more training parameters by scaling the Gated Xaaten Layer.
273   As depicted in Fig 4, we employ a fine-grained MoE architecture. Initially, we replicate the parameters of the
274   FFN of EVLM-Base N times. Subsequently, each replicated FFN is segmented into M fine-grained experts,
275   resulting in a total of N M fine-grained experts. We choose a routing layer that selects the appropriate set of
276   k fine-grained experts to compute the output for the current token. We have set n = 4, m = 4, and k = 4 in
277   our configuration.
278   Drawing from established practices(Dai et al., 2024), we introduce the world expert tasked with learning
279   general knowledge. This expert is involved in the processing of every token. The output from the world
280   expert is then combined with the outputs from the fine-grained experts to derive the final result.
281
      We employ the same training data and configuration of the dense baseline model, and we freeze the LLM
282
      and tune only the cross-attention layers and the last quarter ViT layers. The model obtained at this stage is
283
      called EVLM-MoE.

      4.3     Image Caption
433
434   One of the key capabilities of the Multimodal Large Language Model (MLLM) is DenseCaption of images,
435   which is its most direct application scenario. To enhance MLLM’s performance in this area, we integrate high-
436   quality description data into the Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) dataset based on existing pre-trained models.
437   This enables the MLLM to generate fluent, detailed, accurate, and illusion-free image descriptions. Given the
438   challenges of annotating DenseCaption, including the high cost and inefficiency of manual rewriting, we
439   have designed a comprehensive process for generating high-quality, detailed image description data.
440   The process comprises several key steps:
441
442           1. Multiple Descriptions Generation: Multiple descriptions are generated to ensure comprehensive
443              coverage of all image details.
444
              2. Authenticity Check: These descriptions are split into short sentences for authenticity verification.
445
446
              3. Coherent Description Recombination: The verified sentences are recombined into a coherent
                 description.
447
448           4. Stylization Using GPT-4: Finally, the descriptions are stylized using GPT-4 to ensure they meet
449              specifications and are expressive.
450
451
      Using the data generated by this process, we effectively guided the MLLM’s SFT training, significantly
      enhancing its image description capability to meet or even exceed human satisfaction. This not only improves
452
      MLLM’s performance in practical applications but also provides a robust data foundation for future research
453
      and development.
454
455   Auto Caption Pipeline. To generate high-quality dense descriptions (DenseCaption), we employ various
456   visual language models (VLMs), including self-developed models, internVL, GPT-4V, and GPT-4o. Initially,
457   these models generate image descriptions which are then split into multiple phrases using the Llama2-70B
458
      model.
459   The split phrases are de-duplicated with the help of Llama2-70B, ensuring each phrase is unique. Subsequently,
460   a powerful multimodal large language model (MLLM), such as GPT-4o, checks the authenticity of these
461   phrases, retaining those that accurately match the image details. Following the authenticity check, GPT-
462   4o integrates these phrases into coherent and fluent image descriptions. This step ensures the resulting
463   descriptions are both accurate and low in illusions.
464   To further enhance the detail and completeness of the final image descriptions, we integrate multiple MLLM-
465   generated descriptions. This integration improves the quality of the descriptions by making them more
466   detailed and comprehensive. Finally, we use GPT-4 to refine the linguistic expressions, making the generated
467   image descriptions more fluent, elegant, and consistent with human expression preferences.
468
      Through this multi-step, multi-model synergistic approach, we can generate high-quality, detailed, and
469
      accurate image descriptions, significantly enhancing the application of multimodal macrolanguage modeling.
470
      This method provides robust data support and technical assurance for the development and practical
471   application of multimodal macrolanguage modeling.
472
473   Using the image dense descriptions generated by aforementioned process, we fine-tuned our multi-modal
474   large model. Leveraging our model’s powerful visual feature perception capabilities, as shown in Fig. 6, we
475
      have achieved promising results on the image-dense captioning task. This has notably reduced hallucination
      phenomena in visual descriptions.

      5   Related Work
604
605
      Recently, multimodal large models have garnered increasing attention, with a multitude of notable works
606
      (Alayrac et al., 2022; Li et al., 2023a; Achiam et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023a; Zhu et al., 2023; Dai et al., 2023; Bai
607
      et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2023; Peng et al., 2023) emerging in the field. Most of these studies
608   focus on exploring how to more effectively integrate Large Language Models (LLMs) with other modalities
609   to accomplish multimodal tasks.
610
611   MLLM Input Project Most studies employ visual encoders to extract visual features mapped into Large
612
      Language Models (LLMs). Some approaches Liu et al. (2023a); Chen et al. (2024) directly feed the output of
      visual features through a multilayer perceptron (MLP) and concatenate it with the input of the LLM. Another
613
      method (Zhu et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023a) adopts a transformer-based structure, commonly referred to as
614
      a "q-former," which uses a fixed number of learnable tokens to represent the visual features. Additionally,
615   there are studies Alayrac et al. (2022); Wang et al. (2023) that integrate visual feature outputs into each layer
616   of the LLM, facilitating a deep fusion of modalities.
617
618   MLLM Vision Encoder In the field of multimodal large models, vision models such as CLIP (Radford
619
      et al., 2021; Ilharco et al., 2021), EvaCLIP (Sun et al., 2023), and SigLip (Zhai et al., 2023) are commonly
      used as visual encoders. However, to mitigate the potential for information loss inherent in the features
620
      extracted by CLIP, some studies (Tong et al., 2024) opt to employ an additional vision encoder, such as
621
      DINOv2 (Oquab et al., 2023), which is designed to enhance the feature representation. Furthermore, to
622   capture features at varying resolutions and to accommodate the need for efficient computation, some works
623   integrate a lightweight Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) (He et al., 2016) model.
624
625   MoE The structure of MoE (Mixture of Experts)Jacobs et al. (1991), characterized by sparse activation,
626
      can significantly expand the scale of models or datasets under the same computational resources, thereby
      enhancing model performance. MoE is widely utilized in LLM and MLLM Fedus et al. (2022); Lin et al.
627
      (2024); Dai et al. (2024); Jiang et al. (2023). UpcyclingKomatsuzaki et al. (2022) proposes to train moe from
628
      dense models to reduce training costs. DeepSeek-MoEDai et al. (2024) enhances the specialization of experts
629   through fine-grained MoE. Additionally, there are studiesMcKinzie et al. (2024); Li et al. (2024a) applying
630   MoE in MLLMs to further improve model performance.
631
632   6   Conclusion and Future Work
633
634
      We propose an efficient multimodal visual-language model. We can efficiently handle large-scale image-text
635
      pre-training by leveraging our refined approach to visual inputs. Our model achieves competitive results on

637   can further enhance model performance:
638

      A.1    Caption data and Web-type data
881
      For the caption dataset, we conducted the following data-cleaning processes:
882
883          1. Removed data containing damaged images and solid color images.
884
             2. Removed data with abnormal aspect ratios.
885
886          3. Removed data containing extremely low-resolution images.
887          4. Removed data with text consisting solely of numbers or symbols.
888          5. Removed data with text containing long sequences of digits.
889
             6. Removed data where the text contained duplicate words.
890
891          7. Removed data containing specific terms such as "HTTP", ".com" and ".png" in the text.
892          8. Removed data with an excessively short text.
893          9. Removed data containing date-related text.
894
            10. Converted traditional Chinese characters to simplified Chinese characters.
895
896         11. Utilized the CLIP model to calculate image-text relevance and removed data with low relevance
897
                scores.
898
      For the web-type dataset, we performed the following straightforward processing steps:
899
900          1. Removed data containing damaged images and solid color images.
901
             2. Removed data with abnormal aspect ratios.
902
903          3. Removed data containing extremely low-resolution images.
904          4. Removed data with an excessive number of images in web data.
905          5. Removed data where the text length exceeded 2048 characters.
906
             6. Applied relevance filtering similar to MMC4 (Zhu et al., 2024) to retain highly correlated images.

      A.2    OCR
909
910
      To enhance the OCR capabilities of our model, we have meticulously curated an OCR dataset sourced from a
911   combination of real-world data and synthetic data. The real-world data we gathered includes content from
912   various sources such as videos uploaded to Kuaishou, the Wukong dataset, Common Crawl 2021, street view
913   data, and a plethora of ebooks presented as images to represent authentic scenarios. To ensure the validity of
914   the image-text pairs at the model’s designated resolution, we have implemented the following key steps for
915   processing real-world data:
916
917          1. Employing expert models to extract texts, coordinate boxes, and recognition confidence prob from
                the images.


                                                   17
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


918
           2. Employing image inpainting techniques to eliminate text with characters that are excessively small
919           or possess low recognition confidence.
920
921        3. Filtering out images with inadequate text content.
922        4. Eliminating images that contain redundant text across the entire dataset.
923
924   For the synthetic data component, we have harnessed the power of SynthDog to create a diverse OCR dataset,
925   incorporating the use of LaTeX to produce OCR data with dense text. Throughout the data generation
926   process, we begin by selecting text-free images from Kuaishou videos as backgrounds to simulate real-world
927   scenarios. We then explore a wide array of Chinese and English fonts, encompassing both handwritten and
928   standard styles, to generate text in various formats. Furthermore, we introduce uncommon characters, artistic
929   fonts, and diverse data types to enrich the dataset. To bolster the model’s ability to recognize dense text, we
930   employ LaTeX to generate PDF data containing a higher volume of characters, subsequently converting them
931
      into image-text pairs.
