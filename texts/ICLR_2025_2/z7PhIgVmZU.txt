009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Although open-vocabulary classification models like Contrastive Language Image
012            Pretraining (CLIP) have demonstrated strong zero-shot learning capabilities, their
013            robustness to common image corruptions remains poorly understood. Through
014            extensive experiments, we show that zero-shot CLIP lacks robustness to com-
015            mon image corruptions at increasing severity levels during test time, necessitating
016            the adaptation of CLIP to unlabeled corrupted images using test-time adaptation
017            (TTA). However, we found that existing TTA methods have severe limitations in
018
               adapting CLIP due to their unimodal nature. To address these limitations, we pro-
               pose BAT-CLIP, a bimodal TTA method specially designed to improve CLIP’s
019
               robustness to common image corruptions. The key insight of our approach is
020
               not only to adapt the visual encoders for better image feature extraction but also to
021            strengthen the alignment between image and text features by promoting a stronger
022            association between the image class prototype, computed using pseudo-labels, and
023            the corresponding text feature. We evaluate our approach on benchmark image
024            corruption datasets and achieve state-of-the-art results in TTA for CLIP, specif-
025            ically for domains involving image corruptions. Particularly, with a ViT-B/16
026            vision backbone, we obtain mean accuracy improvements of 9.7%, 5.94%, and
027            5.12% for CIFAR-10C, CIFAR-100C, and ImageNet-C, respectively.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   The emergence of large pre-trained vision-language models (VLMs), such as CLIP (Radford et al.,
033
      2021), has led to their widespread adoption in various visual recognition tasks, including segmen-
      tation (Li et al., 2022; Luo et al., 2023), detection (Bangalath et al., 2022; Lin & Gong, 2023),
034
      classification (Zhou et al., 2022b;a), and image generation (Vinker et al., 2022; Ramesh et al., 2022;
035
      Rombach et al., 2022). Thanks to supervision from massive corpora of paired language and image
036   data, VLMs like CLIP demonstrate strong zero-shot capabilities for these downstream tasks.
037
038   Despite CLIP’s successes in such important applications, its robustness when faced with corrupted
039
      images remains largely underexplored. Our motivation stems from the fact that the vision perception
      system of humans exhibits a level of robustness that real-world vision systems are yet to achieve. For
040
      example, models deployed for safety-critical applications like autonomous driving (Arnold et al.,
041
      2019), could face rapid distributional shifts of blurriness, pixel changes, snowy nights, or other
042   weather conditions (Sakaridis et al., 2021). For instance, our findings on the zero-shot performance
043   of CLIP with a ResNet-101 (He et al., 2016) vision backbone reveals that the accuracy on the test set
044   of CIFAR100 (Krizhevsky et al., 2009) with Gaussian noise of severity level 5, plummets to 10.79%
045   from 49% on the clean set. Similar trends are observed with ViT-B/16, -B/32, and -L/14 (Dosovit-
046   skiy, 2020) as backbones. Behaviors such as these could lead to severe performance degradation of
047   models when faced with image corruption in real-world scenarios.
048   This challenge is not unique to CLIP; it reflects a broader issue in computer vision tasks (Deng
049   et al., 2009; Everingham et al., 2010; Faster, 2015; Chen et al., 2017), which often rely on the
050   assumption that training and test data share the same distribution. When test distributions differ,
051   model adaptation becomes essential to accommodate this shift and maintain accurate predictions.
052   In response to this issue, test-time adaptation (TTA) has garnered significant interest (Wang et al.,
053   2021; Döbler et al., 2023). TTA aims to adapt a pre-trained model to unlabeled batches of test
      data from varying domains in an online fashion—updating the model for each test batch without

073   While there have been few new works on using CLIP for TTA (Shu et al., 2022; Döbler et al., 2024),
074   they come with certain limitations. For example, test-time prompt tuning (TPT) (Shu et al., 2022)
075   tunes the text prompts on the text encoder alone and generates multiple random augmented views,
076   for each test image. The text prompts, initialized to pre-trained values, are optimized by minimizing
077   the marginal entropy of the confident model predictions. The prompts are reset after adaptation to
078   each image. However, such a method is expensive and slow due to performing multiple forward
079   passes through the vision encoder of CLIP, for each image. Also, it relies on hand-crafted prompts
080   for initialization, making it impractical at test-time. Another approach, Vision-Text-Space Ensemble
081
      (VTE) (Döbler et al., 2024), uses an ensemble of different prompts as input to CLIP’s text encoder
      while keeping the encoders frozen. However, since the vision encoder remains frozen, it struggles
082
      to adapt images with severe noise effectively.
083
084   While TPT effectively improves CLIP’s test generalization by dynamically tuning the text prompts,
085   it remains primarily a unimodal approach. This limits the capacity of a multi-modal model like
086   CLIP to fully leverage its multi-modal nature for adaptation. Specifically, it prevents the encoders
087
      from jointly adjusting their features, resulting in suboptimal alignment between the visual and text
      modalities. During TTA, there is no transfer of knowledge, via gradients, between the visual and
088
      text encoders, as the adaptation process focuses solely on one modality. For instance, when a test
089
      image includes common corruptions, the text prompts adapt, but the vision encoder features remain
090   fixed. As a result, the learned prompts lack awareness of the test image distribution, leading to a less
091   effective adaptation.
092
093
      To address these core limitations, we propose, BAT-CLIP, a bimodal adaptation approach of CLIP
      for TTA, where both the visual and text encoders are jointly adapted by exploiting CLIP’s shared
094
      feature space of images and text. The overall objective is to achieve a strong alignment between
095
      image features and text features to enable more effective multi-modal learning and adaptation. The
096   adaptation procedure is two-fold: 1) Similar to TENT (Wang et al., 2021), we adapt the norm layers
097   i.e., LayerNorm layers of both encoders. However, such a model update does not consider the align-
098   ment of the encoder features. So, to improve the alignment between class-specific visual and text
099   features, we introduce a projection matching loss that maximizes the projection of the visual class
100   prototypes with their corresponding text features. 2) To learn more discriminative visual features,
101   we increase the cosine distance between the class prototypes, promoting a more distinct separation
102   in the image feature space. Furthermore, our method is general-purpose, as it does not rely on hand-
103   crafted prompt templates or their ensembles, unlike VTE (Döbler et al., 2024). We leverage batches
104
      of test samples for TTA, rather than focusing on single-image adaptation - making our method fast
      and efficient. In Fig. 7, we show several examples of classification results, as a comparison.
105
106   To the best of our knowledge, the proposed method is the first to perform bimodal test-time adapta-
107   tion (TTA) of CLIP for classification tasks. We draw a comparison of our method against TPT and
      VTE in Fig. 1. Our main contributions are as follows:

122   2   R ELATED W ORKS
123
      Online Test-Time Adaptation (TTA). The objective of TTA is to adapt a pre-trained model, with
124   no access to the source data, to incoming batches of unlabelled test data of a specific domain (Wang
125   et al., 2021; Sun et al., 2020; Schneider et al., 2020; Rusak et al., 2021; Niu et al., 2022; Chen
126   et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2022; Choi et al., 2022). In this approach, the model is reset to its source-
127   domain pre-trained state after adapting to each target domain. As the updates are performed online,
128   TENT (Wang et al., 2021) updates the affine parameters of the normalization layers and minimizes
129   the entropy (Shannon, 1948) of the model predictions. BN Stats Adapt (BN-1) (Schneider et al.,
130   2020) changes only the statistics of the normalization layers with those of the test batch to reduce
131   the covariate shift due to corruptions. RPL (Rusak et al., 2021) argues that self-learning during
132
      adaptation via entropy minimization and pseudo-labels is beneficial. They propose the usage of a
      generalized cross-entropy loss for adaptation. SAR (Niu et al., 2022) filters noisy test samples that
133
      cause a performance drop identified from the gradient space. However, none of these works utilize
134
      CLIP for TTA.
135
136   TTA using CLIP. Lately, CLIP has been finding extensive applications for TTA. TPT (Shu et al.,
137   2022) was the first work to propose prompt tuning using CLIP at test-time. However, this method is
138
      computationally intensive for generating multiple views, per image. Additionally, a key limitation of
      this approach is its reliance on using CLIP’s default hand-crafted template for prompt initialization,
139
      with resets after adaptation to each image. A similar line of work was done in VTE (Döbler et al.,
140
      2024) where ensembles are created in the text and vision space without any CLIP parameter update.
141   Sreenivas & Biswas (2024) focus on single-image TTA for out-of-distribution detection and test
142   generalization (Li et al., 2023; Lee et al., 2023). While TPT is unimodal, our approach is bimodal,
143   involving the joint optimization of both encoders for test adaptation, leading to a strong multi-modal
144   alignment between features. We employ a single generic prompt template, eliminating the need for
145   prompt engineering and making our method more suitable for real-time deployment.

246   3.2   S ENSITIVITY OF CLIP TO PROMPT TEMPLATES
247   In this analysis, we evaluate the impact of prompt engineering by providing “relevant” prompt tem-
248   plates to ftxt for TTA. Each prompt adds context to help CLIP extract more relevant text features.
249   We report the mean classification accuracy (in %) across RN101, ViT-B/16, ViT-B/32, and ViT-L/14
250   backbones at an image corruption severity level of 5 for all datasets, with results summarized in
251   Table 1. The absolute accuracy drop compared to zero-shot performance on the source test set is
252   also reported.
253   Analysis. It is interesting to observe that, irrespective of the backbones used, we do not see any
254   drastic changes in the mean accuracy, for different “relevant” prompt templates. However, the major
255   concern arises in the performance gap of each model and the zero-shot CLIP performance on the
256   corresponding source test set, for the same prompt template. This discrepancy highlights the limited
257   robustness of CLIP’s text encoder ftxt to prompt selection in the context of image corruption. A
258   key reason for this is that, despite the use of “relevant” prompts, the text and visual features remain
259   largely independent and unaware of one another.
260   As expected, RN101 performs worse than the ViT-based backbones, primarily due to its lack of
261   global attention-based modeling inherent to transformers (Vaswani, 2017). Therefore, for the re-
262   mainder of the experiments in this paper, we focus on ViT-based backbones, specifically ViT-B/16
263   and ViT-B/32. Due to GPU memory limitations, we do not use ViT-L/14 for model adaptation.
264   Unsuitability of prompt template selection at test-time. Additionally, at test-time, it is impracti-
265   cal to perform prompt engineering or optimize prompt vectors since 1) Choosing different prompt
266   templates for generating text features is extremely tedious and time-consuming. 2) In real-time
267   deployment involving prompt-tuning, such prompts cannot quickly estimate the distribution of in-
268   coming test batches. TPT (Shu et al., 2022) optimizes pre-trained text prompts for each test image,
269   turning out to be suboptimal since the prompts are optimized ignorant of the distribution of the test

290   4    BAT-CLIP
291
292   The comprehensive analysis in Section 3 reveals that the zero-shot vision encoder fvis of CLIP is
293   very sensitive to image corruption with increasing severity. Similarly, the performance of the text
294
      encoder ftxt is invariant to the different text prompt templates and is also impractical to tune at
      test-time. Therefore, for efficient adaptation, both the image and text features of CLIP need to be
295
      adapted to the incoming domain of test batches. We illustrate our proposed framework, BAT-CLIP,
296
      in Fig. 3.
297
298   From unimodal adaptation to bimodal adaptation. The goal of TTA is to enhance a model’s
299   performance in the current domain to ensure accurate predictions. To handle the complexities of
300
      CLIP adaptation to a specific domain of image corruption, we dissect our analysis of each encoder’s
      adaptation. Consider the unimodal adaptation of the vision encoder fvis via entropy minimization
301
      (Wang et al., 2021). While the image features are adjusted for a specific test batch and domain,
302
      the text features remain fixed, still being optimized for the data from CLIP pre-training, as seen in
303   Fig. 3 (top row). Likewise, if only the text encoder ftxt is updated in this manner, the generated
304   text features may not align properly with the distribution of the incoming data, potentially causing a
305   misalignment between the image and text features.
306
      To benefit from the feature space and learn richer representations across both modalities, we propose
307
      adapting both fvis and ftxt to a domain, enabling an input-aware knowledge transfer between the
308
      encoders and enhancing domain-specific adaptation. While the encoders can be updated based on
309   entropy minimization, they come with inherent limitations. Though entropy models the prediction
310   uncertainty, it does not guarantee increasing the likelihood of alignment between the image and
311   text features. Additionally, due to the image corruption, for images within a test batch, there could
312   be a possible overlap of visual features belonging to different classes (Kurakin et al., 2016). The
313   robustness of CLIP would be challenged in such a case. To address these limitations, we propose
314   two loss components that facilitate more efficient and effective bimodal adaptation of CLIP to new
315   domains at test-time. The losses focus on maximizing alignment between the visual and text features
316   while increasing separation between the visual features to learn good decision boundaries.
317   Projection matching between the visual and text features. We propose learning visual and text

367   For every new task, we reset the model parameters of CLIP following TENT (Wang et al., 2021)
368   since our goal is to adapt to a single domain in an online manner. We use a generic hand-crafted
369   prompt template “a photo of a <CLS>.” at all times, thus avoiding the need for a prompt
370   search. In summary, our bimodal test-time adaptation approach jointly updates the LayrNorm affine
371   parameters of the CLIP encoders that are optimized synergistically through loss components aware
372
      of the input domain, leading to a more robust multimodal learning process.

531
      In this paper, we propose BAT-CLIP, a bimodal online test-time adaptation framework for CLIP
      aimed at handling diverse image corruptions simulating real-time environments (Hendrycks & Di-
532
      etterich, 2019). Our in-depth analysis of CLIP’s zero-shot performance under increasing corrup-
533
      tion severity reveals significant shortcomings in generalization, highlighting the need for effective
534   adaptation. While prior works on TTA for CLIP have predominantly been unimodal focusing on
535   prompt-tuning (Shu et al., 2022) or prompt ensemble (Döbler et al., 2024) with no model updates,
536   our approach encourages learning rich class-separated visual features via vision encoder updates and
537   strengthens alignment between the image class prototype and corresponding text feature via the text
538   encoder updates. Empirical studies, including ablation results, demonstrate the robustness and sub-
539   stantial performance improvements of our approach over existing methods on benchmark corruption
      datasets - CIFAR-10C, CIFAR-100C, and ImageNet-C, through synergistic CLIP encoder updates.

730   A.1   DATASETS
731
      We employ the CIFAR-10C, CIFAR-100C, and ImageNet-C datasets, for our experiments, as
732
      introduced by Hendrycks & Dietterich (2019). Each dataset includes 15 distinct types of image
733
      corruptions, referred to as tasks in a test-time adaptation setting, applied to the test sets of CIFAR10,
734   CIFAR100 (Krizhevsky et al., 2009), and ImageNet (Deng et al., 2009). These corruptions are
735   applied at 5 different severity levels, ranging from mild to severe. For each task, CIFAR-10C and
736   CIFAR-100C contain 10,000 test samples, whereas ImageNet-C has 5000 samples.
737
      The image corruptions are categorized into four primary groups: noise, blur, weather, and digital
738
      distortions. Noise-based corruptions include Gaussian, Shot, and Impulse noise, which introduce
739
      random pixel-level variations. The blur category encompasses Defocus, Glass, Motion, and Zoom
740   blur effects, all of which simulate different types of distorted imagery. Weather-related corruptions,
741   such as Snow, Frost, and Fog, replicate environmental conditions that obscure image details. Lastly,
742   digital distortions include effects like Brightness, Contrast, Elastic Transform, Pixelate, and JPEG
743   compression, which reflect various forms of post-processing or compression artifacts that degrade
744   image quality.
745
      These corruption types, as proposed by Hendrycks & Dietterich (2019), provide a comprehensive
746   framework for assessing model robustness, which has been and is still being studied (Hendrycks
747   & Gimpel, 2016; Metzen et al., 2017; Papernot et al., 2016; Subbaswamy et al., 2021; Liu et al.,
748   2024). Their ability to emulate real-world image degradation scenarios is advantageous, allowing
749   for a more realistic evaluation of a model’s robustness. We provide corruption visualizations, via an
750   image example, in Fig. 6. For further inspection, we urge the readers to check out Hendrycks &
751   Dietterich (2019).

981    A.3.4       P OST- ADAPTATION RESULTS ON SOURCE TEST SETS
982
       Thanks to the natural language supervision and also due to the pre-training on large amounts of
983
       (image, text) pairs, CLIP has shown strong generalization capabilities. However, for an efficient
984
       adaptation to a downstream task, fine-tuning the full model is infeasible due to large model updates.
985    The primary reason is the loss of useful pre-trained knowledge of CLIP, which could eventually
986    lead to overfitting to a downstream task. In our bimodal test-adaptation scheme, BAT-CLIP, we
987    take inspiration from Sreenivas & Biswas (2024) and update only the LayerNorm parameters of
988    the CLIP encoders to a specific corruption task, which makes it parametric-efficient. Now, with
989    continual adaptation to a corruption task, it gets difficult to preserve CLIP’s pre-trained knowledge.
990    Then, a natural question arises -
991
992
        Given that CLIP has been adapted to a specific corruption task, will the zero-shot generalization
                                    still hold back on its source test set?
993
994    In this crucial experiment, we challenge our BAT-CLIP and evaluate its zero-shot generalization
995    performance back on the source test set, to check the preservation of pre-trained CLIP Knowledge.
996    After the adaptation of CLIP on each corruption task, we report the adapted model’s zero-shot
997    performance on its corresponding source test set. We report results for CIFAR-10C and CIFAR-
998    100C in Tables 10 and 11, using ViT-B/16 and ViT-B/32 backbones. For all of the results, we
999    use the prompt template “a photo of a <CLS>.”. As an example, for CIFAR-10C, upon
1000   adaptation of CLIP to Gaussian noise following our approach, we report the adapted model’s zero-
1001   shot accuracy on its source test set - CIFAR10 test set.
1002   From Table 10, we observe that, on average, there is a 2.91% drop in accuracy compared to a zero-
1003   shot evaluation using pre-trained CLIP ViT-B/16. Similarly, for ViT-B/32, we see a drop of about
1004   7.53% in mean accuracy. In Table 11, for CIFAR-100C using a ViT-B/16 backbone, we see an
1005   improvement of 0.76% in mean accuracy.
1006   On the whole, we conclude that since the adaptation for a task happens over multiple test batches,
1007   the zero-shot performance back on the source data largely depends on the distribution of the im-
1008   age corruption. Overall, ViT-B/16 visual backbones preserve larger amounts of CLIP pre-trained
1009   knowledge. This proves the effectiveness of our method BAT-CLIP, on average.
