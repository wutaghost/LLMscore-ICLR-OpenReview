009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Various works have aimed at combining the inference efficiency of recurrent mod-
012            els and training parallelism of MHA for sequence modeling. However, most of
013            these works focus on tasks with fixed-dimension observation spaces, such as in-
014            dividual tokens in language modeling or pixels in image completion. Variably
015            sized, irregular observation spaces are relatively under-represented, yet they occur
016
               frequently in multi-agent domains such as autonomous driving and human-robot
               interaction. To handle an observation space of varying size, we propose a novel
017
               algorithm that alternates between cross-attention between a 2D latent state and
018
               observation, and a discounted cumulative sum over the sequence dimension to
019            efficiently accumulate historical information. We find this resampling cycle is
020            critical for performance. To evaluate efficient sequence modeling in this domain,
021            we introduce two multi-agent intention tasks: simulated agents chasing bounc-
022            ing particles and micromanagement analysis in professional StarCraft II games.
023            Our algorithm achieves comparable accuracy with a lower parameter count, faster
024            training and inference compared to existing methods.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      Spatio-temporal modeling tasks with complex unstructured or semi-structured state and observa-
029
      tion spaces can be identified in a variety of domains. Designing deep learning algorithms to excel
030   at these tasks requires deliberate handling of both the accumulation of knowledge from historical
031   observations, and the summarization of current observations, which can be computationally expen-
032   sive. This is a particular concern in multi-agent domains such as motion prediction and behavior
033   modeling, which have real-time requirements and often need to be performed using relatively low
034   compute resources available on an edge devices. Recurrency is a popular paradigm in deep learn-
035   ing as it naturally maps onto problems which are sequential and causal in nature (Werbos, 1990).
036   While iterative processing of data with recursion can be efficiently performed in O(1) with respect
037   to sequence length L, transformer (Vaswani et al., 2017) and convolution (CNN) methods introduce
038
      compute and memory complexity O(L2 ) and O(L) respectively. However, transformer and CNN
      algorithms are parallelizable at training time, efficiently utilizing hardware and invoking backward
039
      propagation paths uncorrelated with sequence length. This leads to a trade-off between compute
040
      efficiency at training or inference time.
041
042   Proposals to address this trade-off can be grouped into various categories that aim to take advantage
043   of inference efficiency of recurrence and parallelization at training time. One avenue is to address
044
      the O(L2 ) complexity of multi-head attention (MHA) by introducing variations with linearized,
      amortized attention or windowed attention with recurrence (Sun et al., 2023; Peng et al., 2023;
045
      Katharopoulos et al., 2020; Hutchins et al., 2022; Zhai et al., 2021; Didolkar et al., 2022). State space
046
      models (SSMs) (Dao & Gu, 2024; Gu et al., 2022; Fu et al., 2023; Gu et al., 2020) are presented as a
047   compelling alternative to transformers, demonstrating efficacy in a variety of long-range dependency
048   tasks and language modeling. SSMs can be formulated as a convolution for training parallelism or
049   as a recurrent model for inference over long sequences. In each case, these methods are evaluated
050   on sequences with a fixed dimensional observation space O ∈ Rd , such as tokenized text, image
051   pixels, audio spectrogram data and time-series data (Tay et al., 2021; Wu et al., 2021). Focusing on
052   these sequences leaves a blind-spot in tasks with an irregular observation spaces O ∈ Rd(t) such
053   those in as multi-agent interactions, where the number of agents and links between these may vary
      over time (Ettinger et al., 2021; Vinyals et al., 2017).

054
      In this work, we investigate a range of encoding and state space modeling approaches to handle
055   irregular observation settings to provide clarity around the most efficient and best performing ap-
056   proaches. We propose a novel and efficient algorithm that utilizes a 2D latent state and alternates
057   between input sampling, and accumulating historical information as a weighted sum (inclusive scan).
058   This weighted sum can be performed efficiently in parallel on a GPU with an inclusive-scan Merrill
059   & Garland (2016) during training, and incrementally during inference. We show that the resampling
060   cycle is more effective than a continued self-attention block, or not alternating between accumula-
061   tion and processing. This method natively supports a two dimensional latent state. We benchmark
062   against a range of baselines including transformers (Vaswani et al., 2017; Sun et al., 2023), recur-
063
      rent neural networks (RNNs) (Chung et al., 2014; Hochreiter, 1997; Martin & Cundy, 2018), and a
      State-Space Model (SSM) (Dao & Gu, 2024).
064
065   To test the efficacy of these training and inference efficient algorithms on tasks with more complex
066   observation spaces, we use two multi-agent interaction benchmarks. The first is a “gymnasium”
067   style simulation that involves agents chasing randomly assigned particles1 . The second benchmark
068   is based on StarCraft II (SC2), a real-time strategy video game, where we extract instances of
069
      players in combat. Each of these tasks involves a multidimensional time-varying observation space.
      In summary our contributions are as follows:
070
071             • The introduction of two multi-agent interaction challenges to better evaluate sequence mod-
072               eling algorithms with irregular observation spaces.
073             • A novel algorithm to efficiently address sequence modeling tasks with irregular observation
074               spaces. We find that this algorithm achieves comparable accuracy to alternatives with a
075               lower parameter count and improved throughput in training and inference regimes.
076             • Empirically comparing a combination of two encoders and three algorithms to reduce an
077               irregular observation space to a fixed-size amenable for sequence modeling.

      2       BACKGROUND
081
      Efficient and parallelizable sequence modeling is of great interest to the research community, as
082
      attaining efficient utilization of parallel computation at training time while performing inference
083   efficiently are desirable attributes of sequence modeling algorithms. Previously, a trade-off had to
084   be considered between using a model that is more efficient to train, or run inference. Practitioners
085   could choose between O(1) inference efficiency with an RNN, or training parallelism with a CNN
086   or Transformer. With the introduction of SSMs that can be formulated as either a convolution or
087   recurrency, practitioners can take advantage of both efficient incremental inference, and training time
088   parallelism, while also avoiding the O(L2 ) complexity of transformers. While transformers have
089   demonstrated effective adaptability to variety of domains of varying dimensionality and sparsity
090   (Vaswani et al., 2017; Dosovitskiy et al., 2021; Zhu et al., 2022; Yuan et al., 2021), efficient sequence
091
      modeling is often evaluated with a fixed dimensional observation space O ∈ Rd (Tay et al., 2021).
092   Spatio-temporal tasks can be modeled as a state space S with an observation space O that evolves
093   over time according to dynamics model D. In some domains, O is a variably-sized set, Ot , that
094   changes between environment instances, or over the duration of the sequence. This creates chal-
095   lenges in both concisely and effectively compressing a sequence of variably sized observations into
096   some fixed dimensional latent representation of the state of the system St′ := O0...T . This can be
097
      exacerbated if the temporal duration of this task is indefinite as T ∈ N+ .
098   As an example of this class of problem, StarCraft II (SC2) is a real-time strategy game where play-
099   ers build an economy and military in order to defeat the opposing team. Players are given imperfect
100   information, they cannot view parts of the map outside of a line-of-sight dictated by their unit po-
101   sitions. Hence, the observation space of SC2 includes a variably sized set of units and buildings
102   that enter and exit the player line-of-sight as the are built or destroyed. SC2 follows a typical rock-
103
      paper-scissors approach where there are counter-strategies that can be employed against a given
      player. Micromanagement in SC2 games plays an important role in the performance of a player,
104
      and analysis of this can give significant insight into strategy. Novices typically perform a few dozen
105
      actions per minute, whereas professional perform hundreds of actions per minute. These actions are
106   typically taken on variably sized sets of units, buildings, targets or objectives.

126   3       E NVIRONMENTS
127
128   Given the absence of suitable public time series datasets with irregular observations, we introduce
129   a new set of intent recognition (Felip et al., 2022; Ahmad et al., 2016) benchmarks for evaluation.
130   We describe these first, to provide context for the design motivations in Section 4. Each consists of
131
      two sets of agents which interact with one another with some objective. We use these environments
      to test whether models are capable of capturing relevant information from irregular observations of
132
      a complex environment, in order to infer some properties from it.
133
134   The chasing targets environment (Figure 1a) involves an arbitrary number of two-wheeled robots
135   that chase an arbitrary number of particles bouncing around the environment, while trying to avoid
136   colliding with one another. Each robot is randomly assigned a target particle at the beginning of
137
      the simulation and initialized in a random stationary pose. When the robot reaches the particle, it
      is randomly assigned to a new particle to chase. Robots are controlled with a simple cost function
138
      to select the best control inputs that minimizes the robot’s distance to the target’s projected position
139
      and a penalty term if a collision is forecast to occur with another robot. Particles are initialized at
140   random positions and velocities. Here, the goal is to predict which target an agent is chasing, given
141   observations of agent and particle positions and velocities. As a second, more challenging task, the
142   observation space only includes the robots and the model must also predict where chased particles
143   are located in the scene. This is challenging as not only the position of the particles are unknown,
144   but the cardinality must also be estimated. This task is formulated as an occupancy problem, the
145   model needs to estimate the likelihood that a position in the environment is occupied by a particle.
146   The StarCraft II data used is sampled from tournaments hosted between 2019 and 20232 and
147   requires that we predict unit assignment actions based on prior observations of player and enemy
148   units. To focus on battle sequences where players are micro-managing their units in combat, we
149   target parts of the game when damage dealt or received by a player exceeds a threshold. Non-
150   overlapping sequences of a fixed size are created at these instances. We note that observation data
151   is irregularly sampled, hence the time duration of each sequence will vary. This domain has some
152   subtleties compared to Chasing-Targets as units are not always assigned to an enemy unit. They
153   can either be idle, or assigned to a position to move to. Hence, we introduce null option for the
154
      assignment problem. As a second more challenging task, we also consider the case where we are
      required to estimate if a unit has been given a target position command and the location of that
155
      target position. The assignment and the position estimation problem need to be jointly learned and
156
      performed by each model. The observation space for StarCraft II includes a terrain height-map and
157   the position and properties (health, damage, etc.) of player and enemy units. The observation space
158   is restricted to a region-of-interest (ROI) of a fixed size, see Appendix A.7 for detail on how the
159   ROI is calculated. An example of an extracted ROI with unit data and their assignments is depicted
160   in Figure 1b. Units outside of the ROI are truncated, resulting in a time-varying set of units in the
161

234   4.2   BASELINE S PATIO -T EMPORAL E NCODERS
235
236   We empirically evaluate a variety of models from categories mentioned in the Related Work (Section
237   7). As each of these models work on a 1D sequence, individual models process each token from
238   the 2D latent state, with the exception of the spatio-temporal transformer (STT). We evaluate
239   each of the recurrent neural networks included in PyTorch: RNN, GRU and LSTM. We find
240   that a learned initial hidden state performs better than zero initialization (Appendix A.4). Hence,
241   recurrent models use a learned initial state unless otherwise specified. We use Mamba2 (Dao & Gu,
242
      2024) to represent modern SSMs. For Mamba2 specific parameters, we use a state dimension of 64,
      convolution dimension of 4 and an expansion factor of 2.
243
244   We consider three transformer variants for temporal aggregation. The spatio-temporal transformer
245   (STT) processes all the tokens from each time-step in one model. This method is similar to Agent-
246   Former (Yuan et al., 2021), albeit without masking for “agent-aware attention”. The temporal-only
247   transformer (TT) uses individual transformers to process each latent. A learned embedding of the
248
      absolute time-step is added to the input tokens of the aforementioned transformers. We use Ret-
      Net (Sun et al., 2023) as a temporal encoder (no learned time-step embeddings) to represent the
249
      sub-quadratic family of transformers.

252   4.3   S CAN E NCODER
253
      The key design objective of this spatio-temporal encoder is to efficiently aggregate historical infor-
254
      mation with a parallel algorithm, and to use that accumulated knowledge to resample the current
255
      observation. Furthermore, we use a set of tokens to represent our hidden state, rather than a single
256   vector that is common to most algorithms. The motivation behind this is that attention mechanisms
257   can be then utilized effectively with this hidden state, to either inject or extract information from
258   this state. To achieve this, we use a weighted sum of an initially encoded input data from previous
259   steps in the sequence. The accumulation can be performed efficiently in parallel over the sequence
260   dimension with an inclusive scan operation (Merrill & Garland, 2016).
261
      The proposed inclusive-scan algorithm uses the sequential algorithm (Fig. 3c) with X-Attn (Fig.
262   2b) as a recursive query driven sequential modeling approach. Inclusive-scan is performed on the
263   updated variables to accumulate temporal information. When the observation is re-queried in the
264   next layer, it is conditioned on an accumulated history. This process is depicted in Figure 4. For
265   inference, we can calculate the next latent encoding with a scaled copy of the previous time-step
266   L′xt = γ1 L′x(t−1) +Lxt , resulting in O(1) memory and compute complexity with respect to sequence
267   length. We show in Section 5 that cycling between cross-attention and inclusive-scan is superior to
268   a block of cross-attention layers with an inclusive-scan at the end or sampling the input once, and
269   cycling between inclusive-scan and self-attention. We include the number of layers as nomenclature
      for the scan encoder, for example Scan 4× is a scan encoder with four cycles.

451
452   Our benchmark sequences are performed over relatively short time-spans compared to other se-
453   quence modeling benchmarks (Tay et al., 2021). Chasing-Targets accuracy saturates early in the
454   sequence, however we note accuracy decay in some encoders (Fig. 7). SC2 accuracy increases
455
      throughout the sequence. Micromanagement tasks may not elicit long range dependencies to the ex-
      tent of short term ones, with players performing hundreds of actions per minute. There are a number
456
      of multi-agent interaction environments based on real-world observation data of vehicles (Ettinger
457
      et al., 2021; Wilson et al., 2021) and pedestrians (Robicquet et al., 2016). These benchmarks utilize
458   shorter term prediction, often less than 10 seconds and ≤ 10Hz resolution. Furthermore, they often
459   only contain random short sequences (as opposed to a long sequence that is sub-divided for training
460   and evaluating) or are limited in size as motion datasets require costly annotation of agents and their
461   tracks. There may be additional value in the development and benchmarking of the models above on
462   tasks with longer term temporal dependencies and irregular and complex observation spaces. How-
463   ever, we note that Auto-regressive SSM and RNNs are known to perform worse than Transformer
464   and Linear models on long term forecasting benchmarks (Das et al., 2023). Assuming findings in
465   Section 5.2 generalize to long-term forecasting, the Scan Encoder will likely perform similarly.

467   7   R ELATED WORK
468
469   State Space Models (SSMs) like our approach are ubiquitous in sequential modeling tasks requir-
470   ing efficiency. In control and dynamics modeling, they have been applied to learn locally linear
471   latent dynamics models conditioned on exogenous inputs (Watter et al., 2015; Banijamali et al.,
472   2018; Jaques et al., 2021; Levine et al., 2019; Karl et al., 2022; Han et al., 2020). More recently,
473   these models have become popular with endogenous inputs. Here, SSMs parameterize a sequential
474   process with endogenous input u(t) to model the output process y(t) with hidden state x(t),
475                          ẋ(t) = Ax(t) + Bu(t),         y(t) = Cx(t) + Du(t).
476
477
      Parameters (A, B, . . . ) can be approximated in a discrete form (Ā, B̄, . . . ), appropriate for a
      sequence-to-sequence mapping, such that they can be learned with gradient descent. However, these
478
      matrices must be deliberately structured for effectiveness, usually guided by HiPPO theory (Gu
479
      et al., 2020). The key desirability of SSMs is re-parameterization as a convolution to enable parallel
480   computation for training, y = K̄ ∗ u + D̄u, where K = (C̄ Āi B̄)i=0...L−1 for sequence length L.
481   Rather than learning the SSM matrices, Fu et al. (2023) propose learning K̄ directly with various
482   regularization techniques and an efficient long convolution implementation to enable feasibility. Gu
483   & Dao (2023) introduce the notion of time-dependent SSM matrices, similar to an LSTM, adding
484   mechanisms for selectively propagating or forgetting state information dependent on the current to-
485   ken. This is also accompanied by a specialized implementation for performance. In follow up work,
      Dao & Gu (2024) decompose the SSM into diagonal and low-rank blocks for further efficiency.


                                                        9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      Sub-quadratic Transformer Attention has been proposed with various techniques. Linearized
487   Attention is one of the paradigms used to achieve this. Katharopoulos et al. (2020) re-parameterize
488   multi-head self-attention with a non-negative feature map (i.e. elu(x) + 1) applied to K, Q sepa-
489   rately to attain linear time and memory complexity, and enable incremental processing in scenarios
490   with causal masking. RWKV (Peng et al., 2023) utilizes learned position biases, similar to (Zhai
491   et al., 2021), rather than queries from that position. Sun et al. (2023) follows a similar mechanism
492   to our proposed method where a hidden state S is propagated with a decay factor γ and updated
493   with the current input, Sn = γSn−1 + Xn . However, our cyclic scanning and updating conditions
494   the input on the accumulated history, before adding to the time-decaying latent state, as illustrated
495
      in Figure 4. Blocked Attention is another proposed method for avoiding O(L2 ) complexity. How-
      ever, a pure application of blocked attention would omit longer-term dependencies. To address this,
496
      Hutchins et al. (2022) introduce a set of recurrent states for long term context. Two components
497
      of the model run in parallel, one that leans how to gather context temporally (“horizontally”), and
498   another than leans to extract long term information for the current decoding task (“vertically”). Di-
499   dolkar et al. (2022) takes a similar approach, however interleaves long- and short-term information
500   in their “Perceptual Module”, the output of which updates the recurrent information.
501
      Irregular Spatio-Temporal Tasks can be found in many domains, but there has been little to no
502
      focus on efficient temporal modeling with neural networks for this class of problems. Perhaps most
503
      relevant is multi-object tracking, which aims to associate detected objects between frames. Recur-
504   sive processing is popular in end-to-end learning of the multi-object tracking problem (Meinhardt
505   et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2023; Zhu et al., 2022), but this is only suitable when training short-term
506   tracking algorithms on benchmarks with much shorter timescales (Dendorfer, 2020; Dave et al.,
507   2020) than those proposed above. Over a longer horizon, for example in long-term Re-identification
508   tasks, these recursive methods scale poorly, hence further innovation is required. Longer horizon
509   methods (Qin et al., 2023) are typically are not trained end-to-end for this reason. Graph neural
510   networks (GNNs) (Battaglia et al., 2018) are popular architectures for variable dimensional or irreg-
511   ularly structured data due to their permutation invariance. However, these models generally rely on
512
      fixed graph structures for global context, although some approaches attempt to model graph evolu-
      tion graph over time (Pareja et al., 2020; Manessi et al., 2020). This lacks the scalability of state
513
      space models and GNNs are difficult to frame in an incremental manner for inference, typically pro-
514
      cessing the full sequence. For example, Gao et al. (2020) repeatedly construct a graph representation
515   of traffic scenes with agent tracklets and environment context to produce future trajectory estimates.
516
517
518   8   C ONCLUSION
519
520   This work introduces a sequential modeling approach for irregular observation sources of varying
521   dimensionality and cardinality. We investigate the use of various encoders to pack these observations
522   into a fixed dimensional latent, and corresponding sequence modeling approaches. We propose a
523   novel algorithm that alternates between cross attention and a weighted inclusive scan to accumulate
524   context over time and show that this performs on par with existing sequence modeling approaches
525   on two new challenging benchmarks, with comparably higher training and inference speeds and a
526   lower parameter count. A key benefit of the proposed approach is that it can naturally be tweaked
527   to allow a broad range of performance/compute trade-offs (see Appendix A.3 for more extensive
528
      experiments in this vein) as required for a given application.

929   A.5    TASK D ECODING
930
931   In this section, we detail the decoding strategy used for each benchmark task. The foundation of
932
      each task decoder is a cross-attention operation between the ego-agents and latent state to gather the
      temporally aggregated observation information,
933
934                                      O′p = X-Attn(Linear(Op ), L′ ),
935   where the query for MHA is derived from the first argument of X-Attn, and the key-value from the
936   second argument. We note that X-Attn and Linear are learned for each task.
937   Goal Assignment aims to find the correlation between agents and targets. We achieve this by
938   learning a projection of the targets which can be correlated with the agent features,
939
                             O′e = Linear(Oe ), A = CosineSimilarity(O′p , O′e ).
940
941   For the StarCraft II challenge, a learned null token is appended to the set of projected targets. Finally,
942
      the correlation between agents and targets is determined using a dot-product. Soft-max is applied
      along the target dimension to calculate the final assignment score from agent to target. We note that
943
      this is a one-to-many problem, many agents can have the same target, but each agent only has one
944
      target, including a null assignment.
945
946   Negative log likelihood (NLL) is used as a categorical loss between the predicted and the ground
947   truth assignment. However, there are some complications due to the variable agents and targets in
948
      the scene. Invalid agents can simply be omitted from the loss function with a mask. To handle
      invalid targets, we group agents with the same number of targets in the scene and truncate the tensor
949
      to this number. This allows us to apply NLL loss while avoiding gradients from invalid targets and
950
      agents. In the StarCraft II task, there is a significant imbalance between agents with a target and
951   those without. A weighting factor of 0.05 is applied to emphasize non-null assignment losses.
952
953   A Movement Target Assignment task completes the motion action space that units in StarCraft II
954
      can take. Here, the objective is to estimate the coordinates on the map where the unit is moving to.
      This plays an important part of the game as maneuvering units into advantageous positions such as
955
      the high ground or choke-points is an important strategy in game-play. We evaluate several methods
956
      of estimating the target position with both regression and categorical techniques including: global
957   cartesian, relative cartesian and relative polar. The difference between these methods is the number
958   of channels of the final linear layer and the loss used for training. Furthermore, a logit is emitted to
959   estimate the likelihood that the unit is following a position command and position estimate is valid.
960
      L2 loss is used for regression-based position estimation and HL-Gauss (Imani & White, 2018) is
961
      used for categorical-based position estimation. We include additional logic in the HL-Gauss loss to
962
      correct angle wrapping for polar coordinate estimation (Appendix A.6). Binary cross-entropy loss is
963   used for the valid position command logit. Position commands are under-represented in the dataset
964   compared to target commands. To increase the amount of position data, we create pseudo-position
965   commands from target unit coordinates while treating the position logit as false.
966
      Hidden Target Estimation in Chasing-Targets is formulated as an occupancy prediction problem.
967
      An array of coordinates are sinusoidally encoded, P(.), to query the temporal encoding,
968
969                           O(x, y) = Linear(X-Attn(concat.(P(x), P(y)), L′ )),
970   to yield likelihood of hidden target occupancy O at the position (x, y). Focal loss (Lin et al., 2020) is
971   used to address class imbalance between unoccupied and occupied pixels. Although position queries
      are continuous, for simplicity we use a fixed grid.

994    Using a polar coordinate system requires consideration for correct behavior of wrapping θ, espe-

999

1000

1001   targets [−2π, 2π] and checking the original target is reconstructed.

       A.7    S TAR C RAFT II ROI C ALCULATION
1004
1005   The objective of the ROI is to contain the main action happening on the screen, namely the units in
1006   combat. To achieve this, we gather the coordinates of all the units with target or motion commands
1007   over the sequence. K-Means clustering is performed on the positions and sorted by the number of
1008   members in the clusters. If the limits centroids of the K-means clusters can fit within the desired
1009   ROI size, then the mean of the centroids is used for the center of the ROI. If this condition is not
1010   fulfilled, the smallest cluster is removed. This is done iteratively until the condition is satisfied. This
1011
       calculation can be performed live in the dataloader during training, or calculated once and saved a
       key associated with the sample to be re-indexed during training.
