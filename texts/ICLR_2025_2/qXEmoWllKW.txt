009                                                      A BSTRACT
010
011                   Particle methods comprise a wide spectrum of numerical algorithms, ranging from
012                   computational fluid dynamics governed by the Navier-Stokes equations to molec-
013                   ular dynamics governed by the many-body Schrödinger equation. At its core,
014                   these methods represent the continuum as a collection of discrete particles, on
015                   which the respective PDE is solved. We introduce UPT++, a latent point set neural
016                   operator for modeling the dynamics of such particle systems by mapping a par-
017
                      ticle set back to a continuous (latent) representation, instead of operating on the
                      particles directly. We argue via what we call the discretization paradox that con-
018
                      tinuous modeling is advantageous even if the reference numerical discretization
019
                      scheme comprises particles. Algorithmically, UPT++ extends Universal Physics
020                   Transformers – a framework for efficiently scaling neural operators – by novel
021                   importance-based encoding and decoding. Furthermore, our encoding and decod-
022                   ing enable outputs that remain consistent across varying input sampling resolu-
023                   tions, i.e., UPT++ is a neural operator. We discuss two types of UPT++ opera-
024                   tors: (i) time-evolution operator for fluid dynamics, and (ii) sampling operator for
025                   molecular dynamics tasks. Experimentally, we demonstrate that our method reli-
026                   ably models complex physics phenomena of fluid dynamics and exhibits beneficial
027                   scaling properties, tested on simulations of up to 200k particles. Furthermore, we
028
                      showcase on molecular dynamics simulations that UPT++ can effectively explore
                      the metastable conformation states of unseen peptide molecules.

076   The Navier-Stokes equations are the cornerstone of fluid mechanics, and – despite being formulated
077   in the 19th century – continue to present significant challenges to mathematicians and physicists.
078   Most notably, the proof of existence and smoothness of solutions to the Navier-Stokes equations
079   in three dimensions is one of the seven “Millennium Prize Problems” set by the Clay Mathematics
080   Institute (Carlson et al., 2006), with a 1 million prize offered for a solution. What the Navier-Stokes
081
      equations are for fluid mechanics, the Schrödinger equation is for quantum mechanics, i.e., the fun-
      damental building block to describe the behavior of particles at atomic and subatomic scales. Unlike
082
      classical mechanics, which deals with deterministic paths, the Schrödinger equation (Schrödinger,
083
      1926) embraces the probabilistic nature of quantum phenomena, allowing for the superposition of
084   states and the emergence of phenomena like quantum entanglement and tunneling.
085
086   Solving PDEs numerically is also not straightforward, particularly due to the potential for numer-
087
      ical instabilities, which can lead to inaccurate results or convergence issues. A successful class of
      numerical methods to solve certain types of PDEs are particle methods (Pahlke & Sbalzarini, 2023),
088
      which represent the underlying continuum media as a collection of discrete particles. For example,
089
      for many complex phenomena modeled by the Navier-Stokes equations, e.g., free surface dynamics,
090   or multi-phase flows, Lagrangian discretization schemes are prevalent. Lagrangian methods employ
091   finite material points, often termed particles, whose movement aligns with the local deformation
092   of the continuum (Gingold & Monaghan, 1977; Lucy, 1977; Cundall & Strack, 1979; Brackbill &
093   Ruppel, 1986). Similarly, for molecular dynamics, the Born-Oppenheimer approximation (Born &
094   Oppenheimer, 1927) separates the dynamics of electrons and nuclei, which allows one to move the
095   nuclei – when seen as point particles – according to the laws of classical Newtonian dynamics.
096   The discretization paradox. Contrasting numerical discretization schemes and recent advances in
097   deep learning reveal a subtle paradox. While particle-based discretization schemes are often used to
098   model the most complex natural phenomena, deep learning shines at learning continuous representa-
099   tion, e.g., neural fields (Sitzmann et al., 2020; Mildenhall et al., 2021; Xie et al., 2022), at modeling
100   continuous transformation, e.g., diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020) and flow matching (Lipman et al.,
101   2022), and at continuous modulation (Perez et al., 2018; Peebles & Xie, 2023). Naturally, the ques-
102   tion arises, why – if the underlying nature is continuous anyway – we don’t leverage the power of
103   deep learning on continuous representations?
104   Towards this end, we introduce UPT++, which builds on Universal Physics Transformers (Alkin
105   et al., 2024), a latent space neural operator framework for efficiently scaling neural operators to
106   larger physics systems. UPT++ extends Universal Physics Transformers by importance-based en-
107   coding and importance-based decoding schemes, which allows us to convert point cloud represen-
      tations of particle methods into continuous latent space representations. Most importantly, encod-

109   which qualifies UPT++ as a neural operator. The UPT++ operator types we are discussing are time-
110   evolution operators – as used for the time-evolution of the Navier-Stokes equations, and sampling
111   operators – applicable to molecular dynamics simulations.

125   Example: SPH discretization in fluid dynamics.               The incompressible Navier-Stokes equa-
126   tions (Temam, 2001) are defined for the velocity flow field u : X ×[0, T ] → R3 , X ⊂ R3 , and entail
                                                   du
127   momentum and mass conservation, i.e., ρ           = µ∇2 u − ∇p + ρf , and ∇ · u = 0, respectively.
128                                                 dt
      Here, ρ is the density, du/dt is the material derivative, i.e., the rate of change of u of a material
129   element, µ∇2 u is the viscosity, i.e., the diffusion of u modulated by the viscosity parameter µ, p is
130   the pressure, and f an external force. Lagrangian discretization schemes discretize the continuum
131   via finite material points that move according to the local deformation of the continuum. A famous
132   example is given by smoothed particle hydrodynamics (SPH) proposed by Lucy (1977) and Gingold
133   & Monaghan (1977). SPH approximates the field properties using radial kernel interpolations over
134   adjacent particles at the location of each particle. The strength of the SPH method is that it does not
135   require connectivity constraints, e.g., meshes, which is particularly useful for simulating systems
136   with large deformations, complex fluid-boundary interactions, or free surface flows.
137   Example: Nuclei discretization in molecular dynamics. The temporal evolution of quantum sys-
138   tems is governed by the many-body Schrödinger equation, for which analytic solutions are hardly
139   known and are only available for simple systems like free particles or hydrogen atoms. Classical
140   molecular dynamics (MD) enables the simulation of large atomic systems by various approxima-
141   tions reducing the degrees of freedom used to describe the system. A well-known example is the
142   Born–Oppenheimer approximation, where the time-evolving solution is separated into components
143
      for the heavier atomic nuclei and the lighter, faster-moving electrons. Further, the positions of the
      nuclei are updated using numerical integration, where forces on the nuclei are obtained via the nega-
144
      tive gradient of the potential energy given by an approximate solution of the electronic Schrödinger
145
      equation. The potential energy is a parameterized sum of intra- and intermolecular interaction terms;
146   we refer to Appendix B.1 and Frenkel & Smit (2002) for more details on MD. A common applica-
147   tion of MD is for sampling conformation states of biomolecules from the Boltzmann distribution,
148   which is also an active area of research in machine learning (Noé et al., 2019). From ergodic theory,
149   we know that, in most cases, MD generates samples from the Boltzmann distribution when the sim-
150   ulation is long enough (Frenkel & Smit, 2002), which, unfortunately, for biomolecules often means
151   simulating for 1015 integration steps. Data-driven approaches could accelerate this sampling process
152   by either simulating with larger integration steps or directly sampling from the target distribution.
153   Operator learning. The operator learning paradigm (Lu et al., 2019; 2021; Li et al., 2020b;a; Ko-
154   vachki et al., 2021) targets the approximation of mappings between function spaces. Such function
155   spaces can e.g., comprise the solutions of partial differential equations (PDEs). Following Kovachki
156   et al. (2021), we assume U, V to be Banach spaces of functions on compact domains X ⊂ Rdx or
157   Y ⊂ Rdy , mapping into Rdu or Rdv , respectively. Operator learning aims to approximate the ground
158   truth operator G : U → V via Ĝ : U → V, typically framed as a supervised learning problem, where
159   input-output pairs are i.i.d. sampled. However, the notable difference is that in operator learning,
160   the space sampled from is not finite-dimensional. More precisely, with a given data set consisting
161   of N function pairs (ui , vi ) = (ui , G(ui )) ⊂ U × V, i = 1, ...N , we aim to learn Ĝ : U → V, so
      that G can be approximated in a suitably chosen norm.

      A widely adopted approach is to approximate G via three maps (Seidman et al., 2022; Alkin et al.,
163
      2024): G ≈ Ĝ := D ◦ A ◦ E, comprising encoder E, approximator A, and decoder D. In recent
164
      works (Wang et al., 2024; Alkin et al., 2024), the decoder D has been formulated via point-wise
165   queries at the output grid or mesh. In this work, we focus on two instances of the approximator A:
166
167          • Time-evolution operators (Seidman et al., 2022; Alkin et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024),
168            which approximate the deterministic time evolution of a system.
169          • Sampling operators, which are trained to represent the molecular conformation space (Xu
170            et al., 2022; Jing et al., 2022).

172   Neural operators for particle systems. Whereas most state-of-the-art neural operator methods are
173   tailored for grid-based, predominantly regular domains, neural operator formulations for particle- or
174   mesh-based dynamics remain limited. In such cases, graph neural networks (GNNs) (Scarselli et al.,
175   2008; Kipf & Welling, 2017) with graph-based latent space representations offer a promising alter-
176
      native. Often, predicted node accelerations are numerically integrated to simulate the time evolution
      of multi-particle systems (Sanchez-Gonzalez et al., 2020; Mayr et al., 2023; Toshev et al., 2023a).
177
      GNNs inherently possess a strong inductive bias for Lagrangian dynamics, which, however, also
178
      presents a significant downside since the number of nodes, and thus the computational complexity
179   grows with the number of Lagrangian particles. Thus, computational complexity becomes quickly
180   infeasible for an increasing number of particles (Alkin et al., 2024; Musaelian et al., 2023), see Fig-
181   ure 3. Furthermore, the effective degrees of freedom of a particle system are sometimes orders of
182   magnitude less than the degrees of freedom arising from the discretization, especially in simulations
183   showcasing bulk behavior. Lastly, for particle systems, a neural operator formulation is harder, es-
184   pecially with respect to the discretization convergence property (Li et al., 2020a), whereas a neural
185   network is expected to show consistency for increasing input sampling resolutions.

      Deterministic time-evolution: application to fluid dynamics. We consider fluid dynamics prob-
217   lems where the fluid is contained within a domain Ω ⊂ X ⊂ R3 but does not fill the entire domain
218   X . The regions of the domain occupied by the fluid Ω change over time governed by the velocity
219   field of the fluid at each given time. Equivalently, we can consider that we are given two disjoint fluid
220   sets (e.g., air and water) filling the entire domain. Then, we consider the solution state ut to be a
221   compound function, consisting of a velocity field v t and an occupancy field ot , i.e., ut = (v t , ot )T .
222   The velocity field v t maps a certain coordinate x ∈ X to a point-wise velocity at this coordinate, i.e.,
223   v t : X 7→ R3 . The occupancy field ot maps a certain coordinate x ∈ X to whether a fluid particle
224   is at this coordinate or not, i.e., ot : X 7→ {0, 1}. ut is therefore a function ut : X 7→ R3 × {0, 1}.
225   Stochastic conformation sampling: application to molecular dynamics. We assume that a
226   molecule is spatially located in X ⊂ R3 and the specific conformation of a certain molecule is
227   represented by a vector of continuous density fields, i.e., each component of ut represents one
228   specific density field associated with the conformation of the molecule at time t. Each density field
229   represents some specific characteristics of the molecular conformation, i.e., it might, for example,
230   be specific to a certain atom type. We construct each density field analogously to Pinheiro et al.
231
      (2024) and Dumitrescu et al. (2024). Appendix B.2.1 gives further details on the construction of
      the density fields. When we make use of d density fields to represent the overall conformation of a
232
      molecule, then ut is a function ut : X 7→ Rd .

235
            1. How to encode complex point-cloud representations into continuous representations, and
236
               how to decode point-wise from continuous representations?
237
238         2. How to formulate deterministic and sampling operators in the latent space?
239         3. How to efficiently train UPT++?

241   3.1   I MPORTANCE - BASED ENCODING , IMPORTANCE - BASED DECODING
242
243   Importance-based encoding. We introduce importance-based encoding, which consists of four
244
      conceptual steps, see Figure 4. We start with occupancy-based selection, which accounts for the fact
      that in many simulations, disjoint sets fill the entire domain. For example, for many fluid dynamics
245
      phenomena, the state ut is a compound state of a materialized fluid field, and either obstacles or a
246
      second fluid, potentially in the gaseous phase. Similarly, for molecular dynamics, molecules are rep-
247   resented within a box, where not the entire box is filled. Secondly, via importance-based sampling
248   we emphasize information within the occupied regions. For example, a sampling strategy for particle
249   methods could be to upsample denser regions to account for the larger concentration of mass there.
250   Similarly, we sample points around atoms according to density fields consisting of 3D Gaussian
251   density distribution centered at each atomic position. After importance-based sampling, the encoder
252   E first embeds the selected k points into hidden dimension h, adding positional encoding (Vaswani
253   et al., 2017) to the different nodes, i.e., utk ∈ Rk×d → Rk×h . Next, via local aggregation we
254   propagate neighboring information to the respective supernodes (radius graph for connectivity to
255
      keep discretization convergence), and finally global information aggregation pools the information
      into a fixed size and uniform latent space via perceiver blocks (Jaegle et al., 2021a;b). The resulting
256
      continuous latent space contains nlatent latent tokens of dimension h, i.e., z t := E(ut ) ∈ Rnlatent ×h .
257
258   Importance-based decoding.           Importance-based decoding reverses the conceptual steps of
259   importance-based encoding. First, via occupancy decoding (Mescheder et al., 2019), we decode
260   an occupancy field to identify where particles or atoms are located in space. Secondly, we point-
261
      wise decode a field quantity and consider only the occupied points. For example, for fluid dynamics,
      we consider only the flow velocity in regions where occupancy is predicted. Similarly, for molecular
262
      dynamics, we decode whether regions are occupied, and consider our density predictions on those.
263
      Analogous to Alkin et al. (2024), the decoder is implemented via a perceiver-like cross-attention
264   layer using a positional embedding of the output positions as query and the latent representation
265   as keys and values. Since there is no interaction between queries, the latent representation can be
266   queried at arbitrarily many positions without large computational overhead. To train the occupancy
267   field and enable positive and negative learning signals, we sample points at all locations of the do-
268   main. During inference, we simultaneously predict the occupancy field and the corresponding field
269   quantities and only keep the field at occupied points. For the reconstruction of molecular graph
      structures, we adopt a method similar to that of Pinheiro et al. (2024); Dumitrescu et al. (2024),

294   Time-evolution operator. The time-evolution operator, implemented as a transformer (Alkin et al.,
                                         ′
295   2024), A : z t ∈ Rnlatent ×h → z t ∈ Rnlatent ×h , propagates the compressed representation forward

297   applied multiple times, propagating the signal forward in time by ∆t corresponding to each call of
298   the approximator. After inferring one timestep, it is not necessary to decode the latent state and
299   encode it again to compute the result of a further timestep. Instead, in inference mode, we can
300

301
      inference, pay off. However, to enable latent rollouts, the responsibilities of encoder E, approximator
302
      A, and decoder D need to be decoupled, which is further discussed in Section 3.3. It should be
303   mentioned that our integration timestep ∆t is a multiple of the simulation steps used for dataset
304   generation (sometimes up to a factor thousand larger).

      3.4   R ELATED WORK
346
347   In recent years, deep learning has started to make a significant impact in the field of computational
348   fluid dynamics (Guo et al., 2016; Li et al., 2020a; Thuerey et al., 2021; Kochkov et al., 2021; Vinuesa
349   & Brunton, 2022; Gupta & Brandstetter, 2022; Lam et al., 2022; Bi et al., 2022; Brandstetter et al.,
350   2022b;a; Andrychowicz et al., 2023; Bodnar et al., 2024; Herde et al., 2024). Several of those works
351   have applied Transformers to physical systems. Galerkin Transformer (Cao, 2021) uses Galerkin-
352   type attention to address attention complexity, GNOT (Hao et al., 2023) employs linear attention,
353
      OFormer (Li et al., 2023a) uses recurrent MLPs to propagate solutions over time and FactFormer
      (Li et al., 2023b) uses multidimensional factorized attention. However, all these methods apply
354
      attention directly to the input points, which is not scalable in our setting. Transolver (Wu et al.,
355
      2024) reduces the number of tokens by learning a mapping to physics-aware tokens, a concept
356   similar to our use of supernodes. However, their mapping is recomputed in each Transformer layer,
357   whereas we operate within a fixed latent space. OFormer (Li et al., 2023a) also employs a positional
358   embedding combined with a perceiver to query at arbitrary points and perform decoding. However,
359   their approach applies this process directly to the input, followed by a push forward operation,
360   resulting in fixed queries that cannot be altered, thus not suitable for our problem setting. CViT
361   (Wang et al., 2024) is the most similar to our decoding method, but it replaces positional embeddings
362   with learned grid features and uses interpolation to generate the queries.
363   Recent advancements in deep learning have led to an increased interest in its application to
364   molecules. Boltzmann generators (Noé et al., 2019; Köhler et al., 2021) employ flows to draw
365   asymptotically unbiased samples from the Boltzmann distribution, but lack the ability to general-
366   ize across multiple molecules. Unlike UPT++, current approaches apply sampling either in tor-
367   sion (Jing et al., 2022) or Euclidean space (Klein et al., 2024a;b; Midgley et al., 2024). Recent
368   breakthroughs in computer vision using compact latent spaces have achieved high sampling quality
369   (Rombach et al., 2022), suggesting the potential of analogous approaches in the molecular domain.

374
375   We conducted experiments on two material point method (MPM) (Sulsky et al., 1995) datasets for
376   our 2D experiments, namely WaterDrop and WaterDrop-XL from Sanchez-Gonzalez et al. (2020),
377   which consist of a maximum of 1.1k and 7k particles, respectively, see Appendix A.4. Additionally,
      we introduce a new dataset of 3D dam break simulations called DamBreak3D generated using the

      Riemann SPH method (Zhang et al., 2017) and the SPHinXsys library (Zhang et al., 2021). This
379   dataset has between 145k-215k fluid particles and consists of 800/100/100 trajectories of length 250
380   steps, obtained after temporal subsampling at every 100th SPH step, more details in Appendix A.7.
381
382
      Metrics. We evaluate the performance of the models in terms of 1) the intersection over union IoU
      of occupancies and 2) the velocity MSE denoted M SE. To compute these metrics, we evaluate both
383
      occupancies and velocities on a regular grid spanning the full computational domain with a spacing
384
      of around twice the average particle spacing. While these grid values can be directly evaluated by
385   querying the UPT++ decoder, we apply an SPH interpolation to compute them from the dataset and
386   also for the GNN baselines.
387
388
      GNNs for large particle systems. As our main baseline, we choose the established particle-based
      fluid mechanics surrogate GNS introduced by Sanchez-Gonzalez et al. (2020). However, as seen
389
      in Figure 3, such GNN-based approaches do not scale well. To the best of our knowledge, there
390
      are three main directions for scaling GNNs to larger particle systems: A) evaluating subgraphs and
391   combining the solutions (Bonnet et al., 2022), B) limiting the receptive field of the neural network
392   and applying domain decomposition (Musaelian et al., 2023; Kozinsky et al., 2023), and C) using a
393   hierarchy of coarser graphs (Qi et al., 2017; Fortunato et al., 2022; Lino et al., 2022). Regarding A,
394   to cover a mesh with 150k nodes, AirFRANS (Bonnet et al., 2022) evaluates 100 randomly sampled
395   subgraphs of 32k nodes covering the whole domain and averages the outputs on the nodes that
396   have been evaluated multiple times. Regarding B, Allegro (Musaelian et al., 2023) proposes a novel
397   paradigm which, in contrast to message passing, operates on strictly local neighborhoods to allow for
398   straightforward domain decomposition. Although Allegro allows for simulating systems of arbitrary
399
      size, the compute requirement scales linearly with the system size – in a scaling example, Allegro
      distributes 100M atoms over 5k GPUs or roughly 20k atoms per GPU (Kozinsky et al., 2023). Thus,
400
      both A and B approaches have a linear or worse scaling of compute with respect to system size.
401
      As we aim to develop a framework that scales to at least 200k particles (in our experiments), the
402   hierarchical approach C seems most suitable. Thus, to have a competitive baseline, we develop a
403   multi-scale version of GNS, called MS-GNS, which couples the finer (original) particles with coarser
404   particles consisting of randomly subsampled 12.5% of the finer particles. Our approach is inspired
405   by MS-MGN (Fortunato et al., 2022) and connects the two point clouds by a k-nearest neighbors
406   graph with k = 4 from the fine to the coarse nodes, see Appendix A.5 for more details.
407
      Model details. In our experiments, UPT++ encodes the first two velocities of the trajectory and
408   the time-evolution operator acts only in the latent space. In contrast, GNS encodes the first five
409   velocities and then autoregressively predicts and integrates the accelerations. Based on the GNS
410   ablation studies in Sanchez-Gonzalez et al. (2020) and Toshev et al. (2023b), we choose to train
411   a model with 10 message-passing (MP) steps and a latent size of 128, denoted by GNS-10-128 in
412   Table 1. With MS-GNS-15, we denote an MS-GNS model with a processor consisting of: 1 MP
413   layer on the fine particles with a latent size 64, 1 downsampling layer, 11 MP layers on the coarse
414   graph with a latent size 128, 1 upsampling layer, and 1 MP layer as the first one.
415   Results. Our main results are summarized in Table 1, showing that UPT++ performs comparably to
416   GNNs in terms of both IoU and M SE, but can offer more than 50x greater speedups. Notably, we
417   work with the smaller GNS-5-64 model on DamBreak3D as this is the biggest GNS model we could
418   train with one sample per 40GB GPU, compare Figure 3. Qualitatively, the lower IoU of UPT++
419   on the 2D datasets is related to the lack of mass conversation (equivalently volume conservation,
420   as we work with incompressible fluids) in the latent state representation, which manifests itself
421   in having too little or too much fluid along a trajectory. We note that volume conservation is a
422
      problem that GNNs also have, as recently discussed in Neural SPH (Toshev et al., 2024), but in
      contrast to UPT++, GNNs, by construction, preserve the mass, i.e. the number of particles. On the
423
      other hand, UPT++ learns a better representation of the velocity field, which we suspect is easier to
424
      learn as a continuous function. Figure 1 shows one exemplary trajectory rollout of DamBreak3D,
425   demonstrating that UPT++ can adequately model a 3D particle simulation with 200k particles.

519   5   C ONCLUSION , LIMITATIONS AND FUTURE WORK
520
521   We presented UPT++, a generic framework for modeling simulations that are conventionally dis-
522   cretized with particles. UPT++ maps the state of the system to a fixed-sized latent space with novel
523   importance-based encoding and importance-based decoding techniques. The strengths of our ap-
524
      proach are its generic architecture, favorable scaling to larger systems, and potential for significant
      acceleration of numerical simulations. We demonstrated these strengths by training a Lagrangian
525
      fluid dynamics surrogate on up to 200k particles, as well as on molecular conformer generation
526
      across different peptide molecules.
527
528   Amongst others, possible extensions of UPT++ are adoptions to more challenging engineering prob-
529   lems, e.g., complex geometries, solid-liquid interactions, or multi-physics. The fact that the size of
530
      latent representations is constant in UPT++ and, therefore, in principle independent of concrete
      molecule sizes, suggests its application to larger peptides or other larger molecules. One weakness
531
      of a field-based approach like UPT++ is that – by construction – UPT++ is not mass conserving.
532
      This is in contrast to GNNs, which preserve the mass, i.e., the number of particles. Further, for
533   molecular conformation sampling, we decided to incorporate the conditioning conformations via
534   a classifier-free guidance approach since directly conditioning on previous conformations is prob-
535   lematic, as discussed in literature (Li et al., 2024). We leave the exploration of other conditioning
536   strategies to future work. Lastly, improving the decoding scheme could significantly speed up con-
537   former sampling.

      E THICS STATEMENT
541
542
      Accuracy and Reliability of Simulations. While UPT++ offers a computationally efficient alter-
543   native to traditional simulation methods, there is a risk that inaccuracies in the approximations could
544   lead to unintended consequences. For example, in a civil engineering context, designs for flood pro-
545   tection could rely significantly on the accuracy of our simulations. We strongly emphasize that our
546   models should not be blindly relied upon for decision-making in safety-critical areas. Users should
547   always corroborate machine learning predictions with established physical models or additional em-
548   pirical data. Similarly for molecular simulations, the output of our models should be checked with
549   experiments or complemented with classical physical simulations before decision-making.
550   Transparency and Explainability. Given that UPT++ encodes the system’s state into a continuous
551   latent representation, it may offer less transparency and explainability compared to methods that
552   directly operate on the physical state. Our latent space approach can make it difficult for users to
553   fully understand how certain predictions or decisions are reached. Lack of interpretability could lead
554   to challenges in trusting and verifying the model’s outputs, particularly in critical applications. To
555   mitigate these concerns, we advocate for developing methods that enhance explainability and allow
556   users to inspect and understand the underlying decision processes of UPT++, ensuring its safe and
557
      responsible use in real-world scenarios.
558   Environmental and Social Impact. Our models could have significant societal and environmental
559   impacts. For example, in cases like flood prediction or water resource management, inaccurate
560   predictions may lead to poor planning or resource allocation, disproportionately affecting vulnerable
561   communities. We urge that such models be used responsibly, with attention to fairness, inclusivity,
562   and transparency, especially in areas that affect public health, safety, and well-being.

       A.1   S MOOTHED PARTICLE H YDRODYNAMICS (SPH)
976
       In contrast to Eulerian approaches, where discretization of the continuous space is achieved through
977
       spatially fixed finite nodes, control volumes, cells, or elements, Lagrangian methods employ finite
978
       material points, often termed particles, whose movement aligns with the local deformation of the
979    continuum. One of the most prominent Lagrangian discretization schemes is smoothed particle
980    hydrodynamics (SPH), originally proposed by Lucy (1977) and Gingold & Monaghan (1977) for
981    applications in astrophysics. SPH approximates the field properties using radial kernel interpolations
982    over adjacent particles at the location of each particle. The strength of the SPH method is that it does
983    not require connectivity constraints, e.g., meshes, which is particularly useful for simulating systems
984    with large deformations. Since its foundation, SPH has been greatly extended and is the preferred
985    method to simulate problems with (a) free surfaces (Marrone et al., 2011; Violeau & Rogers, 2016),
986    (b) complex boundaries (Adami et al., 2012), (c) multi-phase flows (Hu & Adams, 2007), and (d)
987
       fluid-structure interactions (Antoci et al., 2007). SPH approximates the incompressible Navier-
       Stokes equations (NSE) by the so-called weakly compressible NSE, where the weak compressibility
988
       assumption typically allows for up to ∼ 1% density deviation Monaghan (2005). This ∼ 1% is
989
       enforced for the weakly compressible SPH method while evolving density and momentum:

       The Material Point Method (MPM) is another particle-based technique that represents material as an
       assembly of material points. The motion of each material point is determined by solving Newton’s
1003
       laws of motion. MPM adopts a hybrid Eulerian-Lagrangian scheme, which uses moving material
1004
       points and a fixed computational grid. MPM is particularly useful in the context of large deforma-
1005   tions including fracture and contact scenarios, where traditional mesh-based methods might yield
1006   unrealistic or undesired outcomes due to mesh distortions.

       A.3    I MPLEMENTATION DETAILS
1043
1044   The following outlines the implementation details for UPT++. Table A1 gives a detailed overview of
1045   the hyperparameters used in training. If there is a change in the dimensions between different blocks,
1046   we perform a learnable linear projection. All transformer (Vaswani et al., 2017) and perceiver (Jaegle
1047   et al., 2021b) blocks use standard pre-norm architecture as used in ViT Dosovitskiy et al. (2020)
1048   and are modulated by the timestep using DiT modulation (Peebles & Xie, 2023). We use layer
1049   normalization Ba et al. (2016) in the output of the encoder as well as in the input and output of the
1050   forward operator to always keep the latent representation normalized.
1051   All experiments use the AdamW optimizer (Kingma & Ba, 2015; Loshchilov & Hutter, 2019) and
1052   follow a learning rate schedule that begins with a linear warmup and transitions to cosine decay
1053   (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2017). We perform early stopping and use the best checkpoint in terms of
1054   IoU evaluated on the validation set of each dataset respectively.
1055
       Encoder. First we sample a subset out of all particles and project two consecutive velocities
1056   into a higher dimension using a linear layer. Then we sample nS supernodes from the input point
1057   cloud and perform message passing to points within a radius rS to process the local information. In
1058   message passing, the features of the supernode and the point are concatenated along with a positional
1059   embedding that captures their relative distance. The supernode features are then processed by a
1060   transformer to capture global information. To further reduce the number of tokens in the latent
1061   space, we apply perceiver pooling with learned queries, followed by layer normalization, producing
1062   a normalized latent representation.
1063   Latent space operator. We apply layer normalization to the latent representation before passing
1064   it into the transformer blocks. The output is then added to the original latent representation (after
1065   normalization) and passed through another layer normalization step, ensuring the latent represen-
1066   tation remains consistently normalized throughout the process. The timestep conditioning of the
1067   transformer blocks uses the timestep of the latent representation in the input.
1068   Decoder. The decoder takes the latent representation and processes it with a small Transformer,
1069   which is then fed into two perceiver blocks, one for decoding the state and one for decoding the
1070   occupancy. The positions where we want to query the latent representation are transformed into a
1071   positional embedding and fed through an MLP, resulting in the query used by the perceiver. The
1072   keys and values are the outputs of the Transformer, and the result of the perceiver is projected into
1073   the input dimension of the physical state or into a two-dimensional output for the occupancy.
1074   Timestep modulation.        To incorporate information from the timestep, we encode the current
1075   timestep into a positional embedding. We use the transformer positional encoding Vaswani et al.
1076   (2017); Gupta & Brandstetter (2022), and, therefore rescale all timesteps to the range [0, 200]. The
1077   resulting timestep embedding is then used to perform DiT modulation (Peebles & Xie, 2023) for all
1078   transformer and perceiver blocks. DiT modulation involves applying dimension-wise scaling, shift-
1079   ing, and gating operations to both the attention and MLP modules of the transformer and perceiver
       blocks.

1152   A.5    BASELINES
1153
1154   Our baselines on the Lagrangian fluid dynamics problems are GNS (Sanchez-Gonzalez et al.,
1155   2020) and its multi-scale version MS-GNS. We adopt the Pytorch implementation of GNS from
1156
       github.com/wu375/simple-physics-simulator-pytorch-geometry to our codebase and reuse most
       building block in MS-GNS. In the following, we provide more details about MS-GNS and the train-
1157
       ing hyperparameters.
1158
1159   MS-GNS. To the best of our knowledge, our baseline model MS-GNS is the first multi-scale GNN
1160   for Lagrangian fluid dynamics, and it combines ideas from Fortunato et al. (2022) and our own
1161   importance-based encoder approach. We acknowledge that there are various multi-scale GNNs that
1162
       operate on static objects like point sets (Qi et al., 2017; Lino et al., 2022) or meshes (Fortunato et al.,
       2022; Suk et al., 2023), but because these discretizations are static, one can precompute coarser ver-
1163
       sions thereof using various different algorithms. However, in Lagrangian numerical methods, we
1164
       need to compute a coarse graph at every timestep of the autoregressive rollout evolution, making
1165   advanced algorithms like furthest point sampling (FPS) (Qi et al., 2017) unfeasible – see tested FPS
1166   on DamBreak3D –, taking 10x more time than the model forward evaluation. Thus, for constructing
1167   the coarser graph, we resort to what we do in the sampling-based UPT++ encoding, namely ran-
1168   domly picking a subset of the nodes. The only difference to the encoder is that we do not have a
1169   fixed number of supernodes, but rather a relative ratio of subsampled nodes, which we set to 0.5dim ,
1170   which essentially means that we go to particles with 2x the radius of the finer particles, which also
1171   means that we can just double the cutoff radius for the coarser graph. The obvious disadvantage of
1172   this method is that some fine particles might be far away from the coarser particles, which we rem-
1173
       edy by constructing the mapping from finer to coarser graph using k-nearest neighbors with k = 4
       – 4 basically means that a fine particle sees either its corresponding coarse particle and 3 others, or
1174
       just 4 coarse particles. This way every fine node is guaranteed to get access to information from the
1175
       coarser nodes, and because we subsample the coarse nodes anew at every timestep, the information
1176   propagation is well distributed. Other than the coarse graph generation and the fine-coarse mapping
1177   graph, our approach is almost equivalent to MS-MGN by Fortunato et al. (2022), with the only dif-
1178   ference being that all latent vectors connected with the original finely resolved graph have half the
1179   size of the latent vectors of the coarse graph; this adjustment significantly reduces the memory of
1180   the forward pass. Overall, the message-passing steps that operate only on the fine or coarse graphs
1181   are exactly the GNS layers, and the mapping between the resolutions happens along the same k-NN
1182   graph.
1183   The hyperparameter setting for MS-GNS is one of what Fortunato et al. (2022) found to work well,
1184   namely a simple V-shaped processor scheme which we call MS-GNS-15 consisting of: 1 MP layer
1185   on the fine scale, downsampling layer, 11 MP layers on the coarse graph, upsampling layer, and 1
1186   more MP layer on the fine graph. Regarding the training protocol, we train GNS and MS-GNS with
1187   the same optimizer and learning rate scheduler as our other models. A summary of the hyperparam-
       eters used for training the GNN baselines is given in Table A3, which complements Appendix A.3.

       B.4      M OLECULAR G RAPH R ECONSTRUCTION
1675
1676   We present a systematic procedure for reconstructing the molecular graph, from the predicted den-
1677   sity distribution. Figure B1 provides a visual representation of certain steps.
1678
1679            1. Evaluate positions on our occupancy field and remove all values below a threshold of 0.5
1680               (used throughout our experiments). Note that reconstructing the molecule based solely on
1681               the density channels yields similar results.
1682            2. Peak finding: Identify local maxima in the thresholded occupancy map using a maximum
1683               filter.
1684            3. For each density channel, select the top Nα values, where Nα is the expected number of
1685               atoms of element α in the molecule.
1686            4. Reconstruct bonds 2 using OpenBabel (O’Boyle et al., 2011).
1687
                5. Validate the chemical equivalence of the encoded molecule using InChI codes (Landrum,
