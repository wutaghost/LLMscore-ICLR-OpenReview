009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            We explore model collapse caused by synthetic data, where AI models trained on
012            such data experience a gradual decline in performance. Our initial analysis exam-
013            ines language model pretraining on mixed human and synthetic data, highlighting
014            performance degradation. Further statistical analysis reveals distributional shifts
015            and an over-concentration of n-gram features caused by synthetic data. Inspired
016
               by these insights, we propose token-level editing on human data, to obtain semi-
               synthetic data instead of fully using model outputs. As a proof of concept, we
017
               theoretically demonstrate that token-level editing can prevent model collapse, as
018
               the test error is constrained by a finite upper bound. We conducted extensive ex-
019            periments on pretraining, continual pretraining, and supervised fine-tuning of lan-
020            guage models. The results validate our theoretical proof that token-level editing
021            improves data quality and enhances model performance.

024   1   I NTRODUCTION
025
026   As generative artificial intelligence (AI) such as ChatGPT (Achiam et al., 2023) and Stable Diffu-
027
      sion (Rombach et al., 2021) are now widely used in our daily lives, training next-generation language
      models within an ecosystem of synthetic and human data will be inevitable. How will synthetic data
028
      influence AI training? Recent studies have given rise to two opposing viewpoints: some argue that
029
      synthetic data is the future of AI training, while others claim it leads to model collapse. From a prac-
030   tical perspective, numerous synthetic datasets have been proved to boost the capabilities of language
031   models, like mathematics (Trinh et al., 2024; LI et al., 2024), biomedicine (Zhang et al., 2024),
032   alignment abilities (Ouyang et al., 2022; Cui et al., 2023) and so on. From a theoretical perspective,
033   training models iteratively on their own synthetic outputs results in the continuous accumulation of
034   errors, manifesting as a degenerative process for model learning (Shumailov et al., 2024), i.e., model
035   collapse. Furthermore, model collapse leads to a breakdown of scaling laws, ultimately rendering
036   the incremental computational effort ineffective (Dohmatob et al., 2024b).
037   There are two key questions that require further investigation: (1) Beyond the highly filtered syn-
038   thetic data in post-training, what is the impact of general synthetic data on language model training,
039   and how does it differ from human data? (2) How can we prevent model collapse when synthesizing
040   data, thereby producing higher-quality data?
041
      In this paper, we answer the first question through data mixture pre-training with synthetic and
042   human data, which shows the non-iterative model collapse. Subsequent statistical analysis on dis-
043   tribution and features indicates coverage collapse and over-concentrates n-gram features of syn-
044   thetic data. Based on the above insights, we answer the second question by proposing a token-level
045   editing (ToEdit), which can avoid model collapse in theory and produce high-quality data across
046   experiments, including pre-training, continual pre-training, and supervised fine-tuning in practical.
047
      Remarkable recent works provide a solid foundation for our work. Shumailov et al. (2024); Dohma-
048   tob et al. (2024a) identify the model collapse phenomenon and provide the first theoretical frame-
049   work based on linear regression. Gerstgrasser et al. (2024) demonstrated that if synthetic data is
050   accumulated while retaining the initial real data, the test error will be bounded, thus breaking model
051   collapse. Building on the above frameworks, we prove that our token-level editing can also avoid
052   model collapse. Additionally, Dohmatob et al. (2024b) indicated missing long tails of synthetic data
053   lead to scaling law cutoff, which motivated us to explore data mixture pretraining and statistical
      analysis.

      2.3      P ROPOSED S TRATEGY
243
244   Following these lessons so far, due to the properties of coverage collapse and feature overconcen-
245   tration of synthetic data, our best option is to use totally human data and avoid the inclusion of
246   synthetic data. However, we are still wondering how we can use synthetic data to improve human
247   data. We arrive at a general guideline for synthetic data: full synthetic data will result in model
248   collapse, so we need to keep the main human data distribution. In that case, we propose token-level
249
      editing, which leverages a prior distribution to edit the data. Our method can maintain the source
      distribution and improve the source data, called semi-synthetic data.

257   also obtains better performance.

411
412
413   To validate our proposed method, we conduct experiments across three stages of language model
414   training including: pre-training, continual pre-training (CPT) and supervised fine-tuning (SFT).
415
416   4.1   I MPLEMENTATION
417
418   We use the Llama-3-8B (AI@Meta, 2024) as a prior distribution to estimate the token distribution
419   in each text sample. The modification probability is set to p = 0.99. This means that we resample
420   tokens in positions where the probability exceeds p, and the resampling is based on the conditional
421
      probability given the preceding context. The entire process of our method requires only a single for-
      ward pass, without auto-regressive generation. We integrate the fast inference engine vLLM (Kwon
422
      et al., 2023), allowing the entire data editing process to be completed on a single 4090 GPU. After
423
      completing the data editing, we compared the original data and the edited data on language model
424   training performance across pre-training, CPT, and SFT. Here, we used top-k as the sampling strat-
425   egy with k = 8. We also experimented with top-p and rejection sampling, which produced similar
426   results.

      5     C ONCLUSION
529
530   With the growing prevalence of generative AI models like ChatGPT (Achiam et al., 2023) and Stable
531
      Diffusion (Rombach et al., 2021), when training next-generation AI models, it will be inevitable to
      use a mixture of synthetic data and human data. Therefore, we focus on two key questions: (1)
532
      What is the impact of synthetic data on language model pre-training, and what are the underlying
533
      causes? (2) How can we prevent model collapse and synthesize high-quality data? We found that
534   synthetic data can impair the effectiveness of pre-training when mixed with human data, leading to
535   non-iterative model collapse. Statistical analysis reveals that synthetic data suffers from significant
536   distribution gaps and overly concentrated n-gram features. Based on this, we propose token-level
537   editing instead of relying purely on synthetic data. Specifically, we perform token resampling guided
538   by a trained prior. Moreover, our method can theoretically prevent model collapse. Experimentally,
539   our approach shows improvements over the source data across pre-training, continual pre-training,
      and supervised fine-tuning.

1262   influence future research in the following aspects:
1263
1264   Potential applications of our work: (1) Data optimizations. We can quickly modify and optimize
1265   the current data, using a trained language model with a single forward pass. (2) Regularization in
1266   the data synthesizing process. When synthetic data becomes excessive, we can introduce real data
1267   as an anchor to balance the issues of excessive homogeneity and tail distribution cut-off in synthetic

1274   to inject valuable information into the synthetic data; and second, optimizing methods to reduce
1275   noise during the synthesis process. This approach ensures that synthetic data retains its authenticity
1276   while enhancing its utility in practical tasks.
