009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Masked pre-training with transformers is a popular self-supervised representa-
012            tion learning paradigm, initially showing success in natural language processing
013            (NLP) before moving to computer vision (CV). However, an aspect of masked pre-
014            training that is missing in CV is the ability to capture the distribution of possible
015            outputs. In NLP pre-training methods, the distribution is expressed as a softmax
016            output layer. In CV, the masked autoencoder (MAE) outputs a point estimate of
017            the masked pixels’ RGB values. This formulation is fundamentally limited, as it
018
               models an under-constrained problem as well-posed, leading to difficulties when
               deployed in cluttered scenes, which often contain occluded objects. It only pro-
019
               vides one hypothesis for amodal completion, when in reality, occluded regions
020
               can often be completed in many different ways, e.g., behind an open refrigerator
021            door a scene could contain spoons, cups, pizzas, etc.. This inability to complete
022            multiple modes indicates the weakness of the underlying representation in cap-
023            turing contextual relationships. Towards creating a distributionally-aware formu-
024            lation with contextually-aware representations, we propose the Masked VAE, a
025            transformer-based self-supervised learning method that combines ideas from the
026            MAE and the variational autoencoder (VAE). Additionally, we propose Context-
027            Completion, a benchmark task that uses masked completion to measure the ability
028            of representations to predict multiple potential objects in cluttered scenes. Evalu-
029
               ations show that our method outperforms other methods on Context-Completion,
               while matching state-of-the-art in representation performance in downstream clas-
030
               sification tasks.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035
      Masked pre-training with transformers is a popular self-supervised representation learning
036   paradigm. The idea first showed success in NLP, where masked pre-training methods like BERT
037   (Devlin et al., 2018; Liu et al., 2019; Sanh et al., 2019) produced representations with state-of-the-
038   art results in downstream tasks. This success inspired masked autoencoding (MAE) methods in
039   computer vision (He et al., 2022; Feichtenhofer et al., 2022; Li et al., 2023; Wei et al., 2022), whose
040   learned representations also achieved state-of-the-art results in downstream tasks.
041
      However, the adaptation of masked pre-training to computer vision missed a crucial aspect of the
042   NLP formulation: the ability to capture the distribution of possible outputs. In NLP methods like
043   BERT, the output was a softmax probability distribution over potential token completions (Devlin
044   et al., 2018). However, in vision MAEs, the output was a deterministic point estimate of the masked
045   pixels’ RGB values, instead of a distribution (He et al., 2022). That is, given visible tokens, the
046   MAE always gives the same completion, even though there are often many possibilities. This is a
047   fundamentally limited formulation, as it models this under-constrained problem as well-posed.
048   Standard evaluation methods do not consider the representations’ ability to capture multiple hy-
049   potheses for a given image. While classification accuracy (in which the encoder is used as the clas-
050   sifier backbone) is one way to evaluate representation quality, it does not fully measure the learned
051   contextual relationships. That is, given some objects in an image, we would expect a good repre-
052   sentation to contain some notion of which objects are likely to co-occur. For example, if a partially
053   occluded scene contains a cutting board, its representation should encode the possibility of utensils,
      fruits, etc. Luckily, the masked autoencoding framework gives us a way to evaluate this. After we

054
      pass a partial image through the encoder, we can decode the representation into a full image. If
055   the decoded full image contains contextually plausible inpaintings of the missing parts (e.g., com-
056   pletes the ocean, given a beach), this is evidence that the encoder has learned a contextually-aware
057   representation.
058
      Towards a distributionally-aware formulation, we propose the Masked VAE, a transformer-based
059
      self-supervised learning method that combines ideas from the MAE and the variational autoencoder
060
      (VAE) (Kingma, 2013). Our key insight is to model the latent space tokens corresponding to masked
061   pixels as samples from a multivariate Gaussian distribution, taking after the VAE. But, differently
062   from the VAE, we only model masked tokens with this distribution – visible tokens are modeled
063   deterministically, as in the MAE. Essentially, we replace the originally fixed masked tokens from
064   the MAE with probabilistic latent codes like a VAE. Thus, when our Masked VAE inpaints masked
065   samples given a partial image, it can use the deterministic latent space tokens from the visible por-
066   tion, and sample latent space tokens for the masked portion, thereby allowing it to generate multiple
067   possible completions. To ensure meaningful associations between such samples from the Gaussian
068   distribution and real-space pixel values, during pre-training, we allow another encoder to “peek” at
069
      “masked” image patches and convert them to latent space tokens.
070   Furthermore, towards evaluating the contextual awareness of representations, we propose Context-
071   Completion, a new benchmark task. Given masked images with partially visible context, a trained
072   encoder-decoder method must complete the missing portions. The idea is that a better representation
073   will lead to a distribution of inpaintings that adhere to the context of the scene. This task can be
074   considered a visual analog of the popular cloze tasks in NLP (e.g., MadLibs). When evaluating the
075
      representations in this way, the masked portion should be a contiguous object, rather than randomly
      selected pixels, as a nearest-neighbor inpainting strategy could be reasonably productive in the latter
076
      case, whereas actual contextual knowledge must be leveraged to fill in a block. As such, this task
077
      uses the bounding boxes from the COCO (Lin et al., 2014) dataset. The deterministic nature of the
078   standard MAE most obviously poses problems in these very contextual inference tasks. Ideally, it
079   should produce conditional distributions (which should have entropy) of the hidden image tokens. If
080   we have a partial kitchen scene, the masked-out portion could contain many plausible objects (e.g.,
081   plates, cups, spoons). However, the current deterministic formulation will just inpaint the average
082   pixel values, which are often semantically unmeaningful. A distributionally-aware formulation of
083   the MAE could overcome this limitation by giving multiple semantically plausible inpaintings.
084   We investigate the implications of the design differences between our distributionally-aware Masked
085   VAE and the standard MAE. In particular, we assess each method’s ability in: (1) masked comple-
086   tion, i.e., contextual inference; (2) classification via the self-supervised representations. Our Masked
087   VAE is better at contextual inference, as measured by its ability to capture the conditional distribu-
088   tion of masked objects given context. Regarding the usefulness of the learned representations for
089   classification, our Masked VAE is competitive with the standard MAE, and outperforms other lead-
090   ing methods, as evaluated via end-to-end finetuning. In summary, our contributions are as follows:
091
             • We introduce the Masked VAE, a self-supervised learning method that builds upon the
092
               MAE by introducing distributional-awareness, in line with the ill-posed nature of the
093
               masked completion task.
094
095
             • We propose Context-Completion, a benchmark test of representation quality that uses ob-
               ject inpainting as a way to measure the ability to capture contextual relationships.
096
097          • We investigate the properties of our Masked VAE and find that while distribution-awareness
098            does not seem to be necessary for the representations to be useful for classification, it quali-
099
               tatively and quantitatively helps a great deal in creating contextually plausible completions.
100          • Via end-to-end finetuning, we demonstrate that the representations learned by our Masked
101            VAE are on par with leading methods for classification, without extra inference time cost.

105   2.1   VARIATIONAL AUTOENCODERS
106
107   The variational autoencoder (VAE) is a probabilistic graphical model that can be used for represen-
      tation learning and generative modeling (Kingma, 2013). One of its distinguishing characteristics


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      is the adherence of the latent space to a multivariate Gaussian distribution, enforced by a Kullback-
109   Leibler divergence (KLD) loss (Kullback & Leibler, 1951). Later, Higgins et al. (2017) introduced
110   the beta-VAE, an adaptation of the VAE aimed at learning disentangled representations; this was
111   achieved through the β factor on the KLD loss. We build on these works by adopting the loss
112   function from beta-VAE, but in our formulation not all latent factors are modeled by a multivariate
113   Gaussian distribution.

      2.2   M ASKED P RE -T RAINING
117
      Masked pre-training is a self-supervised learning paradigm in which a sequence, such as a sentence
118
      or an image, is tokenized, with certain tokens being masked out (i.e., invisible). Then, a transformer
119
      model, given the visible tokens, must predict the values of the masked tokens. After this pre-training
120   converges, the representations from the transformer encoder can then be used in downstream tasks
121   (Vaswani, 2017; Devlin et al., 2018; He et al., 2022). This facilitates learning useful representations
122   from vast quantities of unlabeled data, such that we can achieve better performance on downstream
123   tasks with limited compute budgets or amounts of labeled data.
124
      The idea originally gained prominence in the NLP community, with masked pre-training approaches
125
      like BERT achieving state-of-the-art performance in benchmark tasks (Devlin et al., 2018; Liu et al.,
126   2019; Sanh et al., 2019). In the NLP formulation, the objective was to predict a softmax probability
127   distribution over the discrete tokens that had been masked.
128
129
      Later on, the idea was adopted in computer vision. BEiT (Bao et al., 2021) was one of the first such
      proposals; rather than directly optimizing for pixel reconstruction, they used DALL-E’s discrete
130
      VAE (Ramesh et al., 2021) to encode images into discrete token space. After that, they conducted
131
      masked pre-training in the resultant discrete token space. In this discrete token space, they had a
132   softmax objective, thereby achieving a form of distribution-awareness. However, one issue is that
133   training the discrete VAE is non-differentiable, due to the discrete token space. Also, adopting a
134   discrete token space may also not be the ideal formulation for vision problems, since vision data is
135   inherently continuous (subject to machine precision). BEiT v2 (Peng et al., 2022) is a modification
136   to the BEiT that uses a VQ-VAE (Van Den Oord et al., 2017) to generate the discrete token space; it
137   suffers from similar limitations of non-differentiability in training the encoder to discrete space.
138   Later on, fully pixel-based masked autoencoders (MAE) (He et al., 2022; Wei et al., 2022; Feichten-
139   hofer et al., 2022) with vision transformer backbones (Dosovitskiy, 2020) showed great success in
140   representation learning. The objective for such MAE models was to predict the masked RGB pixel
141   values, and did not make use of auxiliary models to convert any tokens to discrete space. At least
142   as it relates to pixel-based methods, one aspect that seems to have been lost in translation from NLP
143   to CV is the distributional awareness. In NLP masked pre-training, the distributional awareness was
144   achieved by providing a softmax probability distribution over the possible tokens. For pixel values,
145   there is not an immediately clear notion of a discrete softmax probability distribution, since pixel
146
      values are inherently continuous. This poses a problem, because one cannot know with certainty
      what the masked value should be. Thus, we hope to build a masked pre-training paradigm for vision
147
      that addresses this.
148
149
150   2.3   S ELF -S UPERVISED V ISION L EARNING
151
152   Besides masked autoencoding, other transformer-based approaches to self-supervised vision learn-
153   ing exist. One of the first was iGPT, which used an autoregressive pixel prediction task for pre-
154   training (Chen et al., 2020a). iGPT’s success was mostly confined to low-resolution images, and
155   did not make use of ViTs (Dosovitskiy, 2020), which were a concurrent work. However, they were
156   able to model the distribution of predicted pixels via discretizing the output space, either through
157   k-means clustering or VQ-VAE (Van Den Oord et al., 2017). Even so, this does not capture the
158
      continuous nature of the vision modality. After ViTs were introduced, DINO (Caron et al., 2021)
      emerged, using a novel label-free knowledge distillation approach. MoCo-v3 (Chen et al., 2021)
159
      used a contrastive approach.
160

      by direct comparison or via a prediction task) between two augmented views of the same image:

      2.4   C ONTEXT-AWARENESS
198
199   We define context as plausible co-occurrence between groups of objects, features, and/or back-
200   grounds. The importance of context in visual representation learning has been long-studied (Tor-
201   ralba, 2003). It is known that neural networks leverage contextual information to make predic-
202   tions (Xiao et al., 2020; Kim et al., 2019; Lovering et al., 2021; Shetty et al., 2019; Winkler et al.,
203   2019; Rosenfeld et al., 2018), and there are many lines of work that attempt to improve the context-
204   awareness of models (Hu et al., 2018; Choi et al., 2011; Malisiewicz & Efros, 2009).
205   Madras & Zemel (2021) presented a formal framework for identifying out-of-context data. Their
206   approach analyzed the co-occurrence statistics of different objects in the same image. To this end,
207   they used the COCO (Lin et al., 2014) dataset, as it already had manually annotated object bounding
208   boxes. We follow this line of work in designing our contextual inference benchmark (Section 4) by
209   analyzing co-occurrence statistics from COCO bounding boxes.

      3.1   D ETERMINISTIC E NCODER ON “V ISIBLE ” T OKENS
217
218   The top branch in Figure 1 is the same as in the standard MAE. The deterministic ViT encoder
219   (Dosovitskiy, 2020) processes the “visible” tokens, outputting token-wise embeddings. These to-
220   kens are then combined with the mask tokens and decoded to reconstruct the original image.
221
222
      3.2   VARIATIONAL E NCODER ON “M ASK ” T OKENS
223
224   Where our method deviates from the original MAE is that rather than having a fixed mask token,
225   we model the mask tokens as samples from a multivariate Gaussian distribution. In principle, taking
226   different samples from the multivariate Gaussian distribution in the latent token space should allow
227   the decoder to construct multiple possible completions of the partial image.
228
      However, it is insufficient to naively replace the traditional mask tokens with Gaussian noise during
229   the pre-training process, because then there would be no association between points in the multivari-
230   ate Gaussian distribution and potential completions of the image. Taking inspiration from the VAE
231   (Kingma, 2013), we add an additional encoder branch that is allowed to “peek” at the “masked”
232   tokens in input pixel space during training time, such that it can meaningfully associate them with
233   points in the latent space. We call this the variational encoder (bottom of Figure 1), and it has the
234   exact same ViT architecture as the deterministic encoder. We add two parallel transformer blocks on
235   top of this variational encoder, one to predict the feature-wise mean of the latent distribution of the
236   mask tokens, and another to predict the feature-wise variance of the latent distribution of the mask
237
      tokens. By doing this, we can enforce a KL-divergence loss (Kullback & Leibler, 1951) on the mask
      tokens to encourage them to come from a Gaussian distribution that is amenable to inference-time
238
      sampling, similar to the procedure for the VAE (Kingma, 2013).
239
240   Empirically, we also found it helpful to have a “fixed” mask token added to the pseudo-“mask”
241   tokens from the variational encoder. This “fixed” mask token is still learnable, but it is fixed in the
242   sense that it is not associated with the input image. We adopt the “fixed” mask token directly from
243
      He et al. (2022)’s implementation.
244   Finally, the variational encoder is only used at training time. At inference time or when transferred to
245   other tasks, it is discarded, such that the model cost is the same as in the original MAE. This allows
246   the model to be applied to masked or unmasked images: the deterministic encoder can process the
247   visible portion in both cases, and for masked images, the unobserved tokens can be replaced with
248   samples from a standard Gaussian distribution before passing through the decoder.

370   we threshold P (i|J ), such that Pi = 1 (P (i|J ) > α). In our experiments we set α = 0.1.
371
      Finally, addressing point (c), there are multiple reasonable ways to do so. We calculate Q by using
372   an off-the-shelf object detector (Faster R-CNN with ResNet50-FPN and V2 weights (Ren et al.,
373   2016; Li et al., 2021) from PyTorch Model Zoo) on the inpainted part of the image only. We do not
374   feed the whole image because we already know the classes present in the visible portion, and only
375   need an automated way to determine which class is present in the inpainted portion. For any class
376   i that is detected with confidence above threshold β in the inpainted region, Qi = 1; otherwise,
377   Qi = 0. We set β = 0.6, and noticed that for many images, no class was detected with high
      confidence, in which case the Qi remained 0.

420
421   Following the setup of He et al. (2022), we evaluate the representation quality of Masked VAE via

423   set (Deng et al., 2009). As noted by He et al. (2022), this is preferable to linear probes for masked
424   autoencoding methods, as the features learned may be strong albeit non-linear. This is especially
425   relevant to the Masked VAE setup: due to the KLD penalty on the “masked” tokens, one could
426   imagine that the training process may also indirectly encourage the “visible” tokens to follow a
427   similar Gaussian distribution, since the decoder must process them all the same way. Accordingly,
428
      it may not be the case that the features are linearly separable, even if they are meaningful.

430   ational Encoder that was pre-trained on the “mask” tokens. We use the hyperparameter settings
431   provided by He et al. (2022)’s official implementation of fine-tuning their normalized pixel variant:
      https://github.com/facebookresearch/mae/blob/main/FINETUNE.md.

691   A.1   BE I T M ODIFICATIONS
692
693
      As mentioned in Section 4.1.2, we had to make non-trivial modifications to BEiT (Bao et al., 2021)
      to enable it to do contextual inpainting in pixel space. Their pre-training task operates in a discrete
694
      token space that is created by DALL-E’s pre-existing discrete VAE (dVAE) tokenizer (Ramesh et al.,
695
      2021) (in some sense making for two layers of autoencoding). Going beyond their original work,
696   we take the extra step of using the dVAE’s decoder to decode the completed image tokens after the
697   original masked completion in token space. Specifically, we take the masked image, pass it through
698   the dVAE encoder, and conduct the masked completion task in token space. We keep the encoded
699   tokens that correspond to visible patches. Then, we simply concatenate the softmax probability
700   distributions of the masked tokens with the visible tokens, and input them to the decoder. This is
701   similar to the method used by Ramesh et al. (2021), although Bao et al. (2021) did not present any
      images generated via this method, to the best of our knowledge.
