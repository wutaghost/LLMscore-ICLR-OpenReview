                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
010
               Inverse generation problems, such as denoising without ground truth observations,
               is a critical challenge in many scientific inquiries and real-world applications.
011
               While recent advances in generative models like diffusion models, conditional flow
012
               matching, and consistency models achieved impressive results by casting gener-
013            ation as denoising problems, they cannot be directly used for inverse generation
014            without access to clean data. Here we introduce Inverse Flow (IF), a novel frame-
015            work that enables using these generative models for inverse generation problems
016            including denoising without ground truth. Inverse Flow can be flexibly applied to
017            nearly any continuous noise distribution and allows complex dependencies. We
018            propose two algorithms for learning Inverse Flows, Inverse Flow Matching (IFM)
019            and Inverse Consistency Model (ICM). Notably, to derive the computationally
020            efficient, simulation-free inverse consistency model objective, we generalized con-
021
               sistency training to any forward diffusion processes or conditional flows, which
               have applications beyond denoising. We demonstrate the effectiveness of IF on
022
               synthetic and real datasets, outperforming prior approaches while enabling noise
023
               distributions that previous methods cannot support. Finally, we showcase appli-
024            cations of our techniques to fluorescence microscopy and single-cell genomics
025            data, highlighting IF’s utility in scientific problems. This work opens up the use of
026            powerful generative models for inversion generation problems.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Recent advances in generative modeling such as diffusion models (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015; Ho
032   et al., 2020; Song & Ermon, 2020; Song et al., 2021; 2022), conditional flow matching models
033   (Lipman et al., 2023; Tong et al., 2024), and consistency models (Song et al., 2023; Song & Dhariwal,
034   2023) have achieved great success by learning a mapping from a simple prior distribution to the data
035   distribution through an Ordinary Differential Equation (ODE) or Stochastic Differential Equation
036   (SDE). We refer to them models as continuous-time generative models. These models typically
037   involve defining a forward process, which transforms the data distribution to the prior distribution
038
      over time, and generation is achieved through learning a reverse process that can gradually transform
      the prior distribution to the data distribution (Figure 1).
039
040   Despite that those generative models are powerful tools for modeling the data distribution, they
041   are not suitable for the inverse generation problems when the data distribution is not observed and
042   only data transformed by a forward process is given, which is typically true for noisy real-world
043   data measurements. Mapping from noisy data to the latent ground truth is especially important in
044
      various scientific applications when pushing the limit of measurement capabilities. This limitation
      necessitates the exploration of novel methodologies that can bridge the gap between generative
045
      modeling and effective denoising in the absence of clean data.
046
047   Here we propose a new approach called Inverse Flow (IF), that learns a mapping from the observed
048   noisy data distribution to the unobserved, ground truth data distribution (Figure 1), inverting the data
049   requirement of generative models. An ODE or SDE is specified to reflect knowledge about the noise
050
      distribution. We further devised a pair of algorithms, Inverse Flow Matching (IFM) and Inverse
      Consistency Model (ICM) for learning inverse flows. Specifically, ICM involves a computationally
051
      efficient simulation-free objective that does not involve any ODE solver.
052
053   A main contribution of our approach is generalizing continuous-time generative models to inverse
      generation problems such as denoising without ground truth. In addition, in order to develop ICM,

079   2.1       C ONTINUOUS - TIME GENERATIVE MODELS
080
081   Our proposed inverse flow framework is built upon continuous-time generative models such as
082   diffusion models, conditional flow matching, and consistency models. Here we present a unified view
083   of these methods that will help connect inverse flow with this entire family of models (Section 3).
084   These generative modeling methods are connected by their equivalence to continuous normalizing
085   flow or neural ODE (Chen et al., 2019). They can all be considered as explicitly or implicitly learning
086   the ODE that transforms between the prior distribution p(x1 ) and the data distribution p(x0 )
087
088                                                                dx = ut (x)dt.                                                            (1)
089
      in which ut (x) represents the vector field of the ODE. We use the convention that t = 0 corresponds
090   to the data distribution and t = 1 corresponds to the prior distribution. Generation is realized by
091   reversing this ODE, which makes this family of methods a natural candidate for extension toward
092   denoising problems.
093
      Continuous-time generative models typically involve defining a conditional ODE or SDE that
094
      determines the p(xt |x0 ) that transforms the data distribution to the prior distribution. Training these
095
      models involves learning the unconditional ODE (Eq. 1) based on x0 and the conditional ODE or
096   SDE (Lipman et al., 2023; Tong et al., 2024; Song et al., 2021) (Figure 1). The unconditional ODE
097   can be used for generation from noise to data.

100
101   Conditional flow matching defines the transformation from data to prior distribution via a conditional
102   ODE vector field ut (x | x0 ). The unconditional ODE vector field vtθ (x) is learned by minimizing
103   the objective (Lipman et al., 2023; Tong et al., 2024; Albergo & Vanden-Eijnden, 2023):
104
105

106
107   where x0 is sampled from the data distribution, and xt is sampled from the conditional distribution
      p(xt | x0 ) given by the conditional ODE.

      The conditional ODE vector field ut (x | x0 ) can also be stochastically approximated through
109   sampling from both prior distribution and data distribution and using the conditional vector field
110   ut (x | x0 , x1 ) as the training target (Lipman et al., 2023; Tong et al., 2024):
111
112
                                            vtθ (xt ) − ut (xt | x0 , x1 ) .                                (3)
113
114
      This formulation has the benefit that ut (x | x0 , x1 ) can be easily chosen as any interpolation between
115   x0 and x1 , because this interpolation does not affect the probability density at time 0 or 1 (Lipman
116   et al., 2023; Tong et al., 2024; Albergo & Vanden-Eijnden, 2023; Albergo et al., 2023). For example,
117   a linear interpolation corresponds to xt = x0 + t(x1 − x0 ) (Lipman et al., 2023; Tong et al., 2024;
118   Liu et al., 2022). Sampling is realized by simulating the unconditional ODE with learned vector field
119   vtθ (x) in the reverse direction.

148   2.1.3   D IFFUSION MODELS
149
      In diffusion models, the transformation from data to prior distribution is defined by a forward diffusion
150
      process (conditional SDE). The diffusion model training learns the score function which determines
151
      the unconditional ODE, also known as the probability flow ODE (Song et al., 2021).
152
153
      Denoising applications of diffusion models Diffusion models are inherently connected to de-
154   noising problems as the generation process is essentially a denoising process. However, existing
155   denoising methods using diffusion models require training on ground truth data (Yue et al., 2023; Xie
156   et al., 2023b), which is not available in inverse generation problems.
157
158   Ambient diffusion and GSURE-diffusion Ambient Diffusion (Daras et al., 2023) and GSURE-
159   diffusion (Kawar et al., 2024) address a related problem of learning the distribution of clean data
160   by training on only linearly corrupted (linear transformation followed by additive Gaussian noise)
161   data. Although those methods are designed for generation, they can be applied to denoising. Ambient
      Diffusion Posterior Sampling (Aali et al., 2024), further allowed using models trained with ambient


                                                            3
162
      diffusion on corrupted data to perform posterior sampling-based denoising for a different forward
163   process (e.g., blurring). Consistent Diffusion Meets Tweedie (Daras et al., 2024) improves Ambient
164   Diffusion by allowing exact sampling from clean data distribution using consistency loss with a
165   double application of Tweedie’s formula. Rozet et al. (2024) explored the potential of expectation
166   maximization in training diffusion models on corrupted data. However, all these methods are restricted
167   to training on linearly corrupted data, which still limit their applications when the available data is
168   affected by other types of noises.
169
170   2.2   D ENOISING WITHOUT GROUND TRUTH
171
172   Denoising without access to ground truth data requires assumptions about the noise or the signal.
173   Most contemporary approaches are based on assumptions about the noise, as the noise distribution is
174   generally much simpler and better understood. Because prior methods have been comprehensively
175
      reviewed (Kim & Ye, 2021; Batson & Royer, 2019; Lehtinen et al., 2018; Xie et al., 2020; Soltanayev
      & Chun, 2018; Metzler et al., 2020), and our approach is not directly built upon these approaches, we
176
      only present a brief overview and refer the readers to Appendix A.3 referenced literature for more
177
      detailed discussion. None of these approaches are generally applicable to any noise types.
178

180   3     I NVERSE F LOW AND C ONSISTENCY M ODELS
181
182   In continuous-time generative models, usually the data x0 from the distribution of interest is given. In
183   contrast, in inverse generation problems, only the transformed data x1 and the conditional distribution
184   p(x1 |x0 ) are given, whereas x0 are unobserved. For example, x1 are the noisy observations and
185
      p(x1 |x0 ) is the conditional noise distribution. We define the Inverse Flow (IF) problem as finding a
      mapping from x1 to x0 which allows not only recovering the unobserved data distribution p(x0 ) but
186
      also providing an estimate of x0 from x1 (Figure 1).
187
188   For denoising without ground truth applications, the inverse flow framework requires only the noisy
189   data x1 and the ability to sample from the noise distribution p(x1 |x0 ). This is thus applicable to any
190   continuous noise and allows complex dependencies on the noise distribution, including noise that can
191
      only be sampled through a diffusion process.
192

      3.2     S IMULATION - FREE I NVERSE F LOW WITH I NVERSE C ONSISTENCY M ODEL
258
      IFM can be computationally expensive during training and inference because it requires solving ODE
259   in each update. We address this limitation by introducing inverse consistency model (ICM), which
260   learns a consistency function to directly solve the inverse flow without involving an ODE solver.
261
262
      However, the original consistency training formulation is specific to one type of diffusion process
      (Karras et al., 2022), which is only applicable to independent Gaussian noise distribution for
263
      inverse generation application. Thus, to derive inverse consistency model that is applicable to
264
      any transformation, we first generalize consistency training so that it can be applied to arbitrary
265   transformations and thus flexible to model almost any noise distribution.
266

497   5   L IMITATION AND C ONCLUSION
498
499   We introduce Inverse Flow (IF), a generative modeling framework for inverse generation problems
500
      such as denoising without ground truth, and two methods Inverse Flow Match (IFM) and Inverse
      Consistency Model (ICM) to solve the inverse flow problem. Our framework connects the family
501
      of continuous-time generative models to inverse generation problems. Practically, we extended the
502
      applicability of denoising without ground truth to almost any continuous noise distributions. We
503   demonstrated strong empirical results applying inverse flow. A limitation of inverse flow is assuming
504   prior knowledge of the noise distribution, and future work is needed to relax this assumption. We
505   expect inverse flow to open up possibilities to explore additional connections to the expanding family
506   of continuous-time generative model methods, and the generalized consistency training objective will
507   expand the application of consistency models.

806   Remark 1: Readers may notice that if q(x0 ) is a point mass, which means the model maps all inputs
807   to a constant, the training objective LIFM will also be minimized, causing the ODE to converge
808   to a trivial solution. However, we find that this trivial solution can be avoided by our design.
809   Specifically, this is because Our approach can be regarded as an optimization process based on
      expectation-maximization (EM):


                                                            15
810
            1. Expectation: We generate a denoised dataset given noisy inputs, x0 ∼ q(x0 |x1 ).
811
812
            2. Maximization: We optimize our IFM/ICM models based on the generated dataset x0 and
               the conditional noise distribution p(x1 |x0 ).
813
814   The choice of the initial prior, which is the initial denoised dataset in our case, is crucial for the
815   EM algorithm. While any initial prior may lead to a local optimum (Wu, 1983; Balakrishnan et al.,
816   2014; McLachlan & Krishnan, 2008), an informed initial prior can prevent convergence to a trivial
817   solution. Our model architecture incorporates the residual connection from consistency models,
818   ensuring that the initial outputs of the model closely resemble the inputs. This design effectively
819   avoids convergence to the trivial solution. In additional experiments (Appendix A.5.1), we further
820   demonstrate that our method is able to converge even under high noise levels (σ = 50), corroborating

      A.3     I NTRODUCTION TO DENOISING WITHOUT GROUND TRUTH
928
929   The most comparable approaches to our method are those that explicitly consider a noise distribution,
930   including Stein’s Unbiased Risk Estimate (SURE)-based denoising methods (Soltanayev & Chun,
931   2018; Metzler et al., 2020) and Noise2Score (Kim & Ye, 2021). SURE-based denoising is applicable
932   to independent Gaussian noise and Noise2Score is more generally applicable to exponential family
933   noise. SURE-based denoising directly optimizes a loss motivated by SURE which provides an
934   unbiased estimate of the true risk, which is a mean-squared error to the ground truth. Noise2Score
935   uses Tweedie’s formula for estimating the posterior mean of an exponential family distribution with
936
      the score of the noisy distribution. The score is estimated by an approximate score estimator using a
      denoising autoencoder.
937
938   Another family of approaches often referred to as Noise2X is based on the assumptions of centered
939   (zero-mean) and independent noise. Noise2Noise (Lehtinen et al., 2018) requires independent noisy
940   observations of the same ground truth data. Noise2Self (Batson & Royer, 2019) is based on the
941   statistical independence across different dimensions of the measurement, such as the independence
942
      between different pixels. Noise2Void (Krull et al., 2019) leverages the concept of blind-spot networks,
      which predict the value of a pixel based solely on its surrounding context. Similarly, Noise2Same
943
      (Xie et al., 2020) employs self-supervised learning using selectively masked or perturbed regions to
944
      train the model to predict unobserved values. Both of them assume independence of noise across
945   dimensions.
