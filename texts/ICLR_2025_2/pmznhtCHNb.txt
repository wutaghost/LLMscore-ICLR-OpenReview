009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Reconstructing dynamic 3D models from clinical videos is crucial for medical
012            applications such as surgical visualization, robot-assisted surgery, and medical
013            training. However, the clinical environment presents unique challenges, including
014            limited surface textures, inconsistent lighting, and the need for expert-level medi-
015            cal knowledge, making it difficult for non-experts to directly apply existing tech-
016
               niques. To address these challenges, we present GaussianClin, a novel approach
               that enhances 3D modeling capabilities in dynamic clinical videos by leveraging
017
               multimodal feature-based Gaussian splatting (GS). By embedding trained multi-
018
               modal feature fields into the radiance field, GaussianClin integrates general med-
019            ical knowledge and improves the performance of GS in tasks like 3D tissue vi-
020            sualization, real-time object enhancement, clinical instrument and organ segmen-
021            tation, and medical visual question answering. To effectively capture temporal
022            dynamics and tissue deformations, we further introduce a spatiotemporal graph
023            distillation, which significantly improves handling deformable tissues compared
024            to standard GS methods. Experimental results demonstrate that GaussianClin en-
025            ables clinical 3D expert models to leverage massive pre-trained 2D multimodal
026            foundation models, paving the way for advancements in robot-assisted surgery.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030
      High-quality reconstruction of surgical videos is becoming increasingly important in modern
031   medicine. It not only provides precise anatomical information for surgical planning but also aids
032   surgeons in making accurate decisions during real-time navigation and offers immersive, realistic
033   virtual environments for medical training. In the field of surgical scene reconstruction, traditional
034   methods such as structured light and SLAM (Fuentes-Pacheco et al., 2015; Kazerouni et al., 2022)
035   have been widely used. However, these techniques often struggle with the complex geometries of
036   soft tissues and the dynamic nature of surgical environments, leading to inaccuracies and incom-
037   plete reconstructions. While volumetric reconstruction approaches have shown potential, they still
038   face challenges in capturing the full complexity of surgical scenes, particularly when dealing with
039
      deformable tissues and inconsistent lighting conditions. These limitations have spurred the develop-
      ment of more advanced reconstruction techniques that aim to meet the increasing demands for both
040
      precision and real-time performance.
041
042   In recent years, Neural Radiance Fields (NeRF) (Mildenhall et al., 2021; Gao et al., 2022), which
043   model 3D scenes by learning the volumetric radiance and density from multiple 2D images, have
044   emerged as a powerful technique for reconstructing surgical scenes. For example, EndoNeRF (Wang
045
      et al., 2022) represents dynamic scenes as canonical fields with a time-dependent displacement field,
      effectively capturing the subtle nuances of deformable tissues. However, NeRF-based methods re-
046
      quire repeatedly querying the radiance field at multiple points and rays to render each view, which
047
      significantly limits their rendering speed and poses challenges for real-time intraoperative applica-
048   tions. To address these limitations, 4D Gaussian Splatting (4DGS) techniques have been widely
049   adopted in surgical reconstruction. 4DGS represents the scene as an optimizable Gaussian model
050   initialized from motion structure techniques. Recent advancements, such as Gaussian SLAM (Yugay
051   et al., 2023), GS-SLAM (Yan et al., 2024), and SGS-SLAM (Li et al., 2024b), have extended 4DGS
052   to optimize camera poses and propose SLAM strategies for rapidly reconstructing 3D scenes from
053   2D videos. Among these, various Gaussian-based methods, such as SurgicalGaussian (Xie et al.,
      2024), have shown particular promise for real-time reconstruction of deformable tissues in mini-

      Despite significant advancements in surgical scene reconstruction techniques (Liu et al., 2024b;
      Yang et al., 2024), a key challenge persists in making the results both comprehensible and func-
083
      tional for practical medical use. Unlike the intuitive nature of natural scene reconstruction, medical
084
      reconstructions—while often technically successful—are difficult for non-professionals to interpret,
085   as they require specialized medical expertise to understand the complex anatomical structures and
086   dynamic surgical environments (Rodrı́guez et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2024a). For instance, interpreting
087   tissue deformations caused by physiological processes or the interactions between surgical instru-
088   ments and tissues requires a deep understanding of human anatomy and surgical procedures (Wang
089   et al., 2022; Zha et al., 2023). This difficulty in interpretation is further compounded by the fact that
090   current 4D Gaussian models struggle to support critical surgery-related downstream tasks, such as
091   real-time scene editing, semantic understanding, and automatic surgical report generation, which are
092   essential for improving surgical efficiency and decision-making (Mahmoud et al., 2017). As a result,
093
      these limitations not only hinder the accessibility of reconstruction outputs but also restrict broader
      potential in advancing surgical automation, preoperative planning, and postoperative evaluation.
094
095   Secondly, surgical environments typically present limited surface textures and inconsistent lighting
096   conditions (Batlle et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2023), which further complicate the reconstruction pro-
097   cess. Traditional reconstruction methods struggle to achieve sufficient rendering quality in areas
098   with smooth tissue surfaces or complex reflections, affecting the overall accuracy and reliability
099
      of the reconstruction. This challenge is particularly pronounced in endoscopic surgeries, where
      surgeons frequently adjust camera positions to track specific tissues and improve visibility, mak-
100
      ing it difficult to capture precise camera trajectories. Additionally, the dynamic nature of surgical
101
      scenes—such as tissue deformations caused by physiological processes and interactions with sur-
102   gical instruments—further increases the complexity of the reconstruction task (Liu et al., 2024b).
103   These limitations highlight the urgent need for innovative approaches to improve reconstruction
104   quality. Therefore, we propose a novel framework that not only enhances reconstruction quality but
105   also improves the accessibility and applicability of these models across various clinical scenarios,
106   particularly under low-light and complex surgical conditions. Such a framework will pave the way
107   for advancements in robot-assisted surgery and medical image processing, while providing precise
      and reliable tools for preoperative planning, intraoperative navigation, and postoperative evaluation.

      To address the above-mentioned challenges, we propose GaussianClin, an innovative framework
109   which enhances the dynamic performance of Gaussian splatting by integrating multimodal feature
110   fields from pre-trained 2D foundation models into the 3D rendering process. By embedding these
111   trained feature fields into the Gaussian radiance field, GaussianClin injects semantically rich medi-
112   cal knowledge, enabling GS to perform tasks such as 3D tissue visualization, object enhancement,
113   scene understanding, and language-guided interactions. Given the gap between 2D foundation mod-
114   els and 3D Gaussian radiance fields, further widened by the temporal dynamics and tissue deforma-
115   tions in clinical videos, we introduce a spatiotemporal graph distillation mechanism to effectively
116   embed these features into the dynamic field. Experimental results demonstrate that GaussianClin en-
117
      ables clinical 3D expert models to leverage large-scale pre-trained 2D multimodal foundation mod-
      els and improves rendering quality in clinical scenarios, especially under challenging conditions like
118
      low lighting and occlusion, paving the way for advancements in robot-assisted surgery. In summary,
119
      our key contributions are as follows:
120
121          • We introduce GaussianClin, which enhances dynamic Gaussian Splatting by embedding
122            foundation model feature fields, addressing challenges in rendering quality and semantic
123            understanding in surgical scene reconstruction.
124          • We propose a spatiotemporal graph distillation mechanism that better captures temporal
125            dynamics and tissue deformations, improving the handling of deformable tissues compared
126            to traditional GS methods.
127

               both training and rendering speeds, enabling advanced intraoperative applications in robot-

      Reconstructing 3D scenes from 2D images is crucial in many fields, especially in surgical settings.
      Traditional methods like Structure-from-Motion (SfM), as in COLMAP (Schönberger & Frahm,
140
      2016), and SLAM-based approaches (Zhou & Jagadeesan, 2019; Song et al., 2017; Zhou & Jayen-
141

142   point clouds. However, these methods often struggle in dynamic surgical environments due to their
143   assumption of scene stability. Recent innovations, such as Gaussian Splatting (Kerbl et al., 2023;
144   Bao et al., 2024), represent scenes as optimizable Gaussian entities initialized from SfM data, of-
145   fering faster and more accurate 3D reconstructions. Extensions like Gaussian-SLAM (Yugay et al.,
146   2023), GS-SLAM (Yan et al., 2024), and SGS-SLAM (Li et al., 2024b) further enhance these meth-
147   ods, while specialized adaptations like EndoGSLAM (Wang et al., 2024) address challenges such as
148   reflections, occlusions, and tissue deformations. These advancements are paving the way for more
149   precise 3D reconstructions in surgical applications, potentially transforming surgical planning and
150
      intraoperative navigation.

194   2.3   F EATURE F IELD D ISTILLATION FROM F OUNDATION M ODELS
195
196
      Feature field distillation extends 2D visual features into 3D space, enhancing 3D reconstruction.
      Recent methods, such as NeRF-DFF (Kobayashi et al., 2022), distill 2D feature similarities into 3D,
197
      while approaches like SA3D and GaussianGrouping (Ye et al., 2023) refine 3D masks and align 2D
198
      masks from foundation models like CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) and SAM (Kirillov et al., 2023) for
199   improved open-vocabulary 3D segmentation. Building on these, our approach enables 3D Gaussian
200   splatting on arbitrary-dimension semantic features via 2D foundation model distillation, improving
201   rendering quality and supporting real-time applications like segmentation, language-guided editing,
202   and medical visual question answering. Prior work, including Semantic NeRF (Zhi et al., 2021)
203   and Panoptic Lifting (Siddiqui et al., 2023), has shown that integrating noisy 2D labels into 3D can
204   yield accurate 3D segmentation. Advancements like Distilled Feature Fields (Shen et al., 2023),
205   LERF (Kerr et al., 2023), and Neural Feature Fusion Fields (Tschernezki et al., 2022) embed pixel-
206   aligned feature vectors for enhanced 3D understanding. However, significant challenges remain in
207
      efficiently lifting 2D models into 3D, particularly given limited 3D supervision data and the high
      computational demands of optimizing neural radiance fields from scratch for each individual scene.

294   3.3   I NSTILLING 2D K NOWLEDGE VIA S PATIOTEMPORAL G RAPH D ISTILLATION
295
296   To address the unique challenges of clinical videos, including rapid camera motion and complex
297   tissue deformations, we introduce a spatiotemporal graph distillation mechanism. This approach
298
      is based on the insight that while 2D dynamics in the video might be complex, the underlying 3D
      motion is often low-dimensional and composed of simpler units of rigid motion.
299
300   Spatiotemporal Feature Graph Our Spatiotemporal graph distillation process fuses information
301   across frames, creating a globally coherent representation of both scene geometry and motion. We
302   define similarity measures for spatial and temporal components with enhanced expressiveness. At
303   the spatial interaction level, we focus on capturing relationships between different regions within
304
      a single frame. The main goal is to identify and fuse information from spatially analogous areas
      across the image plane, which helps in constructing a coherent global representation of the surgical
305
      scene. We define a spatial similarity function based on feature representations of spatial regions. At
306
      the temporal coherence level, we extend the graph-guided distillation to capture temporal dynamics
307   between frames. This step ensures that the motion trajectories across frames are aligned, allowing
308   us to leverage temporal consistency to improve the overall representation. The temporal similarity
309   function is defined similarly to the spatial one, but it operates across time, as follows:

696
697   Our implementation is tailored to address the specific challenges presented by each dataset. For the
698   EndoNeRF dataset, which features complex tissue deformations and tool occlusions, we initialize
699   by randomly sampling 0.1% of points to reduce redundancy. We employ Adam as the optimizer
700   with an initial learning rate of 1.6 × 10-3 . Our training strategy involves a warmup phase to optimize
701   Canonical Gaussians for 1k iterations, followed by optimization of the entire framework for 3k
      iterations. For the EndoVis18 and EndoVis17 datasets, which focus on instrument segmentation


                                                        13
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


702
      and tracking, we adapt our model to handle the temporal aspects of the data. We split the frame
703   data of each scene into 7:1 training and testing sets. For the EndoVis Conversations dataset, which
704   incorporates visual question-answering tasks, we set the epoch to 20, batch size to 16, and learning
705   rate to 1 × 10-5 . This setting allows our model to effectively learn the relationship between visual
706   features and textual queries. All experiments are conducted on a single RTX 4090 GPU. The training
707   process for each dataset takes approximately 2 hours. We also evaluate a high-quality version in our
708   experiments, where we optimize all basis parameters for each Gaussian attribute.
