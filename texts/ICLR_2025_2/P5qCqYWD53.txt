009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            In this paper, we investigate the safety mechanisms of instruction fine-tuned large
012            language models (LLMs). We discover that re-weighting MLP neurons can sig-
013            nificantly compromise a model’s safety, especially for MLPs in end-of-sentence
014            inferences. We hypothesize that LLMs evaluate the harmfulness of prompts dur-
015            ing end-of-sentence inferences, and MLP layers plays a critical role in this pro-
016            cess. Based on this hypothesis, we develop 2 novel white-box jailbreak meth-
017            ods: a prompt-specific method and a prompt-general method. The prompt-specific
018
               method targets individual prompts and optimizes the attack on the fly, while the
               prompt-general method is pre-trained offline and can generalize to unseen harmful
019
               prompts. Our methods demonstrate robust performance across 7 popular open-
020
               source LLMs, size ranging from 2B to 72B. Furthermore, our study provides
021            insights into vulnerabilities of instruction-tuned LLM’s safety and deepens the
022            understanding of the internal mechanisms of LLMs.
023

025   1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027   The capabilities of large language models (LLMs) have improved rapidly in recent years (Achiam
028   et al., 2023; Anthropic, 2023; Touvron et al., 2023). One of the primary ways of deploying LLMs in
029   practice is through chatbots. Instruction fine-tuning is the most common approach for transforming
030   a pre-trained LLM into an effective chatbot (Wei et al., 2021; Ouyang et al., 2022; Chung et al.,
031   2022). This process involves training the model on a variety of prompt-response pairs, marked
032
      with special tokens, to guide the model in following instructions and generating helpful, relevant
      responses. Additionally, safety constraints are incorporated during this fine-tuning process, enabling
033
      instruction-tuned LLMs to recognize and refuse harmful or malicious prompts.
034
035   However, even these instruction-tuned models remain vulnerable to jailbreak attempts (Wei et al.,
036   2023; Zou et al., 2023b; Liu et al., 2023; Zhan et al., 2023). It remains an open question why safety
037   mechanisms fail against certain jailbreak methods, and indeed, it is not fully understood how safety
038
      mechanisms function in the first place. This situation underscores the importance of thoroughly
      understanding safety mechanisms. Only by grasping how current safety systems operate and why
039
      they can be bypassed can we design the next generation of more robust safety models.
040
041   Many studies have aimed to unravel the internal mechanisms behind LLM safety, exploring this issue
042   from feature, weight attribution, and other perspectives. From a feature perspective, several works
043   examine which features trigger model refusal behaviors, investigating how models detect harmful
044
      prompts (Zou et al., 2023a; Zheng et al., 2024; Arditi et al., 2024). From a weight attribution
      perspective, studies have analyzed the contributions of specific decoder layers or MLP neurons to
045
      model safety (Li et al., 2024; Wei et al., 2024). More broadly, many research explores how fine-
046
      tuning instills or weakens a model’s safety (Lermen et al., 2023; Qi et al., 2023).
047
048   Our study focuses on the relationship between LLM safety and MLP layers within the transformer
049   architecture, a topic explored in several prior works. Geva et al. (2022) suggests that enhancing
050
      specific MLP neurons can improve model safety, while Wei et al. (2024) demonstrated that pruning
      MLP neurons can effectively compromise the safety constraints of LLMs. Similar observations have
051
      been made in other studies as well (Lee et al., 2024; Uppaal et al., 2024).
052
053   Existing works typically treat model components across different inferences uniformly. In contrast,
      our study examines the MLP layers involved in various inferences independently. This novel per-

054
      spective reveals that the MLP layers in end-of-sentence inferences play a critical role in the safety
055   mechanisms of instruction-tuned LLMs. By re-weighting the neuron activations in these MLP lay-
056   ers, we demonstrate that the model’s safety can be significantly compromised. We hypothesize that
057   it is during these end-of-sentence inferences that the model assesses the harmfulness of queries, with
058   the MLP layers being crucial in these evaluations.
059
      Building on these observations, we develop 2 jailbreak methods: a prompt-specific method and
060
      a prompt-general method. The prompt-specific method optimizes independent MLP re-weighting
061   factors for different target prompts, enabling these factors to break the safety constraints for each
062   specific prompt. In contrast, the prompt-general method is designed to bypass safety constraints for
063   all harmful prompts. It is pre-trained on a given dataset and has the ability to generalize. As a result,
064   it can turn an instruction-tuned LLM into one that responds freely to any harmful queries without
065   safety constraints.
066
      We evaluate our methods and compare them with other jailbreak approaches. As a result, our
067   prompt-specific method outperforms state-of-the-art prompt-specific methods while requiring less
068   computational time. Our prompt-general method is comparable to state-of-the-art approaches and
069   has a smaller impact on the model’s original capabilities. These results indicate that modifying
070   only the MLP layers in the end-of-sentence inferences is enough to compromise model’s safety,
071   demonstrating their critical role in safety mechanisms.
072
      In general, our study presents new findings related to the safety mechanisms of instruction-tuned
073   LLMs. Based on these insights, we propose novel white-box jailbreak methods that exploit vulnera-
074   bilities in these models. We hope our methods contribute to the broader understanding of mechanism
075   interpretability in LLMs and aid in the development of truly reliable and transparent AI systems.

      2     MLP R E - WEIGHTING
078
079
      In this section, we explore how re-weighting the neuron activations of MLP layers in instruction-
080
      tuned LLMs affects their safety. We first define the notations used and describe the method for
081   applying re-weighting factors to the MLP layers. Then, we present some preliminary experiments
082   demonstrating that MLP re-weighting can compromise the model’s safety, followed by an ablation
083   study showing the critical role of end-of-sentence inferences in this process. Finally, we propose the
084   “harmful assessment hypothesis,” which offers an explanation for the observed behavior.
085

201   significantly compromise the model’s safety. Further experiments demonstrate that similar results

203
      Remark. The results we obtained are quite counterintuitive. Considering the changes made through-
204   out the process, we did not alter any inferences directly responsible for extracting semantic informa-
205   tion from the query, ruling out the possibility that the jailbreak was achieved by directly distorting
206   the model’s understanding of specific tokens. Nor did we modify the inferences directly involved in
207   generating responses, excluding the possibility that harmful responses were produced by increasing
208   the likelihood of harmful tokens.
209
      By limiting the changes solely to the end-of-sentence inferences, our experiments ruled out the two
210   most commonly believed mechanisms by which jailbreaks operate. This raises the question: why
211   did our jailbreak attempt succeed? We hypothesize that end-of-sentence inferences are involved in
212   the model’s internal process of assessing whether a query is harmful. After the semantic information
213   is extracted and aggregated into the end-of-sentence inferences’ residual stream (Wang et al., 2023),
214   the MLP layers assess the harmfulness of the entire query and provide signals that influence the
215   subsequent generation. However, it should be noted that this is only a hypothesis and requires
      further verification.

      3     ATTACKING METHODS
217
218
      Based on the findings from section 2, in this section we develop two methods to jailbreak instruction-
219
      tuned LLMs: a prompt-specific method, which operate independently for different prompts, and a
220   prompt-general method, which can create a safety-constraint-free version of the open-source LLMs.
221   We outline the specific settings and implementation details for these two methods in this section.
222
223
      3.1   P ROMPT- SPECIFIC METHOD
224
225   The prompt-specific method is designed to jailbreak an LLM for a single given prompt, requiring
226   independent training for each target prompt. Typical representatives of this approach include GCG
227   (Zou et al., 2023b), AutoDAN (Liu et al., 2023), and PAIR (Chao et al., 2023).
228
      Recall that in section 2, we did not provide the full definition of the loss function. We now complete
229
      it here. In order to encourage the LLM to produce more positive responses, we first hand craft a
230   small set of postive response prefixes, such as ”Sure! Here are some steps to help”. It is important
231   to note that these prefixes are independent of any specific query and are simply general positive
232   responses. We denote them as y ∈ Y, where each y represents a positive prefix and Y being their
233   collection. In practice, we constructed a small Y containing 9 positive prefixes. Further details on
234   these prefixes can be found in the Appendix A.
235
      Thus, the loss function L(M ; x) takes the following form:

      3.2   P ROMPT- GENERAL METHOD
264
265   The prompt-general method aims to obtain an LLM with safety constraints removed. After offline
266   training, the resulting LLM is expected to provide direct responses to any harmful prompt. Since the
267   prompt-general method requires no additional training during implementation, it has significantly
268   lower deployment requirements and poses greater potential risks. Typical representatives of this
269   approach include multi-prompt GCG (Zou et al., 2023b), ORTHO (Arditi et al., 2024) and reverse
      fine-tuning methods (Zhan et al., 2023; Qi et al., 2023).

281   To extend our prompt-specific method into the prompt-general method, we implicitly rely on the
282   hypothesis that there exists a universal safety mechanism across different harmful prompts. Only
283   with this assumption can we use a single set of MLP re-weighting factors to interfere with the
284
      generation process for all prompts. Subsequent experimental results confirmed this: by pre-training
      the MLP factors offline on a given dataset, these MLP factors demonstrated the ability to generalize
285
      to previously unseen harmful prompts.
286
287   In terms of training, the prompt-general method is not significantly different from the prompt-
288   specific method. The main difference is replacing the loss function L(M ; x), which originally
289   depends on a specific prompt x, with its expectation over a dataset D:

292   We selected and synthesized a collection of harmful questions and commands to create the training
293   dataset D. The primary sources include a cleaned and augmented version of HarmfulQA (Bhard-

295
      categories covered in HarmBench (Mazeika et al., 2024). Since we’ll also evaluate our method
      on HarmBench, we carefully cross-checked the datasets to ensure no overlap in prompts, thus pre-
296
      venting data contamination. It is important to reemphasize that our dataset contains only harmful
297
      queries, with no harmful responses.
298
299   Another minor difference between the prompt-general method and the prompt-specific method is the
300   introduction of early stopping. In the prompt-general method, we stop the optimization when the

479   5   R ELATED W ORKS
480
481   Jailbreaks. Numerous users and researchers have sought to bypass the safety constraints of LLMs,
482   resulting in various jailbreak methods. Techniques such as crafting adversarial prompts (Wei et al.,
483   2023; Carlini et al., 2023) and manipulating the model’s decoding process (Huang et al., 2023) ex-
484   ploit vulnerabilities in both open-source and closed-source models. Additionally, fine-tuning aligned
485   LLMs, even over non-malicious datasets, can inadvertently compromise model’s safety (Qi et al.,
      2023; Yang et al., 2023). Recently, studies by Arditi et al. (2024) and Wei et al. (2024) have lever-


                                                        9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      aged insights from interpretability to develop more efficient and effective jailbreak methods. Our
487   work also falls into this category.
488
489   LLM Safety Mechanism. Understanding the safety mechanisms of LLMs is challenging due to
490   the complexity of their internal mechanisms. Recent studies have successfully identified attributes
491   such as truthfulness and toxicity within these models (Lee et al., 2024; Li et al., 2023). Wei et al.
492   (2024) demonstrate that pruning safety-critical neurons can degrade model safety while preserving
493   most capabilities. In another approach, Zheng et al. (2024) and Zou et al. (2023a) focus on the re-
494   fusal mechanism, revealing that the hidden features associated with refusal differ from those linked
495   to harmfulness. Furthermore, Arditi et al. (2024) show that eliminating refusal feature directions
496
      from model parameters can significantly compromise model safety. Nevertheless, these discoveries
      represent initial steps in understanding LLM safety mechanisms, and a comprehensive understand-
497
      ing of these mechanisms remains elusive, highlighting the need for further investigation.
498
499
500   6   D ISCUSSION
501
502   In this work, we demonstrate that the safety mechanism of instruction-tuned LLMs heavily relies on
503   MLP layers in end-of-sentences inferences and illustrate how vulnerable current open-source LLMs
504
      are to such targeted attacks. Our work is inspired by previous researches’ observations regarding
      the relationship between MLP layers and model safety, and we have also uncovered several new
505
      phenomena that we hope will inspire further research in LLM mechanism study.
506
507
      Limitations. There are several limitations to our work. First, much of the method is heuristic-
508   driven, making it more of a validation experiment rather than an optimal solution. For instance, the
509   design of the loss function is largely based on intuition, leaving considerable room for improvement.
510   Second, our work only points to the significance of MLP layers in the safety mechanism, yet the
511   precise role they play and how they impact subsequent generation processes remain open questions
512   for future investigation.
513
514   Ethics Statement. Research on jailbreaking instruction-tuned LLMs inevitably raises concerns
515   about whether it facilitates new risks. Although our method introduces a simpler and more efficient
516   approach than most existing methods, we believe the risk profile does not fundamentally change,
517
      as the ability to jailbreak instruction-tuned LLMs is already well-documented. Our work is driven
      by the goal of understanding the safety mechanisms of LLMs, which we believe will ultimately
518
      contribute to the development of more robust and transparent AI systems.

      C    OTHER JAILBREAKS
773
774
      More details of other jailbreak methods that we compare in Section 4:
775
776          • GCG, Greedy Coordinate Gradient (Zou et al., 2023b): This method optimizes an adver-
777            sarial suffix by maximizing the probability that the model produces an affirmative response
778            to a given prompt.
779          • AP, AutoPrompt (Shin et al., 2020): Similar to GCG, but employs a different candidate
780            selection strategy. In this paper, we use the adapted version proposed by Zou et al. (2023b).
781
             • ORTHO, Weight Orthogonalization (Arditi et al., 2024): This method compares hidden
782
               states between harmful and harmless prompts to identify refusal directions. It then selects
783            an optimal direction and modifies model weights to eliminate the representation associated
784            with that refusal direction.
785
             • GCG-M, GCG-Multi / Ensemble GCG: The multi-prompt version of GCG, optimizing a
786
