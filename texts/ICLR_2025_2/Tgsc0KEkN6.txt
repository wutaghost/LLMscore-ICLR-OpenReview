009                                                                    A BSTRACT
010
011                Integrating multimodal understanding and generation into a unified framework can
012
                   bridge the domain gap across different modalities. However, existing multimodal-
                   language datasets predominantly offer text descriptions for a single modality,
013
                   treating visual and audio as separate tasks. This approach neglects the inherent
014
                   audio-visual correlations, resulting in annotations that are often monotonous and
015                modality-specific rather than comprehensive and precise. Such oversight hampers
016                the advancement of cross-modality research. To fulfill this gap, we present ViML,
017                a large-scale multi-modality-to-language dataset incorporating 3M video clips with
018                high-quality multimodal captions. In ViML, we propose a systemic captioning
019                framework, achieving various modality annotations with more than 12.2k hours
020                of trailer videos. Here, to ensure the caption retains music perspective while
021                preserving the authority of visual context, we leverage the advanced LLM to merge
022                all annotations adaptively. In particular, the ViML has two main advantages: (1) the
023
                   topics are diverse, and the content characters are of various types, e.g., film, news,
                   and gaming. (2) the corresponding background music is custom-designed, making it
024
                   more coherent with the visual context. In this fashion, our ViML dataset potentially
025
                   paves the path for fine-grained large multimodal-language model training. In
026                experiments, we provide evaluation metrics and benchmark results on our dataset,
027                demonstrating the high quality of our annotation and its effectiveness for model
028                training. We include demo data in https://anonymous.4open.science/w/ViML-4C78

048   Integrating multimodal understanding and generation into a unified framework can bridge the domain
049   gap across different modalities and has a wide range of applications in daily life, such as AI-driven
050   movies and short video production. Clearly, creating vivid videos requires more than just visual frame
051   generation or individual modality-based approaches; it necessitates an integrated understanding and
052   generation capability. Thanks to various large-scale video-language datasets, numerous generative
053   multimodal large language models have been developed to achieve this goal (Blattmann et al., 2023a;
      Long et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2023a; He et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2023; Kondratyuk et al., 2023;

056   overlooking the significance of inherent visual-audio dependencies. This presents a complex challenge

135   2.1    V IDEO GENERATION AND UNDERSTANDING
136
137   Video understanding and text-to-video generation are inherently connected tasks. In recent years,
138   there has been remarkable progress in understanding models (Wu et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2021; Zhao
139   et al., 2022; Bain et al., 2021b; Yang et al., 2022b;a; Lin et al., 2022; 2019; Bertasius et al., 2021;
140   Wu et al., 2019; Zhang et al., 2023a; Chen et al., 2023b), which have greatly contributed to the
141   advancement of text-based video generation techniques. The availability of large-scale datasets and
142
      diffusion models has revolutionized video generation, moving from pixel-level approaches like (Ho
      et al., 2022b; Singer et al., 2022; Ho et al., 2022a) to latent-level video diffusion models (He et al.,
143
      2022; Zhou et al., 2022; Blattmann et al., 2023b; He et al., 2023). Concurrently, understanding
144
      models have also witnessed significant improvements. A series of MLLM-based understanding
145   models (Liu et al., 2024; Maaz et al., 2023; Song et al., 2023; Jin et al., 2023) has reached satisfied
146   understanding abilities. The iterative interaction between video generation and understanding has led
147   to the development of excellent large-scale datasets and models encompassing diverse approaches.
148   Panda (Chen et al., 2024b) introduced an auto-caption model distilled from video understanding
149   models like VideoLlaMA (Zhang et al., 2023a), MiniGPT4 (Zhu et al., 2023b).
150
151   2.2    V IDEO -L ANGUAGE DATASETS
152
153
      Captioned video datasets are essential for text-to-video generation and understanding tasks. MSR-
      VTT (Xu et al., 2016), UCF-101 (Soomro et al., 2012) are commonly used as evaluation sets.
154
      Anna et al. (Rohrbach et al., 2016) presented 118,081 movie clips with descriptions. ActivityNet
155
      Caption (Krishna et al., 2017) by Ranjay et al. is a benchmark involving event detection, natural
156   language description, and event localization. WebVid (Bain et al., 2021a), VideoFactory (Wang et al.,
157   2023b), and other works (Sanabria et al., 2018; Wang et al., 2019; Stroud et al., 2020; Nagrani et al.,
158   2022), contain multilingual video descriptions, video clips, metadata such as titles, descriptions,
159   tags, and channel names, and are used for tasks like video understanding, text-to-video retrieval, and
160   audio-video captioning with weak annotations. Several datasets and approaches have utilized audio
161   to enhance video captioning (Miech et al., 2019; Rohrbach et al., 2016; Zellers et al., 2021; Wang
      et al., 2023c; Chen et al., 2024b; Xue et al., 2021). These datasets consist of movies, web videos,

183   3     V I ML DATASET
184
185   To boost the performance of multimodal generation tasks, we construct a high-quality multimodal
186   dataset based on trailers-like videos, which offers a wealth of multimodal information and diverse
187   categories that distinguish them from existing large-scale video-language datasets. In this section,
188   we introduce an automated data collection and cleaning pipeline to construct the source data for
189   trailers in Section 3.1. We developed a multimodal captioning pipeline that generated rich multimodal
190   captions for video segments in Section 3.2. Additionally, we detail our different subsets in Section 3.3.
191   Moreover, various statistical analysis of ViML Dataset are shown in Fig. 5.

246   Motion Filtering As mentioned, trailer videos are often blurred with large motion, so we applied
247   motion filtering. Motion vectors and optical flow are both mainstream motion quality evaluation
248
      methods. In our case, trailer videos often have rapid cuts and transitions between scenes, posing
      challenges for optical flow-based analysis. Other than optical flow, motion vectors are more robust to
249
      these quick changes, as they rely on larger block units’ displacement rather than individual pixels’
250

251   efficiently. Thus, we leverage motion vectors to filter out clips with problems like static frames, title

295   3.2     V IDEO C APTIONING P IPELINE
296
297   The ViML contains many complex themes, like subtitles and character animations, as shown in
298   Fig. 2, which brings extraordinary complex work for video captioning. At the same time, smooth
299   transition shots also make it impossible for traditional single-frame annotation methods to convey
300   semantics coherently. Therefore, this section introduces a multi-temporal and multimodal caption
301   pipeline containing a detailed video description from frame, motion, and music levels.
302   Frame Caption The auto-captioning pipeline has proven efficient in cutting-edge video generation
303   foundation models. SVD (Blattmann et al., 2023a) and Pandas (Chen et al., 2024b) have given
304   promising results and demonstrated the importance of high-quality frame captions for the generation
305   model. We initially performed image-level captioning on the individual frames of the data. We
306   employed coca (Yu et al., 2022) for each video clip to generate separate captions for three frames(first,
307   middle, and last), resulting in relevant captions.
308   Video Caption Having obtained concise captions for three frames that capture the essential infor-
309   mation, we aimed to obtain fine-grained captions and variations between frames in the video. We
310   concatenated multiple frames into a comic strip format and employed the LLaVA (Liu et al., 2024)
311   image model to guide the description of the dynamic differences between frames. Additionally,
312   leveraging a powerful multimodal language model, we incorporated OCR and more detailed summary
313   descriptions to expand the information within the frame captions.
314   Categories and Background Noticing the LLM-based caption has hallucinations when describing
315   the frame, we further generate word-level labels to enhance the annotation of the main objects and
316   background. Initially, we utilized LLaVA’s QA capabilities to have the model answer questions about
317   the background. Subsequently, through QA, we prompted the model to provide relevant category
318   information. We conducted the word cloud in Fig. 4. and certified caption quality by subjective
319   experience in Section 4.
320   Music Caption Moreover, given that trailer music usually has a well-designed audio effect and
321   background music, we applied the music caption on our dataset rather than a standard audio caption.
322   In our work, we used MusicCaps (Doh et al., 2023b), an LLM-based music captioning model. The
323   caption format is well designed with its description pipeline, which first describes its sound quality, a
      generated speech style, and a detailed description of its instrument and music style. More examples

      contains 1k video clips and multiple multimodal captions. Then, we manually adjust the merged

371   several tasks and models on the test set in Section 4 to show the complexity and difficulties of ViML.
372   The test set has 98.2 words of caption on average and includes 3.2hr video clips.

497   5    C ONCLUSION
498
499   We introduce ViML, a comprehensive and accurate multi-modality visual-audio dataset to address the
500   dataset gap. By utilizing the inherent value of trailers, which integrate visual, audio, and contextual
501   elements, ViML offers detailed and precise multi-modality annotations. Our systematic captioning
502   framework adaptively merges visual and musical perspectives, ensuring that the annotations capture
503   the richness of multimodal content. Experimental results demonstrate the high quality of the ViML
504   dataset, its effectiveness for fine-grained multimodal-language model training, and a variety of down-
505   stream applications. We believe this innovative dataset will unlock new possibilities in video content
506   generation and significantly advance research in visual-audio understanding. The comprehensive and
507
      diverse nature of ViML makes it a valuable asset for the research community, paving the way for
      novel applications that leverage the power of multimodal learning.

510   6    E THICAL I SSUES
511
512   Our dataset adheres to the latest public video-audio-text dataset standards and involves a rigorous
513   pipeline for data processing, ensuring its suitability for research purposes. We will explain the
514   following aspects of our work license.
515   Licensing and access: ViML is a Research-Only dataset, and we refer to the Creative Commons(CC
516   BY-NC-SA 4.0)(https://creativecommons.org/share-your-work/cclicenses/) to release our metadata,
517   same as InternVid Wang et al. (2023c).
518
      Ethics and responsible use: For the development of the community and more strict license require-
519
      ments, the users of ViML must also follow the Research Use of Data Agreement v1.0.(License) same
520
      as Panda-70M (Chen et al., 2024b).
521
522   Consent and privacy: We provide text descriptions only. In ViML, we only provide text descriptions,
523   excluding any sensitive information. Raw videos and metadata containing personal or privacy-
524
      sensitive content are not included. During data processing, all text descriptions are generated from an
      existing open-source model, ensuring they are highly compressed and cannot reconstruct the original
525
      video information. Therefore, users cannot recover raw data or any other sensitive information from
526
      the text descriptions.
527
528   Legal data collection steps We use YouTube’s official API to ensure compliance with the platform’s
529   terms of service and data usage policy. We filter the metadata by (1)Tag Filtering: Use YouTube video
530
      tags to exclude videos that do not meet research purposes or contain sensitive content. (2)Content
      Classification: Automatically filter out inappropriate categories (e.g., adult content, political content,
531
      18+). (3)User-Provided Tags: Combine with user-provided tags to identify and filter potential
532
      sensitive content.
533
534   Test Set and Benchmark: Since we need to release the video of the testing set, we are filtering
535   the ViML-Test set that is strictly followed by the CC(https://creativecommons.org/licenses/by/). We
536
      further applied manual checks to ensure the test set included suitable information only.
537   Generally, our dataset is meticulously curated during collection, adhering to the latest public video-
538   audio-text dataset standards, ensuring its suitability for research purposes. We remain vigilant about
539   the rapidly evolving license requirements and are committed to continuously updating and further
      filtering the data to meet these needs.

845
846   We use the image understanding model LLaVA-13b (Liu et al., 2024) to caption each of our video
847   clips. Specifically, for each clip, we sample frames at positions 0.1, 0.3, 0.5, 0.7, and 0.9, and then
848   horizontally concatenate them into a single image, which serves as input for LLaVA-13b to generate
849   captions. We construct the caption prompt as follows:
850
            These are some keyframes of a video.
851
            Please use one sentence to summarize the content of the video
852
               in detail.
853         Summarize the content of the entire video but not describe
854            keyframes frame by frame.
855
856
      Moreover, we observe that the results of LLaVA captioning often contained some redundant informa-
857
      tion, as illustrated by the green sections in Figure Fig. 11. Therefore, we use LLaMA-13b to refine
858
      the LLaVA captions, eliminating much of the extraneous content and rendering the final captions
859   more in line with human expression. We construct the prompt as follows:
860
861         [{caption}] This is a description of a video.
862         Please polish it to an overall video description in one
863            sentence and give me only the content of the video.
            Do not use the words ’frame’ and ’video’.
