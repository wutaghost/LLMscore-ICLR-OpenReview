011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Federated domain generalization (FedDG) aims at equipping the federally trained
014            model with the domain generalization ability when the model meets new clients
015            with domain shifts. Among factors that possibly indicate generalization, the loss
016
               landscape flatness of the trained model is an intuitive, viable, and widely studied
               one. However, pursuing the flatness of the global model in the FedDG setting is
017
               not trivial due to the restriction to preserve data privacy. To address this issue,
018
               we propose GFM, a novel algorithm designed to seek Global Flat Minima of the
019            global model. Specifically, GFM leverages a global model-constrained adversarial
020            data augmentation strategy, creating a surrogate for global data within each local
021            client, which allows for split sharpness-aware minimization to approach global
022            flat minima. GFM is compatible with federated learning without compromising
023            data privacy restrictions, and theoretical analysis further supports its rationality
024            by demonstrating that the objective of GFM serves as an upper bound on the ro-
025            bust risk of the global model on global data distribution. Extensive experiments
026            on multiple FedDG benchmarks demonstrate that GFM consistently outperforms
027
               previous FedDG and federated learning approaches.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   In recent years, federated learning has emerged as a popular paradigm for distributed learning with
033
      data privacy preservation (Kairouz et al., 2021; Li et al., 2020; McMahan et al., 2017). In federated
      learning, distributed clients keep their data locally and no data are shared across clients. The clients
034
      collaborate on training the global model with the intervention of a central server. In each commu-
035
      nication round, clients train their local models on their respective datasets and upload them to the
036   server. Then, the server aggregates these models to derive a global model, which is subsequently
037   distributed to all clients. In this way, the global model performs well on clients participating in the
038   training. However, in real scenarios, the federally-trained model may be deployed for clients which
039   don’t participate in the training and may experience domain shifts. This challenges the generaliza-
040   tion ability of the trained model, which is known as the federated domain generalization problem.
041
      The challenge of federated domain generalization has garnered significant attention in recent
042   year (Guo et al., 2023b; Zhang et al., 2023a;b; Nguyen et al., 2022; Park et al., 2024). Most promis-
043   ing methods try to align the behaviors of local models from various perspectives. To give a few
044   examples, Zhang et al. (2023a) proposed aligning the feature distribution, Guo et al. (2023b) aimed
045   to learn domain-invariant representations by aligning the gradients, and Park et al. (2024) enabled
046   style sharing among different clients. In contrast to these studies, we concentrate more on the opti-
047   mization solution of the global model from the perspective of loss landscape flatness. There is sub-
048   stantial body of literature (Chen et al., 2021; Izmailov et al., 2018; Jastrz˛ebski et al., 2018; Keskar
049   et al., 2016) on the relationship between loss landscape flatness and the model’s generalization abil-
050
      ity. Moreover, empirical results in many centralized tasks illustrate the effectiveness of seeking flat
      minima, including i.i.d. situations (Keskar et al., 2016; Izmailov et al., 2018; Foret et al., 2020),
051
      centralized domain generalization (Cha et al., 2021), and incremental learning (Shi et al., 2021a).
052
053   However, in federated learning, the flatness of the global model is difficult to estimate and optimize
      due to privacy concerns, making it a challenging problem. Some studies like FedSAM (Caldarola


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      et al., 2022; Qu et al., 2022) bypassed this issue by instead focusing on seeking flat minima of
055   local models, hoping this would facilitate the flatness of the global model. However, it inevitably
056   results in sub-optimal solutions. These methods achieve their objective by employing the Sharpness-
057   Aware Minimizer (SAM (Foret et al., 2020)) during local updates on client models. To address the
058   limitations of local flatness methods, FedGAMMA (Dai et al., 2023) introduced global information
059   into local updates by correcting local gradients, ensuring that all clients adjust their updates toward
060   the global direction. However, this gradient correction is not explicitly connected to flatness, and
061   the SAM optimizer is still applied locally without modifications, making it fundamentally a method
062   for local flatness. FedSMOO (Sun et al., 2023) turned to enforce high consistency in local SAM
063
      perturbations by approximating the global perturbation using ADMM. However, the approximation
      is not strict, as the global perturbation is only computed in each round but required in every iteration.
064
      Alternatively, Li et al. (2023) explored aggregation weights and demonstrated that weight shrinking
065
      leads to flatter global minima. Nevertheless, their method relies on an additional proxy dataset to
066   determine the parameters, which may not always be feasible.
067
068   Given that the challenge arises from the lack of direct access to global data, we try to solve it in a
069
      data-centric manner by decomposing the objective of seeking global flatness into two components:
      seeking local flatness and enhancing global-local consistency. We begin with the homogeneous
070
      setting, where the data from each local client lies in the same global data distribution. We show
071
      that if local models are averaged in a convex combination, the robust risk of the global model is
072   upper-bounded by the convex combination of robust risks of the local models. This suggests that
073   seeking global flat minima by asking for local flatness is practically reasonable if clients are homo-
074   geneous. However, the homogeneous assumption does not hold in FedDG and the only source of
075   global information is the global model itself. Therefore, we propose a global model-constrained ad-
076   versarial data augmentation strategy to augment local data. The augmented data serves as a surrogate
077   for global data, thereby enhancing global-local consistency. These two schemes collaborate on the
078   same goal of approaching global flat minima, each playing a different role: the local flatness objec-
079   tive contributes to the “flatness” of the global model, while the global model-constrained adversarial
080
      data augmentation strategy supplements information of the “global” data distribution. Furthermore,
      theoretical analysis provides additional support for the validity of the proposed method by demon-
081
      strating that the objective of GFM provides an upper bound to the robust risk of the global model on
082
      the global data distribution. The main contributions of our work are summarized as follows:
083
084   • We propose a novel algorithm, GFM, which is specifically designed to seek global flat minima in
085
        the federated learning task, which improve domain generalization performance while simultane-
        ously maintaining data privacy.
086
087   • We have theoretically demonstrated that the objective of GFM constitutes a component of the
088
        upper bound of the risk in the unseen domain. This is evidenced by indicating that the robust
        empirical risks of local clients on augmented samples is an upper bound of the robust risk of the
089
        global model on global data distribution.
090
091
      • Through extensive experiments on a range of benchmarks, we show that our algorithm can achieve
        consistently improved performance compared to previous SOTA methods.

       4.3    F LATNESS COMPARISONS
415
416   In this section, we empirically compare the flatness of solutions found by GFM and other methods.
417   Specifically, we use expected loss value changes Fγ (θ) proposed in (Cha et al., 2021) as a metric.
418   For model with parameter θ, Fγ (θ) calculates the expected loss changes between θ and θ + γ on the
419   sphere of radius γ as follows:
420
                                           Fγ (θ) :=         E            [E(θ′ ) − E(θ)].                                   (15)
421                                                    ||θ ′ ||=||θ||+γ
422
423
      Large Fγ (θ) indicates the loss changes dramatically when moving from θ to the sphere of radius γ,
      which reveals a sharp minimum and vice versa. One can effectively estimate Fγ (θ) with finite sam-
424
      ples according to the Monte-Carlo method, because Fγ (θ) has an unbiased finite sample estimator
425
      and is computationally efficient. In our experiments, Fγ (θ) is approximated with 50 samples. We
426   quantitatively measure Fγ (θ) of the global model trained by FedAvg, FedSAM, and GFM with all
427   unseen domains of the PACS dataset. FedAvg represents the baseline without a special design for
428   flatness, FedSAM focuses on the flatness of local models, while the proposed GFM tries to approach
429   global flatness. The results are reported in Fig. 1. We can conclude from it that both FedSAM and
430   GFM can help improve global flatness, and GFM can find flatter minima than both FedSAM and
431   FedAvg in all experiments and on both the seen train datasets and the unseen test dataset, which
      verifies the effectiveness of GFM for seeking global flat minima.

      4.6   PARAMETER ANALYSIS
490
491
      In this section, we demonstrate the se-
492   lection of hyperparameters. There are
493   two key hyperparameters in GFM: the ra-
494   dius γ and the update interval c. The
495   radius γ is a critical hyperparameter in
496   SAM-based methods, as it determines the
497   range of model perturbation. The optimal
498   value of γ varies across tasks, datasets,
499   and models. In our experiments, we con-
500
      ducted a grid search for γ on the PACS Figure 4: Influences of radius γ and update interval c.
      dataset to determine the appropriate value The values are presented in logarithmic scale.
501
      for both FedSAM and GFM. For GFM,
502
      the update interval c is fixed to 10. The results are shown on the left side of Figure 4. The accuracy
503   first increases and then decreases as γ increases, indicating the existence of a local optimum. This
504   behavior is expected because, with a small γ, FedSAM recovers to the FedAvg baseline (and GFM
505   reverts to the FedAvg+GCA baseline), resulting in reduced performance. Conversely, when γ is too
506   large, the SAM optimizer becomes unstable and struggles to converge. Notably, GFM exhibits a
507   relatively flatter optimum compared to FedSAM. This could be attributed to the improved consis-
508   tency of local models in GFM, which reduces the need for local flatness to achieve sufficient global
509   flatness. In our experiments, we found that γ = 0.02 achieves the optimal performance for both
510   methods. Therefore, we set γ = 0.02 for subsequent experiments on PACS and other datasets.
511   With γ fixed, we tune the update interval c, which controls the update frequency of the augmentation
512   model. With a larger value of c, the augmentation model tends to update less frequently with a
513   relatively low computational cost. As shown on the right side of Figure 4, the performance improves
514   with more frequent updates of the augmentation model. The strategy of alternating one iteration
515   of augmentation with one iteration of classification achieves the best performance. However, this
516   approach incurs a significantly higher computational cost, as illustrated in Appendix C.5. To balance
517
      performance and efficiency, we set c = 10 for related experiments.
518
519   5     L IMITATIONS
520
521   One limitation of GFM is the increased computational cost for local updates. The inclusion of the
522   augmentation method and the SAM optimizer in the local client results in higher computational de-
523   mands compared to the baseline method. Details on the exact computational overhead and potential
524   trade-offs can be found in Appendix C.5. Another potential limitation of our current approach is the
525   restriction in the types of augmentation transformations. At present, the augmentation model is lim-
526   ited to applying color and geometry augmentations. However, other forms of augmentation, such as
527   Fourier-based transformations, could also be beneficial for domain generalization (DG). Identifying
528   and exploring additional augmentation techniques, or even leveraging generative models, represents
529
      a promising avenue for future research.

531   6     C ONCLUSION
532
533
      In this paper, we propose a novel algorithm, named GFM, to seek global flat minima in FedDG. The
534   overall algorithm is explainable by viewing it as minimizing the upper bound of the robust risk of the
535   global model on the global data distribution. Specifically, we propose the global model-constrained
536   adversarial data augmentation strategy to seek a surrogate for global data and use sharpness-aware
537   minimization to pursue flatter minima. Flatness measurement and loss surface visualization experi-
538   ments validate the flatter minima of the global model found by GFM than by FedAvg and the method
539   seeking local flatness. Furthermore, extensive experiments on four FedDG benchmarks confirmed
      the improved performance of GFM when comparing or combining with previous works.

      A     R ELATED WORK
811
812
      Domain generalization Domain generalization aims to address the distribution shift problem
813
      caused by domain gaps and enable the model to perform well not only on train domains but also on
814   test domains. Some researchers focus on the domain alignment idea (Li et al., 2018b;c; Shao et al.,
815   2019; Shi et al., 2021b) by bridging domain distribution gaps. There are also some works (Balaji
816   et al., 2018; Zhao et al., 2021; Li et al., 2019) considering meta-learning strategies to learn from
817   domain shifts. Other techniques including invariant risk minimization (Ahuja et al., 2020; Arjovsky
818   et al., 2019; Krueger et al., 2021), data augmentations (Huang et al., 2021; Xu et al., 2021; Zhang
819   et al., 2022), and self-supervised learning (Carlucci et al., 2019; Kim et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2020)
820   are also validated effectively in domain generalization. However, these centralized methods either
821   require domain labels or need data samples from all domains, which is not achievable in the feder-
822
      ated learning setting due to the data privacy issue.
823
824   Federated domain generalization Federated domain generalization involves both domain gener-
825
      alization and federated learning, which aims to bridge the participating gap of unseen clients with
      domain shifts. Methods with different motivations are proposed to solve it. FedADG (Zhang et al.,
826
      2023a) aligned each seen client’s data representation distribution by adversarial training for get-
827
      ting universal representation, FedSR (Nguyen et al., 2022) tried to learn simple representation for
828   avoiding spurious correlation by regularizing the feature norm and conditional mutual information,
829   FedIIR (Guo et al., 2023b) implicitly learned invariant classifier by gradient alignment, GA (Zhang
830   et al., 2023b) focused on the averaging stage and adjusted coefficients of local models by their per-
831   formance, and StableFDG (Park et al., 2024) and CCST (Chen et al., 2023) proposed to utilize style
832   statistics in seen clients to help local training. Different from them, we try to approach the global
833   flatness for improved domain generalization ability.
834
835   Flat minima A popular perspective on the generalization of deep networks is that flat minima are
836   robust to test distribution shifts. This problem, explored early (Hinton & Van Camp, 1993; Hochre-
837   iter & Schmidhuber, 1994; 1997), has seen a resurgence in recent years (Dziugaite & Roy, 2017; Li
838   et al., 2018a; Jiang et al., 2019), showing a strong relation between flat minima and generalization.
839   Recent works seek flat minima either during optimization (Foret et al., 2020; Kwon et al., 2021;
840
      Zhuang et al., 2022; Sun et al., 2024; Zhong et al., 2022) or via post-processing (Izmailov et al.,
      2018; Cha et al., 2021). The former is exemplified by Sharpness-Aware Minimization (SAM) (Foret
841
      et al., 2020), which minimized robust risk, while later works (Kwon et al., 2021; Zhuang et al., 2022;
842
      Sun et al., 2024; Zhong et al., 2022) overcame the shortcomings of SAM or proposed new theoretical
843   explanations. Wen et al. (2024) comprehensively discussed the relationship between flatness, gen-
844   eralization, and SAM with respect to different architectures and data distributions. Post-processing
845   methods, such as SWA (Izmailov et al., 2018), exploited linear mode connectivity (Draxler et al.,
846   2018; Garipov et al., 2018; Juneja et al., 2022) by averaging models along SGD paths to improve
847   generalization. In this paper, we focus on the flatness of the global model in federated learning,
848   which is not directly optimizable.

       C.5   C OMPUTATION OVERHEAD AND POTENTIAL TRADE - OFFS
991
992    In GFM, an augmentation model is incorporated into local updates to better approach a flat op-
993    timization landscape, albeit with some additional computational overhead. We compare the time
994    and space overheads of GFM with other baseline methods. The space overhead is evaluated in two
995    aspects: the parameters used during local updates (former element in the space column) and the
996    parameters required for communication (latter element in the space column). From the results, it
997    can be observed that the additional parameters introduced by the augmentation model are relatively
998
       small (0.25M for ResNet-18 and 0.12M for ResNet-50), except for the small ConvNet architecture.
       Furthermore, the actual running time increases to approximately 1.5 to 1.7 times that of the FedSAM
999
       baseline, with variations due to differences in data processing times. Notably, among methods that
1000
       aim to achieve flatter minima of the global model (including FedGAMMA, FedSMOO, and GFM),
1001   our method imposes the lowest space constraints.
1002
1003   The increase in running time is influenced by three factors: (1) the forward pass of the augmenta-
1004
       tion model to generate augmented data, (2) the backward pass when optimizing the augmentation
       model, and (3) the inner maximization within the SAM optimizer. Here, we focus on the first two
1005
       components introduced by the augmentation model. One potential trade-off to reduce computa-
1006
       tional cost is to decrease the update frequency of the augmentation model. As shown in Tab. 8,
1007   reducing the frequency of updates for ϕ can lower the time cost by reducing the number of back-
1008   ward passes. Another strategy is to use low-dimensional images as inputs to the deep augmentation
1009   modules c(; ϕc ) and g(; ϕg ) (see Appendix D.2 for further clarification). Specifically, let x̄i be the
1010   low-dimensional (e.g., 32x32) version of xi . The color and geometry parameters, αi , βi = c(x̄i ; ϕc )
1011   and A = g(x̄i ; ϕg ), can be obtained from x̄i and then applied to the original images (αi , βi need
1012   upsampling to the original dimension). This approach reduces the computational cost associated
1013   with the deep model during both the forward and backward passes. As shown in Table 9, dimension
1014   of inputs to the augmentation module have minimal impact on the final performance. By utilizing
1015
       low-dimensional images as inputs, the computational cost of augmentation in GFM is reduced to
       approximately 30% of the FedSAM baseline.

1114   D.2    AUGMENTATION M ODEL
1115
1116   The augmentation model a(; ϕ) is defined as the composition of a color augmentation model c(; ϕc )
1117   and a geometry augmentation model g(; ϕg ). The color augmentation model c(; ϕc ) takes xi ∈
1118   R3×H×W as input and outputs color transformation parameters (αi , βi ), where αi , βi ∈ R3×H×W
1119   represent scaling and shifting factors, respectively. The augmented color is then computed as x̃i =
1120   t(αi ⊙ xi + βi ), where t(·) denotes a triangle wave function. The geometry augmentation model
1121
       g(; ϕg ) : X → R2×3 also takes xi as input and outputs a residual affine parameter A ∈ R2×3 . An
       affine transformation is applied as x̂ = Affine(x̃, A + I), where I is the identity matrix. The entire
1122
       procedure is differentiable, and both ϕc and ϕg are parameters of deep models. For further details,
1123
       refer to Section 4 of (Suzuki, 2022).
