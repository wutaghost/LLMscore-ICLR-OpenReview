009                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011               Multi-modal Large Language Models (MLLMs) exhibit impressive problem-
012               solving abilities in various domains, but their visual comprehension and abstract
013               reasoning skills remain under-evaluated. To this end, we present P OLY MATH, a
014               challenging benchmark aimed at evaluating the general cognitive reasoning abilities
015               of MLLMs. P OLY MATH comprises 5,000 manually collected high-quality images
016               of cognitive textual and visual challenges across 10 distinct categories, includ-
017               ing pattern recognition, spatial reasoning, and relative reasoning. We conducted
018
                  a comprehensive, and quantitative evaluation of 15 MLLMs using four diverse
                  prompting strategies, including Chain-of-Thought and Step-Back. The best scores
019
                  achieved on P OLY MATH are ∼ 41%, ∼ 36%, and ∼ 27%, obtained by Claude-3.5
020
                  Sonnet, GPT-4o and Gemini-1.5 Pro respectively - highlighting the logical and
021               visual complexity of these questions. A further fine-grained error analysis reveals
022               that these models struggle to understand spatial relations and perform drawn-out,
023               high-level reasoning. This is further strengthened by our ablation study estimating
024               MLLM performance when given textual descriptions in place of diagrams. As
025               evidenced by ∼ 4% improvement over textual descriptions as opposed to actual
026               images, we discover that models do not truly comprehend visual diagrams and the
027               spatial information therein, and are thus prone to logical errors. Finally, we evaluate
028               the OpenAI o1 models and find that their performance only matches the human
029
                  baseline, highlighting the difficulty of the benchmark. The results on P OLY MATH
                  highlight the room for improvement in multi-modal reasoning and provide unique
030
                  insights to guide the development of future MLLMs 1 .

034   1       I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Large Language Models (LLMs) (Brown et al., 2020; Jiang et al., 2024; Touvron et al., 2023a;
037   Achiam et al., 2023) and Multi-modal Large Language Models (MLLMs) (OpenAI, 2023c; Team
038   et al., 2023; Su et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023b) have rapidly become a pivotal area of research.
039
      MLLMs with robust reasoning capabilities in visual contexts can solve complex educational problems
      (Seo et al., 2015; Wang et al., 2017), support analysts with logical queries on statistical data (Wu
040
      et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2023), and contribute to advanced research areas such as theorem proving and
041
      scientific discovery (Taylor et al., 2022; Dong et al., 2023; Trinh et al., 2024). Despite their impressive
042   performance in various assessments of human-like intelligence, these models still exhibit notable
043   shortcomings on tasks requiring cognitive and logical reasoning, such as commonsense numerical
044   reasoning, scientific problem-solving, and abstract puzzles (Wang et al., 2023b; Lu et al., 2023a).
045   Existing evaluation benchmarks (Fu et al., 2023a; Liu et al., 2023d; Li et al., 2023b; Fu et al., 2023b;
046   Sun et al., 2024) have focused primarily on specific concrete domains. While general-purpose visual
047   question-answering (VQA) datasets capture some elements of mathematical reasoning, a systematic
048   investigation into abstract and general cognitive reasoning which are essential for tasks like visual
049   puzzles remains an underexplored frontier.
050   In this paper, we present P OLY MATH, a benchmark specifically crafted to evaluate the complex
051   multi-modal cognitive reasoning capabilities of MLLMs. We propose a task taxonomy to guide
052   the development of P OLY MATH: (1) we identify ten distinct reasoning skills, including spatial

101

103   diagram in these questions, model accuracy improves by ∼4-7%. We also conduct an error analysis
104   on Claude-3.5 Sonnet, Gemini-1.5 Pro and GPT-4o, and find that the most common errors stem
105   from misunderstanding diagrams (∼ 60%), misidentifying logical patterns (∼ 25%), and forgetting
106   relational information (∼ 12%). Finally, we evaluate OpenAI o1 models (OpenAI, 2024b) on without

132   2   R ELATED W ORK
133
134   The development of MLLMs builds on the progress of LLMs (Touvron et al., 2023a;b; OpenAI,
135   2023a; Jiang et al., 2024) and large vision models (Kirillov et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023d;c;e).
136   These models extend LLMs to handle a wider range of tasks across multiple modalities, including
137   2D images (Li et al., 2022; Dai et al., 2023; Alayrac et al., 2022; Li et al., 2023a), 3D point clouds
138   (Guo et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2023b), audio (Han et al., 2023; Su et al., 2023), and video (Zhang
139   et al., 2023a; Chen et al., 2023a). Notable examples like OpenAI’s GPT-4V (OpenAI, 2023c) and
140   Google’s Gemini (Team et al., 2023) demonstrate advanced visual reasoning capabilities, setting
141
      new benchmarks in the multimodal space.
142   As MLLMs rapidly advance (Li et al., 2023c), there is a growing need for benchmarks that evaluate
143   mathematical problem-solving in visual contexts. Existing benchmarks, such as GeoQA (Chen et al.,
144   2021a), VQA (Goyal et al., 2017), and UniGeo (Chen et al., 2022a), focus mostly on geometric
145   problems. Other efforts target skills in abstract scenes, geometry diagrams, charts, and synthetic
146   images (Chen et al., 2022a; Masry et al., 2022). Recent datasets also assess external knowledge,
147
      commonsense reasoning, and scientific or medical understanding (Zhang et al., 2023g). MathVista (Lu
      et al., 2023a) expands multimodal math tasks, while MMMU (Yue et al., 2023a) focuses on college-
148
      level problems. Prior work evaluates LLMs across diverse domains like QA, mathematics, and
149
      science (Bubeck et al., 2023; Nori et al., 2023), while recent research (Zhang et al., 2023f) explores
150   whether models like GPT-4V perform vision and language tasks independently or together.
151
152   Existing extensive benchmarks (Fu et al., 2023a; Liu et al., 2023d; Li et al., 2023b; Xu et al.,
153
      2023a) primarily focus on concrete, real-world problems within specific domains. These benchmarks
      often include comparatively simple diagram interpretation questions involving plots or mathematical
154
      questions related to geometry, which primarily evaluate models’ abilities to parse information from a
155
      single image and solve problems using well-established logical principles and formulae. However,
156   they do not sufficiently test models’ capabilities in abstract visual reasoning, including spatial
157   recognition, visual logic and puzzle solving, and pattern recognition. This limitation represents a
158   notable gap, as visual puzzle tasks require logical leaps that differ fundamentally from reasoning
159   patterns over textual or linguistic problems. Moreover, spatial reasoning questions assess models’
160   abilities to internalize and manipulate configurations in 3D space, as well as reason over spatial
161   information and infer implicit relationships based on positional data. This category of questions
      aligns closely with human cognition and reasoning abilities, and evaluating model performance

      3     C URATING P OLY MATH
188
189
      P OLY MATH is curated mainly from questions directed at students taking the National Talent Search
190
      Examination, a nationwide competitive exam held by the National Council of Educational Research
191
      and Training of India. These questions and their solutions are created by experts in their fields and
192   rigorously peer-reviewed, and thus contain minimal errors. These questions aim to assess Scholastic
193   Aptitude (SAT), or the ability to recall domain-specific scientific and mathematical knowledge, as
194   well as Mental Ability (MAT), or the ability to think logically and apply a range of analytical skills.
195   We catalog the skills assessed by each sample along the categorization schema defined in Table 1.
196

      3.1   C OLLECTION P IPELINE
198
199   To guarantee high-quality data, we manually collected image snippets and engineered a streamlined,
200   automated framework for curation and annotation. Continuous human reviews were conducted
201   throughout the process, ensuring quality and preventing error propagation.
202
203          • Step 1: We generate a universally unique identifier (UUID) for a given question paper to
204            identify all the questions curated from it.
205
206
             • Step 2: Annotators manually collected separate snippets of each question and their associated
               contextual information (including disconnected pieces) that apply to multiple questions.
207
208          • Step 3: An image merging script automatically identified and merged question images (in
209            case the question gets split by pages) with their relevant context images.
210
             • Step 4: We used an LLM to transcribe the questions and their ground truth answers. We
211
               also generate additional metadata, including category (§3.2), whether it contains a diagram
212
               (Fig 3), and image description (§3.3). A manual check was performed to ensure the quality
213            of the generated metadata.
214
215          • Step 5: An annotation file, where each row corresponds to a question, is automatically
               created and populated.

251   3.2   DATASET CATEGORIZATION
252
253   We develop a categorization schema that catalogues questions on basis of the information provided
254   and the type of reasoning assessed by the question. Based on the continuous human evaluation during
255   collection, we identify 10 distinct question categories. We enumerate these categories along with
256   their definitions in Table 1. We further distinguish between questions with diagram and without
257   diagram. The with diagram questions are designed around the information presented in the diagrams
258   (Fig 3). We show examples of with diagram and without diagram questions for each category in
259   §F. The overall per-category distribution, along with the with diagram and without diagram split, is
260
      visualized in Figure 2.

263   3.3   A DDITIONAL METADATA
264
265   The complexity of collected question images and the heavy presence of diagram-based reasoning
266   tasks makes P OLY MATH a challenging multi-modal benchmark. To make P OLY MATH usable for
267   both text and vision model evaluations, we provide transcriptions of questions and answers. To further
268   facilitate text-based evaluation, we generate detailed, human-vetted text descriptions of attached
269   diagrams such that a human could visualize the image based on this description (Fig 3). Results on
      text-only characterization of questions in our dataset can be found in §4.3.

290
291   Following the collection and annotation process, we conduct a comprehensive quality check. We

      [3] missing vital information. We also discard samples with noticeable watermarks and other visual

294
      ground truth answers. Concurrently, we verify that the questions belong to their assigned categories,
295
      and correct any observed misalignments therein.

      4.3   E XPERIMENTAL A NALYSIS
455
456   MLLMs Rely More on Image Descriptions than Image To evaluate the visual reasoning ca-
457   pabilities of closed-source models, we conducted inference on the test-img subset, which contains
458   questions with diagrams. Additionally, we generated a text-only version of test-img by replacing all
459   diagrams with detailed textual descriptions. Both experiments were carried out in a zero shot setting.
460   Our analysis reveals three key findings. First, we observed a noticeable decline in performance on
461   test-img, particularly for models like GPT-4o and Claude-3.5 Sonnet, compared to their results on
462
      the testmini subset. This suggests that both models perform well on questions without diagrams,
      and their decreased accuracy on test-img is largely due to the presence of diagram-based problems.
463
      Second, when we replaced the diagrams in test-img with text descriptions, the performance of all
464
      models improved by approximately ∼ 3 − 4%, indicating that the models struggle with visualizing
465   diagrams and benefit from textual representations. Finally, we evaluated popular text-only LLMs
466   such as LLaMA-3 (70B), Reka Flash, and Mistral Large on the text-description version of test-img.
467   Their scores (∼ 15%) were significantly lower than those of the MLLMs (∼ 27%), underscoring the
468   advantage of multi-modal models in handling visually-grounded tasks.
469
470   A Closer Look at Model Errors We analysed total of 236 samples where all three state of the art
471   MLLMs (Claude-3.5 Sonnet, GPT-4o and Gemini-1.5 Pro) gave incorrect answers on testmini. Based
472   on the manual inspection of the responses, we identified 7 types of errors that MLLMs make (Table
473   6). The total error distribution of all three models is present in Table 11. Qualitative examples for
474   category-wise errors are present in §H. The most common error on this dataset was Logical Flaw (LF),
475   occurring in nearly ∼ 60% of incorrect samples. Spatial Misunderstanding (SM), which involves
476   a lack of understanding of diagram structure and content, was a close second (∼ 25%). Figure 4
477
      shows the category-wise distribution of the two types of error. These errors were most prevalent in
      OD, PR, and SC category of questions, as making uncommon logical leaps and fully comprehending
478
      visual information (which models fall short of) is integral to solving these questions. Additionally,
479
      in questions involving extrapolation over multiple weakly connected data points, models came to
480   conclusions that contradicted earlier data, pointing to a lack of information retention. Finally, we
481   find that models fell into the same fallacious reasoning patterns as one another - for example, making
482   the assumption that a pattern holds across each row, when the correct reasoning involves a pattern
483   replicated across columns. The category with the highest % of shared errors was PR, where we
484   observed that GPT4-o, Gemini-1.5 Pro, and Claude-3.5 Sonnet followed the same incorrect reasoning
485   structure on nearly 80% of the analysed samples. Thus, despite their differences, in practice we see
      that MLLMs share the same strengths and shortcomings. For more details, see §G.

526
527   In this work, we introduce P OLY MATH, a benchmark designed to systematically analyze the
528   mathematical reasoning capabilities of state-of-the-art models in visually complex scenarios. Our
529   evaluation of 14 prominent foundation models highlights that significant advancements have been
530   made, especially with the GPT-4o and Claude-3.5 Sonnet models. However, a substantial gap of ∼
531
      24% still exists between Claude-3.5 Sonnet, the best-performing model, and human performance. This
      disparity sets a clear direction for future research, emphasizing the need for models that can seamlessly
532
      integrate mathematical reasoning with visual comprehension. Moreover, our analysis of model
533
      reasoning errors and experiments on samples containing diagrams and their textual representations
534   offer valuable insights for future investigations.

       A    L IMITATION AND FUTURE WORK
1042
1043
       Our benchmark, P OLY MATH, makes key contributions by integrating mathematical and visual tasks.
       While we have made progress in evaluating model performance, we recognize certain limitations.
1044
       One limitation is dataset coverage. Although P OLY MATH covers a wide range of tasks and visual
1045
       contexts, some mathematical problems and visual types may be underrepresented. Additionally,
1046   focusing on mathematical reasoning within visual contexts, especially in domains like competitive
1047   high-school-level questions involving problems in spatial and logical reasoning, requires a more
1048   labor-intensive data collection process than text-only or general-purpose datasets. Consequently, the
1049   scalability and generalizability of our benchmark to other areas remain challenging. Annotations
1050   were performed by the authors meticulously, however, due to the diversity of questions and images
1051   appearing in these sources, the annotations lack a consistent format.
1052   In future iterations, our benchmark will aim to cover a wider range of problems and visual contexts,
1053   with unified and comprehensive annotations. This benchmark is part of an ongoing research effort,
1054   and we are committed to maintaining and refining the datasets, including addressing potential data
1055   noise, based on community feedback. Additionally, we will adapt the leaderboard to reflect new
1056   model developments. In conclusion, despite the limitations of our current approach, P OLY MATH
1057   marks a significant advancement in the field. We remain dedicated to continuously improving the
1058   benchmark to deepen our understanding of AI’s capabilities in mathematical and visual reasoning.

1060   B    E XTENDED R ELATED W ORK
1061
1062   High-quality evaluation datasets and benchmarks are crucial for assessing the progress of machine
1063   learning models in solving real-world tasks (Liao et al., 2021). Mathematical reasoning benchmarks
1064   have emerged as a significant focus area, posing challenges for large foundational models like Large
1065   Language Models (LLMs) and Multi-modal Large Language Models (MLLMs). Initial datasets
1066   addressed basic algebraic (Hendrycks et al., 2021b) and arithmetic (Roy & Roth, 2016) word problems
1067   with limited scope. Subsequent efforts, including MATH (Hendrycks et al., 2021b), GSM8K (Cobbe
1068   et al., 2021), MMLU (Hendrycks et al., 2021a), and others (Zhou et al., 2023; Yue et al., 2023b;
1069   Wang et al., 2024a; Gao et al., 2023a; Luo et al., 2023), expanded the range and quality of textual
1070
       mathematical problems, establishing robust benchmarks for LLM evaluation.
1071   Despite substantial mathematical reasoning encapsulated in visual modalities, most existing bench-
1072   marks (Amini et al., 2019; Cobbe et al., 2021; Mishra et al., 2022; Frieder et al., 2023; Lu et al.,
1073   2023b) are textual only. Moreover, some datasets exhibit performance saturation, with GPT-4 achiev-
1074   ing 92.0% accuracy on GSM-8K (Cobbe et al., 2021), a grade-school mathematics dataset. The rapid
1075   advancement of Large Multimodal Models (LMMs) necessitates robust multimodal benchmarks,
1076
       as current benchmarks (Antol et al., 2015; Kembhavi et al., 2016; Kahou et al., 2017; Mathew
       et al., 2022) provide limited coverage of rigorous scientific domains crucial for general-purpose AI
1077
       assistants.
1078
1079   While these benchmarks assess text-only mathematical reasoning, the rapid progress of MLLMs
       necessitates high-quality benchmarks for evaluating visual mathematical problem-solving. Prior

       Collection Pipeline: To ensure high-quality samples, all data samples were manually collected as
1137
       image snippets from publicly available websites.
1138
1139   We developed a flexible, highly automated data curation framework to streamline the process and
1140   standardize collection and annotation. Continuous human reviews were conducted between steps in
1141   the pipeline to maintain quality and prevent error propagation.
1142
1143              • Step 1: A universally unique identifier (UUID) was generated for each question paper to
1144
                    track all curated questions. This step also updated a shared record containing details of the
                    paper and the annotator’s alias, enabling efficient assignment of questions for peer review.
1145
1146              • Step 2: Annotators manually collected individual snippets of each question, along with
1147                contextual information relevant to multiple questions. For questions requiring additional
1148                context, snippets were labeled accordingly, and only legible, relevant questions (focused
1149                on Mental Ability or Scholastic Ability in mathematics) were included to maintain dataset
1150                integrity.
1151              • Step 3: An image-merging script automatically identified and merged split question images
1152                or context snippets (based on the naming convention) using open-source image processing
1153                tools3 . This resulted in a single image for each sample in the P OLY MATH set of questions
1154                used to test models.
1155
                  • Step 4: The next module in the pipeline created and automatically populated an annotation
1156
                    file, where each row corresponded to a collected sample. Columns included the paper_id
1157                (UUID from Step 1), question number, and image path.
1158
1159              • Step 5: Using an answer key or solution set, LLM-powered transcription extracted the ground
1160                truth answers for each question. Extracted answers were mapped to the corresponding
1161
                    annotation rows, followed by a manual check to ensure alignment with the provided solution
                    and correctness.

       You are given a question designed to test a student on mathematical or logical reasoning. These
1297   questions can be categorized based on the skills and techniques used to solve them.
1298   These are the categories of questions.
1299
1300
1301   Mathematical reasoning: this question purely requires calculations of a mathematical nature. This
1302   includes solving a straightforward equation.
1303
1304   Pattern recognition: this requires the understanding of a one-to-one relationship or pattern and
1305
       replicating that pattern. For example, given the relationship between a and b, determining the
       equivalent of b to c. Questions involving substituting characters and operations in a pre-defined
1306
       pattern fall into this category.
1307
1308   Sequence completion: given a sequence of numbers or figures, this question involves finding the
1309   sequentially next element in a series.
1310
1311   Figure completion: You are given a figure with an arrangement of numbers or characters such that
1312   their relationship to one another based on their position in the figure is consistent. Th goal is to
1313   complete the figure and identify the element missing from a marked position.
1314
1315   Odd one out: given a set of elements, identify the element that is not like the others.
1316
       Spatial reasoning: questions involving reasoning observationally and visualizing the question in
1317
       order to arrive at the answer.
1318
1319   Perspective shift: Questions where a figure is given and you are instructed to morph it according to
1320   the instructions (flip, mirror image, rotate, etc)
1321
1322   Numerical reasoning: questions involving counting the number of elements mentioned. The
1323   elements may be part of a single figure or conform to a specified pattern, but solving these questions
1324   requires counting.
1325
1326   Relative reasoning: the question contains distinct data points, and solving the questions requires
1327
       understanding the relationships between all data points and extrapolating relationships that are not
       explicitly mentioned. Questions involving venn diagrams, family relations, or relative positions
1328
       given a reference point fall into this category.
1329
