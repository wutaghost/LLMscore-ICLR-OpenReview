                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
010            We consider a multi-task contextual bandit setting, where the learner is given a
011            graph encoding relations between the bandit tasks. The tasks‚Äô preference vectors
012
               are assumed to be piecewise constant over the graph, forming clusters. At ev-
               ery round, we estimate the preference vectors by solving an online network lasso
013
               problem with a suitably chosen, time-dependent regularization parameter. We es-
014
               tablish a novel oracle inequality relying on a convenient restricted eigenvalue as-
015            sumption. Our theoretical findings highlight the importance of dense intra-cluster
016            connections and sparse inter-cluster ones. That results in a sublinear regret bound
017            significantly lower than its counterpart in the independent task learning setting.
018            Finally, we support our theoretical findings by experimental evaluation against
019            graph bandit multi-task learning and online clustering of bandits algorithms.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
024
025   Online commercial websites aim to recommend their products to their customers properly, and the
026   performance of these recommendations depends on the knowledge of users‚Äô preferences. Unlike
027
      traditional collaborative-filtering-based methods (Su & Khoshgoftaar, 2009), such knowledge is ini-
      tially unavailable. Therefore, the online recommender systems need to recommend various items to
028
      the users and observe their ratings to explore their preferences. At the same time, the recommender
029
      system should be able to recommend items that attract users‚Äô attention and receive high ratings by
030   exploiting the learned knowledge. The contextual bandit frameworks (Li et al., 2010) have been
031   popularly used to formalize and address this exploration-exploitation trade-off.
032
033
      However, the classical form of contextual bandits (Li et al., 2010; Chu et al., 2011; Abbasi-Yadkori
      et al., 2011) ignores the availability of social networks amongst users and solves the problem for each
034
      user separately. Consequently, such algorithms have some drawbacks when applied to problems
035
      with a large number of users. First, such a large number hinders their computational efficiency.
036   Second, the partial feedback of the bandit settings exposes the algorithms to having weak estimations
037   and impairing their decision-making ability (Yang et al., 2020). Consequently, to improve bandit
038   algorithms‚Äô performance for large-scale applications, structural assumptions that link the different
039   users are usually integrated within bandit algorithms (Cesa-Bianchi et al., 2013; Gentile et al., 2014;
040   Li et al., 2019; Herbster et al., 2021).
041
      Cesa-Bianchi et al. (2013) and yang2020laplacian the prior knowledge of social networks into their
042   contextual bandit algorithms. Both papers propose UCB-style algorithms and exhibit the importance
043   of using the social network graph to achieve lower regrets using Laplacian regularization. The latter
044   regularization promotes smoothness among the preference vectors of users, allowing the transfer of
045   the collected information between them. However, the Laplacian regularization does not account
046   for the smoothness heterogeneity introduced by a piecewise constant behavior over the graph (Wang
047   et al., 2016). On the other hand, algorithms of online clustering of bandits (Gentile et al., 2014;
048   Li et al., 2019) tackle such a piecewise constant behavior by explicitly estimating user clusters.
049   However, their clustering can cause overconfidence in the constructed clusters, potentially leading
050
      to error accumulation.
051   In this paper, we assume access to a graph encoding relations between bandit tasks, and that the
052   task parameter vectors are piecewise constant over the graph. We propose an algorithm that inte-
053   grates the prior knowledge of the piecewise constant structure to update tasks rather than finding the
      clusters explicitly. That way, we mitigate the limitations mentioned above: the piecewise constant

054
      smoothness is naturally integrated into our regularizer, and we do not estimate the clusters so our
055   algorithm does not suffer from overconfidence drawbacks.
056
057   More precisely, we provide the following contributions

               every vertex‚Äôs preference vector using data generated during the interaction between users

079   2   R ELATED WORK
080
081   Lasso contextual bandits. To address the high dimensional setting for linear bandits, several
082   multi-armed bandit papers solve a LASSO (Tibshirani, 1996) problem under different assumptions
083
      (Bastani & Bayati, 2019; Kim & Paik, 2019; Oh et al., 2021; Ariu et al., 2022). They all rely
      on a previously established compatibility or RE condition (BuŐąhlmann & van de Geer, 2011), that
084
      they adapt to the non-i.i.d case resulting from the context selection procedure across rounds. Such
085
      assumptions were also used in the multi-task setting by Cella & Pontil (2021) with a Group Lasso
086   regularization (Yuan & Lin, 2006), and to impose a low-rank structure on the task preference vectors
087   in Cella et al. (2023). In our case, we establish a novel oracle inequality, rather than only generalize
088   an existing one to the non-i.i.d setting, with a newly introduced RE assumption, which can be of
089   independent interest.
090
091   Clustering of bandits. Gentile et al. (2014) introduced sequential clustering of bandits with the
092   CLUB algorithm. The latter starts with a fully connected graph, and then an iterative graph learn-
093   ing process is performed, where edges between users are deleted if their preference vectors are
094   significantly different. As a result, any connected component is seen as a cluster and only one rec-
095   ommendation per cluster is developed. The SCLUB algorithm of Li et al. (2019) generalizes CLUB
096
      via including merging operations in addition to splitting. In contrast to these approaches, Nguyen &
      Lauw (2014) groups users via K-means clustering, and Cheng et al. (2023) rely on hedonic games
097
      for online clustering of bandits. Furthermore, Yang & Toni (2018) make use of community detection
098
      techniques on graphs to find user clusters. Gentile et al. (2017) study the clustering of the contextual
099   bandit problem where their proposed algorithm, named CAB, adaptively matches user preferences
100   in the face of constantly evolving items. Our work fundamentally differs from the previous ones
101   on two aspects. First, we assume access to a graph encoding relations between users, which is
102   more informative than a complete graph. Second, we do not keep track of a model for each cluster,
103   but rather we integrate a prior over the graph via a graph total variation regularizer that enforces a
104   piecewise constant behavior for the estimated preference vectors.
105
106   Multi-task learning. Several contributions assume that the bandit tasks share some underlying
107   structure. In Cella & Pontil (2021), task preference vectors are assumed to be sparse and to share
      their sparsity support, implying that they lie in a low-dimensional subspace with dimensions aligning


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      with the canonical basis vectors. This idea is further generalized in Cella et al. (2023), where the
109   tasks are assumed to be confined to an arbitrary unknown low-dimensional subspace. That work
110   improves upon Hu et al. (2021) by not requiring the knowledge of the small dimension of the task
111   space. It can be considered to solve our problem if the number of clusters is smaller than the
112   dimension, resulting in a low-rank structure. However, our work does not rely on any assumption
113   between the number of clusters and the dimension. The underlying structure linking tasks can also
114   be a graph encoding relations between them (Cesa-Bianchi et al., 2013; Yang & Toni, 2018), which
115   is our case. However, while they assume smoothness as a prior, we assume piecewise constant
116   behavior.

219   an oracle inequality of the network lasso problem given a restricted eigenvalue condition holding

472   7                        C ONCLUSION AND FUTURE PERSPECTIVES
473
474
475   In this work, we proposed a multi-task bandit framework that solves the case where the task prefer-
476   ence vectors are piecewise constant over a graph. To this end, we used the Network Lasso policy to
477   estimate the task parameters, which bypasses explicit clustering procedures. We established a sub-
478   linear regret bound and proved a novel oracle inequality that relies on the small size of the boundary
479   and the high value of the topological centrality index of each node within its cluster. Our exper-
480   imental evaluations highlight the advantage of our method, especially when either the number of
481   dimensions or nodes increases.
482   Due to the technical similarity of our problem with the Lasso, a natural extension would be to ex-
483   tend it to a thresholded approach, in the same vein as (Ariu et al., 2022). Another possible extension
484   would be to use regularization with higher order total variation terms that impose a piecewise poly-
485   nomial signal on a graph, as explained for scalar signals in Wang et al. (2016); Ortelli & van de Geer
      (2019).

1863   Homophily and modularity in social networks Given the large number of users on social net-
1864   works, one may be able to learn their preferences more quickly by leveraging the similarities be-
1865   tween them. This idea relies on the notion of homophily in social networks (McPherson et al., 2001;
1866
       Easley et al., 2010). In modelling social networks, users‚Äô preferences relationships are encoded in
       a graph, where neighboring nodes are users with similar preferences. This graph can be known a
1867
       priori or it can be inferred from previously collected feedback (Dong et al., 2019). Exploiting this
1868
       information and integrating them into bandit algorithms can lead to a significant increase in perfor-
1869   mance Yang et al. (2020). Indeed, the knowledge of user relations allows the algorithm to tackle the
1870   data sparsity issue that is inherent to bandit settings.
1871
1872
       Another fundamental point that can be used to integrate information from social networks is that,
       social networks show large modularity measures (Newman, 2006; Borge-Holthoefer et al., 2011).
1873
       This implies that we have high density of edges within clusters and low density of edges between
1874
       clusters. As a result, users can be clustered based on the graph topology and a preference vector
1875   can be learned for each cluster, substantially reducing the dimensionality of the problem. In other
1876   words, discovering the clustering structure of users can reduce the computational burden of large
1877   social networks. Consequently, there have been attempts in exploiting the clustered structures of
1878   social networks in bandit algorithms (Gentile et al., 2014; Nguyen & Lauw, 2014; Yang & Toni,
1879   2018; Li et al., 2019; Nourani-Koliji et al., 2023; Cheng et al., 2023).
1880
1881
       Bandit meta-learning In contrast to the multi-task setting, meta learning deals with sequentially
1882
       arriving tasks that have to be learnt and generalizing the gained information to improve performance
1883
       for future tasks. Here, as in the multi-task setting, it is assumed that the tasks share some common
1884   structure that is ought to be learnt and exploited. Bilaj et al. (2024) assume that the tasks are sam-
1885   pled from a common distribution and concentrated around an affine subspace learned through PCA
1886   algorithm. The resulting projection matrices could then be exploited to improve learning for new
1887   tasks in an adapted UCB and Thompson sampling approach.
1888   Other lines of work are Cella et al. (2020); Kveton et al. (2021); Basu et al. (2021), which learns
1889   the mean of the distribution under the assumption that the covariance of the prior is known or Peleg
       et al. (2022) which generalizes this assumption and attempts to learn the covariance as well.
