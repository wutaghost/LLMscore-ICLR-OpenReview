011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Protein function prediction is pivotal for uncovering the mechanisms of life pro-
014            cesses. Protein function prediction is a multi-label classification task with nu-
015            merous functional labels that exhibit hierarchical relationships. Relying solely
016
               on unimodal protein features is insufficient for computational models to capture
               complex protein functions adequately. Recently, several methods for protein func-
017
               tion prediction have enhanced the performance by integrating multimodal pro-
018
               tein features. However, since multimodal protein features describe protein func-
019            tions from different perspectives, it is challenging to capture the intricate relation-
020            ships among these multimodal features with different meanings and heterogeneity.
021            Therefore, we propose a multimodal method for protein function prediction that
022            can effectively utilize the intricate internal relationships between spatial structure
023            features (i.e., protein-protein interaction network, subcellular location, and pro-
024            tein domains) and sequence features (i.e., amino acid sequence). In this work,
025            we introduce the Bidirectional Interaction Module (BInM) to facilitate interactive
026            learning between multimodal features by mapping spatial structure and sequence
027
               features of proteins to each other. Moreover, to deal with the difficulty of hier-
               archical multi-label classification in this task, a multi-branch Dynamic Selection
028
               Module (DSM) is designed to select the feature representation that is most favor-
029
               able for current protein function prediction. Comprehensive experiments on hu-
030            man datasets demonstrate that our model outperforms state-of-the-art multimodal-
031            based methods such as Graph2GO, DeepGraphGO, and CFAGO. Furthermore, we
032            assess the efficacy of the features through Davies-Bouldin scores and t-SNE visu-
033            alization experiments. The experimental results show that our method constructs
034            more useful protein representations through bidirectional interaction and dynamic
035            selection mechanisms, leading to improved accuracy in protein function predic-
036            tion. The code in this work will be made public after its acceptance.

039   1   I NTRODUCTION
040
041   Proteins, as essential components of life, play a crucial role in biological research. With the rapid
042   development of bioinformatics (Giamarellos-Bourboulis et al., 2024; Hasselgren & Oprea, 2024),
043   protein function prediction has emerged as a key challenge in the field of biology. Protein functions
044   are standardized through the Gene Ontology (GO) framework. This framework classifies protein
045
      functions into three categories: biological process ontology (BPO), molecular function ontology
      (MFO), and cellular component ontology (CCO)(Aleksander et al., 2023). In recent decades, nu-
046
      merous deep learning-based computational methods (You et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2023) have been
047
      developed to predict protein functions. Most of the previous methods (Kulmanov & Hoehndorf,
048   2020) utilize one of the following types of information: sequence information, structure informa-
049   tion, and protein-protein interaction (PPI) network. In the process of analyzing each type of protein
050   information (Kulmanov & Hoehndorf, 2020), we found that relying on a single-modal feature to
051   predict protein function is often constrained by the conditions of the data itself. For instance, many
052   studies (Fan et al., 2020) have shown that using protein sequence information significantly improves
053   the accuracy of molecular function predictions. However, there are many proteins that share func-
      tional similarities but have dissimilar sequences (Lin et al., 2024). As a result, for proteins with


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      low sequence similarity, the accuracy of predictions may be compromised. Moreover, structure-
055   based methods (Jiao et al., 2023; Gligorijević et al., 2021) leverage the rich structure information
056   provided by resources to improve protein function prediction accuracy. But the high complexity of
057   protein structures and the cost of data acquisition limit the application of structure-based methods
058   (Paysan-Lafosse et al., 2023). Furthermore, the noise introduced during the generation of PPI net-
059   works through high-throughput techniques poses risks to the accuracy of predictions (Chen & Luo,
060   2024). Therefore, integrating these different types of protein data based on multimodal methods and
061   taking advantage of their complementary advantages in functional prediction is an important way to
062   improve the performance of protein function prediction.
063   Recognizing that unimodal representations are insufficient to encapsulate the information contained
064   within proteins, multimodal-based methods have emerged. DeepFRI (Gligorijević et al., 2021)
065   leverages graph convolutional networks to learn features from both protein sequences and structural
066   properties. Graph2GO (Fan et al., 2020)utilized graph networks to consolidate sequence similarity
067   networks and PPI networks, incorporating protein sequence and structural information as node fea-
068   tures for function prediction. However, those using GNNs may amplify noise and face issues with
069
      over-smoothing. To address these limitations, CFAGO(Wu et al., 2023) proposed the incorporation
      of Transformer mechanisms within autoencoders to fuse multimodal protein features.
070
071   However, current multimodal approaches primarily rely on information fusion mechanisms without
072   considering the potential complementarity between different modalities. To address this issue, we
073   propose a bidirectional-interaction and dynamic-selection-driven method (BDGO) that integrates
074   spatial structure information (i.e., PPI network, subcellular location, and protein domains) and se-
075
      quence information (i.e., amino acid sequence) from proteins. In addition, large language models
      play an important role in improving protein function prediction. SaProt(Su et al., 2023), as a large-
076
      scale general-purpose protein language model (PLM) trained on 40 million protein sequence and
077
      structure data, achieved good results in protein function prediction tasks. Inspired by large language
078   models, the protein sequence information in our method is extracted using the pre-trained ProtT5
079   (Elnaggar et al., 2021). In this work, to better learn multimodal information, our proposed BDGO
080   model includes a shared learning branch and an interactive learning branch. In the shared learning
081   branch, we concatenate features from different modalities and perform joint analysis in a unified
082   representation space. Moreover, we introduce the Bidirectional Interaction Module (BInM), which
083   means that each modality not only influences the processing of other modalities but also obtains
084   information from them, thereby enhancing the overall understanding capability.
085   Further, faced with thousands of protein functions, accurately predicting the protein function of a
086   sample remains a challenging issue. Protein function prediction is essentially a complex hierarchi-
087   cal multi-label classification problem. In this situation, we propose the Dynamic Selection Module
088   (DSM) to dynamically select the optimal feature combination for fitting more diverse protein func-
089   tions. Our main contributions can be summarized as follows:
090
091
             • We propose a multimodal feature-based approach for protein function prediction that over-
               comes the limitations of single-modality methods, effectively representing protein func-
092
               tional characteristics to assist the model in understanding protein function.
093
094          • Our proposed BInM incorporates a bidirectional interaction mechanism to promote efficient
095            fusion and information exchange between sequence features and spatial features, enhancing
096
               the model’s ability to capture strong protein information between different modes.
097          • We design the DSM that enables the model to adaptively select channel features most rele-
098            vant to specific functional labels, resulting in enhanced classification performance.

      2.2.2    DYNAMIC S ELECTION M ODULE (DSM)
271
272   In the final feature selection stage, we introduce DSM to enhance key features and mitigate the
273   impact of conflicting ones. As illustrated in Figure 1, this module employs a Mixture-of-Experts
274   (MoE) (Masoudnia & Ebrahimpour, 2014) strategy for dynamical feature selection. The fea-
275   tures extracted by the MSL and MIL branches are combined into the input of DSM, denoted as
276   xdsm = (x1dsm , x2dsm , · · · , xVdsm ), where V is the number of expert networks E(x), each respon-
277
      sible for processing one group of features. We set up a hard gating network G(x) to decide which
      expert should be activated. Unlike traditional MoE systems, which combine outputs from all experts
278
      through weighted averaging, our hard gating network selects a single expert for computation. This
279
      approach allows the model to better adapt to the complex and large-scale protein function predic-
280   tion tasks. The hard gating network is composed of two Linear layers. Inputting xdsm into G(x)
281   for computation yields a V -dimensional one-hot decision vector g = one − hot(arg maxv G(x)v ).
282                                             PV
      Finally, the output of DSM is xϵ = v=1 gv Ev (xv ), where xv represents the v-th groups of the
283   inputted feature of DSM.

720
      is carried out in this section.
721   In this experiment, the pre-training framework is the same as stage 1 in Figure 1. Here, BiMamba
722   Block in the pre-trained encoder can be replaced by Multihead Attention Block (Dosovitskiy et al.,
723   2021), and vice versa. Then, we use the pre-trained encoder for feature extraction in the fine-tuning
724   task. During fine-tuning, we only use an MLP for classification.
725   As shown in Table 6, the model names are formed by combining the feature and framework compo-
726   nents with a hyphen. This indicates that the results are obtained by pre-training the feature with the
727   framework component and then fine-tuning it.
728
      We model the spatial structure features and sequence features using encoders with different compo-
729
      nents. According to the experimental results in Table 6, we find that the encoder using BiMamba
730   Block as a component for modeling spatial structure features shows significant advantages in MFO
731   and CCO. When modeling sequence features, the encoder utilizing Multihead Attention as a com-
732   ponent demonstrates considerable advantages in BPO and CCO. Additionally, we observe that Bi-
733   Mamba Block is significantly more efficient in processing spatial structure information, requiring
734   much less time to predict a protein’s function compared to Multihead Attention. Considering both
735   inference efficiency and performance, we decide to use an encoder with BiMamba Block as the com-
736   ponent for spatial structure features modeling. For sequence information, while BiMamba Block
737   achieves better inference efficiency, we prioritize model performance and opt for Multihead Atten-
738
      tion to model sequence features.
