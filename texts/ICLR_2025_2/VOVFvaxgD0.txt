009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            In this work, we upgrade the multi-head attention mechanism, the core of the
012            Transformer model, to improve efficiency while maintaining or surpassing the
013            previous accuracy level. We show that multi-head attention can be expressed in the
014            summation form. Drawing on the insight that not all attention heads hold equal
015            significance, we propose Mixture-of-Head attention (MoH), a new architecture
016            that treats attention heads as experts in the Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) mechanism.
017            MoH has two significant advantages: First, MoH enables each token to select the
018
               appropriate attention heads, enhancing inference efficiency without compromising
               accuracy or increasing the number of parameters. Second, MoH replaces the stan-
019
               dard summation in multi-head attention with a weighted summation, introducing
020
               flexibility to the attention mechanism and unlocking extra performance potential.
021            Extensive experiments on ViT, DiT, and LLMs demonstrate that MoH outperforms
022            multi-head attention by using only 50%∼90% of the attention heads. Moreover,
023            we demonstrate that pre-trained multi-head attention models, such as LLaMA3-8B,
024            can be further continue-tuned into our MoH models. Notably, MoH-LLaMA3-8B
025            achieves an average accuracy of 64.0% across 14 benchmarks, outperforming
026            LLaMA3-8B by 2.4% by utilizing only 75% of the attention heads. We believe the
027            proposed MoH is a promising alternative to multi-head attention and provides a
028            strong foundation for developing advanced and efficient attention-based models.
029
               The code and pre-train weights will be made available upon publication.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
      Since attention is introduced and becomes a fundamental component of Transformers (Vaswani et al.,
034
      2017), multi-head attention has been the standard architecture for natural language processing (Kenton
035
      & Toutanova, 2019) and computer vision tasks (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021). It is well known that using
036   multiple heads can improve model accuracy. However, not all attention heads hold equal significance.
037   Some works have shown that many attention heads can be pruned without affecting accuracy. For
038   example, Voita et al. (2019) introduces a method to quantify the usefulness of each attention head and
039   prune those that are redundant. Similarly, Michel et al. (2019) challenges the necessity of multiple
040   heads by examining the impact of extensive pruning across various settings. In computer vision,
041   some works also identify attention head redundancy. Bhattacharyya et al. (2023) reduces redundancy
042   to boost performance, while Yun & Ro (2024) develop single-head attention for efficiency. These
043   findings demonstrate that vanilla multi-head attention contains redundant attention heads.
044   Besides, in multi-head attention, each attention head operates in parallel, and the final output is the
045   sum of all attention heads (please refer to Section 3.1). Given that these attention heads operate
046   independently and some may be redundant, we argue that it is possible to build a dynamic attention-
047   head routing mechanism. Such a mechanism would enable each token to adaptively select the
048   appropriate attention heads, enhancing inference efficiency without compromising accuracy.
049   To this end, we introduce Mixture-of-Head attention (MoH), a new architecture that integrates multi-
050   head attention with the Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) mechanism (Jacobs et al., 1991). Specifically, we
051   propose to treat attention heads as experts within the MoE framework. Similar to MoE, MoH consists
052   of multiple attention heads and a router that activates the Top-K heads for each token. Moreover, we
053   replace the standard summation in multi-head attention with a weighted summation. This design
      offers two significant advantages: First, MoH allows each token to select the most relevant attention

054
      heads, improving inference efficiency without sacrificing accuracy or increasing the parameters.
055   Second, by replacing the standard summation in multi-head attention with a weighted summation,
056   MoH enhances the flexibility of the attention mechanism and increases the performance potential.
057   Moreover, to efficiently capture common knowledge across different contexts, we designate a subset
058   of attention heads as shared heads that remain always activated.
059
      We evaluate our proposed MoH across various popular model frameworks, including Vision Trans-
060
      formers (ViT) (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021) for image classification, Diffusion models with Trans-
061   formers (DiT) (Peebles & Xie, 2023) for class-conditional image generation, and Large Language
062   Models (LLMs) (Brown et al., 2020; OpenAI, 2022; Ouyang et al., 2022) for language tasks. We
063   show that MoH achieves competitive performance, or even outperforms multi-head attention with
064   only 50%∼90% of the attention heads. For example, MoH-ViT-B achieves 84.9%/84.7% Top-1
065   accuracy on the ImageNet-1K (Deng et al., 2009) classification benchmark, surpassing well-tuned
066   multi-head attention baselines with only 75%/50% of the attention heads.
067   Furthermore, we demonstrate that pre-trained multi-head attention models, such as LLaMA3-
068   8B (Dubey et al., 2024), can be further continue-tuned into our MoH models. Specifically, us-
069   ing only about 3% (400B tokens) of the original LLaMA3 pre-training data for continue-tuning,
070   MoH-LLaMA3-8B achieves an average accuracy of 64.0% across 14 benchmarks, outperforming
071   LLaMA3-8B by 2.4% by utilizing only 75% of the attention heads. These results show that MoH
072   is a promising alternative to vanilla multi-head attention, laying a solid foundation for developing
073   advanced and efficient attention-based models. The main contributions are summarized as follows:
074
075          • We propose a dynamic attention-head routing mechanism that allows each token to adaptively
076            select the appropriate attention heads, enhancing model performance and inference efficiency
077
               without increasing the number of parameters.
078          • In addition to training from scratch, we demonstrate that pre-trained multi-head attention
079            models, such as LLaMA3-8B, can be further continue-tuned into our MoH models, greatly
080            enhancing the applicability of the proposed MoH method.
081          • A wide range of experiments across various popular model frameworks, including ViT, DiT,
082            and LLMs, confirm that MoH is a promising alternative to vanilla multi-head attention,
083            laying a solid foundation for developing advanced and efficient attention-based models.

086   2   R ELATED W ORK
087
088   Multi-Head Attention. Transformers (Vaswani et al., 2017) have garnered significant interest and
089   success in both natural language processing and computer vision. The success of transformers has
090   been long attributed to the multi-head attention mechanism (Cordonnier et al., 2020). Multi-head
091
      attention mechanism is proposed by Vaswani et al. (2017) to enhance the representation power
      of an attention layer by allowing multiple attention heads to operate on different low-dimensional
092
      projections of the input. The outputs from these heads are then concatenated to form the final result.
093
094   Mixture-of-Experts Models. The Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) method (Du et al., 2022; Lewis et al.,
095   2021; Rajbhandari et al., 2022; Roller et al., 2021; Zhou et al., 2022) is introduced to expand the
096   capacity of deep neural networks without increasing computational costs. In this approach, only
097
      a subset of parameters, known as experts, is activated for each input. Shazeer et al. (2017) first
      introduces an MoE layer between LSTM layers. Switch Transformer (Fedus et al., 2022) further
098
      simplifies the gating mechanism by selecting only the Top-1 expert per token. Gshard (Lepikhin et al.,
099
      2021) improves the Top-2 expert routing strategy. In contrast to MoE, which emphasizes efficient
100   parameter scaling while maintaining manageable computational costs, the proposed MoH focuses on
101   reducing the activation of redundant attention heads without increasing the number of parameters.
102

126   We begin by reviewing the standard multi-head attention mechanism introduced by Vaswani et al.
127
      (2017). The multi-head attention mechanism is based on scaled dot-product attention. Specifically,

135   the query, key, and value, respectively. In self-attention, the input tokens are the same, i.e., X ′ = X,
136   and it is common for the key and value dimensions to be equal, i.e., dv = dk .
137   Concatenation Form.          To enhance the representation power of the attention layer, Vaswani
138   et al. (2017) proposes to allow multiple attention heads to operate on different low-dimensional
139   projections of the input tokens. Specifically, the multi-head attention mechanism computes h different
140   low-dimensional projections of (Q, K, V ), performs scaled dot-product attention for each head,
141   concatenates the results, and applies a final projection to the concatenated output. The concatenation
142   form of the multi-head attention can be formulated as:

144                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                           (2)

      where WQi ∈ Rdin ×dk /h , WK   i
                                        ∈ Rdin ×dk /h , and WVi ∈ Rdin ×dv /h represent the ith projection
147
      matrices for the query, key, and value, respectively. WO ∈ Rdv ×dout is the final projection matrix.
148
149   Summation Form. The multi-head attention mechanism is typically represented in its concatena-
150   tion form. However, from another perspective, if we decompose WO ∈ Rdv ×dout by rows, we can
151   express multi-head attention in a summation form. Specifically, WO can be divided into h matrices
152   by rows, i.e., [WO1 , WO2 , ..., WOh ] = WO , where WOi ∈ Rdv /h×dout . Finally, the summation form
153   of the multi-head attention can then be formulated as:
154
155                                                                                     \textrm {MultiHead}(\bm {X},\bm {X}')=\sum _{i=1}^{h} \bm {H}^{i}\bm {W}_O^{i}. \label {multi-head attention}                                                                                                                                         (3)
156
157   The concatenation form can be viewed as a variant of the summation form, where the sum of the
158   dimensions of all attention heads is exactly equal to the hidden size. As shown in Eq. 3, in standard
159   multi-head attention, each attention head operates in parallel, and the final output is the sum of
160   all attention heads. Since these attention heads function independently, we can build a dynamic
161   attention-head routing mechanism allowing each token to adaptively select the most relevant attention
      heads, improving inference efficiency without compromising accuracy.

      3.2                       M IXTURE - OF -H EAD ATTENTION
163
164   Recently, the Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) method has emerged as a popular approach for scaling the
165   parameters of large language models (Jiang et al., 2024). A typical MoE layer consists of multiple
166   expert networks and a router that activates the Top-K experts. Generally, the number of activated
167   experts K is significantly smaller than the total number of experts to ensure inference efficiency.
168   Heads as Experts.          Inspired by the great success of MoE, we propose Mixture-of-Head at-
169   tention (MoH), which treats attention heads as experts. Specifically, MoH consists of h heads
170   H = {H 1 , H 2 , ..., H h } and a router that activates the Top-K heads. Formally, given input tokens X
171   and X ′ , the output of MoH is the weighted sum of outputs from the K selected heads:
172
173                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                 \textrm {MoH}(\bm {X},\bm {X}')=\sum _{i=1}^{h}g_{i} \bm {H}^{i}\bm {W}_O^{i},                                                               (4)
174
175   where gi represents the routing score. gi is non-zero only when the ith attention head is activated.
176   This design provides two key advantages: On the one hand, MoH enables each token to select the
177   most relevant attention heads, boosting inference efficiency while maintaining accuracy. On the other
178   hand, in contrast to the standard summation in multi-head attention, the weighted summation in MoH
179   enhances the flexibility of the attention mechanism and unlocks performance potential.
180
      Shared Heads. In attention mechanism, some attention heads may capture common knowledge
181   across different contexts, such as grammatical rules in language. Inspired by Dai et al. (2024), we
182   designate a subset of heads as shared heads that remain always activated. By consolidating common
183   knowledge within shared heads, we reduce redundancy among the other dynamically routed heads.
184
      Two-Stage Routing. Moreover, to dynamically balance the weights between shared and routed
185
      heads, we propose a two-stage routing strategy. In this routing strategy, the routing scores are
186
      determined by both the score of each individual head and the score associated with the head type.
187   Specifically, given the tth input token xt ∈ Rdin in X ∈ RT ×din , the routing score gi is defined as:
188
189
190           \quad g_{i}= \begin {cases} \alpha _1\text {Softmax}(\bm {W}_s \bm {x}_{t})_i, &\text {if}\ 1\leq i\leq h_s,\\ \alpha _2\text {Softmax}(\bm {W}_r \bm {x}_{t})_{\rebuttal {i-h_{s}}}, &\text {if}\ (\bm {W}_r \bm {x}_{t})_{\rebuttal {i-h_{s}}}\in \text {Top-K}\big (\{(\bm {W}_r \bm {x}_{t})_{\rebuttal {i-h_{s}}} |h_{s}+1\leq i\leq h\}\big ),\\ 0, & \text {otherwise}, \end {cases} \label {router}
191
192                                                                                                      (5)
193   where hs denotes the number of shared heads. Ws ∈ Rhs ×din and Wr ∈ R(h−hs )×din represent the
194   projection matrices for the shared and routed heads, respectively. The coefficients α1 and α2 balance
195
      the contributions of the shared and routed heads, and are defined as:
196                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                [\alpha _1, \alpha _2]=\text {Softmax}(\bm {W}_h\bm {x}_{t}), \label {alpha}                                                  (6)
197   where Wh ∈ R                                                                                                 2×din
                                                                                                                                                                          is the trainable projection matrix, and din is the hidden size of xt .

      Load Balance Loss Directly training an MoE layer often causes the majority of tokens to be routed
      to a small number of experts, leaving the remaining experts insufficiently trained (Shazeer et al., 2017).
200
      To avoid the unbalanced load in the proposed MoH, following previous MoE methods (Lepikhin
201
      et al., 2021; Wei et al., 2024), we apply a load balance loss. Specifically, for the tth input token
202   xt ∈ Rdin in X ∈ RT ×din , the load balance loss Lb is formulated as:
203
204
          \mathcal {L}_{b}=\sum _{i=h_{s}+1}^{h}f_i P_i,\
f_i=\frac {1}{T}\sum _{t=1}^{T}\mathds {1}(\text {Token $\bm {x}_t$ selects Head $i$}),\ P_i=\frac {1}{T}\sum _{t=1}^{T}\text {Softmax}(\bm {W}_r \bm {x}_{t})_{\rebuttal {i-h_{s}}},
205
206                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                              (7)
207   where T denotes the number of tokens. 1(∗) denotes the indicator function.
208
      Total Training Objective. It is worth noting that the MoH is a general framework. Therefore,
209
      we evaluate our proposed MoH across various popular model frameworks, including Vision Trans-
210
      formers (ViT), Diffusion models with Transformers (DiT), and Large Language Models (LLMs).
211   Depending on the specific task, we require the task-specific loss. Finally, the total training loss is the
212   weighted sum of the task-specific loss Ltask and the load balance loss Lb :
213
                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                               \mathcal {L} = \mathcal {L}_{task} + \beta \mathcal {L}_{b},                      (8)
214
215   where β is the trade-off hyper-parameter to mitigate the risk of routing collapse. By default, the
      weight β for the load balance loss is set to 0.01 for all tasks.

238   4.1    V I T FOR I MAGE C LASSIFICATION
239
240   Model Settings. For Vision Transformers (ViT) (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021), our MoH-ViT models
241
      are implemented based on the TransNeXt (Shi, 2024) framework and trained from scratch on the
      ImageNet-1K dataset (Deng et al., 2009), which contains over 1.2 million images in 1,000 categories.
242
      To ensure a fair comparison, we only replace the standard multi-head attention with the proposed
243
      MoH, while keeping all other training parameters identical to TransNeXt.
244
245   Training Details.      Our MoH-ViT models are trained for 300 epochs using automatic mixed
246   precision across 8 GPUs. We follow the training strategy of TransNeXt, which includes various data
247
      augmentation techniques, including Random Augmentation (Cubuk et al., 2020), Mixup (Zhang,
      2017), CutMix (Yun et al., 2019), and Random Erasing (Zhong et al., 2020). We also apply Label
248
      Smoothing (Szegedy et al., 2016) and DropPath (Huang et al., 2016) to regularize our models. We
249
      optimize our models using AdamW optimizer (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2017) with a gradient clipping
250   norm of 1.0 and a weight decay of 0.05. The initial learning rate is set to 1e-3, with a 5-epoch
251   warm-up starting at 1e-6. A cosine learning rate scheduler (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2016) is employed
252   to decay the learning rate. During training, images are randomly cropped to a size of 224×224. It is
253   worth noting that we do not use Exponential Moving Average (EMA) weights.
254
      Results. As shown in Tab. 1, despite activating only a subset of attention heads, MoH-ViT achieves
255
      highly competitive performance compared to current state-of-the-art methods. For example, MoH-
256   ViT-B achieves 84.9% Top-1 accuracy on the ImageNet-1K classification benchmark with just 75%
257   of the attention head. In contrast, the well-established ViT baseline, TransNeXt, attains a slightly
258   lower accuracy of 84.8% while requiring 100% of the heads to be activated. Tab. 1 demonstrates that
259   MoH-ViT outperforms other models with fewer activated attention heads. This suggests that MoH is
260   a promising alternative to vanilla multi-head attention for vision model design, offering the potential
261   for competitive performance with more efficient attention head usage.

      4.5   A BLATIVE A NALYSIS
473
474   Effect of Each Component of the Proposed MoH. To explore the impact of each component of
475   our MoH method, we provide the ablation results in Tab. 6. “Shared Heads” refers to a subset of
476   attention heads that are always activated. “Two-Stage Routing” represents the dynamic coefficient
477   that balances the weights between shared and routed heads over the routing score, as described in
478   Eq. 5 and Eq. 6. As shown in Tab. 6, shared heads significantly improve model performance by
479   effectively capturing common knowledge, allowing the routed heads to focus more on domain-specific
480   information. Moreover, two-stage routing further enhances model performance by dynamically
481   balancing the weights between shared and routed heads. Our full model achieves the best performance,
482
      demonstrating that both components significantly benefit the attention mechanism.
483   Effect of the Shared Heads Ratio among Activated Heads. In Tab. 7, we provide the ablation
484   study on the shared heads ratio among activated heads. We find that model performance remains
485   relatively consistent across a wide range of shared heads ratios (from 13.9% to 74.0%). These results
      indicate that the performance of the model is stable as long as the shared heads ratio is not extreme.

509   5         D ISCUSSION
510
511   Visualization of the Head Load Distribution. As shown in Fig. 3, we observe significant variation
512   in attention head assignments across different categories and task topics, indicating that the MoH
513   model adapts to diverse tasks by employing distinct head assignment patterns. This characteristic of
514   MoH allows different attention heads to focus on different types of tasks, making parameter utilization
515   more efficient than multi-head attention. For additional visualizations of MoH-LLaMA3-8B and a
516
      detailed analysis of the head load distribution, please refer to Appendix D.
517   The Difference between MoH and MoA. We clarify the differences between MoH and
518   MoA (Zhang et al., 2022) from the following three aspects. First, in terms of motivation, the
519   goal of MoH is to improve the efficiency and performance of the attention mechanism without
520   increasing the number of parameters. In contrast, MoA shares the motivation of MoE, which is to
521   expand model parameters while keeping inference costs low. Therefore, the model settings of MoH
522
      are more stringent than those of MoA. Second, in terms of methodology, our MoH introduces
      shared heads and two-stage routing to enhance the standard MoE method. More importantly, we show
523
      that pre-trained multi-head attention models can be further continue-tuned into our MoH models,
524
      greatly improving the applicability of the proposed MoH method. In contrast, MoA directly combines
525   multi-head attention with MoE. Due to the adoption of shared keys and values, MoA must be trained
526   from scratch, which limits its applicability. Finally, in terms of model frameworks, our MoH is
527   validated across various popular model frameworks and tasks, including ViT, DiT, and decoder-only
528   LLMs, while MoA is only validated for language tasks.

      6         C ONCLUSION
531
532
      In this paper, we introduce MoH, a promising alternative to multi-head attention. MoH enables
533
      each token to adaptively select the appropriate attention heads, improving both model performance
534   and inference efficiency without increasing the number of parameters. Extensive experiments
535   across various popular model frameworks, including ViT, DiT, and LLMs, demonstrate that MoH
536   outperforms multi-head attention, even when using only 50%∼90% of the attention heads. More
537   encouragingly, we show that pre-trained multi-head attention models, such as LLaMA3-8B, can be
538   further continue-tuned into our MoH models, significantly enhancing the applicability of the proposed
539   MoH method. This work represents a promising step toward advanced and efficient attention-based
      models, which may be meaningful and helpful to both the research and industrial communities.

875
876   A.1   L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
877
878
      In this section, we delineate the limitations of our work and outline avenues for future research.
879   Heterogeneous Attention Heads. We find that different attention heads operate in parallel within
880   the attention mechanism, suggesting that different heads can have varying hidden sizes. Future work
881   could explore the use of heterogeneous attention heads based on our MoH framework.
882   Lower Activation Rate. Currently, MoH outperforms multi-head attention by utilizing only
883   50%∼90% of the attention heads. However, this is still a relatively high proportion. Future work
884   could aim to further optimize MoH, reducing head activation to less than 50%.
885
      Multimodal Inputs. Effectively processing information from multiple modalities in the attention
886
      mechanism remains an open question. Recent work (Wan et al., 2024) has shown that visual and
887
      textual tokens exhibit distinct attention patterns in multi-head attention. Future work could explore
888   the attention patterns of MoH with different modal inputs, for example within multimodal large
889   language models (Jin et al., 2024; Lin et al., 2023; 2024; Liu et al., 2024).
890
891
      More Downstream Tasks. We evaluate our proposed MoH across various popular model frame-
      works, including ViT for image classification, DiT for class-conditional image generation, and LLMs
892
      for language tasks. Future work can explore the application of MoH in more downstream tasks, such
893
      as audio tasks and multimodal tasks.
894
895   More Parameters. Due to computational constraints, the maximum number of MoH model
896   parameters in our experiments is limited to 8B (MoH-LLaMA3-8B). However, our MoH method is
897
      highly generalizable and can be scaled to larger models in future research.
