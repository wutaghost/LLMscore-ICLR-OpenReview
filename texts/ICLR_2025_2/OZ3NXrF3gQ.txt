009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011              As AI capabilities advance, their rapid progress is not keeping pace with the need
012              for safe and value-aligned algorithms, raising concerns about autonomous systems.
013              E.g., maximizing expected return in reinforcement learning can lead to unintended
014              and potentially harmful consequences. This work introduces Reward-free Policy
015              Optimization (RFPO), a method that prioritizes goal-oriented policy learning over
016              reward maximization by eliminating rewards as the agent’s learning signal. Our
017
                 approach learns a world model that simulates backward in time, and then uses it
                 to construct a directed graph for planning, and finally learning a goal-conditioned
018
                 policy from the graph. The algorithm has two requirements: (1) the goal has to be
019
                 defined, and (2) the agent needs sufficient world knowledge, enabling it to plan.
020              This method removes the risks associated with reward hacking and discourages
021              unintended behaviors by allowing for human oversight. Additionally, it provides
022              a framework for humans to build transparent and high-level algorithms by using
023              the (low-level) learned policies. We demonstrate the effectiveness of RFPO on
024              maze environments with pixel observations, where the agent successfully reaches
025              arbitrarily selected goals and follows human-designed algorithms. In conclusion,
026              RFPO enables agents to learn policies without rewards and provides a framework
027              for creating high-level behaviors.
028
029                    Keywords: Reward-free, Goal-conditioned, World Models, Planning

      In recent years, the rapid advancement of artificial intelligence (AI) capabilities has brought great
034   opportunities for innovation (Schrittwieser et al., 2020; Brown & Sandholm, 2019; Brown et al.,
035   2020; Radford et al., 2021) and the potential to revolutionize various industries (Levine et al., 2018;
036   Andrychowicz et al., 2020; Kalashnikov et al., 2018). However, the deployment of capable and
037   autonomous AI systems has raised significant concerns (Goodall, 2014; Bostrom & Yudkowsky, 2018;
038   Amodei et al., 2016) about safety (Bengio et al., 2023), unintended emerging behavior (Russell, 2019)
039   and misalignment with human values (Christiano et al., 2017; Hadfield-Menell et al., 2016). Currently,
040   many AI systems are trained using Reinforcement Learning (RL) (Ye et al., 2021) or, in the case of
041   large language models (LLMs), through Reinforcement Learning with Human Feedback (RLHF)
042
      (Ouyang et al., 2022). While these methods have led to considerable successes, they fundamentally
      rely on reward functions for behavior optimization. A major concern lies in the fact that a reward
043
      function, which is designed to guide the learning process, may not always align with human values.
044
      In some cases, the agent may find hidden loopholes to maximize the reward signal in a way that is
045   not intended by its designers (Leike et al., 2017; Baker et al., 2019).
046

      2017) in which a super-intelligent AI system is tasked to maximize paperclip production. It eventually

049
      and society. A game, inspired by this idea, can be played online1 to understand the full scope of
050
      this issue. This thought experiment highlights an important problem: rewards usually represent
051   a proxy target for an underlying objective that is hidden and difficult to formalize (Leike et al.,
052   2017). For example, while the fundamental biological objective (Dawkins, 2016; Williams, 2018)

110   We consider reward-free Markov decision processes (MDP) with discrete time steps t ∈ N, states st ∈
111   S, and discrete actions at ∈ A = {1, . . . , m}, which come from a policy at ∼ π(at | st ). Episodes
112   terminate based on a boolean variable dt ∈ {0, 1} and the transitions of states and termination
113   indicators follow the environment dynamics (st+1 , dt ) ∼ p(st+1 , dt | st , at ). Finally, the objective
114   on the reward-free MDP is to derive a policy π that maximizes the probability of reaching one of the
115   defined goal states s∗ ∈ S ∗ ⊂ S. A normal MDP can be viewed as a reward-free MDP by ignoring
116   the reward function and adding a set S ∗ of goal states.
117   In the following, we introduce Reward-free Policy Optimization (RFPO) which is a goal-driven
118   approach that eliminates rewards as the learning signal. This method is based on the idea to plan
119   backwards in time from goal states to as many previous states as possible. To facilitate the planning
120   we discretize the observed states st .

125   the representation and world model. In the continuous setting, even small prediction errors from the
126

      challenging to construct accurate graphs for planning, as for each simulation step we face the problem
127

131

132   by discretizing the state space S into distinct latent codes Z, hereby ensuring convergence to valid
133   representations. The discretization leads to partially overlapping simulation trajectories, that enable
134   us to construct robust graphs for planning. The discrete representation is learned with an encoder that
135   performs this mapping, which is described in the following section.
136

194   2.2    G RAPH CONSTRUCTION AND PLANNING
195
196   Given a backward world model, we next describe how to construct a graph for planning (also see
197   Figure 1), that helps later to select good transitions for policy learning. Since the whole planning
198   process takes place in the latent space, we will call the latent states in the following simply states.
199   Algorithm 1 illustrates the planning process: initially, we generate numerous simulations from a
200
      given goal state z ∗ using the backward world model. These simulations serve as the foundation for
      constructing a graph and then for identifying sub-goals to start further exploration. By continuously
201
      generating new simulations based on these sub-goals and iteratively updating the graph, the algorithm
202
      incrementally creates a map of the (latent) state space. Eventually, the planning results in a shortest
203   path estimator called SPE. More details for each step follow in the next paragraphs.
204
205   Simulations. Each simulation starts at a goal state z ∗ (or at a selected sub-goal) and unrolls it
206
      into the past to create trajectories [z ∗ , at , zt , at−1 , zt−1 , . . . , at−k , zt−k ] of the environment. These
      simulations use random actions to avoid any selection bias. After completion, each sequence is split
207
      into single-step transitions (z ∗ , at , zt ), (zt , at−1 , zt−1 ), . . . , (zt−k+1 , at−k , zt−k ).
208
209   Graph. The single-step transitions create a directed graph G = (V, E). Every node nzt ∈ V
210   represents a state zt and each edge nzt → nzt−1 represents a transition, but backward in time. For
211   graph construction, we consider each single-step transition (zt , at−1 , zt−1 ) and add nodes nzt , nzt−1
212
      and edge nzt → nzt−1 if they do not yet exist. The actions are not assigned to an edge or added to
      the graph. They will be used later in the policy learning process. Furthermore, each node nzt has a
213
      visit counter v(nzt ) ∈ N, which is incremented with each occurrence of zt in the simulations.
214
215   Sub-goal selection. The expansion of the graph by sub-goal simulations is an important component.
      This process allows RFPO to iteratively explore the state space and extend the goal reachability as far

262
      sequence reaches the goal state. However, these simulations cannot be carelessly used for optimization
263

264   extensively investigated in the literature (Wu et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2021b), highlighting the need

268   essentially transforms randomly generated simulations into useful artificial expert demonstrations to
269   learn from. For each pair (zt , at ), we look at its forward transition (zt , at , zt+1 ) and check with the
      shortest path estimator whether zt+1 gets the agent closer to the goal. If so, the transition is added to

301   2.4   H IGH - LEVEL BEHAVIOR ALGORITHMS BY HUMAN DESIGN
302
      In this paper, we draw inspiration from the concepts of Marvin Minsky’s “Society of Minds” (Minsky,
303
      1988), which suggests that intelligent behavior arises from the interactions among numerous simpler
304
      processes, similar to how a society functions through the contributions of its individuals. By leveraging
305   the SPE as a critic (as defined in Society of Minds), our method allows human or human-aligned
306   LLM involvement (Ahn et al., 2024) for goal formulation and more importantly for programming
307   of high-level behaviors using the learned low-level policies. In our case, this translates to multiple
308   policies (or “minds”) working collaboratively yet independently. For instance, a recovery policy can
309   be activated when the currently active policy fails, which is evaluated by the critic. In the experimental
310   section we develop some high-level behavior algorithms and apply them.
311   Automatic progress tracking. Each learned policy memorizes its assigned goal and can continuously
312   evaluate progress towards this target through the shortest path estimator, which should be stored
313   along the corresponding policy. This allows high-level algorithms to monitor the distance to the goal
314   and possibly switch policies once a goal is reached or no reasonable progress is made.

398   3.2   H IGH - LEVEL ALGORITHMS BY HUMAN DESIGN
399
400   To demonstrate how RFPO helps humans to transparently control AI systems, we consider two
401
      scenarios: (i) the BlockBadBehaviorEnv environment, and (ii) SimonEnv environment. In general,
      programming agents resembles the job of a conductor in an orchestra: While the low-level policies
402
      (learned by RFPO) act on their goals, the high-level human-made algorithm supervises them and
403
      intervenes when a correction is needed. Hereby, we are able to create novel behaviors that are
404   currently difficult to achieve in the reward-based reinforcement learning setting. The complete Python
405   implementation for each algorithm can be found in Appendix A.
406
407
      BlockBadBehaviorEnv. This environment (see Fig. 4) initially allows certain shortcut solutions,
      which are blocked during evaluation time, e.g., due to the emergence of unintended behavior. A
408
      prominent example occurs in OpenAI’s “Hide and Seek” publication (Baker et al., 2019) where the
409
      unintended emerging behavior is called “box surfing”. BlockBadBehaviorEnv simulates a similar
410   situation by placing hidden walls in the environment during testing. In the case of “Hide and Seek”,
411   this corresponds to changing the environment physics or developing other prevention mechanisms.
412   We compare two high-level algorithms based on low-level RFPO policies with PPO and DQN (results
413   are shown in Table 3):
414           • HL-RND. This algorithm (High Level - RaNdoM) aims to maintain progress towards a
415             goal by dynamically adjusting its actions. It monitors the estimated distance to the goal via
416             SPE and, if progress stops for any reason, switches to random actions temporarily. After a
417             short period of random actions, the algorithm resumes using the goal policy and monitors
418             the SPE progress again.
419           • HL-ADV. The algorithm (High Level - ADVanced) extends HL-RND by allowing an
420             additional helper policy, that is trained separately using a human-selected goal state. In our
421             case, this policy is conditioned on a state along the top pathway to improve adaptability
422             at reaching the real goal. The idea is that a supervisor or teacher (e.g. human or LLM) is
423             stepping in and informally expressing: "RFPO, if you get stuck using your preferred strategy,
424
                you can also follow the longer path to reach your goal.". HL-ADV primarily uses the goal
                policy to monitor the estimated distance to the goal via SPE. If progress stops, it temporarily
425
                switches to random actions. But should the random action intervention repeatedly not
426
                result in progress, the helper policy is activated. Once the helper policy reaches its goal
427             (SPE(nz ) = 0, a position in the middle of the top pathway), the algorithm switches back to
428             the goal policy and resumes monitoring the SPE progress.

443   4    R ELATED W ORK
444
445
      AI safety. In the reinforcement learning literature various ideas how to align AI behavior with human
446
      values have been proposed: Deep Reinforcement Learning from Human Preferences (Christiano et al.,
447   2017) integrates human feedback into the training loop, enabling the agent to learn human-aligned
448   behaviors. Leike et al. (2018) propose Value Alignment through Reward Modeling (Leike et al.,
449   1811) to design scalable models that align agent behavior with human values via reward modeling,
450   and Learning from Human Preferences in Reinforcement Learning (Ibarz et al., 2018) combines
451   human feedback and demonstrations to refine agent behavior, even in complex environments like
452   Atari games. In a similar spirit, our work also proposes an approach for value alignment. However, it
453   is purely goal-driven without referring to rewards.
454   Goal-conditioning. Reaching goals has been widely used in combination with reinforcement
455   learning: Schaul et al. (2015) pioneered Universal Value Function Approximators (UVFAs) (Schaul
456   et al., 2015), which extend traditional value functions to incorporate goals as additional inputs,
457   enabling the agent to generalize across different tasks. Florensa et al. (2018) introduced automatic
458   goal generation for RL agents (Florensa et al., 2018), and Nasiriany et al. (2019) presented goal-
459   conditioned imitation learning combined with planning (Nasiriany et al., 2019). Kidambi et al. (2020)
460   develop MOReL(Kidambi et al., 2020), an offline MBRL approach to learn a pessimistic estimate of
461
      the underlying MDP and optimize a policy while minimizing the model bias. Chebotar et al. (2021)
      learn a goal-conditioned Q-function on offline data where they use sub-sequences and relabeling
462
      techniques (Chebotar et al., 2021). Reaching goals is also the central idea of our work, however, we
463
      differ in the fact that we base the policy learning purely on reaching predefined goals along shortest
464   paths on backward graphs and not by using any reward signal.
465
466   Backward world models. Unrolling backwards in time is an idea that has been rarely used in
467
      reinforcement learning. There are only a few examples, that use backward world models to improve
      policy learning, including Goyal et al. (2018); Edwards et al. (2018); Lai et al. (2020); Wang et al.
468
      (2021a); Chelu et al. (2020). Most similar to our work is Pan et al. (2022), who combine imitation
469
      learning with backward world models (Pan & Lin, 2022) where backward simulations are treated
470   as sub-optimal expert demonstrations to improve the expected return of a forward policy. However,
471   none of these papers are fully reward-free and use the backward models for graph construction and
472   for generating goal-reaching trajectories.

476   5    L IMITATIONS
477
478
      Reward-free Policy Optimization (RFPO) introduces a method to learn policies without rewards, but it
479
      has limitations. First, the algorithm requires that the latent trajectory representations overlap in order
480   to construct robust graph structures. If trajectories do not overlap sufficiently, the resulting graph may
481   be sparse and less useful for planning, degrading policy performance. Second, the goals in RFPO
482   must be explicitly identified by humans beforehand, which limits the system’s autonomy and requires
483   human intervention for setting up the initial goals. This could be problematic in environments where
484   goals are dynamic or not easily defined. Lastly, RFPO depends on a well-trained world model to
485   generate accurate backward simulations. Inaccuracies in the world model can lead to faulty graph
      construction and sub-optimal policy learning.

      6   C ONCLUSION
487
488
      In this paper, we explore a novel approach for reward-free policy learning which we call Reward-free
489   Policy Optimization (RFPO). Using a backward world model and graph search, RFPO directly
490   conditions policies on desired states, eliminating rewards as the optimization signal. This approach
491   can help alleviating issues like reward hacking and unintended behaviors, promoting safer autonomous
492   systems. A key component, the shortest path estimator (SPE), refines sub-optimal simulations into
493   high-quality training data, effectively creating artificial expert demonstrations. Our framework
494   enhances human oversight and control, facilitating transparent, high-level algorithms with learned
495   low-level policies. Experiments in maze environments demonstrate that RFPO enables agents to
496   achieve and monitor via SPE multiple goals that can be employed in sophisticated human-designed
497
      algorithms. By going backwards in time from human-aligned goals, instead of carelessly searching
      for rewards, this work aims to provide a safer approach for learning challenging tasks while obeying
498
      to intended behaviors.
