009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            While large language models (LLMs) have revolutionized natural language pro-
012            cessing with their task-agnostic capabilities, visual generation tasks such as im-
013            age translation, style transfer, and character customization still rely heavily on
014            supervised, task-specific datasets. In this work, we introduce Group Diffusion
015            Transformers (GDTs), a novel framework that unifies diverse visual generation
016
               tasks by redefining them as a group generation problem. In this approach, a set
               of related images is generated simultaneously, optionally conditioned on a subset
017
               of the group. GDTs build upon diffusion transformers with minimal architectural
018
               modifications by concatenating self-attention tokens across images. This allows
019            the model to implicitly capture cross-image relationships (e.g., identities, styles,
020            layouts, surroundings, textures, and color schemes) through caption-based corre-
021            lations. Our design enables scalable, unsupervised, and task-agnostic pretraining
022            using extensive collections of image groups sourced from multimodal internet ar-
023            ticles, image galleries, and video frames. We evaluate GDTs on a comprehensive
024            benchmark featuring over 200 instructions across 30 distinct visual generation
025            tasks, including picture book creation, font design, style transfer, sketching, col-
026            orization, drawing sequence generation, and character customization. Our models
027
               achieve competitive zero-shot performance without any additional fine-tuning or
               gradient updates. Furthermore, ablation studies confirm the effectiveness of key
028
               components such as data scaling, group size, and model design. These results
029
               demonstrate the potential of GDTs as scalable, general-purpose visual generation
030            systems. We will release the code and models to support further research.

034
035   The advent of large language models (LLMs) has brought a paradigm shift in natural language
036   processing (NLP) Radford et al. (2019); Raffel et al. (2020); Brown (2020); Ouyang et al. (2022);
037   Zhang et al. (2022); Touvron et al. (2023a;b); Dubey et al. (2024), enabling a wide range of tasks
038   to be approached in a task-agnostic manner. These models, trained on vast corpora, can generate
039
      coherent and contextually relevant content across various domains without the need for task-specific
      fine-tuning, setting a new standard for what is achievable in NLP. However, this level of task gener-
040
      alization has yet to be fully realized in the field of visual generation. Unlike NLP, visual generation
041
      tasks – such as pose transfer Shen et al. (2023); Lu et al. (2024), image translation Ho et al. (2024);
042   Rodatz et al. (2024), customization Jones et al. (2024); Wei et al. (2023), stylization Huang et al.

045   specific datasets and additional modules, such as LoRAs Jones et al. (2024); Smith et al. (2023); Luo
046   et al. (2023), adapters Ye et al. (2023a); Mou et al. (2024), visual encoders Giannone et al. (2022);
047   Kumar et al. (2024); Xu et al. (2024), and ControlNets Zhang et al. (2023); Zhao et al. (2024), to
048   achieve satisfactory performance.
049   This reliance on specialized data and architectures presents significant challenges for scalability and
050   generalization. First, it limits scalability by failing to leverage the vast amount of weakly super-
051   vised data available on the Internet; creating and curating task-specific datasets is human-laboring.
052   Second, it restricts models’ adaptability to unseen tasks. Third, cross-task adaptation is lacking,
053   particularly in compositional control, where multiple tasks are implicitly managed. For example,

161   transformers Peebles & Xie (2023); Esser et al. (2024a); Chen et al. (2023a), termed Group Dif-
      fusion Transformers (GDTs). The core idea is to concatenate self-attention tokens across a group

180   2     A PPROACH
181
182   The core of our approach is to reformulate visual generation tasks into a group generation problem
183   and solve it using minimally modified diffusion transformers. We begin by detailing how these tasks
184   are reformulated, followed by a comprehensive introduction to our model, its architecture, the data
185   employed, the training procedure, and the inference stage.
186
187   2.1   P ROBLEM F ORMULATION
188
      We propose that a vast array of visual generation tasks can be unified under a single framework we
189
      term the group generation problem. In this framework, the objective is to generate a group of n
190
      elements x = {x1 , x2 , · · · , xn }, where each element is conditioned on its respective context (e.g.,
191   image descriptions) c = {c1 , c2 , · · · , cn }. The relationships among these elements are implicitly
192   defined by the interdependencies within their contextual conditions. Optionally, a subset of 0 ≤ m <
193   n elements of x can be provided as reference data, with the task being to generate the remaining
194   (n − m) elements. This formulation naturally encapsulates a variety of tasks:
195
196          • Text-to-Image: A special case where the group size n = 1 and the reference subset size
197            m = 0. The task is to generate a single image from a textual description.
198          • Font Generation: Here, the group size n > 1 corresponds to the number of characters to
199            generate, with m = 0.
200          • Picture Book Generation: Similar to font generation, the group size n > 1 corresponds to
201            the number of picture book pages, with m = 0. The descriptions capture the connections
202            and variations across the pages.
203
             • Identity Preservation: Here, the group size n > 1 corresponds to the number of photos
204            with the same identities to generate, with m = 0. Identity-specific information is reflected
205            in the descriptions, such as names or other identifiers.
206
             • Local Editing: In this task, the group size is n = 2 with a reference subset size m = 1.
207
               One reference image is provided, and the model generates the edited image based on the
208            differences captured in their descriptions.
209
             • Image Translation: Similarly, the group size is n = 2 with a reference subset size m = 1.
210
               A reference image from one domain is converted to another domain according to their
211
               descriptions.
212
213
             • Subject Customization: The task involves generating (n − m) ≥ 1 images, where 1 ≤
               m < n character images are used as references.
214
215          • Style Adaptation: In this task, (n − m) ≥ 1 corresponds to the number of stylized images
               to be generated, with m = 1 being the reference image guiding the target style.

      2.2            M ODEL AND A RCHITECTURES
244
245   To tackle the group generation problem, it is crucial to establish connections between multiple group
246   elements during the generation process, allowing the model to perceive and utilize the correlations
247   among these elements. Our approach involves a straightforward modification: concatenating tokens
248   across group inputs within the self-attention blocks of diffusion transformers. This enables tokens
249   from different data elements to interact with one another throughout the model’s layers.
250
      For different text-conditioned visual generation architectures, we make minimal adaptations to ac-
251   commodate our approach:
252
253                     • Encoder-Decoder: In architectures like PixArt Chen et al. (2023a), each transformer block
254                       includes a self-attention operation for the image, cross-attention for interaction between im-
255                       age and text, and a feed-forward network. We choose to concatenate all the image tokens
256                       in self-attention blocks, which allows every token attends to all the image tokens within the
257                       group. After self-attention operation, concatenated image tokens are split back correspond-
258
                          ingly. Then, in cross-attention blocks, each image token attends only to the text embeddings
                          associated with its respective description. This setup is illustrated in Figure 3 (b).
259
260                     • Encoder-Only: Examples like Stable Diffusion 3 Esser et al. (2024a) and FLUX Labs
261                       (2024) feature transformer blocks with self-attention blocks and feed-forward networks.
262
                          We modify the self-attention operation into a masked version, which is depicted in Fig-
                          ure 3 (c). Specifically, image tokens xi as well as text tokens ci are first concatenated
263
                          with each other all over the group. Then, we calculate the masked self-attention, where
264
                          the mask is designed for allowing every image token attends to all tokens across the
265                       group while allowing context tokens only attend to image tokens as well as themselves.
266                       Concretely, let M (aj , bk ) indicate the attention mask for tokens in aj and bk , where
267                       a, b ∈ {c, x}, 0 ≤ j, k ≤ n. Then, M (aj , bk ) is decided by

452   4.2.2   DATA S CALING
453
      Without the demand of task-specific supervision, it is quite easy to acquire a large abundance of
454   group data from the Internet. We scale the training data to 5k, 50k, and 500k groups, to explore the
455   impact of data scale in GDTs. As illustrated in Table 2, with the increase of the amount of training
456   data, GDTs behave increasingly better in content consistency and prompt adherence. Interestingly,
457   we find that FID would become lower when training on less data, which may be that it is easier to
458   overfit to small datasets. We plan to further scale up our data to the level of hundreds of millions of
459   groups in the future, in order to fully leverage the potential of GDTs.
460
      4.2.3   G ROUP S IZE
461
462   We gradually increase the upper limit of group size to 2, 4, and 8, and perform inference based
463   on that limit. Note that doubling the group size will, in turn, double the sequence length in self-
464   attention, leading to a corresponding increase in computational complexity, so we cap the maximum
465   group size at 8 in our ablation. From the ablated results in Table 2, we find that larger group sizes
466   lead to a more pronounced performance decline in image quality, content consistency, and prompt
467
      adherence. The reason may be that it is more difficult to learn the complex relationships across a
      large group of images. Besides, the scarcity of data of large group sizes prevents the model from
468
      being adequately trained. In the future, we would greatly scale our training data.
469
470   4.2.4   SDE DIT OR I NPAINTING
471
472
      When conditioned on a subset of the group data, using methods like SDEdit Meng et al. (2021)
      or trainable inpainting Xie et al. (2023); Xu et al. (2024), GDTs can be instructed to generate the
473
      remaining data of the group. Specifically, SDEdit is a training-free technique which provides the
474
      reference images that are added with the same noise step as the generated images during the de-
475   noising stage. In contrast, trainable inpainting concatenates the reference image to the noised one in
476   channel dimension, allowing the model to “copy” the reference images and generate the remaining
477   ones. In our ablation study, as illustrated in Table 2, it is observed that trainable inpainting performs
478   better in image quality and content consistency, while the training-free SDEdit is good at prompt
479   adherence. We adopt the model design of trainable inpainting in our GDTs.
480
      4.2.5   Q UALITY T UNING
481
482   While quality tuning is a common practice in visual generation models to enhance aesthetic appeal,
483   we investigate its impact under the paradigm of group generation. As illustrated in Table 2, after the
484   supervised fine-tuning on a small subset of high-quality image groups, GDTs exhibit significantly
485   better image quality. We also find that quality tuning helps generating image groups with higher
      content consistency, while barely compromising the adherence to textual descriptions.

      5.1   T EXT- TO -I MAGE G ENERATION
489
490   The emergence of DDPM Ho et al. (2020) has catalyzed rapid advancements in text-to-image
491   (T2I) generation. Earlier frameworks focused on T2I generation in pixel space, exemplified by
492   GLIDE Nichol et al. (2022) and Imagen Saharia et al. (2022). In contrast, Stable Diffusion Rom-
493   bach et al. (2022) introduced latent space for T2I generation, while DALLE-2 (unCLIP)Ramesh
494   et al. (2022a) expanded this to a multimodal latent space. EMUDai et al. (2023) demonstrated
495
      that supervised fine-tuning on a small set of appealing images can significantly enhance genera-
      tion quality. Unlike U-Net architectures, several approaches, including DiT Peebles & Xie (2023),
496
      Pixart Chen et al. (2023a), HunyuanDiT Li et al. (2024b), and SD3 Esser et al. (2024b), adopt
497
      transformers as their backbone.
498
499
      5.2   C ONTROLLABLE T EXT- TO -I MAGE G ENERATION
500
501   Personalization. Personalization in T2I generation Cui et al. (2024); Salehi et al. (2024); Ham et al.
502   (2024); Wang et al. (2024) aims to capture concepts like subject Li et al. (2023a); Kumari et al.
503   (2023), person Xiao et al. (2023); Li et al. (2024a); Chen et al. (2024b; 2023b), style Liu et al.
504   (2023a); Sohn et al. (2023), and image Ye et al. (2023b); Xu et al. (2023); Ramesh et al. (2022b).
505   Techniques like Textual Inversion Gal et al. (2022) and DreamBooth Ruiz et al. (2022) facilitate
506   concept embedding. Subject-driven methods Valevski et al. (2023); Chen et al. (2024b) use face
507   recognition models for personalization.
508   Spatial Control. Spatial control in T2I generation Li et al. (2023b) is crucial for representing image
509   structure. ControlNet Zhang et al. (2023) and UniControl Qin et al. (2023) are examples of models
510   that incorporate positional signals for spatial control.
511
      Advanced Controllable Text-to-Image Generation. New directions in controllable T2I generation
512   include Attend-and-Excite Chefer et al. (2023), Composer Huang et al. (2023), Cocktail Hu et al.
513   (2023), Cones Liu et al. (2023c), Universal Guidance Bansal et al. (2023), EMU2 Sun et al. (2024),
514   and FreeDom Yu et al. (2023), which aim to enhance text alignment and achieve universal control.
515
516   5.3   G ENERALIZATION A BILITY OF G ENERATIVE M ODELS
517
518   Beyond fundamental generative capabilities, recent approaches are investigating the generalization
519   and versatility of models. ControlNeXt Peng et al. (2024) is designed to support both images and
520   videos while incorporating diverse forms of control information. EMU2 Sun et al. (2024) demon-
521
      strates task-agnostic in-context learning capabilities. MT-Diffusion Chen et al. (2024a) achieves
      multi-modality diffusion through multi-task learning.
522
523   In contrast to the aforementioned methods, Group Diffusion Transformers aim to provide a general-
524   purpose visual generation framework with the following capabilities: 1) no need for task-specific
525   pretraining or finetuning; 2) generating multiple images in parallel; 3) conditioning on text or im-
526   ages; and 4) enabling zero-shot task generalization.
527
528   6     C ONCLUSION AND L IMITATIONS
529
530   We reformulate most visual generation tasks into a group generation problem, thereby introducing
531   a unified framework named Group Diffusion Transformers (GDTs). We present that with scalable,
532   unsupervised, and task-agnostic pretraining on group data, GDTs could achieve competitive zero-
533   shot performance on a vast array of visual generation tasks. Our results demonstrate the potential of
534   GDTs as scalable, general-purpose visual generation systems.
535   Moreoever, we point out that there is still a discrepancy in image quality between GDTs and the
536   state-of-the-art text-to-image models. The amount of group data for pretraining is also not sufficient
537   yet, which has not fully unleashed the model’s capabilities. We are optimistic that with an enlarged
538   group dataset, we can further optimize the model’s performance and reduce the discrepancy. In the
539   future, we also plan to extend the time dimension of GDTs to enable multi-shot video generation,
      which can be naturally expressed under our group generation framework.
