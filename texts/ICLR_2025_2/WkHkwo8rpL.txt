009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Efficiency, specialization, and adaptability to new data distributions are qualities
012            that are hard to combine in current Large Language Models. The Mixture of Experts
013            (MoE) architecture has been the focus of significant research because its inherent
014            conditional computation enables such desirable properties. In this work, we focus
015            on “upcycling” dense expert models into an MoE, aiming to improve specialization
016
               while also adding the ability to adapt to new tasks easily. We introduce Nexus, an
               enhanced MoE architecture with adaptive routing where the model learns to project
017
               expert embeddings from domain representations. This approach allows Nexus
018
               to flexibly add new experts after the initial upcycling through separately trained
019            dense models, without requiring large-scale MoE training for unseen data domains.
020            Our experiments show that Nexus achieves a relative gain of up to 2.1% over the
021            baseline for initial upcycling, and a 18.8% relative gain for extending the MoE with
022            a new expert by using limited finetuning data. This flexibility of Nexus is crucial
023            to enable an open-source ecosystem where every user continuously assembles their
024            own MoE-mix according to their needs.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      In an era of bigger and bigger models (Canziani et al., 2016; Strubell et al., 2019; Rae et al., 2021;
029
      Raffel et al., 2020; Bommasani et al., 2022; Hooker, 2024), there are several key objectives driving
030   state-of-art progress. Doing more with less by improving efficiency (Treviso et al., 2023) remains
031   paramount, but in addition to efficiency, the deployment of these models in the wild means that the
032   ability to adapt to new data (Pozzobon et al., 2023b; Gururangan et al., 2020a; Jang et al., 2022; Jin
033   et al., 2022), and specialization of compute (Zadouri et al., 2024; Shazeer et al., 2018; Riquelme et al.,
034   2021; Du et al., 2022; Fedus et al., 2022) have gained renewed focus. While all these properties are
035   desirable, a formidable challenge is designing architectures that can fulfill all of these requirements.
036   The Mixture of Experts (MoE) approach gained prominence because of its efficiency properties. In
037   contrast to dense models which require significant compute to deploy, MoE approaches only activate
038   a subset of the parameters for every single token. Intuitively, not all parameters are necessary for each
039   request, as some parameters will specialize on certain tasks, and those unrelated to the current request
040   can be ignored. However, while MoEs greatly improved efficiency, the ability to induce meaningful
041   specialization has been more limited, with observations that experts don’t appear to exhibit dedicated
042   expertise (Jiang et al., 2024; Zoph et al., 2022; Zadouri et al., 2023). Furthermore, MoEs tend to
043   suffer from severe training instabilities (Zoph et al., 2022).
044   Recent work has attempted to address both the training instabilities and the lack of specialization.
045   These techniques often train completely separate experts and “upcycle” (combine) them into a single
046   unified MoE model after dense training (Sukhbaatar et al., 2024). This reduces the memory and
047   communication cost, and improves efficiency during training as computations are more local and
048   cross-device communication is reduced (Li et al., 2022; Gururangan et al., 2023). Notably, the other
049   major advantage of these approaches is the increase in specialization with separate experts that are
050
      trained on specific domains, making them clearly responsible for their human-interpretable subset of
      the data. On the other hand, MoEs with a standard router, which needs to be trained on a mix of all
051
      training data, are not designed to maintain domain specialization (Jiang et al., 2024).
052
053   However, efficiently integrating new experts into upcycled MoE models - a setting that is of great
      interest for adaptability objectives - is far less studied. For most practitioners, given the scale of

080   training MoEs repeatedly is an infeasible computational cost. Furthermore, most model development
081   fails to take into account distribution drift in use cases, with limited flexibility and applicability across
082   different tasks and domains (Pozzobon et al., 2023a; Gururangan et al., 2020b). However, human
083   language is shaped by a cumulative culture, constantly building upon itself and evolving over time
084   (Silvey, 2016). Also, specialized use cases such as multilingual, code and math often require tailored
085   additional training.
086   In this work, we attempt to reconcile all three desirable properties: efficiency, specialization, and
087   adaptability. We ask “how can we adaptively combine separately trained specialized experts?” To
088   address this, we introduce Nexus, a novel MoE architecture that parameterizes the router based
089   on domain-specific data by learning to project the embedding of each data domain to an expert
090   embedding. This learnable projection for the router allows for the easy extension of the MoE model
091   with new experts that are trained independently on new datasets of interest. This also avoids the
092   difficulties of MoE training, as our learned router scales with the number of experts without needing
093   to be trained from scratch, which enables adding or removing experts as desired.
094   Our experiments show that Nexus outperforms previous work when upscaling an MoE from separately
095   trained specialized domain experts. Going beyond the single upscaling phase, Nexus can be efficiently
096   extended with a new expert trained on a new domain, by finetuning it with much fewer tokens,
097   compared to the finetuning after the initial upcycling.
098   In summary, our contributions are as follows:
099
100          1. We present Nexus, a novel MoE framework designed to enhance sparse upcycling of
101             specialized dense experts, while reducing the training cost of MoEs by facilitating easy
102             adaptation to unseen data distributions. In Nexus, the traditional linear router from vanilla
103             MoE models is replaced with routing based on the similarity of layer inputs to an expert
104             embedding vector, derived from the average embedding of the corresponding expert dataset.
105          2. Our method outperforms the existing approach for upcycling specialized models into MoE,
106             leading to 2.1% and 1.6% relative increase over the upcycled MoE (linear router) in 470M
107             and 2.8B scales respectively. This enables performance increase in general tasks with 5.8%
                and 7.4% relative gains over the dense seed model at 470M and 2.8B respectively.

134   Sparse Upcycling (Komatsuzaki et al., 2023) initializes an MoE model from a dense Transformer
135   model by copying FFN layers as MoE experts, and the router layer is trained from scratch. BTX
136   (Sukhbaatar et al., 2024) generalize this approach to initialize each MoE expert from the FFN layer

138   In Nexus, we leverage upcycling specialized expert models similar to BTX, however, it diverges in
139   terms of MoE training, in particular with its novel MoE router, which enables to efficiently extend
140   the MoE in multiple rounds after the sparse upcycling. We describe our method in the next section.

143   3   A DAPTIVE ROUTER FOR U PCYCLING S PECIALIZED E XPERTS AS M O E
144
145   The core component of an MoE model is the router, as it determines which experts to activate
146   for any given input. In vanilla MoEs, the router is a learned linear layer that takes the token
147   intermediate representations as input and computes the expert probabilities. However, this router does
148   not necessarily learn specialization as MoEs are commonly trained using an auxiliary load balancing
149   loss to improve training stability (Fedus et al., 2022; Jiang et al., 2024). In Nexus, we propose a
150   novel MoE router where per MoE block we learn a projection layer from given pre-computed domain
151
      embeddings to expert embeddings. We parametrize this projection layer Pr as a two-layer MLP with
      a SwiGLU activation function (Shazeer, 2020):
152
153                                       ei = Pr (di )              (Domain to Expert Embeddings)
154
                                             = W2 · SwiGLU(W1 · di )
155

157   where di ∈ Rm , and ei ∈ Rh are the domain and expert embeddings for the ith domain respectively.,
158   where m and h are the domain embedding and the model dimensions. W1 ∈ R2h×d , W2 ∈ Rl×l
159   are linear layers, and SwiGLU is defined as R2n → Rn . Given the expert embeddings ei and layer
160   inputs x ∈ Rs×h , we then compute routing probabilities si as:
161

      Unlike the standard router, Nexus’s router includes a stronger inductive bias through pre-computed
163   domain embeddings1 that enables expert embedding to specialize. Thus, x · ei gives a high value
164   for input tokens that are closer to the domain of the corresponding expert. Notably, this router is
165   particularly suited for the sparse upcycling setting where the dense experts are separately trained on
166   different domains.
167
      Connection to hypernetworks. Our router parametrization is closely related to hypernetworks (Ha
168
      et al., 2016) as the projection layer Pr generates parameters for the router during runtime for a given
169   input. We use domain embeddings as the input to the projection layer, enabling efficient adaptation
170   and also a better cross-domain transfer based on the similarity between domain embeddings as shown
171   in previous work (Mahabadi et al., 2021; Üstün et al., 2022).

      per Transformer block. Unlike Sukhbaatar et al. (2024), instead of using the original FFN of the
175   seed model as one of the routed experts in an MoE layer, we use it as the “shared expert” FFNs
176   (Rajbhandari et al., 2022; Dai et al., 2024) to better preserve the previous capabilities in the MoE
177   model. For all non-FFN parameters including the attention weights, we merge expert parameters

184   Efficient adaptation to new domains. An important advantage of method is that when a new data
185   domain is present after MoE training, we use the learned projection Pr to compute expert embedding
186   of the new domain as enew = Pr (dnew ). This enables to enhance the trained MoE model with
187   additional dense experts, which are trained in the same way as the initial experts. The FFN parameters
188
      of the new expert are simply appended to the array of existing experts.
189   To adequately preserve the non-FFN parameters of existing experts, we perform a weighted average
190   ϕf = (1 − λ) · ϕmoe + λ · ϕnew where ϕf , ϕe , and ϕmoe are parameters of the final MoE, dense
191   expert, and initial MoE model and λ = 1/(n + 1). This enables efficiently adapting Nexus to new
192   domain by extending it with the new dense expert trained independently. After extending the MoE
193   with a new expert, we perform a lightweight finetuning with a limited number of tokens.

200   corresponding experimental setting:
201
      1. Training specialized expert LMs. For training the dense specialized experts, we use the
202   sub-datasets from the SlimPajama dataset (Soboleva et al., 2023), a 627B token English-language
203   corpus assembled from web data of various sources. We initialize four dense experts from the
204   weights of the seed model and train them on the A R X IV, B OOKS, C4, G IT H UB, S TACK E XCHANGE,
205   and W IKIPEDIA domains.2 As the seed model, we use 470M and 2.8B parameters decoder-only

477   6       R ELATED W ORK
478
479   Routing Variants of MoEs. The most common MoE architecture (Shazeer et al., 2017; Lepikhin
480   et al., 2020; Fedus et al., 2022) employs a linear router with a top-k routing scheme, where k typically
481   equals 1 or 2. In this standard routing schema, only the k experts with the highest router gate
482   values are activated. There is substantial research proposing alternatives to top-k expert assignments
483   (Hazimeh et al., 2021; Lewis et al., 2021; Roller et al., 2021; Zhou et al., 2022; Zuo et al., 2022).
484   DeepSeek-MoE (Dai et al., 2024) introduces a routing variant where a number of experts are “shared”
485
          3
              We report the average performance on Knowledge, Science, Reasoning, and MMLU.


                                                          9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      and always assigned to all tokens. Our work also adopts this approach for our general base expert.
487   However, these efforts primarily focus on improving the general performance and/or training stability
488   of MoEs. In contrast, our work puts emphasis adaptability and extensibility.
489
490
      Efficient MoE Training by Re-Using Existing Dense Models. Training MoEs from scratch is
      computationally expensive (Gale et al., 2023; Fedus et al., 2022) and often challenging due to training
491
      instabilities (Zoph et al., 2022). Alternatively, recent works have explored re-using existing dense
492
      models to initialize MoEs. Sparse Upcycling (Komatsuzaki et al., 2023) re-uses a single dense model
493   to initialize the MoE by replicating the FFN weights in an MoE layer. The router is initialized
494   randomly, and all other parameters are copied directly from the dense model. BTX (Sukhbaatar
495   et al., 2024) extends this approach by upcycling not from a single dense model, but from multiple
496   specialized dense expert models. Furthermore, BAM (Zhang et al., 2024) expands BTX to upcycle
497   not only FFN experts but also attention experts. Our work also leverages this approach by reusing
498   specialized dense experts for an MoE, while extending it further to facilitate on-the-fly adaptations
499   for new experts specialized in unseen data domains.
500   Efficient MoE Architectures. Zadouri et al. (2024) proposes replacing traditional MoE’s
501   computation-heavy feed-forward network (FFN) experts with more efficient experts comprised of
502   smaller vectors and adapters, which are activated in parallel to a single dense FFN. This lightweight
503   architecture necessitates only a limited number of parameter updates when finetuning, offering
504   efficiency advantages. However, unlike our approach, it does not leverage existing specialized dense
505   models and lacks a notion of specialized experts, which are central to our method. Similar to our
506   work, Muqeeth et al. (2024) and Ostapenko et al. (2024) study combining separately trained experts
507
      into a unified model. However, they focus on parameter-efficient adapters such as LoRA (Hu et al.,
      2021) and supervised finetuning. In this work, we focus on efficiently pre-training fully-fledged MoE
508
      models via upcycling.
509
510   Adaptive MoEs and Ensemble Models. ModuleFormer (Shen et al., 2023) also aims to produce
511   adaptable MoEs. The authors achieve adaptability by freezing existing MoE parameters while
512   only training newly added modules with optimization constraints to the router. Unlike our work,
513
      ModuleFormer does not leverage existing expert dense seed models for efficiency gains, nor does it
      have a notion of specialization which is central to our work. Similar to our work, DEMix (Gururangan
514
      et al., 2021) independently trains different FFN experts on specialized data domains, with each expert
515
      functioning as a domain-specific module. Modules can be added on-the-fly for adaptability. Followup
516   works BTM and C-BTM (Li et al., 2022; Gururangan et al., 2023) extend DEMix to create adaptive
517   ensemble models. However, all three works use a router requiring a forward pass for every expert at
518   inference instead of sparsely activating them, which significantly increases inference costs, especially
519   with a large number of experts. Unlike these approaches, our router cost is approximately the same
520   as standard top-k routing during both training and inference, offering a more scalable solution for
521   adaptability.
522
523   7    C ONCLUSION
524
525   We propose Nexus, a new LLM framework that enables efficient upcycling of specialized dense
526   experts into a sparsely activated MoE model. We show that individual experts in our method retain
527   their specialization after upcycling, and that our router based on expert embeddings outperforms
528   previous approaches for combining the dense experts. Furthermore, the model can be extended
529   efficiently with new dense experts after the initial training phase, saving much compute compared to
530   re-training the upcycled model or training from scratch.
531
532   8    L IMITATIONS
533
534   The MoE architecture is often employed for larger models in the multi-billion parameter range, where
535   efficiency is paramount. However, to facilitate a broader set of experiments, we limit our setup to
536   using 2.8B parameter seed models for the main results and 470M parameter seed models for ablations.
537   Furthermore, our dense experts are based on existing data sources in the SlimPajama dataset which is
538   pre-defined. Future work could extend our method by discovering specialized data domains through
539   unsupervised clustering similar to Gururangan et al. (2023).

           A    N EXUS ROUTING ALGORITHM
865
866
867        Figure 7 outlines the code for the Nexus router, which consists of (1) a 2-layer MLP network
868        (domain_to_expert_ffn) to project domain embeddings to expert embeddings, (2) shared and
869
           routed expert FFNs, and (3) sparse Top-k gating. Note that the expert embeddings are independent
           of the input and could be precomputed once and stored as long as the weights of the model do not
870
           change. This means that the routing layer during inference closely resembles a vanilla MoE router,
871
           with the difference being that the router matrix in Nexus is not learnt during training but computed
872        using the domain embeddings as an informative prior.
873
874
875    1 def router(self, inputs, domain_embeddings):
876    2     # domain_to_expert_ffn learns projection domain to expert embeddings
877    3     # domain_embeddings: [e_dim x n_experts]
       4     # expert_embeddings: [h_dim x n_experts]
878
       5     expert_embeddings = self.domain_to_expert_ffn(self.domain_embeddings)
879    6
880    7        # router probs: [batch, seq, n_experts]
881    8        router_probs = nn.softmax(inputs @ expert_embeddings)
       9
882
      10        # Top-1 gate for routed experts
883
      11        index, gate = nn.topk(1, router_probs)
884   12
885   13        # routed_experts_ffns: An MoE layer with FFN experts
886   14        # routed_expert_out: [batch, seq, h_dim]
887   15        # shared_expert_out: [batch, seq, h_dim]
      16        routed_expert_out = self.routed_expert_ffns[index](input)
888
      17        shared_expert_out = self.shared_expert_ffn(input)
889   18
890   19        return shared_expert_out + gate * routed_expert_out

896        B    C OMPARISON OF EXISTING APPROACHES WITH N EXUS
897
898
899
           Table 2 compares Nexus with previous approaches in the field of efficient MoE training. Unlike the
           vanilla MoE architecture (Shazeer et al., 2017; Fedus et al., 2022), the Branch-Train-Merge (BTM;
900
           Li et al., 2022) and the Branch-Train-Mix (BTX; Sukhbaatar et al., 2024) approaches train experts
901
           separately in different domains, reducing training cost and improving specialization. However, they
902        either merge the experts during inference (BTM) or learn an MoE router layer from scratch, where
903        prior domain information is not used (BTX). Our approach trains the MoE router based on domain
904        information, maintaining the specialization and enabling efficient extension of the MoE with a new
905        expert after training.
