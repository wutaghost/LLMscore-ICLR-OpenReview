029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Large Language Models (LLMs), such as GPT-4o and Claude 3.5 Sonnet, have significantly ad-
032   vanced the field of Natural Language Processing (NLP) by demonstrating remarkable capabilities
033   in generating human-like text (Chen et al., 2024; 2023), performing complex language tasks (Wei
034   et al., 2022), and engaging in coherent dialogues. These models have been instrumental across
035   various domains, including content creation, code synthesis, education, and conversational agents,
036   transforming academic research and industrial applications (Shen et al., 2023; Ge et al., 2023).
037   Despite these advancements, the persistent challenge of hallucinations in LLMs remains a significant
038   concern. Hallucinations refer to factually incorrect, logically inconsistent, or nonsensical outputs,
039   yet sometimes appear to be true (Farquhar et al., 2024). Such outputs are typically unwelcome, es-
040   pecially in critical applications like legal consultation, medical advice, or scientific research, where
041   accuracy and reliability are of utmost importance (Gunjal et al., 2024). As a result, significant efforts
042   have been made to mitigate hallucinations through fine-tuning with accurate datasets, improving
043   output consistency, and using post-processing methods like fact-checking (Guan et al., 2024).
044   We propose an emerging perspective that suggests not all hallucinations are entirely detrimental.
045   In contexts that emphasize creativity and innovation, hallucinations can contribute positively by
046   introducing novel ideas or unconventional reasoning paths. Specifically, when a LLM arrives at
047   a correct answer through an alternative reasoning process, it may offer valuable insights to foster
048   creative problem-solving ability in a special way. After presenting this perspective, we further raise
049   the research question in our study: Can certain hallucinations, instead of being purely negative, play
050
      a constructive role in fostering creativity while ensuring accuracy and maintaining correctness?
051   In this study, we explore the notion of good hallucinations—instances where LLMs generate dif-
052   ferent reasoning paths that still lead to correct answers. Examining these alternative and dynamic
053   reasoning processes, we aim to understand how LLMs can contribute to creative thinking without
      compromising factual accuracy. The Torrance Tests of Creative Thinking (TTCT) is the most widely

066   • Introduction of the concept good hallucination: We define and explore the good hallucinations

068   • Development of metrics for quality assessment of good hallucination: We propose metrics
069     focusing on correctness, consistency, and reasoning diversity, align with the TTCT in psychology.
070     Semantic clustering techniques are utilized to evaluate these metrics quantitatively.
071   • Experimental analysis of prompting techniques and hyperparameters: We conduct extensive
072     experiments to understand how different prompting strategies and hyperparameter settings like
073     temperature adjusting influence the generation of creative yet accurate outputs in LLMs.
074
      This research highlights these positive aspects of hallucinations and offers a refined perspective on
075
      their role in LLMs’ outputs. We hope strategies to leverage good hallucinations in applications that
076
      benefit from creative problem-solving, thereby enhancing the flexibility and utility of AI systems.

079   2     M ETHODOLOGY
080
081   Our methodology evaluates the creativity of Large Language Models (LLMs) by introducing a novel
082
      metric that combines both the accuracy and the diversity of their generated reasoning paths. This
      is achieved by sampling multiple outputs from the LLM, clustering them based on semantic simi-
083
      larity, and calculating a composite creativity score that encapsulates both correctness and variety in
084
      reasoning, as illustrated in Figure 1.
085

      2.1     S EMANTIC C LUSTERING FOR R EASONING PATHS
087
088   To assess the diversity of reasoning paths, we generate multiple outputs from the LLM and clus-
089   ter these outputs using two different methods: (1) clustering based on text embeddings, and (2)
090   clustering via LLM-based prompting.
091
092   2.1.1    G ENERATING M ULTIPLE O UTPUTS
093

094

161   LLM processes this information and outputs the clusters C = C1 , C2 , . . . , CK , where each cluster

190   metric is meticulously designed to reflect both the correctness of the responses and the variety in
191   the reasoning paths generated by the LLM, thereby providing a comprehensive assessment of the
192   model’s creative capabilities.

251   2.2.5    I NTERPRETATION AND PARAMETER S ELECTION
252
      The creativity score C allows for balancing accuracy and diversity through the parameter α. When
253
      α = 1, C focuses purely on accuracy. At α = 0, the score shifts to prioritize diversity among correct
254
      responses. For intermediate values of α, the score incorporates both accuracy and diversity, with the
255   specific weight determined by α. For example, choosing α = 0.75 would prioritize accuracy while
256   still considering diverse reasoning strategies.
257
258   2.3     I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
259
260   For each prompt P , we generated 15 reasoning paths to maintain computational feasibility while
261   capturing diverse strategies. Using the baseline LLM, we applied nucleus sampling with a top-
262   p (Holtzman et al., 2020) value of 0.9 to introduce variability without sacrificing coherence. The
263   clustering approach used agglomerative hierarchical clustering (Randriamihamison et al., 2021) with
264
      cosine distance. We set a threshold of 0.3 for merging clusters, determined through preliminary
      experiments to balance clustering granularity.

      3.3.2     C OMPARISON OF I NFERENCE S TRATEGIES
402
403   The choice of inference strategy during text generation plays a crucial role in shaping the quality,
404   diversity, and creativity of the model’s outputs, extending beyond the impact of tuning parameters
405   such as temperature. This section compares standard decoding methods with two advanced strategies
406   recently introduced in the literature.
407
      Standard Decoding Methods Greedy search are commonly employed decoding techniques.
408
      Greedy search deterministically selects the token with the highest probability at each step. While
409
      this approach is efficient, it often leads to homogeneous outputs that lack diversity.
410
411   To introduce variability and enhance the diversity of generated content, stochastic methods like
412   Top-k sampling (Fan et al., 2018) and Top-p (nucleus) sampling (Holtzman et al., 2020) are used.
413
      Top-k sampling limits the candidate tokens to the k most probable options at each decoding step and
      samples from this subset. This method prevents the model from considering low-probability tokens,
414
      reducing irrelevant or nonsensical outputs while still allowing for diversity.
415
416   Top-p sampling, on the other hand, selects tokens from the smallest possible set whose cumulative
417   probability exceeds a predefined threshold p. This dynamic approach adjusts the candidate pool
418   based on the distribution of the probabilities, providing a balance between diversity and coherence.
419
      Both Top-k and Top-p sampling aim to mitigate the shortcomings of greedy search by avoiding
      deterministic and repetitive outputs, thereby improving the overall quality of the generated text.
420
421   Advanced Decoding Strategies Recent advancements in decoding methods have introduced more
422   sophisticated strategies to enhance the quality of generated content. Decoding by Contrasting
423   Layers (DoLa), introduced by Chuang et al. (2024), leverages the internal structure of transformer
424   models by contrasting logits from deeper and earlier layers. In our experiments, we use layer 32
425   as the mature layer and layers 0, 2, 4, 6, 8, 10, 12, 14 as candidate premature layers. This technique,
426
      based on the observation that factual knowledge tends to concentrate in specific layers, emphasizes
      such layers to reduce hallucinations and improve the factual accuracy of generated content. These
427
      strategies were tested using the Llama 3.1-8B model, generating multiple reasoning paths per prompt
428
      and evaluated for accuracy, diversity, and creativity across the GSM8K and StrategyQA datasets.
429

431   GSM8K and StrategyQA. DoLa achieves the highest accuracy, with 86.45% on GSM8K and 75.89%
      on StrategyQA, surpassing greedy search and stochastic methods. Top-k sampling (k = 50) yields

470   4.1   H ALLUCINATIONS IN L ARGE L ANGUAGE M ODELS
471
472   Hallucinations in LLMs are commonly defined as outputs that are factually incorrect, contextually
473   irrelevant, or semantically incoherent (Farquhar et al., 2024; Venkit et al., 2024; Ye et al., 2024).
474   These inaccuracies can manifest as incorrect information, fabricated references, or misleading state-
475
      ments, thus compromising the reliability of LLMs in applications requiring factual precision (Gunjal
      et al., 2024; Cheng et al., 2024). Several factors contribute to hallucinations in LLMs, including
476
      limitations in training data, which often contains inaccuracies, biases, and inconsistencies (Jiang
477
      et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024). The architecture and objectives of LLMs, particularly their reliance
478   on next-word prediction, also play a role in generating plausible but incorrect outputs (?Xu et al.,
479   2024b). Additionally, inference techniques like beam search or temperature settings can influence
480   the likelihood of hallucinations (Holtzman et al., 2020; Xu et al., 2024a).
481
