011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Large vision language models (VLMs) combine large language models with vi-
014            sion encoders, demonstrating promise across various tasks. However, they of-
015            ten underperform in task-specific applications due to domain gaps between pre-
016
               training and fine-tuning. We introduce VITask, a novel framework that enhances
               task-specific adaptability of VLMs by integrating task-specific models (TSMs).
017
               VITask employs three key strategies: exemplar prompting (EP), response distri-
018
               bution alignment (RDA), and contrastive response tuning (CRT) to improve the
019            task-specific performance of VLMs by adjusting their response distributions. EP
020            allows TSM features to guide VLMs, while RDA enables VLMs to adapt with-
021            out TSMs during inference by learning from exemplar-prompted models. CRT
022            further optimizes the ranking of correct image-response pairs, thereby reducing
023            the risk of generating undesired responses. Experiments on 12 medical diagnosis
024            datasets across 9 imaging modalities show that VITask outperforms both vanilla
025            instruction-tuned VLMs and TSMs, showcasing its ability to integrate comple-
026            mentary features from both models effectively. Additionally, VITask offers prac-
027
               tical advantages such as flexible TSM integration and robustness to incomplete
               instructions, making it a versatile and efficient solution for task-specific VLM
028
               tuning.

032
033
      Large Vision Language Models (VLMs) combine the capabilities of large language models (LLMs)
034
      with pre-trained vision encoders, enabling them to process and understand both text and images Liu
035
      et al. (2023a; 2024b); Driess et al. (2023); gpt; Dai et al. (2024); Chen et al. (2023b; 2024); Alayrac
036   et al. (2022); Bai et al. (2023). This integration allows VLMs to perceive visual inputs, comprehend
037   complex queries, and perform sophisticated reasoning across a wide array of tasks and domains.
038   The success of VLMs drives the growing trend of adapting VLMs for a wide range of task-specific
039   applications such as medical diagnosis, autonomous driving, and content creation He et al. (2024);
040   Moor et al. (2023); Li et al. (2024b); Wu et al. (2023); Zhou et al. (2024a); Xu et al. (2024).
041
      Despite the wide applicability of VLMs, recent studies have noted that their performance often
042   often falls short compared to task-specific models (TSMs) when fine-tuned for specific tasks or
043   domains Singhal et al. (2023); Yang et al. (2024). The performance gap between VLMs and TSMs
044   represents a critical limitation, particularly in real-world scenarios that demand high accuracy and
045   reliable service quality. Although substantial progress has been made in enhancing the performance
046   and versatility of VLMs Wu et al. (2023); Liu et al. (2023b); Lai et al. (2024); Wang et al. (2024),
047   most of these approaches do not focus on effectively adapting pre-trained VLMs to specific tasks or
048   datasets. This leads to a fundamental question: can we adapt VLMs to perform as well as, or even
049   surpass, task-specific models?
050   In this study, we use image classification as a case study to investigate why fine-tuned VLMs often
051   lag behind TSMs in performance. We identify two main factors contributing to this decline: 1) Un-
052   specialized Image Representations: Image features learned during pre-training for vision-language
053   tasks are not effective for specific classification tasks. They often miss important details needed for
      these tasks, making it hard for the vision encoder to extract useful information. 2) Indirect Tuning

      Objective: Fine-tuning VLMs typically emphasizes enhancing text generation, such as predicting
      the next word, rather than directly addressing image classification. This approach can hinder the
076
      models from learning the essential features required for effective image classification, resulting in
077
      subpar performance.
078
079   To address these challenges, we propose VITask, a novel framework that combines the strengths
080   of TSMs and VLMs to improve task-specific performance without sacrificing the versatility and
081
      instruction-following capabilities of VLMs. Our main idea leverages small, easily obtainable TSMs
      and a task-specific tuning objective to improve the learning of desired response distributions. To
082
      maintain the vision-language alignment in pre-trained VLMs, we avoid directly updating the vision
083
      encoder for new tasks. Instead, we propose exemplar prompting, using TSM features as exem-
084   plars to enhance VLM adaptability without altering pre-trained image features, while incorporat-
085   ing specialized task representations. Additionally, we introduce response distribution alignment to
086   align the response distributions between VLMs with and without exemplar prompting. This al-
087   lows the VLM to implicitly learn from the TSM by utilizing its own responses during fine-tuning.
088   Finally, we propose contrastive response tuning, which maximizes the likelihood of correct image-
089   response pairs (e.g., p(cat|<cat image>)) while minimizing the likelihood of incorrect pairs
090   (e.g., p(cat|<dog image>)). This approach promotes more discriminative and accurate response
091   rankings for visual instructions, thereby enhancing task-specific performance.
092   We evaluate VITask on 12 medical image diagnosis datasets and show that it consistently outper-
093   forms both TSMs and vanilla instruction-tuned VLMs. Furthermore, VITask demonstrates robust-
094   ness to incomplete instructions, providing flexibility for real-world applications where task descrip-
095   tions may not be comprehensive. Our results highlight the potential of VITask to generalize beyond
096   medical tasks, making it a versatile framework for task-specific VLM tuning.

113   Visual Instruction Tuning. Current VLM training pipelines usually follows a two-stage protocol.
114   First, the vision language alignment stage align the image features from the vision encoder with
115   the word embeddings encoded in LLMs. Second, the visual instruction tuning stage adapts VLMs
116   to follow instructions that involve both visual and textual inputs, making VLMs able to respond
117   to natural language commands or questions based on the content of an image Liu et al. (2023a);
118   Dai et al. (2024). Visual instruction tuning is a crucial step for making VLMs more interactive,
119   versatile, and context-aware, allowing them to follow instructions related to specific tasks, enhancing
120   its accuracy and adaptability to real-world applications where users provide visual and textual inputs.
121
      There are many existing works in the field of visual instruction tuning. Typical research topics focus
      on gaining specialized visual understanding ability Yue et al. (2024); Nisar et al. (2024); Chen et al.
122
      (2023a); Lai et al. (2024), reducing computational costs Hu et al. (2021); Luo et al. (2024); Lee et al.
123
      (2024), mitigating hallucination Leng et al. (2024); Zhou et al. (2024b); Hu et al. (2023), creating or
124   augmenting instruction data Yu et al. (2023); Li et al. (2023b); Liu et al. (2024c); Li et al. (2023a).

      Integrating VLMs and TSMs. Several approaches have been proposed to integrate VLMs with
127
      task-specific models in an attempt to leverage the strengths of both Liu et al. (2023b); Lai et al.
128   (2024); Li et al. (2024a). However, these works primarily focus on utilizing TSMs as task-specific
129   heads or tools for constructing a new VLM, without addressing the challenges of fine-tuning pre-
130   trained VLMs for specific tasks or datasets. Our work focuses on improving the visual instruction
131   tuning paradigm to achieve better task-specific performance, especially when the model faces do-
132   main gaps with downstream task data.

      In this section, we investigate why fine-tuned VLMs may underperform in classification tasks and
      highlight two key issues in the current visual instruction tuning paradigm: 1. Unspecialized Im-
197
      age Representations: The pre-trained vision encoder learns representations optimized for vision-
198
      language alignment, which are often sub-optimal for downstream classification tasks. 2. Indiect
199   Tuning Objective: The tuning objective focuses on next token prediction, which is more suited to
200   text generation than to classification tasks that require fine-grained discrimination. To overcome
201   these challenges, we proposed VITask, a novel framework (Figure 1) that bridges TSMs and VLMs
202   to enhance task-specific adaptability and performance.

279   Contrastive Response Tuning. The success of response distribution alignment suggests that we
280   do not need to teach VLM explicit mappings from instructions to responses; instead, these mappings
281   can be implicitly learned by refining the distribution of desired responses. Motivated by Hewitt et al.
282   (2024), we propose the concept of visual response ranking capability, referring to a VLM’s ability to
283   assign a higher likelihood to correct image-response pairs than to incorrect ones for a given instruc-
284   tion. For two independent image-instruction-response triples (imagei , instructioni , responsei ) and
285
      (imagej , instructionj , responsej ), with instructioni = instructionj and responsei ̸= responsej ,
      the visual response ranking capability holds for a VLM pθ if
286
287             pθ (responsei | imagei , instructioni ) > pθ (responsei | imagej , instructioni ),       (4)

289

290   gree to which a VLM possesses this ranking capability reflects how well it can differentiate between
291   correct and incorrect image-response pairs for a given instruction. We argue that vanilla visual in-
292   struction tuning often fails to establish this ranking capability because it focuses solely on learning
293   instruction-response mappings and does not explicitly account for the critical relationship between
294   images and responses. As a result, an instruction-tuned VLM might rank incorrect image-response
295   pairs higher than the correct ones, leading to suboptimal performance on specific tasks. To address
296   this issue, we propose Contrastive Response Tuning (CRT) to maximize the margin between correct
297   and incorrect image-response pairs. This is done by minimizing the following objective:

326   the distinction between correct and incorrect image-response pairs by maximizing the margin distri-
327   bution qθ (responsei | imagei , imagej , instructioni ). The CRT-tuned VLM shows a clear increase
328   in the probability for correct image-response pairs and a corresponding decrease for incorrect ones,
329   signifying that CRT substantially enhances the model’s ability to generate desirable and accurate
330   responses compared to vanilla instruction-tuned VLMs.
331
332       Takeaway #3: Contrastive response tuning improves the visual response ranking capability.
333
334   VITask Framework. To bring together all the proposed strategies, we introduce the VITask
335   framework, a two-stage pipeline designed for task-specific visual instruction tuning, analogous to
336   the way VLMs are trained. Stage 1: we make the task-specific connector learnable and fine-tune the
337   VLM using vanilla visual instruction tuning in conjunction with EP and RDA. The objective for this
338   stage is: LStage1 = LVan + LEP + αLRDA . The primary goal of Stage 1 is to establish the basic visual
339
      instruction-following ability and learn an effective task-specific connector that aligns TSM features
      with the LLM. Stage 2: After the task-specific connector is trained, we freeze it and then fine-tune
340
      the VLM with all the proposed loss functions. The objective becomes:
341
342                                 LStage2 = LVan + LEP + αLRDA + βLCRT ,                              (7)
343
344
      where α and β adjust the weight of LRDA and LCRT , respectively. In this stage, the model fine-tunes
      its visual response ranking capability through CRT while maintaining the learned visual-instruction
345
      mapping from Stage 1. Although so far our framework and analysis focus on a single task and
346
      dataset, VITask can be generalized to multi-task or multi-dataset settings by expanding the label
347   space and training a joint TSM. This flexibility allows the framework to build more robust, domain-
348   specific VLMs, capable of handling a variety of downstream tasks.
349
350   Advantages. VITask offers several advantages beyond improving task-specific performance. One
351   major benefit is its ability to decouple image representation learning from visual instruction tuning
352   by incorporating TSMs into VLMs. This flexibility allows for the use of any TSM architecture,
353   giving practitioners the freedom to choose the best model for their specific task. Furthermore, once
354   fine-tuned, the VLM can perform inference without needing the TSM, maintaining task-specific
355   adaptability while reducing model complexity.
356   Another key advantage of VITask is its plug-and-play collaboration between VLMs and TSMs.
357   When a new task is introduced, a new TSM can be separately trained and directly connected to
358   the VLM without requiring further instruction tuning. Since TSMs are generally smaller and easier
359   to train than VLMs, VITask provides an efficient way to adapt VLMs to new tasks, making the
360   framework highly scalable and adaptable to multiple domains.
361   Additionally, VITask demonstrates robustness against the content of instructions. Instruction-tuned
362   VLMs often rely on carefully crafted instructions for optimal performance. For instance, in experi-
363   ments with the HAM10000 dataset, detailed class information is typically included in the instruction
364   to enhance accuracy. However, in real-world applications, users may not always know such detailed
365   information in advance. VITask mitigates this limitation by adapting the response distribution based
366   on task-specific information from TSMs rather than solely relying on the instruction itself, enabling
367   strong performance even with more generalized or incomplete instructions.

515   Limitations and Discussions. Our work has several limitations. Firstly, we primarily focus on
516   image classification tasks, where training a single TSM for all tasks is straightforward. However,
517   for other instruction-following tasks, such as image captioning and VQA, training such a TSM may
518   not be as feasible or effective. Extending the VITask framework to these types of tasks remains
519   a challenge and could be an avenue for future research. Secondly, our experiments are limited to
520   medical datasets. While the results demonstrate the effectiveness of VITask in the medical domain,
521   testing across a broader range of domains would be necessary to fully validate its generalizability.
522   Exploring VITask’s applicability to datasets beyond the medical field is an important next step.
523
      Lastly, we focus on task-specific training during the fine-tuning stage. However, we believe that our
      method has the potential to enhance both the pre-training and fine-tuning phases of VLMs to achieve
524
      task-specific model-level performance. Exploring VITask’s application to pre-training could lead to
525
      further improvements in adaptability and performance across diverse tasks.

      In this paper, we proposed VITask, a novel framework that bridges task-specific models (TSM) and
      visual language models (VLM) to enhance task-specific adaptability and performance. Through
532
      exemplar prompting (EP), response distribution alignment (RDA), and contrastive response tuning
533
      (CRT), VITask leverages specialized task features from TSMs and aligns them with the instruction-
534   following capabilities of VLMs. Our experiments demonstrate that VITask outperforms both con-
535   ventional instruction-tuned VLMs and TSMs across a variety of datasets, showcasing its ability to
536   integrate complementary features from both models effectively. VITask not only improves task-
537   specific performance but also introduces practical advantages, such as flexibility in incorporating
538   any TSM architecture in a plug-and-play manner, and robustness to incomplete instructions. By
539   decoupling image representation learning from instruction tuning, VITask offers an efficient and
      adaptable solution for new and unseen tasks without the need for extensive retraining.
