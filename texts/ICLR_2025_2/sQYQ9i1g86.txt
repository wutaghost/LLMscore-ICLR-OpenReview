011                                                 A BSTRACT
012
013            This paper presents Constrained Exploitability Descent (CED), a novel model-free
014            offline reinforcement learning algorithm for solving adversarial Markov games.
015            CED is a game-theoretic approach combined with policy constraint methods from
016            offline RL. While policy constraints can perturb the optimal pure-strategy solutions
017            in single-agent scenarios, we find this side effect can be mitigated when it comes to
018
               solving adversarial games, where the optimal policy can be a mixed-strategy Nash
               equilibrium. We theoretically prove that, under the uniform coverage assumption
019
               on the dataset, CED converges to a stationary point in deterministic two-player
020
               zero-sum Markov games. The min-player policy at the stationary point satisfies the
021            necessary condition for making up an exact mixed-strategy Nash equilibrium, even
022            when the offline dataset is fixed and finite. Compared to the model-based method
023            of Exploitability Descent that optimizes the max-player policy, our convergence
024            result no longer relies on the generalized gradient. Experiments in matrix games, a
025            tree-form game, and an infinite-horizon soccer game verify that a single run of CED
026            leads to an optimal min-player policy when the practical offline data guarantees
027            uniform coverage. Besides, CED achieves significantly lower NashConv compared
028            to an existing pessimism-based method and can gradually improve the behavior
029
               policy even under non-uniform coverage.

032   1    I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Offline reinforcement learning (RL) (Levine et al., 2020) has become an increasingly attractive
035   research topic in recent years since data-driven learning of policies is appealing, especially in scenarios
036   where the interaction with the environment is expensive, e.g., robotic manipulation, autonomous
037   driving, and health care. Offline RL faces an inherent challenge of distributional shift (Ross et al.,
038   2011), which arises from visiting out-of-distribution states and actions. A direct way to address this
039
      issue is to apply policy constraints, which bound distributional shift by constraining how much the
      learned policy differs from the behavior policy (Kakade & Langford, 2002; Schulman et al., 2015).
040
      In single-agent Markov decision processes (MDPs), such constraints can lead to suboptimality of the
041
      learned policy since the optimal policy is usually a pure strategy, which assigns the optimal action
042   probability one at each state (Sutton & Barto, 2018). Since the behavior policy derived from a set
043   of offline transitions can hardly be a pure strategy, applying policy constraints with respect to the
044   behavior policy will sacrifice the optimality of the learned policy, even if the coverage of the offline
045   data is theoretically sufficient for learning the optimal policy (e.g., satisfying uniform concentration).
046
      For multi-agent scenarios, the optimal solution can still be a pure strategy when it is fully cooperative.
047
      However, in adversarial games, e.g., two-player zero-sum Markov games (MGs), we usually charac-
048   terize the optimal solution with the concept of Nash equilibrium (NE), which admits mixed strategies.
049   For example, in a two-player Rock-Paper-Scissors (RPS) game, the unique NE is the mixed strategy
050   ( 13 , 31 , 13 ) for both players. It is thus possible that policy constraint methods under a mixed-strategy
051   behavior policy may not sacrifice policy optimality in MGs. While recent research in the field of
052   game theory has developed various efficient equilibrium-learning dynamics that can be extended
053   into model-free RL algorithms (Lanctot et al., 2017; Lockhart et al., 2019), it has not been examined
      if these algorithms can be further combined with existing offline learning techniques (Siegel et al.,


                                                           1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      2020; Wu et al., 2019) while still guaranteeing to learn an exact Nash equilibrium under sufficient
055   assumptions on the data coverage.
056
057   On the other hand, while the existing pessimism-based methods are provably efficient for solving
058
      offline MDPs and MGs (see Jin et al. (2021); Xiong et al. (2023)), they have certain limitations when
      they are practically applied to real-world games. First, while these methods can be near-optimal
059
      when the environment contains uncertainty, they still require infinitely many samples to fully capture
060
      the stochasticity on the game and achieve the optimal solution, i.e., Nash equilibrium. However,
061   when the game is deterministic (e.g., chess and Go), they become no longer optimal since the game
062   transition can be determined by a finite number of samples, which are already sufficient for finding
063   NE. Second, existing pessimism-based methods usually require the information about game horizon
064   (see Cui & Du (2022a;b); Zhong et al. (2022); Xiong et al. (2023)) or dynamics model (see Yan
065   et al. (2024)) to solve Markov games. Zhang et al. (2023), as an exception, suffers from the problem
066   of computational inefficiency. Therefore, it is still challenging to propose a completely model-free
067   method that is capable of solving infinite-horizon MGs offline and, at the same time, does not lose
068   theoretical guarantee and computational efficiency.
069   With the above-mentioned concerns, we try to answer the following question:
070
071   Is it possible to find mixed-strategy Nash equilibrium offline for adversarial games using model-free
072                           equilibrium-learning dynamics with policy constraints?
073
074   This paper provides a positive answer to this question. Specifically, the contributions are threefold:
075
             • We propose a novel model-free RL algorithm for finding mixed-strategy Nash equilibrium in
076
               adversarial Markov games from a finite offline dataset. The algorithm, named Constrained
077
               Exploitability Descent (CED), is constructed by extending the ideas of policy constraint
078            methods from offline RL and a game theoretic approach, Exploitability Descent (ED).
079
080
             • We prove that, under the uniform coverage assumption, CED converges in deterministic two-
               player zero-sum MGs (Theorem 1) without relying on a generalized gradient like ED. We
081
               further show that the min-player policy becomes unexploitable when the opponent converges
082
               to an interior point of the constrained policy space (Theorem 2). By exchanging the status
083            of the two players and running CED twice, we can obtain a potential mixed-strategy NE.
084
             • We verify the equilibrium-finding capability of CED by conducting experiments in matrix
085
               games, a tree-form game, and a soccer game. Given a dataset with uniform coverage, CED
086
               can find NE policies in all scenarios, with the practical NashConv significantly lower than
087            the baseline derived from a pessimism-based method. As an offline RL algorithm, CED also
088            gradually improves the behavior policy under non-uniform coverage of offline game data.

      2   R ELATED W ORK
092
093
      Pessimism-based methods in offline games. The recent works that directly examine offline games
      basically focus on sample complexity and rely on pessimistic value functions, which have been well
094
      understood in single-agent RL (Rashidinejad et al., 2021; Xie et al., 2021). These works typically
095
      append bonuses to the original Bellman operators and obtain confidence bounds on the duality gap
096   for the policy computed from dynamic programming (Cui & Du, 2022a;b; Zhong et al., 2022; Xiong
097   et al., 2023; Yan et al., 2024). In the theoretical analyses, corresponding concentration inequality
098   is utilized to capture the stochasticity of the transition function. As a fundamental work, Cui & Du
099   (2022a) proves that the coverage assumption of unilateral concentration is sufficient for finding Nash
100   equilibrium offline in two-player zero-sum games by providing algorithms with Hoeffding/Bernstein-
101   type bonuses. Subsequent works improve the sample complexity (see Cui & Du (2022b)) and extend
102   the analyses to more complex scenarios concerning linear/general function approximations (see
103   Xiong et al. (2023); Zhang et al. (2023)).
104   Equilibrium-learning dynamics. The field of algorithmic game theory (Roughgarden, 2016; Nisan
105   et al., 2007) examines a wide range of equilibrium-learning dynamics. While the basic method of
106   dynamic programming (or more simply, backward induction) can only deal with perfect information
107   games like Markov games, game-theoretic learning dynamics, including Fictitous Play (FP) (Brown,
      1951), Policy Space Response Oracle (PSRO) (Lanctot et al., 2017), and Exploitability Descent


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      (ED) (Lockhart et al., 2019), can solve a broad class of games even with imperfect information.
109   Among them, PSRO is already extended through deep reinforcement learning. ED exhibits last-
110   iterate convergence and is conducive to offline RL extensions. While other methods like optimistic
111   multiplicative weights update (OMWU) also enjoy last-iterate convergence (see Lee et al. (2021)),
112   they have not been examined in infinite-horizon games. Therefore, we consider ED as the basic
113   dynamic to construct a new method for solving offline games.

186       3.3   P OLICY C ONSTRAINT M ETHODS

188       In offline RL, the training process is always affected by action distributional shift (Kumar et al., 2019),
189       which is one of the largest obstacles for model-free application of learning dynamics like Algorithm
190       1. In single-agent scenarios, the effect can be weakened by applying constraints to the learned policy
191       π to keep it close to the behavior policy πβ , which follows the distribution of the offline data. This
192       ensures that the process of Q-function computation hardly queries the out-of-distribution actions.
193
          The accumulative error in value estimation can be avoided at the expense of policy suboptimality.
194       Such constraints are commonly realized using direct policy constraints on the policy update (Siegel
195       et al., 2020) or indirect policy penalties on the value functions (Wu et al., 2019). Both methods
196       require using certain measure D(·, ·) (e.g., KL-divergence) to describe the closeness of two policies.

      5     T HEORETICAL A NALYSIS
285
      In this section, we theoretically show that it is possible for CED (Algorithm 2) to find an exact Nash
286
      equilibrium with the following two steps: First, we prove that CED can converge to a stationary point
287
      (Q̄, µ̄, ν̄) (Section 5.1). Second, we prove that the min-player policy ν̄ at the stationary point of CED
288   is unexploitable, like any mixed-strategy Nash equilibrium of full support (Section 5.2). All of the
289   omitted proofs are provided in Appendix A.
290
291
      Throughout our analysis, we require the uniform coverage assumption, which means that the non-
      repetitive transition set D∗ derived from the dataset D covers all state-action tuples (s, a, b). In Cui
292
      & Du (2022a), this assumption is called uniform concentration, and a weaker assumption named
293
      unilateral concentration is analyzed. By constructing a counterexample where the exact NE becomes
294   impossible to learn, they proved that unilateral concentration is somewhat necessary for finding Nash
295   equilibrium offline. However, when the NE is a completely mixed strategy (e.g., the unique NEs
296   of the matrix games in Section 6.1), unilateral concentration is equivalent to uniform concentration.
297   Therefore, the uniform coverage assumption can be necessary for our theoretical analysis on finding
298   mixed-strategy Nash equilibrium.

522   7               C ONCLUSION
523
524   In this paper, by proposing CED and analyzing its convergence properties, we demonstrate for the first
525   time that, unlike in MDPs, an optimal policy can be learned under policy constraints in adversarial
526   MGs. This conclusion is drawn from our theoretical and empirical results. With Theorem 1 and
527   Theorem 2, we prove that under uniform coverage, CED converges to an unexploitable min-player
528   policy without relying on the generalized gradient. In the experiments, our theory is verified by the
529   practical results of CED in multiple game scenarios. We also show that, similar to single-agent offline
530
      RL algorithms, CED can improve the behavior policy even from datasets without uniform coverage.
531   We hope this work will inspire more research on solving offline games. Actually, since CED is
532   constructed based on the game-theoretic approach of exploitability descent, which is also capable
533   of solving imperfect-information games (IIGs), it is possible to use CED as an offline IIG solver
534   by replacing the state and value with information state and counterfactual value. However, how to
535   estimate counterfactual value based on the current policy and offline game data remains an open
536
      problem. In order to guarantee a stable performance, further theoretical analysis is still required.
537   CED has the limitation that it is only able to find the mixed-strategy Nash equilibria in two-player
538   zero-sum games. However, it may not be the unique way of learning Nash equilibrium under policy
539   constraints, as a wide range of algorithms that exhibit last-iterate convergence (e.g., OMWU (Lee
      et al., 2021)) are currently available in the field of game theory. Combining them with existing offline

       C.2   D ISCUSSION ON THE P ERFORMANCE OF CED
981
982    Please note that our current experimental results are based on relatively small-scale games (like soccer
983    game) and the tabular representation of policies. Actually, there is a realistic reason that makes it a
984    rather difficult task to provide an exact evaluation for CED in games with a large scale. Note that the
985    metric NashConv is based on the computation of worst-case utility, which requires the best response
986    of each player against the opponent policy. When the state representation is complex, we cannot
987    avoid using deep reinforcement learning to approximately compute the best response. As a result,
988    the computed value of NashConv is affected by the choice of the algorithm for evaluation and can
989
       deviate from the true value itself. In simpler games like the soccer game, however, this value can
       be exactly computed through tabular-form dynamic programming, and it is practical to generate the
990
       learning curves for comparison purposes.
991
992    For large-scale games, where the uniform coverage assumption is not guaranteed, the practical
993    performance of CED can depend on a variety of aspects. We assume that the performance metric
994    of NashConv can be exactly computed. Then, the influential factors can be the data coverage itself,
995
       the data quality (the closeness of the behavior policy to Nash equilibrium), the hyperparameters of
       CED (including the specific policy constraint measure D(·, ·) for µ), and the network architecture
996
       for state value representation. Based on our existing results and observations, we can provide more
997
       information about how these factors affect the performance of CED.
998
999    In our experiment for non-uniform coverage, at each state, an action for each player (along with the
1000   subsequent states) is directly removed from the dataset of the uniformly random behavior policy.
1001
       Both data coverage and data quality are poor, which could be the primary reason for not learning a
       policy close to Nash equilibrium. Besides, we only use the simplest Euclidean distance in the direct
1002
       policy constraint on µ and do not employ neural networks. A well-tuned policy constraint measure
1003
       and a well-designed network architecture for the specific problem can help improve the performance
1004   of CED in large-scale games with non-uniform data coverage.
1005
1006   Specifically, Theorem 2 requires the converged max-player policy µ to be an interior point of the
1007
       constrained policy set. If the policy constraint measure on µ is well-tuned, this condition can be
       better satisfied, and the ultimate policy could also have a smaller NashConv gap. On the other hand,
1008
       since neural networks may generalize the existing transitions in the dataset to the unknown ones,
1009
       the performance can be better if CED employs an appropriate network architecture designed for the
1010   specific game. Also note that some existing work has pointed out that training value networks using
1011   classifications rather than regressions may significantly improve the performance of DRL algorithms
1012   in non-stationary environments (see Farebrother et al. (2024)). This technique could also be employed
1013   to improve the performance of CED in large-scale games.
