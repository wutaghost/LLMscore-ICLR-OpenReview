009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            Generalization of machine learning models can be severely compromised by data
012            poisoning, where adversarial changes are applied to the training data. This vulnera-
013            bility has led to interest in certifying (i.e., proving) that such changes up to a certain
014            magnitude do not affect test predictions. We, for the first time, certify Graph Neural
015            Networks (GNNs) against poisoning attacks, including backdoors, targeting the
016            node features of a given graph. Our certificates are white-box and based upon (i)
017            the neural tangent kernel, which characterizes the training dynamics of sufficiently
018
               wide networks; and (ii) a novel reformulation of the bilevel optimization problem
               describing poisoning as a mixed-integer linear program. Consequently, we leverage
019
               our framework to provide fundamental insights into the role of graph structure
020
               and its connectivity on the worst-case robustness behavior of convolution-based
021            and PageRank-based GNNs. We note that our framework is more general and
022            constitutes the first approach to derive white-box poisoning certificates for NNs,
023            which can be of independent interest beyond graph-related tasks.

026   1    I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Numerous works showcase the vulnerability of modern machine learning models to data poisoning,
029   where adversarial changes are made to the training data (Biggio et al., 2012; Muñoz-González et al.,
030   2017; Zügner & Günnemann, 2019a; Wan et al., 2023), as well as backdoor attacks affecting both
031   training and test sets (Goldblum et al., 2023). Empirical defenses against such threats are continually
032   at risk of being compromised by future attacks (Koh et al., 2022; Suciu et al., 2018). This motivates
033
      the development of robustness certificates, which provide formal guarantees that the prediction for a
      given test data point remains unchanged under an assumed perturbation model.
034
035   Robustness certificates can be categorized as providing deterministic or probabilistic guarantees, and
036   as being white box, i.e. developed for a particular model, or black box (model-agnostic). While each
037   approach has its strengths and applications (Li et al., 2023), we focus on white-box certificates as
038   they can provide a more direct understanding into the worst-case robustness behavior of commonly
039
      used models and architectural choices (Tjeng et al., 2019; Mao et al., 2024; Banerjee et al., 2024).
      The literature on poisoning certificates is less developed than certifying against test-time (evasion)
040
      attacks and we provide an overview and categorization in Table 1. Notably, white-box certificates are
041
      currently available only for decision trees (Drews et al., 2020), nearest neighbor algorithms (Jia et al.,
042   2022), and naive Bayes classification (Bian et al., 2024). In the case of Neural Networks (NNs), the
043   main challenge in white-box poisoning certification comes from capturing their complex training
044   dynamics. As a result, the current literature reveals that deriving white-box poisoning certificates
045   for NNs, and by extension Graph Neural Networks (GNNs), is still an unsolved problem, raising the
046   question if such certificates can at all be practically computed.
047
      In this work, we give a positive answer to this question by developing the first approach towards white-
048   box certification of NNs against data poisoning and backdoor attacks, and instantiate it for common
049   convolution-based and PageRank-based GNNs. Concretely, poisoning can be modeled as a bilevel
050   optimization problem over the training data D that includes training on D as its inner subproblem.
051   To overcome the challenge of capturing the complex training dynamics of NNs, we consider the
052   Neural Tangent Kernel (NTK) that characterizes the training dynamics of sufficiently wide NNs under
053   gradient flow (Jacot et al., 2018; Arora et al., 2019). In particular, we leverage the equivalence between
      NNs trained using the soft-margin loss and standard soft-margin Support Vector Machines (SVMs)

      (i) We are the first to certify GNNs in node-classification tasks against poisoning and backdoor
092
      attacks targeting node features. Our certification framework called QPCert is introduced in Sec. 3 and
093   leverages the NTK to capture the complex training-dynamics of GNNs. Further, it can be applied to

      5    D ISCUSSION AND RELATED WORK
442
443
      How tight is QPCert? We compute an upper bound on provable robustness using APGD by
444
      differentiating through the learning process. The results in Fig. 4d show that the provable robustness
445   bounds are tight for small pertubation budgets δ but less tight for larger δ, demonstrating one limitation
446   (other settings and attacks in App. I.3). While theoretically, the NTK bounds are tight (Theorem 2),
447   the approach of deriving element-wise bounds on Q to model A leading to a relaxation of P3 (Q)
448   can explain the gap between provable robustness and empirical attack. Thus, we are excited about
449   opportunities for future work to improve our approach for modeling A in the MILP P (Q).
450
      Is QPCert deterministic or probabilistic? Our certification framework is inherently deterministic,
451   offering deterministic guarantees for kernelized SVMs using the NTK as the kernel. When the width
452   of a NN approaches infinity, QPCert provides a deterministic robustness guarantee for the NN due to
453   the exact equivalence between an SVM with the NN’s NTK as kernel and the infinitely wide NN.
454   For sufficiently wide but finite-width NNs this equivalence holds with high probability (Chen et al.,
455   2021), making our certificate probabilistic in this context. However, note that this high-probability
456   guarantee is qualitatively different from other methods such as randomized smoothing (Cohen et al.,
457   2019), in which the certification approach itself is probabilistic and heavily relies on the number of
458   samplings and thus, inherently introduces randomness.
459   Generality of QPCert. While we focus on (G)NNs for graph data, our framework enables white-box
460   poisoning certification of NNs on any data domain. QPCert can be extended to other architecture
461   given the criteria outlined in App. N.4 and other tasks such as graph classification (see App. N.5).
462   Further, it allows for certifying general kernelized SVMs for arbitrary kernel choices if respective
463   kernel bounds as in Sec. 3.1 are derived. To the best of our knowledge, this makes our work
464   the first white-box poisoning certificate for kernelized SVMs. Moreover, the reformulation of the
465
      bilevel problem to MILP is directly applicable to any quadratic program that satisfies strong Slater’s
      constraint and certain bounds on the involved variables, hence the name QPCert. Thus extensions to
466
      certify quadratic programming layers in NN (Amos & Kolter, 2017) or other quadratic learners are
467
      thinkable. Therefore, we believe that our work opens up numerous new avenues of research into the
468   provable robustness against data poisoning.
469
470   Perturbation model. We study semi-verified learning (Charikar et al., 2017). This is particularly
471
      interesting for semi-supervised settings, where often a small fraction of nodes are manually verified
      and labeled (Shejwalkar et al., 2023), or when learning from the crowd Meister & Valiant (2018);
472
      Zeng & Shen (2023). However, this may produce overly pessimistic bounds when large fractions
473
      of the training data are unverified, but the adversary can only control a small part of it. We study
474   clean-label attacks bounded by ℓp -threat models instead of arbitrary perturbations to nodes controlled
475   by A. We refer to App. N.1 for a discussion with which commonly studied empirical attacks this
476   threat model aligns. Goldblum et al. (2023) distinctively names studying bounded clean-label attacks
477   as an open problem, as most works assume unrealistically large input perturbations. Exemplary, in
478   Fig. 2a, QPCert allows us to certify robustness against ℓp -bounded perturbations applied to all labeled
479   data. Most works on poisoning certification work with so-called ‘general attacks’ allowing arbitrary
480   modifications of data controlled by the adversary. In the setting studied in Fig. 2a, this would always
481   lead to 0 certified accuracy and being unable to provide non-trivial guarantees.
482   Related work. There is little literature on white-box poisoning certificates (see Table 1), and existing
483   techniques (Drews et al., 2020; Meyer et al., 2021; Jia et al., 2022; Bian et al., 2024) cannot be
484   extended to NNs. We summarize the most important related work and refer to App. M for more
485   details. The bilevel problem Eq. (3) is investigated by several works in the context of developing a
      poisoning attack or empirical defense, including for SVMs (Biggio et al., 2012; Xiao et al., 2015; Koh


                                                          9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      & Liang, 2017; Jagielski et al., 2018). Notably Mei & Zhu (2015) reformulate the bilevel problem
487   P2 (Q) for SVMs to a bilinear single-level problem similar to P3 (Q) but only solve it heuristically for
488   attack generation and do not realize the possibility of a MILP reformulation and certification. There
489   are no poisoning certificates for clean-label attacks against GNNs. (Lai et al., 2024) is the only work
490   on poisoning certification of GNNs, but differ incomparably in their threat model and are black-box
491   as well as not applicable to backdoors. Lingam et al. (2024) develops a label poisoning attack for
492   GNNs using the bilevel problem with a regression objective and including NTKs as surrogate models.
493   We note that (Steinhardt et al., 2017) develops statistical bounds on the loss that are not applicable to
494   certify classification.
495   Conclusion. We derive the first white-box poisoning certificate framework for NNs through their
496   NTKs and demonstrate its effective applicability to semi-supervised node classification tasks common
497   in graph learning. In particular, we show that our certificate generates non-trivial robustness guaran-
498   tees and insights into the worst-case poisoning robustness to feature perturbations of a wide range of
499   GNNs. The study on node feature perturbations is of practical concern in many application areas of
500   GNNs such as spam detection (Li et al., 2019) or fake news detection (Hu et al., 2024) (see App. N.3
501
      for a more detailed discussion), and certification against them poses unique graph-related challenges
      due to the interconnectedness of nodes. While we address the robustness to node feature perturbations,
502
      certifying against structural perturbations to the graph itself remains an open, complex, but important
503
      problem and we refer to App. N.2 for a technical discussion on the arising challenges. Thus, this
504   offers a valuable direction for future research. Furthermore, as is the case with all deterministic
505   certificates (Li et al., 2023), scaling to large datasets remains challenging. Consequently, research on
506   scaling deterministic certificates is an impactful avenue for future work.

510
      Our method represents a robustness certificate for white-box models. This allows a more informed
511
      decision when it comes to the safety aspects of currently used models. However, insights into
512
      worst-case robustness can be used for good but potentially also by malicious actors. We strongly
513   believe that research about the limitations of existing models is crucial in making models safer and
514   thus, outweighs potential risks. We are not aware of any direct risks coming from our work.

996    C     E QUIVALENCE OF GNN S TO SVM S
997
998    We show the equivalence between GNNs and SVMs by extending the result from Chen et al. (2021),
999    which showed that an infinite-width NN trained by gradient descent on a soft-margin loss has the
1000   same training dynamics as that of an SVM with the NN’s NTK as the kernel. The fulcrum of their
1001   proof that directly depends on the NN is that the NTK stays constant throughout the training (refer to
1002   (Chen et al., 2021, Theorem 3.4)). As we consider the same learning setup with only changing the
1003   network to GNNs, it is enough to show that the graph NTKs stay constant throughout training for the
1004
       equivalence to hold in this case.
1005   Constancy of Graph NTKs. This constancy of the NTK in the case of infinitely-wide NNs is deeply
1006   studied in Liu et al. (2020) and derived the conditions for the constancy as stated in Theorem 4.
1007   Theorem 4 ((Liu et al., 2020)). The constancy of the NTK throughout the training of the NN holds if
1008   and only if (i) the last layer of the NN is linear; (ii) the Hessian spectral norm ∥H∥ of the neural
1009   network with respect to the parameters is small, that is, → 0 with the width of the network; (iii) the
1010   parameters of the network w during training and at initialization is bounded, that is, parameters at
1011   time t, wt , satisfies ∥wt − w0 ∥2 ≤ ϵ.
1012
1013   Now, we prove the constancy of graph NTKs of the GNNs by showing the three conditions.
1014   (i) Linear last layer. The GNNs considered in Definitions 1 and 7 have a linear last layer.
1015
       (ii) Small Hessian spectral norm. Recollect that we use NTK parameterization for initializing
1016
       the network parameters, that is, N (0, 1/width). This is equivalent to initializing the network with
1017
       standard normal N (0, 1) and appropriately normalizing the layer outputs (Arora et al., 2019; Sa-
1018   banayagam et al., 2023). To exemplify, the APPNP network definition with the normalization is given
1019                                                                                (l)     cσ (l)
       in Eq. (10). Similarly for other GNNs, the normalization results in scaling ϕθ as √     ϕ where h is
                                                                                             h θ

                                                                           √            of the graph structure

       I.3                             T IGHTNESS OF QPC ERT
1543
1544   First, we present the tightness of QPCert in Figs. 9 and 10 evaluated with our strongest employed
1545   attacks for each setting: For graph poisoning (Fig. 9), APGD is employed with direct differentiation
1546   through the learning process (QPLayer) for the P U setting in Fig. 9b and for the P L setting in Fig. 9a.
1547   For the backdoor attack setting (Fig. 10), first a poisoning attack is carried out with APGD (QPLayer)
1548   and then, the respective test node is additionally attacked with APGD in an evasion setting. Fig. 10b
1549   shows the result for the BU setting and Fig. 10a for the BL setting. Interstingly, QPCert seems to
1550   be more tight in a backdoor setting than in a pure poisoning setting. However, this could also be
1551
       explained by the fact that an evasion attack is easier to perform than a poisoning attack and thus,
       APGD potentially provided lower upper bounds to the actual robustness than for the pure poisoning
1552
       setting. Another interesting observation is that for the backdoor settings, the rankings of the GNNs
1553
       regarding certified robustness seems to roughly correspond to the robust accuracies obtained by the
1554   backdoor attack.
1555
1556   In Fig. 11 performing a gradient-based attack (APGD) using either exact gradients with QPLayer
1557
       or meta-gradients obtained through MetaAttack’s surrogate model is compared. For both the PL
       (Fig. 11a) and PU (Fig. 11b) setting using exact gradients results in a lower upper bound to the
1558
       robust accuracy (i.e., a stronger attack). Thus, we use the exact gradients from QPLayer to measure
1559
       the tightness of QPCert. Meta-gradients from MetaAttack are obtained by adapting Algorithm 2
1560   in (Zügner & Günnemann, 2019a) to feature perturbations, through setting a maximum number
1561   of iterations as the stop criterion and instead of choosing an edge with maximal score, update the
1562   feature matrix with the meta-gradient using APGD. In MetaAttack, λ trading of the self-supervised
1563   with the training loss is set to 0.5. Interestingly, for small budgets, MetaAttack can lead to the
1564   opposite intended effect. Exemplary, for a GCN in the PL setting with δ = 0.1, the generalization
1565   performance is slightly increased. This indicates that for small perturbation budgets, the meta-gradient
       of MetaAttack’s surrogate model does not transfer well to the infinite-width networks. However, for


                                                                                                                                                         29
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1566
       larger budgets, MetaAttack still provides a strong, albeit weaker attack than exact gradients. In Figure
1567   Fig. 12 we compare performing the above mentioned gradient-based backdoor attack with the simple
1568   backdoor strategy proposed by Xing et al. (2024) with a trigger size of 0.5. We observe that Xing
1569   et al. (2024)’s attack is significantly weaker compared to the gradient-based attack and only starts to
1570   reduce accuracy of the models for high attack budgets. This can be explained by several observations:
1571   For small ℓp -budgets, the backdoor trigger is often distorted in the backdoored nodes by having to
1572   project the perturbation back into the allowed ℓp -ball and secondly, the attack is simple, static and not
1573   adaptive. Concretely, it simply copies certain features to other nodes without considering the attacked
1574   model. We want to note that similar to MetaAttack, for small budgets, for BU we can observe for
1575
       MLP that the change actually results in slightly higher generalization of the model under attack,
       showing that for small budgets, the backdoor strategy in Xing et al. (2024) is not effective.

1714
1715   In Fig. 16 we compare two common poisoning defenses namely GNNGuard (Zhang & Zitnik, 2020)
1716   and ElasticGNN (Liu et al., 2021) with the certified accuracy provided by QPCert. While the
1717   accuracies provided by a defense are not certified accuracies (i.e., they are only upper bounds to
1718   the true robust accuracy) and hence, can only be compared partly with the certified accuracy which
1719
       represents a true lower bound to the robust accuracy. However, a comparison is still interesting as it
       allows to answer the question, of how big the gap between the best-certified accuracy to the robust
1720
       accuracy provided by defenses is and if we could even get a certified accuracy result comparable to a
1721
       poisoning defense’s accuracy. Interestingly, Fig. 16 shows that for small to intermediate budgets, the
1722   certified accuracy of an infinite-width GCN as provided by QPCert is higher than the robust accuracy
1723   provided by the defense baselines. This can be explained by the fact that even ElasticGNN and
1724   GNNGuard show lower base clean accuracy despite significant hyperparameter tuning (experimental
1725   details see below paragraph) paired with a few very brittle predictions. We hypothesize that this is
1726   due to the difficult learning problem a CSBM poses (despite being a small dataset) paired with the
1727   fact that both poisoning defenses have GCN-like base models where the graph / propagation scheme
       is adapted to be more robust to poisoning while potentially trading off clean accuracy.

1729   following Zhang & Zitnik (2020). GNNGuard uses a 2-layer GCN as a baseline model and hyperpa-
1730   rameters are searched in the grid: (i) number of filters {8, 16, 32}, (ii) dropout {0, 0.2, 0.5}, (iii)
1731   learning rate {0.01, 0.001}, (iv) weight decay {5e − 3, 1e − 3, 5e − 4, 1e − 4} over 10 seeds resulting
1732   in 720 models. For ElasticGNN the hyperparameter grid reported in Liu et al. (2021) is explored over
1733   10 seeds resulting in 11520 models due to ElasticGNN having more hyperparameters to tune. It’s

1906   Poisoning certificates. The literature on poisoning certificates is significantly less developed than
1907
       certifying against test-time (evasion) attacks (Li et al., 2023) and we provide an overview in Table 1.
       Black-box certificates for poisoning are derived following three different approaches: (i) Randomized
1908
       smoothing, a popular probabilistic test-time certificate strategy (Cohen et al., 2019), in which
1909
       randomization performed over the training dataset (Rosenfeld et al., 2020; Weber et al., 2023; Zhang
1910   et al., 2022). Other than data partitioning, a common defense is to sanitize the data, and Hong
1911   et al. (2024) certifies diffusion-based data sanitation via randomized smoothing. (ii) Ensembles:
1912   Creating separate partitions of the training data, training individual base classifiers on top of them and
1913   certifying a constructed ensemble classifier (Levine & Feizi, 2021; Jia et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2022;
1914   Rezaei et al., 2023); Jia et al. (2021) and Chen et al. (2022) offer certificates and collective certificates,
1915   respectively, for bagging, while Levine & Feizi (2021) and Wang et al. (2022) derive certificates for
1916   aggregation-based methods tailored for black-box classifiers. (iii) Differential Privacy2 (DP): Ma
1917   et al. (2019) show that any (ϵ, δ)-DP learner enjoys a certain provable poisoning robustness. Liu et al.
1918
       (2023) extend this result to more general notions of DP. Xie et al. (2023) derives guarantees against
       arbitrary data poisoning in DP federated learning setup. However, white-box deterministic poisoning
1919
       certificates remain sparse. Drews et al. (2020) and Meyer et al. (2021) derive poisoning certificates
1920
       for decision trees using abstract interpretations, while Jia et al. (2022) provides a poisoning certificate
1921   for nearest neighbor algorithms based on their inherent majority voting principle. Recently, Bian et al.
1922   (2024) derives a poisoning certificate for naive Bayes classification.
1923
1924
       Poisoning attacks and defense using the bilevel problem. Biggio et al. (2012) and Xiao et al. (2015)
       use the bilevel problem with SVM hinge loss and regularized ERM to generate poison samples, and
1925
       solve it using iterative gradient ascent. Mei & Zhu (2015) also recognize the possibility to transform
1926
       the bilevel problem into a single-level one. However, they only reformulate the problem into a
1927   single-level bilinear one and solve it heuristically w.r.t. to the training data to generate poisoning
1928   attacks. Koh & Liang (2017) also considers the bilevel problem to detect and also generate poisoned
1929   samples using influence functions (gradient and Hessian vector product).

       Graphs. Currently, there are no poisoning certificates for clean-label attacks specifically developed
1931

       N.1    A PPLICABILITY TO C OMMONLY S TUDIED P ERTURBATION M ODELS AND ATTACKS
1947
1948   QPCert applies to any poisoning or backdoor attack that performs ℓp -bounded feature perturbations.
1949   As such, QPCert is directly applicable to clean-label (graph) backdoor attacks as proposed by Turner
1950   et al. (2019) and Xing et al. (2024), and clean-label poisoning attacks such as Huang et al. (2020) and
1951   Geiping et al. (2021). It is not directly applicable to poisoning of the graph structure as performed
1952   by MetaAttack (Zügner & Günnemann, 2019a). However, the MetaAttack strategy can be easily
1953
       adapted to poison node features as done in App. I.3 and we discuss the challenges to extend QPCert
       to structure perturbations in App. N.2. The backdoor attack proposed by Dai et al. (2023) changes
1954
       the node features and training labels jointly and thus, our method is only applicable if the training
1955
       labels will be kept constant or the poisoned nodes are sampled only from the class, the training label
1956   should be changed to. Similar, Xi et al. (2021) develops a backdoor attack that changes the features
1957   and graph structure jointly and thus, QPCert is not applicable given the graph structure changes.

       N.3   P RACTICAL I MPLICATION OF F EATURE AND S TRUCTURE P ERTURBATIONS
2057
2058   Both feature and structure perturbations can find applications in real-world scenarios. In particular,
2059   important application areas for graph learning methods with adversarial actors are fake news detection
2060   (Hu et al., 2024) and spam detection (Li et al., 2019). Regarding fake news detection, feature
2061   perturbations can model changes to the fake news content or to (controlled) user account comments
2062   and profiles to mislead detectors (Hu et al., 2024; Le et al., 2020). Structure perturbations allow to
2063   model a change in the propagation patterns (e.g., through changing a retweet graph) (Wang et al.,
2064   2023). The qualitative difference in the application of feature compared to structure perturbations
2065   is similar for spam detection. Here, feature perturbation can model spammers trying to adapt their
2066
       comments to avoid detection (Li et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2012). However, structure perturbations
       can model behavioral changes in the posting patterns of spammers to imitate real users (Soliman &
2067
       Girdzijauskas, 2017; Wang et al., 2012).

2070   N.4   QPC ERT FOR OTHER GNN S
2071
2072
       While our analysis focused on commonly used GNNs with and without skip connections, QPCert
       is broadly applicable to any GNNs with a well-defined analytical form of the NTK. The follow-
2073
       ing challenges and considerations have to be taken into account when extending QPCert to other
2074
       architectures:
2075
2076         1. NTK-network equivalence: The equivalence between the network and NTK breaks down
2077            when the network has non-linear last layer or bottleneck layers (Liu et al., 2020). Conse-
2078            quently, our certificates do not hold for such networks.
2079
             2. Analytical form of NTK:Deriving a closed-form expression for NTK is needed to derive
2080
                bounds on the kernel. This might be challenging for networks with batch-normalizations or
2081            advanced pooling layers.
2082
2083         3. Tight bounds for the NTK: Ensuring non-trivial certificates requires deriving tight bounds
2084
                for the NTK. Depending on the NTK, additional adaptation of our bounding strategy may
                be necessary.
2085
2086
       Despite these considerations, most message-passing networks satisfy these criteria, making QPCert
2087
       readily applicable to a wide range of architectures.
