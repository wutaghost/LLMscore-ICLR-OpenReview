009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            The advancement of scientific knowledge relies on synthesizing prior research to
012            forecast future developments, a task that has become increasingly intricate. The
013            emergence of large language models (LLMs) offers a transformative opportunity
014            to automate and streamline this process, enabling faster and more accurate aca-
015            demic discovery. However, recent attempts either limit to producing surveys or fo-
016            cus overly on downstream tasks. To this end, we introduce a novel task that bridges
017            two key challenges: the comprehensive synopsis of past research and the accurate
018
               prediction of emerging trends, dubbed Dual Temporal Research Analysis. This
               dual approach requires not only an understanding of historical knowledge but also
019
               the ability to predict future developments based on detected patterns. To evaluate,
020
               we present an evaluation benchmark encompassing 20 research topics and 210
021            key AI papers, based on the completeness of historical coverage and predictive
022            reliability. We further draw inspirations from dual-system theory and propose a
023            framework HorizonAI which utilizes a specialized temporal knowledge graph for
024            papers, to capture and organize past research patterns (System 1), while leverag-
025            ing LLMs for deeper analytical reasoning (System 2) to enhance both summariza-
026            tion and prediction. Our framework demonstrates a robust capacity to accurately
027            summarize historical research trends and predict future developments, achieving
028            significant improvements in both areas. For summarizing historical research, we
029
               achieve a 18.99% increase over AutoSurvey; for predicting future developments,
               we achieve a 7.71% increase over GPT-4o.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
      For over 200,000 years, human intelligence has evolved, with knowledge-building processes under-
034
      pinned by the dual imperatives of learning from the past and forecasting future directions (Sternberg,
035
      2000). From the conceptual foundations of Ramon Llull’s “Tree of Knowledge” to Francis Bacon’s
036   structured approach to human learning, both historical and contemporary scholars have emphasized
037   the critical role of synthesizing past insights to drive future advancements. In recent years, modern
038   frameworks addressing scientific discovery and knowledge structuring have further underscored this
039   dual focus (Fire & Guestrin, 2019; Nagarajan et al., 2015).
040
      The rapid growth of scientific publications presents an unprecedented challenge: researchers must
041
      now sift through vast amounts of literature to extract relevant historical insights and anticipate future
042   trends (Fire & Guestrin, 2019). LLMs offer potential solutions by automating tasks such as retrieval,
043   summarization, and analysis. However, most existing approaches either concentrate on retrospective
044   literature reviews (Wang et al., 2024; Agarwal et al., 2024) or focus solely on generating novel
045   research by using simple concept-level link predictions lacking semantic relationships (Krenn et al.,
046   2023; Lu, 2021; Gu & Krenn; Tran & Xie, 2021). These narrow approaches neglect the essential
047   integration of synthesizing past research with projecting future developments, a combination that is
048   increasingly crucial for scientific discovery (Figure 1).
049   To address this gap, we propose Dual Temporal Research Analysis (DTRA), a novel task that unifies
050   the analysis of past research with the forecasting of future trends. In contrast to traditional method-
051   ologies, which focus on either historical synthesis or future speculation, our task bridges both by
052   leveraging past knowledge to generate informed predictions. This twofold task is especially rele-
053   vant in domains such as artificial intelligence (AI), where understanding prior research trajectories
      is essential for predicting emerging advancements.

075   The DTRA consists of two interconnected phases: it involves consolidating and validating histor-
076   ical research trends, followed by the application of these insights to predict future developments.
077   This approach mirrors the distinction between validation and experimentation, wherein past re-
078   search serves as a foundation for verifying patterns and future predictions represent experimental,
079   data-driven inferences (Chaiken, 1999; Posner, 2020).
080   Our framework HorizonAI (Figure 2) draws inspiration from Dual-System Theory (Chaiken, 1999),
081   which posits that human cognition operates through two systems: System 1, which performs rapid,
082   intuitive assessments, and System 2, which engages in deliberate, analytical reasoning. In the con-
083   text of our framework, System 1 focuses on efficiently organizing historical data into structured
084   formats such as temporal knowledge graphs (TKGs) (Cai et al., 2022), while System 2 conducts
085   in-depth reasoning using Chain-of-Thought (CoT) (Wei et al., 2022; OpenAI, 2024b), to identify
086
      patterns and project future developments. Together, these systems enable a comprehensive analysis
      of both past and future research.
087
088   Given the novelty of the task, no established evaluation benchmarks or standardized methodologies
089   currently exist. To address this, we introduce an evaluation benchmark ResBench that assesses
090   the performance through historical completeness and predictive reliability. Extensive experiments
091   demonstrate the superior performance of our proposed framework, HorizonAI, in tracing historical
092
      trends and making reliable future predictions. In comparison to existing baselines, it achieves higher
      predictive accuracy and generates more coherent, insightful content.
093
094   The main contributions of this paper are summarized as follows:
095   • Integrating Historical Analysis and Future Forecasting: We introduce DTRA, a task that com-
096     bines the analysis of historical research with predictions about future trends. Unlike traditional
097     methods that focus on either past research or future possibilities, this task incorporates both to
098     generate informed projections, providing a more balanced perspective on scientific progress by
099     ensuring that insights from the past inform future directions.
100   • Coginitive-Inspired Framework: Our approach HorizonAI is influenced by Dual-System The-
101     ory, suggesting that human cognition operates through both intuitive and analytical processes. In
102     our framework, System 1 organizes past research into temporal knowledge graphs, while System
103     2 deliberately reasons to uncover patterns and anticipate future developments. This dual approach
104     supports a more comprehensive, precise, and dynamic understanding of research trends.
105   • Innovative Benchmark for Evaluation: We propose a benchmark ResBench designed to evaluate
106     DTRA based on historical coverage and predictive reliability. By incorporating datasets that span
107     both historical and predictive dimensions, this benchmark provides the research community with
        a tool for systematically testing methods that summarize past research while forecasting future

115   More formally, we define the Dual Temporal Research Analysis(DTRA) task as follows:
116   Given the input topic and source paper pair (T , P), the task is to narrate the research history H of
117
      the topic and generate possible research ideas F .
118   To achieve this task, we propose HorizonAI (as illustrated in Figure 2), a framework inspired by
119   Dual-System Theory. We retrieve related papers to represent the history hs∼t = {P1 , P2 , ..., Pn }
120   during the time interval of s ∼ t and structure it into a graph G (i.e. our PaperTKG), then using
121   strategy S to search the graph for timeline generation τs∼t = S(G). The historical narrative H
122   is generated by LLMs using temporal reasoning based on the timeline. We sample possible future
123
      predictions F , p(F |H) > threshold based on H.

      2.2       Q UERY G ENERATION AND T EMPORAL R EASONING
217
218   Query Generation and Graph Exploration Inspired by the local-to-global search strategy for
219   summarization utilized by the GraphRAG (Edge et al., 2024), we design a global-to-local search
220   strategy to narrow down the search range step by step without missing related nodes or relations.
221   We first use global search to locate paper nodes related to the query, then apply local search to get
222   detailed relations and neighbors of the paper node. Global Search: The query for global search is
223   generated from the following three aspects: the application domain, the target problem to solve, and
224
      the method. We traverse all the paper nodes and select the ones related to our query by similarity,
      then we check the timestamps of these nodes to ensure that representative works from each year are
225
      included. Local Search: In the local search phase, we collect the one-hop relevances of the target
226
      paper nodes (i.e. the details of the paper) and structure them into a chain, finally, we get the timeline
227   for the topic.
228
229   Temporal Reasoning Large Language Models (LLMs) are utilized to perform temporal reason-
230
      ing (Yuan et al., 2024) using Chain-of-Thought (CoT) prompting (Wei et al., 2022). They are
      prompted to identify key research milestones, explain the methods and solutions, highlight connec-
231
      tions between works, and emphasize the progression over time, step by step, to generate a coherent
232
      narrative of the research history. The prompt for converting the timeline to logical text can be found
233   in Appendix C.2.

      2.3       R ESULT G ENERATION
236
237   The output of our HorizonAI consists of two components: a historical narrative and a future
238   prediction, with the latter being generated based on the former.
239
240
      Historical Narrative We narrate the history from both local and holistic perspectives using tempo-
241
      ral reasoning through CoT prompts (listed in Appendix C.2). For the local perspective, we structure
242
      the narrative by using the subtitles from the related work sections of the target papers as an outline,
243   producing content resembling related work discussions. For the holistic perspective, we create out-
244   lines based on section titles from selected surveys to represent the overall development of the topic.
245   Each section is then expanded with content following the outline, resulting in a survey-like narrative.
246   Future prediction Existing approaches often emphasize the novelty of generated research ideas,
247   overlooking that feasibility is a more critical factor than pure innovation. To ensure the ideas are
248   grounded in practicality, we reference the local historical narrative and prompt LLMs to outline de-
249   tailed roadmaps for realizing each idea. For each subdomain, multiple potential ideas are sampled,
250   ensuring a balance between originality and implementability.

316

440   Recent search strategies using Knowledge Graphs (KGs) improve retrieval by leveraging structured
441   relationships in Large Language Models (LLMs) to enhance inference and interpretability (Pan et al.,
442   2024; Yang et al., 2024). Structuring LLM interactions with KGs refines retrieval performance,
443
      enabling effective responses to complex queries (Sun et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2023; 2024). Unlike
      traditional RAG methods that rely on text embeddings, KGs serve as indices to enhance precision
444
      by navigating relevant subgraphs. Approaches like KAPING (Baek et al., 2023), G-Retriever (He
445
      et al., 2024), and Graph-ToolFormer (Zhang, 2023) enhance retrieval by using graph metrics to refine
446   search results, while SURGE (Kang et al., 2023) and FABULA (Ranade & Joshi, 2023) leverage
447   KGs for narrative generation grounded in factual subgraphs. Systems like ITRG (Feng et al., 2023)
448   and IR-CoT (Trivedi et al., 2023) facilitate multi-hop question answering by tracing interconnected
449   knowledge nodes, while Selfmem (Cheng et al., 2023) employs KGs for generation-augmented
450   retrieval. GraphRAG (Edge et al., 2024) advances these approaches by introducing a unique local-
451   to-global search strategy with a self-generated graph index, which inspired us for our global-to-local
452   retrieval approach.
453
454   5.2   T EMPORAL K NOWLEDGE G RAPHS (TKG S )
455
456   Temporal Knowledge Graphs (TKGs) extend traditional Knowledge Graphs(KGs) by incorporating
457   temporal information, enabling the representation of dynamically changing facts (Ji et al., 2021).
458   Temporal Knowledge Graph Completion (TKGC) is a key task in TKGs, focusing on filling in miss-
459   ing information and predicting future relationships (Wang et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2023; Zhang et al.,
460
      2023; Xiong et al., 2024b). Additionally, TGKs specialize in applications like event tracking and
      historical data analysis, providing a more nuanced framework for mapping extensive academic liter-
461
      ature. Specialized models such as Know-Evolve (Trivedi et al., 2017) and TA-TransE (Garcı́a-Durán
462
      et al., 2018) have advanced temporal reasoning, while Xiong et al. (2024a) introduced a two-step
463   framework for language-based temporal reasoning that translates narratives into TKGs. Despite
464   these innovations, many existing models remain general and do not specifically address the orga-
465   nizational needs of academic information. Our proposed PaperTKG thus serves as a tailored TKG
466   structure designed explicitly for managing scholarly papers, enabling the tracking of paper evolu-
467   tion, citation networks, and topic trends over time, thereby fulfilling the demand for a specialized
468   TKG in academia.
469
470   5.3   LLM S IN S CIENTIFIC D EVELOPMENT
471
472   Large Language Models (LLMs) are recognized for their transformative potential in scientific re-
473   search, owing to their ability to process and analyze vast datasets beyond human capacity. Recent
474   studies, such as those by Baek et al. (2024), Yang et al. (2023), and Qi et al. (2023), focus on
475   Literature-based Discovery (LBD) (Swanson, 1986), using LLMs to mine academic publications
476   for correlations and generate research insights. Wang et al. (2024) explores the possibility of LLMs
477
      automatically generating survey papers, while other works (Elsevier, 2024; Agarwal et al., 2024)
      emphasize automated retrieval and summarization of existing literature, often neglecting the pre-
478
      diction of future research trends. In a pioneering effort, Li & Flanigan (2024) formalizes future
479
      language modeling, aiming to predict future textual data based on temporal histories. Additionally,
480   several studies (Si et al., 2024; Baek et al., 2024; Zheng et al., 2024) develop LLM-based agents
481   for research idea generation, a critical step in the early stages of scientific inquiry. AI Scientist (Lu
482   et al., 2024) represents the first comprehensive system for fully automated scientific discovery using
483   LLMs, generating novel research ideas independent of prior work, though it requires multiple iter-
484   ations to yield viable outcomes. In contrast, we introduce HorizonAI, a dual-system approach that
485   integrates both the summarization of past research and the prediction of future directions, offering
      superior performance in both tasks compared to existing models.

      In this paper, we introduced Dual Temporal Research Analysis (DTRA), a novel task that integrates
489   the summarization of historical research with the prediction of future trends. Our framework, Hori-
490   zonAI, draws inspiration from Dual-System Theory to organize past research using PaperTKG (a
491   temporal knowledge graph for papers) and employs LLMs for in-depth reasoning to generate both
492   historical narratives and future projections.
493
      Through extensive evaluation on the ResBench benchmark, we demonstrated that bridging the tasks
494
      of historical analysis and future forecasting enhances the performance of both. Our results showed
495   significant improvements in summarizing past works and generating accurate predictions compared
496   to existing methods.

      across multiple domains. Future work will focus on expanding the dataset beyond AI-related topics
