011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            We consider the problem of physically-based inverse rendering using 3D Gaussian
014            Splatting (3DGS) representations (Kerbl et al., 2023b). While recent 3DGS meth-
015            ods have achieved remarkable results in novel view synthesis (NVS), accurately
016
               capturing high-fidelity geometry, physically interpretable materials, and lighting
               remains challenging, as it requires precise geometry modeling to provide accu-
017
               rate surface normals, along with physically-based rendering (PBR) techniques to
018
               ensure correct material and lighting disentanglement. Previous 3DGS methods
019            resort to approximating surface normals but often struggle with noisy local geom-
020            etry, leading to inaccurate normal estimation and suboptimal material-lighting de-
021            composition. In this paper, we introduce GeoSplatting, a novel hybrid representa-
022            tion that augments 3DGS with explicit geometric guidance and differentiable PBR
023            equations. Specifically, we bridge isosurface and 3DGS together, where we first
024            extract isosurface mesh from a scalar field, then convert it into 3DGS points and
025            formulate PBR equations for them in a fully differentiable manner. In GeoSplat-
026            ting, 3DGS is grounded on mesh geometry, enabling precise surface normal mod-
027
               eling, which facilitates the use of PBR frameworks for material decomposition,
               achieving excellent decomposition performance, especially for reflective cases.
028
               This approach further maintains the efficiency and quality of NVS from 3DGS
029
               while ensuring accurate geometry from the isosurface. Comprehensive evalua-
030            tions across diverse datasets demonstrate the superior efficiency and competitive
031            inverse rendering performance of GeoSplatting compared to state-of-the-art in-
032            verse rendering baselines.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
036   The inverse rendering task, i.e., recovering 3D attributes such as geometry, spatially-varying materi-
037   als, and lighting from multi-view images or videos, has been a long-standing goal in computer vision
038   and graphics. It plays a critical role in numerous industrial applications, including film production,
039
      gaming, and VR/AR, for photo-realistic novel-view synthesis and immersive user interactions. This
      task is typically approached using carefully designed 3D representations (Mildenhall et al., 2020;
040
      Müller et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2021; Shen et al., 2021; 2023) coupled with the corresponding dif-
041
      ferentiable rendering techniques (Boss et al., 2021a; Verbin et al., 2022a; Chen et al., 2019; Laine
042   et al., 2020). While great progress has been made recently (Munkberg et al., 2022; Jiang et al., 2023;
043   Gao et al., 2023), efficiently and accurately capturing various 3D attributes remains challenging due
044   to the complexities of light transport in real-world environments, including intricate local geometry,
045   non-Lambertian surface, complex lighting conditions, occlusions, etc.
046
      The key to tackling the inverse rendering task lies in effectively modeling the underlying 3D geome-
047
      try, where Physically-Based Rendering (PBR) techniques can be applied to disentangle materials and
048   lighting. Numerous prior works have developed various 3D representations and their correspond-
049   ing differentiable rendering equations to address this challenge, each offering unique advantages
050   and limitations. Implicit representations like NeRF (Mildenhall et al., 2020; Verbin et al., 2022b)
051   are well-suited for novel view synthesis but are computationally expensive and incompatible with
052   existing graphics pipelines. In contrast, explicit representations like mesh (Munkberg et al., 2022)
053   provide explicit geometry, allowing for well-defined rendering techniques, and facilitating tasks
      like relighting and material editing. However, optimizing explicit representations is challenging,

069   especially when dealing with complex geometries like thin structures. More recently, 3D Gaussian
070   Splatting (3DGS) (Kerbl et al., 2023b) has emerged as an efficient 3D representation for high-quality
071   novel-view synthesis. However, vanilla 3DGS is not designed to provide accurate geometry or dis-
072   entangled materials, limiting its applicability to inverse rendering tasks. To tackle this challenge,
073   various methods have studied assigning a normal direction to each 3DGS point to model local geo-
074   metric surfaces, along with a PBR formula using the approximated normals (Jiang et al., 2023; Shi
075   et al., 2023; Gao et al., 2023). However, these approaches offer only approximations of true normals,
076
      and as a result, may struggle with local minima in regions of complex geometry.
077   In this paper, we propose GeoSplatting, a more principled solution that leverages the strengths of
078   both explicit representations and 3DGS for the inverse rendering task. At the core of GeoSplatting is
079   a differentiable adaptor that integrates differentiable isosurface techniques (Shen et al., 2021; 2023)
080   with 3D Gaussian Splatting. Specifically, we first utilize the differentiable isosurface techniques to
081   extract a mesh from a scalar field that we want to optimize. We then introduce MGadapter, i.e.,
082
      Mesh-to-Gaussian-adaptor, that samples 3D Gaussian points on the mesh surface in a differentiable
      manner, naturally grounding the location of each Gaussian point on the surface geometry, from
083
      which we could estimate precise normal for each point. To render the sampled 3D Gaussian points,
084
      we design an efficient and differentiable PBR framework, leveraging the split-sum model (Karis,
085   2013) and applying it to the 3D Gaussian points. During training, since all the operations are differ-
086   entiable, we can train our model end-to-end.
087
088
      Our GeoSplatting offers several advantages over both 3DGS and explicit mesh-based representa-
      tions. Compared to the vanilla 3DGS (Kerbl et al., 2023b) and its variants, our approach provides
089
      explicit geometric guidance from the isosurface, enabling more accurate normal estimation, which
090
      is crucial for inverse rendering optimization. On the other hand, compared to the mesh represen-
091   tations (Munkberg et al., 2022), GeoSplatting leverages the high efficiency and superior rendering
092   quality inherited from 3DGS. Moreover, while the concept of constraining 3DGS with geometry
093   is not new (Yu et al., 2024; Xiang et al., 2024), existing methods typically rely on a discrete opti-
094   mization strategy where the implicit SDF field (Wang et al., 2021) and Gaussian points are learned
095   separately. In contrast, GeoSplatting explicitly guides Gaussian points with the isosurface and can be
096   optimized in an end-to-end fashion, reducing training time and improving inverse rendering quality.
097   In summary, our key contributions are as follows:
098

      2     R ELATED W ORK
124
      NeRF-based Inverse Rendering Since Neural Radiance Field (NeRF) (Mildenhall et al., 2020)
125
      represented scenes using implicit neural networks and leveraged differentiable volume render-
126
      ing (Drebin et al., 1988) to achieve highly detailed novel view synthesis, a large amount of subse-
127   quent work has extended it to inverse rendering tasks, by enhancing both the representations and the
128   rendering equations to introduce more physical constraints, allowing the separation of physically-
129   based rendering attributes such as normals, materials, and lighting (Boss et al., 2021a;b; Yariv et al.,
130   2021; Wang et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2021a; 2022; Verbin et al., 2022a; Liang et al., 2023; Jin et al.,
131   2023; Ge et al., 2023). While NeRF-based methods encode BRDF in a single radiance field, the
132   use of implicit representations and volume rendering makes them difficult to integrate into existing
133   graphics pipelines, as well as requires dense sampling that leads to slow rendering speed.
134
135   Mesh-based Inverse Rendering To address the rendering efficiency issue, explicit representations
136   (e.g., mesh (Shen et al., 2021; 2023)) combined with differentiable rendering techniques (Chen
137   et al., 2019; 2021; Laine et al., 2020) have demonstrated their ability to extract explicit geometry,
138
      material, and lighting from multi-view images (Munkberg et al., 2022; Hasselgren et al., 2022).
      However, mesh optimization is typically more challenging than implicit fields, which often struggle
139
      with complex geometry (e.g., thin structures), especially under high-frequency lighting-material
140
      interactions (e.g., specular objects).
141
142
      3DGS-based Inverse Rendering Recently, 3D Gaussian Splatting (3DGS) (Kerbl et al., 2023a)
143
      has emerged as a powerful representation for novel view synthesis with significantly superior ren-
144
      dering efficiency to NeRF and its variants. While it can be promising to utilize 3DGS to build more
145   powerful inverse rendering framework, its geometry is often misaligned with the ground truth sur-
146   face and prone to floaters, leading to inability in capturing accurate lighting-material interactions.
147   To improve this, a branch of research extends the vanilla 3DGS with various geometry enhancement
148   techniques by either introducing additional regularizations to ensure Gaussian points closely adhere
149   to the surface and keep them squeezed like disks (Guédon & Lepetit, 2024; Huang et al., 2024; Dai
150   et al., 2024), or jointly training 3DGS with implicit SDF fields (Lyu et al., 2024; Yu et al., 2024;
151   Xiang et al., 2024). Inspired by these geometry enhancement techniques, there are many meth-
152   ods extend the vanilla 3DGS for inverse rendering tasks. For instance, R3DG (Gao et al., 2023)
153
      learns additional normal attributes and regularizes normal directions using rendered depth maps.
      GS-Shader (Jiang et al., 2023) utilizes the shortest axis direction, while GIR (Shi et al., 2023) em-
154
      ploys eigen decomposition to determine surface orientations. While most of these methods produce
155
      reasonable normals by conducting alpha-blending on the per-Gaussian normal attributes, such a
156   blending process encounter much difficulty in obtaining precise normal, which prevent the methods
157   from capturing high-frequency lighting-material interactions, leading to failure cases when model-
158   ing reflective surfaces. In contrast, we combine explicit mesh representation and 3DGS in a unified
159   framework and train the framework in an end-to-end, similar to concurrent work Lin & Li (2024),
160   but employ a novel mesh-to-Gaussian adaptor to achieve geometric alignment, enabling effective
161   material and lighting decomposition, resulting in significantly better quality of both normal estima-
      tion and reflective surface modeling, therefore bringing excellent inverse rendering performance.

171   Background In the vanilla 3DGS (Kerbl et al., 2023b) paper, a Gaussian ellipsoid is represented

172   by a full 3D covariance matrix Σ and its center position µ: G(x) = e− 2 (x−µ) Σ (x−µ) , where
173   x is the location of a 3D point. To ensure a valid positive semi-definite covariance matrix, Σ is
174   decomposed into the scaling matrix S and the rotation matrix R that characterizes the geometry
175   of a 3D Gaussian. Beyond µ, S and R, each Gaussian maintains additional learnable parameters
176   including opacity o ∈ (0, 1) and Spherical Harmonic (SH) coefficients in Rk representing view-

179
      gradients, as well as the culling of Gaussians that are nearly transparent. However, each Gaussian
      point is independent of others and lacks global geometry constraints, which often leads inaccurate

      surfaces and floaters.

      3.3 I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
300   Modeling PBR Attributes GeoSplatting constrains the shape and opacity of each Gaussian point
301   based on the corresponding surface triangle, and learns the PBR material attributes by querying
302   MLP (Müller et al., 2022). Specifically, for the geometry, we optimize a 963 grid using Flexi-
303
      Cubes (Shen et al., 2023). For each Gaussian point, we query its corresponding surface movement
      v, diffuse color kd , roughness r, specular ks and residual color cr from a spatial MLP (Müller et al.,
304
      2022) F : (v, kd , r, m, cr ) = F (µ). Additionally, we learn a 6 × 512 × 512 × 3 environment map to
305
      model the lighting. Details of the network architecture are provided in Appendix B.5.
306
307   Loss Functions GeoSplatting is a fully differentiable pipeline that can be trained end-to-end. Thanks
308   to the geometry guidance, we do not require any surface or normal regularization terms, such as dist
309
      loss (Barron et al., 2022) or pseudo depth normal loss (Jiang et al., 2023; Gao et al., 2023), and
      the network can be supervised by photometric loss. However, similar to NVdiffrec, GeoSplatting
310
      also relies on an object mask loss, as optimizing the surface is more challenging. Specifically, the
311
      photometric loss is computed as: Lphoto = L1 + λssim LSSIM + λmask Lmask , where Lmask =
312   L2 (Mpred , Mgt ). Here we set λssim = 0.2 and λmask = 5.0. Furthermore, we add an entropy
313   loss to constrain the shape, following DMTet and FlexiCubes (Shen et al., 2021; 2023). To achieve
314   better decomposition, we apply light regularization and smoothness regularization on kd and ks ,
315   following NVdiffrec and R3DG (Munkberg et al., 2022; Gao et al., 2023). The final loss L is a
316   combination of the photometric loss and the regularization losses: L = Lphoto +λentropy Lentropy +
317   λsmooth Lsmooth + λlight Llight . Details are provided in Appendix. C.
318   Second Stage Optimization GeoSplatting optimizes the underlying SDF while producing Gaussian
319   points for image rendering. However, due to grid resolution limitations, we find that mesh-produced
320   Gaussian points still struggle to capture detailed textures and thin geometric structures. To address
321   this, we introduce a second-stage optimization where the Gaussian points are freely optimized with-
322   out being constrained by the mesh. In the first stage, the mesh-generated Gaussian points already
323   provide a refined shape and materials. Building on this, the second-stage optimization further en-
      hances geometry and texture details, achieving better decomposition performance.

      Limitation: While GeoSplatting demonstrates state-of-the-art performance in NVS and relighting
524
      tasks, it still faces several challenges that motivate further research. First, its geometry guidance
525
      is derived from the iso-surface. Although this significantly improves the geometry performance of
526   3DGS, it also requires masks during training and is constrained by grid resolution, which limits its
527   application to object-level inverse rendering tasks. A promising direction for future work would be
528   to explore how to eliminate the need for masks and to apply adaptive resolution to accommodate
529   detailed geometry, enabling its extension to scene-level tasks. Furthermore, GeoSplatting currently
530   models only single-bounce specular lighting, leaving the higher-order effects (e.g., inter-reflections)
531   to residual terms. Shadows will be baked into albedo as well, resulting in an inaccurate appearance
532   under relighting conditions. However, since we have access to intermediate meshes, incorporating
533   ray tracing techniques could enable a more comprehensive decomposition of shadows and inter-
534
      reflections. These areas hold great potential, and we aim to explore them in future work.
535   Conclusion: We propose GeoSplatting, a novel hybrid representation that enhances 3DGS with
536   explicit geometric guidance and differentiable PBR equations. GeoSplatting achieves superior effi-
537   ciency and state-of-the-art inverse rendering performance in reflective scenes, which demonstrates
538   its impressive ability in modeling high-frequency lighting-material interaction. Additionally, for
539   general cases, GeoSplatting delivers decomposition results that are comparable to those of state-of-

      B.1   OVERVIEW
719   We first describe the details of our MGadapter. As discussed in Sec. 3.1, the MGadapter takes a
720   triangle mesh as input and generates a set of Gaussian points corresponding to the mesh’s shape.
721   The core idea of MGadapter is to ensure that the shape of the triangle mesh aligns with that of
722   the Gaussian points, serving as a differentiable adapter between the two. However, since Gaussian
723   points lack the discrete geometric boundaries present in meshes, we define geometric alignment as
724   follows: given a triangle face, we can sample several viewpoints and render the triangle from them.
725   The geometric alignment is then measured by the difference between the rendered mesh and the
726   rendered Gaussian points (e.g. L1 loss on depth maps).
727   The intuitive implementation of our MGadapter involves sampling several Gaussian points on the
728   mesh surface. These points are then optimized in terms of scale and rotation to minimize the depth
729   map difference between the Gaussian points and the target mesh. However, this approach introduces
730   an additional optimization step that must be re-executed each time the mesh is modified, resulting
731   in reduced optimization efficiency and an unstable training process.
732   Instead of performing geometric alignment in real-time, we propose utilizing a predefined heuristic
733   function T to achieve an approximate alignment. As described in Eq. 1, the MGadapter T takes
734   arbitrary triangle meshes as input and outputs Gaussian point attributes, including position µ, scale
735   S, rotation R, and normal n. This process acts as a generalized adapter between the input meshes
736   and the corresponding Gaussian points. In Appendix B.2, we provide a detailed explanation of Eq. 1.
737   Then, we discuss the implementation of the surface adjustment in Appendix B.4, which is critical
738   for Gaussian point rendering. Finally, we explain how to query Gaussian point attributes from a
739
      spatial MLP in Appendix B.5 and explain the warm-up stage in Appendix B.3.

964
