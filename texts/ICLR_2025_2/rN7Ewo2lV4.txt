009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               In this paper, we introduce the first diffusion model designed to generate complete
012
               synthetic human genotypes, which, by standard protocols, one can straightfor-
013            wardly expand into full-length, DNA-level genomes. The synthetic genotypes
014            mimic real human genotypes without just reproducing known genotypes, in terms
015            of approved metrics. When training biomedically relevant classifiers with syn-
016            thetic genotypes, accuracy is near-identical to the accuracy achieved when training
017            classifiers with real data. We further demonstrate that augmenting small amounts
018            of real with synthetically generated genotypes drastically improves performance
019            rates. This addresses a significant challenge in translational human genetics: real
020            human genotypes, although emerging in large volumes from genome wide asso-
021
               ciation studies, are sensitive private data, which limits their public availability.
               Therefore, the integration of additional, insensitive data when striving for rapid
022
               sharing of biomedical knowledge of public interest appears imperative.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      Deep learning has enabled significant advancements in the field of computational biology (Jumper
029   et al., 2021; Cheng et al., 2023; Wong et al., 2024). However, the vast majority of such approaches
030   resort to processing smaller portions of human genomes, such as coding regions and their products
031   (proteins), or small local segments of human genomes (Guo et al., 2023; Shen et al., 2022).
032
      The reasons for this are threefold. First, the enormous length of human genomes prevents their
033
      straightforward usage in neural network architectures. Second, whole human genome data are ex-
034
      pensive, because of the massive clinical and experimental efforts required in order to obtain them.
035   Third, whole human genome data are usually subject to strict access regulations because of privacy
036   concerns. The difficulty to process, gather and share sufficient (training) data impedes scientific
037   progress through reliable extraction of knowledge, rapid dissemination of relevant data, and render
038   easy and full reproducibility of already obtained results impossible.
039
      While the first reason is a (challenging) technical concern, the second and the third reason establish
040
      our key motivation from the point of view of applications in biomedicine.
041
042   The solution that we suggest here is the generation of synthetically generated whole genome data
043   that is cheap, easy to gather, and privacy-enhancing. We present a diffusion model based framework
044
      that can generate synthetic whole-genome human genotype data.
045   Despite the relatively small amounts of data used during training, we ensure that our diffusion mod-
046   els do, in fact, generate novel whole genome human genotypes. By means of approved reliable
047   metrics, we ensure that the synthetically generated genotypes are of high quality, that is realistic in
048   terms of stemming from the distribution governing real human genotypes, and also diverse, that is
049   they do not exactly reproduce the individual genomes used for training the diffusion models, which
050
      translates into preservation of privacy in the setting at hand.
051   Note that, unlike previous work (Guo et al., 2023), whole-genome genotypes can be straightfor-
052   wardly expanded into whole DNA-level genomes, by means of applicable genome reference sys-
053   tems. Furthermore, diffusion models enable us to generate disease-affected and non-disease-affected
      genotypes in a targeted manner.

      Beyond demonstrating that the synthetically generated genomes are realistic by the measures that
055   ensure that the diffusion model approximately captures the distribution of human genotypes, we
056   further demonstrate that training classifiers (Luo et al., 2023) using synthetically generated data, by
057   either integration or replacement, and evaluating them on the original data achieves performance
058   rates that rival those of the original classifiers. This provides further evidence that the diffusion
059   model has not only captured the general structure of human genomes, but also has picked up the
060   mechanisms that distinguish diseased from non-diseased genotypes.

      2.2    G ENERATIVE M ODELS
097
098
099
      We prefer diffusion models over alternative generative deep learning models for the following rea-
      sons: a) Diffusion models have become the tool of choice in generative modeling (Dhariwal &
100
      Nichol, 2021), and b) previous successes in the use of diffusion models in regulatory human ge-
101
      nomics (Sarkar et al., 2024; Avdeyev et al., 2023; Senan et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024) further support
102   their usage. Unlike these works, we emphasize that in our work we make use of the full length
103   of genotypes, and do not have to restrict ourselves to smaller portions of the human genome. We
104   considered skipping steps for generation as done in DDIM (Song et al., 2022) as a way to speed up
105   generation, but realized that the high quality generated by using the full step length for our gener-
106   ation was important for our use case. Furthermore, we considered classifier free guidance (Ho &
107   Salimans, 2022) to increase the quality of the conditioning during generation, like in Azizi et al.
      (2023), but observed no positive effects for classification accuracy on the test data.

      2.3   W ORKING WITH L ONG S EQUENCES
109
110   One of the driving problems when working with genetic data is the enormous length of the genomes.
111   This is exacerbated by the long range interactions that affect parts of the genomes that are far apart
112   in terms of the sequential order in which they appear.
113
      It is therefore no surprise that recent genomics research is employing techniques that can accommo-
114   date the large length of human genomes e.g. HyenaDNA (Nguyen et al., 2023), which is a powerful
115   architecture that is able to process up to a million tokens simultaneously via an adapted form of
116   attention, and also, as of most recently, state space models (Schiff et al., 2024), for which similar
117   principles apply. However, even with those recent methodological advances, sequences of billions,
118   and not just millions in length, cannot be processed.
119
      In the domain of diffusion models, works have been presented, for example “Stable Diffusion”
120   (Rombach et al., 2022), that analogously adopt the paradigm of no longer working directly on the
121   raw data, but rather on appropriately embedded versions of it. We adopt this paradigm.
122

      3.1   DATA
132
      We deal with two data sets of human genotypes:
133
134
      ALS Data. The individual genotype profiles that we use for training (and testing) in the following,
135   were raised in the frame of Project MinE pro (2018), which is concerned with the study of amy-
136   otrophic lateral sclerosis (ALS). As a disease, ALS is of particular interest to AI based applications,
137   because ALS is driven by complex, still insufficiently understood mutation patterns that escape the
138   grasp of human-understandable approaches. For exactly these reasons, also earlier studies (Auer
139   et al., 2012; Dolzhenko et al., 2017) focus on ALS, using data gathered through Project MinE.
140
      While Project MinE establishes a data resource that is exemplary in terms of size and compre-
141   hensiveness, access to its data is subject to strict safety regulations. This is the reason why we
142   exclusively deal with a Dutch cohort of people, for which we were provided access, while not with
143   cohorts of genotypes referring to other countries. The Dutch cohort we worked with consisted of
144   3292 individuals affected by ALS and 7213 individuals known not to be affected by ALS, by ances-
145   tral relationships.
146
      Accordingly, for this data, labels y used to steer the generation of new samples, refer to individuals
147   affected with ALS and without ALS.
148
149
      1000 Genomes (1KG). We also consider the 2504 individuals sequenced in Stage 3 of the 1000
150   Genomes project Consortium et al. (2015). For these 2504 individuals, alleles were assigned to
151   ancestors (referred to as ”phased” in genetics), such that one obtains two haplotypes instead of one
152   genotype for each individual, where a haplotype is a binary-valued vector of length N where 0
153   reflects that the reference allele applied at the particular position whereas 1 reflects to observe the
154   alternative allele at the respective SNP site. Adding up the two haplotypes, entry by entry, results in
155   the genotype of the individual. Because, unlike genotypes, the haplotypes assign alleles to ancestors,
156   they carry more information. This is considerably more valuable for genetics, because they provide
157   immediate insight into the ancestral relationships affecting genomes.
158   When dealing with 1KG data, we seek to generate haplotype profiles instead of merely genotype
159   profiles. Unlike with Project MinE, no particulars about the corresponding phenotypes are known in
160   the frame of the 1KG project. The only known phenotype is the population from which they stem.
161   So, the additional conditioning input y refers to these 26 population labels when working with 1KG
      data.

201   3.3   M ODEL A RCHITECTURE
202
203   We base our Diffusion Model on the popular U-Net Architecture suggested by Ronneberger et al.
204   (2015), while applying modifications that account for the sequential nature of the genetic data.
205   We note that the order of the genes along the genome implies a natural order of the different 8-
206
      dimensional gene vectors.
207   We explore different variants of the basic U-Net architecture by replacing the 2D convolutional
208   layers with their 1D counterparts, or with multi-layer perceptrons (MLPs).
209
      We also explore a transformer encoder structure similar to the one presented in Devlin et al. (2019),
210   but with learnable positional embeddings, and two additional tokens accounting for time steps t
211   and class labels y, further equipped with an embedding layer similar to the one used for images in
212   (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020). This serves the purpose of reducing the number of input tokens which is
213   essential for reducing the computational cost. In Figure 2, we visualize the UnetMLP architecture
214   that we propose, it is similar to the convolutional Unet, but does not include multi-headed attention
215   in the intermediate layers. For visualizations of the Transformer and UnetCNN architectures see the
      Appendix.

258   3.4   E VALUATION
259
260   Evaluation of synthetically generated genomes requires careful consideration. The driving underly-
261
      ing principles are realism, on the one hand, and diversity on the other hand. While realism refers to
      synthetic genomes being likely to stem from the true distribution of genomes, diversity is concerned
262
      with sampled synthetic genomes being sufficiently far away from the true training data points. In
263
      image generation common scores to measure these metrics are the Fréchet inception distance (FID)
264   (Heusel et al., 2017) or, the Inception score (Salimans et al., 2016), for example. While human
265   eyesight does not apply in genomics for obvious reasons, the FID and IS cannot be computed either
266   because of the integration of pre-trained large scale networks. In fact, this scenario does not apply in
267   genomics/genetics for exactly the reasons that motivate our work: the lack of available (accessible)
268   large-scale data hampers conventional ML practice. Due to these reasons we are forced to rely on
269   metrics which while proven are a bit more unconventional for the image generation domain namely:
      Adversarial Accuracy and Classifier Performance.

270
      We note that all datasets we have access to are, although fairly large from the point of view of
271   biomedicine, considerably limited in terms of ML concerns (number of samples at most 10 405). So,
272   one cannot expect the diffusion model to perform at the level of realism observed in other complex
273   domains (such as images and text). This explains why the evaluation relates to exploring the upper
274   limits of possibilities in our context. Note however that our approach virtually serves the purpose
275   of training diffusion models on large-scale data, as hosted by large, access restricted databases, in
276   a safe, access-restricted enviroments. These databases could then provide safe, privacy-preserving
277   large-scale synthetic data on demand, by drawing samples from the diffusion model, without having
278   to publish neither real data nor the diffusion model trained on real data.

281   3.4.1   R ECOVERY R ATE
282
283   Employing synthetic data for training reflects using samples from a distribution that was estimated
284   using empirical data. Since all knowledge captured by that distribution stems from the real, empirical
285   data from which it was estimated, any samples drawn from that distribution can, at most, convey
286   the knowledge that contributed to its estimation. In terms of classifier performance, this means
287
      that performance rates achieved when using real data for training establish an upper bound for the
      performance rates that one can achieve when using synthetic data which was generated by observing
288
      the same real data for training.
289
290   Somewhat more formally, consider a classifier C and a generator G, both of which are trained on
291   the same real data Dr . While G explicitly addresses to approximate the distribution that governs
292   Dr , C implicitly approximates it in order to establish sufficiently accurate classification boundaries.
293
      Sampling synthetic data corresponds to drawing data Ds from the distribution approximated by G.
      So, using Ds , the synthetic data, instead of Dr , the real data, for training C cannot lead to gains in
294
      performance, because of the additional layer of approximation that G introduced.
295
296   Any improvements that one observes when using Ds instead of Dr are not due to systematic prin-
297   ciples, but can only reflect artifacts (such as overfitting of C when using Dr ) or random effects
298   (implying that C may not be able to approximate the distribution when using Dr as well as when
299
      using Ds ). If the generator G is pre-trained on another data set this upper limit does no longer exist.
300   In summary, it makes sense to evaluate the quality of generated data in terms of how much of
301   the accuracy of the classifiers achieved on real data one can recover when replacing the real data
302   with synthetic data. We perform this evaluation by establishing recovery rate R(ar , as ) as per the
303   following definition:
304

348   3.4.4   C LASSIFIER T RAINING
349
350   The most important question that we would like to answer is to what degree replacing true training
351   data with synthetically generated training data leads to losses in prediction. For obtaining answers,
352
      we consider two scenarios.
353   First, we focus on predicting the prevalence of the genetic disease Amyotrophic Lateral Sclerosis
354   (ALS), for which the first whole-genome based classifier was presented only recently (Luo et al.,
355   2023). There, training data was selected from 10 405 individual genotypes, referring to 3192 cases,
356   that is individuals affected by ALS, and 7213 controls, that is individuals not affected by ALS, as
357   determined by medical professionals. Further, ALS is known to be a complex and hard to disen-
358
      tangle genetic disease, which means that reliable classification does not depend on a small set of
      genes. This was documented in (Luo et al., 2023), by showing that good performance could only be
359
      established when employing at least on the order of 103 genes. Unlike in (Luo et al., 2023), we train
360
      a common multilayer perceptron (MLP) as a binary (ALS or not) classifier. Note that performance
361   rates achieved here exceed the performance rates displayed in (Luo et al., 2023).
362
363   Second, we consider the 2504 genotypes provided through the 1000 Genomes (1KG) project (Con-
364
      sortium et al., 2015) (stage 3), and the one of 26 population labels assigned to the genotypes, which
      gives rise to a classification task referring to one of 26 different labels. Again, we establish our
365
      primary classifier as an MLP, which parallels the situation for the ALS data.
366
367   To demonstrate that favorable usage of synthetically generated data does not depend on a particular
368   type of classifier, we further experiment with a transformer based (”Transformer”) and a convolu-
369   tional neural network (”CNN”) based classifier.
370   We evaluate each of the classifiers, trained with true data on the one hand, and synthetic data, as
371   generated by the Diffusion Model, on the other hand, on held out test data (ALS: balanced, 520
372   positive/520 negative genotypes; 1KG: 10% of total data, unbalanced, haplotypes). We follow the
373   experimental protocol presented in (Luo et al., 2023) for the ALS data.

      5   C ONCLUSION
507
508
      In this work, we have presented, to the best of our knowledge, the first diffusion model based ap-
509
      proach by which to generate full-length human genotypes. In this, by standard expansion of geno-
510   types using human genome reference systems, we have also presented an approach by which to
511   generate full-length human genomes at the level of DNA.
512
513
      In our experiments we have demonstrated, that the synthetically generated genotypes are realistic
      and that they do not just reproduce the real human genotypes used as input for training.
514
515   To demonstrate the practical usefulness of synthetic genotypes, we have employed synthetically
516   generated genotypes as training data for disease- or population-related classifiers. We have shown
517   that such practice re-establishes original performance rates to a degree that justifies their usage in
518   translational genetics research.
519   Improvements of the diffusion models are readily conceivable by increasing the amount of training
520   data. Note that although limited, one can expect amounts of training data available for training
521   generative models to be larger in real world settings. The reason is that generators can be trained
522   in safe environments, for example as part of the databases that host large numbers of genotype
523   cohorts, which implies that none of the real data has to be shared. Including differential privacy
524   mechanisms may further open up opportunities for usage of generative models in (e.g. federated
525
      learning) settings, where sharing parameters of the generative models may be beneficial.
526   We publish all code at [anonymized for review].

536   E THICS S TATEMENT
537
538   Working with human genetic data involves significant privacy and ethical challenges. Our approach
539   aims to mitigate privacy issues by generating synthetic data using diffusion models. However, there
      remains a potential risk that synthetic samples could inadvertently reveal information about original


                                                        10
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


540
      data. While we conducted experiments to ensure that our model does not reproduce exact copies of
541   real samples, we cannot fully guarantee that original data cannot be inferred from the synthetic out-
542   puts. Future work could incorporate differential privacy mechanisms to provide theoretical privacy
543   guarantees, although this may compromise the model’s performance.
544
      We evaluate our diffusion model on real-world classification tasks from previous studies, including
545
      the identification of ALS patients, a task that holds promise for future therapeutic advancements. We
546
      also use the 1KG Genome ethnicity prediction task, which, while not directly useful to real-world
547   scenarios, serves as a benchmark for evaluating the model’s performance on genome-level data.
548
549   In general we envisioned the diffusion model being trained in a secure environment and the synthet-
550
      ically generated privatised data being released for further research. To obtain provable privacy, we
      suggest mechanisms which lead to provable guarantees, such as differential privacy or multiparty
551
      computation. As the transfer of SNP data in between different locations might harm privacy, such
552
      methods could be combined with federated learning technologies.
553
554   A further challenge is a possible bias which can occur due to a skewed training set. Evaluation
555   whether biases exist can be based on downstream tasks. Bias mitigation could be based on resam-
556
      pling strategies for the training data.
557   Finally, the same as all AI models, we expect that the method is vulnerable to attacks such as data
558   poisoning or adversarial attacks of downstream tasks. Thus, the credibility of data sources and
559   robustness of downstream models needs to be assured. We think, however, that the implementation
560   of these variants is out of the scope of the current paper.

955   D ISCUSSION ON H UMAN G ENOTYPES .
956
957
      The human genome is a sequence of approximately 3 billion letters {A, C, G, T }, reflecting the nu-
      cleotides that form the basis of DNA. The vast majority of the 3 billion letters in human genomes
958
      are identical across all individual genomes; only approximately 3-5 million positions, referred to
959
      as polymorphic sites, vary. Although all types of variations can be observed at such polymorphic
960   sites, the vast majority of such sites exhibit single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs), defined by
961   exchanges of single letters. In the following, we will only deal with SNP sites, and neglect poly-
962   morphic sites characterized by other types of mutations. Restricting oneself to studying SNPs is
963   well justified, because the vast majority of other sites are in linkage disequilibrium (LD) with SNP
964   sites. That is, in other words, they are statistically strongly correlated with SNP patterns nearby,
965   such that one can safely infer non-SNP site contents based on knowing the SNPs of an individual.
966
967   Further, the great majority of SNP sites are bial-
968   lelic, which means that only two letters happen
969   to appear in human genomes; for example, a
970   particular site may be defined by an A show-
971   ing in some genomes, and a T showing in the
      other genomes. The letter that appears in the


                                                                                  18
                                                                                   Figure 10: The (unphased) genotype counts the
                                                                                   number of alternative alleles (blue) in the two an-
                                                                                   cestral genome copies each individual inherits.
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


972
       majority of people is referred to as reference
973    allele, whereas the letter showing in the other,
974    minor fraction of people is referred to as alter-
975    native allele. Every human genome consists
976    of two copies, of which one is inherited from
977    the mother, and the other from the father. Of
978    course, the contents of the two copies can vary
979    at the SNP sites: the reference allele can show in both copies, referred to as homozygous for the
980    reference allele, the reference allele can show in one copy while the alternative allele shows in the
981
       other copy, referred to as heterozygous (for example, while the mother genome copy exhibits the
       reference allele G, the father copy exhibits the alternative allele C, or vice versa) or the alternative
982
       allele can show in both copies, referred to as homozygoous for the alternative allele.
983
984    Let N be the number of SNP sites in human genomes. The genotype profile G is a vector of length
985    N over the entries {0, 1, 2}, where 0, 1, 2 refer to alternative allele counts at the SNP sites. That is,
986    0 reflects a polymorphic site that is homozygous for the alternative allele, 1 refers to a heterozygous
987
       site, and so on. For example, let i ∈ {1, ..., N } refer to the i-th SNP site, and let G be the genotype
       of an individual, then G[i] = 1 reflects that the individual that gives rise to G inherited the reference
988
       allele from one of the ancestors, while having inherited the alternative allele from the other ancestor.
989
990    Note that expanding the genotype G for an individual into a full-length genome over the alphabet
991    {A, C, G, T } corresponds to a straightforward operation: for any non-SNP polymorphic site, insert
992    the by LD principles statistically most likely variant, and for any non-polymorphic site, insert the
993
       only applicable reference letter. While not necessarily matching the real genome that gave rise to
       G in all places, the genome resulting from this expansion operation is highly likely to reflect true
994
       genetic sequence in the great majority of places.
995
996    In our work, the input to our diffusion model are individual genotype profiles G ∈ {0, 1, 2}N ,
997    where N is the number of SNP sites. Individual genotype profiles are the by far predominant way
998    of representing human genomes in the frame of genome wide association studies (GWAS). In the
999
       meantime, large databases have been filled up with individual genotypes of this kind. Of course, ac-
       cess to such genotype profiles is subject to stringent access regulations, because it is straightforward
1000
       to match genotype profiles with individuals in a unique manner—in fact, already the content of 50
1001
       (sufficiently distant) SNP sites suffices to uniquely identify single individuals.
1002
1003   Note that untreated genotype profiles are still too large to serve as input to diffusion models. Prefer-
1004   ably, one can trim down the length of the input from several (3-5) millions to only several tens of
1005
       thousands, that is by a factor of 100. Below, we describe how to embed genotypes into real-valued
       vector spaces of dimension in the tens of thousands.

       Although originally presented in Sohl-Dickstein et al. (2015), diffusion models have only recently
       gained popularity. As generative models, they reflect frameworks that learn probability distributions
1018
       from data that are too complex to draw samples from in other ways. The training procedure is
1019
       driven by iteratively adding Gaussian noise to known examples and, simultaneously, learn the way
1020   back by means of a neural network (NN). After training, sampling reflects to sample pure noise,
1021   and predict the way back to the real data distribution by means of the trained NN. Formally, one
1022   iteration adds Gaussian noise ϵ = N (µ, σ) with variance σ and mean µ = 0 to real data x ∈ D
1023   sampled from Distribution D, thereby generating a sequence of ever noisier xt , t ∈ (0, T ], referred
1024   to as the forward process. Eventually, xT can no longer be distinguished from pure Gaussian noise;
1025   in parallel, the NN seeks to learn how to return from xt to xt−1 for t ∈ (0, T ], which is referred to
       as the reverse process.
