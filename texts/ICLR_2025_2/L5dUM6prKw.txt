011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            As neural language models achieve human-comparable performance on Machine
014            Reading Comprehension (MRC) and see widespread adoption, ensuring their ro-
015            bustness in real-world scenarios has become increasingly important. Current ro-
016
               bustness evaluation research, though, primarily develops synthetic perturbation
               methods, leaving unclear how well they reflect real life scenarios. Considering
017
               this, we present a framework to automatically examine MRC models on natu-
018
               rally occurring textual perturbations, by replacing paragraph in MRC benchmarks
019            with their counterparts based on available Wikipedia edit history. Such pertur-
020            bation type is natural as its design does not stem from an arteficial generative
021            process, inherently distinct from the previously investigated synthetic approaches.
022            In a large-scale study encompassing SQ UAD datasets and various model archi-
023            tectures we observe that natural perturbations result in performance degradation
024            in pre-trained encoder language models. More worryingly, these state-of-the-art
025            Flan-T5 and Large Language Models (LLMs) inherit these errors. Further exper-
026            iments demonstrate that our findings generalise to natural perturbations found in
027
               other more challenging MRC benchmarks. In an effort to mitigate these errors,
               we show that it is possible to improve the robustness to natural perturbations by
028
               training on naturally or synthetically perturbed examples, though a noticeable gap
029
               still remains compared to performance on unperturbed data.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      Transformer-based pre-trained language models demonstrate remarkable efficacy in addressing
035
      questions based on a given passage of text, a task commonly referred to as Machine Reading Com-
036   prehension (MRC) (Devlin et al., 2019; Brown et al., 2020; He et al., 2021; Wei et al., 2022; Touvron
037   et al., 2023; OpenAI et al., 2024). Despite these advancements, high-performing MRC systems are
038   also known to succeed by relying on shortcuts in benchmark datasets rather than truly demonstrating
039   understanding of the passage, thereby lacking robustness to various types of test-time perturbations
040   (Ho et al., 2023; Schlegel et al., 2023; Levy et al., 2023).
041
      Evaluating models’ resilience to textual perturbations during inference aids in identifying adversarial
042   instances that highlight their shortcut behavior and provides insights into mitigating these shortcuts
043   (Ho et al., 2023). While numerous synthetic perturbation approaches have been explored and reveal
044   the vulnerabilities of MRC models to various linguistic challenges (Ribeiro et al., 2018; Jiang &
045   Bansal, 2019; Welbl et al., 2020; Tan et al., 2020; Tan & Joty, 2021; Schlegel et al., 2021; Cao et al.,
046   2022; Tran et al., 2023), a serious concern is that these carefully designed perturbations might not
047   necessarily appear in real-world settings. Consequently, this poses a risk of neglecting the weak-
048   nesses of reading comprehension systems to real challenges when deployed in practical scenarios,
049   thus potentially hindering the improvement of their reliability in practical applications.
050   To counteract this issue, in this paper, we develop a framework to inject textual changes that arise
051   in real-world conditions into MRC datasets and audit how well contemporary language models per-
052   form under such perturbations. We deem them as natural because the perturbation process does
053   not involve any artificial manipulation, in line with the definitions by Belinkov & Bisk (2018);
      Hendrycks et al. (2021); Pedraza et al. (2022); Agarwal et al. (2022); Le et al. (2022) (Figure 1).

072   Results of robustness evaluation are therefore more representative of real-world applications. Sim-
073   ilar to Belinkov & Bisk (2018), our approach utilises Wikipedia revision histories as the source of
074
      natural perturbations, given that the differences between revisions authentically capture the textual
      modifications made by human editors in the real world1 . Despite this, significant differences ex-
075
      ist in the perturbation construction methodology between us. Perturbation in (Belinkov & Bisk,
076
      2018) is restricted to single word replacements and applied on non-English source-side sentences in
077   machine translation. In detail, they build a look-up table of possible lexical replacements by harvest-
078   ing naturally occurring errors (typos, misspellings, etc.) from available corpora of French/German
079   Wikipedia edits (Max & Wisniewski, 2010; Zesch, 2012). Afterwards, they replace every word in
080   the source-side sentences with an error if one exists in the look-up table. Different from (Belinkov
081   & Bisk, 2018), our approach does not restrict the perturbation level and utilise English Wikipedia.
082   By comparing the variances between each adjacent revision, we identify perturbed versions for each
083   Wikipedia reading passage in the original MRC benchmarks (if it exists). This enables us to capture
084   more comprehensive and critical natural perturbation patterns (see Section 5.2) that can not be pos-
085
      sible to capture in (Belinkov & Bisk, 2018). Our perturbation method only alter the reading context,
      while the questions and ground truth answers remain unchanged.
086
087   With the established framework, we conduct extensive experiments on five datasets, evaluating
088   twenty-nine models, including nine recently proposed LLMs. Experimental results on Stanford
089   Question Answering Dataset (SQ UAD) (Rajpurkar et al., 2016; 2018) indicate that natural pertur-
090   bations encompass rich linguistic variations and can lead to failures in the encoder-only models,
091
      while humans are almost undeterred by their presence. Crucially, these errors also transfer to larger
      and more powerful models, such as Flan-T5 and state-of-the-art LLMs. These findings also gen-
092
      eralise to other and more challenging MRC benchmark (e.g., H OTPOT QA (Yang et al., 2018)) re-
093
      sulting in a decrease of SOTA LLMs’ performance, emphasising its harmful effects. Adversarial re-
094   training with either naturally or synthetically perturbed MRC instances can enhance the robustness
095   of encoder-only models against natural perturbations, with the latter sometimes providing greater
096   benefits. However, there is still ample room for improvement, calling for better defense strategies.
097
      The contributions of this paper are as follows:

112   2       R ELATED W ORK
113
114   Robustness Evaluation in MRC A typical approach to evaluate the robustness of MRC models is
115   via test-time perturbation. This line of research develops different perturbation methods as attacks,
116
      such as adversarial distracting sentence addition (Jia & Liang, 2017; Tran et al., 2023), low-level
      attacks (Eger & Benz, 2020), word substitution (Wu et al., 2021), character swap (Si et al., 2021),
117
      entity renaming (Yan et al., 2022) and paraphrasing (Gan & Ng, 2019; Lai et al., 2021; Wu et al.,
118
      2023). Our work also fits within the category of test-time perturbation, but differs from previous
119   works in that we introduce perturbations that naturally occur in real-world scenarios, therefore con-
120   tributing to a more practical robustness examination.
121
122   Natural Perturbation for Robustness Assessment Compared with deliberately crafting the per-
123   turbed instances, the study of natural perturbation is under-explored. In the computer vision do-
124   main, researchers find that real-world clean images without intentional modifications can confuse
125   deep learning models as well, terming them as natural adversarial examples (Hendrycks et al., 2021;
126   Pedraza et al., 2022). Similarly, in the field of Natural Language Processing (NLP), naturally occur-
127   ring perturbations extracted from human-written texts can also degrade model performance in tasks
128   such as machine translation (Belinkov & Bisk, 2018) and toxic comments detection (Le et al., 2022).
129   Motivated by these, we attempt to harvest natural perturbations from available Wikipedia revision
130
      histories and utilise them to modify the original MRC instances. To the best of our knowledge, we
      are the first to investigate MRC model robustness under real natural perturbations. Furthermore,
131
      it should be noted that the concept of natural perturbed examples in this paper differs from what
132
      is defined in previous NLP literature, where the latter measures the extent to which synthetically
133   modified text preserves certain linguistic characteristics such as fluency, coherence, grammaticality
134   and clarity, i.e., its naturalness (Jin et al., 2020; Li et al., 2020; Schlegel et al., 2021; Qi et al., 2021;
135   Wang et al., 2022a; Dyrmishi et al., 2023). Some works also propose that a natural synthetically
136   perturbed sample should be imperceptible to human judges (Li et al., 2020; Garg & Ramakrish-
137   nan, 2020) or convey the impression of human authorship (Dyrmishi et al., 2023). However, this
138   proposition remains a subject of debate (Zhao et al., 2018; Wang et al., 2022b; Chen et al., 2022).

141
142
      We design a pipeline to automatically construct label-preserving stress MRC test sets with noises
143   that occur in real-world settings by leveraging Wikipedia revision histories (Figure 2). Our approach
144   comprises two modules: candidate passage pairs curation and perturbed test set construction.
145
146   Candidate passage pairs curation. For each English Wikipedia article within the development
147   set2 of MRC datasets, we systematically extract its entire revision histories and preprocess them,
148   including the removal of markups and the segmentation of content. Subsequently, we obtain the
149   content differences between each current revision and the previous adjacent one, identifying three
150   distinct editing patterns: addition, deletion, and modification. In the case of an edit falling within
151   the modification pattern, we retain the paragraph from the prior version as the original and the
152   corresponding one from the current version as the perturbed, provided both paragraphs exceed 500
153   characters3 .
154
155   Perturbed test set construction. To generate the naturally perturbed test set, we begin by acquir-
156
      ing all reading passages from the development set of each MRC dataset and identifying their entries
      in the collection of previously extracted candidate original passages, along with the corresponding
157
      perturbed counterparts. Subsequently, for the matched original passages with a single occurrence,

231   4.3     M ODEL E VALUATION M ETRICS
232
233   In line with existing literature, we choose the Exact Match (EM) and (instance-averaged) Token-F1
234   score to assess the performance of both encoder-only and encoder-decoder models (Rajpurkar et al.,
235   2016), as on SQ UAD-style test sets, they are optimised to output the shortest continuous span from
236   the context as the answer (or predict the question as unanswerable) during inference. However,
237
      the outputs of the decoder-only models do not consistently adhere to the instruction due to their
      conversational style, rendering EM and F1 unsuitable for evaluation. Consequently, we employ a
238
      more lenient metric, namely Inclusion Match (IM), which measures whether the response of the
239
      model contains any of the ground truth answers (Bhuiya et al., 2024). Furthermore, if the model’s
240   output includes phrases such as “I cannot answer this/the question” or “unanswerable”4 , we deem
241   that the model believes the question is not answerable. Model robustness is quantified by measuring
242   the relative variation in performance (as reflected in the F1 or IM) under perturbations.

245   5       MRC UNDER NATURAL P ERTURBATION
246
247   In this section, we present the main findings of our study. Our intention in starting with SQuAD and
248   encoder-only models is to establish a baseline evaluation of model behaviour under natural perturba-
249   tions. While SQuAD is less challenging, its simplicity enables a focused and controlled examination
250   of the perturbation effects (Section 5.1), error sources (Section 5.2) and adversarial instance validity
251   (Section 5.3), providing a foundation for generalising our findings to more complex datasets and
252
      model architectures. As we observe that encoder-only models suffer from natural perturbations on
      the SQuAD datasets, we further investigate the transferability of the errors generated by encoder-
253
      only models to other model architectures (Section 5.4)5 . We finally show that the behaviour we
254
      observe in the baseline evaluation (i.e., encoder-only models suffer from natural perturbations on
255   the SQuAD datasets) also carries over to more powerful LLMs and other more complex datasets
256   (Section 5.5).

343   becoming adversarial; otherwise, it might not. Copy Editing appears to alter the answer sentences
344   in the reading passage more frequently, making it the most impactful category that confuses models
345   (contributing to more than 40% of error cases), while other types have a lesser effect. Appendix G
346   presents one perturbed example for each of the C2W, C2C, and W2C categories, respectively, along
347   with the annotated natural perturbation type(s).

349   5.3    VALIDITY OF NATURE A DVERSARIAL E XAMPLES
350
351   To accurately assess a model’s robustness under perturbation, it is vital to examine the validity of ad-
352   versarial example, i.e. whether humans can still find the correct answer under the perturbation (Dyr-
353
      mishi et al., 2023). We first present two human annotators with the same collection of adversarial
      instances, which includes only perturbed contexts and their corresponding questions, and then ask
354
      them to answer the question based on the perturbed context. The annotators are required to select
355
      the shortest continuous span in the perturbed context that answers the question and are allowed to
356   leave the answer blank if they are confident that the question is not answerable. Full instructions
357   given to the annotators can be seen in Appendix F. Subsequently, for both annotators, we measure
358   the correctness (1 or 0) of their provided answers by comparing each of them with the corresponding
359   ground truth answers9 . The inter-annotator agreement is then measured by computing the Cohen’s
360   κ coefficient (Cohen, 1960). We then involve a third human annotator to annotate the adversarial
361   examples on which the first two annotators disagree and then take the majority label as ground truth.
362   We employ this approach to verify the validity of the 210 C2W examples in Section 5.2 and find
363   that 86% of these adversarial examples are valid (0.77 Cohen’s κ), indicating that a substantial
364   proportion of natural adversarial examples for encoder-only MRC model(s) are valid.
365

453   6        D EALING W ITH NATURAL P ERTURBATIONS
454
455   In this section, we provide an initial exploration of methods to defend against natural perturba-
456   tions, focusing on encoder-only models and SQuAD datasets. Expanding to other datasets and
457   architectures could be explored in future work. To enhance model robustness, we conduct adver-
458   sarial training by identifying six encoder-only model architectures that already exhibit the highest
459   robustness to natural perturbations in their respective categories (except albert-xxlarge-v2
460   on NAT V2 C HALLENGE), and presenting them with both original training data and the generated
461
      naturally perturbed training examples. We extract the entire Wikipedia revision histories for the
      392 articles in the original SQ UAD training set, and then obtain 5, 262 (with 22, 033 questions) and
462
      5, 311 (with 32, 993 questions) perturbed contexts to augment the original SQ UAD 1.1 and SQ UAD
463
      2.0 training set, respectively, using the methodology described in Section 3. Table 4 compares the
464   performance of these models on NAT V1 C HALLENGE and NAT V2 C HALLENGE, before and af-
465   ter retraining.
466
467
      Apart from re-training with the same type of noise, we also ask whether exposing models to synthetic
      perturbations can help them confront natural ones. Therefore, we incorporate thirteen synthetic
468
      perturbation techniques spanning character and word levels (see Appendix J). Afterwards, we first
469
      retrain deberta-large with perturbed training samples generated by each synthetic perturbation
470   method, respectively, and assess the performance changes compared to the vanilla version on both
471   NAT V1 C HALLENGE and NAT V2 C HALLENGE (Figure 8 in Appendix K). As we observe that
472   synthetic adversarial training can assist deberta-large in handling natural perturbations, we
473   further retrain five other models in the same manner and quantify the performance difference on
474   NAT V1 C HALLENGE compared to the vanilla version, as shown in Figure 4.
475
      In general, for encoder-only MRC models, retraining with natural perturbations enhances the per-
476   formance on naturally perturbed test sets and improves the robustness to such perturbations as well,
477   though this can lead to varying reductions in performance on the clean test set. Encouragingly,
478   adversarial training with synthetically perturbed examples benefits the model’s capability to handle
479   natural perturbations as well, a phenomenon differs from what is reported in machine translation
480   task (Belinkov & Bisk, 2018). In some cases, the improvement even exceeds what achieved by
481   retraining the model on natural perturbations alone. We also observe that the effectiveness of ad-
482   versarial training varies with model size and architecture. Generally, adversarial training brings the
483   most significant benefits for the weakest distilbert-base, with the benefits diminishing in
484
      larger and more complex model architectures.

523   7     C ONCLUSION
524
525   In this paper, we first study the robustness of MRC models to natural perturbations, which occur
526   under real-world conditions without intentional human intervention. Using the proposed evaluation
527   framework, we show that certain naturally perturbed examples can indeed be adversarial, i.e., lead
528   to model failure, even when the modifications aim to improve the overall passage quality. Natural
529   perturbations also appear to differ significantly from synthetic ones, exhibiting a wide range of rich
530   linguistic phenomena and may be more effective in generating valid adversarial instances. Adver-
531   sarial training via augmentation with either naturally or synthetically perturbed samples is generally
532
      beneficial for enhancing the model’s robustness to natural perturbations; yet, it can decrease per-
      formance on clean test set. Future work includes the exploration of alternative natural perturbation
533
      approaches and the design of more effective defensive strategies against natural attacks.
