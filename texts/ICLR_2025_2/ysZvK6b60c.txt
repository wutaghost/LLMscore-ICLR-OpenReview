009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Model Inversion Attacks (MIAs) aim at recovering privacy-sensitive training data
012            from the knowledge encoded in the released machine learning models. Recent
013            advances in the field of MIA have significantly enhanced the attack performance
014            under multiple scenarios, posing serious privacy risks of Deep Neural Networks
015            (DNNs). However, the development of defense strategies against MIAs is rel-
016
               atively backward to resist the latest MIAs and existing defenses fail to achieve
               further trade-off between model utility and model robustness. In this paper, we
017
               provide an in-depth analysis from the perspective of intrinsic vulnerabilities of
018
               MIAs, comprehensively uncovering the weaknesses inherent in the basic pipeline,
019            which are partially investigated in the previous defenses. Building upon these
020            new insights, we propose a robust defense mechanism, integrating Confidence
021            Adaptation and Low-Rank compression (CALoR). Our method includes a novel
022            robustness-enhanced classification loss specially-designed for model inversion de-
023            fenses and reveals the extraordinary effectiveness of compressing the classifica-
024            tion header. With CALoR, we can mislead the optimization objective, reduce
025            the leaked information and impede the backpropagation of MIAs, thus mitigating
026            the risk of privacy leakage. Extensive experimental results demonstrate that our
027
               method achieves state-of-the-art (SOTA) defense performance against MIAs and
               exhibits superior generalization to existing defenses across various scenarios.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   In recent years, Deep Neural Networks (DNNs) have achieved remarkable advancements, leading
033   to significant success in a wide range of applications, such as face recognition (He et al., 2016),
034   audio recognition (Conneau et al., 2020), and brain decoding (Chen et al., 2024). However, the
035   powerful capabilities that make these models so effective also render them vulnerable to privacy
036   attacks. One of the most critical threats to privacy and security is the Model Inversion Attack (MIA)
037   (Fredrikson et al., 2015), which allows adversaries to reconstruct privacy-sensitive features from
038   the output information of released models. For example, in face recognition systems, MIAs can
039
      produce synthetic images that reveal specific visual characteristics of the private identities even
      without direct access to the private training dataset. This poses a significant risk, as MIAs may
040
      allow unauthorized individuals reconstruct valid facial features to disguise as authorized personnel,
041
      thereby compromising both privacy and security.
042
043   As recent MIAs have experienced astonishing evolution in attack capabilities, however, existing de-
044   fense strategies are backward to resist advanced attacks (Yuan et al., 2023; Qiu et al., 2024). Most
045
      defenses fail to enhance robustness while maintaining high utility for target models when defending
      the latest MIAs. Moreover, to the best of our knowledge, no comprehensive analysis has been con-
046
      ducted on the inherent weaknesses of MIAs in the previous defense researches. To address this gap,
047
      we dive into the nature of MIAs and present the first comprehensive and in-depth analysis of their
048   intrinsic vulnerabilities. We concentrate on the key aspects in the basic pipeline of MIAs, includ-
049   ing attack objective, MI overfitting, and optimization. By jointly interrupting these weaknesses, the
050   defender can comprehensively improve the model robustness when encountering multiple different
051   types of MIAs. Building on these new insights, we propose a new defense framework that exploits all
052   the inherent weaknesses, including a Confidence Adaptation and a Low-Rank compression strate-
053   gies (CALoR). Specifically, we design a novel confidence adaptation loss that slightly reduces the
      confidence of private samples, introducing bias between the attacker’s optimization goal and the

055   cause a fail attack, we propose the low-rank compression strategy to compresses high-dimensional

      2     BACKGROUND AND R ELATED W ORKS
075
      2.1   M ODEL I NVERSION ATTACKS
076
077   Let fθ : X → [0, 1]N denote a general classifier, which processes a private image x ∼ P(X) and
078   computes a prediction ŷc ∈ [0, 1] for each class c ∈ {0, 1, . . . , N }. MIAs aim to reconstruct images
079   that reveal private characteristic features of a specific identity c. In white-box scenarios, attackers
080   have full access to the classifier’s weights and outputs, allowing them to compute gradients across
081   the classifier when executing the attack.
082   In recent years, Generative Adversarial Network (GAN) based model inversion attacks, first pro-
083   posed by GMI (Zhang et al., 2020), have become the standard paradigm for MIAs (Fang et al.,
084   2024). Specifically, attackers initially train a generator G to capture a similar structural prior as
085   the target private data. In the attack stage, they attempt to reconstruct private images x∗ = G(z∗ )
086   labeled with class c to approximate the private distribution P(X), by optimizing the latent vectors z:
087                             z∗ = arg min Lcls (fθ (G(z)), c) + λLprior (z; G),                       (1)
088                                      z

089   where Lcls is the classification loss, λ is a hyperparameter, and Lprior denotes the optional prior
090   knowledge regularization terms, such as the discriminator loss (Zhang et al., 2020; Chen et al.,
091   2021) and the feature regularization loss (Nguyen et al., 2023).
092   Subsequent MIA studies have largely followed this pipeline and improved upon it. Researchers like
093   Chen et al. (2021); Yuan et al. (2023) train target-specific GANs to capture more private information
094   from the target classifier. An et al. (2022); Struppek et al. (2022); Qiu et al. (2024) utilize well-
095   structured StyleGANs (Karras et al., 2020) to generate high-quality and high-resolution samples.
096   Additionally, Struppek et al. (2022); Nguyen et al. (2023); Yuan et al. (2023) have explored various
097   classification losses to mitigate the effects of gradient vanishing issue.
098
099   2.2   M ODEL I NVERSION D EFENSES
100
      Model inversion defenses aim at reducing the threat of MIAs. Wang et al. (2021b) propose the
101
      first MI-specific defense method MID, defending attacks by reducing the mutual information be-
102   tween model input and output. Peng et al. (2022) upgrades from MID with a bilateral dependency
103   optimization (BiDO). They minimize the dependency between inputs and the intermediate repre-
104   sentations while maximizing that between the intermediate representations and outputs. Struppek
105   et al. (2024) explores the connection between label smoothing and model robustness. They indicate
106   that the label smoothing trick with negative factor can enhance the robustness. Ho et al. (2024) first
107   pre-trains the model on public datasets. Then, they freeze the parameters in previous layers when
      fine-tuning on private datasets. More details about the defenses are described in Appendix C.

      3     M ETHODOLOGY
136
      3.1   R E - THINKING THE I NHERENT W EAKNESSES OF M ODEL I NVERSION ATTACKS
137
138   In this section, we will illustrate the inherent weaknesses of MIAs, with an overview in Fig. 1.
139
140   Attack Objective. Given a target classifier fθ and a private image xpri,c labeled with class c, the
141
      forward classification process is defined as ppri,c = fθ (xpri,c ), where ppri,c represents the clas-
      sifier’s prediction probability vector for the private image. In contrast, attackers attempt to perform
142
      an inverse process, denoted as xinv,c = fθ−1 (pc ). Here, pc is the target one-hot probability vector
143
      representing the target class c, and xinv,c is the reconstructed image. The attackers’ goal is to mini-
144   mize the distinction between the private image xpri,c and the reconstructed image xinv,c . However,
145   the ppri,c is not exactly identical to the one-hot pc , whose confidence on other identities remains
146   non-zero. In this case, as the prediction of the reconstructed image approaches pc , the recovered
147   image may deviate further from the original private image. However, to the best of our knowledge,
148   no existing defense mechanisms have effectively exploited such vulnerability.
149
      MI Overfitting. The attacker’s optimization goal is to manipulate the prediction to resemble the
150   target one-hot probability vector. However, as illustrated in Fig. 1, despite massive inputs can meet
151   the optimization goal, only a subset of them can accurately capture privacy-sensitive characteristics,
152   which are defined as success samples. The other inputs fail to reveal private information due to
153   the MI overfitting problem (Nguyen et al., 2023). It means that the reconstructed samples become
154   overly tailored to random variations and noise in the target model’s parameters. Consequently, the
155   MI overfitting problem results in a failure to correctly uncover the private characteristics of the target
156   identity. While attackers can reduce MI overfitting by excluding noisy samples that are not conform
157   to the basic structure of private data with a generative prior, they cannot thoroughly avoid generating
158
      adversarial samples. These adversarial samples tend to overfit to the specific patterns in the target
      model, failing to expose the correct private information and thus leading to unsuccessful attacks.
159
      Nevertheless, the MI overfitting phenomenon may be beneficial for defenses once utilized properly.
160
161   Optimization. In white-box scenarios, the attacker employs gradient-based methods for optimiza-
      tion to extract the information encoded in the model parameters. However, performing optimization

238   3.3      L OW-R ANK C OMPRESSION
239
240   As discussed in Section 3.1, some samples fail to reveal critical privacy attributes owing to the MI
241   overffiting issue to the target models. To achieve effective attack outcomes, recent attacks attempt to
242
      extract more information from the target classifier’s outputs (e.g., by employing data augmentation
      and querying the target model multiple times (Struppek et al., 2022)), thereby increasing the likeli-
243
      hood that the attack results are success samples. To mitigate this, an effective defense mechanism is
244
      to limit the amount of input-related information encoded in the model’s output. Previous works like
245   MID (Wang et al., 2021b) and BiDO (Peng et al., 2022), have explored this approach, though they
246   often face significant performance trade-offs to achieve sufficient defense capabilities.
247
248
      Inspired by the feature compression employed in autoencoders , we initiate our study by examining
      the impact of compressed ranks on the preservation of input information. An autoencoder consists
249
      of two components: an encoder and a decoder. The encoder compresses the input image x into a
250
      latent vector z ∈ Rr , while the decoder attempts to reconstruct the image from z. We train a series
251   of autoencoders on the CelebA dataset (Liu et al., 2015) with varying latent dimensions (rank r) and
252   evaluate them on the test split. The details of the autoencoder experiments are shown in Appendix
253   I.2. We compute the reconstruction mean square error (MSE) loss and re-classification accuracy
254   of the reconstructed samples. The evaluation results are shown in Fig. 4b, with visual samples
255   displayed in Fig. 4a. The results show that lower compressed ranks lead to higher reconstruction
256   error and reduced image utility, indicating greater information loss from the input image. This
257   suggests that a compression into lower ranks reduces more input information. This information
258   reducing approach is crucial for designing models robust against MIAs (Peng et al., 2022).
259   Motivated by aforementioned findings, we propose a low-rank compression strategy to address this
260   challenge. It encourages the model to retain only the information essential for classification while
261   discarding irrelevant details. In practice, we decompose the classifier into two components: fθ =
262   C ◦ E, where E is the encoder, and C is the classification header. In standard classifiers, C is a linear
263   layer with a weight matrix Wm×N , where m denotes the dimensionality of C’s input features and N
264   is the number of classes. To reduce the information contained in the model outputs, We decompose
                                                                                           m×r            r×N
265   C into two linear layers, CA and CB . The weight matrices for these layers are WA          and WB       ,
                                                                                m×N          m×r      r×N
266   respectively. Here, r ≪ min{m, N }. This decomposition satisfies W              = WA WB . In
267   simpler terms, CA compresses the m-dimensional feature space into r dimensions, and CB then uses
268
      this compressed representation to make the final classification predictions.

      Moreover, Glorot et al. (2011) emphasize that certain non-linear activation functions, such as
271   Sigmoid and Tanh, are susceptible to the gradient vanishing problem. To further hinder the at-
272   tacker’s optimization process, we propose introducing a non-linear activation function after the
273   compressed features, specifically positioned between the two linear layers of the classification head.
274   The back-propagation characteristics of various activation functions suggest that the Tanh function
275   is particularly prone to causing the gradient vanishing problem, which will be analyzed in detail in
276   Appendix J. As a result, we choose Tanh as the activation function in our approach.

      5     C ONCLUSION
473
      Deep Neural Networks (DNNs) has tremendous potential across various domains. However, its
474
      applications must ensure robust privacy protections to prevent user privacy leakage, which can be
475
      compromised by model inversion attacks (MIAs). In this paper, we conduct a comprehensive anal-
476   ysis of the vulnerabilities inherent to MIAs by examining three key dimensions: attack objective,
477   MI overfitting, and optimization. To address the risks posed by MIAs, we introduce a novel defense
478   framework that combines Confidence Adaptation with a Low-Rank compression strategy (CALoR),
479   providing a robust defense mechanism. Our CALoR comprehensively defend MIA by introducing
480   bias between the attack objective and the goal of attack optimization, reducing information encoded
481   in the outputs of the model to strengthen the MI overfitting problem, and letting the attacker fall into
482   the gradient vanishing problem. Through extensive experimental validation, CALoR demonstrates
483   SOTA effectiveness across diverse scenarios. Notably, it excels at protecting models while maintain-
484
      ing high performance, surpassing prior defense techniques. We hope this work provides valuable
      insights for mitigating privacy leakage risks in deployed models and fosters a deeper understanding
485
      of defense mechanisms against model inversion attacks.

815   In this section, we briefly summarize previous defense methods and introduce how they defend
816   against MIAs.
817
818   C.1   MI OVERFITTING
819
820   MID (Wang et al., 2021b), BiDO (Peng et al., 2022) and TL (Ho et al., 2024) mainly defend attacks
821
      by reducing information encoded in the model output, making attackers harder to overcome MI
      overfitting issue via getting information from model outputs.
822
823
      Mutual Information Regularization based Defense (MID). Wang et al. (2021b) enhances model
824
      robustness by reducing the mutual information between the model’s input and output. The formula-
825
      tion of the loss is as follows:
826
                                         LM ID = LCE + λI(X, Ŷ ),                                 (9)
827
828   where λ is a hyperparameter and I(X, Ŷ ) denotes the mutual information between inputs and out-
829   puts. However, this mutual information loss term cannot be calculated directly. To overcome this,
830   they apply a variational approach (Alemi et al., 2016) to approximate the mutual information loss
831
      term. In practice, this involves Gaussian sampling at the output of the penultimate layer, similar to
      the approach used in variational autoencoders (VAEs) (Kingma, 2013). The loss functions are as
832
      follows:
833                                                  1
834                             LM ID = LCE + λ(− (1 + log σ 2 − µ2 − σ 2 )),                         (10)
                                                     2
835
      where λ is a hyperparameter, µ and σ are the output of the penultimate layer to execute the Gaussian
836   sampling.
837
838   Bilateral Dependency Optimization (BiDO). Peng et al. (2022) propose to reduce the depen-
839   dency d(·, ·) between the model inputs X and the intermediate feature Z and improve that between
840   Z and labels Y . The loss function is:
841
                                                   M                       M
842
                                                   X                       X
                            LBiDO = LCE + λiz            d(X, Zi ) − λoz         d(Zi , Y ),            (11)
843
                                                   i=1                     i=1
844
845   where λiz and λoz are hyperparameters. Zi , i ∈ [1, 2, . . . M ] represents different intermediate out-
846   put by the model. In our experiment, we set M = 3. The dependency measure d(·, ·) can be
847
      represented by Constrained Covariance (COCO) (Gretton et al., 2005b) or the Hilbert-Schmidt In-
      dependence Criterion (HSIC) (Gretton et al., 2005a). According to the original paper, the HSIC
848
      criterion demonstrates superior performance in defense. Therefore, we utilize the HSIC criterion in
849
      our experiment.
850
851
      Transfer Learning (TL). Ho et al. (2024) analyse the fisher information of each layers in classi-
852
      fication and inversion tasks. Through the analysis of fisher information between the parameters and
853   different tasks, they find that the previous layers in the model are more important for the model in-
854   version task and the last some layers makes more contributions to the classification task. Therefore,
855   to migrate the effect of MIAs, they pre-train the model on public datasets and freeze the parameters
856   in previous layers when fine-tuning with private datasets. The hyperparameter is the freezing ratio,
857   denoted as λ.

866   the inversion attack, while λ < 0 will have a strong defensive effect.

      LS with negative label smoothing factor can effectively impede the optimization process of the at-

1261   I.2   E XPERIMENTS OF AUTOENCODERS FOR L OW-R ANK C OMPRESSION
1262
1263   Autoencoder Architecture. We train multiple autoencoders with different ranks r, where r ∈
1264
       {2, 4, 8, 16, 32, 64, 128, 256, 512}. We decompose the autoencoder into an encoder E and a decoder
       D. E transform the input image into a r-dimension feature vector, and D recover the feature vector
1265
       into the origin images. The encoder use the backbone of ResNet-50 (He et al., 2016) pre-trained on
1266
       ImageNet (Deng et al., 2009), and the decoder is the same as the architecture of generator provided
1267   by GMI (Zhang et al., 2020).
1268
1269   Training. We train the autoencoders on the train split of the CelebA dataset (Liu et al., 2015). All
1270   training samples are augmented through random cropping within a scale range of [0.85, 1.0] and a
1271   fixed aspect ratio of 1.0. Crops are resized back to 224 × 224, followed by random color jittering
1272   with brightness and contrast factors of 0.2, and saturation and hue factors of 0.1. A horizontal flip is
1273   applied with a probability of 50%. The batch size is 32. The autoencoders are trained for 100 epochs,
1274   with Adam (Diederik, 2014) optimizers with initial learning rate of 0.0001 and β = (0.9, 0.999).
1275   The learning rate is reduced by a factor of 0.1 after epochs 50, 75 and 90.
1276
1277   Evaluation. We evaluate the autoencoders on the test split of the CelebA dataset. We compute
1278
       the average MSE loss between the origin images and reconstructed samples outputed by the autoen-
       coders. Moreover, we use a MaxViT (Tu et al., 2022) pre-train on CelebA dataset to re-classify the
1279
       reconstructed samples and calculate the accuracy. The test accuracy of the MaxViT is 96.44%.

1333   In this section, we consider the low-rank compression from another perspective. It enlarge the
1334   inversion space so that the proportion of success space in the inversion space will decrease.

1344   Consider a model fθ (x) = Wx, where x ∈ Rm and W ∈ Rn×m . The attacker’s goal is to invert
1345   the original input x from the model output y = fθ (x). According to the rank-nullity theorem, the
1346   dimension of the null space of W is given by:
1347
                                                    Null(W) = m − rank(W).                                   (13)
1348
1349   This implies that if a solution exists, the dimension of the free variables in the solution for x is
       d = m − rank(W). Therefore, as the rank(W) decreases, the dimension d increases, which


                                                                    25
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1350
       enlarges the inversion space. Consequently, it becomes more difficult for attackers to recover the
1351   original input. In our approach, we decompose the weight matrix W of the classifier into the prod-
                                                                     n×r    r×m
1352   uct of two low-rank matrices, specifically Wn×m = WB              WA       , where r ≪ m, n. Thus,
1353   rank(W) ≤ min{rank(WA ), rank(WB )} ≤ r. We analyse the classification header weights of
1354   various models trained on different datasets and calculate their weight matrices with the NumPy API
1355   function np.linalg.matrix_rank (Harris et al., 2020). Part of the result is shown in Table 19.
1356   They show that these weight matrices are typically full-rank. Therefore, in the absence of low-rank
1357   compression, rank(W) = min(m, n). When low-rank compression is applied, rank(W) = r.
1358   Since r is significantly smaller than min(m, n), our method substantially increases the dimensional-
1359
       ity of the inversion space. Therefore, the proportion of success space in the inversion space decrease,
       making the attackers more difficult to execute success attacks.

1362   M     I MPACT, L IMITATION AND F UTURE W ORK
1363
1364   Deep learning holds tremendous potential across various domains. However, its applications must be
1365   secure and protect user privacy, which can be threatened by MIAs. Our research find that when mod-
1366   els have strong performance, existing defense methods are insufficient to resist the latest attack meth-
1367   ods. Notably, our comprehensive research significantly increase the difficulty of MIAs. Therefore,
1368
       we recommend integrating comprehensive defense strategies when fine-tuning pre-trained models
       for downstream tasks involving sensitive or private data.
1369
1370   Currently, our research, like most MIA-related studies, focuses on classification tasks. In the future,
1371   it will be important to study other tasks such as object detection and image segmentation. Further-
1372   more, with the rise of multimodal large models, it is necessary to explore how these models handle
1373   privacy concerns. Investigating the security and privacy challenges in multimodal systems will be
1374
       essential as deep learning continues to expand into real-world applications.
