009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            The alignment of large language models (LLMs) with human preferences remains
012            a key challenge. While post-training techniques like Reinforcement Learning
013            from Human Feedback (RLHF) and Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) have
014            achieved notable success, they often experience computational inefficiencies and
015            training instability. In this paper, we propose Feature-level constrained Preference
016            Optimization (FPO), a novel method designed to simplify the alignment process
017            while ensuring stability. FPO leverages pre-trained Sparse Autoencoders (SAEs)
018            and introduces feature-level constraints, allowing for efficient, sparsity-enforced
019
               alignment. Our approach enjoys efficiency by using sparse features activated in
               a well-trained sparse autoencoder and the quality of sequential KL divergence
020
               by using the feature-level offline reference. Experimental results on benchmark
021
               datasets demonstrate that FPO achieves an above 5% absolute improvement in win
022            rate with much lower computational cost compared to state-of-the-art baselines,
023            making it a promising solution for efficient and controllable LLM alignments.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      Aligning large language models (LLMs) with human values and practical objectives is a critical
029
      challenge in AI development (Wang et al., 2023). Post-training methods, including fine-tuning (Wei
030
      et al., 2022; Chung et al., 2024) and alignment strategies (Tunstall et al., 2023), have played a
031   significant role in refining LLM behavior. Among these, Reinforcement Learning from Human
032   Feedback (RLHF) (Christiano et al., 2017; Ouyang et al., 2022) has emerged as a leading tech-
033   nique, integrating human feedback to guide models towards producing valuable and useful outputs.
034   Despite its success, RLHF involves complex mechanisms such as reward modeling and policy gra-
035   dients, which introduce significant training complexity and computational cost (Zheng et al., 2023b;
036   Rafailov et al., 2024). To address these limitations, Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) (Rafailov
037   et al., 2024) has been proposed as a more efficient alternative. Unlike reward-based methods such
038   as Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) (Schulman et al., 2017), DPO directly adjusts the model’s
039
      output probabilities based on human preferences, reducing training complexity and computational
      cost. DPO-like approaches can offer a more stable and faster alignment process by bypassing the
040
      challenges associated with reward models and policy updates, making it a compelling solution for
041
      efficient LLM alignment since DPO uses a reference model to stabilize post-training.
042
043   Recent advancements in DPO focus on mainly two directions: efficiency i.e., further simplifying the
044   constraints of DPO, and controllability i.e., keeping the balance between alignment and generation
045
      diversity. In terms of simplicity, methods like SimPO (Meng et al., 2024) and Odds Ratio Preference
      Optimization (ORPO) (Hong et al., 2024) eliminate the need for a reference model by using the
046
      average log probability of sequences as an implicit normalizer, thereby reducing memory usage and
047
      computational demands. However, DPO’s performance is sensitive to the strength of constraints
048   from the reference policy (Liu et al., 2024), and these reference-free alignment approaches (Hong
049   et al., 2024; Meng et al., 2024) can compromise control, resulting in unstable training. In terms of
050   controllability, Token-level Direct Preference Optimization (TDPO) (Zeng et al., 2024) introduces
051   token-level rewards and sequential Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence (Kullback & Leibler, 1951) to
052   tackle issues related to linguistic coherence, diversity, and stability. However, it comes at the cost
053   of increased computational complexity, introducing an additional sequential KL and depending on
      reference models, complicating the loss computation.

069   A natural hypothesis arises: “Is there a method that can strike the right balance between efficiency
070   and controllability?” In response, we propose FPO, Feature-level Constrained Direct Preference
071   Optimization (See Figure 1), introducing an efficient and controllable method for constraining the
072   model at the feature level. Here a feature refers to a salient piece of information for the model
073   decision (Huben et al., 2024). Intuitively, adjusting the model using feature-level preferences al-
074   lows fine-grained adjustment that minimizes the side impact, by avoiding the negative influence of
075
      spurious features in course-grained control such as token level regularization (Zeng et al., 2024).
076   To achieve that, we derive the FPO objective by contrasting SimPO and DPO, showing the constraint
077   term that SimPO misses. We then add such a term by introducing the feature-level constraints as
078   an alternative to the costly sequential KL (Zeng et al., 2024). We use Sparse Autoencoders (SAEs)
079   (Huben et al., 2024), which generate representations where only a few features are active, enhanc-
080   ing computational efficiency (See Figure 2 Right). Furthermore, regularization in the coefficient
081
      space promotes sparsity, stability, and uniqueness in the model’s representations. Since SAEs pro-
      duce sparse representations, only a few dozen out of 16,000 features are active at any given time
082
      (Lieberum et al., 2024). Compared to SimPO, FPO is as efficient in memory and time complexity,
083
      yet has improved controllability due to feature-level constraints; compared to constraint-based meth-
084   ods like TDPO, FPO matches the computational and memory efficiency of methods such as SimPO,
085   and has potentially improved performance as feature-level control can give stronger generalization
086   than token-level control. A contrast between FPO, DPO, SimPO and TDPO is shown in Figure 1.
087
      Our experiments demonstrate that FPO consistently outperforms state-of-the-art methods based on
088
      different sizes of backbone LLMs, achieving up to 5% absolute improvements in win rate (See Table
089
      2) based on AlpacaEval-2 and Arena-Hard benchmarks, up to 0.5 scores on MT-Bench and compet-
090   itive output diversity. By constraining the shifts of these features during the training process, we can
091   achieve results that meet or even exceed the effectiveness of sequential KL, at a significantly lower
092   computational cost (17.6% reductions compared to TDPO2 as shown in Figure 4 Left). Addition-
093   ally, we introduce detailed ablation studies to show that our method maintains a stable performance
094   over different temperatures and the selection of SAE layers.
095
      Overall, we show that FPO enjoys the efficiency of SimPO by using the offline reference control,
096   while also the constraint quality of sequential KL by using the sparse feature-level constraints. To
097   our knowledge, this is the first approach that integrates sparse feature-level constraints into LLM
098   alignment. By incorporating sparse autoencoders with token-level DPO, FPO makes practically
099   meaningful and theoretically solid improvements over existing preference optimization methods
100   along three dimensions: simplicity of implementation, efficiency, and generation diversity.

255   Feature-level Constraints. Currently, the use of constraints δ(x, yw , yl ) in alignment processes
256   typically follows KL divergence-based approach shown in Equation (5) and 6. However, this method
257   has a significant issue: for most LLMs, which generally have a very large vocabulary, where we
258   assume the vocabulary size is V . For each batch with an input length of T , the resulting output
259   probabilities have a size of V × T . This work adopts Gemma (Lieberum et al., 2024), an advanced
260   open-sourced LLM series, which has a massive vocabulary size of 265K. For an input length of
261   1024, this results in a probabilities matrix containing approximately 262M elements, which is nearly
262   1/10 the size of its 2B version model. Therefore, computing the KL divergence incurs considerable
263
      computational overhead to DPO-enhancing methods such as TDPO.
264   LLMs generate these sizable output probabilities by projecting their internal representations onto
265   vocabulary space. In contrast to this, SAE is found to be capable of projecting these representations
266   onto a sparse and more interpretable feature space. Motivated by the efficient nature of sparsity,
267   we leverage the sparse feature activations from SAE to approximate the function that token-level
268   probabilities serve in KL divergence. Specifically, for the output representation h(t,ℓ) from layer ℓ
269   of the model at position t, we can obtain its sparse activation c(t,ℓ) using an SAE as described in

      Building Offline Reference Margin and Constraint. We have justified the implementation of
284   the key components in Equation (9), which is a SimPO-like reward difference with a reference-
285   based adaptive margin and a feature-level constraint. At first glance, the reference model appears
286   to be deeply involved in both the calculation of the margin and the constraint, making its complete
287   elimination challenging. Therefore, instead of directly removing the reference model, we propose
288   a more appropriate approach: separating the computation of the reference model from the training
289   process by computing its output offline. Offline computation means pre-calculating and caching the
290   results related to the reference model needed for training and then reading them during the training
291   loop. This approach allows us to free up the reference model during alignment with only a small
292
      and acceptable I/O demand.
293   To explore an implementation for Equation (10) that enjoys the advantages of SimPO, such as length
294   normalization, while ensuring stability, first, we pre-compute and store the margin γref-LN using the
295   length normalization for each preference pair. Since it is scalar, it only occupies O(N ) space to store
296   it, where N is the number of preference pairs. Next, for the feature-level constraint, we pre-compute
297   and store the sparse activation of each sample in the training dataset following the computation
298
      in Equation (12). Consequently, we only need to pre-compute and store one sparse activation c̄ℓref
      for each sample, which requires O(2 · N · k) space. This results in a significantly smaller space

      Preference Optimization Methods in LLMs. Reinforcement learning (RL) has become a popular
      post-training technique, enabling models to learn implicit rewards from human feedback (Ouyang
506
      et al., 2022; Dubey et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2024a). The traditional RL pipeline involves training
507
      a reward model and updating the policy model via Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) (Schulman
508   et al., 2017). Recent work, such as DPO (Rafailov et al., 2024), leverages the log ratio between pol-
509   icy and target models to directly update policies based on the reward model’s objective. Extensions
510   of DPO have introduced further refinements. KTO (Ethayarajh et al., 2024) eliminates pairwise data
511   by modifying the value function using prospect theory, allowing training on individual sequences.
512   Token-level DPO (Zeng et al., 2024) enforces constraints at the token level to improve generative
513   diversity and also extends to the selection of specific tokens in pre-training (Lin et al., 2024) and
514   post-training (Yang et al., 2024b). To reduce computational costs, ORPO (Hong et al., 2024) and
515   SimPO (Meng et al., 2024) remove the reference model, streamlining training. Our approach sim-
516
      ilarly omits the reference model for computational efficiency but uniquely integrates feature-level
      constraints to achieve both high efficiency and quality in preference learning.
517
518   Interpretating LLMs in Feature Space. One approach to LLM alignment focuses on enhancing
519   transparency via mechanism interpretability (Shen et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2024). A central research
520   goal in this area is understanding how LLMs internally extract, represent, and compute human-
521   understandable features (Rai et al., 2024; Ferrando et al., 2024). Contrary to earlier assumptions,
522
      most neurons in LLMs do not activate exclusively for specific features but form polysemantic neu-
      rons (Mu & Andreas, 2020; Gurnee et al., 2023), a phenomenon termed ‘superposition’ (Elhage
523
      et al., 2022), which arises from compressing numerous learnable features into a limited number of
524
      dimensions (Hänni et al., 2024). Recent work shows that sparse autoencoders (SAE) can address
525   this by decomposing internal representations into sparse, monosemantic features, improving inter-
526   pretability (Huben et al., 2024; Templeton et al., 2024; Gao et al., 2024). Due to its scalability, SAE
527   has been used to analyze LLM monosemanticity. Yan et al. (2024) found that alignment increases
528   monosemanticity, while Marks et al. (2023) revealed that aligned LLMs learned feedback features
529   related to human preferences, enhancing their output alignment. However, SAE has not yet been
530   applied to construct feature-level constraints for improving LLM alignment.
531
532   7    C ONCLUSION
533
534   In conclusion, we proposed FPO, a novel method for efficient and stable alignment of large lan-
535   guage models using feature-level constraints. By leveraging sparse autoencoders and pre-computed
536   offline references, FPO reduced the computational overhead traditionally associated with alignment
537   methods like DPO and TDPO. Our experimental results demonstrate that FPO achieved significant
538   improvements in alignment accuracy and diversity while maintaining low resource consumption.
539   We prove that FPO achieved improvements over current state-of-the-art methods along all three
      dimensions: simplicity of implementation, efficiency, and generation quality.

      C.1   D EFINITIONS AND I NTUITIONS
865
866   Token-Level Embeddings: Token-level embeddings correspond directly to the token output prob-
867   abilities (logits) generated by a model. These embeddings are high-dimensional vectors representing
868   each token in the model’s vocabulary. For a sequence x = [x1 , x2 , . . . , xT ], the token-level embed-
869   dings at position t are computed as:
870
                                               ht = ftoken (xt ) ∈ RV ,
871
872   where V is the vocabulary size, and ftoken is the output projection from the model’s hidden state.
873
874   Feature-Level Representations: Feature-level representations, on the other hand, are high-level
875   abstractions derived from the model’s intermediate layers. These representations capture patterns
876
      and salient features across sequences. Using a Sparse Autoencoder (SAE), the hidden state hℓt at
      layer ℓ can be transformed into sparse activations cℓt , defined as:
877
878                                          cℓt = ReLU(Wenc hℓt + b),
879
      where Wenc ∈ Rm×d , b ∈ Rm , and m ≪ V . This sparse activation ensures only a subset of features
880
      is active, making the representation interpretable and efficient.
881
882
      C.2   C ONCRETE E XAMPLE : A M ATHEMATICAL Q UERY
883
884   Consider the input query:
885
886                                 ”What is the derivative of x2 + 3x + 5?”
887
888   Token-Level Embedding: The token-level output probabilities for each token in the response
889   sequence, such as ”The derivative is 2x + 3.”, involve logits for every token:
890                       logits = [log P (’The’), log P (’derivative’), log P (’is’), . . . ].
891
892   Feature-Level Representation: Using SAE on the 25th layer, the sparse feature representation
893   for the same sequence might activate specific features corresponding to mathematical operations or
894   semantic groupings:
895
                          cℓ = [activation1 (Polynomial), activation2 (Arithmetic), . . . ].
