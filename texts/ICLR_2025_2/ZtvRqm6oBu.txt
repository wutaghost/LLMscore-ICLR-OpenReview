009                                             A BSTRACT
010
011           We investigate whether sparse autoencoders (SAEs) can be used to remove knowl-
012           edge from language models. We use the biology subset of the Weapons of Mass
013           Destruction Proxy dataset and test on the gemma-2b-it and gemma-2-2b-it
014           language models. We demonstrate that individual interpretable biology-
015           related SAE features can be used to unlearn a subset of WMDP-Bio ques-
016           tions with minimal side-effects in domains other than biology. Our results
017           suggest that negative scaling of feature activations is necessary and that zero ab-
018
              lating features is ineffective. We find that intervening using multiple SAE features
              simultaneously can unlearn multiple different topics, but with similar or larger un-
019
              wanted side-effects than the existing Representation Misdirection for Unlearning
020
              technique. Current SAE quality or intervention techniques would need to improve
021           to make SAE-based unlearning comparable to the existing fine-tuning based tech-
022           niques.

025   1     I NTRODUCTION
026
027   Current and future language models may learn inaccurate information, produce toxic or malicious
028   outputs, or possess dangerous capabilities that we would like to remove before deployment, such as
029   advanced bio-weapons knowledge (Ji et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024). However, we do not yet know how
030   to precisely and robustly remove knowledge or unlearn capabilities in these language models. The
031
      goal of this work is to investigate whether sparse autoencoders (SAEs) can be used to perform
      unlearning in an interpretable way.
032
033   Having an interpretable method for unlearning is important as it can allow a higher level of
034   confidence that the model has actually unlearned the knowledge, rather than superficially or
035   temporarily hiding the capability to discuss a given topic. Existing methods for removing spe-
036   cific facts from language models offer interpretable solutions (e.g. Meng et al., 2023), however
037
      these approaches are limited to fact-level unlearning, not the unlearning of conceptual knowl-
      edge (Gandikota et al., 2024). The ultimate goal with an interpretable unlearning is to be able
038
      to properly verify that models have unlearned the correct knowledge. Even if that is not per-
039
      fectly achieved, an interpretable unlearning approach could still help us identify what subset
040   of the dataset has been unlearned and which areas have been overlooked, without requiring a
041   large amount of manual analysis of model behavior on big datasets. In addition, we feel that it
042   is worthwhile to test techniques developed in interpretability research on real-world tasks as a
043   useful measure of the impact of such techniques.
044
      Our current work makes two key contributions: First, we attempt to develop a method for unlearning
045
      knowledge in language models in an interpretable way. Second, we apply SAEs to the task of
046   unlearning knowledge, extending their use beyond previous work. Our approach aims to work
047   towards more transparent and verifiable knowledge removal at a broader scale.
048
049   1.1   R ELATED W ORK
050
051   Recent work on unlearning has typically focused on fine-tuning based methods that have been
052   applied in a variety of contexts to unlearn concepts in language models (e.g. Li et al., 2024; Zou
053   et al., 2024; Eldan & Russinovich, 2023), going beyond prior work that aimed to unlearn specific
      training data points in neural networks (Bourtoule et al., 2020).

      In particular, Li et al. (2024) introduced the Representation Misdirection for Unlearning tech-
079
      nique (RMU). RMU is a fine-tuning based approach for unlearning information from a language
080
      model. It involves fine-tuning model weights at 3 layers within the model using a combination of
081   two loss terms. These terms are (i) the “forget loss”, which changes the direction and scales the
082   norm of model activations on a dataset containing information that you want to unlearn and (ii) the
083   “retain loss”, which preserves model activations based on a Wikitext dataset. The method contains
084   two hyperparameters that control the ratio of the two loss terms, and the scaling of the activations
085   on the “forget” dataset. While relatively successful, fine-tuning approaches such as these are opaque
086   and we lack insight into what exactly is happening in the model (Łucki et al., 2024).
087   Sparse autoencoders (SAEs) use an unsupervised method to learn sparse reconstruction of lan-
088   guage model activations (e.g. Ng, 2011; Bricken et al., 2023; Cunningham et al., 2023; Templeton
089   et al., 2024; Marks et al., 2024; Gao et al., 2024). SAEs have been shown to find interpretable fea-
090   tures in language models. SAEs appear to be a promising approach to help understand complex,
091   abstract features that are used by language models (Templeton et al., 2024). Whether SAEs can be
092   used to make systematic, predictable, interpretable interventions in language models in a variety of
093   contexts remains an open question.

131   2     M ETHOD
132
133   Figure 1 presents an outline of how we use the SAE features to intervene in the language model.
134   At a high level, our method performs interventions dependent on token positions and SAE features.
135   For each feature being considered, at position in the context, we set the feature activation equal to a
136
      fixed negative value if the feature activates, otherwise we leave it unchanged (i.e. equal to 0). Our
      method avoids the extra loss when inserting the SAE by adding back the error term caused by
137
      the SAE in the forward pass, such that if no modifications are made to the feature activations,
138
      there is no impact on the model output. In Section 3 we provide a detailed explanation of our
139   methodology when applied to a single feature.

      2.1   M ODELS AND DATASET
142
143   We focus primarily on two language models, gemma-2b-it (Gemma Team, 2024a) and
144   gemma-2-2b-it (Gemma Team, 2024b). These two models are large enough to contain a signifi-
145   cant amount of knowledge related to the bio-weapons dataset that we are using, but small enough to
146   allow for rapid iteration. We trained SAEs at several intermediate layers (layers 3, 5, 7, 9, 11 and 13,
147   we discuss results for layer 9) of the residual stream in gemma-2b-it using similar techniques to
148   Templeton et al. (2024). We will open source the training code upon successful publication. We also
149   used open-source SAEs Lieberum et al. (2024) on gemma-2-2b-it. Further details are available
150
      in Sec. B.
151   We use the biology subset of the Weapons of Mass Destruction Dataset (WMDP-bio), which con-
152   sists of 1273 multiple choice questions related to hazardous knowledge in biosecurity (Li et al.,
153   2024). This subset was chosen as models performed weaker on the cyber and chemistry sub-
154   sets. WMDP-bio was developed as both an evaluation for hazardous knowledge in language mod-
155   els, and also as a benchmark for unlearning techniques. The questions relate to bioweapons and
156
      bioterrorism, genetics, viral vector research, dual-use virology and enhanced potential pandemic
      pathogens. gemma-2b-it achieves a base score of 560/1273 (44.0%) on the WMDP-bio dataset.
157
      With four multiple choice options, one can expect the model to get ∼ 25% of the multiple choice
158
      questions correct by random chance, without actually having the knowledge to obtain the correct an-
159   swer. However, as we aim to unlearn this information, we want to be as sure as we can that the model

182   2.2   U NLEARNING METRICS
183
184   The goal of an unlearning technique is to remove knowledge from the model, while limiting the
185
      damage to the model’s performance in all other domains. Therefore, there are two key factors
      that need to be quantified; the amount of knowledge removed, and the side-effects caused by the
186
      modification to the model.
187
188   The primary metric for unlearning that we consider is the number of correct answers out of the
189   subset of questions that the base model gets correct under all 24 permutations. We also looked at
190   the probabilities assigned to the correct answers and the impact of re-ordering the 4 multiple choice
191
      options. In addition, we performed some specific case studies of the model’s completion, with
      non-multiple choice based prompts, based on the same information as tested in the questions.
192
193   We quantified the side effect for the model in two different ways, (i) the accuracy on an unrelated
194   multiple choice dataset (Measuring Massive Multitask Language Understanding, MMLU) and (ii)
195   the loss added over 50k tokens of OpenWebText. The multiple choice dataset contained questions
196   related to high school US history, geography, college computer science and human aging. These
197
      questions allowed us to both check for the removal of specific unrelated knowledge, and also to en-
      sure that we were not simply damaging the multiple-choice answering ability of the model. Similar
198
      to the WMDP-bio dataset, we only select the questions that the base model gets correct answers
199
      for under 24 permutations, resulting in 97 questions for gemma-2b-it and 300 questions for
200   gemma-2-2b-it.
201
202   We also compare our unlearning results to an existing technique, the Representation Misdirection for
203
      Unlearning (RMU, Li et al. 2024). Applying the RMU technique to gemma-2b-it shows that it is
      very successful at lowering the number of WMDP-bio questions that are answered correctly, without
204
      impacting unrelated MMLU questions, and little loss added (more detailed results are presented in
205
      Fig. 5). Interestingly, the model modified using RMU answers option “A” on 62% of questions,
206   compared to 25% for the base model.

      2.3   I NTERVENTION M ETHODS
209
210   We explored various approaches to intervene on the model using SAE features. Our primary in-
211   tervention method involves clamping the feature activation to a specific negative value whenever it
212   activates. We also experimented with alternative methods, including (i) scaling the feature activation
213   by a constant negative multiplier, and (ii) clamping the activation to a multiple of the feature’s max-
214   imum activation in the dataset, similar to the steering approach used by Templeton et al. (2024). We
215   found that clamping feature activations to a fixed negative value produces similar unlearning results
      with fewer side effects compared to scaling, especially when intervening on multiple features.

230   2.4   H OW TO SELECT RELEVANT SAE F EATURES
231
232   Selecting the most effective set of sparse autoencoder features is difficult. One simple approach is
233   to create a “forget” and “retain” dataset, as in the RMU technique, and compute feature sparsities on
234   each dataset. The WMDP-bio forget dataset (Li et al., 2024) contains bio-weapon related content,
235
      and the retain dataset contains WikiText (Merity et al., 2016). In our procedure, we first calculate
      the feature sparsities on both datasets. We then discard features that have a sparsity on the retain
236
      dataset larger than a given threshold (e.g. 0.01). Finally, we sort the remaining features by their
237
      sparsity on the forget dataset. The top N features are selected as the bio-weapon related features. In
238   Fig. 8, we plot the sparsities of each feature in the retain and forget datasets for an SAE at layer 3
239   of the residual stream of gemma-2-2b-it.

      3.3   P ERMUTATIONS
315
      We can also examine whether this unlearning is effective for different permutations of the options
316
      in the multiple choice prompt. In this particular case, we find that it unlearns 19 of the 24 prompts.
317
      We find later that this number varies significantly and depends on the feature and prompt, ranging
318   from 2 to 24. A perfectly unlearned model should achieve a score of ≤ 6 out of 24 permutations.
319   This outcome might depend on whether the intervention simply destroys the knowledge that was
320   previously available, in which case we might expect it to start guessing at random and not provide
321   a consistent answer over all 24 permutations, or whether it exchanges the old information for new
322   information and thus has an incorrect answer but is consistent about which answer it chooses. These
323   two unlearning scenarios are quite different, and for now, it does not seem completely clear to us
      which is easier or more desirable to achieve.

330   the existing RMU technique. We evaluate three key metrics. The first is the “WMDP-bio Accuracy”
331   is the number of questions the modified model gets correct divided by the number of questions the
332   original model gets correct, only considering questions which the original model gets correct for all
333
      permutations (i.e. those for which we can be most sure that the model contains information related

334
      the cross-entropy loss in the modified model minus the cross-entropy loss in the original model,
335
      calculated over the same 50k tokens of OpenWebText. Finally, we also present the selected MMLU
336   Accuracy; the combined score across a subset of MMLU questions related to high school US his-
337   tory, geography, college computer science and human aging, considering only the questions which
338   the original model gets correct for all permutations.2 Higher MMLU accuracy suggests fewer side
339   effects.

353   4.2   I MPACT OF ENCODER VS DECODER IN UNLEARNING
354
      Investigating the mechanism of the RMU technique, Arditi & Chughtai (2024) found that by pro-
355   jecting out a single direction from all intermediate activations, it is possible to recover a signifi-
356   cant fraction of the original knowledge. They suggested that some of the unlearning with RMU is
357   achieved by first detecting harmful prompts and then modifying the model’s internal computation
358   by adding a large vector that overwrites the existing residual streams.
359
      In our SAE-based unlearning method, we inject a large vector (i.e. the decoder vector multiplied by
360
      a constant) when the selected features activate. It is possible that the SAE encoder acts as a classifier
361   for the undesired knowledge, the model’s internal representations are pushed out of distribution by
362   the injection of the decoder vector, and that this explains the decrease in accuracy on multiple-choice
363   questions.
364
      To test this possibility, and understand the impact of the SAE encoder and decoder in unlearning, we
365
      used the original SAE encoder to determine feature activations of the selected features. For each fea-
366
      ture, we selected a random feature and clamped this random feature to a large negative value when-
367   ever the original feature had a non-zero activation. Figure 6 shows the results on gemma-2-2b-it.
368   When using the random decoder vectors, the unlearning performance dropped substantially as com-
369   pared to the standard approach, with higher loss added. This suggests that the specific decoder
370   vectors contribute significantly to the unlearning process, rather than simply disrupting the model’s
371   internal representations. Interestingly, we repeat this experiment on gemma-2b-it and found con-
372   trasting results. Replacing the decoder vectors with some random decoder vectors did not diminish
373   the unlearning performance, suggesting that for this model and SAE, the specific directions of the
374   injected vectors may not be as critical (see Figure 10 in Appendix).

      5   C ONCLUSION
456
      In this study, we investigated whether features in SAEs can be used to unlearn knowledge from
457
      language models. Our main contributions are:
458
459        1. We demonstrated that individual SAE features can be used to unlearn knowledge using the
460           WMDP-bio dataset (Section 3).
461        2. Negative scaling of feature activations is necessary and zero ablating features is ineffective
462
              (Section 3.2).
           3. We showed that intervening on gemma-2-2b-it using 10 - 20 SAE features could un-
463
              learn significant proportions of the WMDP-bio dataset (Section 4)
464
           4. We found that SAE-based unlearning results in similar or larger unwanted side-effects than
465           the existing Representation Misdirection for Unlearning technique (Section 4).
466
467   Our SAE-based unlearning method only modifies the model’s activations, without modifying the
468
      model weights. Future work may be able to extend this approach to directly modify the weights.
      For now, it means that only the output of the model is changed and the original knowledge is still
469
      contained in the weights. However, this approach may still be useful to to prevent harmful outputs
470
      when models can be accessed only by API (Li et al., 2024; Greenblatt & Shlegeris, 2024).
471
472   Our research suggests that a large change needs to be made to either the SAE training process, or
473   our intervention method, in order for SAE-based unlearning to be successful. We propose three key
474
      directions for future research to address this shortcoming: (1) Examine how SAE quality and width
      impact unlearning effectiveness. Wider SAEs with specific features may improve performance. (2)
475
      Compare SAE-based unlearning to activation steering using bio-related and non-bio-related prompt
476
      pairs. (3) Investigate how SAE interventions affect related features in subsequent layers to better
477   understand the unlearning mechanism.

      A    M ULTIPLE C HOICE Q UESTION C ONSIDERATIONS

596
      We found that for some questions in the WMDP-bio dataset, it appears that a model could figure out
597   the correct answer without having as much relevant knowledge about biology as one might expect.
598   For example, in the WMDP-bio dataset, we find the longest answer is correct in 42% of questions.
599   Selecting the longest answer is a common technique for answering multiple choice questions in
600   exams.
601
      Some of the possible answers include phrases such as “All of the above” or “Both A and B”. These
602
      answers are correct most of the time in the WMDP-bio dataset. gemma-2b-it is likely to select
603   these as the correct answer, regardless of the other answers. It even selects “All of the above”
604   as the correct answer when we permute the order and it appears as the first answer. Finally, in
605   39 questions out of the 172 questions that gemma-2b-it gets correct under all 24 permutations
606   (23%), gemma-2b-it selects the correct answer under all 24 permutations without the question
607   being included in the prompt. This number rises to 72 out of 172 if the threshold for achieving the
608   correct answer is set to 20 out of 24 permutations.
609
610   B    SAE TRAINING DETAILS
611
612   We trained a sparse autoencoder (SAE) at layer 9 of the residual stream of gemma-2b-it. The
613   SAE was trained on 120M tokens from OpenWebText and has 16k features with an L0 of 20. Layer 9
614   was selected based on activation patching of a selection of the multiple choice questions. Training
615   an SAE of width 16k on a 2B parameter language model can be done on an A6000 in ∼12
616   hours. The compute scales with the SAE width and the size and layer number of the language
617   model (to compute the activations). We will open source the weights of this SAE upon successful
618   publication.
619   We also used Gemma Scope SAEs (Lieberum et al., 2024) trained on gemma-2-2b and apply
620   them to the instruct model gemma-2-2b, since the chat model is better in answering the WMDP-
621   bio questions and they only have SAEs available for the base model. We also tested the loss added
622   on the base model gemma-2-2b and the chat model gemma-2-2b-it when replacing the model
623   activation with the SAE reconstruction activation. The loss added are 0.8631 for the base model and
624   0.8860 for the chat model over 409K tokens in OpenWebText. This shows that the SAE can transfer
625   between the base model gemma-2-2b and the chat model gemma-2-2b-it.

777   F    A LTERNATIVE METHOD USING FEATURE ATTRIBUTION
778
779   As an alternative to our procedure for selecting features based on sparsities in the ”forget” and
780   ”retain” datasets, we tested an approach based on feature attribution. This approach consists of the
781   following steps:
782
            1. For a given question in the unlearning dataset, we calculate the correct logit minus the mean
783
               of the incorrect logits. Using backpropagation, we calculate the gradient of this quantity at
784
               the intervention layer, dot product with each SAE feature decoder direction, and multiply
785            by the feature activation. We do this for each position in the prompt, excluding certain
786            special tokens such as “A”, “:”, or newline tokens.
787         2. We take the top 20 features by feature attribution and check whether they impact the pre-
788            dicted answer by clamping the feature activation to a constant negative value. After some
789            experiments, we used a value of −20.
790         3. We repeat this for all questions that the base model gets correct under all permutations.
791         4. To further filter out multiple choice related features, we test the model’s performance on
792            a set of unrelated MMLU multiple-choice questions and remove features that result in too
793
               many wrong answers (usually set at about 0 - 3 out of 300).
            5. Finally, we find it useful to check the features for loss added as often 3 − 5% of features
794
               add a huge amount of loss for no marginal gain in unlearning capability).
795
796   We used this selection technique on both the entire dataset, and also split the dataset into a test/train
797   set, where we only select the features based on a subset of the WMPD-bio questions and only test
798   for side effects on a subset of the MMLU questions and OpenWebText data. The feature attribution
799
      technique has the advantage that it doesn’t require separate datasets to be available. On the other
      hand, with the feature sparsity method, we don’t have to be as concerned about over-fitting to the
800
      specific multiple-choice questions. On gemma-2b-it, we found that this attribution approach to
801
      be slightly better than the feature sparsity approach at selecting features that produce fewer unwanted
802   side-effects. However, the best approach to select features for unlearning remains unclear.
