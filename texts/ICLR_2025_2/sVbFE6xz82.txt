009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011
               Real-world noise poses a significant challenge in signal processing, especially for
012            denoising tasks. Although end-to-end denoising approaches have achieved excep-
013            tional performance, they are constrained to scenarios with abundant noisy-clean
014            image pairs, which can be technically challenging and resource-intensive to col-
015            lect. To address this issue, several generative methods have been developed to
016            synthesize realistic noisy images from limited real-world datasets. While prior
017            studies require camera metadata during training or testing to handle various real-
018            world noise, the absence of metadata or variations in the information across dif-
019            ferent capturing devices is common in real-world scenarios, such as medical or
020
               microscope imaging, which limits their applicability. Thus, we aim to eliminate
               the need for explicit camera-related labels in both stages, enhancing applicability
021
               in real-world scenarios. To achieve this, we propose a novel framework called the
022
               Metadata-Free Noise Model (MFN), which extracts prompt features that encode
023            input noise characteristics and generates diverse noisy images that adhere to the
024            distribution of the input noise. Extensive experimental results demonstrate the
025            superior performance of our model in real-world noise generation and denoising
026            across various benchmark datasets.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031
      Real-world denoising is particularly challenging in low-level vision tasks due to the inherent com-
032
      plexity and diversity of noise. Unlike additive white Gaussian noise (AWGN), which is introduced in
033   controlled environments, real-world noise originates from various sources, including camera sensor
034   limitations, environmental conditions, and camera settings. Thus, denoising in real-world scenarios
035   is more complex than in AWGN, where noise characteristics can be statistically modeled.
036
      One clear method to address real-world denoising is to acquire a large dataset of noisy-clean image
037
      pairs and train denoising networks in a supervised manner (Zamir et al., 2020b; Chen et al., 2021;
038
      Zamir et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2022). However, this method presents a significant challenge: col-
039   lecting a large-scale dataset is resource-intensive and technically complex (Abdelhamed et al., 2018;
040   2020; Xu et al., 2018; Nam et al., 2016; Plotz & Roth, 2017). To address this limitation, various
041   methodologies for modeling real-world noise and generating datasets have been proposed (Abdel-
042   hamed et al., 2019; Kousha et al., 2022; Zamir et al., 2020a; Kim et al., 2024).
043
      In this work, we aim to eliminate the reliance on metadata from noisy images when learning or
044
      generating them. Specifically, metadata, such as camera manufacturer and ISO settings, plays a
045   crucial role as it offers a compact representation of how the ISP transforms the original RAW-RGB
046   data, while distorting the corresponding noise. Several previous studies (Abdelhamed et al., 2019;
047   Kousha et al., 2022; Fu et al., 2023) utilize metadata to guide the modeling of specific noise types;
048   however, collecting or utilizing metadata in real-world scenarios can be challenging. In practical
049   applications, such as medical imaging, the physical meaning of the information in the metadata is
050   completely different or may even be unavailable. In these situations, conventional methods that
051   depend on standardized metadata face significant limitations. To address this issue, we propose a
052   novel framework, Metadata-Free Noise model (MFN). This model extracts and exploits the under-
053
      lying noise information in an unsupervised manner, without relying on metadata, while maintaining
      the quality of the generated samples.

074   2     R ELATED W ORK
075
      sRGB Noise Generation. The limited availability of real-world noise datasets results in overfitting
076
      issues for denoising networks, particularly in practical applications (Abdelhamed et al., 2019; Za-
077
      mir et al., 2020a; Jang et al., 2021; Wu et al., 2023). To tackle this problem, various methods for
078   generating sRGB noise have been proposed. PNGAN (Cai et al., 2021) treats each noisy pixel as
079   a random variable, disentangling the noise generation problem into components in the image and
080   noise domains. Flow-sRGB (Kousha et al., 2022) leverages normalizing flows to model noise distri-
081   butions based on factors like smartphone type and gain settings. NeCA (Fu et al., 2023) introduces
082   a neighboring correlation-aware noise model for synthesizing realistic noise, explicitly accounting
083   for both signal dependency and neighboring noise correlations. However, these methods depend
084   on explicit noisy image labels (i.e., metadata) for noise generation, limiting their applicability in
085   situations where such information is unavailable. To address this, NAFlow (Kim et al., 2024) in-
086
      troduces a noise-aware sampling algorithm to synthesize sRGB noise without requiring metadata
      during inference. However, it still requires metadata during the training phase. In contrast to these
087
      previous approaches, our approach offers a metadata-free noise generation method for both training
088
      and testing phases by leveraging prompts that capture input-noise-specific information.
089
090   Prompt Learning. Prompt-based methods are commonly employed in natural language processing
091   (NLP) (Brown et al., 2020; Shin et al., 2020; Schick & Schütze, 2021) and vision tasks (Zhou et al.,
092
      2022a; Yao et al., 2024) to offer meaningful context for fine-tuning models on specific tasks. How-
      ever, rather than relying on explicit manual guidance sets as prompts, recent methods (Zhou et al.,
093
      2022b; Potlapalli et al., 2023) have utilized learnable prompts to achieve more efficient parameter
094
      adaptation. While most prompting-based vision approaches focus on high-level tasks, recent low-
095   level vision models (Li et al., 2023; Potlapalli et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024) have been proposed
096   to adaptively restore degraded images by leveraging their interactions with prompts. In particular,
097   PromptIR (Potlapalli et al., 2023) and PromptRestorer (Wang et al., 2024) employ prompt learn-
098   ing to encode and implicitly classify degradation-specific information, enhancing the restoration
099   model’s performance. In this work, we develop a prompt learning method for realistic noisy image
100   generation by introducing a Prompt Autoencoder (PAE) that extracts prompt features capturing the
101   characteristics of the input noise.

187   3.2      OVERALL F LOW
188   We introduce a novel framework called Metadata-Free Noise (MFN), which can generate realistic
189   noisy images without relying on explicit labels or metadata (e.g., smartphone manufacturer, ISO
190   setting). Our MFN is inspired by latent diffusion models (Rombach et al., 2022a; Luo et al., 2023),
191   and its generation process operates within a compact latent space, further achieving speed improve-
192   ments through CM. This approach significantly reduces computational and memory costs compared
193   to methods that function in high-dimensional image space. We illustrate the overall flow of the MFN
194   in Fig. 1, which comprises two main components: the Prompt Autoencoder (PAE) and the Prompt
195
      DiT (P-DiT), trained sequentially in a two-stage manner.
196   Two-Stage Training Phase. First, we train U-Net (Ronneberger et al., 2015) like PAE, which
197   consists of Prompt Encoder E and Decoder D, to learn a compact latent space. The Prompt Encoder
198   E maps the input noise nReal , representing the residual between the paired noisy image I Noisy and
199   clean image I Clean , into a compact latent code z, while also producing prompt features that capture
200   the characteristics of the input noise. Given the clean image I Clean , the Decoder D reconstructs the
201
      noisy image from the encoded latent code.
202   Then, our P-DiT is trained to learn the distribution of the PAE’s latent codes using a CM-based
203   approach by optimizing LCT in Eq. 4. Given the fully trained Prompt Encoder E, our P-DiT learns
204   to synthesize latent codes that embed input-specific noise characteristics by leveraging an encoded
205   latent code z0 and prompt features from Prompt Encoder E, along with the clean image.
206   Inference Phase. During the generation phase, the Prompt Encoder E produces prompt features that
207   capture the noise characteristics given an input noise. Conditioning on these prompts and a clean
208   image, the P-DiT generates a latent code ẑ0 , which is then decoded by the Decoder D to produce
209                         I Noisy given the clean image I Clean .

      As illustrated in the lower part of Fig. 2 (a), the Decoder D consists of numerous convolutional
297   layers, integrating residual blocks and upsampling operations to transform latent codes back into
298   noisy images. The upsampling operation increases the spatial resolution of input features at scale

331   enhance these features through a prompt attention mechanism, referred to as Prompt Attention.
332
333   Prompt Attention. As illustrated in Fig. 3 (b), we use the conditions to generate the key, query,
334
      and value within the attention layer, similar to MMDiT (Esser et al., 2024), enabling the model to
      effectively capture the spatial information of the prompt features. Specifically, we first modulate
335
      the combined conditional features FCond through AdaLN to extract time-dependent information.
336
      Then, we generate the key, query, and value features using a single linear layer and combine them
337   with the corresponding features from the input tokens through element-wise addition. Using these
338   conditioned features, we apply cosine attention (Luo et al., 2018; Karras et al., 2024) with point-wise
339   normalization (Karras et al., 2018) to stabilize the training process (Karras et al., 2024).

      4.1   E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
346
347   Implementation Details. The PAE is trained with the Adam optimizer (Kingma & Ba, 2014) and
348   minimize the L1 loss between the noisy image I Noisy and the reconstructed noisy image Î  I Noisy ,
349   along with the L2 regularization applied to the latent code z for dense representation (Rombach
350   et al., 2022b). We start with an initial learning rate 1e-4, which is then reduced to 1e-6 using a
351   cosine annealing algorithm (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2017) over 400k iterations. We use randomly
352   cropped patches of size 256×256 and a mini-batch size of 64 for training.
353   The P-DiT model is optimized with the RAdam optimizer (Liu et al., 2020) using a fixed learning
354   rate of 2e-4 over 250k iterations, applying the pseudo-Huber loss for Eq. 4, similar to iCT (Song &
355   Dhariwal, 2024). We also use randomly cropped patches of size 256×256 to embed latent codes of
356   size 32×32, with a mini-batch size of 512 during training. For the ablations, the mini-batch size is
357   reduced to 128 to save training time. More details are in Sec. A.1.
358   For denoising, we adopt the DnCNN architecture (Zhang et al., 2017)), optimized with the Adam
359   optimizer (Kingma & Ba, 2014) over 100k iterations. The training follows the setup outlined in (Fu
360   et al., 2023; Kim et al., 2024), with a learning rate of 1e-3 and a mini-batch size of 8.
361
      Dataset. To train the PAE, P-DiT, and DnCNN, we employ the SIDD (Abdelhamed et al., 2018)
362
      dataset, which includes 34 different camera configurations. We adopt the SIDD Medium split, com-
363
      prising 320 noisy-clean image pairs captured with five unique smartphone cameras: Google Pixel
364   (GP), iPhone 7 (IP), Samsung Galaxy S6 Edge (S6), Motorola Nexus 6 (N6), and LG G4 (G4). To
365   evaluate the generated noise quality, we use the SIDD validation set, SIDD+ (Abdelhamed et al.,
366   2020), PolyU (Xu et al., 2018), and Nam (Nam et al., 2016) dataset, and the DND (Plotz & Roth,
367   2017) dataset is additionally employed to assess denoising performance. Note that the SIDD+,
368   PolyU, Nam, and DND datasets were captured using different types of camera sensors.
369
      Metrics. To examine the quality of the generated noise, we use two metrics: Kullback-Leibler
370   Divergence (KLD) and Average Kullback-Leibler Divergence (AKLD) (Yue et al., 2020). Addition-
371   ally, we employ PSNR and SSIM metrics to assess the performance of denoising.

      5      C ONCLUSION
531
      In this work, we propose a novel MFN framework to remove the reliance on metadata when gener-
532
      ating real-world noise. Our MFN comprises two key components: Prompt Autoencoder (PAE) and
533
      Prompt DiT (P-DiT). The PAE encodes input noise, generating a compact latent code and extracting
534   meaningful prompt features at various scales to replace input noise-specific metadata. The P-DiT
535   then synthesizes latent codes using prompts and clean images as conditions. Finally, the generated
536   latent codes are fed into the PAE Decoder alongside clean images to produce realistic noisy images.
537   A key advantage of our method is that it does not require explicit metadata during either the training
538   or testing phases unlike conventional methods, highlighting its strength in real-world applications.
539   Moreover, experimental results on real-world benchmarks demonstrate that our framework excels in
      both real-world noise generation and downstream denoising tasks.

      A.1.3    L ATENT C ODE N ORMALIZATION
769
770   The EDM formulation assumes that the mean and standard deviation of the training data are zero
771   and σdata , respectively, as stated in Eq. 10. Following the approach in (Karras et al., 2024), we also
772   normalize the encoded latent codes using precomputed statistics from the training data. Specifically,
773   we first calculate the channel-wise mean and standard deviation of the latent codes from the training
774   dataset. Then, we subtract the input latent code by the precomputed mean to achieve a mean of zero,
775   and divide it by the precomputed standard deviation, followed by multiplying by σdata , to set the
776
      standard deviation to σdata . When the latent codes are generated, we reverse this procedure before
      transforming them back to the image space via the Decoder.

      A.1.4    P-D I T H YPERPARAMTERS
779
780   Following the approach in (Song et al., 2023; Song & Dhariwal, 2024), we update P-DiT parameters
781   using an exponential moving average with a decay rate of 0.9999 to stabilize the training process.
782   P-DiT’s model hyperparameters are based on DiT-S (Peebles & Xie, 2023), except for the number
783   of blocks (B = 8), to improve efficiency. The input noised latent is tokenized with a patch size of
784   1, allowing for finer noise information to be embedded in the latent code. To mitigate overfitting,
785   we apply a dropout rate of 0.1 to the pointwise feed-forward layer and add minor noise and apply
786   downsampling operation with a factor of 2 to the conditional clean images (Song et al., 2023).

1020   Consequently, both reconstruction performance and generative capabilities are essential for generat-

1022   pipeline requires a careful balance between reconstruction and generative performance. This neces-
1023   sitates extensive experimentation to determine the optimal weight, a process that is both time- and

       thereby ensuring training stability.
