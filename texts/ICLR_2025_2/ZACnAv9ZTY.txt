011                                                  A BSTRACT
012
013             This paper presents a method to evaluate the alignment between the decision-
014             making logic of Large Language Models (LLMs) and human cognition in a case
015             study on legal LLMs. Unlike traditional evaluations on language generation re-
016
                sults, we propose to evaluate the correctness of the detailed decision-making logic
                of an LLM behind its seemingly correct outputs, which represents the core chal-
017
                lenge for an LLM to earn human trust. To this end, we quantify the interactions
018
                encoded by the LLM as primitive decision-making logic, because recent theo-
019             retical achievements (Li & Zhang, 2023; Ren et al., 2024) have proven several
020             mathematical guarantees of the faithfulness of the interaction-based explanation.
021             We design a set of metrics to evaluate the detailed decision-making logic of LLMs.
022             Experiments show that even when the language generation results appear correct,
023             a significant portion of the internal inference logic contains notable issues1 .

026   1    I NTRODUCTION
027
028   The trustworthiness and safety of Large Language Models (LLMs) present significant challenges for
029   their deployment in high-stake tasks (OpenAI, 2023; Wei et al., 2023). Previous evaluation methods
030
      mainly evaluated the correctness of language generation results, in terms of value alignment and
      hallucination problems (Bang et al., 2023; Ji et al., 2023b;a; Shen et al., 2023).
031
032   In this study, we hope to go beyond the long-tail evaluation of the generation results, and focus on the
033   correctness of the detailed decision-making logic used by the LLM behind the language generation
034   result. We focus on the legal LLM as a case study, and the legal LLM may use significantly in-
035   correct information to make judgment, even when the generation result is correct. The alignment of
036
      decision-making logic between the AI model and human cognition is crucial for alleviating the com-
      mon fear of AI models. The alignment of internal logic via communication is the reason why people
037
      naturally trust each other. Particularly, in high-stakes tasks such as autonomous driving (Grigorescu
038
      et al., 2020), the lack of alignment between AI models and human users makes people would rather
039   delegate work to humans and tolerate potential errors, than trust highly accurate AI models.
040
041   Therefore, this paper aims to explore the possibility of aligning the decision-making logic for the
042
      confidence score of the LLM’s judgment with human cognition. To this end, exploring the mathe-
      matical feasibility of faithfully explaining the output score of a neural network as a few interpretable
043
      logical patterns has become a new emerging theoretical problem in explainable AI, and about 20
044
      papers have been published in three years (see related work in Appendix A). Typically, Li & Zhang
045   (2023); Ren et al. (2024) have proved the universal-matching property and sparsity propoerty, and
046   mathematically guaranteed that a DNN usually only encodes a small number of interactions between
047   input variables, and these interactions act as primitive decision-making logic, which well predicts
048   the confidence of the network prediction on various input variations.
049
      As Figure 1 shows, an interaction measures the nonlinear relationship between input tokens of an
050
      input legal case encoded by the LLM. For instance, given an input sentence such as “Andy threatened
051   Bob and took his smartphone,” the LLM may trigger an interaction between a set of input tokens S =

086

091

092
093   The contributions of this paper can be summarized as follows. We propose to utilize interaction-
094   based explanations to evaluate the correctness of decision-making logic encoded by a LLM. We
095   design new metrics to quantify reliable and unreliable interaction effects w.r.t. their alignment with
096
      human cognition of the judgment. Experiments on both English legal LLM and Chinese legal LLM
      show that both LLMs used a significant number of incorrect interactions for inference, although
097
      these LLMs all exhibited high accuracy in judgment prediction.

197   2.2   R ELEVANT TOKENS , IRRELEVANT TOKENS , AND FORBIDDEN TOKENS
198
199   According to above achievements, we can take a small set of salient AND-OR interactions as the
200   faithful explanation for the decision-making logic used by the legal LLM. Thus, in this subsec-
201   tion, we annotate the relevant, irrelevant, and forbidden tokens in the input legal case, in order
202   to accurately identify the reliable and unreliable interactions encoded by the LLM (see Figure 1).
203
      Specifically, the set of all input variables N is partitioned into three mutually disjoint subsets, i.e.,
      the set of relevant tokens R, the set of irrelevant tokens I, and the set of forbidden tokens F, subject
204
      to R ∪ I ∪ F = N , with R ∩ I = ∅, R ∩ F = ∅, and I ∩ F = ∅, according to human cognition.
205
206   Relevant tokens refer to tokens that are closely related to or serve as the direct reason for the
207   judgment, according to human cognition. For instance, given an input legal case “on June 1,
208   during a conflict on the street, Andy stabbed Bob with a knife, causing Bob’s death,”1 the legal
209
      LLM provides judgment “murder” for Andy. In this case, the input variables can be set as N =
      {[on June 1], [during a conflict], [on the street], [Andy stabbed Bob with a knife], [causing Bob’s
210
      death]}. R = {[Andy stabbed Bob with a knife], [causing Bob’s death]} are the direct reason for the
211
      judgment, thereby being annotated as relevant tokens, where all tokens in the brackets [] are taken
212   as a single input variable.
213
214   Irrelevant tokens refer to tokens that are not strongly related to or are not the direct reason for the
215
      judgment, according to human cognition. For instance, in the above input legal case, the set of irrel-
      evant tokens are annotated as I = {[on June 1], [during a conflict], [on the street]}. For example,

      2.3   R ELIABLE AND UNRELIABLE INTERACTION EFFECTS
239
240   The categorization of relevant, irrelevant, and forbidden tokens enables us to disentangle the reliable
241   and unreliable decision-making logic used by a legal LLM. As introduced in Section 2.1, we use
242   interactions as the decision-making logic encoded by a legal LLM. Thus, in this subsection, we
243   decompose the overall interaction effects in Equation (2) into reliable and unreliable interaction
244   effects. Reliable interaction effects are interaction effects that align with human cognition, which
245   usually contain relevant tokens and exclude forbidden tokens. In contrast, unreliable interaction
246   effects are interaction effects that do not match human cognition, which are attributed to irrelevant
247   or forbidden tokens.
248   Visualization of AND-OR interactions. Before defining reliable and unreliable interaction ef-
249   fects, let us first visualize the AND-OR interactions extracted from two legal LLMs, SaulLM-7B-
250   Instruct (Colombo et al., 2024) and BAI-Law-13B (Institute, 2023). SaulLM-7B-Instruct was an
251   English legal LLM, trained on a corpus of over 30 billion English legal tokens. BAI-Law-13B was
252   a Chinese legal LLM, fine-tuned on Chinese legal corpora. We evaluated the legal LLMs on the
253   CAIL2018 dataset (Xiao et al., 2018)5 , just like how (Feng et al., 2022; Fei et al., 2023) did. Fig-
254
      ure 2 shows the sparsity of interactions extracted from the legal LLMs. Interaction strength |I(S|x)|
      of all AND-OR interactions extracted from all legal cases were shown in a descending order. We
255
      found that most of the interactions had negligible effect.
256

258   logic of a legal LLM. The legal LLM correctly attributes the judgment of “robbery” to interactions

323   interaction pattern S consistently appears in both the training and test sets, it demonstrates high generalization
      power. Please see Appendix E for details.

343   3    E XPERIMENT
344
345   In this section, we conducted experiments to evaluate the alignment quality between the decision-
346   making logic of the legal LLM and human cognition. In this way, we identified potential represen-
347   tation flaws behind the seemingly correct language generation results of legal LLMs.
348   We applied two off-the-shelf legal LLMs, SaulLM-7B-Instruct (Colombo et al., 2024) and BAI-Law-
349   13B (Institute, 2023), which were trained for legal judgment prediction on English legal corpora and
350   Chinese legal corpora, respectively. Appendix F shows the accuracy of these LLMs. Given an input
351   legal case, the LLM predicted the judgment result based on the fact descriptions of the legal case.
352   We explained judgments made on legal cases in the CAIL2018 dataset (Xiao et al., 2018), which
353   contained 2.6 million Chinese legal cases, for both legal LLMs5 . Figure 6 shows the universal-
354   matching property of the extracted interactions, i.e., when we randomly masked input variables in
355   the legal case, we could always use the interactions to accurately match the real confidence scores
356
      of the judgment estimated by the LLM.
357   To simplify the explanation and avoid ambiguity, we only explained the decision-making logic on le-
358   gal cases, which were correctly judged by the LLM. For each input legal case, we manually selected
359   some informative tokens or phrases as input variables. Some tokens or phrases were annotated as
360   relevant tokens in R, while others were identified as irrelevant tokens in I. It was ensured that the
361   removal of all input variables would substantially change the legal judgment result.
362   We extracted AND-OR interactions that determined the confidence score v(x) of generating judg-
363   ment results with a sequence of tokens, according to Equation (1). To accurately identify and analyze
364   potential representation flaws from these interactions, in this paper, we mainly focused on potential
365   representation flaws w.r.t. legal judgments in the following three types, i.e., (1) judgments influ-
366   enced by unreliable sentimental tokens, (2) judgments affected by incorrect entity matching, and (3)
367   judgments biased by discrimination in occupation.
368   Problem 1: making judgments based on unreliable sentimental tokens. We observed that
369   although legal LLMs achieved relatively high accuracy in predicting judgment results (see Ap-
370   pendix F), a considerable number of interactions contributing to the confidence score v(x) were
371   attributed to semantically irrelevant or unreliable sentimental tokens. The legal LLM was supposed
372   to focus more on real criminal actions, than unreliable sentimental tokens behind the actions, when
373   criminal actions had been given. We believed these indicated potential representation flaws behind

      Problem 3: discrimination in occupation may affect judgments. We found that the legal LLM
433   usually used interactions on the occupation information to compute the confidence score v(x). This
434   would lead to a significant occupation bias. More interestingly, we discovered that when we replaced
435   the current occupation with another occupation, the interaction containing the occupation token
436   would be significant changed. This indicates a common bias problem, because similar bias may also
437   happen on other attributes (e.g., age, gender, education level, and marital status).

      Figure 5 shows the test of the SaulLM-7B-Instruct model on the legal case, in which Andy, the victim

441   boosted the confidence of the judgment “robbery.” More interestingly, if we substituted the occupa-
442   tion tokens “a judge” to “a volunteer,” the interaction between the occupation “a volunteer,” “a day’s
443   work,” and “belongings” decreased from 0.22 to 0.06. This was an important factor that changed the
444   judgment from “robbery” to “not mentioned.” However, if we replaced “a judge” with law-related
445   occupations, such as “a lawyer” and “a policeman,” the judgment remained “robbery.” Besides, the
446   occupation “a programmer” changed the judgment to “not mentioned.” Please see Appendix G.3 for

468   4   C ONCLUSION
469
470   In this paper, we have proposed a method to evaluate the correctness of the detailed decision-making
471   logic of an LLM. The sparsity property and the universal matching property of interactions provide
472   direct mathematical supports for the faithfulness of the interaction-based explanation. Thus, in this
473
      paper, we have designed two new metrics to quantify reliable and unreliable interaction effects,
      according to their alignment with human cognition. Experiments showed that the legal LLMs often
474
      relied on a considerable number of problematic interactions to make judgments, even when the
475
      judgement prediction was correct. The evaluation of the alignment between the decision-making
476   logic of LLMs and human cognition also contributes to other real applications. For example, it may
477   assist in debugging the hallucination problems, and identifying potential bias behind the language
478   generation results of LLMs.
479
      Limitations. Our analysis does not assess the correctness of the numerical scores for interactions, as
480
      these scores are often determined by many factors. Positive interactions typically indicate logics that
481
      contribute positively to the judgments, while negative interactions may also be intended for other
482   possible correct judgments. Besides, the evaluation based on relevant, irrelavant, and forbidden
483   tokens is only one of the conditions for reliable interactions, and reliable interactions may not always
484   be correct. Nevertheless, this paper presents a precedent for evaluating the correctness of decision-
485   making logic of LLMs.

      A    R ELATED WORK
703
704
      Factuality and hallucination problems. Factuality in LLMs refers to whether the language gen-
705   eralization results of LLMs align with the verificable facts. This includes the ability of LLMs to
706   avoid producing misleading or incorrect information (i.e., factual hallucination), and to effectively
707   generate factually accurate results. For instance, several studies have evaluated the correctness of
708   LLM-generated answers to specific questions (Lin et al., 2021; OpenAI, 2023; Wang et al., 2024).
709   Other works have standardized fact consistency tasks into binary labels, evaluating whether there
710   were factual conflicts within the input text (Honovich et al., 2022). Min et al. (2023) further de-
711   composed language generation results into “atomic” facts, and calculated the proportion of these
712   facts that aligned with a given knowledge source. Additionally, Manakul et al. (2023) introduced
713
      a sampling-based method to verify whether LLMs generated factually consistent results, based on
      the assumption that if an LLM had knowledge of a concept, then the sampled generation results
714
      contained consistent factual information.
715
716   Hallucination in LLMs typically refers to generated content that is nonsensical or unfaithful to the
717   provided source input (Filippova, 2020; Maynez et al., 2020; Huang et al., 2023). Hallucinations are
718   generally categorized into two primary types, namely intrinsic and extrinsic hallucinations (Maynez
719
      et al., 2020; Huang et al., 2021; Dziri et al., 2021; Ji et al., 2023b). Intrinsic hallucinations oc-
      cur when the generated results contradict the source content, while extrinsic hallucinations arise
720
      when the generated results cannot be verified from the provided source. For instance, Bang et al.
721
      (2023) found extrinsic hallucinations in ChatGPT’s responses, including both untruthful and fac-
722   tual hallucinations, whereas intrinsic hallucinations were rarely observed. OpenAI’s latest model,
723   GPT-4 (OpenAI, 2023), has further reduced the model’s tendency to hallucinate compared to prior
724   models such as ChatGPT.
725
      Value alignment. Value alignment in LLMs aims to ensure LLMs behave in accordance with hu-
726
      man intentions and values (Leike et al., 2018; Wang et al., 2023; Ji et al., 2023a). Recent research
727
      has focused on improving the ability of LLMs to comprehend instructions, thereby aligning their be-
728   havior with human expectations. For instance, OpenAI proposed Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) for
729   LLMs, which involved using human-annotated instruction data. LLMs such as InstructGPT (Ouyang
730   et al., 2022) and ChatGPT, both of which employed this technique, have demonstrated significant
731   improvements in understanding human instructions. Ouyang et al. (2022); OpenAI (2023); Touvron
732   et al. (2023) have incorporated the Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) method
733   to further fine-tune LLMs, enhancing their alignment with human preferences (OpenAI, 2023).
734   Using interactions to faithfully explain DNNs. Ren et al. (2023a) first proposed to quantify in-
735   teractions between input variables encoded by the DNN, to explain the knowledge in the DNN. Li
736   & Zhang (2023) discovered the discriminative power of interactions between input variables. Ren
737   et al. (2024) further proved that DNNs usually only encoded a small number of interactions. Futher-
738   more, Deng et al. (2024a) found that different attribution scores estimated by fourteen attribution
739   methods, including the Grad-CAM (Selvaraju et al., 2017), Integrated Gradients (Sundararajan et al.,
740   2017), and Shapley value (Lundberg & Lee, 2017)) methods, could all be represented as a combi-
741   nation of interactions. Besides, Zhang et al. (2022) used interactions to explain the mechanism of
742
      different methods of boosting adversarial transferability. Ren et al. (2023b) used interactions to de-
      fine the optimal baseline value for computing Shapley values. Deng et al. (2022) found that for most
743
      DNNs it was difficult to learn interactions with median number of input variables, and it was dis-
744
      covered that DNNs and Bayesian neural networks were unlikely to model complex interactions with
745   many input variables (Ren et al., 2023c; Liu et al., 2024). Zhou et al. (2024) used the generalization
746   power of different interactions to explain the generalization power of DNNs.
747
748
      Unlike evaluations on language generation results, we propose a method that leverages interaction-
      based explanations to evaluate the correctness of decision-making logic encoded by a LLM. This
749
      approach enables us to evaluate the alignment between the decision-making logic of LLMs and
750
      human cognition.

1326   G.5      E XPERIMENT DETAILS FOR USING THE SAME DATASET FOR COMPARISON
1327
1328
       This section presents the experiment details of using the CAIL2018 dataset (Xiao et al., 2018) to
       ensure a fair comparison between two legal LLMs. For the BAI-Law-13B model, a Chinese legal
1329
       LLM, we directly analyzed the Chinese legal cases from the CAIL2018 dataset. In contrast, for
1330
       the SaulLM-7B-Instruct model, an English legal LLM, we translated the Chinese legal cases into
1331   English and performed the analysis on the translated cases, to enable fair comparisons. To simplify
1332   the explanation and avoid ambiguity, we only explained the decision-making logic on legal cases,
1333   which were correctly judged by the LLM.
1334
       Starting with a complete fact descriptions of the legal case from the CAIL2018 dataset, we first
1335
       condensed the case by removing descriptive details irrelevant to the judgment, retaining only the
1336
       most informative tokens, such as the time, location, people, and events. To prompt the model to
1337   deliver its judgment, we added a structured prompt designed to extract a concise answer. The format
1338   is as follows:
1339
1340
       “Question: [Fact descriptions of the case]. What crime did [the defendant] commit? Briefly answer
       the specific charge in one word. Answer: The specific charge is”
1341
1342   Here, [Fact descriptions of the case] is replaced with the details of the specific legal case, and [the
1343   defendant] is substituted with the name of the defendant.
1344   To identify potential representation flaws behind the seemingly correct language generation results
1345   of legal LLMs, we introduced special tokens that were irrelevant to the judgments. For cases to
1346   assess if judgments were influenced by unreliable sentimental tokens, we added such tokens to
1347   describe actions in the legal case. We then observed whether a substantial portion of the interactions
1348   contributing to the confidence score v(x) were associated with semantically irrelevant or unreliable
1349   sentimental tokens. Similarly, in cases where we aimed to detect potential bias based on occupation,
       we included irrelevant occupation-related tokens for the defendants or victims, and analyzed whether
