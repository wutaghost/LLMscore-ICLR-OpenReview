009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Non-parametric encoders offer advantages in interpretability and generalizability.
012            However, they often perform significantly worse than deep neural networks on
013            many challenging recognition tasks, and it remains unclear how to effectively ap-
014            ply these techniques to such tasks. In this work, we view all AI recognition tasks
015            as function approximation problems and introduce LagEncoder, a non-parametric,
016            training-free feature extraction method based on finite element basis functions.
017            Our encoder features a universal architecture that can be applied to various types
018
               of raw data and recognition tasks. We found that LagEncoder effectively over-
               comes the limitations of neural networks in regression problems, particularly
019
               when fitting multi-frequency functions. The LagEncoder-based model converges
020
               quickly and requires low training costs, as only the head is trained. Additionally,
021            LagEncoder provides a parameter-efficient fine-tuning approach. Our experiments
022            on the ImageNet-1K and WikiText dataset demonstrate that pre-trained models
023            using LagEncoder achieve performance improvements within just one training
024            epoch. Furthermore, it does not require adjustments to the original training recipe,
025            extra training data, and the model’s total parameters remain nearly unchanged.
026            Our evaluation of the scaling law for model performance indicates that using the
027            LagEncoder is more cost-effective than increasing the model size.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Neural networks have played a pivotal role in the evolution of artificial intelligence, particularly
033
      excelling in challenge recognition tasks, where their performance has, in some cases, surpassed
      human-level capabilities. Furthermore, advances in transfer learning have demonstrated that neural
034
      network encoders exhibit notable domain adaptation properties, allowing them to generalize across
035
      disparate tasks and data distributions. For instance, an encoder pre-trained for an image classifica-
036   tion task can be effectively transferred to an object detection task, even when the underlying data
037   distributions differ, while still maintaining robust feature extraction capabilities.
038
039
      However, traditional machine learning models often demonstrate better domain adaptation. They
      provided such feature extractors that require no training and are independent of specific recogni-
040
      tion tasks (Pearson, 1901; Sparck Jones, 1972). For example, the kernel functions used in Support
041
      Vector Machines (SVM) are unrelated to data labels, resulting in non-trainable feature extractors
042   that inherently provide complete domain adaptation. While traditional models typically offer higher
043   interpretability, they often perform significantly worse than neural networks in specific, highly chal-
044   lenging recognition tasks. It is worth noting that many neural network architectures are based on
045   mathematical methods that do not depend on labeled data. For instance, the linear layer and the
046   attention layer rely on inner products for feature extraction, where the vector basis in linear algebra
047   is fixed. Similarly, convolution layers perform feature extraction through convolution, in the Fourier
048   transform, the kernel function is predefined and independent of the target.
049   We view all recognition tasks as function approximation problems and focus on constructing an en-
050   coder with complete domain adaptation for challenging tasks. Through our extensive experiments,
051   we found that, as Fourier transforms, neural networks often struggle to fit multi-frequency functions
052   effectively (see Section 3.1.1). Specifically, when fitting functions with sharp transitions or local-
053   ized features, a large number of Fourier terms may be required, which can reduce computational
      efficiency and potentially lead to overfitting. Similarly, neural networks often need to be much

      2.3   PEFT-L AG E NCODER
205
206   LagEncoder adheres to the universal approximation theorem and exhibits strong interpretability for
207   low-dimensional recognition tasks (see Section 3.1). However, as the data dimension d increases, the
208   output dimension of LagEncoder grows factorially (O(d!)) as described in Eq. (2). This exponential
209   growth makes it computationally expensive for high-dimensional data, such as large images, posing
210   challenges in resource-constrained environments.
211   To mitigate this, LagEncoder can be integrated as a module in PEFT methods. Consider a perfect
212   model F (x) with 100% test accuracy and a pre-trained model f (x; θ). If the pre-trained model
213   already achieves high test accuracy, the residual F (x) − f (x; θ) will be very flat, typically close
214   to zero for different inputs x. This sparsity in the residual’s support set implies that dimensionality
215   reduction on the pre-trained model’s features is likely reversible, enabling the effective application
      of LagEncoder.

      3.1.1      T HE L IMITATIONS OF T RADITIONAL R EGRESSORS AND N EURAL N ETWORKS
342
343   Traditional regressors often struggle with overfitting, while neural networks address this but perform
344   poorly when fitting multi-frequency functions, a phenomenon known as the frequency principle (Xu
345   et al., 2019). As shown in Fig. 4, Support Vector Regression (Platt et al., 1999) fits dataset B but
346   overfits on dataset A, whereas neural networks perform well on A but underfit on B. Neither MLPs,
347   CNNs, nor Transformers can effectively fit sharp transitions in dataset B, often treating these regions
348   as noise to avoid overfitting. Importantly, no existing model can fit the challenging dataset C.
349   LagEncoder addresses this gap by combining the strengths of traditional regressors and neural net-
350   works. It leverages a multiscale mesh to fit high-frequency regions using fine simplices adaptively.
351   Fig. 4 (right) demonstrates the LagEncoder-based model’s unique ability to handle dataset C, a
352   task no other model can achieve. Appendix F.3 (Fig. 9) further illustrates its gradual fitting pro-
353   cess during training. Comparison experiments in Appendix F.1 show that the LagEncoder-based
354   model consistently achieves high R2 scores across all test sets, demonstrating its robustness and
355   effectiveness, even in high-noise and multi-frequency scenarios.

      A unique feature of the LagEncoder-based model in text classification is that its parameter count is
414
      independent of the token count. Compared to word2vec-based networks, which require over 6.13

418
      Through experiments, we found three key limitations of existing PEFT methods:
419           • Dependence on Transfer Learning: Methods like LoRA struggle to outperform pre-trained
420             models on the same or similar datasets, as no domain adaptation is needed. Additional
421             training data is often required for these methods to be effective, as shown in Table 1. Their

425             small learning rates. Without these, performance often deteriorates from the first epoch.
426
              • Task and Architecture Limits: Many PEFT methods, like LoHA (Hyeon-Woo et al., 2021)
427             and IA3 (Liu et al., 2022), are restricted to specific tasks or architectures, such as Conv1D

512   4   F UTURE D IRECTIONS
513
514
      It is important to recognize that LagEncoder has certain limitations. As described in Section 2.3,
515   the high computational cost of extracting features from high-dimensional data restricts its direct
516   application to large-scale datasets. Currently, we address this by incorporating LagEncoder as part
517   of an adaptation method, but we aim to improve the encoder in the future to be able to extract
518   features directly from large images. Additionally, the experiments in Section 3.3 show that while
519   our adaptation method significantly reduces cross-entropy on the ImageNet-1K validation set and
520   consistently improves validation accuracy, the magnitude of improvement of accuracy is relatively
521   modest, which we intend to explore further. We did not compare LagEncoder with models like SVM
522   or KAN, which offer strong interpretability, because their underlying principles are entirely differ-
523
      ent. Moreover, we prioritize performance on challenging tasks where most interpretable models
      often lack relevant experimental results.
524
525
526   5   C ONCLUSION
527
528   We explored domain adaptation in transfer learning and proposed a non-parametric encoder that
529   does not require training. This encoder has a universal design suitable for diverse raw data and
530   tasks. It extracts features by estimating data distribution across a multi-scale grid, enabling strong
531   generalization. Our experiments demonstrated several key advantages of the LagEncoder-based
532   model: 1) Strong mathematical explainability: The empirical model scaling law aligns perfectly
533   with the error-bounds formula of linear interpolation, providing a solid understanding of how it
534   learns representations. 2) Fast training: With only one linear layer to train, the model typically
535   converges in just one or two epochs, making it ideal for large datasets. 3) Not limited to transfer
536
      learning: Unlike most PEFT methods, which are task-specific and underperform on raw datasets,
      our method works effectively without these constraints.
537
538   In summary, LagEncoder is a novel encoder rooted in the universal approximation theorem, requir-
539   ing no extensive training, fine-tuning, or heavy computational resources. It offers an explainable and
      efficient approach to representation learning, providing an alternative to black-box methods.
