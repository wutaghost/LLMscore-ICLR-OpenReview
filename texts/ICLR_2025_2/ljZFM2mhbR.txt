009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Shapley value-based feature attribution methods face challenges in scenarios with
012            complex feature interactions and causal relationships, even when a causal struc-
013            ture is provided. The assumption on the attribution objects of existing meth-
014            ods often deviates from practical scenarios as they cannot capture the exoge-
015            nous influence of features through each edge in the causal graph, leading to un-
016
               reasonable interpretations. To overcome these limitations, we propose a novel
               feature attribution method called DAG-SHAP, which is based on edge interven-
017
               tion. DAG-SHAP treats the exogenous contributions in each ongoing feature
018
               edge as an individual attribution object ensuring that both externality and ex-
019            ogenous contributions of features are appropriately captured. Additionally, we
020            introduce an approximation method for efficiently computing DAG-SHAP. Ex-
021            tensive experiments on both synthetic and real datasets validate the effective-
022            ness of DAG-SHAP. Our code can be found in the anonymous repository at
023            https://anonymous.4open.science/r/dag-30F2.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      In recent years, the field of model interpretation has gained significant attention (Scott et al., 2017;
      Ribeiro et al., 2016; Sundararajan et al., 2017; Chen et al., 2024; Machiraju et al., 2024) due to the
029
      increasing demands and complexity of machine learning (ML) models in real-world applications.
030
      Shapley value-based feature attribution methods have been extensively explored in this domain since
031   they uniquely offer a fair allocation guarantee of cooperative contributions rooted in game theory,
032   with desirable properties including efficiency, symmetry, redundancy, and additivity (Winter, 2002).
033   Moreover, owing to their model-agnostic nature and ease of implementation, Shapley value-based
034   methods are highly user-friendly and versatile. For healthcare, Shapley value-based attribution can
035   help explain which features, such as age, blood pressure, and blood sugar levels, are important when
036   predicting whether a patient is likely to develop diabetes (Ter-Minassian et al., 2023). Similarly,
037   they can also be applied in credit scoring to help financial institutions understand why a customer’s
038   predicted credit score is low or high by identifying the key factors to the predicted credit score (Chen
039
      et al., 2022).
040   However, modeling feature interactions appropriately remains a significant challenge for Shapley
041   value-based feature attribution methods. The off-manifold Shapley value (Scott et al., 2017), one of
042   the pioneering Shapley value-based feature attribution methods, assumes feature independence, which
043   is inappropriate in most practical scenarios. Meanwhile, the on-manifold Shapley value (Scott et al.,
044   2017; Sundararajan & Najmi, 2020) considers the feature dependency. It fills excluded features with
045
      conditional expected values based on their correlations with included features when measuring the
      utility of feature subsets, aiming to make the interpretation more reasonable. However, filling excluded
046
      features based solely on correlation may not align with the data generation process, particularly when
047
      causal relationships exist among features, potentially leading to causal reversion (Jung et al., 2022a).
048   To address this, researchers consider the sequential and causal relationships in data generation,
049   like asymmetric Shapley value (Frye et al., 2020) and causal Shapley value (Heskes et al., 2020).
050   Furthermore, existing attribution methods that focus on feature vertices fail to adequately capture
051   interactions between features (Ter-Minassian et al., 2023). Shapley Flow (Wang et al., 2021)
052   attributes the contribution in each cut of the feature graph by treating paths as players. Recursive
053   Shapley value (Singal et al., 2021) attributes contributions following a top-down principle by first
      attributing to “source” vertices and then flowing them down the directed acyclic graph (DAG).

      Despite the aforementioned significant efforts to enhance feature interactions in the attribution process,
064
      externality and exogeneity in existing approaches are still inadequately addressed. Attribution methods
065   based on feature vertices adopting asymmetry samplings, such as asymmetry Shapley value and
066   asymmetry causal Shapley value, fail to make attribution results satisfy externality. When using
067   the asymmetrical ordering of feature attribution methods (the parent vertex must appear before
068   the child vertex), the marginal contribution of the parent vertex cannot receive externality gains
069   from cooperating with the child vertex. For example in Figure 1, the direct influence X1 → Y
070   cannot receive a marginal contribution from cooperating with X2 which is X2 → Y . In addition,
071   the attribution methods focusing on attributing contributions at each graph cut like Shapley Flow
072   and recursive Shapley value, fail to recognize exogenous contributions of features. Exogenous
073
      contribution refers to the part of the contribution in each feature that is not influenced by other
      features in the explained input. Shapley Flow and recursive Shapley value assume that only feature
074
      vertices without incoming edges have exogenous contributions. Hence, the contribution of exogenous
075
      variable x2 cannot be properly captured. Apparently, the assumption that intermediate vertices have
076   no endogenous contributions is misaligned with real-world feature attribution scenarios.
077
078   In this paper, we explore the potential to enhance the reasonableness of attribution methods through
079
      an advanced investigation of feature interactions. Specifically, we incorporate fine-grained causal
      relationships under the assumption that features are structured as DAG. We briefly summarize our
080
      contributions as follows. (1) We propose an edge intervention feature attribution method DAG-SHAP
081
      that allows for interventions on certain child vertices through their parent vertices without affecting
082   other child vertices of the same parent. (2) Our attribution method not only meets the requirement
083   of externality but also captures the exogenous contributions of each feature. (3) We present an
084   approximate method for the practical computation of DAG-SHAP and validate its effectiveness using
085   both synthetic and real datasets.

120   3     E DGE I NTERVENTION C AUSAL S HAPLEY VALUE
121
122   In this section, we analyze the limitations of existing methods using a simple attribution task. We
123   then introduce DAG-SHAP, a feature attribution method based on edge intervention that leverages the
124   DAG of causal relationships between input features, aiming to enhance the reasonableness of feature
125
      attribution.
126   We provide definitions of several properties below, which will be discussed in detail afterward.
127   Causality: The contribution of a feature to the prediction outcome should be based on its true causal
128   impact, rather than just its statistical correlation with other features. In essence, attribution should
129   reflect the change in the model’s output that is directly caused by the feature, independent of any
130   dependencies on other features.
131
      Efficiency: The sum of the attribution values of all nodes equals to       Pthe difference between the
      prediction outcome when all nodes are added. This can be expressed as i∈V Φ(i) = f (x)−E[f (x)],
132
      where x represents the input features being explained.
133
      Externality: The attribution value of feature vertex k should derive benefit from the cooperation of
134   another feature vertex k ′ if there exists a path to the target label that passes through k but does not
135   pass through k ′ .
136   Exogeneity: The attribution method should identify and measure the independent contribution of
137   each feature to the prediction outcome. Each feature’s effect should be attributed to its exogenous
138   influence, without being confounded by other input features.

140   3.1   M OTIVATION E XAMPLE FOR I LLUSTRATING THE L IMITATIONS OF E XISTING M ETHODS
141
142   Data Generation Process. We use the toy example in Figure 1 to show why existing methods can
143   produce unreasonable interpretations for model output. Suppose there are two input features X1
144
      and X2 and a target feature Y = X1 · X2 . The generation of features follows the process below.
      X1 = x1 , where x1 is a random variable uniformly distributed on (0, 1), representing the exogenous
145
      influence of X1 ; X2 = X1 + x2 , where x2 is another random variable uniformly distributed on (0, 1),
146
      representing the exogenous influence of X2 . In summary, X1 directly influences Y and indirectly
147   influences Y through X2 . X2 influences Y with its own exogenous influence x2 and transfers the
148   indirect influence of X1 . We want to attribute a value to each feature of a specific explained input
149   x∗ = [x∗1 , x∗2 ] = [0.2, 0.8] with respect to the uniform distributions of each feature.
150
      Proposition 1. For off-manifold Shapley value, on-manifold Shapley value, and symmetry causal
151   Shapley value, the attribution results do not satisfy causality.
152
153   For off-manifold Shapley value, the marginal contribution cannot account for the causal interaction
154   between features. For example, the marginal contribution of x∗1 = 0.2 when cooperating with the
155   empty set is E[f (0.2, x1 + x2 )] − E[f (x1 , x1 + x2 )]. However, the value of feature X2 is influenced
156   by x∗1 but the feature value of X2 in the utility E[f (0.2, x1 + x2 )] is independent from x∗1 = 0.2. So
157   the marginal contribution does not incorporate the indirect effect of x∗1 = 0.2.
158   For on-manifold Shapley value, there is causal reversion in the explained results. For example, when
159   calculating the marginal contribution of x∗2 = 0.8 cooperating with the empty set, On-manifold
160   Shapley value yields E[f (x1 , 0.8)|x∗2 = 0.8] − E[f (x1 , x1 + x2 )], where x1 is conditioned on x∗2 .
161   However, X2 cannot influence X1 from a causal perspective. Therefore, calculating contribution
      x∗2 = 0.8 with condition expectation includes the part of contribution which does not belong to x∗2 .

      Symmetry causal Shapley value faces the same causal reversion problem as on-manifold Shapley
163   value. Considering the marginal contribution of x∗2 = 0.8 when cooperating with empty set, it
164   yields E[f (x1 , 0.8)| do(x2 = 0.8)] − E[f (x1 , x1 + x2 )]. However, the utility of x∗2 = 0.8 when
165   cooperating with an empty cannot be E[f (x1 , 0.8)| do(x2 = 0.8)] because x∗2 = 0.8 already has the
166   influence of x∗1 = 0.2.
167   Proposition 2. For asymmetry Shapley value and asymmetry causal Shapley value, the attribution
168   results do not satisfy externality.
169
170   Asymmetry Shapley value and asymmetry causal Shapley value calculate the marginal contributions
171   on the permutations where ancestor vertices before their descendants. However, these asymmetry
172   methods fail to guarantee the externality property in feature attribution. For example, x∗1 = 0.2 has
173
      a direct influence on Y , and the influence is independent of X2 . But the direct influence does not
      receive a marginal contribution from X2 since x∗1 must appear before x∗2 in the permutations. In
174
      other words, the attributed value of x∗1 is independent of x∗2 , which does not satisfy the externality
175
      in cooperation game theory (Shapley, 1953), where x∗1 should benefit from the cooperation with
176   variable x2 .
177
      Proposition 3. For Shapley Flow and recursive Shapley value, the attribution process cannot capture
178
      the exogenous contributions of intermediate vertices.
179
180   Shapley Flow and recursive Shapley value are unsuitable for this scenario due to their cut efficiency
181   property, which requires that the sum of contributions be equal for every cut. This implies that the
182   contribution of x∗1 through x∗2 is considered equal to the direct contribution of x∗2 , an assumption
183   that may not hold true in real-world situations. They do not account for the independent exogenous
184   contributions of each feature.

186   3.2   O UR P ROPOSED M ETHOD : DAG-SHAP
187
      We propose DAG-SHAP, an edge-based feature attribution method suitable for the scenario where
188
      the causal relations of input features can be formulated as a DAG. DAG-SHAP enables us to gain a
189
      deeper understanding of how features collaboratively contribute to the model output using the edge
190   intervention to capture the causal influence within the features.
191
192   Edge Intervention. Given a directed acyclic graph G with a set of vertices V = {1, · · · , n} and
193
      directed edges E = {e1 , · · · , em }, where n represents the number of vertices and m the number of
      edges in G, each vertex corresponds to a feature, and each edge denotes a causal influence between
194
      features. An edge ei = (pi , ci , xpi ) indicates that parent vertex pi ∈ V has a direct causal influence
195
      on child vertex ci ∈ V, where the feature corresponding to pi follows distribution xpi . For a specific
196   explained input x, we instantiate the edges as E = {e1 , · · · , em }, where each edge ei = (pi , ci , xpi )
197   corresponds to a specific value of xpi . The causal influence from pi is then transferred to ci . We
198   define an edge intervention in G as do(eS = eS ), where S is the set of edge indices which are
199   intervened. Such interventions make the effects of parents transfer to childs , providing insights
200   into how changes in one feature directly affect another. This edge intervention isolates the causal
201   pathway’s impact without altering the entire network of child vertices, enabling a targeted analysis of
202   causality within the graph.
203   DAG-SHAP. Let Π denote the set of all permutations of edges E, where each permutation constitutes
204   a topological ordering of E. This arrangement ensures that each edge appears only after all its
205   prerequisite edges have been ordered, thereby allowing interventions to be applied sequentially
206   without violating causal relationships. We define the attribution value of an edge ei for the explained
207   input x as the average marginal contribution of applying the edge intervention on ei , given that
208   interventions have already been applied to the edges preceding it in the permutations within Π.
209   Consequently, the edge intervention causal Shapley value of edge ei can be formulated as

715   maps of the last convolutional layer and the gradient information regarding the prediction of a
716   specific class to generate a Class Activation Map. DeepLIFT (Shrikumar et al., 2017) explains the
717   decision-making process of deep learning models through the differences in activation functions. The
718   DeepLIFT method allocates contribution scores by comparing the activation of each neuron with its
719   "reference activation," thereby revealing the contribution of input features to the model’s prediction.
720   SmoothGrad Smilkov et al. (2017) is a method designed to improve the interpretability of deep
721   learning models, particularly for image classification tasks. It aims to enhance the quality of model
722   prediction explanations by reducing the visual noise in gradient sensitivity maps. The core idea of
723
      this method is to add random noise to the input image to create multiple perturbed images, and then
      average the gradient sensitivity maps of these perturbed images to smooth the original sensitivity map.
724
      Integrated Gradients (IG) (Tauman, 1988) involves integrating the gradients of a model’s output
725
      with respect to its inputs along the path from a baseline to the actual input data. It satisfies important
726   interpretability axioms such as Sensitivity, Implementation Invariance, and Completeness, and is
727   independent of the specific implementation details of the model. Layer-wise Relevance Propagation
728   (LRP) (Montavon et al., 2019) is a popular method for explaining neural networks. It explains
729   the predictions of neural networks by propagating relevance scores from the output layer back to
730   the input layer. MCI measures feature contribution based on the maximum marginal contribution a
731   feature can bring which cannot capture the right causal contributin. It gives some new properties like
732   Super-efficiency and Sub-additivity (Catav et al., 2020).

      The Shapley Additive Global Importance (SAGE) value is a globally attributed explainability method.
      d-SAGE (Luther et al., 2023)observes the conditional independence between features and the model
737
      target, and utilizes Causal Structure Learning (CSL) to infer a graph that encodes (conditional)

741   interventions but generalizes previous approaches to measure the causal contributions of each feature
742   to a target effect induced by a black-box/unknown/inaccessible model. PWSHAP (Path-Wise Shapley
743   effects) (Ter-Minassian et al., 2023) is a method for explaining the impact of specific binary variables,
744   such as treatment effects or ethnicity in policy models, within predictive models. It evaluates the
745   targeted effects in complex outcome models by combining a predictive model with a user-defined
746   Directed Acyclic Graph (DAG). Inspired by causal inference and randomized experiments, researchers
747
      have developed an algorithm to estimate AME (Average Marginal Effect), a measure of the expected
      (average) marginal effect of adding a data point to a subset of the training data sampled from a
748
      uniform distribution. When subsets are sampled from a uniform distribution, AME simplifies to the
749
      well-known Shapley value (Lin et al., 2022). CF-SHAP (?) is a method that combines counterfactual
750   information for feature attribution. It strengthens and clarifies the link between actionable recourse
751   and feature attributions, playing a role in advancing the development of causal feature attribution.
752   Dominik et al. (Janzing et al., 2020) primarily focus on distinguishing between calculating Shapley
753   values based on observational versus interventional conditional distributions. They were among
754   the first to emphasize the role of causality in feature attribution. Besides, Dominik et al. (Janzing
755   et al., 2024) employs structure-preserving interventions to attribute uncertainty (e.g., variance or
      Shannon entropy) in the target to its influencing features. Their approach can be seen as focusing on

       D.3    A C OMPARISON OF E DGE I NTERVENTION AND S TRUCTURE - PRESERVING
1020
              INTERVENTIONS
1021
1022   Structure-preserving interventions are based on the assumption that the distribution of exogenous
1023   variables for each feature is known, and then intervene on the exogenous variable of each feature
1024   accordingly. However, in reality, the distribution of exogenous variables is unknown. The authors use
1025   an additive structural causal model to fit the data generation process. By subtracting the distribution of
       the parent node from the distribution of the child node, they obtain the exogenous variable distribution


                                                            19
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1026
       for the child node. However, most real-world data cannot be represented by an additive structural
1027   causal model. For example, in this case X1 = x1 , where x1 is a random variable uniformly distributed
1028   on [0, 1], representing the exogenous influence of X1 and X2 = X1 · x2 , where x2 is another random
1029   variable uniformly distributed on [0, 1]. In the generation of the X2 feature, the exogenous variable
1030   x2 acts multiplicatively. Thus, fit the data using an additive causal model, we cannot determine the
1031   right causal structure, so the attribution values calculated using this method would not be accurate.
1032   Consider a special case where when x∗1 = 1 and x∗2 = 1, the resulting exogenous variable for x2 is
1033   0. This implies that x2 has no effect on the data generation process, which clearly contradicts the
1034   true data generation process. DAG-SHAP does not require calculating the distribution of exogenous
1035
       variables, nor does it assume the influence of exogenous variables in the data generation process is
       linear. It only need the causal direction between features. We think this is a significant distinction.
