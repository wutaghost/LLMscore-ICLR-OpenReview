026   1     I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Clustering objects based on the information of their similarity is a fundamental task in machine
029   learning. Correlation Clustering, introduced by Bansal et al. (2002; 2004), is an optimization model
030   that mathematically formulates this task. In the model, we are given a set V of n elements, where each
031   pair of elements is labeled either ‚Äė+‚Äô (representing that they are similar) or ‚Äė‚ąí‚Äô (representing that they
032   are dissimilar) together with a nonnegative weight representing the degree of similarity/dissimilarity.
033   In general, the goal of Correlation Clustering is to find a clustering of V that is consistent with the
034   given similarity information as much as possible. The (in)consistency of a clustering of V can be
035   measured by the so-called disagreements, which is defined as the sum of weights of misclassified
036
      pairs, i.e., pairs with ‚Äė+‚Äô label across clusters and pairs with ‚Äė‚ąí‚Äô label within the same cluster. The
      problem of finding a clustering of V that minimizes the disagreements is called M IN D ISAGREE.
037
038   It is known that M IN D ISAGREE is not only NP-hard (Bansal et al., 2002) but also APX-hard even
039   if we consider the unweighted case (i.e., the case where the weights are all equal to 1) (Charikar
040   et al., 2005). A large body of work has been devoted to designing polynomial-time approximation
041   algorithms for the problem. For the general weighted case, Charikar et al. (2005) and Demaine et al.
042
      (2006) independently proposed O(log n)-approximation algorithms, using the well-known region
      growing technique (Garg et al., 1996). The approximation ratio of O(log n) is still the state-of-the-art,
043
      and it is also known that improving it is at least as hard as improving the O(log n)-approximation
044
      for Minimum Multicut (Garg et al., 1996), which is one of the major open problems in theoretical
045   computer science. For the unweighted case, Bansal et al. (2002) presented the first constant-factor
046   approximation algorithm, which has been improved by a series of works so far (Ailon et al., 2008;
047   Cao et al., 2024; Charikar et al., 2005; Chawla et al., 2015; Cohen-Addad et al., 2022; 2023). Notably,
048   the current-best approximation ratio for the unweighted case is 1.437 +   for any   > 0 (Cao et al.,
049   2024). For more details, see Section 2.

053   In this study, we establish Multilayer Correlation Clustering, a novel generalization of Correlation
      Clustering to the multilayer setting. In the model, we are given a series of inputs of Correlation

      Clustering (called layers) over the common set V of n elements. The goal is then to find a clustering
055   of V that is consistent as much as possible with all layers. To quantify the (in)consistency of a
056   clustering over layers, we introduce the concept of multilayer-disagreements vector (with dimension
057   equal to the number of layers) of a clustering, each element of which represents the disagreements of
058   the clustering on the corresponding layer.1 Using the `p -norm (p ‚Č• 1) of this vector, we can quantify
059   the (in)consistency of the given clustering in a variety of regimes. In particular, if we set p = 1, it
060   simply quantifies the sum of disagreements over all layers, whereas if we set p = ‚ąě, it quantifies the
061   maximal disagreements over the layers. For p ‚Č• 1, our problem asks to find a clustering of V that
062   minimizes the `p -norm of the multilayer-disagreements vector.
063   Multilayer Correlation Clustering is motivated by real-world scenarios. Suppose that we want to find a
064   clustering of users of X using their similarity information. In this case, various types of similarity can
065   be defined through analysis of users‚Äô tweets and observations of different types of connections among
066   users such as follower relations, retweets, and mentions. In the original Correlation Clustering, we
067   need to deal with that information one by one and manage to aggregate resulting clusterings. On the
068   other hand, Multilayer Correlation Clustering enables us to handle that information simultaneously,
069
      directly producing a clustering that is consistent (as much as possible) with all types of information.
      As another example scenario, suppose that we analyze brain networks, where nodes correspond to
070
      small regions of a brain and edges represent similarity relations among them. Then it is often the case
071
      that the edge set is not determined uniquely; indeed, there would be at least two types of similarity
072   based on the structural connectivity and the functional connectivity among the small pieces of a brain.
073   Obviously, Multilayer Correlation Clustering can again find its advantage in this context.
074
075
      For this novel, well-motivated generalization, we present a variety of algorithmic results. We first
      design a polynomial-time O(L log n)-approximation algorithm, where L is the number of layers. Our
076
      algorithm is a generalization of the O(log n)-approximation algorithms for M IN D ISAGREE (Charikar
077
      et al., 2005; Demaine et al., 2006) and thus employs the region growing technique (Garg et al., 1996).
078   Our algorithm first solves a convex programming relaxation of the problem. Then, the algorithm
079   iteratively constructs a cluster (and removes it from V as a part of the output), using the region
080   growing technique based on the pseudometric computed by the relaxation, until all elements are
081   clustered. Specifically, in each iteration, the algorithm takes an arbitrary element in V and constructs
082   a ball of center being that element and a radius carefully selected using the similarity information
083   over all layers.
084   We then study an important special case of our problem, namely the problem with the probability
085   constraint, where on each layer, each pair of elements in V has both ‚Äė+‚Äô and ‚Äė‚ąí‚Äô labels, each of
086   which is associated with a nonnegative weight in [0, 1] and the sum of those two weights is equal
087   to 1. For this problem, we first give a polynomial-time (őĪ + 2)-approximation algorithm, where
088   őĪ is any possible approximation ratio for M IN D ISAGREE with the probability constraint or any of
089   its special cases if we consider the corresponding special case of our problem. For instance, we
090   can take őĪ = 2.5 in general (Ailon et al., 2008), őĪ = 1.437 +   for the unweighted case (Cao
091   et al., 2024), and őĪ = 1.5 for the case where the weights of ‚Äė‚ąí‚Äô labels satisfy the triangle inequality
092
      constraint (see Section 3) (Chawla et al., 2015). In the algorithm design, we first reduce our problem
      to a novel optimization problem in a metric space, and devise an algorithm to solve it. We then
093
      design a 4-approximation algorithm for the general probability-constraint case, improving the above
094
      approximation ratio of őĪ + 2 = 4.5. The algorithm first solves a convex programming relaxation as
095   in the aforementioned O(L log n)-approximation algorithm, and then constructs a clustering, using
096   a simple thresholding rule. Our algorithm is a generalization of the 4-approximation algorithm for
097   M IN D ISAGREE of the unweighted case, designed by Charikar et al. (2005).
098
      Finally we conduct thorough experiments using a variety of real-world datasets to evaluate the
099
      performance of our proposed algorithms in terms of both solution quality and running time. We
100
      confirm that our algorithms outperform baseline methods for both Problem 1 of the general weighted
101   case and Problem 1 with the probability constraint. In particular, the objective value achieved by our
102   algorithm for Problem 1 of the general weighted case is often quite close to the optimal value of the
103   convex programming relaxation, i.e., a lower bound on the optimal value of the problem, meaning
104   that the algorithm tends to obtain a near-optimal solution.

112   2    R ELATED WORK
113
114   In this section, we review related literature about special cases and generalizations of M IN D ISAGREE
115   and multilayer-network analysis.
116
117   Special cases of M IN D ISAGREE. For M IN D ISAGREE of the unweighted case, Bansal et al. (2002;
118
      2004) gave the first constant-factor approximation algorithm with the approximation ratio of 17,429.
      Then the approximation ratio has been improved by a series of works. Charikar et al. (2005) designed
119
      a 4-approximation algorithm. Ailon et al. (2008) then gave K WIK C LUSTER, a purely-combinatorial
120
      randomized 3-approximation algorithm. The authors also proved that a variant based on an LP relax-
121   ation improves the approximation ratio from 3 to 2.5. Later Chawla et al. (2015) demonstrated that a
122   more sophisticated randomized construction of the clusters achieves a 2.06-approximation (Chawla
123   et al., 2015), which almost matches the integrality gap 2 of the LP relaxation (Charikar et al., 2005). In
124   a recent breakthrough, Cohen-Addad et al. (2022) designed a (1.994+ )-approximation algorithm for
125   any   > 0, using a semidefinite programming relaxation of the problem, which was further improved
126   to 1.73 +   by introducing a novel preprocessing algorithm (Cohen-Addad et al., 2023). Very recently,
127   Cao et al. (2024) designed a (1.437 +  )-approximation algorithm that runs in O(npoly(1/ ) ) time, by
128   inventing a stronger LP called the cluster LP.
129   For M IN D ISAGREE with the probability constraint, Bansal et al. (2002; 2004) provided an
130   approximation-preserving reduction from the problem to M IN D ISAGREE of the unweighted case.
131   Specifically, the authors proved that any őĪ-approximation algorithm for M IN D ISAGREE of the
132   unweighted case yields a (2őĪ + 1)-approximation algorithm for M IN D ISAGREE with the probabil-
133   ity constraint. Ailon et al. (2008) demonstrated that the counterparts of K WIK C LUSTER and that
134   combined with the pseudometric computed by the LP relaxation achieve a 5-approximation and a
135   2.5-approximation, respectively, both of which improved the 9-approximation based on the above
136
      reduction with the 4-approximation algorithm for M IN D ISAGREE of the unweighted case by Charikar
      et al. (2005). In particular, the approximation ratio of 2.5 is still known to be the state-of-the-art. It is
137
      also known that in the case where the weights of ‚Äė‚ąí‚Äô labels satisfy the triangle inequality constraint
138
      additionally, the approximation ratio can be improved. Indeed, Ailon et al. (2008) proved that their
139   above algorithms achieve a 2-approximation, and later Chawla et al. (2015) improved it to 1.5.
140
141   Gionis et al. (2007) studied the problem called Clustering Aggregation, which is highly related to
142
      M IN D ISAGREE. In the problem, we are given L clusterings of the common set V , and the goal
      is to find a clustering of V that is consistent with the given clusterings as much as possible. The
143
      (in)consistency is measured by the sum of distances between the output clustering and the given
144
      L clusterings, where the distance is defined as the number of pairs of elements that are clustered
145   in the opposite way. Gionis et al. (2007) proved that Clustering Aggregation is a special case of
146   M IN D ISAGREE with the probability constraint and the triangle inequality constraint. We can also
147   directly see that Clustering Aggregation is a quite special case of Multilayer Correlation Clustering
148   of the unweighted case, where each layer already represents a clustering and the parameter p of
149   the `p -norm is set to 1. Gionis et al. (2007) also demonstrated that picking up the best clustering
150   among the given L clusterings gives a 2(1 ‚ąí 1/L)-approximation while an algorithm similar to
151   the 4-approximation algorithm for M IN D ISAGREE of the unweighted case (Charikar et al., 2005)
152   achieves a 3-approximation.
153
      Generalizations of M IN D ISAGREE. The most related generalization would be Multi-Chromatic
154
      Correlation Clustering (MCCC), introduced by Bonchi et al. (2015), as a further generalization of
155
      Chromatic Correlation Clustering (CCC) (Bonchi et al., 2012). Let V be a set of n elements and C a
156   set of colors. Each pair of elements in V is associated with a subset of C, meaning that the endpoints
157   are similar in the sense of those colors. The goal is to find a clustering of V and an assignment of each
158   cluster to a subset of C that is consistent as much as possible with the given similarity information.
159   The (in)consistency of a clustering is evaluated as follows: For each pair within a cluster, a distance
160   between the color subsets of the pair and the cluster is charged, while for each pair across clusters, a
161   distance between the color subset of the pair and the emptyset is charged. Varying the definition of the
      distance, a number of concrete models can be obtained. Although the input of MCCC is essentially

      the same as that of our problem of the unweighted case, ours has three concrete advantages: (i) our
163   objective function is more intuitive but can deal with complex relations among the (in)consistency
164   over all layers; (ii) MCCC asks to specify the colors (i.e., layers in our case) of each cluster for which
165   the cluster is supposed to be valid, but our problem does not require such an effort; (iii) our problem
166   is capable of the general weighted case, while MCCC is defined only for the unweighted case and
167   the way to generalize it to the weighted case is not trivial. For MCCC, Bonchi et al. (2015) gave an
168   approximation ratio proportional to the product of |C| and the maximum degree (when interpreting
169   the input as a graph). Recently, Klodt et al. (2021) introduced a different yet similar generalization of
170   CCC to the multi-chromatic case and devised a 3-approximation algorithm based on K WIK C LUSTER.
171   Multilayer Correlation Clustering can be seen as Correlation Clustering with fairness considera-
172   tion (Ahmadi et al., 2019; 2020; Ahmadian et al., 2020; Ahmadian & Negahbani, 2023; Charikar
173   et al., 2017; Davies et al., 2023; Friggstad & Mousavi, 2021; Heidrich et al., 2024; Kalhan et al.,
174   2019; Puleo & Milenkovic, 2016; 2018; Schwartz & Zats, 2022) and uncertainty consideration (Chen
175   et al., 2014; Joachims & Hopcroft, 2005; Kuroki et al., 2024; Makarychev et al., 2015; Mathieu &
176   Schudy, 2010). For details, see Appendix A.1.
177
      Multilayer-network analysis. Correlation Clustering can be seen as a network clustering model.
178   A multilayer network is a generalization of the ordinary network, where we have a number of
179   edge sets (i.e., layers) over the common set of vertices. Multilayer Correlation Clustering can
180   be viewed as a generalization of Correlation Clustering to multilayer networks. Recently, many
181   network-analysis primitives have been generalized from the ordinary networks to multilayer networks.
182   Examples include community detection (Bazzi et al., 2016; De Bacco et al., 2017; Interdonato
183   et al., 2017; Tagarelli et al., 2017), dense subgraph discovery (Galimberti et al., 2020; Jethava &
184   Beerenwinkel, 2015; Kawase et al., 2023), link prediction (De Bacco et al., 2017; Jalili et al., 2017),
185   analyzing spreading processes (De Domenico et al., 2016; Salehi et al., 2015), and identifying central
186
      vertices (Basaras et al., 2019; De Domenico et al., 2015).

521   We have introduced Multilayer Correlation Clustering, a novel generalization of Correlation Cluster-
522   ing to the multilayer setting, and designed approximation algorithms. As a final remark, we discuss
523
      the limitations of our work, based on which we mention several interesting open problems. In theory,

524

525
      Improve the approximation ratios achieved by our proposed algorithms and/or proving some hardness
526   of approximation for Multilayer Correlation Clustering (beyond that for M IN D ISAGREE). One of the
527   reasonable questions is ‚Äúto what extent can we reduce the term L in the current approximation ratio of
528   O(L log n) of Algorithm 1?‚ÄĚ In practice, our algorithms that solve LPs do not scale to large instances.
529   Therefore, it is also interesting to (further) investigate fast algorithms for Multilayer Correlation
530   Clustering even without approximation ratios. For the detailed descriptions of open problems, see

697   A.1   D ETAILS OF RELATED WORK
698
699   Multilayer Correlation Clustering can be seen as Correlation Clustering with fairness considerations.
700   Indeed, supposing that the similarity information of each layer is given by an agent (e.g., a crowd
701   worker), we see that the problem tries not to abandon any similarity information given by the agents.
      From a fairness perspective, Puleo & Milenkovic (2016; 2018) initiated the study of local objectives

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


702
      for M IN D ISAGREE of the unweighted case. In this model, the disagreements of a clustering are
703   quantified locally rather than globally, at the level of single elements. Specifically, they considered a
704   disagreements vector (with dimension equal to the number of elements), where i-th element represents
705   the disagreements incident to the corresponding element i ‚ąą V . The goal is then to minimize the
706   `p -norm (p ‚Č• 1) of the disagreements vector. If we set p = 1, the problem reduces to M IN D ISAGREE
707   of the unweighted case, whereas if we set p = ‚ąě, the problem aims to minimize the maximal
708   disagreements over the elements. The authors proved that the model with p = ‚ąě is NP-hard and
709   designed a 48-approximation algorithm for any p ‚Č• 1 by extending the 4-approximation algorithm
710   for M IN D ISAGREE of the unweighted case, designed by Charikar et al. (2005). Charikar et al.
711
      (2017) then improved the approximation ratio to 7 by inventing a different rounding algorithm. The
      contribution of Charikar et al. (2017) is not limited to the unweighted case; they  ‚ąö also studied the
712
      above model with p = ‚ąě of the general weighted case and designed an O( n)-approximation
713
      algorithm. Later Kalhan et al. (2019) improved the above approximation ratio of 7 to 5, and

      designed an O(n 2 ‚ąí 2p log 2 + 2p n)-approximation algorithm for any p ‚Č• 1 of the general weighted

      case, matching the current-best approximation ratio of O(log n) for M IN D ISAGREE of the general
716
      weighted case (i.e., the above model with p = 1) (Charikar et al., 2005; Demaine et al., 2006). Davies
717   et al. (2023) gave a purely-combinatorial O(nŌČ )-time 40-approximation algorithm for p = ‚ąě of
718   the unweighted case, where ŌČ is the exponent of matrix multiplication, while Heidrich et al. (2024)
719   improved the above approximation ratio of 5 by Kalhan et al. (2019) to 4 for p = ‚ąě. Very recently,
720   Davies et al. (2024) designed a combinatorial algorithm running in O(nŌČ ) time and outputting a
721   clustering that is a constant-factor approximate solution for all `p -norms simultaneously. Ahmadi
722   et al. (2019) studied the cluster-wise counterpart of the above model with p = ‚ąě (of the general
723   weighted case), where the goal is to find a clustering of V that minimizes the maximal disagreements
724   over the clusters. The authors presented an O(log n)-approximation algorithm together with an

      improved these approximation ratios to 2 +   for any   > 0.
726
727   Another type of fairness has been considered for Correlation Clustering. Ahmadian et al. (2020)
728   initiated the study of Fair Correlation Clustering (of the unweighted case), where each element is
729   associated with a color, and each cluster of the output is required to be not over-represented by any
730   color, meaning that the fraction of elements with any single color has to be upper bounded by a
731
      specified value. For the model, the authors designed a 256-approximation algorithm, based on the
      notion called fairlet decomposition. Ahmadi et al. (2020) independently studied a similar model of
732
      Fair Correlation Clustering, where the distribution of colors in each cluster has to be the same as
733
      that of the entire set. In particular, for the case of two colors that have the same number of elements
734   in the entire set, the authors proposed a (3őĪ + 4)-approximation algorithm, where őĪ is any known
735   approximation ratio for M IN D ISAGREE of the unweighted case. Friggstad & Mousavi (2021) then
736   gave an approximation ratio of 6.18, which cannot be achieved by the above 3őĪ + 4. The authors also
737   studied the model with the aforementioned local objective for p = ‚ąě and designed a constant-factor
738   approximation algorithm. Schwartz & Zats (2022) proved that the model of Ahmadi et al. (2020) of
739   the general weighted case has no finite approximation ratio, unless P = NP. Very recently, Ahmadian
740   & Negahbani (2023) substantially generalized the above models and designed an approximation
741   algorithm that has constant-factor approximation ratios for some useful special cases.
742   Multilayer Correlation Clustering can also be seen as Correlation Clustering with the uncertainty of
743   input by interpreting each layer as a possible scenario of the similarity information of the elements.
744   Most works on Correlation Clustering with uncertainty assume the existence of the ground-truth
745   clustering of V and aim to recover it, based only on its noisy observations. In the seminal paper by
746   Bansal et al. (2004), this type of problem had already been considered, while Joachims & Hopcroft
747   (2005) gave the first formal analysis of the problem. Later, a variety of problem settings have been
748
      introduced in a series of works (Chen et al., 2014; Makarychev et al., 2015; Mathieu & Schudy, 2010;
      Silwal et al., 2023). Very recently, Kuroki et al. (2024) considered another type of problem, which
749
      aims to perform as few queries as possible to an oracle that returns a noisy sample of the similarity
750
      between two elements in V , to obtain a clustering of V that minimizes the disagreements. Specifically,
751   they introduced two novel online-learning problems rooted in the paradigm of combinatorial multi-
752   armed bandits, and designed algorithms that combine K WIK C LUSTER with adaptive sampling
753   strategies.
