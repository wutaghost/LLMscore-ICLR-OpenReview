009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Time series forecasting plays a crucial role in data mining, driving rapid advance-
012            ments across numerous industries. With the emergence of large models, time series
013            foundation models (TSFMs) have exhibited remarkable generalization capabilities,
014            such as zero-shot learning, through large-scale pre-training. Meanwhile, Retrieval-
015            Augmented Generation (RAG) methods have been widely employed to enhance the
016            performance of foundation models on unseen data, allowing models to access to
017            external knowledge. In this paper, we introduce TimeRAF, a Retrieval-Augmented
018
               Forecasting model that enhance zero-shot time series forecasting through retrieval-
               augmented techniques. We develop customized time series knowledge bases
019
               that are tailored to the specific forecasting tasks. TimeRAF employs an end-to-
020
               end learnable retriever to extract valuable information from the knowledge base.
021            Additionally, we propose Channel Prompting for knowledge integration, which
022            effectively extracts relevant information from the retrieved knowledge along the
023            channel dimension. Extensive experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of our
024            model, showing significant improvement across various domains and datasets.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Time series (TS) forecasting has gained significant popularity in recent years due to its vital role
030   in various domains, including finance (Yu et al., 2023), healthcare (Li et al., 2024), weather (Wu
031   et al., 2023b), and traffic (Jin et al., 2021). The popular approach in the past typically learns from
032   single-domain, small-scale datasets (Nie et al., 2023; Zeng et al., 2023), which inherently constrains
033
      their generalization capabilities. However, the landscape of time series analysis is evolving rapidly
      with the advent of large models. Time series foundation models (TSFMs), trained on large-scale,
034
      multi-domain datasets, have demonstrated zero-shot learning abilities, revolutionizing various time
035
      series domains and diverse applications (Liang et al., 2024; Woo et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024).
036
037   Meanwhile, Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) is an increasingly prevalent technique that en-
038   hances the capabilities of foundation models in various domains, including text generation (Karpukhin
039
      et al., 2020) and image generation (Chen et al., 2023). This approach allows models to access external
      knowledge through various information retrieval techniques, enabling them to gather supplementary
040
      information during the generation process. Typically, the retrieval knowledge can be sourced from
041
      external datasets in the same format with the training corpus. For instance, in dialogue systems,
042   RAG can help generate more contextually relevant responses by retrieving previous dialogues or
043   similar interactions from a database (Huang et al., 2023). However, similar studies have garnered
044   little attention in the time series domain. A natural question arises: Can the integration of time series
045   foundation models with retrieval-augmented methods also improve performance, particularly in
046   challenging scenarios that require strong generalization abilities, such as zero-shot forecasting?
047
      As an intuitive example, a pre-trained model trained on a general time series dataset may struggle
048   when forecasting for specific domains such as weather patterns in a particular region, which is
049   illustrated in the left plot of Figure 1. However, by accessing domain-specific external knowledge
050   bases, the model could dynamically retrieve relevant information—such as time series data from
051   similar weather conditions—without requiring extensive parameter updates. This allows the model
052   to integrate domain-specific prior knowledge, improving its zero-shot forecasting capability. In this
053   manner, retrieved external data provides valuable context and serves as an additional source of prior
      information, enabling more accurate predictions. These advancements motivate our exploration of

070   Retrieval-Augmentation for time series Forecasting (RAF). However, designing an effective RAF
071   framework for time series forecasting involves several key challenges: (1) What types of data can
072
      serve as knowledge bases to support time series models? (2) How can relevant knowledge be
      retrieved when encountering inputs from diverse domains? (3) How can retrieved knowledge be
073
      effectively integrated to improve model performance?
074
075   To address these challenges, we introduce TimeRAF, a novel framework designed to leverage retrieval-
076   augmented generation techniques for time series foundation models. As shown in the right of Figure 1,
077   by retrieving and integrating external time series data, we aim to overcome the limitations of existing
078
      TSFMs and enhance zero-shot time series forecasting performance. TimeRAF consists of a retriever
      that scores and selects relevant time series data from an external knowledge base. The knowledge
079
      base can either be a comprehensive database composed of multiple datasets across various domains
080
      or a domain-specific database comprising a singular dataset relevant to test data. Furthermore, an
081   end-to-end learnable retrieval methodology is introduced to ensure that the retrieved data delivers
082   enhancement. To leverage retrieved time series, we introduce an effective approach, named Channel
083   Prompting, to integrate the knowledge from retrieved data. Our extensive experiments on various
084   datasets demonstrate that TimeRAF significantly achieves a substantial improvement over TSFM and
085   outperforms several existing zero-shot time series forecasting methods.
086
      Overall, our contributions can be summarized as follows:
087
088          • We propose TimeRAF, a novel framework that leverages retrieval augmentation techniques
089            to enhance zero-shot time series forecasting. By retrieving relevant data from an external
090            knowledge base and effectively integrating the retrieved information, TimeRAF supplements
091            the pre-trained knowledge of foundation models, enhancing their forecasting capabilities.
092          • We employ a learnable retriever to calculate retrieval scores for time series within the
093            knowledge base and select the best options. To integrate retrieved knowledge, we introduce
094            Channel Prompting to extract valuable information from the retrieved data effectively.
095          • Our TimeRAF demonstrates significant improvement through the incorporation of RAF
096            into TSFM and even outperforms several full-shot methods. Furthermore, we present
097            comprehensive ablation studies and visualizations to evaluate the efficacy of our approach.

      2   R ELATED W ORK
100
101
      Foundation Models for Zero-shot Time Series Forecasting: Recent years have witnessed the rise of
102   TSFMs. TimeGPT-1 (Garza & Mergenthaler-Canseco, 2023) is the first closed-source model offering
103   zero-shot forecasting capabilities. ForecastPFN (Dooley et al., 2024), pre-trained on synthetic time
104   series data, serves as a zero-shot forecaster, but excels primarily in data- or time-limited settings.
105   Lag-llama (Rasul et al., 2023) leveraged the LLaMA architecture (Touvron et al., 2023) with lagged
106   time series features for time series forecasting. TimesFM (Das et al., 2024)is a patch-based, decoder-
107   only foundational model designed for time series forecasting, which employs a larger output patch
      size to enhance decoding efficiency. The model is pre-trained on a comprehensive dataset sourced


                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      from Google Trends and Wikipedia pageviews, in combination with open data. MOIRAI (Woo et al.,
109   2024) introduces LOTSA, a large-scale collection of open time series datasets, and utilizes it to
110   train a foundation model based on a masked encoder architecture. MOIRAI achieves competitive or
111   superior performance as a zero-shot forecaster when compared to full-shot models. Tiny Time Mixers
112   (TTMs) (Ekambaram et al., 2024) leverages a lightweight mixer-style architecture and demonstrated
113   remarkable zero-shot forecasting performance. Since TSFMs have shown potential in zero-shot time
114   series forecasting, our approach aims to enha nce their generalization capabilities by applying RAG
115   techniques to leverage external knowledge.
116   Retrieval Augmented Generation for Foundation Models: Foundation Models like LLMs have
117   achieved remarkable success, though they still face limitations in domain-specific or knowledge-
118   intensive tasks. To address these challenges, various RAG methods have been proposed: DocPrompt-
119   ing (Zhou et al., 2022) curated a retrieval annotation dataset to train a retriever for augmenting input
120   in code generation. DPR (Karpukhin et al., 2020) develops a dense embedding model for indexing
121   passages in a low-dimensional, continuous space. RePlug (Shi et al., 2023) refined the retriever
122   by distilling the knowledge from the language model’s probability. LAPDOG (Huang et al., 2023)
123
      introduces an end-to-end dense retriever framework specifically for personalized dialogue generation,
      emphasizing objective optimization. Beyond NLP tasks, RAG has also been applied to other do-
124
      mains: REACT (Liu et al., 2023) freezes the original model and updates only the additional trainable
125
      weights on the retrieved knowledge, significantly enhancing visual model’s zero-shot performance.
126   Re-Imagen (Chen et al., 2023) uses retrieved information to produce high-fidelity and faithful images,
127   even for rare or unseen entities. Additionally, in time series analysis, RATSF (Wang & Cui, 2024)
128   develops a cross-attention module to integrate additional data for better prediction. But its retrieval
129   process is constrained to historical data. ReTime (Jing et al., 2022) retrieves relational references to
130   improve forecasting and imputation for incomplete time series. However, existing methods cannot
131   adapt to zero-shot tasks. In contrast, our work is specifically designed to address this gap. We use
132   extensive public time series data to build a knowledge base and enhance zero-shot prediction in
133   TSFMs with an effective RAF method.

138
139   An illustration of our TimeRAF framework is provided in Figure 2. Firstly, a retriever is utilized for
140   learning to retrieve relevant data from the external knowledge base (refer to section 3.5). Following
141   this, the proposed Channel Prompting approach is employed for the integration of retrieved knowledge.
142   Therefore, the entire forecaster F is capable of harnessing external knowledge, thereby facilitating
143
      knowledge enhanced forecasting (refer to section 3.4). During training, the backbone of TSFM
      remains frozen. Details of training and inference process are provided in section 3.5.2 and section 3.6.
144
      Besides, the knowledge bases utilized for training and inference are detailed in section 3.3.

      3.2   P ROBLEM F ORMULATION
148
      Following previous work (Nie et al., 2023), we employ the channel inpendent strategy. Let X ∈ Rsl×c
149   be a multivariate time series of length sl and number of channels c. The input can be denoted as
150   x ∈ Rsl×1 , and the forecasting task can be formally defined as predicting the future values ŷ ∈ Rf l×1
151   given the history/lookback window x. Upon completing the analysis of all data channels, the final
152   comprehensive prediction result Ŷ ∈ Rf l×c is derived. Here, f l denotes the forecast length/horizon.
153   The ground truth is denoted as Y ∈ Rf l×c . In the zero-shot forecasting setting, the model generates
154   predictions for future values based on datasets that have not been encountered during training. Given
155   a set of retrieved time series data C from the external knowledge base (details will be elaborated in
156   Section 3.5), we aim to leverage the valuable information within them to enhance the forecasting
157   capability of the forecaster F. The entire process can be formulated as Ŷ = F(X, C).

232   3.5     K NOWLEDGE R ETRIEVAL
233
234   Inspired by DPR (Karpukhin et al., 2020), we employ a dual-encoder retriever to efficiently obtain
235   relevant information from the external knowledge base.
236
237   3.5.1    K NOWLEDGE R ETRIEVAL L EARNING
238
      The retriever adopts a MLP-based encoder to respectively embed the query and the candidates. In
239
      TimeRAF, we utilize the input directly as the query. Then, the retriever calculates the dot product
240
      similarity score between the query and each candidate using their respective embeddings. Finally, the
241   candidates with the k highest similarity scores are retrieved, denoted as C = {c1 , c2 , . . . , ck }.
242
243   Intuitively, by augmenting the model with retrieved knowledge, the goal is to improve predictions
244
      based on desired metrics, such as Mean Squared Error (MSE). However, it is challenging to guarantee
      that retrieved candidates with higher similarity scores will consistently provide more useful knowledge
245
      for forecasting. To address this, we employ the foundation model F as an evaluator, leveraging its
246
      strong forecasting capability to provide feedback and guide the selection of knowledge.
247
248   Specifically, using the retrieved candidate ci , we employ the foundation model F to obtain the the
249   metric values of prediction ŷ = F(x, ci ). If F finds that integrating the knowledge from ci is
250
      beneficial for forecasting, we encourage the retriever to rank the score of ci to be higher. In this
      way, the model can automatically decide the usefulness of the candidates and learn to retrieve more
251
      helpful candidates from the knowledge base. To implement this learning strategy, we first transform
252
      the metric values into a probability distribution as:

      5         C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE WORK
526
527
      In this paper, we introduce TimeRAF, a novel framework designed to leverage retrieval-augmented
528   generation for zero-shot time series forecasting. We develop customized time series knowledge bases
529   that are tailored to the specific forecasting tasks and employ an end-to-end learnable retriever to
530   extract valuable information from the knowledge base. We also introduce Channel Prompting to
531   extract relevant information from the retrieved data for knowledge integration. By leveraging external
532   knowledge, TimeRAF exhibits a notable enhancement in zero-shot time series forecasting.
533
      While TimeRAF achieves phenomenal performance, this represents merely the initial step in the
534   integration of time series methods and RAG. Due to resource constraints, the knowledge base is
535   established based on original time series data data without the implementation of advanced techniques
536   like trend-seasonal decomposition. In terms of architecture, our approach to integrate external
537   knowledge is somewhat heuristic and future work should design a more flexible and elegant approach.
538   Also, the current architecture has ignored the potential interdependencies among different channels,
539   which could be addressed more effectively in future methods. Finally, incorporating multi-modality
      such as tabular or text data is an exciting new direction to provide supplementary knowledge.

      A.1    L IST OF T RAINING DATASETS
813
814   Our fine-tuning employs a subset of about 320 million time points from LOTSA (Woo et al., 2024)
815   and UTSD (Liu et al., 2024). To enhance data integrity, missing values are systematically addressed
816   using linear interpolation techniques. For each univariate, multivariate, or irregular-sampled time
817   series, we store them with timestamps, domains, sampling frequencies and other meta-information in
818   one directory using ARROW format. One dataset may composed of multiple related time series.
819   All datasets can be classified into six distinct domains by their source: Energy, Nature, Transport,
820   Web, Sales, and Healthcare. The datasets exhibit diverse sampling frequencies, ranging from macro
821   intervals such as daily to more fine-grained intervals like hourly and minutely. Notably, several
822   datasets can demonstrate exceptionally high-frequency sampling rates, such as the MotorImagery
823   dataset, which operates at a millisecond frequency.
824
825   A.2    L IST OF I NFERENCE DATASETS
826
827   In the field of time series forecasting, several classical datasets such as ETT (Zhou et al., 2021),
828   ECL (Wu et al., 2021) and Weather (Wu et al., 2021) have become widely recognized benchmarks
829   for evaluating model performance. We also utilize these datasets to evaluate the zero-shot forecasting
830
      performance and perform evaluation in a sliding window fashion following previous work (Nie et al.,
      2023; Ekambaram et al., 2024). Below, we offer a brief overview of these datasets.
831
832
            1. ETT datasets: The four ETT datasets (ETTH1, ETTH2, ETTM1, ETTM2) contain multi-
833
               variate time series data collected from electrical transformers at two stations. ETTH1 and
834            ETTH2 are collected at an hourly interval, while ETTM1 and ETTM2 are collected every
835            15 minutes. All four datasets have 7 channels.
836
            2. Weather: The weather dataset consists of 21 channels, which serve as weather indicators. It
837
               is collected at 10-minute intervals at the Max Planck Institute of Biogeochemistry weather
838
               station.
839
840         2. Electricity (ECL): The Electricity dataset, also known as the ECL dataset, comprises the
841
               hourly electricity consumption data of 321 clients.

845   B.1   K NOWLEDGE BASE FOR T RAINING
846
847   The knowledge base used for training contains approximately 3 million data points, as introduced
848   in section 3.3, selected from the training datasets. The key statistics of datasets in knowledge base
849   is provided in Table 1. The selection process entailed a meticulous curation to ensure a diverse
850   representation of data across various domains. This diversity enhances the robustness of the model,
851
      enabling it to generalize better across different contexts. Each data point has been sourced from
      reputable datasets, ensuring high-quality input that informs the training process. Besides, all the time
852
      series data in the knowledge base has the same length as input, which is 512.

      B.2   T RAINING D ETAILS
856
      Our training is performed on 4 NVIDIA A100 GPUs. Following the backbone configuration of the
857
      foundation model we use (TTMB (Ekambaram et al., 2024)), the input length sl = 512 and f l = 96.
858   Both encoders in the retriever employ a two-layer MLP, with the size of the hidden layer configured
859   to be four times the input dimension. The tanh activation function is utilized in both layers. The MLP
860   in the Channel Prompting also use tanh activation function and has 4 layers. During the training
861   phase, all parameters of the time series foundation model remain fixed, with only the retriever and
862   channel prompting module undergoing training. The learning rate for the retriever is established at
863   0.001, whereas the learning rate for the channel prompting is set at 0.00001. The weight λ is set to 1.
      The entire model was trained for 2 epochs, and for different test datasets, we reported the best results.

887   B.3    BASELINES
888
889   We conduct zero-shot forecasting experiments on seven datasets from iTransformer (Liu et al.). We
890
      apply the same data-split strategy as Autoformer (Wu et al., 2021) and calculate the averaged MSE
      of all predict-96 windows in the test split. We evaluate five open-source time series foundation
891
      model, including Timer (Liu et al., 2024), Moirai (Woo et al., 2024), TimesFM (Das et al., 2024),
892
      Chronos (Ansari et al., 2024), and MOMENT (Goswami et al., 2024). However, closed-source
893   models such as TimeGPT (Garza & Mergenthaler-Canseco, 2023) are not included due to their
894   inaccessibility.
895
896           • MOMENT: MOMENT1 employs a masking modeling approach for zero-shot forecasting
897             by concatenating the lookback series with a mask corresponding to the prediction length.
898             The output of the model, derived from the mask, serves as the forecast. This method involves
899             pre-training a Transformer encoder model in a univariate manner using a curated dataset
900             known as the “Time Series Pile,” which encompasses a diverse range of time series data.
901           • Chronos: Chronos2 is a probabilistic forecaster. ChronosS1 refers to sampling a single
902             prediction trajectory, while ChronosS20 involves averaging 20 sampled trajectories. Chronos
903             tokenizes the input time series and processes these tokens using a large language model,
904
                specifically the T5 model. It is trained on an extensive corpus of time series data, including
                synthetic data, to enhance generalization.
905
906           • TimesFM: TimesFM employs a decoder-style attention model, characterized by causal
907             self-attention, which is pre-trained in a univariate manner on an extensive array of both real-
908
                world and synthetic datasets. We utilize the official checkpoint available on HuggingFace3 ,
                which accommodates a variety of input and output lengths.
909
910           • Moirai: The Moirai family4 has three different sizes, labeled as MoiriaS , MoiriaM , and
911             MoiriaL . Moirai pre-trains a Transformer encoder on the extensive “LOTSA” dataset (27B
912
                time points) by masking the forecast horizon of each target channel and performing mask
                reconstruction. By flattening all channels in a multivariate time series, Moirai supports
913
                pre-training in “any-variate” settings.
