009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            CLIP has greatly advanced zero-shot segmentation by leveraging its strong visual-
012            language association and generalization capability. However, directly adapting
013            CLIP for segmentation often yields suboptimal results due to inconsistencies be-
014            tween image and pixel-level prediction objectives. Additionally, merely combin-
015            ing segmentation and CLIP models often leads to disjoint optimization, intro-
016            ducing significant computational overhead and additional parameters. To address
017            these issues, we propose a novel CLIP-to-Seg Distillation approach, incorporating
018            global and local distillation to flexibly transfer CLIP’s powerful zero-shot gener-
019
               alization capability to existing closed-set segmentation models. Global distillation
               leverages CLIP’s CLS token to condense segmentation features and distills high-
020
               level concepts to the segmentation model via image-level prototypes. Local dis-
021
               tillation adapts CLIP’s local semantic transferability to dense prediction tasks us-
022            ing object-level features, aided by pseudo-mask generation for latent unseen class
023            mining. To further generalize the CLIP-distilled segmentation model, we generate
024            latent embeddings for the mined latent classes by coordinating their semantic em-
025            beddings and dense features. Our method equips existing closed-set segmentation
026            models with strong generalization capabilities for open concepts through effec-
027            tive and flexible CLIP-to-Seg distillation. Without relying on the CLIP model
028            or adding extra computational overhead/parameters during inference, our method
029            can be seamlessly integrated into existing segmentation models and achieves state-
030
               of-the-art performance on multiple zero-shot segmentation benchmarks.

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035   In recent years, semantic segmentation has advanced rapidly, benefiting from deep learning tech-
036   nologies (Long et al., 2015; Chen et al., 2018). However, conventional semantic segmentation mod-
037   els are heavily data-dependent, requiring large volumes of annotated images to achieve satisfactory
038
      performance. Collecting these images and annotations is both time-consuming and expensive.
039   To address this challenge, zero-shot semantic segmentation has been proposed and has gained sig-
040   nificant attention (Xian et al., 2019; Gu et al., 2020). In zero-shot semantic segmentation, models
041   are trained on seen classes and must generalize to unseen classes during inference, relying solely on
042   their text descriptions. To accomplish this, existing methods (Ding et al., 2022a; Zhou et al., 2023)
043   typically utilize Vision-Language models with strong zero-shot generalization capabilities, such as
044
      CLIP (Radford et al., 2021), for pixel-level segmentation tasks.
045   To effectively adapt CLIP for segmentation, existing methods can be categorized into two groups:
046   one-stage methods and two-stage methods, as shown at the top of Fig. 1. In one-stage methods
047   (Han et al., 2023a; Zhou et al., 2023), to maintain CLIP’s generalization capability, the adaptation
048   module or trainable prompts are often inserted after the frozen CLIP visual encoder to adapt the
049   dense tokens for segmentation. Two-stage methods (Ding et al., 2022a; Xu et al., 2022) typically
050
      require a pre-trained, class-agnostic object proposer to identify latent objects in an image. These
      object proposals are then fed into the frozen CLIP visual encoder for classification generalization.
051
052   Despite their effectiveness, both approaches exhibit inherent limitations. In one-stage methods,
053   CLIP is primarily optimized for capturing global context through the CLS token, but it lacks the
      spatial information required to capture fine-grained local details necessary for precise segmentation.

075   However, dense prediction tasks prioritize high-quality pixel-level parsing over image-level under-
076   standing, creating a mismatch between task requirements and CLIP’s capabilities, thus limiting the
077   effectiveness of one-stage methods. Two-stage methods primarily suffer from the disjointed opti-
078   mization between mask proposal generation and CLIP’s class recognition. Additionally, two-stage
079   methods are computationally expensive, as they require processing both mask proposal generation
080   and per-proposal classification.
081   To address the limitations of both approaches, we propose a framework that 1) achieves high-quality
082   segmentation without incurring additional computational costs during inference, and 2) simultane-
083   ously maintains strong zero-shot generalization capabilities for open concepts. We begin by revisit-
084   ing closed-set segmentation models, which are highly optimized for capturing local details essential
085   for precise segmentation (Xie et al., 2021; Guo et al., 2022). However, these models tend to overfit to
086   seen classes due to the absence of data for unseen classes, despite their effectiveness at segmenting
087
      objects. Recent methods have attempted to overcome this limitation by employing knowledge dis-
      tillation to transfer CLIP’s zero-shot capabilities to task-specific models for adapting various down-
088
      stream tasks (Huang et al., 2024; Han et al., 2023b). However, these approaches require matching
089
      feature sizes between teacher and student models (see bottom left of Fig. 1), making it difficult to
090   transfer CLIP’s knowledge from a few tokens to the dense features of various segmentation models.
091
092   These limitations motivate us to propose CLIP-to-Seg (C2S) distillation, which integrates global,
093
      local, and latent embedding distillation to transfer CLIP’s vision-language matching capabilities
      to pixel-level segmentation models. Global distillation adaptively aggregates dense features into
094
      image-level prototypes based on their similarity to global CLS tokens, and then performs efficient
095
      prototype-token distillation to transfer CLIP’s zero-shot generalization capabilities to the segmen-
096   tation model. However, this image-level distillation may overlook non-dominant classes and fine-
097   grained object details. To address this, we propose local distillation to adapt CLIP’s local semantic
098   transferability to dense prediction tasks through object-level prototypes. CLIP’s local tokens and
099   the segmentation model’s local prototypes are generated by mining latent unseen classes, aided by
100   pseudo mask generation. To further generalize the CLIP-distilled segmentation model for unseen
101   classes, we generate latent embeddings for the mined latent classes to help the model perceive their
102   real data statistics during training. The latent embedding generation coordinate the semantic em-
103   beddings and dense features of the mined latent classes, distilling suitable prototypes for subsequent
104
      mask prediction and generalization on unseen classes.
105   Unlike existing approaches that adapt the CLIP visual encoder (Zhou et al., 2022; 2023) or ensemble
106   with CLIP during inference (Ding et al., 2022a; Han et al., 2023a), our method can be seamlessly
107   integrated into existing closed-set segmentation models without relying on the CLIP model or intro-
      ducing additional computational overhead/parameters at inference. Our method achieves state-of-

121   2   R ELATED W ORKS
122
123   Close-set Semantic Segmentation. Close-set segmentation assumes fully annotated images and
124   focuses on the performance of predefined categories within a specific dataset. Existing methods
125   are typically divided into pixel-level classification and mask-level classification. In pixel-level clas-
126   sification, FCN (Long et al., 2015), the first fully convolutional network for end-to-end semantic
127   segmentation, established the paradigm for pixel-level methods. Since FCN, many works, e.g.,
128   DeepLab series (Chen et al., 2018; 2017), Deformable convolution (Dai et al., 2017), aim to enlarge
129   the receptive field to further improve the performance of pixel-level methods. With the introduction
130
      of self-attention (Vaswani et al., 2017) and ViT (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020), many approaches (Zheng
      et al., 2021; Xie et al., 2021; Guo et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2021) replaced the conventional convo-
131
      lutional backbone with self-attention-based models, achieving remarkable performance. An alter-
132
      native approach treats semantic segmentation as a mask classification task. MaskFormer (Cheng
133   et al., 2021a) and Mask2Former (Cheng et al., 2022) are two notable examples of this approach.
134   Specifically, these models first generate object queries corresponding to potential objects. These
135   object queries are then decoupled to perform classification and mask prediction tasks separately.
136   Our method is applied to the more challenging task of zero-shot segmentation, which requires fewer
137   annotations than close-set segmentation.
138
      Knowledge Distillation. Knowledge distillation aims to transfer the capability of a larger teacher
139   model to a student model for comparable performance to the teacher model with a smaller model size
140   (Wang & Yoon, 2021). Existing methods are categorized into logits-based (Hinton, 2015; Yang et al.,
141   2023; Touvron et al., 2021), feature-based (Huang et al., 2024; Tian et al., 2019; Quan et al., 2023),
142   and relation-based approaches (He et al., 2023; Han et al., 2023b). With the rapid development of
143   vision-language models (Radford et al., 2021; Jia et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2023), certain methods
144   aim to distill vision-language matching capabilities into other models (Huang et al., 2024; Quan
145   et al., 2023; Pei et al., 2023). However, these methods distill knowledge between classification
146   models, transferring it from one global context to another. Our method distills the knowledge from
147
      a classification model to a segmentation model where the knowledge is transferred from a global
      context to a local context across different feature sizes.
148
149   Zero-shot Semantic Segmentation. Since close-set segmentation requires pixel-level annotations,
150   research focusing on reducing label dependency has gained significant attention. Before the VLMs,
151   e.g., CLIP (Radford et al., 2021), several works tried to bridge the gap between vision and language
152   by projecting the features from vision models to the semantic space which is spanned by the large
153
      scale of texts (Gu et al., 2020; Xian et al., 2019). The emergence of large-scale VLMs, such as
      CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) and ALIGN (Jia et al., 2021), has revolutionized zero-shot tasks. Due
154
      to their impressive zero-shot ability, researchers aim to transfer this ability to downstream tasks.
155
      Leveraging visual prompt tuning (Jia et al., 2022) and adapters (Houlsby et al., 2019), existing
156   methods are categorized into one-stage and two-stage approaches. One-stage methods introduce
157   trainable parameters or modules to adapt VLMs for semantic segmentation (Xu et al., 2023b; Li
158   et al., 2022; Ghiasi et al., 2022; Zhou et al., 2023; 2022; Guo et al., 2023; Ding et al., 2022a;b; Qin
159   et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2024). Two-stage methods train a mask-proposer (Cheng
160   et al., 2022; 2021a) to find the potential objects in an image and utilize the proposed objects to
161   finetune the VLMs or directly classify the objects (Xu et al., 2022; Shin et al., 2023; Zhou et al.,
      2022; Jiao et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2023a).

199   Task Definition. Before presenting our method, we first define the task of Zero-shot Semantic Seg-
200                                                  M
      mentation (ZSS). Formally, let D = Xi , Yi i=1 represent a dataset, where X are the input images,

201
      Y are the corresponding pixel-level annotations, and A ∈ RN ×D is a set of semantic embeddings
202
      for all categories, with N representing the total number of classes and D the dimensionality of the
203
      embeddings. The semantic embeddings A are partitioned into two disjoint subsets: seen class em-
204   beddings As ∈ RNs ×D and unseen class embeddings Au ∈ RNu ×D , where As ∩ Au = ∅ and
205   Ns + Nu = N . Since seen and unseen classes frequently co-occur in images, removing those con-
206   taining unseen categories is impractical for training. Therefore, in ZSS, only the annotations for
207   unseen classes are removed. ZSS can be categorized into two settings based on the availability of
208   unseen class embeddings Au : Inductive ZSS, where unseen class embeddings are unavailable during
209   training, and Transductive ZSS, where unseen class embeddings are accessible. In both settings,
210   model performance is jointly evaluated on both seen and unseen categories during inference. In
211   this work, we adopt the inductive ZSS setting, which is more challenging and closer to real-world
212
      applications.

222   3.1   T OKEN G ENERATION AND M ASK G ENERATION
223
224   The core idea of CLIP-to-Seg (C2S) distillation is to transfer CLIP’s powerful vision-language align-
225   ment capabilities to a segmentation model regardless of the size differences between the CLIP and
226   the segmentation model. To achieve this, we first generate CLS tokens, both global and local, which
227
      act as the teacher features during the distillation process, as shown in the top left of Fig. 3.
228   Given an input image XH×W ×3 , we first pass it through the CLIP visual encoder to obtain the global
229   CLS token Cg . However, because CLIP inherently focuses on the global context, it may overlook
230   less prominent classes within the image. To address this limitation and capture the semantics of
231   all classes within an image, we additionally extract local CLS tokens. Specifically, for an image
232   X with its corresponding pixel-level annotation Y, we assume that annotations are available for all
233   classes, including unseen ones. We first separate Y into non-overlapping class-specific masks based
234   on unique categories, where Y = {Yi } i = 0O , with Yi representing the binary mask for the ith
235
      class, and O representing the number of unique classes in the image. Using these masks, we pool
      the original image X into class-specific sub-images. Each class-specific sub-image is then passed
236
      through the CLIP visual encoder to extract the corresponding local CLS tokens Cl .
237
238   In practice, annotations for unseen classes are inaccessible, resulting in large unannotated areas
239   within an image. We refer to the classes in these areas as latent classes, as they may either belong to
240   unseen categories or are simply unannotated in the dataset. To further leverage the dense features of
241
      these latent classes, we propose a latent class mining algorithm that clusters the dense visual tokens
      from the CLIP visual encoder. Specifically, we first initialize seeds S by applying sliding windows
242
      of various sizes to average the dense tokens:

318
319   Although CLIP’s vision-language matching capabilities are effectively transferred to segmentation
320   models, the inaccessibility of unseen semantic embeddings leaves large portions of dense features
321   without pixel-level supervision, resulting in suboptimal optimization of the segmentation model.
322   To address this, we propose Latent Embedding Generation, which generates synthetic semantic
323   embeddings for latent classes by calibrating the local feature prototypes with their corresponding
      local CLS tokens, as shown in the bottom left of Fig. 3.

      Given Yp , we first select the binary masks Yu corresponding to the latent classes. Next, we use
325   Eq. 3 to replace Yp with Yu to generate visual prototypes for the latent classes. We then feed Fu
326   into a transformer decoder as Query and input the global and local CLS tokens as Key and Value
327   to generate the latent prototypes A′u . The latent prototypes A′u are treated equivalently to seen
328   semantic embeddings and are used to distinguish between the seen and latent classes. Formally,
329   the class scores for seen and latent categories are Xs = α · F⊤ As and Xu = β · cos(F, Au′ ),
330   where α and β are hyperparameters that control the scale of unseen categories. Note that, since
331   the pseudo labels and potential prototypes are not entirely precise, cosine similarity helps prevent
332   overemphasis on misclassification and aids in distinguishing between seen and potential categories.
333   We then concatenate the logits for both seen and unseen classes as Xlogits = cat(Xs, Xu ), where

      ‘cat’ denotes concatenation along the class dimension. Finally, Yp is used to provide pixel-level
      supervision to the dense features through:

530
531
      In this paper, we propose the CLIP-to-Seg Distillation framework to overcome the limitations of
532
      directly adapting CLIP for segmentation tasks. Our approach integrates both global and local distil-
533
      lation strategies to transfer CLIP’s zero-shot generalization capabilities to closed-set segmentation
534   models. By aligning feature prototypes from segmentation models with CLS tokens from CLIP at
535   both global and local levels, we facilitate effective distillation from CLIP to pixel-level segmentation
536   models. Additionally, introducing synthesized embeddings for latent classes enhances the model’s
537   ability to generalize to new concepts. Without adding extra parameters or computational overhead,
538   our method achieves state-of-the-art performance on zero-shot segmentation benchmarks, offering
539   a flexible and efficient solution to extend the generalization capabilities of existing segmentation
      models.
