009                                           Abstract
010
011           A prevalent assumption regarding real-world data is that it lies on or close
012           to a low-dimensional manifold. When deploying a neural network on data
013           manifolds, the required size, i.e., the number of neurons of the network,
014           heavily depends on the intricacy of the underlying latent manifold. While
015           significant advancements have been made in understanding the geometric
016
              attributes of manifolds, it’s essential to recognize that topology, too, is
              a fundamental characteristic of manifolds. In this study, we investigate
017
              network expressive power in terms of the latent data manifold. Integrating
018
              both topological and geometric facets of the data manifold, we present a
019           size upper bound of ReLU neural networks.
020

022   1   Introduction
023
024   The expressive power of deep neural networks (DNNs) is believed to play a critical role in
025   their astonishing performance. Despite a rapidly expanding literature, the theoretical under-
026   standing of such expressive power remains limited. The well-known universal approximation
027
      theorems (Hornik, 1989; Cybenko, 1989; Leshno et al., 1993; Hanin, 2017) guarantee that
      neural networks can approximate vast families of functions with an arbitrarily high accuracy.
028
      However, the theoretical upper bound of the size of such networks is rather pessimistic; it is
029
      exponential to the input space dimension. Indeed, these bounds tend to be loose, because
030   the analyses are often oblivious to the intrinsic structure of the data. Real-world data such
031   as images are believed to live in a manifold of a much lower dimension (Roweis & Saul,
032   2000; van der Maaten & Hinton, 2008; Jolliffe & Cadima, 2016). Such manifold’s structure
033   can be used to achieve better bounds of network size. It has been shown that the network
034   size can be bounded by exponential of the manifold’s intrinsic dimension rather than the
035   encompassing input space dimension (Chen et al., 2019; Schmidt-Hieber, 2019).
036   However, the intrinsic dimension is only a small part of the manifold’s property. It is natural
037   to ask whether other properties of the manifold, such as topology and geometry, may lead to
038   improved bounds. Safran & Shamir (2016) demonstrate that to approximate the indicator
039   function of a d-dimensional ball, one only needs a network of size quadratic to d. However,
040   this work assumes a rather simplistic input. To extend to a more general setting, one needs
041   to incorporate the topology and geometry of the manifold into the analysis.
042   Early research has probed the geometry and topology of manifolds. Notably, Federer (1959);
043   Amenta & Bern (1998) introduce a pivotal curvature measure, which adeptly captures the
044   global geometric nuances of manifolds and has been embraced in manifold learning studies
045   (Narayanan & Niyogi, 2009; Narayanan & Mitter, 2010; Ramamurthy et al., 2019). On the
046   topological front, descriptors like Betti numbers have been formalized in the language of
047   algebraic topology to characterized the numbers of connected components and holes of a
048   manifold (Hatcher, 2002; Bott et al., 1982; Munkres, 2018; Rieck et al., 2019). In their
049   seminal work, Niyogi et al. (2008) integrate manifold’s geometry and topology, setting forth
050
      conditions for topologically faithful reconstructions grounded in geometric metrics.
051   With the advent of the deep learning era, there has been a burgeoning interest in discerning
052   the interplay between network size and manifold’s intrinsic structural attributes. Existing
053   studies (Dikkala et al., 2021; Schonsheck et al., 2019) bound network size with the geometry
      of manifolds. However, a theoretical framework that successfully integrates network size and

      topological traits has not yet been developed. This is a missed opportunity. The topological
055   complexity of manifolds plays a crucial role in the learning problem, particularly concerning
056   network size. Empirical findings (Guss & Salakhutdinov, 2018; Naitzat et al., 2020) suggest
057   that even with similar geometry, data with larger topological complexity requires a larger
058   network. These empirical observations highlight the need for a theoretical analysis that
059   examines how manifold topology and geometry interact with network size. Addressing this
060   need poses a significant challenge, as incorporating topological descriptors into the current
061   analytical framework is inherently difficult due to the discrete nature of topology.
062   In this paper, we address this gap by presenting an innovative theoretical framework that
063   integrates topology with neural network size. We tackle the challenge by first approximating
064   a homeomorphism to disentangle the geometry and topology of a manifold. Notably, manifold
065   topology remains invariant under a homeomorphism. This allows us to simplify the geometric
066   complexity while maintaining the topological complexity. We then successfully represent
067   discrete topological complexity as a combination of the complexities of basic topological
068   shapes, thus overcoming the challenge posed by the discrete nature of topological descriptors.
069
      The resulting upper bound is obtained by constructing a neural network that first learns a
      low-dimensional embedding of the input manifold, followed by classification in the embedding
070
      space. This approach aligns with modern neural network design principles.
071
072   Our theoretical result reveals for the first time how the data topology, as a global structural
073   characterization of data manifold, affects the network expressiveness. The beauty of the
074   theorem is that it explicitly bounds the network size with both topology and geometry, giving
075
      us insights as to how the two important manifold properties affect learning. To capture
      the manifold’s topology, we use the classic Betti numbers, which measure the number of
076
      connected components and holes within the manifold. For geometric measure, we use the
077
      reach introduced by Federer (1959); Amenta & Bern (1998), describing the manifold’s overall
078   flatness. See Figure 1 for illustrations of these measures.

113   2   Related Works
114
115   Network size with manifold geometry. Multiple studies have formulated network
116
      size bounds across varied manifold learning contexts based on geometry. Schonsheck et al.
                                                2

117
      (2019) establish a bound of O(LdDϵ−d−d /2 (− log1+d/2 ϵ)) on the network size for manifold
      reconstruction tasks. L is the covering number in terms of the injectivity radius, a geometric
118
      property. They utilize an auto-encoder, denoted as D ◦E, for the reconstruction of a manifold.
119
      Both the encoder E and the decoder D are designed to function as homeomorphisms. As a
120   result, the overarching objective is the construction of a homeomorphism within the same
121   space, which elucidates the absence of topological considerations in their outcomes. Our
122   findings include not only the homeomorphism but also the classification network, with the
123   latter being influenced by the manifold’s topology. Chen et al. (2019) demonstrate the
124   existence of a network of size O(ϵ−d/n log 1ϵ + D log 1ϵ + D log D) that can approximate any
125   smooth real function supported on a compact Riemannian manifold. In this context, n
126   denotes the order of smoothness of the function. Their primary objective is to illustrate that,
127   in manifold learning, the manifold dimension chiefly determines network size, with only a
128   marginal dependence on the ambient dimension. Moreover, their smoothness assumption
129   is inapplicable to classification tasks, where the target function lacks continuity. Yet,
130
      the interplay between manifold properties and their impact on network size in manifold
      classification largely remains unexplored.
131
132   Classifier learned on manifold. Dikkala et al. (2021) investigate network size in classifi-
133   cation contexts. However, their foundational assumption is that a manifold’s essence can be
134   distilled into just two parameters: a centroid and a surrounding perturbation. They further
135   assume there is a sensitive hashing property on manifolds. These assumptions are quite
136
      constrained, might not align with real-world complexities, and also overlooks the intrinsic
      properties of the manifold. Nevertheless, the aforementioned studies predominantly concen-
137
      trate on network size and geometric traits, neglecting the equally critical role of topological
138
      features. Buchanan et al. (2021) establish a lower bound on the size of classifiers for inputs
139   situated on manifolds. However, their theoretical framework is restricted to smooth, regular
140   simple curves; it fails to account for complex manifold structures. Guss & Salakhutdinov
141   (2018) provide empirical evidence showing that classifiers, when trained on data with higher
142   Betti numbers, tend to have slower convergence rates. They also highlight that with rising
143   topological complexity, smaller networks face challenges in effective learning. These findings
144   underscore the need for a more comprehensive theoretical understanding.
145   There are some intriguing studies not primarily centered on manifold learning. Specifically,
146   Bianchini & Scarselli (2014) establish a bound for the Betti number of a neural network’s
147   expression field based on its capacity. Nevertheless, their proposed bound is loose, and
148   it exclusively addresses the regions a network can generate, neglecting any consideration
149   of input manifold. Safran & Shamir (2016) explore the challenge of approximating the
150   indicator function of a unit ball using a ReLU network. While their primary objective is to
151   demonstrate that enhancing depth is more effective than expanding width, their approach
152   has provided valuable insights. Naitzat et al. (2020) empirically examines the evolution of
153
      manifold topology as data traverses the layers of a proficiently trained neural network. We
      have adopted their concept of topological complexity. A number of studies, such as those by
154
      Hanin & Rolnick (2019) and Grigsby & Lindsey (2022), concentrate on exploring the potential
155
      expressivity of neural networks. However, these works primarily focus on the network’s
156   inherent capabilities without extensively considering the characteristics of the input data.
157   Theoretically, Birdal et al. (2021); Andreeva et al. (2024) propose a persistent homology
158   generalization bound that accounts for the topology of the training trajectory. They propose
159   a comprehensive metric that encompasses the neural network, optimization process, and
160   network activations. In contrast, our work focuses specifically on approximating the indicator
161   function of the data manifold using a neural network and studying the relationship between
      neural network size and the properties of the data manifold.

      3   Preliminaries
163
164   Topological Manifolds. In this paper, we focus on a specific class of manifolds called
165   the thickened 1-manifold family, which are derived from operations on 1-manifolds. To lay
166   the groundwork, we begin with the concept of a manifold. An n-dimensional manifold is
167   a topological space where every point has a neighborhood that is homeomorphic (topolog-
168   ically equivalent) to Rn . And an n-manifold with boundary allows for points that have
169   neighborhoods homeomorphic to the half-space R≥0 × Rn−1 . For example, a circle (s1 ) is a
170   1-manifold without boundary, known as a closed manifold, while a line segment (I 1 ) is a
171   1-manifold with boundary.
172   Thickened 1-manifold. Our manifolds of interest are those obtained through operations
173   on 1-manifolds, particularly when they are "thickened" to higher dimensions. Specifically, we
174   consider d-thickened 1-manifolds, which are (d + 1)-dimensional manifolds homeomorphic to
175   M1 × B d , where M1 is a compact 1-manifold (with or without boundary), and B d denotes
176   a d-dimensional closed ball. Figure 2 illustrates the 1-thickened 1-manifold. According to
177   the classification theorem for 1-manifolds, M1 is homeomorphic to either a closed interval
178
      I 1 or a circle S 1 . Consequently, a 1-thickened 1-manifold is homeomorphic to either I 1 × B 1
      or S 1 × B 1 .
179
180   Connected Sum and Disjoint Union. To construct more complex manifolds from simpler
181   ones, we utilize operations such as the connected sum and disjoint union. The connected
182   sum of two closed manifolds M and N , denoted M # N , is formed by removing a small
183   open ball from each manifold and then gluing them together along the resulting boundary
184
      sphere. The disjoint union M ⊔ N combines two manifolds by considering them together
      without connecting them. These operations yield new compact manifolds that combine the
185
      topological features of the original manifolds. Although the connected sum of thickened
186
      1-manifolds differs from the standard definition of the connected sum for closed manifolds
187   mentioned earlier, we utilize the fact that the boundary ∂M of a thickened 1-manifold M is
188   a closed manifold. The connected sum of two thickened 1-manifolds is defined by performing
189   the connected sum on their boundaries and extend the gluing to the interiors of identifying
190   corresponding points in the neighborhoods adjacent to the removed sphere. The formal
191   definition of connected sum is provided in section A.1. Figure A.1 illustrates the connected
192   sum between thickened 1-manifolds.
193   With the tools defined above, we can now define the thickened 1-manifold family, which is
194   the class of manifolds we will study in this paper.
195
      Definition 1 (Thickened 1-Manifold Family). Let M1 represent a compact 1-manifold (with
196   or without boundary). A d-thickened 1-manifold is a (d + 1)-manifold homeomorphic to
197   M1 × B d . The family of d-thickened 1-manifolds, denoted by M, includes all such manifolds
198   obtained via finite operations of connected sum and disjoint union.

      4     Main Results
280
      In this section, we explore how the topology of manifolds influence network size in classification
281
      scenarios. Our results, derived methodically through construction, follow two steps. First, we
282
      approximate a homeomorphism between the input manifold and a latent one; second, we carry
283   out classification within this latent manifold. This latent manifold is designed to have simple
284   geometric features, akin to those found in spheres and tori, while retaining the intrinsic
285   topological characteristics of the original manifold. By design, the first phase is purely
286   geometric, as the topological traits remain unaltered, while the subsequent classification
287   phase is predominantly topological. Consequently, the required network size can be delineated
288   into two distinct parts. We employ Betti numbers and the condition number as metrics
289   to gauge topological and geometric complexities, respectively. Specifically, Betti numbers
290   quantify the number of connected components and holes within the manifold, whereas the
291
      condition number characterizes the manifold’s overall curvature.

359   4.2   Overall Complexity
360
      For general manifolds in M ∈ M, due to their inherent complexity, often defy explicit
361
      expression. This hinders the direct use of function analysis for approximating their indicator
362   functions, as was done in previous studies. To tackle this issue, we construct a homeomor-
363   phism, a continuous two-way transformation, between M and its corresponding topological
364   representative M′ . This method only alters the geometric properties, preserving the object’s
365   topological attributes. Therefore, the network size in approximating the indicator function
366   of M is constructed by the approximation of the homeomorphism and the classification
367   of topological representatives. The network size of constructing the homeomorphism is
368   exclusively influenced by the geometric properties, whereas the network size of classifying
369   topological representatives pertains solely to topological properties. The latter we already
370
      figured in previous section. This methodology enables us to distinguish the influence of
      topology and geometry of manifold on classifiers. In this section, we aim to obtain the overall
371
      network size for a classifier.
372
373   To build a homeomorphism from M, we first need to recover the homology of M. The
374   subsequent proposition outlines a lower limit for the number of points essential to recover

490   6   Conclusion
491
492   In this study, we delved into the intricate relationship between network size, and both
493
      geometric and topological characteristics of manifolds. Our findings underscored that while
      many existing studies have been focused on geometric intricacies, it is important to also
494
      appreciate the manifold’s topological characteristics. These characteristics not only offer an
495
      alternative perspective on data structures but also influence network size in significant ways.
496
497   Our proposed network size bounds represent theoretical upper limits, meaning that real-world
498   implementations may yield efficiencies beyond these confines. To attain a more direct and
499
      refined theoretical bound, we may need more comprehensive descriptors of manifolds that go
      beyond merely the Betti numbers and the condition number. We leave this exploration for
500
      future work. We hope that our study acts as a catalyst for further research, pushing the
501
      boundaries of manifold learning and its applications in modern AI systems.
