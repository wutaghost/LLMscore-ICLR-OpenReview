                                                    A BSTRACT
008
009
               A popular approach of automated mechanism design is to formulate a linear pro-
010
               gram (LP) whose solution gives a mechanism with desired properties. We ana-
011            lytically derive a class of optimal solutions for such an LP that gives mechanisms
012            achieving standard properties of efficiency, incentive compatibility, strong budget
013            balance (SBB), and individual rationality (IR), where SBB and IR are satisfied
014            in expectation. Notably, our solutions are represented by an exponentially smaller
015            number of essential variables than the original variables of LP. Our solutions, how-
016            ever, involve a term whose exact evaluation requires solving a certain optimization
017            problem exponentially many times as the number of players, N , grows. We thus
018            evaluate this term by modeling it as the problem of estimating the mean reward
019
               of the best arm in multi-armed bandit (MAB), propose a Probably and Approx-
               imately Correct estimator, and prove its asymptotic optimality by establishing a
020
               lower bound on its sample complexity. This MAB approach reduces the number
021
               of times the optimization problem is solved from exponential to O(N log N ). Nu-
022            merical experiments show that the proposed approach finds mechanisms that are
023            guaranteed to achieve desired properties with high probability for environments
024            with up to 128 players, which substantially improves upon the prior work.

027   1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Multi-agent systems can be made efficient by mediators who make system-wide (social) decisions
030   in a way that maximizes social welfare. For example, in a trading network where firms sell goods
031   to each other or to external markets (Hatfield et al., 2013), the mediator can ensure that those traded
032   goods are produced by the firms with the lowest costs and purchased by those with the highest
033
      needs (Osogami et al., 2023). While such mediators could maximize their own profit by charging
      participants (e.g., today’s tech giants who operate consumer marketplaces or ads platforms), they
034
      would typically take most of the profit, leaving the participants with only a small portion.
035
036   We instead envision an open platform whose purpose is to provide maximal benefits to the partic-
037   ipants in multi-agent systems. This is similar to the purpose of designing an auction mechanism
038   for public resources, which should be given to those who need them most, and there should be nei-
039
      ther budget deficits nor surpluses on the mediator (Bailey, 1997; Cavallo, 2006; Dufton et al., 2021;
      Gujar & Narahari, 2011; Guo, 2012; Guo & Conitzer, 2009; Manisha et al., 2018; Tacchetti et al.,
040
      2022). However, such mechanisms exploit the particular structure of single-sided auctions, where all
041
      participants are buyers, and it may be impossible to achieve desired properties in other multi-agent
042   systems such as double-sided auctions (Hobbs et al., 2000; Zou, 2009; Widmer & Leukel, 2016;
043   Stößer et al., 2010; Kumar et al., 2018; Chichin et al., 2017), matching markets (Zhang & Zhu,
044   2020), and trading networks (Osogami et al., 2023; Wasserkrug & Osogami, 2023) even if those
045   properties could be guaranteed for single-sided auctions.
046
      Here, we design mechanisms for general environments that include all these multi-agent systems,
047
      with the primary objectives of efficiency and strong budget balance (SBB). Specifically, we require
048   that the mediator chooses a social decision such that its total value to the players (or participants)
049   is maximized (decision efficiency; DE) and that the expected revenue of the mediator is equal to a
050   target value, ρ ∈ R (SBB when ρ = 0). As is standard in Bayesian mechanism design (Shoham &
051   Leyton-Brown, 2009), we assume that the value of each social decision to a player depends on the
052   player’s type that is only known to that player, but a joint probability distribution of the players’ types
053   is a common knowledge. Since it would be difficult to achieve DE without knowing the types, we
      also require that the optimal strategy of each player is to truthfully declare its type regardless of the


                                                          1
054
      strategies of the others (dominant strategy incentive compatibility; DSIC). To promote participation,
055   we also require that the expected utility of each player is no less than a target value, θ(tn ) ∈ R, that
056   can depend on the type tn of the player (individual rationality or IR when θ ≡ 0).
057
058
      Although these properties are standard in the literature of mechanism design (Shoham & Leyton-
      Brown, 2009), here we have introduced parameters, ρ and θ, to generalize the standard definitions
059
      of SBB (ρ = 0) and IR (θ ≡ 0) with three motivations. First, it is not always possible to achieve
060
      the four desired properties with the standard definitions of IR and SBB (Green & Laffont, 1977;
061   Myerson & Satterthwaite, 1983; Osogami et al., 2023), while the generalization will enable the
062   exact characterization of when those properties can be satisfied. Second, this generalization will
063   allow us to develop a principled approach of learning a mechanism, where some of the quantities are
064   estimated from samples, with theoretical guarantees. The mechanism designed with our learning
065   approach can be shown to satisfy the four desired properties with high probability. Finally, the
066   additional parameters will allow us to model practical requirements. For example, the mediator
067   might need positive revenue to cover the cost of maintaining the platform or might want to guarantee
068   positive expected utility to players to encourage participation in this platform rather than others.
069   We require SBB and IR in expectation (ex ante or interim) with respect to the distribution of types,
070   while DE and DSIC are satisfied for any realization of types (ex post). While these assumptions
071   are similar to those in Osogami et al. (2023); Wasserkrug & Osogami (2023) for trading networks,
072   the in expectation properties are certainly weaker than the ex post properties usually assumed for
073   auctions (Bailey, 1997; Cavallo, 2006; Dufton et al., 2021; Gujar & Narahari, 2011; Guo, 2012;
074   Guo & Conitzer, 2009; Manisha et al., 2018; Tacchetti et al., 2022). With the weaker properties,
075
      however, we derive analytical solutions of the mechanisms that satisfy all the four desired properties
      in the general environments (and characterize when those properties can be satisfied). This is in stark
076
      contrast to the prior work, where mechanisms are analytically derived only for auctions with a single
077
      type of goods (Bailey, 1997; Cavallo, 2006; Guo, 2011; Guo & Conitzer, 2007; 2009; Moulin, 2009)
078   or with unit demand (Gujar & Narahari, 2011; Guo, 2012). For more complex auctions (Dufton
079   et al., 2021; Manisha et al., 2018; Tacchetti et al., 2022) or trading networks (Osogami et al., 2023;
080   Wasserkrug & Osogami, 2023), mechanisms are computed by numerically solving optimization
081   problems, whose size often grows exponentially with the number of players.
082
      While the numerical approaches proposed in Osogami et al. (2023) have been applied only to the
083
      trading networks with two players, our analytical solutions can be evaluated numerically with ∼ 10
084   players, depending on the number of types. The key bottleneck in our analytical solutions lies in the
085   evaluation of the minimum expected value over possible types of each player. Exact evaluation of
086   this quantity would require computing an efficient social decision for all the K N combinations of
087   types, where K is the number of possible types of each player, and N is the number of players.
088
      To overcome this bottleneck, we model the problem of evaluating this minimum expected value in
089
      a multi-armed bandit (MAB) approach (Lattimore & Szepesvári, 2020) and propose an asymptoti-
090   cally optimal learning algorithm for this problem. While the standard objectives of MAB are regret
091   minimization (Auer et al., 1995; 2002) and best arm identification (Audibert et al., 2010; Maron &
092   Moore, 1993; Mnih et al., 2008; Bubeck et al., 2009), our objective is to estimate the mean reward of
093   the best arm. We propose a probably approximately correct (PAC) algorithm, which approximately
094   (with error at most ε) estimates the best mean with high probability (at least 1 − δ), and proves that
095   its sample complexity, O((K/ε2 ) log(1/δ)), matches the lower bound that we derive. This learn-
096   ing approach substantially reduces the number of computing efficient social decisions from K N to
097   O(K N log N ), enabling us to numerically find mechanisms for ∼ 100 players, depending on K.
098   Our contributions thus revolve around the optimization problem whose solution gives the mechanism
099   that satisfies DE, SBB, DSIC, and IR (see Section 3-4). First, we establish a sufficient condition that
100   ensures the optimization problem has feasible solutions, and prove that this sufficient condition is
101   also necessary when the players have independent types (see Section 5). Second, for cases where
102   this sufficient condition holds, we analytically derive a class of optimal solutions to this optimization
103   problem, which in turn gives mechanisms that satisfy DE, SBB, DSIC, and IR for general environ-
104
      ments including auctions and trading networks (see Section 5). Third, we model the problem of
      evaluating a quantity in the above analytical expressions as best mean estimation in MAB, propose
105
      a PAC algorithm for this problem, and prove its asymptotic optimality (see Section 6). Finally, we
106
      empirically validate the effectiveness of the proposed approach (see Section 7). In Section 2, we
107   start by positioning our contributions to the prior work.

      2   R ELATED WORK
109
110
111   The prior work most related to ours is Osogami et al. (2023), which formulates and numerically
112
      solves the LP whose solution gives the mechanism for a trading network that satisfies DE, DSIC,
      IR, and weak budget balance (WBB; expected revenue of the mediator is nonnegative). While the
113
      objective of the LP is rather arbitrary and SBB is given just as an example in Osogami et al. (2023),
114
      we focus on SBB and drive analytical solutions for this particular objective. Our formulation extends
115   Osogami et al. (2023) with additional parameters and notations that cover environments beyond
116   trading networks, but these extensions are relatively straightforward.
117
118
      In the rest of this section, we discuss related work on mechanism design, with a focus on those
      aiming to achieve SBB, as well as related work on MAB with a focus on PAC algorithms. In
119
      particular, we will see that our learning approach is unique in that we estimate a particular quantity
120
      in the optimal solution that we derive analytically, and this leads us to propose a new PAC algorithm
121   and establish its optimality for an underexplored objective of best mean estimation in MAB.
122
123   In single-sided auctions where only buyers make strategic decisions, Vickrey–Clarke–Groves (VCG)
124
      mechanisms with Clark’s pivot rule (also called VCG auction) satisfy ex post DE (called allocative
      efficiency in auctions), DSIC, IR, and WBB (Nisan, 2007). However, the Green-Laffont Impossibil-
125
      ity Theorem implies that no mechanism can guarantee DE, DSIC, and SBB simultaneously for all
126
      environments (Green & Laffont, 1977; 1979). This has led to a series of work on redistributing the
127   revenue of the mediator to the players as much as possible (i.e., to make budget balance as strong
128   as possible), while satisfying DSIC, DE, IR, and WBB. For auctions with single or homogeneous
129   goods (Bailey, 1997; Cavallo, 2006; Guo, 2011; Guo & Conitzer, 2007; 2009; Moulin, 2009) or for
130   auctions where players have unit demand (Gujar & Narahari, 2011; Guo, 2012), researchers have
131   derived analytical solutions that optimally redistribute the payment to the players. For auctions with
132   multi-unit demands on heterogeneous goods, the prior work has proposed numerical approaches that
133   seek to find the piecewise linear functions (Dufton et al., 2021) or neural networks (Manisha et al.,
134   2018; Tacchetti et al., 2022) that best approximate the optimal redistribution functions.
135   We consider the environments that not only allow heterogeneous goods and multi-unit demands
136   but also are more general than single-sided auctions. In particular, our players may have negative
137   valuation on a social decision. The Myerson-Satterthwaite Impossibility Theorem (Myerson & Sat-
138   terthwaite, 1983) thus implies that, unlike VCG auctions, no mechanism can guarantee ex post DE,
139   DSIC, IR, and WBB simultaneously for all the environments that we consider. We thus derive mech-
140   anisms that achieve DE, DSIC, IR, and SBB in the best possible manner. A limitation of our results
141
      is that IR and SBB are satisfied only in expectation. Such a guarantee in expectation can however be
      justified for risk-neutral mediator and players (Osogami et al., 2023). Our model can also guarantee
142
      strictly positive expected utility, which in turn can ensure nonnegative utility with high probability
143
      when the player repeatedly participate in the mechanism.
144
145   For auctions, there also exists a large body of the literature on maximizing the revenue of the me-
146   diator (Myerson, 1981) with recent focus on automated mechanism design (AMD) with machine
147
      learning (Duetting et al., 2019; Rahme et al., 2021; Ivanov et al., 2022; Curry et al., 2020) and anal-
      ysis of its sample complexity (Balcan et al., 2016; Morgenstern & Roughgarden, 2015; Syrgkanis,
148
      2017). Similar to these and other studies of AMD (Sandholm, 2003; Conitzer & Sandholm, 2002),
149
      we formulate an optimization problem whose solution gives the mechanism with desired properties.
150   However, instead of solving it numerically, we analytically derive optimal solutions. Also, while
151   the prior work analyzes the sample complexity for finding the mechanism that maximizes the ex-
152   pected revenue, we analytically find optimal mechanisms and characterize the sample complexity of
153   evaluating a particular expression in the analytically designed mechanisms.
154
      We evaluate our analytical expression through best mean estimation (BME) in MAB, where the
155
      standard objectives are regret minimization (Auer et al., 1995; 2002) and best arm identification
156   (BAI) (Audibert et al., 2010; Maron & Moore, 1993; Mnih et al., 2008; Bubeck et al., 2009). The
157   prior work on MAB that is most relevant to ours is PAC learning for BAI and analysis of its sample
158   complexity. We reduce the problem of BME to BAI and prove the lower bound on the sample
159   complexity of BME using a technique known for BAI (Even-Dar et al., 2002). However, while this
160   technique does not give tight lower bound for BAI (Mannor & Tsitsiklis, 2004), we show that it
161   gives tight lower bound for BME. Notice that the problem of estimating the best mean frequently
      appears in reinforcement learning (van Hasselt, 2010) and machine learning (Kajino et al., 2023),


                                                        3
162
      but there the focus is on how best to estimate the best mean with a given set of samples (van Hasselt,
163   2013), while our focus is on how best to collect samples to estimate the best mean.

      3    S ETTINGS
167
      The goal of mechanism design is to specify the rules of a game in a way that an outcome desired by
168
      the mechanism designer is achieved when rational players (i.e., players whose goal it is to maximize
169
      their individual utility) participate in that game (Jackson, 2014; Shoham & Leyton-Brown, 2009).
170   Formally, let N := [1, N ] be the set of players and O be the set of possible outcomes. For each
171   player n ∈ N , let An be the set of available actions and Tn be the set of possible types. Let
172   A := A1 × . . . × AN and T := T1 × . . . × TN be the corresponding product spaces. A mechanism
173   µ : A → O determines an outcome depending of the actions taken by the players. Let un :
174   O × Tn → R be the utility function of each player n ∈ N .
175
      We consider Bayesian games where the players’ types follow a probability distribution that is known
176   to all players and the mediator. Before selecting actions, the players know their own types but not
177   the types of the other players. A strategy of each player n ∈ N is thus a function from Tn to An .
178
179
      We assume that an outcome is determined by a social decision and payment; hence, a mechanism
      µ consists of a decision rule and a payment rule. Let D be the set of possible social decisions.
180
      Given the actions of the players, the decision rule ϕ : A → D determines a social decision, and the
181
      payment rule τ : A → RN determines the amount of (possibly negative) payment to the mediator
182   from each player. Let v : D × (T1 ∪ . . . ∪ TN ) → R specify the value of a given social decision to
183   the player of a given type. Then the utility of player i when players take actions a ∈ A is

326   efficient decision ϕ⋆ (t), which is given by a solution to an optimization problem (3). Without any
327   structure in D and v, this would require evaluating the total value for all decisions in D.

      6   O NLINE LEARNING FOR EVALUATING THE ANALYTICAL SOLUTION
331
      To alleviate the computational complexity associated with (20), we propose a learning approach. A
332
      key observation is that the problem of estimating (20) can be considered as a variant of a multi-armed
333   bandit (MAB) problem whose objective is to estimate the mean reward of the best arm. Specifically,
334   the MAB gives the reward θ(tn ) − w⋆ (t) when we pull an arm tn ∈ Tn , where t is a sample from
335   the conditional distribution P[· | tn ]. Following the relevant prior work on MAB (Even-Dar et al.,
336   2002; 2006; Hassidim et al., 2020; Mannor & Tsitsiklis, 2004), we maximize reward in this section.
337
      Since we assume that N , D, and Tn , ∀n ∈ N are finite, there exist constants, θ̄ and v̄, such that
338
      |θ(t′ )| ≤ θ̄ and |v(d; t′ )| ≤ v̄, ∀d ∈ D, ∀t′ ∈ ∪n∈N Tn . Then we can also bound |θ(tn ) − w⋆ (t)| ≤
339   θ̄ + N v̄, ∀t ∈ T , ∀n ∈ N . Namely, the reward in the MAB is bounded. We assume that we know
340   the bounds on the reward and can scale it such that the scaled reward is in [0, 1] surely.
341
342
      We also assume that we have access to an arbitrary size of the sample that is independent and
      identically distributed (i.i.d.) according to P[· | tn ] for any tn ∈ Tn , n ∈ N . When players have
343
      independent types, such sample can be easily constructed as long as we have access to i.i.d. sample
344                                             (i)
345
      {t(i) }i=1,2,... from T , because {(tn , t−n )}i is the sample from P[· | tn ] for any tn ∈ Tn , n ∈ N .
346   Consider the general K-armed bandit where the reward of each arm is bounded in [0, 1]. For each
347   k ∈ [1, K], let µk be the true mean of arm k. Let µ⋆ := maxk µk be the best mean-reward, which we
348   want to estimate. We say that the sample complexity of an algorithm for a MAB is T if the sample
349   size needed by the algorithm is bounded by T (i.e., arms are pulled at most T times).
350   A standard Probably Approximately Correct (PAC) algorithm for MAB returns an ε-optimal arm
351   with probability at least 1 − δ for given ε, δ (Even-Dar et al., 2006; Hassidim et al., 2020). We will
352   use the following definition:
353   Definition 1 ((ε, δ)-PAC Best Arm Identifier (Even-Dar et al., 2006)). For ε, δ > 0, we say that an
354   algorithm is (ε, δ)-PAC Best Arm Identifier (BAI) if the arm Iˆ identified by the algorithm satisfies

360   Definition 2 ((ε, δ)-PAC Best Mean Estimator). For ε, δ > 0, we say that an algorithm is (ε, δ)-PAC
361   Best Mean Estimator (BME) if the best mean µ̂ estimated by the algorithm satisfies
362                                        Pr (|µ̂ − µ⋆ | ≤ ε) ≥ 1 − δ.                                  (22)

365   BAI and BME are related but different. For example, consider the case where the best arm has
366   large variance and µ⋆ = 1/2, and the other arms always give zero reward µn = 0, ∀n ̸= ⋆. Then
367   relatively small sample would suffice for BAI due to the large gap µ⋆ − µn = 1/2, ∀n ̸= ⋆, while
368   BME would require relatively large sample due to the large variance of the best arm ⋆. As another
369   example, consider the case where we have many arms whose rewards follow Bernoulli distributions.
370
      Suppose that half of the arms have an expected value of 1, and the other half have an expected value
      of 1 − (3/2) ε. Then by pulling sufficiently many arms (once for each arm), we can estimate that the
371
      best mean is at least 1 − ε with high probability (by Hoeffding’s inequality), but BAI would require
372
      pulling arms sufficiently many times until we pull one of the best arms sufficiently many times (to
373   be able to say that this particular arm has an expected value at least 1 − ε).

462   We conduct several numerical experiments to validate the effectiveness of the proposed approach and
463   to understand its limitations. Specifically, we design our experiments to investigate the following
464   questions. i) BME studied in Section 6 has asymptotically optimal sample complexity, but how well
465
      can we estimate the best mean when there are only a moderate number of arms? ii) How much can
      BME reduce the number of times w⋆ (t) given by (7) is evaluated? iii) How well θ-IR and ρ-SBB
466
      are satisfied when (20) is estimated by BME rather than calculated exactly? In this section, we only
467
      provide a brief overview of our experiments; for full details, see Appendix A.4. In particular, we
468   find the key empirical property of BME that it can reduce the computational complexity by many
469   orders of magnitude when the number of players is moderate (∼ 10) to large but is relatively less
470   effective against the increased number of types.
471
      For question i), we compare Successive Elimination for BAI (SE-BAI), which is known to perform
472
      well for a moderate number of arms (Hassidim et al., 2020; Even-Dar et al., 2006), against the corre-
473
      sponding algorithm for BME (SE-BME) and summarize the results in Appendix A.4.1. Overall, we
474   find that SE-BME generally requires smaller sample size than SE-BAI, except when there are only
475   a few arms and their means have large gaps. The efficiency of SE-BAI for this case makes intuitive
476   sense, since the best arm can be identified without estimating the means with high accuracy.
477
      For question ii), we quantitatively validate the effectiveness of SE-BME in reducing the number of
478
      times w⋆ (t) is computed when we evaluate (20) in a setting of mechanism design, as is detailed

521
522
523   We have analytically derived optimal solutions for the LP that gives mechanisms that guarantee the
524   desired properties of DE, DSIC, ρ-SBB, and θ-IR. When there are N players, each with T possible
525
      types, the LP involves N T N −1 variables, while our analytical solutions are represented by only N
      essential variables. While Osogami et al. (2023) numerically solves this LP only for N = T = 2,
526
      we have exactly evaluated our analytical solutions for N = T = 8 (see Figure 1). The analytical
527
      solution, however, involves a term whose exact evaluation requires finding efficient social decisions
528   T N times. We have modeled the problem of evaluating this term as best mean estimation in multi-
529   armed bandit, proposed a PAC estimator, and proved its asymptotic optimality by establishing a
530   lower bound on the sample complexity. Our experiments show that our PAC estimator enables
531   finding mechanism for N = 128 and T = 8 with a guarantee on the desired properties.
532
      The proposed approach makes a major advancement in the field and can positively impact society by
533
      providing guarantees on desired properties for large environments, which existing approaches are
534   unable to handle. However, it presents certain limitations and potential challenges, which inspire
535   several directions for further research. Regarding limitations, one should keep in mind that our
536   mechanisms guarantee ρ-SBB and θ-IR only in expectation, and that the our approaches have only
537   been applied to the environments up to 128 players and up to 16 types in our experiments. Regarding
538   societal impacts, our approach might result in the mechanisms that are unfair to some of the players,
539   because DE does not mean that all of the players are treated fairly. These limitations and societal
      impacts are further discussed in Appendix A.5.

      A.3.2    L OWER BOUND
868
869   The lower bound established in Lemma 4 reduces BME to the ε-Biased Coin Problem defined as
870   follows:
871   Definition 3 (ε-Biased Coin Problem). For 0 < ε < 1, consider a Bernoulli random variable B
872   whose mean α is known to be either α+ := (1 + ε)/2 or α− := (1 − ε)/2. The ε-Biased Coin
873   Problem asks to correctly identify whether α = α+ or α = α− .
874
875   A lower bound on the sample complexity for solving the ε-Biased Coin Problem is known as the
876   following lemma, for which we provide a proof in Appendix B for completeness:
877   Lemma 7 (Chernoff (1972); Even-Dar et al. (2002)). For 0 < δ < 1/2, any algorithm that solves
878   the ε-Biased Coin Problem correctly with probability at least 1 − δ has expected sample complexity
879   at least Ω((1/ε2 ) log(1/δ)).
880
      In Lemma 5, we have derived the lower bound for BME using the technique used for a lower bound
881
      for BAI in Even-Dar et al. (2002); however, as we have discussed at the end of Section 6, while
882
      our lower bound for BME is tight, the lower bound for BAI in Even-Dar et al. (2002) is not. The
883   difference in the derived lower bound stems from the following behavior of BME and BAI when all
884   of the arms have mean reward of α− and hence are indistinguishable. The algorithm constructed in
885   Even-Dar et al. (2002) determines that B has mean α+ when either arm i+ or arm i− is identified
886   as the best arm. When the arms are indistinguishable, a PAC BAI would correctly identify each
887   of the K arms, including i+ or i− , as the best arm uniformly at random, which induces an error
888   with probability 1/K. On the other hand, the mean reward estimated by a PAC BME would be
889   approximately correct with high probability, even when the arms are indistinguishable.

1264   A.5.1     L IMITATIONS
1265
1266   While the proposed approach makes major advancement in the field, it certainly has limitations.
1267   Here, we discuss four major limitations of this work as well as interesting directions of research
1268   motivated by those limitations.
1269   First, when types are not independent between players, the sufficient condition in Lemma 1 may not
1270   be necessary (Proposition 1). This means that the LP may be feasible even when the condition in the
1271   lemma is violated, and our results do not provide optimal solutions for those cases. Further research
1272   is needed to understand exactly when the sufficient condition is also necessary. It is also important
1273   to develop efficient methods for solving the LP when the types are dependent.
1274   Second, our mechanisms guarantee strong budget balance (SBB) and individual rationality (IR) in
1275   expectation with respect to the distribution of the players’ types, but this does not guarantee that
1276   those properties are satisfied ex post (for any realization of the types). Although the satisfaction in
1277   expectation is often sufficient for risk-neutral decision makers, it is important to let the mediator and
1278   the participants aware that they may experience negative utilities even if their expected utilities are
1279   nonnegative. It would also be an interesting direction of research to extend the proposed approach
1280   towards achieving these properties ex post.
1281   Finally, our experiments have considered environments with up to 128 players, each with at most 16
1282   types. Although these are substantially larger than the environment studied in Osogami et al. (2023),
1283   they certainly do not cover the scale needed for all applications. While (10-100 times) larger en-
1284   vironments could be handled with improved implementation and greater computational resources,
1285   essentially new ideas would be needed for substantially (over 103 times) larger environments or
1286   continuous type space. It would be an interesting direction of research to identify and exploit struc-
1287   tures of particular environments for designing scalable approaches of mechanism design for those
1288
       environments.
1289
1290   A.5.2     S OCIETAL IMPACTS
1291
1292   We expect that the proposed approach has several positive impacts on trading networks in particular
1293   and the society in general. In particular, the proposed approach enables mechanisms that can max-
1294   imize the efficiency of a trading network and minimize the fees that the participants need to pay to
1295   the mediator. Also, the DSIC guaranteed by the proposed approach would make it more difficult for
       malicious participants to manipulate the outcome of a trading network.


                                                          24
1296
       On the other hand, the proposed approach might have negative impacts depending on where and
1297   how it is applied. For example, although the proposed approach guarantees individual rationality,
1298   some of the participants might get less benefits from the mechanism designed with our approach
1299   than other participants. This can happen, because maximizing the social welfare does not mean that
1300   all the participants are treated fairly. Before applying the mechanisms designed with the proposed
1301   approach, it is thus recommended to assess whether such fairness needs to be considered and to take
1302   any actions that mitigate the bias if needed.
