011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            The goal of mechanistic interpretability is discovering a simple, low-rank al-
014            gorithm implemented by models. While we can compress activations into fea-
015            tures, compressing nonlinear feature-maps—like MLP layers—is an open prob-
016            lem. In this work, we present the first case study in rigorously compressing
017            nonlinear feature-maps. We work in the classic setting of the modular addi-
018            tion models (Nanda et al., 2023), and target a non-vacuous bound on the be-
019            haviour of the ReLU MLP in time linear in the parameter-count of the circuit.
020
               To study the ReLU MLP analytically, we use the infinite-width lens, which turns
               post-activation matrix multiplications into approximate integrals. We discover
021
               a novel interpretation of the MLP layer in one-layer transformers implementing
022
               the “pizza” algorithm (Zhong et al., 2023): the MLP can be understood as evalu-
023            ating a quadrature scheme, where each neuron computes the area of a rectangle
024            under the curve of a trigonometric integral identity. Our code is available at
025            https://tinyurl.com/mod-add-integration.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Neural networks’ ability to generalize suggests they implement simpler, low-rank algorithms despite
032   performing high-dimensional computations (Olah et al., 2020). The field of mechanistic interpretabil-
033   ity has made tremendous progress in finding low-rank approximations of activations—for example,
034   discovering interpretable features (Bricken et al., 2023; Cunningham et al., 2023). However, finding
035   low-rank approximations of feature-maps—particularly MLP layers—is still an open problem (Elhage
036   et al., 2022).
037   Finding low-rank approximations of nonlinear feature-maps becomes particularly important in light of
038   recent work that found evidence that sparse linear features do not fully capture the structure of frontier
039   models (Marks et al., 2024; Engels et al., 2024). Interpretations that lack analyses of feature-maps
040   may not be usable for ambitious applications of mechanistic interpretability, like anomaly detection
041   and worst-case guarantees. The expressivity of MLPs constitutes a large attack surface for perturbing
042   the model, so not compressing feature-maps leaves a lot of free parameters in the interpretation.
043   These free parameters diminish our ability to detect anomalous behaviour, or make strong guarantees.
044   To illustrate the difficulty of compressing MLPs, consider the following toy model comparing the
045   effective parameter count of deep nonlinear networks and deep linear networks: adding or multiplying
046   k matrices of shape m×m. For linear operations, we need only m2 parameters to completely describe
047   the input-output behaviour, regardless of the depth of network. However, introducing nonlinearities
048   like ReLU between these operations increases the effective parameter count to km2 , with the
049   complexity growing exponentially with depth. While deep linear networks can be compressed to
050
      shallow networks of equivalent width without loss of expressivity, nonlinear networks resist such
      compression.
051
052   Even in the classical toy setting of modular addition models, the MLP layer is treated as a black box.
053   Nanda et al. (2023) finds sparse features to describe the input and output to the MLP layer, however,
      they do not tell us how the MLP layer processes the input features to generate the output features.

      In this work, we present a case study in compressing nonlinear feature-maps. We extend the analysis
      in Nanda et al. (2023); Zhong et al. (2023); Gromov (2023) to the MLP layer, opening up the final
070
      black box remaining in the modular addition models (§2). To demonstrate that compressing the
071
      feature-map is essential for reducing free parameters, we use the formal compression metric from
072   Gross et al. (2024). We measure compression by the computational complexity of verifying the
073   interpretation, where proving the same bound with a lower complexity budget would correspond to
074   finding an interpretation with fewer free parameters (§3).
075
      We apply an infinite-width lens, wherein we treat densely connected MLPs as finite approximations to
076
      an infinite-MLP-width limit (Appendix K). The infinite-width lens permits us to turn post-activation
077
      matrix multiplications into approximate integrals and study the remaining operations of the network
078   – including nonlinear operations – analytically. We find a low-rank approximation of the nonlinear
079   function implemented by the MLPs of the “pizza” transformers (Zhong et al., 2023): doubling
080   the frequencies of the input representations, that is, mapping from representations of the form
081   cos( k2 (a + b)), sin( k2 (a + b)) to cos(k(a + b)), sin(k(a + b)). Building on surprising patterns in the
082   phase-amplitude representation of the MLP pre-activations and neuron-logit map, we find that the
083   MLP can be understood as implementing a quadrature scheme for integrals of the form

089   We confirm this interpretation by creating non-vacuous bounds for the outputs of these MLPs in time

096   2     BACKGROUND
097
098   2.1   M ECHANISTIC I NTERPRETABILITY OF M ODULAR A DDITION M ODELS
099
100   Models trained on the modular addition task have become a classic testbed in the mechanistic
101   interpretability literature. Originally, Nanda et al. (2023) studied a one-layer transformer model. They
102   found low-rank features to describe all components of the model, and analysed the feature-map of the
103   final linear layer. While they generated a human-intuitive algorithm of how the model works, they
104
      did not explain how the MLP layers compute logits that fit with the form required by this algorithm.
      Despite the tremendous progress in analysing the non-MLP layers of the model, the ReLU MLP is
105
      still treated as a black-box.
106
107   Zhong et al. (2023) extended the analysis to a family of architectures parameterized by attention
      rate. The architecture from Nanda et al. (2023) corresponds to attention rate 0, while attention rate


                                                                    2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      1 corresponds to a ReLU MLP-only model, i.e. a transformer with constant attention. Depending
109   on attention rate, they showed that models may learn the “clock” or “pizza” algorithm. They
110   exhaustively enumerated inputs to MLP-only model and found a description of the feature-map.
111   However, exhaustive enumeration is not feasible for larger input sizes, and does not constitute an
112   insight-rich explanation. Thus, their approximation of the feature-map is equal to the feature-map
113   itself, failing to provide any compression.
114
      Gromov (2023) considered a cleaner version of the MLP-only architecture considered by Zhong
115   et al. (2023), and used quadratic activations instead of ReLU activations. They presented a formula
116   corresponding to a compressed feature-map. However, they did not present sufficient evidence to
117   show that the trained models follow the formula as suggested. They only showed that the weights
118   are roughly “single-frequency”, i.e. they are well approximated by the largest Fourier component.
119   Establishing that the model in fact uses the stated algorithm as opposed to a different algorithm would
120   require significantly more validation.
121   In this work, we analyze the ReLU MLP with the goal of demonstrating how it computes the functions
122   described in prior work, and establish this with rigorous evidence and a formal proof.
123

      2.3    T HE “P IZZA” ALGORITHM
152
153   Zhong et al. (2023) demonstrated that small transformers using constant attention implement modular
154   arithmetic using the following algorithm, which they call the “pizza” algorithm:
155
156          1. OV (a), OV (b) embed the one-hot encoded tokens a, b as (cos(ka), sin(ka)) and
157             (cos(kb), sin(kb)) for a small handful of key frequencies k
158          2. Before the MLP layer, the representation of the two tokens are averaged:
159             (cos(ka) + cos(ka), sin(ka) + sin(ka))/2 = cos(k(a − b)/2)(cos(k(a + b)/2), sin(k(a + b)/2))
160
             3. The network then uses the MLP layer to “double the frequencies”:

169   They distinguish this from the “clock” algorithm of Nanda et al. (2023), whose logits have no

173   In this work, we seek to understand how the MLP performs its role.

501   7    D ISCUSSION
502
503   We provide a first case study in rigorously compressing nonlinear feature-maps. We demonstrate
504   that interpreting feature-maps reveals additional insight about the model mechanism, even in models
505   that the research community assumes that we understand quite well. Our hope is that this work will
506
      inspire additional study of feature-maps in mechanistic interpretability.
507   The key steps in our derivation of efficient bounds were: splitting the input into orthogonal output-
508   relevant and output-irrelevant directions; decomposing the pre-activations as a product of functions
509   of these axes; reindexing the neurons by the output-relevant direction so that the reindexed post-
510   activations depend only on the output-irrelevant directions; and compressing independently over the
511   output-relevant direction term and the output-irrelevant direction term. We believe that some of these
512
      steps could be adapted to interpret other MLPs, even those where we cannot derive closed-form
      analytic representations. At the same time, the bounds we derive in Appendix H could be improved
513
      to understand how the network is allocating boxes under the curve, which we hope to address with
514
      future work.

1622
       There are several hurdles to replicating this approach to interpreting other neural networks. It is
1623   highly labour intensive, and requires extensive mathematical exploration. Thus, in order for the
1624   approach to have any practical use, we need to develop automated tools to make these approximations
1625   and interpretations.
1626
       For example, we may want to use the first few terms of the Fourier expansion (or other low-rank
1627
       approximations) to approximate the action of various layers in a neural network, and then combine
1628
       those to get algebraic expressions for certain neuron outputs of interest. Such algebraic expressions
1629   will natural admit phenomena like the numerical integration we described above. This sort of method
1630   may be particularly fruitful on problems which Fourier transforms play a large role, such as signal
1631   processing and solutions to partial differential equations.

       M     F UTURE TECHNICAL WORK
1635
       To complete the technical work laid out in section 7, we must accomplish two tasks which we discuss
1636
       in this appendix section: constructing a parameterisation of the MLP which is checkable in less than
1637
       O(p · dmlp ) time, and more generally constructing a parameterisation of the entire ‘pizza’ model that
1638   is checkable in time that is linear in the number of parameters; and establishing a bound on the error
1639   in the model’s logits that does not neglect any terms.
1640
1641   M.1    L INEAR PARAMETERISATION
1642
1643   Constructing a parameterisation of the model which is checkable in less than O(p · dmlp ) time is a
1644   relatively straightforward task, given the interpretation in the body of the paper. We expect that the
1645   parameters are:
1646
              • A choice of nfreq frequencies ki .
1647
1648          • A splitting of the neurons into groups by frequency, and an ordering of the neurons within
1649            each group.
1650          • An assignment of widths wi to each neuron, and an assignment of angles ϕi to each neuron.
1651          • An assignment of orthogonal planes into which each frequency is embedded by the embed-
1652            ding matrix, and by the unembedding matrix.
1653
              • Rotations and scaling of the low-rank subset of the hidden model dimension for each of the
1654            O and V matrices.
1655
1656
       M.2    B OUNDING THE ERROR OF THE MLP
1657
1658   To bound the error of our interpretation of the MLP precisely, we’d need to include a bound on the
1659   primary frequency contribution of the identity component (which integrates to 0 symbolically), and
1660   include bounds on the residual components – OVE on x and y, the MLP bias, and the embed of y, as
1661   inputs to ReLU; and UOVE on x and y and UE on y as output logits.
1662   We could decompose every matrix in our model as a sum of the corresponding matrix from our
1663   parameterized model and a noise term. Expanding out the resulting expression for the logits (and
1664   expanding |x + ε| as |x| + (|x + ε| − |x|)), we will have an expression which is at top-level a sum
1665   of our parameterized model result and a noise term which is expressed recursively as the difference
1666   between the actual model and the parameterized model. We can then ask two questions:
1667
1668         1. What worst-case bounds can we prove on the error terms at various complexities?
1669         2. What are the empirical worst-case bounds on the relevant error terms?
