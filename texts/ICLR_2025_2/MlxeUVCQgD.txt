009                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011             Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) has recently gained traction as a promising
012             approach to align large models with human feedback. It is notable for its effec-
013             tiveness and ease of application across various models, including Large Language
014             Models (LLMs) and Diffusion Models (DMs). However, the quality of preference
015             data used in DPO training has been largely overlooked. Current datasets, whether
016
                annotated by deep learning metrics or crowd-sourced human judgments, often
                contain noisy labels. This noise can adversely affect the performance of DPO. To
017
                address this issue, we propose a novel approach that incorporates a noise-aware
018
                metric into the DPO objective. This metric, which includes intra-annotator confi-
019             dence and inter-annotator stability, helps identify and mitigate the impact of noisy
020             data. We introduce an Adaptive-DPO loss function which improves the DPO loss
021             in two ways: one aims to reduce the influence of noisy samples, while the other
022             is to amplify the impact of clean samples. Our experiments demonstrate that this
023             method effectively handles both synthetic and natural noisy data, leading to im-
024             proved performance in visual and textual generation tasks. This underscores the
025             practical value of our approach in enhancing model robustness amidst noisy pref-
026             erence data.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) has recently been one of the most convenient and effective
031   Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) methods. DPO aims to align the model
032   with human feedback by directly modeling the output of target model as an implicit representation
033   of rewards indicating preference. Its straightforward formulation and robust performance have led
034   to successful applications across a range of large-scale models, including Large Language Models
035   (LLMs) Rafailov et al. (2024) and Diffusion Models (DMs) Wallace et al. (2024).
036   While numerous studies have followed Direct Preference Optimization (DPO), an often-overlooked
037   aspect of the training process is actually the preference data used for training. Most existing pref-
038   erence datasets are annotated in two primary ways: through metrics generated by deep learning
039   models like PickScore Kirstain et al. (2024) or by human annotators via crowd-sourcing. However,
040   machine annotators often produce biased labels due to their limited generalization ability. In con-
041   trast, human annotators provide the assessments of preference from subjective perspective, such as
042   the subjective visual property “which image is better” Fu et al. (2016). Unfortunately, inaccuracies
043   from careless or malicious annotators Kittur et al. (2008) can diminish dataset quality Chen et al.
044
      (2013); Long et al. (2013). Additionally, the lack of standardized judgment criteria leads to uninten-
      tional human errors, suggesting that existing preference datasets intrinsically contain noisy labels1 .
045
      This is illustrated in Fig.1, which presents both visual and textual examples from Pick-a-pick and
046
      HH-RLHF, highlighting the challenges in determining preference. For instance, in Fig. 1(a), the left
047   image shows a large ice cream, while the right image, despite also featuring a sizable ice cream,
048   is of overall higher quality. This ambiguity implies the difficulty in making definitive preferences
049   between samples.
050
      DPO intuitively seeks to learn the underlying rules of preference, which can be quite flexible. How-
051
      ever, biased labels in these preferences may divert the learned rules from our intended outcomes, po-

      tentially leading to significant consequences. For instance, if users aim to avoid copyright-protected
055   symbols to evade costly penalties, such deviations can create serious problems. This raises an im-
056   portant question: “Is DPO truly affected by noisy or biased data in the training set, and if so, how
057   can we address this issue?”
058
      To explore this, we present a comprehensive study. First, we conduct two pilot experiments using
059
      diffusion models to demonstrate that preference datasets contain noise, which can negatively impact
060
      DPO performance. To address this issue, we propose modifying the DPO objective by implementing
061   a novel noise-aware metric. Specifically, we aim to mitigate the adverse effects of noisy preference
062   data by neglecting or downweighting biased preference samples during the DPO training process.
063
064
      We begin by analyzing the DPO objective, discovering that models fine-tuned with DPO can func-
      tion as implicit preference predictors. Building on this insight, we develop a noise-aware metric
065
      consisting of two components to help identify potential noisy data: intra-annotator confidence and
066
      inter-annotator stability. Intra-annotator confidence assesses the difficulty of predicting a sample,
067   which can be instantiated using the DPO loss. Conversely, inter-annotator stability measures pre-
068   diction fluctuations among different annotators by calculating the variance of their assessments for
069   each sample.
070
      Building on the proposed metric, we further propose an Adaptive-DPO loss function that enhances
071
      the original DPO loss in two significant ways. First, it reduces the influence of noisy samples by
072
      reweighting the objective based on the noise-aware metric, effectively mitigating the adverse effects
073   of label noise. Second, an adaptive margin is incorporated into the objective, encouraging the model
074   to prioritize learning from clean samples and enabling it to better leverage reliable annotations.
075
076
      To show the effectiveness of our proposed method, we conduct extensive experiments on both LLMs
      and DMs. The results indicate that our approach effectively addresses synthetic noisy preference
077
      data, with models fine-tuned using our method outperforming those fine-tuned with the original
078
      DPO on clean data. Moreover, our method proves effective against the naturally occurring noise
079   present in existing preference datasets, significantly enhancing both visual and textual generation
080   quality compared to the original DPO, thereby highlighting the practical value of our approach that
081   refines DPO for improved model robustness and reliability across various applications. In summary,
082   the contributions of this work are as follows:
083   1)Investigating the Impact of Noise in Preference Data: We investigate the issue of noisy labels
084   in preference datasets, highlighting their detrimental effects on DPO model performance. Pilot
085   experiments with diffusion models demonstrate the negative impact of this noise, highlighting the
086   need for effective solutions.
087
      2)Development of a Noise-Aware Metric: A novel noise-aware metric is proposed, incorporating
      two components—intra-annotator confidence and inter-annotator stability—to assess and identify
088
      potentially noisy data, thereby informing the training process.
089
      3)Adaptive-DPO Loss Function: We propose an Adaptive-DPO loss function that reweights the
090   training objective based on the noise-aware metric. It aims to reduce the influence of noisy samples
091   and encourages the model to prioritize learning from high-quality, reliable annotations.
092   4)Performance Enhancement: Extensive experiments show that our method effectively mitigates
093   synthetic and natural noisy preference data, outperforming DPO in terms of both visual and textual
094   generation.

201   3.2   I S DPO V ULNERABLE TO N OISY DATA ?
202
      After verifying the existence of noisy labels in preference data, another important problem is whether
203
      DPO can be affected by these data. If DPO is rather robust against noise, then finetuning LLMs and
204   DMs with current preference datasets is reasonable enough. To testify this, we conduct an additional
205   pilot study based on DiffusionDPO using Pick-a-pic v2. Since we cannot detect the noisy data, we
206   propose to manually flip part of the original preference labels. As the proportion used for flipping
207   getting larger, such an operation can result in datasets with different noisy levels. For both SD1.5
208   and SDXL, we randomly flip 10%, 20% and 30% data, train them with original DiffusionDPO, and
209   record several metrics such as ImageReward, PickScore, Aesthetic Score and HPS.
210   As presented in Tab. 1, we can find that with the proportion of noisy labels increasing, the fine-tuning
211   process exhibits a significant decline in its efficacy, eventually resulting in a total deterioration of
212   the model’s performance. Specifically, for SD1.5, 30% noise can even lead to negative image re-
213   ward. This indicates that the DPO algorithm can be easily affected by the noisy labels contained in
214   the training data. Moreover, it is noteworthy that our manually created noisy labels are intrinsically
215   different from the real-case noisy labels. While our randomly selected noisy data forms a uniform
      distribution, the distribution of real noisy data could be related with many factors, such as the dif-

222   4.1   M EASURING N OISE BASED ON P REFERENCE
223
      For the training process of DPO, the supervision information comes from the preference labelling.
224   As mentioned in Sec. 3.1, the labelling process of the preference data is not always reliable, which
225   could lead to unexpected noisy label. As a consequence, the efficacy of DPO would be significantly
226   affected to be worse. To solve this problem, let us first consider the preference indicators as the labels
227   of a binary classification problem, i.e. preferred samples belong to the first class and unpreferred
228   ones belong to the second class. If we assume that the data is not too noisy for the model to learn
229   a decent rule, then through training a binary classifier with such data, there would be three possible
230   phenomena caused by noisy label.
231
232
             • Firstly, consider the situation that one data pair that is mistakenly labeled but cannot provide
               practical supervision to the model to be finetuned. In this way, the model can learn to
233
               predict the true preference. Consequently, the gap between the prediction, i.e. the bias and
234
               the given label would stay significant along the training process.
235
236
             • On the contrary, consider the situation that one data pair that is mistakenly labeled and the
               model can learn the mistaken information easily. In this situation, the pair can hardly be
237
               detected.
238
239          • Finally, if the mistakenly-labeled data pair does affect the finetuning process, models fine-
240
               tuned with different randomness, e.g. various shuffling of the mini-batch sequence, would
               lead to quite unstable predictions regarding these pairs. That is to say, the variance related
241
               to model randomness would be high.
242
243   From the above analysis we can find that if the bias and variance can be well measured, then we
244   can deal with the noisy samples from the first and third case. Therefore the problem is transformed
245   to how to instantiate the two measures as concrete metrics. A straightforward method is to directly
246   relabel a part of the noisy dataset and train a binary classifier based on these data. Then the two terms
247   can be defined according to the output of the classifier. However, relabeling the preference data by
248   human annotator wastes a lot of manpower and material resources, thus being almost impossible
249   for larger datasets. Moreover, such a annotation process would again introduce new noise. Besides,
250
      the binary classifier would require specific design for different input modalities such as images and
      texts, thus introducing extra complexity.
251

253   that Eq. 5 can be rewritten as:

650
      Based on our proposed Adaptive-DPO, future work can focus on exploring more comprehensive
651
      analyses about the noise in the preference data. Investigating the underlying principles of this ap-
652
      proach could offer deeper insights into its mechanics, which may lead to more targeted enhance-
653   ments and wider applicability across different domains. Understanding the theoretical foundations
654   will also contribute to fine-tuning the parameter space for even better performance under different
655   conditions, further reinforcing the method’s adaptability and robustness.

658   B      R ELATED W ORK
659
660   Aligning large scale models. Due to the storage and computational limitation in RLHF, several al-
661   ternative approaches have been proposed. Each method uses a different loss function. DPO Rafailov
662   et al. (2024) optimizes BCE loss to learn policy. SLiC uses a hinge loss plus regularization loss Zhao
663   et al. (2023). IPO Azar et al. (2024) use squared losses. RRHF Yuan et al. (2023) uses a ranking
664   loss plus SFT loss. RSO Liu et al. (2023) uses a method of BCE loss plus rejection sampling. As
665
      for diffusion models, D3PO Yang et al. (2023) combines DPO with Diffusion Model successfully,
      and DiffusionDPO Yang et al. (2023) effectively integrates the optimization objective of DPO into
666
      the denoising process of Diffusion Model.
667
668
669   Learning from noisy labels (LNL). Training a more robust model using dataset with noisy labels
670
      is the target of LNL. Methods employed include robust algorithm and noisy label detection. Robust
      algorithm designs specific modules to ensure that the network can be well trained from the noise data
671
      set which includes the construction of robust networks such as Xiao et al. (2015); Chen & Gupta
672
      (2015), robust loss functions like Ghosh et al. (2017). rDPO Chowdhury et al. (2024) uses a robust
673   loss function to improve the model’s resistance to the noisy label. Wang et al. (2024) provided an
674   insight of why noisy label influence reward model, and give their approaches to solve it.
