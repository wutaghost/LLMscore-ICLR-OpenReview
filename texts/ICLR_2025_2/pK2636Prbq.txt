011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Radiologists play a crucial role by translating medical images into actionable re-
014            ports. However, the field faces staffing shortages and increasing workloads. While
015            automated approaches using vision-language models (VLMs) show promise as
016
               assistants, they require exceptionally high accuracy. Most current VLMs in ra-
               diology rely solely on supervised fine-tuning (SFT). Meanwhile, in the general
017
               domain, additional preference fine-tuning has become standard practice. The chal-
018
               lenge in radiology lies in the prohibitive cost of obtaining radiologist feedback. To
019            address this challenge, we propose an automated pipeline for preference feedback,
020            focusing on chest X-ray (CXR) report generation. Our method leverages publicly
021            available datasets containing pairs of images and radiologist-written reference re-
022            ports with an LLM-as-a-Judge mechanism, eliminating the need for additional ra-
023            diologist feedback. We evaluate and benchmark five direct alignment algorithms.
024            Our results show up to a 57.4% improvement in average GREEN scores, a LLM-
025            based metric for evaluating CXR reports, compared to the SFT baseline. We study
026            reward overoptimization via length exploitation, with reports lengthening by up to
027
               3.2x. To assess a potential alignment tax, we benchmark on six additional diverse
               tasks, finding no significant degradations. A reader study involving four board-
028
               certified radiologists indicates win rates of up to 0.62 over the SFT baseline, and
029
               macro-averaged F1 scores improve by up to 6.7%, highlighting the utility of our
030            approach.

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035
      X-rays are one of the most frequently collected imaging studies in clinical practice, with the advan-
036   tages of wide availability, cost-effectiveness, and low radiation dose. Chest X-rays (CXR) are used
037   for diverse purposes in clinical practice, with approximately 1.4 billion diagnostic X-ray examina-
038   tions collected per year in the world (PAHO, 2012; Organization et al., 2016; Cid et al., 2024). The
039   amount and significance of CXRs can pose a burden for radiologists and a potential negative impact
040   for patients without timely interpretation, especially for those containing critical lesions (Ruutiainen
041   et al., 2013; Hanna et al., 2017; Bruls & Kwee, 2020; Bhargavan et al., 2002; Lyon et al., 2015;
042   Rimmer, 2017).
043   Recent strides in generative vision-language models (VLMs) hold promising implications for this
044   high-stakes and low-data field (Liu et al., 2024; Radford et al., 2021). Typically pre-trained using
045   image-text contrastive learning and supervised fine-tuned using causal language modeling (a.k.a.
046   next-token prediction), recent VLMs have started to demonstrate promising performance in CXR
047   interpretation (Chen et al., 2024; Bannur et al., 2024). In high-stakes fields like radiology, where
048   accurate medical descriptions directly influence disease diagnosis and treatment decisions, the gen-
049   erated outputs must maintain high factual accuracy to ensure patient safety.
050   However, recent studies have shown that supervised fine-tuning (SFT) might be insufficient in
051   the post-training process. For example, Hong et al. (2024) illustrate the limitation of SFT
052   by training on a preference dataset, containing “good” and “bad” completions. By tracking
053   the log probabilities of each during the course of training, they show that the log probabil-
      ities of the bad completions inadvertently increase alongside the good completions. Prefer-

125   2.1       V ISION - LANGUAGE MODELS
126
      Vision-language models (Radford et al., 2021; Li et al., 2021; 2022; 2023; Liu et al., 2024) are a
127
      multi-modal extension to LLMs. In this setting, the prompt x contains images and/or text. Typical
128
      tasks include Vision Question Answering (VQA) and image captioning (e.g., report generation in the
129   field of radiology). There are also a line of works to extend VLMs to the medical domain (Thawkar
130   et al., 2023; Hyland et al., 2023; Chaves et al., 2024) which mainly focus on CXR interpretation due
131   to the wide availability of public datasets (Johnson et al., 2019; Chambon et al., 2024). However,
132   even with strong LLMs and vision-backbones, VLMs have been observed to “hallucinate” and pro-
133   duce outputs that are not factually grounded in the image (Zhou et al., 2024). Such hallucinations
134   represent a significant risk in high-stakes healthcare fields such as radiology. Similar to Zhou et al.
135   (2024), we pose the problem of hallucinations as an alignment problem and propose tackling it via
136   preference fine-tuning.

      2.3   R EWARD OVEROPTIMIZATION
171
172   The reward model in equation 1 is learned, and therefore an imperfect proxy of the ground truth
173   reward R∗ . As this proxy is optimized, ground truth performance might saturate or even deteriorate.2
174   This reward overoptimization, or hacking, phenomena was first studied in Gao et al. (2023) for
175   RLHF. The KL divergence term in equation 1 is included explicitly to mitigate this issue, but has
176   proven insufficient (Gao et al., 2023). Despite not fitting an explicit reward model, similar behavior
177   has been observed empirically for DAAs (Rafailov et al., 2024).
178   Length exploitation, the tendency to learn to produce excessively verbose completions, is one
179   common dimension of reward overoptimization, observed in both RLHF and for DAAs. For in-
180   stance, Park et al. (2024) showed that DPO amplifies verbosity bias embedded in the preference
181   data. In this work, we explore this phenomenon in the context of preference fine-tuning of medical
182   VLMs.

185
186
      In this section, we present our experimental setup. We structure the paper around the following re-
187   search questions: (i) How do different alignment algorithms compare on the CXR report generation
188   task? (ii) To what extent is there an alignment tax? (iii) How does the aligned policy compare to the
189   SFT baseline from a clinical perspective?
190
191   3.1   DATASET AND BASELINE
192
193   Dataset We use the MIMIC-CXR (Johnson et al., 2019) dataset for training, validation and testing.
194   The image-report pairs consists of one or two CXRs and the corresponding free-text findings section.
195   The reports describe the findings in the image at a static timepoint (a single image or two images
196   from the same timepoint) or describe findings using also a prior image (two images from different
197   timepoints). Radiology reports usually include information from prior timepoints in the clinic, but
198
      this remains understudied in the context of automated CXR report generations using VLMs.
199   To limit the computational burden, we randomly sample 80k examples as our training data. To test
200   robustness for the CXR report generation task, we additionally include test data from the CheXpert
201   Plus (Chambon et al., 2024) dataset. To evaluate whether there is an alignment tax, we additionally
202   evaluate our aligned models on six tasks different from CXR report generation: view classification,
203   coarse-grained image classification, single disease identification, multi disease identification, VQA,
204
      and image-text reasoning, using test data from five additional datasets RSNA (Shih et al., 2019),
      SIIM (American College of Radiology, 2019), OpenI (Demner-Fushman et al., 2016), SLAKE (Liu
205
      et al., 2021), and Rad-Restruct (Pellegrini et al., 2023) datasets.
206
207   Baseline We adopt CheXagent (Chen et al., 2024) as a representative example of a state-of-the-art,
208   open source, VLM for CXR interpretation. It has been trained in the canonical way by first adapting
209   the LLM to medical text by continued pre-training. Second, a vision encoder was adapted via vision
210
      pre-training, using contrastive learning on CXR image-text pairs. Third, the two modalities were
      merged by training a vision-language bridger, or adapter network, keeping the LLM and vision
211
      encoder frozen. Finally, the model was instruction tuned. In addition, CheXagent is of average size,
212
      8B, for an open source model, providing a good balance between computational complexity and
213   performance.

      3.2   P REFERENCE DATA
217
218   Expert human feedback from radiologists is the gold standard for preference data generation and
219   evaluation in CXR report generation. However, scaling is impractical due to the limited availability
220   of radiologists for large-scale annotation tasks. In the general domain, leveraging LLMs for cost
221   effective preference data generation has been proposed (Bai et al., 2022b; Dubois et al., 2023; Lee
222   et al., 2024). Zheng et al. (2023), focusing on the related task of automated evaluation, introduced the
223   terminology of “LLM-as-a-Judge” and categorized evaluation methods into pairwise, single answer,
224   and reference-guided grading. In the general domain, pairwise grading is the most common both for
225   preference data generation Dubois et al. (2023); Lee et al. (2024) and evaluation Zheng et al. (2023);
226
      Dubois et al. (2024).
227   These existing methods, however, are tailored for uni-modal, general-domain LLMs and do not
228   directly apply to our multi-modal setting, which involves both visual and textual data. Moreover,
229   factual grounding is essential in medical report generation to ensure clinical reliability. To overcome
230   these challenges, we propose using reference-guided grading, leveraging publicly available datasets
231   that contain paired prompts—including images—and radiologist-written reference reports. This
232
      abundance of high-quality references allows us to provide factually grounded annotations without
      the need for a multi-modal Judge, setting our approach apart from prior studies with multi-modal
233
      Judges, or reward models, such as Sun et al. (2024).
234
235   GREEN (Ostmeier et al., 2024) is a state-of-the-art metric for radiology report evaluation, based on
236   a single answer reference-guided LLM-as-a-Judge mechanism. While no metric is perfect, GREEN
237   better reflect radiologist preferences than general domain metrics such as BLEU (Papineni et al.,
238
      2002), ROUGE (Lin, 2004), and the BERTScore (Zhang et al., 2019), as well as radiology specific
      metrics such as F1RadGraph (Jain et al., 2021). Hence, we treat GREEN as the silver standard,
239
      employing it as a low-cost approximation of expert human judgment.

256   3.3   A LIGNMENT ALGORITHMS
257
258   Due to compute constraints, we only consider offline DAAs and leave their online counterpart as
259   well as on-policy RL algorithms to future work. However, even when restricting the focus to offline
260   DAAs, there are more methods available than would be feasible to include. Hence, we choose
261   representative algorithms from different categories. DPO is the original DAA and serves as our
262   baseline. In addition to DPO, we consider:
263
264

        alized preference, relaxing the assumption of the Bradley-Terry model. The authors argue that
265
        this helps mitigate over-fitting issues observed in DPO even when preferences are transitive. Rel-
266
        atively recent work has shown that IPO indeed seems to be less prone to reward overoptimiza-
267     tion (Rafailov et al., 2024).
268
269   • Kahneman-Tversky optimization (KTO) (Ethayarajh et al., 2024) as an example of a DAA that
        does not require preference pairs, but instead only binary feedback on whether a completion is

          desirable or undesirable. This type of data is much more ubiquitous in practice. In addition, for
271       any given dataset of preference pairs KTO provides twice the number of examples.

275

502   5    L IMITATIONS AND DISCUSSION
503
504   Due to compute constraints, our work focuses on a single model, CheXagent (Chen et al., 2024).
505   Other VLMs, from different families and sizes, may behave differently. For example, it is possible
506   that DPO yielded nonsensical results, increased verbosity with no clinical utility, due to a insuffi-
507   ciently strong baseline–despite being a state-of-the-art model. We do counteract this point, however,
508   by including a range of DAAs with varying sensitivity to the strength of the SFT baseline. Nonethe-
509
      less, to further validate the results in this study, evaluating another VLM is warranted.
510   Moreover, based on previous work (Ostmeier et al., 2024), we treat GREEN as the silver standard,
511   effectively a low-cost approximation of expert human judgment. However, we have observed that
512   verbosity bias is a significant issue. Thus, further work, including considering length-controlled
513   metrics, is necessary. One simple updated to the GREEN metric worth exploring is as follows:
514   GREEN-LC = GREEN / max(length of generated report/length of reference report,1). Where LC
515
      refers to it being length-controlled. Intuitively, this down weights GREEN when the length of the
      generated report is larger than that of the reference. If this is not the case, then the correction does
516
      nothing. Although very simplistic, such a correction will allow us to deal with the apparent trade off
517
      between average GREEN and verbosity. In addition, further investigation of length-controlled align-
518   ment algorithms, such as length-controlled DPO (Park et al., 2024), would be helpful to decouple
519   length and quality of the generated reports. The issue of potential biases extends beyond verbosity,
520   as there might be other societal biases, with regards to for instance race and sex, embedded in the
521   data or the Judge. These biases should be carefully studied and mitigated.
522
      In addition, our hyperparameter search in non-exhaustive and it is possible that the relative ranking
523
      of the methods considered would change with a more extensive search. Finally, we restrict ourselves
524   to only offline DAAs. This leaves out a range of very competitive alignment algorithms, including
525   on-policy RL algorithms, such as PPO (Schulman et al., 2017) and REINFORCE Williams (1992),
526   as well as the online, or iterative, counterparts to the DAAs considered.

      6    C ONCLUSION
530
      Our study highlights the significant potential of including preference fine-tuning in the post-training
531
      pipeline of medical VLMs. Using our approach to preference data generation, we have shown that
532
      DAAs can substantially improve AI-generated reports in clinically meaningful ways without addi-
533   tional radiologist feedback. Results indicate maintained performance on diverse tasks, suggesting
534   no alignment tax. The preference of aligned policies by board-certified radiologists and improve-
535   ments in clinical efficacy metrics, highlight the clinical value of our method. Our systematic analysis
536   yields actionable insights for preference alignment of medical VLMs, paving the way for more ac-
537   curate AI assistance in radiology, potentially addressing workforce shortages and improving patient
538   care.

       To further build intuition, we illustrate the resulting distributions of length in Fig. 5. Consistent
