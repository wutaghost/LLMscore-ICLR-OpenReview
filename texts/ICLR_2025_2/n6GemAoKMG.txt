                                                    A BSTRACT
008
009
               Even a slight perturbation in the graph structure can cause a significant drop in the
010
               accuracy of graph neural networks (GNNs). Most existing attacks leverage gradient
011
               information to perturb edges. This relaxes the attack’s optimization problem from
012            a discrete to a continuous space, resulting in solutions far from optimal. It also
013            restricts the adaptability of the attack to non-differentiable objectives. Instead,
014            we propose an evolutionary-based algorithm to solve the discrete optimization
015            problem directly. Our Evolutionary Attack (EvA) works with any black-box
016            model and objective, eliminating the need for a differentiable proxy loss. This
017            permits us to design two novel attacks that: reduce the effectiveness of robustness
018            certificates and break conformal sets. We introduce a sparse encoding that results
019            in memory complexity that is linear in the attack budget. EvA reduces the accuracy
020
               by an additional ∼11% on average compared to the best previous attack, revealing
               significant untapped potential in designing attacks.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
025
      Given the widespread applications of graph neural networks (GNNs), studying their robustness to
026
      natural and adversarial noise is of great importance. In node classification, GNNs leverage the edge
027
      structure between data points to improve their performance. However, a small perturbation in the
028   graph structure (adding or removing a few edges) can significantly reduce GNNs’ accuracy, even
029   below the performance of an MLP (which completely discards the structure). Similar to images
030   and continuous data, most of the proposed (structure) attacks are gradient-based. They compute the
031   gradients of a loss w.r.t. the adjacency matrix and apply a perturbation according to that. Gradient-
032   based attacks face several challenges. They solve a relaxation of the original combinatorial (discrete)
033   optimization problem – the entries of the adjacency matrix are relaxed from {0, 1} to [0, 1]. They
034   need a differentiable proxy loss function since the actual objective of the attacker (e.g. accuracy) is
035   often not differentiable, and the usual proxy such cross-entropy is suboptimal (Geisler et al., 2023).
036
      They assume white-box access to the model, including the structure and the weights. This limits the
      applicability or requires surrogate models. They can provide a false sense of security since defenses
037
      may be obfuscating gradients (Athalye et al., 2018; Geisler et al., 2023) and can get stuck in local
038
      minima. Their memory complexity grows quadratically w.r.t the number of nodes. Although the
039   adjacency matrix is often sparse, the gradients w.r.t. it are not. As a result, tricks like block coordinate
040   descent are needed (Geisler et al., 2021). We propose a model-agnostic evolutionary attack (EvA)
041   that fixes all five of the above issues.
042
      EvA explores the space of possible perturbations with a genetic algorithm (GA). Our approach
043
      operates in the discrete space of potential perturbations without information from gradients – avoiding
044
      relaxation. It directly optimizes the objective (like accuracy) as long as it provides a meaningful
045   signal. In addition to eliminating the need for a differentiable proxy, this black-box access to the
046   objective enables us to define a broader class of attacks. In fact, it allowed us to easily design two
047   novel attacks on graphs that aim at decreasing the effectiveness of robustness certificates or that break
048   conformal guarantees. EvA shows outstanding effectiveness on vanilla and adversarially trained
049   models compared to SOTA attacks. Unlike the gradient-based attacks, our attack has a O(ϵ · E)
050   memory complexity where ϵ is the perturbation budget, and E is the number of edges. This is because
051   instead of storing a squared block of gradients, which scales with the size of the adjacency matrix,
052   we only store the edge perturbations as an index. During the evaluation, we also maintain the same
053
      sparsity in representation as the graph itself. To take advantage of the available free memory, we
      employ a batch evaluation approach that speeds up the optimization.

073   Certificates provide a robustness guarantee that the prediction will not change given a limited set of
074   possible perturbations (e.g., at most ra additions and rd deletions). The certified ratio is the fraction
075
      of nodes for which the guarantee holds. Here, we define the attacker’s objective as decreasing the

076

077

078   any model’s output to prediction sets with a guarantee to cover the true label with (adjustable) high

081   these two new objectives, the amount of work is nontrivial since there are many non-differentiable
082   components that would need to be relaxed. In contrast, for EvA, designing a new attack is simply a
083   matter of changing the fitness function of the GA.
084   Importantly, perhaps the main contributions of this work is to highlight a scarcely explored research
085   direction for attacks. Even off-the-shelf genetic algorithms significantly outperform gradient-based
086   attacks. Fig. 1 compares EvA to the other attacks proposed over time. Our custom adaptive mutation
087   further improves performance, but we argue that the space of evolutionary (and more broadly search-
088   based) attacks has a lot of untapped potential.

      Problem setup. We focus on attacking the semi-supervised node classification task on graphs via
094   perturbing a small number of edges. Formally, we are given a graph G = (X, A, y) in which X is
095   the features matrix assigning a feature vector xi to each node vi in the graph, A is the adjacency
096   matrix (often sparse) that represents the set of edges E, and y is the partially observable vector of
097   labels. Nodes are partitioned into labeled and unlabeled sets V = Vl ∪ Vu . The GNN is trained on an
098   observed subgraph Gtr that includes the labeled nodes. Gosch et al. (2024) argue that the transductive
099   setup, where Gtr = G is unrealistic since perfect robustness can be achieved by memorizing the
100   training graph. Therefore, we mainly focus on the inductive setting where a model f is trained on an
101   induced subgraph Gtr ⊆ G, validated on Gval ⊆ G and tested on Gtest where Gtr ⊂ Gval ⊂ Gtest = G.
102   Threat model. Our goal is to find a perturbation matrix P ∈ {0, 1}n×n that flips entities of the
103   adjacency matrix Ã = A ⊕ P to decrease the accuracy as much as possible. Here n = |V|, and ⊕ is
104   thePelement-wise XOR operator. For a given function f as the GNN model, the accuracy is defined
105   as vi ∈Vatt (1/|Vatt |) · 1[f (G)vi = yi ] where Vatt is the set of nodes that we attack. In global
106   attacks this is usually the test nodes, while in targeted attacks the target is a single node. To keep the
107   perturbations imperceptible, we assume that the adversary can only perturb up to δ := ϵ · |E[Vatt : V]|
      edges where E[A : B] is the subset of edges between nodes in A and B. Formally, for any generic

      Gradient-based attacks. A common approach to attack the graph structure is to compute the gradient
      of the loss function w.r.t. the adjacency matrix. This requires a relaxation on the domain of A from
118
      {0, 1}n×n to [0, 1]n×n . If the loss function is not differentiable (e.g., accuracy), then a differentiable
119
      surrogate like the categorical cross entropy or tanh-margin (Geisler et al., 2023) is used instead.
120   We compute the derivatives of the loss w.r.t. A and update the perturbation matrix. Finally, the
121   edges are either sampled or rounded from the perturbation matrix, which returns the solution to the
122   binary domain. There are various tricks to improve gradient-based attacks, but most follow a similar
123   high-level procedure.
124
      Related Work. Adversarial attacks on graphs are generally divided into two main categories: evasion
125
      attacks Xu et al. (2019); Zügner et al. (2018); Geisler et al. (2023); Gosch et al. (2024), where the
126   attacker perturbs the graph after the model has been trained, and poisoning attacks Zügner et al.
127   (2020); Lingam et al. (2023); Zügner et al. (2018), where the attacker modifies the graph prior to
128   training. These attacks can be further classified into global attacks (e.g., Geisler et al. (2023); Zhu
129   et al. (2023)), which target multiple node predictions simultaneously, and targeted attacks, which
130   focus on a single node or a subset of nodes. The manipulations can involve altering node attributes,
131   modifying edge structures, or introducing malicious nodes. The earliest adversarial attacks on graphs
132   were inspired by techniques used on continuous data, utilizing gradients to approximate perturbations
133   on inherently discrete edges Xu et al. (2019); Zügner et al. (2018); Geisler et al. (2023). Additionally,
134
      reinforcement learning has been employed as an alternative approach to execute adversarial attacks
      Dai et al. (2018). Attackers leverage reinforcement learning algorithms to refine their attack strategies
135
      and disrupt the learning process of GNNs Sun et al. (2023). Although some new attacks have been
136
      proposed in recent years (e.g., by Zhang et al. (2024; 2023); Wang et al. (2023)), they are all based
137   on some traditional algorithms (like gradient-based methods).

140   3    E VA: E VOLUTIONARY ATTACK
141
142   Our evolutionary-based attack (EvA) uses a genetic algorithm (Holland, 1984) as a heuristic to directly
143   optimize Eq. 1. We define an initial set of possible (candidate) perturbations – called “population” –
144   and iteratively improve this population. In each iteration, the individuals in the population are ordered
145
      based on their fitness, specifically in terms of how much each individual decreases the accuracy. We
      draft the next population by keeping the best individuals and producing new ones as a function of
146
      them. Our population for the next iteration is finalized after a mutation which introduces additional
147
      randomness that helps with exploration. Each element in the population is a possible perturbation,
148   which is encoded as a vector of indices where an edge is flipped.
149
150   Genetic algorithm (GA) in EvA. We can define a genetic solver through the definition of four
151
      main components. (i) Population: It is a set of feasible answers to the problem which gradually
      improve over iterations. In our case each population element is one potential perturbation on the
152
      adjacency matrix. We define mapping Π : xi,t ∈ [ n2 (n − 1)]δ 7→ [n]2 which is an enumeration
153
      on the upper triangle of the n × n adjacency matrix. With that, we define each candidate as set
154   si,t ∈ [ n2 (n − 1)]δ which refers to a perturbation. The corresponding perturbation matrix Pi,t is
155
      simply defined as Pi,t [p, q] = Pi,t [q, p] = 1 ⇔ ∃j : si,t [j] = Π−1 (p, q) for p < q. (ii) Fitness:
156   Is a notion of how close to optimal each population element is. Given any loss function L we
157   define the fitness function fit : [ n2 (n − 1)]δ 7→ R, as fit(s) = L(X, A ⊕ Ps , y). Note that this
158   objective can be non-differentiable, such as accuracy. As long as the loss function has enough
159   sensitivity to differentiate between various individuals, we use it directly as the fitness (see § 4 for
160   extended discussion). (iii) Crossover: Is an operation that defines a new population element by
161   combining two existing ones. The crossover operation at point j defines a new candidate vector
      snew = crossj (s1 , s2 ) := s1 [: j] • s[j + 1 :] where • is the concatenation of two vectors. Crossover

      operation with more than one point is defined recursively in the order of joints. The number of
163   crossovers kcross is a hyperparameter (see § C), and their location is chosen randomly in the range
164   of the perturbation size. (iv) Mutation: Is a random operation that allows further exploration. The
165   function mutate : [ n2 (n − 1)]δ 7→ [ n2 (n − 1)]δ is a random mapping of a candidate to another.
166   One simple mutation function changes each index with some mutation probability p to some other
167   index in the range (uniformly at random). In § 4 we discuss more advanced mutation strategies that
168   significantly improve performance.
169   Given all the ingredients above, GA operates by iteratively evolving the population toward a good
170   solution. The algorithm begins with an initial random population. In EvA, this population is a
                                 np
171   set of vectors S0 = {si,0 }i=1 with random elements, where np is the number of candidates in the
172   population. By definition, our candidates always encode a valid perturbation—the budget of the
173   perturbation is enforced by the length of each candidate vector.
174
      In each iteration, candidates are evaluated using the fitness function. Based on the fitness scores, an
175   elite sub-population of parents and new children is selected to proceed to the next iteration, while the
176   rest of the population is removed. To create a new child, parents are selected through a tournament
177   selection process: in each tournament, ntour random parents are chosen, and the best among them is
178   selected for crossover and subsequent mutation. This process repeats for t generations.
179
      Sparse encoding of the attack. The population in our framework is an encoding of the perturbation
180
      matrix. The naive way for encoding this problem is to create a boolean vector of size N 2 encoding
181   which entries are flipped, which results in memory complexity of O(|S|N 2 ) where |S| is the
182   population size. Instead, we introduce an approach that leverages the sparse nature of the solution
183   and reduces the complexity to O(|S| · ϵ · |E[Vatt : V]|). In this encoding, instead of retaining all
184   possible edges, we only keep the indices of the edges we want to flip. Therefore, any element in
185   the population z ∈ S is a vector of p = ⌊ϵ · |E[Vatt : V]|⌋ dimensions where each entity of it is an
186   index in adjacency matrix z[i] ∈ {1, · · · n(n − 1)/2} with n = |V|. We use diagonal enumeration
187   of an upper triangular n × n matrix as the encoding (see § B). The perturbation vector can contain
188   repeated elements. During the evaluation of the vector, we transform it to a perturbation matrix Pz ,
189   and we compute the perturbed adjacency Ã = A ⊕ Pz . All the mentioned computations are in sparse
190   representation, and each individual of the population takes O(δ) space. Moreover, with this encoding,
191
      we directly enforce the global budget since the size of each individual in the population is by design
      the number of allowed perturbations.
192
193   Acceptable fitness functions. The fitness function in GA is accessed in a black-box manner.
194   Therefore, properties like differentiability are not a requirement, which allows us to use the accuracy
195   directly. However, for scenarios like targeted attacks, where the objective is to only misclassify a
196   single node, the 0-1 loss function is not a suitable fitness function. In other words, with the 0-1 loss,
197
      random search and GA are practically equivalent. Ideally, small changes in the solution should be
      reflected in the fitness function as well. This sensitivity to various individuals prevents GA from
198
      remaining in local optima. In § 5, we discuss the choice of fitness in targeted attacks.
199
200   Drawbacks. The aforementioned setup is the very baseline variant of EvA. While already effective
201   (outperforming SOTA), in Fig. 2, we resolve several drawbacks by changing the definition of the
202   initial population and the mutation function. In the baseline variant, a population is allowed to contain
203
      perturbations that are outside of the receptive field of the GNN for Vatt . This means that (at least for
      initial generations) a proportion of the attacking budget is wasted on ineffective perturbations. Even
204
      for perturbations connecting nodes with both ends outside of Vatt , the defender can easily revert them
205
      by memorizing the training subgraph. In § 4, we discuss further improvements.

208   4    E NHANCING THE S EARCH
209
210   In § 3 we defined the baseline evolutionary attack and discussed the possible drawbacks. As shown in
211   Fig. 2 the baseline EvA already outperforms the SOTA. Additionally, with the following modifications
212   we increase its effectiveness by a notable margin. The key insight is that the baseline attack, same as
213   many gradient-based attacks defined their target space as the entire graph – entire space of n2 (n − 1)
214   possible edges. As mentioned in § 2, perturbations that do have both endpoints in the training subgraph
215   can be easily reverted just by memorizing the training subgraph. Additionally, perturbations outside of
      the receptive field of Vatt are a waste of budget as they do not affect the prediction of the target nodes.

      Initial population. Our baseline initial population consists of random perturbations in the entire
217   space of A. This is a naive approach that disregards closeness to Vatt . Instead, we restrict the initial
218   population to have at least one endpoint in Vatt . This can easily done by randomly sampling both
219   endpoints, one inside Vatt and one in V, and then mapping the edges back to the indices via Π.
220
      Targeted and adaptive mutation. After initialization, another way to balance the exploration and
221
      exploitation power of the algorithm is by introducing diversity in the population. In the baseline
222
      uniform mutation function, we change each edge to another random edge in V with some mutation
223   probability p. Same as in initialization, we define the “targeted mutation (TM)” by restricting the
224   new mutated edge to have at least one end-point in Vatt . Remarkably, this modification shows a
225   significant improvement as shown in Fig. 2. Furthermore, when the attack succeeds in altering
226   a node’s prediction, additional perturbations connected to it do not gain any more performance.
227   Therefore, we exclude them from the endpoint that was restricted to Vatt . Notably, we still allow
228   those nodes to connect to other nodes in Vatt as they can also increase the misclassification risk for
229   other nodes. We call the latter approach “adaptive targeted mutation” (ATM).
230   Stacking perturbations. Each population (at each iteration of EvA) needs to evaluate every individ-
231   ual. This means that each individual requires a forward pass on the perturbed graph. As mentioned
232   before, our population takes O(δ) memory, and during the evaluation, we still maintain the sparse
233   representation of the graph. Therefore, if the memory budget allows, we can evaluate several per-
234   turbations at once by combining perturbed graphs into one large (disconnected) graph and running
235   only one forward pass. In practice, for small datasets like CoraML, we only run one forward pass per
236   iteration, as the entire population of 1024 individuals can be evaluated once.

299   Local attacks. Gosch et al. (2024) argue that the perturbations within a global budget can still cause
300   meaningful changes to the graph structure. For example, a perturbation might add edges to the node,
301   increasing its degree to more than twice its current level while staying within the global budget. This
302   can drastically alter the graph structure locally around that node, making the attack noticeable or, at
303   the very least, impacting the graph’s structural semantics. Therefore, they argue for a threat model
304   that, in addition to a global budget, has a local budget that limits the number of perturbations per
305   node to an ϵloc proportion of its degree.
306   Local constrained mutation. We enforce this constraint as a new mutation applied before finalizing
307   the population. In our mutation, we count the row (or column) summation of the perturbation matrix
308   which quantifies the number of edges added to (or removed from) each node. Calling the nodes with
309   perturbation degree higher than ϵloc · deg(vi ) (the local perturbation budget) as “violating”, we run
310   an iterative refinement procedure where at each step we remove one edge from violating nodes and
311   insert a non-violating edge instead. This refinement procedure continues until the local constraints
312   are satisfied. Additionally, we rewrite the adaptive targeted mutation to account for the local budget –
313
      we restrict the mutation edges to those with remaining local budget for both end-points.
314   Targeted attacks. The targeted attack aims at one node to misclassify it with the least possible
315   number of perturbations. With the discussion in § 4 the binary objective does not capture differences
316   between different solutions. Therefore we use a proxy tanh-margin loss as the fitness function.

      Attacking randomized smoothing-based certificates. A robustness certificate guarantees that the
342   prediction of the classifier remains the same within the threat model. One way to obtain such a
343   guarantee (for a black-box model) is through randomized smoothing. A smoothing scheme ξ is a
344   random function mapping an input x to a nearby point x′ (e.g. additive isotropic Gaussian noise
345   x′ = ξ(x) = x + ϵ, where ϵ ∼ N (0, σ 2 I)). The convolution of the smoothing scheme and the
346   classifier Pr[f (x + ϵ) = y] changes slowly around x and this allows us to bound the worst-case
347   minimum of the smooth prediction probability within B. If this minimum is above 0.5, we can certify
348   that the smooth model returns the same label for any x̃ ∈ B(x). A possible adversarial objective is to
349   reduce the number of nodes that are certified (a.k.a. certified ratio).
350   For certifying a prediction we compute the majority vote – the probability that the classifier predicts
351   the top class for randomized x′ ∼ ξ(x). Then, we find a lower bound for this probability within the
352   perturbation ball (see § D). Exact computation of the majority vote is generally intractable. Instead,
353   we use Monte-Carlo (MC) sampling. These operations are not directly differentiable.
354   A naive implementation of the certified ratio objective is to compute nmc random samples for each
355
      candidate perturbation Ã. This makes the attack extremely slow as in each iteration, we need np · nmc
356
      samples, and for each Monte Carlo sample, we need n2 samples from the Bernoulli distribution.
357   Since statistical rigor is not crucial during the attack, we employ an efficient sampling strategy where
358   we start with initial samples from clean A, and for each perturbation, we only resample for the edges
359   in Ã△A. We use the stacked inference technique (see § 4) on MC samples which ultimately reduces
360   the computation to one inference per each perturbation Ã. Moreover, the certified radius is only a
361   function of the smooth classifier’s probability and it is non-decreasing w.r.t. it. This allows us to
362   reduce all certificate computations to one binary search for the minimum required probability. Then
363   the objective is to minimize the number of nodes with probability above this threshold. We further
364   discuss this attack in § D.
365
      Attacking conformal prediction. Instead of label prediction, conformal prediction (CP) returns
366   prediction sets that are guaranteed to include the true label with 1 − α probability. This post-hoc
367   statistical method treats the model as a black-box and requires only a calibration set of labeled points
368   whose labels were not used during model training. CP is applicable in both inductive and transductive
369   Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) under the assumption of node-exchangeability (Zargarbashi &
370   Bojchevski, 2024). Adversarial attacks on conformal prediction aim to decrease the empirical
371   coverage by perturbing the input. In addition, we also define an attack that reduces the applicability
372   of the prediction sets by increasing the average set size.
373   To compute prediction sets we need to compute a quantile from the set of true calibration conformity
374   scores and compare the scores of the test node to the quantile threshold. This operation is again not
375   directly differentiable which is not a problem for EvA. In our experimental setup, the defender cali-
376   brates on a random subset of Vu (besides the test, this is the only set with labels unseen by the model).
377   Assuming that the unlabeled and test nodes are originally exchangeable (node-exchangeability),
      the conformal guarantee is valid in the inductive setup upon recalibration on the clean graph. By


                                                                           7
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


378
      perturbing the edge structure we can easily break this guarantee. Therefore our objective is to change
379   the edge structure such that the coverage is minimized. Intuitively, this requires maximizing the
380   distribution shift between the test and calibration scores. We can perform conformal prediction for
381   each individual in the population, and we set the coverage of Vatt as the objective function.
382
      Since we don’t know the exact subset of the unlabeled nodes taken as calibration, we can use the
383
      unlabeled set entirely as the calibration set. Given that the defender will randomly sample from
384
      unlabeled nodes during the calibration, the coverage remains roughly the same for exchangeable
385   subsets of Vu (Berti & Rigo, 1997). To the best of our knowledge, so far this is the only adversarial
386   attack on the graph structure to break conformal inductive GNNs. Similarly, by changing the objective
387   to the negative average set size, we can easily attack the usability of prediction sets (see Fig. 4).

522   7                     C ONCLUSION
523
524
525
      In contrast to gradient-based adversarial attacks on graph structure, we developed a new attack (EvA)
      based on a heuristic genetic algorithm. By eliminating differentiation, we can directly optimize for
526
      the objective of the adversary (e.g. the model’s accuracy). This black-box nature enables us to define
527
      complex adversarial goals, including attacks on robustness certificates and conformal prediction. Our
528   novel attacks decrease the certified ratio, and conformal coverage, and increase the conformal set size.
529   We propose an encoding that reduces the memory complexity of the attack to the same order as the
530   perturbation budget which allows us to adapt to various computational constraints. Given the drastic
531   decrease in the model’s accuracy by applying EvA, we highlight that even SOTA gradient-based
532   attacks are far from optimal. Our main message is that search-based attacks are underexplored yet
533   powerful as shown by our results.
534   Limitations. We use an off-the-shelf genetic algorithm. Surely, there is room for designing search
535   algorithms specific to the domain of the problem or hybrids of gradient and evolutionary search. As
536   the graph size increases, the search space expands exponentially which makes convergence harder.
537   While we remove the white-box assumption, we still assume the adversary has full knowledge of the
538   graph and labels (same as most other attacks). This limitation can be easily addressed in future work.
539   EvA uses many forward passes through the model which can be unrealistic in some attack scenarios.
      We leave the design of a further query-efficient variant for the future.

957   Smoothing-based certificate. We define a randomized model as a convolution of the original model
958   and a smoothing scheme. The smoothing scheme ξ : X 7→ X is a randomized function mapping the
959
      given input to a random nearby point. For graph structure, we use the sparse smoothing certificate
      (Bojchevski et al., 2020), which certifies whether within Bra ,rd the prediction of the smooth model
960
      remains the same. Here ra is the maximum number of possible additions, and rd is the maximum
961
      number of edge deletions. The smoothing function is defined by two Bernoulli parameters p+ , and
962   p− ; i.e. for each entity of A, if it is zero, it will be toggled with p+ probability and otherwise with p− .
963
964   The robustness certificate also accesses the model f as a black box and defines a smooth model
965
      as f¯y (x) = E[I[f (ξ(x)) = y]] - each random sample x′ is one vote for class f (x′ ) and f¯y is the
966
      proportion of votes for class y. Let p = f¯y (x); the certificate finds a lower bound probability
967   p ≥ minx̃∈B(x) f¯y (x̃) and acts as a decision function I[y ≥ 0.5]. In other words, the certificate
968
      returns yes, in case it is guaranteed that the smooth model will not return any value lower than 0.5 for
      class y within nB(x). For further details about how to compute the certificate, see (Bojchevski et al.,
969
      2020).
970
971   Adaptive sampling for certificate attack. Statistical rigor is not a necessity while attacking the
      certificate. Therefore, while attacking, we can reduce the cost of resampling by only resampling the


                                                          18
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


972
       subset of the graph that was perturbed. In other words, we initialize the search by computing samples
973
       A1 , . . . , Am , and for each perturbation Ã we only resample the edges in A△Ã. For each edge in
974
       that set, if the edge was added via the perturbations, we resample m Bernoulli variable with p− , and
975    otherwise p+ . We substitute those samples in the same entry of A1 , . . . , Am , and by running this
976    process |δ| times, we assume that Ã1 , · · · Ãm are representative as a new set of m samples for Ã.
977    This adaptive sampling reduces the number of random computations from m · n2 to m · |δ|, which is
978    significantly lower. Surely, to evaluate the final perturbation (the reported effectiveness), we don’t
979    use this approach, as it is statistically flawed and only applicable to reduce the computation during
980    the attack.

982    D.1   F ITNESS F UNCTION
983
984    For a targeted attack, since the objective space is limited to the set {0, 1} , the sensitivity is very
985    low. As a result, the method becomes equivalent to a random search. The zero-one fitness function
986    means that all individuals with different perturbations receive the same score, causing the algorithm
987
       to behave more like a random search, as ties in each tournament are broken randomly. Secondly,
       since only one individual with a score of one is sufficient to halt the algorithm, all elite populations
988
       before success have scores of zero, which again results in random selection from them.

1054
1055   (Dai et al., 2018) proposed a practical black-box attack (PBA), dividing it into PBA-C (with access
1056   to logits - continuous) and PBA-D (access only to the labels - discrete). As stated in (Dai et al.,
1057   2018), a genetic algorithm for global attacks requires PBA-C because it relies on logits, with the
1058   fitness function being the negative log-likelihood. We demonstrate that EvA not only eliminates
1059
       the need for logits but also performs even better by directly optimizing for accuracy rather than
       using log-likelihood. To compare our method with (Dai et al., 2018), we modified the algorithm’s
1060
       fitness function and mutation mechanism to replicate the results reported in (Dai et al., 2018). This
1061
       implementation retains scalability benefits, as it is also built upon our sparse encoded representation.
1062   Note here we re-implement Dai et al. (2018) in our sparse and parallelized framework. Their
1063   original implementation uses dense adjacency matrices and sequential evaluation and would achieve
1064   a significantly worse result within the same memory/run-time constraint. Even with our efficient
1065   re-implementation Dai et al. (2018) is significantly worse than ours. Table 14 provides the results
1066   for the CoraML dataset using the GCN architecture. EvA also significantly outperforms (Dai et al.,
1067   2018). Additionally, since our method is independent of gradients, we established the first attack
1068   on conformal prediction and certification. For conformal prediction, we attack coverage and set
1069   size where the latter criteria are not yet explored (to the best of our knowledge). Attacks tending to
1070
       decrease certificate effectiveness are also under-explored in GNNs. In this work, we aim to achieve
       both attack on certified accuracy and certified ratio.
