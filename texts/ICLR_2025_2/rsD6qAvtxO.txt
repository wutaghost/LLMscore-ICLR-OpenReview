011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Autoregressive next-token prediction, a standard pretraining method for large-
014            scale language models, excels in handling long sequential data. However, its ap-
015            plication to complex visual tasks, particularly biological imaging, faces challenges
016            due to the spatial continuity and high dimensionality of biological images. High-
017            resolution 3D biological images, such as electron microscopy (EM) brain scans,
018
               offer ideal long-sequence data, but existing methods struggle to fully leverage this
               characteristic. To address these challenges, we introduce TokenUnify, a novel
019
               pretraining method that integrates random token prediction, next-token predic-
020
               tion, and next-all token prediction. We provide theoretical evidence demonstrating
021            that TokenUnify mitigates cumulative errors in visual autoregression, particularly
022            when dealing with complex three-dimensional anatomical structures. In conjunc-
023            tion with TokenUnify, we have assembled a large-scale, ultra-high-resolution EM
024            brain image dataset comprising over 120 million finely annotated voxels. This
025            dataset not only represents the largest neuron segmentation dataset to date but,
026            more importantly, provides ideal long-sequence biological image data that fully
027            exhibits spatial continuity. Leveraging the Mamba network, which is inherently
028            suited for long-sequence modeling, TokenUnify capitalizes on the advantages of
029
               autoregressive methods in processing long-sequence data, achieving a 45% per-
               formance improvement on downstream EM neuron segmentation tasks compared
030
               to existing methods. Furthermore, TokenUnify demonstrates superior scalability
031
               over MAE and traditional autoregressive methods, effectively bridging the gap be-
032            tween pretraining strategies for language and vision models. Code is available at
033            https://anonymous.4open.science/r/TokenUnify-3DBF.

      Large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated impressive scaling capabilities, reaching trillions
      of parameters through pretraining Achiam et al. (2023); Touvron et al. (2023a;b). This success is
040
      primarily attributed to high-quality data and effective autoregressive models. These models benefit
041
      from strong scaling laws due to the structured and sequential nature of text data, which allows
042   unification into a single next-token prediction task. However, when extending to multimodal tasks
043   such as Unified IO Lu et al. (2023) and Qwen VL Bai et al. (2023b), these models often fail to
044   achieve state-of-the-art performance on fine-grained image tasks, particularly in biological imaging.
045
      Unlike language, the complexity of visual signals, especially in biological images, has led to diverse
046
      approaches in visual pretraining. Contrastive learning methods like DINO v2 Oquab et al. (2023)
047
      excel in fine-grained representation, while masked reconstruction methods such as MAE He et al.
048   (2022); Chen et al. (2023a) offer good scalability and zero-shot classification abilities. However,
049   these methods exhibit poor scaling laws, where increasing model size does not yield expected per-
050   formance gains Singh et al. (2023) (see Section A in the appendix). To achieve scaling laws similar
051   to language models, approaches like AIM El-Nouby et al. (2024) and LVM Bai et al. (2023c) have
052   introduced autoregressive tasks into the visual domain, showing promising scaling properties. How-
053   ever, image sequence disorder and error accumulation in autoregressive tasks often degrade perfor-
      mance in smaller models Bachmann & Nagarajan (2024). Additionally, the computational burden of

070   We summarize the challenges of visual autoregressive tasks in biological imaging as follows: 1) How

      when dealing with complex anatomical structures? 2) How to develop more efficient computational

075   This paper aims to tackle the above three critical challenges in the context of biological imaging,
076   particularly focusing on EM brain scans. 1) To address the issue of cumulative errors in visual
077
      autoregression, we propose TokenUnify, a novel mixed token prediction paradigm. TokenUnify in-
      tegrates next-token prediction, next-all token prediction, and random token prediction (as illustrated
078
      in Fig. 1), leveraging global information to overcome the limitations of local receptive fields in
079
      complex anatomical structures. We theoretically demonstrate that this mixed approach reduces cu-
080   mulative errors while maintaining favorable scaling laws. 2) To alleviate computational burdens, we
081   introduce the Mamba architecture, which reduces the computational complexity of autoregressive
082   tasks from quadratic (as in Transformers) to linear. This is particularly crucial for processing high-
083   resolution 3D biological images. Detailed comparisons of the scaling properties between Mamba
084   and Transformer architectures reveal that Mamba achieves superior performance and efficiency in
085   large-scale autoregressive visual models, especially for biological imaging tasks. 3) To construct
086   spatially correlated long-sequence relationships, we have collected large-scale, ultra-high-resolution
087   3D electron microscopy (EM) images of mouse brain slices. The ultra-high resolution allows for
088
      thousands of continuous image tokens, ensuring robust spatial continuity crucial for understanding
      complex neuronal structures. We have fully annotated six different functional regions within the
089
      mouse brain, totaling 120 million pixels, resulting in the largest manually annotated neuron dataset
090
      to date. This comprehensive dataset also serves as a unified benchmark for evaluating experimental
091   performance in biological image analysis1 .
092

095

096
      for pretraining large-scale visual models in biological imaging.

117   2     R ELATED WORK
118
119
      Recent advances in large language models (LLMs) have unified various NLP tasks under a single
120
      architecture, formulating them as generation tasks. This architecture can be categorized into BERT-
121
      like Devlin et al. (2018); Xia et al. (2020); Lee et al. (2020); Song et al. (2023); Mo et al. (2024) and
122   GPT-like Brown et al. (2020); Radford et al. (2019); Li et al. (2024a); Zhou et al. (2023) models.
123   The latter, decoder-only autoregressive structure, has been shown to be more effective, as validated
124   by products like ChatGPT. Subsequent works have built upon GPT, introducing techniques like RM-
125   SNorm Zhang & Sennrich (2019), SwiGLU, and RoPE to ensure efficient and stable training. The
126   LLaMA series Touvron et al. (2023a;b) has improved training efficiency, while the Qwen series Bai
127   et al. (2023b;a); Xiang et al. (2024) has focused on data cleaning and filtering for Chinese language
128   models. Currently, LLMs have surpassed human-level performance in many text processing tasks
129   Achiam et al. (2023).
130   In multi-modal tasks, the CLIP Radford et al. (2021) and BLIP series Li et al. (2022; 2023a); Dai
131   et al. (2024) have pioneered contrastive learning on image-text pairs, achieving remarkable zero-shot
132   classification and generalization capabilities. Further works Zhang et al. (2023); Chen et al. (2023b);
133   Wang et al. (2022); Zhou et al. (2024) have applied multi-modal models to specific domains. By
134   processing arbitrary modalities into a unified token format Lu et al. (2023); Wang et al. (2023); Chen
135   et al. (2024a), these models can generate outputs in any modality. However, there is still room for
136
      improvement in fine-grained visual tasks, and training large vision models remains an open problem.
137   Self-supervised pre-training has been a cornerstone for enhancing model representation capabilities.
138   Approaches based on contrastive learning for representation extraction Chen et al. (2024b); Zbontar
139   et al. (2021); He et al. (2020); Li et al. (2021) and masked reconstruction methods He et al. (2022);
140   Chen et al. (2023a); Li et al. (2023c); Ding et al. (2022); Chen et al. (2024d; 2023d) have shown
141   promise. However, current vision models have not exhibited the same sublinear scaling laws as lan-
142
      guage models. To address this issue, some tasks have adopted autoregressive pre-training paradigms
      similar to those used in language models Chen et al. (2020); Bai et al. (2023c); El-Nouby et al.
143
      (2024), though the training costs remain a significant concern. In this paper, we explore the poten-
144
      tial of long visual token autoregressive pre-training and introduce a collaborative training scheme
145   for long token prediction. Our approach aims to balance the scaling laws and training costs, demon-
146   strating improvements in fine-grained visual tasks.

239       3.3     M AMBA ORDERING
240
241       While the aforementioned mix token prediction task improves sequence autoregressive modeling
242       capabilities, it also introduces additional computational complexity for long sequences. Inspired
243       by the Mamba strategy proposed by Gu & Dao (2023), we introduce an enhanced approach to
244
          effectively model long sequences in volumetric EM images. Traditional sequence modeling methods
          often struggle with capturing long-range dependencies due to their rigid sequential nature. Our
245
          enhanced Mamba ordering strategy addresses this by incorporating a more sophisticated and flexible
246
          sequence modeling approach.
247
248       The fundamental idea behind Mamba ordering is to dynamically prioritize regions of the sequence
249       based on contextual significance rather than adhering to a fixed order. This is achieved through
250
          an adaptive mechanism that evaluates the importance of different patches within the sequence and
          adjusts the processing order accordingly. By doing so, Mamba ordering enhances the modelвҖҷs ability
251
          to capture intricate patterns and long-range dependencies more effectively.
252
253       Mathematically, let x = {x1 , x2 , . . . , xK } represent the sequence of patches. Instead of process-
254       ing these patches in a fixed order, we define a dynamic ordering function Пғ : {1, 2, . . . , K} вҶ’
255       {1, 2, . . . , K} that determines the sequence in which patches are processed. The Mamba ordering
256
          objective can be formulated as:

      Dataset. For the pretraining phase of TokenUnify, we collect a vast amount of publicly available
327   unlabeled EM imaging data, from four large-scale electron microscopy (EM) datasets: Full Adult
328   Fly Brain (FAFB) Schlegel et al. (2021), MitoEM Wei et al. (2020), FIB-25 Takemura et al. (2017),
329   and Kasthuri15 Kasthuri et al. (2015). These datasets cover a diverse range of organisms, includ-
330   ing Drosophila, mouse, rat, and human samples, totaling over 1 TB. The details of the pretraining
331   datasets can be found in Table 3. We sample from the datasets with equal probability and ensure that
332   each brain region has an equal chance of being sampled, guaranteeing the diversity of the pretraining
333   data.
334   For downstream fine-tuning and segmentation, we employ two datasets: a smaller dataset, AC3/4,
335   and a larger dataset, MEC, for algorithm validation. The AC3/4 dataset Kasthuri et al. (2015) con-
336   sists of mouse somatosensory cortex datasets with 256 and 100 successive EM images (1024Г—1024).
337   We use the first 80 images of AC4 for fine-tuning, the last 20 images of AC4 for testing, and the first
338   100 images of AC3 for testing. Additionally, we have collected a large-scale electron microscopy
339   dataset, MEC, by imaging the mouse somatosensory cortex, mouse medial entorhinal cortex, and
340   mouse cerebral cortex, achieving a physical resolution of 8nm Г— 8nm Г— 40nm. We select 6 represen-
341
      tative volumes from different neural regions, named wafer4/25/26/26-2/36/36-2, with each volume
      size reaching 125 Г— 1250 Г— 1250 voxels. We perform dense annotation on these selected wafer re-
342
      gions, with a total of over 1.2 billion annotated voxels. To validate the algorithmвҖҷs performance on
343
      a large-scale dataset, we use wafer25/26/26-2/36 for training, wafer4 for validation, and wafer36-2
344   for testing on the MEC dataset.

      Implementation Details. In this work, we employ consistent training settings for both pretraining
355
      and fine-tuning tasks. The network architecture remains the same throughout the training and fine-
356
      tuning phases. During fine-tuning, we optimize the network using the AdamW optimizer Loshchilov
357   & Hutter (2018) with ОІ1 = 0.9, ОІ2 = 0.999, a learning rate of 1e-6, and a batch size of 20 on an
358   NVIDIA GTX 3090 (24GB) GPU. For pretraining, we use a batch size of 8 on an NVIDIA Tesla
359   A40 (48G) GPU due to memory constraints.
360
      We perform distributed training using 8 NVIDIA GTX 3090 GPUs for each segmentation task,
361
      running for a total of 1200 epochs. Similarly, we utilize 32 NVIDIA Tesla A40 GPUs for each
362
      pretraining task, running for 400 epochs. During the pretraining phase, the input consists solely
363   of unlabeled data, whereas in the segmentation phase, both labeled and unlabeled data are used as
364   input. The input block resolution for the network is set to 16 Г— 160 Г— 160. To initialize the network
365   for fine-tuning, we load the weights obtained from the pretraining phase, following the settings of
366   previous works He et al. (2022).

      To generate final segmentation results from the predicted affinities, we employ two representative
368
      post-processing methods: Waterz Funke et al. (2018) and LMC Beier et al. (2017). Waterz iteratively
369   merges fragments based on edge scores until a threshold is reached. We set the quantile to 50% and
370   the threshold to 0.5 based on testing on MEC, and discretize scores into 256 bins. LMC formulates
371   agglomeration as a minimum-cost multi-cut problem, extracting edge features as costs and solving
372   with the Kernighan-Lin solver Kernighan & Lin (1970). We maintain consistent post-processing
373   settings across all experiments to ensure fair comparisons and conclusions about our method.

      Abalation Study. We conducted ablation studies on several components within our experimental
502   setup. The experiments were divided into two main parts. First, we explored the mixed mechanisms
503   of TokenUnify. We experimented with combinations of three different mixing mechanisms, ensuring
504   that the total number of training epochs remained consistent. Table 6 presents the results of these ex-
505   periments on the wafer4 neuron segmentation task (using a 28M EMmamba segmentation network).
506   The results demonstrate that mixed training provides the most significant benefit for downstream
507   tasks, with the combination of Random token and Next token being the next most effective.
508   Second, we performed ablation studies on the fine-tuning schemes. Under the default settings, we
509   fine-tuned all parameters of the network. However, due to computational resource constraints, only a
510   subset of parameters (or additional adapter parameters such as LoRA Hu et al. (2022)) is often fine-
511   tuned for larger models. Based on our network architecture (see Fig. 7), we divided the network into
512   the Mamba part (for token sequence information extraction), the encoder part (for downsampling),
513   and the decoder part (for convolutional upsampling). We fine-tuned only the corresponding subset
514   of weights for each part. Our experimental results are shown in Table 7.
515   We found that in the TokenUnify modeling, using the sequence information priors extracted by
516   Mamba significantly benefits downstream segmentation tasks. Combining the Mamba module with
517   the encoder part yields even greater performance improvements, while fine-tuning only the encoder
518   or decoder weights provides minimal gains. This suggests that in resource-constrained scenarios,
519   fine-tuning only the sequence modeling parameters can be sufficient.

      The favorable scaling laws of TokenUnify present the opportunity to train a unified and generic vi-
524   sual feature extractor, which holds significant importance for visual tasks. A unified visual feature
525   extractor can substantially reduce the cost of fine-tuning models for different visual tasks, thereby
526   facilitating the application of visual technologies across various domains. We have currently val-
527   idated the effectiveness of TokenUnify on long-sequence 3D biological images. Moving forward,
528   we plan to further explore the performance of TokenUnify on natural images and other downstream
529   tasks, thereby expanding its scope of application. Moreover, TokenUnify can be extended to mul-
530   timodal domains such as image-text tasks Chen et al. (2024c); Liu et al. (2023a), demonstrating its
531   utility in multimodal applications. We will also continue to investigate model lightweighting Chen

      et al. (2023c); Chen & Jing (2021) and efficient fine-tuning strategies Liu et al. (2023d); Li et al.
      (2024b). We believe that TokenUnify offers a promising approach for building large-scale, efficient
533
      visual pre-training models, contributing to advancements in the visual domain.

1193   limitations. One major issue is the exposure bias problem Bengio et al. (2015), where the model is
1194   only exposed to ground-truth contexts during training, leading to a mismatch between training and

1198   entire sequence of future tokens given the current context encourages the model to learn more robust
1199   and globally coherent representations.

1372   distribution pt and the modelвҖҷs distribution qt to the KL divergence, which is directly connected
1373   to the training loss L(Оё). By applying concentration inequalities, we control the deviation of the
1374   empirical KL divergence from its expected value over the sequence. The Lipschitz continuity of the

1376   Compared to the autoregressive (AR) models, the NATP approach benefits from predicting future
1377   tokens conditioned on the true context rather than the generated one, thereby avoiding error accumu-
1378   lation due to exposure bias. In AR models, errors can compound over time as the model feeds its own
1379   predictions back into itself. In contrast, NATP trains the model to predict the entire future sequence
1380   at each time step, leveraging the full context and improving global coherence in predictions.

       It is important to note that the assumptions and bounds provided are theoretical and rely on certain
1382   conditions, such as the Lipschitz continuity and finite sequence lengths. In practice, these conditions
1383   may be approximated, but the theoretical framework offers valuable insights into why NATP can be
1384   more effective in handling long-term dependencies and mitigating error accumulation in sequence
1385   modeling tasks.

1491   F.1   S UMMARY OF THE T OKEN U NIFY A LGORITHM
1492
1493   TokenUnify is a novel pre-training method for scalable autoregressive visual modeling. It integrates
1494   random token prediction, next-token prediction, and next-all token prediction to mitigate cumulative
1495   errors in visual autoregression while maintaining favorable scaling laws. The algorithm leverages
1496   the Mamba network architecture to reduce computational complexity from quadratic to linear for
1497   long-sequence modeling.
1498   Pre-training is conducted on a large-scale, ultra-high-resolution electron microscopy (EM) image
1499   dataset, providing spatially correlated long sequences. TokenUnify demonstrates significant im-
1500   provements in segmentation performance on downstream EM neuron segmentation tasks compared
1501   to existing methods. Our pre-training and fine-tuning algorithms are summarized in Algorithm 1
1502   and Algorithm 2, respectively.
1503   The TokenUnify pre-training algorithm captures both local and global dependencies in image data
1504   through mixed token prediction tasks. The Mamba network architecture ensures efficient modeling
1505   of long sequences. During fine-tuning, the pre-trained model adapts to downstream segmentation
1506   tasks using labeled data, achieving state-of-the-art performance on EM neuron segmentation bench-
1507   marks.
