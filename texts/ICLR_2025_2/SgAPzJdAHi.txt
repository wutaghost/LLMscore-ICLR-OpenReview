011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Enhancing the reasoning abilities of lightweight language models (LMs) for tasks
014            like decision-making often relies on instruction-tuning, a method that trains LMs
015
               to mimic the reasoning process using labeled question-rationale pairs, known
               as instruction-tuning datasets, which are typically generated by more powerful
016
               teacher LMs. However, current methods for generating these instruction-tuning
017
               datasets tend to focus solely on the quality of the questions and rationales from the
018            teacher model’s perspective, often neglecting the learning preferences of the stu-
019            dent language model. To fill this gap, we propose ARTE (Aligning TeacheR with
020            StudenT PreferencEs), a novel framework that adapts the teacher LM’s outputs
021            to the student’s preferences, inspired by “responsive teaching” in pedagogy. Our
022            method involves three key steps: (1) generating draft question-rationale pairs from
023            the teacher model, (2) collecting the student’s preferences on these draft pairs via
024            one-shot in-context learning, and (3) aligning the teacher model using Direct Pref-
025            erence Optimization (DPO), then finally curating tailored question-rationale pairs
026
               from the aligned teacher for student training. Through extensive experiments on
               academic reasoning benchmarks, we demonstrate that student models fine-tuned
027
               with tailored datasets by ARTE achieve significant improvements across various
028
               reasoning tasks, outperforming existing instruction-tuning datasets. Moreover, we
029            thoroughly investigate the generalization of ARTE, including the generalization
030            of fine-tuned student models in reasoning ability and the generalization of aligned
031            teacher models to generate tailored training data across tasks and students.

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Lightweight language models (LM) have achieved great success in various tasks like writing and
037   chatting, while it is still challenging for them to perform well in reasoning tasks which is crucial for
038   many applications like decision making and problem solving (Hug, 2024; Banks & Warkentin, 2024;
039
      Hu et al., 2024; AI, 2024; Muralidharan et al., 2024). Instruction Tuning is a popular method for
      improving the reasoning ability of lightweight language models by imitating the reasoning processes
040
      on labeled question-rationale pairs, namely the instruction-tuning dataset, with Supervised Fine-
041
      tuning (Mitra et al., 2023; Hsieh et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2023; Luo et al., 2023; Mukherjee et al.,
042   2023). Given the high cost and scalability issues of human-labeled instruction-tuning datasets, it
043   is common to leverage the Knowledge Distillation (KD) paradigm (Hinton et al., 2015) to distill
044   an instruction-tuning dataset from a powerful teacher language model. Students are then trained
045   on these distilled question-rationale pairs to improve their reasoning ability with Supervised Fine-
046   tuning (Peng et al., 2023; Ivison et al., 2023; Mukherjee et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2023).
047
      To better distill instruction-tuning datasets from a teacher model, the quality of the generated
048   question-rationale pairs is crucial. For questions, Taori et al. (2023); Wang et al. (2022) proposed to
049   use ROUGE-L (Lin, 2004) to filter out the repeated questions to improve the diversity of the gen-
050   erated questions. Xu et al. (2023) propose Evo-Instruct to rewrite the seed questions step by step
051   into more complex questions to improve the complexity of the generated questions. As for ratio-
052   nales, Hsieh et al. (2023); Mukherjee et al. (2023) leverage chain-of-thought (Wei et al., 2022; Yao
053   et al., 2022) to guide the teacher model in producing rationales with detailed reasoning processes to
      improve the quality of the generated rationales. Mitra et al. (2023) contends that the reasoning pro-


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      cesses in the rationales should be adaptive to different question types. While these methods improve
055   question and rationale quality from the teacher model’s perspective, they overlook the learning pref-
056   erences of the student model. In practice, an ideal teacher model should generate question-rationale
057   pairs that are tailored to the student’s preferences and learning needs, to lower the learning barrier,
058   akin to the concept of ‘responsive teaching’ in pedagogy (Gay, 2018; Hattie & Timperley, 2007).
059
      To fill this gap, we propose ARTE, short for Aligning TeacheR with StudenT PreferencEs, a novel
060
      framework that aligns the teacher language model with the student model’s preferences to generate
061   tailored instruction-tuning datasets. Our framework is illustrated in Figure 1. There are three main
062   steps in ARTE: 1) Knowledge Elicitation (Section 2.1): We prompt the teacher model with seed
063   questions to generate draft question-rationale pairs. 2) Preference Collection (Section 2.2): We
064   collect the student’s preferences for each draft pair using one-shot in-context learning on a sampled
065   subset of the validation set of the target reasoning task. 3) Preference Alignment (Section 2.3):
066   We align the teacher model with the student’s preferences using Direct Preference Optimization
067   (DPO) (Rafailov et al., 2023) to improve instructional content. Finally, we repeat the first step with
068   the aligned teacher model to curate tailored instruction-tuning datasets, which are used to fine-tune
069
      the student model via supervised learning.
070   Extensive experiments on academic reasoning benchmarks demonstrate that ARTE outperforms
071   existing instruction-tuning datasets by a significant margin (Peng et al., 2023; Ivison et al., 2023;
072   Mukherjee et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2023; Ding et al., 2023). Specifically, our method achieves im-
073   provements of 9.6%, 1.0%, 0.8%, and 8.5% on logic reasoning, commonsense reasoning, math rea-
074   soning, and knowledge reasoning tasks, respectively, in the Big-Bench-Hard (Suzgun et al., 2023)
075
      benchmark. We also show that ARTE generalizes well across different reasoning tasks and student
      models. 1) We investigate generalization in reasoning tasks through in-context learning on out-of-
076
      domain benchmarks, including PIQA (Bisk et al., 2020), CommonsenseQA (Talmor et al., 2019),
077
      ARC-Easy (Clark et al., 2018), ARC-Challenge (Clark et al., 2018), GSM8K (Cobbe et al., 2021),
078   and MMLU (Hendrycks et al., 2021). In these benchmarks, ARTE consistently outperforms base-
079   line methods, validating the effectiveness of aligning the teacher model with student preferences. 2)
080   We further explore the generalization of the aligned teacher model across different tasks and student
081   models. Results indicate that a teacher model aligned with BBH can generate high-quality examples
082   for unseen reasoning tasks, such as ARC or GSM8K. Furthermore, a teacher model aligned with a
083   specific student model, such as Gemma-2B, can generate tailored training examples for other models
084   with similar parameter capacities, such as Qwen1.5-1.8B or CodeGemma-2B.
085   To summarize, our contributions are as follows:
086
087
          • We propose ARTE, a novel framework inspired by responsive teaching that aligns the teacher
            model with the student model’s preferences to generate tailored instruction-tuning datasets for
088
            reasoning tasks.
089
090       • Extensive experiments on in-domain and out-of-domain reasoning benchmarks demonstrate that
091
            the student model fine-tuned with in-domain datasets generated through ARTE outperforms
            models trained on existing instruction-tuning datasets.
092
093       • We also explore the generalization capability of the aligned teacher model, showing that it can
094         generate high-quality training examples across different reasoning tasks and for other student
095
            models with similar capacities.

135   2.1      K NOWLEDGE E LICITATION
136
137   In this step, we construct a dataset Ddraft = {(q, Rq )} for the target task T , where q is the draft
138   question and Rq = {rq } is the set of rationales associated with the question qi . For question
139   generation, we prompt the teacher model LMt with seed questions to expand them into m draft
140   questions. Specifically, we use three seed questions to construct one question generation prompt
141   which guides the teacher model to brainstorm multiple questions. We decode the question generation
142   prompt at a temperature 1.0 multiple times until we get m draft questions.
143   For rationale generation, inspired by the observation that one question may have multiple different
144   reasoning processes leading to the same answer and the optimal reasoning process may vary for
145   different questions and different models (Mitra et al., 2023). Then, for each draft question q, LMt
146   is guided to generate n rationales rq to form a rationale set Rq using different reasoning techniques,
147   such as Explain Like I’m Five, Step-by-Step, or Math Symbols. Finally, we combine each draft
148   question q with its corresponding rationales Rq to form a draft sample (q, Rq ). Since each draft
149
      question q would be expanded into n rationales, there are m × n draft pairs Edraft = {(q, rq )} in the
      draft dataset Ddraft .

152   2.2      P REFERENCE C OLLECTION
153
154   In this step, we aim to collect the preferences of the student model for question-rationale pairs
155   during Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT). To achieve this, a reliable metric that can reflect the stu-
156
      dent model’s preference for draft pairs is needed. Recent research (Dai et al., 2023; Von Os-
      wald et al., 2023) suggests that language models perform gradient-based updates during In-
157
      Context Learning (ICL), similar to the process of SFT. Additionally, Lin et al. (2023) demon-
158
      strates that ICL can effectively replace SFT in alignment tasks. All these findings suggest that
159   the one-shot ICL performance of the student model with draft pairs can be used as a proxy
160   for preference scores during SFT. To further empirically validate this, we first sample 50 ques-
161   tions from the validation set to construct the preference set Dpref . Then, we collect the one-
      shot ICL performance of the student model with different draft pairs on the preference set Dpref .

      2.3   P REFERENCE A LIGNMENT
187
188   After collecting the preference score of questions and rationales, we aim to align the teacher model
189   with the student model’s preferences to generate tailored pairs for the target task T . There are two
190   main aspects to align, Question Generation and Rationale Generation, towards generating not only
191   tailored rationales but also tailored questions. Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) (Rafailov et al.,
192   2023) is selected to align the teacher model with the student model’s preferences due to its stability
193   and simplicity compared to other methods like PPO (Schulman et al., 2017). For question level
194
      alignment, for the input question generation prompt, we randomly sample out k draft questions with
      the top-25% and bottom-25% preference scores as the chosen and rejected responses respectively.
195
      For rationale level alignment, for each question q, we select the rationale from Rq with the highest
196
      preference score as the chosen response and the rationale with the lowest preference score as the
197   rejected response. Note that the input prompt in rationale level alignment is just a naive step-by-step
198   prompt as shown in Table 14. Finally, we mix the question level and rationale level DPO datasets to
199   perform DPO to align the teacher model with the student model’s preferences.
200
      After the alignment, we repeat the first step to generate tailored pairs for the target task T and use
201
      them to Supervised Fine-Tune the student model. Note that when generating tailored questions and
202
      rationales, we use the same input prompt from the alignment step and decode at temperature τ = 0
203   for deterministic decoding. More details can be found in Appendix E.

251   Implementation In our experiments, we adopt Llama-3-70B-Instruction (Meta, 2024) as the
252   teacher model and Gemma-2B (Banks & Warkentin, 2024) as the student model. We run all of
253   our experiments with 5 different seeds and report the mean of the results in the tables below.
254   In the Knowledge Elicitation step, 1) Question Generation: We decode the question generation
255   prompt in Table 15 with the aligned teacher at a temperature of 1.0 until we achieve a total of
256   250 draft questions per task. 2) Rationale Generation: For each draft question, the teacher model
257   is prompted to generate n = 4 rationales using the prompt in Table 16 with diverse reasoning
258   techniques. Each draft question with its corresponding rationales forms a draft sample (q, Rq ).
259   In the Preference Collection step, we collect the preference scores of questions and rationales
260   through in-context learning on the preference set. Specifically, we use the prompt template in Ta-
261   ble 17 and decoding at a temperature τ = 0 to ensure the model’s answer is deterministic.
262
      In the Preference Alignment step, we utilize preference scores to create the DPO dataset and align
263
      the teacher model with the student model’s preferences. For question generation, we select 50 draft
264   questions with the highest and lowest preference scores per task as preferred and dispreferred respec-
265   tively, creating 50 DPO training examples per task at the question level. For rationale generation, the
266   highest and lowest score rationales per question are selected similarly, resulting in 250 DPO training
267   examples at the rationale level per task. These datasets are combined to form the final DPO dataset.
268   We employ Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) to align the models at a learning rate of 1e − 7,
269   batch size of 16, for 1 epoch, incorporating a linear warm-up over 10% of training steps followed
      by a cosine decay learning rate schedule.

342   craft, or manipulate objects using everyday physical knowledge. 2) CSQA (Talmor et al., 2019)
343   is a question-answering benchmark targeting commonsense reasoning. 3) ARC-Easy and 4) ARC-
344   Challenge (Clark et al., 2018) are multiple-choice question-answering benchmarks designed to test
345   the model’s ability to reason about scientific knowledge. 5) GSM8K (Cobbe et al., 2021) is a math-
346   ematical benchmark that is designed to test language models’ ability in math and logic reasoning.
347
      6) MMLU (Hendrycks et al., 2021) is a new benchmark designed to measure knowledge acquired
      during pretraining by evaluating models exclusively in zero-shot and few-shot settings.
348
349   Table 4 shows the results of our method and baselines on the six benchmarks. On average, our
350   method outperforms the best baseline by 1.5% in accuracy. The results show that our method helps
351   the student model Gemma-2B to achieve better generalization ability on reasoning tasks. In some
352   tasks like PIQA, CSQA, and MMLU, the performance of our method is not the best but still com-
353
      petitive. While in more challenging tasks like ARC and GSM8K, which are both sampled from
      real-world grade school exams, our method outperforms all the baselines. This phenomenon reveals
354
      for more challenging tasks, more carefully curated examples are needed in knowledge distillation.
355
      Compared to the heuristic baselines, the tailored examples generated by the aligned teacher model
356   are more effective in these tasks as they can better capture the student model’s preferences.

410   5   I NSIGHTS FOR G ENERATING R ATIONALE FOR L ANGUAGE M ODELS
411
412   In Step 2 Preference Collection, we collect the preference of small language models, such as
413   Gemma-2B, towards the question-rationale pairs through one-shot in-context learning on the prefer-
414   ence set. Through careful analysis of the collected preference scores, we conclude with two insights
415   for generating a tailored question-rationale pair for language models with limited capacity:
416
417   Insight 1: The more detailed the rationale does not necessarily mean the better the per-
418   formance of the small language model. In previous works (Hsieh et al., 2023; Mukherjee
419   et al., 2023), the teacher model is guided to generate a detailed reasoning process with chain-of-
420   thought (Wei et al., 2022; Yao et al., 2022) to provide more information for the small language
421   model to imitate. However, after taking a closer look at the preference scores, we find that there is
422
      no significant linear correlation between the length of the rationale and the preference score, namely
      the accuracy of the small language model within one-shot in-context learning. Figure 2 shows the
423
      relationship between the word number of the rationale and the one-shot in-context learning accuracy
424
      of the small language model on boolean expressions and sports understanding tasks.
425
426   As shown in Figure 2, the rationale with most words does not necessarily mean the best performance
427   of the small language model, while the rationale with moderate words (75-100 words in Boolean
428
      Expressions and 50-75 words in Sports Understanding) achieves better results. This indicates that a
      rationale with complete and concise reasoning steps is more beneficial for the small language model
429
      to learn, compared to a long meticulous rationale.
430
431   The reasons behind this are twofold: First, the Lost-in-the-Middle Phenomenon (Liu et al., 2024)
      of language models. Language models may lose information when the input context is too long.

482
483   Instruction Tuning & Knowledge Distillation Instruction tuning, which involves fine-tuning pre-
484   trained language models on a labeled dataset known as an instruction-tuning dataset, is a widely
485   adopted technique to enhance both task-specific performance and the ability to follow instructions.
      In this paper, we concentrate on improving the reasoning capabilities of lightweight language mod-


                                                                     9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      els through instruction tuning. Given the high costs and scalability challenges of creating human-
487   labeled instruction-tuning datasets, Knowledge Distillation (KD) is frequently employed to distill
488   such datasets from a powerful teacher model (Hinton et al., 2015). To enhance the quality of gener-
489   ated question-rationale pairs, Taori et al. (2023); Wang et al. (2022) suggested using ROUGE-L (Lin,
490   2004) to filter out repetitive questions, thereby increasing the diversity of the generated content. Xu
491   et al. (2023) introduced Evo-Instruct, which incrementally rewrites seed questions into more com-
492   plex ones, improving their sophistication. For generating rationales, Hsieh et al. (2023); Mukherjee
493   et al. (2023) utilized chain-of-thought reasoning (Wei et al., 2022; Yao et al., 2022) to guide the
494   teacher model in producing rationales that incorporate detailed reasoning steps, thus enhancing ra-
495
      tionale quality. Additionally, Mitra et al. (2023) argued that the reasoning in rationales should be
      adaptable to different question types. While these approaches improve the quality of questions and
496
      rationales from the teacher model’s perspective, they do not account for the student model’s learning
497
      preferences. To address this limitation, we propose ARTE, a novel framework that aligns the teacher
498   language model’s outputs with the preferences of the student model, generating instruction-tuning
499   datasets that are better tailored to the student model’s needs.
500
501   Alignment of Language Models Alignment of Language Models is about aligning the behavior
502   with human or other models’ preferences. It is initially proposed in the context of reinforcement
503   learning from human feedback (Christiano et al., 2017), where the language model is trained to
504   align with human preferences including safety, ethics, common sense, and other human values.
505   Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO, Schulman et al., 2017) and Direct Preference Optimization
506   (DPO, Rafailov et al., 2023) are two popular reinforcement learning algorithms used to achieve this
507   alignment (Ouyang et al., 2022; Touvron et al., 2023; Meta, 2024; Yang et al., 2024). Since collect-
508
      ing human feedback is expensive and time-consuming, it has been extended to align the language
      model with preferences from other AI models or itself, known as RL from AI feedback (RLAF,
509
      Wang et al., 2023; Bai et al., 2022; Yuan et al., 2024). Our work is inspired by this line of research,
510
      where we align the teacher model with preference data collected from the student model to generate
511   tailored training examples for reasoning tasks. Recently, some concurrent works have also inspired
512   by RLAIF to improve the ability of the student model by adding an RLAIF-based loss term in the
513   training process (Agarwal et al., 2024) or using DPO to replace the commonly used Supervised
514   Fine-Tuning (Brown et al., 2020) (Zhang et al., 2024). These works focus on the training process,
515   while our work focuses on the knowledge elicitation process, specifically on how to curate training
516   data from LLMs.
517
518   7   C ONCLUSION
519
520   In this study, we propose ARTE, a novel framework in Knowledge Distillation that aligns the
521   teacher language model with the student language model’s preferences to generate a tailored in-
522   struction tuning dataset, which is inspired by responsive teaching in pedagogy, which effectively
523   lowers the learning barriers for students. Our framework consists of three main steps: Knowledge
524   Elicitation, Preference Collection, and Preference Alignment. Extensive experiments on various
525   academic benchmarks show that our method outperforms existing instruction-tuning datasets dis-
526   tilled from powerful LLMs by a large margin. Moreover, we investigate the generalization of the
527   aligned teacher model, showing that the aligned teacher model can be generalized to other reasoning
528
      benchmarks and different student models.

813   A    L IMITATIONS
814
815   Although most of the data used in ARTE are automatically generated by the teacher model, there
816   still needs some manual efforts to construct the prompts and collect the preference scores. Specifi-
817   cally, there are two main limitations in the current implementation of ARTE: First, in draft rationale
818   generation, to elicit diverse and high-quality rationales, a set of carefully designed prompts is re-
819   quired. In this work, we hand-crafted the system prompts for each task using different reasoning
820
      techniques from the paper authors themselves. Totally, we constructed 4 prompts for each task
      as presented in Table 16. Recently, Wang & Zhou (2024) proposed to Chain-of-Thought Decod-
821
      ing (CoT-Decoding) to uncover reasoning processes of questions from language models without
822
      prompts. In the future, we will explore the possibility of using CoT-Decoding to generate a reason-
823   ing process automatically. Second, in preference collection, a set of labeled examples consisting of
824   questions and answers is required to act as the validation set and preference set. Preference scores
825   are collected on these labeled question-answer pairs to measure the preference of the student model
826   towards the draft questions and rationales. In this work, we simply reuse the data from the original
827   Big-Bench dataset as the validation set. In the future, we will explore the possibility of directly
828   measuring the preference through the internal states of the student model (Kadavath et al., 2022).

911
912   The possible broader impacts of this work lie in the hallucination of language models. Language
913   models have been shown to generate biased and harmful content (Bender et al., 2021; Zhang et al.,
914   2023). In this work, we focus on the enhancement of the student model’s specific capability, such
915   as reasoning, which is not directly relevant to the generation of harmful content. It is worth noting
916   that the user should be cautious when using the student model or the teacher model to generate text,
917   especially when the generated text is used in critical applications such as medical diagnosis or legal
      advice.
