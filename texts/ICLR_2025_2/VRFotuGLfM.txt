009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Recovering human trajectories from incomplete or missing data is crucial for
012            many mobility-based urban applications, e.g., urban planning, transportation, and
013            location-based services. Existing methods mainly rely on recurrent neural networks
014            or attention mechanisms. Though promising, they encounter limitations in captur-
015            ing complex spatial-temporal dependencies in low-sampling trajectories. Recently,
016            diffusion models show potential in content generation. However, most of proposed
017            methods are used to generate contents in continuous numerical representations,
018
               which cannot be directly adapted to the human location trajectory recovery. In
               this paper, we introduce a conditional diffusion-based trajectory recovery method,
019
               namely, DiffMove. It first transforms locations in trajectories into the embedding
020
               space, in which the embedding denoising is performed, and then missing locations
021            are recovered by an embedding decoder. DiffMove not only improves accuracy
022            by introducing high-quality generative methods in the trajectory recovery, but
023            also carefully models the transition, periodicity, and temporal patterns in human
024            mobility. Extensive experiments based on two representative real-world mobility
025            datasets are conducted, and the results show significant improvements (an average
026            of 11% in recall) over the best baselines.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Mobility data plays a prominent role in many urban applications, e.g, next location recommenda-
032   tions (Feng et al., 2018), epidemic prevention (Tang et al., 2023) and urban planning (Yuan et al.,
033
      2014). However, due to privacy concerns or device malfunctions, users may not report their locations
      to the service provider continuously, which makes human trajectories sparsely distributed in space
034
      and unevenly observed in time, and thus affects the effectiveness of downstream applications. For
035
      this reason, human trajectory recovery, which infers human trajectories at a fine-grained level, raised
036   more and more attention recently.
037
038   Existing human trajectory recovery work leverages Recurrent Neural Network (Liu et al., 2016;
039
      Wang et al., 2019) or attention mechanism (Xia et al., 2021) for capturing the spatial-temporal
      dependencies and resort irregular time interval encoding modules for handling unevenly observed
040
      trajectory records. Recent studies (Xia et al., 2021; Sun et al., 2021; Deng et al., 2023) further found
041
      that explicitly utilizing historical trajectory can enhance the performance due to the strong periodicity
042   nature of the human trajectories. However, these approaches face significant limitations in handling
043   key characteristics of human mobility. First, they struggle to capture intricate spatial-temporal
044   dependencies - the interplay between spatial relationships (proximity and spatial transitions between
045   locations) and temporal patterns (sequential dependencies or periodicity of behaviors in historical
046   trajectory). Second, existing methods lack systematic mechanisms for handling data sparsity and
047   irregular sampling from incomplete check-ins. Third, their deterministic nature cannot adequately
048   capture the inherent uncertainty in human movement.
049   Though appealing performance is achieved, these approaches all made the traditional predictive
050   recovery, which has above limitations in complex sparse, irregular, and uncertain scenarios inherently
051   in human mobility. Consequently, the recovery accuracy and scenarios of downstream applications are
052   limited. For instance, a person may follow some routines from home to office daily but occasionally
053   he/she may follow different routes or change his/her preference. In such scenarios, traditional methods
      typically provide a biased deterministic imputed trajectory. However, with a generative approach

054
      to inference, a set of imputed trajectory locations can be generated through sampling or various
055   averaging techniques on imputation samples.
056
057   To address these fundamental limitations, we leverage diffusion models, which have shown superior
058
      performance in many tasks against other generative models, e.g., image generation (Ho et al., 2020)
      and audio synthesis (Kong et al., 2021). Furthermore, conditional diffusion models are recently
059
      developed for the time-series imputation (Tashiro et al., 2021) given observed entries as input, which
060
      inspires us to design a trajectory recovery model in the conditional diffusion manner.
061
062   However, it is non-trivial to apply the conditional diffusion model to solve the trajectory recovery
063   problem due to two issues. Firstly, the imputation targets of conventional diffusion models are
064
      continuous numerical values, which can be directly obtained via the denoising process, while
      those in trajectory recovery are discrete ID-represented locations - in this case, the transition and
065
      periodicity patterns of human trajectories are required to be fully exploited. Secondly, the model
066
      must simultaneously consider those abovementioned limitations in handling key characteristics of
067   human mobility - both temporal dependencies and the complex spatial relationships between current
068   and historical trajectories during the denoising process.
069
070
      To tackle these issues, we propose a novel conditional diffusion model for human trajectory recovery,
      namely, DiffMove. Our model incorporates these specialized components: 1) a novel embedding-
071
      based (with encoding and decoding) conditional diffusion framework that handles discrete locations
072
      while preserving spatial relationships, 2) a Spatial Conditional Block equipped with diffusion-oriented
073   graph neural network and attention mechanism, which captures the sparse spatial transition patterns
074   and periodicity temporal patterns from the current trajectories and historical trajectories. 3) a Target
075   Conditional Block that effectively utilizes historical information despite irregular sampling. 4) a
076   Denoising Network Block to handle uncertainty. Our contributions are three-fold:
077
078   • We propose a trajectory recovery framework DiffMove, which provides a solution to impute discrete
079
        locations leveraging diffusion models by performing the denoising process in embedding space and
        decoding the inferred embeddings back to discrete locations. To the authors’ knowledge, we are
080
        the first to design spatial temporal conditional diffusion models for human trajectory recovery task.
081
082
      • We design Spatial Conditional Block, Target Conditional Block and Denoising Network Block to
        fully fuse the knowledge of the current trajectories and historical trajectories during the conditional
083
        diffusion process and tackle the above challenges.
084
085   • Extensive experiments on two real-world mobility datasets demonstrate that DiffMove significantly
086
        outperforms state-of-the-art baselines, achieving an average improvement of 11% in Recall.
087   2 R ELATED WORKS
088
      Human Trajectory Recovery: The human trajectory recovery problem we address focuses on free-
089
      space settings, unlike MTrajRec (Ren et al., 2021) and RNTrajRec (Chen et al., 2023b), which focus
090
      on vehicles’ trajectories constrained by road networks (road segments). Human trajectory recovery
091   can be categorized into two types: the former treats missing locations in trajectories as continuous
092   two dimensional values, i.e., latitude and longitude, to be imputed (Alwan & Roberts, 1988; Moritz &
093   Bartz-Beielstein, 2017; Wang et al., 2019), while the latter infers locations from a discrete candidate
094   location pool (Liu et al., 2016; Xia et al., 2021). The former is suitable to recover trajectories with
095   high sampling frequency, e.g., vehicle trajectories, where the local context plays a more important
096   role for the imputation, while the latter is more feasible for highly sparse trajectories, e.g., human
097   trajectories, where the transition and periodicity dependency modeling are the main focus. In human
098   trajectory recovery, the de-facto approach is to explicitly utilize historical trajectory when imputating
099
      the current trajectory. For example, AttnMove (Xia et al., 2021) utilizes a multi-stage attention
      mechanism to recover missing locations. PeriodicMove (Sun et al., 2021) constructs day-level graphs
100
      to model complex transition patterns among locations. TRILL (Deng et al., 2023) is a trajectory
101
      recovery model utilizing graph convolutional networks, combining global and local mobility patterns.
102   (Chen et al., 2023a) proposes a framework called TERI, to tackle trajectory recovery in a two-stage
103   process, with a different problem setting focusing on addressing the special cases of irregular time
104   interval. Ours is more addressing on the generative manner using diffusion model to solve the regular
105   trajectory recovery problem. Existing human trajectory recovery work imputes missing locations
106   in a deterministic manner, which omits the uncertain nature of trajectories and thus constrains the
107   recovery accuracy and scenarios of downstream applications. In addition, the relationship between
      locations to be imputed and historical trajectories are not well-modeled.

      Diffusion Model for Temporal and Spatio-temporal Data: Diffusion models have found extensive
109   applications in tasks related to time series and spatio-temporal data generation, imputation, and
110   forecasting due to their competence in modeling high-dimensional data distributions. Diffusion
111   models have been applied to many time series generation tasks, such as the synthesis of electronic
112   health records (EHR) (Alcaraz & Strodthoff, 2023; He et al., 2023; Yuan et al., 2023). Many of
113   these studies adopt the denoising network architecture initially proposed in DiffWave (Kong et al.,
114   2021), which utilizes bidirectional dilated convolution to capture correlations between different
115   time steps. CSDI (Tashiro et al., 2021) leverages diffusion models for probabilistic time series
116   imputation, i.e., generating missing values conditioned on observed data points. DiffTraj (Zhu et al.,
117
      2024) represents the first attempt to generate GPS trajectories using an unconditioned diffusion
      probabilistic model. However, it focuses on generating task of raw GPS data in continuous space
118
      instead of discrete sparse locations that human trajectories always involve. TrajGDM (Chu et al.,
119
      2023) employs a diffusion model to capture universal mobility patterns, for trajectory generation, but
120   it focuses on simulating synthetic human mobility instead of recovery task on current trajectory. A
121   recent work DiffSTG (Wen et al., 2023) studied the spatial-temporal graph forecasting problem and
122   introduced a denoising network UGnet, which is capable of capturing spatial-temporal dependencies
123   among various geographical locations. However, DiffSTG focuses on predicting numerical readings
124   of geographical sensors in different locations across different time, while we focus on recovering
125   discrete locations in human trajectories.

      3.1   P ROBLEM S TATEMENT
129
130   Definition 1 (Trajectory). The trajectory is a chronological sequence of a user’s locations within a
131   single day. Let Tuj : luj,1 → luj,2 ... → luj,k ... → luj,K represent the trajectory of user u on the j-th day,
132   where luj,k denotes the visited location during the k-th time slot within a specified time interval. If the
133   location for the k-th time slot is not observed, luj,k is marked as null, i.e., luj,k is missing.
134   Definition 2 (Current and Historical Trajectory). For a given targeted day J and user u’s trajectory
135
      TuJ , we define TuJ as the user’s current trajectory, while the historical trajectories comprise u’s
      trajectories in the past (J − 1) days, denoted as {Tu1 , Tu2 , ..., TuJ−1 }.
136
137   We follow (Xia et al., 2021) to formulate the human trajectory recovery problem as follows:
138   Problem Definition.             Given user u’s trajectory TuJ along with historical trajectories
139   Tu1 , Tu2 , ..., TuJ−1 , the task is to recover the missing locations, i.e., ∀ null in TuJ , thereby recon-
140   structing the complete trajectory for the current day.

415   5.3    E XPERIMENTAL S ETTINGS
416
      Following (Deng et al., 2023), we mask randomly 10 time slots per day for both the Geolife and
417   Foursquare dataset. The trajectories are split chronologically into training (60%), validation (20%)
418   and test (20%) sets. We utilize the widely adopted metrics Recall@K and Mean Average Precision
419   (MAP) (Wang et al., 2019). Recall@K measures whether the ground truth is present in the top K
420   predictions, averaged over all test cases. MAP evaluates the overall ranking quality by considering
421   the entire prediction list. Larger values for both metrics indicate better performance. Additionally, we
422   use Distance@K, which computes the smallest geographical distance between the centers of locations
423   in the top-K ranked list and the ground truth, averaged across test cases. Lower Distance@K signifies
424   better performance. We report experimental results for Recall@K and Distance@K at K = 1, 5 and
425
      10. This allows a comprehensive assessment of our model’s ability to rank ground truth locations.

450   We conduct ablations by systematically removing individual components. The results of Foursquare

453   significantly to almost nonfunctional status. This emphasizes the inadequacy of relying solely on the

      default diffusion probabilistic model for the trajectory recovery task in latent space and underscores
      the importance of integrating multiple spatial and temporal related specific conditional modules for
455
      effective learning and training. The removal of the Temporal Transformer or Spatial Conditional Block
456
      significantly impacts performance, emphasizing their critical roles in reinforcing spatial and temporal
457   constraints for missing locations, resulting in substantial improvement when leveraging historical
458   information. The removal of the Target Conditional Block leads to decreased model performance,
459   highlighting the role of the target condition in guiding the model to reconstruct specific embeddings
460   in the locations through the diffusion process. Additionally, the Missing Location Decoder is also
461   identified as a crucial component. It can not be compared in the table since its removal renders the
462   model nonfunctional, as this module plays a vital role in converting the reconstructed embeddings of
463   missing locations into a decoded discrete ID space.

      6          C ONCLUSION
532
533
      In conclusion, this research addresses the problem of trajectory recovery from sparse human mobility
534   data by introducing a novel model, DiffMove. Leveraging a conditional diffusion framework, it
535   excels in trajectory recovery by constructing and utilizing conditional information with trajectory
536   spatial patterns, inter-trajectory dependencies, temporal and target location patterns. The model is
537   innovatively designed and integrated with multiple conditional feature extraction modules, tackling
538   the complexity of spatial temporal dependencies in a principled manner. Our extensive experiments
539   demonstrate that DiffMove outperforms all state-of-the-art baselines, showcasing its effectiveness in
      recovering missing locations.

707   The prior investigations (Xu et al., 2019; Wu et al., 2019; Sun et al., 2021) have demonstrated that a
708   gated graph neural network (GGNN) is adept at capturing intricate transition patterns among nodes.
709   This characteristic renders the gated GNN well-suited for addressing our specific problem. In the
710   graph neural network layer, we handle each trajectory independently to unveil the complex transition
711   patterns concealed within each trajectory. To elaborate, we initiate the process by establishing a
712   directed graph for each trajectory. Subsequently, the gated GNN is employed on each of these directed
713
      graphs to refine the location embeddings, thereby capturing the transition patterns into the model.
      Besides this, we manage to conduct data fusion of diffusion step embedding into the gated GNN to
714
      make the transition pattern learning adaptive to the diffusion time step.
715
716
717
      Trajectory Graph Construction: The initial step of the graph neural network layer involves
      constructing a transition graph representation for each historical and current trajectory in the context
718
      of trajectory recovery. Similar to session recommendation, given a location IDs’ trajectory T : l1 →
719
      l2 . . . → lK , we consider each location li as a node and (li−1 , li ) as an edge, representing the user’s
720   movement from li−1 to li in the trajectory T . Consequently, each trajectory can be conceptualized
721   as a directed graph. The graph structure is learned by facilitating communication among distinct
722   nodes. Specifically, let AI , AO denote the weighted transitions of incoming and outgoing edges in
723   the trajectory graph, respectively. To address the possibility of repeated occurrences of locations in a
724   trajectory, we assign each edge a normalized weight, calculated as the edge’s occurrence divided by
725   the outdegree of the start node of that edge. Consider transitions in a trajectory [l1 , l2 , l3 , l2 , l4 ], the

      A.7    D ETAILS OF BASELINES
821
      We evaluate the proposed approach against several baseline methods, including both traditional
822   approaches grounded in our understanding of human mobility and advanced deep learning models
823   capable of capturing intricate mobility patterns:
824
825           • Top: A straightforward counting-based method that selects the most frequently visited
826             location in the training set as the recovery for each user.
827           • Markov (Gambs et al., 2012): A commonly used method treating visited locations as states
828             and constructing a transition matrix to capture first-order transition probabilities.
829           • PMF (Mnih & Salakhutdinov, 2007): An advanced model rooted in conventional collabora-
830             tive filtering, based on the user location matrix.
831
              • LSTM (Liu et al., 2016): A deep learning model that captures sequential patterns through
832             recurrent neural networks, using the predicted next time slot as recovery.
833
              • BiLSTM (Zhao et al., 2018): An extension of LSTM with bidirectional recurrent neural
834
                networks, incorporating spatial-temporal constraints from all observed locations.
835
836           • DeepMove (Feng et al., 2018): A model that jointly considers user preferences and sequential
837
                dependencies for predicting the next location used for recovery.
838           • AttnMove (Xia et al., 2021): A method leveraging various attention mechanisms to capture
839             the regularity and patterns in a user’s mobility.
840           • PeriodicMove (Sun et al., 2021):A recent model that considers factors such as transition
841             patterns among locations and periodicity in human mobility.
842           • TRILL (Deng et al., 2023): The latest state-of-the-art model which is capturing global
843             mobility patterns leveraging graph convolutional networks for mobility patterns.

      For our location representation, We collect the cities’ street map data of Tokyo and Beijing from an

859   in Table 5. Our framework is not inherently tied to the 30-minute interval and can easily adapt to
860   other interval lengths, such as 10 minutes or even finer resolutions, provided the data supports such
861   granularity. We can always treat the time interval as a parameter to tune on the data side. This
862   flexibility makes our method broadly applicable, as it caters to human mobility patterns by modeling
863   discrete location transitions effectively. Human mobility typically involves meaningful transitions at
      specific timeframes (e.g., work, shopping, dining), which align naturally with discrete intervals.

882
      comparing the performance of the DiffMove and best baseline model TRILL across different scaling

883
      performance, as depicted in Figure 8, DiffMove consistently outperforms TRILL across all scaling

887   the experiments, we also found that the average training time per epoch ranges from 4.2s to 22.4s
888   (scaling from 20% to 100% of full training data) which is still comparable with the best baseline.

890   for offline applications of trajectory recovery. These findings suggest that DiffMove exhibits better
891   scalability and accuracy to variations in different data scales, making it a promising solution for
