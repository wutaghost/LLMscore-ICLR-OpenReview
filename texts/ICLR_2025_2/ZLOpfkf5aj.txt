009                                                          A BSTRACT
010
011
                  As the field of multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) progresses towards
012               larger and more complex environments, achieving strong performance while main-
013               taining memory efficiency and scalability to many agents becomes increasingly
014               important. Although recent research has led to several advanced algorithms, to
015               date, none fully address all of these key properties simultaneously. In this work, we
016               introduce Sable, a novel and theoretically sound algorithm that adapts the retention
017               mechanism from Retentive Networks to MARL. Sable’s retention-based sequence
018               modelling architecture allows for computationally efficient scaling to a large num-
019               ber of agents, as well as maintaining a long temporal context, making it well-suited
020
                  for large-scale partially observable environments. Through extensive evaluations
                  across six diverse environments, we demonstrate how Sable is able to significantly
021
                  outperform existing state-of-the-art methods in the majority of tasks (34 out of
022
                  45, roughly 75%). Furthermore, Sable demonstrates stable performance as we
023               scale the number of agents, handling environments with more than a thousand
024               agents while exhibiting a linear increase in memory usage. Finally, we conduct
025               ablation studies to isolate the source of Sable’s performance gains and confirm its
026               efficient computational memory usage. Our results highlight Sable’s performance
027               and efficiency, positioning it as a leading approach to MARL at scale.1

      1   I NTRODUCTION
055
056
057   When considering large-scale practical applications of multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL)
058
      such as autonomous driving (Lian & Deshmukh, 2006; Zhou et al., 2021; Li et al., 2022) and
      electricity grid control (Kamboj et al., 2011; Li et al., 2016), it becomes increasingly important to
059
      maintain three key properties for a system to be effective: strong performance, memory efficiency,
060
      and scalability to many agents. Although many existing MARL approaches exhibit one or two of
061   these properties, a solution effectively encompassing all three remains elusive.
062
063   To briefly illustrate our point, we consider the spectrum of approaches to MARL in terms of (1)
064
      performance (general ability to solve tasks at a moderate scale), (2) memory efficiency (the memory
      requirements to perform joint policy inference at execution time) and (3) scalability (the ability to
065
      maintain good performance as the number of agents grows large).
066
067   Indepedent learning (IL) — memory efficient but not performant or scalable. On the one end
068   of the spectrum lies IL, or decentralised methods, where agents act and learn independently. As
069   intuitively expected, the earliest work in MARL uses this approach (Tan, 1993; 1997). However,
070
      even early on, clear limitations were highlighted due to non-stationarity from the perspective of
      each learning agent (Claus & Boutilier, 1998), failing to solve even simple tasks. When deep neural
071
      networks were introduced into more modern MARL algorithms, these algorithms also followed the IL
072
      paradigm (Tampuu et al., 2017; Witt et al., 2020), with reasonable results. IL algorithms demonstrate
073   proficiency in handling many agents in a memory efficient way by typically using shared parameters
074   and conditioning on an agent identifier. However, at scale, the performance of IL methods remains
075   suboptimal compared to more centralised approaches (Papoudakis et al., 2021; Yu et al., 2022; Wen
076   et al., 2022).
077
      Centralised training with decentralised execution (CTDE) — performant and memory efficient
078
      but not yet scalable. As a solution to the failings of independent learning and lying between
079   decentralised and centralised methods, is CTDE (Kraemer & Banerjee, 2016). Here, centralisation
080   improves learning by removing non-stationarity, while decentralised execution maintains memory
081   efficient deployment. CTDE follows two main branches: value-based and actor-critic. In value-based
082   methods, centralisation is achieved through a joint value function used during training which has
083   a factorisation structure adhering to the individual-global-max (IGM) principle. This means that
084   if each agent acts greedily at execution time it is equivalent to the team acting greedily according
085   to the joint value function. Seminal work along this line include VDN (Sunehag et al., 2017) and
086   QMIX (Rashid et al., 2018), with many followup works (Son et al., 2019; Rashid et al., 2020b; Wang
087
      et al., 2020a; Son et al., 2020; Yang et al., 2020; Rashid et al., 2020a). In actor-critic methods, a
      centralised critic is used during training, and at execution time, policies are deployed independently.
088
      Many popular single-agent actor-critic algorithms have CTDE MARL versions including MADDPG
089
      (Lowe et al., 2017), MAA2C (Papoudakis et al., 2020) and MAPPO (Yu et al., 2022), and have been
090   combined with factorised critics (Wang et al., 2020b; Peng et al., 2021). Although CTDE helps
091   during training to achieve better performance at execution time, centralised training may remain
092   prohibitively expensive, especially if the size of the global state is agent dependent. Furthermore,
093   independent policies, even when trained jointly, are often limited in their coordination capabilities
094   when deployed at larger scale (Long et al., 2020; Christianos et al., 2021; Guresti & Ure, 2021).
095
      CTDE policy optimisation with theoretical guarantees — theoretically sound, performant and
096   memory efficient but not yet scalable. Until fairly recently, both value-based and actor-critic MARL
097   had limited theoretical guarantees. This changed for actor-critic methods with a series of papers
098   developing first trust region learning methods (Kuba et al., 2022a), and subsequently, mirror learning
099   (Kuba et al., 2022b) for MARL, culminating in the Fundamental Theorem of Heterogeneous-Agent
100   Mirror Learning (Kuba et al., 2022c) (Theorem 1). The theorem states that for specifically designed
101   methods, that utilise a particular heterogeneous-agent update scheme during policy optimisation,
102   monotonic improvement and convergence is guaranteed. Stemming from this work, a class of
103   heterogeneous agent RL algorithms (Zhong et al., 2024) have been proposed including HATRPO,
104
      HAPPO, HAA2C, HADDPG and HASAC (Liu et al., 2023a). Although theoretically sound, these
      algorithms generally suffer from the same drawbacks as conventional CTDE methods in terms of
105
      practical performance at scale, for similar reasons (Guo et al., 2024)
106
107   Centralised learning — theoretically sound with state-of-the-art performance but not yet memory
      efficient or scalable. On the other end of the spectrum lie centralised algorithms. These include


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      classical RL algorithms that treat MARL as a single-agent problem with an expanded action space,
109   as well as approaches that condition on global information during execution, e.g. graph-based
110   communication methods (Zhu et al., 2022). A particularly interesting line of work has been to employ
111   the use of transformers (Vaswani et al., 2017; Hu et al., 2021; Gallici et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2024)
112   and re-frame MARL as a (typically offline) sequence modeling problem (Chen et al., 2021; Meng
113   et al., 2021; Tseng et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2023b; Forsberg et al., 2024). One
114   such approach for online learning is the Multi-Agent Transformer (MAT) (Wen et al., 2022) which
115   achieves state-of-the-art (SOTA) performance in cooperative MARL tasks. Although MAT is highly
116   performant and theoretically sound (from Theorem 1 in Kuba et al. (2022c)), it has limitations:
117
      (1) MAT lacks the ability to scale to truly large multi-agent systems due to the inherent memory
      limitations of the attention mechanism (Katharopoulos et al., 2020), and (2) MAT lacks the ability to
118
      condition on observation histories. These limitations significantly impact what is possible to achieve
119
      at scale and in partially observable settings, commonly encountered in the real world.
120
121   Our work — theoretically sound, state-of-the-art performance, memory efficient and scalable. We
122   seek to develop an approach capable of SOTA performance, while being memory efficient and able to
123
      scale to many agents. To achieve this, we take inspiration from the MAT architecture and recent work
      in linear recurrent models in RL (Lu et al., 2024; Morad et al., 2024b) to develop an online sequence
124
      modeling approach to MARL but one that is significantly more memory efficient and scalable. Our
125
      key innovation is to replace the attention mechanism in MAT with an RL adapted version of the
126   retention mechanism used in the recently proposed Retentive Networks (RetNets) (Sun et al., 2023).
127   We call our approach Sable. Sable has theoretical convergence guarantees, is able to handle settings
128   with up to a thousand agents and can process entire episode sequences as memory, crucial for learning
129   in partially observable settings. Through comprehensive benchmarks across 45 different tasks, we
130   empirically verify that Sable significantly outperforms SOTA methods in the majority of cases (34
131   out of 45). This includes outperforming the SOTA fully centralised MAT, while being as memory
132   efficient as fully decentralised IPPO, achieving the best of both sides of the MARL spectrum. We
133   concretely summarise our contributions below:
134
             • We develop (to the best of our knowledge) the first encoder-decoder RetNet that uses cross-
135            retention. We further extend this encoder-decoder RetNet to have resettable hidden states
136            over a temporal sequence to ensure that information does not flow across episode boundaries.
137            This produces a RetNet-based architecture suitable for RL.
138
             • We use the above innovations to build Sable which achieves SOTA performance, is able
139            to reason over multiple timesteps, is memory efficient, scales to many agents and is by
140            design theoretically sound. We believe Sable is the first demonstration of successfully using
141            RetNets for learning policies in RL and more specifically, the first successful use of RetNets
142            as a sequence modelling approach to online MARL. Sable provides the best trade-off in
143            terms of memory efficiency when compared to independent learning, while significantly
144            surpassing CTDE methods and MAT in terms of performance and scalability (Figure 1).

193   version of retention suitable for RL. Sable enables parallel training and memory-efficient execution
194   at scale, with the ability to capture temporal dependencies across entire episodes. We explain how
195   Sable operates during both training and execution, how we adapt retention to work in MARL, and
196   provide different strategies for scaling depending on the problem setting.

282   Adapting the decay matrix for MARL In order for RetNets to work in RL, we make three key
283   adaptations to the decay matrix used during training. First, we ensure that each agent’s observations
284   are decayed by the same amount within each timestep. Second, we ensure that the decay matrix
285
      accounts for episode termination so that information is not allowed to flow over episode boundaries.
      Third, we construct an agent block-wise decay matrix for the encoder to ensure that there is full
286
      self-retention over agents within each timestep. A more detailed discussion on the construction of the
287
      decay matrices as well as an illustrative example are given in Appendix E.
288
289
      Scaling and efficient memory usage In practical applications, there might be different axes of
290   interest in terms of memory usage. For example, scaling the number of agents in the system, or
291   efficiently handling the sequence length that can be processed at training time, i.e. the number of
292   timesteps per update. We propose slightly different approaches for efficient memory use and scaling
293   across each axis.
294
295          • Scaling the number of agents. Scaling to thousands of agents requires a significant amount
296            of memory. Therefore, in this setting, we use MAT-style single-timestep sequences to
297            optimise memory usage and reserve chunking to be applied across agents. This requires
298            only changing the encoder during execution, as the decoder is already recurrent over both
299
               agents and timesteps. However, this change to the encoder makes it unable to perform full
               self-retention across agents, as it cannot be applied across chunks. During training, the
300
               process mirrors that of execution but is applied to both the encoder and decoder.
301
302          • Scaling the trajectory context length. Since the training sequence will grow proportional
303
               to N L in the case where Sable maintains memory over trajectories, training could become
               computationally infeasible for tasks requiring long rollouts. In order to accommodate this,
304
               we chunk the flattened agent-timestep observation along the time axis during training with
305
               the constraint that agents from the same timestep must always be in the same chunk. This
306            allows sable to process chunks of rollouts several factors smaller than the entire rollout
307            length while maintaining a memory of the full sequence during processing.
308
309   Theoretical monotonic improvement and convergence guarantees Sable inherits strong per-
310   formance and convergence guarantees by design through its choice of using the Proximal Policy
311   Optimisation (PPO) objective with autoregressive policy updates. These guarantees stem from recent
312   theoretical results which we briefly outline here (for a more detailed discussion we refer the reader to
313   the Appendix). First, is the advantage decomposition theorem/lemma (Kuba et al., 2021) (Lemma 1),
314   which was used to develop trust region learning approaches for MARL with monotonic performance
315
      improvement guarantees (Kuba et al., 2022a) (Theorem 2). Even though PPO-style algorithms with
      autoregressive updates do not strictly adhere to trust region learning theory, and therefore, do not
316
      have strict monotonic improvement, more recent work has placed PPO within a class of mirror learn-
317
      ing algorithms that indeed enjoy monotonic improvement and theoretical convergence guarantees
318   (Kuba et al., 2022b) (Theorem 3.6). This work has since been extended to the multi-agent setting
319   (Kuba et al., 2022c), (Theorem 1), and in particular, HAPPO (multi-agent PPO with heterogeneous
320   autoregressive updates), was shown to be an instance of multi-agent mirror learning, and therefore
321   theoretically sound. To obtain an instance of mirror learning requires defining a valid drift functional,
322   neighbourhood operator and sampling distribution. In both MAT and Sable, these design choices
323   are exactly as they are for HAPPO, and therefore, we claim that Sable inherits the same theoretical
      monotonic improvement and convergence guarantees as HAPPO, and by extension, MAT.

359       decomposition theorem. To the best of our knowledge, the Multi-Agent Transformer (MAT) (Wen
360       et al., 2022) represents the current SOTA for cooperative MARL on discrete environments, and

366       Evaluation protocol We train each algorithm for 10 independent trials for each task. Each training
367

368

370       with 95% confidence intervals while for aggregations over entire environment suites, we report the
371       min-max normalised inter-quartile mean with 95% stratified bootstrap confidence intervals. Following
372       from Agarwal et al. (2021), we consider algorithm X to have significant improvement over algorithm
373       Y if the probability of improvement score and all its associated confidence interval values are greater
374       than 0.5. All our evaluation aggregations, metric calculations and plotting leverages the MARL-eval
375       library from Gorsane et al. (2022).

458   A new environment for testing agent scalability in cooperative MARL A task in Neom is
459   characterised by a periodic, discretised 1-dimensional pattern that is repeated for a given num-
460   ber of agents. Each agent observes whether it is in the correct position and the previous ac-
461
      tions it has taken. Agents receive a shared reward which is calculated as the Manhattan distance

467   Neom environments. For LBF, experiments involve tasks with 32, 64, and 128 agents, while for
468   Neom we include tasks with 32, 512 and 1024 agents. Given the slower throughput of these tasks,
469   hyperparameter tuning was not feasible. Instead, we take the optimal hyperparameters found on
470   reasonably sized tasks and apply these across all larger tasks. For example, for LBF we use the
471   15x15-3p-5f task as it appears to be one of the hardest (See Appendix Figure 11) and for
472   Neom we use simple-sine-16-ag, half-1-half-0-16-ag and quick-flip-16-ag
473   as reference tasks. To measure the memory usage efficiency on both LBF and Neom environments on
474
      the agents’ axis, we select 32 as the fixed chunk size, which corresponds to the smallest number of

497   4.3                         A BLATIONS
498
499   We aim to better understand the source of Sable’s performance gains compared to MAT. There are
500   two specific implementation details that Sable inherits from the retention mechanism that can easily
501   be transferred to attention, and therefore to MAT. The first is using root mean square normalization
502
      (RMSNorm) (Zhang & Sennrich, 2019) instead of layer normalization (Lei Ba et al., 2016) and the
      second is using SwiGLU layers (Shazeer, 2020; Ramachandran et al., 2017) instead of feed forward
503
      layers. Other than implementation details, the difference between MAT and Sable is that Sable uses
504
      retention instead of attention and it conditions on histories instead of on single timesteps. To determine
505   the reason for the performance difference between Sable and MAT, we adapt MAT to use the above
506   implementation changes, both independently and simultaneously and we compare MAT to a version
507   of Sable with no memory that only conditions on the current timestep. We test all three variants of
508   MAT and the Sable variant on two RWARE tasks (tiny-4ag and medium-4ag) and two SMAX
509   tasks (3s5z and smacv2 5 units) and compare them with the original implementation. We tune
510   all methods using the same protocol as the main results.
511   In Figure 5a, we see that the above implementation details do make a difference to MAT’s performance.
512   In RWARE, MAT’s variants slightly increase in both performance and sample efficiency. However,
513   Sable still achieves significantly higher performance while maintaining a similar sample efficiency.
514   The same cannot be said for Sable without memory which performs similarly to the default MAT
515   and significantly worse than MAT with the implementation improvements. In SMAX, we observe
516   a marked increase in sample efficiency for MAT equalling the sample efficiency of Sable and
517   outperforming Sable’s sample efficiency without memory, but no increase in overall performance.
518   This is likely due to the fact that both MAT and Sable already achieve close to the maximum
519
      performance in these SMAX environments. In summary, we find that these implementation details
      do matter and improve the performance and sample efficiency of MAT, although not to the level of
520
      Sable’s performance. When we compared MAT, Sable, and Sable without memory, we discovered
521
      that Sable’s performance improvement stems from its ability to use temporal memory, rather than
522   from the retention mechanism itself. This is evident because Sable without memory (which performs
523   similarly to MAT) differs from Sable only in how the input sequence is structured.
524
525
      In Figure 5b, we see that even when dividing the rollout trajectories into chunks that are up to a factor
      of 16 smaller than the full rollout length, Sable’s performance remains consistent, while its memory
526
      usage decreases.

529   5           C ONCLUSION
530
531   In this work, we introduced Sable, a novel cooperative MARL algorithm that employs retentive net-
532   works to achieve significant advancements in memory efficiency, agent scalability and performance.
533   Sable’s ability to condition on entire episodes provides it with an enhanced temporal awareness, lead-
534
      ing to SOTA performance. This is evidenced by our extensive evaluation, where Sable significantly
      outperforms other leading approaches in 75% of tasks tested. Moreover, Sable’s memory efficiency
535
      complements its performance by addressing the significant challenge of scaling MARL algorithms as
536
      it is able to maintain stable performance even when scaled to over 1000 agents. Looking ahead, we
537   aim to explore Sable’s integration into more complex, larger-scale, real-world environments.

870
871   Linear recurrent models Recent work in RL has leveraged structured state space models (Lu et al.,
872   2024) for efficient long context memory. It has also been shown that various linear recurrent models
873
      including Linear Transformers (Katharopoulos et al., 2020), Fast and Forgetful Memory (Morad
      et al., 2024a), and Linear Recurrent Units (Orvieto et al., 2023) can be used for temporal memory in
874
      RL (Morad et al., 2024b). Sable falls into this category of algorithms due to leveraging the RetNet
875
      architecture, a linear recurrent model, instead of the Transformer.
876
877   Transformers and RetNets in reinforcement learning Other works have applied Transformers in
878   the context of MARL (Hu et al., 2021; Wen et al., 2022). The closest to our work is MAT (Wen et al.,
879
      2022). In single-agent RL, transformers have been used to enable long range memory (Parisotto
      et al., 2020; Esslinger et al., 2022), most notably the Gated Transformer-XL (Parisotto et al., 2020).
880
      Sable differs from these works for two main reasons: (1) it is a distinctly multi-agent algorithm
881
      and (2) it has no need for appending observation histories to input sequences since it can retain all
882   necessary information from previous timesteps with a hidden state. Moreover, and to the best of our
883   knowledge, Sable is the first architecture to leverage RetNets for learning policies in RL. The only
884   other application of RetNets has been to learn an efficient world model (Cohen et al., 2024).

1032   Adapting the LBF environment for scalability experiments In the original Level-Based Foraging
1033   (LBF) implementation, agents processed complete grid information, including items outside their
1034   FOV, managed by masking non-visible items with the placeholder (-1, -1, 0) where each element of
1035   this triplet stands for (x, y, level). To improve computational efficiency, we revised the implementation
1036   to completely remove non-visible elements from the observation data, significantly reducing the
1037   observation size and ensuring agents process only relevant information within their FOV.
1038   For instance, with a standardised FOV of 2, as illustrated in Figure 8, an agent sees a 5x5 grid centred
1039   around itself. Non-visible items are now excluded from the observation array which makes it easy
1040   to convert the agent’s vector observation with level one from [1, 2, 3, -1, -1, 0, 2, 2,
1041   1, -1, -1, 0] to [ 1, 2, 3, 2, 2, 1]. However, in tasks with numerous interacting
1042   agents, where the dynamics of visible items consistently change, fixed array sizes are required. We
1043   address this by defining the maximum number of visible items as (2 × FOV + 1)2 , filling any excess
1044
       with masked triplets to keep uniform array dimensions.

1110   exclusively on the ”simple-spread” tasks, the only non-communication, non-adversarial setting in the
1111   suite where agents cooperate instead. In this setting, agents aim to cover landmarks to gain positive
1112   rewards and avoid collisions, which result in penalties. We employ a JAX-based clone of the original
1113   environment from the JaxMARL suite (Rutherford et al., 2023).
1114
       Contrary to the suggestions made by Gorsane et al. (2022), we only experiment on the
1115
       simple-spread for the previous reasons, thus, we go beyond only the recommended version of
1116   simple-spread (simple-spread-3ag) and also test on 5 and 10 agents variants.

       Naming convention In our case, the simple-spread tasks used in the MPE environment are
1119
       named according to the following convention:

                                      simple spread <num agents>ag

1123   <num agents>: Indicates the number of agents in the simple spread task where we set the number
1124   of landmarks equal to the number of agents.

       Observation space Agents observe their own position and velocity as well as other agents positions

       and landmark positions.

       Action space Continuous actions space with 4 actions. Each action represents the velocity in all
       cardinal directions.

       penalty if they collide with other agents.

1148   B.6   M A BRAX
1149
1150   MaBrax (Rutherford et al., 2023) is an implementation of the MaMuJoCo environment (Peng et al.,
1151
       2021) in JAX, from the JAXMARL repository. The difference is that it uses BRAX (Freeman
       et al., 2021b) as the underlying physics engine instead of MuJoCo. Both MaMuJoCo and MaBrax
1152
       are continuous control robotic environment, where the robots are split up so that certain joints are
1153
       controlled by different agents. For example in ant 4x2 each agent controls a different leg of the ant.
1154   The splitting of joints is the same in both MaBrax and MaMuJoCo.
1155
1156   The goal is to move the agent forward as far and as fast as possible. The reward is based on how far
1157
       the agent moved and how much energy it took for the agent to move forward.
1158
1159   Observation space Observations are separated into local and global observations. Globally, all
1160
       agents observe the position and velocity of the root body. Locally agents observe the position and
       velocity of their joints as well as the position and velocity of their neighboring joints.
1161
1162
1163
       Action space A continuous space where each agent controls some number of joints n. Each of the n
       actions are bounded in the range [−1, 1] and the value controls the torque applied to a corresponding
1164
       joint.
1165
1166
       Reward Agents receive the reward from the single agent version of the environment. Positive
1167
       reward is given if the agent moves forward and negative reward is given when energy is used to move
1168
       the joints. Thus, agents are incentivised to move forward as efficiently as possible.
1169

       B.7   N EOM
1171
1172   Neom tasks require agents to match a periodic, discretised 1D pattern that is repeated across the given
1173   number of agents. These tasks are specifically designed to assess the agents’ ability to synchronise
1174   and reproduce specified patterns in a coordinated manner and in a limited time frame.
1175
1176   Naming convention The tasks in the Neom environment are named according to the following
1177   convention:
1178
1179                                <pattern-type>-<num agents>ag
1180
1181   Each field in the naming convention has specific options:
1182
              • <pattern-type>: Represents the selected pattern for the agents to create ( ”simple-sine”,
1183
                ”half-1-half-0”, and ”quick-flip”).
1184
1185          • <num agents>: Indicates the number of agents.
1186
1187   Observation space The observation space consists of a binary indicator showing whether the agent
       is in the correct position, concatenated with the agent’s previous actions.

       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1188
       Action space The action space consists of unique elements in the pattern, with each element
1189   defining an actions:
1190
1191           • simple-sine: {0.2, 0.3, 0.5, 0.7, 0.8}
1192           • half-1-half-0: {1, 0}
1193
               • quick-flip: {0.5, 0, -0.5}
1194
1195
       Reward The reward function is calculated using the mean Manhattan distance between the team’s
1196
       current pattern and the target pattern. The reward ranges from 1 for a perfect match to -1 for
1197
       the maximum difference, with normalization applied. Additionally, if the pattern is correct, the
1198   agents receive a bonus that starts at a maximum value of 9.0 and gradually decreases as the episode
1199   progresses, based on how much time has passed.

1543   We make all hyperparameters as well as instructions for rerunning all benchmarks available along with
1544   the code provided at the following link: https://sites.google.com/view/sable-marl. For all on-policy
1545   algorithms on all tasks, we always use 128 effective vectorised environments. For HASAC and
1546   MASAC we use 64 vectorised environments while for QMIX we use 32 vectorised environments.
1547   We leverage the design architecture of Mava which can distribute the end-to-end RL training loop
1548   over multiple devices using the pmap JAX transformation and also vectorise it using the vmap JAX
1549   transformation. For IPPO, MAPPO and Sable we train systems with and without memory. For IPPO
1550   and MAPPO this means that networks include a Gated Recurrent Unit (GRU) (Cho, 2014) layer for
1551
       memory and for Sable this means training over full episode trajectories at a time or only one timestep
       at a time. For MASAC and HASAC (Liu et al., 2023a) we only train policies using MLPs and for
1552
       QMIX (Rashid et al., 2020a) we only train a system with memory due to the original implementations
1553
       of these algorithms doing so.
1554
1555   In cases where systems are trained with and without memory, we report results for the version of the
1556   system that performs the best on a given task. In all hyperparameter tables a parameter marked with
1557
       an asterisk “*” implies that it is only relevant for the memory version of a given algorithm.

1785
1786   RetNet is a decoder-only architecture designed with only causal language modelling in mind. This
1787
       is illustrated by the assumption in Equations 1, 2 and 3 that the key, query and value inputs are

1788
       model to encode observations and decode actions. In order to extend retention to support the cross-
1789
       retention used decoder, Sable takes a key, query and value as input to a retention block in place of X.
1790   Additionally, it uses the key as a proxy for X in the swish gate used in multi-scale retention (Sun
1791   et al., 2023) and the query as a proxy for X for the skip connection in the RetNet block (Sun et al.,
1792   2023).

1796
1797   Since the decay matrix represents the importance of past observations, it is critical to construct
1798   it correctly during training, taking into account multiple agents and episode terminations, so that
1799   no agent is “favoured” and memory doesn’t flow over episode boundaries. To achieve this, we
1800   make three modifications to the original decay matrix formula. First, in cooperative MARL, a joint
1801   action is formed such that all agents act simultaneously from the perspective of the environment.
1802   This means the memory of past observations within the same timestep should be weighted equally
1803

       self-supervised learning, RL requires algorithms to handle episode termination. During acting this
1804

1805

1807   should be reset from index (N td , N td ). Combining these two modifications, we obtain the following

1827   E.2.3    P OSITIONAL E NCODING
1828
       Positional encodings are crucial for Sable to obtain a notion of time. Previous works in single-
1829
       agent reinforcement learning (RL) that utilize transformers or similar architectures (Parisotto et al.
1830   (2020); Lu et al. (2024)) have demonstrated the importance of positional encoding. Empirical results
1831   showed that the best method for sable is absolute positional encoding, as introduced by Vaswani et al.
1832   (2017). In this method, the agent’s timestep is encoded and added to the key, query, and value during
1833   processing. Importantly, we discovered that providing all agents within the same timestep with the
1834   same positional encoding is pivotal for performance. When agents were assigned sequential indices
1835   (e.g., agent i at step t receiving index N × t + i), as is commonly done with tokens in NLP tasks, the
       performance was significantly worse.
