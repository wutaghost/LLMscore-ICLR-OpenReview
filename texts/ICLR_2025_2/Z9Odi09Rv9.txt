                                                      A BSTRACT
010
011
                Diffusion models have been firmly established as principled zero-shot solvers for
012             linear and nonlinear inverse problems, owing to their powerful image prior and ease
013             of formulation as Bayesian posterior samplers. However, many existing solvers
014             struggle in the noisy measurement regime, either overfitting or underfitting to the
015             measurement constraint, resulting in poor sample quality and inconsistent perfor-
016             mance across noise levels. Moreover, existing solvers rely on approximating x0 via
017             Tweedie’s formula, where an intractable conditional score is replaced by an uncon-
018             ditional score network, introducing a fundamental source of error in the resulting
019             solution. In this work, we propose a novel frequentist’s approach to diffusion-based
020
                inverse solvers, where each diffusion step can be seen as the maximum likelihood
                solution to a simple single-parameter conditional likelihood model, derived by an
021
                adjusted application of Tweedie’s formula to the forward measurement model. We
022
                demonstrate that this perspective is not only scalable and fast, but also allows for a
023             noise-aware maximization scheme with a likelihood-based stopping criterion that
024             promotes the proper noise-adapted fit given knowledge of the measurement noise
025             σy . Finally, we demonstrate comparable or improved performance against a wide
026             selection of contemporary inverse solvers across multiple datasets, tasks, and noise
027             levels.

      A Maximum Likelihood Solver We take a different perspective on solving the inverse problem.
252
      As seen in Section 3, both projection and posterior solvers must quantify the discrepancy between
253
      xt and y via the consistency error ||A(x0 ) − y|| at each diffusion step. Due to the complexity of the
254   diffusion process, this involves approximating a fundamentally intractable quantity. In Section 4, we

      6   C ONCLUSION
541
542
      We proposed an effective adjustment to the diffusion-based inverse problem solver framework in the
543   literature that improves speed and stability. Observing that the marginals of the diffusion process
544   which solves the inverse problem is Gaussian distributed at each time t, we derived a simple, single-
545   parameter likelihood model, whose sole unknown variate may be obtained via a tractable maximum
546   likelihood estimation algorithm. This casts a frequentist’s light on the inverse problem framework
547   in diffusion-based solvers, as opposed to the prevailing current of posterior and projection-based
548   perspectives. We leveraged this new perspective to create a noise-aware maximization scheme, and
549   demonstrated the effectiveness of our method via a suite of numerical experiments.

961
962   Our proposed DCS has just two primary hyperparameters, as described in the table below. First is
963   the number of time steps T . This has relatively little effect on our model performance on most tasks.
964   However, it is occasionally helpful to increase T , especially in box inpainting, where there is zero
965   signal from y in the masked region. Here, higher T allows the diffusion model to obtain a better
966   solution in this unconditional diffusion process. Second, we have the choice of minimizer, which
967   is by default the Adam optimizer Kingma and Ba (2014). However, in the case of linear A, this
968
      optimizer can be replaced by the closed form analytical solution to A(x) = y.
969   For nearly all experiments, we use the Adam optimizer with 50 optimization steps and a learning
970   rate of 1. The exceptions are the random inpainting and box inpainting tasks, where there is no
971   conditioning information on the masked pixels. This requires more denoising steps, as the diffusion
      process is totally unconditional inside the mask, up to local correlations learned inside the score

1005
       pling step, which includes a posterior-based guidance step that takes an expensive gradient of the
       noise function. To see this, note that L255 in the resample_sampling function in ddim.py
1006
       calls a function measurement_cond_fn, which is defined at L62 in main.py and passed
1007
       into the resampling function. This function is a member of the class PosteriorSampling
1008   defined in L53 in condition_methods.py. Inspecting this class, we note that it calls
1009   torch.autograd.grad on the diffusion step as a function of x_prev (L33 or L39). In other
1010   words, a gradient is computed for the measurement norm with respect to the input to the diffusion
1011   model, i.e., a DPS step.
1012
       We closely investigated this DPS step in our experiments, ultimately concluding that it has a significant
1013
       effect on the performance of the algorithm, and that it was a more fair comparison to include this step,
1014   rather than removing it. However, the inclusion of this sampling step has two primary effects. First, it
1015   results in further increases the computation time of ReSample. Second it reveals that ReSample relies
1016   significantly on a posterior-based formulation, applying additional resampling steps at each stage.
1017
       In experiments, we note that ReSample is significantly slower than other algorithms during sampling
1018
       (see Table 2). For example, sampling ∼ 1000 images with ImageNet takes more than two weeks
1019
       on an A6000 GPU. Since we run five different experimental conditions for each dataset, this was
1020   an unacceptably long runtime for our academic resources. Therefore, we reduce the number of
1021   diffusion steps T of ReSample in our experiments, from 500 reported in (Song et al., 2024) to 50.
1022   However, we do provide a single experiment from the (Song et al., 2024) paper, where we reproduce
1023   the hyperparameters and dataset (a 100 image subset of FFHQ). We note that (Song et al., 2024)
1024   took a subset of the FFHQ dataset, where performance differed from the full 256×256-1K dataset
1025   performance (c.f. Table 1). Since the subset was not published, we selected a dataset based where
       ReSample obtained the same performance with its default parameters in (Song et al., 2024) (Table 4).

1028   We used the version of DDRM which is implemented in the DDNM codebase. While DDRM may
1029   theoretically be able to handle deblurring tasks, due to the high rank of the forward operators, the

1031   memory-saving versions of these operators. Because of the relatively poor performance of DDRM
1032   compared to DDNM, and the fact that DDRM can be considered a subtype of DDNM (see Appendix
1033   of Wang et al. (2022)), we do not run on deblurring tasks.
