011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Self-supervised multi-frame depth estimation predicts depth by leveraging geo-
014            metric cues from multiple input frames. Traditional methods construct cost vol-
015            umes based on epipolar geometry to explicitly integrate the geometric informa-
016            tion from these input frames. Although this approach may seem effective, the
017            epipolar-based cost volume has two key limitations: (1) it assumes a static en-
018
               vironment, and (2) requires pose information during inference. As a result, this
               cost volume fails in real-world scenarios where dynamic objects and image noise
019
               are often present, and pose information is unavailable. In this paper, we demon-
020
               strate that the cross-attention map can function as a full cost volume to address
021            these limitations. Specifically, we find that training the cross-attention layers for
022            image reconstruction enables them to implicitly learn a warping function within
023            the cross-attention, resembling the explicit epipolar warping used in traditional
024            self-supervised depth estimation methods. To this end, we propose the CRoss-
025            Attention map and Feature aggregaTor (CRAFT), which is designed to effectively
026            leverage the matching information of the cross-attention map by aggregating and
027            refining the full cost volume. Additionally, we utilize CRAFT in a hierarchical
028            manner to progressively improve depth prediction results through a coarse-to-fine
029
               approach. Thorough evaluations on the KITTI and Cityscapes datasets demon-
               strate that our approach outperforms traditional methods. In contrast to previous
030
               methods that employ epipolar-based cost volumes, which often struggle in regions
031
               with dynamic objects and image noise, our method demonstrates robust perfor-
032            mance and provides accurate depth predictions in these challenging conditions.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
037
038   Depth estimation has been essential for a wide range of computer vision tasks, including autonomous
039
      navigation (Geiger et al., 2012), augmented reality (Luo et al., 2020), or 3D reconstruction (New-
      combe et al., 2011). Extensive research has been conducted on self-supervised depth estimation,
040
      as it eliminates the need for laborious depth annotations or specialized sensors. While studies have
041
      explored both single-frame (Godard et al., 2019; 2017) and multi-frame (Watson et al., 2021; Ban-
042   gunharcana et al., 2023; Ruhkamp et al., 2021) methods, multi-frame depth estimation generally
043   outperforms single-frame approaches by leveraging additional geometric cues between frames.
044
045
      In self-supervised multi-frame depth estimation (Watson et al., 2021; Feng et al., 2022; Bangunhar-
      cana et al., 2023), a common method to leverage this geometric cue is to construct a cost volume
046
      based on epipolar geometry. We refer to this as an epipolar-based cost volume. This volume rep-
047
      resents the matching cost between the target feature and the reconstructed target feature based on
048   various depth hypotheses. The reconstructed target feature is obtained by warping the source fea-
049   tures to the target using pixel-wise similarity calculations relative to the epipolar line in the source
050   image. While this method provides valuable depth information based on multi-view geometry, it
051   has two key limitations: 1) Dynamic objects and image noise can cause misalignment in pixel cor-
052   respondences along the epipolar line, leading to violations of the epipolar constraint (Watson et al.,
053   2021; Feng et al., 2022). Previous studies have mitigated this issue by incorporating monocular
      cues (Godard et al., 2019), but this approach remains limited as the final depth prediction perfor-


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      mance heavily relies on the monocular prior. 2) The construction of an epipolar-based cost volume
055   requires an additional pose network during training and inference.
056
057   To address the limitations of epipolar-based cost volumes, a straightforward approach may be to
058
      build a cost volume that calculates pixel-wise similarity for all points between the target and source
      frames, including those that do not lie on the epipolar line. We refer to this as a full cost volume.
059
      Full cost volumes are commonly used in supervised image matching tasks, as they effectively convey
060
      comprehensive geometric information to the model (Cho et al., 2021; Xu et al., 2022). However,
061   training a full cost volume in a self-supervised manner poses significant challenges due to its lack
062   of constraints (Kim et al., 2018; Rocco et al., 2018), compared to epipolar-based cost volumes. This
063   is primarily because repetitive patterns and background clutter can introduce ambiguities in feature
064   similarity calculations, complicating its adaptation for multi-frame depth estimation tasks.
065
      We draw inspiration from the widely used cross-attention mechanism (Carion et al., 2020; Ruhkamp
066
      et al., 2021), which retrieves information by calculating the key that closely matches the query. Our
067   findings demonstrate that the processes of obtaining the cross-attention map and the full cost volume
068   exhibit mathematical similarities. Furthermore, we show that training the cross-attention layers for
069   image reconstruction enables them to implicitly learn a warping function within the cross-attention
070   map, similar to the explicit epipolar warping employed in self-supervised depth estimation. As a
071   result, we argue that the cross-attention learned in this manner enables the cross-attention map to
072   operate as a full cost volume. To address the challenges associated with training a full cost volume
073   in a self-supervised manner, we leverage masked image modeling (Gupta et al., 2023; Weinzaepfel
074   et al., 2022; Bachmann et al., 2022) to facilitate the pretraining of the cross-attention layers. We val-
075
      idate this approach by visualizing the cross-attention map, which demonstrates accurate matching,
      suggesting that it effectively captures sufficient geometric information. Moreover, since the atten-
076
      tion layer is learned without explicit constraints, it does not require additional information, such as
077
      camera pose, thereby enhancing its robustness in dynamic scenarios.
078
079   In this paper, we propose a novel self-supervised multi-frame depth estimation architecture that
080   replaces the epipolar-based cost volume with a cross-attention map, which can be viewed as an
081
      asymmetric full cost volume. However, simply incorporating the cross-attention map into the model
      introduces challenges related to resolving inaccuracies and noise in the warping information present
082
      in the cost volume. To resolve these ambiguities and filter out unnecessary information for depth
083
      prediction, we propose CRoss-Attention map and Feature aggregaTor (CRAFT), designed to com-
084   press and refine the full cost volume through feature aggregation with respect to the target image.
085   Furthermore, to capture finer details and handle low-resolution cost volumes, we arrange the CRAFT
086   modules in a pyramidal structure, refining the cost volumes hierarchically. This approach is similar
087   to other methods that enhance features in a coarse-to-fine manner (Min et al., 2021).
088
      We evaluate the robustness of our model in dynamic scenarios by testing it in environments with dy-
089
      namic objects and various types of image noise, also across overall scenes using the KITTI (Geiger
090   et al., 2013) and Cityscapes (Cordts et al., 2016) datasets. In constrast to traditional self-supervised
091   multi-frame depth estimation methods that rely on epipolar-based cost volumes, our approach fully
092   exploits multi-frame geometric cues from the full cost volume, resulting in enhanced performance
093   in real-world scenarios, particularly in environments with dynamic objects and image noise.

095   2   R ELATED W ORK
096
097   Self-supervised monocular depth estimation. Previous supervised monocular depth estimation
098   methods (Eigen et al., 2014; Eigen & Fergus, 2015; Fu et al., 2018) have achieved notable perfor-
099
      mance by training models with ground truth depth maps. However, acquiring such maps requires a
      labor-intensive annotation process or specialized sensors and cameras, which are often not readily
100
      available to many researchers. As a result, self-supervised depth estimation methods have emerged
101
      as an attractive alternative, framing the training pipeline as an image reconstruction loss using stereo
102   pairs (Xie et al., 2016; Garg et al., 2016; Godard et al., 2017) or monocular video sequences (Godard
103   et al., 2017). Despite the success of (Godard et al., 2017), challenges remain in effectively handling
104   occlusion, noise, and dynamic objects in self-supervised depth estimation from video sequence.
105   Further studies have attempted to tackle these challenges by developing methods for robust image
106   reconstruction loss (Gordon et al., 2019; Godard et al., 2019), discrete depth representation (John-
107   ston & Carneiro, 2020), network architecture improvements (Guizilini et al., 2020), and handling
      moving objects (Gordon et al., 2019; Li et al., 2021; Godard et al., 2019).


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      Multi-frame depth estimation. The aforementioned self-supervised monocular depth estimation
109   methods utilize temporal frames to form an image reconstruction loss but do not leverage this ad-
110   ditional information during inference. To make use of this temporal information, early multi-frame
111   methodologies have employed recurrent networks (CS Kumar et al., 2018; Patil et al., 2020; Wang
112   et al., 2019; Zhang et al., 2019) or test-time optimization techniques (Chen et al., 2019; Kuznietsov
113   et al., 2021; Luo et al., 2020). Recent studies (Watson et al., 2021; Feng et al., 2022; Guizilini
114   et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023; Bangunharcana et al., 2023) has employed temporal information
115   by constructing a cost volume through epipolar geometry. Specifically, given pre-defined or adap-
116   tively determined depth hypotheses and a relative pose between the target and source, the source
117
      features are warped to align with the target according to each depth hypothesis, allowing for the
      measurement of similarity for each warped feature. This epipolar-based cost volume calculates the
118
      depth likelihood of the target, thereby providing the model with coarse depth information. How-
119
      ever, these methods assume static scenes, struggle with moving objects, and are susceptible to noise.
120   Another approach utilizes spatial-temporal attention for multi-frame depth estimation (Ruhkamp
121   et al., 2021). While this approach eliminates the need for an epipolar-based cost volume, it requires
122   auxiliary depth prediction for spatial attention and encounters difficulties when matching objects of
123   similar appearance, resulting in ambiguous attention.
124
125   Masked image modeling Inspired by the self-supervised masked modeling of BERT (Devlin,
126   2018) in NLP, various approaches have been developed to pretrain Vision Transformers (Dosovit-
127   skiy, 2020) using self-supervised techniques, aiming to capture richer context and finer details for
128   better representation learning. ViT (Dosovitskiy, 2020) introduced masked patch prediction to im-
129   prove representations, while BEiT (Bao et al., 2021) extended this idea by predicting discrete visual
130
      tokens. More recently, MIM approaches (Xie et al., 2022; He et al., 2022a; Bachmann et al., 2022),
      which involve reconstructing masked regions of an image, have demonstrated significant success as
131
      a pretraining strategy, achieving promising results in downstream tasks.
132
133   Furthermore, several methods (Gupta et al., 2023; Bachmann et al., 2022; Weinzaepfel et al., 2022)
134   utilize cross-attention layers to reconstruct masked targets by incorporating additional information,
135   using image reconstruction as a means of learning from supplementary data. For instance, Multi-
136
      MAE (Bachmann et al., 2022) learns cross-modality predictive coding through self- and cross-
      attention applied to different modality inputs. CroCo (Weinzaepfel et al., 2022; 2023) learns ge-
137
      ometry by reconstructing a masked target image using the source from the same scene. Similarly,
138
      SiamMAE (Gupta et al., 2023) improves motion understanding in videos by reconstructing one
139   frame using another frame from the same video sequence.

258   4.1.2                            R EVISITING CROSS - ATTENTION
259
260   The cross-attention map obtained by processing the encoded target and source features through the
261   cross-attention layer exhibits a strong similarity to a full cost volume. Cross-attention has mainly
262   been employed in tasks involving multi-modal inputs or interactions between distinct inputs, where
263
      it extracts relevant information from key features corresponding to the query features (Kirillov et al.,
      2023; Carion et al., 2020; Guizilini et al., 2022). Specifically, the similarity information is stored
264
      within the cross-attention map, and by examining the equation for the cross-attention map Cattn ∈
265
      Rh×w×h×w , we can observe that it closely resembles a full cost volume:
266
267                                                                                                                                        Cattn (i, j) = softmax(FtQ (i) · FsK (j)),                                                                                                                                                                      (7)
268
269   where FtQ = PQ (Ft ) and FsK = PK (Fs ) denote the projected features obtained using PQ and PK ,
      representing the query and key projections, respectively. softmax(·) denotes a softmax function.

      Both the cross-attention map (Equation 7) and the full cost volume (Equation 6) measure pixel-by-
271   pixel similarity between target and source features. In contrast to the symmetric full cost volume,
272   the cross-attention mechanism projects the query and key into distinct spaces and applies softmax,
273   introducing directionality that captures only the geometry of the source relative to the target (Kirillov
274   et al., 2023; Carion et al., 2020; Guizilini et al., 2022). Consequently, the cross-attention map can
275   be viewed as an asymmetric variant of the full cost volume. From the viewpoint of the full cost
276   volume, the cross-attention layer functions similarly to feature warping, retrieving the most similar
277   target feature from the source based on the information encoded in the cross-attention map.

279   4.1.3    C ROSS - ATTENTION WITH SELF - SUPERVISION
280
      We demonstrate that cross-attention learned in a self-supervised manner, when tasked with image
281
      reconstruction, enables the cross-attention map to function as a full cost volume. Specifically, we
282
      hypothesize that when cross-attention receives both the target and source as inputs and outputs a
283   reconstructed target, it learns an implicit warping process. This occurs because the reconstruction of
284   the target through feature warping of the source, guided by the attention map, resembles the method
285   used in self-supervised depth estimation, where the source feature reconstructs the target feature
286   using epipolar warping. Analyzing the feature warping equation of cross-attention which produces
287   the reconstructed target reveals:
288

293   key features using PV . This equation, along with epipolar warping (Equation 4), highlights the
294   similarity in performing warping to retrieve features from the source frame using the sampler for
295   reconstructing the target. However, while epipolar warping retrieves only a single point based on
296   explicit geometric information, feature warping gathers information from all points and combines
297   them through a weighted sum, where the weights sum to one. Interestingly, the concept of retrieving
298
      a single point in epipolar warping can be viewed as a special case of feature warping’s weighted
      sum, where one weight is one and all others are zero.
299
300   While the warping equations for both methods are identical, epipolar warping retrieves only a single
301   point, whereas cross-attention aggregates information from all available points, thereby offering
302   enhanced flexibility. However, this increased flexibility presents challenges in training the model
303   to generate an accurately reconstructed target feature. Fortunately, established methods for training
304
      cross-attention, such as masked image modeling (MIM) (Weinzaepfel et al., 2022; Gupta et al.,
      2023; Bachmann et al., 2022), differ from those used to train full cost volumes. By following the
305
      training approach of MIM, we train the model on a large number of frames by reconstructing masked
306
      target frames, enabling the cross-attention to learn geometry:

312
      pretrained encoder features (Figure 3 (c)), the cross-attention map, which is learned through frame
313
      reconstruction loss (Figure 3 (d)) exhibits more accurate matching, indicating a deeper understand-
314   ing of geometry. Furthermore, the attention map focuses on a single point, which can be interpreted
315   as an emergent effect (Wei et al., 2022) arising from training on a large dataset, suggesting that the
316   model has implicitly learned geometry. Additionally, by observing the entire scene rather than being
317   confined to the epipolar line, it effectively captures dynamic regions, making the cross-attention map
318   well-suited for functioning as an asymmetric full cost volume.

321
322   Self-supervised multi-frame depth estimation models utilize cost volumes to offer direct multi-frame
323   cues. Similarly, we intend for our model to leverage multi-frame cue information from the cross-

345   4.3     C OST VOLUME REFINEMENT
346
347   The attention map exhibits significantly low resolution due to the patchifying stage within the trans-
348   former architecture. As a result, it becomes challenging to generate a finely detailed depth map
349   using the low-resolution attention map. To address this issue, we stack the CRAFT modules in a
350   pyramidal structure, allowing for hierarchical refinement of the cost volumes, similar to previous
351
      methods that enhance features in a coarse-to-fine manner (Min et al., 2021). As illustrated in Fig-
      ure 2, we employ a four-stage pyramidal process using CRAFT modules. We select four different
352
      levels of target features {Fl }4l=1 and attention maps {Cattn,l }4l=1 from the transformer decoder. For
353
      hierarchical processing, we adjusted the resolution of each level’s target feature and attention map
354   in a coarse-to-fine manner. To achieve this, we applied the Reassemble operation from DPT (Ranftl
355   et al., 2021) and bilinear interpolation. The interpolated target features and attention maps are then
356   aggregated to obtain a refined cost volume:
357
                  Cref,l = Tfeat ([Tattn (interp(Cattn,l + Vl−1 )), Fl′ ]),     Fl′ = Reassemblel (Fl ),
358
359   where l denotes the stage number, interp refers to bilinear interpolation, Reassemblel indicates the
360   Reassemble blocks for stage l, and Cref,l represents the refined cost volume. Additional details can
361   be found in Section B.2 and Section B.3 of the appendix.

529   6      C ONCLUSION
530
531
      We have introduced a novel self-supervised multi-frame depth estimation architecture that leverages
      the CRAFT module to compress and refine the cost volume through attention and feature aggre-
532
      gation. Our results demonstrate that training the cross-attention layers for image reconstruction
533
      enables the implicit learning of a warping function within the cross-attention map, similar to the
534   explicit epipolar warping used in previous self-supervised depth estimation methods. Therefore, we
535   conclude that this learned cross-attention enables the cross-attention map to effectively function as a
536   full cost volume. Furthermore, by employing masked image modeling for pretraining, we effectively
537   utilize the cross-attention map as a full cost volume to enhance our depth prediction robustness in
538   dynamic scenarios without the need for additional information, such as camera pose. Evaluations on
539   the KITTI and Cityscapes datasets reveal that our approach outperforms traditional methods using
      epipolar-based cost volumes, particularly in environments with dynamic objects and image noise.

769   A.1   I MPLEMENTATION DETAILS
770
771   We adopt the same augmentation scheme as outlined in (Godard et al., 2019), also using three frames
772   {It−1 , It , It+1 } for self-supervision during training. Our models are trained with input resolutions
773   of 640 × 192 for the KITTI dataset and 416 × 128 for the Cityscapes dataset. Experiments were
774   conducted using PyTorch (Aljundi et al., 2019) on an RTX 3090 GPU with a batch size of 8. We em-
775   ploy the Adam (Kingma & Ba, 2014) optimizer, setting the learning rate as 5e − 6 for the pretrained
776   encoder and decoder, and 5e − 5 for the remaining components. We incorporate static augmentation
777
      from ManyDepth (Watson et al., 2021) with a 50% ratio. For the photometric loss function, we set
      λ = 0.001 and α = 0.85.
778
779   To leverage the cross-attention map as a full cost volume, we extract both target feature and the cross-
780   attention map from the transformer decoder blocks and feed them hierarchically into our CRAFT
781   module to produce a refined cost volume. When training from scratch, we trained our network using
782   both reprojection loss (Godard et al., 2019) and image reconstruction loss. When performing image
783
      reconstruction, we set the mask ratio to 0.5 and applied a weighted sum to the loss by multiplying it
      by 0.1. Our pose network is implemented as described in (Godard et al., 2019).

847   B     A RCHITECTURE DETAILS
848
849   We begin by reviewing the notations and dimensions of the input images, cross-attention map, and
850   target features. Specifically, the resized input images It−1 , It ∈ R192×640×3 are patchified (with
851   a patch size of 16) and then embedded for processing by the transformer. This results in a cross-
852   attention map A ∈ Rh×w×hw and a target feature F ∈ Rh×w×d , where h = 12, w = 40 and d =
853
      768. Conventionally, attention maps and features of transformers are expressed in 2D dimensions
      of RN ×N and RN ×d , respectively, where N denotes the number of tokens. Here, we express the
854
      attention maps and features in 3D dimensions, where N = hw, and the channels in the attention
855
      maps represent the number of tokens in It−1 . Note that the cross-attention map is averaged along
856   the head dimension.

863   CRAFT module. The CRAFT module’s output is the refined cost volume. The cross-attention map is
      compressed using two transformer layers (Dosovitskiy, 2020), while the target feature is compressed

      studies (Ranftl et al., 2021). The final refined cost volume input to the depth head has dimensions of
