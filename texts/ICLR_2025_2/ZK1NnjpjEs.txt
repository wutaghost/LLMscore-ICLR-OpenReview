011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013           Large language models (LLMs), primarily built on decoder-only transformer ar-
014           chitectures, excel in natural language generation tasks and have shown promise
015           in adapting to diverse downstream tasks using zero-shot and few-shot prompting
016           techniques. However, these prompting methods often fall short on natural language
017
              understanding (NLU) tasks, where smaller encoder-only models like BERT-base
              consistently outperform LLMs on benchmarks such as GLUE and SuperGLUE.
018
              In this paper, we explore two approaches—supervised fine-tuning and proximal
019
              policy optimization (PPO)—to enhance the NLU capabilities of LLMs. To reduce
020           the computational cost of full-model fine-tuning, we integrate low-rank adaptation
021           (LoRA) layers, restricting updates to these layers during both supervised fine-tuning
022           and PPO stages. In the supervised fine-tuning approach, task-specific prompts are
023           concatenated with input queries and ground-truth labels from the NLU training
024           corpus, optimizing the model using the next-token prediction objective. Despite
025           this, LLMs still underperform compared to encoder-only models like BERT-base
026           on several NLU tasks. To address this gap, we employ PPO, a reinforcement
027           learning technique that treats each token generation as an action and evaluates the
028
              sequence of generated tokens using a reward function based on their alignment
              with ground-truth answers. PPO then updates the model to maximize these re-
029
              wards, effectively aligning its outputs with the correct labels. Our experiments
030
              with the LLAMA2-7B model demonstrate that PPO-based fine-tuning significantly
031           improves performance, delivering an average gain of 6.3 points over supervised
032           fine-tuning on the GLUE benchmark. PPO surpasses zero-shot prompting by 38.7
033           points and few-shot prompting by 26.1 points on GLUE, while also outperforming
034           these baselines by 28.8 and 28.5 points on SuperGLUE. Additionally, PPO exceeds
035           the performance of BERT-large, a strong baseline, with an average improvement
036           of 2.7 points on GLUE and 9.3 points on SuperGLUE. These improvements are
037           consistent across models such as Qwen2.5-7B and MPT-7B, highlighting PPO’s
038           robustness and effectiveness in enhancing the NLU capabilities of LLMs.

      2     R ELATED W ORKS

110   2.1   NATURAL L ANGUAGE U NDERSTANDING
111
112   Natural language understanding (NLU) tasks are crucial for evaluating a model’s ability to com-
113   prehend and process human language in various contexts, such as classification, inference, and
114   reasoning. The GLUE benchmark Wang et al. (2019) serves as a key standard for NLU performance,
115   covering tasks like CoLA, SST-2, MRPC, MNLI, and so on, which assess grammatical acceptability,
116   sentiment analysis, paraphrase detection, and textual entailment. For more complex challenges, the
117
      SuperGLUE benchmark Wang et al. (2020) was introduced, featuring more difficult tasks that require
      advanced reasoning and comprehension. Together, GLUE and SuperGLUE provide a comprehensive
118
      assessment of a model’s language understanding capabilities.
119
120   Models such as BERT Devlin (2018), which utilize a bidirectional encoder architecture, have achieved
121   state-of-the-art performance in NLU tasks. BERT’s architecture allows it to capture bidirectional
122   context. Its pretraining strategy, which uses masked language modeling (MLM), helps the model learn
123
      deep semantic representations. This combination makes BERT highly effective across a wide range
      of NLU tasks. The success of encoder-only models in benchmarks such as GLUE and SuperGLUE
124
      can largely be attributed to their ability to capture rich bidirectional context during pretraining, which
125
      is critical for NLU tasks.
126
127   In contrast, LLMs like GPT-2 Radford et al. (2019), GPT-3 Brown (2020), and LLAMA Touvron et al.
128   (2023b) rely on scaling model size with decoder-only architectures, achieving significant success
129
      in text generation tasks. However, their zero-shot performance with task-specific prompts remains
      suboptimal on NLU tasks, such as those in the GLUE benchmark. This underperformance is attributed
130
      to their autoregressive nature, which limits their ability to capture the bidirectional dependencies
131
      crucial for deep contextual understanding Devlin (2018); Radford et al. (2019); Brown (2020). Efforts
132   to adapt LLMs for NLU have focused on prompt-based methods like few-shot prompting Brown
133   (2020), which show promise but still fall short of the performance achieved by encoder-only models
134   like BERT on these tasks.
135
136   2.2   P OLICY- BASED R EINFORCEMENT L EARNING
137
138   Policy-based reinforcement learning (RL) directly optimizes an agent’s policy by learning its pa-
139   rameters to maximize long-term rewards. Unlike value-based methods like Q-learning Watkins &
140   Dayan (1992) and DQN Hester et al. (2018), which indirectly derive policies through value functions,
141
      policy-based methods represent the policy as a parameterized function. This function, pθ (a|s), defines
      the probability of taking action a in state s, where θ represents the policy parameters. The goal is to
142
      learn optimal parameters θ∗ that maximize the expected cumulative reward, typically through policy
143
      gradient methods Sutton et al. (1999). These methods excel in high-dimensional or continuous action
144   spaces, where value-based methods can struggle Deisenroth et al. (2013).
145
146   Policy-based methods in reinforcement learning (RL) have evolved significantly over time, starting
147
      with REINFORCE Williams (1992), which optimizes policies using the policy gradient theorem
      but suffers from high variance due to its reliance on Monte Carlo estimates of the reward. Monte
148
      Carlo estimates refer to calculating the total reward based on full episodes of interaction, meaning
149
      updates are made only after an entire sequence of actions and rewards is observed, which can lead
150   to noisy and slow learning. To address this, actor-critic methods like A2C and A3C Mnih (2016)
151   introduced a critic that estimates the value of the current state, allowing for smoother updates by
152   reducing the variability in policy updates and speeding up convergence. However, these methods still
153   faced instability when large updates caused the new policy to diverge too far from the previous one.
154   Trust Region Policy Optimization (TRPO) Schulman (2015) tackled this by limiting the size of policy
155   updates using a KL divergence constraint, but its implementation was complex and computationally
156   expensive. Proximal policy optimization (PPO) Schulman et al. (2017a) simplified this process by
157   introducing a clipped objective function that keeps policy updates within a stable range while being
158
      easier to implement. PPO’s balance between simplicity and stability has made it a widely adopted
      method in modern RL research.
159
160   In NLP, PPO has been effectively used in reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) to
161   align LLM outputs with human preferences, as seen in works like InstructGPT Ouyang et al. (2022b)
      and Constitutional AI Bai et al. (2022b). These approaches treat the LLM as a policy, where model

164   applied to enhance LLM reasoning capabilities Ziegler et al. (2019); Havrilla et al. (2024); Hu & Shu
165   (2023). In this work, we apply PPO to fine-tune LLMs on NLU tasks.

      Proximal policy optimization (PPO) Schulman et al. (2017b) is an online reinforcement learning
170
      algorithm. In this section, we describe the process to fine-tune an LLM using PPO. During training,
171   at each timestep t, the LLM (policy) generates a token prediction at (action) based on the state st ,
172   which consists of the sequence of generated tokens up to timestep t − 1. The final generated output is
173   evaluated in the context of the downstream task, where the environment provides feedback in the
174   form of rewards. The model updates its parameters based on these rewards to improve its ability to
175   generate accurate predictions over time.
176

218
      To enhance the performance of LLMs on NLU tasks, we adopt two distinct fine-tuning methods.
219   The first approach involves supervised fine-tuning, where the input consists of a concatenation of
220   the task-specific prompt, query and the ground truth answer, with the model optimized using the
221   next-token prediction objective. The second approach utilizes PPO, framing response generation
222   as a reinforcement learning problem. In this setup, the sequence of input tokens till timestep t − 1
223   represents the state st , and each token generated at timestep t is treated as an action at . After
224   generating the entire sequence, a heuristic-based process extracts the final answer from this generated
225   sequence, and is compared to the ground truth. PPO is then employed to optimize the model
226   by maximizing the cumulative reward derived from this comparison. To reduce computational
227
      complexity, we fine-tune LoRA layers instead of the full model.

229   4.1   TASK -S PECIFIC P ROMPT D ESIGN
230
      We detail the construction of task-specific prompts used to query the LLM for NLU tasks. Each
231
      prompt begins with a clear task description, outlining the necessary background information to
232   guide the model in solving the task. Following this, we specify strict requirements for the output
233   format, ensuring that the response is encapsulated within a predefined structure, specifically between
234   ‘<Judgement></Judgement>’ tags. This structure ensures consistency in the model’s responses,
235   facilitating easier extraction and evaluation of the results.
236
      For example, in the CoLA task, which assesses grammatical acceptability, the prompt is structured as
237
      follows:
238
239   System_prompt:

          You are an assistant to analyze the linguistic properties
          of a sentence. The task is to decide the linguistic acceptability
241       of a sentence. If the sentence is linguistically correct then it
242       is acceptable, else it is not.
243   The result you give should have the following form:
244       <Judgement> {Insert only "Yes" or "No" here} </Judgement>
245
      Prompt:
          Now judge if the sentence "{sentence}" is linguistically acceptable.
246   Assistant:
247       <Judgement>

250   (such as labeling a sentence as acceptable or unacceptable), and concludes with a special start token,
251   <Judgement>, to initiate the model’s response generation.

253   4.2   S UPERVISED F INE - TUNING OF LLM ON NLU TASKS
254
255   Given an NLU training dataset, D(tr) = {(xi , yi )}Ni=1 , where xi represents the input text and yi the
256   ground truth label, we fine-tune the LLM on a sequence consisting of the task-specific prompt p
257   (described in section 4.1) concatenated with the input xi and the ground truth answer yi . The model
258   is trained using the next-token prediction objective, where it predicts the next token in the sequence
259
      by conditioning on all preceding tokens. This objective trains the model to learn to predict the correct
      answer for the NLU task conditioned on the task-specific prompt and input.

      4.3   P ROXIMAL P OLICY O PTIMIZATION FOR LLM F INE - TUNING ON NLU TASKS
263
      We utilize PPO to fine-tune the LLM on NLU tasks, following the training protocol outlined in
264   section 3. The reward function is specifically designed for each NLU task. In this work, we use a
265   simple reward function, where a reward is assigned at the end of the generation based on alignment
266   with the ground truth labels. We use regular expression matching to extract answers from the LLM’s
267   outputs by first locating the text within the ‘<Judgement></Judgement>’ tags. Depending on the task,
268   we then search for task-specific keywords (such as “yes”, “no”, “acceptable”, or “not acceptable”) to
269   identify the answer. These extracted answers are compared with the ground truth to determine the
      appropriate rewards.

      For instance, CoLA, which is a classification task, answers are categorized as acceptable, unaccept-

278   4.4      L OW-R ANK A DAPTATION
279
280   To mitigate the computational cost of full-model fine-tuning, we employ LoRA Hu et al. (2021b)
281   during both the supervised fine-tuning and PPO stages. Instead of updating the entire model, we
282
      restrict the updates to LoRA layers, which significantly reduces the number of trainable parameters
      by decomposing the weight matrices into low-rank matrices.

287   5.1      E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
288
289   We trained and evaluated our models on the GLUE Wang et al. (2019), and SuperGLUE Wang
290   et al. (2020) benchmarks. All experiments were conducted using instruction-tuned LLAMA2-7B
291
      models Touvron et al. (2023a)1 . We perform both single task and multi-task fine-tuning: 1) Single-
      task Fine-tuning: For each subtask within GLUE, and SuperGLUE, a separate task-specific LoRA
292
      module was trained independently. 2) Multi-task Fine-tuning: In the multi-task setting, datasets from
293
      different subtasks within each benchmark were combined, and a single LoRA module was trained to
294   handle all tasks simultaneously.
295
296   Hyperparameter Settings For PPO-based fine-tuning, gird search is performed to select the batch
297   size in 4, 8, 12, and 16 for each task. A batch size of 24 was used across all tasks during supervised
298   fine-tuning (SFT). The PPO epochs is set to 4, that is each sampled batch is used for updating the
299   model four times. The initial learning rate for all tasks was set to 9 × 10−6 . We utilized the Adafactor
300   optimizer for PPO training and AdamW for SFT. A cosine annealing learning rate scheduler with a
301   warmup phase was employed, where the learning rate was gradually increased during the first 10%
302   of training steps and then reduced to one-tenth of the initial value by the end of training. We use a
303   rank r = 16 for the LoRA layers. We trained both PPO and SFT models until convergence on the
304
      validation set. The best hyperparameters were selected based on performance on the validation set.
      The final reported results for the GLUE and SuperGLUE are from their corresponding evaluation
305
      server. For evaluation, multinomial sampling with a temperature of 1 was used to generate a single
306
      response per data sample. The model generated responses with lengths between 12 and 32 tokens,
307   with the generation process concluding using a special identifier “</Judgement>”.

      We fine-tuned the LLAMA2-7B model using PPO on the SuperGLUE dataset and compared its
420   performance against several baselines, including BERT-large, BERT-large++, and zero-shot and
421   few-shot prompting of LLAMA2-7B. The term “BERT++” refers to a BERT model fine-tuned using
422   the supplementary training on intermediate labeled-data tasks (STILTs) approach Phang et al. (2018),

424   tasks. For example, MNLI from the GLUE benchmark Wang et al. (2019) is used as an intermediate
425   task for CB, RTE, and BoolQ Wang et al. (2020). In contrast, our experiments with LLM did not
426   use this method. Our models were only fine-tuned on the datasets included in the SuperGLUE
427   benchmark.
428   As shown in Table 2, the PPO-tuned LLAMA2-7B achieved the highest average performance,
429   surpassing all baselines. PPO demonstrated particularly strong improvements on reasoning-intensive
430   tasks like COPA and MultiRC, where it significantly outperformed both prompting methods and
431   encoder-only models. These results highlight the effectiveness of PPO in enhancing the model’s
      capabilities, particularly for tasks requiring reasoning and contextual understanding.

470   6     C ONCLUSION
471
472   Prompting-based approaches, such as zero-shot and few-shot prompting, have gained popularity for
473   adapting LLMs to downstream tasks. However, when applied to LLAMA2-7B, these methods under-
474   perform on NLU tasks compared to smaller encoder-only models like BERT-base and BERT-large.
475   To address this limitation, we explore two fine-tuning strategies that leverage LoRA layers to reduce
476   computational overhead. First, we employ supervised fine-tuning by concatenating task-specific
477
      prompts, input queries, and ground-truth labels, optimizing the model with the next-token prediction
      objective. While this approach improves LLAMA2-7B’s performance over prompting-based methods,
478
      it still lags behind BERT-base on the GLUE benchmark. To further enhance performance, we adopt
479
      PPO, treating the LLM as a policy that generates the next token (action) based on the current input
480   sequence (state). A reward function then evaluates how closely the generated tokens match the
481   ground-truth labels, guiding updates to the policy. PPO based fine-tuning of LLAMA2-7B, tested
482   across benchmarks like GLUE, and SuperGLUE, resulted in significant performance gains, outper-
483   forming strong baselines like BERT-large. Similar trends were observed in other LLMs, including
484   Qwen2.5-7B and MPT-7B, showcasing the robustness of this approach. These findings underscore
485   the effectiveness of PPO in enhancing NLU capabilities in LLMs. Future work could extend these
      techniques to more diverse datasets and refine reward functions for handling complex NLU tasks.
