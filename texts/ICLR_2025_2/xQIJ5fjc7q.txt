011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Black-box jailbreak attacks and defenses, a critical branch of the large language
014            model (LLM) security, are characterized by their minimal requirement for user
015            expertise and high potential for automation. However, current black-box jailbreak
016
               approaches often adhere to a uniform global algorithmic framework, leading to
               suboptimal solutions due to challenges in local optimization. This limits both their
017
               effectiveness and generalizability. In this paper, we propose DAG-Jailbreak, a
018
               novel framework leveraging Directed Acyclic Graph (DAG) dependency analysis to
019            construct three comprehensive, automated, and logical frameworks for robust jail-
020            break attack, defense, and evaluation methodologies. The core idea is to combine
021            optimal sub-components through DAG and form a more effective global algorithm
022            instance. DAG-Jailbreak consists of three components: DAG-Attack, which
023            creates highly effective attackers based on two global algorithms and is capable
024            of compromising well-aligned LLMs without prior knowledge; DAG-Defense,
025            which introduces a novel global framework based on a Mixture-of-Defenders
026            mechanism, significantly enhancing the generalizability and effectiveness of jail-
027
               break defenses by reducing the attack success rate to below 3% in most cases;
               and DAG-Evaluation, which introduces the concept of Jailbreak Hallucination
028
               and a two-stage evaluation framework to assess the outputs generated by LLMs.
029
               Extensive experiments validate the effectiveness of DAG-Jailbreak.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      Jailbreak attacks (Liu et al., 2023a; Shen et al., 2023; Zou et al., 2023) have posed significant security
035
      threats to large language models (LLMs). These attacks manipulate LLMs into generating harmful
036   or unethical content through carefully crafted malicious prompts. Although LLM developers can
037   mitigate simple jailbreak prompts by fine-tuning models with data aligned to human values, attackers
038   can still achieve their goals using more sophisticated templates or algorithms (Dai et al., 2023; Bai
039   et al., 2022; Li et al., 2023; Piet et al., 2023). As API communication has become the predominant
040   method for utilizing LLMs, addressing jailbreak attacks has become a critical concern for the LLM
041   community in this query-based black-box scenario.
042   However, we have observed that most current black-box jailbreak attacks and defenses are constrained
043   by local optimization due to their reliance on global algorithms. On the one hand, attack strategies
044   (Liu et al., 2023a; Yu et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024d) often focus on optimizing specific sub-components
045   of the global algorithm, while overlooking other crucial sub-components. On the other hand, jailbreak
046   defenses (Cao et al., 2023; Robey et al., 2023; Kumar et al., 2023) tend to target specific types of
047   jailbreak prompts, like those with adversarial suffixes (Zou et al., 2023; Liao & Sun, 2024; Zhang
048   & Wei, 2024), thus limiting their effectiveness against a wider variety of attacks. This persistent
049   cat-and-mouse dynamic remains stuck in local optimization, hindering the overall improvement
050
      of LLM safety, particularly when jailbreak attacks are increasingly employed during red-teaming
      tests before LLMs’ official releases. As a result, enhancing LLMs’ defensive capabilities becomes
051
      challenging without addressing the comprehensiveness of attacks.
052
053   This concern motivates the development of DAG-Jailbreak, a novel framework that leverages
      Directed Acyclic Graph (DAG) dependency analysis to enhance the black-box jailbreak attacks and

054
      defenses. We begin by formally defining the DAG dependency analysis. The key insight is to build a
055   robust global jailbreak attack and defense algorithm by combining each optimal jailbreak optimization
056   sub-component. Specifically, we systematically explore the dependency relationships between attacks,
057   defenses, and evaluations via DAG, and develop DAG-Attack and DAG-Defense, and integrate
058   a multidimensional evaluation process, DAG-Evaluation, to facilitate the understanding of black-
059   box LLM jailbreak. The three components remark three unique contributions.
060
061       • DAG-Attack: We conduct a comprehensive review of existing black-box jailbreak attacks,
062         and categorize them into two global algorithms: mutation algorithm and adversarial generation.
063
            Accordingly, we apply DAG dependency analysis to identify and combine the most effective
            sub-components into robust global attack vectors: DAG-Attack-Mut and DAG-Attack-Gen.
064
065       • DAG-Defense: Similarly, we categorize existing jailbreak defenses into two types: those
066         targeting adversarial suffixes and those addressing malicious semantics. We introduce a novel
067         global defense algorithm, based on the Mixture-of-Defenders (MoD) mechanism, which assigns
068
            different defense methods to their specialized attack vectors, to address the generalizability
            limitations of existing defenses. Using DAG dependency analysis, we construct optimal defense
069
            experts tailored to specific attack vectors.
070
071       • DAG-Evaluation: Using DAG dependency analysis, we compare three jailbreak evaluation
072
            methods: Keywords Matching, Binary Classifier, and LLM-as-a-Judge to comprehensively assess
            LLM-generated outputs. We identify an often-overlooked issue, Jailbreak Hallucination, in
073
            jailbreak evaluations: LLMs frequently provide off-topic responses instead of directly answering
074
            the attacker’s jailbreak prompts. We argue that such responses do not indicate successful
075         jailbreak attacks or defenses, nor value alignment of the victim LLM.
076
077   Through extensive experiments, we demonstrate the effectiveness and rationality of DAG-Jailbreak.
078   Our DAG-Attack reliably break all tested models and our DAG-Defense significantly enhances
079   the jailbreak robustness of LLMs, outperforming existing jailbreak attack and defense approaches.
080   While we do not argue that DAG-Jailbreak is the ultimate jailbreak attack and defense but rather
081   that it should become the minimal test for any new development.

083   2     BACKGROUND
084
085   2.1    B LACK - BOX JAILBREAK ATTACKS
086
087   With API-based interaction becoming the primary method of accessing LLMs, black-box jailbreak
088   attacks, requiring no prior knowledge from the attacker, have gained significant attention and are
089   frequently used in red-teaming processes (Yu et al., 2023; Ge et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2024a). These
090   attacks can be categorized into two types: Static Design and Dynamic Optimization.
091   Static Design exploits the LLMs’ inadequate safety alignment with long-tail knowledge or complex
092   contexts to attack LLMs. For attacks involving long-tail knowledge, attackers translate malicious
093   behaviors into low-resource languages (Li et al., 2024a; Yong et al., 2023) or encode them (Jiang
094   et al., 2024; Lv et al., 2024), leveraging the LLM’s semantic understanding to execute the attack. For
095   those exploiting complex contexts, attackers replace the keywords in pre-designed jailbreak templates
096   with malicious behavior to achieve their goals (Shen et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023b; Yu et al., 2024b).
097   While straightforward, these approaches are not adaptable and can be easily mitigated by safety
098
      fine-tuning algorithms (Dai et al., 2023; Bai et al., 2022; Li et al., 2023; Piet et al., 2023).
099   Dynamic Optimization iteratively refines jailbreak prompts until the attack objective is met. These
100   methods typically follow two global algorithmic frameworks: mutation algorithms (Yu et al., 2023;
101   Li et al., 2024d; Liu et al., 2023a) and adversarial generation (Chao et al., 2023; Mehrotra et al.,
102   2023; Takemoto, 2024). The former modifies jailbreak prompts to incrementally approach the target,
103   while the latter simulates an LLM acting as a red-teaming assistant.
104   Additionally, some black-box jailbreak attacks exploit the similarity between LLMs to conduct
105   transfer attacks (Li et al., 2024c; Sitawarin et al., 2024; Hayase et al., 2024), manipulate decoding
106   parameters (Huang et al., 2023; Zheng et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024f), or leverage special tokens (Zhou
107   et al., 2024; Yu et al., 2024a). However, these approaches require a higher level of technical expertise
      from the attacker. Unless otherwise specified, we primarily focus on the more flexible, automated,

      and widely applicable Dynamic Optimization jailbreak attacks in this paper. It is noted that, whatever
109   Static Design and Dynamic Optimization, they leverage the malicious semantic to attack the victim
110   LLMs. As another branch of jailbreak attacks, Adversarial Suffix Attack will be discussed in Section
111   3.3 and Section 4.2

      2.2   B LACK - BOX JAILBREAK D EFENSES
115
      Existing black-box jailbreak defenses primarily focus on pre-generation, which rewrites malicious
116
      prompts from attackers before inputting them to LLMs (Ji et al., 2024; Robey et al., 2023; Cao
117   et al., 2023; Hu et al., 2024) and post-generation, which filters out the harmful content (Pisano
118   et al., 2023; Helbling et al., 2023; Zeng et al., 2024). However, these approaches encounter three
119   significant challenges: (Challenge-1: Rationality) In real-world scenarios, malicious users constitute
120   only a small proportion of total users, and rewriting benign prompts can severely impair model
121   performance. (Challenge-2: Generalizability) Most current defense strategies are tailored to specific
122   types of jailbreak prompts, such as adversarial suffixes or semantic-based attacks, which limits their
123   applicability to broader contexts. (Challenge-3: Effectiveness) Some jailbreak prompts still succeed
124   in eliciting harmful responses from LLMs, even when the defense is in place.
125
126   2.3   JAILBREAK E VALUATION
127
128   Current jailbreak evaluation methods can be categorized into three types: Keywords Matching defines
129   a list of refusal keywords, where the presence of any keyword in the LLM’s response is deemed a
130   jailbreak failure. Binary Classifier fine-tunes a simple language model, such as RoBERTa (Liu et al.,
131   2019), to serve as the evaluation criterion. LLM-as-a-Judge uses another LLM-based assistant to
132   assess the outputs generated by the victim LLMs. However, two significant issues persist within this
133
      evaluation system. Firstly, some model responses may initially indicate rejection but still contain
      unsafe information in subsequent responses. Secondly, the model output may fail to address the
134
      user’s query directly, resulting in off-topic answers. Despite being highlighted in prior studies (Cai
135
      et al., 2024), these issues have not undergone systematic examination.
136
137
138   2.4   T HREAT M ODEL
139
140   Attack Permission. In the black-box scenario, an attacker denoted as A, can only interact with the
141
      victim LLM MV by crafting a jailbreak prompt PJ . This prompt consists of an initial malicious
      behavior PI (e.g., "How to make a bomb") integrated with a specific malicious template or system
142
      prompt T . We represent the jailbreak prompt as PJ = PI ⊕ T , where ⊕ denotes either replacing a
143
      placeholder in the malicious template with PI or appending PI to the end of the system prompt. The
144   attacker then retrieves the corresponding response R through API queries. Importantly, A has no
145   prior knowledge of MV , such as the probability distribution and decoding hyperparameters.
146
147
      Defense Goal. In the defense context, to demonstrate broad applicability, we focus solely on
      modifying either the jailbreak prompt PJ to PJ′ or the model response R to R′ to ensure effective
148
      defense strategies. This approach assumes that defenders do not have access to model parameters and
149
      other relevant information. To evaluate the generalizability of the defense method, it should be able
150   to mitigate Adversarial Suffix Attack and Malicious Semantic Attack.

153   3     DAG-JAILBREAK : DAG D EPENDENCY A NALYSIS FOR LLM S ’ JAILBREAK
154
155
      In this section, we first introduce DAG dependency analysis. Then, we apply this analysis method
156   to LLM jailbreak attacks, defenses, and evaluations for DAG-Attack, DAG-Defense, and
157   DAG-Evaluation, respectively. This integration is based on two key insights: Firstly, both
158   jailbreak attacks and defenses in current mainstream approaches adhere to global algorithmic frame-
159   works, consisting of multiple sub-components. This decomposition provides a natural strategy for the
160   first step in the DAG construction. Secondly, current jailbreak attacks and defenses for LLMs are
161   currently trapped in local optimization, where much of the focus remains on optimizing individual
      sub-components, rather than achieving an optimal global algorithm.

      3.1   DAG D EPENDENCY A NALYSIS
163
164
      We first formally define the DAG dependency analysis, which decomposes a global algorithmic
165   framework into multiple sub-components. By analyzing the dependency relationships and combining
166   the optimal sub-components, the overall performance of the global algorithm can be optimized.
167
168
      A DAG G consists of a set of nodes N = {n1 , n2 , ..., n|N | } and a set of directed edges E =
169
      {e1 , e2 , ..., e|E| }. Specifically, for a directed edge from node nx to node ny , we denote it as e(nx ,ny ) .
      Each directed edge is assigned an attribute i(nx ,ny ) . The out-degree of node nx is defined as
170
      dnx = |{nx ∈ N |e(nx ,ny ) ∈ E}|. In the DAG dependency analysis, each node represents a distinct
171
      method, typically referring to a specific technique. If a dependency or evolutionary relationship exists
172
      between two nodes nx and ny , they are connected by a directed edge e(nx ,ny ) . This edge indicates
173   that the method associated with the tail node ny enhances certain sub-components of the method
174   associated with nx . For example, consider two prominent jailbreak attacks within the adversarial
175   generation global algorithm, TAP (Mehrotra et al., 2023) and PAIR (Chao et al., 2023): TAP filters
176   out off-topic jailbreak prompts in advance, based on PAIR, while simultaneously leveraging parallel
177   generation to accelerate the generation process. Consequently, the relationship between TAP and
178   PAIR can be represented by the directed edge e(PAIR,TAP) . Furthermore, the attribute i(PAIR,TAP) can be
179   interpreted as "simplifying and accelerating the generation process" to clarify this directed edge.
180   After formally defining the elements in a DAG, we proceed to describe the process of adding a new
181   jailbreak method to an existing DAG in order to achieve an optimal global algorithm instance, as
182   outlined in Algorithm 1.
183
      Step 1: Decomposition. In this step, a new jailbreak method is decomposed into multiple sub-
184
      components, each treated as a function, based on the global algorithm. For instance, in the upper
185   right part of Figure 1, the global adversarial generation algorithm can be decomposed into four
186   sub-components: seed initialization, seed selection, mutation, and judgment. To illustrate, the seed
187   initialization function takes the malicious behavior as input and outputs a series of jailbreak prompts.
188
      Step 2: Analysis. For each sub-component identified in Step 1, its dependency relationships with
189
      each node in the DAG are examined. If dependencies exist between any two nodes, they are connected
190
      by directed edges with their corresponding attributes specified.
191
192   Step 3: Ensembling. This step first selects all the nodes with an out-degree d = 0, which represents
193   the optimal sub-components. Subsequently, this step will construct an optimal global algorithm
194
      instance by ensembling these optimal sub-components.

      3.2   DAG-A T T A C K
197
198
      Global algorithm for jailbreak attacks. As mentioned earlier, black-box jailbreak attacks
199   can be categorized into two global algorithms: mutation algorithm and adversarial generation.
200   (1. Mutation Algorithm) As shown in Algorithm 2 and in the upper-left section of Figure 1, the
201   mutation algorithm can be decomposed into four main steps. First, seed initialization takes the
202   provided malicious behavior and constructs a prompt pool containing various jailbreak prompts, like
203   using human-crafted jailbreak templates. Second, seed selection employs a specific algorithm to
204   choose two parent prompts that are closer to successfully jailbreaking. Next, the mutation generates
205   a new jailbreak prompt based on the parent prompts and inputs it to the target LLM. Finally, the
206   judgment evaluates the LLM’s responses and determines whether the process should be iteratively
207
      repeated. (2. Adversarial Generation) As described in Algorithm 3 and in the upper-right section
      of Figure 1, the adversarial generation involves initialization, generation, judgment, and update. First,
208
      the initialization sets up the adversarial LLM as a red-teaming assistant and initializes the malicious
209
      behavior as a jailbreak prompt. Second, the generation utilizes the LLM’s advanced semantical
210   understanding to generate a new jailbreak prompt based on the results from the judge function and
211   input it into the target LLM. Next, the judgment evaluates whether the prompt bypasses the LLM’s
212   alignment mechanism. If it does not, the update sends its feedback and the LLM’s output to the
213   adversarial LLM, repeating the cycle until a successful jailbreak is achieved. Next, we provide
214   the construction details for two optimal attack instances, DAG-Attack-Mut and DAG-Attack-Gen,
215   using two global attack algorithms respectively. The global attack process and DAGs are depicted in
      Figure 1.

247   mutators, providing stronger semantic transformation capability to avoid the local optima. (3) Seed
248   initialization. GPTFuzzer optimizes the initial malicious behavior by refining the existing jailbreak
249   templates, thus bringing the initial seed closer to a successful jailbreak, while SMJ expands the initial
250   seed pool. Given the three phases for DAG dependency analysis. we combine GPTFuzzer, MHJ, and
251   SMJ, each having an out-degree of zero, and construct DAG-Attack-Mut.
252   Construction details for DAG-Attack-Gen. For the global adversarial generation algorithm, we
253   incorporate methods in Tastle (Xiao et al., 2024), SBJ (Takemoto, 2024), MART (Ge et al., 2023),

255   phases as well. (1) For the design of the adversarial LLM, PAIR replace the supervised fine-tuning
256   with the Chain-of-Thought (CoT) method, enhancing the interpretability of the generated results
257   while reducing fine-tuning costs. (2) For the selection of the adversarial LLM, SBJ employs the
258   same model type of the victim LLM to better understand the alignment mechanism. Furthermore,
259
      Bag-of-Tricks proposes that closed-source models often demonstrate strong abilities. (3) Concerning
      the generation process, as discussed in before, TAP early discard off-topic jailbreak prompts
260
      and accelerate the generation process. Consequently, we combine the optimization of TAP and
261
      Bag-of-Tricks to create an optimal attack instance DAG-Attack-Gen.

291   For the justification process, we first assess whether a user prompt is malicious, allowing benign

300   Construction details for DE-Adv. For defenses against Adversarial Suffix Attacks, Perturbation
301   (Robey et al., 2023) randomly insert characters into the jailbreak prompt to disrupt the fragile
302   adversarial suffix. The Spell-Check (Ji et al., 2024) method exploits the randomness of adversarial
303
      characters, filtering out spelling errors in the prompt while preserving the user’s original input,
      effectively neutralizing the attack. Another research line focuses on eliminating specific parts
304
      of the jailbreak prompt. Suffix-Erase (Kumar et al., 2023) removes a fixed portion of the suffix,
305
      compromising its adversarial structure. Expanding on this, Monte-Carlo Sampling(Cao et al., 2023)
306   randomly deletes words with a probability p to prevent adversarial strings from embedding within the
307   user’s input. For each prompt assigned to DE-Adv, both Spell-Check and Monte-Carlo Sampling are
308   applied to rewrite the prompt, and the two defended versions are submitted to the target LLM.
309
      Construction details for DE-Sem. For defenses against malicious semantic attacks, Self-Reminder
310
      (Wu et al., 2023) initially explores the potential of using system prompts as a defense mechanism.
311
      Building on this, Self-Defense (Phute et al., 2023) adds supplementary information (e.g., assessing
312   whether the prompt contains harmful content), while Summarize (Ji et al., 2024) reduces the length of
313   jailbreak prompts. Furthermore, Bergeron (Pisano et al., 2023) enhances Self-Defense by introducing
314   defense techniques during pre- and post-generation. Meanwhile, Intention Analysis (Zhang et al.,
315   2024), removes malicious semantics from the original jailbreak prompt. For each user input assigned
316   to DE-Sem, both Bergeron and Intention Analysis rewrite the jailbreak prompt before submitting the
317   defended version to the target LLM.

      row reports the number of jailbreak prompts associated with each combination. For instance, in the
339
      combination with the smallest proportion of cases, out of 96 jailbreak results, only 2 cases show that
340
      LLM-as-a-Judge performs worse than the other two methods. On the other hand, there are 9 cases (4
341   + 3 + 2) where LLM-as-a-Judge is ineffective, compared to 11 cases where only LLM-as-a-Judge
342   remains effective. These results suggest that when LLM-as-a-Judge fails, the other methods also tend
343   to be ineffective.
344
      Jailbreak Hallucination and the two-stage global evaluation algorithm. During the preliminary ex-
345
      periment, we observed that many LLM outputs lack harmful content but also fail to demonstrate safety
346
      alignment, i.e., providing an output unrelated to the jailbreak prompt. We define this phenomenon,
347   which has been largely overlooked in prior research, as Jailbreak Hallucination. Unlike traditional
348   hallucinations in LLMs, Jailbreak Hallucination may arise from the LLMs’ inadequate semantic
349   understanding or from prompts crafted by jailbreak algorithms to obscure the malicious intent and
350   circumvent model refusals. This crucial observation motivates us to propose DAG-Evaluation, a
351   two-stage evaluation method. In the first stage, we measure if the outputs contain harmful content,
352   which indicates whether the attack was successful or not. In the second stage, For unsuccessful
353   attack outputs, we evaluate whether the responses are off-topic or explicitly reject the jailbreak
354   prompt, which we classify as either Jailbreak Hallucination or successful safety alignment.
355   Construction details for DAG-Evaluation. To apply DAG dependency analysis to jailbreak
356   evaluation, we adopt two traditional optimization strategies from prompt design: the CoT and few-
357   shot strategies. CoT decomposes complex jailbreak evaluation tasks and provides the reasoning
358   for each step, while the few-shot strategy supplies the LLM with several references to guide the
359   evaluation. The specific prompts used in DAG-Evaluation are detailed in Appendix A.

399   Evaluation details and metrics. For each response generated by the jailbreak prompt, we classify
400   the outcomes into three categories using the two-stage DAG-Evaluation: In the first stage, we
401   measure the jailbreak success rate (JR), representing the proportion of responses containing harmful
402   content. In the second stage, we assess the jailbreak hallucination rate (HR) and alignment rate
403   (AR) in failed jailbreak attempts, quantifying the proportion of responses that either exhibit jailbreak
404   hallucinations or alignment. For attack and defense experiments, we consider JR and AR as the main
405   criterion for comparing the performance between DAG-Jailbreak and baselines.

412   Attack details and baselines. We first evaluate the efficiency of DAG-Attack, with results
413   presented in Table 1. For the global adversarial generation algorithm, we select two widely used
414   methods, PAIR and TAP, as baselines, utilizing GPT-3.5 as a red-teaming assistant to attack other

416   semantic understanding. We increase the maximum number of iterations in PAIR to 10 to align with
417
      the setting in TAP and DAG-Attack-Gen, while keeping other hyperparameters unchanged. In the
      context of the global mutation algorithm, we select two widely used mutation-based attack methods,
418
      AutoDAN-GA and GPTFuzzer as baselines for comparison. In AutoDAN-GA, the victim LLM is set as
419
      LLaMa-2 to generate the set of jailbreak prompts for conducting transferable attacks on other LLMs.
420   For GPTFuzzer, the seed selection method is based on UCB (Auer et al., 2002), and three malicious
421   templates are randomly selected from Liu et al. (2023b) to maintain consistency in the initialization
422   strategy between GPTFuzzer and DAG-Attack-Mut.
423
      Effectiveness of DAG-Attack. (1) DAG-Attack-Gen achieves superior attack performance on all
424
      victim LLMs, surpassing all the baselines by at least 9%. In the cases of GPT-series, DAG-Attack-Gen
425
      is at least 2.6 times more powerful than all baselines. (2) Furthermore, DAG-Attack-Gen exhibits
426   exceptional attack capabilities on GPT-series, LLaMa-series, and Claude, which are recognized for
427   their strong security alignment measures. (3) DAG-Attack-Mut showcases remarkable capabilities
428   within the global mutation algorithm, achieving a JR exceeding 90% across three LLMs. Notably,
429   for Qwen and Mistral, the JR of DAG-Attack-Mut reaches 99%. (4) Additionally, DAG-Attack-
430   Mut exhibits a slightly lower HR compared to other baselines. This underscores the effectiveness
431   of incorporating semantic features in the seed selection and mutation stages, thereby preventing
      deviations from the initial malicious behavior.

443   vations. (1) DAG-Defense demonstrates superior robustness to DAG-Attack across all LLMs.
444
      Notably, DAG-Defense enhances the resilience of the victim LLM in the black-box scenario,
      enabling it to generate content consistent with human values, that is, refusing to answer malicious
445
      prompts. (2) Although JR of DAG-Defense is occasionally slightly less effective than baseline
446
      defenses on LLMs, such as Qwen and Mistral within the mutation algorithm, it consistently limits JR
447   to single digits. (3) We also observe that DAG-Defense significantly reduces HR. We attribute this

450
      Generalizability of DAG-Defense against Static Design. Table 3 presents the results, from which
451
      we draw three observations. (1) Even with the transition to Static Design, DAG-Defense maintains
452
      robust defense performance, confining the JR of most Static Design methods to less than 1%. Similar

454   and Intention Analysis also demonstrate effectiveness in resisting Static Design attacks based on

462   Generalizability of DAG-Defense against Adversarial Suffix Attack. In Table 4, we observe

467
      higher than that of DAG-Defense. We attribute this difference to the tendency of Summarize to

471
472   Limitations and clarifications. DAG dependency analysis and the three DAG-jailbreak instances,
473   DAG-Attack, DAG-Defense, and DAG-Evaluation, exhibit certain limitations. We make
474   some clarifications to improve the understanding of readers in the following. Firstly, the robustness of
475   DAG-Defense is not adequately demonstrated. In formal proof processes, it is essential to evaluate
476   the defensive capabilities of DAG-Defense against adaptive attacks. Unfortunately, the emerging
477
      field of LLM jailbreak lacks established adaptive attacks tailored to specific defenses, complicating
      the assessment of DAG-Defense’s performance under targeted attacks. Nonetheless, existing
478
      experiments indicate that DAG-Defense shows strong defensive performance against mainstream
479
      attacks. Secondly, rigorous dependency analysis requires controlling for all variables except the
480   primary variable to accurately validate dependency relationships. However, it is impractical for all
481   jailbreak attacks and defenses to employ the same sub-components apart from the main optimization,
482   as unavoidable non-primary variables (such as hyperparameters or LLMs’ prompts) can vary. While
483   we believe these variables do not significantly affect established dependencies, conflicts may arise
484   from optimizations of certain sub-components. The inconsistent performance of DAG-Attack-Mut
485   relative to baselines across all LLMs supports this assertion. Thirdly, while we manually analyze the
      dependency relationships among various methods, we believe this process can be automated. Each

521   6      C ONCLUSION
522
523   In this paper, we have systematically studied existing methods in the field of black-box jail-
524   breaks for LLMs and summarized the current challenges regarding attacks and defenses. By for-
525   mally defining and applying DAG dependency analysis, we constructed three powerful instances:
526   DAG-Attack, DAG-Defense, and DAG-Evaluation. This combination presents three key ad-
527   vantages: Rationality: By incorporating edge attributes, we significantly enhance the interpretability
528   of instances constructed by DAG dependency analysis. Additionally, DAG dependency analysis
529   naturally exploits this field’s tendency to adhere to a global algorithm while often trapped in local
530   optima. This application effectively addresses the current challenges outlined in Section 1 and 2.
531
      Scalability: We have constructed two scalable DAGs in Figure 1 and Figure 2. Upon introducing new
      methods, these new attack and defense methods can be swiftly decomposed into sub-components,
532
      facilitating the analysis of their dependencies relative to existing nodes in the DAG, thereby enabling
533
      continuous updates and the development of more robust attackers and defenders. Novelty: This paper
534   is the first to analyze the security of black-box LLMs from the perspective of dependency analysis,
535   providing a fresh viewpoint that fosters trustworthy development within the LLM security community.
536   Through extensive experiments, we have demonstrated the effectiveness of the proposed attacks and
537   defenses. Additionally, this paper presents the design of the MoD-based defense framework and
538   discusses Jailbreak Hallucination. We do not argue that DAG-Jailbreak is the ultimate jailbreak
539   attack and defense. We hope the introduction of DAG-Jailbreak will continue to advance LLM
      jailbreaks and it should become the minimal test for any new development.
