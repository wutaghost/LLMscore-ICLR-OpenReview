010                                               A BSTRACT
011
012            Autoregressive language models are increasingly capable of processing non-text
013            data, such as images or audio. Are language models also a natural choice for
014            numerical data, such as the 3D structure of molecules? In this work, we use
015
               quantum chemistry simulations as a case study in the challenges of applying
               language models to numerical data, building up a set of simple subproblems
016
               that can shed light on key design decisions. We show that language models lag
017
               behind domain-specific models on prediction tasks and provide evidence for and
018            against different hypotheses that explain their failure. Many commonly identified
019            pitfalls such as difficulty performing arithmetic operations and choice of discrete
020            vocabulary fall short of explaining the behavior. In contrast, we show that capturing
021            invariance properties exhibits a strong correlation with predictive performance.
022            Finally, we provide a comparison of language models trained from scratch on
023            numerical data with models pretrained on text. We show that text pretraining often
024            provides a surprisingly limited advantage on prediction tasks, and can even hurt
025            performance, despite prior work showing that text-pretraining can offer advantages.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   A popular goal in machine learning is building a generative model that operates on many data
031   modalities simultaneously. Multiple modalities are useful because each modality can unlock a new
032   source of information or control. For example, a web developer can provide a chat bot writing
033
      code with a sketch of a website, or a biologist can provide a desired function or structure to a
      model that generates protein sequences (Hayes et al., 2024). One modality that is particularly
034
      exciting for scientific discovery is 3D structures, which are intrinsically geometric objects, but
035
      which often co-occur with text descriptions or categorical features. Improving models of these
036   structures facilitates easier drug discovery and materials design. A sequence model that successfully
037   incorporates numerical data might be able to leverage the few-shot abilities of large language models
038   when making predictions about numerical structures or act as alternatives to traditional simulations.
039
      When we consider current state-of-the-art models for predicting the properties of 3D structures,
040
      however, language modeling approaches appear to lag far behind models that leverage domain-
041
      specific knowledge. In other domains, such as images (Wang et al., 2024), time-series (Ansari
042   et al., 2024), and generative modeling of molecular structures (Flam-Shepherd and Aspuru-Guzik,
043   2023), autoregressive language models can compete with other state-of-the-art methods, but the same
044   pattern does not seem to hold true for many predictive tasks on structured numerical data. To find
045   some explanation, we need only look to similar trends observed on simple numerical tasks. Namely,
046   despite many fundamental advances in modeling, it is still challenging to learn algorithms for simple
047   numerical operations that generalize to new input sizes (Zhou et al., 2023). Several authors have
048   speculated on the fundamental challenges language models may face in learning algorithms over
049   numerical inputs, some of which might act as useful frameworks for considering language models on
050
      3D structures and similar geometric objects.
051   In this paper, our goal is to explore the core challenges in applying language models to geometric
052   objects by appealing to frameworks from prior work and running extensive experiments to assess
053   their practical significance. To do so, we train thousands of language models to solve operations from
      linear algebra and simple physical modeling tasks. We show that some facets of language modeling,

      such as causal masking, are not fundamental bottlenecks to current progress on numerical tasks,
055   while properties that affect a model’s invariance to a problem’s symmetries seems to have a strong
056   correlation with predictive performance. We also explore how tokenization and pretraining can affect
057   the predictions and invariances of models, and show that finetuning state-of-the-art language models
058   often leads to worse results than training new language models from scratch.

063   2   R ELATED W ORK
064
065   Learning simple arithmetic operations with language models is longstanding problem. Although
066   addition and multiplication of integers or matrices are in the complexity class of algorithms that can
067   be learned exactly by a transformer-based language model (Merrill and Sabharwal, 2023), they can
068   still be difficult to learn in practice. Zhou et al. (2023) speculate that some numerical operations are
069
      challenging to learn because they lack a simple program that can be expressed by a causal transformer.
      For example, addition of two multi-digit integers can be challenging when digits are ordered from
070
      most significant to least significant, because causal attention has trouble building representation
071
      for a carry operation. McLeish et al. (2024) draw on similar observations to design an improved
072   language model with expanded generalization abilities on addition and multiplication, by reversing
073   number digits and providing special information about each digit’s location. While these studies
074   provide useful frameworks for reasoning about the challenges of language models, they do not study
075   high-dimensional objects, which have much more practical relevance. We show that in many cases
076   the same intuitions do not naturally extend to our settings and different challenges dominate.
077
      Going beyond integer inputs, Charton (2021) shows that language models can learn basic operations
078   from linear algebra like matrix addition, matrix multiplication, and eigenvalue computations. While
079   we use some of Charton (2021)’s tasks in our study and draw inspiration from their numerical
080   string encodings, our analysis differs in fundamental ways because of our underlying motivation
081   to approximate calculations from quantum chemistry. To this end, we focus much more on the
082   invariance properties of learned models, and introduce simple building blocks of physical models
083   that are not studied by Charton (2021). In this way, our work is more closely related to the work of
084   Flam-Shepherd and Aspuru-Guzik (2023), which shows that language models with standard training
085   and simple tokenization methods can be used as strong generative models of 3D structures. However,
086
      like Alampara et al. (2024), we also find that predictive tasks display different dynamics compared
      to generative modeling and that language models are not competitive with best in class predictive
087
      methods.
088
089   Because we apply language models to numerical data and closely study their interactions with choices
090   in tokenization, we also drawn on the work of Golkar et al. (2023), which introduces a continuous
091   alternative to discrete tokenization in language models (xVal) on mixed categorical and numerical
092
      data. xVal models all numbers with a single token and uses a single weight and bias vector for
      inputs and outputs, instead of an embedding matrix for many numerical tokens. xVal is thus akin to
093
      transformers applied directly on continuous inputs or to graph neural network methods applied to
094
      numerical prediction problems. We find that xVal does lead to improvements in many settings and
095   therefore provides valuable perspective on what facets of language modeling are most challenging
096   when learning on geometric data.
097
098
      As we are also interested in how text pretraining can act as a useful inductive bias, our work also
      intersects with work that applies text-pretrained models to zero-shot prediction on other modalities
099
      (Hegselmann et al., 2023; Gruver et al., 2024b). Although text pretraining holds the potential to help
100
      models learn general-purpose circuits over discrete sequences, we find that it is ultimately unhelpful
101   in our tasks.

      Building blocks Our goal in this study is to develop a better mechanistic understanding of the
163   challenges of applying language models to numerical data, and therefore we also attempt to break
164   these goal problems down into their constituent parts and see where language models run into trouble.
165   Both of the datasets we use are derived from density functional theory (DFT), quantum mechanical
166   calculations to approximate the electron density for relatively small sets of atoms. We include a brief
167   primer on quantum chemistry in Appendix A. At their core, DFT calculations rely on a relatively
168   simple set of key operations. These include
169
170
           • Looking up scalar constants (e.g. charge of an electron) or the value of a basis function (e.g.
             spherical harmonic) at a point.
171
172        • Matrix addition and multiplication (e.g. to compute pairwise distances between coordinates).
173        • Iterative procedures in linear algebra and solving differential equations (e.g. calculating
174          eigenvalues or fixed point iteration on the Schrodinger equation).
175
176   This characterization is rough, but it gives a sense of what types of functions language models must
177   be able to learn if they were implementing pre-existing theories of physics in order match the outputs
178   of simulations. Of course, one of the strengths of neural networks is their ability to approximate
179
      expensive procedures with a fixed computation budget (one forward pass during training), so we
      do not necessarily expect language models to recapitulate existing tools from mathematics and
180
      physics. We can, however, use existing methods for approximating physical observations as a way
181
      of debugging current limitations of language models, if not to perfectly understand their internal
182   mechanisms. If language models struggle to learn matrix multiplication but not matrix addition, for
183   example, we can speculate that the scalar multiplication of many operands might be a roadblock, and
184   we can work on this limitation directly.
185
      Theoretical limititions In the a single forward pass of a neural network, there are fundamental
186
      limits on both (1) the number of serial operations and (2) the amount of memory for intermediate
187
      results. When the number of steps that must be performed serially exceeds the depth of the network,
188   the network will not be able to learn the exact function. Therefore, one forward pass of a network
189   with depth 24 will not be able to learn an iterative method that might require 30 steps for convergence.
190   Notably, this limitation is not relevant for functions that can be parallelized, as with matrix multipli-
191   cation, which is in TC0 and therefore should be possible to learn with a single forward pass (Merrill
192   and Sabharwal, 2023). Like serial computation, memory can also be a bottleneck, as with computing
193   the matrix product AT A where A is n × m with n >> m. The number of computations will be
194   approximately O(n2 ) but the input size is approximately O(n). When n is greater than the depth
195   of the network, there can be challenges in storing all intermediate computations in the network’s
196
      activations.
197
198
      Practical challenges In practice, reasoning about what solutions tend to be learned by a particular
      architecture is often more important than fundamental constraints. Even when a function can be
199
      represented in the function class, the statistical nature of the problem and questions of approaching
200
      the solution via approximations can play a larger role. Aside from not being able to represent the
201   exact algorithm, why would autoregressive language models be limited in solving these numerical
202   problems? We formulate 5 hypotheses and in the following sections provide evidence for and against
203   each of these hypotheses.
204
205     1. Mixing conditional and unconditional modeling (section 6): Here we study predictive tasks
206        p(y|x), but language models parameterize a joint likelihood p(x, y). Modeling the joint makes
207        language models flexible but also requires more capacity for p(x) beyond just p(y|x). When
208
           p(x) is intrinsically challenging, learning it can detract from learning a simple p(y|x).
209     2. Causal masking (section 7): Features in autoregressive models are unidirectional, which makes
210        learning some numerical operations challenging. For example, when digits are passed from left
211        to right into a language model, it is challenging to express addition of two numbers using a carry
212        bit. Similarly, any scan-style operation will be order-dependent and more challenging to learn
213
           if subsequences need to be reversed, and if a function cannot be implemented with an ordered
           scan-style operation, it might be very challenging to learn at all.
214
215     3. Lack of symmetries (section 8): Structured numerical data often obey constraints that are easy
           to express analytically (e.g. invariance to rotations). Incorporating these constraints can make


                                                         4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
             learning more sample-efficient or improve generalization (Frey et al., 2023), but language models
217          are typically unconstrained.
218
          4. Poor tokenization (section 9): Tokenization can lead to strange artifacts in text-pretrained
219
             language models (Brown et al., 2020; Wallace et al., 2019) and hinder their application to
220
             numerical inputs (Gruver et al., 2024a). While language models trained on numerical data often
221          explore multiple tokenization schemes (Charton, 2021; Golkar et al., 2023; Flam-Shepherd and
222          Aspuru-Guzik, 2023), they are often presented as ablations rather than analyzed in their own
223          right.
224       5. Too little data or pretraining (section 10): Data for some numerical tasks can be relatively
225          limited or extremely noisy, making language models less likely to succeed compared to models
226          with more domain-specific assumptions. Other works show that text pretraining can serve as a
227          surrogate for domain-specific pretraining or inductive biases (Gruver et al., 2024b).

      5     E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
230
231
      To test our hypotheses, we train thousands of language models that vary in model architecture, model
232
      size, tokenization method, loss function and pretraining method.
233
234   String-encoding and tokenization To turn numbers into tokens, we convert all numbers to a
235   fixed precision and then convert these numbers to variable length strings by ignoring any leading
236
      zeros. These strings are then tokenized using a vocabulary of all numbers up to certain chunk length,
      for example {“1”, “2”, ..., “998”, “999”} for a chunk length of 3. We greedily select the largest
237
      subsequence from right to left. For negative numbers, each negative number is prepended with “-".
238
      These strategies are similar to P10, P1000, and FP15 in Charton (2021), but, in our case, we choose to
239   drop the exponent term used by Charton in favor of variable length because our inputs do not contain
240   many different orders of magnitude. In addition to standard tokenization with an embedding matrix,
241   we also explore Abacus embeddings (McLeish et al., 2024) and xVal (Golkar et al., 2023), which are
242   tokenization methods specifically designed for processing numbers.
243
      Models We present results for both language models trained from scratch and frontier language
244
      models pre-trained on text. Pretrained models can reuse general computational circuits and features
245   developed on the pretraining text data, but may not be as well suited for numerical data in the given
246   format. When training models from scratch, we use the LLaMA-2 (Touvron et al., 2023b) architecture
247   with between 4 and 8 layers and hidden size 512, which translates to between 20 million and 50
248   million parameters. We train models with learning rate 0.0001 or 0.0005 and a cosine schedule. When
249   studying pretrained models, we use LLaMA3.1-8B (Dubey et al., 2024), and the default LLaMA-3
250   tokenization, which, on numerical inputs, is identical to our 3-digit chunking method. We fine-tune
251   the LLaMA3.1 models using LoRA with rank 8 and alpha 32 for one epoch. To make predictions
252   with the models, we draw 10 samples at temperature 1 and calculate the median at each dimension of
253
      the output.
254   Datasets Our datasets are chosen to represent building blocks of common functions on numerical
255   data. They have varying degrees of difficulty, with some being computable exactly by transformers
256   while others can only be approximated. We explore two categories of tasks:
257

258
             evaluate (a) matrix addition (b) matrix multiplication, and (c) calculating real eigenvalues. We
259
             train on matrices of mixed sizes, with a distribution of n weighted n, so that we train on more
260          large matrices. The input matrices have coefficients sampled uniformly from [−10,
261

             resulting eigenvalues having a center distribution with standard deviation σ = 10 n/3.
262
             These tasks have significant variation in difficulty. While matrix sum and product are computable
263
             in theory by a language model, computing an eigenspectrum is not and is more intrinsically
264          serial than sum and product, making it more challenging for transformers. In addition to
265          testing on matrices drawn from the same distribution as the train data, we also include a special
266          generalization setting (marked with ‘+’) in which we train on n ∈ [2, 10] \ {8} and evaluate

      10        I NSUFFICIENT P RE - TRAINING
509
510
      As we’ve seen so far, language models typically require large model sizes in order to capture
      invariances and make good predictions. For sufficiently large datasets, this allows language models
511
      to perform on-par with hand-crafted methods, but in other cases these extra parameters lead to poor
512
      generalization or slower convergence for fixed compute. The typical solution for this problem is
513   extensive unsupervised pretraining, which can unlock the benefits of language modeling, while
514   matching performance on narrow tasks. Prior work shows that text pretraining can serve this role in
515   some cases. For example, Delétang et al. (2023) and Goldblum et al. (2023) show that text-pretrained
516   models are general-purpose compression engines that can match domain-specific compression on
517   non-text modalities.
518
      To explore pre-trained models, we compare our small from-scratch models with LLaMA3.1-8B, a
519   model two orders of magnitude larger. We fine-tune the LLaMA3.1 models for one epoch, which
520   is 1-2 orders of magnitude fewer gradient steps than we take with the smaller models. As with
521   models trained from scratch, we make predictions by drawing 10 samples and taking the median
522   per dimension. Figure 6 (right) shows the resulting errors, for which pretrained models have worse
523   performance in all but matrix multiplication. We posit two possible explanations for this discrepancy:
524   (a) matrix product requires the most capacity to learn effectively (as was already evidenced in
525   Figure 3) (b) matrix product has very high-dimensional outputs consisting of matrices containing
526   large numbers, and text-pretraining is primarily helpful in modeling patterns in long sequences. If
527
      this were true, however, we might also expect some benefit on matrix addition and distance matrices.
528
529   11        D ISCUSSION
530
531   In this work, we explored several explanations of the subpar performance of language models on
532   3D property prediction tasks. Through interventions like modifications of the architecture and loss
533   function, we see that some of the explanations are not supported by the data, while others, such as the
534   importance of invariances, hold up to scrutiny. We also showed that text pretraining is surprisingly
535   unhelpful for learning good subroutines on our numerical tasks, despite its promise in other settings.
536   Our experiments suggest that language models converge to increasingly accurate and nearly invariant
537   solutions when given sufficient model capacity and yet still have a large gap when compared to a
538   method like xVal. One possibility for future work is to close this gap by extensively pretraining on
539   synthetic data, which could be created by running cheap traditional methods (e.g. Hartree-Fock) or
      by distilling from a successful neural network potential evaluated on perturbed training data.
