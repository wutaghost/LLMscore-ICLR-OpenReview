009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               Flow Matching provides a method to train Ordinary Differential Equation (ODE)-
012            based generative models and facilitates various probability path designs between
013            initial and target distributions. Among these designs, straight flows are particu-
014            larly interesting for reducing sampling steps. While some works have success-
015            fully straightened flows and achieved image generation in a few steps, they of-
016            ten suffer from cumulative errors or provide only piecewise or minibatch-level
017            straightness. We propose Adversarial Self Flow Matching (ASFM), which can
018            straighten flows and align the generated data distribution with the real data distri-
019            bution. ASFM consists of two complementary components. Online Self Training
020
               straightens flows by constructing a conditional vector field using paired data, en-
               abling one-step image generation during training. Adversarial Training aligns the
021
               one-step generated data with real data, thereby reducing cumulative errors when
022
               straightening flows. Experiments demonstrate that ASFM can build straight flows
023            across the entire time span between two complete distributions and achieve highly
024            competitive results across multiple datasets among Flow Matching-based meth-
025            ods. For instance, ASFM achieves 8.15 and 14.9 FID scores with NFE=6 on
026            CelebA-HQ (256) and AFHQ-Cat (256), respectively.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031
032   Flow Matching (FM) provides a straightforward method for obtaining ODE-based generative mod-
033
      els by directly regressing on the conditional vector field. Unlike Diffusion Models (DMs), which
      constrain the probability path between distributions to follow a noise-adding and denoising process,
034
      FM offers a much broader design space for probability path. This simplicity and flexibility have
035
      attracted widespread attention across different fields, like image generation (Esser et al., 2024; Sun
036   et al., 2024), structural biology (Huguet et al., 2024; Nori & Jin, 2024), audio generation (Wang
037   et al., 2024) and optimal transport (Tong et al., 2023; Pooladian et al., 2023).
038
039
      ODE-based generative models suffer from long sampling time. Although using more precise solvers
      can generate high-quality data in fewer steps, the core issue lies in the high curvature of the flows
040
      generated by the model (Lee et al., 2023). Given that FM offers a large design space for probability
041
      paths, finding models within this space that can generate straight flows becomes the key to accel-
042   erating sampling in ODE-based generative models. Some recent works have already attempted to
043   straighten the flows. Liu et al. (2022) suggest using the Reflow method to reduce the intersections
044   of straight line paths, thereby straightening the sampling trajectories. However, since generated data
045   is used as the training set during the Reflow training process, an accumulation of errors becomes in-
046   evitable, causing the final generated data distribution to deviate from the real data distribution. The
047   methods proposed in Yan et al. (2024) and Yang et al. (2024) for straightening flows require seg-
048   menting the flow in the time dimension, resulting in piecewise straight flows. However, they do not
049   guarantee that the flow is straight over the entire time span. Both Tong et al. (2023) and Pooladian
050
      et al. (2023) propose non-trivial pairing of noise and data samples within a minibatch during training
      to straighten the flow. Nevertheless, these methods fail to achieve optimal pairing for data outside
051
      the minibatch, making it impossible to construct straight flows between two complete distributions.
052
053   To enable the model to generate straight flows between two complete distributions over the entire
      time span, while ensuring that the generated data does not deviate from the real data distribution,

054
      we propose Adversarial Self Flow Matching (ASFM), and summarize it in Figure 11 . ASFM is
055   primarily divided into two components: Adversarial Training and Online Self Training. Adversarial
056   Training leverages the straight flow characteristic of ASFM to obtain the generated data in a single
057   step during training. This result, along with real data, is fed into the discriminator, ensuring that the
058   distribution of generated data closely aligns with the real data distribution. On the other hand, Online
059   Self Training generates training data using the model itself, progressively straightening the flow by
060   learning the vector field with repaired data, ensuring that the flow remains sufficiently straight over
061   the entire time span and between two complete distributions. These two components complement
062   each other: Adversarial Training ensures that the training data generated by Online Self Training
063
      aligns better with the real data distribution, while Online Self Training straightens the flow and
      thereby improves the quality of one-step generation in Adversarial Training.
064
065   Our contributions can be summarized as follows:
066
067             1. Our method demonstrates that within the broad probability path design space provided by
068
                   Flow Matching, finding straight flows between two complete high-dimensional distribu-
                   tions across the entire time span is feasible. ASFM finds such flows using complementary
069
                   Adversarial Training and Online Self Training, and fundamentally reduces the number of
070
                   steps required for sampling in ODE-based generative models.
071
072
                2. We introduce Adversarial Training into the Flow Matching training process, using a dis-
                   criminator to align the distribution of generated data with that of real data, which reduces
073
                   the cumulative errors in the Online Self Training process. ASFM provides new possibilities
074
                   for integrating adversarial training with ODE-based generative models.
075
076             3. We conducted extensive experiments to validate the effectiveness of our method. On
077
                   CIFAR-10, ASFM can achieve FID scores of 5.07 and 4.56 with NFE=1 and NFE=2, re-
                   spectively. On high-resolution datasets like AFHQ-Cat and CelebA-HQ, ASFM achieves
078
                   FID scores of 14.9 and 8.15 with NFE=6, respectively.

104   Flow Matching Training ODE-based generative models through regression is not feasible, as the
105   ground-truth vector field ut (x) cannot be directly obtained. Flow Matching (FM) (Lipman et al.,
106   2022) proposes regressing on the conditional vector field ut (x|x1 ) to solve this problem. Similarly,
107

108
      Liu et al. (2022) proposes learning the vector field to follow the straight paths between samples
109   from two distributions, while Albergo & Vanden-Eijnden (2022) suggests randomly interpolating
110   between sampling points of two distributions to learn the vector field. These works share similar
111   principles and offer new, simplified methods for training ODE-based generative models. Unlike
112   Diffusion Models (DMs), which constrain probability paths to follow a noise-adding and denoising
113   process, these approaches allow for greater flexibility in designing probability paths. In this case,
114   designing ODE-based generative models that generate straight flows becomes an attractive task,
115   as straight flows can fundamentally accelerate the sampling process. Yang et al. (2024) enforces
116   self-consistency property in the vector field, enabling the flow to exhibit piecewise straight charac-
117
      teristics. Yan et al. (2024) divides the flow into multiple time segments and applies Reflow (Liu
      et al., 2022) within each segment, transforming the flow into a piecewise straight one. Pooladian
118
      et al. (2023) and Tong et al. (2023) straighten the flow within a minibatch by pairing the sampling
119
      points from the two distributions within the minibatch. Additionally, Lee et al. (2023) reduces the
120   trajectory curvature of ODE-based generative models by learning the forward process.
121
122   Adversarial Training Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) (Goodfellow et al., 2020; Sauer
123
      et al., 2022b; 2023a) use adversarial training to improve the quality of generated images: the gen-
      erator tries to create images that can deceive the discriminator, while the discriminator attempts to
124
      determine whether the images come from the real image dataset. Some recent works have attempted
125
      to apply adversarial training to Stochastic Differential Equation (SDE)/ODE-based generative mod-
126   els, but these efforts have primarily focused on DMs and distillation-related tasks. The application
127   of adversarial training in FM is still relatively unexplored. Kong et al. (2024) uses Adversarial Con-
128   sistency Training to reduce the Jensen-Shannon divergence between distributions, improving both
129   generation quality and convergence of DMs. Luo et al. (2024); Xiao et al. (2021); Gong et al. (2024);
130   Xu et al. (2024) propose several diffusion-GAN hybrid models that aim to leverage the strengths of
131   both approaches while mitigating their weaknesses, resulting in models with faster sampling speeds
132   and better mode coverage. Sauer et al. (2023b) applies adversarial training in model distillation,
133   where the discriminator distinguishes between the student model’s output and real images to im-
134
      prove generation quality. Lee et al. (2024) applies adversarial training to flow matching in the field
      of waveform generation. It is important to note that, not only is this work in a different domain from
135
      ours, but the algorithms are also significantly different. Our method leverages the mutual enhance-
136
      ment between Online Self Training and Adversarial Training, continuously straightening the flow
137   while ensuring that the distribution of generated data aligns with that of real data. In contrast, their
138   method does not incorporate any online self training components.

183   3.2     C ONSTRUCTING S TRAIGHT F LOWS POINTING TOWARD REAL DATA DISTRIBUTION
184
185
      Given a set of initial random samples, our task is to construct straight flows that progressively trans-
      form these samples to align with the distribution of real data. Inspired by recent methods (Lipman
186
      et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2022; Sauer et al., 2023b), we continuously straighten flows through Online
187
      Self Training and use Adversarial Training to guide the generated data to align with the real data
188   distribution, creating a novel ODE-based generative model based on the Flow Matching method.
189   Our innovation lies in establishing a training framework where the model continuously generates
190   data for training itself, while incorporating Adversarial Training into FM. The overview of the pro-
191   posed method is illustrated in Figure 1. In Section 3.2.1, we introduce Online Self Training, which is
192   implemented using two components: Flow Matching with paired data and Training Memory. Then
193   Section 3.2.2 discusses how to perform one-step generation during training and apply the generated
194   results in adversarial training. Section 3.2.3 summarizes the algorithm and presents the complete
195   training procedure.

327   Experimental Settings We validated the effectiveness of our method on the task of unconditional
328   image generation using three datasets. Specifically, we used the CIFAR-10 (Krizhevsky et al., 2009)
329   dataset with 32 × 32 images in Section 4.1, and two high-resolution datasets with 256 × 256 images,
330
      i.e., CelebA-HQ (Karras, 2017) and AFHQ-Cat (Choi et al., 2020) in Section 4.2. For the evaluation
      metrics, we selected the Frechet Inception Distance (FID) score (Heusel et al., 2017) and Inception
331
      Score (IS) (Salimans et al., 2016), which are widely used in the field of image generation. The
332
      vector field network f (x, t; θ) in our model is initialized using the checkpoint of the pretrained 1-
333   rectified flow, and its architecture is the U-Net architecture of DDPM++ (Song et al., 2020b). The
334   discriminator part D uses the discriminator model from StyleGAN-T (Sauer et al., 2023a).
335
336
      Baselines We compared ASFM with various methods on the CIFAR-10 dataset, including sev-
      eral typical GANs, Consistency Models, representative Diffusion Models (including some advanced
337
      solvers and distillation methods), and recent works related to FM. On high-resolution datasets, we
338
      mainly compared ASFM with Rectified Flow (Liu et al., 2022), Consistency Flow Matching (Yang
339   et al., 2024), BOSS (Nguyen et al., 2023), and MTC (Lee et al., 2023). It is worth mentioning
340   that although ASFM uses adversarial training, its theoretical foundation remains the same as ODE-
341   based generative models, thus giving it zero-shot capabilities in downstream tasks that GANs cannot
342   achieve. For detailed zero-shot experiments, please refer to Appendix C.

      5   C ONCLUSIONS
493
      We propose Adversarial Self Flow Matching (ASFM), a Flow Matching (FM)-based method that
494
      generates high-quality images in a few steps by leveraging straightened flows. Our method em-
495   ploys Online Self Training to straighten flows, where intersections between the linear interpolations
496   of training pairs are eliminated in real time. Furthermore, our approach incorporates Adversarial
497   Training to reduce the deviation between the generated data distribution and the real data distri-
498   bution caused by ODE solvers. Experiments demonstrate that ASFM can generate straight flows
499   and produce high-quality images in a few steps across three datasets: CIFAR-10 (32), AFHQ-Cat
500   (256), and CelebA-HQ (256). The effectiveness of each component in our method is validated
501   through ablation studies. Additionally, ASFM retains the beneficial capabilities of ODE-based gen-
502   erative models, such as zero-shot image editing. Our method offers a novel approach to attaining
503
      ODE-based generative models that produce straight flows based on FM, highlighting its potential
      applications in image generation tasks.
504
505   However, for more complex tasks like text-to-image generation, extending ASFM to large-scale
506   datasets and enhancing the model’s capability for controllable generation are necessary. In the fu-
507   ture, we will focus on addressing these issues to further improve ASFM’s performance.

734   C     Z EO - SHOT I MAGE E DITING
735
736   Despite incorporating Adversarial Training, ASFM fundamentally remains an ODE-based genera-
737
      tive model, which enables it to perform certain zero-shot image editing tasks. Unlike GANs, which
      cannot find latent space representations for sampling points in the target distribution, ASFM, due to
738
      the reversible nature of ODEs, can identify corresponding sampling points in the initial distribution
739
      for each sampling point in the target distribution, thus serving as latent space representations. By
740   manipulating these latent space representations, ASFM possesses the capability for zero-shot image
741   editing. We will introduce the application of ASFM in image mixture in Section C.1.
742
743   C.1   I MAGE M IXTURE
744
745   We selected an image of a yellow cat and an image of a white cat for mixing. Each image is split
746   in half horizontally, with the upper half of the former combined with the lower half of the latter to
747   create a mixture image. By using the mixture image as the initial value condition to solve the ODE
748   in reverse (with NFE=300 to get the exact solution), we can obtain its latent space representation.
749   Following Liu et al. (2022), we have two ways to manipulate the latent space representation: one
750
      is to multiply it directly by a coefficient α, represented by x′0 = αx0 ; the other is to√compute
751   a weighted sum with a predetermined Gaussian noise ϵ, represented by x′0 = αx0 + 1 − α2 ϵ.
752
      By using the processed latent space representation as the initial value condition to solve the ODE
      forward, the mixed image can be obtained. The results are illustrated in Figures 7 and 8.
753
754   When using the first operation method, a large α allows for a smooth blending of the two images.
755   In the second method, since a predetermined Gaussian noise ϵ is incorporated, a smaller α results in
      the blended image being closer to the image corresponding to that noise.
