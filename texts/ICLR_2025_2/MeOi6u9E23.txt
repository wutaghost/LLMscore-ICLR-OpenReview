                                              Abstract
009
010           This paper proposes a novel combined model named DiT-LSTM-SVAR,
011
              which successfully integrates time series and the Efficient Markets Hypothesis.
              This is the first to combine the microstructure of financial markets with
012
              deep learning networks to improve the performance of portfolios. We employ
013
              the DiT model to predict the upside and downside movements and an
014           information decomposition model based on the SVAR model to identify
015           random walk stocks. The DiT module significantly improves the Matthews
016           correlation coefficient by almost 3%. The annual return of the portfolio is
017           improved by almost 20%. The SVAR module greatly improves the Matthews
018           correlation coefficient by almost 4%. Portfolios constructed using the DiT-
019           LSTM-SVAR module based on market and public information outperformed
020           those created with the DiT-LSTM model. The annual cumulative return of
021           the portfolio is 266.60% and a Sharpe ratio of 1.8.

024   1   Introduction
025
026   Financial markets serve as an example of time series forecasting paradigms. Despite some
027   progress in the field of time series algorithms, these studies often overlook the unique
028   characteristics of financial markets when constructing investment portfolios (Liu et al., 2022;
029   Mandi and Guns, 2020; Lin et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2020). The Efficient Market Hypothesis
030   posits that prices reflect all available information in a strong efficient market, making it
031   extremely challenging to predict future price movements (Fama, 1970). This raises a critical
032   question: Can we honestly and accurately forecast financial market trends, or are we merely
033
      attempting to identify latent patterns that may not exist or cannot be fully captured? If
      such patterns do not actually exist or cannot be thoroughly modeled, forcing such modeling
034
      efforts can lead to inaccurate predictions, overfitting, and poor performance of the models on
035
      actual market data. This, in turn, can harm investors by providing misleading information
036   and suboptimal investment decisions. How do we combine the microstructure of financial
037   markets with deep learning networks to improve the performance of portfolios?
038
039
      This paper presents a novel algorithm, named DiT-LSTM-SVAR, for constructing investment
      portfolios. We demonstrate how the approach enables portfolio construction while ensuring
040
      rigorous empirical guarantees. The predictive model integrates three key components: (a)
041
      Diffusion Transformer (DiT) for data augmentation, (b) Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM)
042   for forecasting stock returns, and (c) Structural Vector Autoregression (SVAR) for assessing
043   the pricing efficiency of stocks, indicating whether they are random walk. Utilizing data from
044   CSI300 in China, we empirically demonstrate how DiT performs well compared to the simple
045   LSTM model. Furthermore, we construct the portfolio based on the DiT-LSTM-SVAR model
046   demonstrate the necessity of employing the SVAR model in filtering random walk stocks.
047
      Our paper contributes to existing literature in several ways. First, to the knowledge, this
048   paper is the first to integrate the microstructure of financial markets with deep learning,
049   thereby building a bridge between them (Mandi and Guns, 2020; Zhang et al., 2020). We
050   use an information decomposition model based on the SVAR model to identify random walk
051   stocks. We categorize price shocks into market information, public information, private
052   information, and noise. DiT-LSTM-VAR model effectively extends existing time series
053   techniques and portfolio construction from a theoretical perspective (Xiang et al., 2022; Chen
      et al., 2021; Sun et al., 2024; Shi et al., 2024; Yu et al., 2019).


                                                    1
054
      Secondly, this is the first time the DiT model has been applied to predict the upside and
055   downside movements of high-frequency stock market prices, thus enriching the literature
056   on time-series prediction and feature enhancement. We use the DiT model to enhance
057   financial market data by reverse sampling. Portfolios constructed using the DiT-LSTM
058   model outperformed those created with the simple LSTM model. The addition of the DiT
059   module greatly improves the Matthews correlation coefficient by almost 3%. The annual
060   return of the portfolio is at least 20% higher than the latter.
061   Third, we demonstrate that selecting stocks with weak-form efficiency with the SVAR
062   module can effectively enhance the cumulative equity of investment portfolios with improved
063   Sharpe ratio, reduced volatility, and maximum drawdowns. We find that stocks with a high
064   proportion of market information are predictable; those with a high proportion of public
065   information exhibit some predictability, while stocks with higher proportions of private
066   information and noise are random walks. The addition of the SVAR module greatly improves
067   the Matthews correlation coefficient by almost 4%. Portfolios constructed using the DiT-
068   LSTM-SVAR module based on market and public information outperformed those created
069
      with the DiT-LSTM model. The annual cumulative return of the portfolio is 266.60% and a
      Sharpe ratio of 1.8.

080   Denoising diffusion probabilistic models (DDPMs). In recent years, DDPMs have
081   made significant advancements in the field of generative models (Song et al., 2020), particularly
082
      in image generation (Tang et al., 2023; Jinhui et al., 2023). They have become an effective
      method, often outperforming previous Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) (Goodfellow
083
      et al., 2020) in many cases. Recent improvements in DDPMs over the past two years have
084
      primarily been driven by optimized sampling techniques (Ho et al., 2020). For example,
085   the introduction of more efficient sampling algorithms has enhanced the modelâ€™s ability
086   to generate high-quality samples (Ho et al., 2020). Classifier-free guidance has also been
087   widely recognized as a technique that significantly improves sample quality (Nichol et al.,
088   2021; Ho and Salimans, 2022; Baykal et al., 2024). This method enhances the flexibility
089   and adaptability of generative models by combining the capabilities of conditional and
090   unconditional generation models. Within this framework, the model can dynamically adjust
091   the influence of conditional information during the generation process, thereby producing
092   high-quality samples that better meet expectations. In terms of architecture selection,
093
      Convolutional U-Nets (Falck et al., 2022) and Transformer (Peebles and Xie, 2023; Bao
      et al., 2023) architectures are currently the primary choices. U-Net networks have become
094
      the default choice for DDPMs due to their effectiveness in handling image generation tasks.
095
      Meanwhile, attention-based Transformer architectures have also been introduced and have
096   shown excellent performance. These architectural improvements not only enhance the
097   modelâ€™s generative capabilities but also excel in diversity and detail handling. In summary,
098   through continuous innovations in sampling techniques, classifier-free guidance methods, and
099   architectural optimizations, DDPMs have shown increasingly outstanding performance in
100   the field of generative models, providing powerful tools for high-quality image generation.
101   This paper applies these techniques to high-frequency stock data to improve stock prediction
102   capabilities.
103   Stock Prediction. While literature has made some progress in stock price prediction,
104   it primarily focuses on the algorithm of sequence forecasting techniques, with insufficient
105   emphasis on financial data characteristics. The literature has made some progress in gradient
106   computation(Mandi and Guns, 2020), loss functions, as well as resampling techniques and
107   overfitting(Zhang et al., 2020). For signal-to-noise ratio and survivorship bias problem,Liu
      et al. (2022) putting forward the FinRL-Meta open library based on reinforcement learning.

108
      In terms of portfolio construction, the literature is based on predicting the probability of
109   directly picking the stocks with the highest probability of constructing a portfolio (Xiang
110   et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2021), and reinforcement learning strategies (Sun et al., 2024; Shi
111   et al., 2024; Yu et al., 2019) . However, according to the efficient market hypothesis, only
112   stocks consistent with weak effectiveness are not randomly walking. If stocks are inherently
113   randomly walking, their predictions may be only by chance and have weak applicability.
114   Therefore, it becomes a critical task to prioritize the identification of these weakly effective
115   stocks.
116   The stock price has theoretical efficient components and the noise created by market in-
117   efficiencies or information asymmetry(Hendershott and Menkveld, 2014). The stationary
118   components(efficient components) of the stock price is determined by all publicly available
119   information, which also includes private information, since it can be inferred(Hasbrouck,
120   2015; Albert J Menkveld and Lucas, 2007) .Brogaard et al. (2022) propose that the sta-
121   tionary component includes market-wide information, firm-specific information revealed
122   through trading on private information, and firm-specific information revealed through public
123
      news.Kerssenfischer and Schmeling (2024) explore that 15%â€“35% of the variance in stock
      returns can be attributed to the release of macro news.
124
125   Therefore, we stress that if noise predominates the stock price movements, then the stock is
126   random walk. Even if such stock movements are predicted, there is a risk of model collapse.
127   However, the literature does not consider these factors.

260   3.5   Portfolio Module Design
261
      After utilizing the DiT-LSTM model to forecast the probability of stock price fluctuations,
262
      we opt for the ten stocks with the highest likelihood of upward movement to form an
263
      investment portfolio. Initially, we set thresholds for private, public, market, and noise-related
264   information, thereby eliminating stocks deemed challenging to predict. Subsequently, we
265   contrast the investment portfolios crafted after removing these unpredictable stocks with
266   those directly derived from the DiT-LSTM and LSTM modelsâ€™ predictions of stock price
267   movements. We evaluate various metrics, including cumulative holding period returns,
268   standard deviation, realized volatility, maximum drawdown, and Sharpe ratio. Throughout
269   the portfolio construction process, we employ both equal-weighting and minimum variance
      strategies based on historical returns and covariance matrices.

      4.2   Baselines
290
      GRU: This model trains a forecasting framework comprising a GRU layer with a hidden
291   size of 32, a linear layer, and an activation layer using Sigmoid.
292
293   LSTM: This model trains a forecasting framework comprising an LSTM layer with a hidden
294
      size of 32 and a linear layer, incorporating market factors.
295   DiT-LSTM: This approach first uses DiT model to generate additional data according to
296   opposite labels to balance samples, and then trains a forecasting framework comprising an
297   LSTM layer with a hidden size of 32 and a linear layer. We also test the case when using DiT
298   model to generate additional data according to same labels and then add it to the training
299   set with a probability of 0.2.
300   DiT-LSTM-SVAR: This approach first uses a DiT block to generate additional data
301   and then filter random walk stocks. We filter random walk stocks with market and public
302   information, market information, and noise information, respectively, based on SVAR model.

305   4.3   Experimental Setup
306
307   Software and Hardware: DiT-LSTM-SVAR is implemented with Python 3.8, Pytorch
308
      1.11.0. We run the experiments on servers equipped with RTX 3080(10GB) GPU and Intel(R)
      Xeon(R) Platinum 8255C CPU @ 2.50GHz CPU.
309
310   DiT Module:We frame the task as a binary classification problem based on the stock
311   returns. During the training process, we augmented the original DiT model by introducing a
312   cross-attention mechanism to explore the impact of information from the Chinese markets
313   on the data generation process. We incorporated this mechanism by integrating it with the
314
      original features using a weighted fusion approach, weighting at 0.5. We set the learning
      rate of the DiT model to 1e-5 and the learning rate of the downstream classification model
315
      to 5e-4. In the generation process, we adopt a classifier-free guidance approach for sampling.
316
317   Portfolio: In calculating the cumulative return on investment, we follow simple assumptions:
318   (1) Traders filter the random stock.(2) Traders spend the same amount on each trading day.
319   (3) There is always enough liquidity in the market. (4)Transaction costs are ignored.
320   Evaluation Metrics for prediction: (1) Accuracy: Accuracy is the proportion of correctly
321   predicted instances out of the total instances. For imbalanced data, it cannot fully capture
322   the performance. (2) F1 Score: The F1 score is the harmonic mean of precision and recall,
323   providing a balance between these two metrics. It considers both false positives and false
      negatives in the evaluation. (3) MCC: Matthews Correlation Coefficient(MCC) is computed


                                                    6
324
      based on all four elements of the confusion matrix (true class, false positive class, true
325   negative class, false negative class), providing a balanced performance evaluation, even in
326   datasets with extremely unbalanced classes.
327
328
      Evaluation Metrics for Portfolio: (1) Cumulative Return: Cumulative return is the
      total return on an investment over a period of time. (2) Annual Return: Annualized rate of
329
      return is the conversion of an investmentâ€™s total return to an annual percentage. (3) MDD:
330
      Maximum drawdown measures the maximum loss of a portfolio from peak to trough over a
331   selected time period. It is an important measure of downside investment risk. (4) Annualized
332   STD: Annualized standard deviation is a measure of the volatility or risk of investment
333   returns. (5) Sharpe: The Sharpe ratio is a measure of an investmentâ€™s return relative to its
334   risk. A high Sharpe Ratio indicates a higher return on investment per unit of risk assumed.
335   (6) Sortino: Sortino ratio, similar to the Sharpe ratio, only takes into account downside risk
336   rather than overall standard deviation. (7) VaR95: Value at Risk quantifies the probability
337   of the largest possible loss in the value of an investment over a given period of time with
338   95% confidence. see Append B.1 for details.

477
      We construct the DiT-LSTM-SVAR to facilitate the development of the time series domain,
      which will have a broad impact on academic researchers and financial practitioners. The
478
      DiT-LSTM-SVAR model is a diffusion-based deep learning prediction model combined with
479
      an SVAR-based portfolio selection model. It eliminates random-walk stocks to construct
480   robust, high-return investment portfolios. We predict the price movements of CSI 300 stocks
481   and constructed investment portfolios by filtering out random walk stocks using market,
482   public, private, and noise information shares. Given that constructing portfolios aims to
483   pick the stocks with the highest returns efficiently, we introduce the concept of effective
484   forecasting accuracy. We demonstrated the necessity of filtering random walk stocks by
485   comparing the performance of portfolios constructed after the SVAR filtering model and
      portfolios directly constructed using the DiT-LSTM model. We also show that incorporating


                                                                  9
486
      the DiT module enhances the data by balancing the sample labels, which can dramatically
487   increase recall and pinpoint rising stocks.
488
489   This paper theoretically bridges the fields of time series analysis and financial data. We
490
      use an information decomposition model based on the SVAR model to identify random
      walk stocks. By integrating the SVAR model into the time series forecasting technique,
491
      our model can be better applied to portfolio construction based on time series forecasting.
492
      This effectively complements the previous literature that did not fully consider portfolio
493   construction and focused on stock price series forecasting(Liu et al., 2022; Mandi and Guns,
494   2020; Lin et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2020).
495
496
      Since market information affects stock prices, we add market factors to the forecasting
      model. Our research findings have significant implications for portfolio construction. We find
497
      that selecting stocks with strong explanatory power in private and public information can
498
      significantly improve portfolio performance.A portfolio constructed using the DiT-LSTM-
499   SVAR model can have up to 0.28 more holding equity than a portfolio constructed using
500   the DiT-LSTM model. Even without adding the market factor, the portfolios constructed
501   based on DiT-LSTM-SVAR outperform the DiT-LSTM model. Our portfolios have higher
502   annualized cumulative returns, higher Sharpe ratios, and lower maximum drawdown than the
503   existing literature(Yu et al., 2019; Shi et al., 2024; Sun et al., 2024).These results underscore
504   the practical value of our model in enhancing portfolio performance. Our DiT-LSTM-SVAR-
505   based model demonstrates a high level of generalization ability, a key advantage over the
506   LSTM model which often suffers from overfitting. This overfitting can lead to less effective
507
      prediction of the later period than the earlier period. By highlighting this superiority, we
      instill confidence in the effectiveness of our model, further reinforcing its practical relevance
508
      and applicability.
509
510   As the data is unbalanced due to more downward movements of stocks during the sample
511   period, we include DiT in our forecasting model to augment the data. The results of
512   our study complement the time series forecasting. We find that the addition of the DiT
513
      module improves the recall of predicting downside movements by nearly 30%, makes the
      portfolios more conservative. The cumulative equity of the portfolios constructed using
514
      the DiT-LSTM-SVAR model is 0.28 higher than that of the portfolios constructed using
515
      the DiT-LSTM model with the market factor, and Sortino rises by 0.32, which effectively
516   reduces the downside risk. These results emphasize the practical value of our DiT model
517   in improving stock forecasting, further enhancing its practical relevance and applicability,
518   especially in financial markets where investors are more sensitive to stock declines than stock
519   rises.

1216   Third, we provide examples of using another financial method, CVaR, to enhance the model.
1217
       Conditional Value at Risk (CVaR), also known as Expected Shortfall, is a risk measure
       used in finance to assess the potential losses of an investment portfolio. It provides an
1218
       estimate of the expected loss beyond a given Value at Risk (VaR) threshold, offering a more
1219
       comprehensive view of tail risk. The results are shown in the FigureF3. Our results show
1220   that the CVaR method not only failed to enhance the existing model, but also reduced the
1221   cumulative return of the model from 1.23 to 1.22. We can see that the effect of using CVaR
1222   method is not as good as SVAR. The reason might be that CVaR primarily focuses on the
1223   average tail risk under normal market conditions, which is the expected loss given that losses
1224   exceed the VaR threshold. It relies on regular market volatility and assumes that historical
1225   data can represent future risks. However, this method may not fully capture the complex
1226   dynamic relationships in the market. In contrast, the SVAR method incorporates structural
1227   information from economic theory, considering the interrelationships and dynamic changes
1228
       between variables to assess risk. This allows SVAR to better capture the interactions and
       potential transmission effects between different market variables, providing a more accurate
1229
       risk assessment. Therefore, in primary markets, SVAR can more comprehensively reflect the
1230
       risk landscape, making it more effective than CVaR.
