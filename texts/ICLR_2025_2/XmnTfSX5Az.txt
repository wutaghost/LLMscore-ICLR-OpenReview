011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Time series anomaly detection is critical in numerous practical applications, yet
014            existing deep learning methods often fall short of real-world demands. These
015
               models fail to swiftly filter out physically implausible anomalies, insufficiently
               address distributional shifts, and lack a comprehensive approach that integrates
016
               both global and local perspectives for anomaly detection. Moreover, most suc-
017
               cessful models rely on channel-dependent methods that tend to treat all features
018            at the same timestamp as a single token and then focus on finding relationships
019            between these tokens. This approach overlooks the unique periodicities, trends,
020            and lagged relationships between different features, leading to suboptimal per-
021            formance. To address these limitations, we propose KambaAD, a model com-
022            prised of an Encoder and Reconstructor. The Encoder integrates the strengths of
023            the Kolmogorov-Arnold Network (KAN), attention mechanism, and the Selec-
024            tive Structured State Space Model (MAMBA). Specifically, KAN is employed to
025            swiftly enforce data consistency, enabling rapid detection of anomalies that violate
026
               physical laws. Attention mechanism ensures balanced processing of global infor-
               mation while enhancing the representation of key data characteristics. We lever-
027
               age MAMBA’s capability as a sequence model to capture anomalies caused by
028
               local variations. Additionally, its internal selection mechanism allows the model
029            to effectively handle distribution shifts, ensuring robustness and adaptability in
030            the presence of changing data distributions. Additionally, the framework incorpo-
031            rates a time-series-specific Reconstructor, which reduces computational complex-
032            ity through patch-based operations that exploit local consistency in time series
033            data. It also employs channel-independent linear reconstruction to prevent in-
034            terference between different features. Through extensive experiments on multiple
035            multivariate datasets, KambaAD consistently outperforms state-of-the-art models,
036            demonstrating its superior performance in anomaly detection.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
039
040
      Time series anomaly detection aims to accurately identify points or subsequences that deviate from
041
      regular patterns within continuous time series data. In the context of digital operations management,
042   this technology is essential for tracking key performance indicators (KPIs) such as CPU utilization,
043   memory usage, and network bandwidth, which generate large volumes of time series data (Zhu
044   et al., 2019). By detecting anomalies such as performance bottlenecks or system failures, operations
045   teams can swiftly mitigate issues, ensuring system resilience, scalability, and high availability while
046   reducing downtime and failure rates (Lindemann et al., 2021). Beyond digital infrastructure, time
047   series anomaly detection is also applied in fields such as economics, meteorology, and finance. For
048   instance, it helps detect abnormal market fluctuations, predict extreme weather events, and identify
049   fraudulent financial transactions (Ahmed et al., 2016; Lee et al., 2018; Hilal et al., 2022). Tradi-
050
      tional anomaly detection methods rely on handcrafted features and statistical assumptions, offering
      simplicity and interpretability but struggling with scalability and adaptability in diverse or high-
051
      dimensional datasets (Teng, 2010; Munir et al., 2019).
052
053   In recent decades, deep learning (DL) methods have been widely adopted for time series anomaly
      detection, excelling at capturing temporal dependencies and nonlinear relationships without manual


                                                        1
054
      feature engineering (Choi et al., 2021). However, DL methods face several challenges: Firstly, they
055   often overlook the overall consistency of the data, making them prone to missing subtle or physically
056   implausible anomalies. If these anomalies are not identified promptly, the model may confuse nor-
057   mal and abnormal patterns, especially when anomalies densely occur within a specific window, as it
058   tries to minimize the overall error for that window. Second, DL methods struggle with distributional
059   shifts over time, where patterns of normality and anomaly evolve. Anomalies during training may
060   become normal later, while new anomalies emerge from previously normal patterns, complicating
061   model robustness in dynamic, non-stationary data (Zeng, 2020; Wen & Keyes, 2019). Third, DL
062   methods often fail to integrate both global and local perspectives—essential for detecting long-term
063
      deviations and short-term fluctuations, respectively (Xia et al., 2023; Albu et al., 2020). Finally,
      most successful models adopt channel-dependent strategies that treat features at the same times-
064
      tamp as a single token, disregarding distinct periodicities, trends, and lagged relationships. This
065
      limits their ability to capture complex interactions in multivariate time series, leading to suboptimal
066   reconstruction (Liu et al., 2022).
067
068   To address the multifaceted challenges in time series anomaly detection, we propose KambaAD, a
069
      robust framework composed of an Encoder and a Reconstructor. The Encoder employs a two-stage
      feature extraction process: Coarse-Grained Anomaly Filtering and Fine-Grained Pattern Recogni-
070
      tion. In the Coarse-Grained Anomaly Filtering stage, KAN replaces traditional weight parameters
071
      with learnable univariate functions, establishing more stable functional relationships between inputs
072   and outputs. This not only reduces the number of parameters but also leverages data consistency,
073   enabling faster preliminary anomaly screening. By processing features from all time steps within
074   the entire time window, KAN effectively captures latent cross-temporal correlations, avoiding issues
075   such as lag effects, periodicity, and feature misalignment that arise from analyzing individual data
076   points. Once KAN has addressed the more apparent anomalies, the process transitions seamlessly
077   to the Fine-Grained Pattern Recognition stage, where the focus shifts to detecting more nuanced
078   and infrequent anomalies. In this stage, we integrate attention mechanism and MAMBA within the
079   representation space. Attention mechanism excels at capturing global dependencies across distant
080
      time steps, making it particularly useful for detecting long-range correlations in complex time series
      (Matar et al., 2023). Meanwhile, MAMBA focuses on detecting local, context-specific anomalies.
081
      As a sequential model, MAMBA iteratively updates its hidden states while leveraging positional
082
      information to capture temporal dependencies. Its dynamic adjustment mechanism adapts the state
083   transition matrix to input changes, addressing distribution shifts. MAMBA’s gating mechanism fur-
084   ther modulates the influence of inputs on hidden states and outputs, reducing the impact of anoma-
085   lous points on the reconstruction process. Additionally, MAMBA employs 1D convolutional opera-
086   tions to efficiently capture local dependencies between adjacent time steps, enhancing its ability to
087   detect short-term anomalies and trend shifts. In the Reconstructor, we employ three complementary
088   techniques: patch-based data division, channel-independent (CI) processing, and linear reconstruc-
089   tion. First, patch-based division allows the model to leverage local similarities, focusing on critical
090   temporal features rather than processing the entire sequence at once, thereby reducing resource con-
091
      sumption (Berral et al., 2021; Scherer et al., 2021; Sabater et al., 2022). Second, the CI strategy
      ensures that each channel is processed independently during reconstruction, allowing the model to
092
      refine individual features without losing the global context, as inter-channel relationships have al-
093
      ready been captured in the Encoder. Finally, linear reconstruction further controls the number of
094   parameters, ensuring the model remains scalable and robust when handling high-dimensional data.
095
096   In summary, the integration of KAN, attention mechanism, and MAMBA in KambaAD enables ef-
097
      fective coarse-grained anomaly filtering and fine-grained pattern recognition across temporal scales.
      Extensive experiments on multiple datasets show a 5% improvement in F1 score, confirming its
098
      effectiveness in time series anomaly detection.

139   3     M ETHODOLOGY
140
141   3.1     OVERVIEW
142
      As shown in Figure 1, the model processes windowed data as input and outputs an anomaly score.
143
      The architecture consists of two parts: feature extraction and reconstruction. In the feature extraction
144   phase, a hybrid encoder combining KAN, attention mechanisms, and MAMBA is used. In the
145   reconstruction phase, the data is divided into patches and fed into channel-independent linear models
146   for reconstruction. The reconstruction error is computed as the mean squared error (MSE) between
147   the input and the reconstructed output.
148
149   3.2     ENCODER
150
151   This section elucidates the mathematical underpinnings of the KambaAD encoder component. Let
152   the input data be denoted as X ∈ Rn×k , where n represents the number of data points and k signifies
153   the number of features. Our primary objective is to extract information-rich features capable of
154
      reconstructing the original data with high fidelity.
155
      3.2.1        STAGE ONE: C OARSE -G RAINED A NOMALY F ILTERING
156
157   In this study, we introduce the use of the KAN for preliminary anomaly detection in time series
158   data. To tackle challenges such as varying periodicities, trends, and lag effects across features, we
159   consider the entire window of features as input, allowing the model to capture relationships across
160   different time steps. The input data is represented as a matrix X ∈ Rn×k , where each data point
161   Xi = {x1 , x2 , . . . , xk } (for i = 1, 2, . . . , n). The operations in this phase are denoted by FKAN ,
      where X is first reshaped into a vector, processed, and then reshaped back into an n × d matrix. For


                                                                                3
162
      details on the KAN architecture, please refer to Appendix C. After processing through FKAN , we
163   apply post-processing steps such as dropout (Srivastava et al., 2014), linear residual combination,
164   and Root Mean Square Normalization (RMSNorm) (Zhang & Sennrich, 2019). This post-processing
165   is formalized as follows:
166                                             X1 = XWα + bα ,                                          (2)
                     n×d
167   where X1 ∈ R        represents the linearly transformed residuals, adjusted to the universal dimension
168   d. This transformation prepares the residuals for combination with the main output of KAN. Here,
169   Wα ∈ Rk×d and bα ∈ Rn×d denote the weight matrix and bias vector, respectively. The final
170   output of the model, XKAN ∈ Rn×d , which captures anomalies in the data, is expressed as:
171                             XKAN = RMSNorm(Dropout(FKAN (X)) + X1 ).                                 (3)
172
173   3.2.2   STAGE TWO: F INE -G RAINED PATTERN R ECOGNITION
174
      In this phase, we refine anomaly detection by first applying a self-attention mechanism to provide a
175
      global perspective, enhancing time series features and highlighting anomalies. Next, the MAMBA
176   model performs recursive updates to capture subtle temporal variations. This two-step process bal-
177   ances long-range dependency capture with local anomaly detection. By combining the global pattern
178   recognition of self-attention with MAMBA’s efficient modeling of local dynamics, our framework
179   effectively detects both prominent and subtle anomalies. We first apply a multi-head attention mech-
180   anism to the time series data, transforming the input, calculating attention scores, and aggregating
181   the results. For each attention head, the output is computed using the query (Q), key (K), and value
182   (V) matrices as follows:
                                                              Qi KTi

183                                      headi = softmax √             Vi ,                            (4)
184                                                              dk
185   where the queries, keys, and values are obtained as:
186                       Qi = XKAN WiQ ,       Ki = XKAN WiK ,      Vi = XKAN WiV ,                     (5)
187
188
      with WiQ , WiK , and WiV being learnable weight matrices, and dk representing the dimensionality
      of the key vectors. The outputs from all attention heads are concatenated and linearly transformed
189
      to form the final output O. This output is combined with the original input XKAN through a residual
190
      connection, followed by dropout and layer normalization:
191
192
                                    XAttn = RMSNorm(Dropout(O) + XKAN ),                                  (6)
193   where XAttn ∈ Rn×d represents the output from attention layer. Subsequently, the data is processed
194   through the MAMBA module, which is specifically designed to detect anomalies by identifying
195
      subtle local variations in the input sequence. The input data, denoted as XAttn , undergoes a series
      of transformations, including linear projection, 1D convolution, and the SiLU (Swish) activation
196
      function. This process produces the intermediate representation X2 ∈ Rn×d , which serves as the
197
      input to the structured state-space model (SSM) module. Mathematically, this transformation can be
198   expressed as:
199                                   X2 = SiLU (Conv1D (XAttn Wβ , h)) ,                                 (7)
200   where Wβ is the learnable linear transformation matrix, h is the 1D convolutional kernel, and
201   SiLU(x) = x · σ(x) (with σ(x) being the sigmoid function) is the activation function. The re-
202   sulting matrix X2 encapsulates the processed data, ready for further temporal modeling. To capture
203   temporal dependencies, we introduce vector notations xt , yt , and ht , where t indexes the position in
204   the sequence. xt ∈ Rd×1 represents the transpose of the t-th row vector of X2 , while ht ∈ Rd×1 and
205   yt ∈ R1×d denote the hidden state and output at time step t, respectively. The temporal dynamics
206   are captured through a recursive update of the hidden state, which is formulated as:
207                                           ht = At ht−1 + bt xt ,                                      (8)
208   where At ∈ R    d×d
                           and bt ∈ R  d×1
                                           are matrices derived from linear transformations of the input xt .
209   These matrices govern how the hidden state ht evolves over time, incorporating both the previous
210   hidden state ht−1 and the current input xt . The output at each time step t, denoted yt , is computed
211   as:
212                                                 yt = ct ht ,                                          (9)
213   where ct ∈ Rd×1 is another transformation matrix applied to the hidden state ht . By stacking the
214   output vectors yt across the time dimension, a matrix representing the final output of the encoder,
215   denoted as Xenc , is constructed. This matrix serves as the final encoded representation of the input
      sequence, encapsulating both local and temporal features for anomaly detection.

459
460   We conducted a sensitivity analysis on KambaAD, examining key parameters (window size,
461   patch size, d state, n head, d model, e layers) and their impact on F1 scores across four datasets. As
462   shown in Figure 2, KambaAD exhibits stability on the SMAP and MSL datasets, where parameter
463   variations have a limited effect on performance. However, on the PSM and MSL datasets, the model
464
      is more sensitive to specific parameters. Notably, on the PSM dataset, setting n heads and e layers to
      2 and 1 respectively leads to a significant performance drop, indicating that these parameter settings
465
      constrain the model’s capabilities. For the SMD dataset, a window size of 60 results in a noticeable
466
      decline in performance, suggesting that a larger context window is beneficial. Overall, KambaAD’s
467   performance remains stable, but further increasing hyperparameters such as d model, n heads, and
468   e layers does not enhance performance, likely due to overfitting.

536      detection. By integrating KAN for initial screening and a combined attention-MAMBA approach

      Statistical methods, particularly effective for low-dimensional data, include moving averages, ex-
792
      ponential smoothing, and the Autoregressive Integrated Moving Average (ARIMA) model (Box &
793
      Pierce, 1970). Moving averages smooth out short-term fluctuations in data to identify trends, while
794   exponential smoothing gives more weight to recent observations. The ARIMA model combines
795   autoregression, differencing, and moving averages to capture temporal dependencies in time series
796   data. These models calculate residuals, where larger residuals may indicate anomalies (Yaacob et al.,
797   2010). If the anomaly score, derived from these residuals, exceeds a specified threshold, the data is
798   classified as anomalous.
799
      Machine learning-based methods encompass a range of approaches, from classical algorithms to
800   advanced deep learning techniques. Classical algorithms include:
801
802         • One-Class Support Vector Machines (One-Class SVM): This method identifies a bound-
803           ary around normal data points, classifying points outside the boundary as anomalies.
804
            • k-Nearest Neighbor (k-NN): It classifies data points based on their proximity to other
805           points, with outliers being far from their neighbors.
806
807
            • Random Forests: An ensemble method that constructs multiple decision trees, with out-
              liers being those that frequently end up in the less common branches.
808
809         • k-means clustering: This method groups data into clusters, where points far from any
              cluster center are considered anomalies.


                                                       15
810
            • Gaussian Mixture Models: These models assume that data is generated from a mixture
811           of several Gaussian distributions, with anomalies being points that don’t fit well into any
812           distribution.
813
            • Isolation Forest: It isolates anomalies by recursively partitioning the data, with anomalies
814
              being the first to be isolated.
815
            • Local Outlier Factor (LOF): This method identifies anomalies by comparing the local
816
              density of each point to that of its neighbors.
817
818   Advanced deep learning techniques include:
819
820         • Recurrent Neural Networks (RNN): Designed for sequential data, RNNs capture tempo-
821           ral dependencies in time series (Hopfield, 1982).
822         • Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM): A special type of RNN, LSTMs are particularly
823           effective at learning long-term dependencies in sequences (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber,
824           1997).
825         • Autoencoders (AE): These neural networks learn to encode data into a lower-dimensional
826           space and then reconstruct it; anomalies are identified by high reconstruction errors
827           (Rumelhart et al., 1986).
828         • Variational Autoencoders (VAE): A probabilistic variant of autoencoders that models
829           data distributions and identifies anomalies through reconstruction errors or latent space
830           deviations (Kingma & Welling, 2013).
831         • Generative Adversarial Networks (GAN): These consist of two networks (a generator
832           and a discriminator) that learn to generate data; anomalies are identified by the discrimina-
833           tor’s failure to classify generated data correctly (Goodfellow et al., 2014).
834         • Transformers: Known for their attention mechanisms, Transformers are effective at pro-
835           cessing sequential data, especially in contexts where relationships between different parts
836           of the sequence matter (Vaswani et al., 2017).
837
            • Graph Neural Networks (GNN): These networks are tailored for data with graph struc-
838           tures, identifying anomalies based on the relationships between nodes in the graph (Kipf &
839           Welling, 2016).
840
            • Diffusion models: A newer approach that uses probabilistic methods to model complex
841           data distributions and identify anomalies based on how well data fits these distributions.
842
            • Mamba: Mamba is an innovative state space model that has recently gained attention in
843
              the field of machine learning and natural language processing. Developed as an alternative
844
              to traditional transformer architectures, Mamba leverages the power of state space models
845           to process sequential data efficiently(Gu & Dao, 2023).
846
847   In anomaly detection, these methods generate a score for each data point, which is then compared
848   to a threshold to determine whether the point is normal or anomalous.
849
      Advanced deep learning methods are increasingly employed for anomaly detection due to their
850   ability to capture complex patterns in high-dimensional data. Techniques such as Recurrent Neural
851   Networks (RNN) (Choi et al., 2021), Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) (Wei et al., 2023), Autoen-
852   coders (AE) (Thill et al., 2021), Variational Autoencoders (VAE) (Chen et al., 2023a), Generative
853   Adversarial Networks (GAN) (Geiger et al., 2020), Transformers (Xu et al., 2021), Graph Neural
854   Networks (GNN) (Deng & Hooi, 2021), Diffusion models (Wolleb et al., 2022) and Mamba (Chen
855   et al., 2024; He et al., 2024) are prominent examples. These methods generate a score for each data
856   point, which is then compared to a threshold to assess whether the point is anomalous or within the
857   normal range.

861   KAN, with its efficient parameterization and ability to approximate complex functions, effectively

863   Arnold Representation Theorem, which states that any multivariate continuous function can be ex-
      pressed as a composition of univariate functions and summation operations. Each univariate function

948   The primary objective of implementing patch segmentation lies in harnessing the inherent local sim-
949   ilarities within time series data to efficiently extract coherent features from adjacent data points. By
950   partitioning the data into smaller, manageable patches, we not only simplify the analysis process
951   but also significantly reduce the computational complexity of subsequent reconstruction tasks. This
952   approach essentially treats each patch as a self-contained entity, fostering a more streamlined pro-
953
      cessing pipeline where each patch’s data is considered and manipulated as a unified whole. As a
      result, the overall reconstruction effort becomes more efficient and manageable, as the complexity
954
      of operations is distributed across smaller, more manageable segments. The reason we choose to
955
      perform patch partition after feature extraction is that anomalies often appear continuously. If the
956   number of anomalous points within a patch exceeds that of normal points, it becomes difficult to
957   reconstruct them into normal data. However, our goal is to make the reconstructed data as normal as
958   possible, so that we can better distinguish points through errors.
959

960

961

962   matrix Xpatch with dimensions ⌈ patchn len ⌉ × patch len × k, where patch len is the predefined number
963

964
965   During this transformation, the data sequence is uniformly divided into segments of length patch len.
966   If the length n of the original data sequence Xenc is not divisible by patch len, the last patch will
967   have a length less than patch len. In such cases, a zero-padding strategy is employed to ensure that
968
      all patches have a uniform length of patch len, maintaining the regularity of the matrix Xpatch for
      subsequent processing or analysis.

1021   F.1   A FFILIATION METRIC : A M ETRIC FOR C OMPREHENSIVE E VENT L OCALIZATION
1022         A SSESSMENT
1023
1024   The Affiliation metric, introduced by Huet et al., represents a sophisticated metric that integrates
1025   both precision and recall to evaluate the accuracy of event localization in a manner that is robust
       to potential interferences. This metric innovatively utilizes the Hausdorff distance to measure the


                                                           19
1026
       disparity between the true and predicted events, thereby providing a comprehensive assessment of
1027   the spatial alignment between the two. Furthermore, it incorporates individual probabilities within
1028   the designated Affiliation region for normalization purposes, enhancing the metric’s sensitivity to
1029   varying degrees of confidence in the predictions.
1030
       A notable strength of the Affiliation metric lies in its creative application of the Hausdorff distance,
1031
       which is known for its effectiveness in quantifying the maximum mismatch between two sets of
1032
       points. This characteristic enables the metric to capture fine-grained discrepancies in event localiza-
1033   tion, offering a nuanced perspective on the performance of the system. Additionally, the integration
1034   of probabilities within the Affiliation region ensures that the score reflects not only the spatial accu-
1035   racy but also the level of certainty associated with each prediction.
1036
       However, it is crucial to acknowledge the limitations of the Affiliation metric as well. Firstly, the size
1037
       of the Affiliation region exerts a significant influence on the resulting score, potentially leading to
1038
       an overestimation of performance when minimal gains in precision are achieved. This highlights the
1039   need for careful calibration of the region’s dimensions to ensure an unbiased evaluation. Secondly,
1040   the metric exhibits a limitation in discriminating between false predictions within the Affiliation
1041   region, potentially masking errors that would otherwise be revealed. Lastly, the Affiliation metric
1042   exhibits a bias towards false positives over false negatives, which may skew the overall assessment
1043   of the system’s performance, particularly in scenarios where a high degree of accuracy is paramount.
1044
       To mitigate these limitations, future research could explore the refinement of the Affiliation region’s
1045   definition, as well as the development of additional metrics that complement the Affiliation metric
1046   in capturing different aspects of event localization performance. By addressing these challenges,
1047   the Affiliation metric can be further strengthened as a valuable tool for assessing and comparing the
1048   accuracy of event localization systems in scientific research.

1058   The Volume Under the Surface (VUS) metric, introduced by Paparrizos et al., represents a ground-
1059
       breaking extension of AUC-based evaluation methodologies, specifically tailored to accommodate
       distance-based anomalies. Its fundamental novelty stems from the innovative label transformation
1060
       technique employed, coupled with the meticulous computation of the volumetric aspect beneath the
1061
       ROC curves constructed across a spectrum of buffer lengths. This intricate approach transcends
1062   traditional binary labeling, transforming it into a continuum of values that inherently biases towards
1063   an overestimation of false positives compared to false negatives, thereby providing a more nuanced
1064   and informative view of anomaly detection performance.
1065
       By seamlessly integrating this sophisticated label transformation mechanism with a meticulous vol-
1066
       umetric assessment beneath the ROC surface, the VUS metric presents a comprehensive and multi-
1067
       faceted evaluation framework for anomaly detection systems. This framework is particularly adept
1068   at capturing nuances in performance that are often overlooked by conventional metrics, particularly
1069   in scenarios where the proximity to the decision boundary is of paramount importance. By enabling
1070   a deeper understanding of how anomaly detection algorithms behave across varying levels of confi-
1071   dence and proximity to the threshold, the VUS metric empowers researchers and practitioners alike
1072   to assess and benchmark the performance of diverse anomaly detection techniques with unprece-
1073   dented precision and rigor.
1074   Furthermore, the VUS metric underscores the importance of considering not just the absolute classi-
1075   fication accuracy but also the confidence associated with each prediction, as well as the distribution
1076   of predictions across the ROC space. This holistic approach enables a more complete and accurate
1077   portrayal of anomaly detection performance, ultimately facilitating the development and refinement
1078   of more effective and reliable anomaly detection systems. In summary, the VUS metric stands as a
1079   valuable and indispensable tool in the ongoing pursuit of enhancing anomaly detection capabilities
       within the scientific community.
