                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
010            Dataset Distillation aims to compress a large dataset into a significantly more
011            compact, synthetic one without compromising the performance of the trained mod-
012            els. To achieve this, existing methods use the agent model to extract information
013
               from the target dataset and embed it into the distilled dataset. Consequently, the
               quality of extracted and embedded information determines the quality of the dis-
014
               tilled dataset. In this work, we find that existing methods introduce misaligned
015
               information in both information extraction and embedding stages. To alleviate
016            this, we propose Prioritize Alignment in Dataset Distillation (PAD), which aligns
017            information from the following two perspectives. 1) We prune the target dataset
018            according to the compressing ratio to filter the information that can be extracted
019            by the agent model. 2) We use only deep layers of the agent model to perform the
020            distillation to avoid excessively introducing low-level information. This simple
021            strategy effectively filters out misaligned information and brings non-trivial im-
022            provement for mainstream matching-based distillation algorithms. Furthermore,
023            built on trajectory matching, PAD achieves remarkable improvements on various
024
               benchmarks, achieving state-of-the-art performance.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029
030   Dataset Distillation (DD) (Wang et al., 2020) aims to compress a large dataset into a small synthetic
031   dataset that preserves important features for models to achieve comparable performances. Ever since
032
      being introduced, DD has gained a lot of attention because of its wide applications in practical fields
      such as privacy preservation (Dong et al., 2022; Yu et al., 2023), continual learning (Masarczyk &
033
      Tautkute, 2020; Rosasco et al., 2021), and neural architecture search (Jin et al., 2018; Pasunuru &
034
      Bansal, 2019).
035
036   Recently, matching-based methods (Zhao & Bilen, 2021c; Wang et al., 2022; Du et al., 2022) have
037   achieved promising performance in distilling high-quality synthetic datasets. Generally, the process
038
      of these methods can be summarized into two steps: (1) Information Extraction: an agent model is
      used to extract important information from the target dataset by recording various metrics such as gra-
039
      dients (Zhao et al., 2020), distributions (Zhao & Bilen, 2021a), and training trajectories (Cazenavette
040
      et al., 2022), (2) Information Embedding: the synthetic samples are optimized to incorporate the
041   extracted information, which is achieved by minimizing the differences between the same metric
042   calculated on the synthetic data and the one recorded in the previous step.
043
044
      In this work, we first reveal both steps will introduce misaligned information, which is redundant
      and potentially detrimental to the quality of the synthetic data. Then, by analyzing the cause of this
045
      misalignment, we propose alleviating this problem through the following two perspectives.
046
047   Typically, in the Information Extraction step, most distillation methods allow the agent model to see
048   all samples in the target dataset. This means information extracted by the agent model comes from
049   samples with various difficulties (see Figure 1a). However, according to previous study (Guo et al.,
050
      2023), information related to easy samples is only needed when the compression ratio is high. This
      misalignment leads to the sub-optimal of the distillation performance.
051
052   To alleviate the above issue, we first use data selection methods to measure the difficulty of each
053   sample in the target dataset. Then, during the distillation, a data scheduler is employed to ensure only
      data whose difficulty is aligned with the compression ratio is available for the agent model.

      In the Information Embedding step, most distillation methods except DM (Zhao & Bilen, 2021a)
079
      choose to use all parameters of the agent model to perform the distillation. Intuitively, this will
080   ensure the information extracted by the agent model is fully utilized. However, we find shallow layer
081   parameters of the model can only provide low-quality, basic signals, which are redundant for dataset
082   distillation in most cases. Conversely, performing the distillation with only parameters from deep
083   layers will yield high-quality synthetic samples. We attribute this contradiction to the fact that deeper
084   layers in DNNs tend to learn higher-level representations of input data (Mahendran & Vedaldi, 2016;
085   Selvaraju et al., 2016).
086   Based on our findings, to avoid embedding misaligned information in the Information Embedding
087   step, we propose to use only parameters from deeper layers of the agent model to perform distillation,
088   as illustrated in Figure 1b. This simple change brings significant performance improvement, showing
089   its effectiveness in aligning information.
090
      Through experiments, we validate that our two-step alignment strategy is effective for distillation
091
      methods based on matching gradients (Zhao et al., 2020), distributions (Zhao & Bilen, 2021a), and
092   trajectories (Cazenavette et al., 2022). Moreover, by applying our alignment strategy on trajectory
093   matching (Cazenavette et al., 2022; Guo et al., 2023), we propose our novel method named Prioritize
094   Alignment in Dataset Distillation (PAD). After conducting comprehensive evaluation experiments,
095   we show PAD achieves state-of-the-art (SOTA) performance.

099
100   Generally, we can summarize the distillation process of matching-based methods into the following
101   two steps: (1) Information Extraction: use an agent model to extract essential information from the
102   target dataset, realized by recording metrics such as gradients (Zhao et al., 2020), distributions (Zhao

      synthetic samples are optimized to incorporate the extracted information, realized by minimizing the
      differences between the same metric calculated on the synthetic data and the one recorded in the first
105
      step.
106

      will introduce misaligned information to the synthetic data.

      2.1        M ISALIGNED I NFORMATION E XTRACTED BY AGENT M ODELS
142
143   In the information extraction step, an agent model is employed to extract information from the target
144   dataset. Generally, most existing methods (Cazenavette et al., 2022; Du et al., 2022; Zhao et al.,
145   2020; Zhao & Bilen, 2021c) allow the agent model to see the full dataset. This implies that the
146   information extracted by the agent model originates from samples with diverse levels of difficulty.
147   However, the expected difficulty of distilled information varies with changes in IPC: smaller IPCs
148   prefer easier information while larger IPCs should distill harder one (Guo et al., 2023).
149   To verify if this misalignment will influence the quality of synthetic data, we perform the distillation
150   where hard/easy samples of target dataset are removed with various ratios. As the results reported in
151   Figure 2, pruning unaligned data points is beneficial for all matching-based methods. This proves the
152   misalignment indeed will influence the distillation performance and can be alleviated by filtering out
153   misaligned data from the target dataset.
154
155
      2.2        M ISALIGNED I NFORMATION E MBEDDED BY M ETRIC M ATCHING
156
157   Most existing methods use all parameters of the agent model to compute the metric used for matching.
158   Intuitively, this helps to improve the distillation performance, since in this way all information
159   extracted by the agent model will be embedded into the synthetic dataset. However, since shallow
160   layers in DNNs tend to learn basic distributions of data (Mahendran & Vedaldi, 2016; Selvaraju et al.,
161   2016), using parameters from these layers can only provide low-level signals that turned out to be
      redundant in most cases.

192   3.2   F ILTERING I NFORMATION E XTRACTION
193
194   In section 2.1, we show using data selection to filter out unmatched samples could alleviate the
195   misalignment caused in Information Extraction step. According to previous work (Guo et al., 2023),
196   TM-based methods prefer easy information and choose to match only early trajectories when IPC is
197   small. Conversely, hard information is preferred by high IPCs and they match only late trajectories.
198   Hence, we should use easy samples to train early trajectories, while late trajectories should be trained
199
      with hard samples. To realize this efficiently, we first use the data selection method to measure
      the difficulty of samples contained in the target dataset. Then, during training expert trajectories, a
200
      scheduler is implemented to gradually incorporate hard samples into the training set while excluding
201
      easier ones.
202
203
      Difficulty Scoring Function Identifying the difficulty of data for DNNs to learn has been well
204   studied in data selection area (Mirzasoleiman et al., 2019; Killamsetty et al., 2020; 2021; Sorscher
205   et al., 2022). For simplicity consideration, we use Error L2-Norm (EL2N) score Paul et al. (2021)
206   as the metric to evaluate the difficulty of training examples (other metrics can also be chosen, see
207   Section 4.3.2). Specifically, let x and y denote a data point and its label, respectively. Then, the
208   EL2N score can be calculated by:
209
                                         χt (x, y) = E||p(wt , x) − y||2 .                                (3)
210
211   where p(wt , x) = σ(f (wt , x)) is the output of a model f at training step t transformed into a
212   probability distribution. In consistent with Sorscher et al. (2022), samples with higher EL2N scores
213   are considered as harder samples in this paper.
214

      3.3      F ILTERING I NFORMATION E MBEDDING
240
241   To filter out misaligned information introduced by matching shallow-layer parameters, we propose
242   to add a parameter selection module that masks out part of shallow layers for metric computation.
243   Specifically, parameters of an agent network can be represented as a flattened array of length L that
244   stores weights of agent models ordered from shallow to deep layers (parameters within the same
245   layer are sorted in default order). The parameter selection sets a threshold ratio α such that the first
246   k = L · α parameters are not used for distillation. Then the parameters used for matching can now be
247   formulated as:
248                               θ̂t+N = {θ̂0 , θ̂1 , · · · , θ̂k−1 , θ̂k , θ̂k+1 , · · · , θ̂L }.        (4)
                                            |           {z         } |           {z            }
249                                                                discard              used for matching
250
251   In practice, the ratio α should vary with the change of IPC. For smaller IPCs, it is necessary to
252   incorporate basic information thus α should be lower. Conversely, basic information is redundant in
253
      larger IPC cases, so α should be higher accordingly.

      4.3.2        H YPER - PARAMETERS OF F ILTERING I NFORMATION E XTRACTION
348   Initial Ratio and Data Addition Epoch To filter the information learned by agent models, we
349   initialize the training set with only easy samples, and the size is determined by a certain ratio of
350   the total size. Then, we gradually add hard samples into the training set. In practice, we use two
351   hyper-parameters to control the addition process: the initial ratio (IR) of training data for training
352   set initialization and the end epoch of hard sample addition (AEE). These two parameters together
353   control the amount of data agent models can see at each epoch and the speed of adding hard samples.
354   In Table 4a, we show the distillation results where different hyper-parameters are utilized. In general,
355   a larger initial ratio and faster speed of addition bring better performances. Although the distillation
356   benefited more from learning simpler information when IPC is small (Guo et al., 2023), our findings
357   indicate that excessively removing difficult samples (e.g., more than a quarter) early in the training
358   phase can adversely affect the distilled data. This negative impact is likely due to the excessive
359   removal, which leads to distorted feature distributions within each category. On the other hand,
360   reasonably improving the speed of adding hard samples allows the agent model to achieve a more
361   balanced learning of information of varying difficulty across different stages.
362
363   Other Difficulty Scoring Functions Identifying the difficulty of data points is the key to filtering
364   out misaligned information in the extraction step. Here, we compare the effect of using other
365   difficulty-scoring functions to evaluate the difficulty of data. (1) prediction loss of a pre-trained
366
      ResNet. (2) uncertainty score (Coleman et al., 2019). (3) EL2N (Paul et al., 2021). As can be
      observed in Table 4b, EL2N performs the best across various IPCs; thus, we use it to measure how
367
      hard each data point is as default in our method. Note that this can also be replaced with a more
368
      advanced data selection algorithm.

421
422   In this section, we analyze why shallow-layer parameters should be masked out from the perspective
423   of trajectory matching. In Figure 5, we present the changes in trajectory matching loss across
424   different layers as the distillation progresses. Compared to the deep-layer parameters of the agent
425   model, a substantial number of shallow-layer parameters exhibit low loss values that fluctuate during
426   the matching. By contrast, losses of the deep layers are much higher but consistently decrease
427   as distillation continues. This suggests that matching shallow layers primarily conveys low-level
428
      information that is readily captured by the synthetic data and quickly saturated. Thus, the excessive
      addition of such low-level information produces noise, reducing the quality of distilled datasets.
429

490   6   R ELATED W ORK
491
492
      Introduced by Wang et al. (2020), dataset distillation aims to synthesize a compact set of data that
      allows models to achieve similar test performances compared with the original dataset. Since then, a
493
      number of studies have explored various approaches. Most of the popular methods can be divided
494
      into three types: matching-based, generative-model-based, and knowledge-distillation-based.
495
496   Matching-based methods. These methods first use agent models to extract information from the
497   target dataset by recording a specific metric (Du et al., 2023; Lee et al., 2022; Shin et al., 2023; Liu
498
      et al., 2023). Representative works that design different metrics include DC (Zhao et al., 2020) that
      matches gradients, DM (Zhao & Bilen, 2021a) that matches distributions, and MTT (Cazenavette
499
      et al., 2022) that matches training trajectories. Then, the distilled dataset is optimized by minimizing
500
      the matched distance between the metric computed on synthetic data and the record one from the
501   previous step. Following this workflow, many works improved the efficacy of the distilled dataset.
502
503   For example, CAFE (Wang et al., 2022) preserves the real feature distribution and the discriminative
504
      power of the synthetic data and achieves prominent generalization ability across various architectures.
      DATM (Guo et al., 2023) proposes to match early trajectories for small IPCs and late trajectories
505
      for large IPCs, achieving SOTA performances on several benchmarks. BLiP (Xu et al., 2023)
506
      discovers the issue of data redundancy in the previous distillation framework and propose to prune
507   the real dataset before distillation. PDD (Chen et al., 2023) identifies the change of learned pattern
508   complexity at different training stages and proposes a multi-stage distillation process where each
509   synthetic subset is conditioned on the previous ones to alleviate the above challenge. Moreover,
510   new metrics such as spatial attention maps (Sajedi et al., 2023; Khaki et al., 2024) have also been
511   introduced and achieved promising performance in distilling large-scale datasets. Despite these
512   advancements, matching-based methods often overlook the misalignment in information extraction
513   and information embedding, restricting their performances to be further improved. Generative-
514   model based methods. GANs (Goodfellow et al., 2014; Karras et al., 2018; 2019; Wang et al.,
515
      2023) and diffusion models (Rombach et al., 2021; Moser et al., 2024; Gu et al., 2023) can also
      be used to synthesize high quality datasets. DiM (Wang et al., 2023) uses deep generative models
516
      to store information of the target dataset. GLaD (Cazenavette et al., 2023) transfers synthetic data
517
      optimization from the pixel space to the latent space by employing deep generative priors. It enhances
518   the generalizability of previous methods.
519
520   Knowledge-distillation-based methods. Different from previous dataset distillation approaches,
521
      methods following this track apply knowledge distillation during the evaluation of synthetic data.
      SRe2 L (Yin et al., 2023) introduces a "squeeze, recover, relabel" procedure that decouples previous
522
      bi-level optimization and achieves success on high-resolution settings with lower computational
523
      costs. RDED (Sun et al., 2023) proposes a computationally efficient DD method that doesn’t require
524   synthetic image optimization by extracting and rearranging key image patches.
525
526
527   7   C ONCLUSION
528
529
      In this work, we find a limitation of existing Dataset Distillation methods in that they will introduce
      misaligned information to the distilled datasets. To alleviate this, we propose PAD, which incorporates
530
      two modules to filter out misaligned information. For information extraction, PAD prunes the target
531
      dataset based on sample difficulty for different IPCs so that only information with aligned difficulty
532   is extracted by the agent model. For information embedding, PAD discards part of shallow-layer
533   parameters to avoid injecting low-level basic information into the synthetic data. PAD achieves SOTA
534   performance on various benchmarks. Moreover, we show PAD can also be applied to methods based
535   on matching gradients and distributions, bringing improvements across various IPC settings.
536
537   Limitations Our alignment strategy could also be applied to methods based on matching gradients
538   and distributions. However, due to the limitation of computing resources, for methods based on
539   matching distributions and gradients, we have only validated our method’s effectiveness on DM (Zhao
      & Bilen, 2021a) and DC (Zhao et al., 2020) (see Table 6 and Table 7).

      A.1      F ILTERING M ISALIGNED I NFORMATION IN DC AND DM
724
      Although PAD is implemented based on trajectory matching methods, we also test our proposed
725   data alignment and parameter alignment on gradient matching and distribution matching. The
726   performances of enhanced DC and DM with each of the two modules are reported in Table 6 and
727   Tabl 7, respectively. We provide details of how we integrate these two modules into gradient matching
728   and distribution matching in the following sections.
729
      Gradient Matching We use the official implementation1 of DC (Zhao et al., 2020). In the Information
730
      Extraction step, DC uses an agent model to calculate the gradients after being trained on the target
731   dataset. We employ filter misaligned information in this step as follows: When IPC is small, a certain
732   ratio of hard samples is removed from the target dataset so that the recorded gradients only contain
733   simple information. Conversely, when IPC becomes large, we remove easy samples instead.
734
      In the Information Embedding step, DC optimizes the synthetic data by back-propagating on the
735
      gradient matching loss. The loss is computed by summing the differences in gradients between
736
      each pair of model parameters. Thus, we apply parameter selection by discarding a certain ratio of
737   parameters in the shallow layers.
738
739   Distribution Matching We use the official implementation of DM (Zhao & Bilen, 2021a), which can
740
      be accessed via the same link as DC. In the Information Extraction step, DM uses an agent model
      to generate embeddings of input images from the target dataset. Similarly, filtering information
741
      extraction is applied by removing hard samples for small IPCs and easy samples for large IPCs.
742
743   In the Information Embedding step, since DM only uses the output of the last layer to match
744   distributions, we modify the implementation of the network such that outputs of each layer in the
745   model are returned by the forward function. Then, we perform parameter selection following the
746
      same practice as before.

780   A.3    C OMPARISON WITH R ECENT A DVANCES
781
782   Although PAD is designed to filter out misaligned information in matching-based methods, we are
783   aware of the recent advancements in the DD field. Therefore, we compare two more recent methods,
784   RDED (Sun et al., 2023) and SPEED (Wei et al., 2023).
785
786   RDED is a non-optimization-based method that selects important image tokens and reconstructs
787   them back to images. During evaluation, knowledge distillation is applied by minimizing the
788
      Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence between the student model’s output and the teacher model’s output
      on the same batch of synthetic data. To compare with such a method, we adopt its knowledge
789
      distillation strategy during evaluation to ensure a fair comparison. Other experimental settings of
790
      PAD are the same. As shown in Table 9, PAD demonstrates superior performances in most of the
791   settings, especially when the compressing ratio is higher (larger IPCs). On small IPCs, the knowledge
792   distillation leads to a drop in PAD’s pure DD performance (Table 1). This is because knowledge from
793   a well-trained teacher exceeds the capacity of small IPCs.
794
795   SPEED is a parameterization-based method that prioritizes proper parameterization of the synthetic
796   dataset. It proposes spatial-agnostic epitomic tokens and sparse coding matrices to reduce spatial
797   redundancy. In Table 10, we show that although SPEED excels at IPC1, it is difficult for the method
798   to scale to higher compressing ratios effectively. By contrast, PAD shows prominent scalability on
799   IPC10 and IPC50 with non-trivial performance improvements. In terms of computational efficiency,
800   both methods use trajectory-matching as the backbone, but SPEED requires more optimizations on
801   the feature-recurrent network.

      influence PAD’s performance within a proper range.

830   We use DATM (Guo et al., 2023) as the backbone TM algorithm, and our proposed PAD is built upon.
831   Thus, our configurations for distillation, evaluation, and network are consistent with DATM.
832   Distillation. We conduct the distillation process for 10,000 iterations to ensure full convergence of
833   the optimization. By default, ZCA whitening is applied in all the experiments.
834
      Evaluation. We train a randomly initialized network on the distilled dataset and evaluate its per-
835
      formance on the entire validation set of the original dataset. Following DATM (Guo et al., 2023),
836   the evaluation networks are trained for 1000 epochs to ensure full optimization convergence. For
837   fairness, the experimental results of previous distillation methods in both low and high IPC settings
838   are sourced from (Guo et al., 2023).
839
      Network. We employ a range of networks to assess the generalizability of our distilled datasets.
840
      For scaling ResNet, LeNet, and AlexNet to Tiny-ImageNet, we modify the stride of their initial
841
      convolutional layer from 1 to 2. In the case of VGG, we adjust the stride of its final max pooling
842   layer from 1 to 2. The MLP used in our evaluations features a single hidden layer with 128 units.
843
844   Hyper-parameters. Hyper-parameters of our experiments on CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100, and TinyIma-
845
      geNet are reported in Table 12. Hyper-parameters can be divided into three parts, including FIEX,
      FIEM, and trajectory matching (TM). For FIEX, the ratio of easy samples removed for all IPCs is
846
      10%. Soft labels are applied in all experiments, we set its momentum to 0.9.
847
848   Compute resources. Our experiments are run on 4 NVIDIA A100 GPUs, each with 80 GB of
849   memory. The amount of GPU memory needed is mainly determined by the batch size of synthetic
850   data and the number of steps that the agment model is trained on synthetic data. To reduce the GPU
851
      usage when IPC is large, one can apply TESLA (Cui et al., 2022) or simply reducing the synthetic
      steps N or the synthetic batch size. However, the decrement of hyper-parameters shown in Table 12
852
      could result in performance degradation.
