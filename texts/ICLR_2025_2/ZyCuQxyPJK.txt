009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Inference in large-scale Markov Random Fields (MRFs) is a critical yet challeng-
012            ing task, traditionally approached through approximate methods like belief prop-
013            agation and mean field, or exact methods such as the Toulbar2 solver. These
014            strategies often fail to strike an optimal balance between efficiency and solution
015            quality, particularly as the problem scale increases. This paper introduces N EU -
               RO L IFTING , a novel technique that leverages Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) to
016
               reparameterize decision variables in MRFs, facilitating the use of standard gradi-
017
               ent descent optimization. By extending traditional lifting techniques into a non-
018
               parametric neural network framework, N EURO L IFTING benefits from the smooth
019            loss landscape of neural networks, enabling efficient and parallelizable optimiza-
020            tion. Empirical results demonstrate that, on moderate scales, N EURO L IFTING per-
021            forms very close to the exact solver Toulbar2 in terms of solution quality, signif-
022            icantly surpassing existing approximate methods. Notably, on large-scale MRFs,
023            N EURO L IFTING delivers superior solution quality against all baselines, as well as
024            exhibiting linear computational complexity growth. This work presents a signifi-
025            cant advancement in MRF inference, offering a scalable and effective solution for
026            large-scale problems.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Markov Random Fields (MRFs) stand as a fundamental computational paradigm for modeling com-
031   plex dependencies among a large collection of variables, permeating a variety of domains such
032   as computer vision (Wang et al., 2013; Su et al., 2021), natural language processing (Almutiri &
033   Nadeem, 2022; Ammar et al., 2014; Lin et al., 2020), and network analysis (Wu et al., 2020; Yun-
034   fei Ma & Razavi, 2022). MRF’s capacity to encode intricate probabilistic interactions underscores
035   its widespread utility. However, unraveling the optimal configurations in high-dimensional settings
036   remains a formidable task owing to the inherent computational complexity involved.
037   Traditional inference methodologies for MRFs bifurcate into approximate and exact strategies, each
038   with its own set of advantages and limitations. Approximate inference techniques, such as belief
039   propagation (Pearl, 2022; Wainwright et al., 2005) and mean field (Saito et al., 2012; Zhang, 1993)
040   approximations, strive for computational efficiency but often at the expense of solution quality,
041   particularly as the scale of the problem escalates. Conversely, exact inference methods, epitomized
042   by the Toulbar2 solver (De Givry, 2023; Hurley et al., 2016), aspire to optimality but are frequently
043   hampered by exponential time complexities that render them infeasible for large-scale MRFs.
044   Despite significant advances, achieving a harmonious balance between efficiency and solution qual-
045   ity in large-scale MRF inference remains a largely unmet challenge. This paper addresses this
046   pivotal issue through the introduction of “N EURO L IFTING” – a neural-network-driven paradigm
047   that extends traditional lifting technique in the context of optimization (Albersmeyer & Diehl, 2010;
048   Balas & Perregaard, 2002; Bauermeister et al., 2022). N EURO L IFTING is a novel approach that
049   reimagines MRF inference by leveraging the potency of Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) alongside
050
      gradient-based optimization techniques.
051   The core innovation of N EURO L IFTING lies in the reparameterization of the decision variables
052   within MRFs utilizing a randomly initialized GNN. While some recent heuristics succeeded in uti-
053   lizing GNNs for solving combinatorial problems (Cappart et al., 2023; Schuetz et al., 2022), an
      effective adaptation to MRF inference remains opaque. Besides, they generally lack an in-depth

      understanding of how GNNs facilitate downstream computation. In this paper, we for the first time
055   bridge such practice to traditional lifting techniques, and further demonstrate that by harnessing the
056   continuous and smooth loss landscape intrinsic to neural networks, N EURO L IFTING simplifies the
057   optimization process for large-scale MRFs, enabling enhanced parallelization and performance on
058   GPU devices.
059
      Empirical evaluations substantiate the efficacy of N EURO L IFTING, showcasing its ability to deliver
060
      high-quality solutions across diverse MRF datasets. Notably, it outperforms all existing approxi-
061   mate inference strategies in terms of solution quality without sacrificing computational efficiency.
062   When juxtaposed with exact strategies, N EURO L IFTING demonstrates comparable solution fidelity
063   while markedly enhancing efficiency. For particularly large-scale MRF problems, encapsulating
064   instances with over 50,000 nodes, N EURO L IFTING exhibits a linear computational complexity in-
065   crease, paired with superior solution quality relative to exact methods.
066
      In summary, the contributions of this paper are threefold. 1) Methodical design: we present N EU -
067   RO L IFTING as an innovative and practical solution to the enduring challenge of efficient and high-
068   quality inference in large-scale MRFs; 2) Non-parametric lifting: we extend the concept of lifting
069   from traditional optimization practices into a modern neural network framework, thereby offering
070   a fresh lens through which to tackle large-scale inference problems; 3) Significant performance:
071   N EURO L IFTING achieved significant performance improvement over existing methods, showing
072   remarkable scalability and efficiency in real-world scenarios.

      An overview of N EURO L IFTING is in Figure 1, with an exemplary scenario involving an energy
127   function devoid of unary terms, yet comprising three clique terms. Initially, the clique-based repre-
128   sentation of this function (depicted in the leftmost shaded diagram) undergoes a transformation to

130   the absence of inherent node feature information in the original problem, we elevate the dimension-
131   ality of decision variables within this framework. This transformation facilitates a paradigm shift
132   from the identification of optimal state values to the learning of optimal parameters for encoding and
133   classification of these variables. Furthermore, we devised a novel approach to circumvent the ab-
134   sence of a traditional loss function, thereby extending the applicability of our framework to Markov

137   3.2   P REPOSSESSING
138
139
      We discuss several necessary preprocessing steps to adapt standard MRF to a GNN style.
140   Topology construction for GNNs. In an MRF instance, the high-order graph structure consists
141   of nodes and cliques, diverging from typical GNNs allowing only pairwise edges (2nd-order). To
142   facilitate the power of GNNs, we need to convert high-order graph into a pairwise one. By the very
143   definition of a clique, any two nodes that appear within the same clique are directly related. Thus,
144   for any two nodes i, j ∈ Ck in a clique Ck , we add a pairwise edge (i, j) to its GNN-oriented graph.
145
      An example can be observed in Figure 1. It is worth noting that an edge may appear in multiple
      cliques; however, we add each edge only once to the graph.
146
147   Initial feature for GNNs. As there is no initial features associated to MRF instances, we initialize
148   feature vectors to GNNs randomly with a predefined dimension d. Detailed information on how we
149   will handle these artificial features to ensure they effectively capture the underlying information of
150   the problem will be provided in Section 3.3.
151   Vectorizing the energy function. The transformed energy function E(X) will serve as the loss
152   function guiding the training of the neural networks. In Section 3.4, we will detail the transformation
153   process and discuss how to effectively utilize it. Note the values of these functions can be pre-
154   evaluated and repeatedly used during the training process. Therefore, we employ a look-up table
155   to memorize all function values with discrete inputs. For unary energies, we denote the vectorized
156   unary energy of variable xi as ϕ(xi ), where the n-th element corresponds to θi (xi = n). Similarly,
157
      we represent the clique energy for clique Ck using the tensor ψ({xl |∀xl ∈ Ck }). This tensor can
      be derived using the same conceptual framework; for instance, the element ψ(xi , xj , xk ) at position
158
      (0, 2, 4) corresponds to the value of θ{i,j,k} (xi = 0, xj = 2, xk = 4) .

188   is in Fig. 2. In this example, we consider the case where |X | = 5. We start with the unary energy
189   vector for xi denoted as ϕ(xi ) = {1, 1, 3}, which has three states. Before padding, the highest value

      element among all original energies or to a significantly larger value compared to the original values,
199
      this approach can dramatically alter the loss landscape. As a result, the model may converge to
200
      an infeasible point in the original problem, leading it to select padded states instead. A similar
201   issue arises when we mask the padded regions during loss calculation. This masking operation can
202   introduce significant interference in the optimization process, preventing the model from achieving
203   a high-quality feasible solution.

289   3.5   A NALYSIS AND D ISCUSSION
290
291
      Relation to lifting. In this innovative framework of using GNNs for inference on MRFs, a natural
      and sophisticated parallel emerges with the classical concept of lifting in optimization (Balas et al.,
292
      1993). By mapping each unary term of an MRF to a node within a GNN and translating clique
293
      terms into densely connected subgraphs, the traditional MRF energy minimization transforms into
294   optimizing a multi-layer GNN with extra dimensionality. This procedure aligns with the lifting tech-
295   nique where the problem space is expanded to facilitate more efficient computation. Akin to the prin-
296   ciple of standard lifting to ease optimization, the GNN-based reparameterization can leverage the
297   gradient descent optimization paradigm inherent in the smooth neural network landscape (Dauphin
298   et al., 2014; Choromanska et al., 2015), ensuring efficient computation and convergence. Therefore,
299   while offering an enhanced approach to inference, the GNN reparameterization mirrors the core
300   principles of lifting by transforming and extending the solution space into a computation-friendly
301   one to achieve computational efficacy and scalability. More empirical evidence is in Sec. 4.4.
302   Complexity analysis. The primary computations in this model arise from both the loss calculation
303   and the operations within the GNN. For the loss function, let cmax denote the maximum clique
304   size. The time complexity for the loss calculation is given by O(|V||X | + cmax |C||X |). For the
305   GNN component, let Nv denote the average number of neighbors per node in the graph. The time
306   complexity for neighbor aggregation in each layer is O(Nv |V|) , and merging the results for all nodes
307   requires O(|V|d) where d is the feature dimension. Thus, for a K-layer GraphSAGE model with
308
      the custom loss function, the overall time complexity can be expressed as O(|X |(|V| + cmax |C|) +
      K|V|(Nv + d)). This analysis highlights the efficiency of the framework in managing large-scale
309
      graphs by leveraging neighborhood sampling and aggregation techniques. The derived complexity
310
      indicates that the model scales linearly with respect to the number of nodes, the number of layers,
311   and the dimensionality of the feature vectors, making it well-suited for large-scale instances.

      A    R ELATED WORK
757
758
759
760
761
762
      MRF and Inference. In Markov Random Fields (MRFs), the energy function is associated with a
763
      graph-structured probability distribution. A key inference challenge is determining the maximum
764   a posteriori (MAP) configuration. Although minimizing the energy function is NP-hard, advances
765   in inference techniques have enhanced model capabilities. For cycle-free graphs, the MAP problem
766   can be effectively addressed using a variant of the min-sum algorithm (Clifford & Hammersley,
767   1971; Besag, 1974; Kumar et al., 2005) , which extends the Viterbi algorithm (Yedidia et al., 2003)
768   to arbitrary cycle-free structures. In graphs with cycles, graph cut methods (Komodakis et al., 2007;
769   Roy & Cox, 1998; Boykov et al., 1998; Ishikawa & Geiger, 1998; Szummer et al., 2008) utilize
770   min-cut/max-flow strategies to efficiently minimize energy, although they require MRFs to be graph-
771   representable and are unsuitable for multi-labeled MRFs. Two graph-cut-based strategies (Ishikawa,
772
      2003; Schlesinger & FLACH, 2006) have been developed: the label-reduction method, for specific
      MRFs requiring binary conversion, and the move-making method, influenced by the size of node
773
      state combinations.
774
775   The belief propagation (BP) algorithm (Pearl, 1982; 1988) , introduced by Pearl in 1982, is a widely
776   used iterative inference method for Bayesian networks, functioning through message passing. How-
777   ever, BP struggles with loops, leading to loopy belief propagation (LBP) (Weiss & Freeman, 2001;
778
      Felzenszwalb & Huttenlocher, 2004; Frey & Mackay, 2002) , which iterates message passing even in
      cyclic graphs. While LBP has shown effectiveness in vision tasks, it lacks guaranteed convergence.
779
      Recent advancements aim to enhance BP’s performance, such as adjusting message significance with
780
      discount factors (Grim & Felzenszwalb, 2023) and constructing hierarchical frameworks for large-
781   scale MRFs (Yan et al., 2023). The Junction Tree Algorithm (JTA) (Aji & McEliece, 2000) provides
782   exact inference for arbitrary graphs but is NP-hard, limiting its practicality. In pairwise MRFs, inte-
783   ger linear programming (ILP) formulations yield solutions through tree-reweighted message passing
784   (TRBP) (Wainwright et al., 2005) , which includes edge-based and tree-based schemes, though they
785   lack guaranteed convergence. The sequential TRW-S (Kolmogorov, 2006) scheme achieves weak
786   tree agreement, ensuring lower bounds stabilize, but requires substantial time for convergence.
787   Lifting in Optimization. Lifting techniques have garnered significant attention in the optimiza-
788   tion field, particularly in tackling combinatorial problems and enhancing the performance of var-
789   ious algorithms (Marchand et al., 2002). These techniques involve transforming a problem into a
790   higher-dimensional space, which facilitates more effective representation and solution strategies.
791   They are applied to both mixed 0-1 integer programming problems (Balas et al., 1993) and more
792   general mixed-integer programming (MIP) problems in conjunction with primal cutting-plane algo-
793   rithms (Dey & Richard, 2008). Additionally, lifting techniques have been integrated with variable
794   upper bound constraints in applications such as the Knapsack problem (Shebalov et al., 2015). The
795
      use of lifting methods has also extended into robust optimization scenarios (Georghiou et al., 2020;
      Bertsimas et al., 2019). Furthermore, combining lifting techniques with Newton’s method has shown
796
      promise in addressing nonlinear optimization problems (NLPs) (Albersmeyer & Diehl, 2010).
797
798   Unsupervised GNNs for Combinatorial Optimization. Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) have
799   been proved to be powerful in optimization (Yu et al., 2019; Ying et al., 2024) and recent advance-
800   ments in unsupervised GNNs have demonstrated their effectiveness in tackling combinatorial op-
801
      timization problems. By leveraging the structural properties of graph data, unsupervised GNNs

950   using MRF to model PCI problems, each random variable represent the identity state of the given
951   node and the interference between devices would be captured by the pairwise energy functions. Next
952   we will introduce how to transform the PCI problem from MIP form to MRF form.
953
      In the original MIP formulation of the PCI problems, three types of constraints are defined. By
954   combining Equation 8a and Equation 8b, we establish that each device must select exactly one state
955   at any given time. Furthermore, the constraint in Equation 8c indicates that interference occurs
956   between two devices only when they select specific states. The overall impact on the system is
957   governed by the value of Lij and its corresponding coefficient. Given that interference is always
958   present, the objective is to minimize its extent.
959
      To transform these problems into an MRF framework, we utilize Equation 8b to represent the nodes,
960   where each instance of Equation 8a corresponds to the discrete states of a specific node. The con-
961   straints set forth in Equation 8a and Equation 8b ensure that only one state can be selected at any
962   given time, thus satisfying those conditions automatically. By processing Equation 8c, we can iden-
963   tify the edges and their associated energies. Ifzni p and znj p appear in the same constraint from
964   Equation 8c, we can formulate an edge (i, j). By selecting different values for zni p and znj p , we
965   can determine the minimum value of Lij that maintains the validity of the constraint.
966   The product of Lij and aij represents the energy associated with the edge (i, j) under the combina-
967   tion of the respective states. Once the states of all nodes are fixed, the values of the edge costs also
968   become fixed. This leads to the conclusion that the objective function is the summation of the ener-
969   gies across all edges. Since the PCI problems do not include unary terms, we can omit them during
970   the transformation process. This establishes a clear pathway for converting the MIP formulation
971   into an MRF representation, allowing us to leverage MRF methods for solving the PCI problems
      effectively.
