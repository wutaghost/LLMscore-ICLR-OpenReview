011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Despite the growing interest in self-supervised speech models, recent research
014            has primarily focused on modifying upstream model architectures and pretraining
015
               techniques, with less attention given to how features from self-supervised models
               are used. In this paper, we explore the use of variational inference to enhance
016
               the performance of self-supervised audio models in downstream tasks. We hy-
017
               pothesize that adaptively reweighting the outputs of the model layers is crucial to
018            improving performance on these tasks. We extensively evaluate our method along-
019            side widely used baselines, demonstrating that understanding sample-specific in-
020            formation is essential for improved performance on several tasks. Our proposed
021            method surpasses existing approaches and generalizes to various speech tasks, in-
022            cluding automatic speech recognition, speaker verification, and emotion recogni-
023            tion. Finally, we analyze our method to provide deeper insight into the importance
024            of our modifications.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      Self-supervised learning (SSL) approaches for speech have emerged to address the need for a cost-
029
      effective way to achieve high performance with minimal annotated data across a wide range of
030   downstream tasks. Modern SSL audio models are trained on massive unlabeled audio corpora and
031   show high performance while fine-tuning for downstream tasks with as little as 1 hour of labeled
032   audio (Chen et al., 2022). Moreover, SSL models can serve as neural feature extractors capable of
033   replacing traditional audio representations such as raw waveforms or spectrograms, and consistently
034   outperform models trained with these classical features (Boigne et al., 2020; Peng et al., 2022).
035
      The most widely used SSL models today include wav2vec 2.0 (Baevski et al., 2020), HuBERT (Hsu
036   et al., 2021), WavLM (Chen et al., 2022), and Data2Vec (Baevski et al., 2022). Architecturally,
037   these models consist of a CNN feature extractor, which is typically kept frozen during fine-tuning
038   on downstream tasks Stafylakis et al. (2022), and a Transformer’s (Vaswani et al., 2017) encoder,
039   which can either be kept frozen or trained during fine-tuning (wen Yang et al., 2021a). There are
040   two most common methods for using the encoders’ outputs to make predictions for downstream
041   tasks: taking the output from the last layer or learning weights for all Transformer layers and using
042   a weighted output (wen Yang et al., 2021a).
043   The motivation behind collapsing self-supervised model output into a single feature using learned
044   weights is based on the observation that different layers of SSL models contain varied information,
045   which may be required depending on the task. One way to conduct layer importance analysis is
046   by comparing the absolute values of the learned weight parameters. Using this method, Chen et al.
047   (2022) demonstrated that the initial layers obtain higher weights than the later layers after training on
048   speaker-dependent tasks, indicating that they contain information essential for those tasks. The last
049   layers of SSL models contain more semantic and phonetic information (Pasad et al., 2023), which is
050
      typically required for Automatic Speech Recognition (ASR) tasks, as demonstrated in Pasad et al.
      (2023) through canonical correlation analysis.
051
052   However, while using a weighted sum with weights learned jointly with the downstream model and
053   analyzing them for task-specific layer importance of the SSL layers is a widely used and straight-
      forward approach, it has several shortcomings. Firstly, this approach does not account for sample


                                                         1

054
      variation and its possible dependence on information from different SSL layers. Secondly, Yang
055   et al. (2024) showed that the performance of individual layers of SSL models does not correlate
056   with the learned weights.
057
058
      In this paper, we address these drawbacks and propose a layer aggregation method for SSL models
      based on variational inference. The proposed method, called VARAN, predicts the weights for each
059
      individual layer based on the particular sample. We demonstrate that the proposed method gener-
060
      alizes well across various tasks and SSL model architectures. We compare the proposed method
061   to state-of-the-art layer-aggregating methods, including weighted sum and layerwise attention pool-
062   ing, and show that our method outperforms existing layer-aggregation approaches on most tasks.
063   Additionally, we conduct an analysis of the proposed method.
064
      Overall, our contributions can be described as follows:
065
066         1. We propose an algorithm to fine-tune self-supervised speech models using variational in-
067            ference.
068
            2. We demonstrate that the proposed method generalizes well across various downstream
069
               tasks and upstream backbones.
070
071
            3. Based on the obtained results, we provide a layer-wise analysis and an ablation study of the
               proposed method’s robustness to its hyperparameters across various tasks.

079   2.1   S ELF -S UPERVISED S PEECH M ODELS N OTATION
080
081   Usually, a self-supervised speech model (upstream encoder E) takes a raw audio signal x ∈ Rn ,
082

083   h ∈ R[N ×T ×d] , where N , T , and d are the number of layers in the Transformer, the encoded
084

086   (PR) or automatic speech recognition (ASR) tasks, the input hidden vector needs to be h ∈ R[T,d] .
087   For classification tasks, the input hidden vector needs to be h ∈ Rd , obtained by applying mean or
088   attention pooling across the sequence length.
089   To accumulate the results across layers, we can either take the input of the last Transformer layer

091   weights parameter.

136   3     M ETHOD
137
138   In this section, we introduce the proposed method for aggregating the outputs of self-supervised
139   speech models and explain how it can be used for training and inferring various downstream tasks.
140   We begin with the overall intuition behind using Variational Inference for layer aggregation, fol-
141   lowed by a detailed explanation of the choice of prior distribution and the prediction of the posterior
142   distribution.
143
144   3.1   F ORMULATING THE D OWNSTREAM TASK IN T ERMS OF VARIATIONAL I NFERENCE
145
146
      To view any downstream task for SSL models from the perspective of variational inference, we
      introduce the intuition behind the approach. We assume that for any downstream task, our goal is
147
      to predict the target y using input data x. One could use a simple linear classifier on top of the last
148
      hidden layer, but we found this setup suboptimal, as we need a different layer to achieve the best
149   performance for different tasks and even different samples. Choosing the right layer or combination
150   of layers is challenging, as the training data does not have the optimal layer distribution. However,
151   we can learn it with the help of variational inference. In our setup, we have a classifier on top of each
152   backbone’s layer pθ (y | x, l = i) and layer classifier qθ (l|x), where l is a discrete random variable
153   and i is a layer index. To maximize p(y|x), we can use the following objective:
154                 L(θ) = −Eqθ (l|x) log pθ (y | x, l) + KL (qθ (l | x)∥p(l)) ≥ − log p(y | x),
155
      where p(l) is a prior distribution on layer index.
156
157   Inspired by β-VAE (Higgins et al., 2017), we use a β scaling factor as the regularization term. With
158   this modification, our objective can be written as:
159
160                        L(θ) = −Eqθ (l|x) log pθ (y | x, l) + β KL (qθ (l | x)∥p(l)) .                  (1)

      3.2   PARAMETRIZATION OF THE D ISTRIBUTIONS
163
164   An overview of the proposed method can be found in Figure 1.
165
166
      We start with the classifiers pϕ (y | x, l = i). First, for tasks that require temporal dimension
      reduction, we obtain the pooled hidden state h via average pooling on the temporal dimension, where
167
      h = E(x). The pooled hidden state is passed into a projector with ReLU non-linearity and then to
168
      a linear classifier, i.e., for the WavLM (Chen et al., 2022) Large model, this results in 24 probability
169   distributions on y. To accumulate these distributions, we use a layer distribution predictor qθ (l | x).
170   The layer distribution predictor is a multi-head self-attention mechanism (MHSA).
171
172
      The input to the MHSA consists of hidden states from the Transformer Encoder blocks. Formally,
173   this is a list with a size equal to the number of layers, L , where each element is a tensor l ∈ Rb×s×h ,
174   with dimensions corresponding to batch size, sequence length, and hidden size. We then apply mean
175   pooling along the sequence length and concatenate the results into a single tensor, resulting in a
176   tensor lpooled ∈ Rb×L×h This tensor serves as input to the standard MHSA mechanism, functioning
177   as a feature mixer between layers. Finally, we apply a linear layer to the last dimension to obtain a
178   tensor o ∈ Rb×L×1 . We then perform a squeeze operation to remove the singleton dimension and
179   apply softmax function along the number of layers dimension to produce an individual posterior
180   distribution for each sample in the batch, p ∈ Rb×L
181
      Note that we are minimizing negative log-likelihood, and it is crucial to marginalize log-probabilities
182   log pϕ (y | x, l) over layer distribution, instead of marginalizing pϕ (y | x, l) and then taking log(·)
183   over it.

      3.3   P RIOR D ISTRIBUTION C HOICE
187
      Selecting a prior distribution is crucial for achieving better performance, as discussed in Section 5.1.
188
      When selecting the prior, we can leverage domain-specific knowledge about the task. Previous work
189   (Yang et al., 2024; Pasad et al., 2023) has shown that different features correspond to different lay-
190   ers: low-level audio features tend to be found in the early layers, while high-level features, such as
191   phonemes, appear in the later layers. Thus, for tasks that are highly dependent on semantic infor-
192   mation, like ASR or PR, we can use a prior distribution that assigns more weight to the later layers,
193   such as a reversed geometric distribution. However, for most tasks, we do not have prior knowl-
194   edge, and the layer importance for a specific task may vary depending on the choice of the backbone
195   model. Therefore, in our setup, we tend to select the best prior distribution using hyperparameter
196   search. We found that the scaled non-central χ2 distribution can be easily adapted to most tasks,
197
      as its shape aligns with our intuition of the task’s prior distribution. We vary the non-centrality pa-
      rameter λ to shift the distribution toward different layers. See Appendix C for more details on the
198
      parametrization.

482
483   6.1   S PEECH S ELF -S UPERVISED M ODELS F INE -T UNING T ECHNIQUES
484
485   To fine-tune an SSL model for a downstream task, one approach is to keep the model parameters
      frozen and fine-tune only the downstream architecture, commonly used for benchmarking different


                                                        9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      upstream models (wen Yang et al., 2021a). While efficient, it doesn’t always yield the best perfor-
487   mance. To improve performance, task-specific downstream architectures like TDNN and ECAPA-
488   TDNN have been introduced for speaker verification (SV) (Fan et al., 2021). These architectures
489   perform well on raw mel-spectrograms (Desplanques et al., 2020) and even better when combined
490   with SSL models (Novoselov et al., 2022).
491
      Sometimes, fine-tuning only the downstream model is not enough for state-of-the-art performance.
492
      For example, fine-tuning Self-Supervised Speech Models for automatic speech recognition (ASR)
493   often involves unfreezing the Transformer Encoder (Chen et al., 2022; wen Yang et al., 2021a). Un-
494   freezing the backbone model’s weights also helps achieve strong performance on many downstream
495   tasks using lightweight architectures (Li et al., 2022; Manila et al., 2024).
496
      In this work, we explore enhanced techniques to fine-tune pretrained speech self-supervised models
497
      by reweighting Transformer Encoder outputs. The closest related work is by Peng et al. (2022), who
498
      proposed a lightweight architecture to enhance the performance of speech self-supervised models
499   on a speaker verification task using a layer aggregation method (see Section 5.2).
500
501
502   6.2   L AYER A NALYSIS OF S PEECH S ELF -S UPERVISED M ODELS
503
      Chen et al. (2022) found that layers at the beginning and middle of the WavLM model had higher
504
      magnitudes of learned weights after fine-tuning on speaker-dependent tasks, indicating their impor-
505
      tance for speaker verification (SV) and speaker identification (SI). Pasad et al. (2023) used CCA
506   analysis to show that models trained to predict discrete units learn phonetic and word information in
507   intermediate layers, with this information concentrated in higher layers. They also demonstrated that
508   layer-wise phone and word content correlate well with downstream task performance for phoneme
509   recognition (PR) and automatic speech recognition (ASR). Yang et al. (2024) showed that weights
510   learned via weighted sum are not correlated with layers’ performance on tasks. Both Yang et al.
511   (2024) and Pasad et al. (2023) found that for some tasks and SSL models, the best individual layer
512   outperforms the learned weighted sum. However, this improvement in single-layer benchmarking
513   is specific to individual SSL models and is not consistent across all models, except for the VC task
514
      (Pasad et al., 2023).
515   So far, there have been few attempts to utilize the observed knowledge about layer-dependent per-
516   formance. One such attempt is MHFA (Peng et al., 2022) propped on the SV task, where two
517   sets of weights were introduced to separately encode speaker-discriminative information and pho-
518   netic units. See the described method in more detail in Section 2.2. Despite these efforts, using a
519   weighted sum for SSL model training remains the most efficient and widely used approach across
520
      all tasks and models.
521
522   6.3   VARIATIONAL I NFERENCE AND E ARLY E XIT
523
524   From one perspective, our method could be seen as VAE (Kingma & Welling, 2014) with layer index
525   l as a latent variable. Therefore, we can successfully adapt some techniques for it, like Higgins et al.
526   (2017). Banino et al. (2021) used a similar approach, but for adaptive computational time (Graves,
527
      2016) inference. This method was also adapted for early exiting during inference (Balagansky &
      Gavrilov, 2022). While reducing inference time of the models is a promising direction, in our work,
528
      we concentrated solely on performance improvement.
529
530
531   7     C ONCLUSION
532
533
      In our work, we present a novel method called VARAN for utilizing self-supervised speech models
534   on downstream tasks. Our method is based on variational inference for choosing the weights of the
535   predictions from all backbone layers in a per-sample manner. Through extensive experiments, we
536   show that our method consistently outperforms other methods for tasks where we need to process
537   both low-level features (i.e., speaker verification) and high-level features (i.e., speech or emotion
538   recognition). We also conduct an in-depth analysis of the proposed methods and provide valuable
539   insights. Our method does not depend on its downstream task and can be used for any of the current
      speech tasks.
