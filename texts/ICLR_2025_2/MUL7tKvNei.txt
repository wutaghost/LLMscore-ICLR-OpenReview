011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Learning the evolutionary dynamics of Partial Differential Equations (PDEs) is
014            critical in understanding dynamic systems, yet current methods insufficiently learn
015            their representations. This is largely due to the multi-scale nature of the solution,
016            where certain regions exhibit rapid oscillations while others evolve more slowly.
017            This paper introduces a framework of multi-scale and multi-expert (M2 M) neural
018            operators designed to simulate and learn PDEs efficiently. We employ a divide-
019            and-conquer strategy to train a multi-expert gated network for the dynamic router
020            policy. Our method incorporates a controllable prior gating mechanism that de-
021
               termines the selection rights of experts, enhancing the model’s efficiency. To op-
               timize the learning process, we have implemented a PI (Proportional, Integral)
022
               control strategy to adjust the allocation rules precisely. This universal controllable
023
               approach allows the model to achieve greater accuracy. We test our approach on
024            benchmark 2D Navier-Stokes equations and provide a custom multi-scale dataset.
025            M2 M can achieve higher simulation accuracy and offer improved interpretability
026            compared to baseline methods.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Many challenges require modeling the physical world, which operates under established physical
032   laws (Karniadakis et al., 2021; Brunton and Kutz, 2024). For example, the Navier-Stokes equations
033   form the theoretical foundation of fluid mechanics and have widespread applications in aviation,
034   shipbuilding, and oceanography (Vinuesa and Brunton, 2022). Various numerical approaches ex-
035   ist to tackle these equations. These include discretization methods such as finite difference (Go-
036   dunov and Bohachevsky, 1959), finite volume (Eymard et al., 2000), finite element (Rao, 2010),
037   and spectral methods (Shen et al., 2011). Although classical physical solvers based on first prin-
038   ciples can achieve high accuracy, they must recalculate when faced with new problems, failing to
039
      generalize and resulting in inefficient solutions. Artificial intelligence-based surrogate models ef-
      fectively address these issues by providing more adaptable and efficient solutions. Understanding
040
      and learning the data that embodies these physical laws is crucial for controlling and optimizing
041
      real-world applications (Lv et al., 2022; Kim and Boukouvala, 2020; Wang et al., 2024a). Mastery
042   of such data-driven insights enables more precise predictions, enhanced system performance, and
043   significant advancements in how we interact with and manipulate the application in the fields of
044   engineering and science (Noé et al., 2020). The growing interest in efficient PDE solvers and the
045   success of deep learning models in various fields has sparked significant attention, such as neural
046   operator methods (Li et al., 2020; Kovachki et al., 2021; Bonev et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024a). Um
047   et al. (2020) proposed a spatial resolution solver to reduce the computation and accelerate physical
048   simulations. Wu et al. (2022); Sanchez-Gonzalez et al. (2020) proposed reducing the dimensions of
049   latent space to map the solution in the surrogate models.
050   Exploring how to integrate and fully leverage performance across different scales while controlling
051   complex learning dynamics is a promising area of research. Based on the frequency principle (Xu
052   et al., 2019), our primary motivation is to enable smaller or more general models to learn low-
053   frequency dynamic data, while delegating high-frequency data to more capable models. The router
      (distribution policy) regulates this allocation, which sets our approach apart from other methods.

075   In this work, we introduce the multi-scale and multi-expert (M2 M) neural operators as an effective
076   surrogate model to learn the dynamics of PDEs and optimize the appropriate allocation law for the
077   different scales with different expert models. Our critical insight lies in leveraging the divide-and-
078   conquer approach among models to learn the capabilities across different scales quickly. Divide and
079   conquer is a fundamental algorithmic technique for solving complex problems by breaking them
080   down into simpler, more manageable sub-problems (Smith, 1987; Huang et al., 2017; Ganaie et al.,
081   2022; Emirov et al., 2024). This approach works on the principle that a large problem can often
082   be divided into two or more smaller problems of the same or similar type. Each of these smaller
083
      problems is then solved independently. Once solutions are obtained for all the sub-problems, they
      are combined to form a solution to the original, more extensive problem. In addition, the model
084
      is designed to master the distribution of the most effective data while minimizing computational
085
      resources. To fairly evaluate the effectiveness of our framework, we standardized the internal models
086   to Fourier Neural Operator (FNO) models with varying numbers of modalities. This strategy enables
087   the model to adaptively determine the best local spatial resolution to evolve the system. The M2 M
088   is trained in an alternating manner, iterating between training the evolution model with supervised
089   loss and allocation policy net. Together, the controllable routing mechanism effectively integrates
090   prior knowledge with model capabilities. The implementation of PID control significantly aids in
091   optimizing the training of Multi-of-Experts (MoE).
092   Our main contributions are as follows:
093
094   1. We propose a controllable multi-expert and multi-scale operator model to embed multiple models
095
      based on specific priors. The multi-expert system embodies the divide-and-conquer philosophy,
      while the multi-scale approach enables efficient learning.
096
097   2. By bridging the control theory-PID, this unified theory demonstrates its strong generalizability.
098   It is a versatile and scalable method for the machine learning and science simulation community.
099   3. We validate the aforementioned method using the standard Navier-Stokes equations and a custom
100   multi-scale dataset, ensuring a balance between speed and accuracy.

128   Deep Learning-based Surrogate Methods. There are two fundamental approaches:
129
130      • Autoregressive Model Approach: The model learns the mapping function fθ from a given ut to
131        the next ut+1 , acquiring discrete representations. This method involves learning the model to
132        predict subsequent time steps based on previous inputs. Such frameworks include CNN-based
133
           models (Wang et al., 2020b; Kemeth et al., 2022), GNN-based models (Pfaff et al., 2020; Li
           et al., 2024), and transformer-based models (Cao, 2021; Geneva and Zabaras, 2022; Takamoto
134
           et al., 2023).
135
136      • Neural Operator Approach: Unlike autoregressive models, the neural operator method (Lu
137        et al., 2021) allows the model to map through multiple time steps, learning infinite-dimensional
138        representations. This approach enables the model to handle more complex temporal dynam-
139        ics by learning continuous representations. Apart from vanilla FNO, there are other operator
140        learning methods such as U-FNO (U-Net Fourier Neural Operator, (Wen et al., 2022)), UNO
141
           (U-shaped neural operators, (Azizzadenesheli et al., 2024)), WNO (Wavelet Neural Operator,
           (Navaneeth et al., 2024)), and KNO (Koopman Neural Operator, (Xiong et al., 2024)).
142
143
      In addition to these two conventional methods, researchers have developed several hybrid ap-
144
      proaches that combine elements of both (Watters et al., 2017; Zhou et al., 2020; Keith et al., 2021;
145   Hao et al., 2023; Kovachki et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024b). For multi-scale PDEs problems, Liu
146   et al. (2020) developed multi-scale deep neural networks, using the idea of radial scaling in the
147   frequency domain and activation functions with compact support. Hu et al. (2023) propose the
148   augmented physics-informed neural network (APINN), which adopts soft and trainable domain de-
149   composition and flexible parameter sharing to further improve the extended PINN further. Xu et al.
150   (2019) firstly find the deep neural network that fits the target functions from low to high frequen-
151   cies. Liu et al. (2024b) demonstrate that for multi-scale PDEs form, the spectral bias towards low-
152   frequency components presents a significant challenge for existing neural operators. Rahman et al.
153
      (2024) study the cross-domain attention learning method for multi-physic PDEs by the attention
      mechanism. However, the aforementioned methods do not efficiently leverage frequency character-
154
      istics, and they lack a controllable mechanism for adjusting the learning process of partial differential
155
      equations. Compared with (Du et al., 2023; Chalapathi et al., 2024), M2 M directly optimizes for
156   the PDEs objective, first using a universal controlled method and multi-scale to learn the policy of
157   allocating experts to achieve a better accuracy vs. computation trade-off. In addition to simplify-
158   ing the computation of attention, the MoE mechanism (Jacobs et al., 1991) has been incorporated
159   into transformer architectures (Fedus et al., 2022; Chowdhery et al., 2023) to lower computational
160   expenses while maintaining a large model capacity. The key distinction of our objective lies in its
161   emphasis on controllability and multi-scale considerations, both of which are crucial factors for all
      fundamental partial differential equation data.

      5    C ONCLUSION AND L IMITATION
475
476
477
      The proposed M2 M model jointly learns the evolution of the physical system and optimizes compu-
      tational assignment to most dynamic regions. In multi-scale and Naiver-Stokes datasets, we show
478
      that our PID method can controllably train the expert’s net and router, which clearly enhances long-
479
      term prediction error than strong baselines of deep learning-based surrogate models. The fitting
480   error has been demonstrated to converge based on control theory in the Appendix 6.5. Furthermore,
481   the PID-based M2 M could improve the convergence speed, showing that this intuitive baseline is
482   suboptimal. Finally, M2 M outperforms its ablation without multi-scale segmentation, showing the
483   divide-and-conquer strategy which can significantly reduce the prediction error. We hope M2 M can
484   provide valuable insight and methods for machine learning and physical simulation fields, especially
485   for applications requiring scalability and multi-physics models. The limitation of M2 M is shown in
      appendix 6.6.

665   6.1     C USTOM M ULTI - SCALE P OISSON EQUATION DATASET
666
      The custom multi-scale dataset is designed to simulate a complex scenario, where data in some
667
      regions change slowly while changing more rapidly in others. We define the task as follows: the
668   input is the solution ulow to a relatively low-frequency equation, while the output is the solution
669   uhigh to a corresponding high-frequency equation. To idealize this dataset, we adopted the form
670   of the classical Poisson equation and used the finite difference method to solve the problem. The
671   concise discrete form is [1, 128, 128] 7→ [1, 128, 128]. The time step is set to 1 and the spatial
672   domain is set to [128, 128].
673
674   6.1.1    F REQUENCY D ISTRIBUTION ON DIFFERENT REGIONS
675
      The source term for each region is a sinusoidal function with a systematically varying frequency.
676
      Specifically, the source term fij (x, y) is defined as follows for i, j = 1, 2, 3, 4:
677
678
                                f11 (x, y) = sin(π · (1 · µx)) sin(π · (1 · µy)),
679
680                             f12 (x, y) = sin(π · (2 · µx)) sin(π · (2 · µy)),
                                                                                                     (15)
681                             f21 (x, y) = sin(π · (3 · µx)) sin(π · (3 · µy)),
682                             f22 (x, y) = sin(π · (4 · µx)) sin(π · (4 · µy)).
683
684   The initial solution of PDEs will be decided by the dimensionless frequency µ and the other solution
685
      for the high frequency is 7 · µ. In this dataset, we sampled 1000 cases with different values of µ,
      which were drawn from a normal distribution N (1, 0.1) using Monte Carlo sampling. Out of the
686
      1000 samples, 700 are allocated for the training dataset, while the remaining 300 are reserved for
687
      the test dataset. To increase the complexity in the varying time PDEs, we assume that the solutions
688   include a two-step solution and that the ground truth (the second time-solution) is the high spatial
689   frequency to be predicted, 7 · µ of each low-frequency domain corresponding to the input domain.
690
691   6.1.2    S OLVER I MPLEMENTATION AND SETTING OF GRIDS
692
693   The Poisson equation is solved numerically using a finite-difference method on each block. The
694
      boundary conditions and the source term f (x, y) determine the solution u(x, y) within each block.
      The computational grid is set into a 128 × 128 grid and divided into 2 × 2 blocks, each of size
695
      64 × 64. After calculation, the boundary condition g(x, y) = 0 is assigned in each block boundary.

1057
1058   A PID controller regulates the control input u(t) by combining three terms: proportional (P ), inte-
1059
       gral (I), and derivative (D). The controller aims to minimize the tracking error e(t), defined as the
       difference between the desired reference signal r(t) and the system output y(t):
1060
1061                                            e(t) = r(t) − y(t)
1062
       The PID control law is given by:

1066   where Kp , Ki , and Kd are the proportional, integral, and derivative gains, respectively.

1071   the PID control effect.
