009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Understanding the inner workings of Transformers is crucial for achieving more
012            accurate and efficient predictions. In this work, we analyze the computation per-
013            formed by Transformers in the layers after the top-1 prediction has become fixed,
014            which has been previously referred to as the “saturation event”. We expand the
015            concept of saturation events for top-k tokens, demonstrating that similar saturation
016            events occur across language, vision, and speech models. We find that these satu-
017            ration events happen in order of the corresponding tokens’ ranking, i.e., the model
018
               first decides on the top ranking token, then the second highest ranking token, and
               so on. This phenomenon seems intrinsic to the Transformer architecture, occur-
019
               ring across different architectural variants (decoder-only, encoder-only, and to a
020
               lesser extent full-Transformer), and even in untrained Transformers. We propose
021            an underlying mechanism of task transition for this sequential saturation, where
022            task k corresponds to predicting the k-th most probable token, and the saturation
023            events are in fact discrete transitions between the tasks. In support of this we
024            show that it is possible to predict the current task from hidden layer embedding.
025            Furthermore, using an intervention method we demonstrate that we can cause the
026            model to switch from one task to the next. Finally, leveraging our findings, we in-
027            troduce a novel token-level early-exit strategy, which surpasses existing methods
028            in balancing performance and efficiency.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      In recent years, Transformer-based models (Vaswani et al., 2017) have achieved state-of-the-art
      performance in various tasks across multiple modalities, including text generation, image classifi-
034
      cation, and automatic speech recognition (Zhang et al., 2023; OpenAI et al., 2024). This has lead
035
      to a growing interest in model interpretability, which tries to explain the internal processes that give
036   rise to these remarkable capabilities. In the language domain, investigation into the way the model’s
037   predictions are constructed has led to the discovery of saturation events, where the model’s top-1
038   prediction is determined in an early layer and remains fixed in subsequent layers (Geva et al., 2022).
039
      In this work, we address the following question – what computation is the Transformer model per-
040
      forming after the saturation event? Taking inspiration from Frydenlund et al. (2022), we treat the
041
      model’s output as a ranking over the labels instead of a probability distribution. Using the logit
042   lens (Nostalgebraist, 2020), we project the hidden states of intermediate layers onto the vocabulary
043   space to extract ranking over tokens and analyze the changes over the layers. For the first time, we
044   show that in a decoder-only text Transformer (GPT2-XL; Radford et al., 2019) saturation events also
045   take place for the top ranking tokens beyond the top-1 (2nd, 3rd, 4th, etc.). Surprisingly, we find
046   that they happen in order of their ranking, i.e. the second-ranking token is determined only after the
047   first-ranking token, and so forth (see in Figure 1). We then generalize the results across different
048   modalities and Transformer variants, including pretrained Transformers for both vision (encoder-
049   only ViT-L/16; Dosovitskiy et al., 2020) and speech (encoder-decoder Whisper-large; Radford et al.,
050
      2023). Next, we demonstrate that sequential saturation seems intrinsic to the Transformer architec-
      ture, occurring even in an untrained randomly initialized model (GPT2-XL).
051
052   We propose that this phenomenon is due to a discrete task-transition mechanism, where each task i
053   corresponds to the model determining the i-th token in the final ranking, and the transition between
      one task and the next happens at the corresponding saturation layer. Furthermore, we find that the

      2     E XPERIMENTS
138
139   In this section, we formulate two experiments to understand what computation the Transformer is
140
      performing in the layers after the top-1 saturation event. To achieve this, we first extend the formal
      definition of top-1 saturation to account for arbitrary i-th ranking token saturation (Section 2.1).
141
      Then, in Section 2.2, we leverage this definition to develop a metric capturing the extent to which
142
      the top tokens are saturated in order. In Section 2.3, we describe a probing method measuring
143   whether it is possible to determine the rank of the token currently being considered by the model
144   solely from the intermediate representation of the layer, without any additional context.
145
146
147
      2.1   D EFINING S ATURATION L AYERS
148
      Definition 2.1 (1st Saturation Layer; Geva et al., 2022). The saturation event occurs at layer l (from
149
      here on referred to as the “1st saturation layer”) for index i in the input if the top-1 prediction of the
150   model remains constant in all subsequent layers after l. Formally, given a model with N layers, a
151   saturation event occurs at layer l ≤ N − 1 if for all layers l′ s.t. l < l′ ≤ N the top-1 token in the
152   ranking induced by that layer remains unchanged. For example, the saturation event marked with a
153   dashed line in Figure 2 occurs on layer 44, since in subsequent layers the top predicted token (“toy”)
154   remains constant.
155
      Definition 2.2 (k-th Saturation Layer). Here, we are interested in examining model behavior beyond
156   the determination of the top ranking token and so naturally extend the definition of top-1 Saturation
157   (Definition 2.1) to capture the layer at which the k-th top token is determined and remains fixed.
158   Formally, the saturation event for the k-th top token at index i in the input occurs at layer lk ≤ N −1
159   (from here on referred to as the “ k-th saturation layer”) if for all following layers l′ s.t. lk < l′ ≤ N
160   the token in position k in the ranking induced by that layer remains unchanged. We note that the
161   saturation event defined in (Geva et al., 2022) happens at l1 . For example, in Figure 2, l1 = 44 as that
      is where the top token (“toy”) is determined; l2 = 45, since the second-most probable token (“ball”)

162
      is determined at layer 45; and similarly l3 = 46, since the third-most probable token (“ribbon”) is
163   determined at layer 46, etc.1 See Appendix B.1 for statistics of 1st and k-th saturation.

166   2.2   E XAMINING THE O RDER OF S ATURATION L AYERS
167
168   We investigate whether the saturation layers of the top-k tokens l1 , l2 , . . . lk are arranged in order,
169   i.e., whether the saturation of the first token happens before the saturation of the second token, the
170   saturation of the second token happens before the saturation of the third token and so on. To this
171
      end, for each token in the input we first calculate these saturation layers for k = 1, . . . , 5 according
      to Definition 2.2 and then for each k compute the rank of the saturation layer of the k-th top token.
172
      We use k = 5 to ensure consistency across models, as it is the highest value of k where the k-th
173
      token reaches saturation in at least 5% of input tokens for all the models analyzed. Following our
174   example from Figure 2, we have l1 = 44, l2 = 45, l3 = 46, l4 = 47,2 and their ranking is [1, 2, 3, 4],
175   since l1 < l2 < l3 < l4 . If the tokens reach saturation in order of ranking, as they do in this case,
176   we would expect the average rank of the saturation layers to increase monotonically with k.
177
178
179   2.3   P ROBING FOR TASK T RANSITION
180
181
      We argue that the mechanism underlying the saturation of the top-k tokens in order is one of task
      transition, such that determining the identity of each token in the final ranking is a separate task, and
182
      the model performs them sequentially: first determining the identity of 1st token, then the identity of
183
      the 2nd token, and so on, and that the transition from one task to the next occurs at the corresponding
184   saturation layer. Additionally, we claim that the specific task number can be inferred from the model
185   embedding at each layer, and that this information is independent of the context or the specific token
186   predicted by the model.
187
      To test this hypothesis, we perform a type of probing in which we train a simple one-versus-all
188
      multi-class logistic regression classifier to predict the number of the task the model is “working on”
189
      from the hidden state embeddings of the model. We collect the data for training by extracting the
190   model’s hidden states during inference and categorize them into 5 classes according to the saturation
191   layers of the top-5 tokens for each instance. This means that for a given input, embeddings from
192   layers up to (and including) the 1st saturation layer are classified as belonging to task 1, embeddings
193   from layers from the next layer until the 2nd saturation layer are classified as belonging to task 2,
194   and so forth, for tasks 1 through 5. For example, in the case of the token ”a” as depicted in Figure 2,
195   the embeddings from layers 1 through 44 would be classified as belonging to task 1; the embedding
196   of layer 45 would be classified as belonging to task 2; the embedding of layer 46 would be classified
197   as belonging to task 3; the embedding of layer 47 would be classified as belonging to task 4; and
198
      as the model reaches the last layer directly afterward there would be no embedding classified as
      belonging to task 5.
199
200   We balance the training data so that embeddings from all layers are represented equally in each
201   class. To show that the task number is encoded in the model’s embeddings and is not an artifact of
202   the classifier’s weights, we construct a control setting where, for each class, we generate random
203   vectors with the same dimension, drawn from a normal distribution with the mean and variance of
204
      the layer embeddings in that class.3

242   3.1   T EXT T RANSFORMER
243
244   We first conduct our experiments on a pretrained GPT2-XL model, an auto-regressive decoder-only
245   LLM, using 60K tokens taken from 100 randomly sampled texts from CNN/DM dataset (Hermann
246   et al., 2015). We additionally reproduce our results using Llama3-8B (Dubey et al., 2024) model
247   on MMLU (Hendrycks et al., 2020) and Hellaswag (Zellers et al., 2019) benchmarks (see Appendix
248   B.2).
249   Tokens reach saturation in order of ranking. Figure 8a shows the average rank of the k-th satu-
250   ration layer for each k. This value increases monotonically with k, and the difference between each
251   two consecutive token ranks is statistically significant with p < 0.001 based on a pairwise indepen-
252   dent samples t-test. This supports our claim that saturation events happen sequentially according
253   to token ranking in this LLM. To statistically validate this phenomenon we use a stricter version of
254   Kendall’s τ coefficient, where we also consider ties as disagreements (see Appendix A.1 for details
255
      and mathematical formulation). This is done to discount cases where two or more tokens reach
      saturation at the same layer. The coefficient takes values in the range [−1, 1] where values close to
256
      1 indicate strong agreement, and values close to -1 indicate strong disagreement between the rank-
257
      ings. To check whether the sequence of saturation layers of the top-k tokens (l1 , .., lk ) is strictly
258   increasing, we use that sequence as one ranking, and the sequence (1, 2, .., k) as the other. k is set
259   independently for each token in the input to be the largest token index such that this token’s reaches
260   saturation by our definition i.e. lk < N . The average τ coefficient indicates moderate agreement
261   between the rankings, which is larger than all values over 1K permutations, where the saturation
262   layers sequence were randomly shuffled for each instance, resulting in p < 0.001.
263
      Task number can be predicted from model embeddings. We split the data into train and test
264   using 5-fold cross validation, and report the mean and standard error of the accuracy. Table 1 shows
265   that the logistic regression classifier trained on embeddings extracted from pretrained GPT2-XL
266   model achieves very high accuracy, while the classifier trained on the random embeddings in the
267   control setting performs approximately at chance level (see Appendix A.3 for accuracy and ROC-
268   AUC scores per class). From this we conclude that the representations of the hidden layers across
269   examples encode task specific information and that the saturation layers as we defined them are the
      points of transition between those tasks.

282   3.2   V ISION T RANSFORMER
283
      Encoder-only image-classification ViTs take as input a sequence of linear projections of equal-sized
284
      image patches with added position embedding and a special “class token” denoted [CLS]. Following
285   the work of (Vilas et al., 2024) we use a version of the logit lens adapted to ViT to project the hidden
286   state representations of each layer in the encoder onto the class embedding space using the output
287   embedding matrix. Importantly this is done only for the [CLS] token for each image under the
288   assumption that it best represents the model’s prediction, since during ViT’s pretraining the only
289   token projected onto the class-embedding space is the [CLS] token from the last layer.
290
      For our experiments we use the ViT-L/16 variant pretrained on ImageNet-21k and fine-tuned on
291   ImageNet 2012, which has 1K classes and 24 layers, and run inference on 5K randomly sampled
292   images from the CIFAR-10 (Krizhevsky et al., 2009) dataset. Figure 3b demonstrates the high
293   correspondence between saturation layer and token rank, and the stricter Kendall’s τ coefficient
294   indicates a moderate agreement between the saturation layers order and the sequence (1, 2, .., k)
295   which is statistically significant with p < 0.001 (see Appendix A.1), supporting our claim that in
296   this domain as well as in text the saturation layers are highly ordered. Furthermore, Table 1 shows
297   that the task index can be predicted from the hidden layer activations with high accuracy well above
298   chance and control setting.
299
300   3.3   S PEECH T RANSFORMER
301
      Whisper is an encoder-decoder Transformer model trained on many different speech processing
302
      tasks, including ASR. Although recently there have been attempts to increase efficiency in ASR,
303   such as Malard et al. (2023), the concept of early exit has yet to be explored in this setting, and
304   to the best of our knowledge there has not been work done concerning saturation events in speech
305   models. We adapt the logit lens and apply it only to the decoder stack of Whisper-large, which has
306   32 layers, under the assumption that representations in the encoder stack are inherently different
307   and projecting them onto the token vocabulary space would not be meaningful. For our dataset we
308   randomly sample 5K audios from LibriSpeech (Panayotov et al., 2015).
309   In addition to reproducing the classical top-1 saturation event established in language and vision
310   models in previous work, we also show in Figure 3c evidence for the tendency of the top-k tokens
311   to reach saturation in order in this model as well, albeit only up to the third token. Moreover, a
312   permutation test performed on the stricter Kendall’s τ coefficient demonstrates that the agreement
313   between the token ranking and the order of saturation layers is statistically significant with p < 0.001
314   (see Appendix A.1). We suspect that the order deteriorates in later tokens due to the fact that each
315   layer in the decoder is conditioned on the last layer of the encoder which may interfere with the task
316   transition mechanism by “blurring the lines” between the tasks. Even so, Table 1 shows that the
317
      task index can be predicted from Whisper’s decoder layers’ embeddings for tasks 1 through 4 with
      accuracy much higher than chance or that achieved in the control setting.

      4.2   I NTERVENING IN L AYER ACTIVATIONS C AUSES TASK S WITCH
366
367   Using the probing analysis, we demonstrated that the tasks, as we defined them, are distinct enough
368   to be separated by a simple classifier, that saturation layers mark the boundaries between them, and
369   that the task index is encoded in the hidden layer embeddings. We argue that in addition, each
370   saturation layer encodes the signal to transition to the next task, and all subsequent layers contain
371   the information that the previous task has been completed and that the relevant token is fixed. This
372   can be thought of as switch being flipped “on” for each token that reaches saturation, and remaining
373   ”on” from the saturation layer onwards.
374   To causally validate this claim, taking inspiration from Stolfo et al. (2023), we perform an interven-
375   tion (visualized in Figure 4) in which we “inject” the output from the 1st saturation layer of sample
376   s1 as input into the subsequent layer in the run on sample s2 and check how this affects the 1st
377   saturation layer of s2 . If these activations contain the signal to switch to the next task, we expect
      this intervention to cause the model to switch to task 2 at the injected layer in the post-intervention

      5.1   N EW E ARLY-E XIT S TRATEGY
441
442   We propose a new token-level dynamic inference method based on the task-transition classifier de-
443   scribed in Section 2.3, where the early exit layer for each token is defined as the earliest layer which
444   is predicted to belong to task 2 by the classifier. The idea being that once the model has transitioned
445   into the second task, it has finished with the first task of determining the top-1 token. To demonstrate
446   the viability and advantages of this method, we compare it to two other local confidence measures
447   introduced by Schuster et al. (2022): softmax response (the difference between the top two values
448   of the logits after softmax) and hidden-state saturation (cosine similarity between two consecutive
449
      layer embeddings), both recently found to be competitive with other early exiting methods (Zhou
      et al., 2024). Since dynamic decoding is not the focus of this paper, we calculate the metrics for
450
      each measure while propagating states from the layers after the “early exit” as in regular inference.5
451
452   Table 17 shows our results on a pre-trained GPT2-XL model and 100 randomly sampled texts from
453   CNN/DM dataset. We evaluate the model on next-word prediction, and compute the speedup ratio
454   for each method as the number of layers it uses for each token divided by the total number of layers
455
      in the model, and average across all tokens. For the two other local confidence measures we calculate
      these metrics at various thresholds (see details in Appendix A.5), while in our measure the class is
456
      selected based on the highest predicted score among all classes. Our strategy outperforms the other
457
      two when considering the trade-off between next-word prediction accuracy and speedup ratio, and
458   requires no training besides that of a simple logistic regression classifier on a relatively small amount
459   of data. We find that the difference in accuracy between our strategy and the other two methods to
460   be statistically significant with p < 0.001 using an independent samples t-test.

      5.2   I MPROVED L ANGUAGE M ODELING
463
464
      Popular decoding methods in language generation such as top-k (Fan et al., 2018) or top-p (Holtzman
465   et al., 2020) sample the next token according to the shifted distribution induced by probabilities of
466   the top ranking tokens. Based on our task-transition mechanism and the assumption that the tasks
467   represent relevant computation, we argue that top ranking tokens that are determined in the last layer
468   represent less meaningful predictions, since the model only had enough layers for the first task in
469   these instances.
470
      To test this hypothesis we compare the accuracy of the second ranked token in the next word predic-
471   tion task between two conditions: (1) the second token’s saturation layer is at least 7 layers before
472   the output, to increase the chances that this is a “true” saturation as the model had enough layers
473   to change its (recurring error) prediction and it is not due to noise; (2) the second token does not
474   saturate, and is determined only in the last layer (See Appendix B.3 for more details). In both cases
475   we only look at examples where the top-1 token is not the correct prediction. The number of layers
476   in the first setting is a hyper-parameter, and future work should investigate its affects on the second
477   ranked token accuracy.
478   Using 100 randomly sampled texts from CNN/DM dataset and pretrained GPT2-XL predictions,
479   we find that in the first condition the accuracy is 33.36%, and in the second condition it is only
480   18.04%. A Two Proportion Z-Test indicates a statistically significant difference between the groups
481   (p < 0.001). This supports our claim that top-k tokens that reach true saturation are more plausible
482   than those that are determined only in the last layer, which has potential implications for generation
483   decoding strategies which consider tokens beyond the top-1.

      6   R ELATED W ORK
487
488
489   There are multiple ways of thinking about the role of intermediate layers in Deep Neural Networks
490   (DNNs) in general, and Transformers in particular. The iterative inference hypothesis interprets each
491
      layer as an iteration from an iterative and convergent process (Simoulin & Crabbé, 2021), suggesting
      that each layer incrementally refines the hidden representation by gradually shaping the next token
492
      prediction (Geva et al., 2022; Belrose et al., 2023; Rushing & Nanda, 2024). We argue that our
493
      findings challenge this view, given the discrete nature of the tasks in the proposed task-transition
494   mechanism and the sharp transitions between them.
495
496   Pruning is another approach, focused on mitigating the redundancy inherent to large machine learn-
497
      ing models by removing unnecessary parameters. Recent work has applied structured pruning
      methods to Transformer based LLMs, dropping whole modules, from self-attention layers (Artzy
498
      & Schwartz, 2024; He et al., 2024) to full Transformer blocks (Sun et al., 2024; Men et al., 2024).
499
      These studies often focus on the middle layers of the model, and claim to reduce memory and
500   computation costs without degrading performance on downstream tasks. It’s important to note that
501   these works evaluate the accuracy before and after pruning based only on the top-1 prediction of the
502   model, even though stochastic generation methods such as top-p (Holtzman et al., 2019) and top-k
503   (Fan et al., 2018) are preferable to deterministic decoding in certain settings such as open-ended
504   tasks as they produce more coherent and varied text (Shi et al., 2024). In light of this and of our
505   results regarding the sequential saturation of top ranking tokens, we suggest that future work takes
506   this into account, since what may seem as redundancy is actually necessary computation that is not
507   reflected in the measured metric.
508   The logit lens has also been used to study intermediate layers in a wide variety of interpretability
509   papers (Yang et al., 2024; Wendler et al., 2024; Halawi et al., 2023). Despite this, Belrose et al.
510   (2023) claim that it can produce implausible results due to the difference in representations between
511   layers. To address this issue they introduce the “tuned lens”, in which an affine transformation is
512   learned for each layer in the model with a distillation loss, so that its image under the unembedding
513   matrix matches the final layer logits as closely as possible. Although this method may be better at
514
      approximating final top-1 prediction from intermediate layers, our work highlights why this might
      actually be a disadvantage when attempting to gain insights into the computational process of the
515
      Transformer, as it could obscure the changing dynamics of the lower ranked tokens.
516
517
518
519
      7   C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
520
521   This paper systematically investigates the unexplored question of what computation is performed by
522   the Transformer layers following a top-1 saturation event. We find that the top-k tokens (for k > 1)
523   go through similar saturation events in the order of their ranking. We argue that this phenomenon is
524   inherent to the Transformer architecture, replicating our results on an untrained model and demon-
525
      strating its robustness over multiple modalities: text, vision, and (to a lesser extent) speech. We then
      provide evidence in support of a task transition as underlying mechanism for this ordered saturation,
526
      showing that we can predict task index from the hidden layers’ embeddings, as well as cause the
527
      model to switch from the first task to the second via an intervention procedure. Our findings also
528   hold promise in improving inference efficiency and next word prediction accuracy as suggested by
529   the preliminary results in the Practical Applications section.
530
531
      Limitations and Future Work Although our analysis sheds light on the high-level task transition
      mechanism behind the ordered saturation of top-k tokens, there is still a need for more work to
532
      determine which components in the Transformer architecture give rise to it, via ablation studies for
533
      example, as well as more concrete explanation for how the model keeps the “chosen” tokens constant
534   after their saturation events across remaining layers. In addition, we did not consider whether the
535   model encountered the data used in our experiments during training as a relevant factor. Finally, as
536   we only explored Transformer architectures, it is necessary to check whether other types of DNNs
537   also determine their top-k tokens in order. Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) might be of particular
538   interest due to their mathematical equivalence to decoder only Transformers (Oren et al., 2024), and
539   based on previous work successfully applying the logit lens to them to extract meaningful predictions
      from intermediate layers (Paulo et al., 2024).
