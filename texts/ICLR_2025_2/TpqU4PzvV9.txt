                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011
               Characterizing quantum states is essential for advancing many quantum technolo-
012
               gies. Recently, deep neural networks have been applied to learn quantum states by
013
               generating implicit representations that map them into classical vectors. Despite
014            their success in predicting state properties, these representations remain a black
015            box, lacking insights into strategies for experimental reconstruction. In this work,
016            we aim to open this black box by developing explicit representations of quantum
017            states through the generation of preparation circuits using a reinforcement learn-
018            ing agent with a local fidelity reward function. Relying solely on measurement
019            data from a few neighboring qubits, our agent accurately recovers properties of
020            target states. Specifically, we design a quantum measurement feature aggrega-
021            tion block which is used to extract global features of quantum states from local
022
               measurement data. We also provide a theoretical guarantee for the proposed local
               fidelity reward function. Extensive experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of
023
               our framework in learning various quantum states of up to 100 qubits, including
024
               those generated by Instantaneous Quantum Polynomial circuits, evolved by Ising
025            Hamiltonians, and many-body ground states. The learned circuit representations
026            can be further applied to Hamiltonian learning as a downstream task utilizing a
027            simple linear model.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Quantum state characterization is a critical task in quantum information, underpinning the develop-
033   ment of quantum computing, quantum communication, and quantum sensing technologies. There
034   are two main approaches to tackling this task: classical methods and quantum methods. Classical
035   methods, such as quantum state tomography (Tóth et al., 2010; Gross et al., 2010; Cramer et al.,
036   2010; Lanyon et al., 2017; Cotler & Wilczek, 2020), reconstructs the quantum state by measuring an
037   informationally complete set of observables. These methods require exponentially increasing sam-
038   ple complexity in measurements as the size of the quantum system grows, making them impractical
039
      for systems with many qubits and thereby limiting their applicability for practical use. Quantum
      methods, represented by variational quantum algorithms (Cerezo et al., 2021), utilize the power of
040
      quantum circuits to learn quantum states. These methods (Peruzzo et al., 2014; Farhi et al., 2014;
041
      Du et al., 2022; Wu et al., 2023a) typically optimize a parameterized quantum circuit to approach
042   the target quantum state. Nevertheless, due to the necessity of calculating gradients with respect to
043   circuit parameters, where the loss landscape is often highly flat, these methods often struggle with
044   issues such as barren plateaus (McClean et al., 2018; Cerezo et al., 2021) and local minima (An-
045   schuetz & Kiani, 2022; Huang et al., 2024), consequently affecting their performance in learning
046   large-scale quantum systems.
047
      To address these issues, recent approaches integrate machine learning techniques to characterize
048   quantum systems. These methods have shown success in quantum state learning (Carleo & Troyer,
049   2017; Sharir et al., 2020; Zhu et al., 2022; Zhang & Di Ventra, 2023; Tang et al., 2024a; Chen
050   & Heyl, 2024; Du et al., 2023; Qian et al., 2024), quantum process learning (Huang et al., 2023;
051   Torlai et al., 2023; Zhu et al., 2023), quantum property estimation (Zhang & Di Ventra, 2023;
052   Wu et al., 2023c; Lewis et al., 2024; Tang et al., 2024a), quantum state classification (Tang et al.,
053   2024b), quantum sensing (Xiao et al., 2022; Zhou et al., 2023) and quantum verification (Wu et al.,
      2023b; Qian et al., 2024). Through leveraging neural networks to learn efficient representations of

      LLM4QPE (Tang et al., 2024a) aim to approximate the quantum state or its properties with fewer
071
      measurements by exploiting the underlying patterns and correlations present within a family of
072
      quantum states. By learning compact and expressive representations, these machine learning-based
073   techniques offer scalable solutions for quantum state characterization, making them particularly
074   valuable for learning large, complex quantum systems where traditional methods are infeasible.
075   However, despite their advantages, these representations are often implicit. While they capture es-
076   sential features and properties of the quantum state, they do not allow for the direct reconstruction
077   of the state from the representation itself. This limitation poses challenges in scenarios where an
078   explicit reconstruction of the quantum state is necessary, e.g., quantum phase estimation (Kitaev,
079   1995) and quantum simulation (Georgescu et al., 2014).
080   In this work, we propose a novel type of explicit circuit representations to characterize quantum
081   states and design a deep reinforcement learning-based framework named QCrep to learn such rep-
082   resentations that can experimentally reconstruct the target states. The circuit representation is a
083   sequence of classical descriptions of the quantum circuit used to prepare the target state. This repre-
084   sentation features the scalability and downstream task applicability of machine learning-based rep-
085   resentations while achieving the experimental reconstructability. A comparison of different methods
086   for state characterization is shown in Table 1. Two main challenges for learning the circuit represen-
087
      tation are the high measurement overhead and the barren plateaus problem. The high measurement
      overhead roots from the fact that exponential number of measurements is required to fully charac-
088
      terize an unknown quantum state. However, for many practical cases, only specific properties of
089
      the states are of interests, making it unnecessary to reconstruct the full state. Therefore, we only
090   use local measurements on a few neighboring sites of the quantum states to construct a local state
091   representation for the target state. Additionally, we propose a novel Transformer-based (Vaswani
092   et al., 2017) measurement feature aggregation block to recover properties of target states from local
093   measurement data. To mitigate the problem of barren plateaus and local minima, we involve deep
094   reinforcement learning that does not require computing gradients with respect to the circuit param-
095   eters. Besides, we design a novel reward function based on local fidelity, and provide a theoretical
096   guarantee for the effectiveness of reconstructing global properties given local fidelity information of
097   the states. The contributions are:
098   (1) We develop a novel type of representations for quantum states, termed the explicit circuit rep-
099   resentations. Unlike conventional implicit state representations in GQNQ (Zhu et al., 2022) and
100   Neural Quantum State (NQS) (Carleo & Troyer, 2017; Sharir et al., 2020; Zhang & Di Ventra, 2023;
101   Chen & Heyl, 2024), circuit representations can be directly utilized to experimentally reconstruct
102   the target states locally, which allows for computing the properties of interest via measuring the
103   output states. Moreover, they possess the advantage of implicit representations that can be applied
104
      to downstream tasks.
105   (2) We design a reinforcement learning-based framework named QCrep to learn the explicit circuit
106   representations for specific families of quantum states using only measurement data from a small
107   number of neighboring sites. The circuits learned by this framework can construct quantum states
      with high global fidelity to the target states, utilizing O(N ) number of observables with respect to

109   local fidelity, our framework circumvents the need for gradient-based optimization of circuit param-
110   eters and mitigates the barren plateaus problem, enabling scalability to larger systems. Notably, our
111   framework is capable of reconstructing quantum states with up to 100 qubits.

      (3) We experimentally demonstrate the effectiveness of our framework by learning four different

123   2     L EARNING EXPLICIT CIRCUIT REPRESENTATIONS FOR QUANTUM STATES
124
125   2.1   TASK DEFINITION
126
127   We define the task of learning explicit circuit representations for quantum states as characterizing
128   a family of unknown quantum states S = {ρs }s by constructing quantum circuits U = {Us }s
129   that can prepare these states with high local fidelity, so that the reconstructed states can be directly
130   measured to predict quantum properties of interests. We assume that the states can only be accessed
131   in a black-box manner, meaning one can measure the states using measurement operators M but
132
      remains agnostic to the underlying circuits used for their preparation. Additionally, we assume that
      the measurement operators can only act on neighboring sites of the quantum states, a setup we
133
      refer to as local measurements. This measurement configuration has been widely adopted in prior
134
      works on quantum state characterization (Lanyon et al., 2017; Friis et al., 2018; Zhu et al., 2022;
135   Kurmapu et al., 2023; Guo & Yang, 2023; Wu et al., 2023c) due to its feasibility for experimental
136   realization. For this learning task, we do not put explicit constraints on the global fidelity between
137   the reconstructed states and the target states, but focus on maximizing the average local fidelity.
138
      Explicit circuit representations. Let ρs be an N -qubit quantum state and Us the quantum     Q         circuit
139
      used to prepare it. Us can be expressed as a product of unitary gates, i.e., Us = t Us,t (ϕs,t ),
140
      where Us,t represents the quantum gates applied at time step t, and ϕs,t denotes the corresponding
141
      parameter(s) for those gates. The reconstructed state ρs = Us |0⟩⟨0|⊗N Us† has high average local
142   fidelity with the target state ρs . The explicit circuit representation of ρs is a sequence of (us,t , ϕs,t )t ,
143   where us,t is the classical description of the gate type of Us,t .
144
145
      Overview. To learn the circuit representations, we first perform local measurements on the target
      states, which is introduced in Section 2.2. After that, we design a reinforcement learning-based
146
      framework, QCrep, to decode the measurement data into quantum circuits, and keep the classical
147
      descriptions of the circuits as the circuit representations. This is described in Section 2.3, wherein a
148   measurement feature aggregation block is proposed to process the local measurements, and a local
149   fidelity reward function is designed to ensure learnability. Background information on quantum
150   computation is introduced in Appendix B.

153   2.2   M EASUREMENT S ETUP
154                                                      N −2
155
      We consider a set of measurements M = {Mi }i=0          , termed local measurements, performed on
156
      neighboring sites of the unknown N -qubit quantum states ρs . Each measurement Mi = (Mij )K   j=1
157   is a positive operator-valued measure (POVM) acting on two neighboring qubits (i, i + 1) of ρs ,
                  PK
158   satisfying j=1 Mij = I. Specifically, we select the measurement operators Mij as the tensor
159   product of two single-qubit Pauli operators, i.e., Mij ∈ {X, Y, Z}⊗2 . We measure each pair of
160   neighboring qubits using all such operators in a fixed order, taking the expectation values of the
161   measurements to obtain the measurement output mi ∈ RK , where K = 9. We repeat this process
      for all qubit pairs and record the measurement data as m ∈ R(N −1)×K .

      Importantly, the measurement operators are discarded when we input the measurement data into
163   the agent. The correspondence between the operators and their expectation values is expected to
164   be reconstructed during training. It is noteworthy that although quantum states and measurement
165   operators are represented by complex-valued numbers, the measurement expectation values are real
166   and range from −1 to 1, since the eigenvalues of Pauli operators are either −1 or 1. This property,
167   along with the removal of measurement operators from the neural network’s input, exempts the
168   neural network from the overhead of processing complex values.

      barren plateau problem. The overall pipeline is shown in Figure 1. A deep reinforcement learning
      agent utilizing a neural network policy is employed to construct the circuit representations for a

      defined as the quantum system. This environment is initialized with the quantum state to be learned,
178    (0)
      ρs = ρs ∈ S, and is responsible for applying gates to the state as the agent iteratively learns to
179
      reconstruct the state. The observations are the local measurement values ms . We define the actions
180   that the agent can take at step t as applying a layer of quantum gates to the quantum state. The

      states. For fair comparisons, the training objective for all methods is fidelity—global fidelity for the

533
534   We propose a novel type of representations of quantum states – the explicit circuit representations,
535   which feature efficient learning and experimental reconstruction of quantum states. To learn this
536   representation, we design a reinforcement learning framework featuring a Transformer feature ag-
537   gregation block and a novel local fidelity reward function. The learning procedure relies exclusively
538   on local measurement data, but can recover the target states with high global fidelity. The learned
539   representations can further be transferred to quantum systems of varying sizes and applied to Hamil-
      tonian learning as a downstream task using a linear model.

      A    R ELATED WORK
811
812   Tomography-based quantum state characterization. Tomography-based methods use direct mea-
813   surement to characterize quantum states. To accurately characterize the full quantum state, Quantum
814   State Tomography (Tóth et al., 2010; Gross et al., 2010; Cramer et al., 2010; Lanyon et al., 2017;
815   Cotler & Wilczek, 2020) has been proposed, which measures the state in exponential number of basis
816   to obtain the state vector. Other methods focus on constructing a partial knowledge of the state. For
817   instance, Shadow Tomography (Aaronson, 2018) targets at characterizing the measurement values
818   of 2-outcome measurements using only a few copies of the states. Classical shadow (Huang et al.,
819   2020; Akhtar et al., 2023) utilizes randomized measurement to efficiently estimate local properties
820
      of the states. Noteworthy, there is a special family of work that uses Tensor network, e.g., Matrix
      Product State (MPS) (Perez-Garcia et al., 2007) and Projected Entangled Pair States (PEPS) (Scarpa
821
      et al., 2020), to approximate the state vector of a quantum state. The original high dimensional state
822
      vector is decomposed into multiple low-rank tensors with restricted bound dimension.
823
824   Variational-based quantum state characterization. Alternative to state tomography, variational
825   quantum algorithms optimize the parameters of a variational ansatz, i.e., a parameterized quan-
826
      tum circuit, to approach the target state. Two representative methods are Variational Quantum
      Eigensolver (VQE) (Peruzzo et al., 2014) and Quantum Approximate Optimization Algorithm
827
      (QAOA) (Farhi et al., 2014). These methods update their output towards the target states, usu-
828
      ally the ground states of a Hamiltonian, by measuring the energy and computing quantum gradient
829   descend via, e.g., parameter shift rule (Mitarai et al., 2018). In addition to optimizing parameters,
830   Quantum Architecture Search has been proposed to optimize the circuit ansatz. Du et al. (2022)
831   traverse a candidate gate set and select the gate configurations that achieve the highest scores on the
832   target objective. Wauters et al. (2020); Yao et al. (2021); Ostaszewski et al. (2024) utilize reinforce-
833   ment learning to optimize the circuit while keeping quantum gradient descend to update parameters.
834   Zhang et al. (2022); Wu et al. (2023a) propose differentiable strategy to simultaneously update the
835   ansatz and parameters.
836   Machine learning-based quantum state characterization. Machine learning can be used to learn
837   the measurement values of states, and predict state properties. The machine learning state charac-
838   terization methods can mainly be categorized into two classes – Neural Quantum State (Carleo &
839   Troyer, 2017; Sharir et al., 2020; Zhang & Di Ventra, 2023; Chen & Heyl, 2024) and Neural State
840   Representation (Zhu et al., 2022; Tang et al., 2024a; Qian et al., 2024). The Neural Quantum State
841   represents a quantum state as a neural network, where sampling the neural network corresponds
842   to measuring the state. Parameters of the neural network can be updated via Variational Monte
843
      Carlo (McMillan, 1965) and Stochastic Reconfiguration (Sorella, 1998) methods. The Neural State
      Representation compresses the quantum state into a classical description, usually a low dimensional
844
      vector, via pretraining. Zhu et al. (2022) adopt a self-supervised manner to predict the measurement
845
      values of some measurement operators given other operators. Tang et al. (2024a) use language mod-
846   eling (Bengio et al., 2003) as the pretraining strategy. In Qian et al. (2024), the vector pretrains the
847   representation by fitting the inner product to fidelity. Afterwards, the pretrained representation can
848   be fine-tuned for downstream tasks, such as predicting the properties of quantum states.
849
      Different from previous machine learning-based methods, we decode the state representation into a
850
      novel circuit representation instead of low dimensional vector to support experimental reconstruction
851
      ability. Our representation is suitable for downstream applications like Hamiltonian learning. Unlike
852   the reinforcement learning for quantum architecture search, our framework circumvents the need of
853   calculating gradients with respect to the circuit parameters, and possesses the ability to characterize
854   a family of states rather than one specific state.

       E    S IMULATION OF QUANTUM SYSTEMS
1006
1007
       To simulate large-scale quantum systems, we use the Matrix Product State (MPS) (Perez-Garcia
1008   et al., 2007) to represent quantum states, rather than directly using the full state vector. MPS de-
1009   composes the state vector into a chain of low-rank tensors through methods such as singular value
1010   decomposition, truncating the singular values to compress the state from O(2N ) to O(N χ2 d) scale,
1011   where d is the physical dimension (typically 2 for qubit systems), and χ is the bond dimension,
1012   which represents the number of singular values retained and is related to the degree of entangle-
1013   ment. For product states, χ = 1, while for maximally entangled state, χ scales exponentially with
1014   the system size. Since the quantum states we consider exhibit a low degree of entanglement, e.g.,
1015   the Ising ground states, the Heisenberg ground states, and states prepared by shallow circuits, we
1016
       restrict χ ≤ 16 throughout our experiments.
1017   Afterwards, to simulate the evolution of states, we apply Matrix Product Operators (MPO) (Hubig
1018   et al., 2017) to MPS. The evolution of quantum states can be viewed as applying unitaries to the
1019   states, which is equivalent to applying MPO to MPS. For single-qubit gates, the MPO is simply the
1020   gate itself. For multi-qubit gates, the corresponding MPO can be derived through tensor decom-
1021   positionPsimilar to MPS. To simulate the time evolution of a state |ψ⟩ governed by a Hamiltonian

1031
       into MPO. Then each tensor of MPS is iteratively updated, sweeping from left to right and from
1032
       right to left. For each tensor, Lanczos method (Lanczos, 1950) is applied to find the eigenvalues
1033   and eigenvectors, and the tensor is optimized to the eigenvector with the minimum eigenvalue. This
1034   procedure is repeated until the energy converges. In our implementation, the MPS is randomly
1035   initialized. We set the maximum dimension of Krylov space to be 10, and the maximum sweep
1036   steps to be 200. The iteration stops if the energy difference between to updates is smaller than 10−4 .
1037   Note that for Hamiltonians with degenerate eigenspace, the ground states found by DMRG can be
1038   different for different initialization of MPS and different parameter specification. Therefore, we turn
1039   to imaginary-time evolution (Motta et al., 2020) to simulate the Heisenberg ground states, which is
1040   steered by TEBD algorithm with the time being an imaginary number. This guarantees deterministic
1041
       ground states if the initial MPS, the time step τ and total steps N are fixed. We set the initial MPS
       to be |0⟩, τ = 0.01 and N = 10.

       G.1    I MPACT OF F INITE SAMPLING
1074
1075   In the experiments, our framework is trained using expectation values of measurement outcome
1076   computed via classical simulation, which requires infinite samples of the quantum states. However,
1077   real-world experiments only allow sampling the states for finite times. Moreover, quantum states
1078   will collapse after measuring, meaning that the same state has to be prepared multiple times. This
1079   results in additional state preparation overhead. Therefore, we design this ablation study to test the
       performance of our framework under finite sampling settings.

1116   the number of measurement shots. Therefore, it would be interesting to investigate the impact of
1117   circuit noise to the construction procedure of circuit representation guided by QCrep.

       H    L IMITATIONS
1178
1179
       In our measurement settings, we specifically focus on two local Pauli measurements. An interesting
       future direction would be to explore more universal local measurements and assess whether measur-
1180
       ing multiple qubits offers advantages in achieving more accurate circuit construction. Additionally,
1181
       the neural network we employed in our framework is relatively shallow. Expanding this to a larger
1182   framework could enhance expressivity, potentially enabling simultaneous learning states across dif-
1183   ferent quantum phases of matter, and learning ground states of more complex Hamiltonians, e.g.,
1184   two dimensional Hamiltonians. Besides, it is also interesting to investigate how entanglement affects
1185   the performance of our framework, e.g., learn quantum states with volumn-law entangled states that
1186   does not allow efficient MPS simulation. Furthermore, for the reinforcement learning algorithm, we
1187   have only considered the standard PPO. Incorporating more advanced techniques, such as Monte
       Carlo Tree Search, could improve training efficiency.
