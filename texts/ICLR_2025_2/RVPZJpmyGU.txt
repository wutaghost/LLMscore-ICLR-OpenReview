009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Sparse mixture of experts (SMoE) is an effective solution for scaling up model
012            capacity without increasing the computational costs. A crucial component of SMoE
013            is the router, responsible for directing the input to relevant experts; however, it also
014            presents a major weakness, leading to routing inconsistencies and representation
015            collapse issues. Instead of fixing the router like previous works, we propose an
016            alternative that assigns experts to input via indirection, which employs the discrete
017            representation of input that points to the expert. The discrete representations
018
               are learnt via vector quantization, resulting in a new architecture dubbed Vector-
               Quantized Mixture of Experts (VQMoE). We provide theoretical support and
019
               empirical evidence demonstrating the VQMoE’s ability to overcome the challenges
020
               present in traditional routers. Through extensive evaluations on both large language
021            models and vision tasks for pre-training and fine-tuning, we show that VQMoE
022            achieves a 28% improvement in robustness compared to other SMoE routing
023            methods, while maintaining strong performance in fine-tuning tasks.

026   1    I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Scaling Transformers with data and compute has demonstrated unprecedented successes across
029   various domains such as natural language processing (NLP) tasks (Du et al., 2022; Fedus et al., 2022;
030   Zhou et al., 2024), and visual representation learning (Riquelme et al., 2021a; Shen et al., 2023b).
031   However, training and inference of a single large Transformer-based model might require hundreds
032   of thousands of compute hours, costing millions of dollars (Kaddour et al., 2023). This issue has
033
      motivated contemporary studies to investigate Sparse Mixture-of-Experts (SMoE) (Shazeer et al.,
      2017; Zoph et al., 2022; Xue et al., 2024; Jiang et al., 2024). SMoE models that are inspired by
034
      (Jacobs et al., 1991a) usually include a set of experts sharing the same architecture and a router that
035
      activates only one or a few experts for each input. Compared to dense models of the same size,
036   SMoE counterparts significantly reduce inference time thanks to not using all experts simultaneously
037   (Artetxe et al., 2022; Krajewski et al., 2024).
038
039
      However, training SMoEs remains a challenge due to representation collapse, that is, either a
      small number of experts receive most of the routed tokens or all experts converge to learn similar
040
      representations. To tackle the issue, several works (Chi et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2023a; Do et al., 2023)
041
      have focused on router policy improvement. However, these do not touch a fundamental question, ‘Do
042   we really need a router in the first place?’ Our research suggests that adopting a discrete representation
043   could help solve the challenges currently faced by the router method. Discrete representation learning
044   in the context of SMoE is motivated by its ability to capture structured and interpretable patterns within
045   data, aligning with the way that humans categorize and process information through distinct symbols,
046   like tokens. This approach enables better generalization and facilitates knowledge transfer across
047   different contexts. Additionally, discrete representations provide a robust and efficient mechanism
048   for selecting and routing inputs to the appropriate experts by clustering them more effectively. By
049   bridging the gap between discrete and continuous representations, this method leads to more stable
050
      and interpretable expert assignments, helping to mitigate issues such as representation collapse and
      overfitting, which are common challenges in SMoE training.
051
052   Employing vector quantization (VQ) techniques to learn discrete representation, this paper proposes
053   a novel mixture of expert framework, named VQMoE, which overcomes the representation collapse
      and inconsistency in training sparse mixture of experts. More specifically, we prove that the existing

      router methods are inconsistent and VQMoE suggests an optimal expert selection for training SMoE.
055   Additionally, our method guarantees superior SMoE training strategies compared to the existing
056   methods by solving the representation collapse by design.
057
058
      We evaluate the proposed method by conducting pre-training of Large Language Models (LLMs)
      on several advanced SMoE architectures, such as SMoE (Jiang et al., 2024), StableMoE (Dai et al.,
059

061
062   In summary, the primary contributions of this paper are threefold: (1) we theoretically demonstrate
063   that learning a discrete representation is an optimal approach for expert selection and that VQMoE
064
      inherently addresses the issue of representation collapse; (2) we propose the use of the Vector Quanti-
      zation method to learn cluster structures and resolve related challenges; and (3) we conduct extensive
065

066
      in-depth analysis of VQMoE’s behavior to showcase its effectiveness.

068   2    R ELATED W ORK
069
070   Sparse Mixture of Experts (SMoE). Sparse Mixture of Experts (SMoE) builds on the Mixture of
071   Experts (MoE) framework introduced by Jacobs et al. (1991b); Jordan & Jacobs (1994), with the
072   core idea that only a subset of parameters is utilized to process each example. This approach was first
073   popularized by Shazeer et al. (2017). SMoE’s popularity surged when it was combined with large
074   language models based on Transformers (Zhou et al., 2022; Li et al., 2022; Shen et al., 2023a), and its
075   success in natural language processing led to its application across various fields, such as computer
076   vision (Riquelme et al., 2021b; Hwang et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2024), speech recognition (Wang et al.,
077   2023; Kwon & Chung, 2023), and multi-task learning (Ye & Xu, 2023; Chen et al., 2023b).
078   However, SMoE faces a major problem in training known as representation collapse, i.e., the experts
079   converge to similar outputs. To address this, various methods have been introduced. XMoE (Chi
080   et al., 2022) calculates routing scores between tokens and experts on a low-dimensional hypersphere.
081   SMoE-dropout (Chen et al., 2023a) uses a fixed, randomly initialized router network to activate
082   experts and gradually increase the number of experts involved to mitigate collapse. Similarly,
083   HyperRouter (Do et al., 2023) utilizes HyperNetworks (Ha et al., 2016) to generate router weights,
084
      providing another pathway for training SMoE effectively. StableMoE (Dai et al., 2022) introduces
      a balanced routing approach where a lightweight router, decoupled from the backbone model, is
085
      distilled to manage token-to-expert assignments. The StableMoE strategy ensures stable routing by
086
      freezing the assignments during training, while SimSMoE Do et al. (2024) forces experts to learn
087   dissimilar representations. Despite these extensive efforts, the representation collapse issue persists,
088   as highlighted by Pham et al. (2024). While most solutions focus on improving routing algorithms,
089   our approach takes a different path by learning a discrete representation of input that points to relevant
090   experts.
091
      Discrete Representation. Discrete representations align well with human thought processes; for
092
      example, language can be understood as a series of distinct symbols. Nevertheless, the use of discrete
093   variables in deep learning has proven challenging, as evidenced by the widespread preference for
094   continuous latent variables in most current research. VQVAE (van den Oord et al., 2017) implements
095   discrete representation in Variational AutoEncoder (VAE) (Kingma & Welling, 2022) using vector
096   quantisation (VQ). IMSAT (Hu et al., 2017) attains a discrete representation by maximizing the
097   information-theoretic dependency between data and their predicted discrete representations. Recent
098   works follow up the vector quantisation ideas and make some enhancements for VAE, for example: (Yu
099   et al., 2022); (Mentzer et al., 2023); and (Yang et al., 2023). Mao et al. (2022) utilize a discrete
100   representation to strengthen Vision Transformer (ViT) (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021). To the best of our
101
      knowledge, our paper is the first to learn a discrete representation of Sparse Mixture of Experts.
102

106   representations for expert selection. As illustrated in Fig. 1a, our approach selects experts directly
107   based on the input representation, eliminating the need for a trained router. To prevent information
      loss, we integrate discrete and continuous representations within the model.

468   5.6   A BLATION S TUDY
469
470   We examine the effectiveness of VQMoE across various hyper-parameter settings, with all experi-
471   ments conducted using the base Transformer architecture on the WikiText-103 dataset.
472   Vector Quantization Method. To learn a discrete representation, we research various types of
473   Vector Quantization methods, including VQVAE (van den Oord et al., 2017), VQGAN (Yu et al.,
474   2022), LFQ (Yu et al., 2023), and ResidualVQ (Yang et al., 2023). We observe that VQGAN using
475   cosine similarity for distance achieves good and stable results in practice as Figure 6a. Interestingly,
476   VQGAN with lower dimensionality also delivers strong performance and exhibits robustness.
477
      Number of codebook impact. The number of codebook entries is a crucial hyperparameter when
478   training Vector Quantization techniques. As shown in Figure 6b, we can see the best performance
479   when the number of codebook entries matches the number of experts. This aligns with the proof by
480   (Dikkala et al., 2023), which demonstrates that in the optimal case, the number of clusters equals the
481   number of experts.
482
      Sensitiveness of VQ loss contribution α. Figure 6c illustrates the impact of α, which controls the
483
      contribution of the Vector Quantization loss to the overall loss. If α is too high, it leads to a better
484   discrete representation but may negatively affect the final target. Conversely, if α is too low, it may
485   result in a poor discrete representation. Therefore, α should be selected based on the data, typically
      within the range of (0.05, 0.15).

      6    C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE D IRECTIONS

488
      This study illustrates Vector-Quantized Mixture of Experts (VQMoE), which is novel and theoretically-
489   grounded architecture to overcome challenges in training SMoE such as representation collapse
490   and inconsistency. We evaluate our method on various Pre-training and Fine-tuning tasks, for both
491   language and vision domains. The results show that VQMoE outperforms the routing methods both
492   theoretically and empirically. Furthermore, fine-tuning VQMoE with the discrete representation for
493   downstream tasks could reduce computational resource usage by 28%. We believe that focusing on
494   discrete representation learning will offer a promising strategy for training and testing sparse mixtures
495   of experts (SMoE) at a large scale. Finally, we believe that our approach opens up new research
496   avenues for effectively training SMoE, where cutting-edge techniques in discrete representation
497
      learning and vector quantization can be harnessed to enhance their performance.
