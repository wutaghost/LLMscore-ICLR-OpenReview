                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011
               Large language models (LLMs) have achieved remarkable progress, but their
012
               extensive number of parameters results in high memory usage, significant loading
013            latency, and substantial computational demands. To address these challenges, post-
014            training quantization (PTQ) has emerged as an effective technique for compressing
015            model weights. In the context of PTQ for LLMs, existing uniform quantization
016            methods, though efficient in terms of memory and computational requirements,
017            often struggle to maintain performance. In this paper, we propose SliM-LLM, a
018            Salience-Driven Mixed-Precision Quantization scheme that achieves group-wise
019            bit-width allocation with mixed precisions for efficient LLMs with high accuracy.
020            Building on our observation that salient/important weights often follow a structured
021
               distribution, we incorporate two core components to preserve post-quantization
               performance in LLMs while maintaining efficiency: 1) Salience-Determined Bit
022
               Allocation adaptively assigns bit widths to groups within each layer based on their
023
               group-level salience, aiming to minimize the reconstruction error of activations;
024            and 2) Salience-Weighted Quantizer Calibration optimizes quantizer parameters
025            by incorporating element-level salience, ensuring that the most critical weights are
026            preserved, further preserving important weights information. With its structured
027            group partitioning, SliM-LLM offers a hardware-friendly quantization approach,
028            maintaining computational and memory efficiency comparable to highly optimized
029            uniform quantization methods. Extensive experiments demonstrate that SliM-
030            LLM significantly improves the accuracy of various LLMs when quantized to
031            ultra-low bit widths. For instance, a 2-bit quantized LLaMA-7B model achieves
032
               nearly 6x memory reduction compared to its floating-point counterpart, alongside
               a 48% reduction in perplexity compared to the leading gradient-free PTQ method,
033
               all while maintaining GPU inference speed. Furthermore, SliM-LLM+, which
034
               incorporates gradient-based quantizers, reduces perplexity by an additional 35.1%.

      Large language models (LLMs) have exhibited exceptional performance across a wide array of natural
040
      language benchmarks (Brown et al., 2020; Hendrycks et al., 2020). Notably, LLaMA (Touvron et al.,
041
      2023a) and GPT (Brown et al., 2020) series have significantly contributed to the ongoing evolution of
042   LLMs towards universal language intelligence. The powerful language understanding capabilities of
043   LLMs have been transferred to multi-modal domains (Li et al., 2024b; Achiam et al., 2023; Team
044   et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2024b), laying the foundation for artificial general
045   intelligence (AGI) (Bubeck et al., 2023). Despite these significant achievements, the substantial
046   computational and memory requirements of LLMs pose huge challenges for real-world applications,
047   particularly in resource-constrained environments.
048   To address resource constraints of LLMs, post-training quantization (PTQ) has emerged as an efficient
049   yet effective compression technique (Dettmers et al., 2022), showing success in quantizing the weights
050   of pre-trained LLMs (Frantar et al., 2022; Lin et al., 2023; Shao et al., 2023; Lee et al., 2023; Chee
051   et al., 2024). As LLMs continue to scale, the demand for more aggressive low-bit compression
052   becomes critical due to limited computational and storage resources in application (Huang et al.,
053   2024a; Tseng et al., 2024). However, significant performance degradation remains a challenge in
      low-bit scenarios (⩽ 3-bit). To mitigate this, unstructured mixed-precision quantization (Shang et al.,

      2023; Huang et al., 2024a; Dettmers et al., 2023) and vector quantization (Chee et al., 2024; Tseng
069   et al., 2024; Egiazarian et al., 2024) methods have been developed to preserve performance. While
070   these approaches have advanced the field, they are often hardware-unfriendly, introducing extra
071   storage requirements such as storing bitmaps or code indices, along with additional computations for
072   vector decoding. This creates a bottleneck, limiting further reductions in memory and computational
073   demands during deployments. In sum, ensuring the accuracy of LLMs while maintaining efficiency
074   during deployment remains a significant challenge for current PTQ approaches.
075   This paper presents a Salience-Driven Mixed-Group LLM (SliM-LLM) framework, an accurate
076   and inference-efficient PTQ method for LLMs (⩽ 3-bit). Our approach is grounded in the key
077   observation that salient or important weights, which are critical to model performance, exhibit a
078   structured distribution, often clustering within certain channels (see Sec. 3.2.1 and Fig. 3). This
079   insight, largely overlooked by prior research (Frantar et al., 2022), forms the basis for designing SliM-
080   LLM as a structured, hardware-friendly mixed-precision low-bit quantization method. It preserves
081   performance through two key designs that retain important weights at both the global group and local
082   element levels. First, we develop a novel Salience-Determined Bit Allocation (SBA) method, which
083
      adaptively assigns bit-widths to each quantization group based on their group-level salience ranking.
      The allocation strategy is optimized to reduce activation reconstruction errors. By applying higher
084
      precision to more important groups and reducing the bit-width for less critical ones, SBA achieves
085
      a low average bit-width while enhancing the overall performance of LLMs. Next, we introduce
086   the Salience-Weighted Quantizer Calibration (SQC), which enhances sensitivity to locally salient
087   weights, ensuring that critical information within groups is preserved. SQC works collaboratively
088   with SBA, exploiting the local and global salience of weights to preserve the performance of LLMs
089   after quantization. Unlike element-wise mixed-precision methods (Shang et al., 2023; Dettmers
090   et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2024a), SliM-LLM is inherently structured, eliminating additional bit or
091   computational overhead while preserving high performance. This is further demonstrated through
092   our deployment of SliM-LLM in an application-level inference tool 1 for LLMs, enabling efficient
093   mixed-precision inference on GPUs with consistently strong performance.
094   Experiments show that for various LLM families, SliM-LLM surpasses existing training-free PTQ
095   methods on diverse benchmarks, particularly in low-bit scenarios. Using GPTQ as the backbone, SliM-
096   LLM improves the perplexity scores of 2-bit LLaMA-13B and LLaMA2-13B on WikiText2 (Merity
097   et al., 2016) from 20.44 and 28.14 to 8.87 and 9.41, denoting performance improvements of over
098   56%, respectively. SliM-LLM even outperforms other element-wise mixed-precision PTQ methods,
099   such as PB-LLM (Shang et al., 2023), APTQ (Guan et al., 2024) and LLM-MQ (Li et al., 2024a),
100
      in a deployment-friendly manner, showcasing its superior low-bit accuracy and efficiency. We also
      integrate SliM-LLM into OmniQuant (Shao et al., 2023) and obtain SliM-LLM+ through gradient
101
      optimization to further improve quantization quality. Moreover, the group-wise mixed-precision
102
      strategy can smoothly be adapted to existing quantization-aware training (QAT) (Liu et al., 2023), fine-
103   tuning based (Guo et al., 2023; Liao & Monz, 2024; Dettmers et al., 2024), or codebook-based (Chee
104   et al., 2024; Egiazarian et al., 2024; Tseng et al., 2024) LLMs compression methodologies. The
105   structure of weight salience we theoretically identify introduces a new practical view of the weight
106   quantization of LLMs.

120   2    R ELATED W ORK
121
122   Large Language Models (LLMs) have been significantly developed in diverse natural language
123   processing domains, establishing a prominent paradigm in these fields (Bubeck et al., 2023; Chang
124   et al., 2024; Zhao et al., 2023; Brown et al., 2020; Touvron et al., 2023a). Nevertheless, the exceptional
125   success of LLMs depends on massive parameters and computations, posing significant challenges
126   for deployment in resource-constrained environments. Consequently, research into the compression
127   of LLMs has emerged as a promising field. Existing compression techniques for LLMs primarily
128   include low-bit quantization, pruning, distillation, and low-rank decomposition (Xu et al., 2023;
129   Ganesh et al., 2021; Frantar et al., 2022; Xiao et al., 2023a; Shao et al., 2023; Chee et al., 2024;
130
      Zhu et al., 2023; Frantar & Alistarh, 2023; Huang et al., 2024a; Qin et al., 2024). Among these
      technologies, low-bit quantization gains remarkable attention, for efficiently reducing the model size
131
      without change of network structure(Zhu et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2023; Chang et al., 2024).
132
133   Quantization of LLMs can be generally divided into QAT and PTQ. QAT, by employing a retraining
134   strategy based on quantized perception, better preserves the performance of quantized models. LLM-
135   QAT (Liu et al., 2023; Ma et al., 2024a) addresses the data obstacle issue in QAT through data-free
136
      distillation. However, for LLMs with huge size of parameters, the cost of retraining is extremely
      inefficient(Chang et al., 2024). Therefore, PTQ has become a more efficient choice for LLMs. For
137
      instance, LLM.int8() (Liu et al., 2023) and ZeroQuant (Yao et al., 2022) explore the quantization
138
      strategies for LLMs in block-wise, which is a low-cost grouping approach that reduces hardware
139   burden. Subsequently, AWQ (Lin et al., 2023) and OWQ (Lee et al., 2023) also propose scaling
140   transformations on outlier channels of weight to preserve their information representation capacity.
141   GPTQ (Frantar et al., 2022) reduces the group quantization error of LLMs through Hessian-based
142   error compensation (Frantar & Alistarh, 2022), achieving commendable quantization performance at
143   3-bit. OmniQuant (Shao et al., 2023) introduces a learnable scaling quantizer to reduce quantization
144   errors in an output-aware manner. To achieve LLM quantization at ultra-low bit-width, recent
145   novel efforts such as QuIP (Chee et al., 2024), QuIP# (Tseng et al., 2024), and AQLM (Egiazarian
146   et al., 2024) promote quantization performance at 2-bit through matrix decomposition with learnable
147
      codebooks and fine-tuning. Recent studies (Qin et al., 2024; Liao & Monz, 2024; Dettmers et al.,
      2024; Guo et al., 2023) have further refined compression techniques by integrating post-training
148
      quantization (PTQ) with parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) to enhance model performance via
149
      additional parameter learning.
150
151   Mixed-Precision Quantization exploits variations in the importance and redundancy of model
152   parameters, assigning different bit-widths to each component. HAWQ V2 (Dong et al., 2020) and
153
      V3 (Yao et al., 2021) optimize bit-width allocation layer-wise in traditional visual networks through
      Hessian analysis and Integer Linear Programming (ILP). Alternatively, OMPQ (Ma et al., 2023)
154
      employs network orthogonality instead of Hessian for similar purposes. In LLMs, APTQ (Guan
155
      et al., 2024) extends HAWQ’s strategy, allocating varied bit-widths to different transformer blocks
156   based on Hessian-trace, thus improving the accuracy of 3-bit LLMs. However, such block-wise or
157   layer-wise mixed-precision allocation at 2-bit still fails to maintain post-compression performance
158   in LLMs. Recent studies such as SpQR (Dettmers et al., 2023), PB-LLM (Shang et al., 2023), and
159   LLM-MQ (Li et al., 2023) have introduced finer-grained partitioning for grouped quantization with
160   element-wise mixed-precision for accurate weight quantization. Nevertheless, these low-bit methods
161   still rely on special structures and fine-grained grouping to ensure accuracy, which brings the huge
      burden of real hardware deployment and inference speed.

      3     S LI M-LLM

164   This section introduces a group-wise mixed-precision quantization method, SliM-LLM, designed to
165   overcome the accuracy and efficiency bottlenecks of mixed-precision LLMs. We devise two novel
166   strategies for LLMs, including the use of Salience-Determined Bit Allocation (SBA) based on global
167   salience distribution to determine group bit-widths, and Salience-Weighted Quantizer Calibration
168   (SQC) to enhance the perception of locally important weight information. We introduce SBA and
169
      SQC in Sec. 3.2 and Sec. 3.3, respectively.

293   3.3     S ALIENCE -W EIGHTED Q UANTIZER C ALIBRATION
294
295   In addition to the global group-wise distribution of salience, we notice that salience within the group
296   still shows local differences in discrete distribution. Common existing quantizers apply uniform
297   consideration across all weights to minimize the effect (error) of quantization, lacking the capability
298   to perceive differences in local salience. Therefore, in this section, we introduce a Salience-Weighted
299
      Quantizer Calibration (SQC) to enhance the information of significant weights within the group by
      amplifying the quantizer awareness of salient weight.
300

      3.3.2      S ALIENCE -W EIGHTED Q UANTIZER C ALIBRATION FOR L OCAL S ALIENCE AWARENESS
325
326   To prevent the degradation of local salient weight information in each group, we propose the Salience-
327   Weighted Quantizer Calibration (SQC), which enhances the expression of salient weights through
328   local salience awareness, thereby reducing the quantization error of these significant elements and
329   improving the compressed performance of LLMs.
330
      Based on a common observation (Dettmers et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2024a), the proportion of
331   relatively salient weights in each group is only 1-5%. Therefore, we employ the 3-σ rule for a mask
332   to select the salience part (w < (µ − 3σ) ∪ w > (µ + 3σ)) in each group (Fig. 2), which accounts
333   for about 1% elements. After the selection, we get wi = wis ∪ wius , where wis is the salient part and
334   wius represents the non-salient elements within group i. To effectively keep the information of local
335   salient weights, SQC first introduces the calibration parameter τ to the SQC quantizer, liberating the
336   perception interval during quantization. Then we define the local salience awareness loss of the SQC
337   quantizer through calibration:
338
339
       argmin ||wis − τ · s{Q(wis , τ · s, τ · z) − τ · z}||22 + ||wius − τ · s(Q(wius , τ · s, τ · z) − τ · z)||22 (5)
            τ
340
341   where Q(·) denotes the quantization process in Eq. (1), || · ||22 represents the ℓ2 loss, aligned with
342   Eq. (3). wis and wius denotes the salient and less salient part of group i, respectively, generated from
343   a mask operation. In Eq. (5), τ expands the solution space of s and z, flexibly adjusts s and z to
344   search the optimal loss under τ ∗ , without bringing additional parameters, as wis and wius share the
345   same quantizer. The search space for τ by linearly dividing the interval [1-λ, 1+λ] into 2n candidates.
346   We empirically set λ at 0.1 and n at 50 to achieve a balance between efficiency and accuracy.
347   Compared to traditional quantizer calibration methods, SQC effectively mitigates the degradation of
348   intra-group local salient weights caused by general average loss by enhancing the loss sensitivity to
349   salient elements during the calibration (more experiments are detailed in Appendix E). Moreover, the
350   SQC process allows wis and wius to share a set of parameters τ ∗ s and τ ∗ z, eliminating the need to
351   differentiate intra-group weights during storage and inference. This facilitates straightforward group-
352   wise dequantization calculations, thereby avoiding the hardware overhead associated with element-
353
      wise bitmap and unstructured grouping. SQC and SBA each capture local salient weight information
      within groups and global salient weight combinations across groups, effectively enhancing the
354
      protection of critical information during quantization, thereby accurately preserving the overall
355
      performance of LLMs at extremely low bit-widths.

      5     C ONCLUSION
531
      In this work, we introduce SliM-LLM, a group-wise mixed-precision PTQ framework tailored for
532
      LLMs, designed to enhance performance with low-bit weights in a deployment-friendly manner.
533
      The essence of SliM-LLM lies in employing the Salience-Determined Bit Allocation to dynamically
534   allocate bit widths, thereby improving the preservation of global salience information. Within
535   groups, the Salience-Weighted Quantizer Calibration is designed to enhance local information
536   perception, further minimizing the loss associated with locally salient weights. Experiments validate
537   the effectiveness of SliM-LLM, showing notable accuracy improvements across various LLMs,
538   and ensuring efficiency in inference. In conclusion, SliM-LLM is versatile and can be seamlessly
539   integrated with different quantization frameworks and successfully improves the performance of
      LLMs supporting practical deployment in resource-constrained environments.

           A LLOCATION
897
      We optimize the mixed-precision configuration based on the output information entropy (KL-
898
      divergence), searching for the optimal compensation bit-width ratio as shown in Eq. (4).
899
900   Initially, we rank each group by their average salience, a metric for quantization, and employ a
901   double-pointer that moves simultaneously from both the beginning (lowest salience) and end (highest
902   salience) of the sorted list. This ensures an equal number of groups at low and high bit-widths,
903
      effectively balancing the global average bit-width compensation. We then calculate the relative
      entropy under the corresponding precision ratio and search for the optimal ratio. Fig 7 displays the
904
      search error curves related to the 2nd , 10th , and 15th Transformer layers in the OPT1.3B model,
905
      showcasing the search curves for certain self-attention layers (Query, Key, Value, FC2).
906
907   Due to the limited range of the search, extreme scenarios involve either a half (N − 1)-bit and half
908   (N + 1)-bit without N -bit or all groups being N -bit (uniform precision). Fig 7 demonstrates that
909
      lower quantization errors can be achieved under mixed-precision compared to quantization at the
      uniform bit-width. We also find that multiple low-error precision combinations are possible within a
910
      group of weights, allowing SBA to flexibly select the optimal ratio through its versatile search.
