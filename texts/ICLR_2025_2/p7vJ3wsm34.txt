009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Sequence-to-sequence natural language tasks often benefit greatly from long con-
012            texts, but the quadratic complexity of self-attention in standard Transformers ren-
013            ders usage of long contexts non-trivial. In particular, during generation, temporary
014            representations (stored in the so-called KV cache) account for a large portion of
015            GPU memory usage, and scale linearly with context length. In this work, we in-
016            troduce KV- DISTILL , a flexible compression framework for large language models
017            (LLMs) that distills long context KV caches into significantly shorter representa-
018
               tions. KV- DISTILL can be trained as a parameter-efficient adaptor for pre-trained
               models, and enables the compression of arbitrary spans of a context while preserv-
019
               ing the pre-trained model’s capabilities, including instruction-tuning. We do this
020
               by treating a compressed-uncompressed cache as a student-teacher pairing and
021            applying a KL-type divergence to match the generated outputs. Our experiments
022            show that KV- DISTILL outperforms other compression techniques in worst-case
023            extractive tasks, and approaches uncompressed performance in long context ques-
024            tion answering and summarization. Furthermore, KV- DISTILL can be fine-tuned
025            on domain-specific contexts to reduce context lengths by up 95% while preserv-
026            ing downstream task performance. We demonstrate the generalizability of KV-
027            DISTILL across various model sizes and architectures. Our code and checkpoints
028            will be made available at https://example.com

      1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Harnessing the full potential of attention-based large language models (LLMs) often requires them
      to condition on long contexts. However, use of expansive contexts is complicated by the quadratic
034
      complexity of self-attention. In particular, during generation, one must maintain a store of all past
035
      key and value representations of past tokens (called the KV cache) that grows linearly with sequence
036   length. The memory burden imposed by the KV cache is significant, and often limits the length of
037   the sequences that a model can handle.
038
039
      Much work has been devoted to architectural improvements to the attention mechanism, with the aim
      of reducing the aforementioned memory burden during generation. Strategies include augmenting
040
      sequences with memory tokens (Rae et al., 2020; Wu et al., 2022), sparsifying attention patterns
041
      (Beltagy et al., 2020), and using conditional computation to only process essential tokens (Ainslie
042   et al., 2023). However, such techniques have seen little widespread adoption due to performance
043   drops on downstream tasks, or inefficient training/inference procedures. Furthermore, even when
044   given long contexts, recent work has shown that LLMs fail to fully utilize them (Qin et al., 2022;
045   Liu et al., 2024; Lu et al., 2024). Taken together, this suggests that long contexts may allow for
046   significant compression while yielding large memory savings.
047
      Prior work in parameter-free context compression has primarily focused on how to select represen-
048   tations in the KV cache for eviction, with promising results (Zhang et al., 2023). However this can
049   suffer large performance drops under high compression ratios. Furthermore, we suppose that there
050   is room for further performance improvements in general-purpose context compression when the
051   model is trained to account for compression. Earlier studies in this area have typically utilized a
052   combination of cross-entropy and autoencoding objectives to pre-train general context compressors
053   (Qin et al., 2024; Ge et al., 2024; Rae et al., 2020). These loss functions have led to signficant perfor-
      mance loss at high compression rates and fail to maintain the carefully-tuned instruction-following

085   need be applied once to a fixed context, has zero overhead during auto-regressive decoding, and can
086   compress arbitrary (sub)spans of a given context. We show improvements on several model fami-
087   lies, considering extractive and abstractive tasks, with both short and long contexts, and at multiple
088   model scales. KV- DISTILL is general purpose and has broad applicability to the LLM community.

      2     BACKGROUND
091
092
      2.1   K EY-VALUE C ACHE
093
094   Transformer-based language models (LMs)(Vaswani et al., 2017) use self-attention to aggregate con-
095   text information and make predictions. A decoder-only transformer LM autoregressively predicts
096   new tokens, and each step requires the LM to obtain the key and value states of all past tokens. To
097   avoid re-computing the KV state of past tokens, most LM implementations (e.g. Wolf et al. (2020))
098   cache the key and values states, in a structure called the KV cache. When making new predictions,
099   self-attention is performed on query states of the new token and the KV -cache, and the new token’s
100
      key and value representations are appended to the KV cache.
101   Because the KV cache grows proportional to the number of tokens generated, maintaining the full
102   KV cache in memory is a primary bottleneck when conditioning on large contexts. The goal of this
103   work is to alleviate this by compressing KV cache in the dimension of sequence length.
104
105   2.2   R ELATED W ORK
106
107   Much prior work has tackled the problem of reducing the complexity of the self-attention mechanism
      itself. Previous work tries to sparsify the attention patterns(Beltagy et al., 2020; Zaheer et al., 2020),


                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      use recurrence attention(Yang et al., 2019), or kernelize the attention matrix(Choromanski et al.,
109   2021), but they require a considerable amount of further training. Another research direction tries to
110   extrapolate the existing LMs to long context without heavily re-training them. For example, Bertsch
111   et al. (2023); Tworkowski et al. (2023) use techniques akin to kNN to directly extend the attention
112   range of LLMs without any further training.
113
      Similar to our work, another line of work involves compressing the hidden states (KV cache) of
114
      past tokens into a shorter sequence of representations. For example, some work learns “soft rep-
115   resenatation” of context(Qin & Eisner, 2021). Mu et al. (2023) compress particular prompts into
116   much shorter “gist tokens”, but do not attempt more general context compression. Furthermore,
117   their method demonstrates poor generalizability, as performance does not scale with the number of
118   gist tokens used. Zeng et al. (2023) propose to recognize and prioritize some important tokens (VIP
119   tokens) during inference. More relevant to our experiments, the following three methods employ a
120   similar idea of dynamically compressing the context prior to inference:
121   Ge et al. (2024) design In-Context Autoencoder (ICAE) to compress long contexts for use in large
122   language models (LLMs). ICAE consists of two main components: a learnable encoder and a fixed
123   decoder. The encoder compresses the input context into a small number of memory slots. These
124   memory slots are then used by the frozen LLMs (decoder) to reconstruct the context or respond to
125   prompts. ICAE is pretrained using autoencoding and language modeling objectives on a large pre-
126   training corpus and further fine-tuned using instruction data to maintain instruction-tuning. How-
127   ever, there is still a gap in downstream task performance when using an ICAE-compressed context,
128   compared to an uncompressed context, and the method falters under high compression ratios.
129   Qin et al. (2024) propose DODO to compress sub-select KV activations to a set of “nugget” tokens,
130   which grow proportionally with the length of context sequence. Their method is trained with auto-
131   encoding or language modeling objectives. However, DODO models operate at a fixed compression
132   ratio, require training both an encoder and decoder, and still show a large gap in downstream task
133   performance when compared to an uncompressed context.
134   Zhang et al. (2023) propose H2 O to reduce memory usage during generation. The technique iden-
135   tifies “heavy-hitter” tokens, which significantly influence attention scores during inference. These
136   tokens are retained while less important ones are evicted in a greedy fashion. Although nearing
137   uncompressed performance, the authors note that H2 O performance falls at compression ratios over
138   20x. More importantly, H2 O offers no way to further improve compressive performance given prior
139   domain knowledge.

144   3.1   OVERALL P ROCEDURE
145
146   In this paper, we consider a transformer-based language model (Vaswani et al., 2017), denoted by
147   LM, that is defined on the vocabulary V. The overall procedure of KV- DISTILL is as follows. First, a
148   set of important tokens in the input context is determined. Next, we use an adapted language model
149   LMθ to encode the context into a KV cache, and sub-select the important tokens from the generated
      KV cache. Finally, the unmodified LM conditions on the compressed KV cache to auto-regressively
150
      generate it’s output.
151

229   3.5   O BJECTIVE F UNCTION
230
231   After generating compressed cache X̃, we aim to match the output of LM when conditioned on X̃ to
232   the output of LM when conditioned on X.
233   Previous compression methods (Ge et al., 2024; Qin & Van Durme, 2023) rely on the autoencoding
234   objective to pretrain LMθ . However, given that LM predicts future tokens during inference, there is a
235   discrepancy in pretraining and downstream usage, which could result in performance loss. Instead
236   we propose matching the next-token probability distribution of tokens conditioned on X and X̃.
237
      Formally, consider a generative language model that predicts the next token yt conditioned on the
238
      past tokens y<t and a fixed context c that is represented by either X or X̃. We would like

243   generation we use the original frozen parameters of LM.

475   6     D ISCUSSION AND C ONCLUSION
476
477   In this paper, we develop a method to reduce the memory requirements of long-context conditioned
478   LM generation. Our method sub-selects tokens from the KV cache, and applies a token-level KL-
479   type loss between the output of the LM when conditioned on sub-selected tokens and when con-
480   ditioned on the uncompressed cache. We evaluate our method on long-context extractive and ab-
481   stractive tasks, and demonstrate improved performance over competing compression methods. We
482
      further demonstrate that continued training on domain-specific data can allow for use of compression
      ratios as high as 20x with negligible losses in performance.
483
484   We plan to further explore the effect that different token sub-selection mechanisms may have on our
485   method, including applying the non-trainable layer-wise selection mechanism described in H2 O .
      We anticipate such a selection mechanism could yield a non-negligible improvement to the perfor-


                                                       9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      mance of KV- DISTILL . Another interesting avenue is applying KV- DISTILL to multi-modal models,
487   where some modalities such as vision may allow for very high compression ratios.
488
489   As part of this work we release distilled checkpoints across various model language families. These
490
      artifacts allow efficient text generation conditioned on significantly larger inputs than before, with
      much lower memory burden, and support compression ratios as high as 1000x. We anticipate these
491
      artifacts will be of great practical benefit to practitioners, enabling exciting new applications and
492
      research directions in language processing.
