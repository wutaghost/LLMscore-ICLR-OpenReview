                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
010            Transformer language models (TLMs) exhibit an impressively general range of
011
               capabilities. A growing body of work aims to harness these models for com-
               plex reasoning problems expressed in natural language. However, recent theoreti-
012
               cal and empirical results have revealed limits to the algorithmic generalization of
013
               TLM reasoning. Transformers trained to solve deduction problems from one dis-
014            tribution fail to solve instances of the same problem type drawn from other distri-
015            butions. We propose to improve the systematic reasoning capabilities of TLMs via
016            a differentiable proof search module, yielding proof-search augmented language
017            models (PSALMs). In a PSALM, a Transformer is responsible for predicting rule
018            and fact representations for a neural theorem prover (NTP). The NTP performs a
019            backward-chaining search over proofs, scoring them based on a soft unification
020            operation. Our results show that PSALMs successfully generalize in deduction
021            tasks where vanilla transformers do not learn systematic behavior, can be adapted
022            to more natural text with only label supervision, and robustly handle large exam-
023            ples where proprietary LLMs make mistakes.
024
025
026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      The general utility of large language models for text- and code-based tasks is a major factor driving
029
      their increasing adoption. Pursuant to this, there is a growing premium placed on their ability to
030   ‘reason’ in order to widen the range of tasks they can handle. Reasoning in this context translates
031   to following rules, integrating information in a consistent way, and being able to solve complex
032   problems. One big challenge is search: strategies like chain-of-thought inference (Wei et al., 2022),
033   in which models generate intermediate steps to break problems down, are fundamentally greedy and
034   can leave models in dead-ends after they commit to an inconsistent rationale. Strategies like tree-of-
035   thought (Yao et al., 2023) that allow backtracking have to navigate the search space of token strings,
036   which is massive, and still fundamentally depend on the model to propose consistent steps.
037   This work aims to bridge the gap between classical proof search in systems like Prolog and the soft
038   reasoning capabilities of transformers. Such a unification has been explored before in the context
039   of the neural theorem prover (NTP) (Rocktäschel & Riedel, 2017); however, NTPs have difficulty
040   scaling to real problem sizes and do not inherently have the ability to operate over natural language.
041   We show how a transformer can effectively translate a natural language statement of a problem into
042   a set of soft rules to be queried through an NTP. We also describe straightforward changes to the
043   NTP that improve its learning dynamics and allow it to handle nontrivial rulesets efficiently.
044   Our system, shown in Figure 1, consists of several steps. First, a pre-trained transformer encodes
045   a set of text rules, and an attentive rule extractor projects the transformer’s encodings into soft rule
046   representations. These rules are fed into a backward-chaining search performing soft unification.
047   This algorithm extends standard NTP inference with dynamic pruning and parallel step processing.
048   We evaluate our architecture on the SimpleLogic dataset from Zhang et al. (2023). On this dataset,
049   vanilla transformers learn spurious correlations and achieve perfect “in-distribution” accuracy, but
050   fail to generalize at higher proof depths to problems with the same logic sampled from a different
051   process. Our results show that our approach is able to generalize across this distribution gap with no
052   major performance loss, supervised either end-to-end or at the rule level. We also demonstrate the
053   ability to use our architecture’s end-to-end differentiability to adapt a model trained on templated
      rules to more natural text with only example-level labels.

      2      BACKGROUND
075
076   Zhang et al. (2023) observe that TLMs trained on deduction problems learn incidental statistical
077   features related to the number of rules and facts, and that the ability of these models to predict
078   provability of goals collapses when they are tested on instances of the same problem drawn from
079   a new distribution where these trends no longer hold. This means that the decision functions they
080   acquire conflate aspects of the problem that we hold independent.
081
      We set out to solve this issue architecturally: we would like to modify the computational structure of
082   the TLM to make it easier (or even possible) for the system to learn a deductive decision function that
083   behaves correctly across distributions, while maintaining the softness and learnability of predictions.
084
085
      The basic hierarchical structure of automated deduction algorithms is backward chaining (Russell &
      Norvig, 2020, pg. 230), which attempts to find a proof for a goal expression as follows: consequents
086
      of the available rules are matched against the current goal, and the antecedents of any matching rules
087
      are then introduced as additional subgoals, until all open goals are discharged and search succeeds
088   or all options are exhausted and search fails. This procedure is carried out almost verbatim by the
089   Prolog automated deduction system and logical programming language (Van Emden & Kowalski,
090   1976; Kowalski, 2014), and forms the backbone of many more advanced deduction systems.
091
092   2.1     T HE NEURAL THEOREM PROVER
093
094   Rocktäschel & Riedel (2017) introduce the neural theorem prover (NTP), a differentiable module
095   with an inference procedure analogous to the backward chaining proof search strategy used by Pro-
096   log. Prolog rules are Horn clauses h :– b1 ∧ b2 ∧ .. ∧ bn . The :– connective is equivalent to ←, a
097   leftward implication: if all the body terms bi are true, then the head h is true. A rule can have zero
098
      body terms, in which case it is simply a fact: an assertion that its head term is true.
099   The core inference rule in Prolog is unification: an open goal can be discharged by applying a
100   rule if the goal syntactically matches the head of the rule, after which that rule’s body terms are
101   introduced as subgoals. Unification in symbolic systems is a discrete operation: either it succeeds,
102   returning a variable assignment under which the two terms are equal, or it fails. The NTP relaxes
103   this discreteness by representing terms as vectors in Rd , making a rule a collection of vectors:
104                                                       h :– b1 .. bn                                                (1)
105
106   The NTP replaces symbolic unification’s exact syntactic comparisons with inner products. If the
107   rule above were applied to a goal term vector g, the unification would result in a score of h · g. We
      would then have b1 , . . . , bn as new subgoals to prove by applying additional rules.

      In Prolog, a proof is successful if all the unifications involved are successful. In the NTP, a proof can
109   be considered “successful” as long as it is well-formed in that there are no open subgoals. Instead of
110   complete proofs having a binary notion of success or failure, a proof comes with real-valued score,
111   defined to be the minimum over its unification scores, intuitively its weakest link. Let hi be the head
112   term vector of the i-th rule. Let bi,k be the k-th body term vector of the i-th rule. A proof P consists
113   of an applied rule r(P ) and subproofs Sk (P ), one for each body term of the applied rule. The proof
114   score of the NTP proof P with goal g is then:
115                       scorepr (P, g) = min(hr(P ) · g, min scorepr (Sk (P ), br(P ),k )                 (2)
                                                               k
116
117   The overall score returned by the NTP process is the maximum score over all considered proofs.
118   As there can be an unlimited number of proofs given one or more non-fact rules, search must be
119   truncated; we impose an upper limit on proof depth and the number of visited search states.
120
      Weber et al. (2019) and Minervini et al. (2020a), among others, have sought to adapt the NTP
121
      to natural language inputs. However, the systems proposed in prior work require pipelined rule
122   prediction and continue to suffer from exploding computational cost with increased proof depth.
123   Our experiments also corroborate the findings of De Jong & Sha (2019): the hard minimum and
124   maximum in the NTP scoring function prevent effective exploration of the space of rule representa-
125   tions during training. We describe our approaches to mitigate these issues in the following sections.

      3     M ETHODS
129
      The PSALM architecture has two main components: a transformer language model, which encodes
130
      input text into continuous rule representations, and a search module, which performs inference based
131
      on the encoded rules in order to make predictions.
132
133
      3.1   RULE ENCODING
134
135   The rule encoder is responsible for predicting rule representations (1) whose term unification scores
136   reflect the semantic compatibility of rule consequents and antecedents: unifying similar terms should
137   result in a high score, and unifying incompatible terms should result in a low score.
138
      Rules may have variable arity on the right hand side. For example, a statement “Alice is tall” has
139
      no preconditions, but a statement “If Alice is open-minded and polite, then she is agreeable” needs
140   two body terms. Rather than modeling this as a hard decision, we do it softly, predicting rules of
141   all arities simultaneously, only some of which will be used. Specifically, the rule extractor takes the
142   hidden state vectors of the transformer as input, and yields a set of candidate rules by predicting
143   term vectors to fill the slots of M different rule templates. We use four rule templates of the form
144   shown in (1), one for each n ∈ [0..3]. The rule extractor predicts one instance of each rule template
145   per input sentence. The rule encoder can accommodate the unused rules (e.g., an arity 2 rule for the
146   sentence “Alice is tall”) by learning to assign term vectors with universally low unification scores to
147   the head slots of these inactive rules, preventing them from appearing in high-scoring proofs.
148

190   3.2     S EARCH
191
192   Our architecture relies on the soft proof search procedure to perform reasoning. This procedure,
193   which we describe in Algorithm 1 and show more abstractly in Figure 2, is derived from the NTP
194   algorithm of Rocktäschel & Riedel (2017) described in §2.1. Two major changes are required to
195   make this algorithm practical at our scale: pruning partial proofs, and parallelizing unification.
196
197   Pruning As we conduct search, early complete proofs provide a useful lower bound on proof
198   scores. We can immediately abandon a partial proof as soon as a rule is applied whose unification
199   score is lower than the current best-scoring proof, as the score of a proof is the minimum across its
200   unification scores, and we only need Algorithm 1 to return the proof with the highest possible score.
201   As originally described, the NTP did not feature pruning (Rocktäschel & Riedel, 2017). Inefficiency
202   posed a problem for its applicability to real problems, motivating subsequent work to restrict the
203   number of instantiated rules (Minervini et al., 2020b, i.a.). However, restricting the number of rules
204   doesn’t solve the underlying issue of being unable to abandon partial proofs. Dynamic pruning
205   allows us to extend the depth of search dramatically beyond what would otherwise be possible, even
206   while maintaining large rule sets.
207
208   Parallelism The naı̈ve recursive implementation of backtracking search is ill-suited to modern
209   compute hardware, as it visits search states in series. In order to take advantage of GPU parallelism,
210   we design Alg. 1 so that the unification operation can be performed for multiple search states
211   at the same time, as opposed to interleaved with each state’s visitation logic. This allows term
212   comparisons, which in our case translate to inner products, to be executed on the GPU as larger
213
      vectorized operations without incurring separate dispatch overhead for each term vector.

      4     L EARNING
271
272   The PSALM NTP module is fully differentiable: unification scores and proof scores have well-
273   defined (sub)derivatives with respect to the TLM’s parameters. This offers us several points at
274   which we can potentially apply supervision during training in order to achieve the kind of reasoning
275   behavior we want out of the system. We consider four different objectives at three levels of granu-
276   larity: examples, proofs, and rules.
277
278   4.1   E ND - TO - END
279
280   The simplest way to train a PSALM is to leave all intermediate structure latent and optimize end-to-
281   end for proving correct statements and not proving incorrect statements. We do this by applying a
282   binary cross entropy loss, LE2E , on the predicted proof score (see Algorithm 1) against the example
283
      label y (provable or not): LE2E (x) = y log σ(ŷ) + (1 − y) log(1 − σ(ŷ)).
284   We will show in Section 6 that this objective is not sufficient to learn the right latent structure. As
285   noted by De Jong & Sha (2019), this is due to the sparsity of the gradient flow in the vanilla NTP
286   definition: at each training step, only a single proof step’s unification score actually receives non-
287   zero gradient in the backward pass. They propose pooling proof scores across multiple alternate
288   proofs; we take this insight a step further. We relax the hard maximum over proof scores to a smooth
289   maximum over the top k highest scoring proofs, but we also relax the hard minimum over step scores
290   to a smooth minimum and add a small amount of Gaussian noise to the unification scores. The cor-
291   responding modifications to Algorithm 1 are described in Appendix A.2; we refer to the end-to-end
292   loss using these modifications as LE2ER .

500   7    R ELATED WORK
501
502
      A significant line of work has sought to augment LLMs with external solvers. These include calcu-
503
      lators (Gao et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023), logical solvers like Z3 (Ye et al., 2023; Pan et al., 2023),
504   planners like PDDL (Liu et al., 2023), and probabilistic programming languages (Wong et al., 2023).
505   These systems are differentiated from ours mainly by their “hard” solvers. Because they use non-
506   neural tools, training signal can’t be passed back to the model from the system’s outputs.
507
      The neural theorem prover (Rocktäschel & Riedel, 2017) was originally motivated by this issue,
508
      aiming to support backward chaining proof search in a differentiable way. However, the networks
509
      described by Rocktäschel & Riedel are exponential in size with respect to the depth of the ‘proofs’
510   required. Subsequent work has sought to make this basic idea practical for larger problems by
511   summarizing or filtering active rules based on the current goal (Minervini et al., 2018; 2020a;b;
512   Morris et al., 2022). Weber et al. (2019) in particular adopt a rule score threshold similar to our
513   pruning policy, although their threshold is not updated recursively, limiting the amount of work
514   that can be avoided as depth increases. Our system also features a richer parameterization of rules
515   computed on the fly by a TLM.
516   Soft proof search is a neural version of a classic discrete algorithm. In this vein, a line of past work
517   has examined data structures like stacks (Grefenstette et al., 2015; Chen et al., 2020), neural Turing
518   machines (Graves et al., 2014), and neural GPUs (Kaiser & Sutskever, 2015). Compared to these ar-
519   chitectures, especially the neural Turing machine, our aim is not to learn a very general computation
520   engine, but to buttress one particularly weak capability of transformer LLMs, namely the ability to
521   do deduction and search. This motivation is shared by other recent work fusing transformers with
522   algorithmic neural modules targeted at reasoning (Bounsi et al., 2024).
523   Differentiable versions of other logical reasoning procedures have been explored, notably proba-
524   bilistic predicate logic (Manhaeve et al., 2018; Huang et al., 2021) and natural logic (Feng et al.,
525   2020; Shi et al., 2021), applied to tasks like textual entailment Beltagy et al. (2013). This last ap-
526   proach takes logical forms from a separate semantic parser, isolating problem interpretation from
527   execution. Our approach, in contrast, can make “late” decisions about rule viability during search.
528

530   8    C ONCLUSION
531
532   In this paper, we present an augmented transformer that can invoke a neurosymbolic proof search
533   module (a neural theorem prover). The transformer instantiates the parameters of the NTP from
534   text inputs, then executes search to find a soft proof of a query, or returns failure if no proof with
535   a high enough score is found. Our experiments analyze several forms of supervision, finding that
536   end-to-end supervision from labels in concert with a relaxed scoring function is sufficient to learn to
537   parameterize latent rules consistently. In order to tackle problems with dozens of rules, we introduce
538   algorithmic improvements to the neural theorem prover that drastically improve its efficiency. Crit-
539   ically, our architecture is able to generalize across problem distributions where standard end-to-end
      trained transformers fail.

      A.5   A DDITIONAL RELATED WORK
865
866   There is a close conceptual connection between the differentiable proof structures produced by NTP
867   search and the network structures predicted by neural module networks (Andreas et al., 2016; Gupta
868   et al., 2020). In a neural module network, differentiable components with specialized inductive
869   biases are composed hierarchically on an example-by-example basis. Each module is treated as a
870   function, and the layout of the network is predicted by a semantic parser conditioned on the prob-
871   lem to be solved. Our approach shares the core idea of generalization through component reuse: if
872
      a complex problem is made up of simple subproblems, we can structure a model’s computational
      abilities so that the model is able to solve complex instances of a problem class by inferring how to
873
      decompose them and applying a set of learned solutions to the elementary subproblems. Strategies
874
      like this offer not only better interpretability, but also better data efficiency, as non-compositional
875   models of compositional problems require the training set to capture a much larger range of combi-
876   nations of properties or steps.
877
878
      Other work has investigated forward-chaining (Tafjord et al., 2021; Gontier et al., 2020) and
      backward-chaining search in natural language (Sprague et al., 2022; Tafjord et al., 2022) and in
879
      conversational reasoning (Arabshahi et al., 2021). Generating forward-chaining proofs autoregres-
880
      sively is challenging, as models must predict which ground facts to introduce from the bottom up;
881   doing this accurately requires inferring the entire proof tree before it can be emitted. Regardless
882   of expansion order, any form of search over generated strings is challenging, as it can quickly run
883   off the rails due to cascading errors; in contrast, our rule representations don’t require decoding
884   to strings and thus allow much more efficient and predictable inference. Approaches like maieu-
885   tic prompting (Jung et al., 2022) limit divergence by only unrolling one or two steps of reasoning.
886   Weir et al. (2023) achieve better control by specializing backward chaining in natural language to a
887   particular domain with tailored templates, at the cost of domain flexibility.
888   Yang & Deng (2023) set out in a less-traveled direction, investigating rule learning for reasoning
889   over text without gradient descent; their basic inference operation is based on string substitution,
890   making use of reverse unification to learn more abstract rules from concrete ones.
