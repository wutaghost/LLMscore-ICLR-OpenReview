011                                                   A BSTRACT
012
013                Many recent methods aim to merge neural networks (NNs) with identical architec-
014                tures trained on different tasks to obtain a single multi-task model. Most existing
015                works tackle the simpler setup of merging NNs initialized from a common pre-
016
                   trained network, where simple heuristics like weight averaging work well. This
                   work targets a more challenging goal: merging large transformers trained on dif-
017
                   ferent tasks from distinct initializations. First, we demonstrate that traditional
018
                   merging methods fail catastrophically in this setup. To overcome this challenge,
019                we propose Foldable SuperNet Merge (FS-Merge), a method that optimizes a
020                SuperNet to fuse the original models using a feature reconstruction loss. FS-Merge
021                is simple, data-efficient, and capable of merging models of varying widths. We test
022                FS-Merge against existing methods, including knowledge distillation, on MLPs
023                and transformers across various settings, sizes, tasks, and modalities. FS-Merge
024                consistently outperforms them, achieving SOTA results, particularly in limited data
025                scenarios 1 .

      1       I NTRODUCTION
029
      Practitioners frequently train identical neural architectures for various tasks and share these models
030
      online, while the original training data is often unavailable due to privacy, proprietary, or other
031
      concerns. This led to an increased interest in the field of model merging (Akhlaghi & Sukhov, 2018;
032   Wortsman et al., 2022a; Goddard et al., 2024), which aims to combine the weights, and sometimes the
033   features, of several models into a single new model (Figure 1). This approach could allow the merging
034   of multiple single-task models into a single multi-task model (Matena & Raffel, 2022; Ilharco et al.,
035   2023), eliminating the need to store and run multiple models or ensembles (Ganaie et al., 2022).
036
      Most existing merging methods have a strong restriction: they assume that the models were initialized
037
      from the same pre-trained model and subsequently fine-tuned. This encourages the models to stay
038   aligned (Ainsworth et al., 2023) and also to remain closer in the weight space Ilharco et al. (2023),
039   and therefore easier to fuse, for example by simply averaging their weights (Wortsman et al., 2022a).
040   However, this restricts the ability to merge models that do not share the same initialization. For
041   example, consider the task of merging the weights of two unrelated models from an online repository
042   (e.g., Hugging Face or GitHub). These models were likely not fine-tuned from the same initial model,
043   making most existing merging techniques inapplicable.
044   To overcome this, a few recent studies explored merging differently initialized models using alignment-
045   based methods (Entezari et al., 2022; Singh & Jaggi, 2020; Verma & Elbayad, 2024; Ainsworth
046   et al., 2023; Stoica et al., 2024). However, these approaches use very simple merging rules and hence
047   struggle with larger, complex tasks such as merging transformers (Stoica et al., 2024).
048
      To illustrate the limitations of traditional and alignment-based merging methods, we pre-trained two
049
      Vision Transformers (ViTs) (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021) with different initializations on ImageNet-1k
050   (Deng et al., 2009), then fine-tuned each on separate tasks: Cars (Krause et al., 2013) and CIFAR10
051   (Krizhevsky et al., 2009). Attempts to merge these models into a single multi-task ViT using various
052   methods (Table 1) were severely unsuccessful. Traditional approaches like weight averaging, SLERP

      Problem formulation. For simplicity, we outline the merging problem for two models A and
136
      B with identical widths, each trained on distinct tasks with unique initializations and separate
137   classification heads (Figure 1a). We also have an unlabeled subset of the training set from each
138   task, DA and DB . Our goal is to develop a new model with the same architecture, that minimizes
139   the losses for tasks A and B. Note that the classification heads are not merged, meaning we retain a
140   separate classification head for each task (Figure 1b).
141
      In this section, we introduce FS-Merge, our proposed approach. We begin by explaining how to
142
      merge MLPs, a relatively easier task, and then proceed to tackle the more complex challenge of
143   merging transformer models.

454   Discussion. As can be seen, FS-Merge outperforming all other merging methods in most cases, and
455   even surpassing ensembles in some cases. This holds for both per-task and joint accuracy across
456
      all settings, despite using fewer learnable parameters than distillation. Additionally, it is evident
      that all local and simple methods (such as Average, SLERP, RegMean, and “Opt”) completely fail
457
      to effectively merge ViTs in this challenging setting, resulting in a merged model that performs
458
      comparably to a random guess. We also find that FS-Merge achieves SOTA results when merging
459   BERTs on NLP tasks, as can be seen in Appendix F.6.
460
461   Our experiments indicate that in the ViT case, initialization is crucial for FS-Merge as it does
462
      not converge when initialized randomly (and see Appendix H.2). Specifically, using the “first”
      initialization in Foldable SuperNet, and KD, not only improves the accuracy of the first task but
463
      also enhances accuracy across all tasks. For an ablation study in the matter, see Appendix G.1 and
464
      Appendix G.2.
465
466   Merging Complexity. FS-Merge and KD are more computationally intensive than standard merging
467   methods such as Averaging and RegMean, which fail catastrophically in our setting. Notably, the
468
      accuracy of both FS-Merge and KD cannot be improved simply by longer training (i.e. more
      resources), as this causes overfitting in our data-scarce regime (this explains why the longest training
469
      duration was not identified as optimal in our hyperparameter search). When merging two models,
470
      FS-Merge and distillation have comparable resource usage. However, when merging multiple models,
471   the resource usage gap between FS-Merge and distillation becomes more significant. To address this,
472   we propose FS-Merge seq., which is comparable to distillation’s resource use while outperforming it
473   in terms of accuracy. Therefore, FS-Merge is recommended for optimal test accuracy, given sufficient
474   computational resources and limited data. If resources are more limited, FS-Merge seq. should be the
475   method of choice. Refer to Appendix C for the full details.

509   4   C ONCLUSION
510
511   Limitations. FS-Merge requires a small unlabeled subset of the original training data, similarly to
512
      most previous merging methods. Additionally, FS-Merge, like distillation, is more computationally
      intensive than standard merging methods; however, these methods completely failed in more chal-
513
      lenging settings. Notably, the main bottleneck in model merging is often data availability rather than
514
      computational resources. Moreover, FS-Merge has fewer learnable parameters than distillation, but
515   they increase linearly with the number of models and hidden width; however, FS-Merge seq. solved
516   the first issue. Lastly, one cannot naively merge two models of different depths using our method; we
517   believe this could be solved in future work.
518
      Summary. In this work, we address the challenging task of merging transformers from different ini-
519
      tializations and tasks into a unified multitask model using a small subset of unlabeled data—a setting
520
      in which traditional methods fail. Our proposed FS-Merge, which employs a feature reconstruction
521   approach to train a Foldable SuperNet, is simple, data-efficient, and can use more sophisticated
522   merging rules compared to other baselines. FS-Merge outperforms traditional methods and achieves
523   SOTA results across various scenarios, model sizes, datasets and modalities.

      A     R ELATED WORK

920   A.1   R ELATED WORK
921
922   Mode Connectivity. A pair of models has mode connectivity when there exists a simple path
923   between them in the loss landscape with low loss (Garipov et al., 2018; Draxler et al., 2018; Lubana
924   et al., 2023). Frankle et al. (2020) demonstrated that this path can be linear (LMC) when the
925   models originate from the same initialization. Tatro et al. (2020); Ainsworth et al. (2023); Entezari
926   et al. (2022) showed that it is feasible to establish LMC between MLPs and CNNs from different
927
      initializations using permutations. Jordan et al. (2023) enhanced these solutions by addressing their
      variance collapse. Wang et al. (2020) used these permutations to match and then merge models within
928
      a federated learning framework. Imfeld et al. (2023); Verma & Elbayad (2024) used permutations to
929
      align two transformers with different initializations that were trained on the same task. Mirzadeh
930   et al. (2021) leveraged LMC for multitask and continual learning. Yunis et al. (2022) expanded LMC
931   to more than two models, discovering a high-dimensional convex hull of low loss. Zhou et al. (2023)
932   introduced Layerwise Linear Feature Connectivity (LLFC), demanding that the features of the models
933   be linearly connected. Singh & Jaggi (2020); Akash et al. (2022) addressed a similar challenge of
934   neuron alignment using optimal transport (Knight, 2008), and Liu et al. (2022) generalized it as
935   a graph-matching task. Navon et al. (2023) solved the neuron alignments problem by training an
936   equivariant deep weight space network. It is important to note that previous works which merged
937   models from different initializations using permutations primarily focused on merging pairs of models
938
      trained on the same task, with many of them concentrating on MLPs and CNNs. In contrast, our
      method is capable of merging larger groups of models, with different initializations and tasks, and is
939
      specifically designed to handle transformers.
940
941
      Model merging. Model merging technique (Wortsman et al., 2022b; Goddard et al., 2024) has
942   gained increasing interest in the past years, allowing the creation of stronger single-task and multi-
943   task models. Akhlaghi & Sukhov (2018) showed that averaging the weights of multiple simple
944   neural networks maintains their performance. Model soups (Wortsman et al., 2022a) proposed
945   averaging multiple models trained on the same task from identical initializations to enhance task
946   accuracy. In addition, Weight averaging has been employed for various purposes, including improving
947   optimization through checkpoint averaging (Izmailov et al., 2018), federated learning (McMahan
948   et al., 2017), developing superior pre-trained models (Choshen et al., 2022; Don-Yehiya et al., 2022),
949   improving Out-of-Distribution Generalization (Rame et al., 2022), achieving success in unseen tasks
950
      (Huang et al., 2023), creating a unified Mixture of Experts model from multiple LLMs (Sukhbaatar
      et al., 2024), and as augmentations for weight space networks (Shamsian et al., 2024). In many cases,
951
      spherical linear interpolation (SLERP) (Shoemake, 1985) is used to average the models’ weights.
952
      Matena & Raffel (2022) utilized Fisher-Weighted Averaging to fuse multiple models from the same
953   initializations but trained on diverse tasks, resulting in a multi-task model. RegMean (Jin et al.,
954   2023) addressed a similar scenario and proposed a closed-form solution that solves a local linear
955   regression problem for each linear layer in the model. Ilharco et al. (2023) defined task vector by
956   subtracting the parameters of a fine-tuned model from those of the pre-trained model, and used it to
957   fuse models fine-tuned from the same pre-trained model. Yadav et al. (2024); Ortiz-Jimenez et al.
958   (2024); Yang et al. (2024); Akiba et al. (2024) analyzed and proposed more merging methods based
959   on task vectos. Sung et al. (2023) fused pairs of models trained on different modalities. ZipIt (Stoica
960   et al., 2024) merged models from various initializations and tasks, focusing on MLPs and CNNs,
961
      by averaging pairs of highly correlated neurons between and within the models (see Appendix A.2
      for more details). In contrast, our work focuses on MLPs and transformers, and can use much more
962
      complicated merging schemes.
963
964
      Distillation. In Knowledge Distillation (KD), a small student model is trained to mimic the outputs
965
      of one or more larger teacher models (Ba & Caruana, 2014; Hinton et al., 2015; Stanton et al.,
966   2021; Gou et al., 2021; Nguyen-Meidine et al., 2021; Li et al., 2022; Shi et al., 2024). Sau &
967   Balasubramanian (2016) implemented a noise-based methodology to simulate learning a single
968   task from multiple teachers. Tan et al. (2019); Khanuja et al. (2021) used several teachers, each
969   translating between a specific language pair, to train a singular multilingual student. Clark et al.
970   (2019); Park & Caragea (2023); Chelaramani et al. (2021) utilized multiple models and true labels to
971   instruct a multi-task model. Pham et al. (2023) employed numerous quantized teachers trained on
      the same task to teach a quantized model, also leveraging true labels. Wu et al. (2021) co-fine-tuned

       multiple teachers in downstream tasks with shared layers to instruct a student. Jacob et al. (2023)
973    simultaneously trained multiple single-task models with a single multi-task student to facilitate the
974    student’s optimization. Similar to our distillation baseline, Vongkulbhisal et al. (2019) utilized KD
975    to unify knowledge from various models with different targets into a single model, relying solely
976    on unlabeled data. Wu et al. (2021); Zagoruyko & Komodakis (2017); Heo et al. (2019b;a) focused
977    on using a single teacher’s outputs and inner features to train a single model. Park & Kwak (2019);
978    Liu et al. (2020) applied multiple teachers trained on the same task, their inner features, and the true

980    function that takes into account the inner features. However, this approach proved ineffective for both
981
       our method and distillation. See Appendix H.3 for details.

985    Adapter modules. FS-Merge also resembles adapter-based methods (Houlsby et al., 2019; Chen
986    et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2024), which perform Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (Hu et al., 2022;
987    Zaken et al., 2021) of large models by injecting a small module ("adapters") into the original model.
988    During fine-tuning, the original weights of the pre-trained model are frozen, and only the adapter
989    weights are optimized. Similarly, FS-Merge injects new parameters into the model, which are the only
990    ones optimized, though there are key differences. First, FS-Merge is used to merge the knowledge of
991    multiple models, in contrast to adapter-based methods, which fine-tune a single model. Additionally,
992    at the end of the training phase (or "merging"), the trainable parameters of FS-Merge are folded into
993
       the original weights, whereas in many adapter methods, the adapters remain in the fine-tuned models,
       adding complexity.

       A.2   C OMPARISON TO Z IP I T
999
1000
1001   Our work strongly relates to ZipIt (Stoica et al., 2024), which also merges models from various
1002   initializations and tasks, with a focus on MLPs and CNNs. We will briefly explain the algorithm and
1003   its limitations. ZipIt uses the original training data to extract features from the models to be merged,
1004   A and B. Then, for each layer l, it employs the features to identify pairs of highly correlated neurons
1005   using a greedy algorithm. Following this, it builds Ml ∈ Rdl ×2·dl , which is zero except for entries
1006   corresponding to a matched pair (i, j) indexed by p, where Ml [p, i] = Ml [p, j] = 12 . The purpose of
1007   this matrix is to merge features by averaging pairs of highly correlated neurons. Then, Ul = 2Ml⊤
1008   is used to reposition the merged features back to their original locations. After building Ml and Ul ,
1009   ZipIt proposes folding them with the original weights (Eq. 5) to create the weights of the merged
1010
       model.
1011   Like other current model merging methods (Ainsworth et al., 2023; Jin et al., 2023; Imfeld et al.,
1012   2023), ZipIt is efficient and focuses only on simple fusion schemes. It limit itself to pairing similar
1013   neurons, addressing only the permutation symmetries of neural networks (Hecht-Nielsen, 1990).
1014   Permutation symmetries mean that it is possible to swap any two neurons of a hidden layer in a neural
1015   network without altering its functionality. However, ZipIt falls short in handling the scale symmetries
1016
       of neural networks (Neyshabur et al., 2015; Badrinarayanan et al., 2015; Phuong & Lampert, 2019),
       or in considering more complicated merging rules.
1017
1018   Probably due to these limitations, ZipIt underperforms on a large scale (such as ResNet-50 trained
1019   on datasets with 200 categories). Furthermore, this merging method is formulated as local prob-
1020   lems, merging one layer at a time, and relies on heuristics to handle more complicated layers (such
1021   as batch normalization and skip connections). This makes it difficult to generalize to more com-
1022
       plex architectures like transformers, where ZipIt struggles with self-attention and skip connection
       structures.
1023
1024   Our work aims to solve a similar problem, but adopts a more expressive approach without relying on
1025   heuristics. Moreover, the global version of our method allows the merging of any architecture by
       simply constructing a Foldable SuperNet that is suitable for it.

       B.1     F OLDABLE S UPER N ET FOR V ISION T RANSFORMERS
1029
1030   Assuming there are two Vision Transformers (ViTs) (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021), trained on two distinct
1031   tasks, A and B, we aim to define a Foldable SuperNet that combines the original weights of the ViTs
1032   with new learnable parameters M, U . This structure is designed so that all skip connections and layer
1033   normalizations (Ba et al., 2016; Xiong et al., 2020) operate on the merged dimension, and that there
1034   is a linear layer before or after every M or U matrix, allowing them to be folded after training. It is
1035   important to highlight that layer norms possess significantly fewer learnable parameters compared to
1036
       other layers in ViTs. Therefore, we can initiate their parameters from a good starting point (e.g., the
       parameters of the first model to be merged) and proceed to optimize the parameters as usual, similar
1037
       to strategies employed in previous merging works (Jordan et al., 2023; Stoica et al., 2024).
1038
1039   The method described here for merging two models can be readily extended to any number of models.
1040   The parameters optimized at this stage are highlighted in red. The notation X̄ represents the outputs
1041   of a layer norm, X̃ represents a feature reconstruction attempt after using the U matrix, and X ∗
1042   represent the merged features.
1043
1044   B.1.1    P RE - PROCESSING
1045   First, each ViT creates patches from the input image and reshapes them into a series of vectors
1046   Iproj ∈ RT −1×din , using W in ∈ Rdin ×d to project these vectors into tokens. It then concatenates the
1047   CLS ∈ Rd token and adds emb ∈ RT ×d which is the positional encoding. For example, for model A:
1048
1049                                Z A = Concat[Iproj W in,A , CLSA ] + embA .
1050

       B.4   M ERGE TASKS SEQUENTIALLY WITH FS-M ERGE SEQ .
1152
1153   Using the global version of FS-Merge on large models like transformers comes with a significant
1154   resource cost. As we have shown, the number of learnable parameters is smaller than in the distillation
1155   case, due to our modeling of the M and U matrices as a concatenation of diagonal matrices plus a
1156   low-rank matrix (Eq. 12). However, we still need to retain the frozen original weights of all models
1157   in memory, leading to increased memory and compute resource demands when merging multiple
1158   models.
1159   To address this issue, we introduce FS-Merge Seq., which merges the models sequentially. For
1160   example, if we wish to merge n models, we start by merging the first two models using the global
1161   FS-Merge (Eq. 4), with the features of these two original models as targets. The M and U matrices
1162   are still modeled as a concatenation of diagonal matrices plus a low-rank matrix, and we apply the
1163   “First” initialization (initialized from the first model).
1164
       After merging the first two models, we use global FS-Merge again to merge the resulting model
1165   (capable of solving the first two tasks) with the third model. In this phase, we use the features of
1166   all models seen so far (the first, second, and third models) as targets, and initialize from the merged
1167   model obtained in the previous step. This process is repeated, merging the previous merged model
1168   with a new original model at each step, until all n models are merged. Note that In FS-Merge Seq., at
1169   each phase, we only merge and load the weights of two models (the previous merged model and a
1170   new original model), even though all features and models are utilized.
1171   Experiments merging groups of four and five ViTs demonstrate that FS-Merge Seq. requires signifi-
1172   cantly less memory and compute resources, and merges models faster compared to regular FS-Merge.
1173   While this approach results in slightly lower accuracy compared to regular FS-Merge, FS-Merge Seq.
1174   still achieves better performance than distillation in most cases.

2084   H.1   M ERGING V ISION T RANSFORMERS : L OCAL VS. G LOBAL PERSPECTIVE
2085
2086
       Observe that in the MLP merging method from Section 2.1, the local version of FS-Merge was
2087
       used, which involves training a Foldable SuperNet individually for each layer. In the ViT case, local
2088   FS-Merge involves training each block of the Foldable SuperNet separately. For example, training
2089   a Foldable SuperNet that merges the first attention blocks, then training a Foldable SuperNet that
2090   merges the first MLP blocks, and so on. We found empirically that this approach leads to a poor
2091   solution in the ViT case, resulting in a dysfunctional merged model with accuracy nearly as poor as a
2092   random guess. Instead, we found that the global version of FS-Merge is much more effective in this
2093   case, involving training the entire Foldable SuperNet of the ViT to reconstruct the features of the last
2094   representation layer of the original models, fL .
2095   A few explanations exist for this issue. First, training the Foldable SuperNet in a local manner
2096   for the ViT, meaning block-wise, must be performed on very unnatural blocks, which “break” the
2097   transformer blocks. This is necessary because M and U matrices must be placed before or after a
2098   linear layer to allow them to be folded after training. Moreover, the attention score computation,
2099   layer normalization, and skip connections must be performed on the merged features (with the lower
2100   dimensionality). These conditions forced the “breaking” of existing ViT blocks, and, for example,
2101   required teaching the Foldable SuperNet of the attention block to reconstruct features that are within
2102
       the next MLP blocks. This complicated structure probably have hindered the optimization process.
2103   Second, the Foldable SuperNet consistently uses the merged features in the skip connection. This
2104   means that when learning block-wise, the features forwarded via skip connection to the next block
2105   are dramatically changed. These new merged features are very different from the original ones, which
       likely severely affected the optimization of the next Foldable SuperNet block.
