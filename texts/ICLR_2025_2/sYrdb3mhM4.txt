011                                                 A BSTRACT
012
013              Spatial transcriptomics (ST) has emerged as a promising technology to bridge
014              the gap between histology imaging and gene expression profiling. However, its
015              application to medical diagnosis is limited due to its low throughput and the need
016
                 for specialized experimental facilities. To address this issue, we develop STFlow1 ,
                 a flow-based generative model to predict spatial transcriptomics from whole-slide
017
                 histology images. STFlow is trained with a biologically-informed flow matching
018
                 algorithm that iteratively refines predicted gene expression values, where we choose
019              zero-inflated negative binomial distribution as a prior distribution to incorporate the
020              inductive bias of gene expression data. Compared to previous methods that predict
021              the gene expression of each spot independently, STFlow models the interaction of
022              genes across different spots to account for potential gene regulatory effects. On
023              a recently curated HEST-1k benchmark, we demonstrate STFlow substantially
024              outperforms all baselines including pathology foundation models, with over 18%
025              relative improvement over current state-of-the-art.

028   1       I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Compared to the early days of bulk RNA sequencing, recent advancements in spatial transcriptomics
031   (ST) technology offer a novel approach to molecular profiling within the spatial context of tissues,
032   providing insights into cellular interactions and the microenvironment (Ståhl et al., 2016; Xiao &
033
      Yu, 2021). One of the promising clinical applications of ST is the prediction of biomarkers in digital
      pathology, often visualized in hematoxylin and eosin (H&E)–stained whole-slide images (WSIs), by
034
      analyzing the gene expression levels in relation to the tissue morphology (Levy-Jurgenson et al., 2020;
035
      Zhang et al., 2022). However, the conventional ST methods (Moffitt et al., 2018; Eng et al., 2019;
036   Ståhl et al., 2016) are low throughput and rely on specialized equipment, limiting their availability
037   compared to standard histology imaging.
038
039
      To address this, recent works resort to deep learning to predict spatially-resolved gene expression
      from H&E images. As illustrated in Figure 1(a), a histology image is segmented into small spots,
040
      with the objective of predicting the gene expression with the spot image and the coordinate. This line
041
      of research has achieved promising results using either an image foundation model to encode local
042   spot-level features (Chen et al., 2024; He et al., 2020; Ciga et al., 2022) or an additional slide-level
043   encoder to incorporate global context (Xu et al., 2024; Chung et al., 2024). However, these methods
044   predict gene expression of each spot independently, thus overlooking the interaction between different
045   genes, i.e. certain genes regulating or influencing the expression of others (Li et al., 2022; Biancalani
046   et al., 2021). To consider such a regulatory effect, we must model the joint distribution over gene
047   expression of all spots in the image, which cannot be solved by single-step regression.
048   In light of this, we propose STFlow, a flow matching model that casts the original task as a generative
049   modeling problem. As shown in Figure 1(b), the denoiser network of STFlow learns a contextual-
050   ized representation of each spot that models gene interaction via a novel spatial attention module.
051   Starting from an initial gene expression sampled from the zero-inflated negative binomial (ZINB)
052   distribution (Virshup et al., 2023; Eraslan et al., 2019), STFlow iteratively refines its prediction with a

      WSI-based spatial gene expression prediction Rapid advances in spatial transcriptomics (ST) (Li
094
      & Wang, 2021) have enabled the detecting of RNA transcript spatial distribution at sub-cellular
095   resolution. This technology segments hematoxylin and eosin (H&E)–stained whole-slide images
096   (WSIs) into small spots, each providing a corresponding gene expression profile. Conventional ST
097   methods rely on in-situ hybridization techniques(Moffitt et al., 2018; Codeluppi et al., 2018; Eng
098   et al., 2019) or next-generation sequencing approaches (Ståhl et al., 2016; Stickels et al., 2021), which
099   are both costly and time-consuming.
100
      Machine learning-based approaches have recently shown promising results in this domain (Lee et al.,
101   2023). The previous studies fall into two categories: (1) spot-based approaches which solely encode
102   the spot and predict the gene expression individually, i.e., modeling p(Yi |Ii )2 (He et al., 2020; Pang
103   et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2024; Ciga et al., 2022; Xie et al., 2024). Some of these methods leverage
104   foundation models pretrained on large-scale digital pathology datasets, achieving promising results
105   in gene expression prediction (Jaume et al., 2024). (2) slide-based approaches which incorporate
106   the slide-level context and predict the gene expression of each spot individually, i.e., modeling
107

      p(Yi |I0 , · · · , IN ) (Pang et al., 2021; Zeng et al., 2021; Jia et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2024; Chung et al.,
109   2024). The main idea of these methods is to aggregate the representations of other spots after the
110   image encoders extract each spot’s features. The key difference between our proposed STFlow and
111   previous methods is that STFlow explicitly utilizes gene-gene dependency for prediction using a
112   generative model, i.e., modeling joint distribution p(Y0 , · · · , YN |I0 , · · · , IN ).
113
114   Flow matching Flow matching is a generative modeling paradigm (Lipman et al., 2022; Albergo &
115   Vanden-Eijnden, 2022; Liu et al., 2022; Jing et al., 2024; Nori & Jin, 2024) that has shown impressive
116   results across various modalities, including images and biomolecules. It defines a sequence of time-
117   dependent probability paths that transform data points from the real distribution to an interpolated
118   sample with a prior distribution. The objective is to approximate the marginal vector field of this path
119
      using a neural network. In this work, we repurpose the gene expression regression as a generative task
      and apply the flow matching since (1) its iterative denoising scheme allows us to incorporate the gene
120
      expression within the modeling, and (2) it offers flexibility in selecting a gene expression-specific
121
      prior distribution, i.e., zero-inflated negative binomial distribution.
122
123   Geometric deep learning Geometric deep learning has achieved significant success in chemistry,
124   physics, and biology (Bronstein et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023). The key to this
125   success lies in generating invariant representations for 3D structures, such as molecular conformations,
126   that remain consistent under E(n) transformations, where n represents the dimension of the Euclidean
127   space. E(n) transformations include translations, rotations, and reflections. Previous methods achieve
128   invariance by leveraging invariant features (Satorras et al., 2021; Schütt et al., 2018; Gasteiger et al.,
129   2021) or employing equivariant transformations, such as irreducible representations (Fuchs et al.,
130   2020; Liao & Smidt, 2022; Weiler & Cesa, 2019) and frame averaging (FA) (Puny et al., 2021; Huang
131
      et al., 2024). The architecture of the denoiser encodes the spatial context of whole-slide images
      (WSIs) using an FA-based Transformer architecture, designed to produce invariant representations
132
      for each spot, regardless of any E(2) transformations.

      Gene interaction is essential for determining the gene expression level. Our key hypothesis is that
180
      the expression levels of certain genes in neighboring regions can strongly indicate the target spot’s
181
      expression (Li et al., 2022; Biancalani et al., 2021; Cordell, 2009). However, this poses a "chicken-
182   and-egg" challenge: the gene-gene dependency we aim to incorporate relies on gene expression as
183   context, which is also what we seek to predict. To address this, we repurpose the gene expression
184   regression model into a generative model, using samples from a prior distribution as input, which is
185   then iteratively optimized instead of performing a one-step prediction.
186
      Specifically, we apply flow matching (Lipman et al., 2022; Albergo & Vanden-Eijnden, 2022) as the
187
      optimization framework, which aims to learn a denoised model fθ (·):

      3.3   D ENOISER A RCHITECTURE fθ
217
218   The STFlow’s denoiser receives visual features Z, coordinates I, and gene expression Yt at time
219   step t as input. The backbone is based on the Transformer architecture (Vaswani, 2017), achieving
220   E(2)-invariance to the coordinates by incorporating frame averaging (FA) within each layer and
221   explicitly encoding spatial dependencies by conducting attention to each spot’s local neighbors.
222
223   Local Spatial Context Cells within the tissues can interact and influence each other’s gene expression,
224
      thereby forming a spatial context with spot-to-spot dependencies. To efficiently leverage such
      dependencies, we encode the local spatial context around each spot i and limit the attention to its
225
      k-nearest neighbors, i.e., N (i), in the WSI. Long-range context information can be captured through
226
      multi-layer attention within the local neighbors of every spot.
227

283   Notes on invariance The Equ.7 demonstrates E(2)-invariance to the coordinates as it encodes and
284
      averages the coordinates across different frames, which is guaranteed by frame averaging framework.
      Consequently, the spatial attention mechanism (Equ.9 and Equ.10) that relies on the output of Equ.7
285
      is E(2)-invariant. However, our proposed attention scheme doesn’t guarantee the invariance of
286
      transformations applied directly to the raw H&E images since we use the embeddings learned by
287   pathology foundation models that are not E(2)-invariant.
288
289   Computational Complexity For spatial attention, FA is efficient due to the low dimensionality of
290   the coordinates (only 2) and the accelerated PCA algorithm, thus we ignore its complexity. The
291   attention calculation involves neighboring spots and linear transformations, resulting in a complexity
292   of O(N kd + N kd2 ), where d is the embedding size, and is efficient since k ≪ N . With flow
293   matching, the computation scales linearly with the number of refinement steps S. In practice, this
294   remains efficient as flow matching requires relatively few steps, a key advantage over diffusion models.
295
      In our experiments, we set S to 5. A wall-clock time comparison can be found in Appendix B.

376   E(2)-Invariant Architecture Comparison To demonstrate the effectiveness of our proposed E(2)-
377   invariant denoiser, we implement two representative E(n)-invariant architectures and replace our
      proposed architecture with them individually:


                                                                                            7
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


378
      • EGNN (Satorras et al., 2021) is a representative E(n) graph neural network that leverages invariant
379     geometric feature distance between coordinates to ensure representation invariance. The model
380     conducts representation aggregation among the k-nearest neighbors for each spot. For a fair
381     comparison, EGNN also receives the input of extracted image features as spot features.
382
      • E2CNN (Weiler & Cesa, 2019) is a representative framework for E(n) convolutional neural
383
        networks that utilizes irreducible representations. In our implementation, we use the extracted
384     features as input channels and construct a tensor of neighboring spots centered around the target
385     spots. This tensor is then fed into a ResNet model built using E2CNN.
386
387   More details regarding the hyperparameters and implementation can be found in Appendix A. The
388   comparison results are presented in Table 3, where we observe that STFlow’s performance decreases
389   to varying degrees when using EGNN or E2CNN as replacements in most cases. We attribute this to
390   the fact that the geometric features used in these models are either simple, as in the case of distances
391
      in EGNN, or extracted through constrained functions, such as group steerable kernels in E2CNN.
      In contrast, FA-based transformation directly leverages the direction vectors, allowing the model to
392
      automatically learn relevant geometric features in the latent space.

468   5    C ONCLUSION
469
470   In this paper, we study the problem of gene expression prediction from histology images. Despite the
471   promising results achieved by the previous methods, we argue that gene interaction which is a key
472   factor regulating gene expression has been overlooked. Motivated by this, we propose STFlow, a
473   flow matching framework incorporating gene-gene dependency with an iterative refinement paradigm.
474   The zero-inflated negative binomial distribution is applied as the prior distribution for utilizing the
475   inductive bias of the gene expression data. Specifically, the denoiser architecture is a frame-averaging
476
      Transformer that integrates spatial context and gene interactions within the attention mechanism. Our
      experimental results across 10 benchmarks show that STFlow consistently outperforms the SOTA
477
      baseline methods.
478
479
      Limitation Our learning framework does not currently include the estimation of the hyperparameters
480   for the ZINB distribution; instead, we use a grid search to identify the optimal hyperparameter
481   combination. A potential improvement would be to initially employ the empirical distribution or
482   a distribution estimation model, such as a Variational Autoencoder (VAE), to estimate the ZINB
483   hyperparameters based on the training set.
484

      Running environment The experiments are conducted on a single Linux server with The AMD
651   EPYC 7763 64-Core Processor, 1024G RAM, and 8 RTX A6000-48GB. Our method is implemented
652   on PyTorch 2.3.0 and Python 3.10.14.
653
654   Training details For all the models, we fix the optimizer as Adam (Kingma & Ba, 2014) and MSE
655   loss as the loss function. The gradient norm is clipped to 1.0 in each training step to ensure learning
656   stability. The learning rate is tuned within {1e-3, 5e-4, 1e-4} and is set to 5e-4 by default, as it
657   generally yields the best performance. Following HEST-1k, all performance metrics are reported
658   using a cross-validation setup, with the mean and standard deviation calculated across the different
659   splits. Besides, all the weights of pathology foundation models are frozen.
660   For each model, we search the hyperparameters in the following ranges: the dropout rate in {0, 0.2,
661   0.5}, the number of nearest neighbors for the slide-based methods in {4, 8, 25}, and the number of
662   attention heads in {1, 2, 4, 8}. All models are trained for 100 epochs, with early stopping applied if
663   no performance improvement is observed for 20 epochs. The implementation and hyperparameters
664   used in each method are shown below:
665
666
      • STFlow: The number of layers, attention heads, and neighbors are 4, 4, and 8, respectively. Besides,
        dropout and hidden sizes are set at 0.2 and 128. The number of sampling steps for flow matching is
667
        set as 5. For the ZINB distribution, zero-inflation probability is fixed as 0.5, the mean is searched
668
        {0.1,0.2,0.4}, and the number of failures is searched in {1,2,4}. For efficient training, each sample
669     is a randomly selected continuous region from the WSI, with its size determined by a proportion
670     sampled from a uniform distribution ranging from 0 to 1. In each training step, we will sample a
671     region from WSI.
672
      • Ciga4 , UNI5 , and Gigapath6 : We download the pretrained weight from the official repository and
673     normalize the input images using the ImageNet mean and standard deviation. The Random Forest
674     model with 70 trees serves as the linear head. Additionally, Gigapath offers three different pretrained
675     versions; we selected the one with the largest hidden size, i.e., "gigapath_slide_enc12l1536d". For
676     the Gigapath-slide, all the spot images of a WSI are input for global attention.
677   • STNet7 : Following the official implementation, we use a pretrained DenseNet121 as the image
678     encoder and an MLP as the linear head. The input spot images are randomly augmented with
679     horizontal flips and rotations and are then normalized using the ImageNet mean and standard
680     deviation. The batch size, i.e., the number of spot images in each training step, is 128.
681   • BLEEP8 : This method trains an image encoder and a gene expression encoder using contrastive
682     loss. For a given spot image, it retrieves the gene expressions of similar spots from a reference
683     set, using the average expression of these spots as the prediction. We use a pretrained ResNet50
684     as the image encoder and MLPs as linear heads to project the extracted visual features and gene
685     expressions. The temperature for the contrastive loss is set to 1, and the number of retrieved spots
686     is 50. For a fair comparison, we directly use the training WSI as the reference set, as there are no
687     additional splits in HEST-1k. The batch size is set as 128.
688   • Hist2ST9 : The architecture of Hist2ST includes a convolution network, a Transformer, and a GNN.
689     The coordinates are embedded with a linear layer. The final representation is aggregated across
690     each GNN layer’s output with an LSTM. The number of layers for each model is 2, 4, and 8.
691     The input spot images are randomly augmented with horizontal flips and rotations and are then
692
        normalized using the ImageNet mean and standard deviation. Similar to STFlow, each training
        sample is a sampled region of WSI.

695     layers, the number of attention heads, the dropout rate, and the hidden size are set as 4, 16, 0.1,
