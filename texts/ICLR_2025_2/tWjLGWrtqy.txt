                                                   A BSTRACT
012
013            The dissemination of Large Language Models (LLMs), trained at scale, and en-
014
               dowed with powerful text-generating abilities has vastly increased the threats posed
               by generative AI technologies by reducing the cost of producing harmful, toxic,
015
               faked or forged content. In response, various proposals have been made to auto-
016
               matically discriminate artificially generated from human-written texts, typically
017            framing the problem as a classification problem. Most approaches evaluate an input
018            document by a well-chosen detector LLM, assuming that low-perplexity scores
019            reliably signal machine-made content. As using one single detector can induce brit-
020            tleness of performance, we instead consider several and derive a new, theoretically
021            grounded approach to combine their respective strengths. Our experiments, using
022            a variety of generator LLMs, suggest that our method effectively leads to robust
023            detection performances.

026   1    I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Large Language Models (LLMs) have greatly improved the fluency and diversity of machine-
029   generated texts. The release of ChatGPT and GPT-4 by OpenAI has sparked global discussions
030   regarding the effective use of AI-based writing assistants. This progress has also introduced consider-
031
      able threats such as fake news generation Zellers et al. (2019), and the potential for harmful outputs
      such as toxic or dishonest content (Crothers et al., 2023), among others. As it seems, the research on
032
      methods aimed at detecting the origin of a given text to mitigate the dissemination of forged content
033
      and to prevent technology-aided plagiarism still lag behind the rapid advancement of AI itself.1
034
035   Many works have focused on tools that could spot such AI-generated outputs and address these
036   underlying risks. From a bird’s eye view, this typically involves using detector models to discriminate
037
      generator models’ outputs from legitimate human writings. Multiple versions of this generic text
      classification task have been considered, varying e.g. the number of possible categories to distinguish
038
      and the amount of supervision (see Section 5). Owing to its large user base and applications, the
039
      largest effort has focused on one specific generator, ChatGPT, for which training and test data is easily
040   obtained. Yet, the corresponding supervised binary problem, with a unique known generator, is not
041   the only way to frame this task. A more challenging problem, that we study here, is generator-agnostic
042   artificial text detection, where the models to detect are not known in advance.
043
      As pointed out e.g., in (Antoun et al., 2024; Hans et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024a), the performance
044
      of artificial text detection systems varies depending on the choices of the detector(s) / generator(s)
045
      pair. The detector may serve to assess text probabilities, as in (Mitchell et al., 2023; Bao et al., 2024),
046   or to regenerate texts, as e.g., in (Mao et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2024). This implies that the search
047   for optimal detection performance should include a systematic exploration of the space of possible
048   detectors. As the number and diversity of LLMs keep increasing, such exploration seems not only
049   challenging but also unrealistic. Furthermore, Dugan et al. (2024) demonstrated that the current
050   detection methods are brittle and easily fooled by simply changing the generator or the associated
051   sampling method, which means that the optimal detector may need to be periodically revised.

081   Our contributions. In this paper, using fundamental information-theoretic principles from universal
082   compression, we derive a new ensemble score that optimally combines the strength of multiple LLMs
083
      to detect forged texts. Our experiments use both standard benchmarks comprising multiple domains
      and languages, as well as a new corpus of machine-generated texts. They confirm that ensembling
084
      strong LLMs yields detectors that can robustly identify a multiplicity of generators and that compare
085
      favorably with several recent proposals using a predefined set of detector models. Our analyses
086   explore the effect of incrementally adding models into the ensemble, and also highlight, using a
087   multilingual dataset, how the contribution of each constituent model changes when the source of
088   artificial texts is modified.

303   3.2   BASELINES
304
305   Machine-generated text detection methods are usually divided into two main categories (Dugan et al.,
306   2024): supervised and unsupervised (metric-based). The former uses supervision data to fine-tune a
307
      pre-trained model for the detection task, typically focusing on some known generators. Our method
      belongs to the latter family, as the MOSAIC score of Eq. (2), which serves to discriminate forged
308
      content, does not require any training data. Accordingly, we compare our method to other zero-shot
309
      unsupervised techniques used for machine-generated text detection:
310
311   Perplexity (PPL) based detectors use a threshold on the text’s log-perplexity, assuming that LLMs
312   usually generate texts that have a lower perplexity than human’s productions (see Vasilatos et al., 2023;
313
      Guo et al., 2023; Mitrović et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024 inter alia). This yields a very straightforward
      criterion for detecting machine-generated texts. We compute this baseline separately for all available
314
      models in our ensemble. Additionally, PPL (average) reports the performance obtained with an
315
      average of all perplexities within our ensemble.
316
317   DetectGPT (Mitchell et al., 2023) generates minor perturbations of a given text passage, then
318   computes the differences in log-probability between the original text and its perturbed versions.
319
      DetectGPT relies on the observation that when slightly perturbed, the log-probability of artificial
      texts consistently decreases, which is not the case for human-generated texts. We used the default

396   An interesting follow-up question is about the respective strength of each detector model: can this
397   be measured using the Blahut-Arimoto weights? For our ensemble, we find that all models get a
398   reasonable (and varying) share of these weights. We see however that Llama-7b instruct consistently
399   receives very large weights (see Figure 4a in Appendix). For the multilingual tests from M4, we
400   observe that the weights of the TowerBase models tend to increase, as compared to when just looking
401   at English texts (see Figure 2 in Appendix). This illustrates the benefits of using complementary
402
      models, each one with its own domain of “expertise”.

406   high perplexity for all models, which makes them difficult to discriminate from human texts (see
407   Figure 3 in the Appendix) with perplexity-based models. This holds similarly for our ensemble
408

409
      including more domain-adapted models in detector ensembles.

449   4.3      AUGMENTING THE ENSEMBLE
450
451   With a Strong Model. MOSAIC makes the augmentation of the ensemble quite easy, as long as
452
      all models use the same underlying tokenizer. To showcase the effect of this feature, we add Phi-3
      (Abdin et al., 2024) in our ensemble and experiment with Binoculars and Ghostbuster datasets. In
453
      this experimental setting, this extension of the ensemble is of little consequence for the former test,
454
      and provides clear gains for the latter (see lines “+phi” in Appendix, Table 9). Accordingly, we
455   observe that Phi consistently gets a substantial share of the Blahut–Arimoto weights (see Figure 5 in
456   Appendix) and plays a significant role in the classification decision.
457
458   With a Weak Model. As noted by Hans et al. (2024), simple random generators are hard to detect
459   for Binoculars (as well as for detectors using a PPL-based threshold): this is because random word
460   salads are “surprising” (have a high perplexity) for well-trained detectors, and tend to be confused
461   with human productions. We reproduce this observation using the corpus generated with a unigram
462   model and observe that all baselines, as well as our proposed detector, achieve AUROC scores close
463   to 0 for this dataset. The PPL of such “word salads" is however much larger than for human texts, so
464   setting an upper bound of the human PPL would, in that case, provide a very easy fix.
465   It is tempting to see whether adding such a poor generator into our combination would be of any help.
466   For this experiment, we reuse the generator unigram language model and combine it with our four
467   baseline detectors. The detector obtained with this extended ensemble remains unable to sort random
468   from human texts: having the random model in the MOSAIC algorithm does not make random texts
469   more likely. The added unigram model is also slightly detrimental for detecting strong generators, as
470   we observe a mild drop in performance compared to using just 4 models (see Appendix, Table 9).
471
      This is because the unigram model predicts unexpected tokens most of the time and therefore often
      gets a significant weight in the Blahut–Arimoto weights (see Figure 6 in Appendix), which leads to a
472
      huge discrepancy between the cross-entropies scores that are averaged in Definition 1. However, this
473
      effect remains small; even with this weak model added, our ensemble detector remains rather strong.

476   5        R ELATED W ORK
477
478   The improved text generation abilities of LLMs raise concerns about potential misuses such as disin-
479   formation (Zellers et al., 2019), abusive content (Crothers et al., 2023), forged academic publications
480
      (Liu et al., 2024), or cheating during exams (Vasilatos et al., 2023). Since such fake texts seem
      difficult for humans to spot (Gehrmann et al., 2019), the issue of automatically detecting machine-
481
      generated texts has been subject to an increasing focus. This problem can be framed as a binary
482
      human vs. non-human decision, as the problem of detecting one known artificial agent (e.g., ChatGPT
483   (Mitrović et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024)), or as discriminating the correct model in a predefined list (Li
484
          10
485       We divide the model logits by the temperature, then apply softmax and perform nucleus filtering with
      p = 0.9. The resulting distribution is smoothed so out-of-nucleus tokens still get a small positive value.


                                                           9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025



486
      et al., 2023). Another distinction is between closed-domain (e.g. scientific (Liyanage et al., 2022),
487
      academic (Liu et al., 2024) or user-generated content (Fagni et al., 2021; Kumarage et al., 2023))
488
      vs. open-domain text detection. Assuming the generator models are known, various settings can be
489   considered, depending on whether models can be openly queried (open parameters), whether they
490   expose their full logits, or just the top prediction (and associated probability), etc.
491
492
      Supervised detection with a single generator often achieves detection rates in the high 90s (Zellers
      et al., 2019; Guo et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024), using classifiers based on Roberta (Conneau et al.,
493
      2020) or T5 (Raffel et al., 2020). However, these approaches are brittle and their success depends on
494
      particular generator-detector pairs (Antoun et al., 2024), prompting e.g. Verma et al. (2023) to design
495   automatic feature extractors from multiple detectors to improve the robustness of their system.
496
497   Unsupervised detection is more challenging. Most approaches rest on the idea that human-
498
      written texts are more “surprising” than artificial texts11 , leading to a difference in token-wise
      log-probability12 . This idea is used in GPTzero13 and thresholding perplexity usually provides strong
499
      baselines (see, inter alia, (Gehrmann et al., 2019; Ippolito et al., 2020; Mitchell et al., 2023)). Such
500
      techniques heavily rely on the detector model(s) used to compute the log-probabilities of input texts,
501   which must be robust to variations in domains, genres, styles, and languages (Wang et al., 2024a);
502   and to variations in the generator itself (Antoun et al., 2024).
503
504
      Mitchell et al. (2023) and Bao et al. (2024) exploit a similar intuition, arguing that small random
      perturbations of an artificial text will on average make it less likely, unlike human-written texts.
505
      They derive a statistical criterion based on the curvature of the log-probability function, and report
506
      near-perfect detection scores on three types of texts, generated by 5 models. The Binoculars score
507   of Hans et al. (2024) also relies on a function of the per-token log-perplexity, contrasted with the
508   cross-entropy of an auxiliary model.
509
510
      These valuable works point to the over-reliance on one specific detector model as a major
      limitation of the state-of-the-art. Our proposed mitigation relies on ensemble techniques, that are
511
      also considered in the supervised detection setting, e.g. in (Verma et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023;
512
      El-Sayed and Nasr, 2023; Liyanage and Buscaldi, 2023).
513
514   Abandoning generator-detector-based techniques altogether, (Mao et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2024)
515   develop effective detection approaches based on regeneration, prompting the (known) generator to
516
      regenerate part of the input text. The intuition is that artificial inputs are likely to be regenerated
      exactly, while human texts exhibit greater redundancy, resulting in the need for longer code-
517
      lengths. Other strategies include text watermarking (Kirchenbauer et al., 2023a;b; Liu and Bu, 2024),
518
      though its efficiency and robustness are still subject to discussions, e.g., (Zhang et al., 2023).
519
520   Recent works focus on detection robustness. Wang et al. (2024b) find that after simple modifications,
521   only watermarking remains able to accurately identify artificial documents. Dugan et al. (2024)
522
      present artificial texts generated with multiple models and sampling strategies, additionally subject to
      various adversarial attacks, observing that most detectors suffer large drops in performance. In their
523
      comparison, Binoculars (Hans et al., 2024) stands out, achieving decent detection scores at False
524
      Positive Rates under 1%.

527   6    S UMMARY AND D ISCUSSION
528
529   Our MOSAIC method effectively harnesses the ensemble’s strength, achieving great results across
530   datasets and languages, eliminating the need to find the best detector while offering a scalable solution
531   that can incorporate future models. However, it is currently computationally costly, as each model
532   must run on the text (while not the goal of this work, some improvements are proposed in Section D).
533
      We also need to develop information-theoretic tools to select the most useful models and filter out less
      effective ones, as shown with the unigram model. Similarly, the proximity of models needs further
534
      study, as we suspect our detectors are too far from the fine-tuned model’s generating distribution used
535
      to create the academic dataset, hence why it evades detection.

959   B.2     C OMPUTATIONAL COMPLEXITY
960
      The computational complexity of the Blahut-Arimoto algorithm can be characterized as follows:
961
962           • Number of iterations. The algorithm typically converges linearly, so the number of
963             iterations required, denoted as T , is proportional to the desired accuracy of the solution.
964
              • Operations per iteration. Each iteration involves updating the probability measures in (21)
965
                and (22), and evaluating the mutual information, which requires matrix manipulations. Let
966             M and N be the cardinalities of the input and output alphabets, respectively. Each iteration
967             involves operations overall input-output pairs, requiring O(M × N ) operations.
968
969   Combining these, the overall computational complexity of the Blahut-Arimoto algorithm is O(T ×
970   n × m), reflecting its dependence on the sizes of M (number of LLMs in the considered family) and
971   N (the vocabulary), and the number of iterations needed for convergence, which depends intrinsically
      on the underlying distributions.

       D    C OMPLEXITY I MPROVEMENTS
988
       Our algorithm currently processes each text in approximately 10 seconds on NVIDIA 32G V100
989
       GPUs. Runtime optimization is an area that should be improved in future work. Below, we outline
990
       limitations of our system and propose potential improvements : In MOSAIC, the texts are processed
991    one-by-one by the LLMs. Each model is loaded onto a separate GPU, and the logits are moved to
992    a central device for performing operations such as Blahut-Arimoto, perplexity, and cross-entropy
993    calculations, after which the final score is computed. This setup has several inefficiencies. For
994    instance, transferring logits to a central device introduces a significant bottleneck. Additionally, while
995    calculations are performed on one GPU, the remaining ones remain idle, resulting in suboptimal use
996    of resources.
997    A more efficient method would involve computing the logits for all texts in parallel, storing them
998    across different GPUs, and performing subsequent calculations concurrently. An even more stream-
999    lined solution would involve loading all models onto a single GPU using quantized or distilled
1000   versions, thus eliminating the need to transfer logits across devices.
1001
       While these optimizations are promising, they have not been implemented in this work, as we focus
1002
       on the algorithmic methodology rather than runtime efficiency.
