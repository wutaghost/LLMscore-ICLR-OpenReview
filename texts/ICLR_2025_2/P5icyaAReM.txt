009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               Contrastive learning has proven to be a powerful paradigm for self-supervised
012            representation learning, yet traditional methods often rely on arbitrary definitions
013            of positive and negative pairs, requiring large batch sizes to manage the tradeoff
014            between contrastive terms effectively. This approach wastes significant compu-
015            tational resources on negative pairs that contribute minimal learning signals. To
016            address these limitations, we propose a novel method that reformulates contrastive
017            learning as a matrix approximation problem using I-divergence, a non-normalized
018            variant of Kullback-Leibler divergence. Our objective function is decomposable
019            across instance pairs, enabling efficient stochastic approximation algorithms that
020
               perform well with fewer negative samples by leveraging neighbor embeddings.
               Additionally, we generalize the scaling factor beyond standard normalization to
021
               adaptively emphasize positive pairs with higher learning potential, reducing com-
022
               putational waste from negative pairs. Experimental results on benchmark datasets
023            such as CIFAR and ImageNet demonstrate that our method outperforms existing
024            contrastive learning approaches, particularly with small batch sizes and as few as
025            one negative pair, highlighting its effectiveness and computational efficiency.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Learning meaningful representations is fundamental to the success of machine learning systems
032   (Bengio et al., 2013; Goodfellow et al., 2016). While conventional supervised approaches have
033
      dominated in recent years, driven by the increasing availability of labeled data, they face scalability
      challenges and are constrained by the need for extensive labeled examples (LeCun et al., 2015;
034
      Balestriero et al., 2023). Self-supervised learning (SSL) has emerged as a transformative paradigm,
035
      leveraging the inherent structure of unlabeled data to generate pseudo-labels (Liu et al., 2023; Jing
036   & Tian, 2020; de Sa, 1993; Goyal et al., 2019). In domains such as computer vision, SSL has
037   demonstrated the capability to rival or even surpass supervised pretraining in effectiveness (Tomasev
038   et al., 2022; Goyal et al., 2019; He et al., 2020; Caron et al., 2020; Misra & Maaten, 2020).
039
      Despite its promise, most contrastive learning methods, a cornerstone of SSL, require large batch
040
      sizes to manage the balance between positive and negative pairs, leading to significant computational
041
      inefficiencies. Specifically, substantial resources are spent on negative pairs that contribute minimal
042   learning signals. Recent advancements, such as SogCLR (Yuan et al., 2022), address some of these
043   inefficiencies by employing an exponentially moving average (EMA) within a decoupled contrastive
044   learning objective (DCL; Yeh et al., 2022), omitting the explicit positive-pair term in their imple-
045   mentation. However, SogCLR introduces additional complexity by requiring EMA-updated scalars
046   for each data instance, which can become inconvenient for large datasets.
047
      To overcome these challenges and enable efficient mini-batch training, we reformulate contrastive
048   learning as a matrix approximation problem using a non-normalized Kullback-Leibler divergence
049   with a scaling factor. Our proposed objective function is decomposable across instance pairs, allow-
050   ing the development of efficient stochastic approximation algorithms that perform effectively with
051   significantly fewer negative samples. Furthermore, we establish a theoretical connection between
052   our approach and SimCLR while extending beyond it by generalizing the scaling factor. This gener-
053   alization enables dynamic prioritization of positive pairs that provide richer learning signals, thereby
      reducing computational waste associated with negative pairs and improving overall efficiency.

054
      We conducted experiments on widely recognized vision benchmark datasets, including CIFAR and
055   ImageNet, to assess the effectiveness of our approach. Our method was evaluated against sev-
056   eral state-of-the-art contrastive learning techniques, demonstrating consistent superiority. The re-
057   sults show that our approach is not only more computationally efficient—requiring smaller train-
058   ing batches and fewer negative pairs—but also achieves higher accuracy and overall performance
059   compared to competing methods. These findings underscore the potential of our approach as a
060   cost-effective and high-performing solution for vision-related tasks.

      2     BACKGROUND AND RELATED WORK

      2.1   C ONTRASTIVE METHODS
065
066   Contrastive Learning (CL) (Bromley et al., 1993; Hadsell et al., 2006; Chopra et al., 2005; Gutmann
067   & Hyvärinen, 2010; Mikolov et al., 2013; Oord et al., 2018; Chen et al., 2020a; Sohn, 2016) has
068   historically been influential in representation learning and is based on push- and pull mechanisms
069   between representations. There are supervised approaches (Khosla et al., 2020) which finds positive
070   pairs based on class labels and unsupervised SSL approaches (Chen et al., 2020a; He et al., 2020;
071   Caron et al., 2020; Wu et al., 2018; Misra & Maaten, 2020; Dosovitskiy et al., 2014) which generate
072
      positive pairs.
073   The Contrastive loss (Bromley et al., 1993; Hadsell et al., 2006; Chopra et al., 2005) and Triplet loss
074   (Schroff et al., 2015; Weinberger & Saul, 2009) persisted on mining strategies for tricky negative
075   pairs to be effective. The Triplet loss configured triplets of one positive and negative sample and was
076   generalized to M negative samples instead in Sohn (2016). SimCLR (Chen et al., 2020a) applied
077   this contrastive loss in SSL, which is similar to CPC’s (Oord et al., 2018) InfoNCE, in a full batch
078
      mode setting where the other positive pairs from the batch configured negative pairs. However
      SimCLR’s CL-loss is not decomposable in minibatch-optimization mode because the negative pairs
079
      are weighted relatively in the minibatch (Chen et al., 2022; 2020a). An unfortunate effect of this is
080
      that it becomes vulnerable in minibatch-optimization, and this degradation of performance is shown
081   in Chen et al. (2020a). Several CL approaches have resorted to memory-banks (He et al., 2020;
082   Chen et al., 2020b; Caron et al., 2020; Misra & Maaten, 2020) or unreasonable batchsizes (Chen
083   et al., 2020a) and there are sampling strategies (Kalantidis et al., 2020; Robinson et al., 2020), but
084   this can be computationally challenging.
085
      Improving the affordability of contrastive representation learning is still an active research area, and
086
      recent related work include among others Yeh et al. (2022); Yuan et al. (2022); Qiu et al. (2023);
087   Chen et al. (2022); Sharma et al. (2023); Shah et al. (2021); HaoChen et al. (2021). DeCL (Chen
088   et al., 2022) consider the non-decomposibility of SimCLR’s CL-loss and address the gradient is-
089   sues. A decomposable spectral contrastive loss was proposed by HaoChen et al. (2021). DCL (Yeh
090   et al., 2022) propose to remove the positive pair from the negative forces which show faster con-
091   vergence and better results over CL baselines with/without memory-banks. SogCLR (Yuan et al.,
092   2022) propose a global contrastive loss by mixing a zero-initialized running average with a local
093   minibatch-estimate of the negative pairs and shows improvement over SimCLR on ImageNet and
094   CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) in the multi-modal setting. SogCLR is improved in Qiu et al. (2023)
095
      with individualized temperatures. AUC-CL (Sharma et al., 2023) propose to combine contrastive
      learning with AUC maximization.

      2.3    N EIGHBOR EMBEDDING METHODS
126
127   Neighbor embedding (Hinton & Roweis, 2002) (NE) is set of approaches which use the neigh-
128   borhood graph from the input space as pseudo-labels for the mapped representations. Two pair-
129   wise probability matrices P and Q denotes the neighborhood graphs where Pij and Qij respec-
130   tively gives the probability of i and j being neighbor in the input-space and representation-space
131   (Hinton & Roweis, 2002; van der Maaten & Hinton, 2008). To obtain this approximation a di-
132   vergence D(P ||Q) is minimized between the pairwise probabilities. Initially SNE (Hinton &
133   Roweis, 2002)P normalized the probabilities row-wise which means minimizing a sum of divergences

137   It is conceivable a practical difficulty to compute the normalized probabilities P and Q. Most solu-
138   tions can evaluate P offline however the probabilities in Q have to be re-normalized each iteration
139   of optimization. Sampling based approaches UMAP (McInnes et al., 2018), TriMap (Amid & War-
140   muth, 2019), LargeVis (Tang et al., 2016), SCE (Yang et al., 2023) and PaCMAP (Wang et al., 2021)

175   3.2   C ONTRASTIVE L EARNING L OSS F UNCTION
176
      SCE cannot be directly applied to contrastive learning due to several limitations: (1) SCE relies on
177
      fixed input similarities and cannot generalize to data points outside the training set; (2) its similari-
178   ties are typically computed using simple metrics like Euclidean distances, which are inadequate for
179   complex data objects such as images; and (3) SCE lacks mini-batch training algorithms tailored for
180   contrastive learning. To address these issues, we introduce three key modifications: (1) we param-

182   network weights; (2) we define similarities based on data and augmented view indices, eliminating
183   the dependency on Euclidean or similar metrics for neighbor search before embedding; and (3) we
184   propose stochastic approximation algorithms for the resulting learning objective and scaling factor.

411   5   A BLATION STUDIES
412
413
      We perform ablations to see the effect of different forgetting rates, weighting rates, global compared
414   to individualized adaptive scaling parameters and M negative samples per anchor in Appendix A.4.
415   The findings are that our methods are robust against hyperparameters and fewer negative samples.
416
417
      This work presented a matrix-method with a one global adaptive scaling factor and a row-method
      with individualized adaptive scaling factors. We study the impact of the respective methods in Table
418
      14 in Appendix. Surprisingly we find that the matrix-method performs better or evenly to the row-
419
      method which has a cost of more variables. Visual example is shown in Figure 3 where we log the
420   positive and negative values with the estimated partition function(s) after each epoch. While the row-
421   method can offer a more specific estimate especially in the early stages it might seem to converge
422   to a similar value in the later stages. We also observe a negligible difference between SACLR with
423   M = 1 negative sample compared to the full batch mode version where M = 2 × 128 − 2.
424
      The hyperparameters of most importance are the forgetting rate ρ and weighting rate α, in addition
425
      to initialization of scaling factors. We tune the forgetting rate ρ on Imagenette in Table 12 and Table
426   13 in Appendix. We find that ρ = 0.99 works best for the matrix-method and ρ = 0.9 for the row-
427   method. The matrix-method re-estimates the scaling factor after each minibatch which is contrary
428   to the row-method which re-estimates per instance approximately at each epoch, see Alg.(1) and
429   Alg.(2) for more details. A consequence of this can be a necessity to use a higher forgetting rate,
430   which is demonstrated in Table 13 with a linear classifier. The weighting rate α is ablated in Table
431   15 on ImageNet for SACLR-1. We initially set α = 0.5 and see that setting a lower weighting-rate
      α = 0.125 gives better performance.

464   6                  C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
465
466   We have approached contrastive learning as a matrix approximation problem. A key component
467   of our method is an adaptive scaling factor, which optimizes separable similarities and enables
468   memory-efficient stochastic approximation algorithms. This allows our approach to require signifi-
469   cantly smaller training batches and as few as one negative pair, making it substantially more efficient
470
      than other contrastive learning techniques. Despite its economical design, our method has demon-
      strated the effectiveness of SACLR on CIFAR and ImageNet classification tasks, with consistent
471
      performance improvements to the compared methods.
472
473   In the future, our work could be extended in several promising directions. One potential area of
474   improvement is integrating contextual learning within individual data instances, in addition to the
475   contrastive learning across data populations. By capturing more detailed relationships within a
476
      single data sample, such as spatial or sequential patterns, we could significantly enhance the model’s
      ability to learn richer, more nuanced representations. This integration could improve performance
477
      in tasks that require fine-grained understanding, like object detection or temporal data analysis.
478
479   Another direction is replacing the traditional convolutional neural network with more advanced ar-
480   chitectures, such as self-attention-based models like the Transformer. Self-attention mechanisms
481   have shown remarkable success in capturing long-range dependencies and global patterns in var-
482
      ious domains. Incorporating this into our framework could lead to more expressive models that
      can handle more complex data types, such as video or multi-modal inputs, while further improving
483
      efficiency and performance.

      A.1     I MPLEMENTATION DETAILS I MAGE N ET

760
      A.1.1    DATASETS AND AUGMENTATIONS
761
762   ImageNet is an eminent image-dataset and contains color images from rich range of different classes.
763   This work use the subsets ImageNet1k (Russakovsky et al., 2015) (ILSVRC2012) which contains
764   1,281,167 training images from 1000 different classes, and ImageNet100 (Wu et al., 2019) which
765   contains 126,689 training images from 100 different classes. The pre-defined training and validation
766   split are used, and we evaluate our methods on the validation set, similar to existing work. For
767   ablations and hyperparameter tuning shorter runs and the image-dataset Imagenette (Howard, 2019)
768
      are used. Imagenette is a subset from ImageNet with 10 different classes. All images from ImageNet
      are resized to two (224 × 224) image views without any multi-crop from Caron et al. (2020). All
769
      images are applied the augmentations from SimCLR (Chen et al., 2020a) during pretraining, and we
770
      use the augmentation implementations from torchvision. We download ImageNet (ILSVRC2012)
771   from the official website and apart from that use the built-in datasets to torchvision.
772
773
      A.1.2    A RCHITECTURE
774
775   The neural network architecture in this work adhere to SimCLR (Chen et al., 2020a), and contains
776   a backbone encoder and a projector. The backbone encoder is a ResNet50 (He et al., 2016) and the
777   projector is the 3-layered MLP from VIC-REG (Bardes et al., 2021). The projector is applied on the
778   output of the ResNet’s avg-pooling layer and the output of each linear layer is 8192-d. We use the
779   ResNet architecture from torchvision.
780
781   A.1.3    O PTIMIZATION
782
783   The neural network optimizer in this work is the LARS-optimizer (You et al., 2017) with the
784   square-root
            √      learning rate scaling for low batchsizes proposed by SimCLR (Chen et al., 2020a)
785   lr = batchsize × 0.075, and weight decay wd = 10−4 . The learning rate is linearly warmed
786   up the 10 first epochs and then annealed with a cosine schedule (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2016) until it
787   reaches lr/1000. The default batchsize is 128.
788
789   A.1.4    L INEAR E VALUATION
790
791   Evaluation follows the linear classifier protocol on frozen backbone representations (Zhang et al.,
792   2016; Balestriero et al., 2023). This work keep the optimization settings from Caron et al. (2020);
793
      Zbontar et al. (2021) with SGD-optimizer, momentum = 0.9, wd = 10−6 and lr = 0.3. The
      learning rate is annealed with a cosine-scheduler down to zero over 100 epochs. During training we
794
      use random-cropping, -resizing and horizontal flipping, and we test with a center crop.
795
796
797   A.1.5    L OSS FUNCTION H YPERPARAMETERS
798
      This work set by default weighting rate α = 0.125. The forgetting rate is tuned ρ ∈
799
      {0.9, 0.99, 0.999} on Imagenette. The adaptive scaling parameter(s) are initialized such that the
800   partition function(s) at start correspond to 0.01, e.g. s = N −2 102 where N is the number of train-
801   ing images.
