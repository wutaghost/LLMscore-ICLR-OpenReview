009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            [Circuit decomposition and counterfactual-based pruning have become the corner-
012            stone framework for mechanism interpretability. However, the unfaithfulness to
013            the output due to cumulative bias in the pruning process hinders more complex and
014            detailed mechanism exploration. To address this, we propose a novel circuit dis-
015            covery framework that faithfully identifies circuit graphs. This framework contains
016            three steps: firstly, the language model is decomposed into a fully linear graph
017            consisting of disentangled “memory circuits”; secondly, greedy search is adopted
018
               to prune while ensuring output faithfulness; finally, we adopt causal analysis on the
               pruned circuit graph to identify salient circuit graph, estimated by counterfactuals
019
               and interventions. Our framework facilitates the discovery of complete circuit
020
               graphs and dissection of more complex mechanisms. To demonstrate this, we
021            explored three generic language skills (Previous Token Skill, Induction Skill and
022            In-Context Learning Skill). Using the circuit graphs discovered through our frame-
023            work, we identify the complete skill paths of these skills.] Our experiments on
024            various datasets confirm the correspondence between our identified skill paths and
025            language skills, and validate three longstanding hypotheses: 1) Language skills are
026            identifiable through circuit dissection; 2) Simple language skills reside in shallow
027            layers, whereas complex language skills are found in deeper layers; 3) Complex lan-
028            guage skills are formed on top of simpler language skills. Our codes are available
029
               at: https://anonymous.4open.science/r/language_skill.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
      [Mechanism interpretability (Elhage et al., 2021; Conmy et al., 2023) is becoming crucial for
034
      understanding how language models work. A common approach (Conmy et al., 2023; Yao et al.,
035
      2024; Syed et al., 2023; Bhaskar et al., 2024) involves breaking down the model into disentangled,
036   more linear components organized as a computational graph. By applying counterfactual techniques
037   and pruning, less important connections are removed, leaving behind a smaller “circuit graph” that
038   highlights the key components contributing to the model’s output.]
039
      [However, existing circuit discovery methods often fail to faithfully represent the output of the model.
040
      Specifically, substituting the model’s forward process with a circuit graph does not ensure that the
041
      predicted output token remains consistent with the original output of the language model. This lack
042   of faithfulness indicates that other yet-undiscovered circuits may significantly influence the output,
043   undermining the argument that the circuit graph fully captures the underlying mechanisms. The core
044   issue lies in the pruning strategies employed by these methods, which are typically optimized for
045   counterfactual scenarios. Decisions to remove an edge are based on the changes in logits between the
046   original output and a ‘corrupted output.’ As a result, the cumulative effect of removing many edges
047   introduces biases that can ultimately alter the model’s output.]
048   [To address this challenge, we propose a two-stage discovery process, decoupling faithful pruning and
049   causal discovery. In the first stage, we employ a greedy search algorithm to identify non-contributing
050   edges in the original circuit graph, under the condition that the original output remains the same
051   after performing each pruning step. This stage ensures a faithful pruning result, keeping the outputs
052   unchanged. The second stage identifies salient circuit graph using counterfactual and intervention
053   techniques. Additionally, to achieve more precise discovery, we completely dissect the transformer
      model into fully disentangled and linear components, known as “memory circuits”, with the addition

      of “compensation circuits” to account for the non-linearity of the MLP module within the transformer,
055   which has not been accomplished in previous works. In summary, our framework encompasses three
056   steps: complete linear circuit decomposition, faithful pruning, and causal analysis.]
057
058
      [Compared to existing methods, our approach has distinct advantages: the lossless and linear
      decomposition holds the potential to identify all components responding to a pattern, while faithful
059
      pruning and causal analysis enable us to dissect more complex patterns. To show the potential ability
060
      for discovering new insights, We select three generic and progressively complex skills which have
061   been introduced in (cro, 2024; Ren et al., 2024; Edelman et al., 2024; Olsson et al., 2022): a) Previous
062   Token skill which is responsible for receiving information from the previous token; b) Induction Skill
063   which duplicates tokens with the same prefix; and c) ICL Skill which perform inference based on
064   similar patterns appeared in demonstrations. Utilizing the circuit graph obtained from our three-step
065   framework, we unveil the complete skill paths of these skills. These skill paths have confirmed some
066   conjectures that have long remained unverified:]
067
068
            1. Identifiability: Language skills are identifiable through circuit dissection and correspond to
               different circuit paths.
069
070         2. Stratification: Simple language skills reside in shallow layers, whereas complex language
071            skills are found in deeper layers.
072         3. Inclusiveness: Complex language skills are formed on top of simpler language skills. For
073            example, the Induction skill, dealing with text formatted as “A B ... A” and producing “B” at
074            the end, requires the Previous Token skill to carry information from “A” to “B”. The ICL
075
               Skill likewise consists of the Induction Skill as an essential mechanism.
076
      In summary, our contributions are 3-fold:
077
078          • We propose a complete and faithful circuit discovery framework, providing a theoretical
079            basis for addressing the research gap in mechanism interpretability.
080          • We devise a 3-step framework to extract the paths of generic language skills in language
081            models.
082
             • Our analysis and experiments verify three properties among the Previous Token Skill,
083            Induction Skill, and ICL Skill, which include identifiability, stratification and inclusiveness.

      3     M ETHOD
109
110
      In this paper we propose a novel 3-step framework to extract the target language skills.
111
112          • Step 1 (Section 3.1): We decouple the architecture of transformer language models into a
113            combination of individual “Memory Circuits”, which independently represents the minimum
114            unit for reading memory. This results in a Complete Circuit Graph, G.
115          • Step 2 (Section 3.2): Keeping the destination token unchanged, we adopt greedy search to
116            remove redundant edges in G, retaining only those paths necessary for predicting the last
117            (destination) token and resulting in an Irreducible Circuit Graph, G∗.
118          • Step 3 (Section 3.3): We estimate the causal effect of each path in G∗ on the target skill
119            and select those paths rendering most significant changes as the skill paths. The final graph
120            formed by the skill paths is named as Skill Circuit Graph, denoted as G S .
121

      3.3   E STIMATION OF C AUSAL E FFECTS FOR L ANGUAGE S KILLS
240
241   It is widely recognized that most texts require more than one language skill for inference (Arora
242   & Goyal, 2023). Therefore, determining which paths are associated with the observed skill can be
243   challenging. For this reason and motivated by endeavors in causal effect analysis (Wang et al., 2023;
244   Vig et al., 2020), we divide the effects of any text on the output token into 3 components: skill effects,
245   background effects, and self effects for destination (abbreviated as self effects).
246   Skill effects refer to the impact of the observed language skill on the output which is the focus of this
247   paper. Self effects denote the impact of using a single destination token to predict, which functions
248   like a “bi-gram model” (a model associating one input token with its output token). Background
249   effects propose a counterfactual scenario, i.e., what would the effect be if this skill is not present in this
250   text4 . We use the typical example of the “Induction” skill for illustration, which works with an input in
251   the form of “... A B ... A”, where A, B refers to different tokens. Here the language model is expected
252   to repeat the pattern (“A B”) it has seen in the context and predict token “B” as the destination token.

291   4     E XPERIMENTAL D ESIGN
292
293   This paper focuses on 3 language skills, spanning from basic to advanced levels:
294
295
      Previous Token Skill: This is a skill to receive information from the previous token.
296   Induction Skill: This skill involves identifying patterns in prefix matching and replicating recurring
297   token sequences.
298
      ICL Skill: This is a complex skill to recognize and replicate the demonstration context, thereby
299   producing outputs based on similar patterns.
300
301   Extensive research has shown that these three skills build on one another in a sequentially encompass-
302
      ing manner (cro, 2024; Olsson et al., 2022; Ren et al., 2024; Edelman et al., 2024). The Induction
      Skill inherently includes the Previous Token Skill. In simple terms, for induction to occur in the
303
      sequence “A B ... A”, the token B must retrieve information from the preceding token A. Likewise,
304
      In-Context Learning must be capable of identifying similar patterns across different demonstrations
305   to generate analogous outputs.
306
307   We select over 10k samples encompassing one of the three above-mentioned skills from large
308
      corpora and popular datasets such as WIKIQA (Yang et al., 2015), SST-2 (Socher et al., 2013),
      BIG-BENCH (Srivastava et al., 2023), OpenOrca (Lian et al., 2023), and OpenHermes (Teknium,
309
      2023). For each instance, we create a background perturbation and a self perturbation (discussed
310
      in Section 3.3). For simplicity, PVT represents the sample set involving the Previous Token Skill
311   and IDT represents the sample set related to Induction Skill. ICL1 represents the ICL sample set
312   from SST-2 datasets; ICL2 represents the ICL sample set from object counting task; ICL3 and ICL4
313   represents those from qawikidata and reasoning about colored objects task. Using GPT2-small as the
314   research model and applying the three-step framework detailed in Section 3 to these samples, we are
315   able to identify high-effect samples through clustering, which clearly reveal distinct skill paths. The
316   details of data preparation and implementation are elaborated in Appendix E, while our validation,
317   findings, and explorations are presented in Sections 5, 6, and 7.

396   Table 4 displays the circuits receiving more than 10 circuit paths (receivers) in the skill graphs. We

398   can be found in Appendix J. From Table 4, we identify 3 interesting patterns:
399
      1. Identifiability: The paths of each skill are identifiable and remain unchanged across most data
400
      instances.
401
402   2. Stratification: The Previous Token Skill (PVT) is one of the simplest language skills, and thus it
403   is located across layers 0-2. The Induction Skill (IDT) is slightly more complex and thus spreads
404
      across layers 0-6. Meanwhile, ICL is the most complex skill and has key receivers across nearly all
      layers. Additionally, all skills share the 11-th layer (final layer).
405
406   3. Inclusiveness: Higher-level skills always entail the key circuits of lower-level skills. It is
407   universally acknowledged that the Previous Token Skill is an integral part of the Induction Skill,
408   which is why circuits such as [2, 14], [2, 18] and [2, 20] (presented in PVT) can be found in the
409   Induction Skill Graph. Similarly, the ICL skill encapsulates the Previous Token Skill and Induction
410
      Skill as necessary sub-skills, which is why circuits that are evident in the Previous Token Skill (such
      as [2, 14], [2, 20], [2, 24]) and those identified in the Induction Skill (such as [3, 14], [4, 5]) can be
411
      found in the ICL Skill Graph. Furthermore, we list all multi-step paths with inclusive sub-path in
412
      Appendix G.

416

417

418
      distinct characteristics (We demonstrate the performances of other circuit discovery methods in

      serves as a query token to attend other tokens, this circuit is shown to consistently carry significant

490   8    L IMITATION AND C ONCLUSION
491
492   We have identified three pressing limitations that need to be addressed. The first is the time complexity
493   of the greedy search the second is the lack of further examination on the representational study, and
494   the third is scalability. Assuming the time for one inference of LLM as O(1), the time complexity
495   of a single greedy search would then be O(L2 N 2 ), i.e., the square of the layer number times the
496
      number of circuits. If we can overlook this time-consuming process, then the G∗ for each input
      would effectively facilitate training. In other words, G∗ could directly instruct LLM which paths
497
      are essential and which are not, thus streamlining the training process. Despite the time complexity,
498
      we recall our contribution on the analysis of LMs which is usually more challenging and does not
499   require large-scale inference. Additionally, the lack of research at the representational level hinders
500   our progress in answering more complex questions such as why certain samples fail to trigger a
501   skill. Recognized that this is a rather challenging topic, we leave it as a promising future work.
502   Finally, we recognized the limitations of testing on a single model and specific skills. Although many
503   studies have validated the GPT-2 series to have public trustworthiness for research in mechanistic
504   interpretability, making us confident in its capacity to support our contribution—the pioneering work
505   in discovering the theoretical foundation and experimental design of language skills—there remains
506   ample scope for scalability across a variety of models and skills for future work.
507   In conclusion, we propose a novel framework including faithful pruning and linear decomposition to
508   completely dissect the language model and discover key components leading to meaningful language
509   skills. Our framework contains three steps, involving [decomposing the LM losslessly into circuits
510   including memory, compensation, and bias circuits], pruning paths preserving the inference outcome,
511   and identifying salient paths for language skills via causal analysis. Through this process, we are
512   able to identify the skill paths necessary for a language model to process texts. Furthermore, we
513
      demonstrate several interesting findings validating existing hypotheses. For example, each language
      skill is bound to specific circuits, and more complex skills are associated with deeper circuits.
514
      Additionally, we find that the evolution of complex skills extends along the path of simpler skills they
515
      encompass, providing strong experimental support for research on emergence discoveries. Lastly,
516   we explored attributions of error samples to the absence of certain skill circuits. These findings
517   could potentially offer novel feedback for the training process. Overall, we believe that our thorough
518   discovery of language skills can generate more insights into the exploration of language models.

                 1443              One of the contributions of this paper is to validate three long-unverified conjectures about language
                 1444              skills: Identifiability, Stratification, and Inclusiveness. The question of why previous work merely
                 1445              “proposed conjectures” while our method could find “strong evidence” will be answered in this
                 1446              section.
                 1447              Firstly, we compared the differences in circuit components among 4 circuit discovery methods,
                 1448              including ours. The other methods are ACDC, OPT-prun, and EAP (introduced in Section 2). Each
                 1449              method used its own circuit discovery strategy to search for corresponding circuit graphs for the three
                 1450              skills we focus on: PVT, IDT, and ICL1. Then, as with Table 4, we investigated the distribution of
                 1451              receiver nodes in these circuit graphs and displayed the normalized results in Figure 14.
                 1452              It is clear from other methods that PVT is more prominent in the shallow layers, IDT in the mid-
                 1453              to-deep layers, and ICL1 tends to cluster in the deep layers. However, the circuit graphs from
                 1454              these methods are insufficient to prove these patterns. For instance, although PVT is significantly
                 1455              concentrated in the shallow layers, there are also components in the deep layers. Yet, the circuit
                 1456              graph discovered by our method provides a more distinct differentiation: PVT circuits only appear in
                 1457              layers 1 and 2, and IDT circuits only appear in layers 1-6. Our method determines the specific layer

       Additionally, to observe the performance of these methods on the conjecture of Inclusiveness, we
1524   investigated their overlap on the three skill circuits: PVT, IDT, and ICL1. The corresponding circuit
1525   graphs are still derived from the circuit discovery strategies proposed by each method, searching in
1526   the corpora corresponding to the three skills proposed in this paper. The rule for calculating overlap
1527   is as follows: let ovlp(A, B) represent what the rate of edges in skill graph A also existing in skill
1528   graph B is. For any edge ei in skill graph A, we set an overlap flag fA,B (ei ). If ei in A also exists in
1529   skill circuit graphs B, then fA,B (ei ) = 1, otherwise fA,B (ei ) = 0. For a circuit graph A with NA
                                                                                       PE
1530   edges, its set of edges is EA . Our overlap is calculated as ovlp(A, B) = N1A eiA∈EA fA,B (ei ).
