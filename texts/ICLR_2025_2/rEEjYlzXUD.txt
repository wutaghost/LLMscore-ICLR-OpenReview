009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            The committor functions are central to investigating rare but important events in
012            molecular simulations. It is known that computing the committor function suffers
013            from the curse of dimensionality. Recently, using neural networks to estimate the
014            committor function has gained attention due to its potential for high-dimensional
015            problems. Training neural networks to approximate the committor function needs
016            to sample transition data from straightforward simulations of rare events, which
017
               is very inefficient. The scarcity of transition data makes it challenging to approx-
               imate the committor function. To address this problem, we propose an efficient
018
               framework to generate data points in the transition state region that helps train neu-
019
               ral networks to approximate the committor function. We design a Deep Adaptive
020            Sampling method for TRansition paths (DASTR), where deep generative mod-
021            els are employed to generate samples to capture the information of transitions
022            effectively. In particular, we treat a non-negative function in terms of the inte-
023            grand in the loss functional as an unnormalized probability density function and
024            approximate it with the deep generative model. The new samples from the deep
025            generative model are located in the region of the transition and fewer samples are
026            located in the other region, which provides effective samples for approximating
027            the committor function and significantly improves the accuracy. We demonstrate
028
               the effectiveness of the proposed method with both simulations and realistic ex-
               amples.

031   1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Understanding transition events between metastates in a stochastic system plays a central role in
034   chemical reactions and statistical physics (Okuyama-Yoshida et al., 1998; E & Vanden-Eijnden,
035   2006; Berteotti et al., 2009; E & Vanden-Eijnden, 2010). The physical process can be formulated as
036   the following stochastic differential equation (SDE)
037                                                         p
038
                                      dXt = −∇V (Xt )dt + 2β −1 dWt ,                                (1)
039   where Xt ∈ Ω ⊂ Rd is the state of the system at time t, V : Ω 7→ R denotes a potential function, β
040   is the inverse temperature, and Wt is the standared d-dimensional Wiener process. For two disjoint
041   subsets of this stochastic system, we are interested in the transition rate, which can be characterized
042   by the commttor function. For two distinct metastable regions A, B ⊂ Ω, and A ∩ B = ∅, denoting
043   by τω the first hitting time of a subset ω ⊂ Ω for a trajectory, the committor function q : Ω 7→ [0, 1] is
044
      defined as q(x) = P (τB < τA |X0 = x), where P denotes the probability. The committor function
      is a probability that a trajectory of SDE starting from x ∈ Ω first reaches B rather than A. By
045
      definition, it is easy to verify that q(x) = 0 for x ∈ A and q(x) = 1 for x ∈ B. This committor
046
      function provides the information of process of a transition, and it is governed by the following
047   partial differential equation (PDE) (Lai & Lu, 2018; Li et al., 2019)
048
049                        −β −1 ∆q(x) + ∇V (x) · ∇q(x) = 0,          x ∈ Ω\(A ∪ B),
050                                                q(x) = 0,          x ∈ A,
                                                                                                            (2)
051                                                q(x) = 1,          x ∈ B,
052                                           ∇q(x) · n = 0,          x ∈ ∂Ω\(A ∪ B),
053
      where n is the outward unit normal vector of the boundary ∂Ω\(A ∪ B). Once the committor


                                                          1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      function q(x) is found, we can use it to extract the statistical information of reaction trajectories (E
055   & Vanden-Eijnden, 2006; 2010).
056
057   Obtaining the committor function q needs to solve the above high-dimensional PDE, which is com-
058
      putationally infeasible for traditional grid-based numerical methods. Some efforts have been made
      to employ deep neural networks to solve it (Khoo et al., 2019; Li et al., 2019; 2022). Training
059
      deep neural networks to approximate the committor function requires data points, which is usually
060
      achieved by sampling from the equilibrium distribution of the SDE. When the transition is rare, the
061   samples from the transition state region are difficult to obtain from the SDE. As shown in the litera-
062   ture (Rotskoff et al., 2022; Kang et al., 2024), if the data points are not generated from the transition
063   paths, then the trained neural network for approximating the committor function will have a large
064   generalization error. To address this problem, we propose a new framework called Deep Adaptive
065   Sampling on rare TRansition paths (DASTR) to train the deep neural network. More specifically,
066   we generate samples in the transition region using an iterative construction. To do this, we define
067   a proper sampling distribution using the current approximation of the committor function by neural
068   networks and the potential function in the SDE. This sampling distribution that reveals the transition
069
      information is approximated by a deep generative model based on which new samples are gener-
      ated and added to the training set. Once the training set is updated, the neural network model for
070
      the approximation of the committer function is further trained for refinement. This procedure is
071
      repeated to form the algorithm of deep adaptive sampling on rare transition paths. In other words,
072   we push the samples into regions where the transition is by constructing a proper sampling distribu-
073   tion step by step. In this way, effective samples are selected to train the model, resulting in a better
074   approximation of the committor function. The main contributions of this work are as follows.
075
076          • We propose a general framework, called deep adaptive sampling on rare transition paths,
077            for estimating the high-dimensional committor function.
078          • We demonstrate the efficiency of the proposed method with numerical studies, including
079            the alanine dipeptide problem.

      2   R ELATED W ORK
083
084
      We summarize the most related lines of this work.
085
086
      Neural Networks for Committor Functions. Committor functions are represented by deep neu-
      ral networks and can be trained by minimizing a variational loss functional. The training data points
087
      for discretizing the variational loss are usually sampled from the Gibbs measure (Khoo et al., 2019;
088
      Li et al., 2020; 2022), which needs to simulate the stochastic differential equations. This sampling
089   method is inefficient due to the scarcity of transition data, especially for realistic systems at low
090   temperatures. So, the committor function cannot be approximated well based on such a sampling
091   strategy. Modified sampling methods are proposed in (Li et al., 2019; Rotskoff et al., 2022; Hasyim
092   et al., 2022; Kang et al., 2024; Lin & Ren, 2024) to alleviate this issue, where a new probability mea-
093   sure for sampling is employed by modifying the potential function to produce enough data points in
094   the transition region. Our approach generalizes these sampling strategies.
095   Adaptive Sampling of Neural Network Solver. The basic idea of adaptive sampling involves
096   utilizing a non-negative error indicator, such as the residual square, to refine collocation points in
097   the training set. Sampling approaches (Gao & Wang, 2023) (e.g., Markov Chain Monte Carlo) or
098   deep generative models (Tang et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024; Tang et al., 2024) are often invoked to
099
      sample from the distribution induced by the error indicator. Typically, an additional deep generative
      model (e.g., normalizing flow models) or a classical model (e.g., Gaussian mixture models (Gao
100
      et al., 2023; Jiao et al., 2023)) for sampling is required. This work uses the variational formulation
101
      and defines a novel indicator for adaptive sampling by incorporating the trait of committor functions.
