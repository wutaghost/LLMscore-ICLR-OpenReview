011                                                   A BSTRACT
012
013             This paper focuses on solving a stochastic variational inequality (SVI) problem
014             under relaxed smoothness assumption for a class of structured non-monotone oper-
015             ators. The SVI problem has attracted significant interest in the machine learning
016
                community due to its immediate application to adversarial training and multi-agent
                reinforcement learning. In many such applications, the resulting operators do not
017
                satisfy the smoothness assumption. To address this issue, we focus on a weaker
018
                generalized smoothness assumption called α-symmetric. Under p-quasi sharp-
019             ness and α-symmetric assumptions on the operator, we study clipped projection
020             (gradient descent-ascent) and clipped Korpelevich (extragradient) methods. For
021             these clipped methods, we provide the first almost-sure convergence results without
022             making any assumptions on the boundedness of either the stochastic operator or
023             the stochastic samples. Furthermore, we provide the first in-expectation unbiased
024             convergence rate results for these methods under a relaxed smoothness assumption.

027   1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029   This paper focuses on the stochastic variation inequality (SVI) problem, which consists of finding a
030   point u∗ ∈ U , such that
031                                 ⟨F (u∗ ), u − u∗ ⟩ ≥ 0     for all u ∈ U,
032   where the operator F (·) is specified as the expected value of a stochastic operator Φ(·, ·) : U × Ξ →
033   Rm , i.e.,
034                                   F (u) = E[Φ(u, ξ)]       for all u ∈ U,
035   where ξ ∈ Ξ is a random vector. Variational Inequality (VI) problems encompass many practical
036   applications, such as optimization, min-max problems, and multi-agent games. In particular, they play
037   a vital role in modeling equilibrium problems where it’s important to capture an interaction between
038   many agents. In machine learning literature, the increasing focus on VIs is due to their relevance to
039   generative adversarial networks (GANs) Gemp & Mahadevan (2018); Gidel et al. (2019), actor-critic
040   methods Pfau & Vinyals (2016), adversarial training, and multi-agent reinforcement learning Sokota
041   et al. (2022); Kotsalis et al. (2022). In many such applications, the corresponding operator is defined
042
      as an expected value of stochastic or finite sum of operators, which motivates us to study SVIs. One
      of the pivotal works Nemirovski (2004); Juditsky et al. (2011) on SVIs proposed and studied the
043
      celebrated Mirror-Prox method under assumptions on monotonicity and Lipschitz continuity of an
044
      operator.1 These assumptions become classical for the analysis of first-order methods for solving
045   SVIs Beznosikov et al. (2022); Hsieh et al. (2019; 2020); Loizou et al. (2021).
046
047   In adversarial and multi-agent training, where the corresponding operator is a gradient of a highly
048
      non-linear neural network model, these classical assumptions might not be satisfied. It is well-known
      that one possible remedy for such non-convergent behavior is in clipping, normalization, or adaptive
049
      stepsizes, such as ADAM (Kingma & Ba, 2015). This effect might be explained by the experiment
050
      conducted in Zhang et al. (2020). In this work, authors observed that when training deep neural
051

057   This observation motivated Zhang et al. (2020) to introduce a new and more realistic assumption on

062   motivates us to delve into investigation of first-order methods for generalized smooth SVIs.

      1.1   R ELATED W ORK
065
066   Weaker Assumption on SVIs. More work has focused on stochastic methods for SVI under more
067   relaxed assumptions to develop and analyze the methods applicable to broader problem classes.
068   In particular, some studies have explored SVIs under pseudo-monotonicity Kannan & Shanbhag
069   (2019), quasi-monotonicity Loizou et al. (2021), co-coercivity Beznosikov et al. (2023) and quasi-
070   sharpness Vankov et al. (2023). Diakonikolas et al. (2021) showed that such conditions may not
071   be satisfied even in two played Markov games and introduced the weakest known structured non-
072
      monotone assumption. Later, weak Minty SVIs were studied Pethick et al. (2023); Choudhury et al.
      (2023); Alacaoglu et al. (2024) under Lipschitz’s assumption on the operator. In our work, we
073
      consider the generalized smooth assumption that goes beyond the existing settings.
074
075
      Normalized and Clipped Methods for SVIs. Jelassi et al. (2022) studied the performance of
076
      normalized stochastic gradient descent-ascent and ADAM and suggested the crucial role of normal-
077
      ization for training GANs. It is worth noting that, with the right clipping parameters, clipped and
078   normalized step sizes are equivalent up to a constant. Another line of works Gorbunov et al. (2022)
079   focuses on smooth SVIs under heavy-tail noise. Using Lipschitz’s continuity of operator and the right
080   choice of clipping parameters, the authors showed a high probability convergence rate for the clipped
081   stochastic Korpelevich method. Recent work Xian et al. (2024) considered generalized smooth
082   stochastic nonconvex strongly-concave min-max problems and provided O( √1K ) convergence rates
083   for variants of stochastic gradient sescent ascent (SGDA) with normalized stepsizes. Due to the NEW
084   specific structure of the minmax problem and the fact that the gradient of the corresponding function
085   is nonmonotone in one variable and strongly monotone in another, it is difficult to compare this work
086   with ours. Moreover, in this work, the crucial part of the analysis is in the fact that the norm of a
087   gradient can be upper bounded by a function residual. One can not use such bounds in SVIs due to
088
      the absence of function values. In our analysis, we develop a new technique to bound the operator
      norm in almost sure (a.s.) sense and in expectation.
089
090
091
      Stochastic Analysis of Clipped Methods for Generalized Smooth Optimization. In Zhang et al.
      (2020), authors analyzed clipped gradient method under a.s. bounded error assumption. Koloskova
092
      et al. (2023) showed that the gradient method with standard clipping may not converge to a solution
093
      even with small stepsizes. The authors analyzed clipped gradient descent as a biased method and
094   provided a convergence rate for non-convex functions. Later, Li et al. (2024) developed a new
095   technique allowing to bound stochastic gradients by the function value residual along the optimization
096   trajectory, which helps to find the convergence rate for the gradient with the right choice of stepsizes.
097   In our work, we do not make an assumption on a.s. bounded noise and bounded stochastic gradients.
098   Furthermore, we provide not only in-expectations but also a.s. convergence of the considered clipped
099   methods.

154   In this section, we provide the necessary concepts and assumptions for the considered SVI problem.
155   We start with a standard definition; the operator F is said to be Lipschitz continuous on a set U if
156   there exists L > 0 such that
157
                                ∥F (u) − F (v)∥ ≤ L∥u − v∥      for all u, v ∈ U.                       (1)

159   So far, Lipschitz continuity of operator was the most common assumption to study SVIs Nemirovski
160   (2004); Yousefian et al. (2014; 2017); Hsieh et al. (2019); Loizou et al. (2021); Alacaoglu et al.
161   (2024). However, this assumption does not hold in modern deep-learning applications. Based on the
      experiments provided in Zhang et al. (2020), the norm of Jacobian of the operator correlates with the

512   6                 C ONCLUSION
513
514   This paper studied the SVI problem under generalized smooth and structured non-monotone assump-
515   tions. Specifically, we consider α-symmetric and p-quasi-sharp operators, a class of generalized
516   smooth and structured non-monotone operators for SVIs. For this wide class of operators, we proved
517   the first-known almost sure convergence of clipped stochastic projection and Korpelevich methods
518
      for all parameters p. We also provided O(1/k) convergence rate for both considered methods when
519
      the operator is p-quasi sharp with p = 2. For p > 2 we provided O(k −2(1−q)/p ) average (or best)
      iterate convergence rate for both methods, where q is a stepsizes parameter 1/2 < q < 1. Despite the
520
      generality of our results, there are still open questions remain. In particular, it would be interesting
521
      to know if it is possible to show in-expectation convergence rates for α-smooth SVI α > 1/2.
522   Another attractive direction of further research in generalized smooth SVIs is in relaxation of p-quasi
523   sharpness assumption to Minty (µ = 0) or weak Minty conditions. We also believe that our technique
524   for proving almost sure convergence and in-expectation rates can be used for the analysis of other
525   methods whose stepsizes are random variables, for example, clipped stochastic Popov method or
526   first-order methods with adaptive stepsizes.
