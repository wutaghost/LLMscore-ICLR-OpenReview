011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013
               In recent years, large language models (LLMs) have significantly advanced the
               field of natural language processing (NLP). By fine-tuning LLMs with data from
014
               specific scenarios, these foundation models can better adapt to various down-
015
               stream tasks. However, the fine-tuning process poses privacy leakage risks, par-
016            ticularly in centralized data processing scenarios. To address user privacy con-
017            cerns, federated learning (FL) has been introduced to mitigate the risks associated
018            with centralized data collection from multiple sources. Nevertheless, the privacy
019            of LLMs themselves is equally critical, as potential malicious attacks challenge
020            their security, an issue that has received limited attention in current research. Con-
021            sequently, establishing a trusted multi-party model fine-tuning environment is es-
022            sential. Additionally, the local deployment of large LLMs incurs significant stor-
023            age costs and high computational demands. To address these challenges, we pro-
024
               pose for the first time a federated discrete and transferable prompt tuning, namely
               FedDTPT, for black-box large language models. In the client optimization phase,
025
               we adopt a token-level discrete prompt optimization method that leverages a feed-
026
               back loop based on prediction accuracy to drive gradient-free prompt optimization
027            through the MLM API. For server optimization, we employ an attention mecha-
028            nism based on semantic similarity to filter all local prompt tokens, along with an
029            embedding distance elbow detection and DBSCAN clustering strategy to enhance
030            the filtering process. Experimental results demonstrate that, compared to state-of-
031            the-art methods, our approach achieves higher accuracy, reduced communication
032            overhead, and robustness to non-iid data in a black-box setting. Moreover, the
033            optimized prompts are transferable.

      1   I NTRODUCATION
036
037   Large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated significant success across numerous natural lan-
038   guage processing (NLP) tasks (Brown et al., 2020; Devlin et al., 2019; Radford et al., 2019). Typ-
039   ically, these models are trained on a vast text corpus and then applied to various downstream tasks
040   through fine-tuning or prompt tuning. However, task-specific data is often necessary for tuning pre-
041   trained LLMs, and this process typically relies on user-labeled data. In practice, securely leveraging
042   these labeled data presents challenges. Data must be collected and stored for training purposes, but
043   sharing and exchanging sensitive information can pose serious security risks and raise privacy con-
044   cerns. To mitigate the risk of potential data leakage, federated learning (FL) is proposed. FL enables
045
      multiple devices to collaboratively fine-tune pre-trained LLMs on decentralized data while main-
      taining data privacy. Recent work, such as the bilevel optimization method (Li et al., 2024), has
046
      demonstrated efficient strategies to reduce communication overhead and improve optimization per-
047
      formance in FL scenarios. Additionally, federated object detection frameworks (Kim et al., 2024)
048   and federated conditional stochastic optimization (Wu et al., 2023) have provided further insights
049   into addressing communication and computational challenges in decentralized learning. Privacy and
050   security remain critical in FL settings, and proactive defenses against model poisoning attacks, such
051   as RECESS (Yan et al., 2023), help safeguard model integrity while fine-tuning LLMs in federated
052   environments. Moreover, techniques like personalized federated learning (Yan et al., 2024) have in-
053   troduced new ways to enhance the adaptability of global models to specific client data, addressing
      the heterogeneity often encountered in FL systems.

054
      When applying federated learning (FL) for tuning pre-trained LLMs, existing approaches can be cat-
055   egorized into federated fine-tuning and federated prompt tuning. However, both methods have their
056   limitations. For federated fine-tuning, the primary challenges include: (1) clients’ limited access to
057   the parameters of pre-trained language models (PLMs), (2) significant computational and storage
058   demands on local clients, and (3) high communication overhead within the FL system. These fac-
059   tors make federated fine-tuning impractical in real-world scenarios. In practice, devices primarily
060   interact with LLMs by invoking LLM APIs, which do not grant clients access to model parameters,
061   thus preventing local training. Moreover, even if access were available, devices with limited com-
062   putational resources would struggle to perform local LLM fine-tuning (Zhou et al., 2024). Several
063
      approaches have been proposed to address the challenges posed by client heterogeneity and com-
      munication costs, such as leveraging model architectures designed to improve performance in FL
064
      systems despite data heterogeneity (Pieri et al., 2023), as well as bilevel optimization methods that
065
      offer communication-efficient solutions for FL systems (Yang et al., 2024b). Additionally, methods
066   like dynamic personalized federated learning (Panchal et al., 2022), model reassembly techniques
067   (Wang et al., 2024), and federated multi-objective optimization frameworks (Yang et al., 2024a) of-
068   fer solutions for efficient model adaptation in decentralized environments. These innovations, which
069   target the optimization of client-specific models and data distribution challenges, may also inform
070   strategies for fine-tuning models in decentralized contexts.
071
      An alternative approach, federated prompt tuning, as proposed by FedBPT (Sun et al., 2023), fo-
072   cuses on optimizing continuous prompts injected into text while keeping the PLM parameters frozen.
073   Although this method reduces computational costs for clients, continuous prompts still face several
074   limitations: (1) they are model-specific and cannot be directly applied to prediction APIs, which only
075   accept discrete inputs, (2) continuous prompts lack interpretability, and (3) they lack transferability,
076   meaning they cannot be seamlessly applied to other LLMs. To improve communication efficiency,
077   methods like spectral co-distillation (Chen et al., 2023) and one-pass distribution sketches (Liu et al.,
078   2024) have been explored, targeting efficient aggregation and reduced overhead. Furthermore, the
079   issue of communication efficiency and local model performance trade-offs has been explored in
080
      works (Li & Huang, 2024), where the tension between local client computations and global model
      performance is thoroughly examined, providing further insight into optimizing federated learning
081
      strategies.
082
083   To address the aforementioned challenges, we propose FedDTPT, On the client side, we employ
084   a token-level discrete prompt tuning strategy. Given the absence of a probability distribution in the
085   inference results, we implement gradient-free prompts optimization through a feedback loop based
086
      on prediction accuracy. On the server side, we utilize an attention mechanism grounded in semantic
      similarity to filter prompt tokens from all clients. This mechanism identifies the most representative
087
      discrete tokens. Additionally, we enhance the filtering effectiveness by employing an inflection point
088
      detection in embedding distances and a Density-Based Spatial Clustering of Applications with Noise
089   (DBSCAN) clustering strategy. We conducted experiments on multiple datasets using SOTA PLMs.
090   The results indicate that, in comparison to the current state-of-the-art techniques, our methodology
091   attains superior accuracy, diminished communication expenses, and resilience to non-iid data within
092   a black-box framework. Furthermore, the refined prompts exhibit transferability. Our contributions
093   include:
094
095
             • Problem Novelty: In this work, we introduce a new problem setting: discrete prompt learn-
096
               ing in black-box federated learning. This setting enables the learning of transferable and
097
               interpretable prompts while safeguarding both the privacy of the server’s model parameters
098            and the client’s data.
099
100
101          • Approach Novelty: In this work, we propose FedDTPT, a novel discrete prompt learning
102            framework in black-box federated learning scenarios. FedDTPT utilizes the novel token-
103            level optimization strategy to update the client prompt and a token selection method based
104            on semantic similarity to aggregate the discrete prompt.
105
106
107          • Experimental effect: Our method achieves high accuracy and low communication over-
               head, and its optimized prompts exhibit transferability.

      LLMs as API Service. Due to the significant computational demands of large language models
111   (LLMs), an increasing number of LLMs are being deployed on servers as API services. From the
112   model supplier’s perspective, this approach allows them to retain proprietary control over their mod-
113   els, avoiding open sourcing due to commercial considerations and the risk of misuse. From the
114   user’s perspective, even when pre-trained LLMs are available, running them locally is often pro-
115   hibitively expensive or even infeasible due to hardware constraints and the need for continuous
116   updates (Bommasani et al., 2022). Given these advantages, deploying LLMs as API services has
117   become a mainstream approach and is now the dominant trend.
118   Federated Learning. Federated Learning (FL) is a decentralized machine learning approach where
119   multiple clients collaboratively train a model while keeping their data local, ensuring privacy
120   (Konečnỳ, 2016). For model suppliers, FL enables large-scale training without accessing user data,
121   reducing liability and complying with privacy regulations like GDPR 1 . For users, it allows participa-
122   tion in model improvements while maintaining control over their data. Although FL offers privacy
123   benefits, challenges like data heterogeneity, communication costs, and system differences remain
124   key research areas. FL is increasingly applied to LLMs, especially in privacy-sensitive applications,
125
      making it a critical tool in privacy-preserving AI.
126   Prompt Tuning. Prompt tuning has gained considerable attention in the field of large language
127   models (LLMs). Its goal is to search for an optimal prompt using minimal examples to guide an
128   LLM towards generating the desired output for a specific downstream task. In NLP applications,
129   there are two main types of prompt tuning methods: (1) continuous prompt tuning and (2) discrete
130   prompt tuning (Liu et al., 2023). In continuous prompt tuning, a sequence of continuous vectors
131
      is appended to the input text embedding. Unlike discrete prompt, which operates at the vocabulary
      level, continuous prompt tuning (Li & Liang, 2021) optimizes the prompt directly in the embedding
132
      space. In contrast, discrete prompt tuning involves a sequence of discrete tokens, which remain
133
      interpretable to humans.

191   3.2      D ESIGN OVERVIEW
192
193   The overview of FedDTPT as shown in Figure 1. In the client optimization phase of FedDTPT,
194
      we adopt a token-level discrete prompt tuning strategy that establishes a new feedback mecha-
      nism for inference results to enable gradient-free prompt optimization. During the federated learning
195
      stage, we employ a semantic similarity-based attention mechanism to sample prompt tokens from
196
      all clients, selecting the most representative discrete tokens to construct optimized prompts. This
197   approach effectively facilitates knowledge transfer across clients while preserving privacy. At the
198   beginning of the optimization process, a public dataset Dg , containing representative samples, is
199   deployed to each client to assist in computing the prediction accuracy during local prompt tuning.
200   In each global communication round, the server first broadcasts a global prompt to all clients. In
201   the initial round, this prompt is based on the global task and can either be carefully designed or
202   straightforward. Subsequently, each client k uses the MLM API to fine-tune the global prompt,
203   recording the tuning information. The tuned prompt is then input into the LLM prediction API to
204   obtain inference results and calculate accuracy. The accuracy and tuning information are aggregated
205
      as optimization feedback and fed back to the MLM API for further fine-tuning. Upon completion
      of local optimization, all clients upload their local prompts to the server for knowledge aggregation.
206
      The server maps the discrete tokens of all prompts to a high-dimensional latent space and employs a
207
      clustering strategy based on the secondary-range elbow judgement strategy and DBSCAN approach
208   to cluster the embeddings. Finally, a latent space similarity-based attention mechanism is applied to
209   sample the embeddings and generate a global prompt.

214   els only output prediction text without probability distributions. The absence of loss information
215   necessitates that prompt optimization be performed solely at the token level, posing significant chal-

348
349   Pre-trained LLMs. In our experiments, we selected two models as backbone models: DeepSeek-
350
      V2-Lite (15B parameters) (DeepSeek-AI, 2024), and Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct (AI@Meta, 2024).
351   Dataset. We conducted experiments on seven datasets from the GLUE benchmark (Wang et al.,
352   2019): SST-2, RTE, QNLI, MRPC, QQP, WNLI, and CoLA. Additionally, we adopted the k-shot
353   approach for prompt training, which will be explained in detail in the following sections. Due to the
354   consistent number of classes across datasets, we used accuracy (ACC) instead of the Matthews Cor-
355   relation Coefficient (MCC) to evaluate the prediction performance for the CoLA dataset. Similarly,
356
      for QQP and MRPC, ACC was used in place of the F1 score as the evaluation metric.
357   Comparison Baselines. We evaluated our pure black-box prompt-tuning federated learning method
358   against seven state-of-the-art (SOTA) approaches. Based on the amount of information obtained
359   about the backbone model, we categorized these methods into white-box and black-box approaches.
360   We define white-box LLM methods as those that have access to the full parameters of the backbone
361   model and can obtain gradient information through backpropagation.
362   The White-Box comparison methods include the following: FedPrompt (Zhao et al., 2023): A
363   SOTA method that offers communication efficiency and privacy protection by employing a prompt
364   exchange strategy to facilitate knowledge transfer between clients in federated learning. Open-
365   FedLLM (Ye et al., 2024): An open-source research library for training large language models
366   (LLMs) in a federated learning setting. OpenFedLLM allows for various configurations through
367   custom FL methods and LLM replacements. In this study, we used the widely adopted FedAvg al-
368
      gorithm to implement federated learning for the backbone model. Manual prompt: It refers to a
      manually designed prompting approach based on commonly used templates for zero-shot inference.
369
370   The Black-Box LLM methods do not have access to the model’s parameters or gradients; they can
371   only retrieve prediction outputs and the full probability distribution generated by the model during
372   forward inference. These methods include the following: FedBiOT (Wu et al., 2024): This method
373   compresses the original LLM into a lightweight model with similar performance, which is then dis-

      5   C ONCLUSION
531
532   We propose FedDTPT, a FL framework that enables clients to tune discrete and transferable prompts
533   with LLMs in black-box settings. Our approach eliminates the need for clients to access model pa-
534   rameters and requires only forward propagation for local training, reducing computational and stor-
535   age demands for both devices and LLM service providers. Additionally, our discrete prompts are
536   interpretable to developers and can be transferred to other LLMs without any modifications. Eval-
537   uations on several datasets using state-of-the-art PLMs show that FedDTPT outperforms existing
538   white-box and black-box methods with significantly lower communication and memory overhead.
539   Furthermore, FedDTPT demonstrates excellent transferability.
