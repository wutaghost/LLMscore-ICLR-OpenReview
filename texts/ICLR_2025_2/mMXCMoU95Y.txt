011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            The increasing integration of AI models in critical areas, such as healthcare, fi-
014            nance, and security has raised concerns about their “black-box” nature, limiting
015            trust and accountability. To ensure robust and trustworthy AI, interpretability is
016
               essential. In this paper, we propose CAuSE (Causal Abstraction under Simu-
               lated Explanation), a novel framework for post-hoc explanation of multimodal
017
               classifiers. Unlike existing interpretability methods, such as Amnesic Probing and
018
               Integrated Gradients, CAuSE generates causally faithful natural language expla-
019            nations of fine-tuned multimodal classifiers’ decisions. CAuSE integrates Inter-
020            change Intervention Training (IIT) within a Language Model (LM) based module
021            to simulate the causal reasoning behind a classifier’s outputs. We introduce a
022            novel metric Counterfactual F1 score to measure causal faithfulness and demon-
023            strate that CAuSE achieves state-of-the-art performance on this metric. We also
024            provide a rigorous theoretical underpinning for causal abstraction between two
025            neural networks and implement this within our CAuSE framework. This ensures
026            that CAuSE’s natural language explanations are not only simulations of the clas-
027
               sifier’s behavior but also reflect its underlying causal processes. Our method is
               task-agnostic and achieves state-of-the-art results on benchmark multimodal clas-
028
               sification datasets, such as e-SNLI-VE and Facebook Hateful Memes, offering a
029
               scalable, faithful solution for interpretability in multimodal classifiers.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      With the rise of Visual Language Models (VLMs), AI systems have evolved to handle multiple data
035
      types like images, text, and audio. Multimodal classifiers, central to this advancement, are crucial
036   in applications such as healthcare, where they combine medical images and patient data to improve
037   diagnostic accuracy for diseases like COVID-19 and Alzheimer’s (Baltrušaitis et al., 2017). Simi-
038   larly, in autonomous driving, they enhance decision-making by integrating visual, LiDAR, and radar
039   inputs (Xiao et al., 2022). These classifiers boost performance by leveraging diverse modalities,
040   making them vital in real-world scenarios.
041
      However, as multimodal classifiers grow in complexity, the need for interpretability becomes
042   paramount. Current interpretability methods, such as Integrated Gradients(Sundararajan et al.,
043   2017a), are designed to highlight explicit input features but fall short of capturing the implicit
044   causal relationships that often drive the decisions of these models. While some techniques, like
045   CausaLM(Feder et al., 2022) and Amnesic Probing(Elazar et al., 2021), aim to incorporate causal
046   mechanisms for interpretability, they struggle with scalability. Other methods, such as Seman-
047   tify(Bandyopadhyay et al., 2024), manage implicit concepts efficiently but are restricted to specific
048   use cases and fail to generate comprehensive natural language explanations.
049   To address these limitations, large Visual Language Models (VLMs) have been utilized to generate
050   natural language explanations for decisions made by visual-text multimodal classifiers. However,
051   these models often inject their own biases and opinions, leading to explanations that are inconsistent
052   or detached from the actual workings of the classifier(Agarwal et al., 2024). Recent studies(Madsen
053   et al., 2024) have highlighted these faithfulness issues, revealing inconsistencies when models are
      further probed.

054
      In this paper, we introduce CAuSE (Causal Abstraction under Simulated Explanation), a novel
055   framework designed to generate faithful natural language explanations for the decisions of a pre-
056   trained classifier, offering post-hoc interpretability. CAuSE combines Interchange Intervention
057   Training(Geiger et al., 2021a) with Language Model (LM)-based modules, ensuring that the gener-
058   ated explanations are both causally accurate and reflective of the classifier’s internal decision-making
059   process. We introduce a new metric, the Counterfactual F1 score, to assess the causal faithfulness of
060   explanations. CAuSE sets a new benchmark on this metric, achieving state-of-the-art performance.
061   Through case studies, we showcase successful generations from our framework and conduct error
062   analysis to identify common mistakes and their underlying causes.
063   Our framework is task-agnostic and demonstrates state-of-the-art performance on benchmark
064   datasets, such as e-SNLI-VE(Do et al., 2021) and Facebook Hateful Memes(Kiela et al., 2021),
065   providing robust, faithful explanations across diverse multimodal tasks. The codes are available at
066   https://anonymous.4open.science/r/CAuSE-5BD0.

      Our framework, CAuSE, generates faithful natural language explanations for decisions made by a
071
      pre-trained multimodal classifier (called the post-hoc classifier). As detailed in Section 3.2, CAuSE
072   acts as a causal abstraction of the post-hoc classifier, ensuring its explanations are rooted in the
073   actual decision-making process. This is supported by the high Counterfactual F1 scores CAuSE
074   achieves compared to the other ablated components, as shown in Table 2. This section introduces
075   the post-hoc classifier and provides a detailed description of the CAuSE framework, with a working
076   diagram of both presented in Figure 1.

078   2.1   P OST- HOC CLASSIFIER
079
080   The post-hoc classifier is assumed to be composed of a multimodal encoder E and a feed-forward
081   neural network (FFN) C1 .
082   Multimodal Encoder. The multimodal encoder E accepts as inputs the text (t ∈ Rm×1 ) and image
083   representation (v ∈ Rm×1 ). The image and text representation are fused via either i) early-fusion
084   or ii) late-fusion modules. The final multimodal representation is denoted as c ∈ Rm×1 , where
085   c = E(t, v).
086
      This module serves as a plug-and-play replacement for any multimodal encoder, whether based on
087
      early-fusion or late-fusion. In our implementation for this paper, we use a late-fusion-based module,
088   which consists of CLIP(Radford et al., 2021) and MFB(Yu et al., 2017), as commonly adopted in
089   the literature(Bandyopadhyay et al., 2024).
090
091
      Classifier C1 . The classifier gets the multimodal representation c and via a chain of feed-forward
      neural nets, it gets transformed into a vector z ∈ RL×1 , where L is the number of classes in the
092
      output label. A softmax function is used which converts logit z into a probability distribution y1 =
093
      sof tmax(z). Supposing the one-hot ground truth probability distribution is ŷ1 , the cross-entropy
094   loss which is used to optimize the post-hoc classifier is
095
096                                           LP H = −[ŷ1 log(y1 )]                                      (1)

106   Specifically, the multimodal representation c is broken into two components c0 and c1 by passing

      bedding dimension R768×1 , such that c0 = F0 (c), and c1 = F1 (c).

      3.1   L INGUISTIC I NFUSION (LI)
163
164   We denote the input to the classifier C1 as c, which is a multimodal encoding from the encoder. This
165   captures the overall encoded representation of the multimodal source input. Through LI, we want to
166   enrich c with input source (t, v) such that the latter could possess enough source information. LI is
167   performed because: We only use a projected version of c as the input token representation c2 to ϕ2 .
168   This essentially serves as a bottleneck and most of the source information is lost when input is given
169   to the LLM.
170   Assuming M = (t, v), in LI, the enrichment of c through source can be defined as the following
171   constrained maximization problem following Plug and Play Language Model (PPLM)(Dathathri
172   et al., 2020).
173
174                            ĉ = arg max P (c|M ) such that C1 (ĉ) = C1 (c)                          (5)
175                                     c
176   Applying Bayes’ theorem, P (c|M ) ∝ P (c)P (M |c). Subsequently, the optimization Equation 5 can
177   be written as: ĉ = arg maxc P (M |c).
178   To estimate P (M |c), we use an autoencoder which tries to predict M from c. Formally, we try to
179   estimate P (d|c) by training an autoencoder which is trained to minimize a loss denoted by LAE =
180   |d − M |. This ensures d becomes as close to M as possible. Specifically, to find ĉ, we train the
181   autoencoder first and then perform gradient descent of c along the loss. We use ĉ ← c − γ∇c LAE
182   as the iterative update formula to get ĉ from c.

184   3.2   C AUSAL I NTERVENTION
185
186   Causal Abstraction. In Geiger et al. (2021c), the authors introduced the concept of causal abstrac-
187
      tion for neural models. They define a neural network, N2 , as a causal abstraction of a higher-level
      causal model, N1 , if the neural representations of N2 exhibit the same causal properties as the
188
      corresponding high-level variables in N1 . This alignment is achieved through the Interchange Inter-
189
      vention Training (IIT) objective.
190
191   A natural extension of this idea is to consider N1 as a structurally identical neural network to N2
192   and apply IIT between them, keeping N1 frozen. This process ensures that N2 becomes a causal
193
      abstraction of N1 . In our framework, we replace N1 with C1 and N2 with C2 . Through IIT, we aim
      to ensure that the structurally identical classifier C2 becomes a causal abstraction of C1 .
194
195   Benefits of Causal Abstraction. The type of causal abstraction learned through IIT is referred
196   to as constructive abstraction in the causality literature. This concept ensures a systematic cor-
197   respondence between interventions on the neurons in N1 and those in N2 . Unlike a traditional
198   teacher-student loss, which merely teaches the student to mimic the teacher’s output, causal ab-
199
      straction ensures that the student model internally mirrors the teacher’s decision-making process.
      Through IIT, we guarantee that interventions on N1 have corresponding effects on N2 , meaning that
200
      N2 operates in the same causal manner as N1 .
201
202   We theoretically demonstrate that applying IIT can have significant implications if specific condi-
203   tions are met. Notably, when the weights of C1 and C2 remain the same throughout the IIT process:
204
             • The LLM machinery (i.e., A, ϕ2 along with F1 , combined as F (z) = (A ◦ ϕ2 ◦ F1 )(z))
205
               perfectly simulates the encoder, such that for any input x, F (E(x)) = E(x). Hence, the
206
               output from the LLM machinery matches that of the encoder [proven in Theorem 1].
207
208
             • Building on this result, under a specific set of assumptions, we further show that the LLM
               machinery, together with C2 (referred to as the “explanator”), forms a causal abstraction
209
               of the encoder and C1 (the “post-hoc classifier”) [proven in Theorem 2].
210
211   Teacher-student objective. Figure 2 illustrates the training process for C2 . A sample input, con-
212   sisting of both an image and a text from the dataset, is passed through the encoder. The encoder
213   produces an output c, represented as a 3-dimensional vector, which is then fed into C1 . Assuming
214   the weights in the first layer are all set to one, the activation of the i1 -th neuron (as shown in the
215   diagram) would be calculated as 1 × 0.1 + 1 × 0.2 + 1 × 0.3 = 0.6. The final activation is then
      computed as y1 = 3 × 0.6 + 2 × 0.6 = 3.

      3.2.1   C ALCULATING C OUNTERFACTUAL F1 SCORE
283
284   Suppose x ∈ T is a data-point from test set. As posed in Feder et al. (2022), the corresponding
285   counterfactual input x′ for the post-hoc classifier would satisfy the following:
286                            x′ = arg min d(x, x′ ) such that C1 (x) ̸= C1 (x′ )                (9)
287                                   x′ ∈T
288   d is any kind of distance metric (e.g. manhattan, euclidean etc) between these data points. C1 (z)
289   denotes the output class from C1 for any input z.
290   Subsequently, any counterfactual for x can be expressed as: x′ = x + µ, where µ = x′ − x
291   is the perturbation between normal and counterfactual input. Note that E(x′ ) could not be a good
292   counterfactual input for the LLM machinery, as x′ ∈ T and high simulation performance between C2
293   and C1 means C2 could easily find label of x′ . Therefore, we resort to the following three constraints
294   while designing a counterfactual input z ′ for the LLM Machinery: i) z ′ should be a counterfactual
295   for C1 , as our task is to measure how many counterfactuals for C1 are also counterfactual for C2 . ii)
296   z ′ should not be representation of any data-point from T , iii) It should be a transformation of the
297   original data-point x and its perturbation µ.
298   We assume z ′ has the following generic form (satisfying ii. and iii.), z ′ = z+T (µ), where z = E(x)
299   is an input to the LLM machinery. So, z ′ = E(x) + T (µ). Note that to ensure T (µ) is an invertible
300   function of µ (satisfying iii.), we use an autoencoder which maps µ to T (µ) and then back to µ
301   again. Finally, to satisfy the first constraint, we ensure the following holds true:
302                                      C1 (E(x) + T (µ)) = C1 (E(x + µ))                              (10)
303   Note that this can be enforced by standard KL divergence loss between C1 and C2 .
304
305   Algorithm 1: Counterfactual F1 Score for C1 and C2
306   Input: Data-point x ∈ T
307   Function CounterFactual(x):
308      x′ ← arg minx′ ∈T d(x, x′ ) s.t. C1 (x) ̸= C1 (x′ ) ;
309      µ ← x′ − x ;                                          // Compute the perturbation
310      z ← E(x) ;                                         // Encode the original input
311      T (µ) ← f (µ) where g(f (µ)) = µ ;              // Transform the perturbation
312      z ′ ← z + T (µ) ;
313
         return z ′ , x′
314   Procedure Calculate Counterfactual F1 score
315
         ZList ← [] ;
         XList ← [] ;
316
         while T ̸= ϕ do
317
             Sample x ∈ T ;                                       // Draw a new data point
318          z ′ , x′ ← CounterFactual(x) ;
319          Ensure: C1 (z ′ ) = C1 (E(x′ )) ;                             // constraint i.
320          ZList ← ZList ∪ {C2 (z ′ )} ;                      // Append C2 (z ′ ) to the list
321          XList ← XList ∪ {C1 (x′ )} ;                       // Append C1 (x′ ) to the list
322          T ← T − {x} ;
323      return F1 − score(XList, ZList)

356   4.1       AUTOMATIC E VALUATION
357
358   The proposed system is evaluated across two verticals: i) Mimicking capability of the explanator
359   when compared to post-hoc classifier, and ii) performance under counterfactual input. The auto-
360
      matic evaluation metric used to evaluate CAuSE performance can be grouped into two categories,
      i) Faithfulness: This is measured by the obtained F1 score measured between the predicted class
361
      by the LLM machinery (or C2 ) and the predicted class by the post-hoc classifier C1 . The predicted
362
      class obtained from the LLM machinery is extracted either from the prediction of ϕ2 or from C2
363   classifier head. ii) Plausibility: This is measured as the BLEU scorePapineni et al. (2002) and
364   BERTScoreZhang et al. (2020) between the generated explanation and the ground truth explanation
365   from the test set.
366
      Baselines. To the best of our knowledge, ours is the first approach that generates faithful natu-
367
      ral language explanations directly from a classifier’s hidden state. Nonetheless, we compare our
368
      method with several Visual Language Model (VLM) baselines as there are no existing techniques
369   for this task in the literature. Specifically, we use zero-shot and few-shot (k = 2 or 3) prompting
370   with i) PaLiGemma(Beyer et al., 2024), ii) LLaVA(Liu et al., 2023), to simulate the predicted class
371   from a given classifier (C1 ), based on previous input-output examples2 . Since it is challenging to
372   simulate a model’s behaviour without access to its hidden activations, few-shot prompting often per-
373   forms similarly or even worse than zero-shot prompting. The faithfulness of the explanations, as
374   measured by the F1 score, is inconsistent and random (below 50% for the Hateful Memes dataset
375   and below 33% for e-SNLI-VE), as shown in Table 3 The fine-tuned models (shown through FT
376   suffix) perform the best, where the F1 score reaches close to ∼ 70%.

406   What is the use of various loss function other than Lϕ1 and Lϕ2 loss? As seen from Table 1, it can
407   be posed as a valid question. Indeed, when using our proposed method which uses LIIT and other
408   losses seem to achieve slightly lower F1 score (indicating slightly lower faithfulness) and slightly
409   lower BLEU score / BERTScore (indicating slightly lower plausibility). Note that this difference is
410   very small and it is compensated by very high counterfactual F1 score as shown in Table 2 obtained
411   by our method.
412   Why is IIT required? As can be seen from Table 1 and Table 2, good mimicking performance
413   under normal condition does not always entail good performance when posed with counterfactual
414   input. IIT ensures causal abstraction between C2 and C1 theoretically and this is also being verified
415   empirically by the high counterfactual F1 score obtained by our method which uses IIT.

      Is Lϕ1 necessary? Lϕ1 which is used to train the LLM (ϕ1 ) which reconstructs the content is re-
417   quired, because that shows better mimicking performance (at least in Hateful meme dataset) coupled
418   with Lϕ2 than using Lϕ2 alone. This can be attributed to the joint training objective which ensures
419   that c possesses enough input information to aid in explanation generation by the second LLM ϕ2 .

      5   R ELATED W ORK
492
      Interpretabiltiy. Interpretability is crucial for building trust in AI systems within human society.
493
      Techniques like LIME, SHAP and RISE (Ribeiro et al., 2016; Lundberg & Lee, 2017; Petsiuk et al.,
494
      2018) explain classifier predictions by providing feature-level explanations for local interpretabil-
495   ity. Although model-agnostic, these methods lack global interpretability, which is addressed by
496   GALE van der Linden et al. (2019), where local explanations are aggregated into a global model
497   understanding. Approaches like SmoothGrad Smilkov et al. (2017) and Integrated Gradients (Sun-
498   dararajan et al., 2017b) utilize input gradients for model explanation, while CAM Zhou et al. (2015)
499   highlights critical pixels for decision making in visual classification. Counterfactual generations
500   (Chang et al., 2019; Mothilal et al., 2020; Goyal et al., 2019) also offer insights into the inner work-
501   ing of the model by revealing decision boundaries. However, most of these methods often overlook
502   implicit features behind model decisions and lack natural language explanations. To address these
503
      limitations, we propose a novel framework for classifier explanations which generates both faithful
      and plausible natural language outputs.
504
505   Causal Interpretability. Causal interpretability refers to the ability to explain a model’s decisions
506   by identifying the cause-effect relationships between input features and the model’s output. Feder
507   et al. (2022) demonstrated how incorporating causal reasoning in NLP tasks can improve model
508   predictions and enhance interpretability by going beyond simple correlations between input features
509
      and outputs. Further works by Geiger et al. (2021b); Vig et al. (2020); Meng et al. (2023) have fo-
      cused on causal abstraction and causal mediation analysis, helping to create causally faithful models
510
      and identify both direct and indirect causal factors behind certain model behaviors. In addition to
511
      generating counterfactuals, testing models on counterfactual inputs is another critical aspect of un-
512   derstanding model behavior. Since creating exact counterfactuals is challenging, Abraham et al.
513   (2022); Calderon et al. (2022), recent research has focused on approximations Geiger et al. (2021b)
514   or counterfactual representations Feder et al. (2021); Elazar et al. (2021); Ravfogel et al. (2021).
515   Our proposed counterfactual metric is inspired by these counterfactual representations. Moreover,
516   most of the existing works focuses on single modality (e.g., text or vision) Feder et al. (2021); Goyal
517   et al. (2020). In contrast, the natural language causal explanation provided by our framework is
518   model-agnostic, task-agnostic, and capable of handling multimodal inputs.
519
520   6   C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
521
522   In this paper, we presented CAuSE (Causal Abstraction under Simulated Explanation), a novel
523   framework for generating causally faithful natural language explanations for multimodal classifiers.
524   By integrating Interchange Intervention Training (IIT) with a Language Model (LM) based module,
525   CAuSE addresses the limitations of existing interpretability methods, ensuring explanations are di-
526   rectly tied to the classifier’s causal reasoning. Our new Counterfactual F1 score highlights CAuSE’s
527   state-of-the-art performance on datasets like e-SNLI-VE and Facebook Hateful Memes.
528   While CAuSE demonstrates robust task-agnostic performance, future work will focus on enhanc-
529   ing fine-grained object-level representations and extending the framework to temporal data, such as
530   video and audio. Additionally, we aim to explore how self-supervised learning and deeper integra-
531   tion of implicit cultural knowledge can further improve the framework’s scalability and contextual
532   understanding in real-world applications.
