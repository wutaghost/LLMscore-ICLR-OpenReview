011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Conventional methods, including Decision Tree (DT)-based methods, have been
014            highly effective in scientific tasks, such as non-image medical diagnostics, sys-
015
               tem anomaly detection, and inorganic catalysis efficiency prediction. However,
               most deep-learning techniques have struggled to surpass or even match this level
016
               of success as traditional machine learning methods. The primary reason is that
017
               these applications involve multi-source, heterogeneous data, where features lack
018            explicit relationships. This contrasts with image data, where pixels exhibit spatial
019            relationships; textual data, where words have sequential dependencies; and graph
020            data, where nodes are connected through established associations. The absence
021            of explicit Feature Relation Patterns (FRPs) presents a significant challenge for
022            deep learning techniques in scientific applications that are not image, text, and
023            graph-based. In this paper, we introduce EAPCR, a universal feature extractor
024            designed for data without explicit FRPs. Tested across various scientific tasks,
025            EAPCR consistently outperforms traditional methods and bridges the gap where
026
               deep learning models fall short. To further demonstrate its robustness, we synthe-
               size a dataset without explicit FRPs. While Kolmogorov–Arnold Network (KAN)
027
               and feature extractors like Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs), Graph Con-
028
               volutional Networks (GCNs), and Transformers struggle, EAPCR excels, demon-
029            strating its robustness and superior performance in scientific tasks without FRPs.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   In various scientific applications, such as non-image medical diagnostics, system anomaly detection,
035   inorganic catalysis efficiency prediction, and etc., traditional machine learning techniques, such as
036   Decision Tree (DT) (Ali et al., 2012) and DT-based method (e.g., Random Forest (RF) and Extreme
037   Gradient Boosting (XGBoost)), have been reported as highly effective (Coşkun & Kuncan, 2022;
038   Mutlu et al., 2023; Alizargar et al., 2023; Schossler et al., 2023; Mallioris et al., 2024). In contrast,
039
      few studies have reported deep learning models as the best-performing methods, indicating that
      more complex deep learning techniques, such as Convolutional Neural Network (CNN)s (LeCun
040
      et al., 1998), Graph Convolutional Network (GCN)s (Kipf & Welling, 2016; Bronstein et al., 2017),
041
      and Transformers (Vaswani, 2017), have not shown the same level of success in those scientific
042   applications.
043
044   The primary reason deep learning techniques often underperform in scientific applications is that
045
      the data in these fields differ significantly from traditional tasks like images, text, and graphs. For
      example, in non-image medical diagnostics, patient data come from diverse sources, such as physical
046
      measurements (e.g., weight, blood pressure) and chemical tests (e.g., glucose levels) (Fig. 1-a-1).
047
      Unlike pixels in images or words in text, which have spatial or sequential relationships (LeCun
048   et al., 1998; Bronstein et al., 2017) (Fig. 1-b-1), or nodes in graphs with known connections (Kipf
049   & Welling, 2016; Bronstein et al., 2017) (Fig. 1-b-2), features in scientific data lack such explicit
050   relationships. This absence of explicit feature relationships is common across various scientific
051   tasks, such as system anomaly detection (Wang et al., 2023; Tian, 2023) (Fig. 1-a-2) and inorganic
052   catalysis efficiency prediction (Sun et al., 2024) (Fig. 1-a-3), where features are collected from
053   heterogeneous sources, such as electrical signals and temperature, and have different units, like pH
      levels and illumination time.

      The feature relationship patterns, including spatial relationships and connections, are referred to as
      Feature Relation Patterns (FRPs) in this work. FRPs are critical for deep learning-based feature
100
      extractors as they contain essential information about feature associations or correlations. For in-
101
      stance, in image and text data, spatial relationships between pixels and words reveal correlations
102   where nearby features have stronger associations and distant ones weaker. This inherent relational
103   information enables feature extractors to quickly identify important combinations of strongly inter-
104   acting features. For example, CNNs in image data leverage spatial relationships to focus on local
105   feature patterns while ignoring irrelevant non-local ones (Bronstein et al., 2017; Yun et al., 2023)
106   (Fig. 1-b-1). Similarly, GCNs use adjacency matrices in graph data to capture meaningful node
107   connections (Bronstein et al., 2017; Schlichtkrull et al., 2018) (Fig. 1-b-2). However, when explicit
      FRPs, like spatial relationships or known connections, are absent, deep learning methods often strug-


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      gle (Fig. 1-b-3) as assumed FRPs may not match the actual implicit relationships. This raises a key
109   question:
110
111   How can we design universal feature extraction modules for data that lack explicit FRPs?
112   In this paper, we propose a feature extraction module, EAPCR, designed as a universal feature
113   extractor for data without explicit FRPs. Traditional feature extraction modules rely on known
114   FRPs to distinguish between important and unimportant feature combinations. However, without the
115   guidance of FRPs, EAPCR adopts a different approach. First, it exposes all possible FRPs. Second,
116   it accelerates the sampling of these combinations to ensure a wide range of feature interactions are
117   evaluated, allowing it to effectively identify important combinations of strongly interacting features.
118   To evaluate EAPCR’s effectiveness, we apply it to various scientific domains, including non-image
119   medical diagnostics (Anderies et al., 2022), system anomaly detection (Tian, 2023), and inorganic
120   catalysis efficiency prediction (Liu et al., 2022). EAPCR consistently outperforms traditional meth-
121   ods in such tasks lacking explicit FRPs. To further assess its robustness, we synthesize a dataset
122   without explicit FRPs, where models like CNNs (LeCun et al., 1998), GCNs (Kipf & Welling, 2016;
123   Bronstein et al., 2017), Transformers (Vaswani, 2017), and KAN (Liu et al., 2024b;a) struggle, while
124
      EAPCR excels in capturing meaningful features.
125   In summary:
126
127          • EAPCR is designed as a universal feature extractor for tasks lacking explicit FRPs, address-
128            ing a critical gap in deep learning for scientific applications. It consistently outperforms
129            other models across various real-world scientific applications.
130
             • We synthesize a representative dataset to investigate the challenges of modeling without
131
               FRPs, revealing the limitations of traditional methods and validating the robustness and
132            effectiveness of EAPCR.

135   Related Works Feature engineering at the early stage: Feature engineering (Bengio et al., 2013)
136
      plays a crucial role in improving the accuracy of classification models. Early approaches primar-
      ily focus on addressing feature redundancies and nonlinear relationships. For instance, principal
137
      component analysis (PCA) (Abdi & Williams, 2010) reduces linear correlations, while nonlinear
138
      methods like nonlinear PCA (Linting et al., 2007) and autoencoders (Bank et al., 2023) handle re-
139   dundancies through nonlinear transformations. Although classifiers such as Support Vector Machine
140   (SVM)s (Hearst et al., 1998), Multi-layer Perceptron (MLP)s (Rumelhart et al., 1986), and more re-
141   cent models like Kolmogorov–Arnold Network (KAN) (Liu et al., 2024b;a) can manage complex
142   nonlinear feature relationships, their performance heavily depends on how input data is represented.
143   For example, using large pre-trained models to encode images improves classification and retrieval
144   accuracy by emphasizing critical features like edges and shapes (Liu et al., 2023; Holliday & Dudek,
145   2020; Zhou et al., 2024). Therefore, more advanced feature extraction techniques are required to go
146   beyond capturing nonlinear relationships, further refining feature representations for better perfor-
147
      mance.
148   Feature engineering for data with FRPs: Accurately capturing implicit correlations between fea-
149   tures is essential for effective classification. For example, determining obesity cannot solely rely on
150   weight; height must also be considered to provide a more accurate assessment. In more complex
151   scenarios, classification depends on interactions between features, where their joint contribution
152   exceeds the sum of their individual effects (Koh & Liang, 2017; Ali et al., 2012; Beraha et al.,
153
      2019; Deng et al., 2022). This is why traditional classifiers, like MLPs (Rumelhart et al., 1986),
      rely on advanced feature extractors to improve performance by identifying complex feature inter-
154
      actions. In this vein, recent advancements, such as ConvNeXt (Woo et al., 2023), Bidirectional
155
      Encoder Representation from Transformers (BERT) (Devlin, 2018), Generative Pre-trained Trans-
156   former (GPT) (Radford et al., 2019), Vision Transformer (ViT) (Dosovitskiy, 2020), and Temporal
157   Fusion Transformers (TFT) (Lim et al., 2021), efficiently capture interaction patterns of features in
158   structured or Euclidean data like images and texts. For non-Euclidean data, techniques like mani-
159   fold learning (McInnes et al., 2018; Tenenbaum et al., 2000) and Graph Neural Network (GNN)s,
160   including Graph SAmple and aggreGatE (GraphSAGE) (Hamilton et al., 2017) and Deep Graph
161   Convolutional Neural Network (DGCNN) (Wang et al., 2019), address unique challenges. Regard-
      less of the data type, these methods rely on explicit FRPs (e.g., spatial, sequential, or relational


                                                        3

162
      connections) of data, which contain the implicit feature correlations essential for effective feature
163   extraction.
164
165   Feature engineering for data without FRPs: As discussed earlier, many scientific tasks lack explicit
166
      FRPs. Machine learning techniques like Decision Tree (DT) and DT-based methods perform well
      in these tasks by handling various data types (numerical and categorical) and automatically captur-
167
      ing interaction effects between features, as each decision split evaluates the relationship between a
168
      feature and the target variable (Gregorutti et al., 2017). In contrast, deep learning models, such as
169   CNNs, GCNs, and Transformers, struggle due to their reliance on predefined FRPs. For example,
170   in heart failure and maternal health risk prediction, the best-performing models are RF (Coşkun
171   & Kuncan, 2022) and DT (Mutlu et al., 2023). Similarly, in hepatitis C (Alizargar et al., 2023),
172   TiO2 photocatalytic degradation (Schossler et al., 2023), and centrifugal pump health status predic-
173   tion (Mallioris et al., 2024), XGBoost, another DT-based method, consistently outperforms deep
174   learning methods. Despite advancements in multimodal techniques (Lahat et al., 2015) and meth-
175   ods for non-Euclidean data (Bronstein et al., 2017), feature heterogeneity does not always align
176   with distinct modalities. Even features from the same source may lack explicit FRPs, rendering
177
      multimodal approaches ineffective. Moreover, inconsistent feature dimensions complicate the def-
      inition of feature distances in Euclidean space. For instance, determining how a 1 kg increase in
178
      weight correlates with a change in height for a particular disease is non-trivial. Data without explicit
179
      FRPs differ from traditional Euclidean and non-Euclidean data (Bronstein et al., 2017), making deep
180   learning techniques less effective in these applications.

      2     EAPCR: A FEATURE EXTRACTOR WITHOUT THE NEED OF EXPLICIT
184         FEATURE RELATION PATTERNS
185
186   In many applications, identifying feature combinations with strong interactions is not difficult, as
187   feature extraction modules like CNNs and GCNs use predefined FRPs to narrow the range of pos-
188
      sible combinations. For example, CNNs leverage spatial relationships between pixels to efficiently
      sample local regions and filter out weak feature interactions, quickly identifying critical patterns like
189
      textures in image recognition. However, when explicit FRPs are absent, searching for important fea-
190
      ture interactions becomes more random and inefficient. Traditional methods often fail in these cases
191   because the FRPs chosen by feature extraction modules may not align with the implicit patterns in
192   the data. Additionally, the sample complexity increases exponentially with the number of features,
193   making exhaustive search impractical. Unlike these traditional approaches, EAPCR exposes all fea-
194   ture combinations to ensure no fundamental interaction patterns of features are missed (Fig. 2-b),
195   then optimizes the efficiency of combination sampling to address this challenge (Fig. 2-c).
196
      Further discussion about why FRPs is important gives in App. F, where the relationship between
197   feature interaction, feature correlation, and FRPs is discussed.
198
199   2.1   E XPOSE POSSIBLE FEATURE RELATIONS : E MBEDDING AND BILINEAR ATTENTION
200
201   Unlike existing works that use one-hot encoding for categorical feature representation (Seger, 2018)
202   and bilinear attention to focus on interactions between two input modalities (Fukui et al., 2016),
203   we leverage Embedding (Mikolov, 2013) and bilinear Attention (Kim et al., 2018) to construct a
204   correlation matrix.
205   For an input with N features, we first convert each feature into a categorical (string-based) one.
206   Categorical features, like gender or catalyst substrate, remain unchanged, while numerical features
207   are discretized into categories (e.g., “high”, “medium”, “low”) based on context-specific thresholds.
208   These thresholds are chosen to balance between overly fine granularity, which leads to sparse cat-
209   egories, and overly coarse granularity, which reduces feature separation. For example, temperature
210   might be categorized into “very high”, “high”, “medium”, “low”, and “very low”. This process
211   generates an input matrix of shape [N, 1], where each element is an integer index, assigned via a
212
      dictionary mapping categorical values to indices.
213   The embedding operation substitutes each component by a corresponding dense vector, giving E
214   with shape [N, Es ], where Es is the embedding size. Then, we consider the bilinear attention defined
215   as:

      2.2   E FFICIENTLY SAMPLE FEATURE RELATIONS : PERMUTED CNN
278
279   To identify the target combinations that consist of features with strong interactions within the matrix
280   A, we leverage CNN to sample such combinations of elements in matrix A defined in equation 1.
281   Because CNNs can efficiently focus on local elements within a matrix (Bronstein et al., 2017).
282   Instead of expanding the receptive field of CNNs by increasing kernel size or layers, we propose a
283   Permuted CNN that efficiently samples diverse element combinations from the matrix A.
284   The designed permutation matrix M . The designed permutation M rearranges the matrix elements
285   of A, bringing originally distant elements closer and pushing originally close elements further apart,
286   as shown in Fig. 2-c. The details of constructing the designed permutation matrix M can be found
287   in App. A. We apply a designed permutation matrix M on A giving a new matrix P , defined as:
288                                              P ≜ M AM ⊤ .                                            (2)
289
290   The permuted CNN. The permuted CNN architecture is designed to capture both local and non-local
291   relationships within the matrix elements by applying the CNN to two different representations of
292   the matrix A, as illustrated in Fig. 2-a-2. Specifically, the CNN is applied to the raw matrix A as
293   well as to a permuted version of A, that is P in equation 2. The CNN varies on different tasks but
294
      is only equipped with a lightweight structure (e.g., a two-layered architecture with kernel sizes of
      3 × 3 and channel numbers of 8 and 16). Afterward, the outputs from the CNN operations on both
295
      the raw matrix A and the permuted matrix P are transformed into vectors. These vectors are then
296
      concatenated, resulting in a single feature vector of size [C, 1] by dense connection, where C is the
297   number of classes.
298
299   Moreover, by incorporating existing feature extraction modules, such as MLP, as a Residual connec-
300
      tion, we combine the strengths of different feature extractors to further enhance feature extraction
      efficiency. As shown in Fig. 2-a-3, the average pooling is applied on E, resulting in a vector z with
301
      shape [N, 1]. Then, an MLP transforms z into a vector with shape [C, 1]. Therefore, the residual
302
      networks also help the model better train the embedding vectors.

      4     C ONCLUSION
528
529
530   The absence of explicit Feature Relation Patterns (FRPs) presents a significant challenge in many
531
      scientific tasks that are often overlooked by the ML community. This limitation contributes to the
      underperformance of deep learning methods compared to traditional methods, such as Decision
532
      Tree-based (DT-based) machine learning approaches, in scientific applications. To address this is-
533
      sue, we introduce an innovative method, EAPCR, specifically designed for data lacking FRPs. We
534   evaluate the effectiveness and efficiency of EAPCR across a variety of real-world scientific tasks
535   and demonstrate that it consistently outperforms established methods on several scientific datasets.
536   Additionally, we synthesize a dataset that deliberately excludes explicit FRPs to further assess the
537   performance of EAPCR. The results demonstrate that EAPCR outperforms CNN, GCN, MLP, RF,
538   Transformer, and KAN on the dataset without explicit FRPs. Our findings underscore the potential
539   of EAPCR as a robust solution for scientific tasks lacking explicit FRPs, bridging the gap where
      deep learning models fall short and paving the way for enhanced data analysis in this domain.

      A    G ENERATING P ERMUTATION M ATRIX M
703
704
      Constructing the designed permutation matrix in Sec. 2.2 is a key aspect of this paper. Although
705
      the method is relatively simple, we describe the process in detail in the appendix. The core idea is
706   to create a reversible operation that rearranges the positions of N elements (1, 2, 3, · · · , N ), ensur-
707   ing that originally adjacent elements are no longer adjacent while non-adjacent elements become
708   adjacent. This generates the reversible permutation matrix M .
709
      The process works as follows: First, arrange the N elements in order into an R × L matrix, where
710
      N = R × L with R and L being roughly equal in size. Transpose of this matrix and then reshape it
711
      into an N × 1 vector. This new sequence represents the transformed positions of the original data.
712   Next, create an all-zero matrix M of size N × N . Using the transformed positions, place 1s in the
713   corresponding row and column positions of M , resulting in the reversible permutation matrix.
714
715
      For example, with N = 9, arrange the numbers 1 to 9 into a 3 × 3 matrix [[1, 2, 3], [4, 5, 6], [7, 8, 9]].
      Transposing the matrix and reshaping it gives the new sequence [1, 4, 7, 2, 5, 8, 3, 6, 9]. Using this
716
      sequence, place 1s in the appropriate positions in a 9 × 9 matrix M . The result is a permutation
717
      matrix where the distance between two adjacent elements in the new sequence is at least 3, mean-
718   ing originally adjacent elements are no longer adjacent, and previously distant elements are now
719   adjacent. Further details can be found in the code.

      2024a). Compared with MLP, KAN does not only rely on fully connected layers to process data but
      builds a network formed by a combination of nested functions. This structure can more effectively

1062
1063
       In this study, we show that FRPs are an important type of prior knowledge for the application of
       deep learning methods in various tasks. Another well-recognized prior knowledge is the annotated
1064
       dataset. Despite significant research on overcoming the scarcity of annotated data in various scien-
1065
       tific applications (Zhou et al., 2022; Gao et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023), fewer efforts have focused
1066   on designing feature extractors without prior knowledge of FRPs.
1067
1068   The key to designing an effective feature extractor is to effectively sample the feature combinations
1069
       consisting of strongly interactive features where the combined effect exceeds the sum of their indi-
       vidual contributions (Koh & Liang, 2017; Ali et al., 2012; Beraha et al., 2019; Deng et al., 2022).
1070
       In this section, we demonstrate that these interactive feature combinations are selected from the
1071
       combinations of correlated features. Typically, FRPs contain the underlying correlation informa-
1072   tion between features. As a result, combinations of related features often lead to combinations of
1073   correlated features, highlighting the importance of FRPs.
1074
1075
       Here, we prove that these interactive features are correlated.
1076   Proposition F.1. If features A and B are independent, then:
1077
                                       IG(Y, A) + IG(Y, B) = IG(Y, A, B)
1078
1079   where:
                                          IG(Y, A) ≜ H(Y ) − H(Y |A)


                                                         20
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1080
                                          IG(Y, B) ≜ H(Y ) − H(Y |B)
1081
1082                                   IG(Y, A, B) ≜ H(Y ) − H(Y |A, B)
1083   with H(Y ) the entropy of Y , IG(Y, A) the information gain of Y given A, H(Y |A) the conditional
1084   entropy of Y given A, and IG(Y, A, B) the information gain of Y given A and B
1085
1086   Proof. Since A and B are independent, their information contribution to Y is completely indepen-
1087   dent; therefore, their joint effect equals the simple sum of their individual effects minus the entropy
1088   of Y , i.e.:
1089                                 H(Y |A, B) = H(Y |A) + H(Y |B) − H(Y )
1090   Substituting the definition of information gain into the assumption of independent conditional en-
1091   tropy:
1092
               IG(Y, A, B) = H(Y ) − H(Y |A, B) = H(Y ) − (H(Y |A) + H(Y |B) − H(Y ))
1093
1094                              IG(Y, A, B) = 2H(Y ) − H(Y |A) − H(Y |B)
1095
       The sum of information gains:
1096
1097   IG(Y, A)+IG(Y, B) = (H(Y )−H(Y |A))+(H(Y )−H(Y |B)) = 2H(Y )−H(Y |A)−H(Y |B)
1098
       Thus, it is proved that IG(Y, A) + IG(Y, B) = IG(Y, A, B).
1099
1100   Proposition F.2. If:
1101                                 IG(Y, A, B) > IG(Y, A) + IG(Y, B),                                   (3)
1102   then, features A and B are correlated.
1103
1104   Proof. Rearranging equation 3 gives us
1105
1106                                  IG(Y, A, B) > IG(Y, A) + IG(Y, B)
1107   Following the definition of information gain, we have:
1108
1109                   H(Y ) − H(Y |A, B) > (H(Y ) − H(Y |A)) + (H(Y ) − H(Y |B))
1110
                              H(Y ) − H(Y |A, B) > 2H(Y ) − H(Y |A) − H(Y |B)
1111
1112
                                   H(Y |A) + H(Y |B) − H(Y ) > H(Y |A, B)
1113   The above inequalities imply that A and B jointly provide more information than the sum of the
1114   information provided individually. This is typically because there is some interaction or dependency
1115   between A and B that causes their combined information gain to exceed the individual gains, hence
1116
       A and B are not independent.
1117   Proposition F.3. If feature A and B have an interaction, i.e.:
1118
1119                              H(Y |A, B) < H(Y |A) + H(Y |B) − H(Y ),                                 (4)
1120   then:
1121                                  IG(Y, A, B) > IG(Y, A) + IG(Y, B)
1122
1123   Proof. Rewriting information gain:
1124
1125   IG(Y, A)+IG(Y, B) = (H(Y )−H(Y |A))+(H(Y )−H(Y |B)) = 2H(Y )−H(Y |A)−H(Y |B)
1126   Substituting equation 4 into the calculation of information gain gives:
1127
1128                                   IG(Y, A, B) = H(Y ) − H(Y |A, B)
1129
                              IG(Y, A, B) > H(Y ) − (H(Y |A) + H(Y |B) − H(Y ))
1130
1131
                                  IG(Y, A, B) > 2H(Y ) − H(Y |A) − H(Y |B)
1132   This implies that the joint information gain IG(Y, A, B) exceeds the sum of the individual gains.
1133   This indicates a positive interaction between features A and B in influencing Y , where their com-
       bined impact reduces the uncertainty of Y more than their individual effects summed simply.

       The effectiveness of our method lies in its ability to accurately reconstruct the correlation patterns
1150   within the data, even when these patterns become less apparent or lost after transformations. As
1151   shown in Fig. 7, whether the images are in their original state or transformed, the matrices recon-
1152   structed by our EAPCR model consistently reflect the true pixel correlation patterns with a recall
1153   rate of 84.6%. This highlights the model’s precision and reliability in restoring these patterns, even
1154   when pixel positions are altered.
1155
       In a more detailed analysis, we observed a 55.0% recall rate when comparing the correlation matrix
1156   of transformed images to the original pixel patterns. Conversely, comparing the correlation matrix
1157   from the original images to the transformed image patterns yielded a recall rate of 66.3%. This
1158   indicates that for data containing different feature correlation patterns, the patterns restored by our
1159   model vary significantly. This demonstrates that our EAPCR is capable of adaptively restoring
1160   the unique hidden relationships between features. EAPCR shows a strong ability to recover un-
1161   derlying data relationships, further emphasizing its robustness and effectiveness in scenarios where
1162   explicit correlation patterns are not directly accessible.
