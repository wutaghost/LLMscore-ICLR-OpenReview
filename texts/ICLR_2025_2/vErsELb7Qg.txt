011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Large Language Models (LLMs) such as ChatGPT can efficiently adapt to few-
014            shot tasks without fine-tuning, making them ideal for data-limited applications
015            requiring real-time responses. However, this adaptability has not yet been repli-
016
               cated in current Visual Foundation Models (VFMs), which require explicit fine-
               tuning with sufficient tuning data. Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA), an effective
017
               fine-tuning approach, adapts VFMs to specific tasks by updating extra lightweight
018
               modules. Thanks to its modularity, users can upload locally tuned LoRAs to pub-
019            lic repositories without exposing private training data. In this paper, we explore
020            the potential of reusing diverse pre-tuned LoRAs without accessing their private
021            training data, to improve the few-shot adaptability of VFMs without requiring fur-
022            ther fine-tuning. To achieve this, we propose a data-free meta-learning framework
023            named LoRA Recycle, which distills a meta-LoRA from diverse pre-tuned LoRAs
024            using synthetic data generated via LoRA Inversion. The VFM, once equipped
025            with the meta-LoRA, is empowered to solve new few-shot tasks in a single for-
026            ward pass without further fine-tuning, akin to the in-context learning of LLMs.
027
               To further enhance efficiency, we propose a double-efficient mechanism that uses
               only the foreground patches and prunes background patches in the synthetic data,
028
               significantly accelerating the meta-training process while maintaining or even im-
029
               proving performance. Comprehensive experiments across eight datasets within
030            both in- and cross-domain scenarios verify the superiority of our framework.

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035
      Large Language Models (LLMs) like ChatGPT demonstrate a profound capacity to solve few-shot
036   tasks without the necessity for fine-tuning, making them ideal for data-limited applications requiring
037   real-time responses. However, this adaptability can not be replicated by current Visual Foundation
038   Models (VFMs), which typically require explicit fine-tuning with sufficient tuning data.
039
      To adapt VFMs to specific tasks, existing work mainly attempts to design advanced fine-tuning
040
      strategies. For instance, Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) (Hu et al., 2021) freezes the pre-trained
041
      model weights and injects trainable rank decomposition matrices into each layer of the Transformer
042   architecture. While promising, (i) explicit fine-tuning is often prohibitive for applications requiring
043   real-time responses, and (ii) fine-tuning with limited data is extremely unstable. As shown in Tab. 1,
044   fine-tuning with limited data makes performance highly sensitive to choices like optimizer, learning
045   rate, and step size. Besides, it also leads to significant time overheads and increased memory usage.
046
      In the present paper, we explore the potential of reusing diverse pre-tuned LoRAs without accessing
047
      their private training data, to improve the few-shot adaptability of VFMs without requiring further
048   fine-tuning (see Fig. 1). Our inspiration comes from the concept of LoRA Market (Huang et al.,
049   2023a), where diverse pre-tuned LoRAs are publicly available and await task-specific reuse. For
050   example, users can download and then insert a task-specific LoRA of interest into the open-source
051   VFM, to obtain a personalized VFM. Moving beyond task-specific reuse, we seek to leverage the
052   vast availability of these LoRAs from a novel perspective, leading to our central research question:
053   Is it feasible to reuse diverse pre-tuned LoRAs without accessing their private training data, to
      improve the few-shot adaptability of VFMs without requiring further fine-tuning? This removes

128   2.2   M ETA -L EARNING & DATA -F REE M ETA -L EARNING (DFML)
129
130   Meta-learning, also known as learning to learn, aims to learn prior knowledge over a distribution
131   of tasks, enabling efficient adaptation to unseen few-shot tasks sampled from similar distributions.
132
      Traditional data-based meta-learning (Finn et al., 2017; Yoon et al., 2018; Khodak et al., 2019; Fu
      et al., 2023a) typically assumes the availability of task-specific data for each meta-training task. Re-
133
      cently, DFML (Wang et al., 2021; Hu et al., 2023a;b; Wei et al., 2024b;a) emerges as a promising
134
      solution to directly meta-learn from pre-trained models available off the shelf. However, existing
135   methods struggle to scale up to large Vision Transformers, whereas our framework only meta-trains
136   a lightweight meta-LoRA. Additionally, we propose a double-efficient mechanism to further accel-
137   erate the meta-training process.
138
139   2.3   T RAINING -F REE A DAPTATION OF F OUNDATION M ODELS
140
141   Compared to explicit fine-tuning, training-free adaptation requires no parameter updates, making it
142   highly suitable for real-time applications with low computational budgets. LLMs achieve training-
143   free adaptation through their inherent in-context learning capabilities (Dong et al., 2022). Existing
144   studies suggest that in-context learning is equivalent to implicitly performing gradient descent (Dai
145
      et al., 2022; Von Oswald et al., 2023), viewing LLMs as meta-learning models (Brown et al., 2020).
      However, this in-context learning ability has not yet been replicated by current VFMs. To address
146
      this, Fifty et al. (2023) explicitly train a sequence model with VFMs to simulate LLM-style in-
147
      context learning. Zhang et al. (2024) adapt the Segment Anything Model in a training-free manner
148   using a one-shot example. Our LoRA Recycle, on the other hand, achieves training-free adaptation
149   to few-shot tasks from a novel perspective, by reusing diverse pre-tuned LoRAs. This avoids the
150   need for additional data collection while leveraging the vast availability of off-the-shelf LoRAs.

199   In this section, we present our proposed framework LoRA Recycle (see Fig. 2), which distills a
200   meta-LoRA from diverse pre-tuned LoRAs using synthetic data. Since we have no access to orig-
201   inal training data, we propose LoRA Inversion to generate synthetic data from pre-tuned LoRAs

203   with these synthetic data to explicitly learn how to adapt to diverse tasks without fine-tuning (see
204
      Sec. 4.2). To further improve efficiency, we propose a double-efficient mechanism significantly
      accelerating the meta-training process while maintaining or even improving performance (Sec. 4.3).

      Efficient data synthesis with token pruning. As shown in Eq. (1), synthetic data generation via
287   LoRA Inversion requires iteratively optimizing all pixels in the input data X. To improve efficiency,
288   we propose to prune unimportant tokens during the inversion process. This is reasonable since
289   foreground tokens typically are more informative than background tokens. As illustrated in the
290   left panel of Fig. 3, at the ith layer, we implement “token pruning” by directly discarding those
291   unimportant tokens, no longer processing them forward or computing backward gradients, thus
292   significantly reducing computational complexity.

314
      tokens at the last layer. Since each token (except for token x[CLS] ) precisely corresponds to a patch
      area in the input image, these remaining tokens can indicate which areas are more important.
315
316   Mask construction. As shown in the right panel of Fig. 3, we construct a mask matrix to highlight
317   the most important areas in the generated image and mask unimportant areas. The mask matrix is
318   constructed by setting values of 1 at the positions of remaining tokens and 0 elsewhere.
319   Meta-training with sparse tokens. We multiply the mask with the generated image to create a masked
320   image, highlighting the important areas (such as foregrounds). When meta-training the meta-LoRA,
321   we only feed forward the unmasked tokens, significantly speeding up the meta-training process.
322   Our visualization results (Fig. 4) demonstrate that the unmasked areas typically correspond to the
323   foregrounds while masked areas correspond to the backgrounds. Interestingly, the findings in Tab. 2
      and Tab. 3 suggest that masking the backgrounds can lead to performance improvements.

      5.1   R ECYCLE I N -D OMAIN L O RA S
383
384   In-domain benchmarks. For the scenario of “recycle in-domain LoRAs”, we leverage four datasets
385   widely used in recent meta-learning works, including CIFAR-FS (Bertinetto et al., 2018), MiniIma-
386   geNet (Vinyals et al., 2016), VGG-Flower (Nilsback & Zisserman, 2008) and CUB-200-2011 (CUB)
387   (Wah et al., 2011). These datasets span a wide range, from general natural images (in CIFAR-FS
388   and MiniImageNet) to more specialized ones, focusing specifically on different species of birds
389   (in CUB) and flowers (in VGG-Flower). Following standard splits in the context of meta-learning
390
      (Finn et al., 2017), we split each dataset into separate meta-training and meta-testing subsets, which
      have non-overlapping label spaces. To construct a N -way K-shot meta-training or testing task, we
391
      randomly sample N classes and K examples per class from meta-training or testing subsets.
392
393   In-domain setup. We collect 100 LoRAs pre-tuned on diverse 5-way tasks constructed from one
394   specific meta-training subset. Our evaluation, in contrast, is based on meta-testing tasks constructed
395   from the corresponding meta-testing subset. This setup ensures the pre-tuned LoRAs and meta-
396
      testing tasks originate from the same domain (dataset) but with non-overlapping label spaces.
397   Implementation details. In the pre-tuning phase, we fine-tune the LoRAs with rank r = 4 and
398   classification heads using the Adam optimizer with a learning rate of 1×10−3 . We find LoRA works
399   quite well when fine-tuned with sufficient data. During meta-training, the meta-LoRA is optimized
400   with the Adam optimizer, with a learning rate of 1 × 10−3 . We use a learning rate schedule that
401   cycles every 100 iterations. It starts with a linear warm-up stage by linearly increasing the learning
402
      rate from 1 × 10−5 to 1 × 10−3 over the first 25 iterations. This initial increase is then followed by a
      cosine annealing stage, where the learning rate smoothly decreases following a cosine curve for the
403
      next 75 iterations. For LoRA Inversion, we optimize the synthetic data using the Adam optimizer
404
      with a learning rate of 0.25 for 2000 iterations. The resolution of the synthetic image is 224 × 224.
405   We empirically set the hyperparameter αR = 0.01. For data processing, we adopt widely used
406   data augmentation techniques including random horizontal flip and normalization in meta-training
407   and only normalization in meta-testing. Discussions on hyperparameter selection and sensitivity
408   analysis are provided in App. C.
409
      Results and analysis. Tab. 2 shows the results for the “recycle in-domain LoRAs” scenario. No-
410
      table findings are as follows: (i) LoRA Recycle surpasses the best fine-tuning-based baselines by
411   considerable margins, especially up to 9.80% for 1-shot learning. It also outperforms the top fine-
412   tuning-free baselines by up to 10.01% for 1-shot learning, confirming its superior adaptability with-
413   out the need for fine-tuning. (ii) “Full fine-tuning” performs the worst, as it tends to overfit when
414   tuning large models with extremely few examples. This issue also leads to poor performance of
415   “P > M > F”, though the second meta-training stage helps mitigate it to some extent. “LoRAs
416   Avg” and “LoRAHub” do not ensure effective generalization to new tasks. The reason is that each
417   pre-tuned LoRA targets different tasks, and the arithmetic operation like averaging in the parameter
418   space lacks precise alignment among different LoRAs. Moreover, these baselines do not explicitly
419
      incorporate meta-learning objectives, which have proven to be useful for enhancing generalization in
      few-shot learning. (iii) Meta-training with sparse tokens can bring performance gains up to 1.34%.
420
      A reasonable explanation is that masking the backgrounds helps prevent overfitting to noise and
421
      avoids spurious correlations between the foreground and background (Ye et al., 2024). More discus-
422   sions on the different performance gains across datasets are provided in App. E.

476
477
      How to choose the overall pruning ratio and pruning layers? To enhance efficiency, we can
      alternatively adopt our proposed double-efficient mechanism. (i) The first step is to determine the
478
      overall pruning ratio (i.e., sparse ratio of the synthetic data). As shown in Tab. 2 and Tab. 3, pruning
479
      overall 50% or 75% of tokens enhances the performance and tightly preserves the foregrounds (see
480   Fig. 4). (ii) The second step is to select the pruning layers. To achieve a 75% overall pruning
481   ratio, we could prune 75% of tokens at shallow layers or deep layers. Choosing different pruning
482   layers only affects the speed of inversion, while the speed of the following meta-training depends
483   solely on the overall pruning ratio. The choice of pruning layers is flexible and depends on needs:
484   Tab. 4 suggests pruning at the deeper layer for better performance or at shallower layers for faster
485   inversion. The choice of middle-layer pruning or multi-layer pruning across shallow and deep layers
      can balance the trade-off between efficiency and performance to some extent.

      6   C ONCLUSION
527
      In this paper, we reveal the limitations of current VFMs, which necessitate (i) explicit fine-tuning
528
      and (ii) sufficient data when adapting to new tasks. These limitations restrict their applicability
529
      in data-limited scenarios requiring real-time responses. From a novel perspective, we explore the
530   potential of reusing diverse pre-tuned LoRAs without accessing their private training data, to im-
531   prove the few-shot adaptability of VFMs without requiring further fine-tuning. To achieve this, we
532   propose a data-free meta-learning framework named LoRA Recycle, which distills a meta-LoRA
533   from diverse pre-tuned LoRAs using synthetic data generated via LoRA Inversion. The VFM, once
534   equipped with the meta-LoRA, is empowered to solve new few-shot tasks in a single forward pass
535   without further fine-tuning. To further improve efficiency, we propose a double efficient mechanism
536   achieving significant meta-training acceleration while maintaining or even improving performance.
537   Comprehensive experiments across eight datasets within both in- and cross-domain scenarios ver-
538
      ify the superiority of our framework in significantly improving the few-shot adaptability of VFMs
      without further fine-tuning.

      Meta-learn what? Our framework meta-trains an extra lightweight LoRA while keeping the orig-
865   inal VFM frozen. Based on the results shown in Tab. 8, we summarize some findings: (i) Meta-
866   training the entire VFM is inferior to only meta-training the extra LoRA. Meta-training the entire
867   VFM might distort the original feature space (Kumar et al., 2022), leading to bias to meta-training
868   tasks and heavy costs of computation and storage. Meta-training the extra LoRAs can preserve the
869   knowledge of foundation models learned from large-scale pretraining while injecting task-specific
870   knowledge into extra LoRAs. (ii) Only meta-training the last 6 LoRA layers can outperform meta-
871   training all LoRA layers. The improvements are more obvious in 5-way 1-shot learning, suggesting
872   that reducing learnable parameters possibly avoids overfitting with limited training data. Only meta-
873
      training the first 6 LoRA layers is less effective. This is because only updating the shallow layers is
      insufficient to develop effective representations compared with updating the deep layers.

      Preliminary of ViTs. Here, we discuss the operational mechanism behind ViTs. ViTs initially
      divide the input image X I belonging to the space RH×W ×C into n + 1 distinct, non-overlapping
954
      patches. These patches are then transformed into n+1 tokens, denoted as X I = [x[CLS] , x1 , ..., xn ]
955

956   classification task. To integrate positional relationships, learnable position encodings are added to
957   all tokens. These tokens are then processed through multiple ViT layers, which are composed of
958   multi-head self-attention (MHSA) modules and feed-forward networks (FFN). Within each MHSA,
959   the token set X I undergoes the transformation into three distinct matrices: the query Q, key K, and
960   value V matrices. The formulation of the attention mechanism is given by

       Discussions on the inconsistent performance gains across various datasets. When we use Lo-
       RAs from the dataset the same as the testing dataset (in-domain setting), those LoRAs can provide
1036
       domain-specific priors. This is particularly useful when the foundation model’s pre-training dataset
1037
       varies from the testing dataset. The main paper’s Tab. 2 confirms this, showing a higher perfor-
1038   mance gain on CIFAR-FS (+10.01%) than other datasets (average +4.98%). The larger disparity
1039   between CIFAR-FS and the pre-training dataset is supported by the baseline NN in the main paper’s
1040   Tab. 2, showing that directly transferring the foundation model to CIFAR-FS results in a lower ac-
1041   curacy (78.06%) compared to other testing datasets (average 85.31%). When we use LoRAs from
1042   datasets different from the testing dataset (cross-domain setting), performance gains across datasets
1043   are relatively stable, since these LoRAs offer limited useful domain-specific priors for all testing
1044   datasets.
1045   Paradigms for Adaptable Foundation Models Several paradigms have been proposed to make
1046   large foundation models more adaptable. These paradigms involve combinations among Pre-training
1047   (P), Meta-learning (M), Fine-tuning (F) or PEFT, and In-context learning (I). Here, we provide a
1048   discussion over three paradigms, including P>F or P>PEFT, P>M>F and P>M>I. > indicates
1049   the sequence. Traditional P>F and P>PEFT (Fu et al., 2023b; Lee et al., 2023; Sun et al., 2019)
1050   often fail to adapt foundation models to data-limited and real-time applications due to their need for
1051   sufficient data and explicit fine-tuning.
1052   An emerging strategy, P>M>F, introduces a meta-learning phase before fine-tuning, preparing the
1053   pre-trained model for subsequent fine-tuning. This paradigm has shown promising results in vision
1054   (Hu et al., 2022; Cai & Shen, 2020), language (Gheini et al., 2023; Bansal et al., 2022; Hou et al.,
1055   2022) and vision-language (Yeh et al., 2023; Najdenkoska et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2023b) domains.
1056   More recently, the P>M>I paradigm has been proposed in language domains, aiming to acquire
1057   more advanced in-context learning ability of LLMs. For example, LLMs are equipped with the
1058   instruction-following ability by meta-training on a broad range of tasks accompanied by instructions
1059   (Iyer et al., 2022; Chung et al., 2022). MetaICL (Min et al., 2022) and ICT (Chen et al., 2022)
1060   explicitly meta-train LLMs to learn to learn in context. However, paradigms for fine-tuning-free
1061   adaptation in VFMs are less explored, hindered by their inherent in-context learning limitations
1062   compared to LLMs.

       Limited scalability to large-scale models. Current data-free meta-learning (DFML) methodolo-
1067
       gies, as discussed in (Wang et al., 2022; Hu et al., 2023a;b), predominantly focus on leveraging
1068   small-scale pre-trained models and meta-learners, such as four-layer CNNs or ResNet12. A critical
1069   limitation of these approaches is their inability to scale up to larger models, particularly those based
1070   on transformer architectures. This scalability issue substantially hinders their practical application in
1071   complex, real-world scenarios. For instance, (Wang et al., 2022) employs a hyper-network with all
1072   pre-trained models as inputs and outputs a single fused model. The efficiency of this method declines
1073   significantly when outputting all parameters of larger models, given the hyper-network’s extensive
1074   input and output dimensions. Similarly, inversion-based DFML methods, such as those in (Hu et al.,
1075   2023a;b), rely on meta-training a meta-learner with data inverted from pre-trained models. The
1076
       model inversion process becomes inefficient for large-scale models. The following meta-training
       process often necessitates the computation of Hessian matrices for second-order derivatives (Nichol
1077
       et al., 2018), which becomes exceedingly resource-intensive for large-scale models.
1078
1079   Inefficiency issues. Beyond scalability challenges, inversion-based methods like (Hu et al., 2023a;b)
       are plagued by the inefficiency of the model inversion processes. These methods typically involve

1080
       iterative forward and backward optimizations, leading to significant computational and storage costs
1081   when applied to large-scale models.
1082
1083   Addressing these issues: our contributions. Our Double-Efficient Data-Free Meta-Learning
1084
       framework presents a novel and efficient solution that is scalable for transformer-based founda-
       tion models. To overcome existing limitations, (i) we propose a data-free meta-learning framework,
1085
       which is specifically designed for large-scale VFM. We only meta-train meta-LoRA, constituting
1086
       only 0.14M parameters (merely 0.1% relative to the VFM). (ii) We propose a meta-learning objec-
1087   tive, as outlined in Eq. (3), that avoids the resource-intensive computation of Hessian matrices. This
1088   is achieved as our fine-tuning-free adaptation in the inner loop does not require gradient computa-
1089   tions. (iii) We propose a double-efficient mechanism that significantly speeds up the meta-training
1090   processes while maintaining comparable or enhanced performance. Our approach not only addresses
1091   the limited scalability and inefficiency issues of existing DFML methods, but also inspires more in-
1092   teractions between meta-learning and foundation models.
