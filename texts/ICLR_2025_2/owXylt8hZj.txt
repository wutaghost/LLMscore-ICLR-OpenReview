009                                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               In this work, we present a comprehensive analysis of causal image modeling and
012            introduce the Adventurer series models where we treat images as sequences of
013            patch tokens and employ uni-directional language models to learn visual repre-
014            sentations. This modeling paradigm allows us to process images in a recurrent
015            formulation with linear complexity relative to the sequence length, which can ef-
016            fectively address the memory and computation explosion issues posed by high-
017            resolution and fine-grained images. In detail, we introduce two simple designs that
018            seamlessly integrate image inputs into the causal inference framework: a global
019            pooling token placed at the beginning of the sequence and a flipping operation
020
               between every two layers. Extensive empirical studies demonstrate the significant
               efficiency and effectiveness of this causal image modeling paradigm. For exam-
021
               ple, our base-sized Adventurer model attains a competitive test accuracy of 84.0%
022
               on the standard ImageNet-1k benchmark with 216 images/s training throughput,
023            which is 5.3× more efficient than vision transformers to achieve the same result.

      In fact, even under good lighting conditions, the area that the human eye can focus on at one time is
077
      very limited. For example, when you force your eyesight to focus on this word, you cannot count
078   how many words there are in this line. The understanding of complex visual scenes actually relies
079   on the rapid movement of the eyes, known as the Saccade Mechanism (Liversedge & Findlay, 2000;
080   Leigh & Zee, 2015). Under this mechanism, the human eye perceives only a very small visual area
081   at a time, and then the eyesight rapidly scans between areas to gain a comprehensive understanding
082   of the whole scene.
083
      This visual understanding mechanism inspires us to consider the possibility of modeling images as
084   1D sequences of patches. However, in contrast to vision transformers (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021)
085   that require referring all other tokens when understanding each patch token and thereby result in
086   quadratic complexity with respect to sequence length, we aim to employ a more efficient causal
087   modeling approach, which scales linearly with the number of patches and better aligns with the low
088   power consumption characteristic of the human visual system.
089
      To this end, a very straightforward way is dividing the image into non-overlapping patches, flattening
090   them as a 1D token sequence, and then processing them simply by a uni-directional language model.
091   Nonetheless, this naive causal modeling approach is not directly applicable to image understanding
092   because in a uni-directional sequence, each token can only receive information from the tokens
093   before it, which results in tokens at the start of the sequence having poor representations due to the
094   lack of global context.
095
      But interestingly, we find that this issue can be effectively addressed with just two simple designs:
096   first, we place an average pooling token at the beginning of the sequence, which is computed by
097   the average of all other tokens in the sequence. This allows tokens at the start of the sequence to
098   access sufficient global information, thus enhancing the quality of their representations. Second,
099   we introduce sequence flipping operations between layers of the model to counteract the informa-
100   tion imbalance caused by positional differences among tokens. We term this two simple designs
101   heading average and inter-layer flipping, respectively. The overall framework of this Causal Image
102   Modeling (CIM) paradigm is shown in Figure 1.
103   To validate the efficiency and effectiveness of our CIM framework, we first integrate it with Vision
104   Transformer (ViT) architectures, with Figure 2a showing the difference in attention masks compared
105   to the standard ViT and a naive causal ViT. As we analyzed earlier, causal models can easily im-
106   pact the representation of tokens at the beginning of the sequence and thereby degrade predictive
107   performance. In Figure 2b, we observe a 1.16% and 1.13% accuracy decrease on ImageNet (Deng
      et al., 2009) when employing a naive causal ViT-Small and Base respectively. However, this issue is

      well addressed by our reformed causal framework, while our causal ViTs can seamlessly match the

111   This experiment can directly demonstrate the following three points:
112
            1. Causal modeling is sufficient for image understanding. We find that when equipped with
113
               the heading average token and sequence flipping operations, the uni-directional language
114            models can be directly used for image encoding and achieve results competitive with stan-
115            dard ViTs. We will further elaborate in the following sections that this modeling approach
116            is also applicable to various visual understanding tasks such as semantic segmentation,
117            object detection, and instance segmentation.
118         2. The standard ViT involves considerable redundant computations. As shown in Fig-
119            ure 2, causal modeling ignores around half of the computation in self-attention but can
120            attain almost the same accuracy of standard ViTs. With appropriate parallel processing
121            mechanisms (Dao et al., 2022), causal modeling can substantially accelerate self-attention.
122            For instance, when processing a sequence of 2,000 tokens, causal attention can achieve a
123            speed increase of about 50% compared to the fully-visible attention.
124         3. Visual backbones can be much more efficient. Besides being able to speed up self-
125            attention, the greater advantage of causal modeling lies in the ability to incorporate RNN-
126            like token mixers such as Mamba (Gu & Dao, 2023; Dao & Gu, 2024), whose computation
127            scales linearly with sequence length. Compared to the quadratic complexity of transform-
128            ers, this unique advantage can effectively solve the problems of computational and memory
129
               explosion when processing high-resolution and fine-grained images.
130
      We term our causal image models Adventurer to echo the thought experiment at the beginning. As
131   shown in Figure 1, the Adventurer models consist of a number of causal token mixers and chan-
132   nel mixers, incorporating our newly introduced mechanisms of heading average and inter-layer se-
133   quence flipping to process image tokens. By default, we use the latest Mamba-2 (Dao & Gu, 2024)
134   structure as the token mixer and SwiGLU MLP (Touvron et al., 2023) blocks as the channel mixer.
135   Empirically, our Adventurer models exhibit strong capabilities in image understanding, showcasing
136   highly competitive results in classification, segmentation, and detection tasks. In dealing with long
137   sequences, our model demonstrates a significant speed advantage. For example, with an input size
138   of 448×448 and a patch size of 8×8, i.e., a sequence of over 3,000 tokens, our Base-sized model
139
      achieves a competitive test accuracy of 84.6% on ImageNet (Deng et al., 2009) with 5.3 times faster
      training speed compared with ViT-Base at the same input scale.

144   Generic vision backbones. Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) (LeCun et al., 1998) have long
145   been the dominant backbone architecture for various vision tasks. Over time, the CNN structures
146   have experienced a series of major innovations (Krizhevsky et al., 2012; Simonyan & Zisserman,
147
      2015; He et al., 2016; Huang et al., 2017; Tan & Le, 2019; Liu et al., 2022) and now remain competi-
      tive to modern visual architectures. Vision transformers (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021), on the other hand,
148
      has recently represented a paradigm shift from CNNs’ hierarchical feature extraction to patch-by-
149
      patch visual encoding. Since the introduction of ViTs, the research community has made substantial
150   strides in developing more robust and efficient training approaches (Touvron et al., 2021; 2022),
151   optimizing model designs (Liu et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2021a; Yuan et al., 2021), and advancing
152   the frontiers of self-supervised learning (Chen et al., 2021b; Caron et al., 2021; Bao et al., 2022; He
153   et al., 2022; Oquab et al., 2023).
154
      Mamba and state space models. State Space Models (SSMs) have long been utilized in control
155
      systems, primarily to handle continuous inputs (Kalman, 1960). Advancements in discretization
156   methods (Tallec & Ollivier, 2018; Gu et al., 2020; Nguyen et al., 2022; Gu et al., 2023) have ex-
157   panded the application of SSMs to deep learning, particularly in modeling sequential data (Gu
158   et al., 2022; 2021; Smith et al., 2022). SSMs broadly include any recurrent models that utilize a
159   latent state, ranging from traditional models like Hidden Markov Models (Eddy, 1996) and RNNs to
160   more modern approaches such as Linear Attention (Katharopoulos et al., 2020), RetNet (Sun et al.,
161   2023), and RWKV (Peng et al., 2023). Recently, Gu & Dao (2023) introduce Mamba, a novel SSM
      block that leverages structured SSMs along with state expansion optimized for hardware efficiency.

      Mamba in visual applications. Similar to the Transformer’s success in NLP and its adoption in
163   vision tasks, Mamba has also been extended to vision fields. For example, Zhu et al. (2024) stack
164   forward and backward scanning blocks to capture bidirectional information, addressing the direc-
165   tionality issue inherent in causal models. Liu et al. (2024b) introduce a hybrid architecture that
166   integrates Mamba with 2D convolution, enabling the capture of contextual information from mul-
167   tiple directions and dimensions. Hatamizadeh & Kautz (2024) utilize a framework that integrates
168   Mamba with self-attention, enhancing the model’s capability to capture long-range spatial relation-
169   ships. Shi et al. (2024) proposes the Visual State Space Duality (VSSD) model, which enhances
170   the performance and efficiency of state space models in sequential modeling tasks by incorporating
171
      non-causality and multi-scan strategies. Mamba-based architectures have extended to a wide range
      of various vision domains (Li et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2024; Huang et al., 2024;
172
      Ren et al., 2024; Lieber et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024a) .

179   Overall, we follow the practice of vision transformers (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021) that incorporate
180
      patch embedding, positional embedding, token mixers and channel mixers to build our Adventurer
      models. Formally, given an input image I ∈ R3×h×w , we first divide it into non-overlapping patches

      of size p × p, flattening them to form a token sequence X ∈ Rhw/p ×d , where d denotes the number
      of hidden channels. For easy notations, here we assume h = w and denote n = h2 /p2 . Similar
183
      to language models, we append the class token at the end of sequence to represent global features.
184
      For positional embeddings, here we simply use a learnable matrix P ∈ R(n+1)×d that is added to
185
      the patch and class tokens. We leave the exploration of position encoding more suitable for causal

      models to future works.

202   We present detailed configurations of our Adventurer models in Table 1. As mentioned above,
203   Mamba (Gu & Dao, 2023) is a RNN-like state space model that has incorporated very efficient
204
      hardware-aware designs to support parallel computation. Due to its recurrent formulation, Mamba’s
      computational complexity scales linearly with sequence length, offering an efficient modeling ap-
205
      proach that allows us to increase input resolution or reduce patch size to obtain more detailed visual
206
      information. For Mamba-2 blocks, we adopt a 2× expand ratio and set the feature dimension to be a
207   multiple of 256 to better leverage its parallel efficiency (Dao & Gu, 2024). Following the recent ad-
208   vances of large language models (Touvron et al., 2023), we opt to use an improved MLP block with
209   SwiGLU activation as channel mixer. We set the hidden dimension of MLP to 2.5× input/output
210   dimension to appropriately reduce the computational load.

509   Designing the heading token. To develop Adventurer models, we have tried many different types of
510   heading tokens to facilitate our one-way scanning approach. Here we present these ablation studies
511   in Table 7. First, instead of using a single heading token that is calculated by globally pooling all
512   patches, we can employ relatively finer-grained tokens to represent the global context. In detail, we
513   attempt to equally divide all patch tokens into N grids in the 2D space, averaging the features within
514
      each grid to form more and finer heading tokens. However, as summarized in Table 7a, this strategy
      does not improve performance and may disrupt the original feature distribution, leading to accuracy
515
      degradation when there are too many (e.g., 9) heading tokens.
516
517   In Table 7b, we also try two more variants that duplicate the class token or train a new learnable
518   token as the heading token, yet both fall short when comparing with our default setup. Additionally,
519   Table 7c proves that it is necessary to recalculate the heading average token at the beginning of each
520
      Adventurer layers. These experiments validate our initial hypothesis about the functionality of the
      heading average mechanism: we need a token that compresses as much global context as possible
521
      to serve as the starting point of the sequence. This helps address the issue of tokens at the beginning
522
      of the sequence receiving insufficient information. Calculating an average token before the start of
523   each layer proves to be the most straightforward and effective method to accomplish this objective.

      5     C ONCLUSION
527
528
529   In this work, we are motivated to build a simple and efficient visual backbone by causal image mod-
530   eling. Through extensive empirical studies across various image understanding tasks, we find that
531
      by employing two simple mechanisms-—Heading Average and Inter-Layer Flipping-—causal mod-
      els can well integrate with visual inputs, matching or even surpassing the predictive performance of
532
      traditional vision transformers. Compared to existing vision Mamba architectures (e.g., Vim and
533
      MambaReg), our Adventurer framework achieves a significant speed improvement of 4∼5 times by
534   simplifying the structure, greatly enhancing the practicality of causal models in vision tasks. Addi-
535   tionally, we demonstrate that our causal image modeling approach is also applicable to transformer
536   architectures, which is able to reduce nearly half of the computational redundancy in self-attention
537   while seamlessly matching the ViT’s original performance, showcasing that Adventurer is a general-
538   izable form of image models. In future works, we will fully exploit the speed and linear complexity
539   advantages of Adventurer, experimenting with higher image resolutions or smaller patch sizes to
      further explore its capabilities in handling very long visual sequences.

696   been found to be able to effectively prevent Mamba’s over-fitting and save training time. The detailed
697   configurations of each stage are shown in Table 8, Table 9, and Table 10, respectively. For the Tiny-
698   sized model, we directly train with the Small/Base’s stage-2 recipe for 300 epochs since the training
699   time is short and the tiny model is not easy to get overfit. To train the Large-sized model, we make
700   major modifications of the recipe compared with Mamba-Register to further shorten training time.
701   For all stages, the actually learning rate is calculated by base lr ∗ batchsize/512; the color jitter
      factor is set to 0.3; the mixup alpha and cutmix alpha are set to 0.8 and 1.0, respectively.
