009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Understanding the evolution of 3D scenes is important for effective autonomous
012            driving. While conventional methods model the scene development with the motion
013            of individual instances, world models emerge as a generative framework to describe
014            the general scene dynamics. However, most existing methods adopt an autoregres-
015            sive framework to perform next-token prediction, which suffer from inefficiency
016            to model long-term temporal evolutions. To address this, we propose a diffusion-
017            based 4D occupancy generation model, OccSora, to simulate the development of
018
               the 3D world for autonomous driving. We employ a 4D scene tokenizer to obtain
               compact discrete spatial-temporal representations for 4D occupancy input and
019
               achieve high-quality reconstruction for long-sequence occupancy videos. We then
020
               learn a diffusion transformer on the spatial-temporal representations and generate
021            4D occupancy conditioned on a trajectory prompt. We conduct extensive experi-
022            ments on the widely used nuScenes dataset with Occ3D occupancy annotations.
023            OccSora can generate 16s videos with authentic 3D layout and temporal consis-
024            tency, demonstrating its ability to understand the spatial and temporal distributions
025            of driving scenes. With trajectory-aware 4D generation, OccSora has the potential
026            to serve as a world simulator for the decision-making of autonomous driving.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   As a critical application of artificial intelligence technology, autonomous driving has garnered
032   widespread attention and research in recent years (Hu et al., 2023b; Fu et al., 2024; Yang et al.,
033
      2023a). Establishing the relationship between perception (Liu et al., 2023c; Chang et al., 2023;
      Chen et al., 2023; Mao et al., 2023), prediction, and planning (Mozaffari et al., 2020; Huang et al.,
034
      2023b; Jia et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024b) in autonomous driving is crucial for a comprehensive
035
      understanding of the field.
036
037   Conventional autonomous driving models (Hu et al., 2023b) rely on the motion of the ego vehicle
038   instances to model the development of scenes, unable to develop a profound understanding of scene
039
      perception and vehicle motion control comparable to human understanding. The emergence and
      establishment of world models (Ha and Schmidhuber, 2018) offer new possibilities for a deeper
040
      understanding of the comprehensive relationship between autonomous driving scenes and vehicle
041
      motion. Based on strong image pretrained models, image-based world models (Hu et al., 2023a;
042   Wang et al., 2023b) are able to generate high-quality driving-scene images with conditions of 3D
043   bounding boxes. OccWorld (Zheng et al., 2023) further learns a world model in the 3D occupancy
044   space, which can be better leveraged for 3D reasoning for autonomous driving. However, most
045   existing methods adopt an autoregressive framework to model the dynamics (e.g., image tokens,
046   bounding boxes, occupancy) of a 3D scene, and thus cannot efficiently produce long-term sequences.
047
      To address this, we propose a 4D world model OccSora to directly generate spatial-temporal repre-
048   sentations with diffusion models as shown in Figure 1, motivated by OpenAI’s 2D video generation
049   model Sora (Brooks et al., 2024). To accurately understand and represent 4D scenes, we design 4D
050   scene discretization to capture the dynamic characteristics of scenes and propose a diffusion-based
051   world model to achieve controllable scene generation in accordance with physical laws. Specifically,
052   in the 4D occupancy scene tokenizer, we focus on extracting and compressing real 4D scenes to
053   establish an understanding of the world model environment. In the diffusion-based world model, we
      employ multidimensional diffusion techniques to propagate accurate spatiotemporal 4D information

      3D Occupancy Prediction. 3D occupancy focuses on partitioning space into voxels and assigning
072
      specific semantic types to each voxel. It is considered a crucial means of representing real-world
073   scenes, following 3D object detection (Mao et al., 2023; Ma et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2024) and Bird’s
074   Eye View (BEV) perception (Yang et al., 2023b; Zhao et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2023a; Zhang et al.,
075   2022), for autonomous driving perception tasks. Early research on this task primarily focused on
076   semantically classifying discrete points from LiDAR (Zhou et al., 2021; Singh et al., 2020; Liu et al.,
077   2023a). In fact, due to the camera containing semantic information far exceeding that of LiDAR and
078   their low cost. Thus, utilizing images for depth estimation or employing end-to-end methods for
079   3D scene perception research is currently the mainstream approach (Huang et al., 2023a; Li et al.,
080   2023; Wei et al., 2023). Considering the advantageous of multi-sensor systems, some studies research
081
      multi-modal fusion for 3D occupancy prediction (Wang et al., 2023c; Zhang and Ding, 2024).
082   In addition to utilizing typical sensor devices for 3D occupancy prediction, some studies focus on
083   other tasks involving occupancy. For instance, OccWorld (Zheng et al., 2023) proposes a spatiotempo-
084   ral generative transformer to predict subsequent scene tokens and the vehicle token, thereby predicting
085   future occupancy and vehicle trajectory. On the other hand, GenOcc (Wang et al., 2024a) utilizes
086   generative models to accomplish occupancy prediction. DriveWorld (Min et al., 2024) introduces
087
      a world-model-based framework for learning in autonomous driving from 2D images and videos,
      addressing tasks such as 3D object detection, online map creation, and occupancy prediction. Al-
088
      though progress has been made in 3D occupancy prediction and continuous 4D prediction, the scope
089
      of these studies remains limited. They usually use autoregressive models in conjunction with scene
090   information from preceding frames to carry out subsequent occupancy tasks, thereby necessitating
091   prior scene or 3D bounding box inputs. Besides, the performance drop over time is significant in
092   both 4D occupancy forecasting and motion planning, demonstrating the struggle of autoregressive
093   models for generation in the autonomous driving field. This is because the complexity of capturing
094   the spatial and temporal information of vehicle movements and scenes is greater than that of language
095   text. Consequently, they lack a genuine understanding of the fundamental relationships between
096   scene and motion, and they do not constitute world models conditioned on actions.
097   Generative Model. Generative models have garnered widespread attention recently due to their pow-
098   erful capabilities. By learning the probability distribution of data, generative models can train models
099   capable of generating new samples. From the emergence of Generative Adversarial Networks (GAN)
100   (Goodfellow et al., 2020) to the recent advent of diffusion models like Variational Autoencoders
101   (VAE) (Van Den Oord et al., 2017), the tasks of generative models have gradually expanded from
102   initial image generation tasks to in-depth studies on videos (Yan et al., 2021). Tasks such as image
103   generation based on the DIT model (Peebles and Xie, 2023) delve into and utilize their generative
104
      capabilities. The Sora video generation model (Brooks et al., 2024) further demonstrates the ability
      to produce high-quality videos with realistic transitions between frames in continuous scenes.
105
106   Similarly, in the field of autonomous driving, controllable image generation can provide various
107   driving scenarios to serve perception, planning, control, and decision-making tasks. For instance,
      MagicDriver (Gao et al., 2023) generates videos depicting various weather scenarios by learning

141   3      O CC S ORA
142   3.1      W ORLD M ODEL FOR AUTONOMOUS D RIVING
143
144   Considering that 4D occupancy can comprehensively capture the structural, semantic, and temporal
145   information of a 3D scene and effectively facilitate weak supervision or self-supervised learning,
146
      it can be applied to visual, LiDAR, or multimodal tasks. Based on these principles, we represent
      the world model χ as 4D occupancy R. Figure 2 illustrates the overall framework of OccSora.
147
      We constructed a 4D occupancy scene tokenizer (Van Den Oord et al., 2017) to compress real 4D
148
      occupancy Ri ∈ RB×D×H×W ×T in both the temporal T and spatial D × H ×W dimensions, capturing
149   the relationships and evolution patterns in 4D autonomous driving scenes. This results in compressed
150   high-level tokens Rm ∈ RB×c×h×w×t and reconstructed 4D occupancy data Ro ∈ RB×D×H×W ×T . We
151   designed a diffusion-based world model that uses trajectory information Rr ∈ RB×T ×2 as control units,
152   training them along with the compressed tokens Rm to generate high-dimensional scene representation
153   tokens To ∈ RB×c×h×w×t . These are then decoded by the 4D occupancy scene tokenizer into physically
154   consistent and dynamically controllable Ro .

      4     E XPERIMENTS
299   As the first 4D occupancy world model in the field of autonomous driving, OccSora offers a deeper
300   understanding of the relationship between autonomous driving scenes and vehicle trajectories without
301
      requiring any input of 3D bounding boxes, maps, or historical information. It can construct a long-
      time sequence world model that adheres to physical laws. We have conducted a series of quantitative
302
      experiments and visualizations to illustrate this.

305
      4.1   I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
306
      Our experiments are conducted on the widely used nuScenes-Occupancy dataset (Caesar et al.,
307   2020), which is currently one of the most mainstream and standard datasets, supporting many well-
308   known research studies (Hu et al., 2023b; Wang et al., 2023c). For the OccSora-Base network, we
309   applied three rounds of compression to 32 consecutive frames and increased its channel dimension
310   to 128. Subsequently, we conducted further comparative and ablation experiments under different
311   components and trajectory scenarios. We trained using the AdamW optimizer with an initial learning
312   rate set to 1 × 10−5 and a weight decay of 0.01. Using 8 NVIDIA GeForce A100 GPUs, we set a
313   batch size of 2 per GPU. For the training of the 4D occupancy scene tokenizer, we needed about
314   42GB of memory per GPU to train for 150 epochs, which took 50.6 hours. For the diffusion-based
315
      world model, we needed about 47GB of memory per GPU to train for 1,200,000 steps, which took
      108 hours. We used the Fréchet distance between the spatial-temporal latent representations, referred
316
      to as FOD, as the evaluation metric for the generation.

527   5     C ONCLUSION AND L IMITATIONS
528
529   In this paper, we have introduced a framework for generating 4D occupancy to simulate 3D world
530
      development in autonomous driving. Using a 4D scene tokenizer, we obtain compact representations
      for input and achieve high-quality reconstruction for long-sequence occupancy videos. Then, we learn
531
      a diffusion transformer on the spatiotemporal representations and generate 4D occupancy conditioned
532
      on a trajectory prompt. Through experiments on the nuScenes dataset, we demonstrate accurate scene
533   evolution. In the future, we will investigate more refined 4D occupancy world models and explore
534   the possibilities of end-to-end autonomous driving under closed-loop data environments.
535
536
      Limitations. The advantage of a 4D occupancy world model lies in its ability to establish an
      understanding of the relationship between scenes and motion. However, due to limitations in the
537
      granularity of voxel data within the dataset, constructing more finely detailed 4D scenes is not feasible.
538
      Moreover, using the real-world occupancy of the first frame to explore long-term 4D occupancy
539   prediction tasks with controllable trajectories is an important direction for future research.

839   8.4   T EMPORAL -S PATIAL E FFICIENCY A NALYSIS
840
841   OccSora operates as a diffusion-based generative framework and OccWorld functions as an au-
842   toregressive approach; the two exhibit substantial differences in generation efficiency. OccWorld
843
      generates data sequentially for each timestep, resulting in a total generation time of TAR = T · tAR
      with a time complexity of O(T ), implying that generation time increases linearly with the sequence
844
      length T . In contrast, OccSora generates the entire sequence in parallel through denoising steps,
845
      with a total generation time of TGM = N · tGM and a time complexity of O(1). Here, the number of
846   denoising steps N is typically much smaller than T . As a result, when T ≫ N, OccSora demonstrates
847   significantly higher temporal efficiency than OccWorld, especially in tasks requiring long-sequence
848   generation. Quantitative analyses are provided in Table 10. Additionally, considering that the test time
849   varies depending on the experimental setup, we provide detailed explanations of the experimental
850   configurations in Table 11. This includes the number of sampling iterations, compression ratio, input
851   dimensions, model dimensions, and evaluation metrics.
