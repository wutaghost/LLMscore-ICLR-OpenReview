009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011             The development of safe and beneficial AI requires that systems can learn and
012             act in accordance with human preferences. However, explicitly specifying these
013             preferences by hand is often infeasible. Inverse reinforcement learning (IRL) ad-
014             dresses this challenge by inferring preferences, represented as reward functions,
015             from expert behavior. We introduce Q-based Variational IRL (QVIRL), a novel
016             Bayesian IRL method that recovers a posterior distribution over rewards from ex-
017             pert demonstrations via primarily learning a variational distribution over Q-values.
018
                Unlike previous approaches, QVIRL combines scalability with uncertainty quan-
                tification, important for safety-critical applications. We demonstrate QVIRL’s
019
                strong performance in apprenticeship learning across various tasks, including clas-
020
                sical control problems and safe navigation in the Safety Gymnasium suite, where
021             the method’s uncertainty quantification allows us to produce safer policies.

024   1       I NTRODUCTION
025
026
      Stuart Russell (2019) has suggested three principles to guide the development of beneficial artificial
027   intelligence, from autonomous industrial robots and autonomous vehicles to advanced future sys-
028   tems: AI’s only objective is realizing human preferences; AI is initially uncertain about what these
029   preferences are; and the ultimate source of information about human preferences is human behavior.
030   Apprenticeship learning1 via Bayesian inverse reinforcement learning (IRL) can be understood as
031   a possible operationalization of these principles: Bayesian IRL starts with a prior distribution over
032   reward functions representing initial uncertainty about human preferences.2 It then combines this
033   prior with demonstration data from a human expert acting approximately optimally with respect to
034   an unknown reward, to produce a posterior distribution over rewards. In apprenticeship learning,
035
      this posterior over rewards is then used to produce a policy that should perform well according to
      the unknown reward function.
036
037   Non-Bayesian IRL has been successfully applied in apprenticeship learning in settings including
038   robotics (Kretzschmar et al., 2016; Okal & Arras, 2016; Woodworth et al., 2018; Das et al., 2021;
039   Liu et al., 2022), navigation in Google Maps on a global scale (Barnes et al., 2023), or autonomous
040   driving (Sun et al., 2018; Rosbach et al., 2019; Huang et al., 2022), including on vehicles deployed
041
      in real-world heavy traffic (Phan-Minh et al., 2022). However, this body of work that has scaled IRL
      to real-world settings generally learns only a point-estimate of the reward function, which can be
042
      problematic for several reasons: first, the IRL task is generally underspecified – there exist multiple
043
      reward functions that equally well explain given behaviour, even in the limit of many demonstration
044   trajectories (Russell, 1998; Cao et al., 2021; Kim et al., 2021; Skalse et al., 2023). Second, further
045   uncertainty is induced by working only with a limited amount of demonstrations. Especially in
046   safety-critical applications, it is desirable to track this uncertainty to subsequently produce policies
047

048         We use the term apprenticeship learning to mean a subarea of imitation learning that uses IRL as an
049   intermediate step. Imitation learning is thus a broader term including non-IRL techniques for learning a good
      policy from demonstrations, such as behavioural cloning.
050       2
            While the reward hypothesis as formulated by Rich Sutton would claim ”that all of what we mean by goals
051   and purposes can be well thought of as maximization of the expected value of the cumulative sum of a received
052   scalar signal (reward).” (Sutton, 2004), it is probably not the case that all preferences can be meaningfully

080   Bayesian IRL addresses these problems head-on by recovering a posterior distribution over reward
081   functions,3 rather than a point estimate, which can be used to produce policies robust with respect
082   to this posterior uncertainty.4 However, prior methods for Bayesian IRL (see the Related Work

      only a handful of dimensions (Ramachandran & Amir, 2007; Mandyam et al., 2023; Bajgar et al.,

086
087   The contribution of this paper is providing a method that preserves both scalability – in terms of the
088   dimensionality of the state space as well as the amount of demonstrations, which we demonstrate
089
      by applying it to higher dimensional settings than any previous article on Bayesian IRL – and full
      posterior uncertainty estimation, whose usefulness we demonstrate by producing risk-averse policies
090
      that can avoid hazards in the Safety Gymnasium. We achieve this by combining variational inference
091
      (generally notably more computationally efficient than Markov chain Monte Carlo used in much
092   previous work) and by primarily working in the space of Q-values rather than rewards, which avoids
093   the need to repeatedly solve the forward planning problem – a notorious obstacle in IRL. We see
094   this as a step in scaling Bayesian IRL methods to higher-dimensional settings, broadening the range
095   of tasks that can benefit from the improved robustness that posterior uncertainty quantification can
096   bring, and opening the door to further work on scaling, robust apprenticeship learning, or active IRL.

145   2.1    R ELATED W ORK
146
      Inverse reinforcement learning (IRL) has been introduced by Russell (1998), though essentially the
147
      same problem had been formulated and studied before as inverse optimal control (Kalman, 1964)
148   (though the two communities have been somewhat separate and using different sets of methods; see
149   Ab Azar et al. (2020) for a comparison). The already vast IRL literature is well reviewed by Arora
150   & Doshi (2021) and Adams et al. (2022) – we will concentrate on approaches closest to ours, i.e.
151   Bayesian inverse reinforcement learning methods.
152
      The problem of Bayesian IRL was first addressed by Ramachandran & Amir (2007) using Markov
153
      chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) sampling. MCMC can produce samples from the true posterior over re-
154   wards, but scales poorly to higher dimensions. Furthermore Ramachandran’s method needs to solve

163   on the most relevant parts of the state-action space. Mandyam et al. (2023) instead tried to reduce
164   the number of times the forward RL problem needs to get solved by solving it a few times with
165   different reward functions and then trying to learn a joint density over rewards and demonstrations
166   using kernel density estimation, but the method can still address only very small problems. Bajgar
167   et al. (2024) significantly reduced the computational burden by performing inference primarily in
168   the space of Q-values, also avoiding the need to repeatedly solve the forward RL problem – a trick
169   we also use. However, the method is still based on MCMC sampling, which is computationally
170   intensive and has trouble scaling to higher-dimensional inference problems.
171   Chan & van der Schaar (2021) have applied the much more scalable variational inference to the
172   problem. Their approach avoids having to repeatedly solve the forward RL problem by jointly
173   learning a reward encoder (a network producing a mean and variance for the reward in any state)

175   by the Bellman equation applied as a soft constraint. The Q-network is then used to produce the
176   apprentice policy. However, since the method models only a point estimate of the Q-function, it
177
      does not provide any uncertainty estimation for the apprentice policy. Furthermore, since the reward
      posterior is tied to this point-estimate of the Q-function, its posterior variance is greatly reduced
178
      relative to the true Bayesian posterior. Our method, focusing on modelling the uncertainty over
179
      Q-values, does not suffer from these flaws.

182   Bayesian reasoning in IRL, but do not provide posterior uncertainty quantification instead learning
183
      e.g. a MAP estimate, while Balakrishnan et al. (2020) use Bayesian optimization to make (otherwise
      non-Bayesian) IRL more efficient. Bayesian IRL has also been extended beyond the basic setting
184
      studied here e.g. to multiple experts (Choi & Kim, 2012).

      3   M ETHOD
191
192
193   We first provide a high-level summary of our method, Q-based Variational IRL (QVIRL), and then
194   proceed by describing each of its components in detail. Our method takes as input a set of demonstra-
195   tions and a prior over the reward functions, and produces as output (1) a variational approximation
196   to the posterior over Q-functions (which can be understood as encoding the optimal policy under the
197   unknown reward), which can be used to deduce (2) a variational distribution over reward functions.
198   Classical Bayesian IRL (Ramachandran & Amir, 2007) evaluates the posterior of each hypothetical
199   reward by calculating the associated optimal Q-values, which are then used to evaluate the likeli-
200   hood (2). However, that can be extremely costly, especially since it needs to be done many times.
201   Thus, we build on an insight used already by Chan & van der Schaar (2021), Garg et al. (2021) and
202   Bajgar et al. (2024): going from the Q-values to the reward is computationally much simpler than
203   going from rewards to the Q-function.
204   Our model therefore centres on modelling a posterior distribution over Q-values. The Q-values can
205   be used to evaluate the likelihood. And the Bellman equations then allow us to deduce the cor-
206   responding rewards in one step, rather than having to solve the whole forward RL problem. By
207   pushing the Q-value distribution through the inverse Bellman operator, we can deduce the corre-
208   sponding distribution over rewards, which can be used to link the distribution over Q-values to the
209   prior over rewards. We approximate both posteriors by variational distributions, which are optimized
210   using the evidence lower bound (ELBO), as is usual in variational inference.
211   The architecture is illustrated in Fig. 1. We will now describe each component in turn. For a clearer
212   initial explanation, we will first concentrate on the case of discrete state and action spaces, though
213   most of the equations almost directly transfer to the continuous cases – an exact continuous version
214   of the equations can generally be obtained by appropriately replacing summation by integration. In
215   practice, the integrals need to be approximated by discrete samples, reverting back to the discrete-
      space equations (where we no longer sum over all states or actions but only over a sample).

      3.1   Q- VALUE VARIATIONAL P OSTERIOR
217
218   We model the posterior distribution over Q values using a variational family qθ , parameterised by a
219   vector of parameters θ ∈ Rd , which we can use to evaluate the joint distribution of Q-values for any
220   given set of state-action pairs. Let us highlight why it is important for the Q-value posterior to be
221   able to model the covariances between different Q-values: Adding a constant to all Q-values does not
222   change the associated Boltzmann policy. Thus, any demonstration data result in a likelihood that is
223   invariant with respect to adding the same value to all Q-values, usually creating positive correlation
224
      amongst them. Also, Q-values of states lying on the same optimal trajectory all depend on rewards
      of states towards the end of that trajectory, thus again creating a positive correlation.
225
226   A range of variational distributions could be used. As a baseline, we work out the details here using
227   a multi-variate Gaussian (in a discrete-space case), or a Gaussian process (in the continuous-state-
228   space case).7
229   The particular architecture we will use for our experiments, and which is outlined in Figure 1 is
230   one based on a neural-network encoder with parameters θ, which for any given state-action pair
231   s, a, produces a mean µQ (s, a; θ), a log standard deviation log σQ (s, a; θ), as well as latent-space
232   embedding e(s, a; θ), which can then be used as an input to a standard Gaussian process kernel k to
233   calculate the correlation matrix of any set of state-action pairs (by default, we will be using a radial-
234   basis function (RBF) kernel, but our method can accommodate any kernel). Thus, for any collection
235   (s1 , a1 ), . . . , (sn , an ) of state-action pairs, the variational posterior will yield a joint multivariate
236

      3.5   A PPRENTICE P OLICIES
325
326   Once a Q-value model (and possibly a reward model) have been trained, they can be used to produce
327   an apprentice policy for performing the task represented by the learnt reward. In the simplest case,
328   this maximizes either the expected Q-value (which is cheaper if environment dynamics remain the
329   same in deployment) or reward (which may be useful for transfer, but requires running forward RL).
330   However, since we retain a full posterior distribution, we can also generate conservative apprentice
331   policies that instead optimize for other statistics of the posterior, such as some lower quantile or
332   conditional value at risk (CVaR), choosing actions that should lead to outcomes robust with respect
333   to the epistemic uncertainty encoded by the model. This can also have an effect discouraging the
334   model from entering regions of the state space unseen during training – something that is often ad-
335   dressed using ad-hoc heuristics in literature based on behavioural cloning. We use this conservative
336   apprentice policy in our experiments on the Mujoco and Safety Gymnasium environments, where
337   we use the lower-confidence-bound (LCB) apprentice policy
338                               πLCB (s) = arg max µQ (s, a) − κσQ (s, a) .                         (11)
339                                              a
340   where κ can be chosen to match a particular quantile.

      5     D ISCUSSION AND C ONCLUSION
487
488
      5.1   L IMITATIONS AND A SSUMPTIONS
489
490
      First-person demonstrations This article assumes that the demonstrations come from an expert
491   acting from the same perspective as the apprentice agent. This may be true in settings such as au-
492   tonomous driving, where the AI apprentice should be trying to get to destination fast but safely,
493   similarly to what a human driver (from which the demonstration data may come) would be trying
494   to achieve. However, in general, a human and an AI assistant may be acting in the same environ-
495   ment together with the common goal of fulfilling the human’s preferences. If the human is observed
496   drinking coffee, our naive IRL framing would teach the AI assistant to also drink coffee. Instead, in
497   such situations, we would want the AI to assist the human in fulfilling their preferences – e.g. brew
498   and bring them a mug of coffee. Such a setting has been formalized as cooperative inverse rein-
499
      forcement learning (Hadfield-Menell et al., 2016) or assistance games (Shah et al., 2020). However,
      solving those is generally more demanding than plain IRL, so we see Bayesian IRL as a natural step
500
      toward applying similar methods in assistance games.
501
502
503   Expert model The method as presented is assuming that the expert is behaving Boltzmann-
504
      rationally (per Eq. 1) with a known rationality coefficient α. Although this model is known to
      be relatively robust, real human experts are unlikely to behave exactly according to this model,
505
      which can constitute model mispecification. A principled approach to tackle this would be to in-
506
      clude uncertainty over reward models, or at the very least, to use Bayesian inference to infer also
507   the rationality parameter, both of which can be readily included in our method, though we have not
508   tested it experimentally so far. More fundamentally, human preferences may be evolving over time
509   and even be influenced by the actions of an AI system (Carroll et al., 2024), all of which would
510   need to be taken into account when robustly aligning AI systems with real users. Still, we hope the
511   probabilistic modelling tools examined in this paper could be used in that endeavour.
512
513   Gaussian variational distribution While the general framework presented in the paper is com-
514   patible with any variational distribution family, the version empirically tested in this paper was using
515   a Gaussian variational distribution. This means that the method assumes that the two posterior dis-
516   tributions (one over Q-values and one over rewards) are well approximated by this Gaussian (e.g., is
517   unimodal and symmetric), which may not hold in general. The variational distribution needs to be
518   adapted to correspond reasonably well to the true posterior. One way of testing the goodness of fit
519   would be to first use one of MCMC-based methods, such as those by Ramachandran & Amir (2007)
520
      or Bajgar et al. (2024), on a smaller instance of a task to produce samples from the true posterior,
      and only then scaling up in a way that preserves the general shape of the distribution.
521
522
523   Naı̈ve sampling of continuous actions The extension of the method to continuous action has
524
      so far used naı̈ve random sampling. This works for low-dimensional action spaces (that is, those
      comprising a few input signals), but it suffers from the curse of dimensionality, which may prevent
525
      this approach from scaling to higher dimensional settings such as robots with many actuators. In
526
      such cases, relevant actions – e.g. meaningful movement of the robot, such as a step forward or
527   grabbing an object – may occupy only a small manifold within the many-dimensional action space.
528   In that case, uniform sampling may mostly produce completely irrelevant actions, such as random
529   jerks in a robot. Instead of uniform sampling, one could train a generative policy model that would
530   propose only a few relevant actions which could then be evaluated using the Boltzmann rationality
531   model, with some similarity to actor-critic methods, which we leave for future work.
532
533
      5.2   C ONCLUSIONS
534
535   We have introduced the first method for Bayesian inverse reinforcement learning that both scales be-
536   yond small environments, and at the same time preserves the desirable posterior uncertainty quan-
537   tification of Bayesian inference. The algorithm performs well not only against baselines within
538   Bayesian IRL, but is competititve also with state-of-the-art algorithms in offline imitation learning.
539   Thus it presents an important step forward in scaling Bayesian inverse reinforcement learning, with
      its desirable uncertainty quantification properties, into higher-dimensional settings.

      B     QVIRL WITH CONTINUOUS SPACES AND UNKNOWN DYNAMICS
781
      Section 3 presented mainly a version of the algorithm for environments with finite, discrete state and
782
      action spaces with a known model of the environment. However, the key ideas are applicable across
783
      a range of settings, including those with unknown dynamics and with continuous state and action
784   spaces. Here, we outline the two versions that were used for our experiments and which together
785   cover both continuous spaces and unknown dynamics.
786
787
      B.1     S TRICTLY- BATCH SETTING
788
789   In our experiments on Cartpole, Acrobot, and Lunar Lander, we follow the setting of the main
790   baseline algorithm, AVRIL, that is the setting of strictly-batch offline imitation learning (Jarrett &
791   Bica, 2020) where we have access to only the expert demonstrations, and do not have access to
792   the environment or its dynamics during training. These environments (and the baseline algorithm)
793   feature a continuous state space and discrete actions, so let us describe how to adapt QVIRL to that
794
      setting.
795   This can be done in at least two ways: firstly, we could try learning a posterior Bayesian model of
796   the environment together with the reward. Secondly, we can replace sampling over transitions which
797   is needed in Equation 6, by the empirical transitions observed in the expert demonstrations. To make
798   comparison between the algorithms closer, we follow Chan & van der Schaar (2021) in the second
799   approach.
800   In that case, Equation 6 becomes
801
802                            R(s, a) ≈ Q(s, a) − γQ(s′ , arg max µQ (s′ , a′ ))                      (15)
                                                                a′
803
804   for s, a, s′ , a′ ∈ D – two consecutive transitions from the demonstration data. The KL divergence
805   to the prior is then evaluated only on these demonstration points. If we adapt Equations 7 and 8
806
      accordingly, we have a version of the algorithm applicable to this continuous-state strictly-batch
      offline setting as we have used it for our experiments.
807
808   However, note that in this setting, even the KL term is evaluated only on the expert trajectories.
809   Thus, away from these trajectories, the Q-function does not receive any training signal and becomes
      unreliable. We think this setting can erase an important part of the advantages that IRL methods can


                                                       15
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
      have over behavioural cloning, which is their ability to leverage the environment dynamics to infer
811   good policy even in states unvisited by the expert. We think our method is more useful in settings
812   where some additional source of information about the environment dynamics is present, such as
813   the ones described in the next subsection.
814
815   B.2   U NLABELLED DATA ( INCLUDING ONLINE DATA )
816
817   The second evaluation setting we considered following Lyu et al. (2024) contains additional unla-
818   belled transition data, collected using an unknown, possibly random, policy. In that case we can just
819   reuse Equation 15 but instead of evaluating only across demonstrations, we can evaluate the equa-
820   tion also across those unlabelled trajectories, which can help the model generalize to parts of the
821   state space not covered by the demonstrations. Following the baseline algorithm, this is the setting
822
      we use for experiments in Section 4.2.
823   Furthermore, if we have access to a simulator of the environment (or have access to the actual
824   environment itself and it is safe to explore using arbitrary policies), we can also periodically collect
825   trajectories during the IRL training process using the apprentice policy corresponding to current
826   approximate posterior distribution and its random perturbations. Using such auxiliary policies from
827   the apprentice policy has the advantage of improving the consistency of the Q values in regions of
828
      the state-action space where it matters the most, since they are likely to be visited by the apprentice
      policy upon deployment. Adding random perturbations further extends this to the neighbourhood of
829
      such apprentice trajectories.
830
831   An important effect that this has is that in regions far from the expert data, this makes the Q-value
832   posterior revert to the implicit prior corresponding to the (explicitly provided) reward prior. In
833   particular, this generally results in the Q-value posterior variance being higher in those regions.
834
      A risk-averse apprentice policy that penalizes high-uncertainty states and actions will then tend to
      avoid such regions unvisited by the expert, recovering what is often added as a heuristic in other
835
      methods (Brantley et al., 2019; Reddy et al., 2019; Lyu et al., 2024).
836
837
838
      B.3   C ONTINUOUS ACTIONS
839
      To adapt the algorithm to continuous-action environments, we simply subsample a set of random
840   contrastive actions from the action space for each training example at each step, and replace the
841   discrete action space by this sampled set of action in all equations. We use 100 sampled actions.
842   Note that this can become inefficient for higher-dimensional action spaces and could be improved
843   by using a trained policy network to propose good candidate actions as is done e.g. by Garg et al.
844   (2021).

1031
1032   AVRIL was proposed in a strictly batch setting, not using the environment dynamics. In the above
1033   case, this would lead to AVRIL never updating the posterior over the reward associated with the ob-

1036   aware version of AVRIL.

1038   reward variational posterior and the prior only on the expert trajectories, we evaluate it across all
1039   state-action pairs, i.e. for each state-action pair s, a, we calculate

       D.4    L IMITATIONS OF AVRIL
1051   Since our method, QVIRL, may seem to resemble AVRIL, the closest baseline, we would like to
1052   re-emphasize some important limitations of AVRIL that our method does not suffer from:
1053
1054          • AVRIL does not provide uncertainty estimates over the Q-values and thus over the optimal
1055            policy. This does not permit one to easily extract risk averse policies, where we can, which
1056            can improve safety as demonstrated in the SafetyGymnasium experiments.
1057          • AVRIL’s posterior over rewards does not seem to track well the true posterior over rewards
1058            even on a simple gridworld. QVIRL’s fit is better.
1059
              • the posterior variance over rewards is extremely sensitive to AVRIL’s hyperparameter λ that
1060            regulates the strength of the consistence constraint between the reward variational distribu-
1061            tion and the Q values and a narrow range of values can make the posterior either collapse to
1062            Dirac delta, or revert to the prior. The paper gives no indication on how to pick the value.
1063            Our QVIRL has no such hyperparameter.
1064
              • the AVRIL paper presents a ”strictly batch setting” where only the expert demonstrations
1065            are used to estimate environment transitions, and thus evaluate the closeness of the posterior
1066            to the prior and the consistency between the Q values and the reward distribution. We think
1067            this largely gives up a crucial advantage of IRL over behavioural cloning – leveraging the
1068            environment dynamics to make inference about rewards away from the expert trajectories,
1069            enabling better generalization. Our paper presents variants of the algorithm also for the case
1070            of known environment dynamics, or an online setting where we have access to a simulator
1071            or the environment itself.
