009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Low light image enhancement (LLIE) is a challenging task, with most existing
012            models often struggling to adapt to diverse dark environments due to insufficient
013            training datasets. In this paper, we propose a novel unsupervised model called
014            Range-null Latent Prior-guided Consistency Model (RLPCM), which integrates a
015            latent consistency model (LCM) into low light enhancement using Retinex-based
016            range-null space decomposition. RLPCM leverages an off-the-shelf LCM as a
017            generative prior to improve both the latent consistency and realness of enhanced
018
               images. Meanwhile, fine-tuning a lighting decoder solely on normal-light images
               to ensure high fidelity in image space. A key contribution is a simple yet effective
019
               global illumination adjustment applied to the range-space component, along with a
020
               natural language guidance module to learn the null-space component. This allows
021            for iterative generation to enhance both consistency and realness in just a few steps.
022            Additionally, we present a new UAV low light dataset (UAV-LL) containing 300
023            image pairs from various UAV scenarios to support comprehensive evaluation.
024            Extensive experiments demonstrate the superior adaptability and effectiveness of
025            our framework across a wide range of low-light environments.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Low light image enhancement (LLIE) is a long-standing problem that influences both human visual
031   perception and related computer vision tasks, such as depth estimation Wang et al. (2021), object
032   detection Hashmi et al. (2023), and semantic segmentation Pan et al. (2024). Current LLIE approaches
033
      are generally categorized into two types: supervised and unsupervised methods. Supervised methods
      Weng et al. (2024); Cai et al. (2023); Jiang et al. (2023) typically rely on paired images to train
034
      end-to-end models, but acquiring pixel-aligned image datasets is particularly challenging, especially
035
      in mobile environments. On the other hand, unsupervised methods Ma et al. (2022); Yang et al.
036   (2023); Liang et al. (2023) have gained traction by minimizing the need for paired data. Among these,
037   diffusion-based models Wang et al. (2024); Jiang et al. (2024a) have attracted significant attention
038   for their powerful generative capabilities. Therefore, in this paper, we focus on diffusion-based
039   approaches for low light image enhancement.
040
      Most diffusion-based models for the LLIE task have integrated low-light image and illumination as
041
      conditional inputs to preserve image details and enhance illumination quality, as shown in Figure 1(a).
042   For instance, QuadPrior Wang et al. (2024) introduces an illumination-invariant prior as a conditional
043   generative model utilizing ControlNet Zhang et al. (2023). Similarly, LightenDiffusion Jiang et al.
044   (2024a) employs a Retinex-based diffusion model that works with unpaired images, decomposing
045   them into reflectance and illumination maps. However, these approaches lack an effective latent prior
046   to guide the conditional diffusion process, often resulting in suboptimal enhancement outcomes and
047   time-consuming procedures.
048   Recently, the denoising diffusion null-space model (DDNM) Wang et al. (2023b) incorporated
049   range-null space decomposition Schwab et al. (2019) into diffusion models to address various image
050   restoration (IR) tasks, such as image super-resolution, colorization, and deblurring. This method
051   identifies appropriate null-space components to ensure realistic results, while applying a specific
052   degradation operator to preserve range-space content for data consistency, thus achieving effective
053   performance. However, DDNM depends on explicit degradation priors, which are challenging to
      obtain for LLIE task, and is often limited by slow inference speed.

      To address these issues, we propose a novel unsupervised framework called Range-null Latent Prior-
069   guided (RLP) for the LLIE task, as illustrated in Figure 1(c). To accelerate the diffusion process,
070   the latent consistency model (LCM) Song et al. (2023) is integrated into low-light enhancement by
071   leveraging Retinex-based range-null space decomposition to identify relevant null-space content,
072   referred to as the RLP Consistency Model (RLPCM) in Figure 2. Our approach integrates a physics-
073   driven model, i.e., Retinex theory Land & McCann (1971), with null-space decomposition to introduce
074   global illumination degradation that guides the range-space content. RLPCM relies solely on a current
075   off-the-shelf latent consistency model as the generative prior, fine-tuning a lighting decoder with
076   normal light images to enhance fidelity in the image space. We introduce a simple yet effective global
077
      illumination prior that fixes the range-space component within the range-null space decomposition.
      Additionally, we design a natural language guidance mechanism to facilitate learning in the null-
078
      space, enabling a few-step iterative generation process that effectively and fast balances the latent
079
      consistency and the realness.
080
081   In this paper, our key findings and contributions are summarized as follows: 1) To the best of our
082   knowledge, we are the first to introduce a consistency model into the LLIE task, effectively bridging
083
      Rang-null space decomposition with the Retinex theory into the consistency model. Compared
      to existing diffusion methods, our approach exhibits superior flexibility and robust across diverse
084
      scenarios. 2) We propose a range-null latent prior-guided framework for the LLIE task, featuring a
085
      simple yet efficient global illumination prior that physically guarantees the reliability of range-space
086   content. this prior can be manually adjusted during inference, thus avoiding the irreversible effects
087   associated with fixed parameters. Additionally, we incorporate language-aware guidance mechanisms
088   to facilitate the learning of null-space content. 3) We present a novel UAV low-light dataset (UAV-LL),
089   comprising 300 image pairs captured in various mobile environments. This dataset allows for a
090   comprehensive evaluation of the generalization capabilities of existing methods under previously
091   unseen conditions. For the related work, please refer to Appendix A.

285   2.3                C ONSTRUCTING I LLUMINATION FACTOR A
286
287   Rather than focusing on precise illumination estimation, we reconsider Retinex theory through the
288   lens of Range-null space decomposition and propose a simple yet effective global illumination
289   intensity. We introduce the following proposition:
290   Proposition 1. We assume that the low-light image mainly suffers from the global degeneration
291   operator ϖ in illumination intensity A, which is a non-zero constant matrix.When we set the constant
292   of A as ϖ, its pseudo-inverse A† can be easily calculated as
293
                                                                                                                                             1
294                                                                                                                       A† =                 I.                                                                              (14)
                                                                                                                                             ϖ
295
296   Hence, we present a simple yet interpretable range-space content A† y for the LLIE task. It is then
297   encoded into latent space as z = E(A† y). The proof can be found in the appendix B.
298   To validate the universality of global illumination reduction, we gather approximately 7,000 low
299   and normal light image pairs from existing benchmark datasets, including LOLv1 Wei et al. (2018),
300   LOLv2 Yang et al. (2021), LSRW Hai et al. (2023), and our UAV-LL dataset. We conducted a study
301   on the illumination degradation between these paired images. Specifically, we first estimated the
302   average global illumination intensity. Following the methodology of Guo et al. (2017), we computed
303   pixel-wise illumination by selecting the maximum value across the three color channels as the global
304   illumination and then calculated the difference between this value and the average illumination.
305   As illustrated in Figure 4(a), the global illumination intensity in low-light images is low and uniformly
306   distributed between 0.1 and 0.4. Additionally, Figure 4(b) shows that the average difference intensity
307   predominantly ranges from 0 to 15, indicating that the pixel-wise illumination is close to the average
308   illumination. By observing toy examples of the illumination and difference maps in Figure 4(c), we
309   have a reason to believe that most low-light images exhibit characteristics of global degradation.

651   Low Light Image Enhancement.
652
653
      low-light image enhancement Lore et al. (2017) is a critical task, influencing both human visual
      perception and the related computer vision applications such as depth estimation Wang et al. (2021),
654
      object detection Hashmi et al. (2023), semantic segmentation Pan et al. (2024).
655
656   To our knowledge, LLNetLore et al. (2017) is a pioneer, taking the lead in introducing deep neural
657   networks into the field of low-light image enhancement, achieving remarkable results through
658   supervised learning. Subsequently, LightenNetCai et al. (2018) used a convolutional neural network
659
      (CNN) to attempt contrast enhancement for a single image. MBLLENLv et al. (2018) further
      innovates and introduces a multi-branch fusion strategy within the CNN architecture to capture
660
      and fuse richer image features. In addition, a series of SOTA methods such as SNR-Net Xu et al.
661
      (2022), Retinexformer Cai et al. (2023), DiffLL Jiang et al. (2023), and MambaLLIE Weng et al.
662   (2024), have attracted considerable attention due to their impressive performance on various low-light
663   enhancement benchmark datasets (e.g., MIT Bychkovsky et al. (2011), LOL Wei et al. (2018), LSRW
664   Hai et al. (2023)). However, despite leveraging advanced network designs incorporating Retinex
665   theory Land & McCann (1971) , Transformers Vaswani et al. (2017), state-space models Gu & Dao
666   (2024), and diffusion models Ho et al. (2020), these supervised methods exhibit limited generalization
667   to unseen scenarios. This is likely due to the comparatively small training datasets, which fail to
668   capture diverse illumination conditions and device degradations. Hence, reducing the reliance on
669   paired image collections for low-light enhancement remains a significant challenge, particularly in
670
      mobile environments.
671   To address this, recent unsupervised approaches have focused on leveraging unpaired datasets or
672   even single low/normal light images datasets for training, thereby reducing the dependency on paired
673   images. Prior state-of-the-art methods, ZeroDCEGuo et al. (2020), RUASLiu et al. (2020) and their
674   subsequent studies such as Ma et al. (2022), Fu et al. (2023), Wang et al. (2024), etc., use physical
675   lighting priors as guidance to achieve image enhancement without external supervision. Currently,
676
      diffusion-based unsupervised models Wang et al. (2024); Jiang et al. (2024a) have attracted significant
      attention for their powerful generative capabilities. QuadPrior Wang et al. (2024) introduces an
677
      illumination-invariant prior as a conditional generative model using the ControlNet-shape framework,
678
      where the entire architecture is trained on COCO dataset Lin et al. (2014). LightenDiffusion employs
679   a diffusion-based model, utilizing unpaired images by decomposing them into reflectance and
680   illumination maps, which serve as latent space inputs for low-light enhancement.
681
682
      Diffusion Model. In recent years, diffusion models have been widely used in image generation
      tasks. At the same time, significant progress has been achieved in low-level vision tasks. RePaint
683
      Lugmayr et al. (2022) utilize pre-trained DDPM as a generative prior to generate high-quality, diverse
684
      inpainted images without the need for mask-specific training. For low-level vision task, SR3 Saharia
685   et al. (2021) adopted a conditional image generation method based on a noise diffusion probability
686   model to achieve image super-resolution through iterative refinement, LPDM Panagiotou & Bosman
687   (2023) introduced the Low-light Post-processing Diffusion Model (LPDM) to model the conditional
688   distribution between low-light images and normal exposure images. DiffPIR Zhu et al. (2023)
689   combined the traditional interpolation image restoration method with a diffusion sampling framework,
690   aiming to exploit the diffusion model as a prior for a generative denoiser. DiffLL Jiang et al. (2023)
691   proposed a wavelet conditional diffusion model (WCDM) that combines the advantages of wavelet
692   transform and the generation capability of diffusion model to achieve high-quality image enhancement.
693
      Diff-Retinex Yi et al. (2023) rethink the low-light image enhancement task by combining a physically
      interpretable model and a generative diffusion model. LatentFD Mei et al. (2023) utilized a latent
694
      feature-guided diffusion model to achieve efficient shadow removal. JoReS-Diff Wu et al. (2023)
695
      improved the generation ability of the diffusion model by introducing Retinex theory as an additional
696   preprocessing condition. GASD Hou et al. (2023) proposed a global structure-aware diffusion
697   process for low-light image enhancement through global structure awareness and uncertainty-guided
698   regularization. PA-Diff Zhao et al. (2024) proposed a new UIE framework that aims to utilize physical
699   knowledge to guide the diffusion process. MDMS Shang et al. (2024) enables the model to adaptively
700   learn the noise distribution and thus improve the quality of the generated image by introducing a
701   space-frequency domain fusion module and combining a multi-domain learning paradigm and a
      multi-scale sampling strategy. CFWD Xue et al. (2024) proposed a wavelet diffusion model based on
