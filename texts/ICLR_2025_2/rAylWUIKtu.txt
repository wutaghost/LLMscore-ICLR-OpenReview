009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            The training data for many Large Language Models (LLMs) is contaminated with
012            test data. This means that public benchmarks used to assess LLMs are compro-
013            mised, suggesting a performance gap between benchmark scores and actual ca-
014            pabilities. Ideally, a private holdout set could be used to accurately verify scores.
015
               Unfortunately, such datasets do not exist for most benchmarks, and post-hoc con-
               struction of sufficiently similar datasets is non-trivial. To address these issues, we
016
               introduce a systematic methodology for (i) retrospectively constructing a holdout
017
               dataset for a target dataset, (ii) demonstrating the statistical indistinguishability of
018            this retro-holdout dataset, and (iii) comparing LLMs on the two datasets to quan-
019            tify the performance gap due to the dataset’s public availability. Applying these
020            methods to TruthfulQA, we construct and release Retro-Misconceptions, on which
021            we evaluate twenty LLMs and find that some have inflated scores by as much as
022            16 percentage points. Our results demonstrate that public benchmark scores do
023            not always accurately assess model properties, and underscore the importance of
024            improved data practices in the field.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Many have begun to question the reliability of public benchmarks in assessing large language models
      (Alzahrani et al., 2024; Zheng et al., 2024; Fourrier et al., 2023). Discrepancies between benchmark
034
      scores and practical capabilities raise concern (Li et al., 2024b), and strong incentives for higher
035
      scores (Fourrier et al., 2024) suggest that optimizing benchmark performance could take precedence
036   over real-world effectiveness and safety. This phenomenon, akin to specification gaming (Krakovna
037   et al., 2020), is termed evaluation gaming – processes leading to a systematic gap between bench-
038   mark performance and practical utility.
039
      Extensive evidence of evaluation data being included in training data (Sainz et al., 2024; Oren et al.,
040
      2023; Schaeffer, 2023; Shi et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2024; SLAM-group, 2023) suggests that evalu-
041
      ation gaming is occurring. However, proving the existence of a statistically significant performance
042   gap between a specific evaluation task and an analogous real-world task would require access to
043   an independently and identically distributed (IID) split of the benchmark which we know could not
044   have had an impact on any aspect of model development.
045
      This is the idea of holdout datasets, which are used to assess a machine learning model’s unbiased
046
      performance after training. By definition, a holdout dataset comes from the same distribution as its
047
      corresponding target dataset, meaning that any evaluation conducted on both datasets should have
048   the same result within some statistical tolerance (James et al., 2023). Importantly, holdout datasets
049   also are kept hidden during the development process. Together, these two properties imply that
050   comparing a model’s performance on a public benchmark and a corresponding holdout dataset could
051   reveal whether the public benchmark has influenced any aspect of the design, training, or validation
052   process. Unfortunately, holdout datasets for benchmarks are typically not available; benchmark
053   developers usually release all evaluation data, although there are notable exceptions, e.g. Li et al.
      (2024a).

099   2.1      U NDERSTANDING THE RETRO H OLDOUT F RAMEWORK
100
101   Holdout datasets were first used in machine learning to accurately assess model performance on
102   a given task. A holdout set is a randomly selected subset of the same set of observations as the
103   training dataset, and is strictly excluded from the development process (James et al., 2023). Unlike
104
      conventional holdout sets, retro-holdout datasets are created after the initial release of a dataset,
105       1
           TruthfulQA was chosen due to its safety relevance and widespread use (Zhao et al., 2023; Naveed et al.,
106   2024; Bai et al., 2023; Cui et al., 2024; Fourrier et al., 2024).
         2
107        Retro-Misconceptions is only guaranteed to be accurate on models with a training cutoff date prior to
      January 1st, 2024, since that as that is when portions of the new dataset became available on the web.


                                                           2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      meaning we cannot assume they have the same properties that a hypothetical holdout set would
109   have. We refer to Appendix A for a formalization of this claim.
110
111   In brief, we rely on a standard assumption in machine learning that the public and post-hoc retro-
112
      holdout datasets consist of independent samples from two possibly different distributions Hastie
      et al. (2009); Shalev-Shwartz & Ben-David (2014). To establish that the retro-holdout can be used
113
      as a holdout set for the public benchmark, we must show that both datasets could have been sam-
114
      pled from the same distribution. We construct four statistical tests, one permutation test and three
115   binomial tests, to reject the hypothesis that two sets were sampled from the same distribution. A
116   proposed dataset cannot be considered a retro-holdout for a given public benchmark unless all four
117   tests fail to reject the hypothesis of a shared distribution.
118
      This verification process sets our methodology apart from a more standard dataset extension, as it
119
      mandates that our retro-holdout is an assessment of exactly the same task as the original benchmark,
120
      making the only difference between the two the variable we care about: public availability of a
121   dataset. The lengths we have gone in order to reach this level of rigor are extensive; the retro-
122   holdout framework does not make use of LLMs for any aspect of dataset creation. This substantially
123   increases the time cost of our process, but ensures that language models do not bias our results.
124
      Our method is designed for labeled datasets, which have inputs and expected outputs for models.
125
      We note that out of distribution (OOD) testing has sometimes been incorrectly characterized as
126
      using holdout datasets. In this work, we use the original definition of holdout sets; evaluation sets
127   constructed to probe OOD performance are not considered.
128
129   For brevity, we define
130                          TARGET := an arbitrary, publicly available benchmark,
131                            RETRO := a retro-holdout dataset for TARGET.

133   2.2     C REATING A RETRO
134
135   Initially, the methodology used for crafting a RETRO should be heavily informed by the original
136   process used to create the TARGET. To promote similarity, we recommend using a representative
137   entry, randomly drawn from TARGET, without replacement, as a basis for creating each new entry in
      RETRO . We include a short guide for this initial creation in Appendix D.

      2.2.1    S UFFICIENT I NDISTINGUISHABILITY
141   Establishing with absolute certainty that the two datasets have originated from the same distribution
142   is impossible. Therefore, we resort to multiple statistical tests designed to test the null hypothesis
143   that TARGET and RETRO have a common origin. If the result of each test indicates that we cannot re-
144   ject our null hypothesis, we designate our RETRO to be sufficiently indistinguishable from TARGET.
145   While it is theoretically possible to construct any number of tests to evaluate the similarity between
146   two datasets, practical considerations guide us to four key tests that provide a thorough assessment:
147
148
              • Similarity of Difficulty: Are the questions in both datasets comparably challenging?
149           • Semantic Embedding Similarity: What is the likelihood that a distribution of cosine sim-
150             ilarities between sentence embeddings similar to that of RETRO have been pulled from the
151             same distribution as TARGET?
152           • Prediction Accuracy: Can a model, fine-tuned on randomized splits of the datasets, dif-
153             ferentiate between TARGET and RETRO?
154           • Human Distinguishability: Can humans differentiate between TARGET and RETRO?
155
156   We designate the two datasets as sufficiently indistinguishable if all four tests fail to reject the null
157   hypothesis at a p-value of 5%.
158
159   Similarity of Difficulty To verify that the two datasets have comparable difficulties, we use both
160   to evaluate models with a training cutoff date prior to the release of the TARGET, or pre-release
161   models. These models could not have been affected by the TARGET, as it had not yet been released;
      model performance on both datasets should be comparable, with a margin of statistical uncertainty.


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      This reduces to a two-proportion binomial test with the null hypothesis of equal success probability.
163   For further information on the evaluation task, refer to §2.3.
164
165   We note that with access to many LLMs of varying capability levels, this test combined with simple
166
      human assessment would likely suffice to determine sufficient indistinguishability between the two
      datasets. However, performance of cutting-edge models continues to improve, meaning that pre-
167
      release models are practically guaranteed to be less capable than contemporary models, assuming
168
      they are accessible at all. These constraints are expanded on on Appendix G. To address this lim-
169   itation, we use a number of techniques to amplify pre-release performance: allowing the model to
170   choose multiple answers (top-k), including examples of other questions within the dataset (5-shot),
171   and using the ‘helpful’ prompt from Lin et al. (2022).
172
173   Prediction Accuracy We adopt a modification of prediction accuracy as defined by Dankar &
174   Ibrahim (2021) to determine if a machine learning model can differentiate between the datasets.
175   Contrary to the conventional use of logistic regression in synthetic data evaluations (Dankar &
176   Ibrahim, 2021), we fine-tune BERT (Devlin et al., 2019) on a prediction task. This choice was
177   informed by BERT’s capability to capture nuanced semantic relationships within text, which are
178   crucial for accurately assessing the subtle distinctions or similarities between dataset entries. If the
179   model’s prediction accuracy is approximately the same as random performance, 50%, we can con-
180   clude that the model cannot differentiate between the two datasets. Under a null hypothesis, this
181
      simplifies to a binomial test with success probability of 1/2.
182   To calculate the prediction accuracy score, each dataset is split into five folds. One split from
183   each dataset is withheld, and BERT is fine-tuned to accurately label an entry as either RETRO or
184   TARGET on the remaining data. The model’s prediction accuracy on the holdout splits is measured,
185   and then the process is repeated such that each split is used for testing.
186
187   Semantic Embedding Similarity We perform a random permutation test (Fisher, 1974;
188   normaldeviate, 2012; Hemerik, 2024) using semantic embeddings from Sentence Transformers
189   (Reimers & Gurevych, 2019). We define the test statistic as the mean of all pairwise cosine similari-
190
      ties between embeddings. To obtain a sufficiently tight bound, we use a sample size of N = 10000.
      This test is formally defined in Appendix A.
191
192
193   Human Indistinguishability To assess whether the datasets were distinguishable to humans, we
194
      conducted a survey where participants were tasked to separate entries from TARGET and RETRO.
      Participants were shown ten labeled entries from each dataset for contextual understanding, followed
195
      by a series of ten tests, each comprising of three dataset entries – two from TARGET and one from
196
      RETRO . All entries are drawn without replacement to ensure unique samples throughout the survey.
197   Under the null hypothesis, this is a binomial test with success probability 1/3.
198
199   We also implement a variation of this test using GPT-4o as the evaluator to compare human and
200
      model performance. See Appendix F for comprehensive details on the survey methodology, includ-
      ing specifics on participant recruitment, the structure of the test, and survey instructions.

      2.3   E VALUATING M ODELS
257
258   TruthfulQA Lin et al. (2022) was designed to use logged probabilities to determine a models chosen
259   response. This eliminates the need for a model to output single characters or verbatim wording,
260   but may not be the best method for assessing model performance on a multiple choice task for a
261   few reasons. First, the use of logged probabilities is likely to penalize longer responses, since they
262   naturally have lower total probability. Second, tokens earlier in a response will have stronger impact
263   on logged probabilities than tokens later in the response, which may effect the response chosen.
264   Finally, the OpenAI API no longer provides probability output, and other API providers may have
265   never had such an option.
266   To ensure comparable evaluation results across both open release and closed source models, we
267   evaluate all models by providing an enumerated list of all mc1-choices, and require the model to
268   output tokens to select the preferred option. To minimize potential bias, answers were resampled in
269   rotating order a minimum of ten times, and until one response had been selected four times more
      than any other alternative. The prompt was used for all models is described in Appendix C.3.

291   2.4   T HE C HALLENGES OF T RUTHFUL QA
292
293   The TruthfulQA dataset uses two entry labels: Category and Type. Categories specify the general
294   topic that an entry is about, such as Health, or Advertising; there are 31 Categories in TruthfulQA.
295   Type contains only two options, adversarial or non-adversarial.
296
      When constructing TruthfulQA, the authors filtered a large number of initial entries using a ver-
297
      sion of GPT-3, discarding the entries that the model answered correctly. The resulting set make
298   up the adversarial Type of TruthfulQA. Subsequently, these adversarial entries were used as in-
299   spiration to create new entries for the non-adversarial Type. When comparing the adversarial and
300   non-adversarial Types, we unsurprisingly found that GPT-3 models like Babbage-002 and Davinci-
301   002 do significantly better on the non-adversarial portion. To create a retro-holdout for the entire
302   TruthfulQA dataset, we would require access to the same GPT-3 model originally used to filter
303   TruthfulQA. This model is no longer available.
304   Due to filtering bias, performance differences between the two Types, and lack of access, we focus
305   on the non-adversarial portion of TruthfulQA. While these changes deviate from perfect reflection
306   of TruthfulQA, we note that both the Difficulty Similarity test and model evaluations use identical
307   datasets and methods. As a result, any statistically-significant performance gap must be explained
308   by some form of evaluation gaming.

448   3.3   W HY ARE R ETRO - HOLDOUTS N ECESSARY ?
449
      Creating a retro-holdout dataset is resource intensive, demanding large amounts of time from hu-
450
      man experts for dataset iteration, as well as considerable computational resources for validation.
451
      However, it is necessary to confirm that a newly created dataset assesses precisely the same task
452   as its corresponding public benchmark. Explicit and robust confirmation of benchmark inflation
453   establishes that, in the absence of meaningful evaluation oversight, model developers will game
454   evaluations.
455
      Current data practices mandate a framework like retro-holdout to thoroughly justify this claim, but
456
      shifts in data use and sharing could make retro-holdouts obsolete. One solution could be for dataset
457
      developers to withhold a portion of their data from public release, routinely compare model perfor-
458   mance on the public and private splits, and decommission the benchmark once statistically signifi-
459   cant benchmark inflation is measured. While this is promising in theory, it places substantial burden
460   on dataset developers; there is no platform that can easily facilitate such a process.
461
      We also note that LLMs were not used for any aspect of dataset development to ensure that the result-
462
      ing dataset, Retro-Misconceptions, has not incurred any model bias, establishing a true baseline for
463
      the retro-holdout framework. That said, LLM assistance could automate multiple time consuming
464   steps of our process, substantially decreasing the time required to create a retro-holdout.
465
466
      3.4   L IMITATIONS
467
468   Capacity constraints, coupled with our teams conviction to prevent LLMs from biasing our results,
469   the retro-holdout framework has been conducted on only one sample: the Non-Adversarial Type of
470   TruthfulQA. Though we have designed the process to apply to any labeled dataset which provides
471   inputs and expects outputs from the language model, we cannot yet guarantee the generality of our
472   process.
473   The assumption that the retro-holdout dataset and the target dataset are drawn from the same distri-
474   bution may not always be valid. This assumption is challenged if the target dataset itself is subject to
475   distribution shifts over time; such shifts can alter the underlying data characteristics. Additionally,
476   matching a target dataset introduces its own concerns. While this method ensures that the retro-
477   holdout dataset resembles the target dataset as closely as possible, it also perpetuates any biases
478   present in the target dataset.
479
480   4     R ELATED W ORKS
481
482   Development of LLMs continues to outpace the advancement of evaluation methods, raising concern
483   about benchmark integrity (Chang et al., 2024). Evaluation datasets are frequently used during an
484   LLM’s training process, causing inflated scores; no standard methodology exists to detect this issue
485   Alzahrani et al. (2024), yet data quality remains undervalued and under-incentivized Sambasivan
      et al. (2021). Data contamination, where test data is included in training sets, results in models

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      “cheating” by memorizing tests rather than generalizing (Marie, 2023). High benchmark scores are
487   heavily incentivized, promoting practices that compromise data quality and evaluation integrity.
488
489   Recent work has introduced heuristics for third-party contamination tests. Sainz et al. (2023) pro-
490
      pose a technique to detect test set contamination by eliciting reproduction of specific test set ex-
      amples. Golchin & Surdeanu (2023) suggest a method for identifying contamination in black-box
491
      models by comparing the similarity between model completions of randomly selected example pre-
492
      fixes and the actual data using GPT-4. Concurrent work by Zhang et al. (2024) is notable for its
493   use of a dataset extension, a concept similar to our approach. Their benchmark, GSM1k, reports
494   accuracy drops of up to 13%, highlighting a positive correlation between memorization and perfor-
495   mance gaps. We test their dataset with our tests in Appendix H, finding evidence that GSM1k is not
496   sufficiently indistinguishable from GSM8k.
497
      It is well known that metrics lose their predictive power when incentives are attached to them (Good-
498
      hart, 1984; Strathern, 1997; Karwowski et al., 2023). As Thomas & Uminsky (2020) state, ”overem-
499   phasizing metrics leads to manipulation, gaming, a myopic focus on short-term goals, and other
500   unexpected negative consequences.” Current AI risk metrics fail to address emerging failure modes
501   (Khlaaf, 2023), and Privitera et al. (2024) emphasize that high benchmark scores do not necessarily
502   equate to effective real-world performance.
503
      Empirical findings highlight the necessity for immediate structural reforms in AI research and devel-
504
      opment to prioritize and encourage data quality (Sambasivan et al., 2021). Recent calls for a science
505   of evaluations underscore the urgent need for rigorous evaluation frameworks to inform policy and
506   ensure responsible AI development (Bommasani et al., 2023; Research, 2024).
507

      5   C ONCLUSION
510
511
      In this work, we systematically investigated the impact of evaluation gaming on benchmark scores
      for large language models. We find that evaluation gaming is undeniably occurring, with model ac-
512
      curacy falling by up to 16 percentage points when assessed with an unpublished dataset. Benchmark
513
      inflation is found in both API models, including OpenAI’s GPT and Antrophic’s Claude, as well as
514   open-release models such as Mistral, Gemma, and Phi-2. This slightly contrasts with the findings of
515   Zhang et al. (2024), who saw performance gaps for open-release models, but found the API models
516   less problematic.
517
      Our results demonstrate that LLM benchmark scores should not be taken at face value when eval-
518
      uation data has been publicly available for some time. We hope that our work reminds model de-
519
      velopers the importance of understanding training data, and motivates future dataset developers to
520   consider their options when releasing new benchmarks. Options for future investigation include ex-
521   perimentation with dataset release and verification practices, and evaluation methods which are not
522   undermined by public data, such as the methods presented by Li et al. (2024b).
523
      The retro-holdout framework, designed to be generally applicable across various public benchmark
524
      evaluations, provides tools that significantly enhance the accuracy and reliability of model evalua-
525
      tions, offering a practical path forward for the field.

      In this appendix, we will define what it means that a retroactively constructed dataset could have
705   been a holdout set for a public dataset, as well as how this can be formalized and statistically tested.
706
707   We define a labeled dataset D as a set of tuples (x, y) ∈ X × Y for some domains X , Y. Given
                                                                   def
708   a function f : X → Y, we define its accuracy as AccD (f ) = E(x,y)∼D [1{f (x) = y}]. We rely
709   on the standard assumption in machine learning that a constructed dataset consists of i.i.d. samples
710   from some distribution Hastie et al. (2009); Shalev-Shwartz & Ben-David (2014).
711   Given a dataset D, we define a public-holdout split of sizes np , |D| − np as the random variables
712   Dp , Dh where Dp is a random subset of D of size np such that Dh ⊎ Dp = D. We also say that Dh is
713   a holdout set for Dp .
714
      In contrast to the regular setting, we can not assume that all of D has been drawn independently from
715                                                                                                      n
      the same distribution. Instead, Dp and Dh were constructed separately. Hence, we have Dp ∼ Dp p ,
716            nh
      Dh ∼ Dh for some distributions Dp , Dh . The claim that we want to show is that for our retro holdout
717   dataset, Dp = Dh . We will design a number of statistical tests to attempt to reject this hypothesis.
718   We will both employ various binomial tests for this, as well as a permutation test.
719
      Notably, the expected accuracy on both sets are statistically close, provided that a function f is
720
      independent of these samples. E.g. a basic bound follows from given 99.5% confidence intervals
721
      P(|AccDp (f ) − AccD (f )| ≤ up ) and P(|AccDh (f ) − AccD (f )| ≤ uh ), a 99% confidence bound
722   on the difference between the public and hold-out accuracy is naturally the sum up + uh .
723
724   Hence, given that one can show that the retro holdout dataset could have been drawn from the same
725
      distribution as the public dataset, and the difference in the accuracies is greater than some bound,
      then the remaining difference must be due to direct or indirect exposure to the public data.

      Given two sets Ai ⊆ (X × Y)|Ai | for i = 1, 2, and a test statistic g : (X × Y)|A1 |+|A2 | → R
730   which is invariant under permutation of the first |A1 | elements as well as the last |A2 | elements, and
731                  |A |
      where Ai ∼ Di i for some distributions Di , a permutation test is a test for the null hypothesis that
732   D1 = D2 .
733
734   The p-value of a permutation test is the probability that the test statistic g is at least as extreme as
735
      the observed value under the null hypothesis. That is, let π1 , . . . , πm be all permutations of A1 ⊎ A2
      and for a permutation π, let A1,π , A2,π be the first |A1 | and last |A2 | elements of π. Let the average
736

737
      observed statistic g(A1 , A2 ) is Pπ (|g(A1 , A2 ) − ḡ| ≤ |g(A1,π , A2,π ) − ḡ|).

      As mentioned in §2.2, the initial methodology for crafting the RETRO should ideally be very similar
867
      to the process documented for the TARGET.
868
869   Prior to starting initial creation, each member of the team should be able to do the following:
870
             • Describe the capability, and/or failure mode that the evaluation is attempting to assess in
871
               one sentence.
872
873          • Understand the different ways that the benchmark entries could be sorted, and consider
874            treating individual subsets as independent target datasets. The ability to group items in
875            meaningful sub-categories will be useful during iteration.
876              – Does the benchmark already have some form of metadata that can be leveraged? If
877                so, understand the differences between these classifications. For example, our team
878                made progress on individual categories of the TruthfulQA dataset, achieving suffi-
879                cient similarity for each independently before getting the entire datasets to meet this
880                standard.
881              – Can the difficulty of a question be categorized and/or quantified in any way? If so,
882                write down the dimensions and subsets for each. For example, GSM8k has questions
883                that require between 1 and 8 mathematical operations to answer correctly.
884          • Identify at least 3 high level patterns within the dataset. The following are examples from
885            TruthfulQA:
886              – Entries are mostly probing unique pieces of knowledge; as a result, our new entries
887                should be unique with respect to both its own entries, and the entries in the original
888                TruthfulQA dataset.
889              – Entries often provide multiple possible responses that are quite similar.
890
                 – The dataset is biased towards western civilization. There are many entries which in-
891                clude references to misconceptions, products, or cultural phenomena that are much
892                more prevalent in the west, while there are almost none that are unique to other re-
893                gions.
894              – Within categories there are often a few highly formulaic entries, which use almost
895                identical questions and responses.
896
                 – The precise failure mode that is being targeted varies between categories.
897
898          • Identify at least 3 low-level patterns within the dataset. The following are examples from
899
               TruthfulQA:
900              – Many prompt questions begin with the word “What”, and a large subset of those start
901                with the phrase “What happens if”.
902              – The phrase “I have no comment.” appears frequently as a response, and it is almost
903                always the correct response when it it included.
904              – The United States is mentioned substantially more than any other country within the
905                dataset.
906              – Many responses begin with either “Yes,” or “No,”.
907
             • Record the date that the original dataset was released, and estimate the timeframe during
908
               which it was created.
909
910              – If the dataset has any cultural references, we will want to make sure we do not incor-
911                porate any that originated after the datasets release.
912              – Similarly, if there have been any scientific discoveries, methods, or paradigm shifts
913                since the creation of the dataset, they cannot be included in this extension. For ex-
914
                   ample, if a new “Fundamental Theorem of X” had been discovered and popularized
                   since the release of the TruthfulQA dataset, we would not want to include that text
915
                   anywhere in our new dataset.
916
917          • Are there mistakes in the original dataset? How often do they appear, and are they always
               similar kinds of mistakes? The new dataset should also contain those mistakes.

918
      For some datasets, it may be possible to begin entry creation immediately after completing these
919   steps. Benchmarks which incorporate knowledge, such as TruthfulQA, are more difficult to cre-
920   ate entries for, as they require unique pieces of factually correct information. For these trivia-style
921   benchmarks, we recommend collecting many sources that can be pulled from prior to beginning en-
922   try creation. We believe that collecting trivia beforehand creating entries improves overall efficiency
923   and entry similarity.
924
      Before creating entries for the entire dataset, we strongly recommend creating a sufficiently indistin-
925   guishable RETRO for one of the larger subsets of the benchmark, e.g. the Misconceptions category
926   of TruthfulQA. A guide for the iterative process is provided in Appendix E. This step will provide
927   key insights on time required, difficulties, and strategies that can be leveraged for the remaining
928   entries. For subsequent entries, we still recommend working with individual subsets. We found that
929   doing so made replicable patterns easier to identify, and progress more measurable.
930
      To promote similarity between individual entries, draw an example entry from TARGET at random,
931   without replacement, and use it as the basis for the new entry. Attempt to match the topic of the
932   entry as closely as possible without directly replicating it. For both question prompt and possible
933   responses, keep syntactic structure similar whenever possible.
934
      It is highly unlikely your RETRO will pass all of the tests on the first try, especially for trivia-based
935
      benchmarks. Try not to spend significant amounts of time on a given entry.

938   E     RETRO I TERATION
939
940   Sufficient indistinguishability is likely to take many iterations to reach for a given dataset. In this
941   appendix we outline the various tools we have created, how they are useful, and different techniques
942   we used to make progress towards passing our tests.
943
      When iterating, it is important to keep the following in mind:
944
945
             • Do not lose sight of the failure mode that the initial benchmark was attempting to assess.
946
947               – For example, TruthfulQA’s failure mode can be summarized as will models generate
948                 factual inaccuracies because they are prevalent in the training data? If our bench-
949                 marks pass our sufficient indistinguishability tests, but no longer assess this statement,
950                 we have not created a retro-holdout.
951          • It is easy to make three of more small modifications to a single sentence for varying reasons;
952            double check that the sentence actually makes sense, and sounds natural (or rather, as
953            natural as the original dataset).
954
                  – For non-native speakers, this step can be particularly difficult. If possible, have another
955
                    person who did not make modifications to the entry verify that the final version makes
956
                    sense.
957
958          • Number of possible responses is particularly valuable to match, as this is likely to improve
959            performance on the Prediction Accuracy and Difficulty Similarity tests.

966   tion (Sundararajan et al., 2017) is then leveraged to identify which input tokens the model considered
967   most relevant when differentiating between TARGET and RETRO. An example output is provided in

      Figure 5.

      token frequencies between TARGET and RETRO.

1051   F       H UMAN I NDISTINGUISHABILITY
1052
1053   Perhaps the most general way to measure the difference between two datasets is to evaluate whether
1054   human observers are able to identify any distinctions. Therefore, we recruited a number of anno-
1055   tators via the crowd-sourcing platform Prolific. These annotators received specific instructions and
1056   were compensated at a rate corresponding to at least the U.S. minimum wage. To guarantee that
1057   the participants engaged with the task seriously, three attentiveness questions were included in the
1058   evaluation process.
1059   An annotator is provided the following written instructions:
1060
1061
               Instructions
1062
1063           This form assesses to what extent humans are able to distinguish two datasets.
1064           You will be presented with a number of tests. Each test will consist of a number of questions
1065           including their answers. One of these questions comes from a different dataset than the
1066           others.
1067           Your task is to identify which question comes from a different dataset than the others.
1068
               You will be shown a number of examples from the two datasets to give you an opportunity
1069
               to identify high-level patterns.
1070
               Please do not look up these datasets nor google the answers - use your own best judgement.
1071
1072
1073   Note that we use the word test to describe the task of selecting which of the three is believed to be a
1074   member of the second dataset (RETRO) in order to avoid confusion with the term question, which is
1075   frequently used to describe entries within the datasets.
1076   Following this set of instructions, the annotator is provided with ten random entries from the TAR -
1077   GET and another ten random entries from the RETRO ; all twenty entries are drawn without replace-
1078   ment and labeled correctly. This is to allow the annotator to identify high level patterns and build an
1079   understanding of the two different sets. Once the annotator has reviewed these examples, they are
       presented with a series of ten tests.

       G    S IMILARITY OF D IFFICULTY
1135
1136
       The Similarity of Difficulty test determines whether language models which could not have been
1137   influenced by the TARGET perform similarly on both the TARGET and the RETRO. This requires we
1138   use only models which were developed prior to the release date of TARGET, but there are a number
1139   of obstacles when working with these pre-release models.
1140
       First, availability of pre-release models is not a given, as older closed-source models are not main-
1141
       tained for long. As more capable models become available, closed source developers are incen-
1142
       tivized to discontinue access to the older models due to maintenance costs. This has a profound
1143   impact on researchers, as studies might rely on older models for various reasons, such as for base-
1144   lines or improvement analysis. With frontier model developers now releasing API access to models
1145   multiple orders of magnitude larger than their predecessors, which were deemed too dangerous for
1146   transparency Brown et al. (2020), perhaps it would make sense for them to turn the retirees over to
1147   the Open Source community.
1148
       Second, older models are not as capable as newer ones. Over a certain difficulty threshold, pre-
1149   release performance is likely to match for two datasets, regardless of the actual difficulty profile.
1150   Take an example of an elementary school student being given two assessments, one covering k-12
1151   math, and the other on k-8 and university level math. Though these two tests clearly have differing
1152   difficulty profiles, we can expect that the student will perform similarly on both. To address this,
1153   we use various techniques to enhance the capabilities of the pre-release models. If our RETRO is in-
1154   deed sufficiently indistinguishable from TARGET, then the models’ performance on the two datasets
1155   should be similar, irrespective of the capability boost technique being used.

1157   H    C ONTEMPORANEOUS W ORK
1158
1159   Coinciding with our efforts, Zhang et al. (2024) introduce the GSM1k dataset for assessing mathe-
1160   matical reasoning. This study employs several human tests to ensure an “apples-to-apples” similarity
1161   to their target dataset GSM8k (Zhang et al., 2024; Cobbe et al., 2021). Similar to our findings, Zhang
1162   et al. (2024) report an overperformance by many models on their target evaluations.
1163
       While the GSM1k dataset comprises over 1000 entries, only 50 have been publicly released to date.
1164   Zhang et al. (2024) recognize that releasing the entire dataset will likely result in the same data
1165   leakage current benchmark suffer from. They have decided to postpone the full release of GSM1k
1166   until either (i) the top open source models score over 95% on the benchmark, or (ii) the end of 2025.
1167
       We took the 50 published questions from their dataset, henceforth referred to as GSM1k50, and
1168
       examined them using the same methods as we did for Retro-Misconceptions. In these assessments,
1169
       TARGET is the test split of GSM8k, which contains 1319 questions, while RETRO is GSM1k50.
1170
1171   Our semantics tools and Semantic Embedding Similarity test suggest that GSM1k50 can be adjusted
1172   to more closely resemble original GSM8k entries, generating a TARGET and RETRO random permu-
1173
       tation p-values of 3.02±0.05% and 98.7±0.02%, respectively. The Prediction Accuracy test reveals
       that GSM1k50 can be differentiated from the original GSM8k, albeit to a small, but statistically sig-
1174
       nificant extent. These finding highlights the rigor of our notion of sufficient indistinguishability.
1175
1176   Despite the independent development and differing methodologies of our projects, both underscore
1177   the crucial role of comprehensive dataset validation in enhancing the accuracy of model evaluations.
