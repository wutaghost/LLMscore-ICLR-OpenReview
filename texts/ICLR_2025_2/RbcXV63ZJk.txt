009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011
               How can we best encode structured data into sequential form for use in large
012            language models (LLMs)? In this work, we introduce a parameter-efficient method
013            to explicitly represent structured data for LLMs. Our method, GraphToken, learns
014            an encoding function to extend prompts with explicit structured information. The
015            encoding function in GraphToken uses graph neural networks to effectively transfer
016            the relational inductive biases in the structured data to an LLM. Unlike other work
017            which focuses on limited domains (e.g., knowledge graph representation), our work
018            is the first effort focused on the general encoding of structured data to be used for
019            various reasoning tasks. We show that explicitly representing the graph structure
020
               allows significant improvements to graph reasoning tasks. Specifically, we see
               across the board improvements - up to 73% points - on a wide variety of node,
021
               edge and, graph-level tasks on benchmarks for graph reasoning (GraphQA) and
022
               molecular property prediction tasks (ChemLLMBench).

026   1    I NTRODUCTION
027   There has been an explosion of recent excitement around using LLMs (Vaswani et al., 2017; Devlin
028   et al., 2018; Raffel et al., 2020; Brown et al., 2020; Touvron et al., 2023; Team et al., 2023) to
029   represent, process, and analyze textual data. These models typically take sequential text as their input
030   but recent work has extended inputs to spatial and temporal modalities (e.g., to images as in Chen
031   et al. (2022b) and to videos as in Arnab et al. (2021)).
032   Despite their success, current realizations of LLMs have noticeable problems – including a tendency
033   to generate outputs which are untrue or unsupported by their prompt, commonly referred to as
034   hallucinations (Wang et al., 2023a). Another intimately related issue is the problem of freshness,
035   where the knowledge required to answer a query exists only after an LLM’s training date (Vu et al.,
036   2023). One mitigation for these problems is through the enrichment of the prompt with additional
037   factual and fresh data. As Kadavath et al. (2022) showed, when LLMs are supplied with new
038   and supporting information, they are capable of adapting their parametric beliefs to effectively
039
      incorporate new evidence. Despite the promise of these approaches to improve generation, most
      work in the area so far has focused primarily on the discovery and inclusion of relevant textual
040
      information (Khandelwal et al., 2019; Guu et al., 2020). However beyond text, there is an abundance
041
      of more structured data in many applications for LLMs. For example, structured data sources such as
042   social and biological networks, chemical compounds, relational databases, rich tables and knowledge
043   graphs are ubiquitous in industry. This begs the question - “How can we best include structured data
044   in a LLM’s context?”
045
      Despite its importance, understanding how to best represent graph data optimally for LLM integration
046
      is an unsolved problem. Currently, the predominant mode of encoding structured data for LLMs is to
047
      use various types of hand-crafted, text-based serialization (Guo et al., 2023a; Wang et al., 2023b;
048   Stechly et al., 2023) This approach can impose significant decoding complexity for the language
049   model: from any text description, the model must first correctly decode and understand the structure
050   before it can utilize the information. Recently, Fatemi et al. (2024) et al., demonstrated that pure text
051   representations of structured data are insufficient for graph reasoning with LLMs. They show that
052   LLMs are not able to utilize structure efficiently when posed with common reasoning tasks that are
053   easily answered by classical graph algorithms. This highlights the need to explore better and more
      expressive ways of representing structured data to a LLM.

054
      In this work, we propose GraphToken (Figure 1), a parameter-efficient method for encoding structured
055   data for LLMs to address this deficiency. Inspired by recent advancements in parameter-efficient fine-
056   tuning (Lester et al., 2021; Xu et al., 2023), GraphToken learns an encoding function that generates
057   fine-tuned soft-token prompts. The soft-token prompt extends a textual prompt with explicit structural
058   information, allowing us to train a much smaller number of GraphToken parameters compared to the
059   total LLM parameter allocation. To enable the model to account for the relational inductive biases
060   in the input, we employ various forms of graph neural networks within GraphToken. Unlike other
061   options available for increasing graph reasoning performance (e.g. changing the model’s pre-training
062   mixture or fine-tuning the model’s parameters Hu et al. (2021)), GraphToken’s GNN adapter works
063
      with a frozen LLM, operating in the token-space of the model.
064   Our work is the first to develop parameter-efficient encoders specifically for general reasoning tasks
065   on structured data. Our experimental results demonstrate that explicitly representing structure leads
066   to significant improvement on the comprehensive GraphQA benchmark (Fatemi et al., 2024). For
067   example, we show that adding a small number of “graph-aware” parameters can allow a large model
068   like PaLM-2 S to outperform its much larger sibling PaLM-2 L by up to 41% accuracy.
069   Specifically, our contributions are as follows:
070
      • GraphToken, a novel parameter-efficient encoder for structured data inclusion in LLMs.
071
      • Experiments: Extensive experiments with a variety of both large proprietary and open source
072
        models that illustrate the benefits of GraphToken. Our experiments on both graph reasoning
073     tasks and molecular property prediction show that our method can significantly improve LLM
074     capabilities, allowing small models to outperform larger ones.
075   • Analysis: We analyze GraphToken generalization on both unseen tasks and graphs.

079
080   We introduce the related work in LLMs, prompting methods, Graph Neural Networks (GNNs), graph
081   encoders, and graph models combined with LLMs.
082
083   2.1   L ARGE L ANGUAGE M ODELS
084
085   Pre-Trained Large Language Models (LLMs): Language models (Rosenfeld, 2000; Zhao et al.,
086   2023) are autoregressive models, assigning likelihoods to tokens given a context of preceding or
087
      surrounding tokens. While earlier methods counted frequencies of N-grams (Jurafsky, 2021, chapter
      2), newer models adopted neural approaches with the advent of distributed word representations
088
      (Bengio et al., 2000; Mikolov et al., 2013). The increased power offered by the neural language
089
      models and the increase in model and dataset sizes has led to a new learning paradigm where large
090   language models (LLMs) are pre-trained in an unsupervised setting on massive amounts of textual
091   data and are used as base (foundation) models (Devlin et al., 2018; Radford et al., 2019). For each
092   downstream application, the base model is fine-tuned on small amounts of task-specific data to adapt
093   the model to the task.
094
      Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning: With the rapid growth in the number of parameters for state-
095
      of-the-art LLMs (Achiam et al., 2023; Team et al., 2023) fine-tuning for each downstream task
096   has become prohibitively expensive in both time and resources. The goal of parameter-efficient
097   fine-tuning (PEFT) (Xu et al., 2023) is to adapt models to new tasks by updating only a small number
098   of (possibly new) parameters. PEFT approaches are distinguished primarily by where parameters are
099   tuned (or added). Adapter-based approaches (Houlsby et al., 2019; He et al., 2021) hold the LLM
100   parameters frozen and add new trainable parameters to various parts of the model. Meanwhile, partial
101   fine tuning and partial masking methods (Zhao et al., 2020; Zaken et al., 2021) only fine-tune or mask
102   a subset of the LLM parameters – no new parameters are introduced. Some methods operate with
103   frozen LLM parameters. Soft-prompt methods (Li & Liang, 2021a; Lester et al., 2021; He et al., 2022)
104
      prepend tokens with learnable parameters to the beginning of the LLM input or to the beginning of
      every LLM layer. Finally, the most popular group of PEFT techniques, LoRA and derivative methods
105
      (Hu et al., 2021; Edalati et al., 2022; Valipour et al., 2022) learn offsets to frozen model weights.
106

      the work of Levine et al. (2022), where the input is provided to a separate trainable neural network to

135   2.2    G RAPH E NCODING WITH N EURAL N ETWORKS
136
137   The field of graph representation learning (Chami et al., 2022) seeks ways to represent discrete
138   structured data in a continuous domain, typically for use in some downstream learning task. While
139   seminal work like DeepWalk (Perozzi et al., 2014) popularized the node embedding problem, later
140   work utilized GNNs to generalize and learn representations of entire graphs (graph embeddings).
141   Many approaches learning graph representations (node or graph embeddings) have followed since
142   (Tsitsulin et al., 2018; Xie et al., 2022).

144
145   2.3    G RAPHS AND LLM S
146
147   The confluence of graph representation learning and reasoning with LLMs is a rapidly growing field
148   of research – like language, structured data surrounds us but, unlike LLM input, it is not sequential.
149   Some of the first graphs represented in the literature were knowledge graphs as in Agarwal et al.
150   (2020), where the retrieval corpus of a retrieval LLM is augmented with text-encoded knowledge
151   graphs. Ye et al. (2023) utilize instruction fine-tuned LLMs for node classification. Similarly,
152   Chen et al. (2023b) leverage LLMs to enhance graph learning models by incorporating rich text
153
      attributes. Wang et al. (2023b) showed that language models demonstrate preliminary abilities for
      graph reasoning tasks. Later, Fatemi et al. (2024) proposed GraphQA – a comprehensive benchmark
154
      to systematically evaluate models for graph reasoning tasks – finding that graph reasoning tasks are
155
      currently difficult and that scaling laws do not seem to apply. Finally, in concurrent work to ours, Chai
156   et al. (2023) study how prefix tuning Li & Liang (2021b) can extend graph reasoning capabilities.
157   Interestingly their results are substantially different than ours. First, they were only able to operate on
158   graphs where each node has rich textual features. With GraphToken we show that it is possible to
159   use abstract graph representations (where the only node features are based on the graph itself (or are
160   learned). Secondly, their proposed method (prefix tuning) is much more expensive than ours (prompt
161   tuning) as adds additional parameters for each layer in the network - they require ∼10× as many
      parameters as our method.

      3     G RAPH T OKEN : S TRUCTURALLY I NFORMED G ENERATION
163
164
165   When considering how to pass graph data to an LLM there are largely two families of options:
166   (1) encoding it as lexical tokens for LLM embedding as in Fatemi et al. (2024) or (2) encoding it
167   directly as a continuous representation via a neural network – skipping any LLM token embedding.
168   While representing a graph as a sequence of lexical tokens has benefits in terms of interpretability, it
169   introduces a core problem – there is often no clear choice in what order to sequentially write the graph
170   data. In fact, this seemingly simple act of choosing a sequential serialization removes the permutation
171   equivariance which is a core inductive bias for geometric machine learning (Bronstein et al., 2017).
172
      We believe a text encoding of structured data prohibits rich, concise, and expressive representations.
      Instead, our method eschews representing a graph in text in favor of directly producing – using a GNN
173
      as an encoder – the continuous representations for the LLM input. Our approach allows the GNN
174
      to provide sample efficient graph learning (and when necessary, other properties like permutation
175   equivariance) for graph algorithmic tasks (Sanford et al., 2024). We refer to these new graph encoder
176   learned soft-tokens in the LLM embedding space as “graph tokens.”
177
178
      To maintain the reasoning and language capabilities of the LLM, we freeze its parameters and teach
      the graph encoder to align its output representations with the LLM embedding space. In other
179
      words, we learn only the parameters of the graph encoder during the training process. This reduces
180
      computational requirements significantly (graph encoder parameters are minuscule compared to the
181   LLM). During our tests, the LLM is prompted with the output of the graph encoder and a task about
182   the graph, for example: ‘Does this graph contain a cycle?’. As such, the quality of the results is
183   purely a function of how well the graph encoder represents the answer to the task and how well the
184   LLM interprets that output.

      3.1   A RCHITECTURE
189
190   An overview of the architecture is provided in Figure 1. At a high level, our model only has two
191   components. First, the graph encoder takes a graph as input and outputs a fixed number of token
192   embeddings. These tokens are then prepended to the sequence of initial token embeddings in the
193   prompt for an LLM, which is then decoded to produce an answer as text.
194   Graph Encoder. GNN models range from simple averaging methods to complex models with multi-
195   headed attention. Thus there are a wide variety of graph representations possible. We suspect that
196   some of these representations are more suited to be consumed by an LLM. Therefore, we conducted
197   a thorough study that includes several well-known graph encoder choices in §4.3. Our graph encoder
198   takes the relational structure of the graph as input, using some form of graph positional encoding as
199   node features (either learned, Laplacian, or a combination thereof) - see §4.3.2 for details.) Next, we
200
      apply a GNN to obtain a representation of the graph, which we read out using one of a few different
      techniques techniques depending on the task.
201
202   • For graph-level tasks (e.g., cycle check) we do global pooling for readout, taking the mean
203     or sum of the representations over all of the nodes.
204   • For node-level tasks (e.g., node degree) we separately output the representation of each node.
205     This can be optionally row-wise concatenated with a graph-level pooling.
206
      • For edge-level tasks (e.g., edge existence), we use a global representation or the two node-
        level representations concatenated.
207
208   We note that the readout used (e.g., mean or sum pooling) is a hyper-parameter chosen during model
209   selection. Whichever the readout technique, this representation is then projected onto the token space
210   used by the LLM with a final dense layer.
211   LLM. For the experiments in the paper we use several LLMs, including PaLM 2 (Anil et al., 2023),
212   Gemma2 (Team et al., 2024), Mistral7B (Jiang et al., 2023), and Phi2 (Javaheripi et al., 2023).
213   however, our method generalizes to nearly any LLM in use today. For our purposes, any language
214   model which can accept a sequence of token embeddings and produce text is acceptable, so long as
215   it is possible to compute a gradient with respect to part of the input sequence. Some details about
      training are available in §7.

      4.3     A NALYSIS : G RAPH E NCODER D ESIGN
325
326   From the results in Table 1, we can see that graph encoders can significantly improve a LLM’s
327   capability on graph reasoning tasks. However the choice of graph encoders has a significant effect on
328   task performance. Here we study how different architecture choices affect the quality of the graph
329   representation for a language model’s use, including the choices of the graph convolution, the features
330   available to the network, and the hyper-parameters.
331
332   4.3.1    C HOICE : G RAPH C ONVOLUTION
333
334   This experiment investigates the impact of graph convolution choice on the performance of GraphTo-
335
      ken. We evaluate the following diverse set of encoders:
336   • Graph Convolutional Network (GCN): One of the earliest GNNs, this model does mean pooling
337     of neighbor features, followed by a non-linearity (Kipf & Welling, 2017).
338   • Message Passing Neural Network (MPNN): A generalization of the GCN, this model allows for

446   So far we have examined the performance benefits of GraphToken, and the design choices necessary

523   6     C ONCLUSIONS
524
525   In this work we have studied the structured data encoding problem for LLMs. Our novel method,
526   GraphToken, learns a graph embedding function through the gradients provided by a frozen LLM.
527   GraphToken is especially well suited for projecting structured data into latent ‘prompt space’. It
528   is a parameter-efficient method as it requires only training the graph encoder and does not update
529
      LLM parameters. Our extensive experimental analysis across 9 graph reasoning tasks shows that
      GraphToken greatly improves graph reasoning in LLMs – we observe up to a 73% improvement on
530
      the GraphQA benchmark. We also illustrated how GraphToken offered significant improvements
531
      to the Gemma2-2B model on molecular property prediction tasks, presenting strong performance
532   against other PEFT methods, and even exceeding the performance of a larger 7B parameter model.
533
534   There is still much to do! We believe that our approach is fundamental for adapting new structured
535
      data sources to LLMs (which are expensive and time consuming to train), and presents an attractive
      way of improving fundamental problems in LLMs, including hallucinations, factuality, and freshness.

541
542
      Note: Our model GraphToken requires gradient access to a LLM in order to train. The primary
543   experiments in this paper are conducted on PaLM 2, a large model with proprietary weights. We
544   believe that understanding how structure can best be incorporated into large models is an important
545   area of work, and that our results show how our parameter efficient method can drastically increase
546   the graph reasoning capabilities of a truly large language model.
547
      However, we also believe that it is important for research to be accessible. To that end, we have
548
      developed a reference implementation compatible with smaller, open-weight models (i.e. Gemma2-
549   2B). Experiments using this implementation for molecular property prediction are shown in Section
550   4.2. We will release this implementation and tutorial materials designed to facilitate the training and
551   use of this reference implementation with smaller LMs upon the paper’s acceptance.
552
553   Source code: To accelerate future research, we will open-source a reference version of GraphToken
554   upon acceptance of the paper.
555
556   Graph Encoders The PaLM 2 GraphToken we describe was implemented in Tensorflow (Abadi
557   et al., 2015) using the TF-GNN library (Ferludin et al., 2023). Many of the graph encoders studied in
558   this paper are reference implementations of graph convolutions available in this TensorFlow library at
559   https://github.com/tensorflow/gnn/tree/main/tensorflow_gnn/models.
560
561   Large Language Model: The largest LLM used in our experiments is the instruction-fine-tuned
562
      Flan (Chung et al., 2022) checkpoint of PaLM 2 S (Anil et al., 2023). A PaLM 2 API is available
      through VertexAI at https://python.langchain.com/docs/integrations/llms/
563
      google_vertex_ai_palm.html. For the smaller open weight models used in our experiments
564
      (Gemma2, Phi2, and Mistral-7b), we used weights and code from HuggingFace (Wolf, 2019).
565
566
      Accelerator usage: Experiments were carried out on Google TPUv3 and TPUv5e (Jouppi et al.,
567   2017).
568
569   GraphToken Training: Our training procedure is very similar to that used by soft prompting
570   methods (Lester et al., 2021). The training input consists of triples (G, T, A) where G is a graph
571   structure, T is a statement or question describing the task (e.g., ‘Does this graph contain a cycle?’ for
572   cycle check) and A is the ground truth answer (‘Yes, there exists a cycle in this graph.’).
573
      In the forward pass, we compute the augmented query Q = E(G)||T (T ), concatenating the Graph-
574
      Token encoding of the graph E(G) with the initial embedding of the task textual representation,
575   T (T ).
576
577   We train by optimizing the final LLM perplexity (total log-likelihood), L(A | Q), of the expected
578
      answer A with respect to the augmented query, Q. We minimize this loss, back-propagating the
      gradient of L with respect to E(G) to the parameters of the GraphToken encoder – keeping all LLM
579
      parameters frozen. We use the Lion optimizer (Chen et al., 2023a) with a learning rate α = 0.05.
580
581
      Model Selection: In order to select the best hyper-parameters for the graph encoder, we used the
582   loss on the training dataset (GraphQATrain ) to perform model selection. Unless specified otherwise,
583   we report the corresponding test scores (from GraphQATest ).

       A.1     A DDITIONAL R ELATED W ORK
975
976    We briefly discuss several additional areas of tangentially related work for completeness.
977
       ’GNN-only’ Pretraining While there is a growing body of work in pre-training, fine-tuning, and
978
       prompt-tuning with GNNs by themselves (Fang et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023b), the research, though
979
       conceptually similar, differs crucially from our work. GNN-based approaches lack the textual
980    understanding capabilities that are central to the integration of LLMs with graph learning and
981    reasoning.
982
983
       Soft Prompt Functions for Images The idea of parameterizable prompts has been explored in the
       visual domain Tsimpoukelli et al. (2021); Alayrac et al. (2022); Luo et al. (2022); Chen et al. (2022a);
984
       Li et al. (2023); Moon et al. (2023). In this light, our work could be viewed as an extension of these
985
       ideas to the modality of graph structured ideas. We note that the visual domain has been studied
986    substantially more than graph neural networks (which are a younger field), and that developing a
987    soft prompt function for graph structured data introduces a number of additional challenges that we
988    describe in this work.
989
       Abstract Parse Information Additionally some work has studied adding semantic parse trees in
990
       order to semantically improve generation (Mager et al., 2020; Ribeiro et al., 2021). Unlike these works
991
       which focus on AMR graphs, we focus on more general graph reasoning capabilities and downstream
992    tasks (such as molecular property prediction) that depend on graph structure. Understanding relational
993    structure is key for these tasks.
994
995
       Concurrent Work Due to the importance of this research area, there have recently been a number of
       concurrent works seeking to combine graph structure and large language models. GraphWiz (Chen
996
       et al., 2024a) proposes an instruction following LM for graph reasoning, showing strong improvements
997
       over SFT tuning of regular language models for textual reasoning over graphs. Similarly, GraphGPT
998    (Tang et al., 2024) uses an instruction tuning recipe, along with a frozen graph tokenizer to improve
999    classic graph learning tasks (node classification, link prediction, etc). Additionally, LLaGA (Kong
1000   et al., 2024) and UniGraph (Chen et al., 2024b) propose different ways of aligning graph structure
1001   and natural language.
1002
       Despite the growing amount of related work, we note that GraphToken still has significant advantages.
1003
       Much of the concurrent work focuses on tuning entire language models – our work shows that this is
1004   frequently unnecessary, and competitive results can be achieved via our parameter efficient method.
1005   In addition, we were the first (to the best of our knowledge) to propose soft prompting via GNN
1006   encoders learned from LLM gradients.
1007
1008   A.2     L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
1009
1010   A.2.1    L IMITATIONS
1011
       Our results have shown that GraphToken is both a flexible and generalizable encoder of graph
1012
       structured data for LLMs. Here we discuss some limitations of the method as inspiration for future
1013
       work.
1014
1015   Encoder Generalizability. The main limitation of GraphToken is that its encoder might learn
1016   spurious correlations due to idiosyncrasies in the distribution of input graphs it was trained on.
1017
       As such, it’s important that the encoder works robustly regardless if its evaluated on a different
       distribution of input graphs (w.r.t. their density, number of nodes/edges, etc). We note that this is a
1018
       general weakness of GNNs and not specific to GraphToken itself. As such, there is a rich literature
1019
       on creating robust GNNs (Zhang et al., 2024) that has made significant progress in creating more
1020   generalizable GNN architectures. We expect that these results will be directly able to be “plugged in”
1021   to GraphToken encoders and will greatly aid in their generalization.

1023   A.2.2    F UTURE W ORK
1024
1025   This work opens up an exciting new avenue of exploration for reasoning with structured data and
       LLMs. Some potential avenues that we consider particularly exciting include:

       • This work considers existing convolutions and measures their effectiveness. An obvious and

       A.3   BASELINES
1039   To rigorously evaluate the performance of GraphToken, we compare it against the following estab-
1040   lished baselines for prompt optimization:
1041
1042
       • ZERO - SHOT. In this approach, the model is given a task description and immediately asked to
         produce the desired output. No additional examples or demonstrations are provided.
1043
       • FEW- SHOT. This approach provides the model with a few examples of the task and their desired
1044
         outputs (Brown et al., 2020). Unlike traditional training, these examples are included directly in
1045     the prompt, allowing the model to learn and adapt during the inference.
1046   • C OT. Chain-of-thought (CoT) prompting (Wei et al., 2022) provides examples each showing
1047     step-by-step reasoning, teaching the LLM to generate its own thought processes for tackling new
1048     tasks.
1049   • ZERO - COT. Zero-shot CoT (Kojima et al., 2022) builds upon Chain-of-Thought (CoT) prompting
1050     by eliminating the need for training examples. The LLM generates its own step-by-step reasoning
1051     process using a simple trigger phrase like “Let’s think step by step”.
1052   • COT- BAG. BAG prompting (Wang et al., 2023b) extends COT to improve the performance of LLMs
1053
         on graph-related tasks by appending “Let’s construct a graph with the nodes and edges first” to the
         prompt.
1054
       • SOFT- PROMPT. This approach uses the standard soft prompt tuning of Lester et al. (2021).
1055
         It optimizes a global static prompt which is shared across problem instances to improve task
1056     performance. Unlike our proposed method, it does not have access to the graph information,
1057     making the results of this approach equivalent to that of a majority classifier.

1059   A.4   G RAPH E NCODERS
1060
1061   Notation. We briefly describe the notation we will use. The graph G = (V, E) contains the set of V
1062   nodes and E edges. While we will only discuss simple graphs, everything discussed can be extended
1063   to heterogeneous graphs w.l.o.g. (Battaglia et al., 2018; Ferludin et al., 2023).
1064   Using the notation of Ferludin et al. (2023), a GNN has two primary operations. First, a next
1065   state function (N EXT S TATE) which computes the hidden state hv of a node (or edge, m(u,v) ) given
1066   information from its neighbors and its previous state, and an aggregation function (E DGE P OOL)
1067   which pools information for a node’s immediate neighborhood into a fixed size representation. More
1068   formally, we can say that the next state of a node is:

1124   A.6    M OLECULAR P ROPERTY P REDICTION WITH G RAPH T OKEN
1125
1126   Here we elaborate on the experimental settings from Section 4.2.
1127
       Datasets. The benchmarking datasets within ChemLLMBench (Guo et al., 2023b) are a collection
1128   of datasets intended to test prediction of different molecular properties, with many of the property
1129   prediction datasets originating from MoleculeNet (Wu et al., 2018). The Blood-Brain Barrier
1130   Penetration (BBBP) dataset tests the ability of compounds to penetrate the blood-brain barrier. The
1131   BACE dataset tests inhibition of human β-secretase 1, while the Tox21 dataset tests the toxicity of
1132   compounds on 12 different nuclear receptor and stress receptor targets. The ClinTox dataset compares
1133   FDA-approved drugs to failed drugs for toxicity. For all property prediction tasks, we evaluate the
       accuracy of model predictions against ground truth molecular property labels.


                                                                   21
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1134
       Processing. In this section, we overview the encoding method used for chemical molecules in the
1135   ChemLLMBench (Guo et al., 2023b) datasets. At a high-level, a molecule is taken as input and
1136   converted into a graph where nodes represent atoms connected by edges that represent atomic bonds.
1137   The GNN encoder component encodes the graph and outputs tokens which are prepended to the LLM
1138   input prompt, which the LLM can then reason over in chemistry tasks.
1139
       To encode structural information about chemical molecules, we follow a principled framework for
1140
       encoding node, edge, and graph-level features pertaining to input molecular graphs:
1141
1142        1. Node-level features comprise of atomic properties of atoms which are represented by nodes
1143           in the input molecular graph. These node feature capture properties such as atomic number,
1144
               mass, and charge.
            2. Edge-level features comprise of information about bonds which connect atoms together in
1145
               the molecular graph. Edge features give information about the type of bond linking atoms
1146
               together.
1147        3. Graph-level features consist of molecular signatures, or fingerprints, of the molecule. These
1148           features are obtained through standardized molecular fingerprint generators such as mor-
1149           dred (Moriwaki et al., 2018), which have predefined molecular descriptors which capture
1150           properties about input molecules such as the presence or count of certain subgroups.
1151
       Given this input feature space, we utilize a GNN encoder to perform message-passing over the
1152
       molecular graph, and perform a pooling readout operation across node tokens and the graph-level
1153   molecular fingerprint features in order to produce the final readout tokens.
1154
1155   Baselines. We evaluate GraphToken against a suite of other parameter-efficient finetuning methods,
1156
       to isolate the performance improvement of GraphToken against other parameter-efficient methods
       by virtue of efficiently encoding structure into the input tokens for the LLM. We compare against
1157
       LoRA (Hu et al., 2021), prompt tuning (Lester et al., 2021), and P tuning (Liu et al., 2023a), three
1158
       established methods for parameter-efficient tuning.
1159
1160   Results. The results of this experiment, summarized in Table 3, demonstrate that GraphToken
1161   significantly outperforms existing parameter-efficient finetuning methods on molecular property
1162
       prediction tasks. GraphToken outperforms the next-closest parameter-efficient baseline, P Tuning
       (Liu et al., 2023a), by up to 23% accuracy on molecular property prediction. Notably, GraphToken
1163
       does well on the highly imbalanced ClinTox dataset, where 80.8% of samples belong to the majority
1164
       class. Finally, we again see that GraphToken allows Gemma2-2B, to outperform a larger LLM
1165   (Mistral-7B) even when it is also augmented with other parameter-efficient finetuning methods.
