009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               Chain-of-Thought (CoT) reasoning holds great promise for explaining language
012
               model outputs, but recent studies have highlighted significant challenges in its
013
               practical application for interpretability. We propose to address this issue by mak-
014            ing CoT causally essential to prediction through two key components: factoring
015            next-token prediction through intermediate CoT text, and training CoT to predict
016            future tokens independently of other context. This results in “Markovian” lan-
017            guage models, where CoT serves as a fixed-size state for future token prediction.
018            Our approach optimizes for “informativeness” – the improvement in next-token
019            predictions using a trained CoT compared to a baseline. Using Proximal Policy
020            Optimization (PPO) for arithmetic problems and policy gradient for GSM8K, we
021            demonstrate effectiveness on both arithmetic problems with Mistral 7B and the
022
               GSM8K benchmark with Llama 3.1 8B, where the model learns to produce CoTs
               that are 33.20% more effective at predicting answers than the pre-trained base-
023
               line. The increased sensitivity of model performance to CoT perturbations pro-
024
               vides strong evidence of CoT reliance. Furthermore, we show that CoTs trained
025            for one model generalize to help other models predict answers, suggesting these
026            CoTs capture reasoning patterns that transfer across different interpreters. This
027            work advances the development of more interpretable language models, poten-
028            tially enabling their extension to arbitrarily long contexts and enhancing AI rea-
029            soning capabilities across various domains.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035   The rapid advancement of language models (LMs) has revolutionized the field of artificial intel-
036   ligence, demonstrating remarkable capabilities in tackling complex cognitive tasks (Brown et al.,
037   2020). However, it can be challenging to understand why an LM gave a particular answer (Burns
038   et al., 2023; Gurnee & Tegmark, 2024; Lamparth & Reuel, 2023), which can be problematic in
039
      high-stakes scenarios (Rivera et al., 2024; Lamparth et al., 2024; Grabb et al., 2024). Interpretability
      techniques analyze the patterns and activations of a neural network in order to extract an explanation
040
      of the network’s behavior (Casper et al., 2023; Meng et al., 2022; Geva et al., 2022; Geiger et al.,
041
      2022; Wang et al., 2022; Nanda et al., 2023; Lamparth & Reuel, 2023). However, since language
042   models already speak natural language and have been trained to be able to use their own internal
043   representations, we could in principle simply ask the language model why it gave a particular an-
044   swer to a question. Asking the language model to explain its reasoning in a “step-by-step” fashion
045   before answering a question is known as Chain-of-Thought (CoT) (Wei et al., 2022; Nye et al., 2022)
046   prompting.
047
      However, there are concerns that CoT is an inadequate or unfaithful explanation for LM-generated
048   text. For example, Turpin et al. (2023) show that biasing the LM to believe a particular answer via a
049   supposedly irrelevant in-context feature such as multiple choice answer order will cause the CoT to
050   rationalize that answer without mentioning the background feature. Also some LMs give the same
051   answers to questions despite changes to the CoT reasoning in their context window (Lanham et al.,
052   2023). While this has some benefits – the model can still answer correctly despite intermediate
053   reasoning errors – it is also an indicator that the CoT does not fully capture the LM’s reasoning
      process. This raises a critical issue with using CoT as a tool for interpretability.


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      Our work introduces a novel perspective on this issue by focusing on informativeness rather than
055   faithfulness, which would imply that the CoT reflects some underlying causal process in the model.
056   Our key insight is to make the CoT text itself causally important in the model’s reasoning by training
057   an LM to generate a minimal-length CoT such that the model can predict the answer given only that
058   CoT. This approach ensures that the CoT is both complete (i.e., each necessary step is included)
059   and maximally fragile (i.e., removing or changing the meaning of any step breaks the CoT and thus
060   leads to a different result) by making the CoT itself a bottleneck in the flow of information that the
061   language model uses to produce text.
062   We assume the LM receives a sequence of observations to predict – this could be a question-answer
063   pair (length two sequence) or many adjacent segments of generic internet text. Our conceptual
064   arguments rely on the size of each observation being larger than the CoT – otherwise the LM could
065   put the answer immediately in the CoT. Though for pragmatic reasons we use short observations, the
066   model does not learn the undesirable behavior of directly answering in the CoT due to the relative
067   difficulty of predicting the answer without any CoT. The primary contributions of this work are:
068
069         1. We introduce a formal definition of informativeness which is used as an optimization target,
070
               providing a principled approach to generating meaningful CoT reasoning.
071         2. We demonstrate our training algorithm’s effectiveness by:
072
073               • Training Mistral 7B V0.2 (Jiang et al., 2023) to solve 15-term addition problems
074               • Training Llama 3.1 8B (Dubey et al., 2024) to achieve a 33.2% performance gain on
075                 the GSM8K (Cobbe et al., 2021) reasoning dataset
076
            3. We verify the causal importance of generated CoTs through perturbation analysis, showing
077
               that training increases the sensitivity of model performance to CoT modifications.
078
079         4. We demonstrate that CoTs trained for one model transfer effectively to other models, sug-
080            gesting they capture generalizable reasoning patterns rather than model-specific artifacts.
081
082   By making CoT causally important in the model’s reasoning, we aim to improve the interpretability
083
      and reliability of language models. This approach offers a novel perspective on understanding and
      steering LM behavior by leveraging the model’s own generated explanations, rather than relying
084
      solely on the analysis of its internal parameters.

      2     R ELATED W ORK
130
131
      Prior work shows that CoT prompting improves language model reasoning capabilities (Wei et al.,
132   2022; Nye et al., 2022). We train the model to produce a strong CoT, as opposed to prompting
133   strategies as in Wei et al. (2022). Scratchpad (Nye et al., 2022) also trains the model to produce a
134   CoT, but they supply correct CoTs during training, whereas our model has to discover useful CoTs
135   for itself. Zelikman et al. (2024) also use RL to improve CoT reasoning, but they do not restrict the
136   model’s attention to the previously generated CoT, making the CoT less of a standalone explanation.
137   State space models also generate state to remember their history (Gu et al., 2021; 2022; Gu & Dao,
138   2023), but we use natural language instead of activation vectors for interpretability.
139   Lyu et al. (2023) improved faithfulness of language model reasoning by restricting the output to
140   a particular formal language so that a deterministic solver could provide the rest of the answer,
141   whereas we do not restrict to production of a formal language, because our future goal is to target
142   general language modeling. Ranaldi & Freitas (2024) directly fine-tune a smaller model using CoT
143   from a more capable model. In contrast, we do not require the existence of a more competent model
144   to learn useful CoTs. Lanham et al. (2023) use robustness to reasoning perturbations as an indicator
145   of unfaithfulness, which we adapt by replacing the variation in multiple choice accuracy with the
146
      variation in log probability assigned to the correct observation. Bentham et al. (2024) respond that
      robustness might simply be an indicator of accuracy, which we ameliorate by removing history from
147
      the context window. In order to address this concern more thoroughly, we would need to demonstrate
148
      the ability to further compress our CoTs.

153
154   We would like a mathematical structure which describes the shape of a language model with a CoT
155   bottleneck, so that we can derive an reinforcement learning algorithm with respect to that formalism.
156   For this reason, we introduce the concept of Markovian Language Models and define a measure of
157   informativeness for their update functions.
158
159   3.1   M ARKOVIAN L ANGUAGE M ODELS
160
161   A regular auto-regressive LM can use its entire context when predicting the next token. In partic-
      ular, when the LM takes a question, produces some reasoning and finishes with a final answer, the


                                                       3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      generation of the final answer can still attend back to the question. Thus, there is no guarantee that
163   the CoT is causally linked to the answer tokens. In contrast, in a Markovian LM, the model is only
164   given access to limited state to make predictions.
165
166
      Formally, we define a Markovian Language Model as a tuple M = (V, S, π, u, s1 ), where:
167
              • V is a finite vocabulary,
168
169           • S is a set of states, representing CoT reasoning,
170           • π : S → ∆(V k ) is a state-conditional distribution over k-token sequences, where ∆(V k )
171             is the probability simplex over k-token sequences from V,
172           • u : V k × S → ∆(S) is a stochastic update function,
173
              • s1 ∈ S is the initial state.
174
175
      The MLM operates sequentially: given a current state st ∈ S and observation xt ∈ V k , it produces
176   a probability distribution π(st ) over the next k-token sequence, and stochastically updates its state
177   to st+1 ∼ u(st , xt ).
178

326   5.1   M ULTI - STEP A DDITION
327
328   We generate random addition problems, where each problem consists of 15 terms and each term is
329   a uniform random natural number less than 100. We fine-tune Mistral 7B Instruct V0.2 to produce
330   CoT tokens such that a frozen copy of the pre-trained language model can predict the correct answer
331   given that CoT, for each training technique in Methods. We plot the mean negative log likelihood
332   over the answer tokens as a function of training batch in Fig. 2. Note that this is both training and
333
      testing loss, since we are always generating fresh arithmetic problems. PPO, our preferred training
      method for arithmetic, can mention the correct answer in up to 90% of CoTs and achieve an average
334
      natural log probability of around -0.7.
335
336   Since the Mistral tokenizer allocates a separate token for each digit, a natural log probability of
337   -0.7 corresponds to an actual probability of e−0.7 ≈ 0.4966, or 50% chance of picking the correct
338   next token on average. A 90% likelihood saying the answer verbatim in the CoT and a 50% of
339
      guessing each digit incorrectly may seem contradictory – however this discrepancy is due to the
      predictor model’s uncertainty around prompt formatting, and specifically about what tokens should
340
      come after “Answer:”. So it is distributing probability mass over the entire vocabulary including
341
      non-numerical tokens, since we are only training CoT production uθ (s′ |o, s), as opposed to training
342   the predictor model π(o|s).
343
344   5.2   GSM8K
345
346   To test our method on more complex reasoning tasks, we train Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct to produce
347   CoT over the GSM8K dataset. Unlike our arithmetic experiments which use PPO, here we use policy
348   gradient with expert iteration (threshold 2.2 standard deviations), along with 150 CoT tokens and a
349   KL penalty of 0.1. Figure 3 shows the results across three training runs. The left plot demonstrates
350   substantial improvements in the log probability that an untrained Llama assigns to the correct answer
351
      given the trained CoT. The right plot shows the proportion of CoTs that contain the answer verbatim,
      indicating the model learns to consistently encode the correct answer in its reasoning.
352
353   Most significantly, we observe a dramatic increase in exact-match accuracy on the test set. Starting
354   from a baseline of 35.94% at batch 0, our best performing run achieves 69.14% accuracy (n=1),
355   representing a 33.2% absolute improvement. The other two runs achieve 58.23% and 62.85% re-
356   spectively, demonstrating the consistency of our method’s effectiveness on complex mathematical
357
      reasoning tasks.

411   5.4   M EASURING F RAGILITY OF C OT
412
      Expanding upon Lanham et al. (2023), we measure the fragility of the CoT reasoning by applying
413   three perturbations to the model-generated reasoning and evaluate how this affects the next-token-
414   prediction loss of the correct answer to the original question. Due to our focus on evaluating arith-
415   metic tasks, we use these three perturbations:
416
417          • Truncating a fraction of the CoT reasoning from the end
418          • Flipping any number (digit) with a probability in the CoT reasoning and replacing it with
419            another random number between 0 and 9
420          • Swapping a fraction of characters with random characters in the CoT reasoning. The se-
421            lection is limited to numbers from 0 to 9, letters from the English alphabet, and simple
422            arithmetic symbols (e.g., “+” and “-”)
423
      We test how much the model relies on its generated CoT reasoning during Markovian training runs
424
      in Fig. 5. The y-axes depicts the log probability of the answer given CoT, normalized so that y = 0
425
      corresponds to the log probability of the answer given the unperturbed CoT. The x-axis denotes
426   training steps, and there is a separate line for each kind and amount of CoT perturbation. At the
427   start of training, when the language model is essentially completely surprised by the answer, the
428   various perturbations are actually mildly helpful. But over the course of training the same amount of
429   perturbation causes more surprise as compared to the trained CoT, showing that training increases
430   sensitivity to perturbations. Notice that a truncation of just 10% from the end becomes impactful
431   relatively early in training, which suggests that the predictor is paying special attention to the final
      CoT tokens, which are more likely to contain answer or immediate precursors to the answer.

      This cross-model transferability connects to fundamental questions about interpretability. When
487   we say a CoT is ”interpretable”, we must ask ”interpretable to whom?” — just as induction heads
488   are interpretable to ML researchers but not to most humans, different CoTs might be naturally in-
489   terpretable to different readers or models. Our experimental design engages with this relativity in
490   two ways: First, we include GPT2, a significantly smaller model, as one of our evaluators. Sec-
491   ond, we test across three distinct model families (Llama, Mistral, and GPT2), preventing the trained
492   model from exploiting architecture-specific patterns. The fact that the trained CoTs transfer effec-
493   tively across this diverse set of evaluators, including a much smaller model, suggests they capture
494   reasoning patterns that are interpretable across a broad range of computational architectures.
495
496   6   D ISCUSSION AND L IMITATIONS
497
498   Our experiments show that it is possible to learn informative and interpretable CoT reasoning via
499   RL on an LM using Markovian training. However, we find that training is unstable, and we present
500   various techniques to prevent the LM from losing its strong language modeling prior.
501
      A weakness in our interpretability argument is that for GSM8K and addition we use more CoT to-
502
      kens than answer tokens, so in principle the LM could learn to put the answer in the CoT directly.
503   However, this did not affect our particular experiments because Mistral struggles to learn to add
504   fifteen terms without intermediate reasoning, and similarly for Llama with GSM8K answers. Addi-
505   tionally, our intepretability technique is currently only verified in myopic question-answer datasets,
506   as opposed to multi-turn trajectories where trained CoTs might provide a lens into longer term fu-
507   ture behavior. Lastly, we only train Mistral to produce CoT that it can interpret (use to predict
508   observations), but in principle future work could optimize CoT for human interpretability directly.
509   Markovian training is essentially language modeling – predicting future tokens from previous tokens
510   – but with an intermediate “action” to produce the LM’s own memory. In this sense, this training
511   paradigm blurs the line between RL and unsupervised learning. But since it comes at the cost of
512   adding expensive serial token generation steps in an otherwise highly parallelizable unsupervised
513   training regime, it would need to have a high payoff in terms of interpretability or perplexity in
514   order to be feasible. But as it stands, we have only tested the technique on question-answer pairs,
515   and are preliminary results are limited in the context of more general language modeling. In future
516   work, we hope to stably optimize this objective in more general contexts.
517
518   7   E THICS S TATEMENT
519
520   Reinforcement learning techniques improve a policy with respect to an arbitrary reward function.
521   But it can be difficult to mathematically specify nuanced human preferences about the policy. Both
522   reinforcement learning from human feedback and Constitutional AI help people specify and opti-
523   mize the properties they would like the AI to have. This increase in controllability makes the AI
524   more of an extension of human intention, for better or for worse. The approach of this paper is much
525   more targeted – we use RL to specifically increase an agent foresight – its ability to predict its future
526
      observations.
527   On its face, this seems like it might be just as dependent on human intentions as RLHF and Con-
528   stitutional AI – if people are more knowledgeable, maybe they could use that extra knowledge to
529   deceive others, for instance. However, better foresight may also give rise to better values, where
530   values are opinions about how to act such that the collective system can attain better foresight.

      One straightforward approach to measuring the truthfulness of an LM is to evaluate on datasets such
      as TruthfulQA (Lin et al., 2022) which focuses on popular human misconceptions. However, this
889
      technique will only continue to work so far as humans can tell which human beliefs are, indeed,
890
      misconceptions. We would like to continue training a model for informativeness on questions that
891   challenge human evaluators.
892
893   Reinforcement learning success stories such as AlphaGo (Silver et al., 2016) and AlphaZero (Silver
894
      et al., 2017) show that a top-ranking Go AI can continue to learn if we have an efficient way to
      compute the success criteria (such as a winning board state). However, many important success
895
      criteria are abstractions, and only exist within a person’s ontology. This problem is discussed at
896
      length in Christiano et al. (2021), and we will use their example to illustrate the situation.
897
898   Suppose we were building a security system AI to watch over a vault containing a diamond. Suppose
899   further that we have a camera pointed at the diamond, and that our security guard AI can competently
900
      predict future camera frames from past frames. How can we train it to classify camera sequences
      according to the ambiguous human concept of whether the diamond is still in the room, even in
901
      difficult scenarios when a person would not be able to provide a ground truth label (e.g., subtle
902
      camera tampering)? If we train the classifier based on scenarios when a person can provide ground
903   truth labels, then the AI’s video classifier has two valid generalization behaviors: (1) to say whether
904   it thinks the diamond is still in the room and (2) to say whether the dataset-labeller would think the
905   diamond is still in the room.
906
      Our approach favors the second generalization behaviour by using RL to train the AI to produce
907
      messages such that the person can themselves predict future camera frames. This idea is based on
908
      the following three insights:
909
910
             • Whereas truthfulness of an LM requires some internal information, informativeness can be
911
               measured using only input-output behavior.
912
913          • We can decompose the definition of informativeness into informativeness of a sender to a
914            receiver, which can be an AI and a person, respectively.
915
916          • We can use reinforcement learning to push past the imitation learning regime, by continuing
917            to train for this relative informativeness objective even when the AI is already the expert
               next-frame predictor.

       E     O N BASELINES FOR FAITHFUL C OT
973
974
975
       The question of appropriate baselines for our method requires careful consideration, as there are
       three distinct interpretations of what could constitute a baseline in this context:
976
977
978    E.1   BASELINES FOR O PTIMIZING I NFORMATIVENESS
979
980    For our specific informativeness objective, we compare against expert iteration with thresholding
981    and policy gradient approaches in Figure 2. While PPO shows superior performance on arithmetic,
982    the preferred optimization technique depends on the particular dataset.
983
984    E.2   BASELINES FOR FAITHFUL L ANGUAGE M ODEL R EASONING
985
986    A more fundamental challenge lies in establishing baselines for the broader goal of generating CoTs
987    that reflect a language model’s underlying reasoning process. This requires first formalizing what
988    we mean by “faithful” reasoning. Our approach takes the stance that a faithful CoT should have
989    the property that perturbing it meaningfully impacts the model’s predictive accuracy. We define this
990    formally through our informativeness objective:
991
992                                       I(u, u′ , P ) = Eτ ∼P,u,u′ [R(τ )]                              (9)
993
994
       where R(τ ) measures how much more accurately the model predicts using the CoT compared to
       without it.
995
996    To our knowledge, there are no other formal definitions of faithfulness for language models that are
997    sufficiently well-specified to serve as training objectives. If such alternatives existed, they would
998    provide natural baselines for comparison.
999
1000   E.3   BASELINES FOR C OT F RAGILITY
1001
1002   We can consider several potential approaches for generating CoTs that are fragile to perturbation:
1003
1004         1. Formal Language CoTs: One could generate CoTs in a precise language like Python,
1005            where the answer could be computed by executing the code. While such CoTs would be
1006            highly fragile to perturbation (due to syntax errors), this approach would not generalize to
1007            general language modeling tasks like Wikipedia text prediction where the “answer” cannot
1008            be computed deterministically.
1009
1010
             2. Question-CoT Pairs: We could maintain the standard approach of keeping both question
                and CoT in context when predicting answers, measuring how perturbations to the CoT af-
1011
                fect predictions. However, this makes it impossible to isolate whether the observed fragility
1012
                stems from the CoT itself or from the interaction between question and CoT.
1013
1014         3. Minimal Prompted CoTs: We could prompt the model to produce minimal CoTs and
1015            measure their fragility to perturbation. This baseline is effectively represented at training
1016            step 0 in Figure 7, where we see minimal difference in log probability between perturbed
1017            and unperturbed CoTs from the pre-trained model.
1018
1019   Each of these potential baselines has significant limitations that prevent direct comparison with our
1020   approach. The formal language approach sacrifices generality, the question-CoT approach intro-
1021   duces confounding variables, and the minimal prompted approach is already captured as the starting
1022
       point of our training process.
1023   This analysis suggests that establishing meaningful baselines for faithful reasoning remains an open
1024   challenge in language model interpretability. Our approach provides one concrete formalization
1025   and optimization target, but we acknowledge there may be other valuable perspectives on what
       constitutes faithful reasoning that could lead to different baseline approaches in future work.
