011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            In this work, we study the system identification problem for parameterized non-
014            linear systems using basis functions under adversarial attacks. Motivated by the
015
               LASSO-type estimators, we analyze the exact recovery property of a nonsmooth es-
               timator, which is generated by solving an embedded ℓ1 -loss minimization problem.
016
               First, we derive necessary and sufficient conditions for the well-specifiedness of
017
               the estimator and the uniqueness of global solutions to the underlying optimization
018            problem. Next, we provide exact recovery guarantees for the estimator under two
019            different scenarios of boundedness and Lipschitz continuity of the basis functions.
020            The non-asymptotic exact recovery is guaranteed with high probability, even when
021            there are more severely corrupted data than clean data. Finally, we numerically
022            illustrate the validity of our theory. This is the first study on the sample complexity
023            analysis of a nonsmooth estimator for the nonlinear system identification problem.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      Dynamical systems are the foundation for the areas of sequential decision-making, reinforcement
      learning, control theory, and recurrent neural networks. They are imperative for modeling the
029
      mechanics governing the system and for predicting the states of a system. However, it is cumbersome
030
      to exactly model these systems due to the growing complexity of contemporary systems. Thus, the
031   learning of these system dynamics is essential for an accurate decision-making. The problem of
032   estimating the dynamics of a system using past information collected from the system is called the
033   system identification problem. This problem is ubiquitously studied in the control theory literature
034   for systems under relatively small independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.) noise due to
035   modeling, measurement, and sensor errors. Nevertheless, safety-critical applications, such as power
036   systems, autonomous vehicles, and unmanned aerial vehicles, require the robust estimation of the
037   system due to the possible presence of adversarial disturbance, such as natural disasters and data
038   manipulation through cyberattacks and system hacking. Although machine learning techniques have
039
      been successful in addressing a wide range of problems, such as computer vision and language
      processing, their application in safety-critical systems has been extremely limited due to the lack
040
      of theoretical guarantees. This paper offers a strong result in this regard, which is concerned with
041
      studying dynamical systems via machine learning techniques.
042
043   As a motivating example, we consider the dynamical system corresponding to a power system (e.g.,
044   the U.S. electrical grid or a regional interconnect of the grid), where the states capture various physical
045
      parameters such as voltage magnitudes and frequencies in different parts of the system. With the
      objective of increasing sustainability, resiliency and efficiency of energy systems, modern power
046
      systems include a large volume of wind turbines, solar panels, and electric cars. The operation of
047
      power systems is further complicated by the fact that people have started to play an active role by
048   observing electricity prices and taking strategic actions in response to the price signals. On the other
049   hand, sensors have been widely installed across the grid to collect data to enable data-driven grid
050   operation. This has raised a major concern since a small strategic data manipulation would cause
051   power suppliers to over-supply or under-supply power electricity, which could lead to a system-wide
052   blackout. This case can be modeled as a nonlinear dynamical system where the input of the system is
053   subject to stealth attacks at various locations leading to injecting wrong values of electricity into the
      system. Given the presence of a large set of new devices in the system coupled by strategic human

      behavior, the power operators do not have a complete model of the dynamical system. Therefore,
055   they may need to learn the system and any possible adversarial attack simultaneously to be able to

059
      The prior system identification literature has mainly focused on attacks on measurements, where the
060
      goal is to extract knowledge from noisy and corrupted measurements (such as the matrix sensing
061   problem in machine learning). However, this paper considers an emerging and overlooked type of
062   attack for safety-critical systems, where the attack on input data or actuators leads to injecting a
063   wrong input signal into the system which affects the states of the system and makes them unstable.
064   More relevant literature focused on the asymptotic properties of the least-squares estimator (LSE)
065   Chen & Guo (2012); Ljung et al. (1999); Ljung & Wahlberg (1992); Bauer et al. (1999), and with
066   the emergence of statistical learning theory, this area evolved into studying the necessary number of
067   samples for a specific error threshold to be met Tsiamis et al. (2023). While early non-asymptotic
068   analyses centered on linear-time invariant (LTI) systems with i.i.d. noise using mixing arguments
069
      Kuznetsov & Mohri (2017); Rostamizadeh & Mohri (2007), recent research employs martingale and
      small-ball techniques to provide sample complexity guarantees for LTI systems Simchowitz et al.
070
      (2018); Faradonbeh et al. (2018); Tsiamis & Pappas (2019). For nonlinear systems, recent studies
071
      investigated parameterized models Noël & Kerschen (2017); Nowak (2002); Foster et al. (2020);
072   Sattar & Oymak (2022); Ziemann et al. (2022), showing convergence of recursive and gradient
073   algorithms to true parameters with a rate of T −1/2 using martingale techniques and mixing time
074   arguments. Furthermore, efforts towards nonsmooth estimators for both linear and nonlinear systems
075   Feng & Lavaei (2021); Feng et al. (2023); Yalcin et al. (2023), particularly in handling dependent and
076   adversarial noise vectors, are limited. Robust regression techniques utilizing regularizers have been
077   developed Xu et al. (2009); Bertsimas & Copenhaver (2018); Huang et al. (2016), yet non-asymptotic
078   analysis on sample complexity remains sparse, especially for dynamical systems due to sample
079   auto-correlation. A more detailed literature review is provided in Appendix.
080   This paper paves the way for the area of online optimal control in presence of adversaries and the
081   first step is to learn the dynamics of the system, known as the system identification problem. More
082   specifically, we study the system identification problem for parameterized nonlinear systems in the
083   presence of adversarial attacks. We model the unknown nonlinear functions describing the system
084   via a linear combination of some given basis functions, by taking advantage of their representation
085   properties. Our goal is to learn the parameters of these basis functions that govern the updates of the
086   dynamical system. Mathematically, we consider the following autonomous dynamical system:
087                        x0 = 0n ,    xt+1 = Āf (xt ) + d¯t ,   ∀t ∈ {0, . . . , T − 1},                (1)
088
089   where f : Rn 7→ Rm is a combination of m known basis functions and Ā ∈ Rn×m is the unknown
090   matrix of parameters. In addition, the system trajectory is attacked by the adversarial noise or
091   disturbance d¯t ∈ Rn , which is unknown to the system operator. At any time instance that the system
092   is not attacked, we have d¯t = 0. In other words, the noise only stems from adversarial attacks. The
093
      goal of the system identification problem is to recover the ground truth matrix Ā using observations
      from the states of the system, i.e., {x0 , . . . , xT }. The adversarial noise d¯t ’s are designed by an
094
      attacker to maximize the impact as much as possible and yet keep the attacks undetectable to the
095
      system operator. The underlying assumptions about the noise model will be given later.
096
097   One of the main challenges of this estimation problem is the time dependence of the collected samples.
098   As opposed to the empirical risk minimization problem, there exists auto-correlation among the
099
      samples {x0 , . . . , xT }. As a result, the common assumption that the samples are i.i.d. instances of
      the data generation distribution is violated. The existence of the auto-correlation imposes significant
100
      challenges on the theoretical analysis, and we address it in this work by proposing a novel and non-
101
      trivial extension of the area of exact recovery guarantees to the system identification problem. Since
102   the adversarial attacks d¯t are unknown to the system operator, it is necessary to utilize estimators
103   to the ground truth Ā that are robust to the noise d¯t and converge to Ā when the sample size T is
104   large enough. Our work is inspired by Yalcin et al. (2023) that studies the above problem for linear
105   systems. The linear case is noticeably simpler than the nonlinear system identification problem since
106   each observation xt becomes a linear function of previous disturbances. In the nonlinear case, the
107   relationship between the measurements and the disturbances are highly sophisticated, which requires
      significant technical developments compared to the linear case in Yalcin et al. (2023).

      4    ATTACK M ODEL
226
227   To capture the severity of an attack strategy, we consider how frequently the system is under attack.
228   We model this by an attack probability p defined below.
229
      Definition 1 (Probabilistic attack model). For each time instance t, the attack vector d¯t is nonzero
230   with probability p ∈ (0, 1), which is also independent from other time instances.
231
232   Note that the attack vectors d¯t ’s are allowed to be correlated over time and Definition 1 is only
233   about the times at which an attack happens. We do not assume that the probability distribution p
234   or the model generating the attack is known. Recall that we define K := {t | d¯t ̸= 0}. Then, with
235   probability at least 1 − exp[−Θ(pT )], it holds that |K| = Θ(pT ). When p is close to 0, the system is
236   rarely under attack. A particular focus of this paper is on the case with p close to 1, meaning that
237
      the system is under attack at almost all times. There are two related problems about attack analysis:
      (1) attack problem: the attacker aims to design the attack vectors d¯t to maximize the damage on the
238
      system, (2) defense problem: the system operator aims to detect any suspicious attack and nullify it.
239
      To design a defense mechanism, there are two common strategies (which may also be combined): (i)
240   inspect the behavior of the input of the system to detect possible attacks, (ii) analyze the collected
241   state values to learn whether there has been any attack. In the next theorem, we show that if there are
242   no assumptions on the attack vectors, the defense mechanism (ii) never works, meaning that there is
243   no estimator that can reliably detect the attacks.
244   Theorem 3. Consider the linear system xt+1 = Āxt + d¯t , together with a linear subspace D ⊂ Rn
245   whose dimension is less than n. Assume that the attack d¯t is always chosen from this not-full-
246   dimensional subspace. Then, there does not exist any estimator that can correctly identify the matrix
247   Ā all the time from the states x0 , ..., xT no matter how large T is.
248
249
      In other words, if the attacker is free to choose the attack values, the best strategy is to restrict them
      to a certain subspace so that no estimator works. However, such biased attacks are easier to detect
250
      using the defense strategy (i), meaning that the operator can study the statistical behavior of the input
251
      directly and realize that it cannot be a natural disturbance and would flag it as attack. Hence, the
252   danger of choosing such extreme attacks by an attacker is that the system operator will have a higher
253   chance to detect and nullify it through a properly designed defense mechanism. As a result, the
254   attacker should strategically avoid extreme attacks and focus on those attacks that have a less chance
255   of being detected.
256
      As an example, consider the first-order system xt+1 = xt + d¯t where d¯t ∈ R. Assume that this is a
257
      physical system which cannot accept any input with a magnitude larger than a given limit γ. Then,
258   the most severe attack would be to choose d¯t to be equal to γ all the time, which makes the state grow
259   as fast as possible. However, a proper defense mechanism can quickly detect this attack by realizing
260   that the injected input is not a natural disturbance but rather a strategically designed input. Hence,
261   the attacker should dial back and select d¯t to be +γ̄ and −γ̄ with γ̄ < γ to achieve two properties:
262   (i) avoiding hitting the maximum limit, (ii) making the attack look like a disturbance by having a
263   zero mean. This attack is much harder to detect but is still impactful since it affects the variance
264   of xt and makes it oscillate. The idea behind this example was that the attacker should sometimes
265   choose positive-value attacks and other times choose negative-value attacks. We can capture this idea
266
      through the notion of attack direction and define the following stealthy condition on the attack, which
      is aligned with the existing conditions in the literature Candès et al. (2011); Chen et al. (2021). To
267
      state the stealthy condition, we define the filtration Ft := σ {x0 , x1 , . . . , xt }.
268
269   Assumption 1 (Stealthy condition). Conditional on the past information Ft and the event that
      d¯t ̸= 0n , the attack direction dˆt = d¯t /∥d¯t ∥2 is zero-mean.

      The above notion of stealthy attacks has been commonly used in real-world systems. To illustrate
271   the idea, consider an energy system with two nodes: node 1 represents a neighborhood of homes,
272   and node 2 is a supplier owned by a utility company. Every five minutes, node 1 reports to node
273   2 the amount of electrical power the neighborhood needs for the next five minutes. Assume the
274   neighborhood requires a constant amount of 1 unit of power for the next five hours. However, an
275   attacker has compromised the communication channel between node 1 and node 2, altering the
276   requested amount from 1 unit to either 1 − e or 1 + e every half hour, where e is a large number
277   compared to 1 unit, and its value depends on |xt | at the current or previous time. Since the average of
278   1 − e and 1 + e is 1, this could serve as a stealthy attack. When node 1 actually needs one unit of
279
      power, but node 2 generates either 1−e or 1+e, the mismatch violates the laws of physics, potentially
      triggering grid instability and leading to a blackout. In this scenario, the attacker infrequently injects
280
      an adversarial input into the system that can take arbitrary values. For the attack to remain stealthy,
281
      the mean of the attack should be zero (in this example, the mean of 1 − e and 1 + e is zero). Attacks of
282   this type have appeared in different parts of the word leading to large-scale blackouts. The cyberattack
283   problem on power systems fits perfectly into our mathematical models. Note that power system
284   operators use hypothesis testing to detect anomalies in data, and if there is a nonzero mean, they
285   would flag it.
286
      In the next two sections, we provide lower bounds on the sample complexity T such that the ground
287
      truth Ā is the unique solution to problem equation 3. The next section relies on Assumption 1. After
288   that, we study unbounded basis functions, which needs Assumption 6 that is somewhat stronger than
289   Assumption 1 but still practical. Note that the former assumption requires the attack directions to
290   have a zero mean while the latter assumption requires the attack directions to be uniform. In both
291   cases, the attack magnitudes are arbitrary (similar to the parameter e in the above energy example)
292   and it can attack arbitrary values to maximize the damage on the system.
293
      Note that the existing results in the literature on finite-time system identifications for nonlinear
294   systems have all assumed that d¯t ’s are i.i.d. disturbances. Our work is the first one studying correlated
295   disturbances and our results contribute not only to the area of attack detection but also to the area of
296   non-attack corollated disturbances. The assumptions made in this paper automatically hold under the
297   assumptions made in the existing literature (such as i.i.d. Gaussian).

354   in Theorem 4. This result has a major implication for real-world systems. On the other hand, the
355   lower bound in Theorem 5 is mainly a theoretical result. Unlike machine learning problems where
356   the problem size is possibly on the order of tens of millions, the number of states for many real-world

      8   C ONCLUSION
527
528
529   This paper is concerned with the parameterized nonlinear system identification problem with ad-
530   versarial attacks. The nonsmooth estimator equation 2 is utilized to achieve the exact recovery
531
      of the underlying parameter Ā. We first provide necessary and sufficient conditions for the well-
      specifiedness of estimator equation 2 and the uniqueness of optimal solutions to the embedded
532
      optimization problem equation 3. Moreover, we provide sample complexity bounds for the exact
533
      recovery of Ā in the cases of bounded basis functions and Lipschitz basis functions using the proposed
534   sufficient conditions. For bounded basis functions, the sample complexity scales with m3 n in terms
535   of the dimension of the problem and with p−1 (1 − p)−2 in terms of the attack probability up to a
536   logarithm factor. As for Lipschitz basis functions, the sample complexity scales with mn in terms
537   of the dimension of the problem and with max{(1 − p)−2 , p−1 (1 − p)−1 } in terms of the attack
538   probability up to a logarithm factor. Furthermore, if the sample complexity has a smaller order than
539   p−1 (1 − p)−1 , the high-probability exact recovery is not attainable. Hence, the term p−1 (1 − p)−1
      in our bounds is inevitable. Lastly, numerical experiments are implemented to corroborate our theory.
