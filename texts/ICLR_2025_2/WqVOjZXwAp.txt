011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Neural codec language models have demonstrated state-of-the-art performance
014            in text-to-speech (TTS) synthesis.      Leveraging scalable architectures like
015
               autoregressive transformers, they capitalize on the availability of large speech
               datasets. When framing voice cloning as a prompt continuation task, these models
016
               excel at cloning voices from short audio samples. However this approach can’t
017
               be extended to multiple speech excerpts and is limited since the concatenation
018            of source and target speech must fall within the maximum context length which
019            is determined during training. In this work, we propose a model that replaces
020            transformers with emergent recurrent architecture such as Gated Linear Attention
021            (GLA). Our model, Lina-Speech, outperforms or matches the baseline models
022            that are up to 4x it’s size. We showcase intial-state tuning as a parameter-
023            efficient fine-tuning technique that optimizes the initial state of the recurrent
024            layers, resulting in compact and expressive speaker embedding with fine-grained
025            control over the speech style. Compared to prompt continuation, it allows voice
026
               cloning from multiple speech excerpts and full usage of the context window
               for synthesis. This approach is fast, deployable and does not rely on auxiliary
027
               modules. It also demonstrates extensive adaptation to out-of-domain data. We
028
               will release publicly our code and checkpoints. Audio samples are available at
029            https://anonymsubm.github.io.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Scaling text-to-speech Betker (2023) (TTS) model and data has led to dramatic improvements with
035   regards to quality, diversity and cloning capabilities. Leveraging neural audio codec Zeghidour
036   et al. (2021); Défossez et al. (2023) and simple problem formulation such as next-token prediction
037   have shown state-of-the-art results in zero-shot voice cloning, extending in-context learning abilities
038   observed primarily on natural language to the codec language. Under this setting zero-shot voice
039
      cloning is formulated as a prompt continuation task and provides state-of-the-art results starting
      from 3s of prompt audio. In contrast with prior works, this approach put more pressure on the
040
      pre-training stage, where large-scale speech dataset are needed in order to get sufficient in-context
041
      learning abilities and less on domain knowledge. In this direction, the transformer has been the
042   leading architecture for scalable auto-regressive modeling of speech.
043
044   While transformer is still the dominant architecture for auto-regressive large-scale generative
045
      modeling, the attention weights learned Lemerle et al. (2024); Jiang et al. (2024) during text-
      to-speech synthesis suggest that self-attention might be a sub-optimal choice for this particular
046
      task. Indeed, as observed in previous works, transformers tend either to focus on local information
047
      Parcollet et al. (2024) with respect to a given time-step or learn tight cross-attention between text
048   and audio. This potential waste of computation reflect also the lack of inductive biases towards
049   monotonicity which result in instabilities compared to NAR TTS models Yang et al. (2024b).
050   Moreover the quadratic complexity of self-attention and the relatively high framerate of neural audio
051   codec prevent training on long context. Also they typically fail at extrapolating to longer lengths.
052   As a consequence during inference, zero-shot voice cloning by prompt continuation face a trade-off
053   between longer prompt containing more information about the target speaker and short prompt that
      allow the model to synthesize over the remaining of the context window. Moreover cloning a voice

      from short excerpts makes challenging to capture elements of the voice such as accent, thus relying
055   primarily on pretraining or a subsequent fine-tuning.

057   In this work, we introduce Lina-Speech, a model that replaces transformers with Gated Linear

      AttentionYang et al. (2024c). Gated Linear Attention is an emergent recurrent architecture for
      language modeling that has shown promising result on natural language modeling while scaling
059
      linearly with the sequence length. Our contributions include the following :
060
061
             • When used in a zero-shot prompt continuation, it shows competitive performance against
062
               baselines that have up to 4x times more parameters.
063
064          • We showcase a parameter-efficient fine-tuning approach (PFET) that is proper to stateful
065            model for voice cloning. This method is fast and deployable (less than 15 seconds in
066            average for 5-30min of speech on consumer grade GPU) and shows extensive aptitude on
067            both in-domain and out-of-domain speech corpus. It enables voice cloning with full usage
068            of the context length.

073   Large-Scale TTS Large-scale TTS state-of-the-art relies massively on transformers for both
074   autoregressive (AR) Wang et al. (2023); Betker (2023); Lyth & King (2024) and non-autoregressive
075   Chang et al. (2022); Shen et al. (2024); Le et al. (2023) (NAR) architectures . NAR transformers
076   for speech synthesis, such as those based on diffusion or flow-matching, traditionally require either
077   precomputed durations or an additional generative model. While fine-grained duration annotations
078   can be challenging to produce for noisy large-scale datasets, recent work has introduced coarse
079   durations at the word or sentence level instead. In contrast, AR models have demonstrated strong
080
      performance when trained on in-the-wild data, without needing intermediate feature representations.
      Although pure NAR models tend to excel in terms of inference speed and robustness, they tend to
081
      suffer from over-smoothness Yang et al. (2024a); Ren et al. (2022), resulting in reduced diversity
082
      and less fidel prosody. Recent research has begun to blend techniques traditionally associated to
083   NAR and AR approaches: Xin et al. (2024) explicitly models durations in an AR transformer
084   for greater robustness, while Yang et al. (2024b) explores AR generative models for prosody and
085   duration modeling on top of a NAR flow-matching acoustic model. While large-scale AR acoustic
086   modeling relies heavily on neural audio codecs, diffusion and flow-matching Le et al. (2023); Betker
087   (2023); Shen et al. (2024) methods have proven being effective at scale for both data space (e.g., mel
088   spectrograms) and latent space modeling.
089
090
      Zero-shot TTS Zero-shot TTS is the task of synthesizing speech from unseen samples at test
091
      time. Traditional approaches includes the use of speaker encoder that produces embedding. Multi-
092   samples approaches have been introduced such as Mega-TTS2 Jiang et al. (2024) in order to close
093   the gap with fine-tuning approach and capture aspect of the prosody that can’t be contain within
094   a single excerpt. In contrast Large-scale TTS models relies on the in-context learning abilities:
095   prompt-continuation Wang et al. (2023); Peng et al. (2024b) and infilling strategies Le et al. (2023)
096   have been shown succes with as few as 3 seconds of audio, including from noisy sources such as
097   spontaneous speech Peng et al. (2024b) or podcasts.
098
099
      Parameter Efficient Fine-Tuning Parameter Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT) Xu et al. (2023)
100
      focuses on identifying the optimal subset of parameters for fine-tuning a model, resulting in a
101
      compact variation of the original model. PEFT has gained popularity across large language models
102   (LLMs) and other large-scale generative models, enabling adaptation on a single GPU in a fraction
103   of the pretraining time. These methods include LoRA Hu et al. (2022); Dettmers et al. (2023)
104   and its variants. Alternative techniques involve tuning embeddings that are not parameters during
105   training, such as prompt tuning Lester et al. (2021); Liu et al. (2022). PEFT can outperform full
106   fine-tuning in small data scenarios, where full fine-tuning may lead to catastrophic forgetting. Most
107   of these techniques were initially applied to natural language processing or image generation. Qi
      et al. (2024) evaluates the use of LoRA for domain adaptation in emotional text-to-speech (TTS).

163
164          • Performance: GLA achieved state-of-the-art results in linear-complexity language
165            modeling, even matching or surpassing transformer models for some tasks at large scale.
166          • Efficiency: GLA admits hardware efficient implementationYang et al. (2024c) by imposing

171            on recall-intensive tasks Arora et al. (2024), we hypothesize that they could mitigate

      4.1   M ODEL ARCHITECTURE AND INFERENCE
189
      Model architecture The text encoder is a non-causal transformer encoder that uses RoPE
190   positional encoding. The acoustic model includes both an audio encoder and a decoder, featuring a
191   transformer-like architecture (without positional encoding), where self-attention is replaced by GLA
192   layers, and SwiGLU Shazeer (2020) is used as feed-forward network,
193
194
                                         Y = X + GLA(LayerNorm(X)),
195                                                                                                        (9)
196                                    Y′ = Y + Swish(LayerNorm(Y)).
197   The decoder takes input from the audio encoder and a cross-attention layer between the text and
198   audio encoder outputs. To improve robustness, we used specialized cross-attention from Lemerle
199   et al. (2024), replacing sinusoidal positional encoding with convolutional positional encoding for
200   enhanced training stability.
201
202   Inference We use top-k sampling with k = 100 for all models and ablations, and treats EOS
203   token as an additional token to the audio codebook. If we use a RVQ as audio codec, we use greedy
204   sampling for the residuals.

212
      that this type of memory can be subject to PEFT for domain adaptation or instruction tuning of large
      language models. Since the state encodes past information without expanding on the time axis, it
213
      offers a compact alternative to prompt tuning. To the best of our knowledge, neither initial state
214
      tuning nor prompt tuning has been successfully applied to speech synthesis.

282   5.1     E XPERIMENTAL SETUP
283
284   Datasets We trained Lina-Speech on a publicly avaible english subset of MLS1 Lacombe et al.
285   (2024) which consists of 10k hours of librivox recordings. We do not use the provided transcription
286   and rather used the Automatic Speech Recognition (ASR) model Nemo 2 . We also added both
287   LibriTTS Zen et al. (2019) and its restored version LibriTTS-R Koizumi et al. (2023) with their
288   normalized transcripts. We used WavTokenizer Ji et al. (2024)3 as a neural audio codec which
289
      encodes speech at a rate of 75 token/s, with a codebook size of 4096 and compared it against
      EnCodec Défossez et al. (2023) Koizumi et al. (2023). For text input, we learn a BPE model with
290
      vocabulary size of 256 based on the lower-cased transcripts from LibriTTS. During init state tuning,
291
      we used Expresso Nguyen et al. (2023) as an out-of-domain dataset for adaptation.
292
293
      Model Configuration We provide detailed model configuration and hyper-parameters in
294
      Appendix A.
295
296
      Training and Inference The main model is trained for next-token prediction with cross-entropy
297
      loss for 500k steps with a batch size of approximately 100k tokens (≈ 22min of speech). We use
298   AdamW optimizer with a learning rate of 2 × 10−4 , a cosine learning rate schedule for the first 1k
299   steps, a weight decay of 0.1 and gradient clipping of 0.1. We group samples of similar lengths within
300   10 buckets in order to avoid padding. The training takes about 4 days on two RTX4090. Inference
301   of audio speech is made by feeding predicted tokens to WavTokenizer decoder and Vocos Siuzdak
302   (2023) for WavTokenizer and EnCodec token respectively. For long synthesis we continue training
303   for 100k steps on librilight-medium. We rely on the official hardware efficient implementation of
304   GLA Yang & Zhang (2024).

423   EXPRESSO, the performance of Lina-Speech degrades slightly compared to the one observed for
424   the in-domain task. Lina-Speech has a WER of 3.6%, a CER of 1.3%, and a cosine similarity to
425   the speaker of 0.93. This indicates the consistency of Lina-Speech, and in particular its efficiency to
426   adapt to out-of-domain speech.
427
428   Subjective evaluation For the in-domain task, Lina-Speech presents the highest score on Libri-
429   TTS both in terms of naturalness and similarity. For the out-of-domain task, this tendency
430   mostly remains observed: Firstly, Lina-Speech and Parler-TTS present comparable performance.
431   The observed difference is marginal and not significant. But secondly, Lina-Speech presents a
      significantly higher similarity to the speaker as compared to Parler-TTS.

432
      In conclusion, the reported results demonstrate the efficiency of the proposed Init State Tuning to
433   condition on new speakers either in or out of domains. This strategy offers a particularly efficient
434   and stable alternative to other strategies as the one represented by Parler-TTS. Efficient: Lina-Speech
435   only needs 15 seconds of speech for the adaptation when Parler-TTS has been trained specifically
436   for this task. Stable: In Lina-Speech the conditioning is effective and can generalize to out-of-
437   domain speakers, conditions, or speaking styles), while Parler-TTS tends to not systematically or
438   fully respect the given prompt.

      6   C ONCLUSION
441
442   In this paper, we introduced Lina-Speech a parameter-efficient model during both pretraining and
443   fine-tuning for text-to-speech synthesis. The proposed architecture leverages on Gated Linear
444   Attention and init state tuning for which we discussed the key properties in the context of text-
445   to-speech synthesis: fast and compact. Lina-Speech has been compared to other existing text-to-
446   speech models, objectively and subjectively, and both on in-domain and out-domain tasks. These
447   evaluations revealed experimentally that Lina-Speech is 1) a particularly efficient zero-shot learner
448   for voice cloning with respect to its size; 2) init state tuning is an effective PEFT method to condition
449   effectively on in and out of domain speakers from small amounts of data. The comparison with other
450
      existing TTS models demonstrates that Lina-Speech is particularly competitive versus much larger
      models, possibly specifically pre-trained or fine-tuned on a given dataset while being extremely fast
451
      and compact.

629   A    M ODEL A RCHITECTURE
630
631   Lina-Speech is a 169M parameters encoder-decoder architecture with text-conditioning through
632   cross-attention.
633
634   Text Encoder Our text encoder consists of a stack 6 non-causal transformer block with SwiGLU
635   feed-forward network Shazeer (2020), base dimension 1024. We use dropout with rate 0.1 on the
636   outputs of each block and RoPE positional encoding.
637
638   Audio Encoder and Decoder It consists for each of a stack of 6 causal GLA transformer with
639   base dimension 1024. The expansion factor of key projection is set to 0.5. We do not use dropout.
640
641   Cross-Attention We use PACA cross-attention Lemerle et al. (2024) with convolutional
642
      embedding. We do not use RoPE on queries and keys.

      Subjective evaluation was also conducted in order to compare the benchmark of algorithms by using
651   human perception. In those evaluations, the human subjects were asked to rate the naturalness of
652   speech samples (N-MOS) and their similarity to a reference speaker (S-MOS) using Mean Opinion
653   Score (MOS) ITU-T P.800.2 (2013) as commonly used in the literature for evaluation text-to-speech
654   synthesis systems. In the following, we provide all details of the experimental protocol following
655   observations reported in Kirkland et al. (2023) and recommendations presented in Cheng-Han et al.
656   (2023). This is achieved in order to improve the transparency and reproducibility of the proposed
657   protocol.

659   B.1     M ETHODOLOGY
660
      Each listener was assigned a single experiment across the 3 we introduced. It consists of 12
661
      evaluation and measured for a median time of experiment of 7 min.

      B.1.1    C REATION AND PRESENTATION OF THE SPEECH STIMULI
664
665   LibriTTS clean - Zero shot We group pair of sentences from a same speaker in the test split. We
666   first draw a prompt candidate randomly between 2 and 5 seconds, then we draw a sample so that the
667   concatenation of both samples remains within 16s in order to account of the context window of the
668   baselines, we discard speakers that do not contains at least 2 samples that satisfy this condition. We
669   draw 40 pairs in this manner.

      Init State Tuning In order to adapt our evaluation to Parler that does not provide test set, we
      synthesized random sentences with Llama3.1 8B with the following prompt.

673   ” Please generate a diverse set of 40 random sentences designed for evaluating text-to-speech
674   synthesis quality. Ensure that:
675
676
              • Variety in sentence length: Sentences should be between 12 and 20 words long.
677           • Phonetic coverage: Include a wide range of phonemes, syllable structures, and sounds.
678           • Incorporate both simple and complex sentence structures.
679           • Diverse syntax and styles: Use varied syntactic forms, rhythms, and styles to capture
680             different speech patterns and intonations.
681
              • Natural and conversational tone: The sentences should sound natural, like everyday speech,
682
                while still offering variability.
683
              • Descriptive and vivid: Include a mix of action-oriented, descriptive, and emotional content
684
                to test prosody and emotional intonation.
685
686           • Non-repetitive: Ensure that all sentences are distinct from each other, with no repetition in
687
                structure or wording.

      ”

      B.1.2    I NTERFACE FOR THE EXPERIMENT
694
695   The experiment was conducted online and implemented in Python. The experiment was preceded
696   by general recommendation to the subject before starting the evaluation:
697
698           • Please use headphones or earphones in a quiet environment.
699           • Adjust the sound level so you can hear subtle sound differences.
700
701   Each experimental run consisted in the evaluation by the subject of 15 speech samples. The order

      presentation biases. Each speech sample was presented and evaluated individually and separately
703   on a dedicated page.
704
705   On top of the page, a reminder of the instructions was presented. In the the center of the page, the
706
      speech sample to be evaluated was presented in the left side and a speech sample of the reference
      speaker was presented in the upper right corner. The criteria to be rated were presented right next to
707
      the speech sample to be evaluated and below the speech sample of the reference speaker The subject
708
      had to provide one and only one rating for all criteria before being allowed to proceed to the next
709   speech sample. Subjects were not allowed to revise their rating of previous speech samples.

712
713   In each experimental run, the subjects were asked to evaluate the naturalness of the speech sample

715   speaker consists of a real speech sample of the reference speaker which was selected systematically
716
      different from the speech sample under evaluation. By doing so, we were intending to prevent the
      subject from confusing the general perception of a speaker identity with one single realisation on
717
      a particular utterance and thus trying to mitigate the linguistic biased in the perception of speaker
718
      similarity.
719
720   The following instructions were given to the subjects:
721
722
              • In this experiment, you have to judge a speech sample with respect to a reference speech
                sample.
723
724           • The reference and the sample to judge does not pronounce the same utterance.
725
      For each speech sample, please rate :
726
727           • The speech NATURALNESS: to which extent you judge the speech sample as natural as
728             real human speech?
729
              • The SIMILARITY to the reference speaker: to which extent the speech sample is judged
730
                close to the reference speaker?
731
732   Complementary recommendations were provided either to precise the definition or the task to be
733   achieved:
734
735         1. The aspects of speech naturalness include: fluency and appropriateness of pronunciation
736            and prosody, and diversity in the expression of styles and emotions.
737         2. The samples may have different recording conditions or background noise. As the scope
738            of this experiment is only focused on speech naturalness, please try to ignore them during
739            your evaluation.
