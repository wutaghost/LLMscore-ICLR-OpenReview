009                                                      A BSTRACT
010
011                With the widespread application of Large Language Models (LLMs) in various
012                tasks, evaluating the performance of LLMs becomes an essential research topic.
013                However, existing judge models lack the specific capability required for error at-
014                tribution (i.e., identify the types of error made in responses). In this work, we
015                first establish a comprehensive Misattribution Framework with 9 primary and 19
016                secondary categories, which are intended to facilitate in-depth analysis and en-
017                hance the performance of LLMs. Based on this framework, we present AttriData,
018
                   a dataset specifically designed for error attribution, encompassing misattributions,
                   along with the corresponding scores and feedback. We also propose MisAttribu-
019
                   tionLLM, a fine-tuned model on AttriData, which is the first open-source, general-
020
                   purpose judge model with error attribution capability which provides valuable in-
021                sights into the model’s weaknesses and enables targeted improvements. Exper-
022                imental results show that MisAttributionLLM achieves the highest Pearson cor-
023                relation with human evaluators among 8 open-source and closed-source LLMs.
024                Furthermore, MisAttributionLLM also obtains the highest accuracy and micro-F1
025                in the performance of error attribution. Extensive experiments and analyses are
026                conducted to confirm the effectiveness and robustness of our proposed method. 1

029   1       I NTRODUCTION
030
031   With the rapid development of large language models(LLMs), assessing the performance of LLMs
032   has become a vital research topic (Xie et al., 2023; Chang et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024). A solid
033   evaluation method is capable of providing high-quality opinions to guide the LLM in its continuous
034
      improvement (Kim et al., 2023b).
035   The application of LLM-as-a-Judge model (Liu et al., 2023; Zheng et al., 2024) has drawn signifi-
036   cant attention because of its potential to rival human assessment. Access to high-performing large
037   language models such as GPT-4 (Achiam et al., 2023) is generally limited to the OpenAI API due
038   to their proprietary. Considering the need to avoid potential risks of commercial APIs like high
039   cost, unstable usage, and data leakage, researchers have commenced training their own judge mod-
040
      els (Kim et al., 2023b; Ke et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2023b). For instance, Kim et al. (2023b)
      proposes PROMETHEUS, an open-source language model designed to induce fine-grained evalua-
041
      tion with feedback, which provides a detailed explanation for why a given answer would be awarded
042
      a specific score. Nevertheless, both the above open-source and closed-source LLMs focus on scores
043   and feedback, which is insufficient for in-depth analysis and model’s targeted improvements.
044
045   Specifically, when evaluating the performance of a large language model, it is essential to focus on
046
      the instances where the model answers display undesirable and inconsistent behaviors, commonly
      referred to as error responses (Kamoi et al., 2024b; Chen et al., 2023). Error responses encompass a
047
      variety of issues, such as generating outputs that are convincingly presented but factually incorrect
048
      or misleading, known as hallucinations (Lin et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2023a), engaging in reason-
049   ing that does not align with the established facts or context, termed unfaithful reasoning (Golovneva
050   et al., 2023; Lyu et al., 2023), and failing to adhere to specified rules or constraints (Zhuo et al.,
051   2023; Wang et al., 2023a). These behaviors undermine the confidence in LLMs and pose substantial
052   challenges to their practical deployment. Unfortunately, researchers concentrate solely on score and

074   To this end, we propose MisAttributionLLM, a 7B LLM with error attribution capability that is
075   not only equipped to score the LLMs’ responses and generate appropriate feedback but also able
076   to provide detailed misattributions. We first establish a comprehensive Misattribution Framework
077

      improvement of LLMs. Based on Misattribution Framework, we present AttriData, a high-quality
078
      Chinese dataset that is manually annotated and crafted to encompass a variety of comprehensive
079

080   samples. With Misattribution Framework, we aim to set a new standard and benchmark in the
081   evaluation of LLMs.
082
083
      By fine-tuning Qwen2-7B (Yang et al., 2024) with the AttriData, we obtain the MisAttributionLLM.
      On the AttriData test dataset, MisAttributionLLM achieves a Pearson correlation of 0.931 with
084
      human evaluators, which is higher than GPT-4 (0.802), and significantly exceeds GPT-3.5 (0.410).
085

086   0.813, surpassing 7 open-source and closed-source LLMs. Furthermore, when human evaluators are
087   tasked with selecting the higher-quality feedback in pairwise comparisons, MisAttributionLLM is
088   chosen over GPT-4 in 53.68% of the cases and outperforms GPT-3.5 with an 83.82% win rate. To
089   the best of our knowledge, we are the first to propose a Misattribution Framework that is capable
090   of being applied for systematic model answer analysis, which leads to insightful evaluation and
091   improvement of LLMs.
092   In conclusion, our work delivers three key contributions:

      2       R ELATED WORK

      Evaluation Method With the development of large language models (LLMs), recent studies have
111
      employed GPT-4 or fine-tuned LLMs as judge models (Kim et al., 2023b; Jiang et al., 2023; Wang
112   et al., 2023b; Ye et al., 2024). For example, Wang et al. (2023b) introduces PandaLM, a fine-tuned
113   LLM designed to assess generated text and provide explanations regarding its reliability across var-
114   ious preference datasets. PROMETHEUS (Kim et al., 2023b) stands out as an open-source LLM
115   tailored for fine-grained evaluation, capable of adapting to a wide range of scoring rubrics. More-
116   over, CritiqueLLM (Ke et al., 2024) demonstrates the beneficial effects of generated critiques as
117   scalable feedback, enhancing the quality of LLM outputs. TIGERScore(Jiang et al., 2023) is guided
118   by natural language instruction to provide error analysis to pinpoint the mistakes in the generated
119   text. More evaluation methods are detailed in Appendix A.
120
121   Error Attribution Although the error attribution has not been formally proposed, research related
122   to it has primarily been conducted in the context of enhancing LLM responses using feedback from
123
      LLMs (Kamoi et al., 2024b; Chen et al., 2023; Gou et al., 2024; Pan et al., 2024). These studies
      focus on self-correction during training but often neglect the analysis of model responses with mis-
124
      attribution. Kamoi et al. (2024a) have begun to address the issue of errors in model responses, but
125
      the types of error they identify are limited, and there is a lack of trained judge models to tackle these
126   issues. To address these limitations, we propose a comprehensive Misattribution Framework and
127   train the judge model to be capable of error attribution.

      We conduct a thorough and in-depth analysis of the error responses associated with LLMs (Zhang
139
      et al., 2023a; Lyu et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023a; Kamoi et al., 2024b) and propose a detailed and
140
      systematic Misattribution Framework. This framework consists of 9 primary categories and 19 sec-
141   ondary categories, effectively capturing the current limitations of LLMs across various application
142   scenarios. The categories are illustrated in Figure 12. The primary categories encompass critical

145   ity (Bhargava & Ng, 2022), Comprehension Ability (Shi et al., 2023), Creative Ability (Ismayilzada
146   et al., 2024), Safety (Qiu et al., 2023), Multi-Turn Dialogue (Yi et al., 2024) and Other Errors. De-
147   tailed explanations of these categories are provided in Table 1 and the detailed examples can be
148   referenced in Figure 9, 10 and 11.

      Annotation Workflow To ensure consistency and accuracy in the annotation process, the annota-
197
      tors should first familiarize themselves with the specific guidelines for each section of the dataset:
198
      score, misattribution, and feedback. As illustrated in Figure 2 (I), the annotators’ task is to read the
199   question, reference answer, and model answer, and then annotate accordingly. Firstly, scores range
200   from 0 to 3 points: 3 point is awarded if the model provides a correct answer without any errors; 2
201   point is given if the answer is partially correct; 1 point is assigned if the answer is completely incor-
202   rect; and 0 point is given if the model provides an off-topic response or violates safety guidelines.
203   Inspired by the score setting of Lin et al. (2024), we have chosen this distribution of scores, which
204   provides a clear and precise representation of the quality of responses corresponding to each score.
205   For misattribution, if the score is less than 3 point, the annotators need to identify the types of error in
206   the model answer referring to Misattribution Framework. The specific Misattribution Framework is
207   described in Section 3.1.If the score is 3 point, the misattribution is marked as NULL. For feedback,
208   we are inspired by (Kim et al., 2023b) and use GPT-4 to generate the feedback. The generated
209   template is shown in Figure 6.

      Dataset Statistics The dataset consists of 22,832 samples, of which 14,295 are satisfactory with-
      out misattribution, and 8,537 with misattribution, 2,031 of which are multi-label. The vast majority
255
      of the AttriData is in Chinese, with an additional 1,321 data in English. The training set contains
256
      19,835 samples, while the testing set contains 2,997 samples. Detailed statistics are presented in
257   Table 2. Unlike previous datasets, AttriData is distinguished by the inclusion of samples with mis-
258   attribution, a feature that has not been addressed before. The information about the amount of mis-
259   attribution data is shown in Table 7. Furthermore, the labels of score and misattribution in AttriData
260   have been manually annotated, different from other benchmarks, which are generated by LLMs.
261   Annotation generated by LLMs is susceptible to limitations and unreliability due to the inherent
262   constraints of the models themselves, whereas our manual annotation offers a substantial advantage
263   in terms of reliability.
264
265   Dataset Quality Given that the batch annotation method we developed ensures a certain degree
266   of annotation accuracy, we further assess the level of agreement among multiple annotators. Specif-
267   ically, we compute Fleiss’ kappa (Moons & Vandervieren, 2023) to evaluate the consistency in
268   labeling the scores and misattributions of the data. The resulting kappa values are 0.875 and 0.832,
269   respectively, suggesting that our annotations can be regarded as almost perfect agreement (Landis,
      1977).

272   We utilize AttriData to fine-tune Qwen2-7B and obtain MisAttributionLLM, equipping it with the

274   2023a; Yao et al., 2024), our fine-tuning process involves sequentially generating feedback, identi-
275   fying misattribution, and then assigning a score. Figure 2 (II) illustrates the supervised fine-tuning
276   process. Utilizing the AttriData training dataset, we fine-tuned Qwen2 to attain the MisAttribution-
277   LLM. For inference, as depicted in Figure 2 (III), given a instruction, a question, a model answer
278   text, and a reference text, the objective is to produce a comprehensive result including a rating score,
279
      a misattribution, and feedback. The detailed prompt utilized can be found in Figure 7 and 8 for
      English and Chinese respectively. For all LLMs, we use uniform prompt. The details of fine-tuning
280
      and inference procedures are provided in Section 4.2.

      The Performance of Error Attribution To evaluate the performance of error attribution in LLMs,
365   we measure from two perspectives: the detection of misattribution and the multi-classification of
366   misattribution. For misattribution detection, this refers to whether the judge model correctly deter-
367   mines that there is an error in the model response. For multi-classification of misattribution, this
368   refers to the process of error attribution (i.e. whether the error is correctly categorized). For misat-
369   tribution detection, we adopt precision, recall, and F1 score to assess the performance of the judge
370   models. For multi-classification of misattribution, we use accuracy and micro-F1 score (Harbecke
371   et al., 2022) to evaluate the capability of the judge models.
372   The results of the error detection and the multi-classification of misattribution are detailed in Table 4.

      To further investigate the impact of misattribution on MisAttributionLLM in scoring setting, we

458   MisAttributionLLM has a minor decrease but is not significantly affected in the absence of misattri-
459   bution. The results are consistent with our hypothesis that misattribution plays an auxiliary role in
460   evaluating the model in scoring setting.

520   6     C ONCLUSION
521
522   In this paper, we construct a comprehensive Misattribution Framework with 9 primary and 19 sec-
523   ondary categories, designed to promote thorough analysis and optimize the performance of LLMs.
524   Based on this framework, we present AttriData, a high-quality dataset with misattributions along-
525   side scores and feedback. We also propose MisAttributionLLM, an innovative open-source, general-
526   purpose large language model with the capability of error attribution, providing insights into model
527   weaknesses and enabling targeted improvements. Extensive experiments and analyses are conducted
528
      to confirm the effectiveness and robustness of our proposed method. We believe that this work will
      contribute to advancing the evaluation and analysis of LLMs.
529
530
531   7     L IMITATIONS
532
533
      Our work still has some limitations: The feedback in AttriData is generated by GPT-4, which is
534   renowned for its high performance in feedback generation (Kim et al., 2023b). However, this also
535   implies that the quality of the feedback in MisAttributionLLM is inherently constrained by the capa-
536   bility of GPT-4. This dependency is an important factor to consider when interpreting the results and
537   assessing the effectiveness of our method. Moreover, due to the limitations in time and resources,
538   we mainly focus on the Chinese dataset, but we are confident that the effectiveness of Misattribution
539   Framework is not language-specific. We leave the further investigation of the English dataset on
      Misattribution Framework as important future work.

      A    R ELATED W ORK OF E VALUATION M ETHOD
757
758
      Before the advent of LLMs, traditional evaluation methods for assessing machine-generated text
759   involved both model-free and model-based metrics. The former refers to metrics that compare the
760   output to a reference text, with BLEU (Papineni et al., 2002) and ROUGE (Lin, 2004) being the
761   most commonly used. However, Krishna et al. (2021) highlighted the shortcomings of reference-
762   based metrics like ROUGE, noting their unreliability for effective evaluation. Recently, there has
763   been a shift towards model-based evaluation methods, including BERTScore (Zhang et al., 2019),
764   BLEURT (Sellam et al., 2020), and BARTScore (Yuan et al., 2021), which focus on capturing se-
765   mantic meaning rather than solely assessing lexical similarities. These are traditional evaluation
766   methods, yet they are not optimally equipped to evaluate the complexity of large language models.
767
768   B    E VALUATION METRICS
769
770         • Pearson is a measure of the linear correlation between two variables, which measures the
771           strength and direction of the linear relationship between the two variables.
772         • Spearman is a nonparametric statistical measure designed to assess the strength and direc-
773           tion of the monotonic relationship between two variables.
774
            • Kendall-Tau is a nonparametric statistical method used to assess the correlation between
775
              two variables, especially when the variables are categorical.
776
777
            • micro-F1 calculates the harmonic mean of precision and recall by considering the contri-
              butions of each prediction equally, regardless of the class.

888                    portion of them.

892                  – Incorrect Answers: This primarily refers to objective questions where the response does
893                    not match the correct answer.
894             • Reasoning Capability
895                  – Process Error: This occurs when there are logical flaws in the reasoning process.
896                  – Result Error:Errors in the final outcomes of reasoning, particularly in disciplines like
897                    mathematics and coding.

899
                     – Reference Error: There is a failure to understand the content that reference pronouns refer
900                    to.
