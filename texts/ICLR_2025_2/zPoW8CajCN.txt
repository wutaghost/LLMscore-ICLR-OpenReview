009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) have emerged as powerful tools for learning on
012            graph-structured data, but they struggle to balance local and global information pro-
013            cessing. While graph Transformers aim to address these issues, they often neglect
014            the inherent locality of Message Passing Neural Networks (MPNNs). Inspired by
015            the fractal nature of real-world networks, we propose a novel concept, ‘fractal
016            nodes’, that addresses the limitations of both MPNN and graph Transformer. The
017            approach draws insights from renormalization techniques to design a message-
018            passing scheme that captures both local and global structural information. Our
019
               method enforces feature self-similarity into nodes by creating fractal nodes that
               coexist with the original nodes. Fractal nodes adaptively summarize subgraph
020
               information and are integrated into MPNN. We show that fractal nodes alleviate an
021
               over-squashing problem by providing direct shortcuts to pass fractal information
022            over long distances. Experiments show that our method achieves comparable or
023            better performance to the graph Transformers while maintaining the computational
024            efficiency of MPNN by improving the long-range dependencies of MPNN.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      GNNs have emerged as powerful tools for learning on graph-structured data, in various domains such
030
      as social network analysis, molecular property prediction, and recommendation systems (Defferrard
031
      et al., 2016; Veličković et al., 2018; Chen et al., 2020a; Chamberlain et al., 2021). At the core of
032   this field lies the MPNN (Gilmer et al., 2017), which iteratively propagates information between
033   neighboring nodes. Recent research has focused on addressing the limitations of MPNN, such as
034   over-smoothing (Nt & Maehara, 2019) and over-squashing (Alon & Yahav, 2021). To overcome
035   these challenges, Transformer architectures (Vaswani et al., 2017) have been introduced to the graph
036   learning community, applying self-attention mechanisms to enable long-range interactions by treating
037   all nodes as tokens (Dwivedi & Bresson, 2021; Wu et al., 2021; Kreuzer et al., 2021b). While graph
038   Transformers have shown promise in capturing global information, they often neglect the inherent
039   locality of MPNNs (Xing et al., 2024). Although approaches such as GraphGPS (Rampášek et al.,
040
      2022) attempt to combine MPNN and Transformer node representations to balance local and global
      information, the computational complexity of Transformers remains a challenge.
041
042
043   Motivation. The limitations of both MPNN and graph Transformers motivate us to seek a novel
044   approach that balances local and global information processing while maintaining computational effi-
045
      ciency. Our inspiration comes from the fractal nature (Mandelbrot, 1983) of real-world networks (Dill
      et al., 2002; Kim & Kahng, 2010; Chen et al., 2020b). This fractality exhibits self-similarity over dif-
046
      ferent scales, meaning that parts of the network resemble the whole. We approach this self-similarity
047
      from two perspectives – the structural aspect, where structural patterns repeat across scales, and
048   the feature aspect, where we aim to enable consistent feature patterns on different network scales.
049   In fractal network analysis, a popular technique is renormalization (see Fig. 1(a)), which involves
050   replacing groups of nodes with “super-nodes” to study how network properties change in scales (Song
051   et al., 2005). The fractality properties and the concept of renormalization motivate us to ask: “Can we
052   design a message passing scheme inspired by fractal geometry and renormalization that effectively
053   captures both local and global structural information in graphs?” Our answer is “yes,” and we
      introduce our main idea.

       Main idea: fractal nodes for enforcing self-similarity. We propose a novel concept called a
      ‘fractal node’, inspired by fractal nature and the renormalization process. Drawing on our perspective
070
       of self-similarity, this approach aims to reflect the characteristics of larger structures in individual
071
       network nodes while enforcing feature self-similarity, thus promoting efficient information flow.
072    Unlike renormalization, which replaces node groups with single nodes, our method partitions the
073    given graph into multiple subgraphs and creates fractal nodes for each subgraph that coexist with the
074    original nodes (see Fig. 1(b)). These fractal nodes represent the information of each subgraph while
075    maintaining connections to the original structure.
076
      By incorporating subgraph features into each node within the given subgraph through direct connec-
077
      tions with fractal nodes, our approach enables smaller units (nodes) to reflect the properties of larger
078   units (subgraphs), effectively enforcing feature self-similarity into the nodes. Specifically, we achieve
079   this by adaptively combining low-frequency (global) and high-frequency (local) components of node
080   features within each subgraph, where the low-frequency component captures common subgraph
081   features while a learnable parameter controls the contribution of high-frequency variations. This
082   process of combining node-specific features with subgraph characteristics enables the seamless
083   integration of fractal nodes into existing MPNNs. In addition, we ensure that the hidden vectors
084   of fractal nodes and original nodes are in the same latent space using the same MPNN layer. This
085   approach allows for the simultaneous consideration of local and global information while maintaining
086
      computational efficiency. Each fractal node adaptively summarizes the information of nodes within
      its corresponding subgraph, going beyond mean pooling to capture subgraph-level characteristics.
087
088   Assigning fractal node to each subgraph contributes to mitigate over-squashing problem. Each
089   fractal node has direct first-order connections to every node within the corresponding subgraph,
090   while preserving the rich node features aggregated across all nodes. This direct connection between
091   the fractal node and the orignal nodes serves as a shortcut pathway to facilitate the propagation
092
      of the information across multi-hop distances, which has been considered as the primary cause of
      over-squashing (Alon & Yahav, 2021). Additionally, we apply an MLP-Mixer (Tolstikhin et al., 2021)
093
      at the last layer to flexibly mix the representations of fractal nodes. This enables inter-subgraph long
094
      range interactions to globally exchange the subgraph context without passing through multiple edges
095   with potential risk of singal degradation as depth grows.
096
097   Contributions. We introduce a novel paradigm, fractal nodes1 , for better propagation by enforcing
098   self-similarity at the subgraph level into individual nodes. Our main contributions are as follows:
099
100   • We propose fractal nodes, which can be integrated into MPNNs, inspired by the fractal nature of
101     networks (Section 3) and discuss the properties of our fractal nodes (Section 4).
102   • We theoretically and empirically show that fractal nodes alleviate the over-squashing problem
103     (Section 5.1) and improve the expressive power over MPNN (Section 5.2).
104
      • Our experiments on various benchmark datasets demonstrate that MPNNs augmented with fractal
105
        nodes achieve performance comparable to or better than state-of-the-art graph Transformer-based
106     models (Section 5.3), while maintaining computational efficiency (Section 5.4).

      Limitations of MPNNs. In several studies, MPNN has been investigated for its expressive power
123
      limitations and over-squashing problems. Simple MPNN is only as powerful as the 1-Weisfeler-
124
      Leman graph isomorphism test (Xu et al., 2019b). The over-squashing problem occurs when MPNNs
125   struggle to propagate information along long paths, resulting in substantial loss of information when
126   aggregating from too many neighbors into a fixed-sized node feature vector (Alon & Yahav, 2021;
127   Di Giovanni et al., 2023). In such scenarios, local information spreading along the natural graph
128   circuits is insufficient to fully capture the local and global context of the graph. This leads to the
129   emergence of graph Transformers that use self-attention, thereby solving the over-squashing problem
130   of self-attention with its “everything is connected to everything”.
131
132   Graph Transformers. Because of successes of Transformers in natural language process-
133   ing (Vaswani et al., 2017), and computer vision (Zhou et al., 2021; Touvron et al., 2021), many
134   previous works have attempted to bring Transformer architecture to the graph domain (Dwivedi &
135
      Bresson, 2021; Müller et al., 2023). Dwivedi & Bresson (2021) proposed the use of graph Laplacian
      eigenvectors as node positional encodings. Subsequent research has explored various strategies to
136
      enhance graph Transformer performance. Rampášek et al. (2022) proposed a general framework,
137
      GraphGPS, that combine MPNN and graph Transformer including self-attentions and positional
138   or structure encoding. Ying et al. (2021) proposed Graphormer that uses attention mechanisms to
139   estimate several types of encoding, such as centrality, spatial, and edge endodings. Wu et al. (2021)
140   applies the MPNN directly to all nodes and then applies a Transformer, which is computationally
141   intensive. He et al. (2023) generalize ViT Dosovitskiy et al. (2021) to graphs and Ma et al. (2023)
142   show that adding inductive biases to graph Transformers removes the need for MPNN modules in
143   GraphGPS. Exphormer improves GraphGPS by using self-attention on expander graphs (Shirzad
144   et al., 2023).
145   One common belief of the advantage of the graph Transformer over MPNN is its capacity in capturing
146   long-range interactions while alleviating over-squashing in MPNN (Alon & Yahav, 2021; Di Giovanni
147   et al., 2023). While graph Transformers have shown promise in addressing the limitations of MPNNs,
148   they often come at the cost of increased computational complexity, typically scaling from O(|E|)
149   to O(|N |2 ), where |E| is the number of edges and |N | is the number of nodes. This computational
150   burden calls for more efficient architectures that can capture global information without the full
151   quadratic cost of attention mechanisms.
152
153
      Augmented MPNNs. To improve information flow and address the limitations of standard MPNNs,
      various strategies have been proposed (Di Giovanni et al., 2023; Shi et al., 2023; Choi et al., 2024).
154
      One approach involves incorporating additional global graph features during the representation
155
      learning process (Gilmer et al., 2017; Hu et al., 2020). Another effective method is rewiring the
156   input graph to enhance connectivity and alleviate structural bottlenecks (Gasteiger et al., 2019; Black
157   et al., 2023; Karhadkar et al., 2023; Nguyen et al., 2023). These adjustments allow for more effective
158   information flow within the network. Another example of graph augmentation is the virtual node,
159   which adds a new node to the graph to enhance information exchange between all pairs of nodes.
160   This heuristic, introduced by Gilmer et al. (2017), has been observed to improve performance on
161   various tasks. Further analysis by Hwang et al. (2022) and Cai et al. (2023) has explored the role of
      virtual nodes in mitigating under-reaching and over-smoothing issues.

162
      Subgraphs in graph learning. Several works introduce hierarchical clustering and coarsening for
163   learning on graphs (Dong et al., 2023). Chiang et al. (2019) use graph clustering to identify well-
164   connected subgraphs on large graphs. HC-GNN (Zhong et al., 2023) shows competitive performance
165   in node classification on large-scale graphs, using hierarchical community structures for message
166   passing. In graph Transformers, several hierarchical models (Zhao et al., 2022; Gao et al., 2022;
167   Zhu et al., 2023; He et al., 2023) attempt to manage computational complexity, though they still face
168   challenges with scalability as all nodes remain within the computational burden of the Transformer
169   architecture. However, our approach, the incorporation of fractal nodes to MPNN, can reduce this
170   computational cost while preserving structural information.
171
172   Fractality and self-similarity in networks. The concept of fractals, introduced by Mandelbrot
173
      (1983), transformed our understanding of complex, irregular structures in nature by revealing self-
      similarity across different scales. This insight has since been applied to various fields, including
174
      network science, where many real-world networks have been shown to exhibit fractal structures and
175
      scale-free properties (Song et al., 2005; Kim et al., 2007; Fronczak et al., 2024). For instance, social
176   networks, the World Wide Web, and even protein interaction networks have been found to have fractal
177   properties (Chen et al., 2020b).
178
179
      In our work, we define fractality as the degree to which subgraph properties resemble those of the
      entire graph when consistently partitioned. While traditional fractal analysis (e.g., renormalization
180
      techniques) commonly uses box-covering algorithms (Kim et al., 2007), we bring this concept to the
181
      constraints of benchmark datasets where absolute node positions are unknown. Instead, we construct
182   subgraphs through graph partitioning.

      Expressive power of fractal nodes. The expressive power of fractal nodes can be understood
      through the lens of existing theoretical results on subgraph-based approaches. The methods have
344
      been shown to increase expressive power beyond MPNNs. Encoding local subgraphs is stronger
345
      than 1-WL and 2-WL tests (Zhao et al., 2022, Theorem 4.3). In the context of subgraph WL (SWL)
346   test (Zhang et al., 2023), fractal nodes achieve expressive power comparable to SWL with additional
347   single-point aggregation and potentially approach SWL with additional global aggregation (Zhang
348   et al., 2023, Theorem 4.4), as the fractal nodes implicitly perform a form of global aggregation within
349   each subgraph. We will empirically verify expressive power in Section 5.2.
350
351   4.2   M ODEL C OMPLEXITY
352
353   Our fractal nodes show improvements in computational efficiency compared to Transformer-based
354   models such as graph Transformers (Dwivedi & Bresson, 2021) and GraphGPS (Rampášek et al.,
355
      2022). The time complexity of our FN method is O(L(|V| + |E|)), where L is the number of layers,
      |V| is the number of nodes, and |E| is the number of edges. The FNM introduces an additional mixing
356
      step through the MLP-Mixer, leading to a time complexity of O(L(|V| + |E|) + Cd2 ). C is the
357
      number of subgraphs and d is the hidden dimension. Given that C is much smaller than |V|, this
358   term does not dominate the overall complexity, preserving the efficiency of the model. In contrast,
359   graph Transformers incur a time complexity of O(L(|V|2 )), due to the quadratic cost of computing
360   self-attention over all node pairs, which is expensive for large graphs. Similary, GraphGPS combines
361   MPNNs with self-attention, resulting in comparable quadratic complexity O(L(|V|2 )). Thus, fractal
362   nodes offer a computational advantage over graph Transformer-based methods.
363
364   4.3   C OMPARISON WITH P RIOR W ORK
365
366   Comparison to graph coarsening methods. Coarformer (Kuang et al., 2022) tries to use coarsened
367
      and original graphs as separate views, where the coarsened graph is input to the Transformer, while
      ANS-GT (Cai et al., 2021) feeds a sequence of node representations to the graph Transformer
368
      by combining original, global, and coarsened node representations formed via adaptive sampling.
369
      Our method, on the other hand, incorporates fractal nodes representing subgraph information into
370   the MPNN and enables fractal nodes to exchange messages with the original nodes and exchange
371   information between fractal nodes via MLP-Mixer.
372
373   Comparison to virtual node. If we do not split into subgraphs, there will be only one fractal node.
374   This can be compared to a virtual node (Gilmer et al., 2017; Hwang et al., 2022; Cai et al., 2023),
375   which is known to have the information of a global node. While both approaches facilitate global or
376   subgraph-level global information exchange, the key difference lies in how they process information.
377   Virtual nodes aggregate global information from the entire graph, whereas fractal nodes operate at a
      subgraph level. A virtual node has its own update and aggregation functions that process messages


                                                        7
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


378
      from all graph nodes, while regular nodes incorporate both their local neighborhood messages and
379   the virtual node’s message. In contrast, our fractal nodes adaptively decompose and process both
380   low and high frequency components of subgraph features. This allows fractal nodes to capture richer
381   information at the subgraph level compared to virtual node implementations that typically aggregate
382   global information.

527   6     C ONCLUDING R EMARK
528
529   We introduced the fractal nodes to enforce self-similarity into MPNNs, inspired by the fractal
530
      nature of real-world networks. Our method effectively combines local and global graph information,
      addressing limitations of both MPNNs and graph Transformers. Experimental results on 6 benchmark
531
      datasets show the superiority of our approach, consistently improving the performance of MPNNs
532
      and competing advantageously with state-of-the-art graph Transformers-based methods.
533
534   Limitations and future directions. While fractal nodes are effective, they are currently designed
535   to extend MPNN architectures. Although efficient and widely used, the use of METIS for subgraph
536   partitioning may not be optimal for all types of graphs. While alternative partitioning methods
537   could be used for large-scale graphs, the computational efficiency of METIS limits our options for
538   more computationally intensive partitioning approaches. Future work could explore better ways to
539   construct subgraphs at scale, and it may be worthwhile to investigate extending our fractal nodes in
      ways better suited for graph Transformers.

965   B.1   M ETIS PARTITIONING FOR F RACTAL N ODE C REATION
966
967   To create fractal nodes, we employ METIS (Karypis & Kumar, 1998), a graph clustering algorithm
968
      known for its excellent balance between accuracy and computational efficiency. METIS partitions a
      graph into a pre-defined number of clusters, maximizing within-cluster connections while minimizing
969
      between-cluster links. This approach effectively captures the community structure of the graph.
970
971   However, using non-overlapping partitions could result in the loss of important edge information,
      particularly at the boundaries between partitions. To address this issue and retain all original edges,


                                                        18
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


972
       we introduce overlapping subgraph. After the initial METIS partitioning, we expand each partition to
973    include nodes from neighboring partitions.
974
975    Formally, we first apply METIS to partition a graph G into C non-overlapping subgraphs:
976
       {V1 , . . . , VC } such that V = {V1 ∪ . . . ∪ VC } and Vi ∩ Vj = ∅, ∀i ̸= j, where C is the num-
       ber of fractal nodes or subgraphs. Then, we expand these subgraphs to include k-hop neighborhoods:
977
978                                        Vi ← Vi ∪ {Nk (j)|j ∈ Vi },                                   (19)
979
980    where Nk (j) defines the k-hop neighbourhood of node j. This expansion ensures that each subgraph
981    retains information about its immediate surroundings. The choice of k allows us to control the degree
982
       of overlap between subgraphs. A larger k value increases the overlap, potentially capturing more
       global information but at the cost of increased computational complexity. This overlapping subgraph
983
       approach allows our fractal nodes to capture both local structural details and broader subgraph-level
984
       information, enhancing the model’s ability to learn multi-scale representations of the graph structure.

1036   C     S TRUCTURAL S ELF -S IMILARITY AND N ODE C ENTRALITY
1037
1038   In this section, we describe how we calculate the self-similarity of a network by comparing the node
1039   centrality distributions between the original graph and its subgraphs using betweenness centrality.
1040   Specifically, we use the Kolmogorov-Smirnov (KS) test to measure the similarity between these
1041
       distributions.
1042
       Fractality definition. We define the fractality of a graph as the degree to which the properties of
1043
       the subgraphs resemble those of the original graph when the graph is partitioned consistently. In
1044
       this work, we focus on how the betweenness centrality distribution of the original graph compares to
1045   those of its subgraphs.
1046
1047   Let Ψ(x) represent the node centrality distribution function for the original graph, and let
1048
       Ψ0 (x), . . . , Ψ32 (x) represent the centrality distributions for each of the subgraphs obtained by
       partitioning the original graph into 32 subgraphs. We aim to quantify the similarity between Ψ(x)
1049
       and the subgraph distributions using the KS test.
1050
1051
       Kolmogorov-Smirnov test. The KS test is a non-parametric test that compares the empirical
1052
       cumulative distribution function (CDF) Ψn (x) of the sample (subgraph centrality) with the CDF
1053   Ψ(x) of the reference distribution (original graph centrality). The KS test statistic D is defined as:
1054
1055
                                                D = sup |Ψn (x) − Ψ(x)|,                                  (23)
                                                       x
1056
       where D represents the maximum distance between the two CDFs. A smaller D value indicates
1057
       higher similarity between the two distributions.
1058
1059   Similarity metric. We define the similarity between the original graph and a subgraph as 1 − D,
1060   where D is the KS test statistic. Therefore, a higher 1 − D value implies greater similarity. For each
1061   graph, we compute the similarity for all C subgraphs, yielding C similarity values.
1062
1063   Fractality calculation. In our fractality evaluation, it is sufficient to identify the subgraph whose
1064   centrality distribution is most similar to that of the original graph. This is because not all subgraphs
1065   need to exhibit self-similarity for the graph to be considered fractal-like; the presence of one or more
1066   highly similar subgraphs is indicative of fractality. Thus, we take the maximum of the C similarity
1067   values (1 − D) as the self-similarity score for the graph:
1068                                     Self-Similarity Score = max(1 − Di ),                            (24)
                                                                    i
1069
1070   where Di is the KS test statistic for the i-th subgraph. This approach allows us to compute a self-
1071   similarity score for a single graph based on betweenness centrality. The comparison according to the
1072
       number of subgraphs is shown in Fig. 6.

1383   Effective resistance and signal propagation. Derived from the field of electrical engineering, the
1384   effective resistance between two nodes u and v in an electrical network is defined as the potential
1385   difference induced across the edges when a unit current is injected at one of each end (Ghosh et al.,
1386   2008). Intuitively, it provides a physical measure of the ease of signal flow from one end to the other.
1387
       Rayleigh’s monotonicity principle, which says that adding paths or shortening existing paths can
       only decrease the effective resistance between two nodes (Thomassen, 1990), leads to the following
1388
       interpretation: more and shorter disjoint paths connecting the nodes u and v lead to a lower resistance
1389
       between them (Black et al., 2023; Devriendt & Lambiotte, 2022). Therefore, edges with higher
1390   effective resistance have fewer alternative paths or shortcuts for signals passing through that edge and
1391   thus, struggle to propagate information, causing bottlenecks. The total effective resistance Rtot , the
1392   sum of the effective resistance among all pairs of nodes (see Equation (26)), is a key measure for
1393   measuring the overall degree of over-squashing across a graph.
1394
1395   Total effective resistance. The resistance between nodes u and v in the graph is given by

       H    C ONNECTION TO R ENORMALIZATION T ECHNIQUES
1621
1622
       Our fractal nodes method draws inspiration from renormalization group techniques in physics,
1623
       where complex systems are analyzed on different scales. While this connection is conceptual, the
1624
       fundamental idea of scale transformation provides intuition for our approach. The renormalization
1625   involves replacing groups of interacting components with effective units. Similarly, our fractal nodes
1626   summarize subgraph information, though we maintain these summary units as fractal nodes alongside
1627   the original graph structure rather than replacing them.
1628
       From a complex network perspective, fractal nodes facilitate a transition from scale-free fractal
1629
       networks to small-world networks. Similar to the renormalization techniques described by Wei
1630
       et al. (2013), our FNM method introduces long-range interactions between the fractal nodes, giving
1631   small-world properties to the network (Albert & Barabási, 2002). We extend beyond renormalization
1632   in 3 aspects: (i) preserving the original structure while adding fractal nodes, (ii) enabling adaptive
1633   information flow through learned parameters, and (iii) maintaining exchange between local and global
1634   scales.
1635
       This architecture enables efficient information propagation through several mechanisms. The fractal
1636
       nodes act as “shortcuts” in the network, reducing the effective distance information must traverse.
1637   Maintaining local and summarized representations enables simultaneous processing at multiple scales
1638   while preserving local network characteristics. This multi-scale processing capability addresses the
1639   over-squashing problem by facilitating efficient global information flow without sacrificing local
1640   structural information.
1641
       The key differences between our approach and classical renormalization highlight the factors we
1642
       introduce specifically for graph learning tasks. While traditional renormalization uses fixed transfor-
1643   mation rules in a unidirectional manner (fine to coarse), our method learns adaptive representations
1644   through trainable parameters and enables bidirectional information exchange. This creates a more
1645   flexible framework that captures complex relationships in graph-structured data while maintaining
1646   computational efficiency.
