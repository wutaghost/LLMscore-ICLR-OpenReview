                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009            We present Bayesian Binary Search (BBS), a novel probabilistic variant of
010            the classical binary search/bisection algorithm. BBS leverages machine learn-
011            ing/statistical techniques to estimate the probability density of the search space
012            and modifies the bisection step to split based on probability density rather than the
013            traditional midpoint, allowing for the learned distribution of the search space to
014            guide the search algorithm. Search space density estimation can flexibly be per-
015            formed using supervised probabilistic machine learning techniques (e.g., Gaussian
016            process regression, Bayesian neural networks, quantile regression) or unsuper-
017
               vised learning algorithms (e.g., Gaussian mixture models, kernel density estima-
               tion (KDE), maximum likelihood estimation (MLE)). We demonstrate significant
018
               efficiency gains of using BBS on both simulated data across a variety of distribu-
019
               tions and in a real-world binary search use case of probing channel balances in the
020            Bitcoin Lightning Network, for which we have deployed the BBS algorithm in a
021            production setting.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
024
025   The concept of organizing data for efficient searching has ancient roots. One of the earliest known
026   examples is the Inakibit-Anu tablet from Babylon (c. 200 BCE), which contained approximately 500
027   sorted sexagesimal numbers and their reciprocals, facilitating easier searches Knuth (1998). Similar
028   sorting techniques were evident in name lists discovered on the Aegean Islands. The Catholicon,
029
      a Latin dictionary completed in 1286 CE, marked a significant advance by introducing rules for
      complete alphabetical classification Knuth (1998).
030
031   The documented modern era of search algorithms began in 1946 when John Mauchly first men-
032   tioned binary search during the seminal Moore School Lectures Knuth (1998). This was followed
033   by William Wesley Peterson’s introduction of interpolation search in 1957 Peterson (1957). A lim-
034   itation of early binary search algorithms was their restriction to arrays with lengths one less than a
035   power of two. This constraint was overcome in 1960 by Derrick Henry Lehmer, who published a
036   generalized binary search algorithm applicable to arrays of any length Lehmer (1960). A significant
037
      optimization came in 1962 when Hermann Bottenbruch presented an ALGOL 60 implementation
      of binary search that moved the equality comparison to the end of the process Bottenbruch (1962).
038
      While this increased the average number of iterations by one, it reduced the number of comparisons
039
      per iteration to one, potentially improving efficiency. Further refinements to binary search contin-
040   ued, with A. K. Chandra of Stanford University developing the uniform binary search in 1971 Knuth
041   (1998). This variant aimed to provide more consistent performance across different input distribu-
042   tions. In the realm of computational geometry, Bernard Chazelle and Leonidas J. Guibas introduced
043   fractional cascading in 1986 Chazelle & Guibas (1986). This technique provided a method to solve
044   various search problems efficiently in geometric contexts, broadening the application of search al-
045   gorithms beyond simple sorted lists. More recently, (Mohammed et al., 2021), proposed a hybrid
046   search algorithm combining binary search and interpolated search to optimize search on datasets
047   with non-uniform distributions.
048   The bisection method, a fundamental technique in numerical analysis, shares conceptual similarities
049   with binary search. This method, also known as the interval halving method, binary chopping, or
050   Bolzano’s method, has origins of the bisection method can be traced back to ancient mathematics.
051   However, its formal description is often attributed to Bolzano’s work in the early 19th century Russ
052   (2004). Bolzano’s theorem, which guarantees the existence of a root in a continuous function that
053   changes sign over an interval, provides the theoretical basis for the bisection method. In the context
      of root-finding algorithms, Burden & Faires (2015) provide a comprehensive treatment of the bisec-

054
      tion method, discussing its convergence properties and error bounds. They highlight the method’s
055   robustness and guaranteed convergence, albeit at a relatively slow linear rate. Variants of the bi-
056   section method have been developed to improve efficiency and applicability. Horstein (1963) first
057   introduced probabilistic bisection. Hansen & Walster (1991) employed interval analysis and bisec-
058   tion to develop reliable methods for finding global optima. Their work demonstrates how bisection
059   principles can be extended beyond simple root-finding to more complex optimization problems.
060
      The connection between bisection and binary search in computer science was explored by Knuth
061   (1998), who discussed both methods in the context of searching and optimization algorithms. This
062   connection underlies our approach in leveraging probabilistic techniques to enhance binary search.
063   Recent work has focused on adapting bisection methods to handle uncertainty and noise. Waeber
064   et al. (2013) introduced a probabilistic bisection algorithm for noisy root-finding problems. Their
065   method, which updates a probability distribution over the root location based on noisy measure-
066   ments, shares conceptual similarities with our Bayesian Binary Search approach. In the context of
067   stochastic optimization, Jedynak et al. (2012) developed a probabilistic bisection algorithm for bi-
068   nary classification problems. Their work demonstrates how Bayesian updating can be incorporated
069
      into the bisection process, providing a precedent for our probabilistic approach to binary search.
      The bisection method has also found applications in various fields beyond pure mathematics. For
070
      instance, Nievergelt (1964) discussed the use of parallel bisection methods in scientific computing,
071
      highlighting the potential for algorithmic improvements through parallelization.
072
073   Probabilistic machine learning, as described by Ghahramani (2015), provides a framework for rea-
074   soning about uncertainty in data and models. Gaussian Processes (GPs), a cornerstone of many prob-
075
      abilistic machine learning methods, were extensively explored by Rasmussen & Williams (2006).
      GPs offer a flexible, non-parametric approach to modeling distributions over functions, making them
076
      particularly suitable for tasks involving uncertainty quantification. In recent years, Bayesian deep
077
      learning has emerged as a powerful approach to combining the expressiveness of deep neural net-
078   works with principled uncertainty quantification. Wang & Yeung (2016) provide a comprehensive
079   survey of Bayesian deep learning techniques, discussing various methods for incorporating uncer-
080   tainty into deep neural networks and their applications in complex, high-dimensional problems. The
081   field of Bayesian Optimization, which shares conceptual similarities with our work, has seen sub-
082   stantial growth. Shahriari et al. (2015) provide a comprehensive overview of Bayesian Optimization
083   techniques, highlighting their effectiveness in optimizing expensive-to-evaluate functions. These
084   methods typically use GPs, Bayesian Neural Networks (BNNs) or other probabilistic models to
085   guide the optimization process, analogous to our use of probability density estimates in Bayesian
086
      Binary Search.

      2   M ETHODOLOGY
089   2.1 C LASSICAL B INARY S EARCH /B ISECTION
090   Binary search is an efficient algorithm for finding a target value in a defined search space. It repeat-
091   edly divides the search interval in half, eliminating half of the remaining space at each step. The
092   algorithm maintains two boundaries, low and high, and computes the midpoint mid = (low + high)
093   / 2. By evaluating the midpoint and comparing it to the target value, it determines which half of
094   the search space to explore next, updating either low or high accordingly. This process continues
095   until the target is found or the search space is exhausted. The efficiency of binary search stems
096
      from its ability to halve the potential search space with each iteration, resulting in a logarithmic time
      complexity.
097
098   2.2 BAYESIAN B INARY S EARCH (BBS)
099   2.2.1 P ROBLEM F ORMULATION
100
      Given a search space S and a target value t, our aim is to locate t in S, or produce a specified
101
      range for t with a given tolerance. Unlike classical binary search, we assume that the distribution of
102   potential target locations is not uniform across S. We represent this non-uniform distribution using
103   a probability density function (PDF) p(x), where x ∈ S. We formulate the binary search algorithm
104   in two parts: search space density estimation, which produces a PDF that is fed into a modified
105   binary search algorithm which begins at the median of the PDF and bisects in probability density
106   space. BBS is equivalent to basic binary search when the search space PDF estimation process is
107   replaced with the assumption of a uniformly distributed target space. This formulation maps to a
      binary search problem on a sorted array in the following way: the search space S corresponds to the


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      sorted array, the target value t is the element being searched for, and the PDF p(x) represents the
109   probability of finding the target at each index in the array using interpolation.

      The effectiveness of BBS depends on the accuracy of the PDF estimation. Some methods for esti-
      mating p(x) can include:

              • Gaussian Process Regression (GPR): GPR provides a non-parametric way to model the

                PDF, offering both a mean prediction and uncertainty estimates.
121

123           • Bayesian Neural Networks (BNN): BNNs combine the flexibility of neural networks with
124
                Bayesian inference, allowing for uncertainty quantification in the predictions.
125
126           • Quantile Regression: Quantile regression estimates the conditional quantiles of a response
127             variable, providing a more comprehensive view of the relationship between variables across
128             different parts of the distribution, without assuming a particular parametric form for the
129             underlying distribution.

135
                Maximization (EM) algorithm) can model complex, multimodal distributions by repre-
                senting the PDF as a weighted sum of Gaussian components.
136
137
138           • Kernel Density Estimation (KDE): KDE is a non-parametric method for estimating PDFs,
139             which can capture complex shapes without assuming a specific functional form.
140
141           • Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE): MLE is a method of estimating the parameters
142             of a probability distribution by maximizing a likelihood function. It can be used to fit
143             various parametric distributions (e.g., normal, exponential, Poisson) to data, providing a
144             PDF that best explains the observed samples according to the chosen distribution family.

      2.6 E XPERIMENTS ON L IGHTNING N ETWORK C HANNEL P ROBING
325
326
      Probing a channel in the Lightning Network is the construction of a fake payment to be sent through
      the network to obtain information on the balance of a channel. Typically, it is currently performed
327
      using a basic binary search/bisection. The response of a given probe can be viewed as an oracle,
328
      which is a continuous and monotonic function, making it suitable for the bisection method as it
329   guarantees there is only one root of the function.
330
331   Each probe is computationally expensive and fundamentally constrained in this domain due to the
332
      max HTLC (Hashed Timelock Contract) limit (483 on Lightning Network Daemon (LND), the
      most popular Lightning Node implementation) on each channel in the Lightning node software
333
      implementation for security purposes. Probes occupying this limit fundamentally constrain network
334
      throughput, which could otherwise process real payments.
335
336   To demonstrate the computational complexity of executing probes on the Lightning Network, we
337   probed 1500 channels from our Lightning node, with an average hop length of 2.26. The probe
338
      time average for each channel had a mean of 3.1 seconds and a standard deviation of 0.7 seconds.
      The density estimation step per channel here in comparison (random forest inference and KDE)
339
      takes 0.18 seconds, and only needs to be done once per channel. The added overhead of density
340
      estimation here is significantly outweighed by the probing computational cost and the domain max
341   HTLC constraint.
342
343   For search space density estimation in the Lightning Network channel probing experiment, we detail
344
      a random forest model to predict the search target (channel balance in this case). We construct a PDF
      from the prediction of the random forest using kernel density estimation (KDE) on the predictions
345
      of individual predictors (trees) in the ensemble. We alternatively could use Bayesian Neural Net-
346
      works or a Gaussian Process Regressor (results in appendix), but the random forest yielded superior
347   predictive performance in our experiments (Rossi et al., 2024).
348
349   2.6.1 D ESCRIPTION OF C HANNEL BALANCE P REDICTION TASK
350   The Lightning Network can be modeled as a directed graph G = (V, E), where V represents the
351   set of nodes and E represents the set of edges. Each node u and each edge (u, v) have associated
352   features, denoted by xu ∈ Rk for nodes and e(u,v) for edges, which contain specific information about
353   those nodes and edges. Additionally, each edge (u, v) has a scalar value y(u,v) ≥ 0, representing the
354   pre-allocated balance for transactions from u to v.
355   Graph G has the constraint that if an edge exists in one direction, it must also exist in the opposite
356   direction, i.e., (u, v) ∈ E ⇔ (v, u) ∈ E. The set of two edges between any two nodes is called
357   a channel, denoted as {(u, v), (v, u)}. For simplicity, we represent a channel by the set of its two
358   nodes: {u, v}. The total capacity of the channel {u, v} is defined as c{u,v} = y(u,v) + y(v,u) .
359   We are provided with the total channel capacities c{u,v} for all channels in the graph, but we only
360   know the individual values y(u,v) for a subset of edges. Note that knowing y(u,v) allows us to deter-
361   mine y(v,u) , since y(v,u) = c{u,v} − y(u,v) . Therefore, we can focus on predicting y(u,v) .
362
      Moreover, since we are given c{u,v} for all edges, and we know 0 ≤ y(u,v) ≤ c{u,v} , we also have
363
      that y(u,v) = p(u,v) c{u,v} , where 0 ≤ p(u,v) ≤ 1. Intuitively, p(u,v) is the proportion of the channel
364
      capacity which belongs to the (u, v) direction. From this, we see that we focus on predicting p(u,v) ,
365
      and then use it to obtain y(u,v) .
366
367   Therefore, our primary task is to predict p(u,v) for all edges where it is not observed.
368   2.6.2 DATA C OLLECTION AND P REPROCESSING
369

      ning Network and crowdsourced information from nodes in the network. A snapshot of the network

      3     D ISCUSSION
466
467   Our experimental results demonstrate the potential of Bayesian Binary Search (BBS) as a promis-
468   ing alternative to classical binary search/bisection, particularly in scenarios where the search space
469   exhibits non-uniform distributions and is costly to access. The performance improvements observed
470   in both simulated environments and real-world applications, such as probing payment channels in
471
      the Bitcoin Lightning Network, highlight the promise of BBS. However, it is important to consider
      these findings in a broader context and acknowledge the limitations and implications of our work.
472
473   3.1   T HEORETICAL A LIGNMENT AND P ERFORMANCE G AINS
474
      The superior performance of BBS over classical binary search in non-uniform distributions aligns
475
      with our theoretical expectations. By leveraging probabilistic information about the search space,
476
      BBS can make more informed decisions at each step, leading to faster convergence on average.
477   This is particularly evident in our simulated experiments with skewed distributions, where BBS
478   consistently required fewer steps to locate the target. The ability of BBS to adapt to the underlying
479   distribution of the search space represents an advance in the design of search algorithms, and a
480   fusion of search theory and probabilistic machine learning/statistical learning.
481
482   3.2   R EAL -W ORLD A PPLICATION AND I MPLICATIONS
483   In the context of the Bitcoin Lightning Network, the improvement in probing efficiency could have
484   significant practical implications. Faster and more accurate channel capacity discovery can enhance
485   routing algorithms, potentially leading to improved transaction success rates. Crucially, probes
      in the Lightning Network consume network-wide computational resources, as each probe requires


                                                                               9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      processing by multiple nodes along potential payment routes. By using BBS to enhance probe ef-
487   ficiency, we effectively reduce unnecessary network load, akin to removing spam from the system.
488   This reduction in network overhead could lead to improved overall network performance and scala-
489   bility.
490
491   3.3 L IMITATIONS AND C HALLENGES
492   Despite the promising results, several limitations of our study should be acknowledged:
493
           1. Computational Overhead: The improved search efficiency of BBS comes at the cost of
494           increased computational complexity, particularly in the PDF estimation step. This overhead
495           may offset the reduction in search steps for certain applications, especially those dealing
496           with small search spaces or requiring extremely fast operation.
497
           2. PDF Estimation Accuracy: The performance of BBS is heavily dependent on the ac-
498
              curacy of the PDF estimation. In scenarios where the underlying distribution is highly
499
              complex or rapidly changing, the chosen PDF estimation method may struggle to provide
500           accurate probabilities, potentially leading to suboptimal performance.
501
502   3.4 F UTURE R ESEARCH D IRECTIONS
503   Our findings open up several exciting avenues for future research:
504
           1. Adaptive PDF Estimation: Developing methods to dynamically adjust the PDF estima-
505
              tion technique based on the observed search space characteristics could further improve the
506           robustness and efficiency of BBS.
507
508        2. Theoretical Bounds: While we provide empirical evidence of BBS’s effectiveness, deriv-
509
              ing tighter theoretical bounds on its performance under various distribution types would
              strengthen its theoretical foundation.
510
511        3. Application to Other Domains: Exploring the applicability of BBS to other areas such as
512           database indexing, computational biology, or optimization algorithms could reveal new use
513           cases and challenges.
514
      3.5 C ONCLUSION
515
516
      Bayesian Binary Search represents a significant step towards more adaptive and efficient search al-
      gorithms. By incorporating probabilistic information, BBS demonstrates the potential to outperform
517
      classical binary search in non-uniform distributions, as evidenced by our experiments in both sim-
518
      ulated and real-world scenarios. In the context of the Lightning Network, BBS not only improves
519   search efficiency but also contributes to reducing unnecessary network load, potentially enhancing
520   the overall performance and scalability of the system. The promising results open up new possibil-
521   ities for optimizing search processes in various domains. As we continue to navigate increasingly
522   complex and data-rich environments, algorithms like BBS that can adapt to the underlying struc-
523   ture of the problem space will become increasingly valuable. Future work in this direction has the
524   potential to not only improve search efficiency but also to deepen our understanding of how prob-
525   abilistic approaches can enhance fundamental algorithms in computer science and their real-world
526   applications.
