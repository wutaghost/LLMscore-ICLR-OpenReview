011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Ensuring awareness of fairness and privacy in Large Language Models (LLMs) is
014            critical. Interestingly, we discover a counter-intuitive trade-off phenomenon that
015            enhancing an LLM’s privacy awareness through Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT)
016
               methods significantly decreases its fairness awareness with thousands of samples.
               To address this issue, inspired by the information theory, we introduce a training-
017
               free method to DEActivate the fairness and privacy coupled Neurons (DEAN),
018
               which theoretically and empirically decrease the mutual information between
019            fairness and privacy awareness. Extensive experimental results demonstrate that
020            DEAN eliminates the trade-off phenomenon and significantly improves LLMs’ fair-
021            ness and privacy awareness simultaneously, e.g., improving Qwen-2-7B-Instruct’s
022            fairness awareness by 12.2% and privacy awareness by 14.0%. More crucially,
023            DEAN remains robust and effective with limited annotated data or even when
024            only malicious fine-tuning data is available, whereas SFT methods may fail to
025            perform properly in such scenarios. We hope this study provides valuable insights
026            into concurrently addressing fairness and privacy concerns in LLMs and can be
027
               integrated into comprehensive frameworks to develop more ethical and responsible
               AI systems. Our code is provided in the supplementary materials.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      In recent years, as LLMs increasingly permeate sensitive areas such as healthcare, finance, and
      education (Li et al., 2023b; Yuan et al., 2023; Al-Smadi, 2023), concerns regarding their fairness
034
      and privacy implications have become critically important (Liu et al., 2023; Sun et al., 2024a). For
035
      instance, when queried for sensitive information such as a social security number, we would expect
036   the LLM to refuse to provide such information. Similarly, a desirable LLM should avoid producing
037   unfair or discriminatory content, as shown in Figure 1(a).
038
039
      In this paper, we focus on LLMs’ awareness of fairness and privacy concerns, i.e., their ability to
      recognize and appropriately respond to requests involving sensitive information (Li et al., 2024; Sun
040
      et al., 2024a). A well-recognized challenge is the trade-off between addressing fairness and privacy-
041
      related concerns (Bagdasaryan et al., 2019; Mangold et al., 2023; Agarwal, 2021) in traditional Deep
042   Neural Networks (DNNs). As a result, many studies have emerged attempting to reconcile this
043   trade-off, proposing techniques to balance these conflicting objectives (Lyu et al., 2020; Cummings
044   et al., 2019). This prompts us to explore an intriguing question: Does trade-off also exist between the
045   awareness of fairness and privacy in the era of LLMs?
046
      Interestingly, our preliminary experimental results indicate that enhancing privacy awareness through
047
      SFT methods decreases the fairness awareness of LLMs, as shown in Figure 1(b). Specifically, we
048   fine-tune LLMs on limited-data conditions (thousands of samples) with Full-parameter Fine-Tuning
049   (FFT) (Devlin et al., 2019) and Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT) methods (Hu et al., 2022;
050   Liu et al., 2024b; Wu et al., 2024), due to challenges in acquiring large volumes of high-quality
051   fine-tuning data in real-world scenarios (Xu et al., 2024; Sun et al., 2024b). Please see Appendix
052   D for more discussions. Such a trade-off phenomenon can be partially explained by the neuron
053   semantic superposition (Elhage et al., 2022; Bricken et al., 2023; Templeton, 2024), i.e., neurons are
      polysemantic and exist a subset of neurons closely related with both fairness and privacy awareness.

070   In this way, fine-tuning LLMs inadvertently affects these coupled neurons and may introduce a
071   conflicting optimization direction for fairness and privacy, leading to the trade-off phenomenon.
072
      Therefore, an effective operation for decoupling fairness and privacy-related neurons is likely to
      mitigate the above trade-off phenomenon.
073
074   Inspired by the information theory (Ash, 2012; Yang & Zwolinski, 2001) that removing the common
075   components of two variables can reduce their mutual information and thus decouple these variables,
076   we propose a simple and effective method, namely DEAN, to decouple LLMs’ awareness of fairness
077   and privacy by DEActivating the coupled Neurons (Figure 1(c)). Specifically, we first identify a sparse
078
      set of neurons closely related to fairness and privacy awareness, respectively. Then, the intersection
      of these two sets of neurons can be considered as coupled neurons. In this way, deactivating
079
      these coupled neurons decouples the awareness of fairness and privacy, i.e., decreasing the mutual
080
      information between fairness-related and privacy-related representations. The decreasing mutual
081   information potentially mitigates the trade-off phenomenon.
082
083   Extensive experimental results demonstrate the advantages of training-free DEAN. Firstly, DEAN
084
      can simultaneously improve both fairness and privacy awareness of the LLM without compromising
      the LLM’s general capabilities, e.g., improving the Qwen2-7B-Instruct’s (Yang et al., 2024a) fairness
085
      awareness by 12.2% and privacy awareness by 14.0%. Secondly, training-free DEAN performs
086
      effectively under limited annotated data conditions, e.g., a few hundred data samples, thereby reducing
087   the reliance on extensive annotation and computational resources.
088
089   Notably, DEAN maintains strong performance even when only malicious fine-tuning data (e.g.,
090
      unfair queries with unfair responses) is available, whereas previous studies (Qi et al., 2024; Yang
      et al., 2024b; Halawi et al., 2024) have shown that using such data for fine-tuning could significantly
091
      degrade model performance. These effectivenesses are attributed to the focus on identifying and
092
      deactivating relevant neurons rather than directing the model to learn from the dialogue data via
093   fine-tuning, which also enjoys better interpretability. We do not expect that DEAN alone can fully
094   address fairness and privacy concerns in LLMs without FFT and SFT methods. In contrast, we
095   consider that DEAN can be flexibly integrated into a comprehensive framework to further contribute
096   to the development of more ethical and responsible AI systems in the era of LLMs.

      Fairness and privacy-related concerns in DNNs. The concerns surrounding fairness and privacy
101
      in deep neural networks (DNNs) have garnered significant attention in recent years (Mehrabi et al.,
102   2021; Caton & Haas, 2024; Mireshghallah et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2020). Fairness research spans
103   various topics (Verma & Rubin, 2018), including but not limited to individual fairness (Dwork
104   et al., 2012; Kusner et al., 2017), which emphasizes treating similar individuals similarly; and group
105   fairness (Dwork et al., 2012; Kusner et al., 2017), which aims to ensure that different demographic
106   groups receive equal treatment. In privacy, topics such as differential privacy (Dwork et al., 2006;
107   Mireshghallah et al., 2020), which ensures that the removal or addition of a single individual’s
      data does not significantly affect the output of the model; and membership inference resistance

      (Shokri et al., 2017; Mireshghallah et al., 2020), which prevents attackers from determining whether a
109   particular data instance was part of the training set, are widely explored. While traditional DNNs are
110   primarily designed for discriminative tasks, LLMs focus more on open-ended generative scenarios
111   in various real-world applications, which shifts the emphasis on fairness and privacy concerns. As
112   mentioned before, we emphasize LLMs’ awareness of fairness and privacy, where a more formal
113   definition can be found in Section 4.
114
      PEFT methods for LLMs. PEFT aims to reduce the expensive fine-tuning cost of LLMs by updating
115   a small fraction of parameters. Existing PEFT methods can be roughly classified into three categories.
116   The first category is Adapter-based methods, which introduce new trainable modules (e.g., fully-
117   connected layers) into the original frozen DNN (Houlsby et al., 2019; Karimi Mahabadi et al., 2021;
118   mahabadi et al., 2021; Hyeon-Woo et al., 2022). The second category is Prompt-based methods,
119   which add new soft tokens to the input as the prefix and train these tokens’ embedding (Lester et al.,
120   2021; Razdaibiedina et al., 2023). LoRA-based methods (Hu et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2023; Liu
121   et al., 2024b; Renduchintala et al., 2023) are the third category of PEFT. LoRA-based methods
122   utilize low-rank matrices to represent and approximate the weight changes during the fine-tuning
123
      process. Prior to the inference process, low-rank matrics can be merged into the original model
      without bringing extra computation costs. In this study, we discover that PEFT methods lead to the
124
      trade-off phenomenon between the awareness of fairness and privacy in LLMs.
125
126   Identifying task-related regions in LLMs. Attributing and locating task-related regions in DNNs
127   is a classic research direction in explainable artificial intelligence (Tjoa & Guan, 2020; Liu et al.,
128   2024a; Ren et al., 2024). Previous studies aim to interpret and control DNNs, by identifying task-
129
      specific regions and neurons. Springenberg et al. (2015); Sundararajan et al. (2017); Shrikumar et al.
      (2017); Michel et al. (2019); Maini et al. (2023); Wang et al. (2023a); Wei et al. (2024); Liu et al.
130
      (2024c) measure the importance score for weights in DNNs based on back-propagation gradients.
131
      Probing-based methods are another perspective for identifying the layers and regions, where the
132   task-related knowledge is encoded in LLMs (Adi et al., 2016; Hewitt & Liang, 2019; Zou et al.,
133   2023). Specifically, training a probe classifier based on the model’s feature representations on some
134   task-related samples, including truthfulness (Li et al., 2023a; Qian et al., 2024), toxicity (Lee et al.,
135   2024), and knowledge Burns et al. (2023); Todd et al. (2023) in LLMs.

138   3     M ETHOD : D EACTIVATING THE C OUPLED N EURONS TO M ITIGATE
139         FAIRNESS -P RIVACY C ONFLICTS
140
141   As demonstrated in Figure 1(b), common SFT techniques tend to introduce a trade-off between
142   LLMs’ awareness of fairness and privacy. In this section, we propose our training-free method DEAN
143   for addressing the trade-off issue. We begin by establishing the theoretical foundation based on
144   information theory (3.1), followed by a detailed description of our proposed DEAN (3.2). Finally, we
145
      provide experimental analysis to verify that DEAN achieves the expected outcomes derived from the
      theoretical foundation (3.3).
146
147
148   3.1   I NSPIRATION FROM I NFORMATION T HEORY
149
150   As discussed in Section 1, one potential explanation for the trade-off between LLMs’ awareness of
151
      fairness and privacy is the neuron semantic superposition hypothesis (Elhage et al., 2022; Bricken
      et al., 2023; Templeton, 2024). This means that certain neurons may simultaneously contribute to
152
      both fairness-related and privacy-related representations. Therefore, fine-tuning LLMs may leads to
153
      conflicting optimization directions in these coupled representation spaces, causing the observed trade-
154   off phenomenon. To understand the interplay between fairness and privacy-related representations
155   in LLMs, we first leverage concepts from information theory, particularly focusing on mutual
156   information between different representations.
157
      Theorem 1 (Proven in Appendix B). Let X, Y , and Z be random variables, then we have:
158
159                                       I[X; Y ] ≤ I[(X, Z); (Y, Z)],                                    (1)
160
161   where I[X; Y ] denotes the mutual information between variables X and Y , and I[(X, Z); (Y, Z)]
      denotes the mutual information between the joint variables (X, Z) and (Y, Z).


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      Remark 1. Theorem 1 indicates that the presence of coupled variable Z contributes to a larger
163   mutual information between X and Y , i.e., I[X; Y ] ≤ I[(X, Z); (Y, Z)]. In this way, deactivating
164   and eliminating the coupled variable Z decreases the mutual information between (X, Z) and (Y, Z).
165   In the context of this study, let (X, Z) and (Y, Z) denote the fairness-related and privacy-related
166   representations in the original LLM, respectively. Therefore, deactivating or eliminating Z can
167   potentially decouple X and Y , i.e., decreasing I[X; Y ]. Building on this insight, we have the
168   following proposition with respect to the LLM’s application.
169   Proposition 1 (Application of Theorem 1). Let ψ(·) denote the representation extraction function
170   of original LLM, and ϕ(·) denote the representation extraction function of LLM where fairness and
171   privacy-related representations are decoupled. Let Qf and Qp represent query sets related to fairness
172   and privacy awareness, respectively. For queries qf ∈ Qf and qp ∈ Qp , we have:
173
                                        I[ϕ(qf ); ϕ(qp )] ≤ I[ψ(qf ); ψ(qp )].                              (2)
174
175
      Remark 2. Proposition 1 indicates that by removing representations associated with both fairness and
176
      privacy (i.e., modify ψ(·) to obtain the ϕ(·)), the mutual information between fairness and privacy
177   representations would reduce, thereby potentially facilitating their decoupling to mitigate the trade-off.
178   In practical terms, we can achieve this goal by identifying and deactivating the neurons that contribute
179   to both fairness-related and privacy-related representations, thereby reducing the coupled information.
180
181   3.2   D ECOUPLING FAIRNESS AND P RIVACY VIA N EURON D EACTIVATION
182
183   Building on the theoretical insights, we propose a method for decoupling the awareness of fairness
184   and privacy in LLMs: deactivating neurons associated with both fairness and privacy semantics.
185   Specifically, we first identify neurons related to fairness and privacy semantics, then deactivate those
186   neurons that are coupled across both representations.
187   Computing importance scores for neurons. We begin with an activation dataset D, where each
                                                                                  l
188   data sample s consists of a query-response pair (xquery , yanswer ). Let Wmodule  denote the weight

190   Perceptron (MLP)) within the layer l of the LLM. For simplicity, we omit layer and module subscripts
191   in the subsequent discussion. Then the importance score matrix IW for the weight matrix W is
192   computed as follows (Michel et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2023a; Wei et al., 2024):
193

195

198   of W , the importance score
199                                  IW (i, j) = Es∼D W (i, j)∇W (i,j) L(s)                                 (4)

201   serves as a first-order Taylor approximation of the change in the loss function when W (i, j) is set

204
      neuron at this position has a stronger association with the dataset D. In practice, we compute IW by
      taking the expectation over the activation dataset D following Michel et al. (2019); Wei et al. (2024).

254

257   privacy-related representations (Proposition 1 and Remark 2). In this subsection, we aim to verify
258   that DEAN achieves the goal of Proposition 1.
259

303   4.1    E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
304
      Datasets. To identify the coupled neurons in LLMs (Section 3.2) and to fine-tune LLMs, we require
305
      datasets in the (query, answer) format. For fairness awareness and privacy awareness datasets, we
306   utilize the open-source preference dataset BeaverTails (Ji et al., 2023) to extract training samples via
307   sensitive phrase matching (Wang et al., 2023b; Qi et al., 2024). For general capabilities datasets, we
308   follow Qi et al. (2024); Wei et al. (2024) to adopt the refined version of the Alpaca (Taori et al., 2023)
309   dataset. Further details regarding these datasets are provided in Appendix C.
310
      Models. To evaluate the effectiveness and generalization ability of DEAN, we conduct experiments
311
      on three representative model families, specifically including Qwen2 model series (Yang et al., 2024a),
312   Mistral-v0.2 model series (Jiang et al., 2023), Vicuna model series (Chiang et al., 2023), and Llama2
313   model series (Touvron et al., 2023).
314
315
      Baselines. To validate the effectiveness of DEAN, we compare it with following baselines: FFT
      (Devlin et al., 2019), where all model parameters are updated for maximum adaptability, though at
316
      the cost of significant computational resources; LoRA (Hu et al., 2022), which only updates low-rank
317
      matrices while freezing the large fraction of model parameters for efficiency; DoRA (Liu et al., 2024b),

321   updating model weights. Recalling the experimental results in Figure 1(b), employing SFTs method
322   to enhance the LLM’s awareness of privacy leads to a significant decrease in model’s fairness
323   awareness. To mitigate this trade-off, we incorporate an equal amount of fairness awareness data into
      the fine-tuning dataset for these SFT methods. More details are provided in Appendix C.

341   evaluation process as follows.
342
343      Evaluating LLMs’ awareness of fairness and privacy
344      1. Input Space Definition: Let Q represent the input space of all possible queries, and
345      let A represent the output space of all possible responses generated by the LLM.
346      2. Evaluation Function: Define an evaluation function g : Q × A → {0, 1}, where
347      g(q, a) = 1 if the response a to query q demonstrates that the LLM is aware of fairness
348
         or privacy issues and provides an appropriate response. Otherwise, g(q, a) = 0.
349
         3. Performance Metric: For a given query set Q     P ⊆ Q and its corresponding response
                                                                          g(q,a)
350      set A ⊆ A, we define the awareness ratio ra = (q,a)∈(Q,A) |Q|       , which measures the
351      proportion of responses that demonstrate awareness and provide appropriate handling of
352      fairness or privacy-related issues. A higher ra indicates a greater level of awareness by
353      the LLM regarding fairness and privacy issues.
354
355   Specifically, we conduct our evaluation using Salad-bench (Li et al., 2024), a safety benchmark
356   specifically designed to evaluate LLMs in generative tasks. From Salad-bench, we extract query
357   subsets under the predefined categories of “unfair representation” and “privacy infringement” to
358
      construct fairness awareness query set Qf and privacy awareness query set Qp , respectively. We
      then employ MD-judge (Li et al., 2024) as the evaluator g to assess the LLM’s generated responses
359
      regarding Qf and Qp . 2) General capabilities. To evaluate LLMs’ general capabilities, we select
360
      several widely used benchmark, i.e., HellaSwag (Zellers et al., 2019), Race (Lai et al., 2017), MMLU
361   (Hendrycks et al., 2021), GPQA (Rein et al., 2024), OpenBookQA (Mihaylov et al., 2018), BoolQ
362   (Clark et al., 2019), and Perplexity (Chen et al., 1998). We utilize the lm-evaluation-harness library
363   (Gao et al., 2023) with default evaluation settings to conduct the evaluation.

499   4.4   A BLATION S TUDY
500
501
      In this subsection, we investigate how changes in the extraction ratio and the choice of target
502
      modules (Section 3.2) affect DEAN’s performance in terms of LLMs’ fairness awareness, privacy
503
      awareness, and general capabilities. Specifically, we vary the extraction ratio within the range of
504   (1 × 10−7 , 1 × 10−3 ) and select MHA, MLP, and ALL (both MHA and MLP) as the target modules.
505   From Figure 6, we can obtain the following observations.
506
507
      Performance degradation with increasing extraction ratio. When the target module is set to either
      ALL or MLP, an increasing extraction ratio generally leads to a decline in performance across all
508
      three capacities. However, we observe a slight performance improvement when the extraction ratio
509
      increased within the range of 1 × 10−7 to 1 × 10−6 . We hypothesize that this initial improvement
510   may be due to a more precise deactivation of the target neurons when the extraction ratio is small. As
511   the extraction ratio continues to increase beyond this range, the introduction of significant noise from
512   deactivating more neurons may inadvertently disrupt neurons crucial for essential functionalities,
513   leading to the overall performance decline.
514
      Performance stability with MHA module. Interestingly, when the target module is set to MHA,
515
      the model’s performance of three tasks remains relatively stable across varying extraction ratios.
516   Moreover, the impact on fairness and privacy awareness is negligible. This observation suggests
517   that neurons associated with fairness and privacy awareness are predominantly encoded within MLP
518   modules. This observation aligns with previous studies (Geva et al., 2021; Dai et al., 2022; Meng et al.,
519   2022; Luo & Specia, 2024), which indicate that the MLP modules in transformer-based language
520   models are more focused on the storage and processing of knowledge.
521
      Based on these observations, we conclude that for practical applications, selecting ALL or MLP
522   as the target module and setting a lower extraction ratio can help achieve a desirable model, i.e.,
523   maintaining general capabilities while simultaneously enhancing awareness of fairness and privacy.
524   We also hope that our work will encourage further fine-grained exploration of the target modules,
525   thereby contributing to a deeper understanding of LLMs.

529   5     C ONCLUSION
530
531
      In this work, we introduce a training-free method DEAN to mitigate the trade-off between fairness
532
      and privacy awareness that arises in SFT methods. Building on theoretical insights from information
533
      theory, DEAN deactivates the coupled neurons responsible for both fairness and privacy in LLMs.
534   Extensive experiments demonstrate that DEAN effectively mitigates the trade-off, leading to simul-
535   taneous enhancements in both fairness and privacy awareness of LLMs. Notably, DEAN exhibits
536   robust performance with limited annotated data or with only malicious fine-tuning data, whereas
537   conventional SFT methods typically fail in these challenging scenarios. We expect that DEAN can be
538   seamlessly integrated into broader frameworks, contributing to the development of more responsible
539   and ethical AI systems. We hope this study provides meaningful insights into the simultaneous
      handling of fairness and privacy LLMs and inspires further related research.

      Theorem 1. Let X, Y , and Z be random variables, then we have:
867
868                                      I[X; Y ] ≤ I[(X, Z); (Y, Z)],
869
      where I[X; Y ] denotes the mutual information between variables X and Y , and I[(X, Z); (Y, Z)]
870   denotes the mutual information between the joint variables (X, Z) and (Y, Z).
871
872   Proof. Step 1. According to the definition of information theory (Ash, 2012; Yang & Zwolinski,
873   2001), we can rewrite the I[(X, Z); (Y, Z)] with entropy terms as follows:
874
875                       I[(X, Z); (Y, Z)] = H(X, Z) + H(Y, Z) − H(X, Z, Y, Z)
876                                         = H(X, Z) + H(Y, Z) − H(X, Y, Z).                     (1)
877
878   By the chain rule of entropy, we have:
879
                                H(X, Z) = H(X) + H(Z|X),
880
                                 H(Y, Z) = H(Y ) + H(Z|Y ),
881
882                            H(X, Y, Z) = H(X) + H(Y |X) + H(Z|X, Y ).                          (2)
883
884   Substituting these into Eq. (1):
885   I[(X, Z); (Y, Z)] = [H(X) + H(Z|X)] + [H(Y ) + H(Z|Y )] − [H(X) + H(Y |X) + H(Z|X, Y )]
886
                        = H(Z|X) + H(Z|Y ) + H(Y ) − H(Y |X) − H(Z|X, Y ).             (3)
887
888
      Step 2. According to the definition of conditional mutual information, we have
889
890                                  I(Z; Y |X) = H(Z|X) − H(Z|X, Y ).                            (4)
891
892   Combining Eq. (3) and Eq. (4) derives:
893
                            I[(X, Z); (Y, Z)] = I(X; Y ) + I(Z; Y |X) + H(Z|Y ).                  (5)
894
895
      Step 3. The difference between I[(X, Z); (Y, Z)] and I[X; Y ] is:
896
897                         I[(X, Z); (Y, Z)] − I[X; Y ] = I(Z; Y |X) + H(Z|Y ).                  (6)
898
899   Step 4. Finally, both terms in Eq. (6) are non-negative, we have:
900
901
902                       I[(X, Z); (Y, Z)] − I(X; Y ) = I(Z; Y |X) + H(Z|Y ) ≥ 0,                (7)
903
904   which completes the proof.
