009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            In the field of scene reconstruction with moving objects, recent studies have uti-
012            lized 3D Gaussian Splatting (3DGS) for spatial representation. This method typ-
013            ically relies on camera poses and point clouds obtained through the Structure-
014            from-Motion (SfM) algorithm. However, in scenes captured with monocular view-
015            points and containing moving objects in each frame, the SfM algorithm struggles
016
               to obtain accurate camera poses and points clouds. As a result, it often either
               removes point clouds of dynamic objects or fails to find camera poses for each
017
               frame, thereby leading to sub-optimal rendering of dynamic scenes. We propose a
018
               novel approach, Initial-Free Monocular Dynamic Gaussian Splatting (IF-MoDGS)
019            which does not require precomputed camera poses and point clouds in dynamic
020            scenes with moving objects. Our approach estimates camera poses using the static
021            background, separated from dynamic objects by a motion mask, and generates
022            point clouds specifically for the dynamic objects. To handle dynamic objects, we
023            define a canonical space and apply deformation to link it with each viewpoint and
024            timestamp. Then, to improve quality in complex spatio-temporal scenes, we utilize
025            a high-dimensional feature loss and an annealing frequency loss. Extensive experi-
026            mental results demonstrate that our method can effectively render dynamic scenes
027
               without relying on precomputed camera poses and point clouds, achieving the
               state-of-the-art performance in dynamic scene rendering tasks using a monocular
028
               camera. Our project will be available at:https://anonymous.4open.science/w/IF-

032   1    I NTRODUCTION
033
034   3D scene reconstruction studies have been a significant topic in the field of computer vision. How-
035   ever, these tasks often face considerable limitations due to the need for extensive manpower, high
036   computational resources, and specialized equipment. As a result, various approaches have been ex-
037   plored to reconstruct 3D spaces using 2D image data obtained from cameras. Neural Radiance Field
038   (NeRF) (Mildenhall et al., 2021), significantly highlighted the importance of spatial representation.
039
      It constructs a continuous volumetric scene function through a simple multi-layer perceptron to syn-
      thesize new views of complex scenes, and it leads to numerous follow-up studies (Barron et al.,
040
      2021; 2022; 2023). Nevertheless, its slow training and rendering speeds have restricted its practical
041
      applications. Subsequently, the 3D Gaussian Splatting (3DGS) (Kerbl et al., 2023) was proposed to
042   reconstruct 2D space by optimizing the parameters of explicit 3D Gaussians and accumulating them.
043   This leads to a significant improvement in training and rendering speeds for 3D reconstruction tasks,
044   making it easier to restore high-quality 3D scenes.
045
      Since most real-world captured sequences involve dynamic scenes, reconstructing these scenes re-
046
      mains a significant challenge. While the reconstruction of static scenes is well-developed (Yu et al.,
047
      2024; Lin et al., 2024; Huang et al., 2024a; Hamdi et al., 2024; Lu et al., 2024a), transitioning
048   to dynamic scene reconstruction presents numerous issues, such as the disappearance of dynamic
049   objects or overfitting to training views. In static scenes or those captured at a single time step, mod-
050   els can easily understand the scene because the objects maintain consistent shapes across different
051   viewpoints. However, in dynamic scenes, the shapes change with each time step, complicating the
052   model’s understanding of the overall scene. To address this, multi-view dynamic approaches (Wu
053   et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2024; Lu et al., 2024b; Li et al., 2024; Sun et al., 2024; Guo et al., 2024) cap-
      ture synchronized data from multiple cameras, while monocular dynamic approaches (Liang et al.,

083

084
      proceeding with reconstruction. However, challenges arise when acquiring camera parameters from
085   image collections that include dynamic objects. SfM typically extracts keypoints from the images
086   during feature extraction, and then, adjusts the feature correspondences between images using the
087   epipolar geometry. It then estimates 3D coordinates through triangulation and minimizes errors using
088   the bundle adjustment and outlier filtering. While dynamic features can be ignored and only static
089   features are utilized during the outlier filtering process, which allows for the acquisition of camera
090   parameters, this approach leads to the inability to acquire point clouds for dynamic objects due to
091   the exclusion of unmatched dynamic points. If the dynamic region occupies a large portion of each
092   frame, the confusion can occur during the filtering process, making it difficult to obtain both camera
093
      parameters and point cloud results. Unlike NeRF-based approaches, where the camera poses are
      crucial, 3DGS based methods require both the camera poses and the initial point cloud as important
094

096   To address these issues, we propose a new method called Initial-Free Monocular Dynamic Gaussian
097   Splatting (IF-MoDGS). It enables the simultaneous estimation of both camera position and initial
098   point cloud from dynamic scenes captured by single RGB camera, facilitating 3D reconstruction
099
      even in dynamic environments. As seen in Figure 1(b), we utilize a motion mask to separate static
      and dynamic regions and explicitly distinguish each region for training. The Static Initial Module
100

101

102   floating and the Canonical Space Mapper (CSM). The floating points are transformed into canonical
103   coordinates through the CSM, resulting in a canonical form that has positions and shapes applicable
104   to the entire scene. Subsequently, the Dynamic Reconstruction Module (DRM) employs a defor-
105   mation network to refine the detailed shape and position according to each viewpoint and temporal
106   information. Through this approach, we can clearly distinguish the Gaussians representing static re-
107   gions from those representing dynamic objects. The separating loss enables us to optimize the Static
      Reconstruction Module (SRM) and DRM independently. When integrating these distinct Gaussians

149   2.1   I NITIAL -F REE 3D R ECONSTRUCTION
150
151   NeRF (Mildenhall et al., 2021) generates a continuous volumetric scene function using an MLP,
152   focusing on novel view synthesis rather than explicit 3D reconstruction. However, its reliance on
153
      MLP makes it prone to overfitting, sensitive to noise, and difficult to edit. Unlike NeRF-based meth-
      ods (Mildenhall et al., 2021; Barron et al., 2021; 2022; 2023; Liu et al., 2020; Yu et al., 2021b;
154
      Chen et al., 2022; Yu et al., 2021a), which need to compute large volumes of empty space, 3DGS
155
      employed a more efficient computation, offering faster rendering times by reducing unnecessary
156   computations in empty space. Despite these advances, both methods still rely heavily on accurate
157   camera parameters and initial point clouds. Recent efforts to overcome these limitations include
158   NeRF-based methods like BARF (Lin et al., 2021a) and RoDyNRF (Liu et al., 2023), which esti-
159   mate camera poses and reconstruct scenes using only RGB images. BARF focuses on static scenes,
160   jointly optimizing both camera poses and an MLP by calculating optical flow and using the Lucas-
161   Kanade algorithm to estimate the displacement vectors of pixel coordinates. RoDyNRF, on the other
      hand, distinguishes between static and dynamic pixels, adjusting alignment of back-projected points.

      For dynamic regions, RoDyNRF uses a scene flow MLP to estimate the motion of 3D points. As
185   a result, it successfully reconstructs dynamic scenes and estimates camera poses using only image
186   data from a single camera. In 3DGS-based research, accurate camera poses and initial point clouds

196   2.2   DYNAMIC R ENDERING
197
198
      Unlike static scene reconstruction, dynamic scene reconstruction is a very challenging task as it
199   involves compositing the desired viewpoint at a specific time. Dynamic scenes can be acquired
200   in two ways: from multi-view cameras (Fridovich-Keil et al., 2023; Cao & Johnson, 2023; Wang
201   et al., 2023) or from monocular cameras (Liang et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024). In addition to this,
202   there are other assumptions, such as whether the camera positions change over time, or whether
203   RGB-D cameras are used, but in general, the dataset is constructed in the same way. In the case of
204   multi-view cameras, there are multiple images for each time step, so there is enough information
205   to use SfM and other methods. However, if we consider the case of a monocular camera, there is
206   only one scene captured at each time step. In the case of dynamic objects, the shape and position
207
      change from time to time, so it is very difficult for the model to understand the reconstruction using
      only that information. Most of the readily accessible videos are single-camera and were recorded
208
      in dynamic scenes. Therefore, it can be seen that dynamic scene reconstruction is a very important
209
      task. In 3DGS, study of monocular dynamic scene reconstruction exists (Yang et al., 2024). This
210   study utilizes a scenario where a single camera is used, but it has the disadvantage of using point
211   clouds acquired using stereo depth. In the case of (Kocabas et al., 2024), the dynamic objects are
212   limited to humans and an additional initial prior called SMPL (Loper et al., 2023) is provided. We
213   overcome the aforementioned challenges in monocular dynamic scenes. In a monocular view with
214   moving objects, we used video frames and corresponding monocular depth to estimate the correct
215   camera pose. Through backprojection, We generate an initial prior for unspecified dynamic objects,
      rather than focusing on a specific dynamic object as SMLP does.

      3     M ETHOD
217
218   Our IF-MoDGS reconstructs and renders dynamic scenes without initial priors. IF-MoDGS consists
219   of the initial module that estimates the initial 3D points and approximates camera poses, and the re-
220   construction module that optimizes Gaussians using the initial points and estimates the exact camera
221   poses as shown in Figure 3. The initial module consists of the SIM, which estimates the initial 3D
222   points and camera poses for static scene, and the DIM, which estimates the 3D points for dynamic
223   objects. The reconstruction module consists of the SRM and the DRM. The SRM takes the initial
224   3D points and camera poses estimated by the SIM as inputs, optimizes the Gaussians, and refines
225   the camera poses for greater accuracy. The DRM takes as input the initial 3D points estimated by
226
      the DIM and optimizes the Gaussians and deformation. The final rendering result can be obtained
      by concatenating the Gaussian from the SRM and DRM.

522   5   C ONCLUSION
523
524   We proposed a new method, IF-MoDGS, in Gaussian Splatting for dynamic scenes. Unlike other
525   methodologies that relied on SfM-based algorithms like COLMAP to obtain initial values, we op-
526   erated without initial point clouds and camera poses. Our method separated static and dynamic
527   components to generate the initial Gaussian points. The initial point was reconstructed using the
528
      monocular depth, and the depth of the dynamic region with incomplete consistency was compen-
      sated using the CSM. It also improved rendering quality by optimizing the Gaussian for static and
529
      dynamic components independently. Finally, we applied a feature loss to induce the segmented static
530
      and dynamic Gaussians to be well combined and rendered. We demonstrated the validity of our new
531   method by achieving SOTA performance on a dataset targeting monocular dynamic scenes.

669   A.1   T RAINING SETUP
670
      We took the camera intrinsic parameter as input and used it. Also, using the pre-trained relative
671   depth estimation model (Ke et al., 2024) and Mask R-CNN (He et al., 2017), we use depth maps and
672   motion masks. Our method performs back-propagation of gradients for camera poses only on the
673   SRM to jointly learn camera poses and 3D Gaussian, and does not learn gradients for camera poses
674   on the DRM. In addition, when learning 3D Gaussian and deformation, the next iteration performs
675   iteration that learns only camera poses in the SRM, as shown below:
676
                                   Lcam = L1static = (Istatic − IGT ) · M,                             (22)
677
678   where Istatic is the render result of the SRM, and IGT is the Ground Truth image. The motion mask
679   M is applied pixel-wise to suppress the effect of the Gaussian on the dynamic object. And the next
680   iteration repeats the process of learning 3D Gaussian and deformation again. Gaussian rendering
681   used COGS (Jiang et al., 2024) rasterizer, in which the gradient calculation for the camera poses
682
      are implemented as CUDA. The Canonical Mapping Network consists of MLP. Both encoder and
      decoder use the softplus function as an activation function, and each layer has a width of 256. In the
683
      case of the scale decoder, it is output through the sigmoid function, and the output of the rotation
684
      decoder is normalized.
685

      A.2   C ANONICAL S PACE M APPER
687
688   The CSM consists of two main components: an CSM encoder E = Θf eat and a CSM decoder
689   D = {θp , θr , θs }. In the CSM encoder, the information of location is expanded using positional
690   embedding as used in NeRF (Mildenhall et al., 2021) and passed through two linear layers. The
691   extracted features are then passed through the CSM decoder D, which has four linear layers. At
692   this time, first layer of the CSM decoder D fuses the feature dimensions using skip-connection to
693   prevent the loss of location information. Finally, the generalized canonical field is constructed by
694   predicting the deformation in position, scale, and rotation. The overall network structure is shown
695
      in Figure 6
