011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            The application scope of Large Language Models (LLMs) continues to expand,
014            leading to increasing interest in personalized LLMs. However, aligning these
015            models with individual values raises significant safety concerns due to harmful in-
016            formation correlated with certain values. In this paper, we identify specific safety
017            risks in value-aligned LLMs and investigate the psychological principles behind
018
               these challenges. Our findings reveal two key insights. First, value-aligned LLMs
               are more prone to harmful behavior compared to non-fine-tuned models and ex-
019
               hibit slightly higher risks in traditional safety evaluations than other fine-tuned
020
               models. Second, these safety issues arise because value-aligned LLMs genuinely
021            understand and act according to the aligned values, which can amplify harmful
022            outcomes. Using a dataset with detailed safety categories, we find significant cor-
023            relations between value alignment and safety concerns, supported by psychologi-
024            cal hypotheses. This study offers insights into the “black box” of value alignment
025            and proposes enhancing the safety of value-aligned LLMs by corresponding in-
026            context alignment methods.
027            Warning: This paper contains contents that may be offensive or upsetting.

      2.1   T HEORY OF BASIC H UMAN VALUES
086
087
      The theory of basic human values, proposed by Schwartz, is a cornerstone of cross-cultural psychol-
088
      ogy and outlines ten universal values, organized into four higher-order groups (Schwartz, 2012).
089
      These values serve as standards for evaluating behavior, with each person holding a unique distribu-
090   tion based on their importance.
091
092   The ten values correspond to specific goals: achievement seeks personal success; power aims for so-
093
      cial status and control; hedonism pursues pleasure for oneself; self-direction values independence;
      stimulation seeks excitement and challenge; security desires safety and stability of society; confor-
094
      mity restrains actions that harm others and violate social expectations or norms; tradition values
095
      cultural and religious customs; benevolence prioritizes the welfare of close personal contacts, and
096   universalism promotes tolerance and protection for all people and nature. These values are catego-
097   rized into four higher-order groups: openness to change(hedonism, stimulation, and self-direction),
098   self-enhancement(achievement and power), conservation(security, conformity and tradition), and
099   self-transcendence(benevolence and universalism).
100
      The basic human values reflect human motives and beliefs, they are closely linked to specific hu-
101
      man behavior. For example, Seddig & Davidov (2018) investigates the association between values
102   and attitudes toward interpersonal violence, and interpersonal violent behavior, finding positive as-
103   sociations with power and stimulation, and negative associations with universalism, benevolence,
104   conformity, tradition, and security. Also, some research suggests relationships between individuals’
105   behaviors, such as attitudes toward drug use, delinquency, white-collar crime, or legal norm accep-
106   tance and cultural basic human values (Askew & Ritter, 2023; Bilsky & Hermann, 2016; Goossen
107   et al., 2016; Bilsky et al., 2022). These findings suggest that training specific human values into
      LLMs may entail different safety risks depending on the values emphasized.

      2.2     P ERSONALIZED VALUE A LIGNMENT FOR LLM S
109
110   Personalized models in NLP have garnered significant attention from researchers and companies
111   due to their potential to enhance user experience by tailoring responses to individual preferences,
112   backgrounds, or conversational styles.(Liu, 2015; Zheng et al., 2020; Zhang, 2018) Research also
113   explores personalizing models by mimicking individual behavior to predict preferences and ac-
114   tions.(Aher et al., 2023) Aligning models with a person’s values is another effective approach for
115   predicting behavior or opinions. Since Schwartz’s theory of values significantly influences individ-
116
      uals’ motives, behaviors, and beliefs, it helps identify the values individuals prioritize based on their
      expressions or actions. In particular, the basic human value theory has been widely applied to AI
117
      due to its ability to broadly cover cultural and societal values. This is evident in various datasets that
118
      incorporate the theory. VALUENET (Qiu et al., 2022) is proposed to uncover the basic human val-
119   ues underlying real-world dialogues, while Touché23-ValueEval(Mirzakhmedova et al., 2023) links
120   arguments related to social issues with basic human values. Notably, Kang et al. (2023) suggests
121   Value Injection Method(VIM) to explore aligning individual values with LLMs to better anticipate
122   behavior and opinions.

124   2.3     AI S AFETY
125
126   Even before the advent of large language models, there is consistent interest in the safety of lan-
127   guage models. Research concerning safety issues of language models in this period is particularly
128   focused on toxicity and bias. Zhao et al. (2018); Gehman et al. (2020); Smith et al. (2022) In recent
129   research, there has been a noticeable surge in attention towards Safety Risks, with notable studies
130
      like hh-rlhf (Bai et al., 2022), Anthropic Red Teaming dataset (Ganguli et al., 2022), and Beaver-
      Tails (Ji et al., 2024) emerging. Rather than solely focusing on improving LLM safety, many papers
131
      now propose methods to categorize and evaluate safety risks or suggest mitigation strategies. The
132
      BeaverTails-Evaluation Dataset classifies 14 risk categories, including hate speech and terrorism.
133   HEx-PHI, by Qi et al. (2023), explores 11 categories like hate/harassment/violence and economic
134   harm. SafetyBench (Zhang et al., 2023) covers categories such as offensiveness, ethics, and physi-
135   cal health. These efforts underscore the importance of systematically understanding and addressing
136   safety risks associated with LLMs.
137
      Concerns about safety risks have arisen not only in general LLMs but also in personalized LLMs,
138
      prompting multiple attempts to address the issue. For instance, Deshpande et al. (2023) found
139   that when ChatGPT is prompted with specific personas, it generates responses that are much more
140   harmful than when no persona is assigned. Similarly, Zeng et al. (2024) demonstrates that using
141   humanized prompts increases the success rate of jailbreaking in various LLMs. Although previous
142   research has shown that providing personalized instructions to LLMs increases their potential for
143   harm, there has been little discussion about the safety issues arising from personalized fine-tuning
144   of LLMs.
145   However, although Yao et al. (2024) suggests the FULCRA dataset to explore the connection be-
146   tween model behavior and the values they represent, there is very little research addressing the
147   relationship between personal values and the potential risks they may pose. Therefore, in this pa-
148   per, we investigate the correlations between values and downstream safety risks, and explore these
149   relationships through psychological analysis.

      3.2.2     VALUE - ALIGNED LLM S
245   We trained 154 value-aligned LLMs using 154 distinct Schwartz value distributions. In this context,
246   the key aspects to highlight are the method by which these distributions were sampled and the
247   approach employed to align the models with the corresponding values.
248
249            • Distribution Sampling: Our sampled distributions are divided into two groups: extreme
250
                 distributions and real distributions. Extreme distributions are initially selected based on the
                 hypothesis that when the variance of value distributions is small, the model may struggle
251
                 to distinguish subtle differences, limiting its ability to generate diverse safety-oriented re-
252
                 sponses. These extreme distributions represent cases where only one of the 10 values is
253              deemed important (rated 6) while all others are unimportant (rated 1), or where only values
254              within one of the four higher-order value groups are considered meaningful, with all other
255              groups disregarded. However, relying solely on 14 extreme distributions posed limitations
256              for conducting an in-depth analysis of value-aligned LLMs and raised concerns about the
257              practicality of the results. To address these issues, we expand the sample size with real dis-
258              tributions, ensuring sufficient variance in value distributions while maintaining the study’s
259              relevance. In this study, survey results from the European Social Survey (ESS)3 are uti-
260              lized. The 10 distributions most similar to each extreme distribution are identified based on
261
                 Jensen-Shannon divergence values. As a result, we employ a total of 154 value distributions
                 in the experiment, including 14 extreme distributions and 140 real distributions.
262
263            • Training Method for Value-Alignment: Research on personal value alignment remains
264              relatively underexplored, resulting in a limited variety of methodologies. Among the avail-
265
                 able approaches, we adopt the Value Injection Method (VIM) method proposed by (Kang
                 et al., 2023), as it demonstrates superior performance compared to in-context learning of
266
                 ChatGPT when applied to personal value distributions. For detailed information on the
267
                 training methodology, please refer to the original paper which suggests VIM.

292   4.1    R ESULTS ON C ONVENTIONAL S AFETY E VALUATION
293
      Even before the safety concerns of LLMs became prominent, numerous benchmark datasets ex-
294
      isted to measure the harmfulness or biases of language models. Among them, we select RealTox-
295   icityPrompts have been widely used in various studies to measure the toxicity and bias in model
296   generations, (Touvron et al., 2023a; Anil et al., 2023; OpenAI, 2024; Deshpande et al., 2023)
297   and HolisticBiasR which has more diverse sociodemographic axes than other bias datasets like
298   BOLD (Dhamala et al., 2021) or WinoBias (Zhao et al., 2018). We explore the possibility that
299   value alignment could make a language model more vulnerable to safety attacks than others even
300   in these conventional safety evaluations. We follow the evaluation metrics proposed by the original
301   studies for each dataset to measure the toxicity and bias of model generations. The corresponding
302   results can be found in Table 2.
303   The results show that value-aligned LLMs demonstrate lower safety across most metrics, particularly
304   in terms of toxicity probability and bias, except for the expected maximum toxicity in RealToxici-
305   tyPrompts, where they exhibit the second-lowest safety. In the expected maximum toxicity metric,
306   the model fine-tuned on the Samsum dataset shows the lowest safety. In RealToxicityPrompts, mod-
307   els fine-tuned on instruction datasets generally show lower toxicity compared to the non-fine-tuned
308   (vanilla) model, while models fine-tuned on traditional NLP tasks show higher toxicity than vanilla.
309
      The results from the HolisticBiasR dataset do not appear to be strongly linked to the nature of the
      fine-tuned datasets. Bias is relatively higher in models fine-tuned on Alpaca and Samsum compared
310
      to vanilla, while models fine-tuned on Dolly and Grammar show relatively lower bias. Nonetheless,
311
      despite representing the average of 154 models, value-aligned LLMs using VIM consistently dis-
312   play lower safety than the vanilla model. These results indicate that the toxicity or bias in model
313   generations is not solely determined by the fine-tuning method.
314
315
      The RealToxicityPrompts dataset evaluates how explicitly toxic a model’s generations are, while
      the HolisticBiasR dataset assesses the negativity of the model’s opinions toward given subjects. If
316
      the dataset used for fine-tuning contains harmful or negative content, it is likely that this will be
317
      reflected in the evaluation of the model’s generations. To verify this, we measure the toxicity of
318   the Touché23-ValueEval dataset using PerspectiveAPI. If the toxicity of Touché23-ValueEval
319   were high, it can indicate that the results stem from fine-tuning on a harmful dataset. According to
320   the developers of PerspectiveAPI, content with a toxicity score above 50% is potentially toxic,
321   and content exceeding 70% can be considered toxic. However, out of more than 8K samples in the
322   Touché23-ValueEval dataset, only 5 had toxicity scores above 50%, and none exceeded 70%. This
323   suggests that the reduced safety of value-aligned LLMs is not due to explicit harmfulness in the
      training dataset.

346   4.2   R ESULTS FOR THE S AFETY E VALUATION DATASET WITH D ETAILED C ATEGORIES
347
348   This section analyzes the detailed AI safety categories and the specific relationships between
349   each category and corresponding values. The safety evaluation datasets used in this section are
350   BeaverTails-Evaluation and HEx-PHI, which contain 14 and 11 detailed safety categories, respec-
351
      tively. We observe that when certain values increase, the safety score for specific safety categories
      in a dataset is affected. We interpret this relationship based on psychological hypotheses to explain
352
      why it occurs. In other words, the analysis in this section aims to uncover the ”black box” behind
353
      why harmful outcomes arise when values are aligned in LLMs.
354
355   Figure 2 shows the calculation of the correlation between category-specific results of the HEx-
356   PHI and BeaverTails datasets and the value scores aligned with each model. Since each model is
357
      simultaneously aligned with 10 value scores, a multiple analysis approach was employed. Using the
      Ordinary Least Squares (OLS) model, we calculate the correlations between the 10 aligned value
358
      scores and the detailed category values of the safety evaluation datasets. Notably, some results are
359
      found to be particularly significant, and we discover that these findings align with psychological
360   hypotheses. Examples of the model’s responses are provided in Table 10.
361
362   Achievement shows negative correlations with both hate speech and sexual content. While one
363
      might assume that the pursuit of success associated with achievement could endorse violence, the
      success sought by achievement is defined by societal standards. Thus, individuals with high achieve-
364
      ment values are likely to reject behaviors that are not socially acceptable. For example, we find
365
      that models aligned to highly prioritize achievement tended to avoid unjustifiably criticizing certain
366   racial groups. As shown in psychological studies, the correlation between direct violence, such as
367   hate speech, and achievement was found to be negative in this study as well (Seddig & Davidov,
368   2018; Bilsky & Hermann, 2016). Furthermore, although the relationship between achievement and
369   sexual content may seem unrelated, individuals focused on socially recognized success would likely
370   avoid actions that could damage their reputation or involve illegal activities. The psychological study
371   also find that students with lower academic achievement are more likely to exhibit early interest in
372   sex and engage in sexual activities (Schvaneveldt et al., 2001). In this context, an LLM aligned
373   with high achievement values might perceive illicit sexual behavior as detrimental to social success,
374
      explaining the negative correlation between the two variables.
375   Conformity shows a positive correlation with political campaigning content. The ”Political Cam-
376   paigning” category in the HEx-PHI dataset consists of prompts about supporting or opposing spe-
377   cific politicians or political agendas, sometimes involving actions that are not easily legitimized.
      Vecchione et al. (2015) suggests that conformity, a value emphasizing adherence to societal norms,


                                                        7
378
      has a low association with political activism. However, individuals with high conformity may also
379   be more inclined to attack out-groups, either to strengthen their in-group or to integrate into a new
380   group after leaving their original one. Therefore, if LLMs trained to prioritize conformity perceive a
381   politician or political affiliation as an out-group, they may respond in line with prompts encouraging
382   attacks on that individual or group. In practice, we observe such the model providing responses
383   opposing certain political views on the grounds that they attacked the in-group.
384
      Hedonism is positively correlated with several safety-related categories, including sexual content,
385   child abuse, physical harm, and political campaigning. This aligns with the nature of hedonism,
386   which emphasizes physical and primal pleasure, making the pursuit of sexual content to the point
387   of raising safety concerns a plausible outcome. The “Child Abuse Content” category encompasses
388   prompts about methods of sexually exploiting or abusing children, while political campaigning con-
389   tent includes illegal and unethical behaviors. These associations are consistent with psychological
390   studies linking hedonism to various unethical behaviors, such as delinquency, rejection of legal
391   norms, and violence (Bilsky & Hermann, 2016; Bilsky et al., 2022). The “Physical Harm” category
392   involves prompts about inflicting harm on oneself or others, which is linked to hedonism’s associ-
393
      ation with risky behaviors, as discussed by Cole et al. (2007). However, the relationship between
      self-directed violence and hedonism requires further consideration. Since hedonism centers on the
394
      pursuit of pleasure, individuals often tolerate short- or long-term pain if it ultimately leads to gratifi-
395
      cation. In the PVQ40, which measures basic human values of participants, a hedonism-related item
396   reads: “Enjoying life’s pleasures is important to him/her. He likes to ‘spoil’ himself.” (Schwartz,
397   2006), suggesting that individuals high in hedonism are prone to self-harm. Additionally, analysis
398   of the Touché23-ValueEval dataset, used for value alignment, reveals arguments supporting the le-
399   galization of marijuana by comparing it to alcohol or cigarettes. Thus, hedonism exhibits a clear
400   positive correlation with physical harm, whether self-inflicted or directed toward others.
401
      Power shows positive correlations with hate speech and discrimination, but negative correlations
402   with abuse, deception, physical violence, terror, and privacy violation. This is understandable, as
403   power is driven by a motivation to seek control, authority, and respect. If verbally attacking others
404   helps assert dominance, individuals with high power values are likely to engage in such behavior. In
405   fact, power is significantly associated with both direct and indirect forms of interpersonal violence.
406   For example, a model fine-tuned to place high value on power generates responses that demean
407   others and convey oppressive sentiments. However, when considering power’s relationship with
408   interpersonal violence, it is also important to assess the training datasets used by LLMs. Upon
409   examining power-related arguments in these datasets, we observe that individuals often state that
410
      violence must be prevented when justifying their positions on certain issues. This suggests that
      LLMs trained on the power value perceive violent acts, such as deception and privacy violations, as
411
      threats to their authority.
412
413   Security negatively correlates with “Tailored Financial Advice.” A psychological study on the rela-
414   tionship between Schwartz values and white-collar crime confirmed a similar negative correlation,
415   indicating that higher importance placed on security reduces engagement in illegal financial activ-
416
      ities such as tax evasion, bribery, and insurance fraud (Goossen et al., 2016). Since the “Tailored
      Financial Advice” category includes queries about illegal financial practices for personal gain, it
417
      can be seen as analogous to white-collar crime. We observe that value-aligned models, prioritizing
418
      security, tended to avoid providing financial advice due to associated risk considerations.
419
420   Self-direction shows positive correlation with sexual content. There is a psychological study that
421   suggests self-directed motives enhance actions to increase sexual satisfaction more than other-
422
      directed motives, and this content for greater satisfaction. (Jodouin et al., 2018) Therefore, if the
      self-direction value is high, an individual will take a more proactive stance towards “Adult Content”,
423
      which could result in lower safety scores in the sexual content category. This phenomenon is also
424
      observed in value-aligned LLMs, which, similar to human behavior, actively generated responses
425   about methods of creating sexual content.

      involve direct or indirect attacks on others. For these reasons, we observe that the model, which

475   5   M ITIGATION
476
477
      One of the simplest and most widely adopted approaches to improving the safety of fine-tuned
478
      models, without altering their learned weights, is to incorporate a safety prompt into the input. By
479
      embedding carefully crafted prompts, this technique—known as prompt engineering—can signifi-
480   cantly enhance a model’s safety without modifying its parameters (OpenAI, 2024; Jiang et al., 2023).
481   However, instead of relying solely on a safety prompt, more detailed methods can be used. Since
482   we have identified specific values that pose risks in particular safety categories, we can leverage this
483   information. For example, when using a model with a high hedonism value, we can specifically
484   require more responsible responses, especially in relation to sexual content. To compare different
485   approaches, we categorize the prompt engineering methods into three types based on the nature of
      the prompts: basic safety prompt, context-based prompt, and value-based prompt.


                                                         9
486
      The safety prompt we used is adapted from the default system prompt in Llama2. When inputting
487   harmful prompts from the safety evaluation datasets into the model, we append the following instruc-
488   tion as the baseline. The context-based prompt explicitly warns the model not to generate content
489   related to specific safety issues when such prompts are introduced. A value-based prompt instructs
490   the model not to consider a specific value in a given context, particularly when that value has a sig-
491   nificant correlation with a specific safety issue. If value-aligned LLMs previously responded based
492   on the relationship between the value and the safety issue, directing them to disregard the value will
493   reduce the likelihood of safety-related problems.
494   For this experiment, we select models from the 154 value-aligned LLMs that are strongly trained
495   on specific values. In Section 4.2, we identify which values are most closely related to particular
496   safety risks. Among them, we find that hedonism and self-direction show positive correlations with
497   sexual content, which we leverage in this study. For each case, we select 11 models: one with an
498   extreme distribution prioritizing hedonism or openness to change (including hedonism, stimulation,
499   and self-direction) and 10 additional distributions from the ESS dataset that closely match this ex-
500   treme distribution. We then conduct experiments using prompts from the ”Adult Content” category
501
      in the HEx-PHI dataset, testing four conditions: providing only the original prompt, adding a safety
      prompt, using a context-based prompt, and using a value-based prompt.
502
503   As shown in Table 9, the safety of models improves when additional prompts are added, compared
504   to using only the original prompts. Notably, value-aligned LLMs show the greatest safety improve-
505   ments when explicitly instructed not to consider values related to specific contexts, rather than when
506   asked to focus on overall safety or avoid generating content related to certain safety categories.
507
      Furthermore, models prioritizing openness to change achieve the highest safety when instructed to
      disregard both values related to sexual content, rather than just one. This suggests that value-aligned
508
      LLMs understand how their aligned values relate to specific safety risks, and that instructing them
509
      to disregard these values can mitigate safety issues more effectively than merely reducing their em-
510   phasis.

516   6   C ONCLUSION
517
518
519
      This paper represents the first study to address the potential risks of value-aligned LLMs and the
520
      reasons behind these risks through a psychological approach. In this research, we observe that
521
      value-aligned LLMs generally exhibit lower safety in conventional safety evaluations compared to
522   those fine-tuned on other datasets. By employing a safety evaluation with detailed safety categories,
523   we reveal that this compromised safety is due to the psychological connection between certain values
524   and unethical behavior. As a result, the safety of value-aligned LLMs decrease or increase in specific
525   situations depending on the aligned values.
526
      However, the robustness of this mitigation approach has certain limitations. First, since the method
527
      does not address harmful content during the training phase of the model itself, it is challenging
528   to predict the model’s behavior in response to unforeseen prompt injections. Additionally, unless
529   the differences among the value scores in the aligned value distribution are significantly pronounced,
530   more meticulous efforts will be required to effectively address the safety degradation observed in the
531   value-aligned LLMs. Advancing robust prompt engineering techniques and other resilient mitigation
532   methods will be a critical direction for future research.
533
      In psychology, the relationship between values and behavior has been measured from various per-
534   spectives, and multiple interpretations can exist. Therefore, the interpretation we propose in this
535   study should not be considered absolute. However, the key point is that the safety of value-aligned
536   LLMs can be evaluated as lower than that of other LLMs because they can become more or less
537   vulnerable in certain safety situations. In other words, our findings provide guidance on how LLMs
538   respond more sensitively or less sensitively depending on the specific safety context. We strongly
539   advocate that developers and users of value-aligned LLMs recognize these issues and pay special
      attention to situations where heightened caution is required.

      7   E THICS S TATEMENT
541
542   This study aims to identify the potential risks associated with value-aligned LLMs and to explore
543   the underlying causes of these risks. The ultimate goal of our research is to provide guidance on how
544   to use value-aligned LLMs in a safe and beneficial manner for people. To this end, we identified
545   correlations showing where value-aligned LLMs exhibit reduced safety. Our findings reveal that
546   certain values increase a model’s vulnerability to specific safety risks, which could, in theory, be
547   misused to create harmful models. We strongly oppose such misuse and hope that our results will
548   be used solely to improve model safety.
549   It is essential for users to understand that the methods we have tested are not intended to reduce the
550   safety of the model. Rather, they should consider, along with the methods we propose, how to use
551   the model ethically.

790
      on distributions that prioritize hedonism and self-direction over others show a Harmfulness Score of
791
      approximately 4.2 and a Harmfulness Rate of 62% when evaluated with the HEx-PHI dataset. When
792   evaluated with the BeaverTails-Evaluation dataset, models trained on distributions that emphasize
793   stimulation and openness to change values show a Harmfulness Rate of around 45%. However, a
794   model trained to prioritize security records a Harmfulness Score of 2.3 and a Harmfulness Rate of
795   15% in the HEx-PHI evaluation. These results indicate that the safety of a model varies depending
796   on which values it has been trained to prioritize. Based on these findings, we conclude that the safety
797   of value-aligned LLMs is influenced by the specific values they have been aligned with.
