009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            The increasing prevalence of text-to-image (T2I) models makes their safety a criti-
012            cal concern. Adversarial testing techniques have been developed to probe whether
013            such models can be prompted to produce Not-Safe-For-Work (NSFW) content.
014            Despite these efforts, current solutions face several challenges, such as low suc-
015            cess rate, inefficiency and lack of semantic understandings. To combat these,
016            we introduce T REANT, a novel automated red-teaming framework for adversarial
017            testing of T2I models. The core of our framework is the tree-based semantic trans-
018            formation. We employ semantic decomposition and sensitive element drowning
019
               strategies in conjunction with Large Language Models (LLMs) to systematically
               refine adversarial prompts for effective testing. Our comprehensive evaluation
020
               confirms the efficacy of T REANT, which not only exceeds the performance of
021
               state-of-the-art approaches but also achieves a overall success rate of 88.5% on
022            leading T2I models, including DALL·E 3 and Stable Difussion.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027
      Text-to-image (T2I) models, like Stable Diffusion (Rombach et al., 2022; sta, 2023), and DALL·E
028
      3 (dal, 2023), have gained popularity due to the advancements of vision and language generation
029
      techniques. However, a significant ethical concern with these models is their potential to generate
030   Not-Safe-for-Work (NSFW) content, including images depicting violence and illegal activity. To
031   mitigate this threat, model developers implement a variety of techniques to prevent the generation
032   of NSFW content. During training, they use filtering to exclude NSFW content from the training
033   data (llm, 2023), or employ safety alignment strategies to rectify model’s knowledge (ope, 2023).
034   During deployment, safety filters are applied to eliminate any NSFW content produced.
035
      However, there is still no universally effective solution to completely prevent NSFW content gener-
036   ation. Consequently, researchers have proposed adversarial testing techniques (known as red team-
037   ing), which challenge the target T2I model to generate NSFW content, for safety evaluation and
038   assessment. There are two strategies to red teaming T2I models. (1) Some techniques are designed
039   to automatically perturb prompts, leading to the generation of NSFW content (Li et al., 2019; Jin
040   et al., 2020a; Garg and Ramakrishnan, 2020a). (2) Some studies focus on the safety filters of T2I
041   models, and manually craft adversarial prompts to bypass them (Rando et al., 2022; Qu et al., 2023).
042   However, these solutions face three primary limitations. First, they struggle to effectively probe the
043   safety filter, leading to excessive numbers of queries with high cost. Second, they tend to focus more
044   on misleading safety filters rather than bypassing them. The generated content is not well aligned
045   with the original intent. Third, while manually-generated prompts may achieve a high success rate
046   for the specific model, they lack scalability for widespread testing of other T2I models.
047
      This paper presents T REANT, to our best knowledge, the first fully automated red teaming frame-
048   work dedicated to assessing the robustness of T2I models against the generation of NSFW content
049   in a black-box setting. The design of T REANT is inspired by two key observations: (1) text safety
050   filters in T2I models are largely dependent on attention mechanisms that hone in on the contex-
051   tual surroundings of specific keywords. (2) Image safety filters may be circumvented by inundat-
052   ing them with irrelevant non-sensitive content. Based on them, T REANT is bifurcated into two
053   principal stages. (1) Semantic decomposition: it isolates sensitive elements (e.g., references to hu-
      man anatomy), and applies this process recursively to the entire prompt to navigate through text


                                                        1
054
      safety filters. (2) Sensitive element drowning: it exploits the models’ ability to render multiple can-
055   vases within a single output image, by embedding non-sensitive elements onto ancillary canvases to
056   overwhelm the image safety filters. Initiating with an intentionally crafted prompt to elicit NSFW
057   imagery, T REANT progressively refines the input through these two stages, culminating in the gen-
058   eration of a prompt that adeptly elicits the creation of NSFW content by the target model.
059
      We introduce several novel techniques to address the limitations of existing solutions. Specifically,
060
      (1) to enhance the query efficiency, we initially decompose the prompt into a Prompt Parse Tree
061   (PPT), a new representation of objects in the adversarial prompt. We then recursively apply sensitive
062   decomposition and sensitive drown to this tree, streamlining the refinement process and reducing
063   redundant queries to the T2I model. (2) To ensure the alignment between the meaningful content and
064   testing goal, we leverage LLMs to steer the refinement towards this goal. We also employ a semantic
065   preservation technique in sensitive drown to improve the quality of the generated NSFW content,
066   preventing the output from being overwhelmed by irrelevant objects. (3) To achieve scalability,
067   we develop a hybrid algorithm, which coordinates with LLMs to monitor, evaluate, and refine the
068   testing goal in an automated manner.
069   We conduct extensive evaluations to validate the effectiveness of T REANT across multiple prohibited
070   content scenarios. The results clearly demonstrate that T REANT significantly outperforms estab-
071   lished baselines in mitigating NSFW content by T2I models. Notably, it achieves an overall success
072   rate of 88.5%, which is appreciably higher than the closest competitor. These findings underscore
073   the robustness and efficacy of T REANT in enhancing the safety mechanisms of T2I models. We
074   provide open access to the codebase of T REANT and datasets it generates in our anonymous project
075
      website1 , thereby supporting and encouraging reproducibility and further scholarly inquiry.

079   2.1      S AFETY OF T2I M ODELS
080
081   Text-to-image (T2I) models (sta, 2023; dal, 2023) create images from text descriptions (i.e.,
082   prompts). Modern techniques typically use diffusion models, which start with random noise, grad-
083   ually removed through a de-noising network. They often use text embeddings from text encoders.
084   Recent studies explore learning-free and zero-shot image generation in large-scale models.
085   Existing T2I models have the potential to generate ”Not Safe For Work” (NSFW) content, which is
086   unsuitable for public or professional scenarios. This includes graphic violence, pornography, nudity,
087   profanity, or other offensive material (Guzman, 2023). To reduce such risk, T2I services commonly
088   implement safety measures to inspect input texts and output images. Specifically, when a user sub-
089   mits a request, it is first evaluated by a prompt safety filter to ensure it follows content policies.
090   If the prompt passes, the T2I model generates the corresponding image, which then undergoes a
091
      secondary check by an image safety filter. Only images that pass both filters are shown to users, en-
      suring safety and user-friendliness. Notably, open-source models like Stable Diffusion have built-in
092
      safety measures to screen out NSFW content, reducing the need for additional external mechanisms.
093
094   Text-to-image generative models, commonly abbreviated as text-to-image models, translate textual
095   prompts into visual representations and have witnessed considerable advancements in their architec-
096   tural and algorithmic foundations, enhancing the fidelity of the imagery they produce. Present-day
097
      methodologies predominantly harness diffusion-based frameworks, where the generation process
      initiates with a random noise pattern and iteratively refines it using a de-noising mechanism. No-
098
      table implementations of this approach are Stable Diffusion sta (2023) and DALL·E 3 dal (2023),
099
      which incorporate text-driven directives, leveraging the semantic understanding derived from text
100   encoders to shape the resultant images. The field continues to innovate, delving into learning-free
101   and zero-shot capabilities within expansive generative models.

137

143   3.1          OVERVIEW
144
145   We present T REANT, a novel automated framework for adversarial testing of T2I models. The design
146   of T REANT is based on two crucial observations: (1) Adversarial prompts that contain sensitive
147
      words (e.g., ”kill”) can be transformed into less sensitive terms (e.g., ”fighting”) to evade textual
      safety filters. (2) To bypass image safety filters, we can integrate benign elements (e.g., ”red liquid”)
148
      with sensitive terms (e.g., ”blood”). These tactics allow us to guide T2I models to produce NSFW
149
      content while still aligning with the original testing objectives.
150
151   Based on these two observations, T REANT leverages a tree-based mutation strategy to transform a
152   user-defined prompt into an effective adversarial prompt that can induce the generation of NSFW
153
      images. Figure 1 shows the workflow of T REANT, which encompasses four pivotal steps. ❶ Con-
      struction of a Prompt Parse Tree (PPT), our innovative representation that details the relations and
154
      properties of objects within the prompt (§ 3.2). Once the initial PPT is established, ❷ T REANT ex-
155
      ecutes semantic decomposition, a process that segments sensitive elements into non-sensitive com-
156   ponents. This is achieved by decomposing objects in the PPT into new subordinate PPTs. Subse-
157   quently, T REANT converts these newly formed PPTs into new adversarial prompts to circumvent
158   text safety filters (§ 3.3). Following this, ❸ T REANT implements sensitive element drowning (§ 3.4)
159   by introducing non-sensitive elements on different canvas, aiming to evade image safety filters. ❹
160   The effectiveness of the two strategies are evaluated by passing the generated adversarial prompt to
161   the T2I model for image generation. In case of failed generation, the response from the model is
      analyzed to determine whether the text safety filter or image safety filter is not bypassed (§ 3.5), and


                                                                                   3
162
      the mutation strategy is triggered accordingly. This iterative process is performed until a success-
163   ful adversarial prompt is generated or the time budget is used up. Ultimately, T REANT outputs the
164   adversarial prompt alongside the NSFW images produced upon successful generation.

166   3.2   P ROMPT PARSE T REE C ONSTRUCTION
167
168   We introduce Prompt Parse Tree (PPT), a novel structure for encoding relationships and attributes
169   of objects in prompts. Its design is inspired by the concept of Parse Tree in natural language pro-
170   cessing (Meng et al., 2013; Jiang and Diesner, 2019; Ranjan et al., 2016). A parse tree, defined
171   within a grammar G = (V, Σ, R, S), comprises nodes representing non-terminal (V ) and terminal
172   (Σ) symbols, with R as production rules and S as the start symbol. The tree’s yield Yield(T ) is the
173
      string w formed by concatenating all terminal symbols and empty string.
174   Building upon this definition, we formally define the Prompt Parse Tree (PPT) as a hierarchical
175   structure composed of three distinct node types: (1) Object Nodes: they explicitly represent the ac-
176   tual objects referred to in the image. (2) Attribute Nodes: they detail the characteristics or qualities
177   of objects, providing comprehensive descriptions or modifiers and shaping the attributes of the ob-
178   jects mentioned in the prompt. (3) Relation Nodes: they map the relationships between objects or
179
      their sub-components within the prompt. They become crucial when complex objects are broken
      down into sub-elements, thereby clarifying their intricate interconnections.
180
181   Examples. We elucidate the structure of PPT with three examples. Figure 2 (a) depicts the simple
182   prompt “Two men fighting against each other,” which consists of a ‘Fighting’ relation node branch-
183   ing out into two ‘Object Nodes,’ ‘Man1’ and ‘Man2,’ each representing the individuals in fighting.
184   In Figure 2 (b), the prompt complexity increases: “Two men are fighting against each other in a
185
      church.” Here, the ‘Contain’ relation node indicates the encompassing setting of the action, branch-
      ing into a ‘Church’ object node for location, and a ‘Fighting’ relation node further splitting into
186
      ‘Man1’ and ‘Man2.’ Figure 2 (c) shows an even more detailed prompt: “One strong man is fighting
187
      against another man in a glorious church. Red liquid in the church.” The ‘Contain’ node is the
188   root, with branches to the ‘Church’ object node, the ‘Fighting’ relation node, and the ‘Liquid’ object
189   node. ‘Man1’ has an ‘Attribute Node’ of ‘strong,’ and the ‘Church’ has ‘glorious,’ while ‘Liquid’
190   is marked with ’red.’ These examples display how PPT dissects prompts into a hierarchical tree,
191   delineating object relationships and attributes within the context.
192
      Our PPT construction grammar begins with the Relation Node, treated as the start symbol S. The
193
      Attribute Node is the terminal symbol (Σ) because it describes its parent Object Node. If an object
194   lacks attribute nodes, we also designate the Object Node as the terminal symbol (Σ). The Relation
195   Node is our non-terminal symbol (V ) as it typically has an Object Node as a leaf node. The pro-
196   cess of deriving Yield and production rules R are executed effectively by LLMs, ensuring the PPT
197   accurately represents the prompt’s syntactic and semantic structure.
198
      Given a predefined testing goal, i.e., adversarial prompt, from testers, T REANT constructs the initial
199
      PPT, which includes a tree of nodes and edges, each characterized by specific properties. This
200   foundational PPT is not static, and it will undergo iterative refinement in subsequent steps.
201

      3.3   S EMANTIC D ECOMPOSITION
203
204   We design a novel algorithm, Semantic Decomposition, to process the prompts generated from
205   PPT. The goal of this algorithm is to circumvent the text safety filter by transforming sensitive
206   elements into non-sensitive ones. Inspired by Observation (1) in § 3.1, we disassemble highly sen-
207   sitive parts of the text and then disperse them throughout the entire prompt. This process effectively
208   reduces the concentration of sensitive elements, facilitating their passage through safety filters.
209   The reason behind this technique is as follows: the attention mechanism (Brauwers and Frasincar,
210   2021) allows T2I models to focus on relevant input parts when predicting outputs, thus capturing
211   contextual information effectively. A key feature is its “locality” property (Brauwers and Frasincar,
212   2021), which gives more weight to the immediate neighborhood around an element, enhancing the
213   model’s ability to generate coherent and contextually relevant outputs. Due to such mechanism, less
214   sensitive short phrases could be identified as risky when grouped together. By randomly distributing
215
      these formatted descriptions, interspersing less sensitive phrases among many non-sensitive ones,
      we can adeptly bypass text safety filters.

      3.4   S ENSITIVE E LEMENT D ROWNING
306
307
308   Once text safety filters are circumvented, the ensuing images may still be subject to image safety
309
      filters, which are often more rigorous. We introduce another novel technique, Sensitive Element
      Drowning, to bypass the image safety filters. The design of this technique is inspired by Observa-
310
      tion (2) in § 3.1: T2I models possess the capability to generate multiple canvases simultaneously.
311
      This feature enables us to submerge sensitive elements on one canvas while inundating other can-
312   vases with a plethora of non-sensitive elements, which may lead to the overloading of image safety
313   filters. To avert the dilution of the intended target image with irrelevant elements, our method in-
314   volves explicitly instructing, via the prompt, to divide the image into several canvases. Subsequently,
315   we populate these separate canvases with non-sensitive elements, dedicating a single canvas to the
316   target image. This technique of prompt augmentation is contextually independent of the original
317   adversarial intent, thereby allowing seamless integration to create an augmented prompt.
318   Examples. Figure 4 illustrates an example of this strategy using PPT. Specifically, Figure 4 (a)
319   shows the original statement where a prompt contains potential sensitive elements that are difficult
320   to decompose further. Figure 4 (b) demonstrates the strategy in action by introducing an additional
321   canvas containing non-sensitive elements (e.g., ‘beautiful woman’), which is contextually indepen-
322   dent from the sensitive content. This additional canvas is designed to potentially saturate the image
323   safety filter with excess semantic content, thereby diverting attention from the sensitive elements
      depicted on the adjacent canvas.

      Baselines. We benchmark T REANT against existing adversarial testing approaches as below: (1)
340
      SneakyPrompt (Yang et al., 2023): This approach utilizes reinforcement learning to iteratively re-
341
      fine adversarial prompts. By continuously interacting with the target T2I model, SneakyPrompt
342   aims to induce the generation of NSFW content, testing the robustness of model safety filters. (2)
343   BAE (Garg and Ramakrishnan, 2020a): This method adopts a token manipulation strategy, specifi-
344   cally focusing on token replacement and insertion. It works by masking portions of the original text
345   and utilizing BERT Masked Language Model to suggest alternative tokens that could fit the masked
346   context, effectively testing the filters’ resilience to subtle linguistic changes. (3) TextFooler (Jin
347   et al., 2020a): This solution employs a synonym substitution technique to evade safety filters. It re-
348   places critical words in the text with their synonyms, preserving the semantic content while altering
349   the prompt’s structure enough to potentially bypass the safety mechanisms.
350   Experimental Setup. Our experiments are conducted on a high-performance workstation equipped
351   with the following specifications: operating system Ubuntu 22.04.3 LTS, powered by 2 NVIDIA
352   3090 GPUs, each with 24GB of memory. For detailed results and more comprehensive information
353   regarding our implementation, please refer to our website1 .
354   To ensure consistency and reproducibility, we impose a strict time limit of ten minutes for each
355   method during every trial, focusing specifically on generating a single adversarial prompt per run.
356   Furthermore, to reduce variability and ensure robust statistical analysis, we repeat each experiment
357   ten times. In the interest of fairness and comparability across all tested methods, we limit the number
358   of queries to 6 for all baselines during the trials.
359
      Dataset. In contrast to the comprehensive content compliance checks provided by OpenAI (ope
360   (2023)), the current publicly available NSFW prompt datasets only include obscene content. To
361   more thoroughly test T REANT’s performance, we have created our own dataset, denoted as NSFW-
362   1k. Building upon the approaches of previous works (Yang et al., 2023; Niu et al., 2024; Shayegani
363   et al., 2023), we take inspiration from a Reddit post (red, 2023) and use ChatGPT (cha, 2023) to
364   generate 100 target prompts for 11 different scenarios prohibited by OpenAI’s content policy (ope,
365   2023), specifically focusing on NSFW content. This process results in a total of 1100 adversarial
366   prompts. In addition, we also conducted extensive testing on the NSFW-200 dataset proposed by
367   Yang et al. (2023), which contains 200 prompts containing obscene content.
368   Target T2I Models for Evaluation. To assess T REANT, we selected four leading T2I models, in-
369   cluding one commercial and three open-source options, all equipped with advanced text and image
370   safety filters designed to block inappropriate content. Specifically, (1) DALL·E 3 (dal, 2023), de-
371   veloped by OpenAI, excels in interpreting complex prompts and generating high-quality images. (2)
372   Stable Diffusion (sta, 2023), a widely respected open-source model, is evaluated via its API. We
373   tested multiple versions—v1.4, v2.1, and XL—to account for variations in text comprehension and
374
      image generation capabilities.
375   Metrics. We evaluate adversarial testing of T2I models using two metrics: (1) Success Rate: The
376   percentage of adversarial prompts that successfully generate NSFW content. Each sample is verified
377   by GPT-4 Vision and manually checked for validity. (2) Number of Queries: The number of queries
      needed to generate a successful adversarial prompt, with fewer queries indicating higher efficiency.
