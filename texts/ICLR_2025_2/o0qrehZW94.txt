011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Recent breakthroughs in text-guided image generation have significantly advanced
014
               the field of 3D generation. While generating a single high-quality 3D object is
               now feasible, generating multiple objects with reasonable interactions within a
015
               3D space, a.k.a. compositional 3D generation, presents substantial challenges.
016
               This paper introduces C OMP GS, a novel generative framework that employs 3D
017            Gaussian Splatting (GS) for efficient, compositional text-to-3D content generation.
018            To achieve this goal, two core designs are proposed: (1) 3D Gaussians Initialization
019            with 2D compositionality: We transfer the well-established 2D compositionality
020            to initialize the Gaussian parameters on an entity-by-entity basis, ensuring both
021            consistent 3D priors for each entity and reasonable interactions among multiple
022            entities; (2) Dynamic Optimization: We propose a dynamic strategy to optimize 3D
023            Gaussians using Score Distillation Sampling (SDS) loss. C OMP GS first automati-
024            cally decomposes 3D Gaussians into distinct entity parts, enabling optimization at
025
               both the entity and composition levels. Additionally, C OMP GS optimizes across
               objects of varying scales by dynamically adjusting the spatial parameters of each
026
               entity, enhancing the generation of fine-grained details, particularly in smaller
027
               entities. Qualitative comparisons and quantitative evaluations on T3 Bench demon-
028            strate the effectiveness of C OMP GS in generating compositional 3D objects with
029            superior image quality and semantic alignment over existing methods. C OMP GS
030            can also be easily extended to controllable 3D editing, facilitating complex scene
031            generation. We hope C OMP GS will provide new insights to the compositional 3D
032            generation. Codes will be released to the research community.

035   1    I NTRODUCTION
036
037   3D content creation is essential to the modern media industry, yet it has traditionally been labour-
038   intensive and necessitated professional expertise. Typically, designing a single 3D object takes
039
      several hours for an experienced designer, and creating complex scenes with multiple 3D objects (as
      shown in Fig. 1) requires even more effort. Inspired by the recent success of diffusion models in
040
      the text-to-image generation Ho et al. (2020); Song et al. (2020a); Rombach et al. (2022a); Podell
041
      et al. (2023), much research has focused on exploring 2D diffusion models for text-to-3D generation.
042   Previous work can be divided into two main methodologies: (1) Feed-forward generation Li et al.
043   (2023); Hong et al. (2023); Xu et al. (2024), which entails training generalizable diffusion models on
044   3D data; and (2) Optimization-based generation Poole et al. (2022); Lin et al. (2023); Metzer et al.
045   (2023); Chen et al. (2023b); Wang et al. (2023a; 2024), which utilizes the pretrained 2D diffusion
046   guidance to optimize 3D representations via Score Distillation Sampling (SDS) Poole et al. (2022).
047
      While existing work has demonstrated the feasibility of generating single 3D objects, they often
048   struggle to produce compositional 3D content with multiple objects and complex interactions. For ex-
049   ample, (1) Feed-forward generation methods struggle in generalizing to complex textual descriptions
050   since the amount of 3D training data is extremely limited Deitke et al. (2023; 2024), and most of the
051   data contains only one object; (2) Optimization-based generation methods face significant challenges
052   with current 2D diffusion guidance when optimizing compositional 3D objects. Typically, using 2D
053   diffusion guidance to optimize a single object is feasible, as itвАЩs easy to incorporate various attributes
      into a single object. However, in compositional 3D generation, 2D guidance struggles to accurately

      3D Gaussians Initialization with 2D Compositionality Unlike the implicit representation, i.e.,
      NeRF Mildenhall et al. (2021), C OMP GS uses 3D Gaussians as the representation, which helps
094
      to achieve feasible parameter initialization with a coarse 3D shape Jun & Nichol (2023); Yi et al.

      (2023). As shown in the bottom row of Fig. 1, we first apply a text-to-image model Betker et al.

098   (a.k.a. entities) according to the entity information in the given prompt. These segmented entities
099   are processed through an image-to-3D model Hong et al. (2023); Tochilkin et al. (2024) to obtain
100   coarse 3D shapes, which are used to roughly initialize the Gaussian parameters in 3D space, thereby
101   transferring the 2D compositionality to 3D representations.
102   Dynamic Optimization. Current 2D guidance Poole et al. (2022); Lin et al. (2023); Metzer et al.
103   (2023) struggles with optimizing multiple 3D objects simultaneously in a scene, often leading to 3D
104   inconsistencies and semantic shifts. To address these issues, we introduce a dynamic optimization
105   strategy based on Score Distillation Sampling (SDS) loss, consisting of two key components: (1)
106   C OMP GS dynamically divides the training process to optimize different parts of 3D Gaussians.
107   Specifically, it alternates between optimizing a single object (entity-level optimization) and the entire
      scene (composition-level optimization). This is achieved by labeling and filtering the Gaussian

      parameters for inference, and updating them through masking gradients. (2) C OMP GS dynami-
109   cally trains the entity-level Gaussians within a normalized 3D space, crucial for compositional 3D
110   generation where object sizes vary. This is particularly challenging when dealing with very small
111   objects, as optimizing Gaussian parameters in a limited 3D space may not adequately capture detailed
112   textures. To mitigate this, we first scale the subspace of each entity to a predefined, standardized

135   Multi-modality 3D Generation Multi-modality 3D generation can basically categorized into the
136
      feed-forward system and optimization-based system. The former one is trained end-to-end on multi-
      view dataset Chang et al. (2015); Deitke et al. (2023) for zero-shot generation, typically based on
137
      3D representations, including NeRF Hong et al. (2023); Tochilkin et al. (2024), 3D Gaussians Xu
138
      et al. (2024); Tang et al. (2024), tri-planes Shue et al. (2023); Wang et al. (2023b) and feature
139   grids Karnewar et al. (2023). Despite the excellent performance achieved, the single-object training
140   data Deitke et al. (2023); Luo et al. (2024); Deitke et al. (2024); Chang et al. (2015) limits their
141   generative abilities in scenarios involving multiple objects. Optimization-based 3D systems lift
142   2D diffusion priors Rombach et al. (2022a;b); Podell et al. (2023); Chen et al. (2023a; 2024a) for
143   3D generation, and typically train 3D representations on a prompt-by-prompt basis. For example,
144   DreamFusion Poole et al. (2022) introduces Score Distillation Sampling (SDS) loss to transfer 2D
145   diffusion models into the 3D domain. Magic3D Qian et al. (2023) employs a coarse-to-fine scheme to
146   improve both efficiency and effectiveness, while Fantasia3D Chen et al. (2023b) decouples the mod-
147
      elling of geometry and appearance. Despite the advancements of these methods addressing various
      challenges, they also usually struggle with compositional 3D generation. T3 benchвАЩs examination of
148
      ten prominent optimization-based models Poole et al. (2022); Lin et al. (2023); Metzer et al. (2023);
149
      Wang et al. (2024; 2023a); Chen et al. (2023b); Shi et al. (2023); Yi et al. (2023) revealed frequent
150   issues in compositional generation. The implicit 2D diffusion guidance used in these methods Poole
151   et al. (2022); Shi et al. (2023); Wang et al. (2024); Chen et al. (2023b) often fails to maintain 3D
152   consistency across different views, leading to significant discrepancies in compositional 3D scenes.
153
      Compositional Generation Compositional generation involves creating content involving multiple
154
      objects with logical interactions. These interactions include, but are not limited to, concept rela-
155
      tions Liu et al. (2022a), attribute association with colors Chefer et al. (2023); Feng et al. (2022); Park
156   et al. (2021), and spatial relationships between objects Wu et al. (2023); Chen et al. (2024b). There is
157   considerable focus on various aspects of compositional 2D generation, such as learning from human
158   feedback Zhang et al. (2023); Lee et al. (2023); Dong et al. (2023); Yang et al. (2024), enhancing
159   image captions Chen et al. (2023a; 2024a); Betker et al. (2023); Dai et al. (2023), designing effective
160   networks Liu et al. (2022b), and etc. T2I-CompBench Huang et al. (2023) further proposed a com-
161   prehensive benchmark and boosted the compositionality of text-to-image models. Compared to 2D
      compositional generation, its 3D counterpart is under-explored due to 3D geometry and appearance

162
      complexities. For example, Set-the-Scene Cohen-Bar et al. (2023) and Scenewiz3d Zhang et al.
163   (2024b) propose to adopt object layouts to generate compositional scenes. However, they are mainly
164   based on the implicit NeRF representations, so the features between objects cannot be well decoupled,
165   resulting in a relatively blurred rendering effect. VP3D Chen et al. (2024c) takes a different strategy
166   to employ visual features for compositional generation. However, its visual features are only used as

      comparisons are in the appendix. We propose C OMP GS to address the above challenges, emphasizing

237   3.2       C OMP GS
238
239   We propose C OMP GS for efficiently transferring the 2D compositionality to facilitate compositional
240   3D generation with 3D Gaussians. The overall framework of C OMP GS is shown in Fig. 2. We first
241   generate a well-composed image from the given complex prompt. After extracting the sub-object
242
      (also named entity) information within the prompt by LLM, we utilize the entity prompt to segment
      the composed image into different parts. Each part will be adopted to initialize a specific space
243
      of Gaussians. During the training stage, we propose a dynamic SDS optimization strategy. This
244
      strategy first automatically decomposes the training to optimize either entity-level Gaussians or
245   composition-level Gaussians. Then, it employs a volume-adaptive strategy to dynamically optimize
246   varying-sized entities within a consistent 3D space. We detail the initialization process and dynamic
247   SDS optimization in the following sections.
248
249   3.2.1        3D G AUSSIANS I NITIALIZATION WITH 2D C OMPOSITIONALITY
250
251   Unlike the implicit NeRF Mildenhall et al. (2021) representations, explicit 3D Gaussians can be
252   easily initialized with 3D shapes and colors, which facilitates introducing rough 3D priors to ensure
253   3D consistency Yi et al. (2023). Although existing text-to-3D or image-to-3D models can generate
254
      a single 3D object, they struggle with compositional 3D generation as mentioned in Sec. 2, which
      hinders the creation of compositional 3D priors needed for initialization. Considering this, we propose
255
      initializing the compositional Gaussians on an entity-by-entity basis.
256
257   As shown in Fig. 2 (left), given a complex prompt V , (e.g., вАШan owl perches on a branch near a
258   pineconeвАЩ), we first adopt a 2D diffusion model Betker et al. (2023) to generate a composed image I
259   that faithfully captures the compositionality of multiple objects. We extract each entity information
260
      in prompt V by prompting LLM to obtain L different entity-level prompts (i.e., L = 3 for v1 вАШan owlвАЩ
      , v2 вАШa branchвАЩ , and v3 вАШa pineconeвАЩ ). These prompts are adopted in a text-guided segmentation
261
      model Kirillov et al. (2023) to decompose image I into various parts {Il }. Until now, each segmented
262
      image has only one entity, facilitating using existing image-based 3D generation models Tochilkin
263   et al. (2024) to predict a rough triangle mesh ml (l вИИ L) of the corresponding 3D entity l. To initialize
264   3D Gaussians ќЄl , we index N points from each mesh; the center positions ¬µli вИИ R3 (i вИИ N, l вИИ L) are
265   the centers of each vertex of ml , and the texture colors cli вИИ R3 (i вИИ N, l вИИ L) are queried from each
266   vertex of ml . During the image-to-3D process, we did not perform any cropping or padding on the
267   image Il , ensuring that the spatial positions of each mesh ml correspond to their 2D spatial positions.
268   Additionally, we have marked the 3D layouts of each entity asset with 3D bounding boxes bboxl
269   for subsequent optimization. The bounding box coordinates are determined by the outer-most center
      positions of the entity Gaussian.

      3.2.2    DYNAMIC SDS O PTIMIZATION
271
272   As mentioned in Sec. 2, existing SDS methods Poole et al. (2022); Shi et al. (2023); Wang et al.
273   (2024); Chen et al. (2023b) struggle in optimizing compositional 3D scenes due to the challenges
274   of using implicit diffusion priors to maintain multi-objects consistency and interactions. To address
275   this, we introduce dynamic SDS optimization to enable existing SDS losses to remain effective
276   in compositional generation. Dynamic SDS optimization is performed through the following two
277   procedures.
278   Automatically Decomposing Optimization to Different Levels We adopt the Decomposed Op-
279   timization (DO) strategy that divides the entire training into L + 1 stages, including L stages for
280   entity-level optimization to ensure 3D consistency of each L entities, and one stage for composition-
281   level optimization to refine inter-entity interactions. In each training iteration, we randomly render
282   either (1) an entity Gaussian ќЄl (l вИИ L) to obtain the entity image xl = g(ќЄl ) (ќЄl вИИ bboxl ), or (2)
283   the composed Gaussians ќЄ to obtain the composed image x = g(ќЄ). Here g denotes the Gaussian
284
      Splatting rendering Kerbl et al. (2023) described in Eq. 1.
285   To update Gaussian parameters, we apply Eq. 3 for gradients backpropagation. Specifically, for
286   entity-level optimization, we substitute the text embedding v by vl and parameters ќЄ by ќЄl in Eq. 3 to
287   optimize entity Gaussian. We adopt MVDream Shi et al. (2023) as ѕХ, which provides multi-view
288   diffusion priors that can better maintain consistency in 3D entity modelling. To ensure that other
289   entityвАЩs parameters remain unchanged, we mask the gradients that are not within the corresponding 3D
290
      bounding boxes bboxl when updating ќЄl . Besides, for composition-level optimization, we integrate
      both 2D Rombach et al. (2022a) and 3D diffusion priors Shi et al. (2023) to jointly optimize the
291
      overall Gaussian parameters ќЄ. The rationale is that 2D priors promote geometry exploration Qian
292
      et al. (2023), which enhances the generation of plausible interactions between different entities.
293
294   Volume-adaptively Optimizing Entity-level Gaussians Compared to the single object generation,
295   compositional 3D generation using SDS presents additional challenges. Specifically, when objects
296
      vary in size, optimizing directly in the original 3D space often results in suboptimal generation for
      smaller objects, as shown the вАШpineconeвАЩ in Fig. 2. To mitigate this issue, we propose a Volume-
297
      adaptive Optimization (VAO) strategy that enhances optimization across objects of varying sizes by
298
      dynamically standardizing each entityвАЩs space to a standardized volumetric scale. This approach is
299   especially beneficial for improving the generation of fine-grained texture details in smaller entities.
300
301   To scale the 3D bounding box of an entity Gaussian ќЄl with a standardized volumetric space bboxstd ,
302
      we first determine the necessary transformations for the centre positions ¬µ. Specifically, we calculate
      the shift parameters ќ≤, and scale parameters ќї, as follows:
303

305

307   standardized space. Then, we zoom-in the entity Gaussian on its center positions by ¬µћВ = ќї(¬µ вИТ ќ≤),

      3.3     P ROGRESSIVE E DITING WITH C OMP GS

315
      The dynamic SDS optimization enables C OMP GS to explicitly control the generation of different
316   parts in the 3D scene. This capability can be utilized for progressive 3D editing in compositional
317   generation. Specifically, given well-trained 3D Gaussians ќЄ, we begin by rendering an image from
318   the front view, denoted as x = g(ќЄ). Unlike previous works that directly edit 3D objects Cheng et al.
319   (2024), we adopt MagicBrush Zhang et al. (2024a) to edit the 2D rendered image x. The edited 2D
320   image can then be utilized to initialize new Gaussians ќЄћВ in the original 3D space. We further train the
321   added 3D Gaussians to finally introduce new objects into the compositional scenes. For example,
322   as shown in Fig. 1, given well-trained 3D Gaussians (e.g., вАШan owlвАЩ), we progressively edit the 2D
323   images to add new objects, such as the вАШbranchвАЩ and вАШpinecone.вАЩ We then train the corresponding 3D
      Gaussians via dynamic SDS optimization to incorporate these new elements into the 3D scenes.

      Implementation Details We implemented C OMP GS using ThreeStudio Guo et al. (2023).
329
      We use DALL¬ЈE Betker et al. (2023) to generate the well-established 2D compositionality,
330
      LangSAM Medeiros (2024) to segment the entity image, and TripoSR Tochilkin et al. (2024) to
331   generate a preliminary 3D prior (i.e., mesh) from each entity image, respectively. During the training,
332   we employed MVDream Shi et al. (2023) to optimize entity-level Gaussians, and both MVDream Shi
333   et al. (2023) and stabilityai/stablediffusion-2-1-base Rombach et al. (2022b) to optimize composition-
334   level Gaussians. The guidance scale for diffusion models was set as 50. Timestamps were uniformly
335   selected from 0.02 to 0.55 for the first 1,000 iterations and then adjusted to a range from 0.02 to
336   0.15 for subsequent iterations. We initialized the Gaussian points at N = 10k, and progressively
337   increased the density to a maximum of 1000k points. We optimized the overall compositional scenes
338   through 10k iterations to achieve optimal results. However, it is worth noting that we empirically
339
      found that training with 5k iterations already produces plausible 3D assets. The learning rates are
      detailed in Appendix A.3. We set the camera parameters, including radius, azimuth, elevation and
340
      FoV (field of view) to be the same as Shi et al. (2023). Experiments were conducted on NVIDIA
341
      A100 GPUs(40G). Our code will be released.
342
343   Evaluation Metrics In addition to user studies and qualitative comparisons, we utilize T3 Bench He
344   et al. (2023) to render 300 prompts in order to evaluate compositional 3D generation on two criteria:
345
      (1) the visual quality of the 3D objects and (2) the alignment between the 3D objects and the input
      prompt. For quality evaluation, we captured multi-focal and multi-view images and sent them to
346
      text-image scoring models, CLIP Radford et al. (2021), to obtain an average quality score of the
347
      generated 3D scene. Regarding the textual alignment, we first followed Luo et al. (2024) to perform
348   3D captioning via BLIP Li et al. (2022) and GPT4, and then computed the recall of the original
349   prompt within the generated caption via ROUGE-L Lin (2004).

      Qualitative Evaluation In Fig. 3, we present qualitative comparisons of compositional 3D generation.
354
      Our model, C OMP GS, is compared with several open-source methods, including DreamFusion Poole
355   et al. (2022), Magic3D Qian et al. (2023), Latent-NeRF Metzer et al. (2023), Fantasia3D Chen
356   et al. (2023b), SJC Wang et al. (2023a), and ProlificDreamer Wang et al. (2024), as well as methods
357   specifically designed to handle intricate text prompts, VP3D Chen et al. (2024c) in Fig. 3. The
358   results demonstrate that C OMP GS generates compositional 3D objects with superior quality, greater
359   consistency, and more plausible interactions. DreamFusion, for example, fails to generate reasonable
360   multi-objects from compositional prompts, revealing limitations in SDS loss for optimizing multiple
361   objects simultaneously. Magic3D and Latent-NeRF, though guided by a 3D mesh prior, struggle
362   with generating complex and compositional 3D geometry. Despite employing advanced SDS loss,
363
      Fantasia3D, SJC, and ProlificDreamer only improve the appearance of the content but do not
      fundamentally address multi-object generation. Notably, VP3D employs features from compositional
364
      images and human feedback to guide compositional 3D generation. However, it often produces
365
      unexpected artifacts (e.g., redundant dots in the top two rows of Fig. 3) or less plausible interactions
366   (e.g., strange spatial relationships in the fourth row). This is likely due to the insufficient utilization of
367   2D compositionality, as VP3D adopts the implicit image features to guide optimization. In contrast,
368   C OMP GS generates high-quality and composed content that strictly aligns with the given complex
369   prompts, demonstrating its effectiveness over others. We provide more visual comparisons with
370   both the close-source and open-source methods (including GALA3D Zhou et al. (2024) in Fig. 9,
371   GraphDreamer Gao et al. (2024), DreamGaussian Tang et al. (2023) in Fig. 10, etc.) in Appendix A.4
372   as well as the attached video.
373   We also demonstrate C OMP GSвАЩs capabilities in generating more diverse contents in Fig. 4. C OMP GS
374   accurately generates both simple spatial relationships (shown in the first row), and more complex
375   interactions, where objects should react to each other (shown in the second row). Besides, C OMP GS
376   is not limited to generating just two objects, it can generate multiple objects by expanding the number
377   of entity-level Gaussians. For example, in the third row, C OMP GS generates scenes with three and
      four entities, each with high-quality 3D shapes and textures.

528   5     C ONCLUSION
529
530   In this paper, we introduced C OMP GS, a user-friendly, optimization-based framework, which achieves
531   compositional text-to-3D generation utilizing Gaussian Splatting. Our three core designs, including
532   initializing 3D Gaussians with 2D compositionality, decomposed optimization for either entity-level
533   or composition-level Gaussians, and volume-adaptive optimization, contribute to the success of
534   C OMP GS. Through both qualitative and quantitative experiments, we demonstrate C OMP GS is
535   capable of generating complex interactions between multiple entities in 3D scene. We hope that our
536
      work inspires further innovation and advancements in the compositional 3D generation.
537   Impacts and Limitations As a text-to-3D model, we do not foresee obvious undesirable ethical/social
538   impacts. One limitation is that when the given prompt includes backgrounds (e.g., ground, sky),
539   C OMP GS may fail to generate these elements adequately. This is due to the current text-guided
      segmentation modelвАЩs inability to segment such abstract concepts. We leave it for future exploration.

       A.8     FAILURE C ASES
1179
1180
       As discussed in Sec. 5, C OMP GS exhibits limitations in generating backgrounds, such as ground and
1181
       sky. This is likely due to the current text-guided segmentation modelвАЩs inability to effectively segment
1182   these abstract concepts. When the background is not well-segmented, we lose the corresponding
1183   2D compositionality needed for initializing 3D Gaussians. This leads to two failure cases: (1) the
1184   absence of background in the compositional 3D scenes, as seen with the missing grass in the second
1185   column of Fig. 13, or (2) background generation of poor visual quality, such as the vague and unclear
1186   depiction of grass in the first column of Fig. 13. ItвАЩs crucial to note that such limitations, whilst exist,
1187   are not the focus of this work. These shortcomings can be overcome by enhancing the capabilities of
       off-the-shelf models, effectively mitigating the manifested issues.
