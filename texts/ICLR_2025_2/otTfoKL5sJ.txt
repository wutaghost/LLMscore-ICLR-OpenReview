                                                   A BSTRACT
008
009
               To know your enemy, you must become your enemy. Sun Tzu stated in The Art
010
               of War. Often, it is crucial to synthesize data containing harmful content using
011            large language models (LLMs) in order to train harmless LLMs. Methods by
012            which synthesized data can be utilized include using it as training data to provide
013            negative signals to the model, as automatic red-teaming data to identify vulner-
014            abilities of the model and more. However, aligned LLMs struggle to generate
015            harmful responses. In this paper, we propose the refusal-free training method to
016            reach a Helpful-Only LLM that maintains the helpfulness of the state-of-the-
017            art (SOTA) LLMs while allowing harmful response generation. The refusal-free
018            training method filters the instances that refuse an user’s request from the datasets.
019
               We demonstrate that the refusal-free training dramatically decreases the rate at
               which the LLM generates refusal responses (refusal rate) by 60.12% without sac-
020
               rificing its helpfulness. Also, we are aware of the possibility that the progress
021
               in this direction could lead to irreversible consequences. A powerful model that
022            does not reject harmful requests and executes them all could be exploited for il-
023            licit purposes such as the creation of indiscriminate weapons or hacking. However,
024            once again, we believe it is important to be the one to break an LLM and study
025            how an LLM can be broken in advance, including understanding the boundaries
026            a Helpful-Only LLM can reach and identifying its inherent tendencies. We em-
027            phasize that this study is wholly for academic purpose and is aimed at paving the
028            way toward a harmless LLM. This study calls for the researchers to acknowledge
029            the potential failures of LLMs and take steps to prevent the breakdowns. Con-
030
               tent Warning: This paper contains examples that may be offensive in nature, and
               reader discretion is recommended.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
034
035
      As the potential of LLMs rises, the value of harmlessness has been consistently emphasized as
036   a key value they should be aligned with (Askell et al., 2021). Most of the SOTA LLMs make
037   considerable efforts to demonstrate the extent of their commitment to harmlessness (Achiam et al.,
038   2023; OpenAI, 2024; Anthropic, 2024; Dubey et al., 2024; Reid et al., 2024). Many organizations
039   emphasize ensuring harmlessness, as LLMs that evolve without this consideration could lead to
040   catastrophic risks and be exploited for illicit purposes such as the creation of indiscriminate weapons
041   or hacking (Hendrycks et al., 2023).
042   In line with this awareness, continuous efforts have been made to align the models with harmless-
043   ness. The efforts include, but are not limited to, tuning the model itself to be more robust to attack
044   queries and generate harmless responses (Bai et al., 2022a;b; Dai et al., 2023), integrating a separate
045   system level safety filter with the model (Markov et al., 2023; Inan et al., 2023; Zeng et al., 2024),
046   and applying a guardrail prompt to the model (Jiang et al., 2023; Lyu et al., 2024; Zheng et al.,
047   2024). As a result of these efforts, today’s SOTA LLMs demonstrate strong alignment with safety
048   considerations. However, this accompanied with certain drawbacks.
049   Data that contains harmful content is essential, as it serves as training data to provide negative signals
050   to the model, and as evaluation data to assess the status the model has reached. Furthermore, even in
051   the presence of harmful data, the capability to generate new harmful data can be highly beneficial,
052   as red-teaming plays crucial role in harmlessness alignment by identifying the vulnerabilities of
053   the models and addressing them in advance (Brundage et al., 2020; Xu et al., 2021). Identifying
      the points where the models fail is a widely adopted step in most machine learning tasks (Xu et al.,

      Most of the organizations that develop LLMs invest considerable effort in defining the policy in
083
      detail. 1 2 3 4 The policy can evolve as time passes. Due to factors such as the discovery of new
084   vulnerabilities or issues that were previously inconsequential but have become significant in light of
085   real-world developments, the policy must adapt with flexibility (Mu et al., 2024). Once the policy
086   has changed, the model must be trained on new data that follows updated policy. However, a model
087   aligned with specific policy struggles to generate the data that adheres to other policies.
088
      Therefore, in situations where a new policy is necessary, the Helpful-Only LLM, aligned with
089
      helpfulness but not with harmlessness (i.e. not with any safety policy), is often employed (Bai et al.,
090   2022b; Mu et al., 2024). The objective of employing a Helpful-Only LLM is to ensure that no
091   user request is refused. Since it complies with any user request, it not only demonstrate the ability to
092   adapt to various safety policy, but also mitigates the prior challenge of generating harmful responses.
093   The data or weight of the Helpful-Only LLM has not been released, but based on the description
094   in the papers, it can be inferred that the model is trained on a dataset from which data collected for
095   harmlessness has been excluded from the entire dataset.
096   A large number of open-source chat instruction datasets (Taori et al., 2023; Chiang et al., 2023;
097   Ding et al., 2023; Ivison et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2024a; Zhao et al., 2024; Cui et al., 2023; Xu et al.,
098   2024b) for training LLMs have been released, leading to the development of numerous models that
099   demonstrate strong performance based on these datasets. We found that, despite the fact that these
100   datasets were not originally collected with a focus on harmlessness alignment, models trained on
101   them exhibit an inherent alignment with harmlessness. We conjecture that this inherent alignment
102   arises from the fact that most of the datasets synthesize data using well-aligned LLMs to distill their

158   2.1   I NPUT- BASED R ED T EAMING
159
160   Natural language prompt-based approaches. Natural approaches (Bhardwaj & Poria, 2023; Anil
161   et al., 2024) seek to subvert the safety policy in an intuitive fashion, either by assigning the model a
      malicious role or appending a few failure examples as natural language form prefix prompts before


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      the input request. While these approaches were effective for early LLMs, they quickly became in-
163   effective as safety alignment reinforced and safety policy evolved. In a more creative way, jailbreak
164   approaches (Shen et al., 2023; Zhou & Wang, 2024) that utilize rather unconventional language
165   continue to emerge, but it is only a matter of time before these too are blocked.
166
      Gradient-based approaches. The approaches that utilize the gradients of the target model to iden-
167
      tify adversarial inputs (Zou et al., 2023; Wichers et al., 2024; Geisler et al., 2024) may also break the
168
      model. However, these approaches have a critical limitation in that they require access to the weight
169   of the target model. Furthermore, all of the input-based red teaming methods, including natural
170   language prompt-based approaches, suffer from serious side effects of compromising the model’s
171   overall capabilities (Mizrahi et al., 2024).
172
173   2.2   R ED T EAMING M ODEL T RAINING
174
175   Red-LM. This approach involves training a separate model with the objective of eliciting harmful
176   responses from the target model (Perez et al., 2022; Hong et al., 2024; Lee et al., 2024; Jiang et al.,
177   2024). Often, the methods primarily utilize RL as a key technique, as the reward can be easily
178
      defined. This approach has a significant limitation in that it can only trigger harmful responses
      from the target model. In order to adapt to policy changes, which is one of the target tasks of
179
      harmlessness alignment, it occasionally requires to trigger the responses comply with the requests
180
      that were previously refused. However, this approach is incapable of perform this task, as it has
181   never trained the such reward.
182
183   Forgetting Safety. This approach involves further fine-tuning of a pre-aligned model using data
184
      from diverse distribution (Qi et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2023; Zhan et al., 2023). The methods suc-
      cessfully remove the alignment of the model. However, this approach suffers from the infamous
185
      issue of catastrophic forgetting (French, 1999). Additionally, the distribution of the data it further
186
      trains on has a critical impact on its capabilities (Qi et al., 2023).

241   rejected response refuses the prompt discourages the refusal responses.

243   3.4.1     AUTOMATIC R EFUSAL F ILTER
244
245   The automatic refusal filter let an LLM classify refusals. It can be any model that can classify
246   refusals. For example, instruction-prompted (Achiam et al., 2023), Chain-of-Thought, few-shot, or
247   fine-tuned LLMs (Xie et al., 2024) could be employed as the automatic refusal filter.
248
249   3.4.2     RULE - BASED R EFUSAL F ILTER
250
251
      The automatic filter is not perfect and may fail with data from distributions it has never encountered
      during training (i.e. out-of-distribution (OOD)). Inspired by the exact-match of advbench (Zou et al.,
252
      2023), to supplement the automatic filter, we introduce the rule-based refusal filter which classifies
253
      refusals using a pre-defined set of keywords. The detail about the keyword extraction process can
254   be found in A.1.
255
256   In order to minimize human labor, we prioritize the use of the automatic filter to the fullest extent
257
      possible, utilizing the rule-based filter only as a supplement. Extending this keyword extraction
      process to RL did not result in significant differences; therefore, we extracted keywords solely during
258
      the SFT stage. The keywords extracted during the SFT stage are utilized to filter both the SFT
259
      dataset and the ranking dataset. The keyword set we extracted can be found in Appendix A.2.
260   In contrast to a test setting where a rule-based filter must handle unseen data, the static nature of
261   training dataset makes the continuous refinement and expansion of the keyword set particularly
262   effective when applying the rule-based filter to the training dataset.

281
282   In order to address the research questions, we evaluate the performance of the model trained with
283   the refusal-free training method on two different types of benchmarks, (1) refusal benchmarks, and

287
288   4.2.1      R EFUSAL B ENCHMARKS
289
      For the refusal benchmarks, once the target model generates responses to the evaluation prompts,
290
      an LLM-based judge classifies refusals, and the refusal rate is automatically assessed. The refusal
291   benchmarks can be further categorized into two distinct types: (1) standard adversarial benchmarks,
292   and (2) over-refusal benchmarks.
293
294
      Adversarial benchmarks contain prompts that request harmful response to the agent.
295            • AdvBench (Zou et al., 2023) is a set of 500 harmful behaviors range over a wide spectrum
296              of detrimental content. The goal of this set is to find a single attack string that will cause
297              the model to generate any response that attempts to comply with the instruction.
298            • SORRY-Bench (Xie et al., 2024) is designed for fine-grained, class-balanced, safety re-
299              fusal evaluation dataset with 45-class taxonomy. The base dataset consists of 450 instruc-
300              tions. Although SORRY-Bench extends the base dataset to 20 different linguistic styles,
301              this work only adopts the base dataset for the sake of efficiency.
302
303
      Over-refusal benchmarks not only contain standard adversarial prompts, but also include safe, yet
      seemingly harmful prompts.
304
305            • XSTest (Röttger et al., 2023) comprises 200 unsafe prompts and 250 safe prompts across
306              ten prompt types that superficially resemble unsafe prompts in terms of the vocabulary.
307            • OR-Bench (Cui et al., 2024) proposes a method for automatically generating seemingly
308              toxic prompts. The benchmark comprises 80,000 seemingly toxic prompts across 10 com-
309              mon rejection categories, a subset of around 1,000 hard prompts and an additional 600
310              toxic prompts. This work only adopts the hard prompts and the toxic prompts for the sake
311              of efficiency.
312
313   4.2.2      G ENERAL I NSTRUCTION F OLLOWING B ENCHMARK
314            • Arena-Hard (Li et al., 2024b), one of the most challenging benchmarks available, filters
315              500 high-quality, challenging prompts from the Chatbot Arena (Chiang et al., 2024). As
316              a metric, a GPT evaluator compares responses generated by the target model and GPT-4
317              (0314) and assesses the rate of responses that the evaluator favors (win rate).

406
      Applying the automatic filter decreases the refusal rate by 39.75%, and the additional rule-based
407
      filter decreases the rate by 52.53% from the non-filtered model. Note that the number of instances
408   removed by the rule-based filter is insignificant, accounting for less than 0.3% of the dataset from
409   which they were removed, in both SFT and DPO, as shown in Table 3. Bianchi et al. (2023) claims
410   that adding small amount of safety data can substantially improve safety of the model. Conversely,
411   removing small amount of safety data can substantially diminish the safety and the effectiveness of
412   the rule-based filter supports this claim.
413
      For DPO, applying both filters clearly decreases the refusal rate in all cases as well. On average,
414   applying both filters in the DPO step reduces the refusal rate by 34.78% compared to the DPO
415   models without the filters. However, the effects of the filters are not as gradual as in the case of
416   SFT. For example, the (Magpie SFT → +RF+KF DPO) model shows worse refusal rate than Auto
417   Helpful-Only LLM, and the (+RF+KF SFT → +RF DPO) model shows worse refusal rate than the
418   (+RF+KF SFT → Magpie DPO) model. Furthermore, the +RF SFT model, despite having a lower
419   initial refusal rate than the Magpie SFT model, eventually reaches a higher refusal rate. This implies
420   that some exploration is needed when applying the filters in DPO step.
421   It is important to note that DPO, in itself, substantially reduces the refusal rate. The Magpie-Align
422   demonstrates 34.63% lower refusal rate than the +RF+KF SFT model. As inferred from the statis-
423   tics, the Magpie DPO dataset has an effect of avoiding refusals in nature. This effect is significant
424   enough that, even in the absence of the filters at the SFT stage, DPO achieves a greater reduction in
425   refusal rates compared to the top-performing SFT model.
426   The results imply the effectiveness of the refusal-free training method on both SFT and DPO
427   stage. The top-performing Helpful-Only LLM reduces the refusal rate by 60.12% compared to the
428   Magpie-Align, and by 87.63% compared to the Magpie SFT model. Furthermore, Auto Helpful-
429   Only LLM, which reduces the refusal rate to the greatest extent without any human labor, reduces
430   the refusal rate 34.67% compared to the Magpie-Align, and by 79.73% compared to the Magpie
431   SFT model. An example of a response from the Helpful-Only LLM and a response from the Auto
      Helpful-Only LLM toward the harmful request can be found in Appendix B.2.

      5.3   G ENERAL I NSTRUCTION F OLLOWING
464
465   Table 4 illustrates the mixed results among the models regarding general instruction following abil-
466   ity. Considering the variability of Arena-Hard results that arises from its difficulty, we conclude this
467   indicates that the refusal-free training neither improves nor diminishes general instruction following
468   ability, but rather maintains it. It has been recognized that there is a trade-off between helpfulness
469   and harmlessness (Bai et al., 2022a;b). However, Bianchi et al. (2023) claims that adding small
470   amount of safety data does not sacrifice the helpfulness of the model if there is sufficient amount of
471   helpfulness data. The refusal-free training not improving the helpfulness supports this claim.
472   In contrast to the claim made in Yang et al. (2023) that it does not compromise the instruction fol-
473   lowing ability, the Shadow-Alignment greatly degrades the win rate in Arena-Hard. We conjecture
474   it may not affect the abilities where the model has already saturated on, but could have a significant
475   impact on more challenging abilities that the model has not yet fully acquire. Also, the data used in
476   methods that further fine-tuning a model, including the forgetting safety approaches, tends to steer a
477   model too heavily. The evidence that demonstrates the distribution shift after the Shadow-Alignment
478
      can be found in C.

483
484   The refusal-free training method makes active use of an LLM-based refusal judge and is greatly
485   influenced by the capability of the judge despite our careful consideration in selecting the judge.
      The judge often fails with OOD data. The finetuned Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.2 judge we utilize often

488   et al. (2023), we sample 500 examples from MATH dataset (Hendrycks et al., 2021) and measure
489   accuracy. To focus on the effect of the refusal judge, we only compare the Magpie SFT model and

501   simply filters out all refusals can degrades honesty of the model. We do not investigate this as it falls

504
505   6.2   F UTURE W ORK
506
507   When we apply the filters to the ranking dataset, we simply omit the instances where the chosen
508   responses are classified as refusals rather than replacing their chosen response with the rejected
509   responses. Replacing the ranking of the responses can cause unexpected consequences since the
510   rejected responses contain various undesirable characteristics not related to safety. In order to steer
511   a ranking dataset toward refusal-free direction, we can add more responses that comply with in-
512   structions containing harmful contents while deliver helpful information as chosen responses or add
513   more responses that refuse as rejected responses. It is challenging to synthesize the former responses
514
      since many high-performing models are already aligned. In contrary, it is not difficult to synthesize
      the responses that refuse (example in Appendix B.4). We leave this Refusal Synthesis task to give
515
      additional negative signal to the model for future work. Simultaneously, to address the limitation,
516
      we will work on to improve the refusal judge.

      ducible Helpful-Only LLM and propose the refusal-free training method to reach it. We show the
523
      effectiveness of the refusal-free training method in building a Helpful-Only LLM through exten-
524
      sive experiments and state the side effects it can have. We hope this study can help shorten the path
525   toward a truely harmless LLM.

530
531
      As previously stated, we are aware that the path to the Helpful-Only LLM can lead to the poisoned
      chalice. As a first precautionary step, we urge entities that utilize the Helpful-Only LLM, which
532
      has the potential for further improvement, to be responsible and be committed to its proper manage-
533
      ment. However, as LLMs begin to affect the real world with capabilities such as tool-use (Qin et al.,
534   2023), not only entities with malicious intent but also those without such intent may also misuse the
535   Helpful-Only LLM inadvertently. Therefore, we believe it is crucial to engage the community in
536   a proactive discussion and develop a strategy to mitigate the damage as much as possible before it
537   becomes irreversible. We release this study with the sole intention of fostering discussions on pre-
538   ventive measures. We hope that studying the Helpful-Only LLM in this study to provide valuable
539   insights into what the Helpful-Only LLM is capable of, and to contribute prevent potential side
      effects eventually.

768   A.1   K EYWORD E XTRACTION P ROCESS
769
770   Figure 3 depicts the keyword extraction process. To identify cases where the automatic filter fails,
771   we begin by generating responses from the evaluation dataset using the model trained on the auto-
772   filtered dataset. Next, the automatic filter is applied to classify any remaining refusals. A human
773   annotator then manually extracts an initial set of keywords from the remaining refusal responses.
774   Finally, the annotator checks whether the initial keyword set filters excessively by manually inves-
775
      tigating the filtering result of initial keyword set on auto-filtered dataset and finalize the keyword
      set.
776
777   While analyzing the extracted keywords, we are surprised to find out that some of the keywords
778   appear frequently in the responses, but the keywords are not detected in the training dataset. This
779   indicates LLMs are not simply copying the training dataset but consider various complex features,
780   and emphasizes necessity of the automatic filters once again.
