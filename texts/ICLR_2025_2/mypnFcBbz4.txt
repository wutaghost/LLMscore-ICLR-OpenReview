011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Dynamic network embedding methods transform nodes in a dynamic network
014            into low-dimensional vectors while preserving network characteristics, facilitat-
015            ing tasks such as node classification and community detection. Several embed-
016
               ding methods have been proposed to capture structural proximity among nodes
               in a network, where densely connected communities are preserved, while others
017
               have been proposed to preserve structural equivalence among nodes, capturing
018
               their structural roles regardless of their relative distance in the network. However,
019            most existing methods that aim to preserve both network characteristics mainly
020            focus on static networks and those designed for dynamic networks do not explic-
021            itly account for inter-snapshot structural properties. This paper proposes a novel
022            unifying dynamic network embedding method that simultaneously preserves both
023            structural proximity and equivalence while considering inter-snapshot structural
024            relationships in a dynamic network. Specifically, to define structural equivalence
025            in a dynamic network, we use temporal subgraphs, known as dynamic graphlets, to
026            capture how a node’s neighborhood structure evolves over time. We then introduce
027
               a temporal-structural random walk to flexibly sample time-respecting sequences
               of nodes, considering both their temporal proximity and similarity in evolving
028
               structures. The proposed method is evaluated using five real-world networks on
029
               node classification where it outperforms benchmark methods, showing its effec-
030            tiveness and flexibility in capturing various aspects of a network.

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035
      Network embedding transforms graph nodes into low-dimensional vectors while preserving network
036   characteristics. These embeddings serve as inputs for tasks like link prediction, node classification,
037   community detection, and graph visualization (Goyal & Ferrara, 2018; Cui et al., 2018). As real-
038   world networks often change over time (Xue et al., 2022), dynamic network embedding is essential
039   to capture this evolving nature. Developing effective node embedding methods requires considera-
040   tion of various structural network properties.
041
      One fundamental network characteristic is structural proximity. Studies have shown that nodes
042   closer in a network often share similar properties or functions. For instance, in protein-protein
043   interaction networks, nearby proteins typically share functions or are part of the same metabolic
044   pathway (De Las Rivas & Fontanillo, 2010; Durek & Walther, 2008). In social networks, individuals
045   tend to connect with others who are similar in demographics, backgrounds, or interests (Block &
046   Grund, 2014).
047
      Structural equivalence, on the other hand, focuses on the similarity between nodes based on their
048   roles or functions within the network, regardless of their proximity. Two nodes are structurally
049   equivalent if they share similar connection patterns. This concept helps identify consistent patterns
050   and roles across different parts of the network. For example, users acting as mediators across a so-
051   cial network, who connect different communities together, might not belong to the same community
052   but instead share similar connection patterns with other users who also act as mediators. Struc-
053   tural equivalence is evident in real-world networks, including social networks (Lorrain & White,
      1971; Charbey & Prieur, 2019), transportation networks (Bai et al., 2021), and biological networks,

      e.g. protein-protein interaction networks (Milenković & Pržulj, 2008; Davis et al., 2015) and brain
055   networks (Finotelli et al., 2021).

059
060   2.1   S TATIC N ETWORK E MBEDDING
061
062   Various methods have been proposed to preserve structural proximity in static networks. Traditional
063   methods include LLE (Roweis & Saul, 2000) and Laplacian eigenmaps (Belkin & Niyogi, 2001).
064   Matrix factorization-based methods, such as HOPE (Ou et al., 2016), preserve higher-order prox-
065
      imities using a high-order proximity matrix and singular value decomposition, while GraRep (Cao
      et al., 2015) captures structural proximities across different neighborhood sizes using k-step proba-
066
      bilities. Random walk-based methods like DeepWalk (Perozzi et al., 2014) and node2vec (Grover &
067
      Leskovec, 2016) extract node neighborhoods with random walks and learn embeddings via a Skip-
068   gram (Mikolov et al., 2013a) model. Deep learning-based methods, such as SDNE (Wang et al.,
069   2016), use a deep learning framework to maintain both local and global network structures.
070
071
      In addition to methods focusing on structural proximity, several static network embedding meth-
      ods address structural equivalence and capture node roles within the network. struc2vec (Ribeiro
072
      et al., 2017) captures structural similarities using node degrees, constructing a multi-layer graph
073
      where each layer encodes different resolutions of structural similarity. Random walks are applied,
074   and Skip-gram is used to learn the final embeddings. GraphWave (Donnat et al., 2018) uses heat
075   wavelets to describe neighborhood structures, effectively summarizing local patterns around each
076   node. Another method (Wang et al., 2020) proposes structural role embedding in hyperbolic space.
077   Additionally, Ahmed et al. (2020) performs attributed random walks to learn role-based embeddings.
078
079
      2.2   DYNAMIC N ETWORK E MBEDDING
080
081   Various dynamic network embedding methods build upon static methods to capture structural prox-
082   imity in time-varying networks. CTDNE (Nguyen et al., 2018) extends DeepWalk by using temporal
083   random walks to generate temporal node sequences, capturing temporal dynamics in node embed-
084   dings. Other methods include T-EDGE (Lin et al., 2020), which considers weighted networks, and
085   tNodeEmbed (Singer et al., 2019), which builds on node2vec and employs LSTM (Hochreiter &
086   Schmidhuber, 1997) for evolving interactions. De Winter et al. (2018) extends node2vec for dy-
087
      namic link prediction. Pandhre et al. (2018) learns embeddings from random walks within the same
      snapshots as well as temporal walks across different snapshots to capture spatio-temporal dynamics.
088
089   Regarding structural equivalence in dynamic networks, Wang et al. (2021) first explores structural
090   roles by extending the idea from struc2vec to dynamic networks. k-hop neighborhoods structural
091   distance is calculated for node pairs at each timestep and aggregated to form a historical structural
092   distance. However, this method does not consider structural dynamics between timesteps, as struc-
093
      tural information is extracted independently at each timestep. Some works focus on a specific kind
      of structure, a triad, and model its evolution process. Zhou et al. (2018) proposes DynamicTriad
094
      which preserves the evolution pattern of a triad by modeling the triadic closure process to get node
095
      embeddings for each time snapshot. Huang et al. (2020) further proposes MTNE that models the
096   triad evolution using Hawkes process (Hawkes, 1971).
097
098
099
      2.3   U NIFYING S TRUCTURAL P ROXIMITY AND E QUIVALENCE
100
      Several static network embedding methods aim to capture both structural proximity and equivalence
101
      simultaneously. Lyu et al. (2017) proposes a node embedding method that uses local subgraphs,
102   or graphlets, to measure structural equivalence among nodes, in addition to node neighborhood
103   information. However, this method limits structural similarity to nearby nodes in the Sth-order
104   neighborhood. Shi et al. (2019) considers graphlet-based structural equivalence between all node
105   pairs and introduces joint representation learning to preserve both structural proximity and equiva-
106   lence. Shi et al. (2021) defines structural equivalence using the graphlet degree vector (GDV) and
107   employs cross-layer random walks on the original and structural similarity networks to capture both
      proximity and equivalence.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      For dynamic networks, Liu et al. (2020) proposes a k-core based temporal Graph Convolution Net-
109   work (CTGCN) that preserves both nodes’ connective proximity and global structural similarity
110   based on k-core (Nikolentzos et al., 2018) subgraphs or maximal subgraphs where all nodes have
111   a degree of at least k. Node features are propagated along the k-core subgraphs within each time
112   snapshot where RNN is then utilized to model the temporal dependency at different timestep. In a
113   similar manner, Li et al. (2024) proposes a temporal Graph Convolution Network based on k-truss
114   (Cohen, 2008) subgraphs (TTGCN) defined based on triangles. However, the topological structure
115   considered to be preserved for both methods is defined within each time snapshot independently, as
116   k-core and k-truss network structures are considered independently at each timestep.

118   3     O UR C ONTRIBUTIONS
119
120   Majority of network embedding methods aim to capture either proximity or equivalence, but not
121   both. Furthermore, existing methods that aim to preserve both characteristics are mostly designed for
122   static networks, while the methods for dynamic networks do not explicitly consider inter-snapshot
123   structural relationships. To address these limitations, we are motivated to propose a unifying net-
124   work embedding framework that preserves both structural proximity and equivalence in a dynamic
125   network, while considering inter-snapshot structural relationships. The main contributions of our
126   work can be summarized as follows:
127
128
             • We propose a unifying network embedding framework which preserves both structural
               proximity and equivalence in a dynamic network. We construct a structural similarity net-
129
               work based on dynamic graphlets (Hulovatyy et al., 2015), which explicitly accounts for
130
               inter-snapshot structural dynamics.
131
132
             • We propose a temporal-structural random walk to generate temporal node contexts that
               capture both temporally close nodes and nodes with similar structural roles. We introduce
133
               the α hyperparameter that can be tuned to capture different degrees of task-specific network
134
               characteristics, offering flexibility and interpretability to the task of network learning.
135
136          • We evaluate the proposed method on five real-world networks for node classification and
137
               compare it with five benchmark methods. Our method outperforms the benchmarks. Addi-
               tionally, we are able to infer the importance of each network characteristic across different
138
               networks using the α hyperparameter.

150   4.1   S TRUCTURAL P ROXIMITY
151
      Structural proximity in a dynamic network refers to how closely connected nodes are to each other
152
      over time. Preserving proximity is useful in preserving communities where nodes in close proximity
153   are considered to be in the same community and share the same characteristics. Specifically, an edge
154   etij = (vi , vj ) ∈ Et between nodes i and j indicates first-order proximity between the two nodes at
155   time t.
156
157   4.2   S TRUCTURAL E QUIVALENCE
158
159   Structural equivalence identifies roles of nodes across different parts of the network based on their
160   structural patterns. Regardless of their distance, a group of nodes can be considered structurally
161   equivalent if they have the same local structures, e.g., mediator users across the network who connect
      different communities together. This concept is formally introduced in this section.

      Graphlets and graphlet degree vector (GDV) have been widely used to capture local topological
163   structure of nodes in real world networks (Sarajlić et al., 2016; Charbey & Prieur, 2019; Finotelli
164   et al., 2021; Milenković & Pržulj, 2008). Graphlets are small, non-isomorphic, induced subnet-
165   works. Nodes within the same graphlet are said to be of the same automorphism orbit, if they can
166   be mapped to one another by automorphism, or in simple terms, have identical connection patterns.
167   Figure 1 (a) shows all graphlets with up to four nodes where for each graphlet, its automorphism
168   orbits are denoted in unique colors. The graphlet degree vector (GDV) of a node, serving as its
169   topological signature, is a vector where each element corresponds to the number of times the node
170   takes part in a specific orbit of a graphlet.

      To quantify the structural equivalence among nodes in dynamic networks, we use a dynamic gen-
      eralization of graphlets, namely dynamic graphlets and dynamic GDV (D-GDV) (Hulovatyy et al.,
195
      2015). Dynamic graphlets introduce temporal information to the edges of graphlets, identifying each
196
      edge in a graphlet with a specific order in time. Hence, the D-GDV of a node, which summarizes
197   the involvement of the node in different dynamic graphlet orbits, provides a topological dynamics
198   signature of that node. Furthermore, since each edge in a dynamic graphlet can correspond to a
199   different timestep, as we will later elaborate, it explicitly accounts for inter-snapshot dynamics in
200   the network. The definitions of dynamic graphlet and dynamic GDV (D-GDV) (Hulovatyy et al.,
201   2015) are as follows:

215   ment corresponds to the number of times the node takes part in a specific orbit of a dynamic graphlet.
      This summarizes the dynamic graphlet involvement of a node and serves as its topological signature.

      et al., 2018), and D-GDV or dynamic graphlet degree vector (Hulovatyy et al., 2015) where PCA
415   decomposition is applied for dimension reduction, with the dimensions kept so that 90% variance in
416   the data remains.
417
418
      The node embeddings from each method are used as inputs to a one-vs-rest logistic regression clas-
      sifier for a node classification task where 5-fold cross validation is applied. Specifically, the model
419
      is trained on four parts and tested on the remaining part, this process is repeated five times with
420
      each part used exactly once as the test set, and the results are averaged to provide a performance
421   estimate. The macro-average scores for Average Precision (AP) and Area Under the Receiver Op-
422   erating Characteristic Curve (AUROC) are reported as mean and standard deviation obtained from
423   the cross validation.

528   8     C ONCLUSION
529
530
531
      In this work, we have studied the dynamic network embedding problem with the goal of capturing
      both structural proximity and equivalence of a dynamic network, while accounting for the inter-
532
      snapshot structural dynamics among nodes. We have quantified structural equivalence between
533
      two nodes in a dynamic network based on dynamic graphlets. A temporal-structural random walk
534   method has been proposed to sample node sequences consisting of temporally close nodes and
535   structurally similar nodes by introducing a hyperparameter α to balance the weight between the two
536   network characteristics. The proposed method has demonstrated the state-of-the-art performances
537   on five real-world datasets on node classification, capturing varying degrees of structural proximity
538   and equivalence in dynamic networks. In this paper, the hyperparameter α was chosen by brute
539   force search; for future work, a supervised strategy could be developed to learn the optimal α using
      node labels in the training set.
