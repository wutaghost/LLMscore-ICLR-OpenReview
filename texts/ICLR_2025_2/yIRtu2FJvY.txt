                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011
               Variant effect predictors (VEPs) are designed to predict the impact of protein vari-
012
               ants on cellular function, traditionally using data from multiple sequence align-
013            ments (MSAs). This assumes that natural variants are fit, a premise challenged by
014            pharmacogenomics, where some pharmacogenes have low evolutionary pressure.
015            In this context, deep mutational scanning (DMS) datasets are of particular interest
016            since they provide quantitative fitness scores for variants. In this work, we propose
017            a transformer-based matrix variational auto-encoder architecture and evaluate its
018            performances on 33 DMS datasets corresponding to 26 drug target and absorption-
019            distribution-metabolism-excretion (ADME) proteins available in the ProteinGym
020            benchmark. Our model trained on MSAs (matVAE-MSA) outperforms a model
021
               similar to the widely used VEPs in pharmacogenomics, and sets a new zero-shot
               prediction benchmark for 2 proteins related to the Noonan syndrome. We compare
022
               matVAE-MSA with matENC-DMS, a model with similar capacity, but trained on
023
               DMS data in a 5-fold supervised cross-validation framework. matENC-DMS out-
024            performs matVAE-MSA for 15 out of 33 DMS datasets, including all ADME, and
025            certain drug target proteins. Although our models do not outperform the best base-
026            line models, our results help shed new light on the role of evolutionary pressure
027            for the validity of the premise of VEP design. In turn motivating the development
028            of DMS datasets to improve VEPs on pharmacogene-related proteins.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Variant effect predictors (VEPs) are mathematical models aiming at predicting the effect of one or
034   multiple variants in a sequence of amino-acids (AAs). The effect of a protein variant is typically
035   defined as a loss or gain of function of a cell carrying the variant, compared to a cell carrying a
036   wild-type (WT) protein without variant. The accurate prediction of variant effect has many promis-
037   ing applications for personalized medicine, particularly in the field of pharmacogenomics, where
038   variants on drug targets or Absorption-Distribution-Metabolism-Excretion (ADME) proteins are of
039
      particular interest (Huang et al., 2016). In this context, VEPs can be used to assess individual patient
      response to chemotherapeutic treatments from their genetic background, thus eliminating the need
040
      for multiple attempts at treatments. The most effective VEPs have been designed using data from
041
      multiple sequence alignments (MSAs) and based on the conservation assumption: fit variants were
042   selected out by nature and thus, learning a distribution over variants found in nature implicitly cap-
043   tures the biochemical constraints that characterize fit variants. New sequencing techniques combined
044   with machine learning could lead to significant advances in variant effect prediction, by providing
045   quantitative data in region of the protein sequence space unexplored in existing MSA datasets. Deep
046   mutational scanning (DMS) has recently emerged as a way to yield large-scale datasets of protein
047   quantitative fitness scores (Fowler & Fields, 2014). The fitness scores can also be obtained with
048   different selection assays, allowing to quantify various effects, e.g. effect on phenotype or effect on
049   structure. DMS thus allows to challenge the conservation assumption of VEPs design from MSAs.
050
      This is in turn is of particular importance in pharmacogenomics, since pharmacogenes are generally
      under low evolutionary pressure (Zhou et al., 2022; Ingelman-Sundberg et al., 2018).
051
052   In this article, we design a VEP and use it to evaluate the validity of the conservation assumption for
053   pharmacogene-related proteins. Our architecture exploits the structure of variational auto-encoders
      (VAEs) and allows models of similar capacity and designs to be trained on both MSA and DMS


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      data. We also exploit a transformer architecture in order to improve upon existing VAE-based VEPs.
055   Both VAE and transformers are key components of the best performing models in the ProteinGym
056   benchmark (Notin et al., 2023a). We experiment with a VAE-based model exploiting multimodal
057   priors, and we derive a matrix encoding scheme inspired from linear matrix decomposition to replace
058   the input flattening operation found for instance in DeepSequence.
059
060   Contributions Our contributions are summarized as follows:
061
062         1. We design protein specific models combining a VAE and a transformer for variant ef-
063            fect prediction. We study their zero-shot prediction performances on 33 deep mutational
064
               scanning (DMS) datasets of drug related and ADME proteins available in the ProteinGym
               benchmark.
065
066         2. We study the impact on performances of using expressive latent prior distributions when
067            the models are trained on MSA data available in ProteinGym. We experiment with standard
068            mixture of Gaussian (MOG) and VampPrior.
069         3. We adapt our models to directly predict labels from DMS datasets using a prediction head
070            from the latent space, thus preserving our model capacity. In light of the comparison in
071            performances of the models trained unsupervised on MSA and supervised DMS label data,
072            we discuss the extent of the validity of the conservation assumption.

074   1.1   R ELATED WORKS
075
      Zero-shot predictors VEPs exploiting site-independent position-wise frequencies of AAs in
076
      MSAs remain the methods of choice in pharmacology, e.g. SIFT or Polyphen-2 (Ng & Henikoff,
077
      2003; Adzhubei et al., 2010; Durbin, 1998). However, other models can achieve much better zero-
078   shot prediction performances on at least one pharmacogene-related protein DMS dataset (Details in
079   Table A.6), according to the recent ProteinGym variant effect prediction benchmark (Notin et al.,
080   2023a). Many of these models compute the functional cellular effect of a variant v compared to a
081   wild-type sequence x(wt) , via the log-likelihood ratio:
082
083                                                      p(x(v) )
                                               ŷ = ln             ,                                    (1)
084                                                      p(x(wt) )
085
      where p(.) is a generative probability density chosen to maximize p(x(v) ), for sequences x(v) from
086
      the MSA. For instance, the Evolutionary Scale modeling (ESM) approaches (Rives et al., 2021;
087
      Lin et al., 2023), rely solely on a transformer-based protein language model (PLM) for modeling
088   the distribution over sequences in MSAs. Tranception (Notin et al., 2022a) additionally integrates
089   predictions using position-wise frequencies of AAs in MSAs. TranceptEVE (Notin et al., 2022b)
090   combines the Tranception model with a VAE-based model (Frazer et al., 2021) for AA sequence
091   modeling. Other methods such as Masked Inverse Folding (MIF) (Yang et al., 2023) learn to predict
092   protein sequences from a given structure. VESPA (Marquet et al., 2022) combines protein sequence
093   embedding from PLMs with known bio-mechanical properties of AAs to predict variant effect with
094   a linear regression model. Other model do not rely on the ratio in (1) to compute variant effect. MSA
095   Transformer (Rao et al., 2021) is based on ESM and uses axial attention to optimize a masking loss
096
      over an entire MSA, rather than on individual sequences. It learns a representation of Hamming
      distances in the MSA and the hamming distance to WT sequence is used as a proxy for variant
097
      effect. GEMME (Laine et al., 2019) predicts variant effect via the distance to WT sequence in an
098
      evolutionary tree. This approach shows very good performances and has several order of magni-
099   tude fewer parameters than transformer-based approaches. DeepSequence (Riesselman et al., 2018)
100   introduces a VAE and approximates the distribution of input data x (Eq. (1)) with the variational
101   evidence lower bound.
102
103   Supervised learning predictors Recently, several models combining DMS and MSA datasets
104   have been proposed Hsu et al. (2022). The general idea is to combine sequence embeddings, e.g.
105   sequence one-hot encoding, with evolutionary fitness scores from pretrained models such as ESM or
106   DeepSequence. ProteinNPT is a conditional pseudo-generative model designed for exploiting DMS
107   data, jointly with MSA data in a semi-supervised framework (Notin et al., 2023b). In addition to
      their novel architecture, the authors introduce several baselines consisting in exploiting prediction


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      scores from zero-shot prediction models pretrained on MSA, including DeepSequence and MSA
109   Transformer. SPIRED is a recent framework able to predict fitness scores as well as protein structure
110   (Chen et al., 2024). A pretrained ESM model is used for sequence embedding, and graph attention
111   networks and multilayer perceptron are trained using DMS data in a supervised framework.
112
113   Multi-modal prior distributions for VAEs VAEs, e.g. DeepSequence, assume that the input data
114   x ∈ {0, 1}L×d are generated from a latent variable of a D-dimensional vector space: z ∈ RD . The
115   latent variable is assumed drawn from a Gaussian prior p(z), and the generative process is modeled
116   with a distribution pθ (x|z). The explicit modeling of the latent variable z is an interesting feature of
117   VAEs, because it allows to put a formal prior distribution on the latent space. The other mentioned
118   models do not impose such structure, although interestingly the learnt representations in ESM was
119
      shown to correlate with known bio-mechanical properties of AAs (Rives et al., 2021). Multimodal
      mixture of Gaussian (MOG) priors have been proposed as latent prior distributions for unsuper-
120
      vised clustering tasks (Dilokthanakul et al., 2017) with VAE. The authors used trainable mean and
121
      covariances in latent space and showed through data sampling that the learnt mixture components
122   corresponded to meaningful characteristics of the input data. This was shown to have potential im-
123   plications for model interpretability in biological contexts (Varolgüneş et al., 2020). Further, the
124   VampPrior has been designed so that the statistics of the mixture components explicitly depend on
125   input space prototypes Tomczak & Welling (2018). This provides meaningful variables to probe for
126   interpretability rather than using a sampling scheme. To the best of the authors knowledge, current
127   methods employing VAEs for VEP have only been designed with unimodal prior distributions.

      2.1.1    M ATRIX DECOMPOSITION AND ENCODING
152
153   Matrix decomposition is a set of methods in linear algebra consisting in decomposing a matrix x ∈
154   RL×d (here d < L), into two (or more) matrices with interesting structure, e.g. unitary, triangular,
155   diagonal. For instance, the QR decomposition of x ∈ RL×d is of the form x = Q[RT , 0T ]T ,
156   where Q ∈ RL×L is unitary, R ∈ Rd×d is upper triangular and 0 is a (L − d × d)-dimensional
157   matrix of zeros. We call matrix encoding the use of the low dimension factor, here the R matrix,
158   as a compressed representation of the input x. For QR decomposition, the matrix encoding can be
159
      formulated:
                                         [sT , 0T ]T = Wx ∈ RL×d ,                                    (2)
160
161   where W = Q−1 ∈ RL×L is a linear transform and s = R. Linear decomposition methods are
      often related to singular value decomposition, for instance the diagonal elements of R in the QR


                                                            3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      decomposition are the singular values of x. However, for one-hot encoded sequences of AAs, the
163   singular values are counts of each AA in the sequence, and the singular vectors are a permutation
164   of Id determined by the ordering of the counts of the AAs. In other words, the encoding produced
165   with such linear methods does not account for the global or relative position of AAs in the sequence,
166   and in particular, randomly permuting the rows of x leads to the same encoding. To ensure that the
167   model is flexible enough to learn a useful encoding, we propose to learn a representation of x with a
168   transformer, prior to reducing the first dimension to a fixed H < L with a trainable linear transform.
169   This is formulated as follows:
170                                        x′ = Transformer(x) ∈ RL×d ,
171
                                            s = DwFC(x′ ) ∈ RH×d ,

173   where Transformer(.) and DwFC(.) are specified in the paragraphs below.

175   Transformer Transformers are effective sequence models that can transfer information between
176
      any two positions within a sequence. The model we use is similar to the multi-layer encoding
      transformer in (Vaswani et al., 2017). Individual layers encode an input sequence x ∈ RL×d into a
177
      sequence x′ ∈ RL×d as follows:
178
179                                   x1 = Norm(τ1 x + (1 − τ1 )Attn(x)),
                                                                                                         (3)
180                                   x′ = Norm(τ ′ x1 + (1 − τ ′ )FC(x1 )),
181
      where Norm(.) is a Layer Normalization (Ba et al., 2016), FC(.) is a fully connected (FC) network
182   with ReLU activations, and Attn(.) is the masked scaled dot product attention from (Vaswani et al.,
183   2017). We use trainable τ1 , τ ′ ∈ [0, 1]2 to control the gradient flow in the gated skip connections
184   (He et al., 2016). Note that we do not use positional encoding since (Rives et al., 2021) showed that
185   PLMs did not necessarily benefit from it. Instead, structure information is encoded in the mask of
186   the attention layer (See section A.2).
187
188   Dimension-wise FC (DwFC) We call DwFC the FC linear layer inspired from Eq. 2. This layer
189   is similar to a flattening followed by a linear transform with bias, but requires less parameters since
190   the same linear transform is used across dimensions. This operation replaces the direct flattening of
191   the input in DeepSequence. x′ ∈ RL×d is encoded in a protein length independent representation
192
      s ∈ RH×d as follows:
193                                                 s = Ux′ + b,                                         (4)

                          and b ∈ R   H
                                          are trainable weight and bias parameters.

196   2.1.2   F ULLY CONNECTED BOTTLENECK (FCB)
197
198   The FCB is similar to a classical VAE (Kingma & Welling, 2014). s ∈ RH×d is flattened and then
199   encoded into a latent representation of fixed dimension:
200                                            h = FC(Vec(s)) ∈ RD ,                                     (5)
201
      where Vec(.) denotes the flattening operation. h is then used to compute the statistics of the latent
202
      vector z ∈ RD :
203                                      qϕ (z|x) = N (z; fµ (h), fσ (h)) ,                             (6)
204
      where h is a function of x and fµ , respectively fσ , is a 1-layer fully connected network returning
205
      a mean vector, respectively a diagonal covariance matrix. For training, we introduce robustness
206   by drawing the latent vector z ∼ qϕ (z|x) using the reparameterization trick. At test time, we use
207   z = fµ (h) to reduce stochasticity. The latent distribution qϕ (z|x) is learnt to be close to a prior
208   distribution p(z) with respect to the Kullback-Leibler divergence (KLD). The latent vector z ∈ RD
209   is then used as input to a decoder network that aims to reconstruct the input s. The output to the
210   decoder, and thus of the FCB, is denoted ŝ ∈ RH×d .

      3.1   M ODEL C OMPARISON
299
300   Performance metrics The performances of our protein specific models are measured and reported
301   on corresponding individual DMS datasets, both in terms of Spearman’s Rank Correlation coef-
302   ficient (SpearmanR) and area under receiver operating characteristic (AUROC). The SpearmanR
303   measures the correlation between the ranks of the predicted scores and the ranks of the target scores.
304   Additionally, the target score is binarized in order to compute the AUROC. To allow for a mean-
305   ingful comparison of the AUROC scores, we use the binarization threshold used in ProteinGym. In
306   brief, given a DMS dataset, a threshold on the scores is selected manually between modes in case the
307
      distribution of scores is bimodal, and as the median in case the distribution of scores is unimodal. In
      the rest of the paper we will primarily compare spearmanR performances.
308
309   For matENC-DMS, we train our model on DMS data with supervised learning in a 5-fold cross-
310   validation framework. This first ensures that no variant/label pair is used for both training and
311   testing. Secondly, this ensures that the evaluation framework is comparable to that of matVAE-
312   MSA, with all variants in the DMS dataset used exactly once for validation. When a protein has
313
      multiple DMS datasets, both datasets were split in 5 folds and the training (resp. validation) subsets
      were merged. The performances on individual DMS datasets are then reported as the average of the
314
      5 models trained on independent training sets.
315
316
      Model design choices We experimented with mixture of diagonal Gaussian (MOG) priors with
317
      K = 1, 10, 100 mixture components. For K = 1, the mean and standard deviation of the prior
318   distribution are fixed to µ1 = 0 ∈ RD and Σ1 = diag(0.01) ∈ RD×D . For K = 5, 10, 100,
319   the trainable means are initialized randomly from a Normal distribution and for k = 1, . . . , K,
320   Σk = diag(0.01) ∈ RD×D is fixed. We also experimented with a VAMP prior and K = 5, 10, 100
321   mixture components. The prototypes in input space were initialized randomly and all trainable.
322   In addition to experimenting with different prior distribution hyperparameters, we performed an
323   ablation study of our model trained on DMS data. We report the performances of models with and
      without Transformer(.) and DwFC(.) layers. The different models are summarized in Table 1.

      4.3   F UTURE WORK
487
488   Our results experimenting with expressive multi-modal priors did not show improvements compared
489   to simple Gaussian prior. The investigations of the potential pitfalls of our current approach as well
490   as further biology-relevant interpretations of the latent prior modes are left for future works.
491
      Next, the joint use of both DMS and MSA data for training is an important next step towards im-
492
      proved model performances. In our work, our primary objective was to evaluate the information
493
      provided by MSA and DMS data separately for variant effect prediction. DMS and MSA data could
494   nonetheless be used jointly for training, for instance in a fine tuning approach, where a VAE model
495   is first trained on MSA data, and the encoding part is fine tuned on DMS data. This might lead to
496   improved performances since both datasets would then contribute to the model performances. To-
497   gether with experimentation on a wider range of proteins, fine tuning on DMS data is an interesting
498   research direction that we leave as future work.
499
      Lastly, the design of a model able to learn from multiple proteins is also an interesting next avenue
500   for research. Our current architecture can be extended to work for proteins of different lengths
501   L1 , L2 , . . . . This could be done by slightly modifying the DwFC layer (Eq. (4)). An idea would
502   be to first define LM = max(L1 , L2 , . . . ) and initialize U ∈ RH×LM . At run time, with an input
503   protein encoding x′ ∈ RL×d , the first L columns from matrix U ∈ RH×LM can be used, which
504   leads to: s = U(:,1:L) x′ + b, where U(:,1:L) denotes the sub-matrix of U which includes all the
505   rows and the first L columns of U. The complexity of the function described in (4) depends on
506   the length of the protein under consideration, while the memory complexity scales linearly with the
507   length of the longest protein. This is the same as our current approach where one model is fit to
508
      individual proteins. It differs in that the weights included up to a column l would be shared between
      all the proteins of length at least l. This makes the transformer learn to organize information, by
509
      placing what is relevant to all proteins in the first rows of the representation x′ ∈ RH×d . An issue
510
      with this approach when training on DMS data is that the meaning, support and distributions of the
511   DMS scores vary largely across DMS datasets, thus requiring the fitness scores to be standardized
512   (Fig. A.11). An interesting future research direction is the quantification of similarities in selection
513   assays of DMS datasets, so that they can exploited in regression models.
514
515
516   5     C ONCLUSION
517
518   We proposed a transformer-based matrix variational auto-encoder and evaluated its performances
519   on DMS datasets of drug target and ADME proteins. We showed that advanced priors such as
520   mixture of Gaussians and VampPrior did not provide improvement over a simple Gaussian prior
521   for the latent space when our model was trained on MSA data (matVAE-MSA). matVAE-MSA
522   nonetheless outperformed on average a model similar to widely used VEPs in pharmacogenomics.
523   Moreover, matVAE-MSA outperformed the best benchmark models in ProteinGym for 2 proteins
524   related to the Noonan syndrome. Our architecture allowed to compare performances with models
525
      of similar capacity but trained on DMS datasets (matENC-DMS) instead of MSA. Although DMS
      datasets were often much smaller, matENC-DMS outperformed matVAE-MSA for 15 out of 33
526
      DMS datasets, including those of all ADME and certain drug target proteins. MSAs may thus be
527
      limiting the performances of VEPs for some proteins for which the conservation assumption does not
528   hold. This in turn motivates the development of DMS datasets and the study of their relationships,
529   in order to further improve variant effect prediction.

      A.1.1    P REPROCESSING OF MSA SEQUENCES

716   et al., 2018). Sequences are removed from MSAs if they include more than 50% gaps. Columns

735   A.2     M ODEL ARCHITECTURES HYPER - PARAMETERS
736
737   The encoding and decoding transformers of matVAE-MSA are designed with 3 transformer layers
738   described in (3). The embedding dimensions of all the layers are identical and equal to d. The use of
739   3 layers allows to use information from neighbors up to order 3 according to the graph defined by the
740
      attention mask. The attention mask is a thresholded distance matrix derived from the WT protein
      structures predicted by Alphafold2 (Jumper et al., 2021). This means that queries are allowed to
741
      attend to keys in the attention dot product if the predicted distance in 3d between the corresponding
742
      AAs is ≤ c. We chose c = 7Å which had the best performances for 4 out of 5 graph neural
743   network-based models predicting variant effects in (Gelman et al., 2021, Table S3). The structures
744   are readily available in the ProteinGym repository as PDB files (Notin et al., 2023a). For DwFC,
745   we chose H = min(Hmin , L) with Hmin = 200. This means that the dimension is not reduced
746   for proteins with small enough sequence length L ≤ Hmin . Following the discussion in Section
747   2.1.1, we experimented with Hmin ≈ d on some proteins, but could not get good performances.
748   For FCB, we used a 2-layer ReLU network with 1000 and 300 neurons, and output latent space
749   dimension D = 50, this is similar to the design of DeepSequence (Riesselman et al., 2018). The
750   networks fµ and fσ are both 1-layer FC ReLU networks with D neurons and output dimension
751   D. For stable computation of the closed form KLD, fσ practically outputs ln σ 2 using an output
752
      pointwise operation: x 7→ ln (ln (ex + 1)). Symmetrical design choices were used for the decoding
      part of matVAE-MSA.
753
754   For matENC-DMS, the D-dimensional latent vector in the FC bottleneck is passed into a prediction
755   head with a 2-layer fully connected ReLU network, with 50 and 25 neurons, and an output dimension
      of 1. The decoding parts of matVAE-MSA are not used.

      A.3   M ODEL TRAINING
757
758   For matVAE-MSA, the models are trained on protein specific MSAs to minimize the negative ELBO
759   in (9). The expected reconstruction error is approximated with a 1-sample Monte Carlo method. The
760   reconstruction error is the cross-entropy between the true x ∈ RL×d and the reconstructed x̂. For
761   matENC-DMS, no variational formulation is used. The loss function is the mean squared error
762   (MSE) between the true label y ∈ R and the reconstructed label ŷ.
763   For all our included proteins, the loss function for matVAE-MSA is optimized using the ADAM
764   optimizer, with a fixed learning rate λ = 8e − 5, a batch size of 256 and 300, 000 training steps. For
765   matENC-DMS, the loss function is optimized with a fixed learning rate λ = 1e − 4, a batch size of
766   512 and 100, 000 training steps. The learning rates were chosen similar to the optimal one reported
767   for a graph neural network model in (Gelman et al., 2021, Table S3). The batch sizes were chosen
768   to obtain the most efficient use of our hardware. In matVAE-MSA, the memory footprint is mainly
769   due to the attention matrices in the encoder and decoder transformer. We double the batch size for
770
      matENC-DMS compared to matVAE-MSA since matENC-DMS only has an encoder transformer.
