009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011
               Transformer-based methods have made significant strides in time series forecast-
012            ing tasks in recent years. However, we observe underfitting in numerous samples,
013            e.g., pattern shifts or excessive deviation in extreme value regions, when testing
014            the transform-based model that converges on the training set. Through the pro-
015            posed spectral analysis of adjacent embedding sequences, we identify a frequency
016            collapse issue in the embedding features generated by the top layer of the trans-
017            former backbone. To address this, we propose the Post-Embedding ReMapping
018            (PErM) strategy that improves the frequency-domain representation of embed-
019            dings using fixed non-linear functions. Both two kinds of PErM functions that we
020
               insert into the model can effectively resolve the frequency collapse issue and lead
               to significant improvements in prediction performance. Experimental results show
021
               that our method outperforms state-of-the-art algorithms across multiple datasets.
022
               We will release our code after the review phase.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Time series forecasting is a critical task across various real-world fields, including but not limited
029   to finance, healthcare, weather, and supply chain management. Accurate time series forecasting
030   allows organizations to make informed decisions, optimize processes, and anticipate future trends,
031   making it a valuable tool for strategic planning and resource allocation. In recent years, with the
032   rapid advancement of deep learning, an increasing number of learning-based time series forecasting
033
      methods have emerged (Zeng et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024a; Xu et al., 2024b). In particular, fol-
      lowing the remarkable success of transformer (Vaswani et al., 2017) in the field of natural language
034
      processing (Brown et al., 2020), a series of efforts have been made to adapt transformers more
035
      effectively for time series forecasting tasks (Wu et al., 2021; Nie et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024b).
036
037
038   However, when testing a converged transformer model adapted for
039
      time series tasks, we still observe significant deviations between the
      predicted values and the patterns of the original time series, even in
040
      segments with clear periodic characteristics, and this deviation is par-
041
      ticularly noticeable in the fitting of extreme value regions as shown in
042   Fig. 1. Given the rapid signal variations in these regions, we hypothe-
043   size that these underfitting phenomena are related to the loss of certain   Figure 1: Example of un-
044   high-frequency signals in the embeddings generated by the model.            derfitting.
045
      Unfortunately, it is unreasonable to directly apply time-frequency
046
      analysis methods, such as spectral analysis, to embeddings of one
047
      time series sample. The model’s forward process disrupts the internal temporal structure of the
048   samples, thus the temporal information within each embedding is disordered. This means that per-
049   forming spectral analysis directly on the embeddings is meaningless. In time series tasks, we observe
050   that the sliding window sampling method allows the sample sequence to directly reflect the internal
051   temporal structure of each sample. Thanks to this finding, we propose a spectral analysis method
052   based on adjacent embedding sequences, and we further prove that the absence of high-frequency
053   signals in the embedding sequence directly leads to the loss of high-frequency signals in the predic-
      tion target, which will result in underfitting.

      Through our analysis, we confirm that training solely based on transformers can lead to a lack of
      high-frequency signals in the embeddings, which we refer to as frequency collapse. To address this
067
      issue, we propose the Post-Embedding reMapping (PErM) strategy. We introduce a pre-defined non-
068
      linear remapping layer between the top layer of the transformer backbone and the model’s prediction
069   head as shown in Fig. 2. This layer employs a non-linear mapping function to project high-frequency
070   features from the original signals into a new frequency domain, while also re-integrating information
071   within the embeddings. As the high-frequency features can be mapped to some lower freqencies in
072   the new domain, this helps the model to capture high-frequency features more easily. Experiments
073   show that using various PErM functions effectively alleviates the frequency collapse phenomenon
074   and improves the final prediction performance.
075   In summary, we make the following contributions:
076
077          • We propose a framework for embedding-level spectral analysis using adjacent embedding
078            sequences, and validate that the frequency collapse in the embedding sequence can lead to
079            underfitting.
080          • We propose the Post-Embedding reMapping strategy to alleviate the issue of frequency
081            collapse that arises during the training of the transformer on time series forecasting tasks,
082            thereby improving the model’s prediction accuracy.
083
             • Compared with the state-of-the-art time series forecasters, our PErMformer achieves com-
084
               petitive performance across multiple time series forecasting datasets.

259   5.1   G ENERAL T IME S ERIES T RANSFORMER M ODEL
260
261   In the processing of time series features, we adopt several well-known and empirically validated
262   strategies specifically designed for time series Transformers. Meanwhile, the encoder in our model
263   employs a pure Transformer backbone architecture. We will introduce these processing strategies
264   and the backbone architecture in detail:
265   Channel Independence (CI). The Channel Independence strategy proposed by Nie et al. (2023)
266   splits the multi-dimensional time series data into multiple one-dimensional sequences, which are
267   then fed into the model for training separately. This approach decouples the model structure from
268   the number of time series dimensions, enabling the model to simultaneously utilize time series
269   data from datasets with varying dimensionalities for training, thereby providing a consistent data
      standardization for building time series foundation models.

290   Reversible Instance Normalization (IN & RevIN). We employ the Reversible Instance Normaliza-
291   tion (RevIN) strategy ( Kim et al. (2022)) in our base model. Specifically, for each one-dimensional
292   time series sample, we normalize the sample before feeding it into the network and then denormalize
293   the model output sequences (prediction targets). This helps to suppress non-stationary information
294   in the intermediate layers, thereby mitigating the impact of data bias on the model’s performance.
295   Patching. We segment each time series sample into multiple patches, with each patch corresponding
296   to a token, and input these token sequences into the model. In contrast to treating each data point as
297   a token, this approach not only effectively enhances the model’s performance but also significantly
298   reduces the inference time due to the reduction in the token count by a factor of the patch length.
299
      Transformer Backbone. As the field of general pre-trained transformers continues to evolve, the
300
      network architecture of transformer blocks has also been undergoing continuous refinement. Sev-
301   eral language models, including LLaMA (Touvron et al., 2023), Mistral (Jiang et al., 2023), and
302   Gemma (Team et al., 2024), have introduced improvements over traditional structures. We believe
303   that the rationale behind these architectural enhancements is applicable to general tasks. Conse-
304   quently, we choose the latest Gemma transformer block as our backbone. This block employs the
305   pre-Norm strategy and enhances the computational efficiency of attention through a matrix merging
306   mechanism.
307   Since time series data inherently exhibits autocorrelation, employing positional embedding may
308   disrupt this autocorrelation. Therefore, we abandon the Positional Embedding layer and remove the
309   Rotary Position Embedding (RoPE) module from the selected transformer block.

      5.2   P OST-E MBEDDING RE M APPING
313
      The key point to address the above frequency collapse issues is guiding the model to enrich the
314
      embeddings with high-frequency representations that are correlated with the original time series. To
315
      this end, we propose the Post-Embedding reMapping (PErM) strategy. Specifically, we introduce a
316   non-trainable nonlinear mapping function δ(E) between the transformer encoder and the prediction
317   head. This function can alleviate frequency collapse in embeddings by mapping the challenging
318   learning objectives that contain high-frequency information into a combination of easier-to-learn
319   low-frequency learning objectives during the training process.
320
      We explore two kinds of mapping functions under the PErM strategy:
321
322   Random Fourier Feature Mapping (RFF). In this case (Fig. 8a), we use
323
                                          δ(E) = [cos(RE), sin(RE)]

      as the mapping layer, where R is a square matrix sampled from a Gaussian Distribution. As the
325   feature dimension has become twice as large as before, we introduce a trainable matrix W to project
326   δ(E) back to the dimension of E. The random matrix R maps the embeddings into other feature
327   spaces, introducing diversity and stochasticity into the representations. The sine and cosine functions
328   make the mapping non-linear. Since Tancik et al. (2020) has demonstrated that using the random
329   Fourier feature is an effective way to augment raw image features, we attempt to explore whether
330   this mapping can guide the model to more effectively learn high-frequency representations of time
331   series in the embedding feature space generated at the top layer.
332   Layers of Pretrained Transformers (LPT). Recent works have shown that leveraging the layers
333   of pre-trained transformers, especially the layers of large language models, can effectively boost
334   the performance of non-language tasks, such as image classification and logical reasoning tasks

      Evaluation Metrics and Tasks Settings. Following the common practice in most recent time se-
509   ries forecasting works, we employ Mean Square Error (MSE) and Mean Absolute Error (MAE) as
510   the evaluation metrics. For each dataset, we compare our method with baselines using four pre-
511   diction horizons H ∈ {96, 192, 336, 720}. The length of the lookback window L is set to 512 for
512   PErMformer, while that for the baseline is set according to their settings in the paper. Following the
513   practice of UniTime, we combine all datasets together to train a unified model and test separately
514   on each dataset. The dataset setup is widely used for foundation models, and it imposes higher
515   demands on the model’s fitting and generalization capabilities. This means our method uses only
516   one set of hyperparameters for all datasets, while most of the baselines specifically choose a set of
517
      hyperparameters for each dataset.
518   Platform. All experiments are conducted on the server equipped with 12 Intel(R) Xeon(R) Gold
519   5317 CPUs @ 3.00GHz and 4 NVIDIA GA102 GeForce RTX 3090 GPUs. The operating system is
520   Ubuntu 22.04 LTS. Each experiment is conducted with one GPU.
521   Results. Due to the space limitations, we select a few more competitive methods as baselines
522   in Table 3 and use MSE and MAE as the evaluation metric. The full results of all baselines are
523   provided in the Appendix J. Across multiple datasets, the simple transformer backbone equipped
524   with the PErM strategy outperforms existing state-of-the-art (SOTA) models. The comparisons with
525   UniTime and PatchTST are particularly noteworthy. As the methods that use the universal dataset
526   setup, PErMformer outperforms UniTime by an average of 10% across various settings. Structurally,
527   PatchTST is quite similar to PErM-None. When comparing PErMformer with PatchTST, we can
528   also observe that the PErM strategy effectively enhances the model’s prediction accuracy.

530   8           C ONCLUSION
531
532
      Owing to the spectral analysis from the perspective of adjacent embedding sequences, we observe
533
      that the embeddings generated by models trained solely with a transformer backbone exhibit a fre-
534   quency collapse phenomenon, and we preliminarily demonstrate that this may lead to poor fitting
535   for certain samples. To address this, we design the Post-Embedding reMapping strategy, introduc-
536   ing a remapping layer between the top layer of the transformer’s backbone and the prediction head.
537   This layer re-integrates the spectral representation of the embeddings and make the model easier to
538   learn the high-frequency information. In future work, we aim to design more flexible and refined
539   remapping functions to better model the frequency features, and further verify the effectiveness of
      this strategy on more time series foundational models trained with larger-scale datasets.

731   B.1   L IMITATIONS OF E XISTING S PECTRAL A NALYSIS ON T IME S ERIES TASKS
732
733   Several recent works have focused on applying spectral analysis to motivate or improve their meth-
734   ods in time series topic (Fu et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2022; Xu et al., 2024b). However, these methods
735   perform spectral analysis merely on the raw time series to provide more information for the model.
736
      Rather than conducting spectral analysis at the data level (input or output vector), we are
      more interested in performing spectral analysis at the embedding level to explore whether the
737
      model can effectively capture the diverse spectral information in the time series, as shown in
738
      Fig. 10. By jointly analyzing the spectrum at the embedding level and the spectrum at the data level,
739   we can preliminarily explore whether the model is capable of effectively capturing sufficiently rich
740   frequency domain details. This can help us better understand the prediction results of the black-box
741   models. For example, the loss of high-frequency information in the embeddings could result in the
742   final predictions failing to accurately construct some detailed patterns of the time series.

      B.2             C HALLENGES IN E MBEDDING - LEVEL S PECTRAL A NALYSIS
757
758   The biggest challenge in implementing embedding-level spectral analysis lies in the fact that, unlike
759   data-level spectral feature which can be easily captured in one sample, the embedding feature in one
760   sample has little useful spectral information. After performing operations such as linear transforma-
761   tions, nonlinear activations, or even self-attention on the original time series sample [x1 , · · · , xm ],
762   the generated embedding vectors [e1 , · · · , el ] are unlikely to retain the periodic information of the
763   original sequence. This can be easily proved, as for any two-layer neural network, we can con-
764
      struct an embedding that is different but functionally identical by swapping the positions of the
      intermediate layer’s nodes and their connection matrices. As illustrated in Fig. 11, the transformed
765
      embeddings no longer exhibit meaningful spectral information. In other words, per-sample em-
766
      bedding spectral analysis lacks physical significance (i.e., disruption of temporal structure). Thus,
767   thanks to the unique property in time series forecasting where the input vector and the input vector
768   sequence share the temporal structure, we can perform the spectral analysis from the perspective of
769   adjacent embedding sequence proposed in Sec. 3.

781
782   C.1             T HEORETICAL AND E MPIRICAL S UPPORT FOR F REQUENCY C OLLAPSE
783
784   In recent years, several works are dedicated to explore the behavior of deep neural networks (DNN)
785   in a theoretical or empirical manner, especially under the framework of frequency analysis (Xu et al.,
786
      2024a; Molina et al., 2024; Luo et al., 2019; Rahaman et al., 2019a). They prove the existence of
      spectral bias at data-level: a DNN tends to learn a target function from low to high frequencies dur-
787
      ing training, which is also called Frequency Principle (F-principle). Moreover, Luo et al. (2019)
788
      theoretically proved that for the final training process of a DNN with parameter θ and L2 loss func-
789   tion, consider dividing the MSE loss function L(θ) into two parts, the high-frequency part L+ (θ)
790   and low-frequency part L− (θ), the proportion of gradients corresponding to high-frequency com-
                   +
791
      ponents, dL    (θ)/dt
                 dL(θ)/dt , has a theoretical upper bound, and the proportion of gradients corresponding
792                                                                −
793   to low-frequency components, dL    (θ)/dt
                                      dL(θ)/dt , has a theoretical lower bound. This means compared to
794   low-frequency signals, high-frequency signals are more difficult to learn, which is also confirmed
795   by some empirical studies (Xu et al., 2024a). This can shed light on the embedding-level frequency
796   collapse phenomenon even if the model is more complex and the perspective is not limited to data
797   level.

825
826   According to the theoretical support in C.1 that high-frequency signals are harder to fit, PErM
827   strategy helps the model to learn the high-fequency information from a low-frequency perspective.
828

      structe a stochastic process using sinusoidal functions with multiple frequencies w, random phases
829                                            P3

      ϕ, and random amplitudes a: X(t) = i=1 ai sin(ωi t + ϕi ), and apply a simplified PErM func-
      tion cos(0.1X(t)) (a special case of RFF strategy). The spectrums of the stochastic process before
831
      and after the operation are shown in Fig. 12. According to the case, such a simplified remapping
832
      function can introduce more high frequency components and potentially result in harmonic com-
833   ponents. Conversely, this implies that high-frequency signals in the final embedding can be rep-
834   resented by some lower-frequency signals before the PErM layer. According to F-principle, these
835   lower-frequencies are easier to learn. In this way, the model can eventually model the signals more
836   easily and precisely. RFF is a manually constructed mapping based on trigonometric functions and
837   random factors. Since RFF is a more complex mapping, it allows the model to learn more robust
838   mappings between frequencies from a greater number of channels. LPT function can be seen as a
839   black-box, high-order non-linear mapping function that obtained from the ”World Model” and have
840   been empirically validated to have certain effects in some domains, thus we make an attempt to
