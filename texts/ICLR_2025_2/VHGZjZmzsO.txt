009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Combinatorial Optimization is crucial to numerous real-world applications, yet
012            still presents challenges due to its (NP-)hard nature. Amongst existing approaches,
013            heuristics often offer the best trade-off between quality and scalability, making
014            them suitable for industrial use. While Reinforcement Learning (RL) offers a flex-
015            ible framework for designing heuristics, its adoption over handcrafted heuristics
016            remains incomplete within industrial solvers. Existing learned methods still lack
017            the ability to adapt to specific instances and fully leverage the available computa-
018
               tional budget. The current best methods either rely on a collection of pre-trained
               policies, or on data-inefficient fine-tuning; hence failing to fully utilize newly
019
               available information within the constraints of the budget. In response, we present
020
               MEMENTO, an approach that leverages memory to improve the adaptation of neu-
021            ral solvers at inference time. MEMENTO enables updating the action distribution
022            dynamically based on the outcome of previous decisions. We validate its effective-
023            ness on benchmark problems, in particular Traveling Salesman and Capacitated
024            Vehicle Routing, demonstrating its superiority over tree-search and policy-gradient
025            fine-tuning; and showing it can be zero-shot combined with diversity-based solvers.
026            We successfully train all RL auto-regressive solvers on large instances, and show
027            that MEMENTO can scale and is data-efficient. Overall, MEMENTO enables to
028            push the state-of-the-art on 11 out of 12 evaluated tasks.

031   1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Combinatorial Optimization (CO) encompasses a vast range of real-world applications, ranging
034   from transportation (J-F Audy & Rousseau, 2011) and logistics (Dincbas et al., 1992) to energy
035   management (Froger et al., 2016). These problems involve finding optimal orderings, labels, or
036   subsets of discrete sets to optimize given objective functions. Real-world CO problems are typically
037   NP-hard with a solution space growing exponentially with the problem size, making it intractable to
038   find the optimal solution. Hence, industrial solvers rely on sophisticated heuristic approaches to solve
039
      them in practice. Reinforcement Learning (RL) provides a versatile framework for learning such
      heuristics and has demonstrated remarkable success in tackling CO tasks (Mazyavkina et al., 2021).
040
041   Traditionally, RL methods train policies to incrementally construct solutions. However, achieving
042   optimality in a single construction attempt for NP-hard problems is impractical. Therefore, pre-
043   trained policies are often combined with search procedures. The literature introduces a range of these
044   procedures from stochastic sampling (Kool et al., 2019; Kwon et al., 2020; Grinsztajn et al., 2023),
045
      beam search (Steinbiss et al., 1994; Choo et al., 2022), Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) (Browne
      et al., 2012) to online fine-tuning (Bello et al., 2016; Hottung et al., 2022) and searching a space
046
      of diverse pre-trained policies (Chalumeau et al., 2023b). One popular online fine-tuning strategy,
047
      Efficient Active Search (EAS) (Hottung et al., 2022), uses transitions from generated solutions to
048   derive policy gradient updates. However, it suffers from the inherent drawbacks of back-propagation,
049   in particular having each data point only impacting the update as much as what is enabled by the
050   learning rate. The leading strategy, COMPASS (Chalumeau et al., 2023b), relies on a space of diverse
051   pre-trained policies for fast adaptation, but is limited to selecting the most appropriate one, and lacks
052   an update mechanism using the collected data. Meanwhile, the use of memory has grown in modern
053   deep learning, demonstrated by the success of retrieval augmented approaches in Natural Language
      Processing (Lewis et al., 2020), and to a smaller extent, its use in RL (Humphreys et al., 2022). These


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      mechanisms create a closer link between collected experience and policy update, making them a
055   promising candidate to improve adaptation.
056
057   In this vein, we introduce MEMENTO, a method to efficiently update the action distribution of neural
058
      solvers online, leveraging a memory of recently collected data. Importantly, MEMENTO is model
      agnostic and can be applied to many existing neural solvers. MEMENTO is able to learn expressive
059
      update rules, which prove experimentally to outperform classic policy gradient updates. We evaluate
060
      our method on two popular routing problems - Traveling Salesman (TSP) and Capacitated Vehicle
061   Routing (CVRP). We evaluate all methods both in and out of distribution, and tackle instances up to
062   size 500 to better understand their scaling properties. We experimentally show that MEMENTO can
063   be combined with existing methods and provide systematic improvements, reaching state-of-the-art
064   on 11 out of 12 tasks. We provide an analysis of the update rules learned by MEMENTO, examining
065   how it enables to adapt faster than policy gradient methods like EAS.
066
      Our contributions come as follows: (i) We introduce MEMENTO, a flexible and scalable method
067   – composed of a memory and a processing module – enabling efficient adaptation of policies at
068   inference time. (ii) We provide experimental evidence that MEMENTO can be combined with
069   existing approaches to boost their performance in and out of distribution, even for large instances and
070   unseen solvers. (iii) Whilst doing so, we train and evaluate leading construction methods on TSP
071   and CVRP instances of size 500 solely with RL, proving to outperform all existing RL methods, and
072   release the checkpoints. (iv) We open-source the implementation of MEMENTO in JAX (Bradbury
073   et al., 2018), along with test sets and checkpoints to facilitate future research advancements.

      2    R ELATED WORK
076
077
      Construction methods for CO Construction approaches in RL for CO refer to methods that
078   incrementally build a solution one action after the other. Hopfield & Tank (1985) was the first to use
079   a neural network to solve the TSP, followed by Bello et al. (2016) and Deudon et al. (2018) who
080   respectively added a reinforcement learning loss and an attention-based encoder. These works were
081   further extended by Kool et al. (2019) and Kwon et al. (2020) to use a general transformer architecture
082   (Vaswani et al., 2017), which has become the standard model choice that we also leverage in this
083   paper. These works have given rise to several variants, improving the architecture (Xin et al., 2021;
084   Luo et al., 2023) or the loss (Kim et al., 2021; 2022; Drakulic et al., 2023; Sun et al., 2024). These
085   improvements are orthogonal to our work and could a priori be combined with our method. It is to
086
      be noted that construction approaches are not restricted to routing problems: numerous works have
      tackled various CO problems, especially on graphs, like Maximum Cut (Dai et al., 2017; Barrett
087
      et al., 2020), or Job Shop Scheduling Problem (JSSP) (Zhang et al., 2020; Park et al., 2021).
088
089   Construction methods make use of a predetermined compute budget to generate one valid solution
090   for a problem instance, depending on the number of necessary action steps. In practice, it is common
091   to have a greater, fixed compute budget to solve the problem at hand such that several trials can
092
      be attempted on the same instance. However, continuously rolling out the same learned policy is
      inefficient as (i) the generated solutions lack diversity (Grinsztajn et al., 2023) and (ii) the information
093
      gathered from previous rollouts is not utilized. How to efficiently leverage this extra budget has drawn
094
      some attention recently. We present the two main types of approaches that augment RL construction
095   methods with no problem-specific knowledge (hence excluding solution improvement methods).
096
097
      Diversity-based methods The first type of approach focuses on improving the diversity of the
098   generated solutions (Kwon et al., 2020; Grinsztajn et al., 2023; Chalumeau et al., 2023b; Hottung
099   et al., 2024). POMO (Kwon et al., 2020) makes use of different starting points to enable the same
100   policy to generate diverse candidates. Poppy (Grinsztajn et al., 2023) leverages a population of agents
101   with a loss targeted at specialization on sub-distribution of instances. It was extended in Chalumeau
102   et al. (2023b) and Hottung et al. (2024), respectively replacing the population with a continuous latent
103   space (COMPASS) or a discrete context vector (PolyNet).
104
105   Policy improvement at inference time The second category of methods, which includes ME-
106   MENTO, addresses the improvement aspect. These methods are theoretically orthogonal and can be
107   combined with those mentioned earlier. EAS (Hottung et al., 2022) employs a parameter-efficient
      fine-tuning approach on the test instances, whereas SGBS (Choo et al., 2022) enhances this strategy


                                                           2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      by incorporating tree search. While these two methods demonstrate impressive performance, they
109   rely on rigid, handcrafted improvement and search procedures, which may not be optimal for diverse
110   problem domains or computational budgets.
111
112
      In contrast, MOCO, FER and MOCO (Dernedde et al., 2024; Jingwen et al., 2023; I. Garmendia
      et al., 2024) learn the policy improvement update. MOCO (Dernedde et al., 2024) introduces a meta-
113
      optimizer that learns to calculate flexible parameter updates based on the reinforce gradient, remaining
114
      budget, and best solutions discovered so far. MARCO (I. Garmendia et al., 2024) aggregates graph
115   edges’ features and introduce problem-dependent similarity metrics to improve exploration of the
116   solution space. Both approaches enable to learn adaptation strategies but are tied to heatmap-based
117   policies, preventing their applicability to certain CO problems like CVRP. FER (Jingwen et al., 2023)
118   learns a rule to update the instance nodes’ embedding, whereas MEMENTO directly updates the
119   action logits, hence being architecture-agnostic, scaling better and achieving higher performance.

      3.1   P RELIMINARIES
124
125   Formulation A CO problem can be represented as a Markov Decision Process (MDP) denoted by
126   M = (S, A, R, T, H). This formulation encompasses the state space S with states si ∈ S, the action
127   space A with actions ai ∈ A, the reward function R(r|s, a), the transition function T (si+1 |si , ai ),
128   and the horizon H indicating the episode duration. Here, the state of a problem instance is portrayed
129   as the sequence of actions taken within that instance, where the subsequent state st+1 is determined
130   by applying the chosen action at to the current state st . An agent is introduced in the MDP to engage
131   with the problem and seek solutions by learning a policy π(a|s). The standard objective considered
132   in RL works is the single shot optimisation, i.e. finding a policy that generates a trajectory τ which
                                                                                         PH
133   maximises the collected reward: π ∗ = arg maxπ Eτ ∼π [R(τ )], where R(τ ) = t=0 R(st , at ).
134   The specificity of most practical cases in RL for CO is that the policy is given a budget of shots B to
135   find the best possible solution, rather than a single shot. Consequently, the learning objective should
136   rather be : π ∗ = arg maxπ Eτi ∼π [maxi=1,...,B R(τi )]. It is hence beneficial to learn policies with
137   higher entropy to provide better exploration, and ideally, policies that can use their previous attempts
138   to (i) explore decisions they have not tried yet (ii) put more focus on promising regions of the space.
139
140   3.2   MEMENTO
141
142   Adaptation to unseen instances is crucial for neural solvers. Even when evaluated in the distribution
143   they were trained on, neural solvers are not expected to provide the optimal solution on the first shot,
144
      due to the NP-hardness of the problems tackled. Making clever use of the available compute budget
      for efficient online adaptation is key to performance.
145
146   A compelling approach is to store all past attempts in a memory which can be leveraged for subsequent
147   trajectories. This ensures that no information is lost and that promising trajectories can be used more
148   than once. There are many ways of implementing such framework depending on how the information
149   is stored, retrieved, and used in the policy. We would like the memory-based update mechanism to
150
      be (i) learnable (how to use past trajectories to craft better trajectories should be learnt instead of
      harcoded) (ii) light-weight to not compromise inference time unduly (iii) agnostic to the underlying
151
      model architecture (iv) able to leverage pre-trained memory-less policies.
152
153
      Overview To this end, we introduce MEMENTO, a method that dynamically adapts the action
154
      distribution of the neural solver using online collected data. This approach, illustrated on Fig. 1,
155
      achieves the four desiderata: it stores light-weight data about past attempts, and uses an auxiliary
156   model to update the action logits directly based on the previous outcomes. This allows MEMENTO
157   to be used on top of any pretrained policy and to be totally agnostic to its architecture. In principle,
158   MEMENTO can be combined with most existing construction RL algorithms: Attention-Model (Kool
159   et al., 2019), POMO (Kwon et al., 2020), Poppy (Grinsztajn et al., 2023), COMPASS (Chalumeau
160   et al., 2023b), which we confirm experimentally in Section 4.2. It learns an update rule during
161   training, enabling data-efficient adaptation compared to policy gradient methods. The data retrieved
      from the memory and used by the auxiliary model to compute the new action logits contains more

228   Training Existing construction methods are trained for one-shot optimization, with a few exceptions
229   that are trained for few-shots (Grinsztajn et al., 2023; Chalumeau et al., 2023b; Hottung et al., 2024).
230   We adapt the training process to take into account the budgeted multi-shot setting in which methods
231   are actually used in practice. We hence rollout the policy as many times as the budget allows on the
232   same instance before taking an update. Our loss is inspired by ECO-DQN (Barrett et al., 2020) and
233   ECORD (Barrett et al., 2022): for a new given trajectory, the reward is the ReLU of the difference
234
      between the return and the best return achieved so far on that instance. Hence, those summed rewards
      equal the best score found over the budget. In practice, to account for the fact that it is harder to
235
      get an improvement as we get closer to the end of the budget, we multiply this loss with a weight
236
      that increases logarithmically as the budget is consumed. See Appendix B for explicit formula
237   and Appendix B for pseudo-code.
238
239   Inference At the end of the training process, we obtain the parameters of the memory processing
240   module, which can be used with any neural solver with no back-propagation needed anymore. The
241   parameters of the base neural solver and the weights of the memory processing module are fixed, and
242   the adaptation rules while simply be derived by filling the memory with the collected attempts and by
243   processing the data retrieved from the memory. All hyperparameters can be found in Appendix C.

      Setup For routing problems like TSP and CVRP, POMO (Kwon et al., 2020) is the base single-
301   agent, one-shot architecture that underpins most RL construction solvers. In this set of experiments,
302   MEMENTO and EAS both use POMO as a base policy, and adapt it to get the best possible
303   performance within a given budget. We train MEMENTO until it converges, on the same instance
304   distribution as that used for the initial checkpoint. When assessing active-search performance, each
305   method operates within a fixed budget of 1600 attempts, a methodology akin to Hottung et al. (2022).
306   In this setup, each attempt comprises of one trajectory per possible starting point. This standardized
307   approach facilitates direct comparison to POMO and EAS, which also utilize rollouts from all starting
308   points at each step. Following recent works, we do not use augmentations with symmetries which
309
      have proved to not be critical and greatly increase the cost (Chalumeau et al., 2023b).

501   Time complexity Time performance at scale is key for the wide adoption of neural solvers since
502   the number of sequential batches of attempts that can be achieved within a time budget will mostly
503   depend on it (plus on the accessible hardware). Adaptation methods must be able to tackle large
504   instances in reasonable time, and they must also scale well with the size of the base solver used,
505
      since Luo et al. (2023) demonstrated the importance of using large and multi-layered decoders for
      performance in neural CO (although they could not yet train them with RL).
506
507   Hence, we evaluate MEMENTO and EAS on a set of increasing instance sizes and decoder sizes and
508   report their time complexity on the right part of Fig. 7. These plot show that, despite being slower
509   for small settings, MEMENTO’s adaptation mechanism scales better than EAS. For an instance size
510   of 1000, MEMENTO becomes 20% faster to use the budget. And even for small instances (size
511
      100), using 10 layers in the decoder’s architecture (1M parameters) makes EAS 40% slower than

512
      rather than relying on back-propagation to adapt neural solvers at inference time.

516   5    C ONCLUSION
517
518
519
      We present MEMENTO, a method to improve adaptation of neural CO solvers to unseen instances.
      MEMENTO enables to condition a policy directly on data collected online during search, facilitating
520
      rapid adaptation and efficient use of the search budget. In practice, it proves to outperform stochastic
521
      sampling, tree search and policy-gradient fine-tuning of the policy, and shows it can combined with
522   another solver than the one used during training, with no further re-training needed. Additionally,
523   MEMENTO respects several key properties, like robustness to low-budget regimes, and favorable
524   time performance scalability, both in instance size and base solver size.
525
      We demonstrate the efficacy of MEMENTO by achieving state-of-the-art single-policy adaptation
526
      on a standard benchmark on TSP and CVRP, both in and out-of-distribution. Moreover, we find
527
      that MEMENTO realises interpretable update rules to the underlying policy; trading off exploration
528   and exploitation over the search budget and outperforming REINFORCE-style updates. We further
529   demonstrate the flexibility of this approach by achieving zero-shot combination of MEMENTO and
530   COMPASS, achieving overall state-of-the-art on a benchmark of 12 tasks. The simplicity and efficacy
531   of this method makes it amenable to a very large set of problems, laying another stone towards the
532   application of neural solvers to real-world problems.
533
      Limitations and future work. MEMENTO incurs additional compute and memory-usage compared
534   to the memory-less base policies which it augments. Practically, we find that the performance gain
535   significantly outweighs these overheads. A potential mitigation could be to derive the mathematical
536   update learned by MEMENTO to avoid relying on the MLP computation. The adaptation enabled
537   by MEMENTO is determined by the data collected by the base policy, therefore this could limit the
538   efficiency when evaluation instances are very far from the training distribution, or when the base
539   policy has harmfully poor initial performance. Finally, we believe that the capacity of MEMENTO
      could be improved by training on broader distributions, in particular using variable instance sizes.

785   Times reported When possible, we decide to report the time to solve one instance rather the entire
786
      dataset following four main observations: (i) First, the literature use datasets of varying sizes, e.g.

787
      enable to clearly see how methods’ solving time scale with instance size. (ii) Second, the default
788
      real-world application consist in solving one instance at a time, or solving several instances at the
789   same time but on separated hardware. (iii) Additionally, measuring on the entire dataset mixes several
790   aspects, i.e. the instance solving time is mixed with the batch scalability of the method. We do think
791   that this property is very interesting to know, but should be considered on the side, rather than mixed
792   with the instance solving time. (iv) Moreover, this property will express differently depending on the
793   hardware available. For instance, on a small hardware POMO sampling with n attempts will be n
794   times slower than POMO greedy; but given a large enough GPU, POMO sampling can be parallelised
795   n times and hence take exactly the same amount of time as POMO greedy.
796   We were able to do so on the standard benchmark since we have implementations of most methods we
797   were comparing in a single framework. Since we are reporting several external methods in Section 4.3,
798   we could only report time taken for the full dataset in that case.

      B     T RAINING PROCEDURE
912
913   This section give a detailed description of how an existing pre-trained model can be augmented with
914
      MEMENTO, and how we train MEMENTO to acquire adaptation capacities.
915   Firstly, we take an existing single-agent model that was trained using the REINFORCE algorithm
916   and reuse it as a base model. In our case, the base model is POMO. We augment POMO with a
917   memory module and begin the training procedure which aims to create a policy that is able to use past
      experiences to make decisions in a multi-shot setting. We initialize the memory module weights with


                                                                 17
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


918
      small values such that they barely affect the initial output. Hence, the initial MEMENTO checkpoint
919   maintains the same performance as the pre-trained POMO checkpoint.
920
921   In the training procedure, the policy is trained to use data stored in the memory to take decisions and
922
      solve a problem instance. This is achieved by training the policy in a budgeted multi-shot setting
      on a problem instance where past experiences are collected and stored in a memory. The memory
923
      is organised by nodes such that only information about a specific node is found in a data row that
924
      corresponds to that node. The policy retrieves data from the memory at each budget attempt and
925   learns how to uses the data to decide on its next action.
926
927   The details of the MEMENTO training procedure are presented in Algorithm 1 and can be understood
928
      as follows. At each iteration, we sample a batch B of instances from a problem distribution D.
      Then, for each instance ρi where i ∈ 1, . . . , B, for K budget attempts, we retrieve data from the
929
      memory. This is done as follows; given that the current selected node is aj , we only retrieve data
930
      M (aj ) associated with node aj and its starting point. However, the method is agnostic to starting
931   point sampling and would work without it. The features associated with the retrieved action are then
932   normalised. We add remaining budget as an additional feature and process the data by a Multilayer
933   Perceptron (MLP) which outputs a scalar weight for each action. We compute correction logits by
934   averaging the actions based on their respective weights. The correction logits are added to the logits
935   of the base policy. We then rollout the resulting policy πθ̃ on the problem instance (i.e., generate a
936   trajectory which represents a solution to the instance). After every policy rollout attempt, the memory
937   is updated with transitions data such as the action taken, the obtained return, the log-probability of
938   the action and the log-probability of the trajectory. These data are stored in the row that corresponds
939
      to the current selected node.
940
941
      Details about the loss For each problem instance, we want to optimise for the best return. At each
942   attempt, we apply the rectified linear unit (ReLU) function to the difference between the last return
943   and the best return ever obtained. We use the rectified difference to compute the REINFORCE loss at
944   each attempt to avoid having a reward that is too sparse and perform back-propagation through the
945   network parameters of our model (including the encoder, the decoder and the memory networks).
946   The sum of the rectified differences is equal to the best return ever obtained over the budget. As the
947   budget is used, it becomes harder to improve over the previous best, the loss terms hence getting
948   smaller. We found that adding a weight to the terms, with logarithmic increase, helped ensuring
949   that the last terms would not vanish, and thus improved performance. We provide the mathematical
950
      formulation below.
951   Given a problem instance, we unroll B trajectories that we store iteratively in the memory. Each trajec-
952   tory τi generates a return R(τi ). The advantage for each trajectory is defined as R̃(τi ) = max(R(τi )−
953   Rbest , 0), where Rbest is the highest return found so far. The total loss for updating the policy is cal-
                                                           PB                           P
954   culated using the REINFORCE algorithm: L = − i=1 log(1 + ϵ + i)R̃(τi ) t log πM (at |st , Mt ),
955   where πM is the policy enriched with the memory Mt , using the logits lM defined in eq.1 in the
956   paper. ϵ is a small number ensuring that the first term is not zero.
957
      To keep the computations tractable, we still compute a loss at each step, estimate the gradient, and
958   average them sequentially until the budget is reached, at which point we take a gradient update step
959   and consider a new batch of instances.
960
961
      In practice, we also observe that we can improve performance further by adding an optional refining
      phase where the base model is frozen, and only the memory module is trained, with a reduced learning
962
      rate (multiplied by 0.1), for a few hours.

       In Section 3.2, we presented the architecture used by the auxiliary model that processes the memory
1072
       data to derive the new action logits. The intuition behind this architecture choice is that it should
1073
       be able to learn the REINFORCE update rule. Indeed the REINFORCE loss associated with a
1074
       new transition is R log(πθ (a)), such that ∂R log(π

                                                                  = R(1 − πθ (a)). Therefore, simply having
1075
1076
       HθM (fa ) match R(1 − πθ (a)) would recover a REINFORCE-like update. This is feasible, as R and
       log(πθ (a)) are included in the features fa .

       G    A BLATION S TUDY: MEMENTO INPUT FEATURES
1081
1082
       In Section 3.2, we present MEMENTO, in particular, we present all the inputs that are used by the
1083
       neural module to derive the new action logits from the memory data. These inputs, or features,
1084
       are information associated to each past action taken, and that help decide whether those actions
1085   should be taken again or not. In Section 4.1, we compare the rule learned by MEMENTO compared
1086   to the policy-gradient update REINFORCE. This comparison is made on the features that both
1087   REINFORCE and MEMENTO use: i.e. the action log probability, and the return (or advantage if
1088   a baseline is used). Although REINFORCE only uses those two features, MEMENTO uses more,
1089   which enables to refine even more the update, and also to adapt it to the budget remaining in the
1090   search process. As a recall, in addition to the log probability and return, MEMENTO uses: the log
1091   probability of the full trajectory, the log probability of the rest of the trajectory after the action was
1092   taken, the budget at the time the action was taken, the action logit suggested by MEMENTO when
1093
       the action was taken, and the budget currently remaining.
1094   To validate the impact of all the features used in the memory, we provide an ablation study of those
1095   features. To highlight the interest of using all the additional features, we report the performance of
1096   MEMENTO using only the return and the log probability, against the performance of MEMENTO
1097   with all features, on Fig. 8. We also report on Fig. 9 a bigger ablation where components are added
1098   one after the others.
1099   We can extract two main observations: (i) first, adding the remaining budget completely changes the
1100   strategy. We can see on the right panel of Fig. 8 that having access to this additional feature enables
1101   MEMENTO to explore much more, and then to focus on high-performing solutions when it gets
1102   closer to the end of the budget; (ii) then, Fig. 9 confirms empirically that all features contribute to
1103   improving the overall performance.
