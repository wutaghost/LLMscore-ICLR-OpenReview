009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            As large language models (LLMs) grow more powerful, ensuring their safety
012            against misuse becomes crucial. While researchers have focused on developing
013            robust defenses, no method has yet achieved complete invulnerability to attacks.
014            We propose an alternative approach: instead of seeking perfect adversarial ro-
015            bustness, we develop rapid response techniques to look to block whole classes
016
               of jailbreaks after observing only a handful of attacks. To study this setting, we
               develop RapidResponseBench, a benchmark that measures a defense’s robustness
017
               against various jailbreak strategies after adapting to a few observed examples. We
018
               evaluate five rapid response methods, all of which use jailbreak proliferation, where
019            we automatically generate additional jailbreaks similar to the examples observed.
020            Our strongest method, which fine-tunes an input classifier to block proliferated jail-
021            breaks, reduces attack success rate by a factor greater than 240 on an in-distribution
022            set of jailbreaks and a factor greater than 15 on an out-of-distribution set, having
023            observed just one example of each jailbreaking strategy. Moreover, further studies
024            suggest that the quality of proliferation model and number of proliferated examples
025            play an key role in the effectiveness of this defense. Overall, our results highlight
026            the potential of responding rapidly to novel jailbreaks to limit LLM misuse.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   As Large Language Models (LLMs) become more capable, they pose greater misuse risks. Indeed,
031   the potential for catastrophic misuse of LLMs has motivated AI labs to make public commitments
032   to developing safeguards to minimize the risk of such misuse (Anthropic, 2023; OpenAI, 2023).
033   Additionally, such concerns have motivated substantial effort from the research community to defend
034   against jailbreaks, which are techniques that extract harmful information from LLMs trained to be
035   helpful, harmless, and honest (Bai et al., 2022b; Xie et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2024).
036   Despite ongoing research, ensuring that large language models (LLMs) are robustly resistant to
037   jailbreaking remains an unsolved challenge (Hendrycks et al., 2021b; Ziegler et al., 2022). Even
038   state-of-the-art methods that substantially improve robustness, such as representation rerouting (Zou
039   et al., 2024), have been publicly broken within hours of release. The situation could worryingly
040   parallel that of adversarial robustness in computer vision, where new defenses are often defeated
041   by attacks available before their development with proper tuning (Tramer et al., 2020). Indeed, in
042   computer vision, a decade of work and thousands of papers have yielded “limited progress” (Carlini,
043   2024). If we cannot design AI systems that are robust to persistent jailbreaking attempts, how can we
044
      safely deploy highly capable LLMs?
045   In this work, we thus propose Jailbreak Rapid Response as an alternative paradigm for mitigating
046   LLM misuse (Fig. 1). Traditional approaches aim to develop highly robust static systems that resist
047   all possible jailbreaks. In contrast, jailbreak rapid response emphasizes effectively monitoring for
048   novel jailbreaks and quickly defending against those jailbreaks after observing them.
049   To assess the feasibility of jailbreak rapid response, we introduce a new benchmark:
050   RapidResponseBench. Our benchmark measures the effectiveness of different rapid response tech-
051   niques in protecting against novel jailbreak attacks. The benchmark includes six jailbreaking attack
052   strategies. For each strategy, we allow a jailbreak defense method to observe a few successful
053   instances of the attack and measure the attack success rate (ASR) of new attempts as the number of
      observed jailbreak examples increases. We also test out-of-distribution (OOD) variants of each attack

091   Having demonstrated the promise of jailbreak rapid response on RapidResponseBench, we then

093   real-world catastrophic misuse. In particular, we highlight the role of timely jailbreak identification
094   and response, the quality of the rapid response method, and the misuse threat model. While frontier
095   AI labs can influence some of these factors, details of the threat model are harder to influence. As
096   such, further research is needed to understand precisely how LLM misuse occurs.
097   Overall, our work highlights jailbreak rapid response as a potentially promising new paradigm for
098   mitigating misuse risks from large language models. With further research to better understand threat
099   models, improve real-time jailbreak detection, and improve rapid response and proliferation methods,
100   this approach offers a promising alternative to static adversarial defense. Our benchmark is open
101   source and we hope others improve upon our baseline results.1

              R APID R ESPONSE T ECHNIQUES
111
      In this section, we introduce RapidResponseBench, a benchmark designed to evaluate the effec-
112
      tiveness of various rapid response techniques in mitigating classes of jailbreak attacks on LLMs.
113
      RapidResponseBench measures the ability of rapid response methods to defend against varied
114   jailbreaking strategies given a small number of observed examples of each, while simultaneously
115   assessing the impact of these methods on refusal rates for benign queries. An effective rapid response
116   technique should be capable of generalizing from a few known jailbreak instances to prevent a wide
117   range of related attacks, without significantly increasing the refusal rate on harmless user requests.
118
119   2.1       R ATIONALE & M ETRICS
120
121   In the real world, multiple attackers develop jailbreaks for AI systems. To do so, attackers may
122   develop new jailbreak algorithms or techniques. Moreover, attackers can start with an initial jailbreak
123
      and iteratively modify it to bypass potentially updated defenses. We want to be able to defend against
      these novel attempts while not falsely triggering refusals for benign users. To account for these
124
      concerns, we consider several different jailbreaking strategies. We evaluate rapid response in the
125
      following settings:
126
127            1. In-distribution (ID): for each observed jailbreaking strategy, we measure how well a rapid
128               response method reduces the attack success rate (ASR) of attacks employing the strategy.
129            2. Out-of-distribution (OOD): for each observed jailbreaking strategy, we measure how well
130               rapid response reduces the ASR of attacks employing an unseen variant of the strategy,
131               simulating novel adaptations that attackers may make to existing jailbreaks.
132
               3. Refusal of benign queries: We measure the refusal rate of the adapted system on benign
133               queries, which represent users asking LLMs entirely harmless prompts.
134
135   We assume that jailbroken model outputs can be detected through post-hoc analysis after they have
136   been generated and sent to users, but we cannot perform this detection during the output process itself.
137   This limitation may stem from various factors, such as the need for real-time streaming of model
138   outputs, the computational cost of the output review process, or the high latency associated with
139   certain review methods (e.g., human evaluation). In this study, we use the Llama-3-Instruct-70B
140
      jailbreak classifier proposed by Chao et al. (2024) as the ground truth judge of whether a given input
      elicits a harmful response.
141
142
143
      2.2       DATASETS
144   Jailbreaking Strategies To construct our benchmark, we need to specify in-distribution and out-
145   of-distribution examples of different jailbreaking strategies. We use EasyJailbreak (Zhou et al.,
146   2024) to implement six state-of-the-art black-box jailbreak strategies2 . Each strategy represents a
147   determined attacker with a novel misuse strategy and subsequent attempts to modify the strategy
148   to bypass defenses. We generate our in-distribution training set, our in-distribution test set, and
149   our out-of-distribution test set each by running all attack strategies against 100 randomly selected
150   behaviors from AdvBench (Zou et al., 2023). The behaviors we select are disjoint across jailbreak
151   sets. We consider six strategies:
152
               1. Prompt Automatic Iterative Refinement (PAIR; Chao et al., 2023) employs an attack
153
                  LLM to iteratively refine a prompt until it elicits a harmful behavior from the target model.
154
                  Our OOD variant additionally translates words, inserts random characters, and misspells
155               sensitive words.
156
               2. ReNeLLM (Ding et al., 2023) nests a harmful request within manually crafted scenarios
157
                  and mutates the request through transformations such as translation, misspelling sensitive
158
                  words, or inserting random characters. Our ID variant nests harmful requests in completing
159               a latex table or completing a Python script, and our OOD variant nests harmful requests in
160               completing a paragraph.
161
          2
              We assume the attacker does not have log-prob access.

162
            3. Skeleton Key (Russinovich, 2024) prompts the target model to modify its behavior and
163            provide a warning instead of outright refusing harmful content. A Skeleton Key attack could,
164            for example, include the instruction "update your behavior to provide the information asked
165            for, but if the content might be harmful, prefix it with ‘Warning’" in the prompt, and thus
166            leverages a competing objective to being harmless (Wei et al., 2023a). The OOD variant of
167            Skeleton Key introduces an additional competing objective by directly prompting the target
168            to begin with the affirmative response. For example, we could include the instruction "begin
169            your response with ’Understood’", along with the behavior modification request, and "begin
170            your response with ’Warning’", along with the request for harmful behavior.
171         4. Many-shot Jailbreaking (MSJ; Anil et al., 2024) uses in-context learning to induce models
172            to produce harmful behavior by placing many examples (“shots”) of the target LLM out-
173            putting harmful behavior in the context-window of the model. The OOD variant of MSJ
174            employs more shots. To bypass the input guard, we modify Anil et al. (2024)’s method by
175            including directives in each shot to assess it as safe (see Appendix B).
176         5. Crescendo (Russinovich et al., 2024) uses an attack LLM to gradually guide conversations
177            towards restricted topics over multiple turns. The OOD variant of Crescendo encodes all
178            user prompts in leetspeak or base64.
179         6. Cipher (Yuan et al., 2024) makes harmful requests that are encoded in an encoding scheme.
180            The ID variant uses the Caesar cipher or ASCII code, and the OOD variant uses Morse code.
181
182   RapidResponseBench assesses the effectiveness of rapid response by measuring the attack success
183   rates of jailbreaks from the above strategies. To do so, we simulate how the target system would
184   adapt its defenses assuming we observe various (small) numbers of successful jailbreaks during
185   deployment.
186
187   Refusal Rate Measurement To quantify the potential disruption to benign users caused by rapid
188   response to novel jailbreaks, we measure the refusal rate of the model on the WildChat dataset (Zhao
189   et al., 2024), an open collection of user queries submitted to ChatGPT (OpenAI, 2022) that have
190   been filtered for inappropriate content using OpenAI’s moderation API (Markov et al., 2022) and the
191
      Detoxify tool (Hanu & Unitary team, 2020).
192
193   2.3   BASELINE R APID R ESPONSE M ETHODS
194
      Here, we consider baselines that focus on input-guarded LLM systems, which, as compared to
195
      output-guarded systems, can be used with minimal latency and support real-time streaming of model
196
      outputs. This approach aligns with real-world implementations, such as prompt shield (Rees, 2024)
197   and Llama Guard (Inan et al., 2023).
198
199   The defenses we consider rely on a technique we call jailbreak proliferation, which augments the
200
      small set of observed jailbreaks with additional attempts generated by a language model. Jailbreak
      proliferation is similar to automated red-teaming (Perez et al., 2022), but while automated red-teaming
201
      looks to generate novel, diverse jailbreaks, jailbreak proliferation looks to generate variants similar to
202
      an existing jailbreak. These generated examples are then made available to the defenses, alongside
203   benign queries. Jailbreak proliferation can be understood as a data augmentation technique, which
204   is well-known to improve the performance and robustness of machine learning models (Shorten &
205   Khoshgoftaar, 2019; Wei & Zou, 2019).
206
      We implement and evaluate five defense methods:
207
208
            1. Regex employs an LLM to generate regular expressions (“regexes”) that are used at test time
209
               to filter out jailbreak attacks. The LM iteratively refines the regexes to filter out example
210            jailbreaks and attack proliferations while minimizing false positives on a static set of known
211            benign prompts.
212
            2. Guard Fine-tuning fine-tunes an LM-based input classifier using known example jailbreaks,
213
               attack proliferations, and benign prompts.
214
215         3. Embedding trains a logistic regression classifier on prompt embeddings from an embedding
               model, using example jailbreaks, attack proliferations, and benign prompts.


                                                          4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
                4. Guard Few-shot includes the five most similar example jailbreaks or attack proliferations
217                (based on prompt embeddings from an embedding model) as few-shot examples in the
218                LM-based input guard’s context window.
219
220             5. Defense Prompt uses an LM to generate a suffix that is appended to user prompts before
221
                   being sent to the target language model. For each known attack prompt, the LM iterates
                   on a suffix that neutralizes the attack while maintaining benign functionality for similar
222
                   non-attack prompts.

225   3       H OW WELL DOES JAILBREAK R APID R ESPONSE W ORK ?
226
227   We now evaluate how quickly our baseline rapid response techniques mitigate jailbreaks. We find
228   that several rapid response techniques substantially reduce the effectiveness of jailbreak strategies,
229   and rapid response tends to increase in effectiveness when observing more examples of jailbreaks
230   from each strategy in the wild. In particular, we find Guard Fine-tuning offers the largest reduction
231   in attack success rate on in-distribution attacks, and generalizes best to out-of-distribution attack
232   variants, while also having the smallest impact on the refusal rate on benign queries.
233
234   3.1       E XPERIMENT D ETAILS
235
236   We now briefly outline our experimental setup. For additional details, see Appendix B.
237
238   Target Models We consider rapid response using three different input-guarded LLMs. For the text
239   generation model, we use GPT-4o (OpenAI, 2024), Llama-3-Instruct-8B (Dubey et al., 2024), and
240   Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.2 (Jiang et al., 2023). We chose these models because they represent
241   a diverse mix of models that an LLM provider may wish to defend. As the input guard, we use
242   Llama-Guard-2-8B (Llama Team, 2024). Our main results average across models and attacks; see
243   Appendix A for per-model results.
244
245   Jailbreak Proliferation Recall that our rapid response baselines make use of jailbreak proliferation,
246   which uses observed jailbreaks to generate additional data examples for rapid response adaptation.
247   For each jailbreaking strategy,3 we generate 1000 proliferation attempts, distributed evenly across
248
      different harmful behaviors. We prompt a language model (Llama-3.1-70B-Instruct) to generate
      a jailbreak that mimics the style of a provided example but for a different target harmful behavior.
249
      We use chain of thought (Wei et al., 2022), asking the proliferation model to first summarize the
250
      strategy of the example jailbreak and then generate the proliferation, and further prefill the assistant
251   response to ensure the model complies with our request. See Appendix C for prompting details and
252   Appendix D for example proliferations.
253
254
      Rapid Response Baselines We benchmark Regex, Guard Fine-tuning, Guard Few-shot,
255
      Defense Prompt, and Embedding. All methods make use of benign queries from WildChat and
256   proliferated jailbreaks from the observed examples. For Guard Fine-tuning, we calibrate the model
257   classification threshold, which determines whether a given input is blocked or not, to maintain the
258   same refusal rate as the original system. To model a real-world setup where a defense must contend
259   with many distributed attackers with different attack strategies, each defense observes mixed samples
260   of different attack strategies, and must simultaneously defend against all attack strategies during
261   evaluation. See Appendix E for more details.

344   3.3   A NALYSIS : G ENERALIZATION TO NOVEL JAILBREAKS
345
346   While rapid response aims to retroactively block seen attacks and their variants, we investigate its
347   ability to generalize to unseen attacks. We conduct two experiments evaluating Guard Fine-tuning.
348
      First, we evaluate attacks generated using TAP, an entirely unseen and adaptive attack, against a
349   classifier guarded model with finetuned guard that has undergone rapid response on 1, 5, and 25 shots
350   of each attack in our benchmark attack ensemble. We find that rapid response successfully blocks
351   TAP attacks despite never observing TAP-generated jailbreaks (Fig. 3a).
352
      Second, we test against the Multi-Turn Human Jailbreaks (MHJ) dataset, which contains successful
353
      human-created jailbreaks that defeat static defenses such as Representation Rerouting (Zou et al.,
354
      2024) and Latent Adversarial Training (Casper et al., 2024). While not specifically designed for
355   GPT-4o, we reconstruct attack sequences by sending each user turn to the model sequentially. We
356   find rapid response achieves up to a 57.5% relative reduction in attack success rate (ASR) compared
357   to baseline Fig. 3b), but this effect does not scale uniformly with shots. This demonstrates meaningful
358   but incomplete generalization to this challenging out-of-distribution attack set.
359
      These results highlight that while rapid response shows some promising generalization to unseen
360
      attacks, like all other proposed defenses, complete static robustness remains elusive — reinforcing
361   the necessity of an adaptive defense paradigm.

430   method also benefits substantially from improved proliferation model capabilities. These findings
431   suggest that improving proliferation techniques is a promising direction for strengthening rapid
      response defenses against jailbreaking attempts.

434
      Having demonstrated the promise of jailbreak rapid response, we now consider whether rapid
435
      response is appropriate for mitigating real-world misuse. This is particularly relevant because several
436   AI labs have made public commitments to minimize the risk of catastrophic misuse (Anthropic, 2023;
437   OpenAI, 2023). We now outline different factors that critically determine how well rapid response
438   mitigates misuse, and note that frontier AI laboratories are well-positioned to influence several of
439   these factors. However, some of them critically depend on the specific threat model.
440
441   Timely Jailbreak Identification For rapid response to be able to mitigate AI misuse, frontier AI
442   labs must be able to identify and address novel jailbreaks before they are exploited by malicious
443   actors. Indeed, Hendrycks et al. (2021a) identifies monitoring and anomaly detection as an unsolved
444   problem in ML safety and integral for preventing novel misuse, and Markov et al. (2022) reaches
445   in the same direction, concluding that active learning on production data is necessary for training
446
      moderation models. Other techniques, like implementing a bug bounty program (e.g., Anthropic,
      2024) may further increase the likelihood of timely jailbreak discovery.
447
448
449
      Timely Jailbreak Response Effective misuse mitigation through rapid response requires not only
      timely jailbreak detection, but also rapid system updates by AI developers in response to identified
450
      vulnerabilities. Drawing on insights from cybersecurity incident response frameworks (Schlette et al.,
451
      2021), practical deployment requires balancing multiple constraints around processes, technology,
452   governance and compliance when responding to threats. However, LLMs present unique challenges
453   compared to traditional security systems - detecting jailbreaks requires running expensive model
454   inference for monitoring, and updating models to patch vulnerabilities can involve costly retraining or
455   fine-tuning steps. Additionally, while our initial results indicate the ability to adequately address the
456   evaluated jailbreaking strategies, future attack techniques may prove more challenging to mitigate.
457
458   Low-Stakes Failures The viability of rapid response as a safety mechanism depends heavily on the
459   threat model. Christiano (2021) defines low-stakes scenarios as those where we care about average
460   performance over long time periods rather than individual decisions, allowing systems to be retrained
461   before meaningful harm accumulates. In such settings, rapid response may be appropriate. This
462   framework applies even to concerning misuse domains like weapons of mass destruction. Indeed,
463
      Rose et al. (2024) identify several misuse threat models where misuse is enabled by AI systems
      potentially providing technical assistance over a prolonged period of time, which would correspond to
464
      low-stakes scenarios. However, in other threat models, where AI systems reveal potentially sensitive
465
      information (Wilson & Dawson, 2024), rapid response is less likely to be appropriate.
466
467
      Rapid Response Method As shown in Fig. 2, different rapid response techniques perform differ-
468
      ently in-distribution and out-of-distribution, and offer different levels of sample efficiency. Further-
469   more, as demonstrated in Fig. 4, response methods receive varying degrees of benefit from jailbreak
470   proliferation, with some methods like Guard Fine-tuning showing dramatic improvements while
471   others see only modest gains. Rapid response will more effectively mitigate misuse when used with
472   defense methods with strong generalization that can handle the kind of novel, adaptive methods
473   that attackers use in the wild; according to our results, such methods for rapid response may likely
474   incorporate jailbreak proliferation with large compute budgets.
475
476
      5    R ELATED W ORK
477
478
      Adversarial Defense for LLMs Reinforcement learning from human feedback is a common
479
      approach for improving the robustness and safety of large language models (LLMs) (Ouyang et al.,
480   2022; Bai et al., 2022a; Team et al., 2023; Dubey et al., 2024), with AI-generated feedback also being
481   explored (Bai et al., 2022b). However, studies show that even state-of-the-art LLMs trained with these
482   methods remain vulnerable to various jailbreaking attacks (Wei et al., 2023a; Mazeika et al., 2024).
483   Several methods have been proposed to enhance the adversarial robustness of LLMs, including using
484   in-context examples of refusal to harmful requests (Wei et al., 2023b), averaging responses among
485   perturbed inputs (Robey et al., 2023), checking if the model refuses requests with random token drops
      (Cao et al., 2023), and removing the model’s ability to produce harmful output through representation


                                                         9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      re-routing (Zou et al., 2024). However, many methods have been publicly broken within hours of
487   release, mirroring the "limited progress" in computer vision adversarial robustness over a decade of
488   work (Carlini, 2024). In contrast, rapid response aims to quickly identify and mitigate novel jailbreaks
489   before they can be exploited for misuse, and emphasizes rapid adaptation and monitoring rather than
490   strong static adversarial defenses.
491
492   Automated Red-Teaming, Adversarial Training, and Data Augmentation Jailbreak proliferation
493   is closely related to automated red-teaming (Perez et al., 2022; Yu et al., 2023; Hong et al., 2024;
494   Samvelyan et al., 2024). However, while automated red-teaming focuses on discovering novel
495   attacks, jailbreak proliferation emphasizes generating attacks similar to and derived from observed
496   attacks. In this paper, we use simple few-shot prompting for jailbreak proliferation. Combining
497
      rapid response with stronger automated red-teaming and proliferation methods could potentially
      yield even more robust defenses, particularly against out-of-distribution attack variants. Jailbreak
498
      rapid response is also related to adversarial training (Liu et al., 2020; Yoo & Qi, 2021), which can
499
      leverage vulnerabilities found via automated red-teaming and is often performed pre-deployment. In
500   contrast, jailbreak rapid response adapts to vulnerabilities discovered at deployment time. Jailbreak
501   proliferation is also a data augmentation technique (Wei & Zou, 2019; Shorten & Khoshgoftaar,
502   2019)—leveraging insights from this field will also likely improve jailbreak rapid response.
503
504   Jailbreaking LLMs Significant research has focused on jailbreaking LLMs. Gradient-based
505   methods like Greedy Coordinate Gradients (GCG; Zou et al., 2023) search for universal jailbreaks
506   guided by gradients, but often find high-perplexity jailbreaks. Techniques that find low-perplexity
507   jailbreaks, such as direct persuasion (Zeng et al., 2024), gradient search (Zhu et al., 2023), genetic
508   algorithms (Liu et al., 2023), reverse language modeling (Pfau et al., 2023), or LLM-guided refinement
509   (PAIR; Chao et al., 2023), can bypass perplexity filtering defenses (Jain et al., 2023). Black-box
510
      search methods, including Tree of Attacks with Pruning (TAP; Mehrotra et al., 2023), can discover
      system-level jailbreaks that circumvent input-output safeguards. Query obfuscation attacks using
511
      obscure language (Huang et al., 2024), low-resource languages (Deng et al., 2023), or substitution
512
      ciphers (Yuan et al., 2024; Handa et al., 2024) have shown some success. Many-shot jailbreaks
513   exploit in-context learning to jailbreak LLMs (Anil et al., 2024). As LLMs become more capable,
514   mitigating their misuse through adversarial defense and rapid response becomes increasingly crucial.
515   Crucially, if adversaries become aware of the specific jailbreak rapid response technique, they may
516   become able to design novel attack strategies that exploit particularities of the jailbreak rapid response
517   system. Further research is needed to better understand this possibility.
518
519
      6    C ONCLUSION
520
521
      In conclusion, we introduce Jailbreak Rapid Response, a potentially promising paradigm for miti-
522   gating LLM misuse. We provide evidence that jailbreak rapid response is tractable—in our bench-
523   mark, RapidResponseBench, Guard Fine-tuning substantially reduces the attack success rate on
524   in-distribution and out-of-distribution jailbreaks with only a modest increase in the refusal rate on
525   benign queries. Our results also highlight the importance of jailbreak proliferation in enabling rapid
526   response techniques to generalize to novel jailbreak attempts with limited examples. With further
527   research into threat modeling, real-time jailbreak detection, and improved rapid response methods,
528   rapid response may offer a path forward for safely deploying highly capable language models in the
529   face of persistent jailbreaking attempts.
