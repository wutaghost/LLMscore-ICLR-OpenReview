009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011
               Hyperbolic deep learning has become a growing research direction in computer
012            vision for the unique properties afforded by the alternate embedding space. The
013            negative curvature and exponentially growing distance metric provide a natural
014            framework for capturing hierarchical relationships between datapoints and allowing
015            for finer separability between their embeddings. However, these methods are still
016            computationally expensive and prone to instability, especially when attempting to
017            learn the negative curvature that best suits the task and the data. Current Rieman-
018            nian optimizers do not account for changes in the manifold which greatly harms
019            performance and forces lower learning rates to minimize projection errors. Our
020
               paper focuses on curvature learning by introducing an improved schema for popular
               learning algorithms and providing a novel normalization approach to constrain
021
               embeddings within the variable representative radius of the manifold. Additionally,
022
               we introduce a novel formulation for Riemannian AdamW, and alternative hybrid
023            encoder techniques and foundational formulations for current convolutional hy-
024            perbolic operations, greatly reducing the computational penalty of the hyperbolic
025            embedding space. Our approach demonstrates consistent performance improve-
026            ments across both direct classification and hierarchical metric learning tasks while
027            allowing for larger hyperbolic models.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032
033
      With the recent rise in the use of hyperbolic manifolds for deep representation learning, there is a
      growing need for efficient, flexible components that can fully exploit these spaces without sacrificing
034
      stability. This has led researchers to focus on two main derivations of hyperbolic space: the Poincaré
035
      manifold and the hyperboloid. The Poincaré ball, equipped with a gyrovector space, supports various
036   well-defined operations, including generalized vector addition and multiplication, but it suffers
037   from significant stability issues. On the other hand, the hyperboloid, or Lorentz space, lacks these
038   operations but offers much better operation stability, as demonstrated in the study by Mishne et al.
039   (2022).
040
      To address this gap, previous works have sought to provide Lorentzian definitions for common deep
041
      learning operations such as the feed-forward layer (Chen et al., 2022; Dai et al., 2021; Ganea et al.,
042   2018), convolutional layer (Chen et al., 2022; Qu & Zou, 2023; Dai et al., 2021), and MLRs (Bdeir
043   et al., 2024). This increased focus on hyperbolic modeling has led to its gradual integration into
044   computer vision architectures, as detailed in the survey by Mettes et al. (2023). Specifically, the
045   hyperboloid model has been employed as a sampling space for VAEs (Nagano et al., 2019), a decoder
046   space for vision tasks in hybrid settings (Guo et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2020; Khrulkov et al., 2020;
047   Qu & Zou, 2022), and ultimately for fully hyperbolic Lorentzian vision encoders (Bdeir et al., 2024)
048   simultaneously with its Poincaré counterpart (van Spengler et al., 2023).
049   This paper furthers the development of hyperbolic learning for vision tasks, specifically for the Lorentz
050   manifold. Our primary focus is on the challenge of learning the manifold’s negative curvature.
051   The driving principle behind this, is that the model embeddings may exhibit varying degrees of
052   hyperbolicity depending on the innate hierarchies in the datapoints themselves, the problem task that
053   is being considered, and the specific locations of hyperbolic operation integrations. To accomodate
      for this, we can adjust the embedding space’s hyperbolic metric to be less or more Euclidean which

055   main blocks in the architecture further increasing representative flexibility.

057   We also recognize that despite recent advances, Lorentz models continue to struggle with issues of

073   2    R ELATED W ORK
074
075   Hyperbolic Embeddings in Computer Vision With the success of employing hyperbolic manifolds
076   in NLP models (Zhu et al., 2021; Dhingra et al., 2018; Tifrea et al., 2018) hyperbolic embeddings have
077   extended to the computer vision domain. Initially, many of the works relied on a hybrid architecture,
078   utilizing Euclidean encoders and hyperbolic decoders (Mettes et al., 2023). This was mainly due to
079   the high computational cost of hyperbolic operations in the encoder, as well as the lack of well-defined
080   alternatives for Euclidean operations. However, this trend has begun to shift towards the utilization of
081   fully hyperbolic encoders as can be seen in the hyperbolic Resnets by Bdeir et al. (2024) and van
082   Spengler et al. (2023). Both works offer hyperbolic definitions for 2D convolutional layer, batch
083
      normalization layer, and an MLR for the final classification head. Bdeir et al. (2024) even attempt
      to hybridize the encoder by employing the Lorentz manifold in blocks that exhibit higher output
084
      hyperbolicity. While this has led to notable performance improvements, both models suffer from
085
      upscaling issues. Attempting to replicate these approaches for larger datasets or bigger architectures
086   becomes much less feasible in terms of time and memory requirements. Instead, our approach places
087   higher focus on efficient components to leverage the beneficial hyperbolic properties of the model
088   while minimizing the memory and computational footprint.
089
090   Curvature Learning Previous work in hyperbolic spaces has explored various approaches to curva-
091   ture learning. In their studies, Gu et al. (2018) and Giovanni et al. (2022) achieve this by using a radial
092   parametrization that implicitly models variable curvature embeddings under an explicitly defined,
093   fixed 1-curve manifold. This method enables them to simulate K-curve hyperbolic and spherical
094   operations under constant curvature for the mixed-curve manifold specifically, a combination of the
095   Euclidean, spherical, and Poincaré manifold. Other approaches, such as the one by Kochurov et al.
096
      (2020), simply set the curvature to a learnable parameter but do not account for the manifold changes
      in the Riemannian optimizers. This leads to hyperbolic vectors being updated with mismatched
097
      curvatures and others being inaccurately reprojected, resulting in instability and accuracy degradation.
098
      Additionally, some methods, like the one by Kim et al. (2023), store all manifold parameters as
099   Euclidean vectors and project them before use. While this approach partially mitigates the issue
100   of mismatched curvature operations, it remains less accurate and more computationally expensive.
101   In comparison, our proposed optimization schema maintains the parameters on the manifold and
102   optimizes them directly by performing the necessary operations to transition between the variable
103   curvature spaces.
104
105   Metric Learning Metric learning relies on the concept of structuring the distribution in the em-
106   bedding space so that related data points are positioned closer together, while less related points
107   are placed further apart. To facilitate this process, numerous studies have introduced additional loss
      functions that explicitly encourage this behavior. Contrastive losses, for instance, operate on pairs


                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      of data points and propose a penalty that is proportional to the distances between negative pairs
109   and inversely proportional to the distance between positive pairs (Chopra et al., 2005). Triplet loss
110   extends this idea by considering sets of three points: an anchor, a positive sample, and a negative
111   sample (Wang et al., 2014). Instead of changing the distances between points absolutely, it ensures
112   that the distance between the anchor and the positive sample is less than the distance between the
113   anchor and the negative sample, plus a margin, thus enforcing a relational criterion.
114
      These approaches have also been adapted to hierarchical problem settings under hyperbolic manifolds
115   (Yang et al., 2022; Kim et al., 2023). Notably, Kim et al. (2023) developed a method for learning
116   continuous hierarchical representations using a deep learning, data-mining-like approach that relies
117   on the innate relationships of the embeddings rather than their labels. They employ a proxy-based
118   method that models the data on the Poincaré ball, facilitating a more natural extension to hierarchical
119   tasks. Building on this, we extend the approach by modeling the loss function in the Lorentz manifold
120   and incorporating a learnable curvature to better handle data with varying levels of hierarchy.

      3     M ETHODOLOGY
123
124
      Hyperbolic embeddings offer significant advantages for computer vision tasks that benefit from
125   hierarchical structure modeling, but their effectiveness is hindered by practical challenges, including
126   mathematical inconsistencies in curvature learning, the absence of a Riemannian AdamW optimizer,
127   stability issues with float32 precision, and high computational costs from inefficient convolutions.
128
      To address these issues, the following sections introduce four key advancements: a more stable
129
      curvature learning method, a Riemannian AdamW optimizer, a normalization scheme for improved
130
      float32 precision stability, and efficient hyperbolic convolutions to reduce memory and computational
131   costs.

184   the curvature updates, which neglects to update the hyperbolic operations after updating the curvature.
185   In the current Riemannian optimization approach, Euclidean gradients and their momentums are
186   projected onto the Riemannian manifold and then used to update the hyperbolic parameters. These
187   operations inherently depend on the curvature of the space. However, when the curvature is adjusted
188   during learning, the projections, gradients, and hyperbolic parameters can become inconsistent, as
189   they mix the projection and update operations from the new curvature with vectors defined under the
190   previous curvature.
191   To illustrate this, Figure 1 shows two manifolds with different curvatures. It is evident that points
192   defined on the first manifold do not lie on the second, and the tangent planes of the first manifold are
193   not aligned with those of the second. Thus, using the new curvature operations on points from the old
194   model is not mathematically sound practice. This inconsistency introduces instability, undermining
195   the reliability of curvature learning and often leading to invalid values during the parameter updates.
196   To address this, we propose a method that tracks both the old and new curvature values throughout
197
      updates. Specifically, all updates are applied under the old curvature, after which the hyperbolic
      vectors are mapped from the old manifold to the new one.
198
199   While GeoOpt does not address the optimization challenges involved in learning manifold curvature,
200   it does implement an ”N-stabilize step”. This step aims to reduce instability by recalculating the
201   time component of the hyperbolic parameters every N-steps to ensure they remain on the manifold.
202   However, this approach fails to prevent invalid parameter values before the update steps and suffers
203
      from high inaccuracies due to the mathematically flawed parameter optimization.
204   Moreover, the time re-calculation projection used in the stabilize step alters the relative magnitudes of
205   the hyperbolic vectors and gradients, and even the directions of the momentums, which can degrade
206   performance and lead to training instability. Instead, we recommend projecting points onto the
207   tangent space at the old origin using the logarithmic map and then projecting back after the curvature
208   update. Gradients and their momentum can be similarly projected or parallel transported to the
209
      tangent space. Parallel transport is chosen here because it preserves the vectors’ directions which is
      important for the optimization procedure.

299   2D convolutional layer by breaking down the convolution operation into a window-unfolding step
300   followed by a modified version of the Lorentz Linear Layer from Chen et al. (2022). This approach
301   ensured that the convolution outputs remained on the hyperboloid. However, the manual patch
302   creation combined with matrix multiplication made the computation extremely expensive, as it
303   prevented the use of highly optimized CUDA implementations for convolutions.
304

385   Moving to Lorentz Space To adapt the HIER model to the hyperboloid we first replace the Eu-
386   clidean linear layer with a Lorentzian linear layer in the model neck and implement our max distance
387   scaling operation after. We then set the hierarchical proxies as learnable hyperbolic parameters and
388   optimize them directly on the manifold using our Lorentzian AdamW. Finally, we change the Poincaré
389   distance to the Lorentz distance for the LHIER loss and set the hierarchical proxies to be scaled
390
      beforehand. We continue to use FP16 precision during curvature learning to evaluate the stability
      of the new optimization scheme. Our experiments also include both CNN-based and transformer
391
      models to assess whether differences in architecture affect the performance of the hyperbolic heads.

530   5      C ONCLUSION
531
532   In our work, we present many new components and schemas for the use of hyperbolic deep learning
533   in hyperbolic vision. We test these components in three vision tasks and prove the potential of these
534   new components even in float16 conditions.
535
      However, there is still significant room for improvement. Further optimizations to the batch nor-
536
      malization layers could enhance the efficiency of hyperbolic models. Additionally, a key challenge
537   remains with the hyperbolic linear layers when reducing dimensionality. Currently, we match norms
538   to simulate a rotation operation, but we encourage exploring alternative approaches that align more
539   naturally with the mathematical properties of the manifold.
