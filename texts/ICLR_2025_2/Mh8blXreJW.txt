009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Model pruning is an effective approach for compressing Large Language Models
012            (LLMs) and improving inference efficiency. However, this process often leads
013            to significant degradation of model capabilities. While post-training techniques
014            such as instruction tuning are commonly employed to recover model performance,
015            existing methods often overlook the uneven deterioration of model capabilities and
016            incur high computational costs due to extensive recovery training. Moreover, some
017            instruction data irrelevant to model capability recovery may introduce negative
018
               effects. To address these challenges, we propose the Post-training dAta Selection
               method for Efficient pruned large language model Recovery (PASER). PASER
019
               aims to identify instructions where model capabilities are most severely compro-
020
               mised within a certain recovery data budget. Our approach first applies manifold
021            learning and spectral clustering to group recovery data in the semantic space, re-
022            vealing capability-specific instruction sets. We then adaptively allocate the data
023            budget to different clusters based on the degrees of model capability degradation.
024            In each cluster, we prioritize data samples where model performance has declined
025            dramatically. To mitigate potential negative transfer, we also detect and filter out
026            conflicting or irrelevant recovery data. Extensive experiments demonstrate that
027            PASER significantly outperforms conventional baselines, effectively recovering the
028            general capabilities of pruned LLMs while utilizing merely 4%-20% of the original
029
               post-training data and substantially reducing training computational overhead.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
033
      Model pruning, which aims at reducing the model size while maintaining or enhancing model
034
      capabilities, has been a promising approach towards large language model (LLM) compression and
035
      efficient inference. Mainstream LLM pruning schemes include unstructured (Frantar & Alistarh,
036   2023), semi-structured (Sun et al., 2024), and structured pruning (Ma et al., 2023). In practice, the
037   capability degradation of the pruned model compared with the original LLM is almost unavoidable,
038   especially under high pruning ratios. This degradation phenomenon is more severe for the structured
039   pruning scheme, which has been widely adopted in industrial LLM compression thanks to its
040   hardware-friendly property. Therefore, first pruning, then post-training has been one of the standard
041   pipelines, where the recovery post-training is incorporated to restore the critical capabilities and adapt
042   to the reduced model capacity (Ma et al., 2023; Zhao et al.).
043   While various types of post-training data, including pre-training corpora and extensive fine-tuning
044   datasets, have been explored (Xia et al., 2024; Sun et al., 2024), instruction tuning data has demon-
045   strated unique advantages for efficient capability recovery (Ma et al., 2023; Zhao et al.; Zhang et al.,
046   2024; Chen et al., 2023). Compared to pre-training that requires massive computational resources, in-
047   struction tuning enables effective recovery with much smaller data scale through explicit supervision.
048   Besides, instruction tuning helps maintain the model’s general-purpose abilities through diverse task
049   coverage while avoiding overly specialized recovery.
050   Conventional schemes (Ma et al., 2023) usually employ the full version of publicly available instruc-
051   tion tuning datasets like Alpaca (Taori et al., 2023) to conduct the recovery post-training. However,
052   this can bring significant computation overhead and even unsatisfactory recovery performance, as
053   shown in Figure 1. An intuitive solution is to take part of the original data for training, thus con-
      suming less data and correspondingly reducing the computation resource demand. Nevertheless,

072   Though previous works have noticed the significance of selecting high-quality data to conduct the
073   general instruction tuning (Wang et al., 2024), few of them are specifically designed for the recovery
074   post-training. Note that general high quality does not necessarily mean useful for recovery.
075
      Considering above limitations, the ideal recovery training data selection approach should exhibit
076   following properties: (1) Targeted and Balanced Capability Recovery: The ideal selection method
077   should address the disproportionate impact of pruning on various model capabilities by enabling
078   targeted recovery of critically affected ones, while maintaining a balanced approach overall. It should
079   curate a diverse instruction set that prioritizes severely impacted capabilities without neglecting
080   holistic model functionality. (2) Recovery Training Efficiency: Limited computing resources pose
081   serious efficiency challenges to the LLM recovery post-training. An ideal method should be able to
082   select instructions that are both most beneficial for recovery and low in computational cost, thereby
083   accelerating the recovery training process and optimizing resource utilization. (3) Mitigation of
084
      Negative Transfer: Recognizing that not all instruction data is beneficial for model recovery, an
      optimal approach should not only identify the most advantageous instructions, but also filter out
085
      potentially harmful or irrelevant ones. This significantly reduces the risk of negative transfer during
086
      the recovery training, ensuring that the selected data contributes positively to model performance.
087
088   To achieve such goals, we propose the Post-training dAta Selection method for Efficient pruned
089   large language model Recovery (PASER). First, we perform semantic-structural recovery instruction
090
      clustering to obtain different data groups corresponding to different LLM capabilities. Second,
      we select the recovery instructions in a capability degradation-aware manner, where the overall
091
      data budget is allocated to different clusters based on their corresponding capability degradation
092
      degrees. In particular, the computation cost of each sample is also taken into consideration during the
093   inner-capability sample selection. Finally, we construct the concept consistency graph to maintain
094   the semantic consistency across the selected instructions, thus preventing introducing conflicting
095   or irrelevant samples. We take the LLaMA 2/3 and Baichuan2 as the target LLMs and perform the
096   experiments under different LLM pruning schemes and different-sized instruction tuning datasets. The
097   comparison with random and conventional instruction tuning data selection baselines demonstrates
098   that PASER can more effectively enhance the recovered LLM performance on language modeling and
099   various reasoning tasks. Meanwhile, the recovery training overhead can also be reduced significantly.

101   2    R ELATED W ORKS
102
103   Large Language Model Pruning can be generally divided into three categories: unstructured
104   pruning, semi-structured pruning, and structured pruning. Unstructured pruning removes individual
105   weights without structural constraints, with representative works including SparseGPT (Frantar &
106   Alistarh, 2023), Wanda (Sun et al., 2024), BESA (Xu et al., 2024b), and OWL (Yin et al., 2024).
107   This technique allows for maximum flexibility in weight selection and can achieve high compression
      rates while maintaining model performance. However, the resulting irregular sparsity patterns limits


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      the practical acceleration. Semi-structured pruning (Guo et al., 2024; Malla et al., 2024; Frantar
109   & Alistarh, 2023; Sun et al., 2024) targets specific patterns like N:M sparsity, balancing flexibility
110   and hardware efficiency. Structured pruning approaches like LLM-Pruner (Ma et al., 2023) and
111   SliceGPT (Ashkboos et al., 2024) remove entire structural components, offering better hardware
112   compatibility and attracting industry attention (Ko et al., 2023; An et al., 2024; Song et al., 2024; Xia
113   et al., 2024). However, structured pruning faces more severe performance degradation, highlighting
114   the importance of recovery post-training.
115   Instruction Tuning has emerged as a crucial technique for enhancing LLMs (Wei et al.; Wang et al.,
116   2023), improving their adaptability to novel tasks (Sanh et al.; Liang et al.; Zhou et al., 2024). Recent
117   works have explored instruction tuning as a post-compression recovery mechanism (Zhao et al.; Ma
118   et al., 2023). While promising, this combination faces challenges from reduced model capacity and
119   computational costs. Most current approaches use general instruction datasets without considering
120   compressed model’s characteristics or disproportionately affected capabilities. Our work addresses
121   these gaps by proposing a novel framework for post-training data selection in pruned LLM recovery.

138   4     M ETHODOLOGY
139
140   In this section, we introduce the three main components of the PASER framework: semantic-structural
141   recovery instruction clustering, capability degradation-aware adaptive recovery instruction selection,
142   and negative transfer mitigation. Furthermore, we provide the time complexity analysis for PASER in
143   the selection process.

145   4.1   S EMANTIC -S TRUCTURAL R ECOVERY I NSTRUCTION C LUSTERING
146
      During the LLM pruning process, different model capabilities may be affected unevenly by pruning.
147
      To ensure balanced recovery, we need to identify and group data points that target similar capabilities.
148
      To achieve this, we hypothesize that distinct geometric topological structures of recovery instruction
149   data in the high-dimensional semantic space may correspond to different aspects of LLM capabilities.
150   This hypothesis is based on the intuition that instructions requiring similar capabilities are likely
151   to cluster together in the semantic space, forming identifiable topological structures. In detail, we
152   employ a two-step approach on the embedding space of instructions. First, we apply a diffusion
153   kernel to SentenceBERT (Reimers & Gurevych, 2019) embeddings for manifold learning to uncover
154   the intrinsic geometric structure in the semantic space and reduce dimensionality while preserving
155   non-linear relationships:
156                              e(xi ) = DiffusionKernel(SentenceBERT(xi )).                               (2)
157
158

159

160
      leveraging the low-dimensional manifold representation:

244   4.3   N EGATIVE T RANSFER M ITIGATION
245
246   To prevent performance degradation due to conflicting or irrelevant information, we need to detect
247   and mitigate potential negative transfer. We introduce a Concept Consistency Graph (CCG) to model
248
      relationships between concepts in the selected data. Here, a concept refers to a key knowledge unit
      or semantic element extracted from an instruction tuning sample. Concepts play a crucial role in
249
      capturing the essential information within instructions and help in identifying potential conflicts that
250
      could lead to negative transfer. By managing relationships between concepts, we aim to maintain
251   semantic consistency across the selected instruction tuning dataset, thereby reducing the risk of
252   learning conflicting or irrelevant information during the recovery process.
253
      Definition 1 (Concept Consistency Graph). A CCG is a graph G = (V, E) where vertices V represent
254
      concepts, and an edge (vi , vj ) ∈ E exists if concepts vi and vj co-occur in at least one instruction
255   tuning sample without conflict.
256
257   For each new sample (x, y), we extract its concepts C(x, y) and check for consistency:
258
259
                     IsConsistent(x, y) = ∀vi , vj ∈ C(x, y) : (vi , vj ) ∈ E or {vi , vj } ̸⊂ V           (8)
260
261   We only add samples that are consistent with the existing CCG, updating the graph with each addition.
262   This approach ensures that we maintain a coherent set of instructions, minimizing the risk of negative
263   transfer by avoiding the introduction of conflicting concepts during the recovery training process.
264   The full version of our algorithm is provided in Algorithm 1.

530   6     C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORKS
531
      Recovery post-training has been an important procedure after large language model pruning to restore
532
      the critical capabilities. Previous works directly utilize the full instruction tuning dataset, facing high
533
      computation cost and risks of untargeted recovery and negative transfer. In this work, we propose
534   the post-training data selection method for efficient pruned model recovery. According to capability
535   degradation degrees, we allocate selection budget among different capability data obtained through
536   semantic-structural clustering. We then select samples where model behavior has been severely
537   affected while considering computation cost, and introduce a concept consistency graph to mitigate
538   negative transfer. Extensive experiments on different LLMs and pruning schemes demonstrate the
539   effectiveness of our framework. Future work will explore other optimization approaches like data
      augmentation and scheduling to further improve recovery efficiency.

790   B         E XTENDED E XPERIMENTS ON P OST- QUANTIZATION R ECOVERY T RAINING
791
792   Though the method descriptions and the experiments in the main body are mainly around the
793   LLM pruning scenario, our PASER framework can actually be extended seamlessly to other LLM
794   compression scenario. To further demonstrate its applicability, we conduct the experiments on
795   post-quantization recovery training and compare our PASER with corresponding instruction tuning
796
      data selection baselines. In detail, we choose two most representative methods: Round-To-Nearest
      (RTN) (Frantar & Alistarh, 2022; Yao et al., 2022), GPTQ (Frantar et al., 2023), to perform the
797
      LLM quantization. It should be clarified that RTN method, which rounds all weights to the nearest
798
      quantized value on exactly the same asymmetric per-row grid, is actually the fundamental technique
799   in most works about LLM quantization (Frantar & Alistarh, 2022; Yao et al., 2022; Park et al.).
800   Its runtimes scales well to the models with many billion parameters due to the direct rounding.
801   According to the results provided in Table 5 and 6, we can observe that the PASER can still effectively
802   enhance the recovery performance and outperform the data selection baselines on averaged reasoning
803   performance and zero-shot perplexity for both LLaMA2-7B and LLaMA2-13B models. Meanwhile,
804   recovery data selection baselines can indeed achieve the stronger performance than full data and
805   random baselines, which validates the necessity of conducting recovery data selection even in the
806   LLM quantization scenario. Furthermore, comparing these results with Table 1 and 3, it can be
807
      noticed that the improvement space of PASER in Table 5 and 6 has been reduced to some extent. This
      is because the post-compression performance of such quantization schemes has been competitive
808
      enough, which can reflected from the w/o training row.
