009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            The optimal transport barycenter (a.k.a. Wasserstein barycenter) is a fundamen-
012            tal notion of averaging that extends from the Euclidean space to the Wasserstein
013            space of probability distributions. Computation of the unregularized barycen-
014            ter for discretized probability distributions on point clouds is a challenging task
015            when the domain dimension d > 1. Most practical algorithms for approximat-
016            ing the barycenter problem are based on entropic regularization. In this paper,
017            we introduce a nearly linear time O(m log m) and linear space complexity O(m)
018            primal-dual algorithm, the Wasserstein-Descent HŐá1 -Ascent (WDHA) algorithm,
019            for computing the exact barycenter when the input probability density functions
020
               are discretized on an m-point grid. The key success of the WDHA algorithm
               hinges on alternating between two different yet closely related Wasserstein and
021
               Sobolev optimization geometries for the primal barycenter and dual Kantorovich
022
               potential subproblems. Under reasonable assumptions, we establish the conver-
023            gence rate and iteration complexity of WDHA to its stationary point when the
024            step size is appropriately chosen. Superior computational efficacy, scalability, and
025            accuracy over the existing Sinkhorn-type algorithms are demonstrated on high-
026            resolution (e.g., 1024 √ó 1024 images) 2D synthetic and real data.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   The Wasserstein barycenter, introduced by Agueh & Carlier (2011) based on the theory of optimal
032   transport (OT), extends the notion of a Euclidean average to measure-valued data, thus representing
033   the ‚Äúaverage‚ÄĚ of a set of probability measures. Direct applications of the Wasserstein barycenter
034   include smooth interpolation between shapes (Solomon et al., 2015), texture mixing (Rabin et al.,
035   2012), and averaging of neuroimaging data (Gramfort et al., 2015), among others. More impor-
036   tantly, the computation of the Wasserstein barycenter often serves as a key stepping stone to derive
037
      more advanced machine learning and statistical algorithms. For instance, centroid-based methods
      for clustering distributions rely on the computation of Wasserstein barycenters (Cuturi & Doucet,
038
      2014; Zhuang et al., 2022). Additionally, regression models and statistical inference methods for
039
      distributional data that utilize Wasserstein geometry often employ the barycenter as an ‚Äúanchor‚ÄĚ
040   measure to map distributions to a linear tangent space (Dubey & MuŐąller, 2020; Zhang et al., 2022;
041   Chen et al., 2023; Zhu & MuŐąller, 2023; Zhu & MuŐąller, 2024; Jiang et al., 2024).
042
043
      Despite the widespread applications, computationally efficient or even scalable algorithms of the
      Wasserstein barycenter with theoretical guarantees remain to be developed. Existing approaches
044
      to compute the Wasserstein barycenter of a collection of probability density functions ¬Ķ1 , . . . , ¬Ķn
045
      in Rd rely on a Wasserstein analog of the gradient descent algorithm, which requires to compute
046
      n OT maps per iteration (Zemel & Panaretos, 2019; Chewi et al., 2020). AŐĀlvarez-Esteban et al.
047
      (2016) propose a fixed point approach that is effective for any location-scatter family. Classical
048   OT solvers, such as Hungarian method (Kuhn, 1955), auction algorithm (Bertsekas & Castanon,
049   1989) and transportation simplex (Luenberger & Ye, 2008), scale poorly for even moderately mesh-
050   sized problems. This presents a substantial computational barrier for computing the barycenter of
051   multivariate distributions. On the other hand, various regularized barycenters have been proposed
052   to mitigate the computational difficulty (Li et al., 2020a; Janati et al., 2020; Bigot et al., 2019;
053   Carlier et al., 2021; Chizat, 2023), and Sinkhorn‚Äôs algorithm is perhaps one of the most widely
      used algorithms to compute the entropy-regularized barycenter (PeyreŐĀ & Cuturi, 2019; Lin et al.,

054
      2022; Carlier, 2022). Also, Li et al. (2020b) propose a new dual formulation for the regularized
055   Wasserstein barycenter problem such that discretizing the support is not needed. However, the
056   computation thrifty of these methods is subject to the approximation accuracy trade-off (Nenna &
057   Pegon, 2024). In low-dimensional settings such as 2D images, this is often manifested in visually
058   undesirable blurring effects on the barycentric images. To break the curse of dimensionality, scalable
059   algorithms using input convex neural networks (Korotin et al., 2021) and generative models (Korotin
060   et al., 2022) have been investigated for the Wasserstein barycenter problem.
061   In this paper, we recast the unregularized optimal transport (a.k.a. Wasserstein) barycenter prob-
062   lem as a nonconvex-concave minimax optimization problem and propose a coordinate gradient

064   Wasserstein and Sobolev spaces. The key innovation of our WDHA algorithm is to combine two
065   different yet closely related primal-dual optimization geometries between the primal subproblem

      1.1   C ONTRIBUTIONS
075
076
      Current work is among the first to combine the Wasserstein and homogeneous Sobolev gradients to
077
      derive a simple, scalable, and accurate primal-dual coordinate gradient algorithm for computing the
078   exact (i.e., unregularized) OT barycenter. The proposed WDHA algorithm is particularly suitable
079   for computing the barycenter for discretized probability density functions on a large m-point grid
080   such as images of 1024√ó1024 resolution. The proposed WDHA algorithm enjoys strong theoretical
081   properties and empirical performance. The following summarizes our main contributions.
082
      ‚ÄĘ Discretizing the input probability density functions ¬Ķ1 , . . . , ¬Ķn onto a grid of m points, the per
083
        iteration runtime complexity of our algorithm is O(m log m) for updating each Kantorovich po-
084     tential. This is in sharp contrast with the time cost O(m3 ) for computing an OT map between
085     two distributions supported on the same grid via linear programming (LP). In addition, the space
086     complexity for the HŐá1 gradient is O(m), which also substantially reduces the LP space complexity
087     O(m2 ).
088   ‚ÄĘ Under reasonable assumptions, we provide an explicit algorithmic convergence rate and iteration
089     complexity of the WDHA algorithm to its stationary point with appropriately chosen step sizes
090     for the gradient updates. In particular, WDHA achieves the same convergence rate O(1/T ) as in
091     the Euclidean nonconvex-concave optimization problems.
092   ‚ÄĘ We demonstrate superior numeric accuracy and computational efficacy over Sinkhorn-type al-
093     gorithms on high-resolution 2D synthetic and real image data, where the standard Wasserstein
094
        gradient descent algorithm cannot be practically implemented on such problem size.
095   For limitations, the current approach is mainly limited to computing the Wasserstein barycenter of
096   2D or 3D distributions supported on a compact domain.

533   In this paper, we introduced a Wasserstein-Descent HŐá1 -Ascent (WDHA) algorithm for computing
534   the Wasserstein barycenter of n probability density functions supported on a compact subset of Rd .
535   Our key technique is motivated by the recent progress in nonconvex-concave minimax optimization
536   problems in the Euclidean space. Compared to existing methods for high-resolution densities, the
537   WDHA algorithm is computationally more efficient and produces a clearer, sharper, and more de-
538   tailed barycenter. We believe that our work not only advances computational techniques for Wasser-
539
      stein barycenters but also sheds new light on optimizing nonlinear functionals using a combination
      of geometric structures.
