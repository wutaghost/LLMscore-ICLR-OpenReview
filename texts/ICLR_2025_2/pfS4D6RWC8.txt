009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Diffusion models excel at capturing complex data distributions, such as those of
012            natural images and proteins. While diffusion models are trained to represent the dis-
013            tribution in the training dataset, we often are more concerned with other properties,
014            such as the aesthetic quality of the generated images or the functional properties
015            of generated proteins. Diffusion models can be finetuned in a goal-directed way
016
               by maximizing the value of some reward function (e.g., the aesthetic quality of
               an image). However, this may lead to reduced sample diversity, significant devia-
017
               tions from the training data distribution, and even poor sample quality due to the
018
               exploitation of an imperfect reward function. The last issue often occurs when the
019            reward function is a learned model meant to approximate a ground-truth “genuine”
020            reward, as is the case in many practical applications (e.g., using a learned estimator
021            of aesthetic quality). These challenges, collectively termed “overoptimization,”
022            pose a substantial obstacle. To address this overoptimization, we frame the fine-
023            tuning problem as entropy-regularized control against the pretrained diffusion
024            model, i.e., directly optimizing entropy-enhanced rewards with neural SDEs. We
025            present theoretical and empirical evidence that demonstrates our framework is
026            capable of efficiently generating samples with high genuine rewards, mitigating
027
               the overoptimization of imperfect reward models.

      While these methods allow us to generate samples with high “nominal” (approximate) rewards, they
055   often suffer from overoptimization (reward collapse). Overoptimization manifests as fine-tuned
056   models produce samples with low genuine rewards that are still scored as having a high “nominal”
057   reward under the (learned) reward model, as illustrated in Figure 1. This issue arises because nominal
058   rewards are usually learned from a finite training set to approximate the genuine reward function,
059   meaning that they are accurate only within their training distribution. Consequently, fine-tuning
060   methods quickly exploit nominal rewards by moving beyond the support of this distribution.
061   Our goal in this paper is to develop a principled algorithmic framework and its fundamental theory
062   for fine-tuning diffusion models that both optimize a reward function and stay close to the training
063   data, thus alleviating overoptimization. To achieve this, we frame the fine-tuning of diffusion models
064   as an entropy-regularized control problem. It is known that diffusion models can be formulated as
065   stochastic differential equations (SDEs) with a drift term and a diffusion term (Song et al., 2020).
066   Based on this formulation, in a fine-tuning step, we consider solving stochastic control by neural
067   SDEs in a computationally efficient manner. Here, we introduce a loss that combines a terminal
068   reward with entropy regularization against the pre-trained diffusion model and optimize with respect
069
      to both a drift term and an initial distribution. This entropy-regularization term enables us to maintain
      the bridges (i.e., the posterior distributions of trajectories conditioned on a terminal point) of pre-
070
      trained diffusion models, akin to bridge-matching generative models (Shi et al., 2023), such that the
071
      fine-tuned diffusion model avoids deviating too much from the pre-trained diffusion model.
072
073   Notably, we theoretically show that the fine-tuned SDE, optimized for both the drift term and initial
074   distribution, can produce specific distributions with high nominal rewards that are within the support
075
      of their training data distribution. Hence, our approach effectively mitigates the overoptimization
      problem since nominal rewards accurately approximate genuine rewards in that region. Furthermore,
076
      our theoretical results shed light on an intriguing new connection with classifier guidance (Dhariwal
077
      and Nichol, 2021).
078
079   Our contribution can be summarized as follows: we introduce a computationally efficient, theoretically
080   and empirically supported method for fine-tuning diffusion models: ELEGANT (finE-tuning doubLe
081
      Entropy reGulArized coNTrol) that excels at generating samples with high genuine rewards. While
      existing techniques in image generation (Fan et al., 2023; Prabhudesai et al., 2023; Clark et al., 2023)
082
      include components for mitigating overoptimization, we demonstrate stronger theoretical support
083
      by explicitly characterizing target distributions in our key Theorem 1 (among methods that directly
084   backpropagate through differentiable rewards) and superior empirical performance (compared to a
085   KL-penalized PPO). Additionally, unlike prior work, we apply our method to both image generation
086   and biological sequence generation, demonstrating its effectiveness across multiple domains.

      We provide an overview of related works. We leave the discussion of our work, including fine-tuning
090
      LLMs, sampling with control methods, and MCMC methods to Appendix A.
091
092   Diffusion models. Denoising diffusion probabilistic models (DDPMs) create a dynamic stochastic
093   transport using SDEs, where the drift aligns with a specific score function (Song et al., 2020; Ho
094   et al., 2020). The impressive performance of DDPMs has spurred the recent advancements in bridge
095   (flow)-matching techniques, which construct stochastic transport through SDEs with drift terms
096
      aligned to specific bridge functions (Liu et al., 2022; Shi et al., 2023; Tong et al., 2023; Lipman et al.,
      2023; Somnath et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023; Delbracio and Milanfar, 2023; Shi et al., 2023).
097
098   Guidance. Dhariwal and Nichol (2021) introduced classifier-based guidance, an inference-time
099   technique for steering diffusion samples towards a particular class. More generally, guidance uses an
100   auxiliary differentiable objective (e.g., a neural network) to steer diffusion samples towards a desired
101   property (Graikos et al., 2022; Bansal et al., 2023). In our experiments, we show that our fine-tuning
102   technique outperforms a guidance baseline that uses the gradients of the reward model to steer the
103
      pre-trained diffusion model toward high-reward regions.
104   Fine-tuning as RL/control. Lee et al. (2023); Wu et al. (2023) employ supervised learning
105   techniques to optimize reward functions, while Black et al. (2023); Fan et al. (2023) employ an
106   RL-based method to achieve a similar goal. Clark et al. (2023); Xu et al. (2023); Prabhudesai et al.
107   (2023) present a fine-tuning method that involves direct backpropagation regarding rewards, which
      bears some resemblance to our work. Nevertheless, there are several notable distinctions between

109   distribution, both of which play a critical role in targeting the desired distribution. We present novel

191   4.1   I MPORTANCE OF KL R EGULARIZATION
192
      Before explaining our approach to sample from ptar , we elucidate the necessity of incorporating
193
      entropy regularization term in (2). This can be seen by examining the limit cases as α tends towards 0
194
      and when we fix α = 0 a priori. To be more precise, as α approaches zero, ptar tends to converge to a
195   Dirac delta distribution at x⋆tar , defined by: x⋆tar = argmaxx∈Rd :pdata (x)>0 r(x). This x⋆tar represents
196   an optimal x within the support of pdata . Conversely, if we directly solve (2) with α = 0, we may
197   venture beyond the support:x⋆ = argmaxx∈Rd r(x). This implies that the generated samples might
198   no longer adhere to the characteristics of natural images in image generation or biological sequences
199   within the biological space. As we mentioned, since r(x) is typically a learned reward function
200   from the data, it won’t be accurate outside of the support of pdata (x). Hence, x⋆ would not have a
201   high genuine reward, which results in “overoptimization”. For example, this approach results in the
202   unnatural but high nominal reward images in Figure 1.

388   7.1     L IMITATION : S OURCES OF A PPROXIMATION E RRORS
389   Lastly, we explain the factors contributing to approximation errors in our algorithm. First, our method
390   relies on the precision of neural SDE solvers, specifically, the expressiveness of neural networks and
391   errors from discretization (Tzen and Raginsky, 2019). Similarly, in the sampling phase, we also incur
392   errors stemming from discretization. Additionally, our method relies on the expressiveness of another
393   neural network in value function estimation.
394
      As another limitation, readers might wonder about (1) computational cost of learning initial distribu-
395   tions, (2) memory complexity, and (3) choice of α. We defer the discussion to Appendix 7.1.

      Readers may want to know the effectiveness of learning the initial distribution. To address this, we

530   are lower than those achieved by our full algorithm, which learns the initial distribution. These
531   findings demonstrate that learning the initial distribution is effective in mitigating overoptimization
532   and achieving higher genuine rewards in specific practical scenarios.

      A    A DDITIONAL R ELATED W ORKS
757
758   In this section, we discuss additional related works.
759
760   Fine-tuning large language models. Much of the recent work in fine-tuning diffusion models is
761   inspired by the wide success of fine-tuning large language models for various objectives such as
762   instruction-following, summarization, or safety (Ouyang et al., 2022; Stiennon et al., 2020; Bai et al.,
763   2022). Many techniques have been proposed to mitigate reward collapse in this domain, but KL
764   regularization is the most commonly used (Gao et al., 2023). For a more comprehensive review, we
765   direct readers to Casper et al. (2023). In our experiments, we compare to a KL-penalized RL baseline,
766   which is analogous to the current dominant approach in language model fine-tuning.
767
768   Sampling and control. Control-based approaches have been extensively employed for generating
769
      samples from known unnormalized probability densities in various ways (Tzen and Raginsky, 2019;
      Bernton et al., 2019; Heng et al., 2020; Zhang and Chen, 2021; Berner et al., 2022; Lahlou et al.,
770
      2023; Zhang et al., 2023; Bengio et al., 2023). Notably, the most pertinent literature relates to path
771
      integral sampling (Zhang and Chen, 2021). Nevertheless, our work differs in terms of our target
772   distribution and focus, which is primarily centered on fine-tuning. Here are more differences:
773
774    • We address how to utilize pre-trained diffusion models by properly setting rewards in the control
775
         problem.
       • Their works assume an initial distribution as the Dirac delta distribution, which does not apply to
776
         diffusion models. We explore how to relax this assumption.
777
       • We provide several proofs to show our main statement. The proof in Section C.2 is similar to
778      that in the path integral sampler proof. However, the proofs in Sections C.1 and C.3 are novel.
779      In particular, the proof in Section C.1 highlights the connection with bridge (flow) matching, as
780      discussed after Lemma 4.
781
      Our research also shares connections with path integral controls (Theodorou and Todorov, 2012;
782
      Theodorou et al., 2010; Kappen, 2007) and the concept of control as inference (Levine, 2018).
783
      However, our focus lies on the diffusion model, while their focus lies on standard RL problems.
784
785   Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC). MCMC-based algorithms are commonly used for sam-
786   pling from unnormalized densities that follow a proportionality of exp(r(x)/α) (Girolami and
787   Calderhead, 2011; Ma et al., 2019). Numerous MCMC methods have emerged, including the first-
788   order technique referred to as MALA. The approach most closely related to incorporating MALA for
789   fine-tuning is classifier-based guidance, as proposed in Dhariwal and Nichol (2021); Graikos et al.
790   (2022). However, implementing classifier-based guidance is known to be unstable in practice due to
791   the necessity of training numerous classifiers (Clark et al., 2023).
792
793   Additional Works on Fine-Tuning Diffusion Models. Domingo-Enrich et al. (2024) and Zhang
794   et al. (2024) have addressed problems similar to ours, but their approaches still differ significantly.
795
      Domingo-Enrich et al. (2024) addressed the initial bias issue discussed in Section 6 by modifying
      the noise schedule, whereas we tackled it by introducing an additional optimization problem. Zhang
796
      et al. (2024) approached a related problem by designing an objective function inspired by a detailed
797
      balance loss in Gflownets, while our algorithm directly solves the control problem using neural SDE.
798   Although its algorithm has certain benefits when rewards are differentiable like PPO, our primary
799   focus is more on how to mitigate overoptimization from both theoretical and empirical perspectives.
800   Marion et al. (2024) has explored fine-tuning in diffusion models, framing it as a bilevel optimization
801   problem. However, they do not appear to discuss strategies for constructing objective functions, such
802   as incorporating KL regularization to prevent overoptimization.

1101   In this section, we provide an overview of continuous-time generative models. The objective is to
1102   train a SDE in such a way that the marginal distribution at time T follows pdata . While pdata is not
1103   known, we do have access to a dataset that follows this distribution.
1104
1105   Denoising diffusion models DDPMs (Song et al., 2020) are a widely adopted class of generative
1106   models. We start by considering a forward stochastic differential equation (SDE) represented as:
1107
1108                                          dyt = −0.5yt dt + dwt , y0 ∼ pdata ,                          (20)
1109
1110   defined on the time interval [0, T ]. As T tends toward infinity, the limit of this distribution converges
1111
       to N (0, Id ), where Id denotes a d-dimensional identity matrix. Let Q be a measure on C induced
       by the forward SDE (20). Consequently, a generative model can be defined using its time-reversal:
1112
       xt = yT −t , which is characterized by:
1113
1114                         dxt = {0.5xt − ∇ log QT −t (xt )}dt + dwt , x0 ∼ N (0, Id ).
1115
1116   A core aspect of DDPMs involves learning the score ∇ log Qt by optimizing the following loss with
1117   respect to S:
1118
                                             EQ [∥∇ log Qt|0 (xt |x0 ) − S(t, xt )∥22 ].
1119
1120   A potential limitation of the above approach is that the forward SDE (20) might not converge to a
1121   predefined prior distribution, such as N (0, Id ), within a finite time T . To address this concern, we
1122   can employ the Schrödinger Bridge formulation (De Bortoli et al., 2021).
1123
1124   Diffusion Schrödinger Bridge. A potential bottleneck of the diffusion model is that the forward
1125   SDE might not converge to a pre-specified prior N (0, Id ) with finite T . To mitigate this problem,
1126   De Bortoli et al. (2021) proposed the following Diffusion Schrödinger Bridge. Being inspired by
1127   Schrödinger Bridge formulation (Schrödinger, 1931) they formulate the problem:
1128
1129                                  argmin KL(P∥Pref ) s.t.P0 = νini , PT = pdata .
                                         P

1132   P at time 0, and PT is similarly defined. To solve this problem, De Bortoli et al. (2021) proposed
1133   an iterative proportional fitting, which is a continuous extension of the Sinkhorn algorithm (Cuturi,
       2013), while learning the score functions from the data as in DDM.

       G     F URTHER L IMITATIONS AND T HEIR R EMEDY
1513
1514
       In this section, we discuss further possible limitations in our work.
1515
1516
       G.1    C OMPUTATIONAL C OST: C OST OF L EARNING I NITIAL D ISTRIBUTIONS
1517
1518   Our overhead in learning the initial distribution is low. When learning a second diffusion chain, our
1519   goal is to learn exp(V0⋆ (x))νini (x). This distribution is much simpler and smoother compared to
1520   the target distribution exp(r(x)/α)pdata (x) in the first diffusion chain. Therefore, we require fewer
1521   epochs to learn this distribution. For instance, in image generation, we use only 200 reward queries
1522   to learn initial distributions while the main diffusion chain takes 12000 queries for finetuning. The
1523   wall time of learning the initial distribution (i.e., the second diffusion chain) is 30 − 40 minutes in
1524   image experiments, while the wall time of learning the second chain is roughly 1800 minutes.
1525
1526   G.2    M EMORY C OMPLEXITY OF ELEGANT
1527
       When updating a single gradient, while ELEGANT consumes O(L) memory (where L represents the
1528
       number of discretizations), PPO only requires O(1) memory. This may initially seem like a limitation
1529   of our approach. However, in practical scenarios, we are able to manage highly-dimensional data
1530   effectively by implementing gradient checkpointing and accumulating gradients while maintaining a
1531   large batch size, as we did in our experiments.
1532
1533   G.3    C HOICE OF α
1534
1535   A sophisticated way to choose α is our future work. Typically, we observe α is helpful regardless of
1536   its specific choice as long as it is too small or too large enough, as we did ablation studies in Figure 2
1537   and Figure 3c.
1538   Here, we discuss a practical way to choose it and associated experimental results. In many scenarios,
1539   we typically know the feasible upper bound of true rewards. In such cases, by appropriately selecting
1540   α to ensure that the final learned reward falls within the range of 0.98-0.99 of the upper bound, we can
1541   effectively attain high rewards while mitigating overoptimization, as shown in Table 2. Approaches
1542   without entropy regularization may easily lead to overoptimization, wherein the learned reward may
1543   reach 1, despite the actual reward being relatively low, as we show in Table 2.
1544
1545   G.4    I NFERENCE T IME
1546
1547
       Inference time is a critical aspect of many works in diffusion models. One potential approach would
       be to use recent distillation techniques to accelerate inference time after fine-tuning. We will add
1548
       more such discussion in the next version.
