039                                              A BSTRACT
040
041           Aligning large language models with human preferences has emerged as a crit-
042           ical focus in language modeling research. Yet, integrating preference learning
043           into Text-to-Image (T2I) generative models is still relatively uncharted territory.
044           The Diffusion-DPO technique made initial strides by employing pairwise pref-
045
              erence learning in diffusion models tailored for specific text prompts. However,
              Diffusion-DPO overlooks the valuable information that can be derived from the
046
              contrast between images with related prompts, which could further enhance the
047
              quality of text-to-image (T2I) generation. We introduce Diff-contrast, a new
048           method designed to align diffusion-based T2I models with human preferences
049           more effectively by optimizing relative preferences. This approach leverages both
050           prompt-image pairs with identical prompts and those with semantically related
051           content across various modalities. Furthermore, we have developed a new evalu-
052           ation metric, style alignment, aimed at overcoming the challenges of high costs,
053           low reproducibility, and limited interpretability prevalent in current evaluations of
              human preference alignment. Our findings demonstrate that Diff-contrast outper-

054
               forms established methods such as Supervised Fine-Tuning and Diffusion-DPO
055            in tuning Stable Diffusion versions 1.5 and XL-1.0, achieving superior results in
056            both automated evaluations of human preferences and style alignment.

059   1   I NTRODUCTION
060
061   Diffusion-based Text-to-Image (T2I) models have become the gold standard in image generative
062   technologies. These models are typically pre-trained on extensive web datasets. However, this one-
063
      stage training approach may generate images that do not align with human preferences. In contrast,
      Large Language Models (LLMs) have seen significant advancements in producing outputs that cater
064
      to human preferences, primarily through a two-stage training process involving pre-training on web
065
      datasets followed by fine-tuning on preference data (Rafailov et al., 2023; Yin et al., 2024; Chen
066   et al., 2024). Extending this preference fine-tuning approach to T2I models (Wallace et al., 2024)
067   presents opportunities to tailor image generation models to cater to diverse user preferences, en-
068   hancing their utility and relevance.
069
      Recent research has concentrated on refining diffusion-based T2I models to better reflect human
070
      preferences, such as aesthetics and text-image alignment, through Reinforcement Learning from
071
      Human Feedback (RLHF) (Black et al., 2024; Clark et al., 2024; Fan et al., 2024; Lee et al., 2023;
072   Prabhudesai et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2024). This process typically involves pretraining a reward model
073   to represent specific human preferences and then optimizing the diffusion models to maximize the
074   reward of generated images. However, developing a robust reward model that accurately mirrors hu-
075   man preferences is challenging and computationally expensive. Over-optimizing the reward model
076   often leads to significant issues of model collapse (Prabhudesai et al., 2023; Lee et al., 2023).
077
      Diffusion-DPO (Wallace et al., 2024) and incorporates Direct Preference Optimization (DPO)
078   (Rafailov et al., 2023) into the preference learning framework of T2I diffusion models. DPO in
079   LLMs focuses on contrasting chosen and rejected responses, bypassing the need for training addi-
080   tional reward models. However, DPO may not fully capture the nuances of human learning, which
081   benefits from analyzing both successful examples and relevant failures (Dahlin et al., 2018). D3PO
082   Yang et al. (2024) incorporates the DPO framework in online setting by sampling preference pairs
083   from the T2I model. However, the on-the-fly perference generation incurs extra computing cost.
084   Relative Preference Optimization (RPO) (Yin et al., 2024) proposes a learning method akin to human
085   learning, where insights are derived from contrasting both identical and similar questions. RPO
086   contrasts all chosen and rejected responses within a mini-batch, weighting each pair according to
087   the similarity of their prompts. This contrastive preference learning approach has shown convincing
088   improvements in aligning human preferences in LLMs compared to other baseline methods. Further
089   discussions on related works can be found in Appendix B.
090   In this paper, we aim to design an alignment algorithm for text-to-image (T2I) models that ef-
091   fectively leverages the information contained in data with non-identical prompts. By contrasting
092   images generated from non-identical prompts, Diff-contrast can help the model discern overarching
093   patterns in color, lighting effects, and composition that align more closely with human preferences
094   (Palmer et al., 2013). However, designing such a contrastive alignment algorithm for T2I diffusion
095   models presents several significant challenges. Firstly, the log density of the final generated images
096   is implicit and challenging to quantify in diffusion models because it involves integrating out all
097   intermediate steps. Additionally, T2I diffusion models are inherently multi-modal, involving input
098
      prompts and output images in different modalities, which complicates the measurement of similarity
      between these multi-modal input-output pairs.
099
100   To overcome these challenges, we have 1) derived the Diff-contrast loss for diffusion models, sim-
101   plifying it to apply relative preference alignment across each timestep, and 2) implemented the CLIP
102   (Radford et al., 2021) encoder to project prompts and images into the same embedding space. This
103   allows for the accurate measurement of similarity between multi-modal prompt-image pairs. Our
104
      experiments, as illustrated in Figure 2, validate our approach, demonstrating that learning prefer-
      ences across non-identical prompts significantly enhances the alignment of generated images with
105
      human preferences in T2I models.
106
107   Moreover, our experiments highlight several shortcomings in the current evaluation metrics for
      human preference alignment. Traditional approaches like Diffusion-DPO rely on human evaluators,

      3   S TYLE A LIGNMENT DATASET
297
298
      The state-of-the-art evaluation metric for human preference learning on T2I models consists of two
299
      components: (a) Human evaluation via employed labelers, (b) Automatic evaluation leveraging hu-
300   man preference reward models like HPSV2 (Wu et al., 2023) and Pick Score (Kirstain et al., 2023).
301   (1) Cost and Reproducibility in Human Evaluation: Diffusion-DPO (Wallace et al., 2024) evalu-
302   ates employed labelers on Amazon Mechanical Turk to compare image generations which is costly
303   and suffers from poor reproducibility due to the subjective nature of human preferences. (2) Dif-
304   ficulties in Automatic Evaluation: As suggested by Wallace et al. (2024), diffusion-based T2I
305   models are pre-trained on datasets designed to align with human preferences. Consequently, Vanilla
306   T2I models are inherently task-tuned towards human preference alignment. This scenario presents
307   two significant challenges:
308   (a) Preference learning shows limited improvement in task-tuned models, a phenomenon also ob-
309   served in the LLM summarization task by Rafailov et al. (2023). (b) The performance of Supervised
310   Fine-Tuning (SFT) varies depending on the capacity of the Vanilla model selected. Both the pre-
311   training and fine-tuning data are collected to enhance alignment with human preferences, making
312   the quality of the preference dataset pivotal to SFT performance. SFT can dramatically improve
313   evaluation scores when the preference data is of higher quality than the model’s generations. Con-
314
      versely, it can degrade performance if the model was pre-trained on images of superior quality. This
      variability can undermine the effectiveness of automatic evaluations.
315
316   These issues highlight the complex interplay between model capacity and data quality of fine-tuning
317   models that are already implicitly preference-tuned during pre-training.
318   To address the limitations inherent in current image preference evaluation metrics, we introduce
319   a novel downstream task with datasets specifically designed for image preference learning, which
320   we term Style Alignment. Style alignment aims to fine-tune T2I models to generate images that
321   align with the offline data to achieve style transfer (Gatys et al., 2015; 2016) on T2I models. These
322   styles are intentionally chosen to be visually distinct from those in the Pick-a-Pic V2 dataset, thereby
323   enhancing the divergence between downstream tasks and pretraining. We constructed three datasets
      in Van Gogh, Sketch, and Winter styles each containing 10,000 preference pairs.


                                                         6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      Preference Pair Construction The concept of style transfer is particularly well-suited to the field
325   of image preference learning. We label the edited image after style transfer as preferred and the
326   original image as rejected to fine-tune T2I models towards generating images with desired style.
327   Our datasets are crafted based on subsets of Pick-a-Pic V2 (Kirstain et al., 2023). For each pair of
328   images in the subset, we randomly pick one as rejected and process it with state-of-the-art image
329   editing models: Prompt Diffusion (Wang et al., 2023a) for Sketch and Winter style and Instruct
330   Pix2Pix (Brooks et al., 2023) for Van Gogh style. The new image after style transfer is labeled
331   as preferred. Notationally, the new datasets follow the same format as Pick-a-Pic V2: (y w , y l , x),
332   where y w is the image after style transfer, y l is the image from Pick-a-Pic.
333
334   Content-Safer Dataset Pick-a-Pic V2 is collected through webapp without safety checking. We
335   observed substantial amount of inappropriate content when exploring the dataset. To foster a safe
336
      and ethical research environment, we manually deleted the inappropriate prompts when creating our
      style transfer datasets. We will release our datasets to the public soon.

363
      In our study, we conduct experiments designed to address the following research questions: (1)
      Can Diff-contrast enhance the T2I models’ ability to generate images that better align with human
364
      preference? (2) How do preference learning methods perform when applied to style alignment
365
      tasks? (3) What are the key factors that affect the performance of Diff-contrast? (4) Could style
366   alignment effectively address the challenges encountered in aligning with human preferences in
367   image preference learning evalutaions?

370
371   Models and Datasets Following Wallace et al. (2024), our human preference alignment experi-
372   ments are conducted on the Pick-a-Pic V2 dataset (Kirstain et al., 2023). We conduct style alignment
373   experiments on our style alignment datasets (Van Gogh, Sketch, and Winter). The experiments are
374   conducted on Stable Diffusion 1.5 (SD1.5) and Stable Diffusion XL (SDXL).
375
376   Evaluation For human preference alignment, we evaluated the Pick Score, HPSV2 , LAION Aes-
377   thetics score (Schuhmann, 2022), CLIP (Radford et al., 2021), and ImageReward (Xu et al., 2024),

      With each reward model, we report both average scores and win rates between Diff-contrast and
379   the baselines. For style alignment, we quantified model performance using the Fréchet Inception
380   Distance (FID) (Heusel et al., 2017), which measures the difference between the preferred images
381   in our training set and those generated from the training prompts by the fine-tuned T2I model.
382
383   Implementations We build Diff-contrast upon the codebase of Diffusion-DPO (Wallace et al.,
384   2024). We leverage the CLIP encoder (Radford et al., 2021) to compute multi-modal embeddings.
385   For human preference alignment, we performed one-stage fine-tuning on both models for SFT and
386   Diff-contrast. We directly use the checkpoints provided by Diffusion-DPO1 . For style alignment,
387   we also conduct one-stage fine-tuning. Additionally, following Rafailov et al. (2023), we perform

465   4.4   A BLATION S TUDIES
466
467   We conducted ablation study on the distance temperature parameter τ with the aim of understanding
468
      how much the focus should we put on semantically related prompt-image pairs to enhance preference
      alignment. In our experiment, we compare the performance of Diff-contrast under different distance
469
      temperatures in both human preference alignment and style alignment. In Table 3, we report reward
470
      model scores from automatic evaluations and FID for style alignment. We find that a lower distance
471   temperature (τ =0.01) leads to overall best performance being highest on HPS (Wu et al., 2023),
472   Image Reward (Xu et al., 2024) and second highest on Pick Score (Kirstain et al., 2023). On the
473   style alignment task, it is observed that the style alignment performance improves as we increase
474   the temperature. This contrast between human preference alignment and style alignment can be
475   attributed to the differing natures of the two tasks: In human preference alignment tasks, a lower
476   temperature focuses more on related prompt-image pairs to improve prompt-specific details. In style
477   alignment tasks, however, correctly learning the overall image style becomes a major challenge. This
478   is tackled by a higher temperature that results in more uniform weights for all preference pairs since
479
      any preference pair entails characteristics of image style that contributes to the alignment of styles.
      Due to page limit, we defer the results to appendix C.

      B.1     D IFFUSION BASED T EXT- TO -I MAGE M ODELS
813
814   Diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020; Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015; Song et al., 2020; Dhariwal & Nichol,
815   2021) have been the state-of-the-art in image generation. A diffusion model consists of a forward
816   process that gradually adds noise to the image and attempts to learn to reverse this process with
817   a neural network. Diffusion-based T2I models have also achieved impressive results in producing
818   high-quality images that closely adhere to the given caption (Nichol et al., 2022; ram, 2022; Rom-
819
      bach et al., 2022b; Saharia et al., 2022; Podell et al., 2023a). However, these models are pre-trained
      on large web dataset and may not align with human preference. Our work aims to tackle this issue
820
      by improving T2I model alignment using semantically related prompt-image pairs
821
822

846   Relative Preference Optimization (Yin et al., 2024) (RPO) draws inspiration from human cognition
847   that often involves interpreting divergent responses, not only to identical questions but also to those

849   batch and weight them by the similarity of the prompts between preferred and rejected data. Assume
