009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Oversmoothing is a common phenomenon in a wide range of graph neural networks
012            (GNNs), where node representation becomes homogeneous and thus model per-
013            formance worsens as the number of layers increases. Various strategies have been
014
               proposed to combat oversmoothing, but they are based on different heuristics and
               lack a unified understanding of their inherent mechanisms. In this paper, we revisit
015            the concept of signed graphs and show that a wide class of anti-oversmoothing
016            techniques can be viewed as the propagation on corresponding signed graphs with
017            both positive and negative edges. Leveraging the classic theory of signed graphs,
018            we characterize the asymptotic behaviors of existing methods and reveal that they
019            deviate from the ideal state of structural balance that provably prevents oversmooth-
020            ing and improves node classification performance. Driven by this unified analysis
               and theoretical insights, we propose Structural Balanced Propagation (SBP) where
021
               we explicitly enhance the structural balance of the signed graph with the help of
022            label and feature information. We theoretically and empirically prove that SBP can
023            improve the structural balance to alleviate oversmoothing under certain conditions.
024            Experiments on synthetic and real-world datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of
025            our methods, highlighting the value of our signed graph framework.

028   1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Graph neural networks (GNNs) are a powerful framework for processing graph-structured data across
031   a wide range of fields, such as drug discovery, recommender systems and social networks (Gori et al.,
032   2005; Scarselli et al., 2009; Bruna et al., 2014; Duvenaud et al., 2015; Defferrard et al., 2016; Battaglia
033   et al., 2016; Li et al., 2016). Most GNN models follow the message-passing paradigm, where node
034
      features are computed by recursively aggregating information from all neighboring nodes along the
      unsigned edges (Kipf & Welling, 2017; Wu et al., 2019; Veličković et al., 2018; Xu et al., 2019).
035
      Despite notable advancements, oversmoothing remains a prevalent issue for GNNs characterized by
036   the convergence of all node features to a constant when stacking a substantial number of layers (Li
037   et al., 2018; Oono & Suzuki, 2020; Cai & Wang, 2020; Wu et al., 2023a). Several strategies have been
038   developed to counteract oversmoothing in GNNs, such as normalization layers (Guo et al., 2023; Zhao
039   & Akoglu, 2019; Ioffe & Szegedy, 2015), random edge dropping (Rong et al., 2019; Do et al., 2020)
040   and residual connections (Wang et al., 2021; Gasteiger et al., 2018; Xu et al., 2018). However, there
      lacks a unified understanding of these different strategies for dissecting their limitations, as it seems
041
      that these method can only mitigate oversmoothing to certain extent, and the model performance still
042   degrades significantly after a large number of propagation steps (Li et al., 2018; Wu et al., 2023b).
043
044   In this paper, we revisit signed graphs (Derr et al., 2018; Shi et al., 2019) where each edge has a
      positive or negative sign and propose that edge signs can serve as a remedy to combat the overmooth-
045
      ing issue. Moreover, we collect eight oversmoothing countermeasures in the existing literature. Our
046   analysis shows that all these methods can be interpreted as injecting negative edges, with different
047   designs, into the original unsigned graph and conducting message-passing over the resulting signed
048   graphs, leading to a clear repulsion among neighboring nodes when oversmoothing was supposed to
049   occur when edges are unsigned.
050   This observation not only reveals how these methods work to alleviate oversmoothing, but also
051   inspires us to further make use of the signed graph to theoretically address the oversmoothing issue.
052   We explore a fundamental signed graph property, known as structural balance (Cartwright & Harary,
053   1956). In a structurally balanced signed graph, nodes can be grouped into clusters where only positive
      edges exist within each cluster, and only negative edges exist between clusters, as illustrated in

075   any additional learnable parameters, which we denote as Label-SBP. We theoretically demonstrate

076
      repelling nodes from different label classes (Proposition 4.7). Built upon Label-SBP, we further
077   design a feature-induced variant to accommodate scenarios without sufficient ground truth informa-
078   tion, dubbed Feature-SBP. Empirically, we conduct extensive experiments on nine synthetic and
079   real-world benchmark datasets and SBP consistently outperforms existing oversmoothing counter-
080   measures across all deep depths and datasets. The main contributions of our work are summarized as
081   follows:
082
          • We provide a signed graph perspective to unify three major types of anti-oversmoothing tech-
083         niques and show that all of them amount to certain implicit designs of adding negative edges to
084         the original graph, highlighting the insight provided by our signed graph perspective.
085
          • We introduce the concept of structural balance from the signed graph theory as an ideal state of
086         graph propagation. By measuring the degree of structural balance, we find that due to the lack of
087         class-awareness, existing oversmoothing couteracting techniques fail at improving the structural
088         balance of the graph, which explains their inefficacy under more propagation steps.
089       • Driven by the structural balance theory, we propose two parameter-free strategies—Label-
090         SBP and Feature-SBP by explicitly designing the addition of negative edges to improve the
091         structural balance of the graph and thus prevent undesirable oversmoothing across different
092         classes. Experiments on nine synthetic and real-world graphs show that our method attains
093
            superior performance at counteracting oversmoothing in both homophilic and heterophilic
            settings.

098   Theory of Oversmoothing The concept of oversmoothing was initially introduced by Li et al.
099   (2018): when the number of layers becomes large, the representations of different nodes tend to
100   converge to a common value after excessively exchanging messages with neighboring nodes. Oono
101
      & Suzuki (2020); Wu et al. (2023a) rigorously show that the convergence of node representations to a
      common value happens at an exponential rate as the number of layers increases to infinity, for GCNs
102
      and attention-based GNNs, respectively. Wu et al. (2023b) theoretically proves that oversmoothing
103   can start to happen even in shallow depth under certain random graph settings. Zhou et al. (2021)
104   proposed an appropriate residual connection according to the lower limit of Dirichlet energy and
105   connected to previous methods qualitatively.
106
107   Signed Graph-Based Methods In the heterophilic graphs, various methods are inspired by the
      signed graph propagation (Tsitsulin et al., 2023; Song et al., 2023; Yan et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2022;

135   Structural Balance Structual balance theory has gained significant attention in recent years (Derr
136   et al., 2018; Yan et al., 2022; Liang et al., 2024a; Wang et al., 2022). Inspired by the structural balance
137   theory, Derr et al. (2018) characterizes the balanced path intuitively to learn both balanced and
138   unbalanced representations on each layer. Liang et al. (2024a) predicts the signed adjacency matrix by
139   an off-the-shelf neural network classifier to generate pseudo labels with the low-rank assumption. Shi
140
      et al. (2019) introduces the definition of the Laplacian for signed graphs and develops a comprehensive
      mathematical theory. In this paper, we rigorously show that structural balance is the optimal solution
141
      under the signed graph message-passing and propose practical solutions to oversmoothing based on
142   the property without any additional learnable parameters.
143
      In addition to the above methods which explicitly make use of the signed graph propagation, in this
144
      paper, we also revisit a wide class of previous anti-oversmoothing methods that do not explicitly
145   claim to use signed message-passing. We find that all of them can be attributed to some kind of
146   design of negative edges to the original graph.

271   of the node set into V = V1 ∪ V2 with V1 and V2 being nonempty and mutually disjoint, where any

534
535
      In this work, we propose a novel unified signed graph perspective by revisiting the concept and theory
536   of signed graphs to study the oversmoothing issue in GNNs. We find that many previous methods
537   alleviating oversmoothing can be seen as adopting different negative graphs, and further propose
538   two novel methods Label-SBP and Feature-SBP inspired by the structural balance theory. Our work
539   provides new insights from signed graphs on analyzing and addressing the oversmoothing issue,
      which helps motivate further research in this direction.

816   will transform the concrete adjacency matrix A to the normalized adjacency matrix Â by the degree
817   matrix. Define D = diag(d1 , d2 , . . . , dn ) where di is the degree of the node i. Then the normalized
                                  1       1
818   adjacency matrix Â = D− 2 AD− 2 . Moreover, many theoretical works simplified the normalized
819   adjacency matrix to be D A or AD−1 as the raw-normalized or column-normalized stochastic
                                 −1

820   matrix where the sum of every raw (column) is 1 and every entry is non-negative. In this paper, we
                    1      1
821   use Â = D− 2 AD− 2 .
822   Different GNNs typically share a common propagation mechanism, where node features are aggre-
823   gated and transformed along the network’s topology to a certain depth. The k-th layer propagation
824   can be formalized as

826                  H(k) = PROPAGATE(X; G; k) = Trans Agg G; H(k−1)                    ,            (8)

831   Trans(·) is the corresponding layer-wise feature transformation which often includes a non-linear
832   activation function (e.g., ReLU) and a layer-specific learnable weight matrix W for transformation

1218   Discussion with training methods While Peng et al. (2024) questions the existence of oversmooth-
1219   ing in trained GNNs, their observations are primarily based on specific experimental settings that may
1220   not fully capture the oversmoothing challenge present in the literature. Specifically, the empirical
1221   observations in Peng et al. (2024) are based on 10-layer GCNs, which, while useful for their argument,
       may not represent the behavior of deeper networks or other GNN architectures. Moreover, Figure
1222
       2 in Peng et al. (2024) is based on a normalized metric, which might not be the most appropriate.
1223   To see this point, suppose one wants to classify two points. In one case, we have 0.01 vs -0.01 and
1224   in the other case, we have 100 vs -100. While the normalized distance considered in Peng et al.
1225   (2024) would be the same for these two cases, the latter case has a much larger margin, and it would
1226   be thus much easier to learn a classifier. On the other hand, Cong et al. (2021) suggests that the
1227   trainability of deep GNNs is more of a problem than over-smoothing. However, over-smoothing
1228   naturally presents challenges for training deep GNNs, as when oversmoothing happens, gradients
       vanish across different nodes. Besides, Cong et al. (2021)compares 3 models GCN, ResGCN and
1229
       GCNII, proving that GCNII is the best backbone. We have adapted our SBP to GCNII in Table 13
1230   and the results showed that our SBP outperforms GCNII on average, especially in the middle layers.

1651   L.3.5    C OMBINE SBP TO OTHER METHODS
1652
1653
       In this paper, we focus on introducing a novel theoretic signed graph perspective for oversmoothing
       analysis, so we do not take many tricks into account or carefully fine-tune our hyperparameters.
1654
       Thus, our results in the paper are not as comparable to previous baselines (Chen et al., 2020b; Luan
1655   et al., 2022; Eliasof et al., 2021). However, existing oversmoothing researches are indeed hard to
1656   compare, because they are often composed of multiple techniques — such as residual, BatchNorm,
1657   data augmentation — and the parameters are often heavily (over-)tuned on small-scale datasets. And
1658   it becomes clear that to attain SOTA performance, one needs to essentially compose multiple such
1659   techniques without fully understanding their individual roles. For example, GCNII uses both initial
1660
       residual connection and identity map, futher combined with dropout.
1661   Our goal is to provide a new unified understanding of these techniques, so we justified it by showing
1662   that SBP as a single simple technique can attain good performance. And we believe that it would
1663   work complementarily with other techniques in the field, because oversmoothing is still challenging
1664
       to solve with a very larger depth.
1665   To further verify the effectiveness, we combine our SBP to one of the SOTA settings GCNII (Chen
1666   et al., 2020b) and the results are as seen in Table 13. The results indicate that after combining our
1667   method, GCNII demonstrates greater robustness as the layers go deeper, particularly in the middle
1668
       layers (layer=8), highlighting the efficacy of our signed graph insight.
