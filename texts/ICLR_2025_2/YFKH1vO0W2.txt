023                                                          A BSTRACT
024
025                    Unsupervised representation learning, in particular, sequential disentanglement,
026                    where the goal is to learn disentangled static and dynamic factors of variation,
027                    remains a significant challenge due to the absence of labels. Existing models,
028                    based on variational autoencoders and generative adversarial networks, achieved
029                    success in certain domains, but they often struggle with disentangling sequences,
030                    especially when dealing with real-world complexity and variability. Further, there
031                    is no real-world evaluation protocol for assessing the effectiveness of sequential
032
                       disentanglement models. Recently, diffusion autoencoders have emerged as a new
                       promising generative model, offering semantically rich representations by gradual
033
                       noise-to-data transformations. Despite their advantages, these models face limi-
034
                       tations: they are non-sequential, fail to disentangle the latent space effectively,
035                    and are computationally intensive, making them difficult to scale to sequences.
036                    In this work, we introduce our diffusion sequential disentanglement autoencoder
037                    (DiffSDA), a novel approach effective on real-world visual data and accompanied
038                    by a new and challenging evaluation protocol. DiffSDA is based on a new prob-
039                    abilistic modeling and is implemented using latent diffusion models and efficient
040                    samplers, facilitating processing of high-resolution videos. We test our approach
041                    on several real-world datasets and metrics, and we demonstrate its effectiveness
042                    in comparison to recent state-of-the-art sequential disentanglement methods.

      1             I NTRODUCTION
045
046
      As deep learning models progress, the need for extensive labeled data made unsupervised learning
047
      crucial (Bengio et al., 2012). Within unsupervised learning, disentangled representation learning is
048   particularly important (Bengio et al., 2013). This approach aims to factorize latent encodings from
049   data so that each factor represents a distinct variation, enhancing explainability (Liu et al., 2020),
050   reducing biases (Creager et al., 2019), and improving generalizability (Zhang et al., 2022). A key
051   challenge is modeling sequential data, such as videos, where latent encodings are split into static and
052   dynamic factors. For example, in a video of a person talking, facial appearance is the static factor,
053   and facial motions encode the dynamic factors. This paper focuses on disentangling and evaluating
      real-world, high-quality visual sequential information in an unsupervised manner.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      Most existing sequential disentanglement works, e.g., (Tulyakov et al., 2018; Yingzhen & Mandt,
055   2018; Bai et al., 2021; Han et al., 2021; Naiman et al., 2023) are based on variational autoencoders
056   (VAEs) (Kingma, 2013) and generative adversarial networks (GANs) (Goodfellow et al., 2014). Un-
057   fortunately, processing real-world data is challenging for VAEs as they produce blurry images and
058   although several approaches have been proposed to address this issue (Razavi et al., 2019; Vahdat
059   & Kautz, 2020), these methods often result in a significantly larger hierarchical latent space, poten-
060   tially complicating disentanglement. In practice, state-of-the-art sequential disentanglement mod-
061   els struggle to capture the complexities of real-world, high-quality datasets, and thus, they mostly
062   demonstrate their applicability on simple, often toy, examples, see Fig. 3. Another related limita-
063
      tion is the lack of a real-world benchmark. Such absence goes beyond the current evaluation on
      simple data—there is no standardized, robust, and reliable evaluation protocol involving real-world,
064
      high-quality visual sequential data and unsupervised metrics. We advocate that improving these two
065
      objectives, namely, designing new models and proposing new evaluation protocols for real-world
066   data and tasks may lead to fundamental advances in unsupervised sequential disentanglement.
067
068   One approach for improving real-world capabilities of sequential disentanglement techniques is to
069
      incorporate them within diffusion models (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015), which have been showing
      superior results over GANs’ and VAEs’ visual generative quality (Ho et al., 2020; Preechakul et al.,
070
      2022). Unfortunately, the latent variables of diffusion models lack a semantic structure that is cru-
071
      cial for disentangled learning. Consequently, recent work has extended diffusion models to have
072   an autoencoder structure, facilitating the learning of meaningful representations (Preechakul et al.,
073   2022; Wang et al., 2023). However, diffusion autoencoder (DiffAE) models can not be used for
074   unsupervised sequential disentanglement directly due to three main limitations. First, these mod-
075   els were primarily designed for non-sequential information. Second, while semantic information
076   becomes available in DiffAE, it is not factorized to separate disentangled factors. Finally, existing
077   DiffAE works are computationally restrictive, requiring many resources to process high-quality and
078   high-resolution data.
079   We address the first objective above by proposing a novel diffusion sequential disentanglement au-
080   toencoder (DiffSDA) that extends DiffAE works by alleviating their challenges. We improve the
081   first two issues by basing our encoder on a new probabilistic disentanglement model that has no
082   constraints on the prior distribution of the static and dynamic latents, unlike previous models. This
083   flexibility facilitates learning of expressive representations, it significantly reduces the number of
084   required hyper-parameters, and it is easy to code using vanilla recurrent modules and a U-Net (Ron-
085   neberger et al., 2015). To alleviate the third challenge, our decoder exploits the recent advances on
086
      stochastic differential equations (SDEs) (Song et al., 2021) and diffusion models, supporting train-
      ing via a simple score matching loss. In addition, we adapt an efficient and lightweight sampling
087
      framework known as EDM (Karras et al., 2022) to our setup, allowing fast sampling with only 63
088
      network evaluations. Finally, to process high-resolution visual sequential data with reasonable com-
089   putational requirements, we incorporate a latent diffusion module (LDM) (Rombach et al., 2022)
090   into our pipeline. Overall, we develop an SDE-based LDM-EDM decoder.
091
092
      Toward addressing the second objective, we introduce a new evaluation protocol for visual sequen-
      tial disentanglement models. Inspired by the existing quantitative and qualitative evaluation stan-
093
      dard protocol (Bai et al., 2021), we adopt current datasets and metrics, as well as introduce new
094
      components. For data, we use three real-world and high-resolution visual datasets that have not
095   been previously used for sequential disentanglement. For metrics, we utilize qualitative conditional
096   and unconditional swap tasks. We also measure quantitatively by borrowing estimators from anima-
097   tion for assessing if objects and motions are preserved and suggest a new unsupervised swap metric.
098   Further, we propose a new test for assessing multifactor disentanglement capabilities. Finally, we
099   suggest a new realistic zero-shot test case where DiffSDA is trained on one dataset but evaluated
100   on unseen data. Through extensive tests, we show that DiffSDA disentangles real-world data well
101   while outperforming recent SOTA approaches. Our contributions can be summarized as follows:
102         1. We introduce DiffSDA, a novel diffusion sequential disentanglement autoencoder model,
103            based on a new probabilistic bias and implemented using common neural modules.
104         2. We extend current disentanglement evaluation protocols to include real-world, high-quality
105            visual data, metrics, and new multifactor exploration and zero-shot tests.
106         3. We extensively demonstrate our model’s superiority in qualitative and quantitative disen-
107            tanglement tasks in comparison to existing SOTA models.

      2     R ELATED W ORK

      Disentangled representation learning is a long-standing problem (Bengio et al., 2013). Many
111   works proposed variational autoencoder models for non-sequential (Higgins et al., 2017; Chen et al.,
112   2018; Kim & Mnih, 2018) and sequential (Hsu et al., 2017; Yingzhen & Mandt, 2018; Zhu et al.,
113   2020; Bai et al., 2021; Han et al., 2021; Naiman et al., 2023; Berman et al., 2024; Simon et al., 2025)
114   information. However, a significant drawback of VAE-based approaches is their low reconstruction
115   quality, often resulting in blurry images when applied to real-world data (Kingma et al., 2016; Berg
116   et al., 2018; Vahdat & Kautz, 2020; Bredell et al., 2023). Another research direction has focused
117   on GANs for disentanglement with non-sequential (Tran et al., 2017; Karras et al., 2020; Ren et al.,
118   2021) and sequential (Villegas et al., 2017; Tulyakov et al., 2018) approaches. Unfortunately, GAN-
119
      based techniques are challenging to train and they suffer from learned distributions with insufficient
      expressiveness, exhibiting mode collapse issues (Goodfellow, 2016; Lucic et al., 2018). Currently,
120
      most sequential disentanglement approaches present factorized results on simple real-world datasets
121
      that are far from in-the-wild, with the exception of some preliminary results in SPYL (Naiman et al.,
122   2023). Recently, the rise of diffusion models has led to new non-sequential disentanglement ap-
123   proaches (Kwon et al., 2022; Yang et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2024; Zhu et al.,
124   2024; Baumann et al., 2024), producing high-resolution images with high-quality disentangled fac-
125   tors. However, to the best of our knowledge, we are the first to suggest a two-factor (i.e., static and
126   dynamic) diffusion sequential disentanglement model that is evaluated on a real-world and high-
127   resolution visual benchmark. Finally, a related body of works in animation (Siarohin et al., 2019;
128   Hu, 2024; Xu et al., 2024), which leverages video priors for disentangling object and movement,
129   can be applied to similar swapping tasks as demonstrated in Sec. 5.1 and Sec. 5.2. However, our
130
      method is designed for general sequential data, as it also extends to audio Sec.5.5 and also enables
      the learning of multifactor disentangled representations, as shown in Sec. 5.3
131
132
      Diffusion Models (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015) and score matching (Hyvärinen & Dayan, 2005;
133
      Vincent, 2011) have emerged as strong alternatives to VAEs and GANs (Ho et al., 2020; Dhariwal
134
      & Nichol, 2021). They excel in generating high-quality images through iterative denoising of latent
135   variables and are unified in a score-based modeling framework (Song et al., 2021). However, diffu-
136   sion models construct non-semantic latent codes, and thus, recent efforts have focused on structuring
137   their latent representations. For instance, DiffAE (Preechakul et al., 2022) design an autoencoder to
138   manipulate visual features, whereas InfoDiffusion (Wang et al., 2023) adds loss regularizers. Still,
139   these approaches are not designed to handle sequences: they do not produce static and dynamic
140   factors and are computationally demanding due to high-dimensional latents and long sampling. Re-
141   cent advances in diffusion models have partially addressed their computational representation and
142   sampling shortcomings. In particular, latent diffusion models (LDM) (Rombach et al., 2022) de-
143
      sign their denoising process in a small latent space, significantly reducing computational time and
      memory requirements. Finally, multiple approaches focused on improving diffusion samplers (Song
144
      et al., 2023; Song & Dhariwal, 2024), with EDM (Karras et al., 2022; 2024) standing out as an
145
      efficient and lightweight framework with fast sampling times and high generation quality.

150   3.1   D IFFUSION M ODELS
151
      Diffusion models (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015) are a family of SOTA generative models, that were re-
152
      cently described using stochastic differential equations (SDEs), diffusion processes, and score-based
153   modeling (Song et al., 2021). We will use diffusion models and score-based models interchange-
154   ably. These models include two processes: the forward process and the reverse process. The forward
155   process (often not learnable) is an iterative procedure that corrupts the data by progressively adding
156   noise to it. Specifically, the infinitesimal change to the state xt can be formally described by
157
                                           dxt = f (xt , t)dt + g(t)dw ,                                    (1)
158
159   where w is the standard Wiener process, f (·, t) is a vector-valued function called the drift coefficient,
160   and g(·) is a scalar function known as the diffusion coefficient. From a probabilistic viewpoint,
161   Eq. 1 is associated with modeling the transition from the given data distribution, x0 ∼ p0 , to pt ,
      the probability density of xt , t ∈ [0, T ]. Typically, the prior distribution pT is a simple Gaussian


                                                          3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      distribution with fixed mean and variance that contains no information of p0 . The reverse process,
163   which is learnable, de-noises the data iteratively. The reverse of a diffusion process is also a diffusion
164   process, depending on the score function ∇x log pt (x) and operating in reverse time (Anderson,
165   1982). In our approach, we utilize the conditioned reverse process
166
167
                             dxt = [f (xt , t) − g(t)2 ∇x log pt (xt | u)]dt̄ + g(t)dw̄,                             (2)
168   where w̄ is a standard Wiener process as time progresses backward from T to 0, dt̄ is an infinitesimal
169   negative timestep, and u is a condition variable. Diffusion models are generative by sampling from
170   pT and use ∇x log pt (xt | u) to iteratively solve Eq. 2 until samples from p0 are recovered.

172   3.2     D IFFUSION AUTOENCODERS
173
174   Although diffusion models are powerful generative tools, they are not inherently designed to learn
175
      meaningful representations of the data. To address this limitation, several works (Preechakul et al.,
      2022; Wang et al., 2023) have adapted diffusion models into autoencoders, resulting in diffusion
176
      autoencoders (DiffAEs). These models have demonstrated the ability to learn semantic represen-
177
      tations of the data, allowing certain modifications of the resulting samples by altering their latent
178   vectors. To this end, DiffAEs introduce a semantic encoder, taking a data sample x0 and returning
179   its semantic latent encoding zsem . Then, the latter vector conditions the reverse process, enhancing
180   the model’s ability to reconstruct and manipulate data samples. In practice, the denoiser is also
181   conditioned on a feature map h and the time t, combined using an adaptive group normalization
182   (AdaGN) layer (Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021). The AdaGN block is defined as
183
                                AdaGN(h, t, zsem ) = zs (ts GroupNorm(h) + tb ) ,                                    (3)
184
185   where zs is the output of a linear layer applied to zsem , ts and tb are the outputs of a multi-layer
186   perceptron (MLP) applied to the time t, and multiplications are done element-wise.
187

190   In this section and the subsequent ones, the subscripts represent time in the diffusion process, and
191   superscripts indicate time in the sequence, e.g., a sequence state of the diffusion process is denoted
192   by xτt , t ∈ [0, T ] and τ ∈ {1, . . . , V }. T and V represent the maximum diffusion and sequence
193   times, respectively. In particular, note that we consider discrete time sequences of continuous time
194   diffusion processes; however, our modeling can be extended to additional settings.

      Below, we detail our architectural contributions in extending DiffAE neural networks (Preechakul
257
      et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023), toward achieving unsupervised sequential disentanglement. Our ar-
258
      chitecture, shown in Fig. 2, addresses three limitations of current DiffAEs: 1) it processes sequential
259   data, 2) it factorizes data into separate static and dynamic components, and 3) it is computationally
260   efficient. From a high-level viewpoint, our network is similar to DiffAEs in that it is an autoencoder,
261   consisting a (sequential) semantic encoder, a stochastic encoder, and a stochastic decoder.
262
263
      Encoders. Inspired by works in sequential disentanglement (Yingzhen & Mandt, 2018; Bai et al.,
264   2021; Naiman et al., 2023), we design a novel sequential semantic encoder to extract s0 and d1:V    0 .
265   Particularly, it consists of a U-Net (Ronneberger et al., 2015) and linear modules that operate on each
266   sequence frame independently. Then, there is an LSTM module that summarizes the sequence into
267   a latent representation h1:V . The last hidden, hV , is passed to a linear layer to produce s0 , whereas
268   h1:V are processed with another LSTM and a linear layer to produce d1:V   0 . Our stochastic encoder is
269   EDM (Karras et al., 2022). This encoder adds noise to every frame xτ0 by sampling ϵ ∼ N (0, σt2 I),
      yielding xτt = xτ0 + ϵ. These encoders realize in practice the posterior in Eq. 5.

340   5.1   C ONDITIONAL S WAPPING
341
342   We begin our evaluation with the classic conditional swapping task (Yingzhen & Mandt, 2018;
343   Bai et al., 2021). Given two sample videos x, x̂ ∼ p0 , the goal in this experiment is to create
344   a new sample x̄, conditioned on the static factor of x and dynamic features of x̂. This is done
345   by extracting the latent factors z = (s, d1:V ) and ẑ = (ŝ, dˆ1:V ) for x and x̂, respectively. The
346   new sample x̄ is defined to be the reconstruction of z̄ = (s, dˆ1:V ) through Alg. 1, see Sec. 4. In
347   an ideal swap, x̄ preserves the static characteristics of x while presenting the dynamics of x̂, thus
348   demonstrating strong disentanglement capabilities of the swapping method. We show in Fig. 1 (left)
349   a swap example of DiffSDA, where the top two rows are real videos, and the third row shows the
350   new sample obtained by preserving the static features of the first row and using the dynamics of the
351
      second row. Remarkably, while the people in these sequences are very different, many fine details
      are transferred, including head angle and orientation, as well as mouth and eyes orientation and
352
      openness. In Fig. 3, we present additional swap results on CelebV-HQ (left), VoxCeleb (middle), and
353
      TaiChi-HD (right), comparing DiffSDA (third row) to SPYL (fourth row). Notably, our approach
354   produces high-quality samples, while swapping the dynamics of the second row into the first row,
355   whereas SPYL struggles both with the reconstruction and swap.
356
357
      In addition to the above qualitative evaluation, we also want to quantitatively assess the effective-
      ness of DiffSDA. We report in App. E results from the traditional quantitative benchmark, where a
358
      pre-trained judge (classifier) is used to determine if swapped content is correct (Bai et al., 2021).
359
      However, there are two main issues with the benchmark: i) it depends on labeled data, making it
360   relevant to only a small number of datasets; and ii) results are sensitive to the expressivity and gen-
361   eralizability of the judge. For instance, swapping a smiling expression from person A to person B,
362   may result in person B having a smile, different from the one in the data. In these cases, the judge
363   may wrongly classify a different expression to the smiling person B, see App. E.
364
      Towards addressing these issues, we propose new unsupervised swapping metrics to quantitatively
365
      measure the model’s disentanglement abilities. We adopt estimators commonly used in animation
366   for assessing whether objects and motions are preserved (Siarohin et al., 2019). Specifically, we
367   utilize the average Euclidean distance (AED) that is based on the distances between the latent
368   representations of images. Further, we also employ the average keypoint distance (AKD) which

      5.5   AUDIO M ODALITY
501
502   Our method is inherently modality-agnostic and not constrained to the video domain. Unlike video-
503   focused methods, which often require substantial adjustments when applied to new modalities, our
504   approach can adapt to various modalities with only minor modifications to the backbone architecture.
505   For instance, to support audio, we replace the U-Net with a simple MLP. This flexibility aligns with
506   prior sequential disentanglement methods such as SPYL (Naiman et al., 2023) and DBSE (Berman
507   et al., 2024). In Tab. 3, we demonstrate the adaptability of our model by successfully disentangling
508   audio data on the TIMIT dataset, a widely used benchmark in speech-related tasks. Following the
509   speaker identification benchmarks established by Yingzhen & Mandt (2018) and Zhu et al. (2020),
510
      we evaluate disentanglement quality using the Equal Error Rate (EER), a standard metric in speech
      tasks. Notably, our model improves the disentanglement gap by over 11%, achieving 42.29% dy-
511
      namic EER compared to 31.11% by DBSE, thereby outperforming current state-of-the-art methods.
512
      This result underscores the effectiveness of our approach in handling the audio modality. Additional
513   details about the dataset, metrics, and implementation are provided in the appendix.
514
515   6     C ONCLUSIONS
516
517   The analysis and results of this study underscore the potential of the proposed DiffSDA model to
518   address key limitations in sequential disentanglement, specifically in the context of complex real-
519   world visual data and speech audio. By leveraging a novel probabilistic framework, diffusion au-
520   toencoders, efficient samplers, and latent diffusion models, DiffSDA provides a robust solution for
521   disentangling both static and dynamic factors in sequences, outperforming existing state-of-the-art
522   methods. Moreover, the introduction of a new real-world visual evaluation protocol marks a signif-
523   icant step towards standardizing the assessment of sequential disentanglement models. Neverthe-
524
      less, while DiffSDA shows promise in handling high-resolution videos and varied datasets, future
      research should focus on optimizing its computational efficiency and extending its applicability to
525
      more diverse sequence modalities, such as sensor data, and general time series. Each of these modal-
526
      ities presents unique challenges, such as varying temporal characteristics and distinct data patterns,
527   which may require adapting the model architecture and training strategies. Additionally, our sug-
528   gested benchmark is designed for visual or speech information. Consequently, extending our work
529   to additional modalities also requires designing new benchmarks to account for, e.g., trend predic-
530   tion or temporal coherence. Finally, while the benchmark presents significant progress in handling
531   real-world data in a sequential disentanglement context, it primarily consists of structured data, such
532   as single objects or single views. Modeling and evaluating unstructured data, such as multiple ob-
533   jects within a video or multiple viewpoints (Jabri et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2024), remains an open
534   challenge in the context of sequential disentanglement. This raises an important and compelling
535
      direction for future research.

      A.2    H IGH -R ESOLUTION D ISENTANGLED S EQUENTIAL D IFFUSION AUTOENCODER
835
      In addition to transitioning to real-world data, our goal is to manage high-resolution data for un-
836   supervised sequential disentanglement, for the first time. Drawing inspiration from Rombach et al.
837   (2022), we incorporate perceptual image compression, which combines an autoencoder with a per-
838   ceptual loss (Zhang et al., 2018) and a patch-based adversarial objective (Dosovitskiy & Brox, 2016;
839   Esser et al., 2021; Isola et al., 2017). Specifically, we explore two main variants of the autoencoder.
840   The first variant applies a small Kullback–Leibler penalty to encourage the learned latent space
841   to approximate a standard normal distribution, similar to a VAE (Kingma, 2013; Rezende et al.,
842   2014). The second variant integrates a vector quantization layer (Van Den Oord et al., 2017; Razavi
843   et al., 2019) within the decoder. Empirically, we find that the VQ-VAE-based model performs better
844
      when combined with our method. Given a pre-trained encoder E and decoder D, we can extract
      xτ0 = E(xτ0 ), which represents a low-dimensional latent space where high-frequency, imperceptible
845
      details are abstracted away. Finally, xτ0 can be reconstructed from the latent xτ0 by applying the
846
      decoder xτ0 = D(xτ0 ). The EDM formulation in Eq. 7 makes relatively strong assumptions about the
847   mean and standard deviation of the training data. To meet these assumptions, we opt to normalize
848   the training data globally rather than adjusting the value of σdata , which could significantly affect
849   other hyperparameters (Karras et al., 2024). Therefore, we keep σdata at its default value of 0.5 and
850   ensure that the latents have a zero mean during dataset preprocessing. When generating sequence
851   elements, we reverse this normalization before applying D.

957
      MUG. The MUG facial expression dataset, introduced by Aifanti et al. (2010), contains image
958
      sequences from 52 subjects, each displaying six distinct facial expressions: anger, fear, disgust,
959
      happiness, sadness, and surprise. Each video sequence in the dataset ranges from 50 to 160 frames.
960   To create sequences of length 15, as done in prior work (Bai et al., 2021), we randomly select 15
961   frames from the original sequences. We then apply Haar Cascade face detection to crop the faces
962   and resize them to 64 × 64 pixels, resulting in sequences of x ∈ R15×3×64×64 . The final dataset
963   comprises 3,429 samples. In the case of of the zero shot experiments we resize the images to
964   256 × 256 pixels.
965
966   TaiChi-HD. The TaiChi-HD dataset, introduced by Siarohin et al. (2019), contains videos of
967   full human bodies performing Tai Chi actions. We follow the original preprocessing steps from
968   FOMM (Siarohin et al., 2019) and use a 64 × 64 version of the dataset. The dataset comprises 3,081
969   video chunks with varying lengths, ranging from 128 to 1,024 frames. We split the data into 90% for
970   training and 10% for testing. To create sequences of length 10, similar to the approach used for the
971   MUG dataset, we randomly select 10 frames from the original sequences. The resulting sequences
      are resized to 64 × 64 pixels, forming x ∈ R10×3×64×64 .

974    tracted, and the videos are processed at 25/6 fps. The dataset comprises 22,496 videos and 153,516

999    Average Keypoint Distance (AKD). To evaluate whether the motion in the reconstructed video
1000   is preserved, we utilize pre-trained third-party keypoint detectors on the TaiChi-HD, VoxCeleb,
1001   CelebV-HQ, and MUG datasets. For the VoxCeleb, CelebV-HQ and MUG datasets, we employ the
1002   facial landmark detector from Bulat & Tzimiropoulos (2017), whereas for the TaiChi-HD dataset,
1003   we use the human-pose estimator from Cao et al. (2017). Keypoints are computed independently
1004   for each frame. AKD is calculated by averaging the L1 distance between the detected keypoints
1005   in the ground truth and the generated video. The TaiChi-HD and MUG datasets are evaluated at a
1006
       resolution of 64 × 64 pixels, and the VoxCeleb and CelebV-HQ datasets at 256 × 256 pixels. If the
       model output is at a lower resolution, it is interpolated to 256 × 256 pixels for evaluation.
1007
1008
       Average Euclidean Distance (AED). To assess whether the identity in the reconstructed video is
1009
       preserved, we use the Average Euclidean Distance (AED) metric. AED is calculated by measuring
1010
       the Euclidean distance between the feature representations of the ground truth and the generated
1011   video frames. We selected the feature embedding following the example set in Siarohin et al. (2019).
1012   For the VoxCeleb, CelebV-HQ, and MUG datasets, we use a VGG-FACE for facial identification
1013   using the framework of Serengil & Ozpinar (2020), whereas for TaiChi-HD, we use a network
1014   trained for person re-identification (Hermans et al., 2017). TaiChi-HD and MUG are evaluated at a
1015   resolution of 64 × 64 pixels, and VoxCeleb and CelebV-HQ at 256 × 256 pixels.
1016
       To ensure fairness when measuring AED and AKD, we created a predefined dataset of example pairs,
1017   ensuring that all models are evaluated on the exact same set of pairs. This is important because when
1018   measuring quantitative metrics, the results may vary depending on the dynamics swapped between
1019   two subjects, as e.g., the key points in AKD in the original video are influenced by the identity of
1020   the person. To address this issue, we establish a fixed set of pairs for a consistent comparison across
1021   all methods.

1077   model (See App. D), which is a neural network trained to classify both static and dynamic factors. If
1078   the disentanglement method disentangles these factors effectively, we expect the judge to correctly
1079   identify the dynamics while outputting different predictions for the static features, since the latter
       were randomly sampled and should differ from the original static factor.

1111   Surprised by our results on MUG, we investigated the failure cases to understand the limitations
1112   of our model. In particular, we examined scenarios where we freeze the dynamics and swap the
1113   static features between two samples, and then we generate the corresponding output. In Fig. 7, we
1114   show an example where the static features of the second row are swapped with those of the first
1115
       row, and the resulting generation is displayed in the third row. We observe that while the dynamics
       from the second row are well-preserved, the generated person retains the identity of the first row.
1116
       However, the classifier incorrectly predicts the dynamics for the sequence. To further investigate
1117
       this, we extracted a ground-truth example of the person from the first row in the dataset expressing
1118   the expected emotion and the predicted one. In the last two rows of Fig. 7, we show the same person
1119   with predicted dynamics (fourth row) and the same person with the dynamics that the classifier
1120   predicted (fifth row). We provide another example of the same phenomenon in Fig. 8.
1121
       We observe that while the judge predicts the wrong label for our generated samples in rows C, the
1122
       facial expressions of the people there align better with the actual dynamics in rows B. This suggests
1123
       that the classifier is biased towards the identity when predicting dynamics, potentially forming a
1124   discrete latent space where generalization to nearby related expressions is not possible. Importantly,
1125   the judge attains > 99% accuracy on the test set. We conclude that utilizing a judge can be prob-
1126   lematic for measuring new and unseen variations in the data. This analysis motivates us to present
1127   the AKD and AED, as detailed above in App. D.
