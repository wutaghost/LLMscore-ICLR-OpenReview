009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            Large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated remarkable emergent capa-
012            bilities, reshaping the landscape of functional tasks by leveraging external tools
013            to tackle complex problems, such as those requiring real-time data or special-
014            ized input/output processing. Existing research primarily focuses on equipping
015            LLMs with a broader array of diverse external tools (e.g., program interpreters,
016
               search engines, weather/map applications) but overlooks the necessity of tool us-
               age, invoking external tools indiscriminately without assessing their actual need.
017
               This naive strategy leads to two significant issues: 1) increased latency due to
018
               prolonged processing times, and 2) potential errors arising from communication
019            between LLMs and external tools, resulting in faulty outputs. In this paper, we
020            introduce a concept we term meta-cognition as a proxy for LLM self-capability,
021            and we propose an adaptive decision-making strategy for invoking external tools,
022            referred to as MeCo. Specifically, MeCo focuses on representation space to cap-
023            ture emergent representations of high-level cognitive phenomena that quantify the
024            LLM’s meta-cognitive scores, thereby guiding decisions on when to use external
025            tools. Notably, MeCo is fine-tuning-free, incurring minimal cost, and our exper-
026            iments demonstrate that MeCo accurately detects the model’s internal cognitive
027
               signals. More importantly, our approach significantly enhances decision-making
               accuracy in tool use for multiple base models across various benchmarks.

030   1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Equipping Large language models (LLMs) with tool learning capabilities represents a promising
033   paradigm to address complex tasks relying on external/real-time sources (Komeili, 2021; Tang et al.,
034   2023), specialized format/schema (Yang et al., 2023; Gao et al., 2023; Lu et al., 2024), domain-
035   specific knowledge (He-Yueya et al., 2023; Schick et al., 2024), and so on. Although existing re-
036   search has focused on increasing the number and types of tools available within this paradigm (Qin
037   et al., 2023; Hao et al., 2024) and optimizing their usage of these tools (Patil et al., 2023; Shen et al.,
038
      2024), the decision-making process regarding when tools are necessary remains underexplored.
039   The prevailing strategy adopted by existing paradigms, which involves enhancing tool usage by fine-
040   tuning LLMs on carefully crafted datasets, is often hampered by the quality of these datasets. On the
041   other hand, if an LLM always relies on external tools to respond to user queries, it encounters two
042   notable limitations. Primarily, it leads to increased latency (Qu et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024), as
043   using an external tool (e.g., search engine) can take significantly longer than leveraging the model’s
044
      internal knowledge. Furthermore, the heavy reliance on external APIs and tools poses risks of
      robustness and integration issues. It potentially introduces incorrect or inconsistent outputs when
045
      tools malfunction (Qin et al., 2023) or are unnecessarily used (Lu et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2024).
046
047   To address aforementioned limitations by indiscriminate use of external tools, we propose an adap-
048   tive tool use strategy aimed at improving decision-making in LLMs concerning tool utilization. We
049   introduce a novel approach, termed MeCo, a Meta-Cognition-oriented trigger that facilitates more
050
      judicious use of external tools. We define meta-cognition as the model’s ability to self-assess its
      own capabilities and limitations, discerning whether it can address a user’s query independently or
051
      if it needs to utilize external tools. Overall, MeCo integrates the following key principles:
052
053   1. Meta-Cognition-Oriented Trigger Mechanism: This core component ensures that LLMs main-
         tain an ongoing assessment of their capabilities and limitations, enabling them to determine the

100

149   3   A PPROACH
150
151   In the context of tool use in LLMs, meta-cognition refers to the model’s ability to self-assess its
152   own capabilities and limitations to determine whether it can address a user query independently or
153   if it needs to engage external tools. This self-assessment involves evaluating the complexity and
154   requirements of the query in relation to the model’s internal knowledge and functions. To quantify
155   meta-cognition, we train a probe that detects the model’s level of meta-cognitive awareness. This
156   probe evaluates the rationale behind the model’s decision-making process, providing a score that
157   reflects the model’s self-assessment accuracy.
158   For instance, when the model receives a complex mathematical query, the meta-cognition probe eval-
159   uates whether it should solve it independently or use an external calculator. A high meta-cognition
160   score indicates that the model accurately recognizes its capabilities or limitations and makes the cor-
161   rect decision, whether to use the tool or not. A low score indicates that the model either incorrectly
      attempts the task itself or unnecessarily uses the tool.

195   3.2   D ECISION -M AKING S TRATEGY BASED ON M ETA -C OGNITION
196
197   After developing accurate probes to detect the model’s internal meta-cognition, we design a
198   decision-making strategy utilizing these detection results. Given a user query, the LLM generates a
199   response consisting of m tokens, each associated with a meta-cognition score for every layer in the
200   LLM. This yields a meta-cognition detection array with dimensions (m, n), where n represents the
201
      number of layers in the LLM. Our objective is to make a final decision-“Yes”, indicating the need
      to use external tools or RAG, and “No”, indicating that LLMs can respond directly without external
202
      tools or RAG)-based on this result array.
203
204
      Reducing m to 1. We examine various prompting strategies (detailed in Appendix C) and find
205
      that the Yes/No+Explanation strategy, where the model answers with “Yes” or “No” followed by
206
      a brief explanation, yields the best performance. Therefore, we focus on the first token of the
207   model’s response as it provides a clear signal of whether the model decides to rely on external tools.
208   Extracting the meta-cognition score of the first token to represent the whole response simplifies our
209   decision-making process, as calculating an overall meta-cognition score for the entire response is
210   challenging due to varying response lengths and content across different queries. Since the model
211   always responds with “Yes” or “No” as the first token, basing the trigger mechanism on the first
212   token’s meta-cognition score is both reasonable and practical.
213
214   Reducing n to 1. We select a single probe from the multiple trained probes across different layers
215   to assign the final meta-cognition score to a token. In Zou et al. (2023) and Huang et al. (2023), a
      mean score from multiple probes’ results is usually used to represent the token’s final quantification.

      However, our experiments show that scores predicted by different probes vary significantly, and
217   simply averaging multiple scores does not yield accurate results. We found that probes in shallower
218   layers (e.g., layer -5 to -2) tend to be more effective, with appropriate score distributions, ranges, and
219   lower variances. Therefore, we use the probe with the highest classification accuracy in the layer -5
220   to -2 (as shown in Figure 3), as our final probe and rely on its prediction results.

      After reducing the meta-cognition results into one scalar value, we adopt the thresholding strategy

226   4    B ENCHMARK -M E C A
227
228   Benchmark Domains: We evaluate MeCo using a public benchmark: Metatool (Huang et al.,
229   2023). In addition, we introduce a new benchmark, named Meta-Cognitive Tool Assessment
230   (MeCa), where each query underwent a thorough human review. MeCa expands on Metatool by
231   incorporating a broader range of scenarios for assessing tool usage. We also include tasks to evaluate
232
      adaptive RAG in MeCa. Below are the details of these two benchmarks.
233   Metatool: Metatool consists of 1,040 queries designed to assess whether LLMs can recognize when
234   to rely on external tools to solve user queries that they cannot address directly. The tool names and
235   descriptions in Metatool are retrieved from OpenAI’s plugin list (OpenAI, 2023), and there are 166
236   distinct tools in the benchmark. Metatool primarily focuses on evaluating the model’s awareness of
237   tool usage for individual tools, where LLMs are provided only with a user query and must indepen-
238
      dently decide whether or not to resort to external tools without any tool names or descriptions.
239   Metatool is limited to evaluating user queries without any supplementary information or tool provi-
240   sions, real-world tasks typically involve more complex intents and a diverse array of requirements.
241   To more accurately reflect these multifaceted scenarios, we developed a new benchmark named
242   MeCa. This benchmark is divided into two main components: Tool and RAG. Each query is rigor-
243   ously reviewed by humans to ensure data quality and relevance. MeCa provides several significant
244
      improvements over Metatool:
245   MeCa-Tool was meticulously designed to evaluate scenarios where the invocation of external tools
246   is either necessary or unnecessary. MeCa-Tool is segmented into the following main categories:
247
248   • Tool Usage Assessment. This category expands the Metatool to evaluate the decision-making
249     capability of the LLM regarding tool usage in more comprehensive scenarios, specifically whether
250
        to invoke any external tools. It includes:
251       – Queries that can be handled by the LLM’s internal capabilities without external tools.
252       – Queries that necessitate the use of one or more external tools, indicating tasks beyond the
253         LLM’s standalone capacity.
254
      • Provided Tool Evaluation. In this category, the LLM assistant is provided with available tools
255
        alongside the user query, with the task of determining tool usage based on the tools’ relevance and
256
        necessity:
257
258       – Cases where the external tools are unnecessary and the LLM assistant can successfully re-
259         solve the queries independently.
260       – Situations where external tools are essential and are provided, enabling the LLM assistant to
261         effectively address the user queries.
262       – Instances where the required tools to solve the queries are absent from the provided list.
263
      • Multi-turn Interaction. This category evaluates the LLM’s decision-making regarding tool usage
264     in multi-turn dialogues, involving extended interactions and long context accumulation. This
265     setup tests the LLM’s adaptability and decision-making in complex, evolving scenarios that also
266     encompass the aforementioned categories
267
268   Based on the main categories outlined, MeCa-Tool offers a broader and more varied test set com-
269   pared to Metatool by integrating diverse scenarios through combinations of these main categories.
      Specifically, the final configuration covers 6 tasks with 7,000 queries. Please refer to Table 4 in

      testing data. We fit the thresholds for both PYes and MeCo using the remaining data. The complete
      evaluation results are summarized in Table 1 and Table 2. Our key observations are as follows:
483
484   Superiority of MeCo: On both benchmarks, MeCo significantly enhances the model’s naive de-
485   cision accuracy regarding tool use, outperforming PYes by a considerable margin, indicating the
      effectiveness of the meta-cognition-based trigger mechanism. Notably, MeCo’s superiority is con-

      sistent across multiple backbone models and various evaluation settings, including both with and
487   without context, as well as pre- and post-fine-tuning.
488
489   Importantly, the improvement achieved with MeCo incurs minimal costs, as it involves a fine-tuning-
490
      free and easy-to-integrate module. Note that fine-tuning and MeCo are two orthogonal approaches,

525
      In this paper, we introduce the concept of adap-
526
      tive tool use to advance existing tool learning paradigms, which typically rely on external tools
527   without discrimination to address user queries. Drawing on insights from representation engineer-
528   ing, we develop a computationally efficient plug-in module, MeCo, that assesses the meta-cognitive
529   states of LLMs. Our approach utilizes a meta-cognition probe to detect signals associated with
530   meta-cognition, and leverages these quantification results to inform a decision-making strategy that
531   enables LLMs to make more accurate determinations about when to invoke external tools. To sup-
532   port evaluation, we introduce a new benchmark, MeCa, specifically designed to evaluate LLMs’
533   awareness of tool use as well as the timing for retrieval. We empirically validate the effectiveness of
534   MeCo using both the Metatool and MeCa, demonstrating significant improvements in the model’s
535
      decision-making accuracy regarding the timing for tool use and retrieval. Our findings suggest that
      by integrating meta-cognition into the tool usage framework, we can enhance the operational effi-
536
      ciency and decision-making capabilities of LLMs across diverse contexts.

800   utilizing external tools based on natural language instructions. This evolution expands their appli-
801
      cation beyond basic conversational tasks to enable dynamic interactions across diverse functional
      domains, such as facility management and professional services (Patil et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023b;
802
      Qin et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023). For example, Toolformer (Schick et al., 2024) enables LLMs
803
      to use external tools via simple APIs through a supervised fine-tuning (SFT) model. Liu et al.
804   (2024c) demonstrate strong executable functional API calls across different domains. ToolACE (Liu
805   et al., 2024b) trained on synthesized data, achieves state-of-the-art results on the Berkeley Function-
806   Calling Leaderboard (Yan et al., 2024), even with a relatively small model size of 8B parameters.
807   Despite their growing popularity and capabilities, tool use in LLMs often depends on strategies like
808   verbal feedback, which are hampered by the quality of the datasets used for fine-tuning. Several
809   benchmarks/datasets have been developed to support tool use in a data-centric way, such as API-
      Bank (Li et al., 2023), which provides a set of tool-use dialogues with various APIs to assess the


                                                        15
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
      LLM’s tool use capabilities, Toolalpaca (Tang et al., 2023) constructs a comprehensive tool-use cor-
811   pus derived from collected real-world APIs, designed specifically to fine-tune LLMs for better tool
812   utilization. ToolBench (Qin et al., 2023) focuses on creating a synthetic instruction-tuning dataset
813   for tool use. However, these methods rely solely on superficial textual information, without probing
814   deeper into the LLM’s internal states to explain or justify when and why a tool should be called,
815   resulting in an inability to accurately determine the optimal timing for tool invocation.
816
817   Adaptive RAG RAG has shown success in supporting AI systems that require up-to-date informa-
818   tion or access domain-specific knowledge, particularly where the scope of queries is not seen in the
819   training data of LLMs (Lewis et al., 2020; Ren et al., 2023; Vu et al., 2023; Izacard et al., 2023). This
820   paper is also consistent with the trend of towards adaptive RAG paradigm, which is designed to as-
821   sess whether a query can be directly answered by the LLMs or requires external data retrieval (Asai
822
      et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2023). Specifically, a simple query within the LLM’s knowledge should
      be directly answered by the LLMs themselves. On the other hand, for complex queries or questions
823
      about data they have not been trained on, RAG intervenes to prevent incorrect out-of-date answers or
824
      hallucination (Ji et al., 2023). This mechanism allows RAG to dynamically adjust operational strate-
825   gies of retrieval-augmented LLMs by assessing the boundary of LLM’s self-knowledge and the com-
826   plexity of the query, thereby minimizing unnecessary computational overhead when the queries are
827   answerable by LLMs themselves. Similar to the LLMs’ function-calling, the decision of retrieval
828   timing typically hinges on three primary methods: (i) explicit verbal feedback from LLMs (Ding
829   et al., 2024), (ii) enhancements through fine-tuning (Asai et al., 2023), or (iii) probability-based
830   metrics (Kadavath et al., 2022; Jiang et al., 2023). Specifically, He et al. (2021) proposed enhancing
831   the retrieval time efficiency by computing the probability of the next token via interpolating an LLM
832   with a distribution calculated from the k nearest context-token pairs. Drozdov et al. (2022) further
833
      extend kNN-LM to the adaptive paradigm by assigning the interpolation coefficient according to the
      retrieval quality measured by semantic similarity. Asai et al. (2023) introduce Self-RAG to improve
834
      generation quality and factuality by enabling adaptive retrieval and self-reflection. In contrast, this
835
      paper conceptualizes RAG as an external tool and highlights the importance of understanding the
836   internal states of an LLM when developing the retrieval policy.
837
838
      Explainability of LLMs However, there is a considerable discrepancy between LLM’s decision
839   mechanisms (often based on verbalized responses) and their internal cognition (Zou et al., 2023).
840   The internal workings of LLMs are usually unclear, and this lack of transparency poses unwanted
841   risks in downstream decision-making. Therefore, understanding and interpreting LLMs is crucial for
842   elucidating their behaviors and limitations. To address this challenge, various explanations that pro-
843   vide insights into the inner workings of LLMs have been proposed (Zhao et al., 2024): (i) Probing-
844   based explanations: Probing uses vector representations to measure embedded knowledge (Peters
845   et al., 2018; Jawahar et al., 2019) or examines specific knowledge during the LLM’s generation
846   process (Li et al., 2022), (ii) Neuron-level explanation: neuron analysis identifies critical neurons
847
      that are essential for model’s performance (Antverg & Belinkov, 2021; Bills et al., 2023), (iii) rep-
      resentation engineering (RepE): RepE leverages techniques inspired by cognitive neuroscience to
848
      identify and enhance the transparency of LLMs by uncovering their internal cognitive states (Zou
849
      et al., 2023). In this paper, we aim to detect the internal cognition of LLMs, and intervene LLM’s
850   decisions, i.e., ensuring more precise decisions on tool use and retrieval timing.

      Before delving into the analysis, we provide some background on the concept of calibration in the
957
      context of Large Language Models (LLMs). Calibration refers to the alignment between a model’s
958
      predicted probabilities and the actual likelihood of those predictions being correct. A well-calibrated
959   model generates probability scores that accurately reflect the true probability of its predictions.
960

962
      decisions. Our key observations are as follows:
963
             1. When the model is given detailed instructions and few-shot examples, it demonstrates poor
964             calibration. As illustrated in Figure 6(a), the distributions of P(Yes) scores for correct and
965             incorrect decisions do not show a clear distinction.
966
             2. Conversely, when the model lacks detailed context and must rely on its internal beliefs to
967             make decisions, it exhibits improved calibration. In Figure 6(b), the peak of the distribution
968             for correct scores clearly deviates from that of incorrect scores.
969
             3. After fine-tuning, the model displays significantly better calibration, as shown in Figures

                6(c) and (d). Most correct decisions have P(Yes) scores of either 1 (indicating ”Yes”) or 0
