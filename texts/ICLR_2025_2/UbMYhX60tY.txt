011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Denoising diffusion probabilistic models (DDPMs) estimate the data distribution
014            by sequentially denoising samples drawn from a prior distribution, which is typ-
015
               ically assumed to be the standard Gaussian for simplicity. Owing to their ca-
               pabilities of generating high-fidelity samples, DDPMs can be utilized for signal
016
               restoration tasks in recovering a clean signal from its degraded observation(s), by
017
               conditioning the model on the degraded signal. The degraded signals are them-
018            selves contaminated versions of the clean signals; due to this correlation, they
019            may encompass certain useful information about the target clean data distribu-
020            tion. However, naively adopting the standard Gaussian as the prior distribution in
021            turn discards such information. In this paper, we propose to improve conditional
022            DDPMs for signal restoration applications by leveraging a more informative prior
023            that is jointly learned with the diffusion model. The proposed framework, called
024            RestoreGrad, exploits the correlation between the degraded and clean signals to
025            construct a better prior for restoration tasks. In contrast to existing DDPMs that
026
               just settle on using pre-defined or handcrafted priors, RestoreGrad learns the prior
               jointly with the diffusion model. To this end, we first derive a new objective func-
027
               tion from a modified evidence lower bound (ELBO) of the data log-likelihood, to
028
               incorporate the prior learning process into conditional DDPMs. Then, we suggest
029            a corresponding joint learning paradigm for optimizing the new ELBO. Notably,
030            RestoreGrad requires minimum modifications to the diffusion model itself; thus,
031            it can be flexibly implemented on top of various conditional DDPM-based signal
032            restoration models. On speech and image restoration tasks, we show that Restore-
033            Grad demonstrates faster convergence (5-10 times fewer training steps) to achieve
034            on par or better perceptual quality of restored signals over existing DDPM base-
035            lines, along with improved robustness to using fewer sampling steps in inference
036            time (2-2.5 times fewer steps), advocating the advantages of leveraging jointly
037
               learned prior for efficiency improvements in the diffusion process.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
040
041
      Denoising diffusion probabilistic models (DDPMs) (Ho et al., 2020; Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015) are
042   latent variable generative models that have shown impressive results in various generative modeling
043   tasks. DDPMs typically consist of i) the forward process, where the original data samples are
044   gradually corrupted by adding Gaussian noise to eventually become a standard normal prior; ii) the
045   reverse process, in which a neural network model is responsible for recovering the original data
046   samples from the corrupted data by learning to sequentially reverse the diffusion process. Thanks
047   to their exceptional capabilities of generating high-quality data, DDPMs can be applied to various
048   signal restoration tasks – recovering the missing components in a signal due to contamination (e.g.,
049   audio recorded with environmental noise (Lu et al., 2021; 2022; Tai et al., 2023b), images obstructed
050
      by bad weather conditions (Özdenizci & Legenstein, 2023) or various measurement noises (Croitoru
      et al., 2023), etc.), by conditioning the DDPM model on the degraded observations.
051
052   However, for the diffusion model to adequately learn the reverse process, a large number of training
053   iterations is typically required, leading to potentially slow model convergence. Such inefficiency
      was recently related to the discrepancy between the real data distribution and the accustomed choice

089

097
098

               efficient, being applicable to DDPM-based signal restoration models for various modalities
099
               including images and audio while requiring minimum increase in model complexity.

176   Signal restoration by conditional DDPMs. Signal restoration problems are concerned with recov-
177   ering the original signals from their degraded observations, which are of paramount importance in
178   reality while remaining challenging, as noises are ubiquitous and may be strong enough to cause
179   significant degradation of the signal quality. Recently, adoption of deep generative models (Kingma
180   & Welling, 2014; Goodfellow et al., 2014; Ho et al., 2020) for signal restoration tasks has consid-
181   erably increased due to their remarkable capabilities of generating missing components in the data,
182   with conditional DDPMs (Croitoru et al., 2023; Cao et al., 2024) demonstrating substantial promise.
183   More formally, let y denote the degraded observation of the clean signal x0 . The task of recovering
184   x0 given y by a model θ can be cast as maximizing the conditional likelihood of data pθ (x0 |y). The
185   problem is in general intractable, but can be approximated by using a DDPM conditioned on y. The
186   main idea is, without modifying the forward diffusion process (1), to learn a conditional diffusion
187   model θ with y provided as input to the reverse process (Özdenizci & Legenstein, 2023):

297   4    R ELATED WORK
298
      Diffusion model efficiency improvements. Das et al. (2023) utilized the shortest path between
299
      two Gaussians to reduce the number of diffusion steps. Song et al. (2020) generalized DDPMs via
300
      a class of non-Markovian diffusion processes, giving rise to implicit models that use much fewer
301   sampling steps. Nichol & Dhariwal (2021) introduced a few simple modifications to improve the
302   log-likelihood and sampling efficiency. Pandey et al. (2022; 2021) combined the VAE with DDPM to
303   achieve high-fidelity generation, by using DDPM to refine VAE-generated samples. Rombach et al.
304   (2022) performed the diffusion process in the lower dimensional latent space of an autoencoder to
305   achieve high-resolution image synthesis, and Liu et al. (2023b) studied using such latent diffusion
306   models for audio. Popov et al. (2021) explored using a text encoder to extract better representations
307   for continuous-time diffusion based text-to-speech generation. More recently, Nielsen et al. (2024)
308   explored using a time-dependent image encoder to parameterize the mean of the diffusion process.
309
      Orthogonal to the above improvements, PriorGrad (Lee et al., 2021) and follow-up work (Koizumi
      et al., 2022) studied utilizing informative prior extracted from the conditioner data for improving
310
      learning efficiency. However, they are still sub-optimal when the conditioner data are degraded
311
      versions of the target as in signal restoration applications, and only focused on speech-related tasks.
312
313   Diffusion model based signal restoration. Built on top of the diffusion models for audio generation
314   and synthesis, e.g., Kong et al. (2021); Chen et al. (2020); Leng et al. (2022), many SE models have
315
      been proposed. One of the pioneering work may be CDiffuSE (Lu et al., 2022), which introduced
      conditional DDPMs to the SE task and demonstrated the potential. Other works (Serrà et al., 2022;
316
      Welker et al., 2022; Richter et al., 2023; Yen et al., 2023; Lemercier et al., 2023; Tai et al., 2023a)
317
      have also attempted to improve SE by exploiting diffusion models. In the vision domain, diffusion
318   models have also demonstrated impressive performance for IR tasks (Li et al., 2023; Zhu et al., 2023;
319   Huang et al., 2024; Luo et al., 2023; Xia et al., 2023; Fei et al., 2023; Hurault et al., 2022; Liu et al.,
320   2023a; Chung et al., 2023b;a; Zhou et al., 2024; Xiao et al., 2024; Zheng et al., 2024). A notable
321   work utilizing conditional DDPMs for IR is Özdenizci & Legenstein (2023) that achieved impressive
322   performance on several benchmark datasets for restoring vision in adverse weather conditions. Our
323   goal is to add to this interesting body of signal restoration work using diffusion models by exploring
      the idea of jointly learning the prior distribution and diffusion process for improved model efficiency.

330   Dataset. We validate the SE performance on the benchmark SE dataset VoiceBank+DEMAND
331
      (Valentini-Botinhao et al., 2016). The dataset consists of clean speech clips collected from the
      VoiceBank corpus (Veaux et al., 2013), mixed with ten types of noise profiles from the DEMAND
332
      database (Thiemann et al., 2013). Specifically, the training utterances from VoiceBank are artificially
333
      contaminated with the noise samples from DEMAND at 0, 5, 10, and 15 dB signal-to-noise ratio
334   (SNR) levels, amounting to 11,572 utterances. The testing utterances are mixed with different noise
335   samples at 2.5, 7.5, 12.5, and 17.5 dB SNR levels, amounting to 824 utterances.
336
337
      Evaluation metrics. We consider: PESQ: Perceptual Evaluation of Speech Quality (ITU-T Rec.
      P.862.2, 2005). SI-SNR: Scale-Invariant SNR (Le Roux et al., 2019). CSIG, CBAK, COVL:
338
      Mean-opinion-score predictors of signal distortion, background-noise intrusiveness, and overall sig-
339
      nal quality, respectively (Hu & Loizou, 2007). In all metrics, a higher score indicates better SE.
340
341   Models. The following models are compared:
342   • Baseline DDPM: We adopt the CDiffuSE (Base) model as the baseline DDPM from Lu et al.
343     (2022), which is based on DiffWave (Kong et al., 2021) with 4.28M learnable parameters.
344   • PriorGrad: We implement the PriorGrad (Lee et al., 2021) on top of CDiffuSE by changing the
345     prior distribution from the standard Gaussian to N (0, Σy ), where Σy is the covariance of the data-
346     dependent prior computed based on the conditioner y, using the approach for the application to
347     vocoder in Lee et al. (2021).
348   • RestoreGrad: We incorporate Prior Net and Posterior Net on top of CDiffuSE. Both modules
349     adopt the ResNet-20 architect (He et al., 2016) suitably modified to 1-D convolutions for wave-
350     form processing, each has only 93K learnable parameters (only 2% of the CDiffuSE model).
351   Configurations. We adopted the basic configurations same as in Lu et al. (2022). The waveforms
352   were processed at 16kHz sampling rate. The number of forward diffusion steps was T = 50. The
353   variance schedule was βt ∈ [10−4 , 0.035], linearly spaced. The batch size was 16. The fast sampling
354   scheme in Kong et al. (2021) was used in the reverse processes with S = 6 steps to reduce inference
355   complexity. The inference variance schedule was βtinfer = [10−4 , 10−3 , 0.01, 0.05, 0.2, 0.35]. Adam
356   optimizer (Kingma & Ba, 2014) was utilized with a learning rate of 2 × 10−4 . We set η = 0.1 and
357   λ = 0.5 for (10) (we discuss different choices of (η, λ) in Appendix C.1). The models were trained
358
      on one NVIDIA Tesla V100 GPU (32 GB CUDA memory) and finished 96 epochs in 1 day.

362   better convergence behavior over PriorGrad (handcrafted prior) and CDiffuSE (standard Gaussian
363   prior). For example, PriorGrad reaches 2.4 in PESQ at 96 epochs, whereas RestoreGrad
364   reaches it in (roughly) 10 epochs, indicating a 10× speed-up. The results suggest that jointly
365   learning the prior distribution can be beneficial for DDPMs.
366   Robustness to reduced number of reverse steps in inference. RestoreGrad can potentially reduce
367   the inference complexity too. In Figure 5, we show how the trained diffusion models tolerate reduc-
368   tion in the number of inference steps. In each model, we trained the network for 96 epochs and then
369   inferenced with S = 3 reverse steps to compare with the originally adopted S = 6 steps in Lu et al.
370   (2022). The noise schedule for S = 3 was βtinfer = [0.05, 0.2, 0.35], a subset of the S = 6 schedule
371   that resulted in best performance. We can see that the baseline DDPM is most sensitive to the step
372   reduction, while PriorGrad shows certain tolerance as leveraging a closer-to-data prior distribution.
373   Finally, RestoreGrad barely degrades with reduced sampling steps, echoing that a better prior

      has been obtained as it recovers higher fidelity signal even in fewer reverse steps.

533   6    C ONCLUSION
534   We investigated the potential of jointly learning the prior distribution with the conditional DDPM
535   for improved efficiency. The proposed RestoreGrad provides a more systematic way of estimating
536   the prior than existing selections for diffusion models. Via experiments on SE and IR tasks, we
537   demonstrated the advantages of leveraging learning-based prior with RestoreGrad. A limitation of
538   the current work is that we only focus on signal restoration applications, where we suitably assume a
539   zero-mean Gaussian prior and only learn its covariance. In the future, it can be interesting to research
      on using a more generic prior form and extend the idea to other modalities and applications.

           from the VoiceBank corpus. There are totally 11527 utterances for training and 824 for testing. Note

1007       B.2.2    M ODEL ARCHITECTURE
1008
1009       Baseline DDPM-based SE model. The baseline SE model considered in this work, i.e., CDiffuSE
1010       (Lu et al., 2022), performs enhancement in the time domain. We utilized the CDiffuSE base model,
1011
           which has approximately 4.28M learnable parameters, from the implementation at: https://
           github.com/neillu23/CDiffuSE. The model is implemented based on DiffWave (Kong
1012
           et al., 2021), a versatile diffusion probabilistic model for conditional and unconditional waveform
1013
           generation. The basic model structure of CDiffuSE is similar to that of DiffWave. However, since
1014       the target task is SE, CDiffuSE uses the noisy spectral features as the conditioner, rather than the
1015       clean Mel-spectral features used in DiffWave utilized for vocoders. After the reverse process is
1016       completed, the enhanced waveform further combine the observed noisy signal y with the ratio 0.2
1017       to recover the high frequency speech in the final enhanced waveform, as suggested in Abd El-Fattah
1018       et al. (2008); Defossez et al. (2020).
1019
           PriorGrad. We implemented the PriorGrad (Lee et al., 2021) on top of the CDiffuSE model by us-
1020       ing a data-dependent prior N (0, Σy ), where Σy is the covariance of the prior distribution computed
1021       based on using the mel-spectrogram of the noisy input y. Following the application to vocoder in
1022       Lee et al. (2021), we leveraged a normalized frame-level energy of the mel-spectrogram for acquir-
1023       ing data-dependent prior, exploiting the fact that the spectral energy contains an exact correlation to
1024       the waveform variance (by Parseval’s theorem (Stoica et al., 2005)). More specifically, we computed
1025       the frame-level energy by taking the square root of the sum of exp(Y) over the frequency axis for
           each time frame, where Y is the mel-spectrogram of the noisy input y from the training data. We


                                                                     19
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1026
       then normalized the frame-level energy to a range of (0, 1] to acquire the data-dependent diagonal
1027   variance ΣY . Then we upsampled ΣY in the frame level to Σy in the waveform-level using the
1028   given hop length of computing the mel-spectrogram. We imposed the minimum standard deviation
1029   of the prior to 0.1 through clipping to ensure numerical stability during training, as suggested in Lee
1030   et al. (2021).
1031
       Prior Net and Posterior Net for RestoreGrad. The additional encoder modules for the Restore-
1032
       Grad adopt the ResNet-20 architect (He et al., 2016) using the implementation from: https:
1033   //github.com/akamaster/pytorch_resnet_cifar10. We suitably modified the orig-
1034   inal 2-D convolutions in ResNet-20 to 1-D convolutions for waveform processing. The modified
1035   ResNet-20 model has only 93K learnable parameters (only 2% of the size of CDiffuSE model). The
1036   Prior Net takes the noisy speech waveform y as input, while the Posterior Net takes both the clean
1037   and noisy waveforms, x0 and y, as input, which are concatenated along the channel dimension. We
1038   employed the exponential nonlinearity at the network output for estimating the variances of the prior
1039   and posterior distributions.

1041   B.2.3    O PTIMIZATION AND INFERENCE
1042
       We used the same configurations of CDiffuSE (Base) (Lu et al., 2022) for optimizing all the models,
1043
       where the batch size was 16, the Adam optimizer was used with a learning rate of 2 × 10−4 , and the
1044   diffusion steps T = 50 with linearly spaced βt ∈ [10−4 , 0.035]. For RestoreGrad, we imposed the
1045   minimum standard deviation σmin = 0.1 by adding it to the output of the Prior Net and Posterior Net
1046   to ensure stability during training. The fast sampling scheme in Kong et al. (2021) was used in the
1047   reverse processes with S = 6 and the inference schedule βtinfer = [10−4 , 10−3 , 0.01, 0.05, 0.2, 0.35].
1048   The models were trained on one NVIDIA Tesla V100 GPU of 32 GB CUDA memory and finished
1049   training for 96 epochs in 1 day.

       Baseline DDPM-based IR models. The baseline IR models considered in this work, i.e., the Rain-

1102
       restoration of the images. The models perform diffusion process at the patch level, where over-
1103   lapping p × p patches are taken as input. When sampling, all p × p patches extracted from the

1106   we considered p = 64 and r = 16, which correspond to the RainDropDiff64 and WeatherDiff64

1112   learnable parameters, which is less than 0.3% of the size of RainDropDiff and WeatherDiff. The
1113   Prior Net takes the noisy image y as input, while the Posterior Net takes both the clean and noisy
1114   images, x0 and y, as input, which are concatenated along the channel dimension. We employed the
1115   exponential nonlinearity at the network output for estimating the variances of the prior and posterior
1116   distributions.
