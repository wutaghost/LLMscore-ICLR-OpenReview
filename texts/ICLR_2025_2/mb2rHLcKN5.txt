009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Formal theorem proving, a field at the intersection of mathematics and computer
012            science, has seen renewed interest with advancements in large language models
013            (LLMs). This paper introduces SubgoalXL, a novel approach that synergizes
014            subgoal-based proofs with expert learning to enhance LLMs’ capabilities in formal
015            theorem proving within the Isabelle environment. SubgoalXL addresses two criti-
016            cal challenges: the scarcity of specialized mathematics and theorem-proving data,
017            and the need for improved multi-step reasoning abilities in LLMs. By optimiz-
018
               ing data efficiency and employing subgoal-level supervision, SubgoalXL extracts
               richer information from limited human-generated proofs. The framework inte-
019
               grates subgoal-oriented proof strategies with an expert learning system, iteratively
020
               refining formal statement, proof, and subgoal generators. Leveraging the Isabelle
021            environment’s advantages in subgoal-based proofs, SubgoalXL achieves a new
022            state-of-the-art performance of 56.1% in Isabelle on the standard miniF2F dataset,
023            marking an absolute improvement of 4.9%. Notably, SubgoalXL successfully
024            solves 41 AMC12, 9 AIME, and 3 IMO problems from miniF2F. These results
025            underscore the effectiveness of maximizing limited data utility and employing
026            targeted guidance for complex reasoning in formal theorem proving, contributing
027            to the ongoing advancement of AI reasoning capabilities.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Formal theorem proving, a field at the intersection of mathematics and computer science, has flour-
033
      ished alongside the development of languages like Lean (de Moura et al., 2015) and Isabelle (Paulson,
      1994). These two prominent communities have been instrumental in advancing the field’s core
034
      challenge: mechanizing mathematical reasoning and proof verification (Li et al., 2020). Through the
035
      creation of rigorously verified proofs, this discipline strengthens the foundations of mathematical
036   certainty, potentially opening doors to new mathematical discoveries.
037
038   The field has recently garnered renewed attention, driven by advancements in large language models
039
      (LLMs). Despite their impressive capabilities, current LLMs often face limitations in performing
      complex reasoning tasks required for formal theorem proving, including the need for logically
040
      rigorous, multi-step proofs (Wu et al., 2022; Jiang et al., 2022a; Zhao et al., 2024; Xin et al., 2023;
041
      Lin et al., 2024). Conventional approaches struggle to align informal human intuition with the strict
042   formalism required by theorem-proving languages, leading to inefficiencies in generating high-quality
043   proofs. This highlights a pressing need to refine models that not only handle the depth of logical
044   reasoning but also make more efficient use of available data while bridging the gap between informal
045   and formal mathematical reasoning.
046
      In this work, we introduce SubgoalXL (Figure 1), a novel approach that synergizes subgoal-based
047
      proofs with expert learning to enhance LLMs’ capabilities in formal theorem proving. SubgoalXL
048   tackles the scarcity of specialized mathematics and theorem-proving data (Lin et al., 2024; Wu
049   et al., 2024) by maximizing data utility through subgoal-level decomposition of proofs, allowing
050   for more granular supervision and iterative refinement. This approach extracts deeper structural
051   information from human-generated proofs by focusing on intermediate subgoals, effectively breaking
052   down the reasoning process into smaller, manageable steps. Consequently, SubgoalXL enhances
053   multi-step reasoning abilities, ensuring that each generated subgoal aligns with both the informal
      intuition and the formal proof structure. At its core, SubgoalXL integrates subgoal-oriented strategies

096   Formal theorem proving has advanced significantly through machine learning, focusing on enhancing
097   proof search strategies and leveraging Large Language Models (LLMs) for autoformalization Polu
098   & Sutskever (2020); Polu et al. (2022); Jiang et al. (2022a). Improvements in the proof search
099
      include self-supervised strategies in Expert Iteration (Polu et al., 2022) and PACT (Han et al., 2021),
      integrations of language models with automated provers in HyperTree Proof Search (HTPS)(Lample
100
      et al., 2022) and Thor(Jiang et al., 2022b), and transformer-based premise selection in Magnusham-
101
      mer (Mikuła et al., 2023). Despite these advancements, scalability remains a challenge due to
102   the increasing complexity of theorems. The application of LLMs for autoformalization and proof
103   generation has also been explored, with Wu et al. (2022) and Jiang et al. (2022a) demonstrating
104   the conversion of mathematical problems into formal specifications. Baldur (First et al., 2023)
105   further enhances proving capabilities by producing full proofs and incorporating a proof repair model.
106   Additionally, LEGO-Prover (Xin et al., 2023) and Lyra Zheng et al. (2023) contribute uniquely to
107   theorem proving by focusing on the incremental development of reusable theorems and integrating
      error messages from external verifiers for proof post-processing, respectively. DeepSeek-Prover (Xin

108
      et al., 2024a) highlights the potential of large-scale synthetic data, while Lean-STaR (Lin et al., 2024)
109   leverages informal information to boost theorem-proving capabilities by training language models to
110   produce informal thoughts before each proof step. InternLM2-StepProver (Wu et al., 2024) addresses

      Nevertheless, challenges remain in addressing data scarcity and enhancing deep, multi-step rea-
115   soning in formal theorem proving. Building upon the insights from subgoal-based demonstration
116   learning (Zhao et al., 2024), we introduce SubgoalXL, a novel framework that combines subgoal-
117   based proofs with an expert learning system. This approach iteratively enhances formal statement,
118   proof, and subgoal generation, aiming to improve data efficiency and achieve robust performance.
119   SubgoalXL complements existing methods by focusing on maximizing the utility of limited data and
120   refining complex reasoning strategies.

131
      model can generate both the informal proof p and the formal proof P, following the distribution
132

133   the prefix and P representing the suffix. In cases where an informal proof p is available, the model
134   can directly generate the formal proof P following pfpg (P | s, S, p).
135
136
      The challenges mainly lie in (1) the limited effectiveness of informal proofs in I due to discrep-
      ancies between human-written informal proofs and the established practices of formal proofs in
137
      theorem-proving languages; and (2) the difficulty in constructing the full training dataset, which
138
      requires aligned (s, S, p, P) quadruples. Inspired by Zhao et al. (2024), we use subgoal-based
139   proofs (Figure 1a) to replace informal proofs in I, achieving better consistency with the structure of
140   formal proofs (see §3.2). Additionally, we develop an expert learning framework (Figure 1b) that
141   samples (s, S, p, P) quadruples by estimating their optimal distributions through iterative refinement,
142   leveraging probabilistic modeling and gradient estimation techniques (see §3.3).

145   3.2   S UBGOAL - BASED P ROOF
146
147   To annotate subgoal-based proofs for the informal statements in I, we begin by manually creating
148   demonstration examples to serve as input for in-context learning (see Figure 1a). We select a subset
149   of problems from the miniF2F validation set and manually construct the verified formal proof for
150
      each problem. Then, we prompt GPT-4o to generate subgoal-based proofs g, conditioned on the
      informal statement s, formal statement S, and formal proof P. This process ensures that: (1) the
151
      subgoal-based proofs are produced by autoregressive models; (2) they exhibit a consistent style,
152
      reducing the learning burden, as noted by Gu et al. (2018); and (3) each subgoal corresponds to a
153   corresponding formal intermediate goal in Isabelle. These demonstrations are then used as in-context
154   examples to annotate subgoal-based proofs for the informal statements in I (see Appendix C for
155   further details).

159
160   We introduce the SubgoalXL framework, which comprises a formal proof generator (pfpg ), a formal
161   statement generator (pfsg ), and a subgoal generator (psg ). Inspired by gradient estimation in proba-
      bilistic modeling (Schulman et al., 2015), this framework estimates optimal training data distributions

      for each component and iteratively refines these components by fine-tuning on data sampled from the
163   respective distributions.1 The overall algorithm is presented in Algorithm 1.
164
165   Components. The core components include a formal statement generator, a formal proof generator,
166   and a subgoal generator. The formal statement generator annotates formal statements for informal
167   ones in I following pfsg (S | s). Subsequently, the formal proof generator produces formal proofs for
168   the informal data in I, based on pfpg (P | s, S, g) and using subgoal-based proofs (see §3.2). The
169   subgoal generator labels subgoal-based proofs for formal data in F according to psg (g | s, S) after
170   informal statements have been generated for each data point in F (refer to Appendix D.2 for details).
171   Additionally, the formal proof generator assesses the performance of the subgoal generator by
172   evaluating the likelihood of reconstructing formal proofs in F. Conversely, the subgoal generator
173   evaluates the formal statement generator by assessing the likelihood of reconstructing subgoal-
174   based proofs in I. We also introduce an auxiliary component, the posterior subgoal generator
175   ppsg (g | s, S, P), which evaluates the formal proof generator based on the likelihood of reconstructing
176   subgoal-based proofs in I. The formal statement generator, formal proof generator, and subgoal
177   generator iteratively improve through expert learning, with only the formal proof generator used
178
      during testing.

284   4.2    BASELINES
285
286
      To assess the performance of our approach, we compare it against several established baselines.
287
288   Symbolic Automated Provers. We first apply Sledgehammer, a proof automation tool extensively
289
      used within the Isabelle environment. Sledgehammer incorporates a 120-second timeout and utilizes
      five automated theorem provers (Z3, CVC4, SPASS, Vampire, E). Following Jiang et al. (2022a), we
290
      enhance Sledgehammer with a set of 11 common tactics (e.g., auto, simp, blast, fastforce, force, eval,
291
      presburger, sos, arith, linarith, auto simp: field simps). If these tactics fail or take longer than 10
292   seconds, the system defaults to the basic Sledgehammer configuration.
293
294
      Search-based Approaches. We also employ search-based methods, particularly Monte-Carlo tree
295   search (Silver et al., 2016), to explore proof possibilities. This includes Thor (Jiang et al., 2022b)
296   and a version enhanced with expert iteration on autoformalized data (Thor+expert iteration (Wu
297   et al., 2022)). Thor integrates language models with automated theorem provers to efficiently
298   select premises from large libraries, while Thor+expert iteration further refines this by training on
299   autoformalized theorems.
300
301   LLM-based Approaches. In the LLM-based category, we evaluate several frameworks: Draft,
302   Sketch, and Prove (DSP) (Jiang et al., 2022a), LEGO-Prover (Xin et al., 2023), Lyra (Zheng et al.,
303   2023), and Subgoal-Prover (Zhao et al., 2024). DSP uses the 540B Minerva model (Lewkowycz
304   et al., 2022) to generate formal sketches from informal proofs. LEGO-Prover incrementally develops
305   reusable theorems to enhance proof efficiency, while Lyra integrates feedback from external verifiers
306
      to optimize the verification process. Subgoal-Prover improves LLM performance in formal theorem
      proving by replacing informal proofs with subgoal-based proofs and using diffusion models to
307
      organize demonstrations optimally. Notably, all these methods employ Sledgehammer for consistency
308
      across evaluations.
309
310   Comparisons with theorem proving methods based on Lean (de Moura et al., 2015), a system utilizing
311   distinct tactics and automation mechanisms that are not directly comparable to Isabelle, are deferred
312
      to Appendix B for thorough analysis.

534      the Isabelle environment. By addressing the challenges of complex multi-step reasoning, SubgoalXL

      A     F ORMAL E NVIRONMENT AND P ROOF T OOLS
649
650
      Interactive Theorem Provers. Interactive Theorem Provers (ITPs), such as Isabelle (Paulson,
651
      1994), are essential tools in modern mathematical verification. They help incorporate mathematical
652
      definitions and theorems into a consistent logical framework, such as Higher-Order Logic or De-
653   pendent Type Theory, which their kernels implement. The kernel is vital in the verification process,
654   carefully checking each theorem to ensure it is correctly recognized by the ITP, thus maintaining
655   system integrity. Using an ITP involves expressing the theorem in its programming language and then
656   breaking it down into simpler goals or subgoals. A theorem is proven once it is reduced to known
657   facts. We chose Isabelle for our paper because it has an intuitive interface, supports various logical
658   frameworks, and offers a comprehensive library of formalized mathematics.
659
660   Sledgehammer. Sledgehammer (Paulsson & Blanchette, 2012) is a powerful tool for automating
661   reasoning within the interactive theorem prover Isabelle. It works by translating the goals expressed in
662   Isabelle/HOL’s higher-order logic into other types of logic, such as first-order logic. These translated
663   goals are then sent to automated theorem provers like E, CVC4, Z3, Vampire, and SPASS. If any of
664   these automated provers succeed in finding proof, Sledgehammer reconstructs the proof within the
665   Isabelle/HOL framework using certified provers, such as metis, meson, and smt. This reconstructed
666
      proof, being more understandable to humans, greatly improves the system’s usability and enhances
      the efficiency and effectiveness of interactive theorem proving.

694   techniques that differ significantly from those used in the Isabelle proof assistant, making direct
695   comparisons within the main body of our evaluation less meaningful.
696   Table 4 demonstrates the significant performance variation across Lean-based and Isabelle-based
697   approaches on the miniF2F dataset. Lean-based methods generally operate on a much larger scale,
698   utilizing between 15× and 120× more synthesized proofs than Isabelle-based systems. For example,
699   DeepSeek-Prover-V1.5 employs 9 billion tokens of synthesized data, while our approach uses only
700   38k theorems. Furthermore, these Lean methods typically operate with a search budget that is
701   approximately 12.5× larger than ours. InternLM2-StepProver, for instance, operates with a search
      budget of 64 × 3200, compared to our search budget of 16,384.
