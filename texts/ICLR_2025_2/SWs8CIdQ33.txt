011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) is employed to develop autonomous
014            agents that can learn to adopt cooperative or competitive strategies within complex
015
               environments. However, the linear increase in the number of agents leads to a
               combinatorial explosion of the action space, which may result in algorithmic insta-
016
               bility, difficulty in convergence, or entrapment in local optima. While researchers
017
               have designed a variety of effective algorithms to compress the action space, these
018            methods also introduce new challenges, such as the need for manually designed
019            prior knowledge or reliance on the structure of the problem, which diminishes the
020            applicability of these techniques. In this paper, we introduce Evolutionary action
021            SPAce Reduction with Knowledge (eSpark), an exploration function generation
022            framework driven by large language models (LLMs) to boost exploration and prune
023            unnecessary actions in MARL. Using just a basic prompt that outlines the overall
024            task and setting, eSpark is capable of generating exploration functions in a zero-
025            shot manner, identifying and pruning redundant or irrelevant state-action pairs, and
026
               then achieving autonomous improvement from policy feedback. In reinforcement
               learning tasks involving inventory management and traffic light control encompass-
027
               ing a total of 15 scenarios, eSpark consistently outperforms the combined MARL
028
               algorithm in all scenarios, achieving an average performance gain of 34.4% and
029            9.9% in the two types of tasks respectively. Additionally, eSpark has proven to
030            be capable of managing situations with a large number of agents, securing a 29.7%
031            improvement in scalability challenges that featured over 500 agents. The code can
032            be found in https://anonymous.4open.science/r/0CDH-0DF8/.

035   1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037   Multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) has emerged as a powerful paradigm for solving complex
038   and dynamic problems that involve multiple decision-makers Zhang et al. (2021); Wang et al. (2021).
039
      However, the intricacies of agent interplay and the exponential expansion of state and action spaces
      render the solution of MARL problems difficult. Researchers have proposed the Centralized Training
040
      with Decentralized Execution (CTDE) framework Oliehoek et al. (2008) and parameter sharing
041
      methods, decomposing the value or policy functions of a multi-agent system into individual agents
042   and sharing model parameters among all agents. As experimentally verified by many of the most
043   prominent MARL algorithms such as Multi-agent PPO (MAPPO) Yu et al. (2022), QMIX Rashid
044   et al. (2020), QPLEX Wang et al. (2021) or QTRAN Son et al. (2019), these methodologies have been
045   demonstrated to be robust strategies for surmounting the challenges posed by MARL. MARL methods
046   based on parameter sharing and CTDE have achieved notable success in a variety of well-established
047   tasks, including StarCraft Multi-Agent Challenge (SMAC) Li et al. (2023); Wang et al. (2020), the
048   Multi-Agent Particle Environment (MPE) Lowe et al. (2017), and Simulation of Urban MObility
049   (SUMO) Wei et al. (2019); Lu et al. (2023).
050   Despite the great success of parameter-sharing CTDE methods, their practicality dwindles in real-
051   world tasks involving large number of agents, such as large-scale traffic signal control Mousavi
052   et al. (2017), wireless communication networks Zocca (2019), and humanitarian assistance and
053   disaster response Meier (2015), where centralized training becomes impractical due to large problem
      scale Munir et al. (2021). Fully Decentralized Training and Execution (DTDE) methods, such as


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      Independent PPO (IPPO) de Witt et al. (2020), offer a scalable solution where resource consumption
055   does not escalate drastically with an increase in the number of agents. However, due to the lack
056   of consideration for agent interactions, they often struggle to find optimal solutions and fall into
057   local optima. Current strategies for addressing large-scale MARL tasks involve introducing task-
058   specific structures to model agent interactions or dividing agents into smaller, independently trained
059   groups Ying et al. (2023); Chen et al. (2020). These methods, however, are constrained by their
060   dependence on task-related structuring, limiting their applicability to a narrow range of problems.
061   Additionally, the "curse of dimensionality" presents a significant challenge in multi-agent systems Hao
062   et al. (2022b;a), where agents are required to navigate through an expansive action space saturated with
063   numerous actions that are either irrelevant or markedly suboptimal (relative to states). While humans
064   can deftly employ contextual cues and prior knowledge to sidestep such challenges, MARL algorithms
065   typically engage in the exploration of superfluous and extraneous suboptimal actions Zahavy et al.
066   (2018). Besides, prevailing parameter sharing can exacerbate this exploratory dilemma, as will be
067   elucidated in Proposition 2. The issue occurs primarily because agents with shared parameters often
068   prefer suboptimal policies that present short-term advantages, rather than exploring policies that may
069
      potentially deliver higher long-term returns.
070   Exploration is crucial for overcoming local optima, as it encourages agents to discover potentially
071   better states thus refining their policies. While single-agent exploration techniques like the Upper
072   Confidence Bound (UCB) Auer (2002), entropy regularization Haarnoja et al. (2018), and curiosity-
073   based exploration Groth et al. (2021); Pathak et al. (2017) have shown promising results, they struggle
074   with the escalated complexity in MARL scenarios, compounded by issues like deceptive rewards
075
      and the "Noisy-TV" problem Burda et al. (2018). Integrating domain knowledge into exploration
      could significantly enhance exploration efficiency, by helping identify critical elements and problem
076
      structures, thereby aiding in the selection of optimal actions Simon (1956). However, the integration
077
      of this knowledge within a data-driven framework poses significant challenges, particularly when
078   manual input from domain experts is required, thus reducing its practicality.
079
080   Recently, Large Language Models (LLMs) such as GPT-4 Achiam et al. (2023) have shown formidable
081
      skills in language comprehension, strategic planning, and logical reasoning across various tasks Yao
      et al. (2023); Zhu et al. (2023). Although not always directly solving complex, dynamic problems,
082
      their inferential and error-learning abilities facilitate progressively better solutions through iterative
083
      feedback Ma et al. (2024). The integration of LLMs with MARL presents a promising new avenue
084   by facilitating exploration through the pruning of redundant actions. In this paper, we introduce
085   Evolutionary action SPAce Reduction with Knowledge (eSpark), a novel approach that utilizes
086   LLMs to improve MARL training via optimized exploration functions, which are used to prune the
087   action space. eSpark begins by using LLMs to generate exploration functions from task descriptions
088   and environmental rules in a zero-shot fashion. It then applies evolutionary search within MARL to
089   pinpoint the best performing policy. Finally, by analyzing the feedback on the performance of this
090   policy, eSpark reflects and proposes a set of new exploration functions, and iteratively optimizes
091   them according to the aforementioned steps. This process enhances the MARL policy by continuously
092
      adapting and refining exploration. To summarize, our contributions are as follows:
093
            1. We introduce the eSpark framework, which harnesses the intrinsic prior knowledge and
094            encoding capability of LLMs to design exploration functions for action space pruning, thus
095            guiding the exploration and learning process of MARL algorithms. eSpark requires no
096            complex prompt engineering and can be easily combined with MARL algorithms.
097
            2. We validate the performance of eSpark across 15 different environments within the
098            inventory management task MABIM Yang et al. (2023) and the traffic signal control task
099            SUMO Behrisch et al. (2011). Combined with IPPO, eSpark outperforms IPPO in all
100            scenarios, realizing an average profit increase of 34.4% in the MABIM and improving
101            multiple metrics in SUMO by an average of 9.9%. Even in the face of scalability challenges
102            where the DTDE methods typically encounter limitations, eSpark elevates the performance
103            of IPPO by 29.7%.
104         3. We conduct controlled experiments and ablation studies to analyze the effectiveness of each
105            component within the eSpark framework. We first validate the advantages of knowledge-
106            based pruning. Subsequently, we conduct ablation studies to demonstrate that both retention
107            training and LLM pruning techniques contribute to the performance of eSpark. These
               effects are even more pronounced in the more complex MABIM environment.

      2     R ELATED WORKS
109
110
      LLMs for code generation. LLMs have exhibited formidable capabilities in the domain of code
111   generation Roziere et al. (2023); Nejjar et al. (2023). More recently, LLM-based approaches to self-
112   improving code generation have been applied to address challenges in combinatorial optimization Liu
113   et al. (2024); Ye et al. (2024), robotic tasks Liang et al. (2023); Wang et al. (2024), reinforcement
114   learning (RL) reward design Ma et al. (2024), and code optimization Romera-Paredes et al. (2024).
115   For the first time, to the best of our knowledge, we propose the use of LLMs to generate code with
116   the aim of pruning the redundant action space in MARL environments, and through a process of
117   autonomous reflection and evolution, iteratively enhancing the quality of the generated output.
118   LLMs for RL and MARL. The integration of LLMs into RL and MARL has sparked considerable
119   research interest Sharma et al. (2022); Kwon et al. (2023); Li et al. (2024). Some works Hill et al.
120   (2020); Chan et al. (2019) incorporate the goal descriptions of language models that help in enhancing
121   the generalization capabilities of agents designed to follow instructions. Further studies have extended
122   this approach to complex tasks involving reasoning and planning Huang et al. (2023; 2022). Moreover,
123   LLMs have been employed to guide exploration and boost RL efficiency Du et al. (2023); Chang et al.
124   (2023); Hu & Sadigh (2023). However, scaling to high-complexity, real-time, multi-agent settings
125
      remains a challenge. Our method mitigates this by generating exploration functions to navigate the
      policy space, thus facilitating the application to complex MARL scenarios without direct LLM-agent
126
      decision-making interaction.
127
128   Action space pruning in RL and MARL. Pruning the action space has been shown to be effective
129   in guiding agent behaviors in complex environments Lipton et al. (2016); Fulda et al. (2017).
130   Techniques include learning an elimination signal to discard unnecessary actions Zahavy et al. (2018),
131
      and employing transfer learning that pre-trains agents to isolate useful experiences for later action
      refinement Shirali et al. (2023); Lan et al. (2022); Ammanabrolu & Riedl (2018). Some approaches
132
      use manually designed data structures based on prior knowledge to filter actions Dulac-Arnold et al.
133
      (2015); Padullaparthi et al. (2022); Nagarathinam et al. (2020). However, training pruning signals or
134   applying transfer learning is inherently difficult with many agents, and the need for expert knowledge
135   in manual pruning rules hampers their transferability, limiting the applicability of current methods.
136   We harness the abundant knowledge embedded within LLMs for action space pruning, demonstrating
137   universal applicability across a multitude of scenarios.

      4.2   E VOLUTIONARY SEARCH
279
280   During the generation, however, it should be noted that the initially generated exploration function
281   may not always guarantee executability and effectiveness. To address this, eSpark performs an
282   evolutionary search paradigm that selects the best-performing policy in one iteration and uses its
283   feedback for subsequent generation Ma et al. (2024). Specifically, eSpark samples a batch of K
284   exploration functions in each generation to ensure there are enough candidates for successful code
285   execution. Performance is assessed at regular checkpoints within an iteration, with the final evaluation
286   based on the average of the last few checkpoints. The policy achieving the highest performance
287   is selected, and the feedback obtained from this policy is integrated to optimize the exploration
288
      functions in the following steps.
289   Due to the dynamic nature of exploration, the exploration function generated based on feedback from
290   the best-performing policy may not be applicable to other policies. As the proof of Proposition 1
291   demonstrates, when an exploration function is incapable of intelligently pruning, it may even impair
292   the performance of the policy. To this end, we utilize retention training to maintain continuity of
293   exploration. Let ϕi−1
                          best represent best-performing policy from the (i − 1)-th iteration. For the i-th
294   iteration except for the first, at the start of the iteration, we set:

298   performance incrementally. We will verify the impact of retention training in Section 5.4.

518   6     C ONCLUSIONS , LIMITATIONS AND FUTURE WORK
519
520
      We present eSpark, a novel framework for generating exploration functions, leveraging the advanced
521
      capabilities of LLMs to integrate prior knowledge, generate code and reflect, thereby refining the
522   exploration in MARL. eSpark has surpassed its base MARL algorithm across all scenarios in
523   both MABIM and SUMO environments. In terms of pruning strategies, pruning based on the prior
524   knowledge from LLMs outshines both random and heuristic approaches. Ablation experiments
525   demonstrate the indispensable role of retention training in accurately improving exploration functions
526   based on policy flaws and enhancing sample efficiency. The LLM checker and detailed policy
527   feedback prompt design together ensure the superior performance of eSpark.
528   Nevertheless, eSpark also has certain limitations. First, currently eSpark is only applicable to
529   tasks involving homogeneous agents. For heterogeneous agents, a potential method could be to
530   generate distinct exploration functions for each agent; however, this approach becomes impractical
531   when the number of agents is too large. Moreover, eSpark benefits from policy feedback to refine
532   the exploration functions. When feedback is not informative regarding how to modify the exploration
533   (e.g., in tasks with sparse rewards, end-of-episode feedback alone is too limited to develop automated
534   feedback), eSpark may struggle to improve and need extra expert input for effective reflection.
535   Future work encompasses numerous potential directions. Existing research advocates for assigning
536   different roles or categories to agents Christianos et al. (2021); Wang et al. (2020), which could offer
537   a compromise for the application of eSpark in heterogeneous multi-agent systems. Furthermore,
538   state-specific feedback for more granular improvement represents an intriguing avenue Subramanian
539   et al. (2016). Our future endeavors will investigate these questions, striving to develop algorithms
      that are robust and exhibit strong generalizability.

       D.1.2    (S, s) ALGORITHM
1114
1115   The (S, s) inventory policy serves as a robust framework for managing stock levels. Under this
1116   policy, a restocking order is triggered once the inventory count falls below a predefined threshold,
1117   identified as s. The objective of this replenishment is to elevate the stock to its upper limit, designated
1118   as S. Empirical analyses have substantiated the efficacy of this protocol in optimizing inventory
1119   control processes. As a result, it is adopted as a benchmark heuristic, with the aim of algorithmically
1120
       ascertaining the most efficacious (S, s) parameter for each discrete SKU in the given inventory
       dataset. In our implementation, we conduct a search on the training set to identify the optimal values
1121
       of s and S, after which we apply these values consistently to the test set.

       D.1.3    F IXED - TIME CONTROL ALGORITHM
1124
1125   The Fixed-Time Control (FTC) algorithm is a traditional traffic signal control strategy predicated on
1126   predefined signal plans. These plans are typically designed based on historical traffic flow patterns
1127   and do not adapt to real-time traffic conditions. The FTC operates on a static schedule where the
1128   signal phases at intersections change at fixed intervals. This approach is straightforward and easy to
1129   implement but may not be optimal under variable traffic conditions due to its lack of responsiveness
1130
       to dynamic traffic demands.
1131   In our implementation, the FTC follows a fixed sequence of signal phases: ’WT-ET’, ’NT-ST’,
1132   ’WL-EL’, ’NL-SL’, ’WL-WT’, ’EL-ET’, ’SL-ST’, ’NL-NT’. Here, ’W’, ’E’, ’N’, and ’S’ denote
1133   westbound, eastbound, northbound, and southbound traffic, respectively, while ’T’ indicates through
       movement, and ’L’ signifies a left turn. Each phase has a duration of 30 seconds

       D.1.4     M AX P RESSURE ALGORITHM
1135
1136   The MaxPressure algorithm represents a more advanced traffic signal control strategy that dynamically
1137   adjusts signal phases in response to real-time traffic conditions. It calculates the "pressure" at each
1138   intersection, defined as the difference between the number of vehicles on the incoming and outgoing
1139   lanes. The algorithm aims to optimize traffic flow by selecting signal phases that reduce the maximum
1140
       pressure across the network, thus alleviating congestion and enhancing network throughput. Unlike
       FTC, MaxPressure is adaptive and can continuously optimize signal timing based on the current
1141
       traffic state, making it more suitable for managing fluctuating traffic volumes.

       D.3.1     R ANDOM PRUNING
1168   Random pruning is implemented by randomly masking a certain percentage of the available actions.
1169   During the action selection process, each agent will have p percent of its available actions randomly
1170   masked. To balance the observability of the pruning’s impact with the preservation of the algorithm’s
1171   capacity to utilize prior experience, we set p = 0.3.
1172
1173   D.3.2     (S, s) PRUNING
1174
       According to the (S, s) algorithm, for a given SKU, a replenishment quantity of ∆ = S − s is
1175
       ordered when the current inventory level falls below the threshold s; otherwise, no order is placed.
1176
       We extend the reference replenishment quantity ∆ to a range [r1 × ∆, r2 × ∆], where r1 , r2 are both
1177   real numbers and 0 ≤ r1 ≤ 1 and r2 ≥ 1. Actions within this interval are deemed available, while
1178   those outside of this range are masked. In our implementation, we select r1 = 0.5 and r2 = 2.
1179
1180   D.3.3     M AX P RESSURE PRUNING
1181
1182   The MaxPressure pruning method utilizes the heuristic concept of "pressure" at an intersection to
1183   prune actions. We calculate the pressure associated with each action, and these pressures are then
1184
       ranked. The actions with the top-k highest pressures are rendered available for selection. Actions not
       meeting this threshold are subsequently masked.
1185
1186   A standardized intersection warped through the GESA is modeled as a four-arm intersection compris-
1187   ing eight potential actions. We empirically set k = 4 to ensure effective pruning while maintaining a
       sufficient number of available actions.
