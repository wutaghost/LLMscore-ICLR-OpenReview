011                                                 A BSTRACT
012
013             Recently, many studies have demonstrated that exclusively incorporating OCR-
014             derived text and spatial layouts with large language models (LLMs) can be highly
015             effective for document understanding tasks. However, existing methods that in-
016
                tegrate spatial layouts with text have limitations, such as producing overly long
                text sequences or failing to fully leverage the autoregressive traits of LLMs. In
017
                this work, we introduce Interleaving Layout and Text in a Large Language Model
018
                (LayTextLLM) for document understanding. In particular, LayTextLLM projects
019             each bounding box to a single embedding and interleaves it with text, efficiently
020             avoiding long sequence issues while leveraging autoregressive traits of LLMs.
021             LayTextLLM not only streamlines the interaction of layout and textual data but
022             also shows enhanced performance in Key Information Extraction (KIE) and Visual
023             Question Answering (VQA). Comprehensive benchmark evaluations reveal signifi-
024             cant improvements, with a 27.2% increase on KIE tasks and 12.0% on VQA tasks
025             compared to previous state-of-the-art document understanding MLLMs, as well as
026             a 15.1% improvement over other SOTA OCR-based LLMs on KIE tasks.

035

036   a text-sensitive MultiModal Large Language Models (MLLMs) based on existing LLMs, particu-
037   larly aimed at enhancing Visually Rich Document Understanding (VRDU), have made significant
038   progress (Ye et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024c; Bai et al., 2023). Although existing MLLMs show
039   promising results in document understanding, they often encounter challenges related to image
040   resolution. When the input image is of low resolution, it is too blurry to extract visual features
041   effectively. Conversely, high-resolution images require additional computational resources to capture
042   detailed textual information (Liu et al., 2024c).
043    Concurrently, another line of research employs off-the-shelf OCR tools to extract text and spatial
044    layouts, which are then combined with LLMs to address VRDU tasks. These approaches assume that
045    most valuable information for document comprehension can be derived from the text and its spatial
046    layouts, viewing spatial layouts as “lightweight visual information” (Wang et al., 2023). Following
047    this premise, several studies (Liu et al., 2024c; Perot et al., 2023; Luo et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2023a;
048    He et al., 2023) have explored various approaches that integrate spatial layouts with text for LLMs,
049    achieving results that are competitive with, or even surpass, those of MLLMs.
050    The most natural method to incorporate layout information is by treating spatial layouts as tokens,
051    which allows for the seamless interleaving of text and layout into a unified text sequence (Perot et al.,
052    2023; Chen et al., 2023a; He et al., 2023). For example, Perot et al. (2023) employ format such as
053   “HARRISBURG 78|09” to represent OCR text and corresponding layout, where “HARRISBURG” is
       OCR text and “78|09” indicates the mean of the horizontal and vertical coordinates, respectively.

      2.1   OCR- BASED LLM S FOR D OCUMENT U NDERSTANDING
111
112   Early document understanding methods (Hwang et al., 2020; Xu et al., 2020; 2021; Hong et al.,
113   2022; Tang et al., 2022) tend to solve the task in a two-stage manner, i.e., first reading texts from
114   input document images using off-the-shelf OCR engines and then understanding the extracted texts.
115   Considering the advantages of LLMs (e.g., high generalizability), some recent methods endeavor to
116   combine LLMs with OCR-derived results to solve document understanding. For example, inspired
117   by the “coordinate-as-tokens” scheme proposed in ICL-D3IE (Perot et al., 2023), He et al. (2023)
118
      propose to use “[x_min, y_min, x_max, y_max]” to introduce the layout information, which can
      fuse the layout information and texts into a unified text sequence and fully exploit the autoregressive
119
      merit of LLMs. To reinforce the layout information while avoiding increasing the number of tokens,
120
      DocLLM (Wang et al., 2023) designs a disentangled spatial attention mechanism to capture cross-
121   alignment between text and layout modalities. Recently, LayoutLLM (Luo et al., 2024) utilizes
122   the pre-trained layout-aware model (Huang et al., 2022), to insert the visual information, layout
123   information and text information. However, the aforementioned methods neither suffer from the
124   computational overhead leading by the increasing tokens or hardly take advantage of autoregressive
125   characteristics of LLMs. Thus, it is an urgent problem to address how to better incorporate layout
126   information without significantly increasing the number of tokens.
127
128   2.2   OCR- FREE MLLM S FOR D OCUMENT U NDERSTANDING
129
130   Another approach to solve document understanding tasks is the OCR-free method. Benefiting from
131
      the end-to-end training framework, it involves processing the text content of documents directly,
      without relying on OCR engines. Donut (Kim et al., 2022) first presents an OCR-free method through
132
      mapping a text-rich document image into the desired answers. Pix2Struct (Lee et al., 2023) is trained
133
      to parse masked screenshots of web pages into simplified HTML, where variable resolution inputs
134   are supported. While these approaches eliminate the need for OCR tools, they still necessitate
135   task-specific fine-tuning. With the increasing popularity of LLMs/MLLMs (Feng et al., 2023b; Hu
136   et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024c; Tang et al., 2024a; Chen et al., 2024; Dong et al., 2024; Li et al.,
137   2024; Liu et al., 2024a), various methods are proposed to solve the document understanding task
138   through explicitly training models on visual text understanding datasets and fine-tuning them with
139   instructions to perform a zero-shot prediction. LLaVAR (Zhang et al., 2023) and UniDoc (Feng
140   et al., 2023b) are notable examples that expand upon the document-oriented VQA capabilities of
141   LLaVA (Liu et al., 2024b) by incorporating document-based tasks. These models pioneer the use of
142
      MLLMs for predicting texts and coordinates from document images, enabling the development of
      OCR-free document understanding methods. Additionally, DocPedia (Feng et al., 2023a) operates
143
      document images in the frequency domain, allowing for higher input resolution without increasing
144
      the input sequence length. Recent advancements in this field, including mPLUG-DocOwl (Ye et al.,
145   2023), Qwen-VL (Bai et al., 2023), and TextMonkey (Liu et al., 2024c), leverage publicly available
146   document-related VQA datasets to further enhance the document understanding capability. Although
147   these OCR-free methods have exhibited their advantages, they still struggle with the high-resolution
148   input to reserve more text-related details.

153

154   parameter overhead, we apply Partial Low-Rank Adaptation (Sec. 3.1.2). We also introduce two
155   training tasks: Layout-aware Next Token Prediction (Sec. 3.2.1) to align layouts with text during
156   pre-training, and Shuffled-OCR Supervised Fine-tuning (Sec. 3.2.2) to enhance the generalizability
157   of the model. An illustration of our approach is shown in Fig. 2.

184   3.1.1   S PATIAL L AYOUT P ROJECTOR (SLP)
185
186   A key innovation in LayTextLLM is the Spatial Layout Projector (SLP), which transforms a spatial
187   layout into a singular bounding box token. This enhancement enables the model to process both
188   spatial layouts and textual inputs simultaneously. Each OCR-derived spatial layout is represented by
189
      a bounding box defined by four-dimensional coordinates [x1 , y1 , x2 , y2 ], these coordinates represent
      the normalized minimum and maximum horizontal and vertical extents of the box, respectively. The
190
      SLP maps these coordinates into a high-dimensional space that the language model can process
191
      as a single token. The process can be computed as z = W · c + b, where c ∈ R4 is the vector of
192   the bounding box coordinates. W ∈ Rd×4 is a weight matrix with d represents the dimension of
193   the embedding, b ∈ Rd×1 is a bias vector, z is the resulting bounding box token represented as
194   an d-dimensional embedding. As illustrated in Fig. 2, the resulting bounding box token z will be
195   interleaved with corresponding textual embeddings to put into LLMs. Note that the SLP is shared by
196   all bounding box tokens so very limited number of parameters are introduced.
197

      3.1.2   L AYOUT PARTIAL L OW-R ANK A DAPTATION
199
200   After using the SLP to generate bounding box tokens and a tokenizer to produce text tokens, these
201   two modalities are then interacted using a Layout Partial Low-Rank Adaptation (P-LoRA) module
202   in LLMs. P-LoRA, introduced in InternLM-XComposer2 (Dong et al., 2024), is originally used to
203   adapt LLMs to the visual modality. It applies plug-in low-rank modules specified to the visual tokens,
204   which adds minimal parameters while preserving the LLMs inherent knowledge.
205   Formally, for a linear layer in the LLM, the original weights WO ∈ RCout ×Cin and bias BO ∈
206   RCout are specified for input and output dimensions Cin and Cout . P-LoRA modifies this setup by
207   incorporating two additional matrices, WA ∈ RCr ×Cin and WB ∈ RCout ×Cr . These matrices are
208   lower-rank, with Cr being considerably smaller than both Cin and Cout , and are specifically designed
209   to interact with new modality tokens, which in our case are bounding box tokens. For example, given
210   an input x = [xb , xt ] comprising of bounding box tokens (xb ) and textual tokens (xt ) is fed into the
211   system, the forward process is as follows, where x̂t , x̂b and x̂ are outputs:

229
      LayTextLLM is trained with innovative layout-aware training procedure, which consists of two stages:
230
      Layout-aware Next Token Prediction pre-training and Shuffled-OCR Supervised Fine-tuning.

      3.2.1     L AYOUT- AWARE N EXT T OKEN P REDICTION
233
234   Inspired by the next token prediction commonly used in current LLM pre-training (Achiam et al.,
235   2023; Yang et al., 2023; Team et al., 2023; Anthropic, 2024; Reid et al., 2024; Bai et al., 2023;
236   Lu et al., 2024a), we propose the Layout-aware Next Token Prediction (LNTP). Fig. 3 presents
237   the contrast of the proposed Layout-aware Next Token Prediction and the conventional next token
238   prediction task. The traditional next token prediction (Fig. 3(a)) relies solely on the textual content,
239
      predicting each subsequent token based on the prior sequence of tokens without considering their
      spatial layouts. Layout-aware next token prediction (Fig. 3(b)), however, interleaves the spatial
240
      information encoded by SLP (i.e., bi ) with the text tokens (i.e., ti ). This integration considers both
241
      the content and its layout within the document, leading to a richer, more precise understanding of
242   both the structure and the content.
243
244   Similarly, primary objective of LNTP is to maximize the likelihood of its predictions for the next
245
      token. Thus the loss function is defined as

269   by designs such as Rotary Position Embeddings (RoPE) (Su et al., 2024). Specifically, tokens
      that are close together in the input sequence are likely to receive higher attention scores, which is

313   4.2    I MPLEMENTATION D ETAIL
314
315   The LLM component of LayTextLLM is initialized from the Llama2-7B-base (Touvron et al., 2023),
316   which is a widely-used backbone. Other parameters including SLP and P-LoRA are randomly
317   initialized. During pre-training, the LLM is frozen, and the parameters of SLP and P-LoRA modules
318   are updated. During SFT, all parameters are fine-tuned. Detailed setup can be found in Appendix B.
319   We implemented LayTexLLM using Llama2-7B, consistent with previous OCR-based methods like
320   DocLLM (Wang et al., 2023), which also use Llama2-7B. We also replicated the results of the
321   coor-as-tokens scheme using Llama2-7B for consistency. Noting the LayoutLLM (Luo et al., 2024)
322   utilizes Llama2-7B and Vicuna 1.5 7B, which is fine-tuned from Llama2-7B. Thus, for the majority
323   of our comparisons, the models are based on the same or similar LLM backbones, allowing for a fair
      comparison between approaches.

      (DDD) and Layout-aware SFT data. Our first version, LayTextLLMzero , follows this zero-shot

342
343   OCR-free baselines In the category of OCR-free MLLMs, we have chosen the following SOTA
344   models as our strong baselines due to their superior performance in both document-oriented VQA
345   and KIE tasks. These include UniDoc (Feng et al., 2023b), DocPedia (Feng et al., 2023a), Mon-
346   key (Li et al., 2023), InternVL (Chen et al., 2023b), InternLM-XComposer2 (Dong et al., 2024),
347
      TextMonkey, and TextMonkey+ (Liu et al., 2024c).
348   OCR-based baselines For OCR-based baseline models, we implemented a basic approach using
349   only OCR-derived text as input. This was done using two versions: Llama2-7B-base and Llama2-
350   7B-chat. We also adapted the coordinate-as-tokens scheme from He et al. (2023) for these models,
351   resulting in two new variants: Llama2-7B-basecoor and Llama2-7B-chatcoor . It is important to note
352   that we did not employ the ICL strategy with these models, as it would significantly exceed their

483   symbol) is heavily focusing on layout information, as seen by the significant attention on the bounding
484   box embedding “<unk>” token before “(Qty”. This highlights the model’s effort to orient itself
485   spatially and understand the structural context of the tokens. At this stage, the model is developing a
      cognitive understanding of how the elements are laid out on the page. We extract and visualize the

512   LayTextLLM adheres to an auto-regressive approach, focusing its attention solely on preceding
513   information. Furthermore, interleaving bounding box and text embeddings creates strong attention
514   connections between textual and spatial representations, as shown in Fig.5. In contrast, DocLLM
515   integrates spatial information into the calculation of attention score which is implicitly. As shown
516

517
      decoded back into coordinates even without inputing visual information, as discussed in Appendix F.
518
519   Sequence Length Tab. 5 presents statistics on the average input sequence length across differ-
520   ent datasets. Intriguingly, despite interleaving bounding box tokens, LayTextLLM consistently
521   exhibits the shortest sequence length in three out of four datasets, even surpassing DocLLM,
522
      which is counterintuitive. We attribute this to the tokenizer mechanism. For example, using
      tokenizer.encode(), a single word from the OCR engine, like “International” is encoded
523
      into a single ID [4623]. Conversely, when the entire OCR output is processed as one sequence,
524
      such as “... CPC,International,Inc...”, the word “International” is split into two IDs [17579, 1288],
525   corresponding to “Intern” and “ational” respectively. This type of case occurs frequently, we provide
526   further discussion in Appendix C.

854
855    BPE operates by building a vocabulary of commonly occurring subwords (or token pieces) derived
856    from the training data. Initially, it tokenizes the text at the character level and then progressively
857    merges the most frequent adjacent pairs of characters or sequences. The objective is to strike a
858    balance between minimizing vocabulary size and maximizing encoding efficiency.
859    Thus, when tokenizing a single word like “International” on its own, the tokenizer might identify it
860    as a common sequence in the training data and encode it as a single token. This is especially likely if
861   “International” frequently appears as a standalone word in the training contexts. However, when the
862    word “International” is part of a larger sequence of words such as including in a long sequence of
863    OCR-derived texts like “...335 CPC,International,Inc...”, the context changes. The tokenizer might
       split “International” into sub-tokens like “Intern” and “ational” because, in various contexts within


                                                                     16
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


864
      the training data, these subwords might appear more frequently in different combinations or are more
865   useful for the model to understand variations in meaning or syntax.
866
867   When using LayTextLLM, we input word-level OCR results into the tokenizer, typically resulting in
868
      the former situation, where words are encoded as single tokens. Conversely, with DocLLM, the entire
      OCR output is processed as one large sequence, leading to the latter situation and a longer sequence
869
      length than in LayTextLLM. This difference underscores the utility of LayTextLLM in achieving
870
      both accuracy and inference efficiency due to its shorter sequence length.
