009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Self-play methods have achieved remarkable success in two-player zero-sum
012            games, attaining superhuman performance in many complex game domains. Par-
013            allelizing learners is a feasible approach to handling large-scale games. However,
014            parallelizing learners often leads to suboptimal exploitation of computational re-
015            sources, resulting in inefficiencies. In this study, we introduce the Mixed Hierar-
016            chical Oracle (MHO), designed to enhance computational efficiency and perfor-
017            mance in large-scale two-player zero-sum games. MHO enables the paralleliza-
018
               tion of reinforcement learning tasks through a hierarchical pipeline that balances
               exploration and exploitation across oracle levels. It also avoids cold-start issues by
019
               using a ”model soup” initialization strategy. Additionally, we present MiniStar, an
020
               open-source environment focused on small-scale combat scenarios, developed to
021            facilitate research in self-play algorithms. Through extensive experiments on ma-
022            trix games and the MiniStar environment, we demonstrate that MHO outperforms
023            existing methods in terms of computational efficiency and performance.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Two-player zero-sum games, often modeled as competitive interactions in sports and e-sports,
029   present a rich area of study Ye et al. (2020a); Silver et al. (2017); Vinyals et al. (2019); Peng et al.
030   (2017); Ye et al. (2020b). Advancements in artificial intelligence and self-play algorithms have
031   led to superhuman performances in complex tasks across various games, including MOBA games
032   Berner et al. (2019); Ye et al. (2020a;b); Wei et al. (2022), Go Silver et al. (2017), and StarCraft
033
      Peng et al. (2017); Vinyals et al. (2019). Despite the potential for a wide range of applications,
      self-play algorithms have limitations such as possible convergence to suboptimal strategies and high
034
      computational resource demands Yang et al. (2021).
035
036   Traditional self-play algorithms McMahan et al. (2003); Heinrich & Silver (2016); Hernandez et al.
037   (2019) improve an intelligent agent’s strategy by having it repeatedly play against itself, allowing
038   the agent to explore a variety of strategies and enhance its decision-making capabilities without ex-
039
      ternal training data. However, in complex game environments, these methods may struggle to find
      optimal strategies due to the vastness of the strategy space. To address this challenge, the Policy
040
      Space Response Oracle (PSRO) algorithm Lanctot et al. (2017) extends the Double Oracle (DO)
041
      algorithm McMahan et al. (2003) to large-scale games by using reinforcement learning (RL) to ap-
042   proximate best responses. Empirical Game-Theoretic Analysis (EGTA) Wellman (2006); Wellman
043   et al. (2024) is a framework for studying meta-strategies obtained through simulation in complex
044   games. PSRO combines EGTA with RL and introduces a Meta-Strategy Solver (MSS) to assist
045   in selecting adversarial strategies, thereby improving strategy selection in self-play and ensuring
046   convergence towards an approximate Nash equilibrium.
047
      To effectively handle large-scale game scenarios, parallelization methods are often employed to
048   solve best-response problems Lanctot et al. (2017); McAleer et al. (2020). Deep Cognitive Hierar-
049   chies (DCH) Lanctot et al. (2017) and Pipeline PSRO (P2SRO) McAleer et al. (2020) exploit the
050   iterative nature of the Policy Space Response Oracle (PSRO) algorithm to parallelize oracle com-
051   putations. However, their approach to oracle parallelism allocates computational resources almost
052   equally among all oracles, which can lead to suboptimal computational efficiency. Moreover, in
053   P2SRO, when active policies generate data by playing against each other, the data produced by
      lower-level active policies is not utilized for training, resulting in data inefficiency. Additionally,


                                                         1

054
      PSRO relies on EGTA to compute the meta-game, which incurs higher computational overhead as
055   the number of training iterations increases and the environment becomes more complex.
056
057   To address these issues, we introduce the Mixed Hierarchical Oracle (MHO), an abstraction and im-
058
      provement based on the P2SRO method. MHO removes the dependence on EGTA to reduce extra
      overhead during training and improve training efficiency. MHO incorporates three key approaches:
059
      parallelizing oracles at different levels to enlarge the solver’s capacity; utilizing samples generated
060
      when high-level oracles compete against low-level oracles for training the corresponding oracles;
061   and initializing newly added oracles using a ”model soup” strategy to avoid cold starts when com-
062   peting against low-level policier. Different levels of oracles have distinct exploration factors, with
063   high-level policies favoring exploration and low-level policies favoring exploitation. Since MHO is
064   an abstraction of P2SRO, it can also be applied to PSRO to improve its performance.
065
      We also introduce a mini-environment called MiniStar, developed from StarCraft II (SC2)Vinyals
066
      et al. (2019) and SMACv2 Ellis et al. (2024), tailored to focus on small-scale combat scenarios and
067   self-play research. By narrowing the scope to tactical engagements rather than full-game strategies,
068   we reduce the complexity associated with long-time-series decision-making. This simplification
069   allows us to concentrate on the core aspects of self-play algorithms without the prerequisite of ex-
070   tensive reinforcement learning optimization.
071
      To summarize, in this paper we provide the following contributions:
072
073          • We propose MHO, a self-play algorithm that enhances computational efficiency and per-
074            formance in large-scale gaming scenarios by avoiding cold starts and balancing exploration
075            and exploitation across oracle levels.
076          • We introduce MiniStar, small-scale combat scenarios specifically designed for self-play
077            research.
078          • We demonstrate the effectiveness of MHO through extensive experiments on matrix games
079            and the MiniStar environment.

      2   R ELATED WORKS

      Self-play Methods In self-play methods, agents are trained by repeatedly playing against their latest
084
      versions. Fictitious Self-Play (FSP) Heinrich et al. (2015) enables agents to play against their past
085   selves to learn optimal strategies. Neural Fictitious Self-Play Heinrich & Silver (2016) is a modern
086   variant that combines FSP with deep learning techniques, using neural networks to approximate the
087   best response (BR). Preferred Fictitious Self-Play (PFSP) Vinyals et al. (2019) utilizes a preference
088   function to assign higher selection probabilities to higher-priority agents. The Double Oracle (DO)
089   McMahan et al. (2003) method approximates Nash Equilibria in large-scale zero-sum games by
090   iteratively creating and solving a series of sub-games with a restricted set of pure strategies. Policy
091   Space Response Oracle (PSRO) Lanctot et al. (2017) is a generalization of DO, using reinforcement
092   learning as an oracle to enable decision-making in complex gaming environments. It introduces the
093
      concept of a Meta-Strategy Solver (MSS) to assist in the selection of adversarial strategies, which
      guarantees convergence to an approximate Nash equilibrium. Pipeline PSRO (P2SRO) McAleer
094
      et al. (2020) realizes the parallelization of PSRO while simultaneously guaranteeing convergence.
095
      Online Double Oracle (ODO) Dinh et al. (2022) combines no-regret analysis from online learning
096   with the DO approach to improve the speed of convergence to a Nash equilibrium and the average
097   payoff. Anytime PSRO McAleer et al. (2022b) and Self-Play PSRO McAleer et al. (2022a) aim to
098   incorporate more difficult-to-exploit strategies into the strategy population, thus facilitating faster
099   convergence. These variants further enhance the performance and applicability of the self-play
100   method by introducing new strategy evaluation mechanisms and optimizing the strategy selection
101   process.
102   Simulation Environment Compared to traditional board and card games, simulation environments
103   are typically characterized by real-time operations, long periods, and higher complexity of environ-
104   mental state transitions, such as StarCraft II (SC2) Vinyals et al. (2019), Google Research Football
105   (GRF) Kurach et al. (2020), and Multiplayer Online Battle Arena (MOBA) games like Dota 2 Berner
106   et al. (2019) and Honor of Kings Ye et al. (2020a;b); Wei et al. (2022). These environments present
107   agents with real-time, partially observable settings requiring continuous decision-making over ex-
      tended time horizons. Agents must handle large, continuous action spaces and deal with uncertain-

      ties introduced by dynamic opponents and environments. The complexity and high dimensionality
109   of these environments necessitate extensive reinforcement learning and engineering optimization
110   before effective self-play can be conducted. For instance, AlphaStar Vinyals et al. (2019) combines

112   vast computational resources. OpenAI Five Berner et al. (2019) demonstrated that self-play could

118
      work of pre-training and model optimization to address training difficulties caused by cold starts
119
      before proceeding to self-play.

202
203   In this section, we introduce the Mixed Hierarchical Oracle (MHO) methods that significantly im-
204
      prove the training and performance of reinforcement learning agents in self-play algorithms, es-
      pecially in complex environments. MHO comprises three key components: Parallelized Oracle,
205
      Model Souping, and Hierarchical Exploration. These components aim to address the computational
206
      efficiency of the policy learning process from different perspectives—sample utilization, cold-start
207   issues, and exploration mechanisms—and are ultimately integrated to work synergistically. The
208   detailed framework is illustrated in Figure 1.

      4.2   M ODEL S OUPING
239
240   At the model initialization stage of each new training round, if the model is initialized from scratch,
241   agents may struggle to learn effective policies in the later stages of self-play as opponents become
242   stronger. To avoid this cold-start problem, we employ a model fusion method, known as ”model
243   souping,” to achieve a warm start.
244   Specifically, after each round of training, a new top-level active policy is obtained by parameter
245   fusion of the lower-level active policies and the fixed policies. This fusion shares the knowledge
246   learned among different active policies, enhancing data utilization and accelerating learning in sub-
247   sequent training rounds.
248

262   4.3   H IERARCHICAL E XPLORATION
263
264   In self-play algorithms, it is often necessary to truncate the approximate best-response operator at
265   each iteration, which can lead to suboptimal training outcomes. To migrate this issue, we introduce
266
      a hierarchical exploration mechanism within the Parallelized Oracle framework, where different
      exploration factors are assigned to different tiers of the active policy pool.
267
268   Specifically, the highest-level active policies are more inclined to explore during training after ini-
269   tialization and gradually shift towards exploitation as training progresses. To achieve this, we incor-
      porate an entropy regularization term in the computation of the optimal response. The entropy term

290   AlphaStar888 Czarnecki et al. (2020) is an empirical game derived from the solution process of
291   StarCraft II Vinyals et al. (2019), featuring a payoff table involving 888 reinforcement learning
292   strategies. It can be viewed as a zero-sum symmetric two-player game with only one state. In
293   this state, there are 888 legal actions, and any mixed strategy corresponds to a discrete probability
294
      distribution over these actions. Due to the size of the 888 × 888 payoff matrix, the computational
      time required for strategy optimization can significantly vary among algorithms, making it suitable
295
      for demonstrating differences in time overhead.
296
297   In this experiment, we additionally included the REFINED algorithm for comparison to enrich the
298   experimental results. The experimental results are shown in Figure 2. During the experiments, we
299   recorded the exploitability Lanctot et al. (2017) of each algorithm’s current policy set and the com-
300
      putation time each algorithm spent on performing policy optimization. We plotted the exploitability
      against the computation time, with computation time on the horizontal axis and exploitability on the
301
      vertical axis.
302
303   The experimental results in Figure 2a show that the exploitability of the MHO algorithm decreases
304   significantly and improves even further when combined with the PSRO algorithm. This improve-
305   ment is mainly due to the use of Nash Equilibrium (NE) as the weighting mechanism in the model
306
      soup within PSRO. Although the MHO algorithm slightly increases the total computation time for
      strategy optimization, it plays a crucial role in enhancing the overall performance of the algorithm.
307
      Additionally, comparisons of SP vs. MHO, PSRO vs. P2SRO, and PSRO vs. PSRO with MHO
308
      indicate that due to the presence of the Parallelized Oracle, computational resources are partitioned,
309   which leads to a decrease in the speed of convergence in the early stages. However, in the long term,
310   MHO demonstrates more robust performance.
311

      We conducted two ablation experiments to verify the performance of Parallelized Oracle, Model
      Souping, and Hierarchical Exploration in MHO using Self-Play and the PSRO. The experimental
313
      results are shown in Figures 2b. From these results, we observe that Model Souping has the greatest
314
      impact on improving strategy performance. The impact of Hierarchical Exploration varies across
315   different algorithms due to differing hyperparameters, leading to different performance outcomes.
