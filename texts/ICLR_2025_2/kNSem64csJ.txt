009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Driving scene reconstruction and rendering have advanced significantly using the
012            3D Gaussian Splatting. However, most prior research has focused on the ren-
013            dering quality along a pre-recorded vehicle path and struggles to generalize to
014            out-of-path viewpoints, which is caused by the lack of high-quality supervision
015            in those out-of-path views. To address this issue, we introduce an Inverse View
016
               Warping technique to create compact and high-quality images as supervision for
               the reconstruction of the out-of-path views, enabling high-quality rendering re-
017
               sults for those views. For accurate and robust inverse view warping, a depth boot-
018
               strap strategy is proposed to obtain on-the-fly dense depth maps during the opti-
019            mization process, overcoming the sparsity and incompleteness of LiDAR depth
020            data. Our method achieves superior in-path and out-of-path reconstruction and
021            rendering performance on the widely adopted Waymo Open dataset. In addition,
022            a simulator-based benchmark is proposed to obtain the out-of-path ground truth
023            and quantitatively evaluate the performance of out-of-path rendering, where our
024            method outperforms previous methods by a significant margin.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      3D reconstruction in driving scenes is a cornerstone of a high-quality driving visual simulator. Lever-
029
      aging NeRF (Mildenhall et al., 2021) and the emerging 3D Gaussian Splatting (Kerbl et al., 2023b),
030   the community has made significant progresses (Zhou et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2023a; Guo et al.,
031   2023; Yan et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023; Lu et al., 2023) in this area, possessing
032   impressive rendering quality in the pre-recorded driving trajectories.
033
      However, a significant issue hinders the current methods from being used in a practical simulator: the
034
      rendering quality declines significantly when the viewpoint deviates from the vehicle’s trajectories
035
      for data collection. Fig. 1 demonstrates this issue. The essential cause for this issue is the unavail-
036   ability of ground-truth visual observations from out-of-path viewpoints in driving scenes (Sun et al.,
037   2020; Caesar et al., 2019; Geiger et al., 2013), where only pre-recorded images along a single-pass
038   driving trajectory are available.
039
      To address this issue, UniSim (Yang et al., 2023b) first introduces the concept of “lane shift” in their
040
      driving simulator, where they leverage GAN-generated supervision to refine the rendering quality of
041
      out-of-trajectory viewpoints. LidaRF (Sun et al., 2024) proposed to warp colors from in-path views
042   to the target out-of-path views through the sparse LiDAR points, creating pseudo ground truth of the
043   out-of-path views. However, due to the sparsity of LiDAR points and occlusion in the target view,
044   the pseudo ground truth is usually broken and irregular, having quite different appearances from the
045   real images captured by cameras. These limitations raise a natural question: can we create a regular
046   and complete pseudo ground truth for reconstructing the out-of-path views?
047
      We propose Inverse View Warping (IVW) to solve this challenge. Intuitively, IVW is the inverse
048   process of the aforementioned color warping method. Considering an in-path view A and an adjacent
049   out-of-path view B, all content of A can be captured at viewpoint B if we omit the slight color
050   change and occlusions caused by their different view direction. Inverse View Warping (IVW) tries
051   to render the complete content of A at the viewpoint B. In this circumstance, we can directly use
052   A as the ground truth to reconstruct the out-of-path view B. Specifically, to achieve this warping,
053   we first unproject the pixels of view A into 3D points and project those points into view B, forming
      a warped ray map. We then perform occlusion-aware rasterization according to the warped ray

084
085           1. We propose Inverse View Warping, which creates high-quality supervision for out-of-path
086              viewpoints in street scenes, significantly improving reconstruction quality from these novel
087              viewpoints.
088
              2. We propose a novel depth bootstrapping strategy to obtain a dense and accurate depth map,
089
                 enabling more robust Inverse View Warping.
090
091           3. We build a new novel view synthesis benchmark upon the CARLA simulator to evaluate
092              the out-of-path views.
093

094              achieves superior performance in the out-of-path views, validated by quantitative and qual-
095              itative results in Waymo dataset and our proposed benchmark.

      3DGS in Autonomous Driving Simulation Great efforts have been made to achieve higher recon-
      structing quality for autonomous driving scenes. Such reconstruction is essential for creating an
124
      autonomous driving environment. Although simulation environments such as CARLA (Dosovitskiy
125
      et al., 2017), and AirSim (Shah et al., 2018) exist, they require significant manual effort to create

128   Rematas et al., 2022; Tancik et al., 2022; Tonderski et al., 2024). These methods primarily concen-
129   trate on altering the autonomous driving scene along the data collection trajectory. For example,
130   they can modify the lanes of neighboring cars or remove specific objects. However, simulating an
131   autonomous driving scenario requires more than just these adjustments. The simulation environ-
132   ment must also accommodate maneuvers such as cut-ins, parallel parking, and turns. Achieving this
133   necessitates flexible rendering capabilities, which have not been thoroughly explored in previous

      research.

      In this subsection, we first offer an overview of the proposed FlexDrive. Its overall architecture is
139
      demonstrated in Fig. 2, which has two major components including Inverse View Warping (IVW,
140
      Sec. 3.2) and Depth Bootstrapping (DB, Sec. 3.1). IVW creates high-quality visual supervision for
141   training and improving the rendering at out-of-path virtual viewpoints. Since IVW relies on depth
142   estimation, DB provides accurate and dense depth maps to enhance the IVW. Furthermore, we also
143   improve dynamic object modeling (Sec. 3.3) to make the FlexDrive better support the reconstruction
144   of dynamic scenes in out-of-path viewpoints. We summarize our optimization objectives in Sec. 3.4.

146
      3.1    D EPTH B OOTSTRAPPING
147
148
      Depth Bootstrapping leverages sparse LiDAR information to repeatedly rectify the dense depth map
149   rendered from current reconstructed 3D Gaussians. We have two steps in Depth Bootstrapping:
150   Sparse Depth Initialization and Dense Depth Rectification. The first step accumulates multi-frame
151   LiDAR points and projects them into a training view to initialize the sparse depth map. The second
152   step adopts an efficient linear optimization to minimize the gap between the rendered dense depth
153   map and the sparse depth map.
154
155
      Sparse Depth Initialization We first transform the 3D sparse LiDAR points into the 2D pixel
156   plane of each in-path training view. For a view at time step t, we first accumulate the 3D LiDAR
157   points in multiple frames (30 frames in our experiments) [t, t + T ] into frame t with the provided
158   LiDAR poses. For dynamic objects, we leverage their bounding boxes to move the in-box points
159   to the corresponding positions in frame t. Although this transformation process is straightforward,
160   there are two challenges: (1) Multiple points may be projected to the same image coordinates; (2)
161   The points of occluded objects could penetrate the occluder due to the sparsity of LiDAR points and
      be mistakenly projected into the 2D pixel plane.


                                                                       3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      To address the two challenges, we propose several simple yet effective rules. Let pk denote the k-th
163   LiDAR point, and its corresponding image coordinates are denoted as ik . τ (k) and d(k) stands for
164   the timestamp and depth of the k-th point, respectively. We then use the following rules to select a
165   subset of these points to build the sparse depth map.
166
167            1. If the depth of point ik deviates from the current rendered depth from 3D Gaussians1 over
168               a given threshold (e.g., 5% current depth), the point is removed.
169            2. If point ik1 and point ik2 occupy the same pixel position with τ (k1 ) < τ (k2 ), we keep ik1
170               and remove ik2 .
171            3. If point ik1 and point ik2 occupy the same pixel position with depth d(k1 ) < d(k2 ), we
172               keep ik1 and remove ik2 .
173
174   Intuitively, rule (1) indicates we only utilize the relatively accurate sparse depth and rule out the
175   occluded points. Rule (2) and (3) indicate we prefer the “early appeared” points and closer points.
176   Dense Depth Rectification Although the sparse depth map is relatively accurate, it only occupies
177   a very small portion of the whole image plane, leading to several problems. (1) The supervision for
178   Gaussian depth is sparse and makes it hard for the Gaussian field to render a smooth and continuous
179   depth map. (2) Floaters, especially floaters in the regions that LiDAR cannot cover, cannot be
180   effectively removed. (3) More importantly, with a sparse depth map, we can only build sparse visual
181   supervision for the out-of-path viewpoint in the IVW (Sec. 3.2), making our method less effective.
182   To tackle these problems, we propose to densify the sparse depth map into a dense one. Our den-
183   sification process is inspired by the observation that the rendered depth map is highly linear to the

337   Training Scheme Our training process can be divided into three stages: (1) the warm-up stage,
338   (2) the bootstrapping stage, and (3) the out-of-path training stage. During the warm-up stage, we
339
      initialize the 3D Gaussian primitives using multi-frame LiDAR points and conduct training in the
      in-path views without Gaussian densification. Single-frame sparse LiDAR supervision is directly
340
      employed in the in-path views, following (Chen et al., 2023; Sun et al., 2024). In the second stage,
341
      we enable the proposed depth bootstrapping and the densification strategy in the original 3DGS. The
342   parameters in the linear transformation (Eq. (1)) are solved by the least squares method. Finally, we
343   begin the out-of-path training stage. In this stage, we sample an in-path view and randomly generate
344   a nearby out-of-path view for each iteration, as Fig. 2 (b) shows. The rendering results in the in-path
345   view and out-of-path view are supervised by the in-path ground truth images and virtual ground
346   truth image created in Sec. 3.2. The three stages take 5k, 15k, and 10k, respectively. More detailed
347   hyperparameters can be found in the appendix.

      To summarize, the proposed FlexDrive introduces the Inverse View Warping and Depth Bootstrap
489   strategy for enhancing the reconstruction quality of street scenes, particularly from out-of-path view-

598   The Fréchet inception distance (FID) is a metric for measuring the distance between two distribu-
599   tions. With the normal distribution assumption, FID scores can be computed with the following
600   formula:                                                       √
601                            F ID = ∥µ1 − µ2 ∥ + Tr(σ1 + σ2 − 2 σ1 ∗ σ2 )                          (8)
602   In our setting, we compute the FID scores between the in-vehicle path views (µ1 , σ1 ) and the ren-
603   dered shifted views (µ2 , σ2 ). Because the shifted views are different from the in-vehicle path views
604   by nature, thus µ1 ̸= µ2 and σ1 ̸= σ2 . Now even if we simply shift the mean of µ2 to µ1 , we can
605   reduce the FID scores lower than the GT views. For this reason, we claim that FID score is not a
606   good metric for the flexible rendering task.
