011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            To make large language models (LLMs) more helpful across diverse cultures,
014            it is essential to have effective cultural knowledge benchmarks to measure and
015            track our progress. Effective benchmarks need to be robust, diverse, and chal-
016            lenging. We introduce C ULTURAL B ENCH: a set of 1,227 human-written and
017
               human-verified questions for effectively assessing LLMs’ cultural knowledge,
               covering 45 global regions including the underrepresented ones like Bangladesh,
018
               Zimbabwe, and Peru. Questions - each verified by five independent annotators -
019
               span 17 diverse topics ranging from food preferences to greeting etiquettes. We
020            evaluate models on two setups: C ULTURAL B ENCH-Easy and C ULTURAL B ENCH-
021            Hard which share the same questions but asked differently. We find that LLMs are
022            sensitive to such difference in setups (e.g., GPT-4o with 27.3% difference). Com-
023            pared to human performance (92.6% accuracy), C ULTURAL B ENCH-Hard is more
024            challenging for frontier LLMs with the best performing model (GPT-4o) at only
025            61.5% and the worst (Llama3-8b) at 21.4%. Moreover, we find that LLMs of-
026            ten struggle with tricky questions that have multiple correct answers (e.g., What
027            utensils do the Chinese usually use?), revealing a tendency to converge to a single
028
               answer. Our results also indicate that OpenAI GPT-4o substantially outperform
               other proprietary and open source models in questions related to all but one re-
029
               gion (Oceania). Nonetheless, all models consistently underperform on questions
030
               related to South America and the Middle East.

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Uneven cultural representation has been a notorious recurrent limitation of LLMs (Santy et al.,
036   2023; Cao et al., 2023; Arora et al., 2023). Yet, establishing a quality benchmark to effectively
037   gauge LLMs’ nuanced multicultural knowledge remains a formidable challenge (Hershcovich et al.,
038   2022). Effective benchmarks need to be robust, diverse, and challenging. We believe the previous
039
      and existing cultural benchmarks may not be satisfactory to be effective. The concrete consequence
      is that no recent major LLM releases have included cultural evaluation performance in their technical
040
      reports (OpenAI et al., 2023; Dubey et al., 2024; Anthropic, 2024). Conventional human-written
041
      benchmarks are static and often fail to keep pace with the evolving capabilities of LLMs (Yang
042   et al., 2023). Alternatively, existing auto-generated benchmarks cannot reflect the real struggles of
043   models and the true concerns of users on multicultural knowledge. They often rely on web resources
044   e.g., Wikipedia (Naous et al., 2023; Fung et al., 2024), and LLMs’ responses on established human
045   surveys e.g., World Value Survey (Durmus et al., 2023b; Li et al., 2024). Those benchmarks could
046   be less effective since the scraped web sources have been used directly on training and the surveys
047   have limited cultural concepts. The latest synthetic data benchmark approach (Rao et al., 2024; Fung
048   et al., 2024), despite their scalability, risk propagating existing data distribution bias in models that
049   they are meant to measure (Liu et al., 2024).
050   Drawing insights from recent red-teaming approaches on LLMs’ safety (Ganguli et al., 2022) and
051   interactive model evaluation and data collection efforts (Kiela et al., 2021; Chiang et al., 2024),
052   we develop CulturalTeaming, an AI-assisted interactive red-teaming data collection and validation
053   pipeline. CulturalTeaming aims to construct a robust, diverse and challenging benchmark. The
      pipeline consists of three parts as shown in Fig. 1 – (1) Red-teaming data collection (2) Human

077   Quality Check (3) Filtering. The goal of the red-teaming platform is to guide and encourage humans
078   in iteratively create challenging questions for models. Specifically, humans provide diverse cultural
079   scenarios based on their daily observations and unique cultural knowledge. The AI helper provides
080   writing assistance to alleviate the burden of formulating questions.
081   We introduce our collected C ULTURAL B ENCH with 1,227 high-quality questions, each of which
082   has been verified by five independent annotators. These questions span 45 global regions includ-
083   ing less represented ones such as Bangladesh in South Asia, Zimbabwe in Africa, and Peru in
084   South America, with details in Fig. 6. They are diverse with 17 cultural topics identified in Fig.
085   3, that reflect a broad spectrum of cultural elements in different countries/regions e.g., food, lan-
086   guage/communication, visiting etiquette, and celebrations.
087   To capture the cultural diversity in each region, our C ULTURAL B ENCH contains two types of ques-
088   tions: (i) Single-mode: one correct answer and (ii) Multiple-mode: multiple correct answers, as
089   shown in Fig. 1. During the human quality check, we allow annotators to respond to each ques-
090   tion in a multi-label format, recognizing that multiple valid answers can coexist for some questions
091   (Boratko et al., 2020). For instance, for a question of “what utensil do Chinese people usually use
092   everyday?”, the most likely answer is “chopsticks” (which is a common utensil for eating Chinese
093
      food). However, other answers such as “spoon” may also reflect the reality of the Chinese popula-
      tion, depending on the specific foods being served. We have strict criteria on filtering out questions
094
      with no answer having majority vote (i.e., ≥ 4 out of 5 annotators), ensuring our C ULTURAL B ENCH
095
      is robust and captures accurate cultural representations.
096
097   There are two evaluation setups on our C ULTURAL B ENCH– (1) C ULTURAL B ENCH-Easy, which
098   evaluate the model on multiple choice questions; (2) C ULTURAL B ENCH-Hard, which converts the
099
      multiple choice question into binary questions (True/False) for each of the four options as shown
      in Fig. 1. After collecting data, we first designed and constructed our C ULTURAL B ENCH-Easy, di-
100
      rectly using the 1,227 standardized questions with four options. Although there are performance dif-
101
      ferences (28%) between the worst and best-performing models, the best-performing model achieves
102   88%, which only slightly lags behind the human baseline (92.4%). Inspired by the recent studies
103   on binary setting to accurately test models’ reasoning capabilities (Kadavath et al., 2022; Zhang
104   et al., 2024), we construct our C ULTURAL B ENCH-Hard by converting the 1,227 multiple-choice
105   questions to 4,908 binary questions (four per original question). We test 30 models from different
106   families (e.g., OpenAI GPT, Llama, Qwen) across different model sizes (e.g., 8b, 70b, and 405b).
107   We found this setup to be much more challenging for LLMs with the best performing model at only
      61.5% accuracy and the worst at 21.4%, compared to a human performance of 92.6%.

      Looking to understand why models perform drastically different on C ULTURAL B ENCH-Easy and -
109   Hard, we wondered if models can simply guess the most likely option under multiple-choice format
110   found in the C ULTURAL B ENCH-Easy setup. We designed an experiment that shows that models
111   can get 40% accuracy (substantially above random chance of 25%) by simply choosing the option
112   that has greatest embedding similar to the name of the culture (without seeing the question). This
113   shows the potential limitation of assessing models’ capabilities under the multiple-choice setting in
114   C ULTURAL B ENCH-Easy since they could rely on such heuristics without needing to demonstrate
115   cultural understanding. In contrast, C ULTURAL B ENCH-Hard can more effectively assess the cultural
116   knowledge of models, because such heuristics cannot be easily applied to game evaluation.
117   Moreover, our evaluation on different question types shows that even the best models struggle with
118   questions that have multiple correct answers, revealing a tendency to LLMs to over-converge on a
119   single option. This is evident by a significant drop (-19.8%) in accuracy on questions with multiple
120   correct answers, as compared with questions with a sole correct answer. Through our analysis of
121   questions relating to various sub-continents in C ULTURAL B ENCH-hard, we find that models per-
122   form well on questions relating to regions (e.g., North America and South Asia) that are highly
123
      represented in web-source data (e.g., United States, as part of North America) and large-scale hu-
      man annotation sources (e.g., India in South Asia). However, models underperform on questions
124
      relating to less well-represented regions such as Eastern Europe. This observation holds even for
125
      models developed by providers based outside of the United States (e.g. Alibaba Qwen and Mistral),
126   which might possibly be attributed to the availability of the data used in various stages of training.
127   Overall, OpenAI’s GPT-4o outperforms other proprietary providers and open source model builders
128   uniformly across all but one region (Oceania). With C ULTURAL B ENCH and our analysis on various
129   models, we provide an effective benchmark for testing the cultural knowledge of various LLMs, with
130   the hopes of encouraging model developers to easily perform cultural evaluations in the journey to
131   develop more culturally-sensitive LLMs.

      Poor diversity of topics Many benchmarks have topics predefined prior to data collection, mean-
191   ing that they are unlikely to fully capture the multi-faceted natured of cultural knowledge (Adi-
192   lazuarda et al., 2024). Many prior works topics focus on narrow topics such as food (Nguyen et al.,
193   2022), dating (Fung et al., 2024), social etiquette like dining (Palta & Rudinger, 2023; Dwivedi
194   et al., 2023), visiting (Rao et al., 2024), and special elements in wider society like religions (Nguyen

      Over-reliance on Web Sources Existing benchmarks often rely on web sources directly such as
      web corpus (Nguyen et al., 2022), Wikipedia (Naous et al., 2023), and incorporated with LLMs’
202
      generation (Rao et al., 2024; Fung et al., 2024). These non-human written benchmarks may not be
203
      challenging since the scraped web sources may be used during models pretraining (Petroni et al.,
204   2019) and LLM generations may inherit the potential cultural bias (Arora et al., 2022; Cao et al.,
205   2023; Liu et al., 2024). Given the performances of best-performing models ranging from 81.4% to
206   93.1% in the existing benchmarks in Table 1, those benchmarks are likely not sufficiently challeng-
207   ing for modern frontier LLMs. Our proposed C ULTURAL B ENCH is substantially more difficult with
208   the best model (GPT-4o) only reaching 61.5% despite humans reaching 92.6%.

214   Our data collection pipeline consists of three steps, as illustrated in Fig. 2: (1) Data collection via
215   AI-assisted red-teaming (2) Human quality check on full data (3) Filtering with majority vote. Such
      a multi-step process enables us to collect robust data for C ULTURAL B ENCH.

      3.1      S TEP 1: DATA COLLECTION VIA INTERACTIVE AI- ASSISTED RED TEAMING
217
218   Question Formulation. Human annotators are instructed to brainstorm culturally relevant scenarios
219   based on their personal experiences of their cultures (e.g., Singaporeans use tissue packet to reserve
220   seats). A step-by-step guideline with detailed examples is provided to inspire them, as shown in
221   Appendix H. The AI helper bot then transforms the scenario into a structured question with four
222   options, which the annotators can review and edit afterward.
223   Question Verification & Revision. Human annotators can use the formulated question as basis
224   to challenge the AI verifier in our interactive platform. The platform provide further assistance in
225   revising the questions to make it more challenging by offering various revision strategies along with
226   drafted examples (e.g., “Negate the Question”), as shown in Appendix H.
227
      Internal Filtering. After collecting over 2,600 questions, the researchers carefully reviewed and
228
      removed those that are not relevant to any countries/regions (e.g., Bangladesh, Peru), resulting in a
229   filtered set of 2,486 cultural questions.
230
231
232   3.2      S TEP 2: H UMAN Q UALITY C HECK
233
      Recruitment Criteria. We collected questions at the country/regional level, pairing each question
234
      with a specific region. To ensure culturally attuned and thorough verification, we recruited five
235
      annotators for each region through the Prolific platform 1 . We set two main criteria to ensure that
236   the recruited annotators have a deep understanding of the culture of the targeted country or region –
237   (1) Nationality (2) Primary residence before age 18. For certain cultures (e.g. the United States, the
238   United Kingdom), when the platform allowed more detailed selections and the collected question
239   targeted specific groups in the country/region, we added detailed criteria such as ethnicity (e.g.
240   African American, Native American), and place of residence (e.g., Wales).
241
      Multiple Selection Settings. To better reflect the true representation of each cultural question, we
242   allow annotators to select multiple answers on our questions with four options. As a result, some
243   questions may have more than one majority-vote answer. This approach also helps test models’
244   mode-seeking behavior, examining whether they rely solely on cultural stereotypes (i.e., modes)
245   without considering broader cultural diversity.

      3.3      S TEP 3: F ILTERING BY M AJORITY VOTE & C ONSTRUCTING B ENCHMARKS
248
249   Majority Vote Criteria. To build a robust benchmark that captures the accurate representation on
250   cultural knowledge, we set the majority-vote threshold to be >= 4 out of 5 annotators. During
251   human validation, we first filtered out questions without majority consensus, resulting in a final set
252   of 1,227 questions. Subsequently, we further processed the remaining questions. To construct our
253   C ULTURAL B ENCH in two setups (C ULTURAL B ENCH-Easy: Multiple-choice, C ULTURAL B ENCH-
254   Hard: True/False), we processed the questions differently depending on the numbers of majority
255   votes they contain.
256   (1) Single-Mode Questions (Only one majority vote). For C ULTURAL B ENCH-Easy, we directly
257   keep the original question with four options. The gold label is the option with a majority vote (i.e.,
258   A, B, C or D). For C ULTURAL B ENCH-Hard, we transform the question with four options into four
259   binary questions. For instance, the question drafted (e.g., “What do Singaporeans ...? A. Tissue ...
260   D. ...”) will form binary questions (e.g. “Is this answer true or false for this question? Answer True
261   or False only. Question: What do Singaporeans ...? Answer: Tissue.”).
262   (2) Multi-Mode Questions (More than one majority votes). For C ULTURAL B ENCH-Easy, we
263   reframe the question to allow multiple statements. The four drafted options (e.g., “A. Tissue”)
264   become the four statements in questions (e.g., “statement (i) Tissue”). To ensure the models know
265   the possibility of questions containing multiple correct labels, we add the instruction on question
266   directly with (“Select the options with all applicable statements”). For C ULTURAL B ENCH-Hard,
267   we follow the same construction approach (transforming four options to four binary questions) as
268
      single-mode questions.

297   Our benchmarks cover a wide range of global regions, spanning 45 countries and regions, includ-

302   instance, a question of “What do Singaporeans usually use to reserve seats?” with options of “A.
303   Tissue ... D. Book” as shown in Fig. 1. The gold label is the correct option (A, B, C or D). For multi-
304   mode questions (i.e., questions with more than one answer), we added an instruction of “Selecting
305

306

308   whitespace-separated words on average, specifically option A with 5.48 words (σ = 4.24), option B
309   with 5.44 words (σ = 4.27), option C with 5.57 words (σ = 4.24), and option D with 5.57 words
310   (σ = 4.24).
311
      C ULTURAL B ENCH-Hard. In this dataset, each question is transformed into four binary true/false
312
      questions, requiring models to evaluate each option separately. For example, the earlier multiple-
313
      choice example in C ULTURAL B ENCH-Easy will transform into four binary questions such as “Is
314   this answer true or false for this question? Question: What do Singaporeans usually use to reserve
315   seats? Answer: Tissue.”, as shown in Fig. 2. The gold label in this case is either True or False. This
316   set contains 1,227 ×4 = 4,912 True/False judgement questions. The questions contain 14.3 words
317   (σ = 5.27) and the answers contain 5.72 words (σ = 4.21).

319   4.2     D IVERSE T OPICS D ISCOVERED ACROSS C ULTURES
320
321   Most existing cultural benchmarks have predefined topics to collect data on, typically on universal
322   topics such as dining (Adilazuarda et al., 2024). However, this approach can overlook cultural el-
323   ements unique to specific regions. To capture a broader spectrum of cultural topics, we adopted a
      discovery-based approach by encouraging human annotators to brainstorm cultural concepts from


                                                             6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      their personal experiences. The detailed instruction for annotators can be found in Appendix H.
325   C ULTURAL B ENCH spans a diverse range of cultural elements with 17 topics under three categories
326   (Daily life, Social Etiquette, and Wider Society), as shown in Fig. 3. Daily life relates to the every-
327   day experiences of people e.g., Workplace. Social Etiquette means the acceptable norms in society
328   e.g., Greeting. Wider Society included special elements for broader spectrum of cultural topics e.g.,
329   Celebrations. We classified questions into topics by prompting GPT-4o-mini. The classification
330   prompt and the topic detailed definitions are in Appendix C.
331   To collect diverse data for each culture, we allow each annotator to create at most 3-7 questions,
332   depending on the availability of annotators for each region. Notably, in curating C ULTURAL B ENCH,
333   we observed that people from different regions focused on distinct topics. For instance, annotators
334   from Italy and Mexico provided more questions related to Food, with 15 out of 35 questions and
335   13 out of 49 questions respectively. In contrast, participants from South Africa and India focused
336   more on Religion, contributing 19 out of 58 questions and 14 out of 46 questions respectively. Our
337   discovery-based approach allow us to capture diverse cultural elements from people in different
338   regions without being limited by a predefined set of topics.

342   We evaluate 30 current LLMs in a zero-shot setting on C ULTURAL B ENCH in two setups: (1) C UL -
343   TURAL B ENCH -Easy: Multiple choice; (2) C ULTURAL B ENCH -Hard: True/False. We prompted the
344   models to ensure they follow the output format to allow fair comparison. The detailed prompt is in
345   Appendix D. To avoid exposing the correct answers to models for fair comparison, our annotation
346   platform, which involves using OpenAI APIs did not allow the collected data to be used for further
347
      training.
348   C ULTURAL B ENCH-Easy. We evaluate model performance by measuring accuracy, specifically
349   whether the model correctly identifies the label for each multiple-choice question. A random base-
350   line can achieve 25%.
351   C ULTURAL B ENCH-Hard. We evaluate model performance based on the proportion of tasks in
352   which the model can get all four options predicted correctly. For each task, an LLM has to make
353   four binary judgements (True/False) from the transformation of four options in each multiple choice
354   question. To demonstrate robust cultural knowledge, we believe the LLM has to accurately which
355   option(s) are False as well as which option(s) are True. A random baseline can achieve 0.54 =
356   6.25%.

      Why do the two setups on C ULTURAL B ENCH have such model performance difference? We
389
      hypothesize that the models can guess for the most possible answer on C ULTURAL B ENCH-Easy
390
      under the multiple-choice setting. We compute the embedding for the country name and separately
391   for each option using OpenAI text-embedding-3-small. By using a simple heuristic of choosing the
392   option with highest cosine similarity with the country name (e.g. Bangladesh), we attain 40.42%
393   accuracy. This is intriguing as it is substantially above the random baseline (25%), without needing
394   considering the question at all. We find that the cosine similarity difference between the correct
395   option and the country name is significantly higher than the difference between options average
396   and the country (0.166 vs. 0.145; Kruskal-Wallis p-value≤0.01). This shows the possibility of
397   models guessing based on one (out of many possible) heuristics in multiple-choice setup without
398   understanding (or even knowing) the question. This stresses the importance on using the binary
399
      (True/False) for each of the four options per question in C ULTURAL B ENCH-Hard to accurately
      assess cultural knowledge of LLMs.

      6   L IMITATIONS AND FUTURE WORK
487
488
489
      While C ULTURAL B ENCH has several advantages over existing cultural benchmarks, we would also
      like to clarify some of its current limitations as well as ways to address them in the future.
490
491   Multilingual vs. Multicultural. We develop an English-only benchmark as the initial step in
492   evaluating models’ cultural knowledge. This approach facilitates fair comparisons of cultural under-
493   standing across different regions. For instance, in underrepresented regions such as Bangladesh, the
494   availability of training data in local languages is often limited. As a result, models lacking sufficient
495
      exposure to these languages may struggle to comprehend questions phrased in them (Yong et al.,
      2023). By employing an English-only benchmark, we can assess models’ cultural knowledge re-
496
      garding these underrepresented areas without considering their (lack of) proficiency in low-resource
497
      languages. Additionally, prior research on multilingual models’ emotional understanding (Havaldar
498   et al., 2023) and reasoning skills (Liu et al., 2023) indicates that a model’s multilingual capabili-
499   ties may not necessarily correlate with its multicultural competencies. Notably, our discovery-based
500   benchmark includes language elements on some questions, particularly in the Language and Com-
501   munication topic with 6.2%. For example, we included questions like: “What do Singaporeans
502   usually say at the end? A. lah ...”. As we await advances in developing stronger multilingual abili-
503   ties in models for low-resource languages, our goal is to establish a robust, diverse, and challenging
504   benchmark to track our progress toward addressing the uneven representation of cultural knowledge.
505   Small sample of human verifiers on subjective cultural knowledge. Due to the limitations of
506   crowd-sourcing platforms like Prolific, the number of available annotators from underrepresented
507   regions, such as Bangladesh, is quite small (fewer than 30 active human annotators). As a result,
508   we were able to recruit only five annotators for consistency verification. To enhance the robustness
509   of our dataset, we allow human verifiers to select multiple labels for each question, ensuring that all
510   possible answers are captured. Additionally, we establish a strict majority-vote threshold (majority
511   votes ≥ 4 out of 5). During the annotation process, we also provide two extra options: “I don’t
512
      have knowledge” and “This question is unanswerable” – to enable annotators to indicate when they
      cannot provide a response.
513
514   Further fine-grained culture classification. We noticed that the country/region classification
515   adopted by our C ULTURAL B ENCH may not capture the cultural diversity within each region. How-
516   ever, the data annotation platform we accessed does not have a further fine-grain classification when
517   recruiting human annotators for most of the regions except for the United States and the United
518
      Kingdom. To capture the diversity on these two countries, we revisited the data that have been fil-
      tered by having not enough majority votes and with mostly responses of “I don’t have knowledge”.
519
      For example, questions asking for the Welsh custom in the United Kingdom may not be answerable
520
      for people living in England. Then, we conducted a second round of human quality check by as-
521   signing those questions for the specific groups of human annotators (e.g., people living in Wales in
522   the United Kingdom), as explained in Section 3.2. We hope to see more data annotation tools for
523   different local cultures to facilitate more fine-grained cultural data collection.
524
      Strong instruction prompts and strict evaluation criteria on models’ outputs. We evaluated
525
      models on zero-shot setting with the prompts. However, for some models such as Claude-3 Haiku,
526
      they need more instructions to have the right formatting. Therefore, we have added one-line instruc-
527   tion for all models to ensure they outputting the answer in the correct format on our evaluation, as
528   described in Appendix D. However, with the strong instruction prompt, sometimes they still refuse to
529   answer the questions e.g., “This question ...” rather than outputting the four options (i.e., A, B, C, or
530   D) on C ULTURAL B ENCH-Easy or the binary labels (i.e., True/False) on C ULTURAL B ENCH-Hard.
531   To ensure the fair evaluation, we set the output token to be 2. The model is treated as answering
532   correctly when its output contains the correct labels only.

      across more geographical regions.
