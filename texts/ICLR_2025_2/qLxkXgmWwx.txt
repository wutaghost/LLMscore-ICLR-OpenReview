011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated strong capabilities in text un-
014            derstanding and generation. However, they often lack factuality, producing a mix-
015            ture of true and false information, especially in long-form generation. In this work,
016
               we investigates the factuality of long-form text generation across various large lan-
               guage models (LLMs), including GPT-4, Gemini-1.5-Pro, Claude-3-Opus, Llama-
017
               3-70B, and Mistral. Our analysis reveals that factuality scores tend to decline in
018
               later sentences of the generated text, accompanied by a rise in the number of
019            unsupported claims. Furthermore, we explore the effectiveness of different eval-
020            uation settings to assess whether LLMs can accurately judge the correctness of
021            their own outputs: Self-Known (the percentage of supported atomic claims, de-
022            composed from LLM outputs, that the corresponding LLMs judge as correct) and
023            Self-Unknown (the percentage of unsupported atomic claims that the correspond-
024            ing LLMs judge as incorrect). The results indicate that even advanced models like
025            GPT-4 and Gemini-1.5-Pro fail to achieve perfect Self-Known scores, while their
026            Self-Unknown scores remain notably above zero, reflecting ongoing uncertainty
027
               in their self-assessments. Moreover, we find a correlation between higher Self-
               Known scores and improved factuality, while higher Self-Unknown scores are
028
               associated with lower factuality. Interestingly, even without significant changes
029
               in the models’ self-judgment (Self-Known and Self-Unknown), the number of
030            unsupported claims can increases, likely as an artifact of long-form generation.
031            These findings show the limitations of current LLMs in long-form generation, and
032            provide valuable insights for improving factuality in long-form text generation.

035   1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037   The long-context capabilities of large language models (LLMs) (OpenAI, 2023b; AI@Meta, 2024;
038   Jiang et al., 2024; GeminiTeam, 2024; Anthropic, 2024) have seen significant advancements in re-
039   cent years. Lots of work (Shaham et al., 2023; Bai et al., 2024; An et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024;
040   Kuratov et al., 2024) have explored the ability of LLMs to handle long contexts, however, relatively
041   few have examined their ability for long-form text generation.
042   Despite LLMs have the impressive generative abilities, these models are prone to producing hallu-
043   cinations (Li et al., 2023; Min et al., 2023) where the generated content often blends factual and
044   fabricated information. This tendency not only undermines performance but also poses substantial
045   risks in practical applications. To assess the factuality of responses from LLMs, recent research (Fan
046   et al., 2020; Wright et al., 2022; Min et al., 2023; Manakul et al., 2023) has introduced a method
047   that breaks down generations into atomic claims – short statements each containing a single piece
048   of information. These atomic claims are then individually evaluated to determine whether they are
049   supported by evidence or unsupported.
050   To ensure the reliable use of LLMs, it is also crucial that they possess the ability to recognize not
051   only ”what they know” but also ”what they don’t know.” Recent studies, such as those by Kadavath
052   et al. (2022); Liu et al. (2022); Guerreiro et al. (2023), have shown that language models can assess
053   the validity of their own claims. However, Srivastava et (2023); Yin et al. (2023) have pointed out
      the limitations of LLMs in recognizing their own knowledge gaps.

054
      In this work, we investigate the factuality patterns of long-form text generation across various LLMs.
055   We first assess the factuality of long-form generation at different relative positions using two anno-
056   tated datasets and two models: ChatGPT and PerplexityAI (which integrates a search engine). Our
057   findings indicate that sentences generated earlier in the sequence generally demonstrate higher fac-
058   tuality. However, these later-generated sentences also contain more unsupported claims and fewer
059   supported claims.
060
      We further examine the effectiveness of different evaluation settings to assess whether LLMs can
061   accurately evaluate the correctness of atomic claims in their own generated outputs. To quantify the
062   corresponding models’ ability to judge the correctness of atomic claims, we calculate two metrics:
063   the Self-Known score (the percentage of supported atomic claims judged as correct by the LLMs)
064   and the Self-Unknown score (the percentage of unsupported atomic claims judged as incorrect by
065   the LLMs). Our exploration includes three methods, notably a novel approach where the final an-
066   swer option is replaced with ”None of the above”. This modification appears to provide a more
067   accurate measure of the LLMs’ abilities, as evidenced by a higher flip rate for supported claims and
068   an increasing flip rate at later relative positions. This suggests that the model reassesses its confi-
069
      dence when faced with an option signaling uncertainty. In contrast, the low flip rate for unsupported
      claims indicates a consistent judgment of their incorrectness. These results suggest a nuanced un-
070
      derstanding by LLMs of supported versus unsupported claims and underscore the importance of
071
      specific evaluation settings to accurately gauge model performance. Our findings align with human
072   annotations for two LLMs, although some discrepancies, particularly with the PerplexityAI model,
073   suggest gaps in estimation.
074
075
      The main contributions of our work are as follows:
076   1. We explored the factuality patterns of long-form text generation across various model families
077   (GPT-4, Gemini-1.5-Pro, Claude-3-Opus, Llama-3-70B, and Mistral). We found that even the most
078   advanced LLMs typically exhibit lower factuality scores in the later segments of long-form text.
079   Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) systems show a similar trend, although they tend to main-
080   tain higher factuality overall.
081   2. We analyzed Self-Known and Self-Unknown ratios for these LLMs across different segments of
082   their own generated texts. The results showed relatively higher Self-Known scores; however, even
083   strong LLMs (GPT-4, Gemini-1.5-Pro, Claude-3-Opus, etc.) generally achieved only about 50%
084   on the Self-Known score. The Self-Unknown scores were significantly above zero. These findings
085   indicate that even the most advanced LLMs still produce outputs with limited self-acknowledgment
086   ability.
087   3. We developed a mathematical framework linking Self-Known and Self-Unknown scores to factu-
088   ality, providing deeper insights into their relationship. Both empirical and theoretical results demon-
089   strate that higher Self-Known scores correspond to improved factuality, while higher Self-Unknown
090   scores are associated with reduced factuality. Moreover, even without significant changes in the
091   models’ self-judgment (Self-Known and Self-Unknown), the number of unsupported claims may
092   still increase, likely reflecting the inherent challenges of long-form generation.

      3        S ELF -K NOWN AND S ELF -U NKNOWN

      To investigate these questions, we examine whether the corresponding LLMs recognize their atomic
147
      claims by computing two metrics: Self-Known (the percentage of supported atomic claims that the
148   corresponding LLMs judge as correct) and Self-Unknown (the percentage of unsupported atomic
149   claims that the corresponding LLMs judge as incorrect). While there is related work, such as Ra-
150   jpurkar et al. (2018); Xiong et al. (2024), our approach differs in two key ways: (1) Evaluation
151   is conducted on atomic claims, which are derived from sentences in long-form generation, rather
152   than assigning a score to the entire model output; (2) Our focus is on factuality (whether an atomic
153   claim is true or false), rather than on uncertainty scores (i.e., ”How likely is the above answer to be
154   correct?”).
155   We explore the computation of Self-Known and Self-Unknown using the following three ap-
156   proaches ( with the corresponding prompt templates provided in Appendix Section A.2):
157
158             • Direct-Asking: In this approach (Rajpurkar et al., 2018), the atomic claim is directly given
159               to the corresponding LLMs and be asked whether the statement is true or false.
160             • Question-Answering: Given an atomic claim, a question-answer pair can be de-
161               rived (Trischler et al., 2017; Rajpurkar et al., 2018; Hu et al., 2024) with GPT-4 Turbo.
                  For example, ”Lanny Flaherty is an American.” can be used to derived a question-answer

                    pair (”What nationality is Lanny Flaherty?”, ”American”). Then, given the question and
163                 answer, we ask the corresponding LLMs whether the answer is true or false.
164
                  • Question-Answering w/NOA: Similar to the above approach, a question-answer pair is
165
                    derived according to each atomic claim. One big different is: given question and answer,
166
                    one more addition choice ( ”None of the above”) (Rajpurkar et al., 2018) is given to the
167                 corresponding LLMs. This is a well-defined evaluation because it can check whether the

397   How to Improve Factuality Score? In Equation 3, we propose estimating the factuality of a LLM
398   using Self-Known and Self-Unknown scores. A higher Self-Known score typically corresponds to
399   higher factuality. However, does this mean LLMs would achieve 100% factuality if they had a
400   100% Self-Known score and 0 Self-Unknown score on their own generation? The answer is no.
401   It is a necessary condition, not a sufficient one for achieving 100% factuality. In the derivation of
402   Equation 3, several additional assumptions are made6 .
403   According to our results, a higher Self-Known score is usually associated with higher factuality,
404   while a higher Self-Unknown score is associated with lower factuality for LLMs. This indicates that
405   it is challenging for LLMs to recognize unsupported claims on their own. Therefore, a judgment
406   model that incorporates an external knowledge source is necessary for this recognition.
407   A reasonable question arises: Is the decoding error of LLMs caused by the absence of relevant
408   knowledge? Can Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG), which provides additional knowledge,
409   resolve the issue of lower factuality in later stages of generation? In next section, we present some
410   RAG experiments.
411
412   5.3   R ETRIEVAL -AUGMENTED G ENERATION
413
414   Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) is a widely used approach for enhancing language model
415   performance in various applications. In RAG, relevant text segments are retrieved from an external
416   knowledge source and integrated into the model’s responses. For our retrieval corpus, we utilized
417
      the English Wikipedia as of April 1, 2023, with each page divided into chunks of up to 256 tokens.
      These retrieved passages, containing facts relevant to the entity, were incorporated into the LLMs’
418
      context to improve the factual accuracy of the generated content.7 .
419
420   According to Figure 6a, in the RAG setting, although there are significantly fewer unsupported
421   atomic claims overall, a notable increase in the number of unsupported claims is observed in later
422   stages of generation. As shown in Table 2, LLMs can still response with lots of unsupported claim
423
      even given context knowledge. This increase is likely due to error propagation within the LLMs,
      highlighting the challenges of long-form generation even when relevant parts are provided.
424
425   Figure 6a demonstrates that the RAG system exhibits significantly lower Self-Known scores and

454   6       R ELATED W ORK
455
456   Factuality Evaluation Recent advancements have seen significant efforts in quantifying the fac-
457   tuality of LLM generations. For short answers, factuality often correlates with fact verification,
458   which directly assesses whether the generation aligns with extensive knowledge sources and refer-
459
      ences (Thorne et al., 2018; Honovich et al., 2022) or utilizes language models (Lin et al., 2022).
      However, evaluating factuality in long-form content poses greater challenges due to the complexity
460
      of the generation process. Recent studies (Fan et al., 2020; Wright et al., 2022; Min et al., 2023)
461
      have approached this challenge by breaking down long generations into atomic claims. While these
462   approaches predominantly focus on factual precision, some studies (Wei et al., 2024) also consider
463   evaluating factual recall. In our work, we concentrate on factual precision akin to Min et al. (2023).
464   Moving forward, the development of more robust automatic tools will be crucial for advancing fac-
465   tuality exploration in long-form generation tasks.
466
467   Self-Know and Self-Unknown Recent studies have extensively explored the concepts of Self-
468   Known and Self-Unknown in language models. For instance, Kadavath et al. (2022); Liu et al.
469   (2022); Guerreiro et al. (2023) demonstrated that language models are capable of assessing the va-
470   lidity of their own claims and predicting their ability with answering true/false questions accurately.
471   Meanwhile, Srivastava et (2023); Yin et al. (2023) highlighted the limitations of LLMs in acknowl-
472   edging their unknowns, focusing on their ability to recognize unknown knowledge. In our work,
473   we specifically investigate whether LLMs can identify and reconsider unsupported claims generated
474   from their own outputs. Our results indicate that LLMs struggle to accurately judge unsupported
475
      atomic claims from their own generations. We find that a lower Self-Unknown score or a higher
      Self-Known score corresponds to higher factuality.

478   7       C ONCLUSION
479
480   In this study, we investigate the factuality of long-form text generation across different model fam-
481   ilies and relative positions. Our findings reveal a trend of lower factuality in sentences generated
482   later in the sequence. Additionally, we propose methods for enabling LLMs to accurately assess the
483   correctness of atomic claims derived from their own outputs. We introduce an estimation of factu-
484   ality using Self-Known and Self-Unknown scores, finding that higher Self-Known scores correlate
485   with increased factuality, whereas higher Self-Unknown scores correlate with decreased factuality.
      These insights provide valuable directions for enhancing factuality in text generation.
