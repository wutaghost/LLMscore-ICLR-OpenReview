010                                               A BSTRACT
011
012            Diffusion models, praised for their success in generative tasks, are increasingly
013            being applied to robotics, demonstrating exceptional performance in behavior
014
               cloning. However, their slow generation process stemming from iterative denoising
               steps poses a challenge for real-time applications in resource-constrained robotics
015
               setups and dynamically changing environments. In this paper, we introduce the
016
               One-Step Diffusion Policy (OneDP), a novel approach that distills knowledge
017            from pre-trained diffusion policies into a single-step action generator, significantly
018            accelerating response times for robotic control tasks. We ensure the distilled
019            generator closely aligns with the original policy distribution by minimizing the
020            Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence along the diffusion chain, requiring only 2%-
021            10% additional pre-training cost for convergence. We evaluated OneDP on 6
022            challenging simulation tasks as well as 4 self-designed real-world tasks using the
023            Franka robot. The results demonstrate that OneDP not only achieves state-of-the-
024            art success rates but also delivers an order-of-magnitude improvement in inference
025
               speed, boosting action prediction frequency from 1.5 Hz to 62 Hz, establishing
               its potential for dynamic and computationally constrained robotic applications. A
026
               video demo is provided here, and the code will be publicly available soon.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Diffusion models (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015; Ho et al., 2020) have emerged as a leading approach to
033
      generative AI, achieving remarkable success in diverse applications such as text-to-image generation
      (Saharia et al., 2022; Ramesh et al., 2022; Rombach et al., 2022), video generation (Ho et al., 2022;
034
      OpenAI, 2024), and online/offline reinforcement learning (RL) (Wang et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2023b;
035
      Hansen-Estruch et al., 2023; Psenka et al., 2023). Recently, Chi et al. (2023); Team et al. (2024);
036   Reuss et al. (2023); Ze et al. (2024); Ke et al. (2024); Prasad et al. (2024) demonstrated impressive
037   results of diffusion models in imitation learning for robot control. In particular, Chi et al. (2023)
038   introduces the diffusion policy and achieves a state-of-the-art imitation learning performance on a
039   variety of robotics simulation and real-world tasks.
040
      However, because of the necessity of traversing the reverse diffusion chain, the slow generation
041
      process of diffusion models presents significant limitations for their application in robotic tasks.
042   This process involves multiple iterations to pass through the same denoising network, potentially
043   thousands of times (Song et al., 2020a; Wang et al., 2023). Such a long inference time restricts
044   the practicality of using the diffusion policy (Chi et al., 2023), which by default runs at 1.49 Hz,
045   in scenarios where quick response and low computational demands are essential. While classical
046   tasks like block stacking or part assembly may accommodate slower inference rates, more dynamic
047   activities involving human interference or changing environments require quicker control responses
048   (Prasad et al., 2024). In this paper, we aim to significantly reduce inference time through diffusion
049   distillation and achieve responsive robot control.
050   Considerable research has focused on streamlining the reverse diffusion process for image genera-
051   tion, aiming to complete the task in fewer steps. A prominent approach interprets diffusion models
052   using stochastic differential equations (SDE) or ordinary differential equations (ODE) and employs
053   advanced numerical solvers for SDE/ODE to speed up the process (Song et al., 2020a; Liu et al.,
      2022; Karras et al., 2022; Lu et al., 2022). Another avenue explores distilling diffusion models into

090   In this paper, we introduce the One-Step Diffusion Policy (OneDP), which distills knowledge from
091   pre-trained diffusion policies into a one-step diffusion-based action generator, thus maximizing
092   inference efficiency through a single neural network feedforward operation. We demonstrate superior
093
      results over baselines in Figure 1. Inspired by the success of SDS (Poole et al., 2022) and VSD (Wang
      et al., 2024) in text-to-3D generation, we propose a policy-matching distillation method for robotic
094
      control. The training of OneDP consists of three key components: a one-step action generator, a
095
      generator score network, and a pre-trained diffusion-policy score network. To align the generator
096   distribution with the pre-trained policy distribution, we minimize the KL divergence over diffused
097   actions produced by the generator, with the gradient of the KL expressed as a score difference loss. By
098   initializing the action generator and the generator score network with the identical pre-trained model,
099   our method not only preserves or enhances the performance of the original model, but also requires
100   only 2%-10% additional pre-training cost for the distillation to converge. We compare our method
101   with CP and demonstrate that it outperforms CP with a higher success rate across tasks, leveraging a
102   single-step action generator and achieving 20× faster convergence. A detailed comparison with this
103   approach is provided in Sections 3 and 4.
104   We evaluate our method in both simulated and real-world environments. In simulated experiments,
105   we test OneDP on the six most challenging tasks of the Robomimic benchmark (Mandlekar et al.,
106   2021). For real-world experiments, we design four tasks with increasing difficulty and deploy OneDP
107   on a Franka robot arm. In both settings, OneDP demonstrated state-of-the-art success rates with
      single-step generation, performing 42× faster in inference.

      2.1    P RELIMINARIES

112   Diffusion models are powerful generative models applied across various domains (Ho et al., 2020;
113   Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015; Song et al., 2020b). They function by defining a forward diffusion process
114   that gradually corrupts the data distribution into a known noise distribution. Given a data distribution
115   p(x), the forward process adds Gaussian noise to samples, x0 ∼ p(x), with each step defined as
116   xk = αk x0 + σk ϵk , where ϵk ∼ N (0, I). The parameters αk and σk are manually designed and
117
      vary according to different noise scheduling strategies.
118   A probabilistic model pθ (xk−1 |xk ) is then trained to reverse this diffusion process, enabling data
119   generation from pure noise. DDPM (Ho et al., 2020) uses discrete-time scheduling with a noise-
120   prediction model ϵθ to parameterize pθ , while EDM (Karras et al., 2022) employs continuous-time
121   diffusion with x0 -prediction. We use epsilon prediction ϵθ in our derivation. The diffusion model is
122   trained using the denoising score matching loss (Ho et al., 2020; Song et al., 2020b).
123   Once trained, we can estimate the unknown score s(xk ) at a diffused sample xk as:

144   Action sampling through the vanilla diffusion policies is notoriously slow due to the need of tens to
145   hundreds of iterative inference steps. The latency issue is critical for computationally sensitive robotic
146   tasks or tasks that require high control frequency. Although employing advanced ODE solvers (Song
147   et al., 2020a; Karras et al., 2022) could help speed up the sampling procedure, empirically at least ten
148   iterative steps are required to ensure reasonable performance. Here, we introduce a training-based
149   diffusion policy distillation method, which distills the knowledge of a pre-trained diffusion policy
150
      into a single-step action generator, enabling fast action sampling.
151   We propose a one-step implicit action generator Gθ , from which actions can be easily obtained as
152   follows,

252   Diffusion Policy. Following Chi et al. (2023), we construct a diffusion policy using a 1D temporal
253   convolutional neural network (CNN) (Janner et al., 2022) based U-Net and a standard ResNet18
254   (without pre-training) (He et al., 2016) as the vision encoder. We implement the diffusion policy
255   with two noise scheduling methods: DDPM (Ho et al., 2020) and EDM (Karras et al., 2022). We
256   use ϵ noise prediction for discrete-time (100 steps) diffusion and x0 prediction for continuous-time
257   diffusion, respectively. The EDM scheduling is essential for Consistency Policy (Prasad et al., 2024)
258   due to the use of CTM (Kim et al., 2023a). For DDPM, we set λ(k) = 1 and use the original SDE and
                                                                                     σ 2 +σ 2
259   DDIM (Song et al., 2020a) sampling. For EDM, we use the default λ(k) = (σkk σd )d2 with σd = 0.5.
260   We use the second-order EDM sampler, which requires two neural network forwards per discretized
261   step in the ODE.

      We evaluate OneDP on a wide variety of tasks in both simulated and real environments. In the
318   following sections, we first report the evaluation results in simulation across six tasks that include
319   different complexity levels. Then we demonstrate the results in the real environment by deploying
320   OneDP in the real world with a Franka robot arm for object pick-and-place tasks and a coffee-machine

      with single-step generation, yielding a 0% success rate for all tasks. DP with 10 steps under DDIM

497   Diffusion Policies. Diffusion models have shown promising results as policy representations for
498   control tasks. Janner et al. (2022) introduced a trajectory-level diffusion model that predicts all
499   timesteps of a plan simultaneously by denoising two-dimensional arrays of state and action pairs.
500   Wang et al. (2022) proposed Diffusion Q-learning, which leverages a conditional diffusion model to
501
      represent the policy in offline reinforcement learning. An action-space diffusion model is trained to
      generate actions conditioned on the states. Similarly, Chi et al. (2023) used a conditional diffusion
502
      model in the robot action space to represent the visuomotor policy and demonstrated a significant
503
      performance boost in imitation learning for various robotics tasks. Ze et al. (2024) further incorporated
504   the power of a compact 3D visual representations to improve diffusion policies in robotics.
505
506   Diffusion Distillations. Although diffusion models are powerful, their iterative denoising process
507
      makes them inherently slow in generation, which poses challenges for time-sensitive applications
      like robotics and real-time control. Motivated by the need to accelerate diffusion models, diffusion
508
      distillation has become an active research topic in image generation. Diffusion distillation aims to
509
      train a student model that can generate samples with fewer denoising steps by distilling knowledge
510   from a pre-trained teacher model (Salimans & Ho, 2022; Luhman & Luhman, 2021; Zheng et al.,
511   2023; Song et al., 2023; Kim et al., 2023b). Salimans & Ho (2022) proposed a method to distill a
512   teacher model into a new model that takes half the number of sampling steps, which can be further
513   reduced by progressively applying this procedure. Song et al. (2023) introduced consistency models
514   that enable fewer step sampling by enforcing self-consistency of the ODE trajectories. CTM (Kim
515   et al., 2023b) improved consistency models and provided the flexibility to trade-off quality and speed.
516   (Luo et al., 2024; Yin et al., 2024) leverage the success of stochastic distillation sampling (Poole
517   et al., 2022) in text-to-3D and proposes KL-based score distillation for image generation. Beyond
518
      KL, Zhou et al. (2024) proposes the SiD distillation technique derived from Fisher Divergence.
      However, leveraging diffusion distillation to accelerate diffusion policies for robotics remains an
519
      underexplored and pressing challenge, particularly for real-time control applications. Consistency
520
      Policy (Prasad et al., 2024) explored applying CTM to reduce the number of denoising steps and
521   accelerate inference of the diffusion policies. It simplifies the original CTM training by ignoring
522   the adversarial auxiliary loss. While this approach achieves a considerable speed-up, it leads to
523   performance degradation compared to pre-trained models, and its complex training process and slow
524   convergence present challenges for robotics applications. In contrast, OneDP employs expectational
525   reverse KL optimization to distill a powerful one-step action generator, achieving comparable or
526   higher success rates than the original diffusion policy, while converging 20× faster.

529   5    C ONCLUSION
530
531   In this paper, we introduced the One-Step Diffusion Policy (OneDP) through advanced diffusion
532   distillation techniques. We enhanced the slow, iterative action prediction process of Diffusion Policy
533   by reducing it to a single-step process, dramatically decreasing action inference time and enabling
534   the robot to respond quickly to environmental changes. Through extensive simulation and real-world
535   experiments, we demonstrate that OneDP not only achieves a slightly higher success rate, but also
536
      responds quickly and effectively to environmental interference. The rapid action prediction further
      allows the robot to complete tasks more efficiently.
537
538   However, this work has some limitations. In the experiments, we did not test OneDP on long-horizon
539   real-world tasks. Furthermore, in the real-world experiments, we limited the robot’s operation
      frequency to 20 Hz for controlling stability, which underutilized OneDP ’s full potential. Additionally,

914   Diffusion models are robust generative models utilized across various domains (Ho et al., 2020;
915   Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015; Song et al., 2020b). They operate by establishing a forward diffusion
916   process that incrementally transforms the data distribution into a known noise distribution, such as
917   standard Gaussian noise. A probabilistic model is then trained to methodically reverse this diffusion
      process, enabling the generation of data samples from pure noise.

987    Comparison with VSD. VSD is designed to distill image-level knowledge from powerful 2D
988    priors, specifically pretrained text-to-image diffusion models, to facilitate 3D content generation.
989    Its overarching objective—reverse KL optimization—is widely applied across multiple domains,
990    including VAEs. In this work, we also apply reverse KL optimization for diffusion policy distillation.
991    However, the implementation and derivation for different domains required major efforts. This
992    extensive process involved adjustments to noise scheduling (DDPM and EDM), proper initialization,
993    balancing the convergence of the generator and its score network, tuning parameters, designing
994
       experiments in dynamic environments, and conducting both simulated and real-world robotics
       experiments—an undertaking that should not be underestimated. Furthermore, OneDP considered
995
       temporal control characteristics by predicting action chunks, each comprising a sequence of actions
996
       (K=16). This approach addresses the temporal dependencies inherent in many robotics tasks, which
997    are not considered in VSD.

1001   Training Cost Comparison of OneDP-D and OneDP-S. OneDP-S and OneDP-D differ in their
1002
       computational requirements. The training cost for OneDP-S is approximately twice that of OneDP-D,
       due to the inclusion of the generator score network. When accounting for evaluation during training,
1003
       the total time for OneDP-S is about 1.5 times longer than that of OneDP-D. For example, on the
1004
       small dataset PushT, training and evaluation for OneDP-D take about 30 minutes, while OneDP-S
1005   requires approximately 45 minutes. On the larger ToolHang dataset, OneDP-D takes roughly 6 hours,
1006   compared to about 8 hours for OneDP-S. These details will be further elaborated in future revisions
1007   to provide a comprehensive view of the trade-offs between stochastic and deterministic policies in
1008   terms of both performance and computational efficiency.
