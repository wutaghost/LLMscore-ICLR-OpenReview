011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            The rapid proliferation of generative AI, especially large language models
014            (LLMs), has led to their integration into a variety of applications. A key phe-
015            nomenon known as weak-to-strong generalization – where a strong model trained
016            on a weak model’s outputs surpasses the weak model in task performance – has
017            gained significant attention. Yet, whether critical trustworthiness properties such
018            as robustness, fairness, and privacy can generalize similarly remains an open ques-
019            tion. In this work, we study this question by examining if a stronger model can
020
               inherit trustworthiness properties when fine-tuned on a weaker model’s outputs,
               a process we term weak-to-strong trustworthiness generalization. Specifically,
021
               we examine whether a strong model can inherit or even enhance trustworthiness
022
               attributes when fine-tuned on a weak model’s outputs. To address this, we in-
023            troduce two foundational training strategies: 1) Weak Trustworthiness Finetuning
024            (Weak TFT), which leverages trustworthiness regularization during the fine-tuning
025            of the weak model, and 2) Weak and Weak-to-Strong Trustworthiness Finetuning
026            (Weak+WTS TFT), which extends regularization to both weak and strong models.
027            Our experimental evaluation on real-world datasets (Adult, OOD Style Transfer,
028            AdvGLUE++, and Enron Emails) reveals that while some trustworthiness proper-
029            ties, such as fairness, adversarial, and OOD robustness, show significant improve-
030            ment in transfer when both models were regularized, others like privacy do not
031
               exhibit signs of weak-to-strong trustworthiness. As the first study to explore trust-
               worthiness generalization via weak-to-strong generalization, our work provides
032
               valuable insights into the potential and limitations of this method. Our findings
033
               highlight the importance of systematically studying trustworthiness transfer to de-
034            velop AI systems that are not only accurate but also ethically aligned and reliable
035            in critical applications.

039   1   I NTRODUCTION
040
041
      Over the past few years, there has been a rapid proliferation of generative artificial intelligence
042   (AI), particularly large language models (LLMs) like GPT-3, GPT-4, and their successors. These
043   models have demonstrated remarkable capabilities across a wide range of tasks, including language
044   comprehension (Radford et al., 2019), reasoning (Bubeck et al., 2023) and tabular data generation
045   (Borisov et al., 2023). Their emergent behaviors – unexpected capabilities that arise as models scale
046   – have captured the attention of both academia and industry, leading to widespread adoption and
047   integration into various applications (Wei et al., 2022; Schaeffer et al., 2024).
048   One intriguing key phenomenon observed in LLMs is known as weak-to-strong generalization
049   (Burns et al., 2024). In this context, a “weak” model, typically smaller or less capable, is used
050   to supervise the training of a larger-sized “weak-to-strong” model. Remarkably, this larger model
051   often surpasses the weak model in performance, even when trained solely on the weak model’s out-
052   puts. For example, prior research has shown that when a large model is fine-tuned on the predictions
053   of a smaller teacher model for tasks like sentiment analysis or machine translation, the larger model
      not only learns the task but also generalizes better to new, unseen data (Burns et al., 2024).

066   While performance improvements are valuable, trustworthiness has emerged as a critical aspect of
067   AI systems, especially as LLMs are increasingly deployed in high-stakes domains like healthcare,
068   finance, and legal services (Wang et al., 2023). Trustworthiness encompasses properties such as
069   fairness (avoiding biases against certain groups), privacy (protecting sensitive information), and ro-
070   bustness (maintaining performance under adversarial conditions or distribution shifts). Ensuring
071
      these properties is essential to prevent harmful outcomes, comply with regulations, and maintain
      public confidence in AI technologies. Given the importance of trustworthiness, a vital yet unex-
072
      plored question arises.
073
074
            Research Question: Can a strong model inherit or potentially enhance fairness, privacy,
075
           and robustness from a weak model through fine-tuning on trustworthy weak model outputs?
076
077
078
      In this work, we investigate this critical question by exploring the generalization of trustworthiness
      properties from weak to strong models, a process we term weak-to-strong trustworthiness general-
079
      ization. We specifically examine whether a strong model can inherit and enhance the trustworthiness
080
      properties of a weak model, in addition to improving task performance. To this end, we introduce
081   two novel fine-tuning strategies aimed at enhancing trustworthiness transfer. First, we apply trust-
082   worthiness regularization during the fine-tuning of the weak model only. This involves modifying
083   the loss function of the weak model to include fairness, robustness or privacy constraints. We refer to
084   this training strategy as Weak Trustworthiness Fine-tuning (Weak TFT). Second, in addition to using
085   a trustworthiness regularized weak model, we also add trustworthiness regularization to the weak-
086   to-strong transfer where we finetune the weak-to-strong model using a trustworthiness regularizer on
087   the trustworthy weak labels. We call this second strategy Weak and Weak-to-Strong Trustworthiness
088   Fine-tuning (Weak+WTS TFT). These training strategies are summarized in Figure 1.
089   We conduct rigorous empirical experiments using the Pythia model suite (Biderman et al., 2023) to
090   evaluate these training strategies on multiple real-world datasets including Adult (fairness), OOD
091   Style Transfer (OOD robustness), AdvGLUE++ (adversarial robustness), and Enron Emails (pri-
092   vacy). Our results demonstrate that while naive fine-tuning of the strong model on the standard weak
093   model outputs only leads to limited weak-to-strong trustworthiness, our proposed training strategies
094   significantly enhance weak-to-strong trustworthiness. Specifically, strong models not only retain but
095
      also consistently amplify fairness and robustness properties when both models are regularized. In
      summary, our contributions can be summarized as follows:
096
097          • Novel Trustworthy Learning Paradigm: This is the first work to investigate if trustworthi-
098            ness properties can transfer from a weak to a strong model using weak-to-strong supervision,
099            a process we term weak-to-strong trustworthiness generalization.
100          • Foundational Training Strategies for Weak-to-Strong Trustworthiness Generalization:
101            We introduce two baseline training strategies, Weak TFT and Weak+WTS TFT, designed to
102            facilitate weak-to-strong trustworthiness generalization.
103          • Weak-to-Strong Trustworthiness Generalization is Feasible: Our experiments show that
104            some trustworthiness properties can indeed be generalized and even enhanced from weak to
105            strong models.
106
107   Our findings provide new insights into systematically transferring and scaling trustworthiness prop-
      erties from weaker to stronger models. They suggest a viable pathway for developing trustworthy AI


                                                                                2
108
      systems without requiring full access to model internals or extensive human supervision. By demon-
109   strating that trustworthiness can be effectively inherited and even enhanced through weak-to-strong
110   generalization, we contribute to the foundational understanding necessary for aligning powerful AI
111   systems with ethical principles.

114   2   R ELATED W ORK
115
116
117   This work is the first to leverage regularization techniques to study if trustworthiness properties
118
      transfer from a weak to a strong model, and one of the first to study weak to strong generalization in
      large language models. Below we discuss related works for each of these topics.
119
120   Fairness. Unfair outcomes can arise in language models when they inadvertently encode biases
121   present in the training data, leading to discriminatory practices against certain groups based on sen-
122   sitive attributes like race, gender, or age (Bolukbasi et al., 2016). Recent efforts to improve fairness
123   in LLMs include data pre-processing, post-processing, and adversarial training such as augmenting
124
      training data to balance gender representations (Zhao et al., 2018) and debiasing word embeddings
      (Huang et al., 2020). Our study sets itself apart by focusing on fine-tuning LLMs using a mod-
125
      ified loss function explicitly designed to enhance fairness. Unlike approaches that treat fairness
126
      constraints separately or apply post-processing adjustments, we integrate fairness directly into the
127   model’s learning objective during fine-tuning.
128
129   Out-of-distribution robustness. Out-of-distribution robustness describes a model’s ability to per-
130
      form well on inputs that differ from its training distribution. Arora et al. (2021) identify two types
      of OOD scenarios: 1) semantic shift, where new classes appear at test time, and 2) background
131
      shift, where domain or style changes affect the input’s presentation without altering core semantics.
132
      Various methods aim to enhance OOD robustness, including data augmentation techniques like ad-
133   versarial perturbations (Madry, 2017; Lecuyer et al., 2019), EDA (Wei & Zou, 2019), and FreeLB
134   (Zhu et al., 2019), as well as training modifications like label smoothing (Szegedy et al., 2016) and
135   focal loss (Lin, 2017). However, recent research has shown that many of these methods do not reli-
136   ably improve OOD robustness and may even degrade performance on in-distribution tasks; standard
137   fine-tuning often remains a strong baseline (Yuan et al., 2023). In this work, we employ adversarial
138   perturbation as a representative robustness technique. Unlike prior approaches, we focus on gen-
139   eralizing OOD robustness from weaker models to stronger ones, both with and without the use of
140   robustness-enhancing regularization.
141   Adversarial robustness. Machine learning model outputs can be changed by introducing minimal
142   perturbations to a benign input, causing the model to malfunction (Szegedy et al., 2014; Goodfellow
143   et al., 2015; Madry et al., 2018). Several adversarial attack algorithms have been developed that
144   can degrade a large language model’s performance on natural language processing tasks such as
145   sentiment analysis, question answering, text classification, and entailment (Jin et al., 2020; Zang
146   et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2020; Li et al., 2020; Garg & Ramakrishnan, 2020). Our work differs from
147
      these existing studies and is the first to examine if adversarial robustness properties can transfer from
      a small model to a larger model trained on the outputs of the small model.
148
149   Model distillation and privacy. Prior research has explored the use of knowledge distillation as a
150   mechanism to mitigate privacy attacks. One of the most prominent examples is the PATE framework
151   (Papernot et al., 2016), where knowledge distillation is employed to reduce an ensemble of teacher
152   models into a single model with provable privacy guarantees (Dwork et al., 2006). Other works have
153
      built on this idea, such as Zheng et al. (2021) and Tang et al. (2022) who construct privacy-preserving
      model ensembles and then use distillation to consolidate these models. In these approaches, knowl-
154
      edge distillation is often one component of a larger privacy-preserving model, which helps to build
155
      models with privacy guarantees. Some research suggests that distillation alone can serve as an ef-
156   fective privacy defense (Shejwalkar & Houmansadr, 2021). Building on this, Mazzone et al. (2022)
157   investigate the use of repeated distillation to protect against membership inference attacks. How-
158   ever, Jagielski et al. (2024) demonstrate through privacy attacks that distilled models without privacy
159   guarantees can still leak sensitive information. In contrast to prior work, our research focuses on the
160   privacy implications of weak-to-strong training, where a large model is trained on the outputs of a
161   smaller model. This approach is the inverse of traditional model distillation, where smaller models
      are typically trained using the outputs of larger models. Relatively little is known about the privacy


                                                         3
162
      risks and benefits when this process is reversed, making our investigation an important contribution
163   to the field.

166   3     M ETHODOLOGY
167
168
      We now present our methodology for investigating the generalization of trustworthiness properties
      from weak to strong models. Our approach systematically explores whether and how fairness, pri-
169
      vacy, and robustness can be effectively generalized from weaker to stronger models. The broader
170
      issue we address is: Under what conditions can a weaker model, despite its limitations, most effec-
171   tively transfer properties such as fairness, privacy, and robustness to a more powerful model? We
172   begin by outlining the weak-to-strong training process, followed by techniques for eliciting specific
173   trustworthiness properties in language models. Finally, we introduce a simple yet effective three-
174   stage training approach that allows us to examine weak-to-strong trustworthiness generalization
175   under different fine-tuning strategies.

      3.2   E LICITING W EAK - TO -S TRONG T RUSTWORTHINESS IN L ARGE L ANGUAGE M ODELS
262
263   We break the analysis into three stages, each building on the last by varying the regularization
264   applied to the weak and weak-to-strong models.
265
266
      No trustworthiness finetuning (No TFT). In this phase, we establish baseline performance by
      training the weak, strong, and weak-to-strong models without applying any trustworthiness regular-
267
      ization, following the approach outlined in Burns et al. (2024):
268
269   • Weak model: We use small, pretrained LLMs as weak supervisors, referred to as weak mod-
        els. These weak models are finetuned on ground truth labels to generate predictions. Using the


                                                          5
270
        finetuned weak models, we create weak labels by having the weak models make predictions on a
271     held-out validation set.
272
      • Weak-to-strong transfer: To evaluate weak-to-strong generalization, we fine-tune a strong
273
        model using the weak labels generated by the weak model. This model is referred to as the
274     strong student, and its resulting performance is called the weak-to-strong performance.
275
276   Weak trustworthiness finetuning (Weak TFT). In this phase, we explore whether a trustworthiness
277
      regularized weak model can influence the trustworthiness property of a vanilla strong model trained
      solely on the output of the trustworthy weak model:
278
279   • Trustworthy weak model: We use small, pre-trained LLMs as weak supervisors, but unlike in
280     Phase 1, these weak models are fine-tuned on ground truth labels using a trustworthiness regu-
281     larizer. This regularizer enforces specific trustworthiness properties, such as fairness, privacy, or
282     robustness, during fine-tuning. These models are referred to as trustworthy weak models. Using
283
        these models, we generate weak labels by making predictions on a held-out validation set.
284   • Weak-to-strong transfer: To assess whether trustworthiness properties can be transferred from
285     a weak to a strong model, we fine-tune a vanilla strong model using the weak labels generated by
286
        the trustworthy weak model.
287   Weak and weak-to-strong trustworthiness finetuning (Weak+WTS TFT). In the final phase,
288   we investigate whether adding trustworthiness regularization to both the weak and weak-to-strong
289   models can further enhance trust transfer (and performance).
290   • Trustworthy weak model: The trustworthy weak model is the same as in Phase 2, where the
291     weak model is fine-tuned on ground truth labels using a trustworthiness regularizer to enforce
292     properties like fairness, privacy, or robustness.
293   • Trustworthy weak-to-strong transfer: In this step, we directly assess how well trustworthiness
294     properties can be transferred from the weak model to the strong model. Unlike in Phase 2, where
295     the strong model was fine-tuned without any regularization, here we finetune the strong model
296     using a trustworthiness regularizer on the weak labels generated by the trustworthy weak model.

301   In Section 4.1, we empirically evaluate the effectiveness of generalizing trustworthiness properties
302   from a weak to a strong model using the three weak-to-strong training strategies introduced in the
303   previous section. Then, in Section 4.2, we perform a thorough sensitivity analysis, varying the
304   trustworthiness regularization strength, model size, and key hyperparameters specific to weak-to-
305   strong transfer training. We begin by describing the real-world datasets used in our experiments,
306   followed by an overview of the LLMs and relevant baselines used for comparison.
307   Datasets. We evaluate the transfer of trustworthiness properties from small models to large models
308   using four datasets, previously explored by Wang et al. (2023), including the Enron Email dataset
309   (Klimt & Yang, 2004), the Adult dataset (Ding et al., 2021), the OOD Style Transfer dataset (Wang
310   et al., 2023), and the AdvGLUE++ dataset (Wang et al., 2023). For all datasets, we show average
311   results over multiple runs and usually report ±1 standard deviation across runs.
312   • Enron Emails: This dataset contains over 600,000 emails generated by employees of the Enron
313     Corporation. This dataset includes sensitive personal information, such as email addresses, phone
314     numbers, credit card numbers, and Social Security Numbers, which could be memorized and
315     extracted by language models. For finetuning, we randomly subsampled 10,000 data points.
316   • Adult: This dataset is derived from the 1994 U.S. Census database and contains 48,842 instances
317     with 14 attributes. The task is to classify whether an individual’s income exceeds $50,000 (USD)
318     per year. We use the reconstructed Adult dataset provided by Ding et al. (2021) and selected the
319     “sex” feature as the protected attribute to evaluate fairness-related properties.

322     guage style, vocabulary, syntax, and tone) are applied at both the word and sentence level while
323     preserving the original meaning. For instance, some transformations involve substituting words
        with Shakespearean equivalents. The task is to correctly classify the sentiment of inputs.

404   We present our results for all four trustworthiness properties across the three phases in Figure 2. For
405
      each property, we used Pythia 14M as the weak model and Pythia 410M as the strong model.
406   No TFT. In the initial No TFT phase, where models are trained without any trustworthiness regu-
407   larization, we expected no clear trustworthy weak-to-strong transfer trends, as no regularization is
408   in place to explicitly enforce trustworthiness properties. Surprisingly, for both OOD and adversarial
409   robustness evaluation we observe that the models demonstrate a trustworthy weak-to-strong trend.
410   Despite the absence of regularization, the stronger models exhibited improved robustness compared
411
      to the weaker models, suggesting that some trustworthiness properties may naturally transfer even
      without explicit constraints. For fairness, we do not observe a trustworthy weak-to-strong trend.
412
      The level of unfairness remains constant, regardless of whether we examine the weak model or the
413
      weak-to-strong transfer model.
414
415   Weak TFT. In the Weak TFT phase, regularization is applied to the weak models, which, as ex-
416   pected, improves their trustworthiness in terms of fairness, OOD robustness, and adversarial robust-
417
      ness compared to the No TFT phase. This improvement aligns with our expectations, as the weak
      models are now being explicitly regularized to enhance their trustworthiness. The only trustwor-
418
      thiness weak-to-strong trend observed in the Weak TFT phase pertains to OOD robustness. In this
419
      case, the improvement is substantial, increasing robust accuracy from 72% to 75%, representing
420   a gain of 3 percentage points. For all other cases, we do not observe weak-to-strong trends. For
421   privacy, while we do not observe a trustworthy weak-to-strong trend, the gap in extraction rates
422   between the WTS-Naive model and the weak model widens. This widening occurs because the
423   smaller model benefits from privacy regularization, which limits the leakage of information about
424   the training data. In contrast, the WTS-Naive model is trained without any regularization on a sec-
425   ond, disjoint dataset, meaning the privacy guarantees from the first model’s training phase do not
426   apply. As a result, without explicit regularization during WTS training, the extraction rate for the
427   explicitly regularized weak model decreases much more rapidly, dropping from 18% to 14%. In
428
      contrast, the implicitly regularized WTS-Naive model, trained on the outputs of the weak model,
      experiences a much smaller decline in extraction rate, from 45% to 44%.
429
430   Weak+WTS TFT. The Weak+WTS TFT phase introduces an additional layer of trustworthiness
431   to the weak-to-strong transfer process, as the training of the WTS model itself is now regularized
      on top of the existing regularization applied to the weak model in the Weak TFT phase. With both

      4.2   S ENSITIVITY A NALYSIS
469
470   In this section, we conduct a comprehensive sensitivity analysis to explore how various pa-
471   rameter values influence the transfer of trustworthiness properties from weak to strong mod-
472   els. Specifically, we examine the impact of different model sizes and the regularization strength
473   (λFair , λAdv , λOOD , λP ) in the trustworthiness loss functions. We continue the sensitivity analysis for
474   the auxiliary loss weighting parameter (α) used during weak-to-strong transfer in Appendix A. This
475   analysis aims to validate the robustness of our findings from the previous section and to understand
476
      the conditions under which weak-to-strong trustworthiness transfer is most effective.
477   Sensitivity to Model Size. To assess the effect of model capacity on trustworthiness transfer, we
478   experimented with different combinations of weak and strong model sizes. We analyzed experi-
479   ments for four weak/strong configurations: Pythia 14M/410M, Pythia 14M/1B, Pythia 70M/410M,
480   Pythia 70M/1B. Our analysis reveals that the weak-to-strong trends observed in the previous section
481   generally hold across these model sizes for most trustworthiness properties. Specifically, for fair-
482
      ness and OOD robustness, the strong models continued to inherit and, in some cases, enhance the
      trustworthiness attributes from the weak models across all configurations (Figure A3, Figure A5).
483
484   However, we observed a disruption of the weak-to-strong trend for adversarial robustness when
485   using 70M as the weak model. The weak-to-strong trend in adversarial robustness was disrupted
      in the Weak+WTS TFT phase in the 70M/410M and 70M/1B configurations; the strong models did


                                                          9
486
       not exhibit the expected improvement in adversarial robustness over the weak models (Figure A4).
487    This contrasts with the results from using a 14M weak model, where the strong models did show
488    enhanced adversarial robustness. This disruption suggests that as the weak model becomes more
489    capable, the transfer of adversarial robustness to even stronger models may not follow the same
490    patterns. One possible explanation is that the strong model may already possess sufficient capacity
491    to capture adversarial robustness independently, or the differences in model capacities may affect
492    the dynamics of knowledge transfer. On the other hand, increasing the weak model size from 14M
493    to 70M generally led to improvements in the weak models trustworthiness during the Weak TFT and
494    Weak+WTS TFT phases for both OOD robustness and adversarial robustness. This is expected, as
495
       larger weak models have greater capacity to learn complex patterns and trustworthiness properties,
       providing better supervision for the strong models.
496
497    Sensitivity to Regularization Strength (λ). We also investigated how varying the regularization
498    strength in the trustworthiness loss functions affects the transfer of trustworthiness properties. For
499    each property—fairness, robustness, and privacy—we experimented with a range of λ values to ob-
500    serve their impact on both the weak and strong models. The trustworthiness weak-to-strong trends
501
       described in the previous section maintained across λ values in the Weak+WTS TFT phase. The
       plots of trustworthiness metrics against varying lambda values showed consistent improvements
502
       in the WTS-Naive and WTS-Aux-Loss models’ trustworthiness attributes when both weak and
503
       WTS models were regularized (Figure 3). This consistency suggests that the effectiveness of the
504    Weak+WTS TFT approach is robust to the choice of lambda, provided it is within a reasonable
505    range. Moving from the Weak TFT to the Weak+WTS TFT phase generally made the weak-to-
506    strong trends more apparent across different lambda values. This behavior confirms our analysis
507    in Section 4.1 that weak-to-strong trends are enhanced with increased regularization. Applying
508    trustworthiness regularization to both the weak and strong models amplified the transfer of trustwor-
509    thiness properties from Figure A2 to Figure 3.
510
511    5   C ONCLUSION
512
513    In this paper, we have investigated the critical question of whether trustworthiness properties such as
514    fairness, robustness, and privacy can be transferred from weak to strong models via weak-to-strong
515    generalization. We termed this transfer process weak-to-strong trustworthiness, and introduced two
516    novel approaches aimed at enhancing this transfer. First, Weak Trustworthiness Finetuning (Weak
517    TFT) applies trustworthiness regularization during the fine-tuning of the weak model. Second, Weak
518    and Weak-to-Strong Trustworthiness Finetuning (Weak+WTS TFT) extends this regularization to
519    both the weak and strong models during fine-tuning. Our comprehensive experimental evaluation
520
       across real-world datasets reveals that certain trustworthiness properties, namely fairness, adversar-
       ial robustness, and out-of-distribution (OOD) robustness, show significant improvement in transfer
521
       when both models are regularized. However, we observed that privacy did not exhibit signs of weak-
522
       to-strong trustworthiness, highlighting the nuanced nature of transferring different trustworthiness
523    attributes.
524
525    Future Directions. Our study offers several open avenues for future exploration:
526
      1. Scope of trustworthiness properties: While we focused on fairness, robustness, and privacy, other
527
         critical trustworthiness aspects such as explainability, accountability, and alignment with human
528      values were not examined. Future work should consider a broader spectrum of trustworthiness
529      attributes to provide a more holistic understanding.
530
      2. Privacy transfer: The lack of observed weak-to-strong transfer for privacy suggests that privacy
531      preservation may require fundamentally different approaches. Future research should explore
532      mechanisms that enable privacy properties to transfer or develop new strategies that ensure privacy
533      in the context of weak-to-strong generalization.
534
      3. Evaluation Metrics: The metrics used to assess trustworthiness properties may not capture all
535      facets of these complex attributes. Developing more comprehensive evaluation frameworks would
536      provide deeper insights into the models’ behavior.
537
538    Our work is the first to systematically explore the transfer of trustworthiness properties via weak-to-
539    strong generalization. By emphasizing the potential of this approach, our study provides valuable
       insights and lays the groundwork for future research in this area.

       C.2      A DDITIONAL A DVERSARIAL ROBUSTNESS DATASET D ETAILS
1230
1231   In this section, we evaluate the adversarial robustness of the weak-to-strong models and compare
1232   with the weak baseline and the strong ceiling. We use Pythia 14M as the weak model and Pythia
1233   410M as the strong model. We create training, holdout and test subsets of the AdvGLUE++ dataset
1234   using 40%, 40% and 20% of samples, respectively, from each task in the dataset. We use the training
1235   subset to fine-tune our models to be adversarially robust. We use the holdout subset to generate labels
1236   from the weak model to be used in the weak-to-strong training process. To evaluate the clean and
1237   adversarial accuracy of our models, we evaluate them on a test subset of the AdvGLUE++ dataset
1238
       and average the performance across the six NLP tasks in this dataset.
1239   In particular, to evaluate weak-to-strong trends in adversarial robustness, we use the AdvGLUE++
1240   dataset (Wang et al., 2023), an extension of the AdvGLUE dataset (Wang et al., 2021). Ad-
1241   vGLUE++ is a comprehensive benchmark designed to test adversarial robustness across multiple
       natural language processing (NLP) tasks and adversarial attack algorithms. This dataset includes


                                                          23
1242
       adversarial examples for six widely used NLP tasks, each representing a distinct domain or linguis-
1243   tic challenge. The Stanford Sentiment Treebank (SST-2) task involves sentiment analysis, requiring
1244   the classification of sentences as having a positive or negative sentiment. The Quora Question Pairs
1245   (QQP) task identifies whether two questions convey the same meaning. The Multi-Genre Natural
1246   Language Inference (MNLI) task requires reasoning about entailment, contradiction, or neutrality
1247   between pairs of sentences. It includes a mismatched variant, MNLI-mm, where validation and
1248   test data originate from out-of-domain sources, increasing the challenge of generalization. The
1249   Question-answering NLI (QNLI) task is framed as an entailment problem between a question and
1250   an answer candidate. The Recognizing Textual Entailment (RTE) is a binary entailment task that
1251
       aims to determine whether the meaning of one text can be inferred from another.
1252   Adversarial examples in AdvGLUE++ are generated using a variety of attack algorithms, each
1253   representing a distinct perturbation strategy. TextBugger introduces typo-based perturbations that
1254   minimally alter characters while preserving the utility of benign text. TextFooler generates embed-
1255   ding similarity-based perturbations by substituting words with contextually plausible alternatives.
1256   BERT-ATTACK leverages BERT’s language modeling capabilities to create context-aware adver-
1257
       sarial samples. SememePSO relies on semantic representations and combinatorial optimization to
       generate knowledge-guided perturbations. SemAttack employs semantic optimization-based tech-
1258
       niques by manipulating various semantic spaces to produce natural-looking adversarial texts.
1259
1260   The experimental results for adversarial robustness are presented as aggregated accuracy values
1261   across all six tasks and five attack algorithms. This approach enables us to evaluate the weak-
1262   to-strong trends in a comprehensive and robust manner. The results show that our findings are
1263
       consistent across a wide range of NLP tasks and adversarial attacks, indicating that they are not
       influenced by the specific characteristics of any single setting.

       C.3   A DDITIONAL OOD DATASET D ETAILS
1267
       We use the same OOD data created by Wang et al. (2023). For ID data, we use the original SST-2
