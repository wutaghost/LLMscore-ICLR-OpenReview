009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            We focus on species distribution modeling using global-scale presence-only data,
012            leveraging geographical and environmental features to map species ranges, as in
013            previous studies. However, we innovate by integrating taxonomic classification
014            into our approach. Specifically, we propose using a large language model to ex-
015            tract a latent representation of the taxonomic classification from a textual prompt.
016
               This allows us to map the range of any taxonomic rank, including unseen species,
               without additional supervision. We also present a new proximity-aware evalua-
017
               tion metric, suitable for evaluating species distribution models, which addresses
018
               critical shortcomings of traditional metrics. We evaluated our model for species
019            range prediction, zero-shot prediction, and geo-feature regression and found that it
020            outperforms several state-of-the-art models. We will share code, data, and model
021            checkpoints after acceptance.

024   1   I NTRODUCTION
025
026   Species distribution modeling (SDM) is a challenging remote-sensing task that involves establish-
027
      ing the relationship between geographical, environmental, and biophysical conditions and species
      presence. The goal of SDM is to produce large-scale range maps for species. Such maps can then
028
      be used to develop tools for modeling habitat suitability, predicting future distributions, etc. These
029
      tools are essential for biodiversity conservation and understanding the environmental effects of cli-
030   mate change (Beery et al., 2021).
031
032   Traditionally, species ranges were mapped using geographical and environmental features. The
033
      approaches were either limited to single-species models or modeled a fixed set of species. While the
      current approaches have their merits, we contend they fall short in accurately representing the inter-
034
      species relationships inherent in the taxonomic hierarchy. For example, species belonging to the
035
      same genera tend to be found in similar locations. They often share many common characteristics,
036   including their general habitat preferences (Thorson et al., 2016). This pattern diminishes as one
037   moves up in the taxonomic hierarchy. To capture these complex hierarchical relationships between
038   the species, we use large language models (LLMs) to encode their taxonomic information in text (as
039   shown in Figure 1) in the form of species-specific embeddings. Moreover, by leveraging the vast
040   knowledge of LLMs, our approach enhances the identification of rare taxa (Caradima et al., 2019).
041   To our knowledge, no previous work has incorporated LLMs for SDM.
042   Previous approaches to SDM (Mac Aodha et al., 2019; Cole et al., 2023; Lange et al., 2023) tried to
043   answer the question, “Which species are likely to be found at a given location?” However, to incor-
044   porate species-specific rich information into SDM, it is necessary to reformulate the SDM problem.
045   Therefore, in this paper, we reformulate the SDM problem as “Which locations are likely for a given
046   species to be found?” Assuming uniform prior over the species distribution (Mac Aodha et al., 2019;
047   Lange et al., 2023), this is an equivalent formulation. But by doing so, we can now incorporate a
048   variety of information, including species-specific embeddings or any other metadata, which opens
049   up new opportunities for more accurate modeling. Alternatively, this formulation makes it easier to
050
      incorporate unseen species that were not present in the training data.
051   Previously, SDMs were trained using environmental features while ignoring the geographic location
052   aspect. Recently, several works showed the benefit of incorporating geographic locations in various
053   applications including SDM (Cole et al., 2023; Botella et al., 2018; Tang et al., 2018) and fine-
      grained image classification (Ayush et al., 2021; Mac Aodha et al., 2019; Chu et al., 2019). A series

089   2    R ELATED W ORKS
090
091   Species distribution modeling (SDM) methods use environmental, geographic, and species obser-
092   vation data to predict species distribution across geographic space and time (Elith & Leathwick,
093
      2009; Beery et al., 2021). In this context, we restrict ourselves to species range mapping over geo-
      graphic space only. In the past, SDM relied on hand-crafted geographic and environmental features
094
      as input to capture the implicit spatial correlations in data (Elith* et al., 2006; Norberg et al., 2019;
095
      Valavi et al., 2022). These approaches were limited to one species per model due to their reliance
096   on traditional supervised learning algorithms. With advancements in deep neural network architec-
097   tures, end-to-end frameworks can model joint species distributions and complex implicit relations
098   in data (Botella et al., 2018; Chen & Gomes, 2019; Cole et al., 2023).
099
      Another important aspect of SDM is the type of species occurrence data. Species occurrence data
100
      may exist in one of the two forms: presence-only and presence-absence. Collecting presence-
101
      absence observations is challenging, requiring expert surveys especially to confirm the absence of
102   a given species in some region (MacKENZIE, 2005). However, once collected, this kind of data
103   can be easily fed into supervised learning frameworks, without requiring sophisticated modifica-
104   tions (Fink et al., 2010; Chen et al., 2016; Franceschini et al., 2018; Botella et al., 2018; Teng et al.,
105   2023a). With no expert knowledge, presence-only data can be recorded when any species of interest
106   is encountered. This way of collecting data does not require the data collector to confirm absences.
107   However, since there are no negative samples, it is difficult to use standard supervised learning
      frameworks (Chen & Gomes, 2019; Johnston et al., 2020; Botella et al., 2021).

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      Most recent works formulate the problem of SDM using presence-only data as a multi-label classi-
109   fication task (Mac Aodha et al., 2019; Cole et al., 2023). Here, the goal of SDM is to predict the
110   likelihood of species for a given location and environmental features. Many multi-label learning
111   losses have been proposed in the literature that can easily be plugged in for this task. For instance,
112   the AN-full (full assume negative) loss (Mac Aodha et al., 2019) randomly samples locations that
113   are assumed to be negative during training. The ME-full (full maximum entropy) (Zhou et al., 2022)
114   tries to maximize the entropy of predictions of negative samples and at randomly sampled locations
115   during training. Other losses such as ASL (asymmetric loss) (Ridnik et al., 2021), Hill loss (Zhang
116   et al., 2021), and RAL (robust asymmetric loss) (Park et al., 2023) weigh the negative samples in a
117
      way that easy negative and potential false negative samples are down-weighted. Cole et al. (2023)
      evaluated the performance of SDMs trained with losses involving pseudo-negative sampling. We
118
      extend their work by evaluating the impact of training their model with the ASL and RAL losses.
119
120   Several studies have attempted to assess the performance of species distribution models trained on
121   presence-only data (Segurado & Araujo, 2004; Wilson, 2011; Norberg et al., 2019; Konowalik &
122   Nosol, 2021; Valavi et al., 2022). They measure performance using Kappa index, Kullback–Leibler
123
      divergence, area under the curve (AUC), or mean average precision (MAP). However, these metrics
      are inadequate in quantifying the performance of models on pixel-level data. It is worth noting that
124
      none of these metrics consider the spatial proximity of predictions to their ground-truth observations,
125
      hence making them sensitive to spatial shifts in predictions.
126
127   Finally, another line of work uses high-resolution satellite imagery at each species occurrence lo-
128   cation to guide SDM methods (Botella et al., 2019; Cole et al., 2020; Lorieul et al., 2021; 2022;
129
      Sastry et al., 2024; Teng et al., 2023b; Dollinger et al., 2024). For example, (Teng et al., 2023b) uses
      satellite imagery and geolocation to directly predict the likelihood over species while (Sastry et al.,
130
      2024) uses a contrastive learning framework to establish similarity between ground-level images of
131
      species and corresponding satellite imagery. These methods are limited in geographic scale due to
132   the high-resolution nature of raw data (Cole et al., 2023). Except Teng et al. (2023b); Dollinger
133   et al. (2024), all the works view this problem as an information retrieval task rather than a species
134   prediction task.

      3.1   P ROBLEM D EFINITION
140
      We consider presence-only data in the form of occurrence records: D = {(li , yi )| i = 1, ..., N },
141   where li ∈ R2 is the geolocation of each observation and yi ∈ [1, ..., S] is the species of the observed
142   organism. Additionally, a set of environmental features ei ∈ Rn is included for each location li .
143   Many previous studies (Mac Aodha et al., 2019; Lange et al., 2023; Cole et al., 2023) have tackled
144   the task of SDM by training a neural network to model the conditional probability of p(y| l, e). This
145   translates to the problem of “How likely a species can be found at a given location?” However,
146   our approach involves modeling the probability of p(r| y, lr , er ), which answers the question “Is it
147   likely for a given species to be found at a given location?” Here, r ∈ [1, ..., L] is a set of discrete
148   locations that are of interest when predicting the range of species belonging to some class. In this
149
      framework, we define the set of locations r as a contiguous grid that is laid over the surface of the
      globe. The variables lr and er represent fixed parameters in the model, namely the location vectors
150
      and environmental features defined at each location in r respectively. It is easy to see that the input
151
      y and the parameters (lr , er ) can have any arbitrary representations. So, this new formulation allows
152   for flexible encoding of both the species class and geographic location, compared to the previous
153   formulation which only allowed flexibility in representing the geographic location.
154
155

      as input the fixed parameters: lr and er . The network processes the global environmental covariates

189   3.3   S PECIES -S PECIFIC E MBEDDINGS
190
191   One of the novel contributions of this paper is incorporating natural language descriptions of species
192   for SDM. Many popular crowdsourcing science platforms, such as iNaturalist (Van Horn et al.,
193
      2018), contain not just occurrence records, but also valuable species-specific information. This
      information may include the complete taxonomic hierarchy of each species (such as class, order,
194
      family, etc.) or the IUCN red list category (such as endangered, near threatened, etc.). While
195
      often ignored, this fine-grained information could be incorporated to enhance the performance and
196   interpretability of SDMs. In this work, we incorporate the taxonomic hierarchy of species into our
197   SDM in the form of text. Each species is uniquely identified by its taxonomic classification ranks,
198   which can be represented as key-value pairs. For instance, the key-value pairs that represent the
199   wild duck are defined as: t = {class: Aves, order: Anseriformes, family: Anatidae, genus: Anas,
200   species: Anas platyrhynchos}.
201
      Recently, Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated excellent capabilities in representing
202   implicit knowledge, linguistic structure, and hierarchical concepts when trained on massive amounts
203   of data (Anil et al., 2023). With no finetuning, pretrained LLMs have shown impressive zero-shot
204   performance with zero in-domain data. In this work, we use the recently released, LLaMA-2 (Tou-
205   vron et al., 2023) to encode the species-specific key-value pairs as text. We experiment with three
206   variants of LLaMA-2 of which the 70B model produced the best performance.

295   4.2   P ROXIMITY-AWARE E VALUATION
296
297   When predicting species range, the evaluation process typically involves using range maps defined
298
      by experts or curated carefully (Norberg et al., 2019; Valavi et al., 2022). However, these maps can
      often be sparse and difficult to obtain. Alternatively, using crowdsourced species observation data
299
      is challenging since the absence of a given species is not recorded. Intuitively, models with false
300
      predictions close to their true locations are better than those with distant false predictions. This is
301   because models in the former case learn spatially localized species distributions.
302
303   Previous studies have used the area under the curve (AUC) (Norberg et al., 2019; Valavi et al., 2022)
304
      and the mean average precision (MAP) (Cole et al., 2023) to evaluate SDMs. However, these metrics
      ignore the spatial distribution of predictions and weigh them equally, making them highly sensitive
305
      to spatial shifts in the predictions. This makes evaluating SDMs a challenging task since it requires
306
      the comparison of spatially distributed probability values. Pixel-wise comparison methods are not
307   well-suited for this task since they are susceptible to spatial prediction shifts.
308
309   A well-known spatially explicit distance used for comparing any two distributions is the Earth-
310
      Mover (Wasserstein) Distance. Due to its computational requirements and infeasibility on large-
      scale data points, Chamfer Distance is a popularly used proxy (Bakshi et al., 2023). However, it has
311
      only been developed for point clouds and there doesn’t exist a chamfer distance measure to compare
312
      two spatial probability distributions. Inspired by (Wu et al., 2021), we modify their Density-Aware
313   Chamfer Distance (DCD) metric that works for comparing a predicted probability distribution map
314   (P1 ) with a binary probability map (P2 ) representing the ground truth. We add a likelihood prob-
315   ability term which weights the distance term. We call this metric One-Way Probability Weighted
316   Chamfer Distance (PWCD), which measures the false positive rate in a proximity-aware manner.
317   This metric is between [0,1] and could also be used to compute the number of false positives instead
318   of the rate. It is given by:

      5   L IMITATIONS
512
513
      As pointed out in (Cole et al., 2023), the crowdsourced data is biased towards regions with heavy
      human traffic. Most often, rich biodiverse regions are underrepresented limiting the performance
514
      of SDMs. In this work, we do not explicitly deal with the observation bias in presence-only SDM.
515
      However, our goal was to incorporate taxonomic information into SDM to enhance unseen species
516   discovery.
517
518   Previous studies have evaluated species SDMs using expert range maps, which are only available
519
      for a limited number of species. Our proposed evaluation metric allows for the evaluation of SDMs
      using crowdsourced data, which provides a more comprehensive representation of numerous species.
520
      However, we note that none of these methods are error-free. Yet, with our proposed evaluation
521
      metric, innovative methods could be developed for the semi-automatic evaluation of SDMs in the
522   future, to strike a balance between accuracy and efficiency.
523
524
525   6   C ONCLUSIONS
526
527
      We introduced a novel approach for species distribution modeling that uses a large-language model
      to generate a representation of a species. This provides flexibility to generate distributions at dif-
528
      ferent levels of the taxonomic hierarchy and for unseen species. In addition, we introduced a new
529
      evaluation metric, Probability Weighted Chamfer Distance. This metric computes the false positive
530   rate in species range mapping tasks in a proximity-aware manner. While our focus was on species
531   distribution modeling, the integration of large-language models points to some intriguing potential
532   future use cases. It might be possible, for example, to generate distribution models from arbitrary
533   text, such as “a large white bird” or “a flock of red-winged blackbirds”. This is potentially more
534   useful for non-expert users. Finally, alternative text prompts could be explored as future work which
535   may better reason about a species and its dependence on a particular geographic location.

766   B    P ROBABILITY W EIGHTED C HAMFER D ISTANCE
767
768   We proposed the Probability Weighted Chamfer Distance (PWCD) which is a spatially-aware proxy
769   for false-positive rates when comparing two probability maps. This distance could also be used
770   in other metrics such as precision, MAP, or AUC to make them spatially aware. In Listing 1, we
771   describe NumPy-style pseudocode for computing this distance metric.
772
      We use the xrspatial’s proximity function for computing the nearest-neighbor distance map
773   given the binary ground-truth range map. All the distances are measured in pixel units using Eu-
774   clidean distance. The minimum possible distance is 0 which is for the positive pixels having species
775   presence. The maximum possible distance depends on the spatial resolution of the range map. In
776   our case, the spatial resolution of 0.2o gives a maximum distance of 2012.46. It is noteworthy that
777   this distance function could be replaced with any other distance function for example the haversine
778   distance.

      We visualized the pixel-level dPWCD map in Figure 6. Notice that the predictions spatially close to
796
      the true presence of species are weighted less than the predictions that are distant. This weighting is
797
      due to the nearest-neighbor distances of the pixels making the metric spatially-aware. The chamfer
798   distance map describes the weighting of predictions as a function of distance from the nearest pres-
799   ence pixel. The weight gradually increases as the distance from the nearest presence pixel increases.
800   Note that the nearest-neighbor distance map differs from the chamfer distance map, which is in the
801   range of [0,1]. The chamfer distance map is computed using the nearest-neighbor distance map,
802   using the formula for dPWCD and a constant likelihood term equal to one.
803
      In Figure 7, we show the effect of temperature parameter α on the dPWCD metric. The figure shows
804   chamfer distance as a function of nearest-neighbor distance at a fixed likelihood probability of one
805   and varying values of α. The parameter α controls the exponential weighting of the nearest-neighbor
806   distance in the metric. Increasing α gradually increases the weight of nearby pixels. The metric starts
807   penalizing more of the nearby pixels in the computation of false positive rates. A large value of α
808   will make the metric more sensitive to spatial shifts in the predictions while a lower value of α will
809   be more robust towards spatial shifts. However, an extremely low value of α will make the metric
      ignore distant false positives. As a result, an optimal value of α is essential to evaluate SDMs.
