011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            In recent years, methods that selectively fine-tune or reduce the number of layers
014            in large language models (LLMs) have garnered attention as an efficient alterna-
015
               tive to traditional fine-tuning, where all layers are trained. In this paper, we revisit
               the concept of Layer Freezing, a simple yet effective fine-tuning strategy, and
016
               introduce detailed strategies that improve the training efficiency of LLMs by se-
017
               lectively fine-tuning only a portion of the layers. We tested various freezing ratios
018            and positions, and found that by freezing the bottom 25% or 50% of transformer
019            layers during fine-tuning of an LLM with sub 3 billion parameters, we can achieve
020            performance equal to or better than full model fine-tuning and Low-Rank Adap-
021            tation (LoRA), while significantly reducing memory usage and training time. Our
022            experiments on natural language inference tasks show that this approach reduces
023            memory consumption by about 30% and 50%, and improves training speed by
024            20-30%.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      Recent advancements in Natural Language Processing (NLP) have been largely driven by the emer-
029
      gence of Large Language Models (LLMs) such as GPT-3 (Brown et al., 2020), PaLM (Chowdhery
030   et al., 2023), and LLaMA (Touvron et al., 2023). These models leverage extensive pre-training to
031   learn a wide range of linguistic patterns and knowledge. They demonstrate high performance across
032   various tasks through techniques like prompt engineering (Liu et al., 2023) and in-context learning
033   (Xie et al.). As a result, LLMs have become indispensable tools in numerous NLP tasks, including
034   translation, question answering, and document generation.
035
      However, several limitations are associated with the training and application of LLMs: First, high-
036   performing LLMs typically contain over 7 billion parameters, making them computationally expen-
037   sive and requiring vast resources and time for both training and inference. Additionally, LLMs tend
038   to exhibit inconsistent performance in unfamiliar domains or tasks that were not encountered during
039   pre-training (Hendrycks et al., 2020). This is a chronic problem for pre-trained models, which are
040   constrained to generating outputs based on their pre-trained knowledge. To address this fundamental
041   issue, it is imperative to implement knowledge updates through fine-tuning processes, enhancing the
042   model’s adaptability (Gururangan et al., 2020). However, due to the immense size of modern LLMs,
043   with parameter counts ranging from billions to hundreds of billions, even fine-tuning demands sig-
044
      nificant computational effort.
045   To address these computational challenges, we revisit the concept of Layer Freezing, a simple yet
046   effective fine-tuning strategy, and introduce detailed strategies that extend this approach. Previous
047   studies have explored freezing layers of small language models such as BERT (Devlin et al., 2019)
048   during fine-tuning, but these efforts have mainly focused on improving speed and have encountered
049   challenges due to the complexity of freezing techniques (Ben Zaken et al., 2022; Tang et al., 2024).
050   In contrast, we have found that simply freezing a subset of layers can achieve better computational
051   efficiency and superior performance compared to fine-tuning the entire model. Rather than introduc-
052   ing additional layers or parameters, our goal is to reduce costs and maximize training efficiency by
053   focusing on fine-tuning only a subset of layers within the existing LLM. The discovered method has
      the following advantages:

055              need for complex analysis or modifications to the model architecture.

058
059            • Performance Improvement: Experimental results show that this method not only im-

                 proves computational efficiency but also enhances model performance compared to fine-

105   2.1     NATURAL L ANGUAGE I NFERENCE
106
107   NLI, also known as recognizing textual entailment (Dagan et al., 2005), is a fundamental task in
      NLP. This task involves determining the logical relationship between a premise and a hypothesis.


                                                           2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      Specifically, a model must classify this relationship as entailment (the hypothesis necessarily follows
109   from the premise), contradiction (the hypothesis contradicts the premise), or neutral (the hypothesis
110   may or may not be true given the premise).
111
112
      NLI serves as a crucial indicator for evaluating a model’s language understanding and reasoning
      capabilities, including comprehension of semantics, context, and logical relationships. Models that
113
      demonstrate high performance in NLI tasks tend to excel in other language understanding tasks as
114
      well. Success in NLI challenges often indicates a level of language understanding that generalizes
115   across various domains and linguistic tasks (Poliak et al., 2018). Consequently, NLI has established
116   itself as a valuable benchmark for assessing language models (Wang et al., 2018).
117
118   2.2   G ENERAL F INE - TUNING A PPROACHES
119
120   The traditional fine-tuning approach involves retraining all or some of the parameters of a pre-trained
121   model to adapt it to a new task (Howard & Ruder, 2018). This method adjusts the weights of the
122   pre-trained model, utilizing various strategies such as learning rate adjustment, gradual unfreezing,
123   and discriminative learning rates (Peters et al., 2019). While this is useful for training task-specific
124
      models, it has limitations in terms of computational cost and resource efficiency when fine-tuning
      all parameters in recent large-scale models. To address these issues, techniques that reduce the num-
125
      ber of trainable parameters, such as LoRA, or reduce memory usage, such as LLM quantization
126
      (Dettmers et al., 2022), are gaining attention.
127
128
      2.3   L AYER F REEZING IN F INE - TUNING
129
130   Since the emergence of language models, there have been attempts to improve the efficiency of
131   fine-tuning by freezing certain layers. Ben Zaken et al. (2022) proposed a method that fine-tunes
132   only the bias parameters instead of the weights in transformer-based masked language models, re-
133   ducing memory usage and improving speed. Tang et al. (2024) introduced a technique to accelerate
134   the training process by gradually freezing layers based on their impact during training. However,
135   both studies primarily focus on speed improvement, which often leads to performance degradation.
136
      Additionally, these studies require complex mechanisms for layer freezing.
137   In contrast, our study employs a simple freezing method that can be applied to any model while
138   also demonstrating performance improvement. This straightforward approach stands out in that it
139   not only reduces computational cost but also enhances model performance.
140
141   3     L AYER S ELECTION FOR F INE - TUNING
142
143   This study proposes a method to improve model training efficiency by fine-tuning only a subset of
144   layers in an LLM and aims to validate it experimentally. In contrast to conventional LLM fine-tuning
145   methods that involve training all layers, this study demonstrates that selectively freezing specific
146   layers and fine-tuning only the remaining ones can lead to improved performance, faster learning
147   speed, and reduced memory usage.
148
149   3.1   F IXED F REEZING
150
151
      The main strategy used during the model training process is to freeze specific layers of the model
      and fine-tune only the remaining layers. We evaluated the impact of various layer selection methods
152
      on model performance and training efficiency.
153
154          • Bottom-Up Freezing: We experimented with a method that sequentially freezes the
155            model’s bottom layers, allowing only the remaining upper layers to be fine-tuned. As the
156            bottom layers are primarily responsible for the basic linguistic expressiveness of the lan-
157            guage model, while the upper layers tend to learn task-specific representations (Rogers
158            et al., 2020), we hypothesized that this freezing approach would preserve fixed linguistic
159            knowledge while enabling task-specific adaptation.
160          • Top-Down Freezing: We tested an approach that freezes the top layers and trains only the
161            bottom layers. This method anticipates that the bottom layers will be tuned to the task based
               on the fixed higher-level concepts in the frozen upper layers.


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
             • Interval Freezing: This method involves freezing layers at intervals of n, meaning that
163            every n-th layer is frozen during training. This approach aims to allow both upper and
164            lower layers to be appropriately adjusted simultaneously, encouraging information to be
165            learned evenly across various layer levels.
166
167   3.2   A DAPTED F REEZING
168
169   As an alternative to the Fixed Freezing strategy, we propose an Adaptive Freezing approach with
170   dynamic layer selection. In this approach, we track the weight changes of each layer during training
171   to identify layers with significant or minimal changes. Based on these changes, we automatically
172   identify the layers that play a crucial role in performance. The Top-N layers, according to the mag-
173
      nitude of weight changes, are then selectively frozen before proceeding with training. The following
      outlines the operational sequence of this adaptive layer selection method:
174
175
            1. Weight Change Tracking: We calculate the change in weights for each layer by comparing
176            the layer-wise weights before training and after the first 5 steps of training. The magnitude
177            of weight changes for multiple parameters within a single layer was quantified as a single
178            scalar value by computing the L2 norm of the changes and then taking the mean across all
179            parameters in the layer.
180         2. Top-N Layer Selection: We select the top N layers with either the largest or smallest
181            weight changes, freeze them, and then resume training. Through this, we aimed to under-
182            stand the roles that layers with large and small weight changes play in the fine-tuning.
183
184   3.3   F REEZING S TRATEGIES
185
186   Through these Fixed and Adapted Freezing methods, we aim to experimentally demonstrate that
187   fine-tuning only a subset of layers can reduce memory and computational costs compared to fine-
188   tuning the entire model. We hypothesize that this approach can potentially improve performance or,
189
      at minimum, maintain it without degradation.

      4.2   E XPERIMENTAL D ESIGN
252
253   In this study, we conducted a series of experiments to evaluate the efficacy of fine-tuning strategies
254   that selectively utilize specific layers of neural networks. Our experimental design focused on var-
255   ious freezing techniques, enabling a comparative analysis of performance variations resulting from
256   each approach. The primary objective of these experiments was to conduct a comprehensive assess-
257   ment of model performance, memory utilization, and training efficiency to determine the optimal
258   freezing methodology.
259   Finding the Optimal Freezing Ratio: First, we conducted experiments to determine at which ratio
260   of frozen layers the model exhibits the highest performance. To achieve this, we froze a certain
261   proportion of the model’s layers and trained only the remaining layers. For the IN T strategy, we
262   applied freezing ratios of 25%, 33.3%, and 50%, while for the BOT , T OP , ADT -L, and ADT -H
263   strategies, we used ratios of 25%, 50%, and 75%. Through this approach, we aimed to identify the
264   threshold at which performance sharply declines when more than a certain proportion of layers are
265   frozen, thereby deriving the optimal freezing ratio.
266   Finding the Optimal Freezing Position: Next, we analyzed which positions within the model’s
267   layers have the most significant impact on performance when frozen. The freezing positions were
268   categorized as BOT (Bottom-Up; freezing layers sequentially starting from the lower layers), T OP
269   (Top-Down; freezing layers sequentially starting from the upper layers), and IN T (Interval; freezing
      layers at regular intervals). By comparing the performance for each freezing position, we examined


                                                        5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      which layers play more crucial roles. Additionally, we tracked the weight changes during the training
271   process and adaptively froze layers to understand the significance of the weight changes.
272
273   Finding the Optimal Freezing Strategy: Finally, we aimed to identify the optimal freezing strat-
274
      egy by comprehensively considering the performance, training speed, and memory usage of each
      strategy. As it is challenging to fairly and objectively quantify these diverse aspects into a single
275
      metric, we first evaluated each aspect quantitatively and then conducted a comprehensive analysis.

445   5   D ISCUSSION
446
447   Our study on NLI tasks has uncovered a remarkably simple yet effective approach to enhance learn-
448   ing efficiency in LLMs. Through our experiments, we have discovered that fine-tuning only specific
449   layers of a 3B parameter LLM can yield performance that matches or even surpasses that of full
450
      model fine-tuning.
451   Enhanced Performance: Specifically, we found that freezing the bottom 25% or 50% of trans-
452   former layers during fine-tuning not only maintained high performance but often exceeded the re-
453   sults of full model fine-tuning and LoRA. This approach led to a substantial reduction in memory
454   usage, approximately 30% and 50% respectively, without compromising model effectiveness. No-
455   tably, the training speed increased by 20-30%, which can be attributed to the reduced computational
456
      load. we posit that this phenomenon may be attributed to the model’s capacity being disproportion-
      ately large relative to the complexity of the NLI task. This aligns with observations in techniques
457
      like LoRA, where freezing the majority of the model and training only a small number of additional
458
      parameters can lead to performance improvements.
459
460   Memory Reduction: The reduction in memory usage observed with our partial fine-tuning ap-
461   proach is logically consistent with the decreased number of trainable parameters. However, we no-
462
      ticed that interval freezing strategies, where layers are frozen in a distributed pattern throughout the
      model, did not yield significant memory savings. This suggests that contiguous freezing of layers is
463
      more beneficial for memory optimization. Additionally, freezing the top layers of the model resulted
464
      in less pronounced memory savings compared to freezing bottom layers. We conjecture that this dis-
465   crepancy may be related to CUDA optimization techniques and underlying hardware architectures.
466
467   Learning Speed Improvements: While the speed improvements did not completely match the re-
468
      duction in memory usage, the observed 20-30% increase in training speed is nonetheless significant.
      We attribute this to the substantial computational overhead inherent in processing large language
469
      models.

472   6   C ONCLUSION
473
474   In this study, we investigated the effectiveness of fine-tuning only a portion of the layers in large
475   language models (LLMs) for natural language inference (NLI) tasks. Our experiments, conducted
476   on an LLM with approximately 3 billion parameters, demonstrated that freezing the bottom 25% or
477   50% of transformer layers can achieve performance equal to or better than full model fine-tuning
478   and LoRA, while significantly reducing memory usage and increasing training speed. This indicates
479   that our simple application of layer freezing, despite being an existing methodology, is particularly
480   effective for NLI tasks. Our approach offers a practical and efficient strategy for utilizing large LLMs
481   in resource-constrained environments.
482   Future work will focus on extending this method to a broader range of NLP tasks, including text
483   classification, named entity recognition, and machine translation, to assess its generalizability. We
484   aim to investigate the scalability of this approach by applying it to larger models with parameters in
485   the hundreds of billions. Furthermore, we intend to explore the synergistic effects of combining this
      method with other efficiency-enhancing techniques such as quantization and pruning.

      7   L IMITATION
487
488
      This study demonstrated the efficiency of fine-tuning only certain layers in LLMs. However, the
489   following limitations exist:
490
491   Experiments Limited to Small LLMs: This study primarily conducted experiments on small-scale
492
      LLMs with 3 billion parameters or fewer, such as Gemma, Phi-2 and MiniCPM. This choice was
      due to the experimental conditions set to enable training in a single GPU environment. Therefore,
493
      further research is needed to determine whether the proposed methodology demonstrates similar
494
      performance improvements and efficiency in extremely large models (such as PaLM and LLaMA).
495   In extremely large models, memory requirements or training patterns may differ, necessitating ex-
496   periments on these models to expand the scope of this research.

498
      Dataset Limited to NLI Tasks: This study focused on Natural Language Inference (NLI) tasks,
      conducting experiments only on NLI-related datasets (such as RTE and CB) from GLUE and Super-
499
      GLUE benchmarks. While NLI tasks are specialized in evaluating a model’s ability to infer logical
500
      relationships, other types of tasks (e.g., text generation, question answering, translation, etc.) may
501   have different model characteristics and learning requirements. Therefore, further experiments on di-
502   verse tasks and datasets are necessary to assess the effectiveness of the proposed fine-tuning method
503   across a broader range of natural language processing tasks.
504
      To address these limitations, future studies should validate the generalizability and efficiency of the
505
      proposed methodology across a range of larger-scale LLMs and diverse tasks.
