011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Diffusion models have demonstrated significant potential in speech synthesis tasks,
014            including text-to-speech (TTS) and voice cloning. However, their iterative denois-
015            ing processes are inefficient and hinder the application of end-to-end optimization
016            with perceptual metrics. In this paper, we propose a novel method of distill-
017            ing TTS diffusion models with direct end-to-end evaluation metric optimization,
018            achieving state-of-the-art performance. By incorporating Connectionist Temporal
019            Classification (CTC) loss and Speaker Verification (SV) loss, our approach op-
020
               timizes perceptual evaluation metrics, leading to notable improvements in word
               error rate and speaker similarity. Our experiments show that DMDSpeech con-
021
               sistently surpasses prior state-of-the-art models in both naturalness and speaker
022
               similarity while being significantly faster. Moreover, our synthetic speech has a
023            higher level of voice similarity to the prompt than the ground truth in both hu-
024            man evaluation and objective speaker similarity metric. This work highlights the
025            potential of direct metric optimization in speech synthesis, allowing models to
026            better align with human auditory preferences. The audio samples are available at
027            https://dmdspeech.github.io/demo/

031   1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Text-to-speech (TTS) technology has witnessed remarkable progress over the past few years, achieving
034   near-human or even superhuman performance on various benchmark datasets (Tan et al., 2024; Li
035   et al., 2024a; Ju et al., 2024). With the rise of large language models (LLMs) and scaling law
036   (Kaplan et al., 2020), the focus of TTS research has shifted from small-scale datasets to large-scale
037   models trained on tens to hundreds of thousands of hours of data encompassing a wide variety of
038   speakers (Wang et al., 2023a;c; Shen et al., 2024; Peng et al., 2024; Łajszczak et al., 2024; Li et al.,
039
      2024b). Two primary methodologies have emerged for training these large-scale models: diffusion-
      based approaches and autoregressive language modeling (LM)-based methods. Both frameworks
040
      enable end-to-end speech generation without the need for hand-engineered features such as prosody
041
      and duration modeling as seen in works before the LLM era (Ren et al., 2020; Kim et al., 2021),
042   simplifying the TTS pipeline and improving scalability.
043
044   Diffusion-based speech synthesis models have demonstrated superior robustness compared to LM-
045
      based approaches (Le et al., 2024; Lee et al., 2024). By generating all speech tokens simultaneously
      rather than sequentially, diffusion models avoid the error accumulation inherent in autoregressive
046
      models and offer faster generation times for longer sentences since their inference speed is not
047
      directly proportional to speech length (Ju et al., 2024). However, a significant drawback of diffusion
048   models is their reliance on iterative sampling processes, which can be computationally intensive and
049   time-consuming (Liu et al., 2024). Additionally, unlike LM-based models that directly estimate the
050   likelihood of the output token distribution, diffusion models focus on estimating the score function
051   rather than generating speech tokens end-to-end (Yang et al., 2023). This characteristic makes it
052   inconvenient to optimize directly on the target distribution space using techniques such as perceptual
053   loss (Johnson et al., 2016) or reinforcement learning with human feedback (RLHF) (Bai et al., 2022),
      as the models do not produce explicit token probabilities that can be easily adjusted.


                                                         1
054
      To address these limitations, diffusion distillation techniques have been proposed to reduce sampling
055   time (Salimans & Ho, 2022; Song et al., 2023; Sauer et al., 2023; Yin et al., 2024b). By distilling the
056   diffusion model into a one-step generator, it becomes possible to access the final output directly and
057   apply metric optimization methods to improve the desired attributes in the synthesized speech.
058
      In this work, we introduce DMDSpeech, a distilled diffusion-based speech synthesis model designed
059
      for efficient and high-quality zero-shot speech generation. By employing distribution matching
060
      distillation (DMD) (Yin et al., 2024b;a), we transform the diffusion teacher model into a distilled
061   student model that generates speech in 4 steps. The distilled model provides a direct gradient pathway
062   between the noise input and the speech output, enabling end-to-end optimization of any differentiable
063   metrics. We leverage two metrics relevant to zero-shot speech synthesis: speaker similarity via speaker
064   verification (SV) loss and intelligibility and robustness via connectionist temporal classification (CTC)
065   loss. With the SV loss, we enhance the model’s ability to reproduce the unique characteristics of
066   the prompt speaker, obtaining higher similarity to the prompt judged by both human evaluations
067   and speaker verification models. Additionally, the CTC loss helps improve word error rate (WER),
068   enhancing the intelligibility and accuracy of synthesized speech and achieving lower WER than
069
      ground truth and other end-to-end baseline models.
070   Our contributions are threefold:
071
072          • We present a distilled diffusion model for zero-shot speech synthesis that achieves state-of-
073            the-art performance with significantly reduced inference time.
074          • We introduce a new speech synthesis framework for end-to-end optimization of perceptual
075            metrics. We demonstrate that optimizing SV loss and CTC loss leads to improvements in
076            speaker similarity and WER, respectively.
077          • We present an in-depth analysis of our model’s ablations of DMD and direct metric opti-
078            mization, demonstrating the correlations between human evaluations and perceptual metrics,
079            while highlighting the trade-off between sampling speed and diversity (mode shrinkage).

      2    R ELATED W ORKS
082
083
      Zero-Shot Text-to-Speech Synthesis Zero-shot TTS generates speech in an unseen speaker’s
084
      voice using a small reference sample without additional training. Initial methods relied on speaker
085   embeddings from pre-trained encoders (Casanova et al., 2022; 2021; Wu et al., 2022; Lee et al., 2022)
086   or end-to-end speaker encoders (Li et al., 2024a; Min et al., 2021; Li et al., 2022; Choi et al., 2022),
087   which required extensive feature engineering and struggled with generalization, limiting scalability.
088   More recently, prompt-based methods using in-context learning with reference prompts have scaled
089   models using autoregressive (Shen et al., 2024; Le et al., 2024; Ju et al., 2024; Lee et al., 2024; Yang
090   et al., 2024; Eskimez et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024) and diffusion frameworks (Jiang et al., 2023b;
091   Wang et al., 2023a;c; Jiang et al., 2023a; Peng et al., 2024; Kim et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024b; Meng
092   et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2024; Lovelace et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024). While these models scale well,
093
      they suffer from slow inference due to iterative sampling. Our model, DMDSpeech, addresses this
      by combining the scalability of prompt-based methods with the efficiency of non-iterative sampling.
094
      Through distribution matching distillation, we transform a diffusion-based TTS model into a student
095
      model capable of generating speech in a few steps, accelerating inference and enabling direct metric
096   optimization for state-of-the-art speaker similarity and speech quality.
097
098   Diffusion Distillation Diffusion models generate high-quality audio but suffer from slow inference
099
      due to iterative sampling (Popov et al., 2021). Diffusion distillation accelerates this process by train-
      ing a student model to efficiently replicate the teacher’s behavior. Previous methods approximated the
100
      teacher’s ODE sampling trajectories. ProDiff (Huang et al., 2022) used progressive distillation (Sali-
101
      mans & Ho, 2022) to reduce sampling steps, while CoMoSpeech (Ye et al., 2023) and FlashSpeech
102   (Ye et al., 2024) employed consistency distillation (Song et al., 2023). Rectified flow methods, such
103   as in VoiceFlow (Guo et al., 2024) and ReFlow-TTS (Guan et al., 2024), aimed to accelerate sampling
104   by straightening sampling paths. However, these methods often compromise quality by forcing
105   the student to follow the teacher’s path, which may not suit its reduced capacity. An alternative is
106   distribution matching, either adversarially (Sauer et al., 2023; 2024) or via score function matching
107   (Yin et al., 2024b), aligning the student with the teacher in distribution to maintain quality. However,
      these methods may reduce diversity as the student model might prioritize high-probability regions


                                                         2
108
      of the distribution. Additionally, many distillation techniques require generating noise-data pairs
109   (Sauer et al., 2024; Yin et al., 2024b; Liu et al., 2023), which is computationally expensive. We utilize
110   DMD2 (Yin et al., 2024a), which bypasses the need for pair generation and enhances quality through
111   adversarial training. Interestingly, we find that the reduction in diversity through DMD distillation
112   can also reduce the chance of sampling from low-probability regions of the distributions, which can
113   lead to unwanted artifacts and hallucinations. Consequently, distillation may enhance the human
114   perceived quality, similar to recent findings in autoregressive diffusion models (Liu et al., 2024).
115   Direct Metric Optimization Optimizing generative models using perceptual metrics has gained
116   attention recently. MetricGAN (Fu et al., 2019) optimized speech enhancement models using PESQ
117   and STOI as rewards in an adversarial setting. Reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF)
118   has also been used to improve speech naturalness by optimizing predicted MOS scores (Zhang et al.,
119   2024; Chen et al., 2024a). However, these approaches are challenging to apply to many state-of-
120   the-art models due to non-differentiable components like duration upsamplers (Li et al., 2024b; Ye
121   et al., 2024) or iterative sampling (Lee et al., 2024; Peng et al., 2024). In contrast, DMDSpeech
122   allows end-to-end differentiable speech generation without iterative processes. We employ speaker
123
      verification (SV) loss and connectionist temporal classification (CTC) loss to optimize speaker
      similarity and text-speech alignment. By directly optimizing these metrics, we significantly improve
124
      speaker similarity and intelligibility, aligning synthesized speech more closely with human auditory
125
      preferences, marking the first application of direct metric optimization in speech synthesis models.

130   3.1   P RELIMINARY: E ND - TO -E ND L ATENT S PEECH D IFFUSION

132
      Our model starts with a pre-trained teacher model based on an end-to-end latent speech diffusion
      framework such as SimpleTTS (Lovelace et al., 2023) and DiTTo-TTS (Lee et al., 2024). This section
133
      outlines the formulation of the diffusion process, noise scheduling, and the objective function.
134
135   We begin by encoding raw audio waveforms y ∈ R1×T , where T is the audio length, into latent
136   representations x0 = E(y) using a latent autoencoder E. The latent autoencoder follows DAC
137   (Kumar et al., 2024) with residual vector quantization replaced by the variational autoencoder loss
138
      (see Appendix C.1 for more information). We denote the ground truth latent distribution as pdata . The
      diffusion process involves adding noise to x0 ∼ pdata over continuous time t ∈ [0, 1] through a noise
139
      schedule. Our noise schedule follows Lovelace et al. (2023), which is a shifted cosine noise schedule
140
      formulated with αt and σt that control the amount of signal and noise (see Appendix C.2.1).
141
142   During training, the model learns to remove noise added to the latent representations. Given a latent
143   variable x0 and noise ϵ ∼ N (0, I), the noisy latent xt at time step t is generated as xt = αt x0 + σt ϵ.
144
      We use a binaray prompt mask m to selectively preserve the original values in regions corresponding
      to the prompt. The noisy latent xt is adjusted as xt ← xt ⊙ (1 − m) + x0 ⊙ m, where ⊙ denotes
145
      element-wise multiplication. The binary mask m is randomly sampled to mask between 0% to 50%
146
      of the length of x0 . We define a reparameterized velocity v = αt ϵ − σt x0 , which serves as the
147   training objective as in Huang et al. (2022). We train our diffusion transformer (Peebles & Xie, 2023)
148   model fϕ , parameterized by ϕ, to predict the target v given the noisy latent xt , conditioned on text
149   embeddings c, prompt mask indicators m, and the time step t:
150
151                  Ldiff (fϕ ; pdata ) = Ex0 ∼pdata ,t∼U (0,1),ϵ∼N (0,I) ∥v − fϕ (xt ; c, m, t)∥2 .      (1)
152
      During inference, the model takes noise z ∈ N (0, I) with fixed size [d, L] where L is the total
153   duration of the target speech. L is estimated by multiplying the number of phonemes in the target text
154   with the speaking rate of the prompt speech (see Appendix C.2.3 for more implementation details).

274   We employ an alternating training strategy where the student generator Gθ , the student score estimator
275   gψ , and the discriminator D are updated at different frequencies to maintain training stability.
276   Specifically, for every update of Gθ , we perform five updates of gψ . This ensures that the score
277   estimator gψ can adapt quickly to the dynamic changes in the generator distribution pθ . Unlike Yin
278   et al. (2024a), where D are updated five times for every single update of Gθ , we update D and Gθ at
279
      the same rate. This prevents the discriminator from becoming too powerful and destabilizing training.
280   The learning rates for Gθ and gψ play a critical role in maintaining training stability since both
281   models are initialized from the teacher’s parameters, ϕ. Treating this as a fine-tuning process, we set
282   their learning rates close to the teacher model’s final learning rate to prevent catastrophic forgetting
283   and training collapse. The teacher model was trained using a cosine annealing warmup scheduler,
284   which gradually reduced the learning rate over time. Thus, starting with a high learning rate for Gθ
285
      and gψ can cause them to deviate significantly from the pre-trained knowledge, leading to training
      failure. Conversely, the learning rate for D is less sensitive and does not require such precise tuning.
286
287   Balancing the different loss components in the overall objective function is crucial for successful
288   training. The primary loss, LDMD , is responsible for transferring knowledge from the teacher model,
289   aligning the synthesized speech with the text. Other losses, such as Ladv , LSV , and LCTC , need to
290   be scaled properly to match the gradient of LDMD . We set λadv = 10−3 to ensure the gradient norm
291
      of Ladv is comparable to that of LDMD . During early training stage, we observed that the gradient
      norms of LSV and LCTC were significantly higher than LDMD , likely because Gθ was still learning
292
      to generate intelligible speech from single step. To address this, we set λCTC = 0 for the first 5,000
293
      iterations and λSV = 0 for the first 10,000 iterations. This allows Gθ to stabilize under the influence
294   of LDMD before integrating these additional losses. After that, both λCTC and λSV are set to 1.

299   4.1      M ODEL T RAINING
300
      We conducted our experiments on the LibriLight dataset (Kahn et al., 2020), which consists of
301
      57,706.4 hours of audio from 7,439 speakers. The data and transcripts were obtained using Python
302
      scripts provided by the LibriLight authors1 . All audio files were resampled to 48 kHz to match the
303   configuration of our DAC autoencoder, and the text was converted into phonemes using Phonemizer
304   (Bernard & Titeux, 2021). To manage memory constraints, we segmented the audio into 30-second
305   chunks using WhisperX (Bain et al., 2023). The teacher model fϕ was trained for 400,000 steps
306   with a batch size of 384, using the AdamW optimizer (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2018) with β1 = 0.9,
307   β2 = 0.999, weight decay of 10−2 , and an initial learning rate of 10−4 . The learning rate followed a
308   cosine decay schedule with a 4,000-step warmup, gradually decreasing to 10−5 . Model weights were
309   updated using an exponential moving average (EMA) with a decay factor of 0.99 every 100 steps.
310   The teacher model consists of 450M parameters in total. For student training, we initialized both the
311
      student generator Gθ and the student score model gψ with the EMA-weighted teacher parameters.
312
      The initial learning rate was set to match the final learning rate of the teacher model (λ = 10−5 ),
      while the batch size was reduced to 96 due to memory constraints. Reducing the batch size further
313
      negatively impacted performance, as a sufficiently large batch size is required for accurate score
314
      estimation due to the Monte Carlo nature of DMD (see Section 4.4 for further discussion). The
315   student generator Gθ and the discriminator D were trained for an additional 40,000 steps, and the
316   student score model gψ for 200,000 steps accordingly using the same optimization settings as the
317   teacher. All models were trained on 24 NVIDIA A100 40GB GPUs.

500   Effects of Direct Metric Optimization We first demonstrate that the metrics we directly optimize
501   are significantly correlated with human subjective ratings at the utterance level. Figure 3 shows the
502   scatter plot between human-rated similarity SMOS-V and SIM, one of the optimized metrics, with a
503   correlation coefficient ρ = 0.55. Another metric, word error rate (WER), is significantly correlated
504   with naturalness (MOS-N) even at the utterance level, with a correlation ρ = −0.15 (see Figure 5).
505
      These correlations suggest a notable impact of these metrics on their associated subjective ratings.
506   When using only the CTC loss, we observe a substantial reduction in WER (from 5.67 to 1.79), but no
507   improvement in speaker similarity, alongside a slight reduction in diversity and a minor improvement
508   in naturalness. This aligns with the correlation between WER and human-rated naturalness with
509   ρ = −0.15 (p ≪ 0.01). In contrast, with only the SV loss, we see significant improvements in
510   all speaker similarity metrics (SMOS-V, SMOS-S, SIM), but these gains come with a decrease in
511
      naturalness and sound quality, as well as an increase in WER. This suggests that while SV loss
      can enhance speaker similarity, it negatively impacts intelligibility and naturalness. Therefore,
512
      combining both CTC and SV losses achieves a balance between these metrics, yielding the best
513
      overall performance, with improvements across speaker similarity, intelligibility, and naturalness.

516   5   C ONCLUSIONS
517
518   In this work, we presented DMDSpeech, a distilled diffusion-based text-to-speech (TTS) model
519   based on prompt continuation. By employing distribution matching distillation (DMD), our model
520   generates high-quality speech in just 4 steps and enables direct metric optimization. Through
521   speaker verification (SV) and connectionist temporal classification (CTC) losses, DMDSpeech
522   significantly improves speaker similarity and text-speech alignment, outperforming several state-of-
523   the-art baselines.
524   The ability to directly optimize any differentiable metrics offers substantial progress in bridging
525   the gap between generative modeling and human perception. As these metrics continue to improve,
526   the alignment with human auditory preferences is expected to strengthen. This creates promising
527   future directions, such as using reinforcement learning from human feedback to further improve TTS
528   systems. Additionally, developing new differentiable metrics that better capture human perception
529   could provide more robust optimization targets, aligning models more closely with human preferences.
530   However, DMDSpeech raises important ethical concerns. Our model has demonstrated the ability
531   to generate speech with higher perceived similarity to the prompt than real utterances by the same
532   speaker, as judged by both human listeners and speaker verification systems. This highlights limita-
533   tions in current speaker verification models and presents risks of misuse, such as deepfake generation.
534   To mitigate these risks, more advanced speaker verification techniques capable of distinguishing
535   synthetic from real speech are necessary, alongside robust watermarking to identify synthesized audio.
536
      Clear ethical guidelines and legal frameworks will also be crucial to prevent abuse in sensitive areas.
537   We also observed that while DMDSpeech benefits from fast sampling and direct metric optimization,
538   this comes with a trade-off in speech diversity. The reduction in diversity, which arises from
539   prioritizing sampling speed and quality, warrants further investigation and improvement. Scaling the
      model with larger datasets and incorporating diverse languages may help mitigate this trade-off.

936   C.1   DAC VARIATIONAL AUTOENCODER
937
938   We utilize a latent audio autoencoder to compress raw waveforms into compact latent representations
939   for diffusion modeling. Our architecture follows the DAC model proposed by Kumar et al. (2024),
940   with a key modification to use a variational autoencoder (VAE) bottleneck instead of residual vector
941
      quantization, enabling continuous latent spaces and end-to-end differentiable training.
942   The DAC consists of an encoder E, a VAE bottleneck, and a decoder D. The encoder maps the input
943   waveform y ∈ R1×T into a latent representation x ∈ RC×L , where C and L denote channels and
944   downsampled temporal resolution. The VAE bottleneck introduces stochasticity by modeling x as a
945   distribution, and the decoder reconstructs the waveform by minimizing the reconstruction loss.
946   The encoder applies an initial convolution followed by residual units with dilated convolutions at
947   scales 1, 3, 9 to capture multi-scale temporal features. After each block, strided convolutions reduce
948   the temporal resolution by a factor of 1200. For 48 kHz audio, the encoded latent is 40 Hz, making it
949   ideal for efficient speech synthesis tasks. The latent channel dimension of our autoencoder is C = 64.
950
      The encoder’s output is split into mean µ and scale σ parameters:
951
952
                                         z = µ + σ ⊙ ϵ, ϵ ∼ N (0, I),                                  (18)
953
      where z is sampled using the reparameterization trick (Kingma, 2013). The decoder mirrors the
954   encoder with transposed convolutions and residual units to upsample latent representations back to
955   the original waveform ŷ = D(z), where ŷ is the reconstructed waveform. The encoder and decoder
956   architectures are the same as DAC (Kumar et al., 2024).

1047   The embedding layer maps input tokens and latent variables into continuous embeddings. Specifically,
1048   IPA tokens are embedded into vectors of size 1024 using an embedding matrix, and speech latents
1049   are projected from dimension 64 to 1024 using a linear layer. A binary mask prompt indicating
1050   prompt positions m in the latent sequence is encoded into a mask embedding, and a sinusoidal time
1051   embedding represents the diffusion timestep t. Positional embeddings are added to both IPA and
1052   latent embeddings to encode positional information.
1053   The encoder processes the embedded IPA tokens through 8 layers, each containing multi-head
1054   self-attention and feed-forward sublayers with layer normalization and residual connections. The
1055   feed-forward sublayers use a hidden dimension of 3072 and the SwiGLU activation function. The
1056   encoder outputs the text condition c.
1057
       The decoder generates latent representations conditioned on the encoder outputs and additional
1058
       embeddings over 16 layers. Each layer includes self-attention, cross-attention with the encoder
1059   outputs, and feed-forward sublayers. Adaptive layer normalization (AdaLN), conditioned on the
1060   timestep embedding, is applied within the decoder. The output layer projects the decoder outputs
1061   back to the latent space dimension of 64 using a linear layer.

       C.3    L ATENT CTC- BASED ASR M ODEL
1101
       To directly optimize word error rate (WER) within our speech synthesis framework, we implement a
1102   Connectionist Temporal Classification (CTC)-based ASR model that operates on latent speech repre-
1103   sentations. Traditional ASR models work on raw audio or mel-spectrograms, adding computational
1104   overhead and potential mismatches when integrated with latent-based synthesis since we need to
1105   decode the latent back into waveforms before computing the ASR output. Our latent ASR model
1106   processes these representations directly, enabling efficient, end-to-end computation of the CTC loss
1107   and direct WER optimization.
1108   The ASR model is based on the Conformer architecture (Gulati et al., 2020), which effectively captures
1109   local and global dependencies using convolution and self-attention. Input latent representations
1110   z ∈ RT ×d are processed through a 6-layer conformer stack and the model outputs a logit for each
1111   latent token over IPA phonemes.
1112
       The ASR model is trained using the CTC loss, allowing alignment-free training of sequence-to-
1113
       sequence models. The CTC loss is defined using softmax function:
1114

1116
       where y is the target IPA sequence, o represents the logits over the IPA symbols, and p (y | o)
1117   is computed by summing over all valid alignments between the input and target sequences. The
1118   probabilities are calculated as:
1119                                                 exp (ot,πt )
1120
                                        pπt (t) = PV                .                            (37)
                                                    k=1 exp (ot,k )

1122   We trained our ASR model on CommonVoice (Ardila et al., 2019) and LibriLight (Kahn et al., 2020)
1123   datasets for 200k steps with the AdamW (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2018) optimizer. The optimizer
1124   configuration is the same as teacher training described in Section 4.1.
