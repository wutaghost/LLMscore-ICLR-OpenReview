009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Large language models are trained on massive internet datasets, which may inad-
012            vertently memorize illegal copyrighted content, making its inclusion unavoidable.
013            Unlearning is a potential solution to remove such content. However, existing
014            unlearning methods often suffer from over-forgetting, where the process uninten-
015            tionally erases knowledge similar to the copyrighted content that falls under fair
016            use and should be preserved. To address this issue, we propose MOUCHI, a novel
017            unlearning framework that introduces the concept of derivative knowledge, a subset
018
               of information derived from copyrighted content that must be retained during
               unlearning. MOUCHI first generates derivative knowledge and then incorporates a
019
               derivative loss function into the unlearning process to mitigate over-forgetting in
020
               unlearning copyrighted content. Due to its plug-and-play nature, MOUCHI can be
021            effortlessly integrated into existing unlearning methods. Experimental results show
022            that MOUCHI reduces unintended knowledge loss, improving performance by up
023            to 145% compared to baseline methods when evaluated on the derivative set.

026   1    I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Large language models (LLM) have grown exponentially in scale and sophistication, driven by
029   increasing parameter sizes and extensive training datasets. However, this reliance on vast amounts
030   of data introduces a significant challenge of inadvertently including copyrighted material, often
031   propagated illegally online. Copyright concerns in LLMs have become especially prominent due
032   to their widespread use, making these models more vulnerable to misuse (Carlini et al., 2021;
033
      Hernandez et al., 2022; Chang et al., 2023). Karamolegkou et al. (2023) observe a linear correlation
      between the size of a language model and its tendency to generate verbatim copies of famous books,
034
      which constitutes a clear violation of copyright law. Moreover, new regulations, such as the EU AI
035
      Act (Friedl & Gasiola, 2024), mandate that all general-purpose AI systems, including LLMs, adhere
036   to union copyright laws and the general data protection regulation, granting rights holders the ability
037   to protect their works from unauthorized text and data mining and ensuring the right to be forgotten
038   (Hoofnagle et al., 2019). Consequently, there is a growing need for efficient methods to remove
039   copyrighted data from LLMs upon request (Ren et al., 2024; Wei et al., 2024).
040
      Machine unlearning has emerged as a novel approach to address this issue. By applying the principles
041
      of unlearning, it is possible to systematically remove specific information from a trained model,
042   thereby mitigating the concern of copyright infringement within LLMs. Recent papers (Yao et al.,
043   2023; Eldan & Russinovich, 2023; Jang et al., 2022) use machine unlearning to remove harmful
044   content (e.g., personal information, toxic passage, and copyrighted information) from LLMs by
045   employing variants of gradient ascent (GA) during the unlearning process. GA adjusts the model
046   parameters to maximize the loss on specific data points being unlearned, thus diminishing the model’s
047   ability to generate outputs related to the undesired data. However, these techniques have notable
048   shortcomings, particularly the problem of over-forgetting, which we introduce in this paper.
049   Over-forgetting refers to an unintentional erasure of more than just the targeted data. It is a critical
050   issue that occurs when we cannot fully control the GA process. In tasks focused on removing
051   toxic passages or private information, over-forgetting is less problematic since the goal is complete
052   removal. In contrast, when the target data involves copyrighted content, over-forgetting becomes more
053   problematic, as it not only erases the targeted copyrighted material but also leads to the unintended
      loss of relevant knowledge that falls within the bounds of fair use. We illustrate this problem in Figure

074   To address the issue of over-forgetting, this paper proposes a novel framework for Mitigating Over-
075   forgetting in Unlearning CopyrigHted Information within LLMs, MOUCHI, which identifies the
076   problem of over-forgetting in existing methods and introduces the concept of derivative knowledge—a
077
      subset derived from the target copyrighted information that must be retained during unlearning. This
      subset consists of knowledge that falls within fair use boundaries and must be preserved during the
078
      unlearning process to prevent unintentional loss. Our MOUCHI framework generates derivative
079
      knowledge from the target information, using KL divergence as a metric to assess semantic closeness.
080   By doing so, we enable the model to identify the derivative set while distinguishing it from the target
081   forget set. As in the lower part of Figure 1, MOUCHI preserves the fair use content (the dots within
082   the red and yellow boundaries), allowing the model to retain its ability to answer general or related
083   questions while still removing the infringing content. It then incorporates the derivative loss into the
084   unlearning process, ensuring that the derivative knowledge is preserved while the copyrighted content
085   is removed. Moreover, MOUCHI is model-agnostic and can be seamlessly integrated into existing
086   unlearning methods, providing enhanced control over the unlearning process without compromising
087   performance. By virtue of the derivative knowledge, MOUCHI effectively maintains the model’s
088
      overall capabilities while mitigating over-forgetting. Our contributions are as follows.
089   • We analyze and identify the over-forgetting problem in current LLM unlearning methods.
090   • To address the over-forgetting in LLM unlearning, we propose MOUCHI, a new unlearning
091     framework that generates and incorporates a set of derivative knowledge. A derivative loss function
092     is also introduced to realize the unlearning process under our framework.
093   • We demonstrate that MOUCHI can be seamlessly integrated into existing unlearning methods,
094     maintaining performance comparable to traditional approaches while providing better control over
095     over-forgetting. The experimental results show that the models with MOUCHI exhibit up to 145%
096     higher utility on the derivative set compared to the baselines.

      LLM Unlearning Traditional machine unlearning techniques, which aim to remove specific knowl-
101
      edge from models without full retraining, face challenges when applied to LLMs due to scalability
102   issues and the decentralized nature of their training data (Liu et al., 2024b). §A reviews general
103   machine unlearning techniques and their limitations. In response to these challenges, recent research
104   has proposed scalable and effective unlearning approaches tailored specifically for LLMs. Lu et al.
105   (2022); Jang et al. (2022); Yao et al. (2023) explore various fine-tuning methods, including reward-
106   reinforced model fine-tuning and gradient ascent-based fine-tuning, to unlearn specific content from
107   LLMs. Other methods (Wang et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2023; Chen & Yang, 2023) involve techniques like
      KL-divergence-based fine-tuning, weight importance-informed relabeling, and parameter-efficient


                                                                                2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      task vector tuning. Lately, a study (Zhang et al., 2024) introduces negative preference optimization
109   to address the problem of catastrophic collapse caused by the lack of control over gradient ascent
110   variants at higher temperatures. Despite these advancements, most methods focus only on general
111   unlearning in LLMs, overlooking the specific issue of unlearning copyrighted information.
112
113   Unlearning Copyrighted Information Research on the unlearning of copyrighted content from
114   LLMs remains relatively underexplored. This unlearning requires distinct treatment from general
115   unlearning due to a heightened susceptibility to over-forgetting. Li et al. (2024); Henderson et al.
116   (2023) discuss the potential risks of copyright infringement in LLMs and how to detect the copyright
117   content from the output. However, they do not focus on the method of removal. Eldan & Russinovich
118
      (2023); Chen et al. (2024) explore unlearning for specific books like Harry Potter and The Lord of
      the Rings. However, these approaches lack scalability as they are demonstrated only in a specific
119
      book. They also fail to observe the differences between copyrighted content unlearning and general
120
      unlearning. To address these limitations, Dou et al. (2024) propose a method that sequentially
121   removes copyrighted content from multiple books; meanwhile, a few papers introduce novel datasets
122   for copyright removal, along with baselines for unlearning tasks (Maini et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024c).
123   In addition, recent studies (Liu et al., 2024a; Dou et al., 2024) mention an open problem of unintended
124   knowledge loss, particularly the erasure of fair-use content during the unlearning process. As a
125   solution, MOUCHI effectively mitigates this issue by introducing derivative knowledge that falls
126   within the bounds of fair use.

297   Datasets We adopt two publicly available datasets, TOFU (Maini et al., 2024) and MUSE (Shi
298   et al., 2024), to comprehensively validate the effectiveness of MOUCHI. Both datasets are specifically
299   designed to facilitate unlearning in LLMs, making them particularly well-suited for our scenarios.
300   The TOFU dataset consists of 200 fictional authors, with each author associated with 20 questions and
301
      answers about their books and personal information. The MUSE benchmark features two unlearning
      corpora: one derived from the Harry Potter series (MUSE-Books) and the other from BBC news
302
      articles (MUSE-News).
303
304   In our experiment, we use the full TOFU dataset and create a split where 25% of the data is designated
305   as the forget set Dfgt . We select a 25% split to increase the difficulty of the unlearning task as handling
306   a larger forget set poses a more significant challenge. Also, smaller splits of 5% and 10% are explored
307
      in §5.4 to assess the impact of different forget set sizes on the unlearning process. For both the
      MUSE-News and MUSE-Books datasets, we use the entire forget and retain sets from the knowmem
308
      subset, following the original settings.
309
310   Due to the limited number of questions in the original datasets, we extend the dataset by generating
311   the derivative set Ddrv from the forget set Dfgt , as described in §4.3. We generate an amount similar
312   to the size of the forget set to ensure a balanced evaluation. We also modify the forget set to make
313
      the questions more closely resemble infringing content. Furthermore, for TOFU, we combine the
      world_fact and real_author subsets into a single normal set Dnor to assess the performance of the

      unlearned model on general knowledge questions since the results from existing approaches show no

      significant difference in performance between them.

343   Evaluation Metrics Evaluating the performance of unlearning methods is inherently challenging
344   and remains an active area of research. To ensure that our evaluation is valid and comprehensible, we
345
      follow the guidelines outlined by Maini et al. (2024). We assess and compare the unlearned models
      using two key metrics: model utility and forget quality.
346
347   • Model Utility evaluates the model’s ability to answer questions from each subset (Dfgt , Drt , Ddrv ,
348     Dnor ). We use ROUGE (Lin, 2004), a widely adopted metric in natural language processing for
349     sentence comparison. For the derivative, retain, and normal subsets, higher ROUGE scores indicate
350     better performance, while for the forget subset, lower scores are better. However, it is important
351
        to note that a ROUGE score close to 0 on the forget subset suggests catastrophic collapse (Zhang
        et al., 2024), denoted as ‘*’, meaning that the model produces nonsensical answers.
352
353   • Forget Quality is measured using the p-value from the Kolmogorov-Smirnov test (Maini et al.,
354
        2024). Intuitively, high p-values, where we cannot reject the null hypothesis that the two distribu-
        tions are the same, indicate strong forgetting. Conversely, when the p-value is low, we can assert a
355
        difference between the unlearned model and the retained model, suggesting potential copyright
356
        infringement and poor unlearning. The main goal of unlearning is to obtain a model that replicates

            5.3                           M ORE I N -D EPTH A NALYSIS
463
464        Better Control over the Unlearning Process MOUCHI provides better control over the unlearning
465        process by slowing it down and preserving the derivative set. As shown in Table 1, catastrophic
466        collapse occurs in the vanilla GA and DPO baselines. However, incorporating MOUCHI reduces the
467        severity of catastrophic collapse in both cases while maintaining high utility across the remaining
468        sets. Furthermore, this enhanced control improves the overall capability of the unlearned model.
469        This improvement is evident in the utility scores of the normal and retain sets, where most of
470        the performance of the derivative-augmented models surpasses its counterpart. Nevertheless, the
471        inclusion of the derivative loss only mitigates the issue and does not eliminate it entirely. The forget
472
           set still achieves scores around 0.1 to 0.2, indicating that some outputs are still nonsensical.
473
474
            Better Model Utility and Forget Quality Trade-Off Figure 5 illustrates the trade-off between
            model utility and forget quality for all the unlearning methods. As observed from the figure, the
475
            methods augmented with MOUCHI generally perform better than their counterparts in terms of
476
            trade-off. Notably, despite the incorporation of Ldrv , MOUCHI maintains a balance between model
477         utility and forget quality. These results suggest that the inclusion of the derivative knowledge does
478         not significantly degrade performance while mitigating the over-forgetting problem.
479
480         Qualitative Results Figure 6a and 6b present example questions, expected answers, and generated
481         answers from all baselines alongside their MOUCHI-augmented counterparts. Figure 6a shows
482         that integrating MOUCHI with GA prevents catastrophic collapse. The results also show that
483         MOUCHI closely mirrors the behavior of the underlying baseline models. For instance, DPO +
484         MOUCHI generates the “I don’t know” variant, similar to the original DPO model. Moreover,
485         Figure 6b demonstrates that MOUCHI-augmented baselines consistently produce reasonable answers,
            successfully mitigating over-forgetting and maintaining model utility.

524   6     C ONCLUSION
525
526   This paper proposes MOUCHI, a novel unlearning framework for LLMs, designed to mitigate
527   the over-forgetting effects when unlearning copyrighted content. We introduce the concept of the
528   derivative knowledge, which refers to a subset of information related to the target copyrighted content
529   that must be preserved to adhere to fair use standards. By integrating the derivative knowledge into
530   the existing unlearning algorithms via the derivative loss function, MOUCHI effectively reduces the
531   over-forgetting problem, preserving valuable knowledge while enhancing the model’s robustness
532
      across various tasks. Results from extensive experiments demonstrate that MOUCHI not only
      prevents the loss of related information but also improves the generalization capabilities of the model
533
      being unlearned, offering a balanced solution that respects copyright obligations while ensuring
534
      comprehensive and functional models.

705   A       R ELATED WORK ON G ENERAL M ACHINE U NLEARNING
706
707   Exact Unlearning The concept of Machine Unlearning has emerged as a response to the need
708   to remove specific knowledge from trained models without retraining them from scratch. Early
709   attempts at machine unlearning sought to achieve exact unlearning, aiming to eliminate the influence
710   of targeted data from the model completely. Papers such as Cao & Yang (2015), Ginart et al. (2019),
711   and Brophy & Lowd (2021) tried to solve exact unlearning on specific algorithms such as Naive
712
      Bayes, k-means and random forest, respectively. Furthermore, Bourtoule et al. (2021) proposes a
      method to partition datasets into chunks to make the unlearning process more effective. However,
713
      these methods, as highlighted in various studies Xu et al. (2024); Thudi et al. (2022), have proven
714
      to be impractical. They are not only time-consuming and algorithm-specific but also require direct
715   access to the initial training datasets—a requirement that is often unfeasible, especially in the context
716   of LLMs, where datasets are vast and not always readily accessible.
717
718   Approximate Unlearning Acknowledging these limitations, the focus has shifted towards approxi-
719   mate unlearning, a strategy aimed at reducing the influence of specific data on the model without
720   the necessity for complete data removal. Papers such as (Golatkar et al., 2020; Guo et al., 2020;
721   Marchant et al., 2022) utilize variations of the influence function, introduced by Koh & Liang (2017)
722   to remove the influence of the forget data. Furthermore, more recent approaches (Thudi et al., 2022;
723   Neel et al., 2021) use gradient ascent variations to make the unlearning process more efficient and
724   algorithm-agnostic. Although promising, pure approximate unlearning seems unfeasible for use in
725   LLM settings.
