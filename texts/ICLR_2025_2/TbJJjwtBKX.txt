009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Federated learning (FL) protects data privacy by enabling distributed model training
012            without direct access to client data. However, its distributed nature makes it vulner-
013            able to model and data poisoning attacks. While numerous defenses filter malicious
014            clients using statistical metrics, they overlook the role of model redundancy, where
015            not all parameters contribute equally to the model and attack performance. Current
016            attacks manipulate all model parameters uniformly, making them more detectable,
017            while defenses focus on the overall statistics of client updates, leaving gaps for
018
               more sophisticated attacks. We propose an attack-agnostic augmentation method
               to enhance the stealthiness and effectiveness of existing poisoning attacks in FL,
019
               exposing flaws in current defenses and highlighting the need for fine-grained FL
020
               security. Our three-stage methodology, including pill construction, pill poisoning,
021            and pill injection, injects poison into a compact subnet (i.e., pill) of the global
022            model during the iterative FL training. Experimental results show that FL poisoning
023            attacks enhanced by our method can bypass 8 state-of-the-art (SOTA) defenses,
024            gaining an up to 7x error rate increase, as well as on average a more than 2x error
025            rate increase on both IID and non-IID data, in both cross-silo and cross-device FL
026            systems.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   With the increasing need of machine learning and cloud computing, Federated Learning
032   (FL) (Konečnỳ et al., 2016; McMahan et al., 2017) has become a prominent method for train-
033
      ing models using distributed data from numerous clients. Unlike traditional centralized machine
      learning, FL does not require direct data access, thus reducing communication overhead and preserv-
034
      ing data privacy. However, its distributed architecture makes FL vulnerable to attacks if clients are
035
      compromised. Many studies (Baruch et al., 2019; Fang et al., 2020; Bhagoji et al., 2019; Shejwalkar
036   & Houmansadr, 2021; Cao & Gong, 2022; Bagdasaryan et al., 2020) have explored these poisoning
037   attacks, where malicious clients alter the global model’s behavior. These attacks are categorized as:
038   1) Model poisoning, where attackers directly modify local updates to skew global parameters (Fang
039   et al., 2020; Shejwalkar & Houmansadr, 2021); and 2) Data poisoning, where malicious samples are
040   injected into local training datasets (Bagdasaryan et al., 2020; Tolpegin et al., 2020; Xie et al., 2020;
041   Sun et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2020; Chen et al., 2017; Liu et al., 2018; Qi et al., 2022). Poisoning
042   attacks pose significant risks to FL (Lyu et al., 2020; Kairouz et al., 2021; Mothukuri et al., 2021;
043   AbdulRahman et al., 2020), undermining its integrity and reliability.
044   To mitigate these attacks, defenses have been proposed, including adaptive client filtering(Blanchard
045   et al., 2017; Cao et al., 2021; Xu et al., 2021; Nguyen et al., 2022; Rieger et al., 2022; Yan et al.,
046   2023b), statistical parameter aggregation(Yin et al., 2018; Guerraoui et al., 2018; Fung et al., 2018;
047   Panda et al., 2022), client-dominant detection(Guo et al., 2021; 2024; Sun et al., 2021a; Zhang et al.,
048   2023b; Zhu et al., 2023), and other advanced metrics and pipelines(Xie et al., 2019; 2021; Cao et al.,
049   2023; 2022; Zhang et al., 2022a). These methods focus on detecting abnormal updates, which are
050
      typically obvious in existing attacks, especially in existing model poisoning attacks that treat all
      neural network parameters uniformly.
051
052   We argue that modifying all parameters uniformly is not a cost-effective approach. Studies on model
053   pruning (Frankle & Carbin, 2018; Lin et al., 2018; Han et al., 2015; Mugunthan et al., 2022; Jiang
      et al., 2022) show that parameters do not contribute equally to a model’s performance. Altering


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      redundant parameters wastes resources and reduces attack stealthiness. A more effective strategy is
055   to target critical parameters (Zhang et al., 2023a), which significantly impact performance, thereby
056   increasing the attack’s effectiveness while maintaining stealthiness.
057
058
      Thus, we propose a novel attack-agnostic augmentation method that enhances model poisoning
      attacks using a three-stage pipeline: pill construction, pill poisoning, and pill injection. In the first
059
      stage, we design a pill blueprint and identify its corresponding subnet instance in the target model.
060
      During pill poisoning, existing FL attacks are applied in an attack-agnostic manner to poison the
061   selected pill subnet. Finally, in pill injection, the poisoned pill is inserted into an estimated benign
062   update, and a two-step adjustment is used to minimize the difference between the poisoned and
063   benign updates. This approach dynamically generates, poisons, and injects a pill into the global
064   model, augmenting existing FL poisoning attacks.
065
      We conduct extensive experiments to evaluate the effectiveness of our augmentation method. We
066
      apply it to four baseline poisoning attacks: sign-flipping attack, trim attack (Fang et al., 2020),
067   krum attack (Fang et al., 2020), and min-max attack (Shejwalkar & Houmansadr, 2021). Using
068   both the original and augmented versions, we measure error rates (i.e., the proportion of incorrect
069   predictions) of the global model trained with nine aggregation rules: FedAvg (McMahan et al., 2017),
070   FLTrust (Cao et al., 2021), Multi-Krum (Blanchard et al., 2017), Median (Yin et al., 2018), Trim (Yin
071   et al., 2018), Bulyan (Guerraoui et al., 2018), FLDetector (Zhang et al., 2022a), DnC (Shejwalkar
072   & Houmansadr, 2021), and Flame (Nguyen et al., 2022). These aggregation rules represent most
073   existing defense metrics. We also design an adaptive defense where the defender has full knowledge
074   of our pipeline and implementation. Results show our method substantially improves existing FL
075
      poisoning attacks, leading to over a 2x average increase in model prediction error rates under existing
      defenses, and up to a 7x increase in some cases.
076
077   Our contributions are summarized as follows:
078
079          • We propose a generic, attack-agnostic augmentation method that enhances poisoning attacks
080
               against robust FL by encapsulating model poisoning attacks into well-defined subnets (i.e.,
               pills) with comprehensive metric-based adjustments.
081
082          • Extensive experiments on three common datasets against nine aggregation rules demonstrate
083            that our method helps baseline attacks bypass almost all existing defenses, which cannot be
084            successfully attacked by the original baseline attacks.
085          • We identify limitations of existing poisoning attacks and defenses in FL, highlighting the
086            need and potential for fine-grained FL security.

103   2.2   P OISONING ATTACKS IN FL
104
105   Based on prior investigations (Shejwalkar et al., 2022; Khan et al., 2023; Jere et al., 2020), existing
106   poisoning attacks in Federated Learning (FL) can be classified into model poisoning attacks and
107   data poisoning attacks, according to the techniques employed by attackers. In model poisoning
      attacks, attackers may directly compromise the global model by manipulating the updates from local


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      models (Baruch et al., 2019; Fang et al., 2020; Shejwalkar & Houmansadr, 2021; Cao & Gong, 2022;
109   Bhagoji et al., 2019) by compromised clients. Alternatively, in data poisoning attacks, they may
110   poison their local datasets to indirectly influence the global model (Tolpegin et al., 2020; Bagdasaryan
111   et al., 2020; Xie et al., 2020; Sun et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2022b). More details
112   of existing FL attacks are presented in Appendix A.1.
113
      Additionally, our method’s pill design is inspired by a specialized data poisoning attack known as
114
      the subnet replacement attack (SRA) (Qi et al., 2022). This approach concentrates backdoor attacks
115   within an arrow-width subnetwork of the original model. It trains this selected subnet using poisoned
116   data and replaces the corresponding parameters of the target model with those from the trained
117   subnetwork. Once the replacement is complete, SRA severs the connections between the poisoned
118   subnetwork and the original model to preserve the efficacy of the attack. The stealthy yet effective
119   design of SRA inspires our method. In particular, we devise a new subnet structure, referred to as
120   the pill blueprint, which features heterogeneous widths to better accommodate a variety of existing
121   FL poisoning attacks. Besides, unlike SRA’s one-time injection, our method gradually poisons the
122   global model throughout the entire FL training, achieving better effectiveness against a wide range of
123
      defenses in the FL setting.
124
125   2.3   D EFENSES AGAINST P OISONING ATTACKS IN FL
126
127   Existing defenses can be categorized based on the mitigation strategies that they utilize, including
128   Adaptive Client Filtering, Statistical Parameter Aggregation, Client-dominant Detection, and Other
129   Advanced Metrics and Pipelines. More details are presented in Appendix A.2.
130   To comprhensively evaluate our method, we use Multi-Krum (MKrum) (Blanchard et al., 2017),
131   Trimmed Mean (Trim) (Yin et al., 2018), Coordinate-wise Median (Median) (Yin et al., 2018),
132   Bulyan (Guerraoui et al., 2018), FLTrust (Cao et al., 2021), FLDetector (FLD) (Zhang et al., 2022a),
133   DnC (Shejwalkar & Houmansadr, 2021), and Flame (Nguyen et al., 2022), a set of representative
134   methods, as our baselines. More details are shown in Appendix A.2.
135
136
      2.4   T HREAT M ODEL
137
138   We follow the typical threat model used in existing studies (Fang et al., 2020; Shejwalkar &
139   Houmansadr, 2021), where the attacker has access to a subset of compromised clients and aims to
140   increase the error rates of the global model on specific classes or across all classes. In this scenario,
141   defenses cannot directly analyze the data on each client as the defender’s setting in Blanchard et al.
142   (2017); Yin et al. (2018); Cao et al. (2021); Guo et al. (2021). Instead, they identify malicious clients
143   by analyzing the uploaded client updates. Further details are provided in Appendix B.

      3     D ESIGN O BJECTIVES AND C HALLENGES
147
148   After analyzing the drawbacks and various implementations of existing FL poisoning attacks, we
149   define three main objectives for our attack augmentation method: 1) For stealthiness, the augmentation
150
      method should stay stealthy while achieving comparable performance with original attacks. 2) For
      compatibility, the augmentation should be compatible with most of the existing FL poisoning attacks
151
      with few modifications on their implementations. 3) For generality, the attack augmentation should
152
      be able to bypass general detection methods with different detection metrics.
153
154   Corresponding to each objective, three challenges need to be addressed:
155
156          • It presents a significant challenge that the attack augmentation method must use significantly
157            fewer parameters while still achieving similar results as the original attacks.
158
             • It is challenging to develop a uniform augmentation method for various FL poisoning attacks
159            since they require different information and are implemented in different training stages.
160
161          • It is difficult to devise a general strategy that bypasses all common detection approaches,
               while guaranteeing the attack effectiveness.

      4.2   P ILL C ONSTRUCTION
224   This stage aims to construct a pill structure for augmenting the stealthiness while retaining the attack
225   effectiveness before being augmented. The pill is carefully crafted to involve a minimal subset of
226   parameters from specific positions of the target model. We first define a pill’s blueprint as the pill’s
227   graphic structure, independent of target model parameters. Then, we propose a dynamic pill search
228   algorithm to identify and map concrete parameters from the target model to the blueprint.
229
      Designing Pill Blueprint. The blueprint design is inspired by SRA (Qi et al., 2022), which shows that
230   poisoning a narrow subnetwork (one neuron/channel in each layer) is adequate to effectively inject
231   backdoors into machine learning models (not in the FL setting). However, their technique cannot be
232   used for our purposes as their subnet architecture is very specific. It does not support attacking various
233   targets; it is a fixed and pre-selected subnet without considering the dynamics of model training in FL;
234   and its poisoned subnet is not stealthy, having substantially larger weight values compared to others
235   due to the need to disseminate the poison effect through such a small pre-selected network. Therefore,
236   we propose a novel blueprint method, in which the subnet structure is general, and its instantiations
237   (i.e., the concrete subnets) vary across steps in the FL training procedure, including important neurons
238
      by a dynamic search algorithm. This allows small weight changes because poisoning important
      neurons enables easy dissemination, maximizing attack stealthiness. In particular, the pill blueprint is
239
      designed to accommodate various target classes of different FL poisoning attacks. It achieves this by
240
      manipulating the outputs relevant to multiple classes simultaneously, via disrupting all the output
241   neurons together. Hence, our pill blueprint design follows the rules below:
242
243         1. The pill blueprint only contains one neuron in each linear layer or one channel in each
244            convolutional layer, except for the last two layers. Suppose Nip represents the neuron/channel
245            number in Layer i in our pill blueprint, then Nip = 1 when i < L − 1, where L is the total
246            layer counts in our pill blueprint.
                                                               p
247         2. In the last two layers of our pill blueprint, NL−1   = NLp = number of classes.
248
249
      Dynamic Pill Search. According to existing studies on neural network pruning (Frankle & Carbin,
      2018; Lin et al., 2018; Han et al., 2015; Mugunthan et al., 2022; Jiang et al., 2022), parameters
250
      with a larger magnitude typically dominate the model’s performance. The optimal solution is hence
251
      to examine the model parameters to search for a globally optimal pill that encompasses the most
252   important parameters.
253
254   However, such a globally optimal pill could be identified via a pruning-based method (Wu et al.,
255
      2020; Sun et al., 2021b), and hence our attack could be easily detected. Besides, searching for a
      globally optimal pill is inefficient when the model has a large number of parameters. Thus, we search
256
      for an approximate pill instead, with an attacker-defined start point, and only evaluate a small subset
257
      of the entire model’s parameters. We name the search algorithm as “approximate max pill search”.
258   The key idea is to perform a targeted neuron search at each layer by focusing only on the neurons
259   connected to the selected neurons from the previous layer, following a high-sum-of-weights-first
260   principle that prioritizes neurons based on the cumulative sum of their connection weights to the
261   previously selected neurons. The entire search contains four steps:
262
263   Step 1 Random Start Point Selection: Randomly select neurons from the first layer of the target
264
      model, denoted as V1 , based on the pill’s blueprint and neuron count N1p in its first layer. These
      neurons are fixed as start points throughout the search.
265
266   Step 2 Layer-wise Search: For each subsequent layer li , we compute the sum of weights connecting
267   neurons in Vi−1 to neurons in li and rank the neurons in li based on the descending order of the sum
268
      of weights. Top Nip neurons are chosen for Vi .
269   Step 3 Output Neuron Pairing: Pair the selected neurons VL−1 in the final hidden layer with the
      neurons in the output layer lL , ensuring a one-to-one correspondence.

      4.3                   P ILL P OISONING
292
      In the pill poisoning stage, we aim to condense the poison into the pill using existing attacks. To
293   achieve compatibility, our method simply reuses existing FL poisoning attacks, without any intrusive
294   modification to their original implementations. We only modify the input of existing FL poisoning
295   attacks by replacing the base model update with the update from a model that has undergone extra
                                       m
296   training rounds, denoted as ∆ĝt+1  . Additionally, we restrict changes to parameters within the pill.
297   The output is a poisoned pill that will be used in the next pill injection stage.
298
      The motivation to use an extra-trained model update as the reference model update is shown in
299   Figure 2. As shown in the figure, with the increasing number of extra training rounds on the malicious
300   clients, the generated malicious model update becomes less opposite to the FLTrust (Cao et al., 2021)
301   server’s model update. Thus, we adopt the extra training in our method and limit the extra training
302   epoch number Eextra to less than the number of malicious clients m times the benign local training
303   epoch number E, denoted as Eextra ≤ m · E. With this extra training epoch number limit, we do not
304   violate the threat model since the attacker can utilize the data and computational resources of all the
305   compromised clients.

341

510   to maintain consistent performance enhancements regardless of the dataset or model complexity
511   involved. Specifically, our method helps all four baseline attacks bypass all nine baselines on CIFAR-
512   10 dataset, achieving 0.288 error rate increase on average, presented in Table 4. More detailed results
513   on MNIST dataset are shown in Appendix J.
514
      We also explore the impact of the pill search algorithm in our method in Appendix M. The results show
515
      that the "approximate max pill search" algorithm outperforms the "approximate min pill search" in 41
516   out of 56 cases (approximately 73%), underscoring its effectiveness in leveraging the most influential
517   parameters to enhance attack impacts. Additional results on ablation studies and generalizability are
518   presented in Appendix P to Appendix U.

      (white-box scenario), we design an adaptive defense and present the experimental details in Ap-

531
532
      In this paper, we propose a novel attack-agnostic augmentation method to enhance existing poisoning
533
      attacks in FL by concentrating attacks into a pill (a tiny subnet). Our approach is constructed with
534   three stages, including pill construction, pill poisoning, and pill injection. Accordingly, we first use a
535   dynamic pill search algorithm to determine the concrete pill based on the pill blueprint. Then we
536   poison the pill using existing FL poisoning attacks, and carefully inject the poison pill into the target
537   model with two pill-related masks and a two-step adjustment. Our method enables existing poisoning
538   attacks to achieve more than 2x error rates on average compared with their original implementations.
539   The effectiveness of our method in exploiting and exacerbating the inherent weaknesses of current
      FL defenses highlights the critical need for more refined detection measures in FL.

759   A.1    A DDITIONAL D ETAILS OF E XISTING ATTACKS IN FL
760
761   Although model poisoning attacks are effective, existing attacks have limited stealthiness and can
762   be detected by many existing defenses. Our goal is hence to demonstrate that such attacks can be
763   augmented in a uniform way. Model poisoning attacks directly manipulate the parameters uploaded
764   by clients, with a minimal interference to the local training process. Among these attacks, the simplest
765   form is the sign-flipping attack, which directly flips the model update and scales it with a constant
766   factor. A-Little-is-Enough (Baruch et al., 2019) generates malicious updates within a calculated
767   perturbation range to deceive the global model. Adaptive attacks (Fang et al., 2020), such as the
768   Trim attack and the Krum attack, dynamically scale malicious updates based on parameter values and
769
      distances. The Min-Max and Min-Sum Attacks (Shejwalkar & Houmansadr, 2021) provide a dynamic
      scaling for malicious updates based on different distance-based criteria. MPAF (Cao & Gong, 2022)
770
      aims to drive the global model towards a predefined target model with poor performance on given
771
      FL tasks. For the sake of generality, we employ the sign-flipping attack, two types of adaptive
772   attacks (Fang et al., 2020) (the Trim attack and the Krum attack), and the Min-Max attack (Shejwalkar
773   & Houmansadr, 2021) as the baseline attacks in this paper.
774

      A.2    A DDITIONAL D ETAILS OF E XISTING D EFENSES IN FL
777
778
779
      Adaptive Client Filtering. These techniques such as Krum and Multi-Krum (Blanchard et al., 2017)
      filter out malicious clients through single or multiple rounds of client selection based on distance
780
      scores. FLTrust (Cao et al., 2021) computes trust scores using the cosine similarity between each
781
      client update and the server model’s update for weighted averaging. SignGuard (Xu et al., 2021)
782   employs sign-based clustering combined with norm-based thresholding to identify and filter malicious
783   clients. Flame (Nguyen et al., 2022) and Deepsight (Rieger et al., 2022) propose adaptive clustering
784   and clipping to safeguard against backdoor attacks. SkyMask (Yan et al., 2023b) clusters trainable
785   feature masks of clients to assess each client’s risk level.
786
      Statistical Parameter Aggregation. Approaches like Median and Trim (Yin et al., 2018) use coordinate-
787
      wise median or trimmed mean values to aggregate model updates. Bulyan (Guerraoui et al., 2018)
788   enhances robustness by integrating Krum with Trim techniques. Fool’s Gold (Fung et al., 2018)
789   applies an adaptive learning rate based on inter-client contribution similarity to mitigate the effects of
790   malicious updates. SparseFed (Panda et al., 2022) aggregates sparsified updates, reducing the risk of
791   model poisoning attacks.
792
      Client-dominant Detection. Siren (Guo et al., 2021) and Siren+ (Guo et al., 2024) set proactive
793
      accuracy-based alarms at the client level with the corresponding server-side decisions to counter
794   various model poisoning attacks. FL-WBC (Sun et al., 2021a) introduces client-side noise to diminish
795   the efficacy of attacks and shorten their duration. FLIP (Zhang et al., 2023b) achieves higher
796   robustness through client-side reverse-engineering defenses against extensive poisoning strategies.
797   LeadFL (Zhu et al., 2023) uses a client-side Hessian matrix optimization to reduce the impact of
798   adversarial patterns on backdoor and targeted attacks.
799
      Other Advanced Metrics and Pipelines. Various studies employ other sophisticated metric pipelines
800   designed for detection to ensure robust defense against poisoning attacks. These include techniques
801   proposed in studies such as Zeno (Xie et al., 2019), CRFL (Xie et al., 2021), FedRecover (Cao
802   et al., 2023), FLCert (Cao et al., 2022), FLDetector (Zhang et al., 2022a), and MESAS (Krauß &
803   Dmitrienko, 2023).

805

                                                                                              (i)
807   metric. In each round, the Krum server sums the distances between each client update gt and its
808   K − m − 2 neighbors, and uses these sums as the scores for all the clients. The Krum server then
809   selects the client’s model update with the lowest score. Multi-Krum is a variant of Krum that uses
      iterative Krum to pick multiple candidates for aggregation.

      B    D ETAILED T HREAT M ODEL
849
850
      Attacker’s Goal and Capabilities. This paper focuses on improving the effectiveness of existing
851
      poisoning attacks in FL. Similar to previous work (Fang et al., 2020; Shejwalkar & Houmansadr,
852   2021), an attacker aims to raise the error rates of the global model on a specific class or multiple
853   classes by sending poisoned model updates via compromised clients during the iterative aggregation.
854   Our method does not require any additional knowledge compared with existing FL poisoning
855   attacks. Hence we reuse the typical threat model in existing studies (Fang et al., 2020; Shejwalkar &
856   Houmansadr, 2021). The attacker has a complete control of the compromised clients, including their
857   local data, local training, and uploading process. With the aggregated resources on the compromised
858   clients, the attacker may aggregate the local data from the compromised clients to do extra training or
859   aggregate their local updates to do model estimation. The attacker may or may not know the updates
860
      of other benign clients, depending on the confidentiality of the communication channels between
      the server and clients. Besides, the attacker cannot access the server’s information, including the
861
      aggregation rules or the selected clients in each round.
862
863   Defense Settings. Most of the defenses in FL are deployed and executed on the server. We adopt a
      similar defense setting as existing studies (Blanchard et al., 2017; Yin et al., 2018; Cao et al., 2021;


                                                              16
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


864
      Guo et al., 2021). The server cannot directly analyze the local data or the local training of clients. It
865   can only detect malicious clients through model updates from different clients. The server can collect
866   and possess a root test dataset to provide more accurate and robust detection, while the data of such a
867   root test dataset cannot be derived from clients. The data distribution of this root test dataset may or
868   may not be the same as the data distribution across the clients.

      A complete procedure of the “approximate max pill search” algorithm consists of the following four
890   steps. To improve readability, we use “neuron” to represent both neurons in fully connected layers
891   and channels in convolutional layers, and we use the classification task as an example:
892
893   Step 1 Random Start Point Selection: At the beginning of the search, we randomly choose a subset
894   of neurons from the first layer l1 of the target model, based on the structure of the first layer l1p in the
895   pill’s blueprint and its neuron number N1p . The selected neurons are termed as V1 , and defined as
896   start points, which are then fixed across the entire FL training.
897   Step 2 Layer-wise Search: For each subsequent layer li in the target model, we first calculate the
898   sum of the weights from the selected neurons Vi−1 in layer li−1 to each neuron in li . Then, we rank
899   all the neurons in li based on the parameter value sums and choose top Nip neurons in li as the new
900   Vi , where Nip represents the number of neurons in the ith layer of pill’s blueprint. Vi and all the
901
      parameters from Vi−1 to Vi are recorded.
902   Step 3 Output Neuron Pairing: After visiting lL−1 layers, where L is the total number of layers in
903   the target model, ||VL−1 || should equal to the neuron number in lL of the target model, which also
904   equals to the number of classes. We select all the neurons in the target model’s lL layer into our pill
905
      to construct VL . Then, we only record the parameters from one neuron in VL−1 to only one neuron
      in VL based on the index order (i.e., the first neuron in VL−1 is paired with the first neuron in VL ).
906
      Since ||VL−1 || equals to ||VL ||, the number of recorded parameters equals to the number of classes,
907
      avoiding poisoning too many parameters in a single layer.
908
909
      Step 4 Pill Mask Construction: With the recorded Vi and the corresponding parameters, we con-
      struct two masks M and Mdisc . The mask M records the pill’s parameters in the target model, and
910
      the disconnection mask Mdisc records the parameters of the connections between the pill and the
911
      rest of the target model. M is used for poisoning, while Mdisc is used to disconnect the poison
912   pill from the target model, maintaining the integrity and performance of the pill during poisoning.
913   The two masks have the same shape as the target model’s parameters. To construct M , we set the
914   locations corresponding to the pill parameters to 1, and the others to 0. Based on M , we can similarly
915   obtain the corresponding disconnected mask Mdisc , which sets the locations corresponding to the
916   parameters from neurons other than Vi−1 to Vi in each model layer li (except for the Lth layer since
917   we choose all the neurons from it), and also those corresponding to parameters from Vi−1 to other
      pill irrelevant neurons in each model layer li to 1. The two masks are used in the Pill Injection Stage.

      We first design three searching strategies, including one-time searching strategy, repeated searching
959   strategy, and adaptive searching strategy.
960
961   In the one-time searching strategy, we search the pill based on the initial global model using the
962
      “approximate max pill search” algorithm introduced in §4.2, and keep this pill unchanged in the whole
      FL training. The one-time searching strategy benefits the formation of pill in the global model, while
963
      the initial pill may be less effective with the increasing training rounds of the global model, due to
964
      the changing importance of the model parameters.
965
966   On the contrary, the repeated searching strategy runs the ‘approximate max pill search” algorithm in
967   every training round. The repeated searching strategy can help our method modify more parameters
968
      in the global model, and make the pill less traceable. While the attacking effects may be reduced due
      to the constantly changing pill.
969
970   Considering advantages and disadvantages of both the one-time searching strategy and the repeated
971   searching strategy, we design a more flexible searching strategy, termed as “adaptive searching
      strategy”. In the adaptive searching strategy, our method searches the new pill only when the pill is

1008
1009        Similarity-based Adjustment. As shown in Line 1-12 of Algorithm 2, we first compute the
1010        maximum cosine similarity Smax between the normal model updates from the compromised clients
1011        and the estimated global model update in the current round. Then, we iteratively and alternately
1012        reduce the magnitude of the poison pill’s parameters with the down-scaling factor C↓ , and increase
1013        the magnitude of the rest estimated global model update’s parameters with the up-scaling factor C↑ ,
1014        until the cosine similarity between the entire poisoned model update and the estimated global model
1015        update is greater than Smax or the adjustment total iteration is greater than the threshold Citer .

1017        we reuse the up-scaling factor C↑ and the down-scaling factor C↓ to adjust the magnitude of the entire

       Model, Dataset, and Hyper-Parameters. In our experiments, we employ a four-layer Convolutional
1029   Neural Network (CNN) and a simplified version of AlexNet (Krizhevsky et al., 2012). The structures
1030   of the models and their corresponding pill blueprints are detailed in Appendix C. We evaluate our
1031   method on three widely-used datasets: MNIST (LeCun, 1998), Fashion-MNIST (Xiao et al., 2017),
1032   and CIFAR-10 (Krizhevsky et al., 2009). We use the CNN model on MNIST and Fashion-MNIST
1033   datasets, and the AlexNet (Krizhevsky et al., 2012) on CIFAR-10 dataset. Each experiment is repeated
1034   five times to ensure reliability, with the mean and standard deviation (std) of the results reported.
1035   IID and Non-IID Data Settings. Our method was assessed under both IID and non-IID data
1036   distributions to understand its performance across data heterogeneity. For IID data setting, we
1037   uniformly split all the training data into K shards, and distribute each shard to a random client. For
1038   non-IID data setting, we utilize the non-IID degree p as defined in prior studies (Fang et al., 2020;
1039   Guo et al., 2021). A higher p indicates greater data heterogeneity among the clients. Specifically,
1040   when p = 0.1, the data configuration is essentially IID. We set p = 0.5 to to intensify the non-IID
1041   condition, under which we create and allocate K non-IID data shards to all the clients, simulating
1042   a more realistic and challenging FL environment. Given that FLTrust necessitates a root dataset at
1043
       the server, we select this dataset first from the available training data. Subsequently, we distribute
       the remaining data among the clients according to the aforementioned IID and non-IID rules. This
1044
       approach ensures that there is no overlap between server’s data and client’s data.
1045
1046   Configurations of Dynamic Patterns in Our Method. As outlined in Section 4.2, we design six
1047   dynamic patterns for the pill search. We systematically evaluate all six patterns and present the results
1048   of the most effective strategy.
1049   Evaluation Metrics. We use error rates – defined as the proportion of incorrect predictions – to
1050   evaluate attack effectiveness. Given that the model poisoning attacks discussed are all untargeted,
1051   higher error rates indicate more effective attacks. To assess the stealthiness of our method in delivering
1052   malicious updates, we employ two metrics: 1) cosine similarity score, measuring alignment with the
1053   server’s model update in FLTrust; 2) distance score, used in Multi-Krum to evaluate the closeness of
1054   poisoned updates to benign updates.

1333
       Our method significantly enhances non-state-of-the-art (non-SOTA) model poisoning attacks, en-
1334
       abling them to SOTA results against various prevalent defenses. This is accomplished through a
1335   pill-based, attack-agnostic augmentation pipeline. We not only demonstrate our method’s capabilities
1336   but also expose fundamental vulnerabilities within the current designs of defense mechanisms.
1337
1338
       For future attacks in FL, it is essential for attackers to meticulously evaluate the importance of each
       parameter in their implementation. By targeting specific subsets of parameters, attackers can devise
1339
       more flexible and adaptive attacks, improving stealthiness and complicating defense efforts. As
1340
       for future defenses, while individually checking each parameter might seem viable, its practical
1341   deployment is hindered by high overheads, making it infeasible in real-world applications.
1342
1343   Thus, there is a pressing need for more sophisticated defenses that can conduct fine-grained analyses
1344
       of the roles of different parameters in neural networks, while executing without imposing prohibitive
       computational costs.
