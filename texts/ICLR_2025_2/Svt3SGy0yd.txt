009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            The ubiquitous deployment of deep learning systems on resource-constrained Edge
012            devices is hindered by their high computational complexity coupled with their
013            fragility to out-of-distribution (OOD) data, especially to naturally occurring com-
014            mon corruptions. Current solutions rely on the Cloud to train and compress models
015            before deploying to the Edge. This incurs high energy and latency costs in trans-
016            mitting locally acquired field data to the Cloud while also raising privacy concerns.
017
               We propose Growing Efficient, Accurate, and Robust neural networks (GEARnn)
               to grow and train robust networks in-situ, i.e., completely on the Edge device.
018
               Starting with a low-complexity initial backbone network, GEARnn employs One-
019
               Shot Growth (OSG) to grow a network satisfying the memory constraints of the
020            Edge device using clean data, and robustifies the network using Efficient Robust
021            Augmentation (ERA) to obtain the final network. We demonstrate results on a
022            NVIDIA Jetson Xavier NX, and analyze the trade-offs between accuracy, robust-
023            ness, model size, energy consumption, and training time. Our results demonstrate
024            the construction of efficient, accurate, and robust networks entirely on an Edge
025            device.
026
027   1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029   The ubiquitous practical deployment of deep neural networks is mainly hindered by their lack
030   of robustness and high computational cost. Prior art has shown that these deep networks are ex-
031   tremely fragile to adversarial perturbations Szegedy et al. (2013)Goodfellow et al. (2014) and
032   out-of-distribution (OOD) data Hendrycks & Dietterich (2019)Mintun et al. (2021). Natural corrup-
033   tions Hendrycks & Dietterich (2019) (a specific type of OOD data) are more commonly encountered
034   at the Edge where real-time data is being continually acquired, e.g., video sequences acquired by
035   on-board cameras in autonomous agents (self-driving cars, field robots, drones), which tend to
036
      be distorted by weather and blur. The state-of-the-art defense against these corruptions employs
      robust data augmentation Hendrycks et al. (2019; 2021); Modas et al. (2022) which incurs a huge
037
      computational cost when implemented on an Edge device. Fig. 1 indicates that it takes more than
038
      2 days to robustly train a VGG-19 network Simonyan & Zisserman (2014) on a simple CIFAR-10
039   dataset when implemented on NVIDIA Jetson Xavier NX Edge device NVIDIA (a). Even for a small
040   5% VGG-19 network it takes more than a day, thus highlighting the non-trivial nature of the problem.
041   This is a huge concern because Edge devices are typically battery-powered and such large training
042   costs reduce their operational life-time.
043
      Traditional solutions for reducing network complexity such as pruning Han et al. (2015); Li et al.
044
      (2016); Diffenderfer et al. (2021), quantization Rastegari et al. (2016); Hubara et al. (2016) and
045   neural architecture search (NAS) Liu et al. (2018); Zoph et al. (2018) mainly target Edge inference,
046   and are not suited for Edge training since they start with hard-to-fit over-parameterized networks
047   that require the large computational resources of the Cloud. However, transmitting local data to
048   the Cloud incurs energy and latency costs while raising privacy concerns, thus requiring training to
049   happen completely on the Edge. Given the above challenges, the primary objective of our work is: To
050   design and train compact robust networks completely in-situ on Edge devices. Our proposed solution
051   Growing Efficient, Accurate, and Robust neural networks (GEARnn) is based on the family of growth
052   algorithms Chen et al. (2015); Wu et al. (2020); Evci et al. (2022); Yuan et al. (2020) that gradually
053
      increase the size of an initial backbone network to reach the robust accuracy of a full network but at a
      fraction of its size, training complexity, and energy consumption.

      2 BACKGROUND AND R ELATED W ORK
097
      Robust Data Augmentation: This is the most commonly used method for addressing corruptions
098
      due to its ease of integration into the training flow and ability to replicate low-level structural
099   distortions. AugMix Hendrycks et al. (2019), PRIME Modas et al. (2022) and FourierMix Sun
100   et al. (2021) combine chains of stochastic image transforms and enforce consistency using a suitable
101   loss function to generate an augmented sample from a clean image. DeepAugment Hendrycks
102   et al. (2021) randomly distorts the parameters of an image-to-image network to generate augmented
103   images. CARDs Diffenderfer et al. (2021) combines data augmentation Hendrycks et al. (2019)
104   and pruning Frankle & Carbin (2018) to find compact robust networks embedded in large over-
105   parameterized networks. Adversarial augmentations Zhao et al. (2020); Rusak et al. (2020); Calian
106   et al. (2021) have also been proposed to handle common corruptions. Unlike our proposed GEARnn
107
      algorithm, all these techniques significantly increase the complexity over vanilla training and are thus
      inappropriate for Edge deployment.

132   3     N OTATION AND P ROBLEM S ETUP
133
134   Notation: Let f : Rd → [C] be a hard classifier which classifies input x ∈ Rd into one of C classes.
135   We choose f to be a convolutional neural network (CNN) with L layers (depth), {wl }L     l=1 output
136   channels (widths), and (K, K) sized kernels. The network f is trained on n samples (x, y) ∼ Din ,
137   where (x, y) ∈ Rd × [C] and Din denotes the “in-distribution” or “clean” data. LCE represents the
138   cross-entropy loss and Laug = LCE + λLJSD represents the augmentation loss where LJSD is the
139
      Jensen-Shannon divergence loss described in Hendrycks et al. (2019).
140   During inference, f can be exposed to samples from both Din and Dout (“out-of-distribution” or
141   “corrupted” data). In case of common corruptions, (xout , y) ∼ Dout is obtained by xout = κ(xin , s),
142   where (xin , y) ∼ Din , κ is a corruption filter and s is the severity level of the corruption. We denote
143   pe = Pr(ŷ ̸= y) as the classification error at inference where ŷ = f (xtest ). When (xtest , y) ∼ Din ,
144   we define (1 − pe ) as clean accuracy Acln , and when (xtest , y) ∼ Dout , we define (1 − pe ) as robust
145
      accuracy Arob . The value of pe is determined empirically in this work.
146   Problem: Our primary objective is to maximize the empirical clean and robust accuracies (Acln and
                                                         PL
147   Arob ) while ensuring the network complexity ( l=1 wl ) is small. Along with these two criteria, we
148   also prioritize reduction in training time (ttr ) and training energy consumption (E) on hardware.

460   8.2                      R ATIONALE FOR 2-P HASE A PPROACH
461
462
      In this section, we provide insights for the efficacy of GEARnn-2 and the 2-Phase approach. In
      particular, we highlight why training or growth done on clean data provides a good initialization
463
      for robust training. We look at the loss curves for the 1-Phase approaches (Small (AugMix), Small
464
      (ERA), GEARnn-1) and the 2-Phase approach (GEARnn-2) in Fig. 7. The initial dip in GEARnn-2
465   loss function in Fig. 7a is due to the loss landscape being different for Phase-1 done on clean data
466   compared to Phase-2 done on augmented data. One can clearly see that GEARnn-2 achieves a lower
467   loss at a faster rate compared to the other 1-Phase approaches, thus justifying the importance of clean
468   growth initialization. We also plot the filter normalized loss curves Li et al. (2018) in Fig. 7b to
469   observe the loss landscapes around the converged weights. GEARnn-2 finds the smallest minima
470   while also having a wide curve which enables better generalization Li et al. (2018).
471   The above explanation illustrates why GEARnn-2 has a good training and generalization performance.
472   However, in order to understand why initialization with clean data aids faster convergence of
473   robust training, we look at the Fourier spectrums of the clean, augmented and corrupted images
474   in Fig. 8. Fig. 8a indicates that the clean images lie in the low frequency domain, while the corrupted
475   samples occupy a wide range of frequencies (Figs. 8b & 8c). Crucially, the spectrum containing all
476   the augmentations (in AugMix) Fig. 8d and all the corruptions (in CIFAR-10-C) Fig. 8e is also in
477   the low-frequency domain, similar to the clean image spectrum Fig. 8a. This is unlike the scenario
478   of adversarial or Gaussian noise perturbations, which lie in the high-frequency domain Yin et al.
479
      (2019) and hence may not benefit from clean data initialization. Thus, robust training for common
      corruptions benefits from initialization with clean data.

523   10 C ONCLUSION
524   We addressed the problem of growing robust networks efficiently on Edge devices. Specifically,
525   we concluded that a 2-Phase approach with distinct clean growth and robust training phases is
526   significantly more efficient than a 1-Phase approach which employs augmented data for growth. We
527   encapsulated this result into the GEARnn algorithm and experimentally demonstrated its benefits
528   on a real-life Edge device. An interesting and non-trivial extension of our work would be to use
529   unlabeled data for growing efficient and robust networks. Another extension would be to design
530   robust networks for complex tasks such as object detection on highly resource-constrained Edge
531
      platforms.

888   In this section, we explain how the splitting and growing new neurons in Firefly Wu et al. (2020)
889   (and our growth technique G) is implemented. We explain it in terms of fully-connected layers and
890   neurons, but this can be easily extended to CNNs. Consider a multi-layered perception with two
891   neurons in the hidden layer as shown in Figure 1 of Wu et al. (2020). If x is the input to the neurons,
892   θi and 1 are the weights, σ is the activation function, then we can write the input to the final layer as
893   σ(x, θi ). In case of splitting growth, we add a new incoming weight by perturbing the existing weight
894
      (σ(x, θi − εi δi )) and adding the new perturbed weight (σ(x, θi + εi δi )). When adding a random
      new-grown weight, we add a randomly initialized weight δi such that the input to the final layer is
895
      εi σ(x, δi ). We can write the function as follows:
896

939   E.1   M OTIVATION FOR O N -D EVICE E DGE T RAINING
940
941   GEARnn performs isolated on-device Edge training. However, there are two other scenarios (fine-
942   tuning and federated learning) which can be used interchangeably with GEARnn depending on the
943   application and constraints. But all three methods are important and none is a replacement for the
944
      other. Below we highlight the scenarios where on-device Edge training is necessary:
945   Fine-tuning: There are scenarios where existing pre-trained networks are not useful for the task at
946   hand due to lack overlap in data domains (eg: sensitive medical data, geographical data from other
947   planets). Apart from this, fine-tuning of large models can lead to over-fitting when limited data is
948   available. This is countered by our method since growth involves training extremely small models
949   that gradually increase in size (the problem of over-parametrization is avoided and thus over-fitting).
950
      Along with that, our growth-based approach allows us to design custom parameter-efficient models
      based on the dataset (Section 8.1). This is not possible in existing methods since they either fit large
951
      models on the Edge, or compress the existing models on the Cloud without the local data available
952
      at the Edge. Lastly, even if robust fine-tuning is used instead of GEARnn, our method provides a
953   lesson to fine-tune on clean data before moving to augmented data (2-Phase approach) for efficient
954   fine-tuning.
955
956
      Federated Learning: It was proposed to preserve privacy by transmitting the weights to the Cloud
      instead of the data directly. However, recent works in security have shown that training data can be
957
      extracted from the weights of these models Carlini et al. (2021) thus putting the privacy of Federated
958
      Learning in jeopardy. Practical Federated Learning also suffers from convergence issues due to
959   non-IID data Tang et al. (2018). Lastly, there are several medical or defense applications where the
960   user does not want to share any data or weights and would prefer on-device training.
961
962
      With regard to the concern about lack of labeled data available on a single Edge device, there are
      several ways such as active learning Bengar et al. (2021); Shan et al. (2024), sel-supervision Qin et al.
963
      (2024), semi-supervision Nukavarapu & Nadeem (2021) and quality sampling Liu et al. (2019) to
964
      convert the abundant unlabeled data from the Edge device sensor into trainable data.

       E.3   C HOICE OF N ETWORK S IZE
973
974    Our choice of network size is determined by the growth ratio γ. The growth ratio is chosen such
975    that the model lies within the device memory constraints while achieving the desired accuracy.
976    Analytically estimating the memory consumption during training is challenging due to the dynamic
977    computation graphs, gradient calculations, data movement and hardware optimizations implemented.
978    Hence we resort to an empirical thumb rule - to ensure the nominal batch size used for this task
979    (128 in this case) is maintained at the Edge without any reduction. The growth ratio γ is chosen
980
       accordingly for each network.
