011                                             A BSTRACT
012
013           The advent of generative AI models is revolutionizing drug discovery, generating
014           de novo molecules at unprecedented speed. However, accurately identifying and
015
              rescuing drug candidates among countless generated molecules remains an open
              problem. The essence of this drug-likeness prediction task lies in constructing a
016
              compact subspace that encompasses majority of approved drugs with only a small
017
              number of unknown compounds (drug candidates) inside. Computational chal-
018           lenges arises in constructing a decision boundary on an unbound chemical space
019           that lacks definite negatives, i.e, non drug-likeness. Approved drugs exist highly
020           dispersed across structural space, making it more harsh to effectively separate
021           drugs from non-drugs through existing classifiers. Addressing such challenges,
022           we introduce B OUN D R .E: a novel approach for learning a compact boundary of
023           drug-likeness through an Expectation-Maximization (EM)-like iterative optimiza-
024           tion process. Specifically, we refine both the boundary and the distribution of the
025           embedding space via metric learning, allowing the model to iteratively tighten the
026
              drug-like boundary while pushing non-drug-like compounds outside. Augmented
              by integration of biomedical context within knowledge graphs via multi-modal
027
              alignment, our model demonstrates 10% increase in F1 score over the previous
028
              state-of-the-art, along with strongest robustness to cross-dataset validation. Zero-
029           shot toxic compound filtering and comprehensive drug discovery pipeline case
030           studies further showcases its utility in large-scale screening of AI-generated com-
031           pounds. To facilitate in silico drug discovery, we provide the code and benchmark
032           data under various splitting schemes at: anonymous.4open.science/r/boundr e.

072   Existing methods for drug-likeness prediction tend to fall short. Supervised models (Sun et al.,

082   likeness that refines the boundary and the embedding space iteratively using an EM-like optimiza-

084   the boundary to enclose as many drug-like compounds as possible while pushing non-drug-like
085   compounds outward through metric learning. By continuously adapting the boundary, B OUN D R .E
086   improves upon the overly rigid approaches of conventional one-class classification (OCC) models,
087
      ensuring a tighter, more precise boundary for drug-likeness prediction.

089   modal knowledge alignment, integrating molecular structure with biological and pharmacological
090   data through a novel knowledge-enhanced mixup technique. This fusion of information allows our
091   model to capture more biologically meaningful features of drug-likeness, improving performance
092   in time-based and scaffold-based splits and generalization to unseen chemical scaffolds. Our con-
093
      tributions can be summarized as following: 1) Novel formulation of drug-likeness prediction as
      a one-class classification without reliance on negatives. 2) Proposal of EM-like optimization of
094

095
      Knowledge-integrated multi-modal alignment of structure and biomedical knowledge embeddings
096   for defining drug-likeness with machine learning.

107   Computational Prediction of Drug-likeness Computational identification of drug-like com-
      pounds has long been a focus in drug discovery (Clark & Pickett, 2000), starting with molecular

108
      descriptor-based metrics including the Rule of Five (Ro5) (Lipinski et al., 1997) and QED (Bick-
109   erton et al., 2012). However, these methods are limited to acting as “necessary conditions” for
110   drug-likeness, not as definitive classifiers as pointed out by several studies (Lee et al., 2022; 2023;
111   Li et al., 2024). Recent graph neural network-based approaches including D-GCAN (Sun et al.,
112   2022) and DeepDL (Lee et al., 2022) employ binary classifiers and PU learning, but their reliance
113   on explicit negative sets makes them less effective in open-world chemical spaces where negatives
114   are undefined. Unsupervised methods, such as DrugMetric (Li et al., 2024), utilize VAE-generated
115   latent spaces combined with Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs) to assess drug-likeness.
116   One-class boundary models One-class classification (OCC) methods aim to define a boundary
117   around a positive class (e.g., drugs) without relying on negatives, making them more suited for
118   open-world problems. Techniques like OC-SVM (Schölkopf et al., 2001), Support Vector Data
119   Description (SVDD) (Tax & Duin, 2004), and DeepSVDD (Ruff et al., 2018) seek to minimize the
120   volume around positive samples, assuming the feature space to be fixed and optimal. However, their
121   static nature and reliance on fixed feature spaces often lead to overly broad boundaries and high
122
      false-positive rates in expansive chemical spaces. Our proposed B OUN D R .E model addresses these
      limitations by dynamically refining both the boundary and the feature embedding space through an
123
      iterative EM-like process.
124
125   Deep Multi-modal alignment Multi-modal alignment or multi-modal learning refers to the pro-
126   cess of mapping diverse data modalities, such as image, text, video, and audio, into a unified em-
127
      bedding space that enables effective joint learning and generalization across various downstream
      tasks (Girdhar et al., 2023). A prominent example of multi-modal alignment is CLIP (Radford et al.,
128
      2021), which learns representations by aligning text descriptions with images through contrastive
129
      learning. Several frameworks extend CLIP-based multi-modal learning through finetuning (Goyal
130   et al., 2023) or training-free approaches (Zhang et al., 2022) for more robust optimization. One such
131   method is the recently proposed Geodesic Mixup (Oh et al., 2024), which ensures that multi-modal
132   mixed samples lie on a geodesic path, preserving the structure of L2-normalized embeddings well
133   mixed on a hypersphere.
134
      In the biochemical domain, CLOOME (Sanchez-Fernandez et al., 2023) has been proposed to mod-
135
      ify the CLIP loss through leave-one-out boosting with continuous modern Hopfield networks for
136   chemical and bioassay image alignment. Recently, contrastive learning has also been actively inte-
137   grated into the fields of drug-target interaction prediction (Ye et al., 2021) and element knowledge
138   integration (Fang et al., 2022; 2023). Despite the significant advancements in multi-modal learning,
139   there has not been an attempt to extend such concepts to align the knowledge graph embedding
140   space with the structural embedding space through multimodal-alignment to construct an approved
141   drug chemical space for drug-likeness prediction.

145   3.1   P ROBLEM D EFINITION
146
147   We propose a new perspective on the problem of drug-likeness prediction as constructing a compact
148   and adaptive one-class boundary B around drug-like compounds in a theoretically unbounded chemi-
149   cal space. Let this space of all chemical compounds be denoted as Xcomp , with subset Xdrug ⊂ Xcomp
150   representing drug-like compounds. The approved drug set Ddrug represents a subset of the Xdrug ,
151   while compound set Dcomp is a biased subset of Xcomp , where its small yet unknown portion are
152
      potential drugs that are to be rescued. (Appendix A.6)
153   Given the highly dispersed nature of drugs in chemical space and their approval based on both
154   structure and biomedical knowledge, our framework combines these two modalities into a unified
155   space for more accurate boundary construction, followed by iterative refinement of a hyperspheri-
156   cal boundary to capture drug-like compounds (Figure 2). The alignment of the embedding spaces
157   and the boundary optimization are key to addressing the challenges posed by an unbounded chem-
158
      ical space and the absence of explicit negatives. Notations throughout this paper are organized in
      Appendix D.
159
160   Knowledge-integrated multi-modal alignment The first step in our framework involves the
161   alignment of two complementary drug spaces: the structural space S, and the biomedical knowl-
      edge space K. The key objective is to learn a structural encoder that can also map non-drugs, which

182   leverages the contexts from both molecular structure and biomedical knowledge, capturing a richer
183   representation of drug-like properties.
184
185   Drug-Like Boundary Optimization Once the multi-modal embeddings are aligned into the uni-
186   fied space U, we define a hyperspherical boundary B in a latent space Z, which is generated by an
187   encoder fθ : U → Z. This boundary is characterized by its center c and radius r, and the goal is
188   to optimize B such that it encapsulates as many drug-like compounds as possible while minimizing
189   the inclusion of non-drug-like compounds, leading to decreased in-boundary compound ratio ρ.
190   The optimization of B is an EM-like iterative process, with each iteration improving the compactness
191   of the boundary and reducing the false-positive rate. The iterative refinement not only adapts the
192   boundary B but also dynamically adjusts the embedding space Z through the encoder, making the
193   model more flexible in handling the complex and heterogeneous nature of drug-likeness.

195   3.2     K NOWLEDGE - INTEGRATED MULTI - MODAL ALIGNMENT
196
197   We propose a multi-modal alignment approach, using a knowledge-guided soft CLIP loss augmented
198   with geodesic mixup, to blend structural and biomedical embeddings into a unified space. This
199   process ensures smooth transitions between the two distant domains by interpolating embeddings
200
      on a hypersphere (Figure 3).
201   We begin by aligning two key embedding spaces of: the biomedical knowledge graph embeddings
202   kdrug ∈ K (Bang et al., 2023) and the molecular structural embeddings sdrug ∈ S (Morgan Fin-
203   gerprint). This integration is crucial as it enriches drug representations by combining molecular
204   structures with their biomedical contexts. We train two encoders: a knowledge encoder Eκ : K → U
205   and a structural encoder Eσ : S → U, where both map their respective embeddings to a unified
206
      latent space U ⊂ Rd . The details of the aligned spaces are explained in Appendix C.1.
207

      Effect of multi-modal alignment with softened CLIP loss We compared our softened CLIP loss
458
      with alternative alignment strategies, including CLIP (Radford et al., 2021), Geodesic Mixup, naive
459
      manifold alignment (Ham et al., 2005), and unaligned space (i.e., molecular fingerprints) (Table 4).
460   Our proposed method significantly improves boundary quality due to the enriched representation that
461   aligns molecular structure with biomedical knowledge. The resulting embedding space produces a
462   tighter drug boundary, leading to improved drug-like compound identification performances. The
463   full ablation study results including each component of S-Mix, K-Mix and KS-Mix are provided in
464   Appendix E.5, which also support the utility of integrating all the components.
465
      Effect of EM-like optimization We evaluated the advantage of our EM-like boundary optimiza-
466   tion against traditional binary classifiers, PU learners, and one-class models (Table 5). Our model
467   achieves the lowest ICR (or FPR), showcasing the strength of iterative boundary refinement, which
468   iteratively increases the out-boundary compounds (Appendix E.5.1). Figure 6 shows the robust-
469   ness of our method under increasing compound-to-drug ratios (from 1:1 to 1:100), where our model
470   maintains performance compared to baselines, as the non-drug compounds vastly outnumber drugs.
471
      These ablations confirm the complementary nature of multi-modal alignment and boundary opti-
472   mization in improving drug-likeness prediction.

      5     C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORKS
522
523
      In this work, we introduced B OUN D R .E, a framework for drug-likeness prediction that combines
524
      knowledge-aligned embeddings with EM-like one-class boundary optimization. By leveraging
525   structural and biomedical knowledge through a softened CLIP loss, B OUN D R .E creates a robust
526   multi-modal embedding space. Our experiments show that B OUN D R .E consistently outperforms
527   state-of-the-art models, excelling at identifying drug-like compounds while effectively filtering out
528   toxic molecules, with case studies demonstrating its utility as initial screen of drug candidates.
529
      Several opportunities for improvement remain in our framework. The EM-like strategy still requires
530
      solid approaches for reaching global optima, and lower reliance to initialization points. Further ex-
531   perimental validation of the screened compounds, including efficacy, toxicity and PK/PD profiles,
532   may provide more convincing results on the utility data-driven drug filters in drug discovery en-
533   deavours. In particular, the applicability of our model to specific therapeutic area can be further
534   elaborated. Nonetheless, we believe our model is a promising complementary solution for prioritiz-
535   ing drug-like compounds in early-stage development for efficiency in drug discovery.

848   For our EM-like optimization algorithm, we applied a convergence criterion based on the in-
849   boundary compound ratio (ICR) metric. We initially considered using a traditional loss-based con-

      that both approaches are consistent with the model’s objectives.

891   M-step (Neural Network Optimization): In the M-step, the primary computational effort in-

893   ber of operations in layer h, and N as the dataset size, then the complexity for a forward pass is
              PH
894   O(N · h=1 Fh ). Given that the backward pass is approximately twice as computationally expen-
                                                                              PH
895   sive, the overall complexity for each EM iteration is O(N × D) + O(N · h=1 Fh ).
896
      These complexities illustrate the model’s linear behavior with respect to data size and dimensional-
897   ity, making it efficient for large-scale drug discovery tasks. To validate these claims empirically, we
898   trained our model with approximately 200 drugs and 2,000 non-drug compounds around 100 epochs
899   using single NVIDIA RTX 3090 GPU, and the total training time was consistently under 5 minutes,
900   demonstrating the alignment between theoretical analysis and practical performance.

902   A.5     M ULTIPLE -EM APPROACH FOR AVOIDANCE OF LOCAL OPTIMA
903
904   Avoiding local optima and searching for globally optimal parameters is the core challenge of ma-
905   chine learning. However, classical EM algorithms, including K-means clustering and GMMs, are
906   prone to local optima convergence due to their deterministic and hill-climbing nature of monotonic
907   increase in likelihood, which leads to the model’s sensitivity to initialization conditions.
908   While our model’s stochasticity applied with mini-batch training through Adam optimizer allows
909   flexibility to escape monotonic increase and knowledge-aligned embedding space further pro-
910   vides informative initialization point, we aimed to provide a more direct solution to tackle the
911   initialization-sensitiveness of our framework.
912   Inspired by successful strategies in EM-based models, such as the Multiple Expectation maximiza-
913   tions for Motif Elicitation (MEME) gene motif search algorithm (Bailey & Elkan, 1995), we ini-
914   tialize our boundary optimization process multiple times from different random seeds (for our ex-
915   periments, 0 ∼ 9) and retain the best-performing model based on the validation set performance
916   without any reliance on the test set. This approach has proven effective in enhancing performance
917   by mitigating the risk of poor local optima. This variant of our model is refered as B OUN D R .E M ULT
      throughout the manuscript.

920   Our problem setting roots on the idea to rescue any non-drugs from the compound libraries by not
921   treating any as ‘negative drugs’. This motivation naturally led us to apply an one-class classification
922   based approach.
923
      On the other hand, PU learning typically assumes that the distribution of unlabeled data, Punlabeled ,
924
      can be expressed as a mixture model: Punlabeled ∼ Ppositive + Pnegative . This leads to training objectives
925
      rooted in empirical risk minimization that assume a tractable and bounded space of both positive
926   and negative examples with the dataset as a representative subset of such space. In this context,
927   PU methods often aim to minimize classification error with cross entropy-based loss functions by
928   estimating the contribution of a negative distribution, frequently relying on class prior (ratio of
929   positive/negative in the dataset) estimates.
930
      Conventional methods in drug-likeness prediction mainly employ binary classification and some-
931
      times Positive-Unlabeled (PU) learning frameworks, seeking to classify compounds by minimizing
932   the risk of misclassification between positive (drug-like) and negative (non-drug-like) examples with
933   cross entropy-based objectives. However, these approaches rely on defined negative sets or a rep-
934   resentative dataset from Pnegative distribution, which may not be feasible in the vast and partially
935   known chemical space.
936

937
      Pnegative . The chemical space is vast, partially explored, and inherently complex, with any sampled
938   “negative” set non-representative of the true distribution of non-drug compounds. Therefore, instead
939   of attempting to estimate a boundary between positive and potential negatives, we propose a one-
940   class classification framework that constructs a drug-likeness boundary to capture the compact space

1025   challenging. In order to retain the essence of the hierarchical ATC relationships without complex ad-
       justments to the architecture that may significantly increase computational overhead and complicate


                                                          19
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1026
       the model training process, we utilized the concept of semantic similarities between terms within
1027   the hierarchy and integrated them as prior knowledge to our softened CLIP loss.
1028
1029   Information Content (IC) We adopt the semantic similarity measure introduced by Jiang & Con-
1030   rath (1997). To quantify the semantic similarity of drugs within the ATC hierarchy, we first need
1031   to calculate the Information Content (IC) of each entity. IC measures how informative an entity
1032   is, based on its frequency or position within a hierarchical structure. For a term c, IC is inversely
1033   proportional to the number of child terms Nchild (c), meaning that terms with fewer descendants
1034   have higher IC, as they provide more specific information. The IC for a term in a tree-structured
1035   hierarchy is computed as:
1036
                                                       log(Nchild (c) + 1)
                                        IC(c) = 1 −
1037                                                    log(Nchild (root))
1038   This formulation ensures that IC values are normalized within the range [0, 1], where the root entity
1039   has an IC of 0.
1040
1041
       Semantic Similarity Given two entities c1 and c2 and their Most Informative Common Ancestor
       (MICA), the semantic distance between them is calculated as:
1042
1043
                            dist(c1 , c2 ) = IC(c1 ) + IC(c2 ) − 2 × IC MICA(c1 , c2 )
1044   Since the maximum possible distance is 2 (when IC is 1 for both entities), we normalize the distance
1045   into a similarity score in the range [0, 1) using the following equation:

1046                                                             dist(c1 , c2 )
                                           sim(c1 , c2 ) = 1 −
1047                                                                  2
1048   We compute pairwise similarities for all drugs based on their ATC codes, generating a similarity
1049   matrix S ∈ Rn×n , where n is the number of approved drugs.

1051   C.3     DATA SPLITTING SCHEMES
1052
1053   Two data splitting schemes are employed to rigorously evaluate model generalizability to unseen
1054
       compounds: a scaffold-based split, which ensures structurally novel compounds appear in the test
       set, and a time-based split, where drugs approved after a certain time point are assigned to the test
1055
       set. Since the structural complexity of approved drugs tends to increase over time, with molecular
1056
       properties diverging (Stegemann et al., 2023), the time-based split is considered a more challenging
1057   evaluation compared to scaffold-based splits.
1058
1059   To simulate real-world drug discovery conditions, where the chemical space is much larger than the
1060
       number of approved drugs, we follow a multi-step procedure: first, split the approved drugs into
       train-valid-test sets in an 8:1:1 ratio, then sample 10 times the number of test drugs from the 100k
1061
       ZINC compounds to account for the larger compound space.
1062
1063
       C.3.1    S CAFFOLD - BASED SPLIT
1064
1065   In drug discovery, scaffold diversity is a key concern, as new drugs often emerge from novel scaf-
1066   folds that were previously untested. The scaffold-split evaluation aligns closely with these real-world
1067   scenarios, making it a more rigorous and realistic test of generalization than a random split, where
1068   similar scaffolds are likely to appear in both training and test sets.
1069   Drugs are first grouped based on their scaffolds, defined using Bemis-Murcko scaffolds (Bemis &
1070   Murcko, 1996), which capture core molecular ring systems and linkers, ensuring that structurally
1071   similar drugs are grouped together. Then, the scaffold sets are split into 10 parts for 10-fold cross-
1072   validation (CV), with an 8:1:1 ratio for train, validation, and test sets. Each fold ensures that test
1073   sets contain unseen scaffolds. The 100k ZINC compounds are also grouped by Bemis-Murcko
1074   scaffolds, then split similarly to match the number of drug scaffolds in each fold. For the test set,
1075   ZINC scaffolds are sampled to include 10 times the number of drugs.
1076   Our pilot study demonstrates how prediction performance significantly decreases when using
1077   scaffold-split compared to randomly splitted setting (Table 10), indicating that the model’s ability to
1078   handle unseen scaffolds is inherently more challenging. This underscores the necessity of scaffold-
1079   split as a more appropriate evaluation scheme for understanding the impact of scaffold sparsity and
       further evaluate the models’ generalizability.

       E.5.2     E FFECT OF MULTI - MODAL ALIGNMENT WITH SOFTENED CLIP LOSS
1440
1441   Our multi-modal alignment loss encompases four modules; softened-CLIP loss, S and K-mix, and
1442   KS-mix. While softened-CLIP loss is designed to integrate prior knowledge as ATC semantic sim-
1443   ilarity, geodesic mixup-inspired loss terms—S-mix, K-mix, and KS-mix—facilitate the learning of
1444   the intermediate space between conflicting representations. Specifically:
1445
                • S-mix & K-mix: These loss terms focus on intra-space interpolation within the struc-
1446
                  tural (S-mix) and knowledge-based (K-mix) embeddings, respectively. By encouraging the
1447
                  model to interpolate between known data points, it learns a smoother and more continuous
1448              embedding space, reducing sensitivity to local conflicts.
1449
                • KS-mix: This component specifically targets inter-space interpolation, blending structural
1450
                  and biomedical representations. It creates synthetic data points that reflect a balanced com-
1451
                  promise between structural and biomedical features, enabling the model to harmonize in-
1452              consistencies and achieve a unified representation.
1453
1454   We evaluated the performance of the model by selectively removing each component the final setup
1455   (Table 17). The results indicate that each component contributes uniquely to the model’s perfor-
1456   mance. Replacing the softened CLIP loss with the original CLIP loss brought 10 percent point
1457   loss in F1 score, highlighting the importance of knowledge integration in our model’s accurate per-
       formances. Removing both S-mix and K-mix resulted in a drop of 36 percent points in F1 score,

1458
       indicating their contribution to aligning embeddings across diverse drug classes and scaffolds in
1459   each of structural and knowledge spaces. Additionally, without KS-mix, the model showed a reduc-
1460   tion of 22 percent point in F1 score, underscoring the importance of a balanced contribution from
1461   both structural and semantic features.
1462
       Overall, our results show that the combination of all three strategies yields the best performance,
1463

1468   E.6.1     D ETAILS ON UTILIZED PROPERTY- BASED FILTERING CRITERIA
1469
1470
1471
1472   PAINS filter The PAINS (Pan-Assay Interference Compounds) filter is designed to identify and
1473   eliminate molecules that are likely to produce false-positive results in high-throughput screening
1474
       assays. These compounds often interfere with biological assays through non-specific mechanisms
       such as covalent binding, redox activity, or fluorescence interference. The PAINS filter operates by
1475
       detecting specific substructures known to cause assay interference. In our pipeline, each compound
1476
       is scanned against a comprehensive library of PAINS substructure patterns. Compounds that do not
1477   contain any of these substructures are considered clean and retained for further analysis. This filter
1478   ensures that the remaining molecules have a reduced likelihood of assay-related artifacts, enhancing
1479   the reliability of downstream predictions.
1480
1481
1482
       Lipinksi’s Rule of 5 Lipinski’s Rule of Five (Ro5) is a widely accepted guideline to assess the
1483
       drug-likeness of a molecule based on its physicochemical properties. The rule includes four criteria:
1484
1485           1. Molecular Weight must be less than or equal to 500 Daltons.
1486
               2. LogP (Partition Coefficient) must be less than or equal to 5, ensuring favorable lipophilicity.
1487
1488
               3. No more than 5 hydrogen bond donors (sum of OH and NH groups).
1489           4. No more than 10 hydrogen bond acceptors (sum of O and N atoms).
1490
       Compounds that adhere to all four criteria are considered to have favorable pharmacokinetic proper-
1491
       ties, such as good oral bioavailability and permeation, and are retained for further consideration. By
1492
       applying this rule, we effectively filter out molecules that are less likely to succeed in later stages of
1493   drug development due to poor absorption or bioavailability.
1494
1495
1496
1497   Predicted IC50 Binding affinity prediction is a critical step for assessing the potential biological
1498
       activity of a compound. We employed XGBoost models to predict IC50 values, which represent the
       concentration of a compound required to inhibit a biological process by 50%. These models were
1499
       trained on bioassay datasets from with IC50 values in ChEMBL database, specifically: BCR-ABL
1500
       (CHEMBL2096618), EFGR (CHEMBL203), and CDK6 (CHEMBL2508) (accessed 16 November
1501   2023).
1502
1503   The input features for these models were Morgan molecular fingerprints, which capture key struc-
1504
       tural and functional aspects of each compound. Compounds predicted to have an IC50 below 10 µM
       are classified as ”active” and retained. This threshold was selected to balance the need for potent bi-
1505
       ological activity with the feasibility of further development, ensuring that only promising candidates
1506
       proceed to subsequent stages of evaluation.
