031                                                           A BSTRACT
032
033              Text-to-video generation has trailed behind text-to-image generation in terms of
034
                 quality and diversity, primarily due to the inherent complexities of spatio-temporal
                 modeling and the limited availability of video-text datasets. Recent text-to-video
035
                 diffusion models employ the image as an intermediate step, significantly en-
036
                 hancing overall performance but incurring high training costs. In this paper,
037              we present I4VG EN, a novel video diffusion inference pipeline to leverage ad-
038              vanced image techniques to enhance pre-trained text-to-video diffusion models,
039              which requires no additional training. Instead of the vanilla text-to-video infer-
040              ence pipeline, I4VG EN consists of two stages: anchor image synthesis and an-
041              chor image-augmented text-to-video synthesis. Correspondingly, a simple yet ef-
042              fective generation-selection strategy is employed to achieve visually-realistic and
043              semantically-faithful anchor image, and an innovative noise-invariant video score
044              distillation sampling (NI-VSDS) is developed to animate the image to a dynamic
045
                 video by distilling motion knowledge from video diffusion models, followed by
                 a video regeneration process to refine the video. Extensive experiments show
046
                 that the proposed method produces videos with higher visual realism and textual
047
                 fidelity. Furthermore, I4VG EN also supports being seamlessly integrated into ex-
048              isting image-to-video diffusion models, thereby improving overall video quality.

080   This paper explores a novel video diffusion inference pipeline that leverages advanced image tech-
081   niques to enhance pre-trained text-to-video diffusion models, focusing on the following two insights:
082
      Image conditioning for text-to-video generation. Recent methods (Blattmann et al., 2023a; Zhang
083
      et al., 2023b; Girdhar et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024a; Li et al., 2023; Hu et al., 2023) have adopted
084   image-guided text-to-video generation, where an initial image generation step significantly enhances
085   video output quality. This paradigm benefits from the strong capabilities of text-to-image models
086   by using the generated images as detailed references for video synthesis. While effective, these
087   approaches incurs additional high training costs. This paper builds on this insight but innovates by
088   designing a novel video diffusion inference pipeline to leverage image information, thereby enhanc-
089   ing text-to-video generation performance without additional training expense.
090   Zero terminal-SNR noise schedule. A prevalent issue in diffusion models is the non-zero termi-
091   nal signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) (Guttenberg; Lin et al., 2024). The mismatch between the training
092   phase, where residual signals persist in noisy videos at the terminal diffusion timestep T , and the
093   inference phase, which uses pure Gaussian noise at the timestep T , creates a gap that degrade the
094   model performance. As illustrated in Fig. 2, noisy videos exhibit temporal correlation that is dis-
095   tinctly different from the independent and identically distributed pure Gaussian noise. This paper is
096   dedicated to reconfiguring the inference pipeline to circumvent this issue.
097   Motivated by these insights, we propose a novel video diffusion inference pipeline, called I4VG EN,
098   which enhances pre-trained text-to-video diffusion models by incorporating image information into
099   the inference process. This method requires no additional learnable parameters and training costs,
100   and can be seamlessly integrated into existing text-to-video diffusion models, circumventing the
101   non-zero terminal SNR issue and improving output quality.
102   Specifically, instead of the vanilla text-to-video inference pipeline, which fails to leverage image
103   reference information, I4VG EN decomposes the inference process into two stages: anchor image
104   synthesis and anchor image-augmented text-to-video synthesis. For the former, a simple yet ef-
105   fective generation-selection strategy is introduced, which involves synthesizing candidate images
106   and selecting the most suitable one using a reward-based mechanism, thereby obtaining a visually-
107   realistic anchor image that is closely aligned with the text prompt. For the latter, we develop an
      innovative noise-invariant video score distillation sampling (NI-VSDS) to animate the anchor image

129
130   Video diffusion models. Aligned with the framework of image diffusion models, Video diffusion
131   models (VDMs) predominantly utilize the paradigm of latent diffusion models (LDMs). Unlike
132   traditional methods that operate directly in the pixel space, VDMs function within the latent space
133   defined by a video autoencoder. Specifically, a video encoder E (·) learns the mapping from an input
134   video v ∈ V, v = {f 1 , f 2 , · · · , f F } to a latent code z = E (v) = {z1 , z2 , · · · , zf }. Subsequently,
135   a video decoder D(·) reconstructs the input video, aiming for D (E (v)) ≈ v. Typically, image
136
      autoencoder is used in a frame-by-frame processing manner instead of the video one, where F = f .
137   Upon training the autoencoder, a Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Model (DDPM) (Ho et al., 2020)
138   is employed within the latent space to generate a denoised version of an input latent zt at each
139   timestep t. During denoising, the diffusion model can be conditioned on additional inputs, such as a
140   text embedding c = fCLIP (y) generated by a pre-trained CLIP text encoder (Radford et al., 2021),
141   corresponding to the input text prompt y. The DDPM model ϵθ (·), a 3D U-Net parametrized by θ,
142
      optimizes the following loss:
143
                             L = Ez∼E(v),c=fCLIP (y),ϵ∼N (0,1),t ∥ϵ − ϵθ (zt , c, t) ∥22 ,

146   During inference, a latent variable zT is sampled from the standard Gaussian distribution N (0, 1)
147
      and subjected to sequential denoising procedures of the DDPM to derive a refined latent z0 . This
      denoised latent z0 is then fed into the decoder to synthesize the corresponding video D (z0 ).

150   2023a) employs the priors of pre-trained text-to-image models to facilitate text-conditioned 3D gen-

152   c = fCLIP (y) corresponding to the text prompt y, SDS optimizes a set of parameters ϕ of a differ-
153
      entiable parametric image generator G(·) (e.g., NeRF (Mildenhall et al., 2020)) using the gradient
      of the SDS loss LSDS :

      3     I4VG EN
187
188
      This section introduces I4VG EN, a novel video diffusion inference pipeline designed for enhancing
189   the capabilities of pre-trained text-to-video diffusion models. As illustrated in Fig. 3, we factorize
190   the inference process into two stages: (1) anchor image synthesis to generate the anchor image x
191   given the text prompt y, and (2) anchor image-augmented video synthesis to generate the video v by
192   leveraging the text prompt y and the anchor image x. This section provides the detailed explanations
193   of both stages in Sec. 3.1 and 3.2, respectively.
194
195   3.1     A NCHOR IMAGE SYNTHESIS
196
197   The goal of this stage is to synthesize visually-realistic anchor images x that accurately correspond
198   to the given text prompts y. This image serves as a foundation to provide appearance information
199
      for enhancing the performance of the subsequent video generation. As illustrated in Fig. 3 (Top), a
      simple yet effective generation-selection pipeline is employed to produce the anchor image, which
200
      consist of candidate images synthesis and reward-based anchor image selection.
201
202   Candidate images synthesis. Instead of generating a single image, our approach produces a set of
203   candidate images to ensure the selection of the best example. Utilizing a pre-trained image diffusion
204   model Dimg (·), we construct the candidate image set as follows:
205                         x1 , x2 , · · · , xN = Dimg (y, z1 ) , Dimg (y, z2 ) , · · · , Dimg (y, zN ) ,                                 (3)
206
      where N denotes the number of candidate images, and zi represents Gaussian noise.
207
208   Reward-based anchor image selection. With the help of the image reward model R(·) (Xu et al.,
209   2023), a promising automatic text-to-image evaluation metric aligned with human preferences, the
210   candidate image with the highest reward score s is selected as the anchor image x, as defined by:
211                                 x = xi ,    where i = arg max si = arg max R (xi ) .                                                   (4)
212                                                                  i                       i

213   The generation-selection design facilitates the acquisition of a high-quality anchor image, partic-
214   ularly beneficial for complex text prompts (see Fig. 5). Notably, our method accommodates both
215   user-provided and retrieved images, extending its applicability to a variety of custom scenarios, as
      discussed in Sec. 4.5.

      3.2      A NCHOR IMAGE - AUGMENTED VIDEO SYNTHESIS
244
245   Upon obtaining the anchor image x, we replicate it F times to create an initial static video v̂ ∈
246   V, v̂ = {x, x, · · · , x}. The goal of this stage is to convert this static video into a high-quality video
247   reflecting the text prompt y. As illustrated in Fig. 3 (Bottom), we introduce static video animation
248   and video regeneration.
249
      Static video animation. A straightforward approach to animate the static video involves applying
250
      a diffusion-denoising process to transition from the static to dynamic state. However, this approach
251   still encounters a training-inference gap, as the text-to-video diffusion model is trained on dynamic
252   real-world videos but tested on static videos, leading to sub-optimal motion quality due to the intro-
253   duction of static priors, as discussed in Sec. 4.4.
254
      To address this limitation, we propose a novel approach leveraging the motion prior from the pre-
255
      trained text-to-video diffusion model to animate static videos. Drawing inspiration from score dis-
256
      tillation sampling (SDS) as introduced in (Poole et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023a), we develop the
257   noise-invariant video score distillation sampling (NI-VSDS). Unlike vanilla SDS, which optimizes
258   a parametric image generator, our approach directly parameterizes the static video v̂ and applies
259   targeted optimization to it. The NI-VSDS loss function is defined as follows:
260
                                                     ∇v̂ LNI-VSDS = w(t) (ϵθ (v̂t , c, t) − ϵ) ,                                                            (5)
261
262
      where v̂t represents the noisy video at timestep t perturbed by Gaussian noise ϵ. Furthermore, we
      incorporate three strategic modifications:
263
264     • Instead of resampling the Gaussian noise at each iteration as in traditional SDS, we maintain a
265       constant noise across the optimization, enhancing convergence speed.
266     • Optimization is confined to the initial stages of the denoising process, where noise levels are
267       higher, focusing on dynamic information distillation.
268     • We implement a coarse-to-fine optimization strategy, evolving from high to low noise levels,
269       specifically from timestep T to τNI-VSDS , where T > τNI-VSDS > 0. This approach stabilizes the
          optimization trajectory and yields superior motion quality.

      5      C ONCLUSION
470
      The paper introduces I4VG EN, a novel video diffusion inference pipeline to leverage advanced im-
471
      age techniques to enhance pre-trained text-to-video diffusion models, which requires no additional
472
      learnable parameters and training costs. I4VG EN decomposes the text-to-video inference process
473   into anchor image synthesis and anchor image-augmented video synthesis. Correspondingly, a sim-
474   ple yet effective generation-selection strategy is applied to produce a high-quality anchor image, and
475   an innovative noise-invariant video score distillation sampling (NI-VSDS) is designed to animate the
476   image, followed by a video regeneration process to enhance the final output. I4VG EN effectively
477   alleviates non-zero terminal signal-to-noise ratio issues and demonstrates improved visual realism
478   and textual fidelity when integrated with existing video diffusion models.
479   Limitation and discussion. I4VG EN improves the video diffusion model but requires more in-
480   ference cost. As discussed in Sec. 4.3, the inference time of I4VG EN is over double the baseline.
481   Enhancing inference efficiency remains a future goal, with distillation techniques as a potential ap-
482   proach. Furthermore, removing the generation-selection strategy can reduce inference costs to some
483   extent. As shown in Table 2, our method still significantly outperforms the baseline under this set-
484   ting. Additionally, although our method and FreeInit are orthogonal, integrating both by replacing
485   video regeneration with FreeInit fails to produce notable benefits.

763   A    R ELATED W ORK
764
765   Video Generative Models. The domain of video generation has seen significant advancements
766   through the use of Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) (Vondrick et al., 2016; Saito et al.,
767   2017; Tulyakov et al., 2018; Wang et al., 2020; Saito et al., 2020; Tian et al., 2021; Fox et al.,
768   2021; Yu et al., 2022; Skorokhodov et al., 2022; Brooks et al., 2022; Shen et al., 2023; Wang et al.,
769   2023f), Variational Autoencoders (VAEs) (Mittal et al., 2017; Li et al., 2018; He et al., 2018), and
770   Autoregressive models (ARs) (Yan et al., 2021; Ge et al., 2022; Wu et al., 2022; Hong et al., 2023;
771
      Villegas et al., 2023; Fu et al., 2023; Yoo et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2023b). Despite these developments,
      synthesizing videos from text prompts remains challenging due to the complexities of modeling
772
      spatio-temporal dynamics. Recent innovations driven by the successes of diffusion models (Ho
773
      et al., 2020; Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021; Song et al., 2021), which have been applied effectively in
774   image generation (Rombach et al., 2022; Nichol et al., 2022; Ramesh et al., 2022; Saharia et al.,
775   2022; Gu et al., 2023; Balaji et al., 2022; Xue et al., 2023; Meng et al., 2022; Guo et al., 2024a) and
776   audio synthesis (Kong et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2021; Popov et al., 2021; Leng et al., 2022; Liu et al.,
777   2022), and underscore the emergence of substantial headway (Ho et al., 2022b;a; He et al., 2022;
778   Singer et al., 2023; Blattmann et al., 2023b; Yu et al., 2023c; Ruan et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2023a;
779   Chen et al., 2023a;b; Esser et al., 2023; Ge et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024a; Geyer et al., 2024; Ma
780   et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023e; Zhang et al., 2023a;b; Hu et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023d; Feng
781   et al., 2023; Guo et al., 2024b; Girdhar et al., 2023; Blattmann et al., 2023a; Gupta et al., 2023;
782
      Wang et al., 2023b;c; Luo et al., 2023) in research endeavors devoted to video synthesis from text
      input.
783
784   The foundational contributions of the Video Diffusion Model (VDM) (Ho et al., 2022b) represents a
785   milestone in leveraging diffusion models for video generation by adapting the 2D U-Net architecture
786   used in image generation to a 3D U-Net capable of temporal modeling. Successive researches, such
787   as Make-A-Video (Singer et al., 2023) and Imagen Video (Ho et al., 2022a), expand video generation
788
      capabilities significantly. To enhance efficiency, subsequent models have transitioned the diffusion
      process from pixel to latent space (He et al., 2022; Zhou et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023b; Blattmann
789
      et al., 2023b;a; Guo et al., 2024b; Wang et al., 2023e), paralleling advancements in latent diffusion
790
      for images (Rombach et al., 2022).
791
792   However, the direct generation of videos from text prompts remains intrinsically challenging. Recent
793   approaches (Blattmann et al., 2023a; Zhang et al., 2023b; Girdhar et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023a;
794
      2024a; Li et al., 2023; Hu et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2023a; Ren et al., 2024) have employed text-to-image
      synthesis as an intermediary step, enhancing overall performance. Despite these advancements,
795
      these methods still face the challenge of high computational training costs. In this study, we explore
796
      a novel training-free methodology aimed at bridging the existing gap in the field.
797
798   In addition, (Chen et al., 2024b) (contemporary researches) introduces additional operations in the
799   attention layer, i.e., cross-frame self-attention control, to enhance the video model. However, this
800
      necessitates modifications to the model architecture, whereas our method does not.
801   Signal-Leak Bias. Diffusion models are designed to generate high-quality visuals from noise
802   through a sequential denoising process, which is consistent in both image and video diffusion mod-
803   els. During training, Gaussian noise corrupts the visual content, challenging the model to restore it
804   to its original form. In the inference phase, the model operates on pure Gaussian noise, transforming
805   it into a realistic visual content step-by-step.
806   Unfortunately, most existing diffusion models exhibit a disparity between the corrupted image dur-
807   ing training and the pure Gaussian noise during inference. Commencing denoising from pure Gaus-
808   sian noise in the inference phase deviates from the training process, potentially introducing signal-
809   leak bias. For image diffusion models, (Guttenberg; Lin et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024) point out flaws
      in common diffusion noise schedules and sample steps, and propose to fine-tune the diffusion model


                                                         15
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
      to mitigate or eliminate the signal-leak bias during training, leading to improved results. (Everaert
811   et al., 2024) attempts to exploit signal-leak bias to achieve more control over the generated images.
812   For video diffusion models, this issue becomes more pronounced. (Wu et al., 2023b; Ma et al.,
813   2023) invert the retrieved video or generated low-quality to construct initial noise to alleviate the
814   problem of signal-leak, improving inference quality. However, they suffer from limited diversity
815   and cumbersome inference. At the same time, first-round inference of FreeInit (Wu et al., 2023b)
816   still exhibits a training-inference gap.
817   In contrast to existing methods, our approach utilizes images as the stepping stone for text-to-
818   video generation. This novel pathway aims to produce visually-realistic and semantically-reasonable
819   videos while maintaining manageable computational overheads, as detailed in Sec. 4.
