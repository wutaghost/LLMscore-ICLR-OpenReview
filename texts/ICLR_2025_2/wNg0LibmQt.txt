009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Augmenting language models with image inputs may enable more effective jail-
012            break attacks through continuous optimization, unlike text inputs that require dis-
013            crete optimization. However, new multimodal fusion models tokenize all input
014            modalities using non-differentiable functions, which hinders straightforward at-
015            tacks. In this work, we introduce the notion of a tokenizer shortcut that approxi-
016            mates tokenization with a continuous function and enables continuous optimiza-
017            tion. We use tokenizer shortcuts to create the first end-to-end gradient image at-
018            tacks against multimodal fusion models. We evaluate our attacks on Chameleon
019
               models and obtain jailbreak images that elicit harmful information for 72.5% of
               prompts. Jailbreak images outperform text jailbreaks optimized with the same ob-
020
               jective and require 3× lower compute budget to optimize 50× more input tokens.
021
               Finally, we find that representation engineering defenses, like Circuit Breakers,
022            trained only on text attacks can effectively transfer to adversarial image inputs.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027
      Adapter-based vision language models were an early attempt to augment large language models
028
      (LLMs) with image inputs (Liu et al., 2024). They use a pretrained image embedding model, like
029
      CLIP (Radford et al., 2021), and train adapters to map image embeddings directly into the embed-
030   ding space of a pretrained LLM. However, separate input spaces can limit multimodal understanding
031   and do not support native generation of images. In contrast, early-fusion multimodal models have
032   been introduced as a more general approach that supports unlimited modalities as both input and
033   output (Chameleon Team, 2024; Gemini Team, 2023; OpenAI, 2024). These models project all
034   modalities into a shared tokenized space and are pretrained from scratch on multimodal inputs. In
035   this work, we will refer to early-fusion multimodal models as multimodal fusion models.
036   Just like LLMs, most vision language models are trained to behave safely and reject harmful re-
037   quests (Bai et al., 2022). Carlini et al. (2024) demonstrated that bypassing safeguards in adapter-
038   based vision language models is easy because input images can be continuously optimized to max-
039   imize harmful outputs. This is in contrast to text input optimization, which requires less efficient
040   discrete optimization methods (Zou et al., 2023). Unlike adapter-based models, multimodal fusion
041   models tokenize all modalities, creating a non-differentiable step between the input and the output
042   token spaces. As a result, optimizing any modality becomes again a discrete optimization problem.
043   Building upon prior work on adversarial examples against quantized image classifiers (Athalye et al.,
044   2018), we introduce the notion of a tokenizer shortcut that approximates image tokenization with
045   a differentiable function. Backpropagating the model loss through the shortcut provides surrogate
046   gradients to enable continuous end-to-end optimization. We propose different tokenizer shortcut
047   designs, and use them to introduce the first end-to-end attack against multimodal fusion models. We
048   evaluate it on Chameleon models (Chameleon Team, 2024) under white-box access.
049   We optimize jailbreak images and evaluate their success in eliciting harmful responses for prompts
050   in JailbreakBench (Chao et al., 2024). We find successful jailbreak images for more than 70% of
051   the prompts. The performance is superior to text-only attacks (GCG: 64%) and requires 3× less
052   compute. Our jailbreak images exhibit better transferability across prompts than text-only attacks
053   (73% vs. 50%). However, similar to concurrent work on adapter-based models (Schaeffer et al.,
      2024), our jailbreak images do not transfer across models.

086   2   P RELIMINARIES
087
088   Multimodal models. Adapter-based vision language models combine pretrained architectures by
089   training adapters to map image embeddings into the embedding space of a language model (Liu
090   et al., 2024). On the other hand, multimodal fusion models—which we focus on—are trained
091   from scratch on multimodal inputs. For this, all modalities are mapped into a shared tokenized
092
      space that can serve as both input and output for autoregressive architectures. In this work, we
      evaluate the family of Chameleon models (Chameleon Team, 2024), the only open-source early-
093
      fusion multimodal models at the time of writing. To convert images into tokens, Chameleon trains
094
      a separate VQ-VAE (Gafni et al., 2024) that encodes the image into 1024 vectors that are then
095   quantized into discrete tokens.
096
097
      Jailbreaking language models. Researchers continuously find jailbreaks to bypass safeguards
098   and extract unsafe information from language models (Wei et al., 2024). On one hand, some attacks
099   use specific prompting strategies (Liu et al., 2023b; Shah et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023a; Wei et al.,
100   2024) that do not require access to model weights. On the other hand, Zou et al. (2023) proposed
101   GCG, a discrete optimization algorithm that searches for prompts that elicit unsafe responses. For
102   adapter-based multimodal models, input images can be used as a more efficient space for continuous
103   optimization (Carlini et al., 2024; Anwar et al., 2024). However, this approach does not directly ap-
104   ply to multimodal fusion models, as these tokenize inputs with non-differentiable functions, thereby
105   reverting adversarial optimization to a discrete optimization problem.
106
107   Threat Model. We assume an attacker with white-box access to a multimodal fusion model, which
      was trained to behave safely and refuse harmful requests. The attacker’s goal is to find a universal

      The goal of our attack is finding adversarial images that, when appended to harmful prompts, can
117
      elicit a harmful response from the model. For this, inspired by the GCG objective (Zou et al., 2023),
118
      we optimize the image using gradient descent to maximize the probability of model generations to
119   start with a non-refusal prefix (e.g. “Sure, I can help you with that”). We can additionally enhance
120   the loss to simultaneously minimize the probability of generic refusal tokens. More formally, we
121   optimize the image to minimize the following loss

129
130   However, image tokenization in multimodal fusion models relies on quantization which is non-
131   differentiable, and it is thus not possible to compute ∂L/∂image naively. Previous work on image
132   classification showed that even rough approximations of quantization can provide gradients to guide
133   adversarial optimization (Athalye et al., 2018). We introduce the notion of a tokenizer shortcut
134   that maps the image embeddings before quantization to a continuous model input space, creating a
135   fully differentiable path between the image and the output space. See Figure 2 for an illustration.
136   These shortcuts map image embeddings to one of the two continuous input spaces to the model
137
      (vocabulary embedding or soft 1-hot encoding), enabling end-to-end continuous optimization of
      images with respect to the model prediction loss.
138
139
140
      Tokenizer shortcut. We use a 2-layer fully connected network as our tokenizer shortcut to ap-
      proximate tokenization with a differentiable function. We propose two shortcuts that take as input
141
      each of the 1024 latent vectors from the VQ-VAE (R1024×256 ). The first shortcut maps the VQ-VAE
142
      embeddings directly to the LLM embedding space (R1024×4096 ); we will refer to this shortcut as the
143   embedding shortcut. The second shortcut maps the VQ-VAE embeddings to the one-hot encoding
144   over the vocabulary tokens, producing a soft one-hot encoding over tokens (R1024×16384 ) that is then
145   used as input to the model; we will refer to this shortcut as the 1-hot shortuct. Figure 2 depicts our
146   proposed shortcuts.
147
      Since the original Chameleon pretraining dataset is proprietary, we train both shortcuts on a subset of
148
      the open-source MIMIC-IT dataset (Li et al., 2023). The embedding shortcut is trained to minimize
149   the cosine similarity between the shortcut predictions and the embedding that would be obtained
150   for the original image tokens. The 1-hot shortcut produces a distribution over the vocabulary and
151   minimizes the cross-entropy loss with the token that would be assigned to each vector through
152   quantization. After training the shortcuts, we use them to compute end-to-end gradients and update
153   the image pixels according to Equation 2.

284   5.2      C OMPARISON WITH T EXT O PTIMIZATION
285
      We compare the performance and efficiency of optimization over text and image tokens. We use
286
      GCG as the best-known method to update text tokens with gradient information. For GCG, we
287
      optimize an adversarial prefix—instead of an image—for each test prompt. Both successful image
288   jailbreaks and GCG suffixes require around 100 optimization steps, but their computational com-
289   plexities differ. For each step, jailbreak images require 1 forward and 1 backward pass on 1024
290   image tokens; totaling ∼ 100, 000 forward and backward token operations per successful jailbreak
291   image. In contrast, GCG uses 20 additional tokens with 1 backward pass and 512 forward passes
292   per iteration, resulting in ∼ 20, 000 backward and ∼ 1M forward token operations. Assuming con-
293   stant FLOPs per token operation3 and that backward operations require 3 times more FLOPs than
294   forward operations, we estimate that jailbreak images require an additional 400,000 FLOPs/token,
295   while GCG needs an extra 1,060,000 FLOPs/token to obtain a successful attack4 .
296   Table 3 summarizes the attack success rate for both methods. Jailbreak images outperform text-
297   based attacks, and require 3× less compute. Image optimization also offers a larger attack surface
298   since the attacker can modify the 1024 image tokens at a lower overall cost than GCG requires for
299   only 20 text tokens.

      We now evaluate the transferability of jailbreak images to unseen prompts and models. Instead of
349
      optimizing a separate image for each test prompt, we create a universal jailbreak image optimized
350
      over N held-out train prompts, and evaluate the attack success rate on the unseen test prompts. Since
351   each prompt in JailbreakBench targets a distinct tasks, our results demonstrate jailbreak images’
352   ability to elicit undesired behaviors across different scenarios.
353
354
      We use 2 text-only baselines to contextualize our findings. We again use GCG, but, instead of opti-
      mizing for a single propmt, we optimize text suffixes over the same set of train prompts to increase
355
      transferability. Additionally, we use refusal suppression (Arditi et al., 2024), a representation en-
356
      gineering method that requires a set of train prompts to detect a direction in the model’s activation
357   space that is responsible for refusal5 . This direction is subtracted at inference time, disabling the
358   model’s ability to refuse harmful requests. We summarize the main findings from the results in
359   Table 5.
360
361   Increasing the number of train prompts improves generalization. Jailbreak images optimized
362   on a single image exhibit good generalization to unseen prompts (∼30%) but computing the loss
363   over a larger number of prompts improves generalization for both 1-hot and embedding shortcuts.
364   However, after a certain point, increasing the number of prompts does not result in improved attack
365   success rates. For both shortcuts, we find very similar success for 10 and 20 training prompts.
366
367   Embedding shortcut can find more transferable jailbreak images. Although generalization for
368   images optimized on a single prompt is similar for 1-hot and embedding shortcuts, the embedding
369
      shortcut can obtain much higher attack success rates on unseen prompts when optimized over more
      than 10 prompts (53.8% vs. 72.5%). In fact, the performance obtained with the jailbreak image
370
      optimized with embedding shortcut on 10 prompts can jailbreak as many prompts as optimizing a
371
      single image per test prompt (see Section 5), reducing computational cost significantly.
372
373
      Representation engineering can still outperform jailbreak images. Suppressing the refusal di-
374   rection during the forward pass obtains the best results overall on our test set (81.3%). This suggests
375   that our image jailbreaks suffer from similar limitations as existing text-only attacks because it is
376   restricted to the input space.

409   7   D ISCUSSION AND F UTURE W ORK
410
411   Image jailbreaks are promising but have a long way to go. Our work is the first attempt to
412   jailbreak multimodal architectures using end-to-end gradient attacks. We present promising results
413   that suggest that optimization might be smoother and more efficient than on text tokens. However,
414   we think there is still significant room for improvement upon our methods. While we ablated most
415   of the relevant hyperparameters we identified, our results indicate that attack success can still vary
416   drastically with their choice. Future work may explore the optimization dynamics in more detail to
417   come up with more effective ways of using the gradients. Similarly, future work may explore more
418
      flexible target functions (Thompson & Sklar, 2024); e.g. combining the output and activation space.
419
420   Transferability of jailbreak images remains an open problem. Concurrent work on adapter-
421
      based models (Schaeffer et al., 2024) demonstrated that jailbreak images do not generalize across
      models even when optimizing on an ensemble of models. Our results indicate that this problem also
422
      persists in multimodal fusion models. Future work may focus on looking for ways to regularize the
423
      optimization to improve transferability.
424
425
426   Attack dynamics may change for newer models. Although most companies have already an-
427   nounced proprietary multimodal architectures (Gemini Team, 2023; OpenAI, 2024), Chameleon
428   models are the only open-source models trained with this architecture. We expect more models to
429   come out in the coming months and we encourage researchers to assess the success of this attack on
430   future models and architectures. We find that Chameleon models are overly safe in that they often
431   refuse benign instructions. We believe this might be a result of a specific stance in the utility-safety
      tradeoff (Bai et al., 2022) for Chameleon that may not hold true for other models.

      8   R ELATED W ORK
433
434
      Multimodal Models. Adapter-based vision language models (VLMs) are a popular approach for
435
      integrating image understanding into language models (Liu et al., 2024; Karamcheti et al., 2024).
436   They typically combine pre-trained language models with image embedding models. VLMs train an
437   adapter to transform pre-trained image embeddings (e.g., from CLIP) into a representation compat-
438   ible with language models. However, state-of-the-art proprietary models are shifting towards early-
439   fusion multimodal models (Gemini Team, 2023; OpenAI, 2024). These models autoregressively
440   process tokens representing multiple modalities in a joint tokenized space. Our work focuses on
441   Chameleon models (Chameleon Team, 2024), currently the only open-source family of early-fusion
442   multimodal models. Chameleon models support image inputs, are safety-tuned, and are available in
443   7B and 30B parameter sizes.
444
445   Adversarial Examples. Adversarial examples are inputs designed to fool machine learning mod-
446   els, first explored in the context of image classification (Szegedy, 2013; Madry, 2017). Adversarial
447   images examples are created by adding perturbations to valid inputs. These perturbations are opti-
448
      mized using the gradient of the target loss with respect to the input.
449
450   Jailbreaks. Jailbreaks are adversarial inputs designed to bypass the safeguards implemented in
451   large language models (LLMs) and get them to generate harmful content. Jailbreaks are often
452
      black-box—they do not require access to model weights— and involve specific conversational strate-
      gies (Liu et al., 2023b; Shah et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023a; Wei et al., 2024). On the other hand,
453
      there are white-box jailbreaks that use model weights and gradients to guide the attack. However,
454
      unlike in image classification, text inputs cannot be directly optimized using gradients with respect
455   to the target loss—because the input space is discrete and sparse—and require approximate meth-
456   ods. GCG is the most prominent white-box attack (Zou et al., 2023). GCG optimizes a suffix that
457   can be appended after harmful instructions to prevent refusal. Interestingly, GCG suffixes optimized
458   on open-source models transfer to black-box and proprietary models.
459
460   Jailbreaks against vision language models. Carlini et al. (2024) demonstrated that incorporating
461   image inputs can enable more effective and efficient jailbreaking attacks. Concurrent to our work,
462   Schaeffer et al. (2024) explored whether optimizing jailbreak images on adapter-based vision lan-
463   guage models exhibit the same transferability properties as GCG prompts. Their results indicate that
464   jailbreak images are effective against adapter-based vision language models under white-box access
465   but they do not generalize across models.

      I MPACT S TATEMENT
468
469
      Our research contributes to the safety and responsible development of future AI systems by exposing
470
      limitations in current models. While acknowledging the potential for misuse in adversarial research,
471
      we believe our methods do not introduce any new risks or unlock dangerous capabilities beyond
472   those already accessible through existing attacks or open-source models without safety measures.
473   Finally, we believe that identifying vulnerabilities is essential for addressing them. By conducting
474   controlled research to uncover these issues now, we proactively mitigate risks that could otherwise
475   emerge during real-world deployments.
