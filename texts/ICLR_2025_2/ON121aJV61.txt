009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Federated Learning (FL) is a privacy-preserving distributed machine learning
012            paradigm. Nonetheless, the substantial distribution shifts among clients pose a
013            considerable challenge to the performance of current FL algorithms. To mitigate
014            this challenge, various methods have been proposed to enhance the FL training
015            process. This paper endeavors to tackle the issue of data heterogeneity from
016            another perspective—by improving FL algorithms prior to the actual training stage.
017            Specifically, we introduce the Client2Vec mechanism, which generates a unique
018            client index for each client before the commencement of FL training. Subsequently,
019
               we leverage the generated client index to enhance the subsequent FL training
               process. To demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed Client2Vec method,
020
               we conduct three case studies that assess the impact of the client index on the
021
               FL training process. These case studies encompass enhanced client sampling,
022            model aggregation, and local training. Extensive experiments conducted on diverse
023            datasets and model architectures show the efficacy of Client2Vec across all three
024            case studies. Our code will be publicly available.

027   1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      Federated Learning (FL) is an emerging machine learning paradigm that preserves clients’ privacy
030   by only exchanging model parameters between clients and server, and maintains the local data
031   not exchanged. As the de facto algorithm in FL, FedAvg (McMahan et al., 2016) proposes to use
032   local SGD to improve the communication efficiency of FL. However, the non-i.i.d. nature of local
033   distributions significantly reduces the performance of FL algorithms (Lin et al., 2020b; Karimireddy
034   et al., 2020b; Li et al., 2020). Despite the great success of existing methods in addressing the non-i.i.d.
035   problem in FL (Li et al., 2021; Acar et al., 2020), most existing studies center on the training process
036   of FL by improving the key stages of the FL training, such as client sampling (Fraboni et al., 2021;
037   Luo et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023), model aggregation (Wang et al., 2019; Lin et al., 2020a; Chen
038
      et al., 2023), and local training (Li et al., 2020; 2021).
039   An additional line of research in FL aims to find efficient methods to improve the performance before
040   the training stage. Yet only a limited number of works exist, either utilizing dataset distillation before
041   the FL training (Yang et al., 2023), or generating global shared synthetic pseudo-data (Guo et al.,
042   2023b; Tang et al., 2022). Despite promising, these approaches incur additional computation costs
043   on local devices with a limited number of applicable scenarios and are incompatible with other FL
044
      training stages like client sampling and model aggregation.
045   Taking inspiration from the Word2Vec technique in Natural Language Processing (NLP)
046   tasks (Mikolov et al., 2013) and domain indexing in Domain Generalization tasks (Xu et al., 2022),
047   we introduce a novel mechanism below, namely Client2Vec. Client2Vec generates an index vector
048   for each client, serving as their identity by incorporating information about the client’s local data
049   distribution. These vectors are used to measure label and feature distribution shifts among clients,
050
      seamlessly integrate into the FL training pipeline, and allows efficiently (1) operating independently
      of the FL training process, (2) combining with existing FL methods, while imposing a minimal addi-
051
      tional computational load on local devices, and (3) enhancing FL training performance throughout all
052
      stages.
053
      Our contributions can be summarized as follows:


                                                          1
054
      • We explore a novel mechanism in FL called Client2Vec, which involves creating an index vector
055     for each client before the FL training phase. These vectors incorporate information about the local
056     distribution of clients and subsequently enhance the FL training process.
057   • We present the Distribution Shifts Aware Index Generation Network (DSA-IGN), a network
058     specifically designed to generate the client index prior to FL training. Our visualization results
059     demonstrate the effectiveness of the client index in measuring the similarities in clients’ local
060     distributions.
061   • We conduct three case studies, including client sampling, model aggregation, and local training, to
062     illustrate the potential and effectiveness of the generated client index. Our experiments, conducted
063
        on various datasets and model architectures, consistently demonstrate significant performance
        improvements with the use of Client2Vec.

066   2     R ELATED W ORKS
067
068   Information sharing in FL. Federated Learning (FL) is a distributed training methodology where
069   local data is retained and not exchanged between the central server and clients (Li et al., 2020;
070   Karimireddy et al., 2020b;a; Guo et al., 2023b; Jiang & Lin, 2023). FedAvg (McMahan et al., 2016;
071   Lin et al., 2020b), a foundational algorithm, uses local Stochastic Gradient Descent (local SGD)
072
      to reduce communication. Nevertheless, the performance of FL algorithms is substantially impeded
      by distribution shifts among clients. To address distribution shift in FL, existing works share local
073
      distribution statistics (Shin et al., 2020; Zhou & Konukoglu, 2022), data representations (Hao et al.,
074
      2021; Tan et al., 2022), and prediction logits (Chang et al., 2019; Luo et al., 2021). FedMix (Yoon
075   et al., 2020) and FedBR (Guo et al., 2023b) enhance local training with privacy-protected
076   augmentation data. VHL (Tang et al., 2022) employs randomly initialized generative models to
077   produce virtual data, regularizing local features to closely align with those of same-class virtual
078   data. FedFed (Yang et al., 2023) proposes a dataset distillation method, amalgamating distilled
079   datasets into all clients’ local datasets to mitigate distribution shifts. Compared to existing methods,
080   Client2Vec has the following advantages: (1) decouples index generation from FL training, reducing
081   FL training load; (2) generates one client index per client, improving efficiency; (3) contributes to
082   the entire FL training process, including client sampling, model aggregation, and local training.
083
084   Domain indexing. Domain Generalization (DG) tackles cross-domain generalization by generating
085
      domain-invariant features. While conventional DG methods strive to make a data point’s latent
      representation independent of its domain identity using a one-hot vector (Ganin et al., 2016; Tzeng
086
      et al., 2017; Zhao et al., 2017), recent studies propose using real-value scalars or vectors as domain
087
      indices to improve performance (Wang et al., 2020; Xu et al., 2021). However, obtaining domain
088   indices may be impractical. To address this, Xu et al. (2022) introduced variational domain indexing
089   (VDI) to infer domain indices without prior knowledge. Yet, challenges arise when applying VDI
090   to FL due to communication costs, privacy concerns, and neglect of label shifts. Further discussions
091   on related works can be found in Appendix B.

      For FL scenarios where feature and label shifts occur simultaneously, the sample index ui,j consists
136
137   of two parts: sample feature index ufi,j ∈ Rdi and sample label index uli,j ∈ Rdi , encoding the
138   feature and label information of the data sample (xi,j , yi,j ), respectively. Similarly, the client index
139   βi is represented as βi = [βif ; βil ] ∈ R2di , where βif ∈ Rdi is the client feature index, and βil ∈ Rdi
140   is the client label index.
141   However, obtaining client and sample indices may not always be trivial in practice. To address this,
142   we extend the domain index idea in Xu et al. (2022) and define the expected properties of sample
143   index ui,j and client index βi below.
144
145   Definition 3.1 (Sample Index). Given the data sample (xi,j , yi,j ) and its corresponding encoding
146   zi,j , which encode information that can be used to predict yi,j , the sample index ui,j of data sample
147   (xi,j , yi,j ) is expected to satisfy the following properties:
148
       • Independence between ufi,j and zi,j . Sample feature index ufi,j is independent of client-invariant
149
150      data encoding zi,j . This aims to encourage the sample feature index ufi,j to encode the
151      client-dependent information—specifically, the distinct information about the client’s local
152
         distribution, and unrelated to label prediction.
       • Maximizing information in uli,j and zi,j for label prediction. The data encoding zi,j and sample
153
154
         label index uli,j should contain as much information as possible to predict label y.
155    • Information Preservation of ufi,j and zi,j . Data encoding zi,j and sample feature index ufi,j
156      preserves as much information as possible to recover data xi,j .
157
158   Remark 3.2. Different from the definitions of domain index outlined in Xu et al. (2022) (refer to
159   Definition C.1), Definition 3.1 encompasses both label and feature distribution shifts by introducing
160   the sample label index uli,j . Additionally, generating domain-level index in Definition C.1 requires
161
      to gather all sample indices ui,j from various data sources. We simplify this procedure and calculate
                                  PNi
      the client index as βi = N1i j=1    ui,j .

163
164   In this section, we describe the generation of βi and ui,j based on Definition 3.1, as depicted in
165   Figure 1. We use image datasets for clarity, and details for language datasets can be found in

168
      Encoding data using CLIP. Since the client index, denoted as βi , is computed as an average across
169
      sample indices ui,j , the primary challenge in generating the client index lies in generating these
170
      sample indices ui,j . According to Definition 3.1, we can observe that the sample label index uli,j
171   solely encodes label information, while the sample feature index encodes image feature information
172   independently of label information. Consequently, to generate the sample index, we must devise
173   a method that maps label information and image information into the same space, facilitating the
174   extraction of label-dependent sample label index uli,j and label-independent sample feature index ufi,j .
175
176
      We propose to leverage CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) to ease the index generation process. CLIP
      is a pre-trained cross-modality model that contains an image encoder and a text encoder, aligning
177
      image and text embedding. As shown in Figure 1, for a given input image-label pairs (xi,j , yi,j ),
178
      we utilize a CLIP image encoder to produce image embedding Di,j and a text encoder to generate
179   label embedding Li,j :
180
181   • Label Embedding Li,j : The Li,j only encodes label descriptions such as “A photo of a/an
182
        {object}”. Therefore, we use Li,j as the embedding that only contains label information, which
        is naturally invariant among clients.
183
      • Image embedding Di,j : The Di,j is extracted from the whole image, serving as a compact feature
184
        containing both client-independent (label information) and client-specific (background, style,
185     etc.) features.
186
187   Consequently, the original local dataset Di = {(xi,j , yi,j )} is transformed into the CLIP embedding
188
      set Ei = {(Di,j , Li,j )}. The Ei is then utilized to generate the index. Note that other cross-modality
      models, such as BLIP (Li et al., 2022) and BLIP2 (Li et al., 2023a), can also align vision and language
189
      like CLIP. Exploring the effectiveness of these models could be a valuable future research direction.
190
191
192   Generating sample indices using CLIP embedding and DSA-IGN. Given that Li,j only encodes
193   label information, we directly set uli,j = Li,j . On the contrary, by Definition 3.1, the sample
194   feature index ufi,j needs to encode label-invariant client-specific information. Thus, we decompose
195   Di,j —which contains both label and client-specific information—to generate ufi,j while isolating
196   the client-specific information from Di,j .
197

199

202

203     ensuring their independence. The decomposition block can be any non-linear neural network
204     architecture, and we use a three-layer transformer encoder as the decomposition block for the
205     sake of simplicity1 .

207     have a large cosine similarity with the label embedding Li,j , ensuring that zi,j encodes as much

285   4    I MPROVING FL VIA C LIENT 2V EC
286
287   In this section, we showcase improving the FL training process by leveraging the trained client index
288   βi . Specifically, we explore three case studies aimed at refining crucial aspects of the FL training
289   pipeline: client sampling, model aggregation, and local training.
290   The intuition of using client index comes from the following reasons:
291
292
       • The client index can measure the distance between clients, which helps for improving the
         client sampling (case study 1) and model aggregation (case study 2) stages. As depicted in
293
         Figure 3, clients with similar distributions tend to exhibit smaller distances between their respective
294
         Client Indexes. The rationale for utilizing this distance information is derived from the analysis
295      presented in CyCP (Cho et al., 2023) and empirical findings in class incremental learning (He et al.,
296      2022). These sources indicate that reduced distances among client groups sampled in consecutive
297      rounds contribute to improved performance.
298    • The client feature index is orthogonal to the ideal client-invariant features, which are defined
299      as distribution bias in the domain generalization area. This orthogonal property contributes
300      to improved local training (case study 3). According to Definition 3.1, the Client Index is
301      independent of client-invariant features, which are highly desirable in practical applications.
302      Therefore, the objective of enhancing local training is to ensure that the model features maintain
303
         their independence from the Client Indexes.
304   We will introduce the details of the three case studies in the following parts of this section.
305

309   groups sampled in adjacent rounds improves performance—we propose a greedy sampling approach.

      B     R ELATED W ORKS
811
812
      Distribution shifts in FL. Federated Learning (FL) is introduced as a methodology for training
813
      machine learning models in a distributed manner, wherein local data is retained and not exchanged
814   between the central server and individual clients. FedAvg (McMahan et al., 2016; Lin et al., 2020b),
815   serving as a foundational algorithm in this domain, advocates the use of local Stochastic Gradient
816   Descent (local SGD) to alleviate the communication burden. Nevertheless, the performance of FL
817   algorithms is substantially impeded by distribution shifts among clients. Addressing these local
818   distribution shifts has emerged as a primary focus in FL research (Li et al., 2020; Karimireddy et al.,
819   2020b;a; Guo et al., 2023b; Jiang & Lin, 2023). Many existing works address label distribution
820   shifts by incorporating additional regularization terms (Li et al., 2020; Karimireddy et al., 2020b;
821   Guo et al., 2021; Lee et al., 2022; Mendieta et al., 2022), enhancing feature learning (Tang et al.,
822
      2022; Shi et al., 2023; Li et al., 2021; Zhou et al., 2023), and improving classifiers (Luo et al., 2021;
      Li et al., 2023c). Regarding feature distribution shifts, the majority of FL methods concentrate on
823
      the out-of-domain generalization problem. This objective aims to train robust models capable of
824
      generalizing to previously unseen feature distributions (Nguyen et al., 2022; Li et al., 2023b; Guo
825   et al., 2023a). Approaches include investigating special cases (Reisizadeh et al., 2020), integrating
826   domain generalization algorithms in FL scenarios, such as domain-robust optimization (Mohri et al.,
827   2019; Deng et al., 2021), and training domain-invariant features (Peng et al., 2019b; Wang et al., 2022;
828   Shen et al., 2021; Sun et al., 2022; Gan et al., 2021). Notably, recent research has also considered
829   concept shifts by leveraging clustering methods (Jothimurugesan et al., 2022; ?; Guo et al., 2023c). In
830   this study, we address the challenge of distribution shifts in FL from another perspective—enhancing
831   the performance of FL algorithms prior to the training stage. Our approach holds the potential
832   for seamless integration with the aforementioned algorithms, and consider both feature and label
833
      distribution shifts.
834
835   Information sharing in FL. Various methods have been developed to address the challenge of
836   distribution shifts among clients (Zhao et al., 2018; Jeong et al., 2018; Long et al., 2021). One
837
      approach involves the sharing of information among clients, such as the exchange of local distribution
      statistics (Shin et al., 2020; Zhou & Konukoglu, 2022), data representations (Hao et al., 2021; Tan
838
      et al., 2022), and prediction logits (Chang et al., 2019; Luo et al., 2021). Additionally, techniques
839
      leveraging global proxy datasets have been introduced to enhance FL training (Lin et al., 2020a; Duan
840   et al., 2019). Notably, FedMix (Yoon et al., 2020) and FedBR (Guo et al., 2023b) generate privacy-
841   protected augmentation data by averaging local batches, subsequently improving the local training
842   process. VHL (Tang et al., 2022) employs randomly initialized generative models to produce virtual
843   data, compelling local features to closely align with those of same-class virtual data. FedFed (Yang
844   et al., 2023) proposes a dataset distillation method, amalgamating distilled datasets into all clients’
845   local datasets to mitigate distribution shifts. In comparison to existing approaches, Client2Vec
846   presents several advantages: (1) the index generation process is decoupled from the FL training
847   process, thereby avoiding any additional burden on FL training; (2) Client2Vec generates only one
848
      index vector per client, enhancing efficiency; (3) Client2Vec contributes to the whole FL training
      stage, encompassing client sampling, model aggregation, and local training processes.

      C.1   D OMAIN I NDEXING
857
858   The Domain Generalization (DG) tasks are designed to address the cross-domain generalization prob-
859   lem by generating domain-invariant features. Typically, DG methods aim to establish independence
860   between a data point’s latent representation and its domain identity, represented by a one-hot vector
861   indicating the source domain (Ganin et al., 2016; Tzeng et al., 2017; Zhao et al., 2017). However,
862   recent studies have demonstrated that utilizing a domain index, which is a real-value scalar (or vector)
863   embedding domain semantics, as a substitute for domain identity, significantly enhances domain
      generalization performance (Wang et al., 2020; Xu et al., 2021).


                                                        16
864
      For example, in the work by Wang et al. (2020), sleeping stage prediction models were adapted
865   across patients with varying ages, using "age" as the domain index. This approach yielded superior
866   performance compared to traditional models that categorized patients into groups based on age,
867   employing discrete group IDs as domain identities.
868
      Nevertheless, obtaining domain indices may not always be feasible in practical scenarios. To
869
      overcome this challenge, Xu et al. (2022) formally defined the domain index and introduced variational
870
      domain indexing (VDI) to infer domain indices without prior knowledge. The definition of the domain
871   index in (Xu et al., 2022) is illustrated as follows.
872
873
      Definition of domain index. Consider the unsupervised domain adaptation setting involving a
874
      total of N domains, each characterized by a domain identity k ∈ K = [N ] ≜ {1, . . . , N }. Here,
875   k belongs to either the source domain identity set Ks or the target domain identity set Kt . Every
                                                                                                          n
876   domain k comprises Dk data points. The task involves n labeled data points {(xis , yis , kis )}i=1
877                                        s                                            t t m
      originating from source domains (ki ∈ Ks ) and m unlabeled data points {xi , ki }i=1 from target
878                                                                                 m
      domains (kit ∈ Kt ). The objectives are twofold: (1) predict the labels {yit }i=1 for the target domain
879                                                         Bβ
      data, and (2) deduce global domain indices βk ∈ R for each domain and local domain indices
880   ui ∈ RBu for each data point. It is important to note that each domain possesses a single global
881   domain index but multiple local domain indices, with one corresponding to each data point in the
882   domain. The data encoding generated from an encoder that takes x as input is represented as z ∈ RBz .
883   The mutual information is denoted by I(·; ·).
884   Definition C.1 (Domain Index). Given data x and label y, a domain-level variable β and a data-level
885   variable u are called global and local domain indices, respectively, if there exists a data encoding z
886   such that the following holds:
887
888          • Independence between β and z: Global domain index β is independent of data encoding
889            z, i.e., β ⊥
                          ⊥ z, or equivalently I(β; z) = 0. This is to encourage domain-invariant data
890            encoding z.
891
             • Information Preservation of z: Data encoding z, local domain index u, and global domain
892            index β preserves as much information on x as possible, i.e., maximizing I(x; u, β, z). This
893            is to prevent β and u from collapsing to trivial solutions.
894
895          • Label Sensitivity of z: The data encoding z should contain as much information on the label
896            y as possible to maximize prediction power, i.e., maximizing I(y; z) conditioned on z ⊥
                                                                                                     ⊥ β.
897
               This is to make sure the previous two constraints on β, u, and z do not harm prediction
               performance.
898
899
      In this paper, we extend the Definition C.1 to Definition 3.1 by incorporating both client feature index
900   and client label index.

      C.2   CLIP
903
904   CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) is a cross-modal model that establishes a connection between vision and
905   natural language by projecting image and text embeddings onto a shared space. When presented with
906   an image I and a corresponding descriptive sentence denoted as T, the CLIP image encoder and text
907   encoder encode the image and text into image embedding D and text embedding L, respectively.
908   Subsequently, the embeddings D and L are aligned to achieve a large cosine similarity, thereby
909   harmonizing the vision and language embedding spaces.

1001   Model architectures and training details of DSA-IGN. The projection layer utilizes a three-
1002   layer transformer encoder. Each transformer encoder layer consists of 8 attention heads, with the
1003   model dimension set to 32, and the feed-forward layer dimension set to 2048. The projection
1004   layer is represented as a matrix with dimensions 1024 × 512. Given a batch of CLIP embeddings
1005   D ∈ RN ×512 , the input for the decomposition block is constructed as Ii,j = [D, D] ∈ RN ×1024 .
1006   Subsequently, I is reshaped into Ĩ = (N × 32 × 32), indicating that each sample comprises 32
1007   patches, and each patch has a dimension of 32.
1008
       The reshaped Ĩ is fed into the decomposition block, producing an output Õ ∈ (N × 32 × 32), which
1009
       is then reshaped to O = (N × 1024) = [Z, U]. Here, Z ∈ RN ×512 represents the data encoding z
1010
       as defined in Definition 3.1, and U ∈ RN ×512 corresponds to the sample feature index u. The input
1011   to the projection layer is identical to the output of the decomposition block, represented as O.
