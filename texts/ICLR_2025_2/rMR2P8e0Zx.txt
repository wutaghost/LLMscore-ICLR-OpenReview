030                                               A BSTRACT
031
032            A song typically comprises the vocal track and the music track. Creating lyrics
033            and melodies for the vocal track in a symbolic format, known as song composition,
034
               plays a significant role in the song generation. This delicate and complex task
               demands expert musical knowledge of melody, an advanced understanding of
035
               lyrics, and precise alignment between them. Despite achievements in sub-tasks
036
               such as lyric generation, lyric-to-melody, and melody-to-lyric, etc, a unified model
037            for song composition has not yet been achieved. In this paper, we introduce
038            SongComposer, a pioneering step towards a unified song composition model
039            that can readily create symbolic lyrics and melodies following instructions.
040            SongComposer is a music-specialized large language model (LLM) that, for the first
041            time, integrates the capability of simultaneously composing lyrics and melodies
042            into LLMs. To achieve this goal, three non-trivial efforts are introduced. 1)
043            Sheet music understanding, we designed a flexible tuple format to load lyric
044            and note attributes, fostering word-level alignment between lyrics and melodies,
045
               and enabling SongComposer to generate lyrics with accompanying well-aligned
               melodies. 2) Song note tokenizing, the vocabulary of the tokenizer is extended
046
               for song notes, and we find a proper scalar-manner initialization of new tokens
047
               based on musical prior is essential for the model to understand musical rhythm. 3)
048            Structural music generation, we propose a multi-stage pipeline for progressively
049            capturing the musical structure. Initially, we extract and feed motif-level melody
050            patterns to SongComposer to build its basic generation capabilities. Later, we insert
051            special tokens into the whole-song data to denote phrase-level structure, promoting
052            logical repetition and smooth coherence. Extensive experiments demonstrate that
053            SongComposer outperforms advanced LLMs, including GPT-4, in tasks such as
               lyric-to-melody generation, melody-to-lyric generation, song continuation, and

061   1       I NTRODUCTION
062
063   Symbolic song composition aims to generate the vocal track of a song as a sequence of symbols
064   representing lyrics and melodies. It is a vital task in song generation and requires professional
065   knowledge. Recently, this field has become a highly active area of research in both academic and
066   industrial domains. Previous efforts have made significant progress in isolated sub-tasks of song
067   composition such as lyric generation (Zhang et al., 2022c), lyric-to-melody (Yu et al., 2021a; Ju et al.,
068   2022; Sheng et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2022a) or melody-to-lyric generation (Sheng et al., 2021; Ma
069
      et al., 2021). However, the absence of a unified framework for generating both lyrics and melodies
      concurrently while adhering to specific instructions poses a challenge for seamless adaptation, thereby
070
      creating a higher hurdle for everyday amateurs.
071
072   The recent surge in large language models (LLMs) has dramatically revolutionized the artificial
073   intelligence landscape, especially in natural language understanding and generation (Brown et al.,
074   2020; Chiang et al., 2023; Wei et al., 2021; Chowdhery et al., 2023; Raffel et al., 2020; Devlin et al.,
075
      2018). These models have established new benchmarks for parsing and producing human language,
      showcasing human-level proficiency in complex language environments. Given that symbolic song
076
      representation shares structural similarities with human language, it seems plausible that LLMs could
077
      facilitate the creation of symbolic songs. Furthermore, unlike previous non-LLM methods (Sheng
078   et al., 2021; Ju et al., 2022) that handle only specific tasks, LLMs can integrate various sub-tasks of
079   song composition into a single model due to their instruction-following capabilities.
080
081
      However, enabling LLMs to compose full-length songs that harmonize melody and lyrics is not a
      trivial task. First, as illustrated in Figure 2(a), symbolic song representation would decompose a
082
      song into its lyrics and note attributes (pitch, beat) and form a strict word-level alignment. Therefore,
083
      aligning lyric and melody attributes in a unified and efficient manner for LLMs is indispensable
084   yet remains unexplored. Secondly, a song typically features a well-organized and hierarchical
085   structure (Dai et al., 2022). For example, a composer usually uses the concept of motif and phrase to
086   enrich the unity of a song. A motif is a recurring musical idea that serves as a fundamental building
087   unit, and a phrase is a broader segment of music that forms a complete thought or expression. As
088   shown in Figure 2(b), a single song may have a clear high-level phrase structure like Verse-Chorus,
089   and across the whole song, there may be repetitive patterns known as motifs. Thus, enhancing LLMs
090   to understand these succinct musical structures is of vital importance and may require explicitly
091   curated knowledge input and design. Last but not least, current symbolic song datasets (Yu et al.,
092
      2021b; Wang et al., 2022; Huang et al., 2021) are either limited in quantity or lacking in quality.
      They often miss precise alignments between melody and lyrics, impeding progress in symbolic song
093
      generation.
094
095   To address the aforementioned challenges, we introduce SongComposer, an LLM capable of generat-
096   ing whole-song compositions that harmoniously integrate both melodies and lyrics. To the best of
097   our knowledge, this is the first attempt to generate lyrics and melody simultaneously using LLMs.
098   Specifically, we propose a word-level tuple format to construct melody and lyric attributes in a flexible
099   and unified manner, providing an efficient interface for aligning melody and lyrics. Besides, we
100   introduce a scalar initialization method to seamlessly initialize pitch tokens based on the existing
101   vocabulary of LLMs. This method initializes a central pitch first and then sets the remaining note
102   pitches as multiples of the central pitch embeddings. In this way, we explicitly introduce and reinforce
103   the relationship between pitches to LLMs.
104   To learn the hierarchical structure of a song, we use a progressive training approach with Song-
105   Composer, enabling the model to recognize patterns of motifs and phrases. Initially, we extract
106   highly repetitive melody snippets and treat these as general motifs for motif-level melody training.

      Regarding the dataset, we have carefully compiled and curated a comprehensive high-quality dataset,

137   terms of quality and adherence to the prompt. Moreover, we excel in the traditional model (Sheng et al.,
138   2021; Ju et al., 2022) on specific lyric-to-melody tasks. In addition, we conduct a thorough ablation
139   study to verify the effectiveness of the proposed components. We also include a memorization
140   test (Carlini et al., 2022; Agostinelli et al., 2023) to check for inappropriate copying from the
141   dataset, revealing that SongComposer’s output significantly differs from the original sequences in the
142   pretraining dataset.

155
156   Symbolic Song Composition. Symbolic song composition encompasses several key tasks, including
157   the creation of song lyrics, the composition of melodies, and the mutual generation between lyrics
158   and melodies. Lyric generation focuses on producing meaningful and coherent song lyrics using deep
159   learning techniques(Malmi et al., 2016; Zhang et al., 2022c; Xue et al., 2021). The goal of melody
160   generation (Wu et al., 2019; Colombo et al., 2017) is to autonomously create musical melodies
161   that can stand alone. Taking a step further, lyric-to-melody generation (Yu et al., 2021a; Ju et al.,
      2022; Sheng et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2022a) involves generating melodies that align with given


                                                          3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      lyrics. The reverse task, melody-to-lyrics generation (Bao et al., 2019; Li et al., 2020; Sheng et al.,
163   2021; Ma et al., 2021) focuses on producing lyrics that match a given melody. While these methods
164   are effective within their specific song composition tasks, they often cannot handle comprehensive
165   composition tasks with a single model. However, SongComposer can process both melody and lyrics
166   simultaneously in a unified format with the power of LLMs.
167
      Symbolic Music Generation. Recent years have seen significant progress in symbolic music
168
      generation. The majority of studies have focused on converting music information into symbolic-style
169   tokens and then processing these sequences with Transformers (Qu et al., 2024; Huang et al., 2019;
170   Huang & Yang, 2020; Lu et al., 2023; Yuan et al., 2024; Liang et al., 2024; Deng et al., 2024).
171   Music Transformer (Huang et al., 2019) is a pioneering Transformer model that generates music with
172   long-term structure by leveraging a novel memory-efficient relative attention mechanism. Building
173   on Music Transformer, REMI (Huang & Yang, 2020), a novel MIDI-derived event representation,
174   enhances models with beat-based awareness and improves rhythmic structure in the generation of
175   expressive Pop piano compositions.
176   Large Language Models. Recent advancements in large language models (Raffel et al., 2020;
177   Radford et al., 2018; Chowdhery et al., 2023; Touvron et al., 2023; OpenAI, 2023; Ouyang et al.,
178   2022; OpenAI, 2022; Ouyang et al., 2022; Chiang et al., 2023; Qian et al., 2024) have significantly
179   enhanced natural language processing, showcasing impressive capabilities across diverse tasks. In
180   the domain of symbolic music creation, recent endeavors (Yuan et al., 2024; Deng et al., 2024)
181   propose employing large language models for generating symbolic pure music. However, crafting
182   compositions encompassing both lyrics and melodies with LLMs remains an open problem. Inspired
183
      by the powerful human-level language capabilities of LLMs, we have developed the first unified
      LLMs framework that expands their application to lyric and melody composition for song generation.
184
185   Paired Lyric-Melody Singing Dataset. Singing data annotated with paired lyrics and melodies is
186   important for song generation. Specifically, JVS-MuSiC (Tamaru et al., 2020), PopCS (Liu et al.,
187   2022), and OpenSinger (Huang et al., 2021) offer a broad range of singing data but lack the crucial
188   lyric-melody temporal alignment. NUS-48E (Duan et al., 2013), NHSS (Sharma et al., 2021), Tohoku
189
      Kiritan (Ogawa & Morise, 2021), and Opencpop (Wang et al., 2022) provide singing corpora across
      English, Japanese, and Chinese with manually aligned lyrics and melodies. However, they are limited
190
      in scale, featuring only monophonic singers and styles. More recently, M4Singer (Zhang et al.,
191
      2022b) compiles approximately 700 Chinese songs with lyric and melody pairs, but this amount is
192   still insufficient for training an LLM for symbolic music generation. In this work, we collect around
193   8K symbolic songs for both English and Chinese from scratch to train SongComposer.

198
199   Pure Melody Format. Inspired by the beat-based REMI representation (Huang & Yang, 2020),
200   we first decompose the notes into three symbolic attributes: note pitch p, note duration d, and rest
201   duration r. The pitch range p is from MIDI note numbers 48 to 83, corresponding to notes C3 to
202   B5, which is the most common range for human vocal performance. Given the tempo of the melody,
203   measured in beats per minute (bpm), we measure the note duration d ∈ Z and rest duration r ∈ Z in
204   the number of 1/16 beat:
205             bpm                                                bpm
       dk = ϕ(       (note-endk − note-startk ) × 16), rk = ϕ(          (note-startk+1 − note-endk ) × 16),
206              60                                                 60
207   where note-start and note-end are times in seconds, k denotes the note index number and ϕ(·) is an
208   operator that constrains the value to the nearest integer within the range [1, 256].
209   Then each note of pure melody is formatted in a tuple as follows:
210
                             ⟨bom⟩ bpm is {bpm}. Total {num} lines.
211
                             The 1-st line: ⟨p1 ⟩, d1 | ⟨rest⟩, r1 | ⟨p2 ⟩, d2 | ⟨rest⟩, r2 · · ·
212
213                          The 2-nd line: · · · ⟨eom⟩
214   where we treat ⟨rest⟩ as a type of note and skip the rest tuple if r < 8. ⟨bom⟩ and ⟨eom⟩ indicate the
215   beginning and end of the melody, respectively. Note that ⟨·⟩ represents special tokens we add outside
      the existing vocabulary.

241   3.2   P ITCH I NITIALIZATION
242
243   Motivated by the strong logical and mathematical relationship between different pitches, we argue
244   that initializing pitch tokens with a strong prior on their relationships would be beneficial for the
245   model to interpret pitch elements. Therefore, we attempt four initialization methods for pitch tokens
246   to verify our intuition.
247   Average Initialization creates the embedding for new pitch tokens ⟨p⟩ by averaging the existing
248   token embeddings of left bracket (⟨), pitch number (p), and right bracket (⟩).
249
      Gaussian Initialization generates embeddings for new pitch tokens using a Gaussian distribution,
250
      with the mean and variance calculated from existing token embeddings.
251
252   Interpolation Initialization initializes the embeddings for the lowest and highest pitch tokens (⟨48⟩
253   and ⟨83⟩) using Gaussian initialization. The embeddings for the pitches in between are linearly
254
      interpolated between these two.
255   Scalar Initialization begins by initializing a central pitch token ⟨66⟩ using Gaussian initialization.
256   The embeddings for the remaining pitches are then set as multiples of this central pitch embedding,
257   where multipliers range from [− ln(e + 17), · · · , − ln(e), ln(e), · · · , ln(e + 17)]. Compared to
258   interpolation initialization, scalar initialization is more like a special form of extrapolation.
259   To further interpret the learned pitch tokens under different initialization methods, we visualize the
260   embeddings in Figure 3. The average initialization distinguishes between pitch and rest tokens but
261   fails to capture the inherent pitch information, resulting in a collapsed cluster. The Gaussian method
262   fails to differentiate between pitch tokens and other tokens effectively and does not learn a discernible
263   pattern. For the remaining two methods, both initialization methods result in distinct patterns. The
264   interpolation method positions pitch tokens far away from other tokens, while the scalar method
265   results in a pattern where the mean cluster still lies among the existing tokens. Therefore, scalar
266
      initialization stays closer to the existing semantic spaces which may lead to a better generation than
      the interpolation method.
267
268   Empirically, we find that the scalar initialization works best for pitch modeling. For more details,
269   please refer to the ablation study as shown in Table 5. Therefore, we use scalar initialization on pitch
      tokens for SongComposer.

      3.3   P ROGRESSIVE S TRUCTURE - AWARE T RAINING
283
284   Structure is crucial in song composition, with a typical song comprising multiple levels of struc-
285   ture (Dai et al., 2022). Therefore, we meticulously devise three stages of training for SongComposer
286   to emphasize structural information at varying levels of time granularity.
287
      Motif-Level Melody Training. Motif, in song composition, denotes a recurring musical idea that is
288
      key to enhancing the structure and coherence of the piece. Typically, a motif comprises a sequence of
289   notes that repetitively appear throughout the song. Motivated by this concept, we intentionally select
290   highly repetitive short note sequences to construct motif-level melody data. Subsequently, we kick
291   off the training process of SongComposer by introducing this finely repetitive structure. In this way,
292   the model is introduced to learn the motif-level composition.
293
      Independent Whole-Song Lyric and Melody Training. After gaining insight into the basic units of
294
      composition through motif-level melody training, we extend the training of SongComposer to the
295   whole-song level. However, directly training the model to establish alignments between melody and
296   lyrics may expose challenges to SongComposer. Therefore, we continue to train the model using
297   pure lyric and pure melody datasets to establish a foundation for basic whole-song understanding.
298
       Paired Lyric and Melody with Phrase-level Token Training. Having a broader temporal dimension
299
       than a motif, the concept of phrases is also pivotal in structuring a song. A phrase is a sentence-level
300
       pattern that expresses a complete musical thought. To incorporate this understanding into composition,
301    SongComposer trains on paired lyric and melody data, integrating the concept of phrases into the
302    paired data. In our paper, we focus on five commonly used phrases such as ‘intro’, ‘verse’, ‘chorus’,
303   ‘bridge’, and ‘outro’, while unifying less common phrases as ‘other’. Each phrase in a song would be
304    outlined by two special tokens to indicate its beginning and end, resulting in a total of 6 × 2 special
305    tokens. To maintain the model’s ability to process melodies and lyrics separately, we train an equal
306    amount of pure melody, pure lyric, and paired data. In contrast to the previous stage, both the pure
307    melody and pure lyric data are now decorated with phrase-level special tokens.

312
313   To train the SongComposer, we curate a large-scale song pretraining and supervised fine-tuning
314   dataset SongCompose. For more details, please refer to our Appendix A.
315
      Pure-lyric Dataset. We collect 283K song lyrics from two online sources, including 150K English
316
      lyrics and 133K Chinese lyrics. After a series of lyric-cleaning processes, we gather high-quality
317   lyrics from various genres and styles.
318
319   Pure-melody Dataset. We collect 20K MIDI files and extract melody attributes, including note pitch,
320
      note duration, and rest duration. We employ the pretty midi Python module (Raffel & Ellis, 2014) to
      parse MIDI files and extract the ”melody” or ”vocal” tracks as the pure melody.
321
322   Paired Lyric-melody Dataset. We create from scratch a dataset of 8K pairs of lyrics and melodies
323   from the Internet, with roughly half being in Chinese and the other half in English. Melodies and
      lyrics are matched at the word level.

      Supervised Finetuning Dataset. We curate instruction-following data for song-generation tasks
325   including creating melodies for given lyrics, writing lyrics for melodies, extending song segments,

      4.2   T RAINING D ETAILS
331
332   We adopt InternLM2-7B (Cai et al., 2024) as our base model and set the maximum token length as
333   5120. Except for the pitch tokens using Scalar initialization, all the other newly added special tokens
334   adopt Gaussian initialization. We train the whole model to predict the next token based on prior text,
335   maximizing the log-likelihood of tokens in the given examples. For optimization, we use AdamW
336   optimizer (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2019) with a learning rate of 10−5 , β1 = 0.9, β2 = 0.95, and a
337   weight decay of 0.1. The entire dataset is iterated through once, with a batch size of 1. Additionally, a
338   linear warm-up of the learning rate is applied during the initial 1% of training steps, increasing from
339
      10−6 to 10−5 . Afterwards, a cosine schedule is applied, reducing the learning rate to a minimum of 0.
      This setting is consistent across both the pretraining and supervised fine-tuning stages. The whole
340
      training of SongComposer is conducted on 16 Nvidia A100 (80G) GPUs for approximately 2 days.

343   4.3   O BJECTIVE E VALUATION M ETRICS
344
      We construct a validation set of 100 songs, evenly split between Chinese and English, none of which
345
      were seen by our model during training.
346
347   Melody Generation. For assessing the similarity between the generated melodies and the ground-
348   truth, we adopt the metrics proposed by SongMASS (Sheng et al., 2021): Pitch Distribution Similarity
349   (PD), Duration Distribution Similarity (DD), and Melody Distance (MD). Besides, we propose a
350
      recall rate to assess the repetition capability and partly indicate the structure within the song. This
      rate is calculated by dividing the total number of melodic lines by the number of unique melodic
351
      lines, with a minimum recall rate of 1 indicating no repetition.
352
353   Lyric Generation. We evaluate the similarity between generated and original lyrics using three
354   metrics from different perspectives. We use a CoSENT (Cosine Sentence) model (Xu, 2023),
355   specifically the base-multilingual version, to compute sentence-level cosine similarity. Additionally,
356
      we apply the ROUGE-2 score (Lin, 2004) to measure bigram overlap and the BERT score (BS) (Zhang
      et al., 2019) to assess similarity based on the contextual embeddings from the BERT-base model.
357
358
359   4.4   S UBJECTIVE E VALUATION M ETRICS
360
361   For the subjective evaluation, we conduct a user study with 30 participants 10 cases per task. We
362
      develop two metrics for each task and ask the participants to rate them. The rating scale is 1 to 5,
      where higher scores denote superior quality. In this way, we collect feedback on the quality of the
363
      generated content from a human perspective.
364
365   The evaluation criteria for different tasks are as follows: For Lyric-to-Melody Generation, we assess
366   Harmony and Melody-Lyric Compatibility. For Melody-to-Lyric Generation, we evaluate Fluency
367   and Melody-Lyric Compatibility. Song Continuation quality is assessed based on Overall Quality
368
      and Coherence to the Song Prompt. Text-to-Song generation is evaluated in terms of Overall Quality
      and Relevance to the Text Input.
369
370   In summary, each task is evaluated using two metrics: one that assesses the overall musical quality
371   of the samples produced, and another that specifically addresses the challenges of each task. More
372   detailed descriptions of the tasks and metrics can be found in Appendix B.

517   5   C ONCLUSION
518
519
      In this paper, we introduce SongComposer, a novel large language model designed to generate
      detailed music scores that synchronize lyrics and melodies. The model leverages a tuple format to
520
      align lyrics and notes at the word level. Additionally, SongComposer employs scalar initialization
521
      for note pitch, which facilitates the efficient modeling of pitch information. When training on the
522   large corps of song data, a multi-stage pipeline is implemented for structural capture, beginning with
523   motif-level melody data and advancing to phrase-level indicators to enhance coherence and promote
524   logical repetition. Our experiments show that SongComposer outperforms traditional methods and
525   other large language models, including GPT-4, in tasks such as converting lyrics to melodies and
526   vice versa, as well as in continuing existing songs and creating new songs from text. These results
527   highlight SongComposer’s potential as a valuable tool for assisting in music creation.
528   Limitations and Future work. SongComposer primarily focuses on generating symbolic music
529   that synchronizes lyrics and melodies. However, producing corresponding audio currently requires
530   supplementary singing voice synthesis tools. While the musical quality of the audio is partly
531   dependent on the generated score (SongComposer’s output), it also significantly relies on the singer’s
532   performance, including timbre and vocal techniques—areas outside the scope of symbolic music
533   generation. This distinction is important for evaluating the performance of our model, as the perceived
534   audio quality is heavily influenced by the synthesis tool used, not solely by our work. Additionally,
535   SongComposer currently lacks the capability to generate multi-track accompaniments.
536   Symbolic music generation offers fine control and superior editability, whereas acoustic methods
537   provide impressive musical expressiveness and listenability. In future work, we aim to integrate sym-
538   bolic and acoustic approaches to create full-track songs. This integration will enable the generation of
539   precise scores alongside their corresponding high-quality audio, achieving a balance between control
      and auditory appeal.

      E THICS S TATEMENTS
757
758
      The proposed work, SongComposer, a large language model designed for generating songs, has
759   the potential impact on various aspects of society. On the positive side, SongComposer effortlessly
760   creates high-quality songs with melodies and lyrics which can optimize the music creation process,
761   allowing individuals with limited musical training to express their creativity and contribute to the
762   music landscape.
763
      However, as SongComposer generates songs autonomously, there is a risk of potential copyright
764
      infringement or misuse of intellectual property. We have conducted a preliminary memorization
765   analysis shown in Figure 5. However, proper measures still need to be in place to ensure that the
766   generated songs adhere to copyright laws and protect the rights of original composers and authors.
767
768
      In conclusion, while SongComposer presents exciting possibilities for the music industry and creative
      expression, its development should be accompanied by careful consideration of ethical and societal
769
      implications.

      To organize the melody dataset into a text-based structure, we collect MIDI files. Using MIDI files
799
      for our pure melody dataset offers inherent structural simplicity, enabling efficient extraction and
800   manipulation of melodies without complex audio processing. Among our collection, 45K entries
801   come from the LMD-matched MIDI dataset Raffel (2016), while approximately 80K are acquired
802   through web crawling.
803
      For parsing MIDI files, we employ pretty midi Raffel & Ellis (2014), a Python module designed
804
      for creating, manipulating, and analyzing MIDI files. We extract the ”melody” or ”vocal” tracks from
805
      these MIDI files. Since melody in MIDI is represented as a sequence of musical notes over time and
806   each note has a specific pitch, start and end timestamp, we obtain a list of melody attribute triplets
807   consisting of {note pitch, note duration, rest duration}.

822   A.3     PAIRED LYRIC - MELODY DATASET
823
      To build paired data with precise alignment, we process web-scraped information on a large scale
824
      efficiently, creating a dataset of 4K classic Chinese songs and 4K English songs. As illustrated in
825   Figure 6, the pipeline for collecting lyric-melody data is as follows:
826
827          (1) Source Data Crawling: We crawl the web to gather a large dataset of mp3 files and their
828
                 corresponding lyric files, encompassing sentence-level timestamps.
829          (2) Lyrics Cleaning: We use GPT-4 to clean irrelevant details from lyric texts, such as song
830              titles, artist names, and production information.
831          (3) Segment Slicing: To mitigate the challenges and error accumulation for long-time align-
832              ments, we slice the audio and lyrics into paired segments of approximately 10 seconds
833              (roughly three sentences each) based on timestamps provided in the lyric files.
834          (4) Music Source Separation: We utilize UVR5 , a public music separation tool, to separate the
835              vocal from the accompaniment part in the original audio.
836          (5) Singing Voice Transcription: Using a singing voice wav input, FL Studio6 , a digital audio
837              workstation software, automatically generates the preliminary musical score, capturing note
838              pitch and start-end times of each note.
839          (6) Word Boundary Annotation: We obtain the boundaries of each word in lyrics with an audio
840              alignment tool, Montreal Forced Aligner7 .
841          (7) Word-level Alignment: The dynamic time warping (DTW) Müller (2007) algorithm is
842              utilized to align words and notes based on start-end times.
843
      For information at the phrase level, we use the All-In-One music structure analyzer Kim & Nam
844   (2023) to extract it. Finally, we develop a dataset comprising 8K paired lyric-melody entries, with
845   approximately 4K in Chinese and 4K in English.
846
847
      We also conduct the statistical analysis of the paired lyric-melody dataset shown in Figure 7. We
      find that most pitch numbers fall within the range of 50 to 80 and the majority of words are paired
848
      with a single note, and around 10% of words correspond to two or more notes. When examining
849
      note durations, we observe that they primarily vary between 0 to 1 second, and durations of rests are
850   predominantly zero, reflecting a concise musical structure.

      Lyric-to-Melody Generation asks to create a fitting melody based on the given lyrics. The melody
903
      is assessed on: (1) Harmony (HMY.): Evaluates the overall quality of the melody. (2) Melody-Lyric
904
      Compatibility (MLC.): Examines how well the generated melody fits the given lyrics.
905
906   Melody-to-Lyric Generation aims to produce lyrics that match a provided melody. The lyrics are
907   evaluated on: (1) Fluency (FLN.): Considers the grammatical correctness and semantic coherence
908
      of the generated lyrics. (2) Melody-Lyric Compatibility (MLC.): Examines how well the generated
      lyrics fit the given melody.
909
910   Song Continuation involves extending a given song segment both melodically and lyrically. We
911   evaluate the continuation quality on: (1) Overall Quality (OVL.): Measures the overall quality of the
912   generated song in terms of its musical appeal. (2) Coherence to the Song Prompt (COH.): Analyzes
913   the natural integration of the continuation with the provided song prompt, assessing coherence in
914
      melody, lyrics, and other musical elements.
915   Text-to-Song Generation generates a complete song based on textual description, capturing its
916   essence musically and lyrically. The evaluation focuses on: (1) Overall Quality (OVL.): Measures the
917   overall quality of the generated song in terms of its musical appeal. (2) Relevance to the Text Input
      (REL.): Examines how well the generated song aligns with and derives relevance from the input text.

1003   To explore the impact of specialized datasets on our model’s learning, we conduct training exper-
1004   iments using paired data combined with different pure-lyric and pure-melody datasets. Table 8
1005
       demonstrates that omitting both the pure-lyric and pure-melody datasets significantly reduces perfor-

       training stages.

1009   pure-lyric dataset mainly enhances performance in the lyric-related generation, while the pure-melody
1010   dataset significantly boosts melody generation. This finding aligns with the intuitive understanding
1011
