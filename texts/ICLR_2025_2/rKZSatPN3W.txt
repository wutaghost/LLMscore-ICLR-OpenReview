009                                                    A BSTRACT
010
011                Automatic radiology report generation is an advanced medical assistive technol-
012                ogy capable of producing coherent reports based on medical images, akin to a
013                radiologist. However, current generative methods exhibit a notable gap in clinical
014                metrics when compared to medical image classification. Recently, leveraging di-
015                agnostic results to improve report quality has emerged as a promising approach.
016                We are curious whether training a classifier that encompasses all possible long-
017
                   tailed and rare diseases could enhance the robustness of reports. To investigate
                   this question, this study designs an evaluation framework that integrates long-tail
018
                   scenarios and summarizes potential combinations of LLM-based report genera-
019
                   tion models. We assess the impact of classification on report quality across four
020                benchmarks. Initially, we introduce LLM-based language and clinical metrics
021                and develop a pipeline to evaluate the model’s performance on both in-domain
022                and out-of-distribution (OOD) long-tail scenarios. Subsequently, we conduct a
023                systematic evaluation of all potential model combinations. Our findings reveal
024                that: 1) the impact of classification on report quality is positively correlated with
025                the performance of classifiers, but the gap still exists, and 2) while classification
026                can enhance report quality in in-domain long-tail scenarios, its benefits for OOD
027                scenarios are limited.

      1       I NTRODUCTION
031
      Automatic radiology report generation (ARRG) (Jing et al., 2017) has emerged as a significant re-
032
      search area within medical imaging and natural language processing (NLP). The objective of ARRG
033
      systems is to accurately generate comprehensive and clinically meaningful reports from medical im-
034   ages, which has the potential to alleviate the workload of radiologists, reduce diagnostic errors, and
035   improve patient outcomes. Furthermore, such systems can enhance accessibility to high-quality
036   healthcare by providing diagnostic support in regions with limited medical resources.
037
      Despite significant advances in deep learning for medical image analysis, generating coherent and
038
      precise medical reports remains highly challenging due to the complexity of visual information and
039
      the nuances of medical language. Traditional methods, such as retrieval-based (Li et al., 2019; 2018)
040   and template-based (Biswal et al., 2020; Harzig et al., 2019; Li et al., 2018) approaches, often rely
041   on fixed rules or knowledge closely tied to training data for generating radiology reports. In recent
042   years, LLM-based methods (Li et al., 2024; Bannur et al., 2024; Tu et al., 2024) have become an at-
043   tractive research direction, leveraging the powerful extrapolation and in-context learning capabilities
044   to enhance the accuracy of generated reports and improve the interactivity of ARRG systems. How-
045   ever, when evaluating the diagnostic accuracy on specific radiology findings, we observe that the
046   accuracy of reports generated by existing methods falls significantly short compared to the perfor-
047   mance of basic medical image classification approaches. For example, on the MIMIC-CXR dataset,
048
      state-of-the-art generation models exhibit accuracy that is at least 20% lower than that of image
      classification models1 . To address this issue, some studies (Wang et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2024b;
049
      Jin et al., 2024) have sought to integrate diagnostic results to improve the accuracy and reliability of
050
      generated reports. This raises an important question: is classification all you need for radiology
051   report generation? Specifically, this paper aim to conduct a comprehensive study to determine
052   whether training a classifier that encompasses all possible radiology findings in the training data,

      including long-tailed and rare diseases, would improve the robustness of report generation when its
055   diagnostic results are incorporated.
056
057   To validate this hypothesis, we design a benchmark framework by modifying the existing evaluation
058
      setup and introducing a set of baseline methods from a newly proposed LLM based design space
      for report generation. Current evaluation metrics primarily include language metrics and clinical
059
      metrics. Language metrics, such as BLEU (Papineni et al., 2002) and ROUGE (Lin, 2004), focus
060
      on n-gram overlap and sequence alignment, while clinical metrics, like CheXpert F1 (Smit et al.,
061   2020) and RadGraph metric (Jain et al., 2021), emphasize clinical events described in radiology
062   reports, such as pathological entities, their locations, and severity, based on predefined categories.
063   Conventional language metrics, however, primarily focused on grammatical and lexical similarities,
064   often fail to accurately reflect the precision required in clinical diagnostic reports. Clinical met-
065   rics, constrained by a limited set of predefined categories, struggle to capture the intricate diversity
066   of clinical scenarios depicted in medical documents. Furthermore, current clinical metrics are not
067   well-equipped to evaluate nuanced distinctions in inclusive relationships (e.g., differentiating be-
068   tween the left upper lobe and the entire left lung) and near synonyms (e.g., distinguishing a nodule
069
      from an opacity). To address these challenges, besides these conventional evaluation metrics, we
      proposed to introduces two extra metrics that leverages large language models (LLM) to mitigate
070
      the shortcomings of both metric types. LLM-based language metrics provide analytical capabilities
071
      that transcend simple sentence similarity, enabling the comprehension of clinical terminology for
072   assessing report similarity. We reference LLM-RadJudge (Wang et al., 2024) as our LLM-based
073   language metric, an LLM-based language metric that evaluates report similarity across six distinct
074   levels. Additionally, we propose a clinical metric based on LLM that automatically extracts all pos-
075   sible radiology findings from reports, including long-tail and rare disease categories that may not be
076   part of any predefined finding set.
077
      In comparing baselines in our study, we propose an LLM-based design space for report genera-
078   tion models, outlining three key components of existing LLM-based methods: the vision encoder,
079   classifiers, and the LLM itself. The vision encoder, such as CLIP and DINO, extracts abstract
080   features from medical images, transforming them into vision tokens. The classifier derives easily
081   interpretable radiology observations, including probabilities and diagnosis confidence, which can
082   be represented as classification tokens for further processing. The LLM module aggregates and
083   processes all tokens, generating reports in an auto-regressive manner. We analyzed potential combi-
084   nations of these components and identified four baseline models, as illustrated in Fig. 1, which align
085   with the model design space of most existing methods.
086   Under the proposed benchmark framework, we conducted extensive experiments across four bench-
087   marks, revealing a counterintuitive phenomenon: while diagnostic results significantly improve re-
088   port quality in in-domain scenarios, they do not enhance report quality for long-tail diseases and
089   out-of-distribution (OOD) data scenarios when using powerful foundation models such as Llama 3.1
090   70B (Dubey et al., 2024) and OpenAI GPT-4. To better understand this phenomenon, we performed
091   detailed case studies and analyses. We found that LLM effectively utilize information provided by
092
      classifiers to generate final reports, sometimes including observations not mentioned in actual clin-
      ical reports. Consequently, the information from the classifier may mislead LLM-based generative
093
      methods, leading to incorrect results. In summary, our findings indicate that incorporating addi-
094
      tional classification information can enhance report quality in in-domain scenarios but may
095   severely compromise performance in zero-shot settings. Experimental results demonstrate that
096   LLM can modify and augment original reports based on classification information, potentially cor-
097   recting initial erroneous conclusions and narrowing the gap between classification and generation.
098   At the same time, it will also amplify the misclassification errors of the classifier in the long-
099   tail scenario. We hope these findings will inspire further exploration of LLM-based metrics and
100   classification-based report generation in this field.

      2.3     E VALUATION FRAMEWORK
169
170   In this part, our objective is to design a comprehensive benchmark framework that can evaluate
171   LLM-based methods in long-tail scenarios. We assess the robustness of a report generation model
172   from both in-domain and out-of-distribution (OOD) perspectives. First, we introduce the training
173   datasets, as well as the long-tail datasets for both in-domain and OOD scenarios. Next, we present
174   our evaluation metrics, which include traditional language and clinical metrics, alongside LLM-
175   based metrics.

177   2.3.1    DATASETS AND DATA P RE - PROCESSING
178
179   We train all baselines on MIMIC-CXR 2.0.0 (Johnson et al., 2019c;b), a large dataset of chest radio-
180   graphs in DICOM format with free-text radiology reports, containing 377,110 images corresponding
181   to 227,835 radiographic studies. Following (Hyland et al., 2023), we extract the Findings and In-
182   dication sections for each report, and discard all studies for which Findings could not be extracted.
183
      Unlike (Hyland et al., 2023), we used png files from MIMIC-CXR-JPG (Johnson et al., 2019a) as
      vision inputs, instead of the original DICOM files, the former of which show better compatibility in
184
      our experimental setup. Following (Hyland et al., 2023), we used the available finding parts from
185
      the official MIMIC-CXR test split, totaling 2,461 samples.
186
187   For evaluation, we utilized four datasets: MIMIC-CXR, CXR-LT (Holste et al., 2023; Goldberger
188   et al., 2000), PadChest (Bustos et al., 2020), and IU X-Ray (Demner-Fushman et al., 2016). We
189
      divide them into in-domain and OOD long-tail datasets:
190   The In-domain Long-tail Dataset. We use CXR-LT as a dataset to verify the performance of the
191   model in the in-domain longtail scenario. It is an extension version of MIMIC-CXR, to evaluate the
192   performance of report generation models in long-tailed scenarios, containing 377,110 CXRs from
193   26 long-tail observations.
194   The OOD Long-tail Dataset. We introduce two out-of-distribution (OOD) long-tail datasets to
195   evaluate the model’s generalization capability in different X-ray positions as well as different lan-
196   guages and reporting styles. PadChest is a large-scale, high-resolution, labeled chest X-ray dataset
197   designed for automated medical image analysis, accompanied by corresponding reports. It contains
198   over 160,000 images from 67,000 patients. From this dataset, we randomly sampled 500 instances,
199   comprising 99 observations, to form the test set. Additionally, we employed GPT-4 to translate the
200   original Spanish reports into English. The IU X-Ray dataset consists of 7,470 chest X-ray images
201
      paired with diagnostic reports. We categorized the labels based on primary disease classifications,
      yielding a test set of 756 samples across 82 observations.
202
203   For image processing, we resize all images to 224×224 and 518×518 to adapt various vision back-
204   bones, e.g., CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) and Rad-DINO (Pérez-Garcı́a et al., 2024), and we do not
205   apply any data augmentation to images. For text processing, we utilize a same processing pipeline
206   in (Chiang et al., 2023).

208   2.3.2    E VALUATION M ETRICS
209
210   To more comprehensively evaluate the performance of different methods, we introduce the following
211   metrics: language, clinical, and LLM-based metrics, including:
212   Language Metrics. ROUGE-L (Lin, 2004), this metric assesses the length of the longest common
213   subsequence of words, normalized by the lengths of both the predicted and reference texts; BLEU-1/-
214   4 (Papineni et al., 2002), these metrics evaluate n-gram precision, with BLEU-1 focusing on single
215   words and BLEU-4 considering up to four-word sequences. A brevity penalty is applied to mitigate
      the impact of excessively short predictions; METEOR (Banerjee & Lavie, 2005), this metric aligns


                                                         4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      unigrams from the prediction and reference texts while maintaining their order, and calculates a
217   weighted harmonic mean of precision and recall, incorporating a penalty for fragmented sequences.
218
219   Clinical Metrics. CheXpert F1, this metric utilizes the CheXbert automatic labeler (Smit et al.,
220
      2020) to categorize observations into ‘present’, ‘absent’, or ‘uncertain’ for each of the 14 CheXpert
      pathological conditions. We provide macro- and micro-averaged F1 scores for both the 5 major
221
      observations and all 14 observations, termed “[Macro/Micro]-F1-[5/14]”; RadGraph metric (Jain
222
      et al., 2021; Delbrouck et al., 2022), this metric measures the overlap of entities and relations sepa-
223   rately, and then computes their average. Entities are matched based on identical text spans and types,
224   while relations are matched based on the endpoints and the relation type, termed RGER score. This
225   evaluation is conducted using the radgraph package2 .
226
      LLM-based Metrics. LLM-Radjudge (Wang et al., 2024) uses large language models to assess the
227
      quality of radiology reports, providing a detailed description and classification of errors. It includes
228
      six error levels: levels 1 and 2 describe the number of observational errors, level 3 describes the
229   number of location errors, level 4 describes the number of severity errors, and levels 5 and 6 compare
230   with previous reports. We report the average values for levels 1-4; Long-tailed & OOD F1, this
231   metric is used to validate the generalization ability of the generation model to diseases that have
232   never been seen in the training set. We use the OpenAI GPT-4o API to extract the disease categories
233   from the generated reports and then compute the F1 score of these extracted categories. Specifically,
234   we use “[Macro/Micro]-F1-[LT26/LT99/OOD82]” to represent the result on 26 observations in the
235   CXR-LT dataset, 99 observations in the PadChest dataset, and 82 observations in the IU X-ray
236   dataset, respectively. Note that the metrics used in this study indicate that higher values are better
237
      for all metrics except LLM-Radjudge where lower values are better.

      4     R ELATED W ORK
487
488
      4.1   AUTOMATIC R EPORT G ENERATION
489
490   Automatic report generation has gained attention in healthcare and NLP, with various approaches
491   leveraging NLG and deep learning (Jing et al., 2017; Bannur et al., 2024; Jin et al., 2024; Tu et al.,
492   2024). Early methods used retrieval-based (Li et al., 2019) and template-based techniques (Biswal
493   et al., 2020; Harzig et al., 2019; Li et al., 2018), which lacked flexibility. Advances in large lan-
494   guage models (LLMs) (Bannur et al., 2024; Hyland et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023; Zhao et al.,
495
      2024b) have enabled more sophisticated systems, showing improved coherence in report genera-
      tion (Li et al., 2024; Zhao et al., 2024a). Multi-modal data integration, combining text and images,
496
      further enhances report interpretability. However, diagnostic accuracy remains an issue compared to
497
      traditional methods, prompting efforts (Jin et al., 2024; Zhao et al., 2024b; Wang et al., 2023) to im-
498   prove accuracy by incorporating diagnostic imaging. More discussions are present at the appendix.
499
500
      4.2   R ADIOLOGY R EPORT E VALUATION
501
502   Radiology report evaluation focuses on accuracy and clinical relevance, assessed via language and
503   clinical metrics. Language metrics, like BLEU (Papineni et al., 2002), ROUGE (Lin, 2004), ME-
504   TEOR (Banerjee & Lavie, 2005), and BERTScore (Zhang et al., 2019), measure similarity but lack
505   clinical depth. Clinical metrics assess medical events, with tools like CheXpert and CheXbert (Smit
506   et al., 2020), RadGraph (Jain et al., 2021), and cosine similarity, though limited by predefined en-
507   tities. Recent methods, including RadCliQ (Yu et al., 2023), RadEval (Calamida et al., 2023), and
508
      LLM-based approaches (Wang et al., 2024), show improved adaptability and performance. More
      discussions are present at the appendix.
509
510
511   5     D ISCUSSION AND L IMITATIONS
512
513   The gap between report generation and classification. Our experiments reveal a significant gap
514   between report generation and classification, both in in-domain and OOD long-tail scenarios. Clas-
515   sification only requires determining whether an observation is positive, while report generation de-
516
      mands detailed text that mirrors the target report, including specifics like location and severity. The
      absence of descriptive details in classification can cause hallucinations in LLM methods, leading to
517
      poor reports. Some studies (Wang et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2024b) have improved report quality
518
      by using more complex information. A promising approach is to enable LLMs to selectively use
519   additional data during fine-tuning, such as dynamically adjusting attention weights (Chefer et al.,
520   2023; Zhou et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024b).
521
522
      Evaluation Framework. Developing a robust evaluation framework for free-text reports remains a
      challenge. Current language and clinical metrics are inadequate: language metrics focus on gram-
523
      matical similarities but miss the precision required for clinical diagnostics, while clinical metrics
524
      are too narrow to capture the diverse scenarios in medical reports. In this paper, we enhance both
525   using LLMs, as they can interpret complex texts and support knowledge extrapolation. However,
526   this has limitations, especially in OOD scenarios, where varying granularity in observation names
527   across datasets requires semantic transformation. In long-tail datasets, overlapping observations can
528   further reduce the reliability of LLM-based metrics.
529
530
      6     C ONCLUSION
531
532
      In this study, we explore and understand how diagnossis results impact the overall quality of LLM-
533
      based report generation models. We design a long-tail evaluation framework that incorporates both
534   in-domain and out-of-domain (OOD) elements, utilizing LLM-based language metrics and clini-
535   cal metrics. Furthermore, we conducted a high-level analysis of the effective combinations of the
536   primary components of LLM-based generation models, assessing how classification information im-
537   pacts report quality across four benchmarks. Our findings reveal that the classifier’s performance
538   in long-tail observations directly influences the overall performance of the LLM-based generation
539   model. We hope these findings inspire further enthusiasm for more robust report evaluation metrics
      and more effective report generation models.

845   E.2      R ADIOLOGY R EPORT E VALUATION
846
847   The evaluation of radiology reports is essential for ensuring their accuracy, completeness, and
848   clinical relevance. Traditional evaluation methods can be categorized into two main types: lan-
849
      guage metrics and clinical metrics. Language metrics include BLEU (Papineni et al., 2002),
      ROUGE (Lin, 2004), and METEOR (Banerjee & Lavie, 2005) scores, as well as more recent met-
850
      rics like BERTScore (Zhang et al., 2019), which utilize embeddings from pre-trained models. These
851
      metrics are commonly employed to assess the similarity between generated reports and ground-truth
852   reports. However, they often overlook the clinical events described in radiology reports, resulting in
853   limited clinical significance.
854
855
      On the other hand, clinical metrics focus on the clinical descriptions within radiology reports, which
      are vital for practical applications. These metrics capture all clinical events illustrated in medical
856
      images, such as the nature, location, and extent of pathology. A widely used metric is CheXpert,
857
      which categorizes 14 types of pathologies and indicates their presence or absence with labels. Tools
858   like CheXbert (Smit et al., 2020), along with metrics such as cosine similarity and RadGraph (Jain
859   et al., 2021), are employed for this evaluation. However, these extraction-based methods have limi-
860   tations due to their dependence on predefined entities and strict matching rules. Efforts to enhance
861   these methods, such as RadCliQ (Yu et al., 2023) and RadEval (Calamida et al., 2023), which com-
862   bine different metrics—still struggle to fully evaluate clinical descriptions. Recently, an innovative
863   approach (Wang et al., 2024) that leverages large language models for assessment offers improved
      adaptability and performance comparable to that of radiologists.
