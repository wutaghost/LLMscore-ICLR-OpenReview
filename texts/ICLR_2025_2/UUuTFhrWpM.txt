009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            As the size of language models increases, they deliver substantial performance
012            improvements across a variety of applications. However, this growth also leads
013            to greater computational demands, making deployment on resource-constrained
014            devices—such as personal computers and mobile or wearable devices—more
015            challenging, and significantly raising inference costs on cloud servers. To address
016            these challenges, we introduce a method to streamline language models. We
017            observe that language models pretrained on general datasets often include redundant
018
               components that are unnecessary for particular tasks. Our approach identifies and
               removes these redundant parts, retaining only the essential components for the
019
               intended applications. Specifically, we represent the weight matrices of language
020
               models as a linear combination of base components, eliminate the irrelevant bases,
021            and introduce new bases that enhance performance for target tasks. Evaluations
022            show that our method reduces model size much more significantly—by up to 1.7
023            times—while maintaining similar accuracy, compared to state-of-the-art techniques,
024            across a range of applications.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Large language models (LLMs) have significantly enhanced the performance of various applications
030   in natural language processing, computer vision, and beyond. However, their large model sizes pose a
031
      bottleneck for many practical uses. The substantial computing resources required for LLM inference
      make it challenging to deploy them on devices with limited capabilities, such as personal computers
032
      and mobile/wearable devices. Moreover, even on hardware platforms with ample computing power,
033
      deploying LLMs consumes a significant amount of energy, raising concerns about sustainability.
034   Therefore, it is essential to reduce the size of LLMs after pretraining to ease their computational
035   demands and lower energy consumption.
036
037
      Our approach exploits the relationship between pretrained models and specific target applications.
      Large language models (LLMs) are typically pretrained on vast datasets encompassing a wide range
038
      of tasks, many of which share common characteristics. This shared pretraining fosters synergies
039
      that enhance the performance of LLMs. However, the diversity among these tasks also introduces
040   redundancy into the models. As demonstrated by our interpretation results in Section 3 shows, LLMs
041   contain a significant number of redundant components that are unnecessary for a specific target
042   application. By removing these redundant parts and retaining only the relevant ones, we can reduce
043   the model’s size while preserving its performance on the target application. Since many scenarios
044   only require support for a specific type of application, this approach effectively lowers the computing
045   resource requirements and reduces inference costs.
046   However, how do we identify the beneficial and redundant components of LLMs for a specific
047   application? In this work, we address this problem through the lens of matrix factorization. Singular
048   Value Decomposition (SVD) (Golub & Van Loan, 1996) factorizes          a weight matrix W into the
      product of three matrices U, S, and V, i.e., W = USVT = i si ui viT , where si are (positive)

071   Inspired by this observation, we propose Basel, a low-rank decomposition approach to effectively
072   compress LLMs for target applications. Figure 1 illustrates the key idea of Basel. We view each
073   weight matrix in LLMs as a linear combination of bases ui vi T with singular values si as their
074
      weights. These bases are valuable representations stored in the pretrained model, learned from large
      pretraining datasets. For a target application, some bases are advantageous while many others are not.
075
      To select the bases beneficial for the target application, we propose retraining the singular values (i.e.,
076
      the weights of the bases) while keeping the bases fixed, using the training set of the target application.
077   After retraining, we prune the bases associated with small singular values, as they are less important
078   for the target application, and retain only those with large singular values, which are most critical for
079   the target application. This approach allows us to eliminate the redundant parts of the original LLMs
080   and retain only the components essential for the target application. To handle the data distribution
081   differences between the pretraining dataset and the target application, we also augment the model
082   with new bases learned from the training set of the target application during the pruning process. This
083   enables us to learn the new bases necessary for the target application that are absent in the pretrained
084   model.
085   We evaluate Basel on two models—Llama 2-7B and Llama 2-13B (Touvron et al., 2023)—and
086   two tasks—mathematical reasoning and code generation. We evaluate the pass@1 accuracy of the
087   compressed models on GSM8K (Cobbe et al., 2021) and MATH (Hendrycks et al., 2021) for the
088   mathematical reasoning task and HumanEval (Chen et al., 2021a) and MBPP (Austin et al., 2021) for
089   the code generation task. Compared to state-of-the-art baselines, our approach achieves substantially
090   better performance, improving accuracy by up to 16% when the compression ratio 1 exceeds 6
091
      for mathematical reasoning and 4 for code generation. This also indicates that, in cases of deep
      compression, our method reduces model size by up to 1.7 times while maintaining comparable
092
      accuracy to baseline methods.

094   This paper makes the following critical contributions:
095
096             • We analyze the relationship between pretrained models and target applications, highlighting
097               the opportunity and underlying rationale for using low-rank decomposition to compress
098               large language models while maintaining performance on target applications.
099

                • We propose Basel, a low-rank decomposition approach to compress pretrained large language
                  models for target applications. Basel identifies the beneficial and redundant components of

      2       R ELATED W ORK
109
110
      Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) (Golub & Van Loan, 1996) has been applied to reduce the
111   size of machine learning models. Prior research (Xue et al., 2013; Jaderberg et al., 2014; Denton
112   et al., 2014; Zhang et al., 2015; Povey et al., 2018; Chen et al., 2018; Acharya et al., 2019; Noach &
113   Goldberg, 2020) has developed various SVD algorithms to compress different components of models,
114   such as DNNs, CNNs, and embedding layers for a range of applications including natural language
115   processing, speech, and vision. The primary distinction between our work and these prior studies is
116   that they do not relearn the importance of bases using the training data of target applications. Instead,
117   they typically prune bases according to the singular values from the original or finetuned models.
118   FWSVD (Hsu et al., 2022) evaluates the importance of individual weights rather than bases during
119
      SVD. As highlighted in Sections 1 and 3, the bases hold significant physical meanings. Considering
      importance at this level of granularity results in improved performance. As shown in Section 4, our
120
      approach surpasses FWSVD in deep compression performance.
121
122   A recent study (Sharma et al., 2024) applied SVD to large language models. Its focus is on determining
123   the optimal rank for each layer, while our emphasis is on basis selection. The two methods are
124   orthogonal but complementary and can be combined. (Chen et al., 2021b) and (Yu & Wu, 2023)
125
      suggest reconstructing bases based on feature mimicking. These approaches are orthogonal to
      ours—they concentrate on basis reconstruction, whereas we focus on basis selection. Their methods
126
      complement ours and can be integrated together to achieve enhanced compression results.

        weights (while keeping the bases fixed) on the training set of the target application. The aim is that,
217     after retraining, the bases important for the target application will have larger singular values, whereas
218     those that are useless or detrimental will have zero or very small singular values. This allows us to
219     identify and prune the less useful bases. Relearning the importance of bases for the target application
220     distinguishes our approach from previous methods. Prior approaches either use the singular values in
221     the original model (Xue et al., 2013; Jaderberg et al., 2014; Denton et al., 2014; Zhang et al., 2015;

225

        each filter, catering to the needs of the target application.
226
227     In the second term, ũj and ṽj are learnable vectors included for two primary purposes. First, due to
228     differences in data distribution between the pretrained dataset and the target application, some bases
229     necessary for the target application might be absent in the pretrained dataset. We use these vectors
230     to learn such bases. Second, during pruning, although each pruned basis may individually have
231
        minimal impact on the target application, their cumulative performance loss can be significant. These
        additional vectors help compensate for the performance loss caused by the pruned bases. The number
232
        of learnable vectors r̃ is referred to as the additional dimension. From a signal processing perspective,
233
        this second term allows us to include additional, new filters to enhance the model performance on the
234     target application.

        The performance of low-rank compression algorithms is evaluated on two tasks: mathematical
280
        reasoning and code generation. For each task, Llama 2-7B and Llama 2-13B models (Touvron et al.,

284
         For the mathematical reasoning task, we utilize two evaluation datasets: GSM8K (Cobbe et al.,

285
         described mathematical questions, containing 1,319 samples used for evaluation. The Hendrycks’
286
         MATH dataset covers more complex topics such as linear algebra and geometry, consisting of 5,000
287      question-answer pairs used for evaluation. Due to its complexity, the Hendrycks’ MATH dataset
288      necessitates more sophisticated computations and reasoning, resulting in lower accuracy compared to
289      GSM8K.
290
         For the code generation task, we use two evaluation datasets: MBPP (Austin et al., 2021) and
291
         HumanEval (Chen et al., 2021a). Both datasets evaluate the models’ ability to generate Python code.
292

293      questions.

        2014; Zhang et al., 2015; Povey et al., 2018). FWSVD (Hsu et al., 2022) enhances SVD by evaluating

409     dimension (denoted by r̃ in equation equation 6) and pruning times. The additional dimension
410     compensates for information loss during pruning, especially for deep compression. Figure 8 compares
411
        the performance of compressing Llama 2-7B for the mathematical reasoning task with and without an
        additional dimension of 32. As expected, incorporating an additional dimension improves accuracy
412
        at higher compression ratios (above 6). Similarly, pruning the model lightly but multiple times
413
        allows it to gradually adapt to the reduction in parameters after each pruning. This is particularly
414     beneficial for achieving extreme compression ratios. Figure 9 demonstrates this effect by comparing
415     the performance of pruning Llama 2-7B 100 times vs. 2 times on the same task. Here, pruning 100
416     times leads to better accuracy at compression ratios exceeding 10.

         5            C ONCLUSION
467
        The significant size of large language models leads to high inference costs and demands substantial
468
        computing resources. To mitigate these issues, we focus on compressing large language models to
469
        meet the specific requirements of target applications. Our approach involves examining these models
470     through the lens of matrix factorization. By viewing the weight matrix of large language models as
471     a linear combination of a group of bases, we have identified that pretrained models often contain
472     many redundant bases that are less useful for target applications. To address this, we propose Basel, a
473     compression algorithm that evaluates the importance of each base for target applications and prunes
474     those that are less significant. Experimental results demonstrate that Basel significantly outperforms
475     state-of-the-art low-rank compression algorithms in achieving deep compression. Basel greatly
476     reduces the inference cost of large language models, making them more accessible and practical for
477     a wider range of applications. This advancement has the potential to democratize the use of large
478
        language models, facilitating their adoption and integration across diverse fields and industries.
