011                                                     A BSTRACT
012
                   Federated Learning (FL) has emerged as the state-of-the-art approach for learning
013
                   from decentralized data in privacy-constrained scenarios. However, system and sta-
014                tistical challenges hinder real-world applications, which demand efficient learning
015                from edge devices and robustness to heterogeneity. Despite significant research
016                efforts, existing approaches (i) are not sufficiently robust, (ii) do not perform well
017                in large-scale scenarios, and (iii) are not communication efficient. In this work,
018                we propose a novel Generalized Heavy-Ball Momentum (GHBM), proving that it
019                enjoys an improved theoretical convergence rate w.r.t. existing FL methods based
020                on classical momentum in partial participation, without relying on bounded data
021                heterogeneity. Then, we present F ED HBM as an adaptive, communication-efficient
022
                   by-design instance of GHBM. Extensive experimentation on vision and language
                   tasks, in both controlled and realistic large-scale scenarios, confirms our theoretical
023
                   findings, showing that GHBM substantially improves the state of the art, especially
024
                   in large scale scenarios with high data heterogeneity and low client participation 1 .

      1       I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Federated Learning (FL) (McMahan et al., 2017) is a paradigm to learn from decentralized data in
029   which a central server orchestrates an iterative two-step training process that involves 1) local training,
030   potentially on a large number of clients, each with its own private data, and 2) the aggregation of
031   these updated local models on the server into a single, shared global model. This process is repeated
032   over several communication rounds. While the inherent privacy-preserving nature of FL is appealing
033
      for decentralized applications where data sharing is restricted, it also introduces some challenges.
      Since local data reflects characteristics of individual clients, limiting the optimization to use only the
034
      client’s data can lead to issues caused by statistical heterogeneity. This becomes problematic when
035
      multiple optimization steps are performed before models are synchronized, causing clients to drift
036   away from the ideal global updates (Karimireddy et al., 2020). Indeed, heterogeneity has been shown
037   to hinder the convergence of F EDAVG (Hsu et al., 2019), increasing the number of communication
038   rounds to reach a target model quality (Reddi et al., 2021) and impacting final performance.
039
      Several studies have proposed solutions to mitigate the effects of heterogeneity. For instance,
040
      SCAFFOLD (Karimireddy et al., 2020) relies on additional control variables to correct the local
041
      client’s updates, while F ED DYN (Acar et al., 2021) uses ADMM to align the global and local client
042   solutions. Albeit theoretically grounded, experimentally these methods are not sufficiently robust to
043   handle cases of extreme heterogeneity, low client participation, or large-scale problems, exhibiting
044   slow convergence and instabilities (Varno et al., 2022; Reddi et al., 2021).
045
046
      Momentum-based FL methods appear to be a promising solution for addressing these challenges. By
      accumulating past update directions, momentum can help clients overcome the inconsistencies of
047
      local objectives introduced by heterogeneous data. Several works explored incorporating momentum
048
      in FL, either at the server (Hsu et al., 2019) or at client-level to correct local updates Ozfatura
049   et al. (2021); Xu et al. (2021). Notably, M IME (Karimireddy et al., 2020; 2021) has been proposed
050   as a framework to make clients mimic the updates of a centralized model trained on i.i.d. data
051   by leveraging extra server statistics at client side. While the theoretical benefits of momentum in
052   FL have been demonstrated in full participation Cheng et al. (2024), existing FL methods based
053

055   as our work demonstrates, experimentally present limitations in settings with low participation,
056   high heterogeneity and real-world large-scale problems. Moreover, current approaches often incur
057   increased communication costs due to the exchange of additional information required to correct
058   local updates (Karimireddy et al., 2020; 2021; Xu et al., 2021; Ozfatura et al., 2021). This can be a
059   significant drawback in communication-constrained environments, further hindering the practical
060   adoption of FL in real-world scenarios and underscoring the critical need for more robust, effective,
061   and communication-efficient FL algorithms.
062   In this work, we present both empirical evidence and theoretical justification for the failure cases of
063   previous momentum-based FL approaches. Specifically, we demonstrate that the interplay of data
064   heterogeneity and partial participation is not properly addressed, leading to the classical momentum
065   term employed in these methods to be updated with a biased estimate of the global gradient, which
066   diminishes its effectiveness in correcting client drift. To address these challenges, we propose a
067   novel Generalized Heavy-Ball (GHBM) formulation of momentum that consists of calculating
068   it as a decayed average of τ past momentum terms. This design choice makes the momentum
069   term not to be biased towards most recently selected clients, and allows GHBM to converge under
070
      arbitrary heterogeneity even in partial participation. Then, we present F ED HBM as an adaptive,
      communication-efficient by-design instance of GHBM, and show experimentally significantly im-
071
      proved performance over the state of the art.

073   Contributions. We summarize our main results below.
074
      • We present a novel formulation of momentum called Generalized Heavy-Ball (GHBM) momentum,
075
        which extends the classical heavy-ball (Polyak, 1964), and propose variants that are robust to
076
        heterogeneity and communication-efficient by design.
077   • We establish the theoretical convergence rate of GHBM for non-convex functions, extending the
078     previous result of Cheng et al. (2024) of classical momentum, showing that GHBM converges
079     under arbitrary heterogeneity even (and most notably) in partial participation.
080   • We empirically show that existing FL algorithms suffer severe limitations in extreme non-iid scenar-
081     ios and real-world settings. In contrast, F ED HBM is extremely robust and achieves higher model
082     quality with significantly faster convergence speeds than other client-drift correction methods.

084   2    R ELATED WORKS
085
      The problem of statistical heterogeneity. The detrimental effects of non-iid data in FL were first
086   observed by (Zhao et al., 2018), who proposed mitigating performance loss by broadcasting a small
087   portion of public data to reduce the divergence between clients’ distributions. Alternatively, (Li &
088   Wang, 2019) uses server-side public data for knowledge distillation. Both approaches rely on the
089   strong assumption of readily available and suitable data. Recognizing weight divergence as a source
090   of performance loss F ED P ROX (Li et al., 2020) adds a regularization term to penalize divergence from
091   the global model. Nevertheless, this was proved ineffective in addressing data heterogeneity Caldarola
092   et al. (2022). Other works (Kopparapu & Lin, 2020; Zaccone et al., 2022; Zeng et al., 2022; Caldarola
093   et al., 2021) explored grouping clients based on their data distribution to mitigate the challenges of
094
      aggregating divergent models.
095   Stochastic Variance Reduction in FL. Stochastic variance reduction techniques have been applied
096   in FL (Chen et al., 2021; Li et al., 2019) with SCAFFOLD Karimireddy et al. (2020) providing for
097   the first time convergence guarantees for arbitrarily heterogeneous data. The authors also shed light
098   on the client-drift of local optimization, which results in slow and unstable convergence. SCAFFOLD
099   uses control variates to estimate the direction of the server model and clients’ models and to correct
100   the local update. This approach requires double the communication to exchange the control variates,
101   and it is not robust enough to handle large-scale scenarios akin to cross-device FL (Reddi et al., 2021;
102
      Karimireddy et al., 2021). Conversely, our novel formulation of momentum yields a graceful decay
      of old and stale gradients while achieving robustness to extreme heterogeneity and low participation.
103
      Based on it, we propose an algorithm that does not require any additional data exchange.
104
105   ADMM and adaptivity. Other methods are based on the Alternating Direction Method of Multi-
106   pliers (Chen et al., 2022; Gong et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2022). In particular, F ED DYN(Acar et al.,
107   2021) dynamically modifies the loss function such that the model parameters converge to stationary
      points of the global empirical loss. Although technically it enjoys the same convergence properties


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      of SCAFFOLD without suffering from its increased communication cost, in practical cases it has
109   displayed problems in dealing with pathological non-iid settings (Varno et al., 2022). Other works
110   explored the use of adaptivity to speed up the convergence of FedAvg and reduce the communication
111   overhead (Xie et al., 2019; Reddi et al., 2021).
112
113   Use of momentum as local correction. As a first attempt, Hsu et al. (2019) adopted momentum at
114
      server-side to reduce the impact of heterogeneity. However, it has been proven of limited effectiveness
      under high heterogeneity, because the drift happens at the client level. This motivated later approaches
115
      that apply server momentum at each local step (Ozfatura et al., 2021; Xu et al., 2021), and the more
116
      general approach by Karimireddy et al. (2021) to adapt any centralized optimizer to cross-device FL.
117   It employs a combination of control variates and server optimizer state (e.g. momentum) at each client
118   step, which lead to increased communication bandwidth and frequency. A recent similar approach
119   (Das et al., 2022) employs compressed updates, still requiring significantly more computation
120   client-side. Rather differently from previous works, F ED HBM is based on our novel Generalized
121   Heavy-Ball Momentum (GHBM): it consists in a decayed average of the previous τ momentum terms
122   instead of considering only the last one, and it is designed to more steadily incorporate the descent
123   information of clients selected at past rounds, to be used into local steps as client drift correction.
124   Indeed, the classical heavy-ball (Polyak, 1964) is a special case of GHBM. Remarkably, we show
125
      that our formulation is crucial to effectively counteract the effects of statistical heterogeneity and
      client sampling, and it is communication efficient by design.
126
127   Lowering communication requirements in FL. Researchers have studied methods to reduce the
128   memory needed for exchanging gradients in the distributed setting, for example by quantization
129   (Alistarh et al., 2017) or by compression (Mishchenko et al., 2019; Koloskova et al., 2020). In the
130   context of FL, such ideas have been developed to meet the communication and scalability constraints
131   (Reisizadeh et al., 2020), and to take into account heterogeneity (Sattler et al., 2020). Our work
132   focuses on the efficient use of the information already being sent in vanilla F EDAVG, so additional
133
      techniques to compress that information remain orthogonal to our approach.

136   3.1   S ETUP
137
      In FL a server and a set S of clients collaboratively solve a learning problem, with |S | = K ∈ N+ .
138   At each round t ∈ [T ], a fraction of C ∈ (0, 1] clients from S is selected to participate to the learning
139
      process: we denote this portion as S t ⊆ S . Each client i ∈ S t receives the server model θit,0 ≡ θt−1 ,
140
      and performs Ji local optimization steps, using stochastic gradients g̃it,j evaluated on local parameters
141
      θit,j−1 and a batch di,j , sampled from its local dataset Di . During local training, θit,j is the model
142
143
      of client i at round t after the j-th optimization step, while θit ≡ θt,Ji is the model sent back to the
      server. The server then aggregates the client updates g̃it := (θt−1 − θit ), building pseudo-gradients
144
      (Reddi et al., 2021) g̃ t that are used to update the model.

146   3.2   A DDRESSING CLIENT DRIFT WITH MOMENTUM
147
      One of the core propositions of federated optimization is to take advantage of local clients’ work,
148   by running multiple optimization steps on local parameters before synchronization. This has been
149   proven effective for speeding up convergence when local datasets are i.i.d. with respect to a global
150   distribution (Stich, 2019; Lin et al., 2020; McMahan et al., 2017), and is particularly important for
151   improving communication efficiency, which is the bottleneck when learning in decentralized settings.
152   However, the statistical heterogeneity of clients’ local datasets causes local models to drift from the
153   ideal trajectory of server parameters. One way of addressing such drift is to use momentum during
154   local optimization, based on the idea that a moving average of past server pseudo-gradients can
155   correct local optimization towards the solution of the global problem. At each round, FL methods
156
      based on momentum typically use the gradients of the selected clients, whether computed at local
      (Xu et al., 2021; Ozfatura et al., 2021) or global (Karimireddy et al., 2021) parameters, to update the
157
      momentum term server-side.
158
159   Partial Participation and Biased Momentum. We claim that existing momentum-based methods
160   overlook a critical aspect of federated learning: partial client participation. Indeed, when only a
161   portion of clients participate in the training rounds, the server pseudo-gradient used to update the
      momentum estimate can be biased towards the previously selected clients, hampering its corrective

304   4      T HEORETICAL DISCUSSION
305
306   In this section, we establish the theoretical foundations of our algorithms. Our analysis reveals that:
307
      (i) the momentum update rule implemented by GHBM in eq. (4) approximates an update with
      global gradient, with τ controlling the trade-off between heterogeneity reduction and the lag due to
308
      using old gradients; (ii) thanks to this algorithmic design choice, GHBM converges under arbitrary
309
      heterogeneity even in partial participation, whereas FL methods based on classical momentum
310   inevitably require to assume bounded data heterogeneity. The proofs are deferred to Appendix B.
311

534   In this work, we propose a novel Generalized Heavy-Ball Momentum (GHBM), motivating its
535   principled application in FL to counteract the effects of statistical heterogeneity. Based on GHBM,
536   we present F ED HBM as an adaptive instance which is additionally communication-efficient by design.
537   Our results in large-scale scenarios largely improve the state of art both in final model quality and
538   communication efficiency. The generality and versatility of the novel GHBM formulation expands
539   its potential applications to a wider range of scenarios where communication is a bottleneck, such as
      distributed learning.

      A.1 E XTENDED R ELATED W ORKS
760
761
      Recently, similarly based on variance reduction as SCAFFOLD, (Mishchenko et al., 2022) propose
      S CAFF N EW to achieve accelerated communication complexity in heterogeneous settings through
762
      control variates, guaranteeing convergence under arbitrary heterogeneity in full participation. The
763
      work by Mishchenko et al. (2024), under the assumption of second-order data heterogeneity, proposes
764   an algorithm which can reduce client drift by estimating the global update direction as well as
765   employing regularization. Similarly to the already discussed M IME (Karimireddy et al., 2021),
766   Karagulyan et al. (2024) propose the SPAM algorithm and leverage momentum as a local correction
767   term to benefit from second-order similarity.
768
      Among momentum methods with similar guarantees than ours, SCAFFOLD-M (Cheng et al., 2024)
769
      integrates classical momentum into SCAFFOLD to attain a slightly better convergence rate and
770   maintaining robustness to unbounded heterogeneity in partial participation. However, SCAFFOLD-
771   M inherits the same limitations of SCAFFOLD: it requires clients to keep local client states across
772   rounds, making the algorithm not well-suited for cross-device FL (Reddi et al., 2021); and it is
773   limited by the ineffectiveness of variance reduction in deep learning (Defazio & Bottou, 2019).
774   Conversely, momentum has proven fundamental to accelerate training in deep learning, and GHBM
775   is the first algorithm that can use only momentum to converge under arbitrary heterogeneity in partial
776   participation

822   A.4    B ROADER I MPACT AND L IMITATIONS
823
824   The algorithms presented in this work offer a substantial advancement in federated training efficiency.
825   By significantly improving performance while reducing computational, communication, and energy
826   costs, our approach contributes to a more sustainable and scalable federated learning ecosystem. This
827
      marks a notable step towards wider adoption of FL in real-world applications, particularly in the
      challenging cross-device setting, where our methods have demonstrated remarkable flexibility and
828
      effectiveness. Despite these significant improvements over the state-of-the-art, challenges remain
829
      in fully realizing the potential of cross-device FL. Our results underscore the critical importance
830   of accurately estimating the global direction for rapid algorithm convergence. Both GHBM and
831   F ED HBM leverage this insight, correcting client drift through global direction estimation. However,
832   the accurate estimation of this direction in extremely large-scale scenarios (e.g., millions of clients
833   with low participation rates) remains an open research problem.

1532   C IFAR -10/100 We consider C IFAR -10 and C IFAR -100 to experiment with image classification
1533   tasks, each one respectively having 10 and 100 classes. For all methods, training images are pre-
1534   processed by applying random crops, followed by random horizontal flips. Both training and test

1536   experimentation, we used a model similar to LeNet-5 as proposed in (Hsu et al., 2020). To further
1537   validate our findings, we also employed a ResNet-20 as described in (He et al., 2015), following the
1538
       implementation provided in (Idelbayev, 2021). Since batch normalization Ioffe & Szegedy (2015)
       layers have been shown to hamper performance in learning from decentralized data with skewed
1539
       label distribution (Hsieh et al., 2020), we replaced them with group normalization (Wu & He, 2018),
1540
       using two groups in each layer. For a fair comparison, we used the same modified network also in

1603   C.2 S IMULATING HETEROGENEITY
1604   For C IFAR -10/100 we simulate arbitrary heterogeneity by splitting the total datasets according to
1605   a Dirichlet distribution with concentration parameter α, following Hsu et al. (2020). In practice,
1606   we draw a multinomial qi ∼ Dir(αp) from a Dirichlet distribution, where p describes a prior class
1607   distribution over N classes, and α controls the heterogeneity among all clients: the greater α the
1608
       more homogeneous the clients’ data distributions will be. After drawing the class distributions qi , for
       every client i, we sample training examples for each class according to qi without replacement.
1609
1610   In the main paper, we considered only two levels of heterogeneity: the first uses α = 0 and is used to
1611   simulate a pathological non-iid scenario, while the second uses α = 10k and corresponds to having
1612   homogeneous local datasets. To further investigate the impact of heterogeneity, we provide the results
1613   for different values of α in section C.6 of this supplementary.

1804   C.8                  GHBM AS SERVER - SIDE MOMENTUM
1805
1806
       In this section, we show a comparison between GHBM applied at the client side, as proposed
       in this work, and its use as a server-side optimizer. In the latter case, clients simply run vanilla
1807
       SGD locally, their gradients are then aggregated and the resulting server pseudo-gradient is used
1808
       to update the momentum term and the global model. The momentum update rule itself remains
1809   unchanged, i.e. follows eq. (7), the only difference is that momentum is applied server-side, similarly
1810   to F EDAVG M (Hsu et al., 2019) and accordingly to the F ED O PT framework (Reddi et al., 2021). As
1811   shown in Fig. 8, server-side GHBM shows surprisingly good results, very close to client-side GHBM,
1812   but only with one of the architectures adopted in this study. This underscores that, if clients have not
1813   irremediably drifted during the local update, then server-side GHBM can be highly effective, even
1814   in the most heterogeneous settings. However, these findings are not consistent across architectures;
1815   indeed server-side GHBM does not show the same improvement when the experiment is carried
1816   out on a simpler CNN. In summary, these results confirm the findings in previous work about the
1817
       necessity of integrating drift correction locally at clients (Karimireddy et al., 2020; 2021).

1837   In this section, we conduct an experimental verification that F ED CM in partial participation, even
1838   when enforcing cyclic participation (see assumption 4.3), does not behave like GHBM, i.e. the
1839   algorithms are not equivalent.
1840   We propose to analyze a theoretical setting where a federation of K = 10 clients with non-iid
1841   local datasets, collaboratively learns a quadratic function. In the experiment, K · C = 2 clients are
1842
       cyclically selected at each round. We compare, F ED CM (equivalent to GHBM with τ = 1) and
       GHBM using the theoretical optimum τ = 1/C = 5. As it is possible to notice from the training
1843
