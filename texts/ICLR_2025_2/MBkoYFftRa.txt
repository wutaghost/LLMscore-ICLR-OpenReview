034                                              A BSTRACT
035
036           We propose Inner Loop Feedback (ILF), a novel approach to accelerate diffu-
037           sion models’ inference. ILF trains a lightweight module to predict future features
038           in the denoising process by leveraging the outputs from a chosen diffusion back-
039           bone block at a given time step. This approach exploits two key intuitions; (1)
040           the outputs of a given block at adjacent time steps are similar, and (2) performing
041
              partial computations for a step imposes a lower burden on the model than skipping
              the step entirely. Our method is highly flexible, since we find that the feedback
042
              module itself can simply be a block from the diffusion backbone, with all set-
043
              tings copied. Its influence on the diffusion forward can be tempered with a learn-
044           able scaling factor from zero initialization. We train this module using distillation
045           losses; however, unlike some prior work where a full diffusion backbone serves as
046           the student, our model freezes the backbone, training only the feedback module.
047           While many efforts to optimize diffusion models focus on achieving acceptable
048           image quality in extremely few steps (1-4 steps), our emphasis is on matching
049           best case results (typically achieved in 20 steps) while significantly reducing run-
050           time. ILF achieves this balance effectively, demonstrating strong performance
051           for both class-to-image generation with diffusion transformer (DiT) and text-to-
052
              image generation with DiT-based PixArt-alpha and PixArt-sigma. The quality of
              ILF’s 1.7x-1.8x speedups are confirmed by FID, CLIP score, CLIP Image Quality
053
              Assessment, ImageReward, and qualitative comparisons.

073   1   I NTRODUCTION
074
075   Since its introduction as an alternative to generative adversarial networks (GANs) Goodfellow et al.
076   (2014) for image synthesis Dhariwal & Nichol (2021), diffusion has been one of the most prominent
077   methods for generative tasks. These methods deliver stable training, high quality generations, and
078   easy alignment to a variety of conditions for generative tasks Yang et al. (2024). However, the actual
079   generation process is quite expensive. While GANs generate images in a single model forward pass,
080   diffusion models require many model forward passes to iteratively progress from random noise to
081
      clean images. As a result, many researchers have focused on trying to improve the efficiency of
      diffusion models, while retaining the quality. Some of these are training-free, focusing on caching
082
      features for cheaper inference; others involve expensive distillation to dedicated-purpose few step
083
      models.
084
085   We propose inner loop feedback (ILF) for diffusion models, seeking to achieve higher quality at
086   better efficiency than caching, as shown in Figure 1, without the training cost and inflexibility of
087
      distillation-based approaches. With this approach, we can take any frozen pre-trained transformer-
      based diffusion model, and make each of its steps more powerful by training a new block, the
088
      feedback module, to take features from a block b at one step, t, and predict features for prior blocks
089
      b − 1, b − 2, ..., b − l corresponding to the next step, t − r, as shown in Figure 2. In contrast to
090   these distillation-based methods that seek to learn models that can achieve reasonable quality with
091   4 or fewer inference steps, we focus instead on matching or surpassing the best-case performance
092   of the original model, in less time. Furthermore, with our approach, one does not need to store an
093   entire additional set of models weights, instead only those corresponding to the lightweight learnable
094   module.
095
      From caching literature focused on the U-Net architecture, we already know that features for these
096   U-Net diffusion models are very similar for a given block, b, at adjacent time steps, t and t − r Ma
097   et al. (2023); Wimbauer et al. (2024). We find this remains consistent for transformer-based diffusion
098   models, in Figure 3, with an implementation described in Section 2.2. Caching methods leverage
099   this concept to skip computation for certain blocks at certain steps, simply re-using the prior features.
100   However, as Figure 3 also shows, leveraging these similar adjacent features changes the make-up of
101   the features themselves.
102   The caching schedule itself is noticeable; since features are cached on every other step, instead of
103   smooth change over blocks and time, feature pairs become noticeable on the time axis. Furthermore,
104   except for the last steps, the final model outputs become less distinct between neighboring steps. The
105   same phenomenon manifests when we consider how the features evolve across blocks (Figure 4);
106   once we introduce caching, features of the last block do not become as dissimilar to the first step
107   for the steps on which the caching occurs. This is clearly not optimal, and results in poorer quality
      images, as shown in Figure 5. Caching often loses key details, and produces blurrier, less appealing

127   generations. This motivates our inner loop feedback; we want to take advantage of the redundancy
128   between blocks across time, without compromising the quality of the final images.
129   For flexibility of adaptation to novel architectures, we propose leveraging the model architecture
130   itself to design the feedback module. The module consists of a single block, copied from the block
131   design of the corresponding pre-trained diffusion transformer backbone. That is, for the basic DiT,
132   our feedback module is a single DiT block. This way, our feedback handles conditions, input sizes,
133   and output sizes in the same way as the base model.
134   Different diffusion models have different learned weight values, and training quickly, without over-
135   fitting, is quite challenging. To allow for fast training we use distillation. However, unlike prior
136   works, we do not have a separate teacher and student. Instead, the model without feedback is the
137   teacher, and the model with feedback is the student – but only the feedback is learnable. Further-
138   more, while prior works perform multiple teacher iterations, which is expensive, we propose an
139   approach that allows us to only use one. We find this Fast Approximate Distillation works equally
140   well, at lower cost. To avoid over-fitting, we add Learnable Feedback Rescaling, where we learn
141   an integer scaling term on the feedback before we add it to the features in the pretrained model.
142
      Initializing this to zero allows the model to learn quickly from the distillation, without diverging due
      to excessively large error. Neither Fast Approximate Distillation nor Learnable Feedback Rescaling
143
      require any hand-tuned hyperparameters, allowing for easy implementation and extension to other
144
      models and data.
145
146   One challenge introduced by our approach is that since we attempt to predict features corresponding
147   to future steps, it is not immediately clear how to set the noise steps for the backbone and sched-
148
      uler. While we find that using default schedulers works well, this does not hold true for the model
      backbone itself, where we must use a rescaled step for the time conditioning at inference time.
149
      Furthermore, we find that for larger inner loops, it is often best to skip feedback on some steps
150
      altogether. We instantiate these findings collectively as Feedback-aware Inference Scheduling.
151
152   In summary, we achieve high quality efficient generation with the following contributions:
153
154          • We propose diffusion with Inner Loop Feedback (ILF), a feedback mechanism which cre-
155
               ates a powerful inner loop within transformer-based diffusion backbones for optimal time-
               quality trade-offs.
156
157          • We develop Learnable Feedback Rescaling and Fast Approximate Distillation for speedy
158            training, and Feedback-aware Inference Scheduling to adaptively leverage the power and
159
               speed of ILF at inference time.
160          • We achieve superior results to caching for 1.7x-1.8 speedups compared with the baseline
161            model, with an average +7.9 improvement for Image Reward, +0.14 CLIP, -0.22 MJHQ
               FID, and +1.4 CLIP IQA Score.

177

182   et al. (2022); Blattmann et al. (2023); Liu et al. (2024b). These models can be conditioned on
183   text encodings Saharia et al. (2022); Ramesh et al. (2022); Nichol et al. (2022); Ruiz et al. (2023);
184   Podell et al. (2023) from powerful models including CLIP Radford et al. (2021) and T5 Raffel et al.
185   (2023). Recently, to allow for more flexible scaling, transformer-based diffusion models Peebles &
186   Xie (2023); Bao et al. (2023) have become the predominant architecture for state-of-the-art diffu-
187
      sion models Chen et al. (2023; 2024b;a); Esser et al. (2024); Liu et al. (2024b). Due to the recent
      trend towards diffusion transformers, we choose to focus our work primarily on this family of ar-
188
      chitectures, including the original DiT Peebles & Xie (2023) for class-to-image generation, and

193
      as forward and reverse noising Song et al. (2022); Karras et al. (2022); Lu et al. (2023). Recent

      existing inference schedulers.

      2.2   FASTER D IFFUSION I NFERENCE
198
199   A significant body of work focuses on speeding up diffusion inference to generate good images
200   in fewer steps. The majority of these approaches can be divided between training-free caching
201   approaches, and training-based distillation approaches.
202
      Caching. Prior caching work focuses mainly on U-Net architectures. While the methods share a key
203
      intuition, that features are similar at adjacent time steps, their configurations differ. Some cache only
204   encoder features Li et al. (2023), others cache outer layers of both encoder and decoder Ma et al.
205   (2023), and others automatically discover ways to cache a variety of layers Wimbauer et al. (2024).
206   Concurrent work has started to approach caching for DiTs Selvaraju et al. (2024); Liu et al. (2024a),
207   including some work which focuses on learnable routing for the caching Ma et al. (2024). One major
208   difference between U-Nets and DiTs is the absence of encoder-decoder distinction, which changes
209   the caching approach substantially. Furthermore, many of these approaches focus on generation
210   of lower resolution images, using class conditioning, with many time steps. By contrast, more
211   modern text-to-image models use fewer steps. With fewer steps, the feature maps change much less
212
      smoothly over time, mitigating the suitability of caching; furthermore, errors and blurriness become
      even more glaring at higher resolution (see Figure 5 for examples). Nevertheless, we implement
213
      caching as a point of comparison for ILF.
214
215   For this caching, we store the attention and feedforward results for each cacheable block on the
      first step. Then, at subsequent steps, for all cacheable blocks, we only recompute attentions and

250   3.1   I NNER L OOP F EEDBACK D ESIGN
251
252   We propose a lightweight learnable module that leverages similar intuitions to caching, but with
253   a different mechanism and superior results. This method, illustrated in Figure 2, starts with some
254   pre-trained, transformer-based diffuion model. Standard diffusion forward passes attempt to predict
255   ϵ̂(xt , t), for some noised latent xt and time step t. By contrast, with our feedback mechanism, we
256   attempt to make the forward pass more powerful, where we instead predict ϵ̂(xt−r , t − r), where r is
257
      some positive integer, meaning t − r is some subsequent time step. This allows us to generate high
      quality images with fewer, but more powerful, inference steps.
258
259   We design the feedback module itself by simply copying the architecture of the model blocks them-
260   selves, such that for a standard N block DiT, we introduce a (N + 1)th block. However, instead of
261   simply appending, prepending, or inserting the block, we dramatically alter the flow of information.
262   We first set a location for the inner loop, denoted by the beginning (b) block, Bb , and the ending
263
      (e) block Be . For some time step t, the feedback module takes as its input, the output of Be , fe,t ,
      along with the embedded time and text conditions. The feedback model gives its output, ffeed . We
264
      then rescale the ffeed for separately for each block in the inner loop, {Bb , Bb+1 , ..., Be }, by multi-
265
      plying each by its corresponding learnable floating point scaling factor, {sb , sb+1 , ..., se }. For the
266   first block, Bb , we compute its result as

      3.2   T RAINING I NNER L OOP F EEDBACK
271
272   One cannot train this feedback mechanism with basic random initialization; the magnitude of the
273   feedback will be too large, and the training will diverge. Furthermore, standard training is needlessly
274   slow. To keep the training stable and time-efficient, we leverage both novel Learnable Feedback
275   Rescaling as well as Fast Approximate Distillation.
276   Learnable Feedback Rescaling. As mentioned in Section 3.1, we rescale the feedback outputs,
277   ffeed with some learnable scalar si for each block in the inner loop. This simple multiplication
278   operation is cheap, and allows us to use a single feedback computation to improve the features used
279   by all blocks within the inner loop. Furthermore, by zero-initializing and learning s we are able to
280   avoid needing to set any hard-coded hyperparameters.
281
      Fast Approximate Distillation Standard diffusion models train with a reconstruction loss. We use
282   this same loss, and a novel pseudo-self-distillation loss between the output of diffusion with ILF
283   (student) and diffusion without ILF (teacher). To align with our objective to predict future noise
284   outputs, we perform the distillation using less noisy images. Specifically, whenever our ILF input
285   during training is noised to step t, we noise the teacher input to step t/2. We then compute standard
286   mean squared error loss between their predictions. Note that while we refer to diffusion with ILF
287   as the student, only the feedback module and the rescaling parameters are learnable. This novel
288   formulation saves on training cost.
289
290   3.3   F EEDBACK - AWARE I NFERENCE S CHEDULING
291
292
      With ILF, we are training the feedback to take inputs for one time step t, and produce predictions
      for a future time step t − r. However, in practice, we still need to denoise the actual original input,
293
      xt . Treating this as if it were a more clean input, xt-r , and removing the corresponding amount of
294
      noise, would be counterproductive. So, we still use the sigmas corresponding to t rather than t − r
295   for the backwards diffusion process itself. Thus, our method is akin to conditioning the model to
296   generate a more reliable noise prediction, which can be used reliably for more spacing diffusion
297   (fewer inference steps).
298
      However, time step is not only used for the noise subtraction process. Rather, it is also a condition
299
      for the diffusion backbone itself. Hence, we must change the time condition used for all computation
300
      in the each forward pass that occurs after the feedback module forward. For the subsequent compu-
301   tation, we find that using an intermediate step, weighted for the size of the inner loop, is appropriate.
302   So, for an inner loop with m blocks in a model comprised of n total blocks, and a scheduler with
303   consecutive time steps t and t − i, we compute the intermediate post-feedback time step, tpost , with
304
305
                                               tpost = t − i ∗ (n/m)                                       (4)

308   tpost = t − i/2. We also observe that as we continue to train the feedback mechanism, it will “over-

310   find we can take advantage of it. First, we modify our rescaling to anneal over time. For t = 999,

475     5                 C ONCLUSION
476
477     We propose diffusion with Inner Loop Feedback (ILF), which lets us perform diffusion inference
478
        with fewer, more powerful inference steps. As a result, we can leverage pre-trained diffusion mod-
        els to generate high-quality images in less time. With Learnable Feedback Rescaling and Fast Ap-
479
        proximate Distillation, we are able to train feedback for efficient megapixel image generation in
480
        approximately 100 GPU hours. Our method outperforms the training-free caching baseline, and is
481     substantially cheaper and more flexible than any distillation-based alternative. Future work could
482     explore our method as a way to cheaply finetune a diffusion model on new data, as well as try to
483     achieve better performance at extremely low steps. Additionally, with some adjustments (accounting
484     for encoder-decoder skip connections), our work could be adapted for U-Nets, though we consider
485     this out of scope due to the rising popularity of DiTs.
