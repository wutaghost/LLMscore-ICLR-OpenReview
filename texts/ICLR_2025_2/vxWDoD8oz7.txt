010                                               A BSTRACT
011
012
               Embedding tree-like data, from hierarchies to ontologies and taxonomies, forms
               a well-studied problem for representing knowledge across many domains. Hyper-
013
               bolic geometry provides a natural solution for embedding trees, with vastly su-
014
               perior performance over Euclidean embeddings. Recent literature has shown that
015            hyperbolic tree embeddings can even be placed on top of neural networks for hi-
016            erarchical knowledge integration in deep learning settings. For all applications, a
017            faithful embedding of trees is needed, with combinatorial constructions emerging
018            as the most effective direction. This paper identifies and solves two key limitations
019            of existing works. First, the combinatorial construction hinges on finding maxi-
020            mally separated points on a hypersphere, a notoriously difficult problem. Current
021            approaches lead to poor separation, which degrades the quality of the correspond-
022            ing hyperbolic embedding. As a solution, we propose maximally separated De-
023
               launay tree embeddings (MS-DTE), where during placement, the children of a
               node are maximally separated through optimization, which directly leads to lower
024
               embedding distortion. Second, low-distortion requires additional precision. The
025
               current approach for increasing precision is to use multiple precision arithmetic,
026            which renders the embeddings useless on GPUs in deep learning settings. We
027            reformulate the combinatorial construction using floating point expansion arith-
028            metic, leading to superior embedding quality while simultaneously retaining their
029            use on accelerated hardware.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
      Tree-like structures such as hierarchies are key for knowledge representation, from biological tax-
034
      onomies (Padial et al., 2010) and phylogenetics (Kapli et al., 2020) to natural language (Miller, 1995;
035
      Tifrea et al., 2018; Yang et al., 2016), social networks (Freeman, 2004), visual understanding (Desai
036   et al., 2023) and more. To obtain faithful embeddings of trees, Euclidean space is ill-equiped; even
037   simple trees lead to high distortion (Sonthalia & Gilbert, 2020). On the other hand, the exponential
038   nature of hyperbolic space makes it a natural geometry for embedding trees (Nickel & Kiela, 2018).
039   This insight has resulted in rapid advances in hyperbolic machine learning, with vastly superior
040   embedding (Sala et al., 2018) and clustering (Chami et al., 2020) of tree-like data.
041
      Recent literature has shown that hyperbolic tree embeddings are not only useful on their own, they
042   also form powerful target embeddings on top of deep networks to unlock hierarchical representation
043   learning (Peng et al., 2021; Mettes et al., 2024). Deep learning with hyperbolic tree embeddings
044   has made it possible to effectively perform action recognition (Long et al., 2020), knowledge graph
045   completion (Kolyvakis et al., 2020), hypernymy detection (Tifrea et al., 2018) and many other tasks
046   in hyperbolic space. These early adoptions of hyperbolic embeddings have shown a glimpse of the
047   powerful improvements that hierarchically aligned representations can bring to deep learning.
048   The rapid advances in hyperbolic deep learning underline the need for hyperbolic tree embeddings
049   compatible with GPU accelerated software. Current tree embedding algorithms can roughly be
050   divided in two categories; optimization-based and constructive methods. The optimization-based
051   methods, e.g. Poincaré embeddings (Nickel & Kiela, 2017), hyperbolic entailment cones (Ganea
052   et al., 2018), and distortion optimization (Yu et al., 2022b), train embeddings using some objective
053   function based on the tree. While these approaches are flexible due to minimal assumptions, the
      optimization can be unstable, slow and result in heavily distorted embeddings. On the other hand,


                                                        1

054
      the constructive methods traverse a tree once, placing the children of each node on a hypersphere
055   around the node’s embedding (Sarkar, 2011; Sala et al., 2018). These methods are fast, require no
056   hyperparameter tuning and have great error guarantees. However, they rely on hyperspherical sep-
057   aration, a notoriously difficult problem (Saff & Kuijlaars, 1997), and on multiple precision floating
058   point arithmetic, which is incompatible with GPUs and other accelerated hardware.
059
      Our goal is to embed tree-like structures with minimal distortion yet with the ability to operate on
060
      accelerated GPU hardware even when using higher precision. We do so in two steps. First, we
061   outline MS-DTE, a new generalization of Delaunay tree embeddings (Sarkar, 2011) to arbitrary di-
062   mensionality through hyperspherical energy minimization. Second, we propose HypFPE, a floating
063   point expansion arithmetic approach to enhance our constructive hyperbolic tree embeddings. We
064   develop new routines for computing hyperbolic distances on floating point expansions and outline
065   how to use these on hyperbolic embeddings. Furthermore, we provide theoretical results demonstrat-
066   ing the effectiveness of these floating point expansion routines. Floating point expansions allow for
067   higher precision similar to multiple precision arithmetic. However, our routines can be implemented
068   using standard floating point operations, making these compatible with GPUs. Experiments demon-
069
      strate that MS-DTE generates higher fidelity embeddings than other hyperbolic tree embeddings and
      that HypFPE can be used to further increase the embedding quality for MS-DTE and other methods.
070
      We will make two software libraries publicly available, one for arbitrary-dimensional hyperbolic
071
      tree embeddings and one for GPU-compatible floating point expansions.

117   2.2   R ELATED WORK
118
      Hyperbolic tree embedding algorithms. Existing embedding methods can be divided into two
119
      categories: optimization-based methods and constructive methods. The optimization methods typ-
120
      ically use the tree to define some loss function and use a stochastic optimization method such as
121   SGD to directly optimize the embedding of each node, e.g. Poincaré embeddings (Nickel & Kiela,
122   2017), hyperbolic entailment cones (Ganea et al., 2018) and distortion optimization (Sala et al.,
123   2018; Yu et al., 2022b). Poincaré embeddings use a contrastive loss where related nodes are pulled
124   together and unrelated nodes are pushed apart. Hyperbolic entailment cones attach an outwards ra-
125   diating cone to each node embedding and define a loss that forces children of nodes into the cone
126   of their parent. Distortion optimization directly optimizes for a distortion loss to embed node pairs.
127   Such approaches are flexible, but do not lead to arbitrarily low distortion and optimization is slow.
128   Constructive methods are either combinatorial methods (Sarkar, 2011; Sala et al., 2018) or eigende-
129
      composition methods (Sala et al., 2018). Combinatorial methods first place the root of a tree at the
      origin of the hyperbolic space and then traverse down the tree, iteratively placing nodes as uniformly
130
      as possible on a hypersphere around their parent. (Sarkar, 2011) proposes a 2-dimensional approach,
131
      where the points have to be separated on a circle; a trivial task. For higher dimensions, (Sala et al.,
132   2018) place points on a hypercube inscribed within a hypersphere, which leads to suboptimal dis-
133   tribution. We also follow a constructive approach, where we use an optimization method for the
134   hyperspherical separation, leading to significantly higher quality embeddings. The eigendecompo-
135   sition method h-MDS (Sala et al., 2018) takes a graph or tree metric and uses an eigendecomposition
136   of this matrix to generate low-distortion embeddings. However, it collapses nodes within some sub-
137   trees to a single point, leading to massive local distortion.
138
139   Deep learning with hyperbolic tree embeddings. In computer vision, a wide range of works have
140   recently shown the potential and effectiveness of using a hyperbolic embedding space (Khrulkov
141
      et al., 2020). Specifically, hierarchical prior knowledge can be embedded in hyperbolic space, after
      which visual representations can be mapped to the same space and optimized to match this hierarchi-
142
      cal organization. (Long et al., 2020) show that such a setup improves hierarchical action recognition,
143
      while (Liu et al., 2020) use hierarchies with hyperbolic embeddings for zero-shot learning. Deep
144   visual learning with hyperbolic tree embeddings has furthermore shown to improve image segmen-
145   tation (Ghadimi Atigh et al., 2022), skin lesion recognition (Yu et al., 2022b), video understanding
146   (Li et al., 2024), hierarchical visual recognition (Ghadimi Atigh et al., 2021; Dhall et al., 2020),
147   hierarchical model interpretation (Gulshad et al., 2023), open set recognition (Dengxiong & Kong,
148   2023), continual learning (Gao et al., 2023), few-shot learning (Zhang et al., 2022), and more. Since
149   such approaches require freedom in terms of embedding dimensionality, they commonly rely on
150   optimization-based approaches to embed the prior tree-like knowledge. Similar approaches have
151   also been investigated in other domains, from audio (Petermann et al., 2023) and text (Dhingra
152
      et al., 2018; Le et al., 2019) to multimodal settings (Hong et al., 2023). In this work, we provide a
      general-purpose and unconstrained approach for low-distortion embeddings with the option to scale
153
      to higher precisions without losing GPU-compatibility.
154

      Floating point expansions. The use of floating point expansions (FPEs) to increase precision in
156   hyperbolic space was recently proposed by (Yu & De Sa, 2021) and implemented in a PyTorch li-
157   brary (Yu et al., 2022a). However, their methodology is based on older FPE arithmetic definitions
158   and routines by (Priest, 1991; 1992; Richard Shewchuk, 1997). In the field of FPEs, more effi-
159   cient and stable formulations have been proposed over the years with improved error guarantees
160   (Joldes et al., 2014; 2015; Muller et al., 2016). In this paper, we build upon the most recent arith-
161   metic framework detailed in (Popescu, 2017). We have implemented this framework for PyTorch
      and extend its functionality to work with hyperbolic embeddings.

284   While hyperbolic space enjoys numerous potential benefits, it is prone to numerical error when
285   using floating point arithmetic. Especially as points move away from the origin, floating point
286   arithmetic struggles to accurately represent or perform computations with these points. For larger
287   values of τ or maximal path lengths ℓ, the embeddings generated by the construction often end
288   up in this problematic region of the Poincaré ball. As such, the precision required for hyperbolic
289   embeddings is often larger than the precision provided by the floating point formats supported on
290   GPUs. Increased precision can be attained by switching to arbitrary precision arithmetic. However,
291
      this makes the result incompatible with existing deep learning libraries.
292   Here, we propose HypFPE, a method to increase the precision of constructive hyperbolic approaches
293   through floating point expansion (FPE) arithmetic. In this framework, numbers are represented as
294   unevaluated sums of floating point numbers, typically of a fixed number of bits b. In other words, a
295   number f ∈ R is represented by a floating point expansion f˜ as
296                                                          t

301   terms that the floating point expansion f˜ consists of. Each term f˜i is in the form of a GPU supported
302   float format, such as float16, float32 or float64. Moreover, to ensure that this representation is unique
303   and uses bits efficiently, it is constrained to be ulp-nonoverlapping (Popescu, 2017).

      floating point arithmetic. Moreover, Proposition 1 shows that the effective radius of the Poincaré ball
351

352   of our FPE expansions. Therefore, this effective radius increases linearly with the number of bits.
353   The same holds when using arbitrary precision floating point arithmetic, so FPE expansions require

462   dimensions 4, 7 and 20 are shown in Appendix I. These illustrate the strength of the combinatorial
463   constructions. The optimization methods PE, HEC and DO perform relatively poor for all evaluation
464   metrics. This performance could be increased through hyperparameter tuning and longer training.
465   However, the results will not come close to those of the other methods. The h-MDS method performs
466   well in terms of Dave , but very poorly on Dwc and MAP. This is because h-MDS collapses leaf
467
      nodes, leading to massive local distortion within the affected subtrees. However, between subtrees
      this distortion is much smaller, explaining the low Dave . Figure 2 illustrates the issue with-h-MDS
468
      and the superiority of our approach. Each of the white squares in the h-MDS plot corresponds to a
469
      collapsed subtree, which renders the embeddings unusable for downstream tasks since nearby leaf
470   nodes cannot be distinguished. We conclude that MS-DTE obtains the strongest embeddings overall.

531
532
      In this paper we introduce MS-DTE, a novel way of constructively embedding trees in hyperbolic
533
      space, which uses an optimization approach to maximally separate points on a hypersphere. Empir-
534   ically, we show that MS-DTE outperforms existing methods, while maintaining the computational
535   efficiency of the combinatorial approaches. Additionally, we introduce HypFPE, a framework for
536   floating point expansion arithmetic on tensors, which is adapted to extend the effective radius of
537   the Poincaré ball. This framework can be used to increase the precision of computations, without
538   losing the benefit of hardware acceleration, paving the way for highly accurate hyperbolic neural
539   networks. It can be added on top of any of the combinatorial methods, leading to low-distortion and
      GPU-compatible hyperbolic tree embeddings.

811   each vertex v has ||v||∞ = 1, each vertex is of the form (±1, . . . , ±1)T , so we can represent v as
812   some binary sequence s. The distance between two such vertices can then be expressed in terms of
