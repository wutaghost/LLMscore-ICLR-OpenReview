011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013
               How neural networks process overlapping input patterns is a fundamental ques-
               tion in both neuroscience and artificial intelligence. Traditionally, overlaps in
014
               neural activity are viewed as interference, requiring separation for better perfor-
015
               mance. However, an alternative perspective suggests that these overlaps may en-
016            code meaningful semantic relationships between concepts. In this paper, we pro-
017            pose a framework where persistent overlap between episodic patterns represent
018            semantic components across episodic experiences, and the statistics of these over-
019            laps how each semantic concept relates to others.
020            To explore this idea, we introduce an Episode Generation Protocol (EGP) that
021            defines a mapping between the semantic structure of episodes and input pattern
022            generation. Paired with our EGP, we use Homeostatic Binary Networks (HBNs),
023            a simplified yet biologically-inspired model incorporating key features such as ad-
024
               justable inhibition, Hebbian learning, and homeostatic plasticity.
               Our contributions are threefold: (1) We formalize a link between episodic seman-
025
               tics and neural patterns through our EGP. This EGP can be used for systematic
026
               study of semantic learning in artificial neural networks. (2) We introduce HBNs
027            as an analytically tractable network that extracts semantic structure in its internal
028            model (3) We show that HBNs align their performance with Maximum A Posteri-
029            ori and Maximum Likelihood Estimation strategies depending on the homeostatic
030            regime. Similarly, we provide an example of how our EGP can be used as an
031            experimental protocol in neuroscience to make different models of learning com-
032            pete.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036
      Understanding how neural networks learn and process overlapping patterns is a central question in
037   neuroscience and artificial intelligence (AI) (O’Reilly, 2000). Overlapping patterns are many times
038   viewed as sources of interference or noise, which need to be separated in order to avoid a degraded
039   performance (French, 1999; Goodfellow et al., 2013). Another view is that overlaps might not only
040   represent interference but also encode meaningful semantic relationships. In particular, overlaps
041   are proposed to encode the similarities between the concepts represented by different patterns
042   (De Falco et al., 2016; Gastaldi et al., 2021; Gastaldi & Gerstner, 2024) (see Fig. 1A). However,
043   this outlook typically assumes that each pattern separately represents a concept, and that the overlap
044   is a consequence of the concepts being semantically related. A different interpretation, which we
045
      explore here, is that activity patterns correspond to the full content of episodes, and the overlaps
      between patterns represent common concepts in the episodes encoded by both patterns (Fig. 1B).
046
      Understanding the meaning of these overlaps is tightly related to the notion of semantics. In this
047
      sense, the interplay between semantics and learning has puzzled researchers in AI and cognitive
048   sciences for decades, with a recovered popularity in the relatively recent years (Saxe et al., 2019;
049   Chrysanthidis et al., 2022; Ben-Shaul et al., 2023; Ravichandran et al., 2024).
050
051   In order to study how semantics impact learning, we propose an Episode Generation Proto-
052   col (EGP), which samples input with a well-defined semantic structure. Paired with this EGP, we
053   introduce Homeostatic Binary Networks (HBNs), a minimal model that incorporates biologically-
      inspired elements such as adjustable inhibition, Hebbian learning, and homeostatic plasticity. Due

054
      to their simplicity, for certain regimes, one can obtain the closed-form trajectories of semantically-
055   labeled populations of weights in these networks.
056
057   The main contributions of this work are:
058
059          • Proposing an Episode Generation Protocol (EGP) with an associated Semantic Structure,
060            which allows testing semantic learning in artificial neural networks.
061          • Presenting Homeostatic Binary Networks (HBNs). This simplification of biologically-
062            inspired neural networks allows a direct link between a formally defined Semantic Structure
063            and its learning dynamics.
064          • Using the EGP to obtain different behavioural signatures of a plethora of models used in
065            neuroscience, relating them to responses in prototype learning and masked input prediction.

069   2.1   E PISODE G ENERATION P ROTOCOL (EGP) FOR S EMANTIC L EARNING
070
071   To test neural networks’ ability to extract semantic relationships from inputs, we propose an Episode
072   Generation Protocol (EGP). This framework connects episodic patterns to semantic structure, en-
073
      abling systematic testing of learning systems. The EGP defines episodes as combinations of con-
      cepts (e.g., Italy and pizza) across attributes (e.g., place and food) (Fig. 1C, left), with sampling
074
      probabilities specifying the stochastic relationships between concepts (Fig. 1C, center).
075
076   Each episode maps to an input pattern where concepts activate subsets of neurons, forming a list of
077   K One-Hot encodings for each attribute. Having multiple neurons coding for each concept allows
078   further noise injection without totally erasing the original meaning. Here, variability is introduced
079
      by randomly choosing Nswap /2 active neurons, and Nswap /2 inactive neurons, and flipping their
      activity. This results in a total of Nswap neurons stochastically changing their original state, but
080
      overall preserving the total number of active neurons. The process episode −→ input pattern −→
081
      noise defines the episode-input mapping of the EGP (Fig. 1C, right), which could however take
082   alternative forms to the one proposed here.
083
084   The semantic structure of the EGP is formalized as a matrix SS, where each entry SSij represents
085
      the conditional probability of episode concept j given episode concept i (Fig. 1E). For example,
      in an EGP where Italy only appears with pizza, the probability of pizza given Italy is 1, while Italy
086
      given pizza is 0.5 due to the shared occurrence of pizza with France (Fig. 1D). This structure encodes
087
      asymmetries and causal relationships between concepts, extending beyond standard methods such as
088   Representational Similarity Analysis (Kriegeskorte et al., 2008; Schapiro et al., 2017a) or Point-wise
089   Mutual Information (Fano, 1968; De Falco et al., 2016).
090
091
      Importantly, the EGP generalizes (and formalizes the semantic structure of) previously used over-
      lapping input protocols, providing a general framework for testing semantic learning. In Appendix
092
      A.3, we re-frame prior datasets (Schapiro et al., 2017b; Singh et al., 2022; Fung & Fukai, 2023)
093
      within this protocol, highlighting its applicability to formalize existing experiments.

      2.2   S EMANTIC L EARNING IN R ECURRENT N EURAL N ETWORKS
096
097   We start examining how the semantic structure of an EGP influences the learned connectivity in
098   various recurrent network architectures. Recurrent neural networks (RNNs) are networks in which
099   every unit receives input from the rest, and are widely used in machine learning and neuroscience.
100   Here, we train three different types of RNNs: a Hopfield-Tsodyks network (Tsodyks & Feigel’man,
101   1988), a Boltzmann Machine (Ackley et al., 1985), and a feed-forward network (Appendix B) where
102   the output has the same size as the input. We show the connectivity both early and late in training
103   of the feed-forward network.
104   The networks are trained on episodes sampled from an EGP with a simple yet asymmetric semantic
105   structure (Fig. 2A-B). After training, synaptic strength is normalized between 0 and 1, (Fig. 2C).
106   For each network, we calculate the correlation coefficient rsem between this normalized connectivity
107   matrix and the semantic structure of the EGP (maximum value of cosine similarity considering both
      the original weight matrix and its transpose).

176   2.3   H OMEOSTATIC B INARY N ETWORKS (HBN S )
177
      Building on the previous results, we propose a biologically-constrained recurrent neural network
178
      (RNN) designed to extract episode semantics without the need for symmetric connections: the
179
      Homeostatic Binary Network (HBN). HBNs are inspired by classic models of Hebbian learn-
180   ing (Hopfield, 1982; Tsodyks & Feigel’man, 1988) and competitive learning (Rumelhart & Zipser,
181   1985). Additional homeostatic mechanisms, that here take the form of a generalization of winner-
182   take-all dynamics (using a top-K operation) and synaptic renormalization, are crucially included
183   to facilitate semantic learning. These homeostatic principles align with regularization methods in
184   artificial neural networks (Hofmann & Mäder, 2021), which constrain network activity and weight
185   distribution.
186
187   Network Architecture The network consists of two distinct regions (place and food; Fig. 3A),
188   corresponding to inputs representing different attributes. HBNs operate in two modes (Fig. 3A):
189
            • Input-Driven Mode: Input determines the activity, ignoring recurrent weights (equa-
190
              tion 3).
191
192         • Pattern Completion Mode: Recurrent weights drive network activity to recall previously
193
              learned patterns (equation 4 and equation 10)
194
195   Activity-Level Homeostasis. HBNs ensure homeostasis at the activity level through binary acti-
196
      vation and fixed sparsity constraints. After both input and recurrent processing (Fig. 3A; Eq. equa-
      tion 3 and equation 4), the network applies a top-K activation function (equation 5). This operation
197
      activates the K highest values in each region while suppressing the rest (Fig. 3B), mimicking the
198
      effect of adjustable inhibition observed in biological neural networks (Table 1, equation 6).
199
200

248   2.4     B EHAVIORAL S IGNATURES OF S EMANTIC L EARNING
249
250   Ultimately, one of the goals of HBNs and other networks studied here is to serve as models of the
251   nervous system. While we have shown that HBNs can encode semantic structures in their connec-
252   tions with a high degree of fidelity, this does not necessarily mean they mimic biological neural
253   networks. In this section, we study the behavioural signatures of HBNs and alternative models pre-
254
      viously used to describe learning in humans. To do so, we train different networks using our EGP,
      and then study network responses in two different tasks: (i) pattern completion of noisy input and
255
      (ii) pattern completion of semantically-masked input. In addition to the previous models, we also
256
      include here Modern Hopfield network (Ramsauer et al., 2020), which we did not study in section
257   2.2 due to the absence of explicit recurrent connections. In this sense, the results presented here
258   could be extended to any network that has been trained to recover/generate input patterns.
259
260
      One motivation of this section is characterizing how different types of network make predictions
      over out-of-distribution input. But, more importantly, this section also provides with a battery of
261
      model-specific predictions that could be tested using a similar protocol in human or animal subjects,
262
      for example using the matrix heatmap representation of input vectors as visual cues.

      3   D ISCUSSION
433
434
435   To study the impact of episode semantics in learning (semantic learning), we have proposed an
436   Episode Generation Protocol (EGP) -and a corresponding Semantic Structure. We have tested
437
      how different Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) are able to extract semantics into its recurrent
      weights. While there exists a non-negligible correlation between the semantic structure and the
438
      learned weights, none of the tested networks were able to extract semantics in full. We have pro-
439
      posed a model designed for this purpose (Homeostatic Binary Networks, HBNs). We show that
440   these outperform previously tested networks, with an almost perfect alignment with the semantic
441   structure of the EGP. Then, we explore behavioural signatures of different models typically used in
442   neuroscience. Results suggest that HBNs, in different regimes of synaptic homeostasis, are biased
443   to perform Maximum A Posteriori (MAP) or Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE). Similarly,
444   we propose that our EGP can be used in behavioural experiments with animals or humans to vali-
445   date/falsify the ability of the different networks to explain learning in biological agents.
446   Our EGP supposes a step forward in the study of semantic learning in artificial neural networks,
447   generalizing frameworks that assume associations between input and output (McClelland & Rogers,
448   2003; Saxe et al., 2019) and also moving beyond defining semantics as a mere segregation of con-
449   cepts (Ben-Shaul et al., 2023), which ignore the semantic web that these concepts form. In this
450   sense, we hope our EGP and Semantic Structure inspire future experiments in interpretability in
451   artificial intelligence , providing a framework to systematically study how neural networks capture
452   input statistics in its internal structure. While not in the main scope of this study, an example of the
453   interplay between learning via back-propagation of errors and semantic learning has been shown in
454
      ??. In this sense, we have shown how the semantic structure guides learning trajectories in initial
      learning phases even in a simple setup where a feed-forward network is trained to be an identity.
455
      Crucially, when this training was performed over masked input, the correlation between network
456
      and semantic structure was kept consistently high. This result aligns with recent studies report-
457   ing facts in Large Language Models being stored in the feed-forward connections of its transformer
458   blocks (Nanda et al., 2023), considering that next-word prediction can be seen as a formed of masked
459   self-supervised learning.
460
      HBNs build upon long-standing ideas like Hopfield networks (Hopfield, 1982) and competitive
461
      learning (Rumelhart & Zipser, 1985). One relatively novel aspect in HBNs is incorporating outgo-
462
      ing homeostasis, which has also recently been included in Fung & Fukai (2023). While their work
463   focuses on learning in feed-forward connections, competition for presynaptic resources (outgoing
464   homeostasis) was already found to help learning with overlapping patterns.
465
466
      The learning dynamics in HBNs are heavily impacted by the Semantic Structure of episodes,
      with final weights actually matching the Semantic Structure matrix when outgoing homeostasis
467
      dominates, and its transpose when incoming homeostasis dominates. While their performance
468
      in prototype learning is comparable to that of classic models of sparse storage, as can be the
469   Hopfield-Tsodyks (Tsodyks & Feigel’man, 1988) connectivity, HBNs can also capture asymmetric
470   semantic relationships. Furthermore, our results challenge the view that patterns with an overlap
471   over a certain threshold become indistinguishable (Gastaldi et al., 2021). This is crucially allowed
472   by adjustable synaptic inhibition (via our top-K activation function), without which one would get
473   representational collapse. Our study has implications in the reconciliation of error-free (Hebbian)
474   and error-driven (predictive) learning (Kumar, 2021; Zheng et al., 2022). In this sense, although
475   our network model is essentially Hebbian and does not use an explicit error signal, it is able to find
476   asymmetric predictive relations among its input and goes beyond purely associative learning, as
477
      shown by its ability to perform Maximum A Priori Estimation (MAP) and Maximum Likelihood
      Estimation (MLE).
478
479
      Our work can be placed in the context of Complementary Learning Systems (CLS) theory
480   (McClelland et al., 1995; O’Reilly et al., 2014), via both our Episode Generation Protocol and
481   Homeostatic Binary Networks. Our EGP can be used to formalize the semantic structure of standard
482   input generation protocols in the literature, adding one level of complexity to quasi-orthogonal
483   inputs typically used and adding the notion of semantic structure to those that already use highly
484   overlapping input patterns. As computational models of biological neural networks, HBNs, on
485   the other side, present several advantages over other models of semantization and/or systems
      consolidation: (i) HBNs are analytically tractable, (ii) HBNs do not use less biologically-plausible


                                                         9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      learning mechanisms such as back-propagation (Saxe et al., 2019) or Contrastive Hebbian Learning
487   (Singh et al., 2022), (iii) HBNs are computationally very inexpensive, which strikes compared to
488   other biologically-inspired architectures, many times using spiking networks (Remme et al., 2021;
489   Tomé et al., 2022; Chrysanthidis et al., 2022).
490
491   Our work also presents several limitations. In the case of the EGP, the episode-input map-
492   ping proposed here is, besides the variability introduced by neuronal activity swapping, the
493   simplest one could think of, making it essentially a one-hot encoding. Future work could study
494   what representations emerge in more complex episode-input mappings, for example introducing a
495
      linear-nonlinear relationship between episode concepts and input patterns. Another limitation here,
      where simplicity in the input has been prioritized in favour of a mechanistic yet intuitive study, is
496
      the absence of application to more complex problems to study learning in modern artificial neural
497
      networks. Future work could leverage on this limitation by exploring EGPs based on standard
498   benchmarks. An example of this would be imposing a specific semantic structure between pairs of
499   MNIST digits and then presenting a network with stacked images that follow these statistics. While
500   self-supervised learning (Bromley et al., 1993; Gui et al., 2024) has been shown to project input
501   into a latent manifold that is semantics-aware (Ben-Shaul et al., 2023), future work could explore if
502   it also captures semantic relationships between concepts (in this case right and left digit). Finally,
503   another limitation of the EGP in the form presented here, as a model of episodic input generation, is
504   that it does not take into account temporal correlations present in real-life episodes. Extending the
505   protocol in this sense could lead to better understanding how temporal aspects of semantics can be
506
      extracted in neural networks.
507   HBNs, while presenting multiple advantages, crucially depend on parameters such as K that here
508   where imposed to match known input structure and statistics. Understanding how K (which is
509   equivalent to the sparsity levels) can be meta-learned to optimize learning would be primordial to
510   asses its applicability both in AI and neuroscience. Additionally, the model and the theoretical
511   derivations included make several assumptions that also challenge its validity, such as the ability of
512
      the network to perfectly track input statistics and disconnect from recurrent weights during learning.
      While these are standard practices in computational models of biological learning (Clark & Abbott,
513
      2024), activity and learning dynamics are not perfectly decoupled in actual neural systems. Finally,
514
      connected to a limitation of the EGP previously highlighted, learning in HBNs does not take into
515   account temporal sequences. The ability to make predictions in time is crucial for episodic and
516   semantic memory, and extensions of the model to allow this, as done in Chaudhry et al. (2024)
517   would be crucial to obtain a model that aims at fully capture learning in biological agents.

      A.1   E PISODE G ENERATION P ROTOCOL
652
653   The Episode Generation Protocol (EGP) is a generative process that samples input neural activity.
654   Our proposed EGP is defined as follows:
655
656   E PISODES , EPISODE ATTRIBUTES AND EPISODE CONCEPTS
657
658   All episodes share a structure, such that every episode contains one episode concept per episode
659
      attribute. In this sense, if attributes are A, B, C..., an episode is constructed by selecting a concept
      a ∈ A, another b ∈ B, another c ∈ C... The set of all possible episodes can thus be defined as:
660
661                              E = {{a, b, c, . . . } | a ∈ A, b ∈ B, c ∈ C, . . . }                   (13)
662
      In other words, an episode e ∈ E is a collection of episode concepts a, b, c, ... such that concept a
663
      belongs to episode attribute A, concept b belongs to episode attribute B, etc.
664
665   In this study, for illustrative purposes, we assume that episodes are related to lunch experi-
666   ences, such that one attribute is place and another attribute is food. Episodes are therefore specified
667   by choosing pi ∈ place and fj ∈ f ood, resulting in episodes of the form
668
                                     E = {{pi , fj } | pi ∈ place, fj ∈ food}                            (14)

      E PISODE P ROBABILITY D ISTRIBUTION
671
672   As a generative process, our EGP samples episodes as a previous step to sampling an input pattern.
673   This is done by fixing a probability distribution over episodes. In our particular example of place
674   and food, one has to fix

676   such that                                            X

      After an episode (pi , fj ) has been sampled, the EGP returns an input pattern. Here, we assume
682
      the input vector can be split into a place and a food region. Then, for each region, we use a
683   list of K repeated One-Hot encodings, where the encoding represents which of all the concepts
684   corresponding to that attribute is present in the episode. Intuitively, each region corresponds to a
685   single attribute, and can be visualized as a matrix that contains as many rows as possible episode
686   concepts in that attribute, and K columns. All entries of the row that correspond to the concept
687   present in the episode are 1 and the rest are 0.

690   though they contain the same concepts, will be exactly the same), we further add some stochasticity
691
      by randomly picking Nswap /2 inactive neurons and Nswap /2 active neurons and inverting their
      activity (a total of Nswap neurons randomly change their activity). This ensures that activity sparsity
692
      in the sensory layer is maintained (the number of flips from 0 to 1 is the same as the number of flips
693
      from 1 to 0).

      A.2   S EMANTIC S TRUCTURE OF AN EGP
696
697   We refer to semantic field theory (Bussmann et al., 2006) in order to define what is the semantic
698   structure of an EGP. According to this school, the meaning of a word is not isolated but dependent
699   on its relation to the rest of the words. While our task is not one of language, we can use
700   this same paradigm to define the meaning of episode concepts. In this sense, the meaning of
701   our episode concepts depends on how they are related to the rest. Intuitively, even if a house
      is exactly the same for two dogs, the meaning of that house for each will be very different if it

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


702
      is always presented with food to one dog and always presented with an annoying whistle to the other.
703
704   In this light, we use the conditional probabilities of being present in an episode between
705   episode concepts
706
707

                          P (i ∈ e|j ∈ e) =                    ∀i, j ∈ A ∪ B ∪ C ∪ ...                 (17)
708                                              P (j ∈ e)
709   as a proxy for the semantic structure of an EGP, which we define as the matrix of these conditional
710   probabilities:
711                         semantic structure ≡ {SSij } ; SSij = P (i ∈ e|j ∈ e)                    (18)
712   In other words, extracting the semantics of an EGP is equivalent to: (i) identifying episode concepts,
713   and (ii) extracting how likely is one episode concept i to be present in an episode e if an episode
714   concept j is also present.
715
716   One detail that should be noted is that episode attributes are simply groups of concepts the
717   sub-matrices of which are diagonal (they never co-occur together), and one does not necessarily
718   have to define them explicitly (as we do with place and food), nor they have to exist for that matter
719   (it could happen that all pairs of concepts have a non-zero probability of co-occurrence, so each
720
      concept is its own episode attribute). However, here we use the notion of episode attributes to obtain
      simpler semantic structures the intuition of which can be grasped more easily (by enforcing many
721
      zeros in the matrix SS). Thus, while in practice the episode attributes are a property of the semantic
722
      structure and do not need to be specified a priori in the EGP, here we do so as a story-telling trick,
723   slightly sacrificing the generality of our EGP definition.
