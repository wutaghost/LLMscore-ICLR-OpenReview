009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Advancements in AI image generation, particularly diffusion models, have pro-
012            gressed rapidly. However, the absence of an established framework for quanti-
013            fying the reliability of AI-generated images hinders their use in critical decision-
014            making tasks, such as medical image diagnosis. In this study, we address the task
015            of detecting anomalous regions in medical images using diffusion models and pro-
016
               pose a statistical method to quantify the reliability of the detected anomalies. The
               core concept of our method involves a selective inference framework, wherein sta-
017
               tistical tests are conducted under the condition that the images are produced by a
018
               diffusion model. With our approach, the statistical significance of anomaly detec-
019            tion results can be quantified in the form of a p-value, enabling decision-making
020            with controlled error rates, as is standard in medical practice. We demonstrate
021            the theoretical soundness and practical effectiveness of our statistical test through
022            numerical experiments on both synthetic and brain image datasets.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027
      Advances in image generation AI, such as diffusion models, have been remarkable (Song & Ermon,
      2019). They can generate highly realistic and detailed images, which leads to innovations in various
028
      tasks across various fields. For example, image generation AI significantly enhances medical image
029
      diagnosis by improving accuracy and efficiency. It can generate highly detailed and enhanced images
030   from standard medical scan images, potentially offering doctors to detect anomalies and diseases
031   with greater precision. Furthermore, image generation AI can be used to create alternative versions
032   of medical images to consider what-if scenarios. For example, it can generate virtual images of a
033   patient when they are healthy, which allows for comparing the current actual images with the virtual
034   healthy images, making it possible to provide a diagnosis tailored to the individual patient.
035
      On the other hand, when using virtual images generated by AI for critical decision-making, such
036   as medical diagnosis, it is crucial to ensure the reliability of the decisions. Given that images are
037   generated by an AI algorithm, such as a deep learning model trained on historical data, they may
038   inherently contain biases and errors. Therefore, treating virtual synthetic images as equivalent to real
039   images in decision-making tasks carries the risk of biased and erroneous outcomes. When making
040   critical decisions based on generated images, it is necessary to be able to assess their reliability
041   by properly taking into account the fact that the images were generated by AI. However, to our
042   knowledge, there are no studies that can quantify the reliability of decision-making based on image
043   generation AI.
044   In this study, we address this challenge using the statistical hypothesis testing framework. We intro-
045   duce a statistical inference framework called selective inference (SI), which has gained attention over
046   the past decade in the statistics community as a novel approach for data-driven hypotheses (Taylor &
047   Tibshirani, 2015; Fithian et al., 2015; Lee & Taylor, 2014). In SI, statistical inference is performed
048   based on the sampling distribution of the test statistic under the condition that the hypothesis being
049   tested was selected based on the data. Our core idea is to formulate decision-making tasks involv-
050
      ing generated images as statistical hypothesis testing problems, and to incorporate SI framework to
      accurately quantify the reliability of decisions influenced by these generated images.
051
052   As an example of decision-making tasks based on image generation AI, we focus on the problem of
053   detecting anomalous regions in medical images (Wolleb et al., 2022; Baur et al., 2021) (see Figure 1).
      Initially, a diffusion model is trained exclusively on normal images during the training phase. In the

      Related work. Diffusion models have been effectively utilized in anomalous region detection
083   problems (Wolleb et al., 2022; Pinaya et al., 2022; Fontanella et al., 2023; Wyatt et al., 2022;
084   Mousakhan et al., 2023). In this context, the denoising diffusion probabilistic model (DDPM) is
085   commonly used (Ho et al., 2020; Song et al., 2022). During the training phase, a DDPM model
086   learns the distribution of normal medical images by iteratively adding and then removing noise. In
087   the test phase, the model attempts to reconstruct a new test image. If the image contains anoma-
088   lous regions, such as tumors, the model may struggle to accurately reconstruct these regions, as it
089   has been trained primarily on normal regions. The discrepancies between the original and the re-
090   constructed image are then analyzed to identify and highlight anomalous regions. Other types of
091
      generative AI has also been used for anomalous region detection task (Baur et al., 2021; Chen &
      Konukoglu, 2018; Chow et al., 2020; Jana et al., 2022).
092
093   SI was first introduced within the context of reliability evaluation for linear model features when they
094   were selected using a feature selection algorithm (Lee & Taylor, 2014; Lee et al., 2016; Tibshirani
095   et al., 2016), and then extended to more complex feature selection methods (Yang et al., 2016; Suzu-
096   mura et al., 2017; Hyun et al., 2018; RuМҲgamer & Greven, 2020; Das et al., 2021). Then, SI proves
097
      valuable not only for feature selection problems but also for statistical inference across various data-
      driven hypotheses, including unsupervised learning tasks (Chen & Bien, 2020; Tsukurimichi et al.,
098
      2021; Tanizaki et al., 2020; Duy et al., 2022; Le Duy et al., 2024; Lee et al., 2015; Gao et al., 2022;
099
      Duy et al., 2020; Jewell et al., 2022). The fundamental idea of SI is to perform an inference con-
100   ditional on the hypothesis selection event, which mitigates the selection bias issue even when the
101   hypothesis is selected and tested using the same data. To conduct SI, it is necessary to derive the
102   sampling distribution of test statistic conditional on the hypothesis selection event. To the best of
103   our knowledge, SI was applied to statistical inferences on several deep learning models (Duy et al.,
104   2022; Miwa et al., 2023; Shiraishi et al., 2024b; Miwa et al., 2024), but none of them works on
105   image generation by diffusion models.
106

      bution is the introduction of a statistical testing framework for quantifying reliability in decision-

173   3   S TATISTICAL T EST ON G ENERATED I MAGES BY D IFFUSION M ODELS
174
175   In this section, we formulate the statistical test for detecting anomalous regions using images gen-
176   erated by a trained DDPM model. As shown in Figure 1, anomalous region detection by diffusion
177   models is conducted as follows. First, in the training phase, the diffusion model is trained only on
178
      normal images. Then, in the test phase, we feed a test image which might contain anomalous regions
      into the trained diffusion model, and reconstruct it back from a noisy image xT вҖІ at step T вҖІ < T . By
179
      appropriately selecting T вҖІ , we can generate a normal image that retain individual characteristics of
180
      the test input image. If the image does not contain anomalous regions, the reconstructed image is
181   expected to be similar to the original test image. On the other hand, if the image contains anoma-
182   lous regions, such as tumors, the model may struggle to accurately reconstruct these regions, as it
183   has been trained primarily on normal regions. Therefore, the anomalous regions can be detected by
184   comparing the original test image and its reconstructed one.
185
186   Problem formulation. We develop a statistical test to quantify the reliability of decision-making
187   based on images generated by diffusion models. To develop a statistical test, we interpret an image
188   as a sum of a true signal component Вө вҲҲ Rn and a noise component Оө вҲҲ Rn . We emphasize that the
189   noise component Оө should not be confused with the noise Пө used in the forward process. Regarding
190   the true signal component, each pixel can have an arbitrary value without any particular assumption
191
      or constraint. On the other hand, regarding the noise component, it is assumed to follow a Gaussian
      distribution, and their covariance matrix is estimated using normal data different from that used for
192
      the training of the diffusion model, which is the standard setting of SI. Namely, an image with n
193
      pixels can be represented as an n-dimensional random vector

461         5.2                      R EAL DATA E XPERIMENTS
462
463         We conducted experiments using T2-FLAIR MRI brain scans from the Brain Tumor Segmentation
464         (BraTS) 2023 dataset (Karargyris et al., 2023; LaBella et al., 2023), which comprises 934 non-skull-
465         stripped 3D scans with dimensions of 240Г—240Г—155. From these scans, we extracted 2D 240Г—240
466         axial slices at axis 95, resized them to 64 Г— 64 pixels, and categorized them based on the ground
467         truth annotations into 532 normal images (without tumors) and 402 abnormal images (with tumor
468         regions). For each scan, we estimated the mean and variance from pixel values excluding both the
469
            non-brain regions and tumor regions identified in the ground truth, followed by standardization. We
            randomly selected 312 normal images for model training. The model was trained with T = 1000
470
            and the initial time step of the reverse process was set at T вҖІ = 300, with reconstruction performed
471
            through 5 step samplings. We set the threshold О» = 0.85 and the kernel size of the averaging filter to
472         3. Note that, when testing images, the non-brain regions are not treated as anomalous regions MX .
473         The results of the DMAD-test and naive are shown in the Figure 4. The naive p-values are low
474         for both normal and abnormal images, while the selective p-values are high for normal images and
475         low for abnormal images. This result indicates that the DMAD-test detected anomalous regions as
476         statistically significant while avoiding misidentification of normal image as anomaly.
477

479         6                    C ONCLUSIONS
480
481         In this study, we proposed a novel statistical test for anomalous regions in medical images detected
482         by using a diffusion model. With the proposed DMAD-test, the false detection rate can be controlled
483         with the significance level because statistical inference is conducted conditional on the fact that the
484         anomalous regions are identified by using a diffusion model. We believe this study marks a step
485         toward bridging the gap between generative AI and rigorous statistical inference in medical imaging
            analysis.
