011                                                  A BSTRACT
012
013            Probabilistic circuits (PCs) are a class of tractable deep probabilistic models that
014            compute event probabilities by recursively nesting sum and product computations.
015            Unfortunately, this is numerically unstable. To mitigate this numerical stability
016            issues, PCs are usually evaluated in log-space via the LogSumExp trick. In this
017
               paper we present an alternative to the ubiquitous LogSumExp trick, which we
               dub normalized float trick. Experimentally, we show that by simply changing the
018
               scheme guaranteeing numerical stability (from the LogSumExp to the normalized
019
               float trick) we can consistently and considerably boost the performance of PCs on
020            common density estimation benchmarks,

      1    I NTRODUCTION
025
026
      Probabilistic circuits (PCs) (Darwiche, 2003; Poon & Domingos, 2011) are a class of machine learn-
      ing models that perform density estimation by constructing deeply nested mixture models. That
027
      is, they recursively nest mixture models in order to construct joint probability distributions. This
028
      has two advantages. Firstly, the so-obtained model is more expressive than usual shallow mix-
029   ture models. Secondly, PCs retain interesting properties with regard to tractability, such as any-
030   order marginalization. Concretely, consider the probability distribution p(x1 , . . . , xN ) expressed
031   as a circuit. The (hierarchical) mixture model nature then allows us to marginalize out in poly-
032   time (in size of the circuit) any variable xi , i.e. to compute in poly-time the following marginal
033   p(x1 , . . . , xi−i , xi+i , . . . xN ). We give a graphical representation of a probabilistic circuit in Fig-
034   ure 1 (left).
035   From a numerical stability perspective, deeply nesting mixture models pose a problem, however.
036   When evaluation a circuit we end up repeatedly multiplying floating point numbers with each other
037   that lie in the unit interval [0, 1]. The problem is then that this quickly results into numerical un-
038   derflow. For this reason virtually all implementations of probabilistic circuits use the log-domain to
039   perform probabilistic inference (Peharz et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2024). However, naively performing
040   computations in the log-domain does not entirely resolve numerical stability issues. A common
041   technique to resolve this is the LogSumExp trick.1
042   In this paper we introduce an alternative to the LogSumExp trick that we dub the normalized float
043   trick or NoFlo trick and use it to evaluate probabilistic circuits. A curious experimental finding is
044   that using our new NoFlo trick instead of the usual LogSumExp trick leads to improved density esti-
045   mation capabilities of probabilistic circuits – simply by changing the numerically stable computation
046   scheme.
047   The remainder of the paper is organized as follows. We first give the necessary preliminaries on
048   probabilistic circuits (Section 2). We then introduce the NoFlo trick and contrast it to the LogSum-
049   Exp trick in the context of PCs (Section 3). In Section 4 we give experimental evidence that the
050   NoFlo trick achieves consistently better density estimation than the LogSumExp trick on a suite of
051   density estimation benchmarks. We end the paper with concluding remarks in Section 5.
052

      inference we only discuss it in more detail in Appendix B.

074   2   P ROBABILISTIC C IRCUITS AS PARTITION C IRCUITS
075
076   Inspired by simple feed-forward neural networks, Peharz et al. (2019; 2020) introduced the concept
077
      of layered probabilistic circuits These layered circuits are amenable to trivial parallelization and can
      be run on modern discrete GPUs and constitute the de facto standard approach towards constructing
078
      probabilistic circuits. In Figure 1 we give a graphical representation of such a layered circuit where
079
      we follow the construction introduced by Shih et al. (2021). For the sake of conciseness we describe
080   here only so-called structured decomposable and smooth PCs. For a broader overview we refer the
081   reader to the excellent work of Vergari et al. (2021).
082
083
      Layers within a layered PC constitute blocks of computational units that are processed sequentially
      in a bottom-up fashion. Layers consist themselves of so-called partitions. The circuit in Figure 1 has
084
      four partitions in the leaf layer (very bottom), two in the subsequent sum and product layers, and a
085
      single partition in the last product layer and in the final root node (top most sum). By construction,
086   partitions in the same layer have disjoint scopes. That is, they are functions over disjoint sets of
087   variables. This property is called structured decomposability in the PC literature (Darwiche, 2011).
088   Note also that layered PCs are by construction smooth (Darwiche, 2001): the union of the scopes of
089   the partitions within a layer is exhaustive. That is, the union of the scopes equals the set of variables
090   given as input to the circuit.
091
      A (layered) PC is then parametrized by weighing the inputs to the sum units with positive real
092   numbers – giving rise to an unnormalized probability distribution over the input variables. Due to
093   the properties of (structured) decomposability and smoothness the distribution can be normalized
094   in time polynomial in the size of the circuit (Peharz et al., 2015). Thanks to the properties of
095   smoothness and (structured) decomposability we can also marginalize out single variables from a
096   PC. Again in time polynomial in the size of the circuit.
097
      Recently, Zuidberg Dos Martires (2024) introduced the concept of partition trees to abstract away
098   certain aspects of smooth and structured decomposable probabilistic circuits. We give such a par-
099   tition tree on the right side of Figure 1. We now take this abstraction a step further and define
100   the computations performed by the probabilistic circuit not on the atomic computation units of the
101   circuit itself (i.e. sum and product units) but use the nodes in the partition tree as the elemental
102   computation units. In other words, we regard the partition tree as a computation graph. To make this
103   distinction explicit we dub these computation graphs partition circuits.

106   computation units: leaf and internal units, as well as a single root. Units at the same distance from
107   the root form a layer. Furthermore, let πk denote the root unit or an internal unit. The unit πk
      then receives its inputs from two units in the previous layer, which we denote by πkl and πkr . Each

      4.1     D ENSITY E STIMATION WITH THE N O F LO AND L OG S UM E XP T RICKS
292
293   We compare the density estimation capabilities of PCs using the NoFlo trick to the ones using the
294   traditional LogSumExp trick. To this end we encode gray-scale pixels by associating each of the 256
295   possible pixel values to the outcome of a categorical random variable. For MNIST this means that
296   we have 28 × 28 categorical random variables. These form the leaves of the probabilistic circuits.
297
      We then construct two circuits with two distinct structures. The first structure, which we dub
298   neighbor merges in an alternating fashion neighboring rows and columns of the 2D image.
299   Specifically, in the first layer rows 0 and 1 get merge and rows 2 and 3 and so on. In the next
300   layer the columns get merged. This pattern repeats until only a single partition is left, and we reach
301   the root. In the case of layers with an uneven number of rows/columns we also allow for merging
302   three neighboring rows/columns
303
      For the second circuit structure, to which we will refer as cross, we merge rows/columns by
304   merging consecutive evenly and unevenly numbered rows/columns. This means, for instance, that
305   in the first layer rows 0 and 2 are merged, rows 1 and 3, rows 4 and 6, and so on. In alternating
306   layers we then alternate between merging rows and columns. We exemplify the cross structure in
307   Figure 2 for the 1D case.3
308
      We then train both circuit structures by maximizing the log-likelihood using, on the one hand the
309
      NoFlo trick ,and on the other hand the LogSumExp trick. We compare the two schemes for nu-
310   merical stability using the bits per dimension metric, which is calculated from the average negative
311   log-likelihood (N LL) as follows: bpd=N LL/(log 2×D) (D=282 for MNIST datasets).
312
313

      otherwise default hyperparameters. The batch size was set to 50 and the maximum number of epochs
314
      was set to 50 as well. The best model was selected using a 95 − 5 train-validation data split where
315
      we monitored the negative log-likelihood on the validation set. The validation split was also used
316   for early stopping: training stopped preemptively when there has not been any improvement on the
317   validation set for five epochs.

414   4.3                        A C URIOUS A BLATION S TUDY
415
416   In order to find the root cause for the improved performance in terms of density estimation of the
417   NoFlo trick over the LogSumExptrick we performed the following ablation study. We first trained
418   a circuit (using the cross structure) with the NoFlo trick. On the test set we then evaluated the
419   circuit with the NoFlo trick as well as with the LogSumExp trick. We performed this experiments
420   on the MNIST dataset for varying number of components per partition. We report the results in
421   Figure 4 (left). We also performed the analogous experiment where we performed the training with
422
      the LogSumExp trick and evaluated on the test set with both tricks. These results can be found in
      Figure 4 (right).
423
424   Inspecting the histograms in Figure 4 we observe some curious behavior. We do not only obtain
425   lower bpd with the NoFlo vs. the LogSumExp trick, when comparing the blue bars from the left
426   and right figures. We also obtain higher bpd when evaluating NoFlo-trained circuits using the Log-
427   SumExp trick and lower bpd when evaluating LogSumExp-trained circuits using the LogSumExp
428
      trick.
429   Initially we suspected numerical precision issues to be the reason for this discrepancy between eval-
430   uations of one and the same circuits but with different numerical stable computation schemes. How-
431   ever, when repeating the experiment with 64 bit precision instead of 32 bits, this phenomenon still
      manifested itself.

      As of now we are not certain where exactly the circuit evaluations with the LogSumExp trick lose the
433   probability mass that leads to these lower bpd. We stipulate that it might be related to the repeated
434   application of logarithms and exponentiation and that these operations are not exact reciprocals of
435   each other when using finite precision. A counterargument to this thesis, however, is that increasing
436   the precision from 32 to 64 should then have alleviated the issue. However, we were not able to
437   observe such an improvement when increasing the precision.

      5   C ONCLUSIONS
440
441   In this paper we introduced normalized float trick, a new scheme to ensure numerically stable com-
442   putations when evaluating probabilistic circuits. Experimentally we have shown that simply using
443   the NoFlo trick instead of the standard LogSumExp trick leads to improved performance on suite
444   of density benchmarks at very limited computational overhead. For practitioners this means that
445   using the NoFlo trick constitutes a sensible implementation choice for boosting the performance of
446   probabilistic circuits. However, it is also unclear where exactly this increase in performance exactly
447   originates from. We leave the resolution of this open question to future work.

563   B    L OG -P ROBABILITIES AND THE L OG S UM E XP T RICK
564
565   The naive way of performing probabilistic inference is simple addition and multiplication of real-
566   valued numbers from the unit interval [0, 1]. Unfortunately, due to the finite precision of physical
567   machines, this leads to numerical stability issues in practice. The go-to technique to avoid such
568   numerical stability issues is to map probabilities to log-probabilities:
569                                                 λ = log π                                           (27)
570
571   where π ∈ (0, 1] and λ ∈ (−∞, 0]. We can now also map operations in linear space to adequate
572
      operations in log-space. For linear space multiplication we have:
573                                π1 × π2 = π3 ⇔ log π1 + log π2 = log π3                              (28)
574                                             ⇔ λ1 + λ2 = λ3 .                                        (29)
575
576   In other words multiplication in linear space maps to addition in log-space. Moreover, for the
577   addition we have:
578                          π1 + π2 = π3 ⇔ log (π1 + π2 ) = log π3                                     (30)
579
                                          ⇔ log (exp log π1 + exp log π2 ) = λ3                         (31)
580
                                          ⇔ log (exp λ1 + exp λ2 ) = λ3                                 (32)
581                                         |         {z         }
582                                                   LSE(λ1 ,λ2 )
583
      We see that the log-space operation that is equivalent to addition in linear space requires first expo-
584   nentiation, then addition, followed by taking the logarithm. This is often abbreviated as LSE(·, ·).
585
586   Unfortunately, exponentiation here is numerically unstable.           In order to compute
587
      log (exp π1 + exp π2 ) in a numerically stable fashion the usual trick is to make use of the
      LogSumExp trick:
588
589                   log (exp λ1 + exp λ2 ) = log (exp(λ1 − a + a) + exp(λ2 − a + a))                  (33)
590                                          = log (exp(λ1 − a) + exp(λ2 − a)) + a.                     (34)
591
592   Here a is a constant and usually chosen to be max(λ1 , λ2 ). The effect of subtracting a from the log-
593
      probabilities before exponentiation is that we avoid numerical underflow issues and the computation
      of LSE(·, ·) becomes numerically stable.
