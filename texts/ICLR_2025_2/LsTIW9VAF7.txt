009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Deep neural networks are fundamental in security-critical applications such as
012            facial recognition, autonomous driving, and medical diagnostics, yet they are
013            vulnerable to backdoor attacks. Clean-image backdoor attack, a stealthy attack
014            utilizing solely label manipulation to implant backdoors, renders models vulnerable
015            to exploitation by malicious labelers. However, existing clean-image backdoor
016            attacks likely lead to a noticeable drop in Clean Accuracy (CA), decreasing their
017            stealthiness. In this paper, we show that clean-image backdoor attacks can achieve a
018
               negligible decrease in CA by poisoning only a few samples while still maintaining
               a high attack success rate. We introduce Generative Adversarial Clean-Image
019
               Backdoors (GCB), a novel attack method that minimizes the drop in CA to less
020
               than 1% by optimizing the trigger pattern for easier learning by the victim model.
021            Leveraging a variant of InfoGAN, we ensure that the trigger pattern we used
022            has already been contained in some training images and can be easily separated
023            from those feature patterns used for benign tasks. Our experiments demonstrate
024            that GCB can be adapted to 5 datasets—including MNIST, CIFAR-10, CIFAR-
025            100, GTSRB, and Tiny-ImageNet—5 different architectures, and 4 tasks, including
026            classification, multi-label classification, regression, and segmentation. Furthermore,
027            GCB demonstrates strong resistance to backdoor defenses, successfully evading all
028            detection methods we know. Code: anonymous.4open.science/r/GCB.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Deep Neural Networks (DNNs) are widely used in applications like facial recognition (An et al.,
      2023), autonomous driving (Han et al., 2022), and medical image diagnosis (Li et al., 2021a); however,
034
      backdoor attacks threaten their trustworthiness. By poisoning a small portion of the training data (Li
035
      et al., 2022), adversaries can inject backdoors that cause models to make erroneous predictions when
036   specific inputs are presented. Recent studies reveal that backdoors can be implemented without
037   modifying images, known as clean-image backdoors—a significant concern when data annotation is
038   outsourced to third parties. For instance, Chen et al. (2022a) induced a one-to-one backdoor attack
039   in multi-label classification by relabeling images from a source label to a target label, though this
040   method is less adaptable to general image classification tasks. To address this limitation, Jha et al.
041   (2024) proposed a label-optimization technique that constructs a surrogate poisoned-image backdoor
042   model and optimizes soft labels to mimic its behavior.
043   Although these methods achieve a high Attack Success Rate (ASR), they experience a significant
044   drop in Clean Accuracy (CA), limiting their stealthiness in practice. For instance, the state-of-the-art
045   method FLIP (Jha et al., 2024) shows a CA reduction of 1.7% and a poison rate of 2% when averaged
046   across all classes in a one-to-one scenario (one source and one target class). However, a closer
047   analysis reveals that the CA drop and poison rate are much more pronounced at the class level. As
048   shown in Fig. 1, 15.8% of inputs from the source class were poisoned, resulting in a 13.3% CA drop
049   for that class. Even in the all-to-one scenario, FLIP leads to a consistent CA drop of 4.0% across
050
      all classes. This significant drop should alert the victim to a potential backdoor when examining the
      accuracy of each class on a clean test set.
051
052   The significant drop in CA can be attributed to a phenomenon known as the natural backdoor trigger
053   in clean-image backdoor, first introduced in (Rong et al., 2024). When a small percentage (e.g., 5%)
      of training images are relabeled to train a poisoned victim model, the i.i.d. properties of the training

078   a GAN generator to construct a trigger function, ensuring that all generated images, including trigger

      2.2   GAN-BASED R EPRESENTATION L EARNING
131
132   In GCB, we employ C-InfoGAN, a GAN-based representation learning technique, to enhance our
133   injected backdoor’s efficiency. Significant developments have marked the evolution of GANs in
134   interpretable and controllable representation learning. The original GAN (Goodfellow et al., 2014) es-
135   tablished foundational frameworks but faced challenges with unstructured latent noise. Advancements
136   like cGAN (Mirza and Osindero, 2014) incorporated label information for controlled generation,
137   and InfoGAN (Chen et al., 2016) introduced mutual information loss to enhance interpretability.
138
      StyleGAN (Karras et al., 2019) provided nuanced control over the noise vector, enabling precise
      image adjustments. However, these models primarily manipulated generated images and had limited
139
      ability to edit real-world images. GAN Inversion (Xia et al., 2022) tackled this by adding a network
140
      to map real images into the GAN’s latent space, increasing complexity. In our paper, we propose a
141   novel architecture called C-InfoGAN, which integrates feature editing capability directly into the
142   GAN architecture while ensuring interpretability of the controlled features.

      4     M ETHODOLOGY
185
186
      4.1   OVERVIEW
187
188   GCB aims to minimize the CA drop while maintaining a high ASR for clean-image backdoors. In
189   these scenarios, a portion of training images are deliberately mislabeled, but the images themselves
190   remain unchanged. To select images to mislabel, we introduce a new network C-InfoGAN, that is
191   trained to recognize patterns present in some training images but distinct from those patterns used
192   for benign tasks. The GCB framework is illustrated in Fig. 2. GCB comprises three stages: attack
193
      preparation, poisoning, and inference. During attack preparation, the C-InfoGAN is trained to identify
      these specific patterns. Subsequently, we utilize the Q component of C-InfoGAN to identify training
194
      images with the pattern and mislabel them. In the inference stage, we use the G component to convert
195
      any image into a triggered input, misleading the victim model to predict yt .
196
197
      4.2   C-I NFO GAN
198
199   Essentially, given a fixed poison rate (limiting the number of mislabeled images), our goal is to
200   maximize both ASR and CA. However, it is a challenge in clean-image backdoor settings, as we
201   can only modify the labels of images, leading to a discrete hard-label issue. Even advanced discrete
202   optimization methods like GCG can only maximize ASR but struggle to maintain a high CA.
203
      Our observations lead us to model this problem as a divergence maximization problem constrained
204   by three factors: (a) Existence: The trigger pattern must be present within the training data, enabling
205   backdoor injection via label manipulation alone. (b) Separability: The images with and without the
206   trigger must be distinctly separable, allowing easier backdoor learning and reducing the required
207   poison rate. (c) Irrelevancy: The trigger should not interfere with benign class features to prevent a
208   significant CA drop, as feature overlap can disrupt class semantics. To satisfy these constraints, we
209   introduce Conditional Information Maximizing GANs (C-InfoGAN). In C-InfoGAN, we introduce a
210   discrete random variable c following a Bernoulli distribution as the latent variable. The generator G,
211   conditioned on c, generates two distinct series of images depending on whether c is 0 or 1.
212   (a) Existence. A crucial property of clean-image backdoors is that the trigger pattern must exist
213   within the clean image set. To satisfy this, we employ a standard GAN framework (Goodfellow et al.,
214   2014). Training the discriminator D ensures that all images generated by the generator G follow
215   the same distribution as real images. By conditioning G on the latent variable c, we can generate
      images with (c = 1) or without (c = 0) the trigger pattern. Consequently, one of the two image series


                                                                 4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      generated, P (x̂|c = 1), becomes a subset of the real image distribution. This series, P (x̂|c = 1), can
217   thus be safely used as the trigger function, guaranteeing its existence within the original image set.
218
219   (b) Separability. To ensure separability, we follow the concept of InfoGAN (Chen et al., 2016),
220
      which maximizes the mutual information between selected latent variables and the generated data to
      learn interpretable and disentangled representations. The recognition network Q (originating from
221
      InfoGAN) is tasked with distinguishing between images generated with c = 1 and c = 0 as accurately
222
      as possible by introducing an information loss term Linfo . Q converges once it can easily determine
223   which series an image belongs to, indicating strong separability.
224
225   (c) Irrelevancy. Another crucial attribute of backdoors is that the trigger should not interfere with the
226
      benign task. This indicates that the trigger pattern needs to be irrelevant to the patterns utilized for
      the benign task. To ensure this, we use the input image’s ground-truth label y as an auxiliary input to
227
      both the GAN generator G and discriminator D, along with the condition variable c, ensuring c is
228
      independent of y. Thus, when c = 1 (triggered image), the generated image is unrelated to the input
229   image class, minimizing the trigger’s impact on the benign task.
230
231   Objective Function. In practice, our loss function combines the GAN loss from Wasserstein
232
      GAN (Arjovsky et al., 2017) and the mutual information loss from InfoGAN (Chen et al., 2016). The
      GAN loss is LGAN = Ex̂∼Pg [D(x̂)] − Ex∼PX [D(x)], where PX represents the distribution of real
233
      inputs and Pg denotes the distribution generator’s outputs, penalizing for the low consistency between
234
      these two distributions. The mutual information loss is Linf o = −Ec∼Pc ,x∼PX [log Q(c|G(x, c))],
235   where Pc is the Bernoulli distribution of c, represent the negative log-likelihood for predicting c
236   based on generated images G(x, c). The overall loss function integrates these two components as
237   L = LGAN + λLinf o , where λ is the trade-off hyperparameter.
238
      Theory Provement. We also provide a theoretical analysis for our GCB attack in Appendix A.
239
      From the perspective of information theory, we show that minimizing information loss can maximize
240
      the JS-Divergence between G(·, c = 0) and G(·, c = 1) (Lemma A.1). In addition, GAN ensures
241   that the generated images G(x, ·) and the corresponding real images x share the same distribution.
242   Consequently, the JS-Divergence between the real images of the two series is maximized, allowing
243   the Q component to distinguish them effectively. Both parts mentioned above act as a special case of
244   InfoGAN (Chen et al., 2016) and can be easily proved. Moreover, we prove that the total conditional
245   entropy of the backdoor task: H(Y ′ |X) (where (X, Y ′ ) is the poisoned dataset), is minimized
246   when the divergence between real images of the two series is maximized (Proposition A.2). Such
247   minimizing indicates that our backdoor task is easily learned, thereby ensuring a high ASR.
248
249   4.3   ATTACK D EPLOYMENT
250
251   Poisoning Stage. We select a subset X1 from the original training set X and change their labels
252   to the target label yt . The key challenge is selecting which images to manipulate. We introduce a
253   score function to assign poison scores to each clean image, where a higher score indicates greater
254
      suitability for label manipulation. The recognition network Q from InfoGAN effectively serves as
      this score function. Q is trained to recognize the value of c in generated images x̂. Since the GAN
255
      has converged, x and x̂ follow the same distribution, allowing Q can recognize both generated x̂
256
      and real images x. After scoring all input images, we apply a top-k quantile threshold to select the
257   top-scoring images, where k is the total number of poisoned samples needed. These selected images
258   have their labels flipped and are then submitted to train the victim model.
259
260
      Inference Stage. During the inference stage, to create a triggered image, we input any image x into
      the generator G conditioned on c = 1, producing G(x, c = 1), which contains the trigger pattern. The
261
      triggered images exactly correspond to the selected images in the poisoning stage, thereby effectively
262
      activating the backdoor to mislead the victim model into predicting the target label yt .

401   5.3     D IFFERENCE FROM C LEAN I MAGES
402
403   To study how different trigger images from clean images are, we employ seven metrics introduced
404   by BackdoorBench (Wu et al., 2024). Specifically, SSIM measures the structural similarity between
405   each poisoned sample and its corresponding clean sample. FID assesses the distance between the
406   distributions of poisoned samples and target-class clean samples. Additionally, BRISQUE, along with
407
      metrics for quality, noisiness, naturalness, and realism, evaluates the quality of individual samples.
      As shown in Fig. 6, clean-image backdoor attacks, such as FLIP and GCB, achieve better stealthiness
408
      (close to “Clean” line) compared to poison-image backdoors like Blended (Chen et al., 2017) and
409
      IA (Nguyen and Tran, 2020a). This is because clean-image backdoors utilize specific benign features
410   to construct the backdoor, resulting in poisoned data that closely resemble benign images in quality
411   and attributes. Thus reduce the effectiveness of image-quality-based detection methods including
412   BRISQUE, Quality, Noisy, Natural, Real and SSIM. On the other hand, FID assesses distributions
413   and can potentially detect differences between benign and triggered sample distributions caused by
414   clean-image backdoors. While, our experiments demonstrate that GCB causes triggered images to
415   closely resemble clean images in all tested metrics, including FID.

536   We introduced Generative Adversarial Clean-Image Backdoors (GCB), a stealthy and adaptive
537   backdoor attack that uses C-InfoGAN to optimize trigger patterns embedded within training images.

539   drop in clean accuracy and low poison rates. GCB resists existing defenses, highlighting the need for
      more robust protections.

       converge quickly and attain high ASR are more easily detected by simple defense methods. The
1414   primary reason for GCB’s effectiveness in both metrics is that it is inherently an asymmetric backdoor
1415   attack. During the poisoning stage, the images of poisoned samples contain relatively weak trigger
1416   information, which makes training-stage defenses less effective. In contrast, during the inference
1417   stage, the generated triggered images carry very strong trigger information, resulting in a high ASR.
1418
       We provide a visualization of this phenomenon in Figure 21, which illustrates how we select samples
1419
       to poison and add triggers in the two stages from the perspective of the latent space.

1688
1689   To validate the hyperparameter sensitivity of our method, we conducted experiments on two key
1690   parameters: the learning rate and the weight factor of the information loss λ. These two terms
1691   are also considered crucial in the original InfoGAN paper (Chen et al., 2016). We evaluated the
1692   training outcomes based on three aspects: (1) ASR, (2) visualization of triggered test images, and (3)
1693   visualization of selected training images.
1694
1695   (a) Effect of Learning Rate We tested five different learning rates: 1 × 10−5 , 3 × 10−5 , 1 × 10−4 ,
1696   3 × 10−4 , and 1 × 10−3 . We found that the ASR remained high (over 90%) across all learning
1697   rates. However, when the learning rate was very low or very high (1 × 10−5 or 1 × 10−3 ), strong
1698   artifacts were observed in the triggered test images, making these samples easier to detect at test time.
1699   Interestingly, we also found that different learning rates sometimes converged to different trigger
1700   patterns. At a learning rate of 3 × 10−4 , the trigger became a frame around the image, while other
1701
       learning rates resulted in triggers with special colors. This finding—that different learning rates result
       in different patterns—was also observed in the original InfoGAN (Chen et al., 2016).

       K     L IMITATIONS
1744
1745
       While our proposed GCB (Generated Clean-image Backdoor) attack demonstrates strong effectiveness
       in the image domain, extending it to other data modalities presents certain limitations and challenges
1746
       that we acknowledge.
1747
1748
       K.1    E XTENSION TO THE AUDIO D OMAIN
1749
1750   GANs have been successfully applied to continuous data types like audio. We believe that our GCB
1751   attack can be adapted to the audio domain by redefining the generator and discriminator architectures
1752   to process temporal data—for instance, by replacing convolutional neural networks (CNNs) with
1753   temporal convolutional networks (TCNs) or recurrent neural networks (RNNs). This adaptation
1754   would allow the generator to create audio signals with embedded backdoor triggers while maintaining
1755   the naturalness of the audio. However, this process is non-trivial and requires careful handling of
1756   the unique characteristics of audio data, such as temporal dependencies and frequency components.
1757   Additionally, designing imperceptible yet effective triggers in the audio domain poses its own set of
1758
       challenges, including ensuring that the triggers do not distort the audio quality or become detectable
       by human listeners or automated detection systems.
1759
1760
1761   K.2    C HALLENGES IN T EXT AND G RAPH D OMAINS
1762
       Adapting the GCB attack to discrete data domains like text and graphs is more complex due to the
1763
       inherent discreteness of these data types and the limitations of GANs in generating discrete outputs.
1764   In the text domain, generating coherent and semantically meaningful sentences that contain backdoor
1765   triggers without altering the original intent or raising suspicion is particularly challenging. Similarly,
1766   in graph data, which often represent relationships or network structures, modifying graphs to include
1767   backdoor triggers without disrupting their fundamental properties requires sophisticated techniques.
1768
       One potential approach to address these challenges involves a two-stage process:
1769
1770         1. Identify Poison Domain: Examine the latent representations in language models or graph
1771            neural networks to find label-irrelevant features that define a “poison domain.” This involves
1772            analyzing embeddings or node features that can be manipulated without affecting the primary
1773            task performance.
1774
             2. Design Trigger Function: Develop encoder-decoder models or use style transfer techniques
1775            to incorporate specific features into data samples, effectively creating a trigger effect. For
1776            text, this could involve subtle stylistic changes or synonymous substitutions; for graphs,
1777            it might include adding or reweighting edges in a way that is imperceptible to standard
1778            analysis.
1779
1780   This approach requires extensive exploration and the adaptation of representation learning techniques
1781   suitable for discrete data. The effectiveness and stealthiness of such triggers in these domains remain
       to be thoroughly investigated.


                                                          33
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1782
       K.3   C OMPUTATIONAL AND P RACTICAL C ONSIDERATIONS
1783
1784   Another limitation is the reliance on GANs, which are known to be challenging to train due to issues
1785   like mode collapse and training instability. The computational resources required for training GANs,
1786   especially on extremely large datasets or complex data modalities, may limit the practicality of the
1787   GCB attack in real-world scenarios. Exploring alternative generative models or more efficient training
1788   strategies could be necessary to overcome these barriers.
1789
1790   K.4   E THICAL C ONSIDERATIONS
1791
       Finally, we acknowledge the ethical implications of developing more advanced backdoor attacks.
1792
       While our work aims to highlight vulnerabilities to improve defense mechanisms, there is a risk that
1793
       such methods could be misused. It is imperative that research in this area is conducted responsibly,
1794   with a focus on enhancing the security and robustness of machine learning systems rather than
1795   exploiting them.
