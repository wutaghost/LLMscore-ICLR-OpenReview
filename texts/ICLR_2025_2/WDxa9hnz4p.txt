011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Batch prompting is a common technique in large language models (LLMs) used
014            to process multiple inputs simultaneously, aiming to improve computational effi-
015            ciency. However, as batch sizes increase, performance degradation often occurs
016            due to the model’s difficulty in handling lengthy context inputs. Existing methods
017            that attempt to mitigate these issues rely solely on batch data arrangement and
018            majority voting rather than improving the design of the batch prompt itself. In this
019            paper, we address these limitations by proposing “Auto-Demo Prompting,” a novel
020
               approach that leverages the question-output pairs from earlier questions within a
               batch as demonstrations for subsequent answer inference. We provide a formal
021
               theoretical analysis of how Auto-Demo Prompting functions within the autore-
022
               gressive generation process of LLMs, illustrating how it utilizes prior outputs to
023            optimize the model’s internal representations. Our method effectively bridges the
024            gap between batch prompting and few-shot prompting, enhancing performance
025            with only a slight compromise in token usage. Experimental results across five
026            NLP tasks demonstrate its effectiveness in mitigating performance degradation
027            and occasionally outperforming single prompts. Furthermore, it opens new av-
028            enues for applying few-shot learning techniques, such as demonstration selection,
029            within batch prompting, making it a robust solution for real-world applications.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Large language models (LLMs), such as GPT (Brown et al., 2020), and PaLM (Chowdhery et al.,
036   2023), have demonstrated an extraordinary ability to perform in-context learning (ICL), where they
037   utilize provided examples or contextual information to adapt and solve a wide range of downstream
038   tasks. This capability enables LLMs to generalize from few-shot or even zero-shot examples without
039
      requiring task-specific fine-tuning, significantly enhancing their versatility across diverse applica-
      tions (Song et al., 2023). The success of ICL in these models highlights their potential as powerful
040
      tools for natural language processing and as adaptable frameworks for learning in dynamic, data-
041
      constrained environments, offering broader implications for machine learning and AI research.
042
043   Recently, the batch prompting method has attracted growing research interest (Cheng et al., 2023;
044   Lin et al., 2024; Fan et al., 2024), where models are presented with a set of homogeneous questions
045
      - queries that share similar structure or content - within a single prompt. The main objective of
      batch prompting is to enhance interaction efficiency with LLMs by reducing computational costs,
046
      especially by minimizing the number of tokens processed. By grouping similar questions, this tech-
047
      nique streamlines the model’s handling of multiple tasks, optimizing resource usage while ensuring
048   consistent performance across repeated or related queries. As illustrated in Figure 1 (a), the model
049   processes multiple sentences with similar structures in a single batch, identifying and correcting
050   grammatical errors for each input sentence. This demonstrates how batch prompting reduces to-
051   ken usage when working with structurally similar tasks. Moreover, advancements in hardware and
052   algorithmic design have further expanded the capacity of LLMs to retain and process longer in-
053   put contexts, enabling more effective batch prompting (Munkhdalai et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2023;
      Ainslie et al., 2023).

080   While batch prompting provides significant advantages in terms of efficiency, it also presents a
081
      fundamental challenge, as handling lengthy context inputs is particularly difficult for LLMs based on
      transformers. Transformer models experience significant performance degradation when accessing
082
      relevant information from the middle of long contexts, leading to a decline in overall effectiveness
083
      (Liu et al., 2024). This issue becomes more pronounced in tasks that require LLMs to process long
084   contextual inputs. The root of the challenge lies in the quadratic complexity of the self-attention
085   mechanism within transformers, where computational costs increase dramatically with input length.
086   As batch prompting is applied, the combined input length grows, exacerbating this issue and further
087   degrading model performance. Therefore, developing a more effective batch prompting strategy
088   is crucial for mitigating these performance limitations and unlocking the potential of large-scale
089   applications in in-context learning, particularly for tasks involving long sequences of input data.
090   From the internal representations of LLMs, we observe that batch prompting shares similarities with
091   few-shot prompting, a technique that has proven crucial in enhancing the performance of LLMs by
092   providing a few example demonstrations (Brown et al., 2020). However, an intriguing contradiction
093   emerges: while few-shot prompting typically boosts performance, batch prompting often results
094   in performance degradation. It was found that batch prompting can sometimes outperform single
095   prompts in tasks with smaller batch sizes (Cheng et al., 2023), yet this line of investigation remains
096   underexplored. This raises a key question: Could we bridge the gap between batch prompting and
097   few-shot prompting to leverage the benefits of both?
098   Interestingly, previous research has found that even when incorrect labels are present in few-shot
099   demonstrations during in-context learning, the decrease in accuracy is generally minimal, typically
100   between 0% and 5% (Min et al., 2022). This suggests that using model-generated answers as demon-
101   strations in few-shot prompts is a viable approach, regardless of the potential for model hallucination
102   or limitations that may lead to incorrect answers.
103   In this paper, we tackle the performance degradation problem from the perspective of connecting
104   few-shot prompting with batch prompting. A key insight emerges: the outputs generated during
105   earlier iterations of autoregressive LLMs can, with proper design, be automatically recognized
106   as demonstrations for subsequent text generation, without needing to explicitly include them
107   in the prompt. Building on this, we propose “Auto-Demo Prompting,” a novel batch prompting
      technique that instructs the model to repeat each question before answering it. The model then au-

      tomatically treats the resulting question-answer pairs as demonstrations for the following questions
109   in the batch, eliminating the need to manually pack them into the input prompt.

111   As illustrated in Figure 1 (b), Auto-Demo Prompting directs the model to produce a sequence of
112
      question-answer pairs instead of just answers. This structure ensures that, during the autoregressive
      generation process, each subsequent question in the batch has access to the generated question-
113
      answer pairs from the previous iterations. Consequently, all questions in the auto-demo prompt
114
      effectively receive 0 to N − 1 additional demonstrations, potentially mitigating the performance
115   degradation associated with long context inputs.
116
117   By integrating demonstrations directly into the batch prompting process, our method not only en-
118
      hances the model’s capacity to manage longer inputs but also improves overall task performance,
      aligning batch prompting more closely with the proven success of few-shot prompting. To assess
119
      the effectiveness of Auto-Demo Prompting, we conducted extensive experiments using mainstream
120
      models, GPT-4o and GPT-4o-mini, across a variety of downstream tasks. The results demonstrate
121   that the proposed method significantly improves model performance in both an efficient and inter-
122   pretable way. Our contributions can be summarized as follows:
123
124         1. The proposed “Auto-Demo Prompting” represents a pioneering effort in constructing few-
125            shot demonstrations directly within the LLM’s inference. While previous research has
126            focused on various external factors associated with batch prompts, our approach uniquely
127
               modifies the batch prompt design to enable the model to generate demonstrations as part of
               its output process.
128
129         2. Through extensive comparisons between few-shot prompting and batch prompting, we pro-
130
               posed and validated the hypothesis that Auto-Demo Prompting is approximately equivalent
               to batch prompting supplemented with few-shot demonstrations. All experimental results
131
               showed that Auto-Demo Prompting outperformed traditional batch prompting, supporting
132
               our hypothesis and addressing the limitation of performance degradation in batch prompt.
133
            3. We discovered that batch data selection in Auto-Demo Prompting influences the selection
134
               of demonstrations (question-answer pairs) during the autoregressive generation steps of
135
               inference. By employing a common demonstration selection technique, our results showed
136            significant improvements compared to random batch data selection.
137
            4. The experimental results show that “Auto-Demo Prompting + Batch Data Selection,” with
138
               batch sizes of 16 and 32, outperforms the single prompts with a batch size of 1. This high-
139            lights the considerable potential of integrating additional few-shot prompting techniques
140            into Auto-Demo Prompt to serve as an efficient alternative to traditional single prompts
141            with enhanced accuracy and efficiency.

144   2   AUTO -D EMO P ROMPTING
145
146   Prompt engineering is essential for unlocking the capabilities of large language models (Marvin
147
      et al., 2023). Typically, a standard prompt consists of a task description and a single data point,
      leading to the development of various prompting techniques to enhance LLM performance on down-
148
      stream tasks (Sahoo et al., 2024; Besta et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2023; Wei et al., 2022). However,
149
      relying on single prompts limits inference to just one data point, making this approach less efficient
150   for large-scale datasets or real-world applications. In contrast, Batch Prompting allows for the pro-
151   cessing of multiple data points in a single inference. Cheng et al. (2023) suggests that while batch
152   prompting performs well with smaller batch sizes, there is a tendency for performance to decline as
153   batch sizes grow. Additionally, Lin et al. (2024) noted that varying the order of batch data can yield
154   different results, proposing the integration of these outcomes through majority voting to enhance
155   overall performance.
156   Unlike previous works, Auto-Demo Prompting aims to maximize the potential of batch prompting
157   and enhance its performance during single inference of large language models. This method guides
158   LLMs to generate a question-answer pair for each input question in the batch, rather than simply
159   providing an answer. By innovating the design of batch prompts with a new output control format,
160   Auto-Demo Prompting ensures that LLMs maintain a consistent output structure across all ques-
161   tions. A key factor in this process is the autoregressive generation mechanism of the decoder in
      LLMs, which facilitates coherent and contextually relevant output generation.

211   2.1   AUTOREGRESSIVE G ENERATION P ROCESS IN L ARGE L ANGUAGE M ODEL
212
213   Large Language Models can be classified into three categories: encoder-only, encoder-decoder, and
214   decoder-only. The mainstream LLMs are primarily decoder-only and encoder-decoder models. The
215   GPT series developed by OpenAI, including notable examples such as GPT-3, GPT-4, and GPT-
      4o, consists entirely of decoder-only models and marks significant milestones in the evolution of


                                                                            4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      generative language models. The Llama series represents open-source LLMs that are also decoder-
217   only. In contrast, examples of encoder-decoder models include T5 (Raffel et al., 2020) and BART
218   (Lewis, 2019). This paper focuses specifically on decoder-only LLMs, where the decoder generates
219   one token at each step of the autoregressive generation process. A single inference of an LLM
220   typically requires multiple steps to produce a complete answer, with the token generated at the
221   previous step added to the current input, serving as the new input for the next token. This iterative
222   process of token generation is known as autoregressive generation.
223   Based on the generation mechanism of LLMs, we introduce a key insight into the autoregressive
224   generation process: the outputs from earlier steps can serve as prompts or demonstrations for gen-
225   erating subsequent tokens. By employing batch prompting, the model can process multiple input
226   data sequentially, enabling consistent and repetitive responses. This periodicity allows for the use of
227   answers from earlier questions as demonstrations for later ones, thereby enhancing coherence and
228   context retention throughout the generation process.
229
230   2.2    AUTOREGRESSIVE G ENERATION OF D EMONSTRATIONS
231
232   As illustrated in Figure 2, Auto-Demo Prompting creatss a loop where demonstrations are generated
233   in an autoregressive manner during the inference of LLMs. This technique guides LLMs to produce
234   question-answer pairs that align with the format of demonstrations used in context learning. Due
235   to the autoregressive nature of token generation, the previously generated output is appended to the
236
      current input for the next step, serving as a demonstration for all subsequent questions in the batch.
      When the batch size is N , there will be {1, 2, 3, ..., N − 1} demonstrations formed in the process.
237

281   2.3   BATCH DATA S ELECTION
282
283   In the proposed Auto-Demo Prompting framework, batch data selection - a conventional technique
284   for improving batch prompting performance - can be viewed as demonstration selection. As illus-
285
      trated in Method 3, the batched data (i.e., questions) is also present within the question-answer pairs
      generated by the LLM, which serve as demonstrations for subsequent questions. Therefore, we pro-
286
      pose the following hypothesis: the selection of batched data in Auto-Demo Prompting achieves a
287
      similar effect to the selection of demonstrations in few-shot prompting.
288
289
290                            Batch Data Selection ≈ Demonstration Selection                            (2)
291
      Based on this hypothesis, demonstration selection methods can be effectively adapted for batch data
292   selection in Auto-Demo Prompting. Current approaches to demonstration selection are diverse, uti-
293   lizing criteria such as similarity, mutual information, perplexity, and diversity (Yang et al., 2023).
294   Notably, demonstration selection based on text similarity has proven effective for text pair classifi-
295   cation and multiple-choice tasks (Peng et al., 2024; Su et al., 2024).
296
      Inspired by demonstration selection method, we designed the ”Batch Data Selection with Retrieval”
297
      algorithm to identify similar questions within a single batch. As detailed in Algorithm 1, this algo-
298   rithm features a data retrieval loop aimed at gathering similar data into one batch. For each batch
299   with size N , a target data point is randomly selected. Subsequently, the N − 1 most similar data
300   points are identified using an embedding model to calculate the pairwise similarity.

      3.1.1    DATASETS
325
326   BoolQ: The Boolean Questions (BoolQ) dataset comprises 15,942 yes/no questions derived from
327   real-world queries to the Google search engine (Clark et al., 2019). Each example includes a ques-
328   tion, passage, and answer, with an optional page title for context. The dataset is split into 9,427
329   training examples and 3,270 validation examples, noted for its complexity requiring advanced rea-
330   soning and entailment inference.
331   RTE: The Recognizing Textual Entailment (RTE) dataset, compiled from the RTE1, RTE2, RTE3,
332   and RTE5 challenges (Poliak, 2020), includes 2,490 training samples, 277 validation samples, and
333   3,000 testing samples. It features premise-hypothesis pairs, each labeled to indicate their entailment
334   relationship, making it a valuable resource for advancing natural language inference research.
335
      GSM8K: The Grade School Math 8K (GSM8K) dataset consists of 8,500 diverse grade school
336
      math word problems designed for multi-step reasoning tasks (Cobbe et al., 2021). It is divided into
337   7,500 training problems and 1,000 test problems, with each problem requiring 2 to 8 steps to solve.
338   Solutions are provided in a step-by-step format, and the dataset is available in both standard and
339   ”Socratic” formats, which include meta-reasoning prompts.
340
      QQP: The Quora Question Pairs (QQP) dataset contains over 400,000 question pairs from the Quora
341
      platform, each labeled to indicate semantic equivalence. This dataset is essential for tasks like
342
      paraphrasing and duplicate question identification, enabling researchers to explore various machine
343   learning techniques for semantic textual similarity (Wang et al., 2017).
344
345   SVAMP: The SVAMP dataset features 1,000 simple math word problems aimed at assessing NLP
346
      models, designed for up to fourth-grade level and involving single-variable equations (Patel et al.,
      2021). Created to challenge models with variations of existing problems, it tests their contextual
347
      understanding and problem-solving accuracy.

      the prompting process.

447   3.2.2                 D ISCUSSION
448
449   The results of our experiments underscore the significant potential of Auto-Demo Prompting. These
450
      findings corroborate our earlier observations regarding equations 1 and 2, demonstrating that Auto-
      Demo Prompting consistently enhances Batch Prompting across most experimental scenarios. It
451
      is particularly noteworthy the performance improvement observed when batch data selection is ap-
452
      plied. By grouping similar data within each batch, we found that Auto-Demo Prompting with larger
453   batch sizes outperformed configurations utilizing a batch size of one, which is the standard prompt
454   used in mainstream LLM applications. This advancement signifies a promising avenue for achieving
455   both enhanced performance and increased efficiency.
456
      Furthermore, recent research by (Agarwal et al., 2024) demonstrates that many-shot demonstrations
457
      can outperform few-shot demonstrations, a trend observed across various domains (Jiang et al.,
458
      2024; Moayedpour et al., 2024). As the input and output length limits of LLMs continue to expand,
459   the adoption of a more effective batch data selection method is likely to enhance these performance
460   gains, further solidifying the benefits of our approach. Given the diverse and numerous demonstra-
461   tion selection methods available for in-context learning, the optimal choice can vary across different
462   datasets. This variability encourages future research to evaluate their effectiveness and to develop
463   novel approaches that are better suited for the Auto-Demo Prompting.

      4             R ELATED W ORK
466
467
      4.1               BATCH P ROMPTING
468
469   Batch prompting has emerged as a significant area of research in LLMs to facilitate efficient batch
470   processing of many data points simultaneously. Cheng et al. (2023) first introduced the concept
471   of batch prompting to reduce computational costs, with experiments focusing on batch sizes of
472   fewer than six demonstrating comparable performance to standard prompting. Building on this, Lin
473   et al. (2024) proposed “BatchPrompt,” which highlights the variability in results caused by different
474   data orders and introduces a self-consistency method to address these discrepancies. With its cost-
475
      saving benefits, batch prompting has been applied in various NLP applications; for instance, Fan
      et al. (2024) proposed a framework utilizing a covering-based demonstration selection strategy for
476
      entity resolution, effectively balancing matching accuracy and computational cost, while Zhang et al.
477
      (2023) explored the impact of batch size on several data preprocessing tasks.
478
479
      4.2               D EMONSTRATION S ELECTION IN I N -C ONTEXT L EARNING
480
481   To improve the performance of in-context learning, previous studies have explored the optimization
482   of the selection and arrangement of few-shot demonstrations (Rubin et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2022;
483   Wu et al., 2023; Fu et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2023). It was found that the choice of demonstra-
484   tions is both data-dependent and model-dependent, leading to the proposal of a selection method
485   that considers both factors (Peng et al., 2024). Empirical research by (Min et al., 2022) has demon-
      strated that preserving the structured format of demonstrations, which consists of text-label pairs, is


                                                                                      9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      essential for optimal performance. They also found that randomly altering the labels within these
487   demonstrations has a negligible effect on performance, providing a foundation for our research.
488
489   Our work diverges from previous research in several key aspects. While existing studies have ex-
490
      plored batch prompting and its applications, they often overlook the relationship between few-shot
      prompting and batch prompting, which we believe presents an emerging opportunity for directly
491
      enhancing batch prompting. To address this gap, we introduced the Auto-Demo Prompt, which not
492
      only improves performance but also offers greater interpretability by establishing a close alignment
493   with the principles of in-context learning and demonstration selection techniques.

496   5   C ONCLUSION
497
498
      In this paper, we introduce the Auto-Demo Prompt, a novel batch prompting method aimed at im-
      proving the performance of batch prompting. We provide a comprehensive overview of its oper-
499
      ational mechanism and clarify the key concepts that establish the relationship between few-shot
500
      demonstrations in in-context learning and batch prompting. Importantly, we show that ’Batch Data
501   Selection’ can be conceptualized as ’Demonstration Selection’ within the framework of Auto-Demo
502   Prompting, facilitating the transfer of insights from few-shot learning research to our approach. Our
503   extensive experiments validate the effectiveness of Auto-Demo Prompting, underscoring its signifi-
504   cance as the input and output lengths of LLMs increase. As these lengths expand, the importance of
505   efficient and high-performing batch prompting methods, such as Auto-Demo Prompting combined
506   with Batch Data Selection, will become increasingly pronounced. Further research is encouraged
507   to investigate demonstration selection in greater detail, as well as to explore the potential syner-
508   gies between Auto-Demo Prompting and other emerging techniques such as prompt learning and
509
      explainable AI. By delving deeper into these areas, we can uncover new insights that may enhance
      the adaptability and effectiveness of LLMs, ultimately paving the way for advancements in their
510
      applications toward achieving artificial general intelligence (AGI).
