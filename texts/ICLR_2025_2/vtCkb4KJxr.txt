009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            We address the problem of submodular maximization where objective function
012            f : 2U → R≥0 can only be accessed through i.i.d noisy queries. This problem
013            arises in many applications including influence maximization, diverse recommenda-
014            tion systems, and large-scale facility location optimization. We propose an efficient
015            adaptive sampling strategy, called Confident Sample (CS), that is inspired
016            by algorithms for best-arm-identification in multi-armed bandit, which signifi-
017            cantly improves sample efficiency. We integrate CS into existing approximation
018            algorithms for submodular maximization, resulting in algorithms with approxi-
019
               mation guarantees arbitrarily close to the standard value oracle setting that are
               highly sample-efficient. We propose and analyze sample-efficient algorithms for
020
               monotone submodular maximization with cardinality and matroid constraints, as
021
               well as unconstrained non-monotone submodular maximization. Our theoretical
022            analysis is complemented by empirical evaluation on real instances, demonstrating
023            the superior sample efficiency of our proposed algorithm relative to alternative
024            approaches.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      Submodularity is a property of set functions that arises in many applications such as cut functions in
030
      graphs Balkanski et al. (2018), coverage functions Bateni et al. (2017), data summarization objectives
031   Tschiatschek et al. (2014), information theoretic quantities such as mutual information Iyer et al.
032   (2021), and viral marketing in social networks Kempe et al. (2003). A function f : 2U → R≥0
033   defined over subsets of the universe U of size n is submodular if for all X ⊆ Y ⊆ U and u ∈      / Y,
034   f (Y ∪ {u}) − f (Y ) ≤ f (X ∪ {u}) − f (X). In addition, in many applications of submodular
035   functions f is monotone (Tschiatschek et al., 2014; Iyer et al., 2021; Kempe et al., 2003), meaning
036   that for all X ⊆ Y ⊆ U , f (X) ≤ f (Y ). Proposed algorithms for submodular optimization typically
037   are assumed to have value oracle access to f . That is, f is a black box that can be queried for any
038   X ⊆ U , and the value of f (X) is returned Nemhauser et al. (1978); Badanidiyuru & Vondrák (2014);
039
      Balkanski et al. (2019a); Buchbinder et al. (2015).
040   However, in many optimization scenarios, we can only make noisy queries from some random
041   distribution to estimate the objective. For example, in applications such as diversified recommender
042   system Yue & Guestrin (2011); Hiranandani et al. (2020), data summarization with human feedback
043   Singla et al. (2016), influence maximization Kempe et al. (2003); Wen et al. (2017), feature selection
044   tasks Krause & Guestrin (2005), querying the exact value of f is unrealistic, and instead a more
045
      realistic assumption is that we can query f subject to some random noise. In particular, we assume
      that the noisy sampling of f is random and is i.i.d sub-Gaussian, which is also referred to as bandit
046
      feedback in the submodular bandit literature Singla et al. (2016); Chen et al. (2017). In a related
047
      setting, submodular optimization algorithms that leverage the multilinear extension F of the function
048   f may only be able to access F via i.i.d noisy random samples and this is a major bottleneck in
049   terms of the efficiency of these algorithms Calinescu et al. (2011); Badanidiyuru & Vondrák (2014).
050   In this setting, the common approach is to use existing submodular optimization algorithms and
051   apply the fact that the objective can be evaluated to arbitrary precision by taking sufficiently many
052   samples and applying concentration inequalities in order to achieve a fixed-precision (see Section
053   2) approximation of the objective function Kempe et al. (2003); Calinescu et al. (2011). However,
      modern massive datasets demand algorithms that are as efficient as possible in terms of runtime, and

054
      in the case of submodular optimization algorithms, the main computation time bottleneck for the
055   above approach would be the noisy queries to f .
056
057   Motivated by the above, our main insight is that an algorithm doesn’t necessarily need to approx-
058
      imate f with such fine precision at every query in order to find a solution with an approximation
      guarantee comparable to the exact value oracle setting. Instead, we propose methods of adaptively
059
      approximating the function f based on decisions that the algorithm must make, with an emphasis on
060
      minimizing the total number of noisy queries. Methods of efficient sampling in order to determine the
061   best action is related to the best-arm identification in submodular bandit, where the objective is that to
062   identify a super-arm (subset of the universe) with comparable approximation ratio in as few samples
063   as possible (Audibert et al., 2010; Singla et al., 2016). Therefore our algorithmic contributions and
064   analysis are inspired by ideas used in best-arm-identification in the bandit setting. In particular, the
065   contributions of the paper are as follows:
066
067           (i) We propose the adaptive sampling algorithm Confident Sample (CS) in Section 3,
068               which can be used to determine if the mean of a random variable X is approximately
069               above or below a given threshold w with high probability, in relatively few random samples.
070               Intuitively, the required number of samples is inversely proportional to the gap between
071               EX and w, and therefore we can significantly decrease the number of samples relative
072
                  to the fixed-precision approach by sampling less when the gap is large. CS is related but
                  significantly different from algorithms used for best-arm-identification in bandit, as we
073
                  explain in detail in Section 4. CS is used as a subroutine for all proposed algorithms for
074
                  submodular maximization problems in the paper, and as a result the proposed algorithms
075               exhibit an improved sample complexity compared with fixed-precision approximation.
076          (ii) We address the problem of Monotone Submodular Maximization with Cardinality constraint
077               (MSMC) in Section 4, which is defined to find the set arg max{f (X) : X ⊆ U, |X| ≤ κ}.
078               We prove two results for the proposed Confident Threshold Greedy algorithm
079               (CTG), Theorem 3 and Theorem 4. Theorem 3 is demonstrated to achieve an improved
080               sample complexity compared with that of the related work of Singla et al. (2016), while
081               achieving the same approximation guarantee. Theorem 4 is proved to achieve a better sample
082               complexity compared with the sampling before-hand approach in the application of influence
083
                  maximization.
            (iii) In Section 5, the algorithm Confident Continuous Threshold Greedy (CCTG)
084
                  is proposed and analyzed for the problem of Monotone Submodular Maximization with
085
                  Matroid constraint (MSMM). MSMM is to find the solution of arg maxS⊆M f (S), where
086               M is a matroid defined on subsets of the ground set U . CCTG accesses the multilinear
087               extension of f via noisy samples, since the multilinear extension can be difficult to compute
088               in general Calinescu et al. (2011); Badanidiyuru & Vondrák (2014). In particular, we
089               demonstrate that CCTG has an improved sample complexity compared with the one proposed
090               in Badanidiyuru & Vondrák (2014).
091         (iv) In Section E, we propose Confident Double Greedy (CDG) for Unconstrained Sub-
092               modular Maximization (USM). The goal is to find a subset S ⊆ U that maximizes f (S)
093               where f is not necessarily monotone. The theoretical guarantee on sample complexity is
094
                  presented in Theorem 15 in the appendix.
             (v) Finally, as a demonstration of our approach, we experimentally analyze CTG on instances
095
                  of noisy data summarization and influence maximization. We compare CTG to several
096
                  alternative methods including the algorithm of Singla et al. (2016) which is discussed in
097               more detail in Section 1.1 and in the appendix. CTG is demonstrated to be a practical choice
098               that can save many samples relative to alternative approaches.

101   1.1     R ELATED WORK
102
103   Approximation algorithms for the maximization of a submodular objective function subject to various
104   constraints have been extensively studied in the literature Nemhauser et al. (1978); Badanidiyuru &
105   Vondrák (2014); Mirzasoleiman et al. (2015); Calinescu et al. (2011) with the assumption of oracle
106   access to f . The runtime of these algorithms is generally measured in queries to f as this is the main
107   bottleneck (see Section A for a more comprehensive discussion on the runtime of algorithms for
      various submodular optimization problems).


                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      While there are many works assuming value oracle access to f , algorithms developed assuming noisy
109   access to f are relatively less explored Horel & Singer (2016); Singla et al. (2016); Hassidim &
110   Singer (2017); Qian et al. (2017); Crawford et al. (2019); Huang et al. (2022). One related setting to
111   ours is that we have noisy access to f , but this noise is persistent Horel & Singer (2016); Hassidim
112   & Singer (2017); Qian et al. (2017); Crawford et al. (2019); Huang et al. (2022). Our noisy setting
113   departs from this direction in that the noisy feedback is random and repeated samples should be
114   taken to diminish the noise. Another related but different setting is that of stochastic submodular
115   optimization Karimi et al. (2017); Staib et al. (2019); Özcan & Ioannidis (2023) which assumes the
116   optimization objective f is the expectation over some unknown distribution over a set of monotone
117
      submodular functions. Therefore a sample average function can be built, which is also monotone
      and submodular, and algorithms run on it. In contrast, in our setting, it is only assumed that we can
118
      sample noisy queries at each subset X ⊆ U . The algorithm ExpGreedy of Singla et al. (2016) is for
119
      a noisy setting identical to ours and is developed for the MSMC problem specifically. ExpGreedy
120   also incorporates an adaptive sampling approach. In particular, their algorithm combines the standard
121   greedy algorithm with the best arm identification problem found in combinatorial bandit literature
122   Chen et al. (2014). Their approach is still very different from ours, and an extensive comparison
123   of our algorithms and results with Singla et al. (2016) are presented in the appendix, as well as an
124   experimental comparison in Section 6.
125
      The intuition behind CS is similar to the best-arm-identification problem in the multi-armed bandit
126   literature Kalyanakrishnan et al. (2012). Both the algorithm LUCB of Kalyanakrishnan et al. (2012)
127   and CS share a common underlying intuition: they leverage the difference between expectations to
128   reduce the number of noisy queries required. In LUCB, this difference is between the expectation of
129   the optimal arm and other arms, while in CS, it is between the expectation of the input variable and
130   the threshold value w.

759   A     A DDITIONAL R ELATED W ORK
760
761   Approximation algorithms for submodular maximization problems with exact value oracle have
762   been extensively studied in the literature Nemhauser et al. (1978); Badanidiyuru & Vondrák (2014);
763   Mirzasoleiman et al. (2015); Balkanski et al. (2019a). For MSMC, the standard greedy algorithm
764   produces a solution set with the best possible 1 − 1/e approximation guarantee in O(n2 ) queries of f .
765   Badanidiyuru & Vondrák (2014) proposed a faster greedy-like algorithm that gives an approximation
766   guarantee of 1 − 1/e − O(ϵ) while reducing the sample complexity to O( nϵ log nϵ ).
767   Another variant is USM Buchbinder et al. (2015); Feige et al. (2011); Buchbinder & Feldman (2018).
768   Notably, Buchbinder et al. (2015) introduced a deterministic algorithm that gives a 1/3 guarantee in
769   O(n) queries to an oracle for f , and a randomized version of their algorithm yields the best possible
770   1/2 guarantee in expectation in the same number of queries.
771
      The final variant of submodular maximization we consider is MSMM Balkanski et al. (2019b);
772
      Friedrich & Neumann (2014); Fisher et al. (1978). The greedy algorithm only yields an approximation
773   ratio of 1/2 in this setting Fisher et al. (1978). But by extending the discrete submodular function to
774   its continuous counterpart, known as the multilinear extension (see the definition in Section 2), and
775   by solving the problem in this regime, it is proved that an approximation ratio arbitrarily close to the
776   best possible 1 − 1/e can be achieved Badanidiyuru & Vondrák (2014); Calinescu et al. (2011).
777
      Our work is also related to the best-arm-identification in multi-armed bandit literature Audibert et al.
778
      (2010); Kaufmann et al. (2016); Jun et al. (2016), where the objective is to estimate the best action by
779   choosing arms and receiving stochastic rewards from the environment. The most widely considered
780   setting is the PAC learning setting Even-Dar et al. (2002); Kalyanakrishnan et al. (2012); Zhou et al.
781   (2014).
782
      Our paper studies the same noisy setting as Singla et al. (2016). There are essentially two versions of
783
      ExpGreedy, one gives an approximation guarantee of about 1 − 1/e with high probability (like our
784
      algorithm CTG does), and the other gives the same approximation guarantee but is randomized. The
785   benefit of the latter over the former is better sample complexity. The bounds given on the sample
786   complexity of ExpGreedy and the ones given in this paper for CTG are instance-dependent and
787   incomparable to one another. We discuss how our algorithm relates to ExpGreedy in more depth
788   in Section B, but we briefly list here the potential advantages of our algorithm CTG compared to
789   ExpGreedy: (i) Our algorithm has an approximation guarantee of about 1−1/e with high probability
790   as opposed to an approximation guarantee of about 1 − 1/e in expectation as in the randomized
791   version of ExpGreedy; (ii) Our algorithm is not as sensitive to small differences in marginal gain
792   between elements since it is not based on the standard greedy algorithm as ExpGreedy is; (iii) The
793
      algorithm of ExpGreedy has greater time complexity beyond just the sample complexity because
      it requires O(n log n) computations per each noisy query to ∆f ; (iv) Our algorithm makes less
794
      estimations of ∆f overall since it is based on a faster variant of the greedy algorithm (TG). We further
795
      compare the algorithms experimentally in Section 6.2.
796

807
808   In this section, we provide more discussion about the related algorithm ExpGreedy of Singla
809   et al. (2016). ExpGreedy combines the standard greedy algorithm with the best arm identification
      algorithm used in combinatorial bandit literature Chen et al. (2014).


                                                        15
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
      In particular, the standard greedy algorithm for MSMC Nemhauser et al. (1978) goes as follows:
811   A solution S is built by iteratively choosing the element u ∈ U that maximizes the marginal gain
812   ∆f (S, u) until the cardinality constraint κ is exhausted. ExpGreedy follows a setting like ours, so
813   instead of choosing the element of maximum marginal gain at each iteration, they follow the standard
814   greedy algorithm but adaptive sampling following techniques from the best-arm identification problem
815   is done in order to identify the element(s) with the highest marginal gain. The simplest version of
816   their algorithm identifies one element with the highest marginal gain at each iteration, and this version
817   has a guarantee of about 1 − 1/e with high probability as in CTG. This algorithm is EXP-GREEDY in
818   Section 6. However, a downside of this approach is that many samples are often needed to distinguish
819
      between elements of nearly the same marginal gain. In contrast, notice that our algorithm CTG does
      not need to compare marginal gains between elements and therefore does not have this issue.
820
821   In order to deal with the sample inefficiency, ExpGreedy is generalized to a randomized version. The
822   randomized version of ExpGreedy involves a subroutine called TOPX, which adaptively samples
823   marginal gains until a subset of elements with relatively high marginal gains have been identified.
824   Then a randomly selected element among the subset is added to the solution set. In particular, given an
825
      integer 0 < κ′ ≤ κ, the TOPX algorithm runs TOP-l selection algorithms for each l ∈ {1, 2, ..., κ′ },
      and each of the TOP-l selection algorithm runs until it returns a subset of l items with highest
826
      marginal gain with high probability. The TOPX algorithm stops once there exists some l such that
827
      the TOP-l selection algorithm ends. This randomized version of ExpGreedy has an almost 1 − 1/e
828   approximation guarantee, but it holds in expectation and with high probability. The case where κ′ = κ
829   is EXP-GREEDY-K in Section 6.

879   C.1   C OMPARISON OF CS TO FIXED ϵ- APPROXIMATION
880
881   In this section, we present a comparison of our result with the fixed ϵ-approximation. A fixed ϵ-
882   approximation is essentially when one applies a concentration inequality such as Hoeffding’s or the
883   Chernoff Bound for a fixed number of noisy samples such that the empirical mean of the evaluated
884   random variable X, which is denoted as X̂, satisfies that |X̂ − E[X]| ≤ ϵ. (see also discussion in
885   Section 2).
886   The fundamental reason this approach is less efficient compared to CS is that we are only interested
887   in determining whether f (X) is approximately above a threshold or not, not in obtaining a precise
888   approximation. In other words, we don’t need the guarantee that the |X̂ − EX| ≤ ϵ in Hoeffding’s
889   inequality; instead, we care about whether EX ≥ w. Ideally, we would approximate f (X) just finely
890   enough to determine if it’s above the threshold or not. However, this isn’t feasible with the fixed
891   ϵ-approximation, because we don’t have any prior knowledge of how far f (X) is from the threshold.
892   Consequently, we can’t determine the required number of samples, and the fixed ϵ-approximation
893   approach requires that there be a single batch of i.i.d. samples, which limits flexibility.
894   In contrast, CS uses an adaptive sampling approach where samples are iteratively taken one-by-one
895   until an evolving confidence interval crosses a threshold. The goal of CS is to use fewer samples
896   compared to a fixed ϵ-approximation. While CS might initially seem similar to fixed ϵ-approximation,
897   there are several critical differences that introduce unique technical challenges in its development and
898   analysis:
899
900          • Fixed ϵ-approximation approaches have a batch of samples in which a single application of
901
               a concentration inequality is applied in order to approximate EX. In contrast, in CS, we
               apply a concentration inequality after every single sample, and then take a union bound over
902
               all the applications. However, this is challenging because we don’t know how many samples
903
               we will end up taking to approximate the mean value sufficiently well since that depends on
904            the result of the sampling. So we have to carefully design our confidence intervals.
905
906
             • Fixed ϵ-approximation approach takes a predetermined number of samples, independent of
               the sampling results. In contrast, the CS algorithm dynamically determines the number of
907
               samples based on the outcomes of previous samples. Additionally, CS reuses samples across
908
               multiple applications of concentration bounds, enhancing its efficiency.
909
910
             • In CS, the size of the confidence interval evolves with each additional sample, shrinking as
               the number of samples increases (see Theorem 1). Additionally, when applying concentration
911
               inequalities, the failure probability is adjusted dynamically based on how many samples
912
               we’ve taken so far (see proof of Lemma 6). The   benefit  of the varying failure probability is
913                                                                 q
                                                      8R2      16R2    2
914            that the obtained sample complexity ϕ2 log ϕ2           δ won’t suffer from small values of
                                                        X         X
915
               ϵ.
916          • In Theorem 2 and 4, we use a combination of Hoeffding and Chernoff that is well-suited
917            to the threshold algorithms, rather than using one or the other. This approach improves the
               sample complexity from O(R2 ) in Theorem 1 to O(R) when R is large.

       H.1.1    N OISY DATA SUMMARIZATION
1945
1946   In data summarization, U is a dataset that we wish to summarize by choosing a subset of U of
1947   cardinality at most κ. The objective function f : 2U → R≥0 takes a subset X ⊆ U to a measure
1948   of how well X summarizes the entire dataset U , and in many cases is monotone and submodular
1949   Tschiatschek et al. (2014). However, in real instances of data summarization, we may not have
1950   access to an exact measure f of the quality of a summary, but instead, we may have authentic human
1951   feedback which is modeled as noisy queries to some underlying monotone and submodular function
1952   Singla et al. (2016).
1953   Motivated by this, we run our experiments using instances of noisy data summarization. Our underly-
1954   ing monotone submodular function f is defined as follows: U is assumed to be a labeled dataset, e.g.
1955   images tagged with descriptive words, and for any X ⊆ U , f takes X to the total number of tags
1956   represented by at least one element in X Crawford (2023). Notice that this is essentially the instance
1957   of set cover.
1958
1959   H.1.2    I NFLUENCE MAXIMIZATION
1960
1961   Another application is the influence maximization problem in large-scale networks Kempe et al.
1962   (2003). In this application, the universe is the set of users in the social network, and the objective
1963   is to choose a subset of users to seed with a product to advertise in order to maximize the spread
1964   throughout the network. The marginal gain of adding an element s to set S is defined as ∆f (S, s) :=
1965   Ew∼D(w̄) ∆f (S, s; w), where w is the noisy realization of the graph from some unknown distribution
1966   D(w̄), and ∆f (S, s; w) = f (S ∪ {s}; w) − f (S; w). In a noisy graph realization with parameter w,
1967   f (S; w) is the number of elements influenced by the set S under some influence cascade model. It is
1968
       #P-hard to evaluate the objective in influence maximization Chen et al. (2010). Many of the previous
       works Chen et al. (2009) assume the entire graph can be stored by the algorithm and the influence
1969
       cascade model is known. The algorithm first samples some graph realizations to approximate the
1970
       true objective and run submodular maximization algorithms on the sampled graphs. In contrast, our
1971   setting and algorithm do not assume that a graph is stored or the model of influence is explicitly
1972   known, only that we could simulate it for a subset. Therefore our approach could apply in more
1973   general influence maximization settings than the sampled realization approach.
1974
