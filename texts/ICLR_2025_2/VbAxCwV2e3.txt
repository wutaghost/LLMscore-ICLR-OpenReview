009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Diffusion models have achieved great success in generative tasks, with the qual-
012            ity of generated samples guaranteed by their convergence properties, typically
013            derived within the context of stochastic differential equations(SDE) and often in-
014            volving Kolmogorov equations for proofs. This paper introduces a novel method
015            for proving the convergence of diffusion models, which relies on direct estima-
016            tion of distributions without the need for SDE tools. This approach inspires
017            a Divide-and-Conquer strategy for approximating the reversed transition kernel
018
               of Diffusion Probabilistic Models (DC-DPM), which is not derived from SDEs,
               making previous convergence methods inapplicable. However, our method can be
019
               easily extended to accommodate this. As our DC-DPM learns specific kernels for
020
               each partition , these kernels require merging. According to the proof of conver-
021            gence, we design two merging strategies for these cluster-specific kernels along
022            with corresponding training and sampling methods. Experimental results demon-
023            strate the superior generation quality of our method compared to the traditional
024            single Gaussian kernel. Furthermore, our DC-DPM can synergize with previous
025            kernel optimization methods, enhancing their generation quality, especially with
026            a small number of timesteps.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Diffusion models have recently gained prominence in generating multi-modal content across various
032   tasks, including image generation (Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021; Ho et al., 2020; Rombach et al., 2022;
033   Saharia et al., 2022; Ramesh et al., 2022), image super-resolution (Li et al., 2022), video generation
034
      (Ho et al., 2022a;b), text-to-speech synthesis (Popov et al., 2021), 3D generation (Poole et al., 2022),
      and motion planning (Carvalho et al., 2023).
035
036   Diffusion models generate data by iteratively predicting noise and solving diffusion SDEs to de-
037   noise (Song et al., 2020b). Given the complexity of this process, achieving convergence towards
038   the desired data distribution is not a straightforward task, particularly when using a score function
039   approximated by a neural network.
040   Significant research advancements have been made to validate the convergence of diffusion models.
041   Lee et al. (2022) were the first to provide polynomial convergence guarantees for diffusion models.
042   The scope of this convergence was further expanded to a broader range of data distributions (Chen
043   et al., 2022; Lee et al., 2023). De Bortoli (2022) demonstrated convergence when the data is only
044   supported on a lower-dimensional manifold. Chen et al. (2024); Benton et al. (2023) focused on
045   the development of convergence for deterministic sampling. Additionally, Li et al. (2023) were able
046
      to achieve a superior error bound by making additional assumptions on the Jacobian of the score
      functions. However, the aforementioned convergence relies heavily on tools from SDEs, such as the
047
      Kolmogorov equations (Lee et al., 2022) and the Girsanov theorem (Chen et al., 2022). This reliance
048
      makes it challenging to adapt these methods to cases where the reverse process is not derived from
049   a SDE.
050
051   In this paper, we introduce a novel method to demonstrate the convergence of diffusion models.

      neural network approximation. Subsequently, we tackle the corner cases at t = 0 and t = 1. As a

072
      Inspired by the proof above, we propose DC-DPM, a novel approach to represent the transition
      kernel in the reverse process of diffusion probabilistic models (DPM) using a Divide-and-Conquer
073
      strategy. To attain the convergence of DC-DPM, we employ the convexity of the Kullback-Leibler
074
      divergence to transform the error term into the form of a single Gaussian case. This altered form
075   is consistent with the scenario outlined in our newly proposed proof method. As a result, we easily
076   prove the convergence of the newly proposed DC-DPM.
077
078
      According to the convergence proof of DC-DPM, we cluster the data into different partitions, DC-
      DPM learns reversed transition kernels for each data cluster and models the overall transition kernel
079
      as a composition of these cluster-specific kernels. To determine how to combine the cluster-specific
080
      kernels, we design two strategies along with corresponding training and sampling methods. DC-
081   DPM can collaborate with previous diffusion sampling optimization methods, particularly those
082   focused on optimizing the Gaussian transition kernel design, such as Extended-Analytic-DPM and
083   GMS, by utilizing the representations proposed in these works for each cluster-specific kernel in our
084   DC-DPM. Moreover, the convergence is not dependent on the data partitions, implying that any data
085   division pattern will lead to a convergent result.
086
      Experimental results on 2D toy datasets and image datasets demonstrate that our method enhances
087   the generation quality of diffusion models compared to the traditional single Gaussian transition ker-
088   nel representation. Furthermore, our approach significantly improves the performance of previous
089   transition kernel optimization methods, including Extended-Analytic-DPM and GMS, especially in
090   scenarios with limited sampling steps.

512   5     R ELATED W ORK
513
514   Significant research has focused on improving diffusion model performance on fewer timesteps,
515   broadly categorized into three approaches. Training-based methods includes trainable sampling
516   schedules (Watson et al., 2021), truncated diffusion (Lyu et al., 2022; Zheng et al., 2022), neural
517   operators (Zheng et al., 2023a), and distillation (Salimans & Ho, 2022; Sauer et al., 2023; Meng
518   et al., 2023; Song et al., 2023; Luo et al., 2023). The second category enhances the efficiency of
519   SDE and ODE solvers in the reverse process, including faster SDE and ODE solvers (Lu et al.,
520   2022a;b; Zheng et al., 2023b; Xu et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024), adaptive step size solvers (Jolicoeur-
521   Martineau et al., 2021), predictor-corrector methods (Song et al., 2020b; Zhao et al., 2023), and
522
      stochastic-calculus-based optimization (Sabour et al., 2024).
523   The third category focuses on improving the design of the transition kernel in the diffusion reverse
524   process. Analytic-DPM (Bao et al., 2022b) and Extended-Analytic-DPM (Bao et al., 2022a) esti-
525   mate the optimal variance. Our work also falls within this category, with the most closely related
526   prior work being GMS (Guo et al., 2024). GMS represents the transition kernel as a mixture of
527   two Gaussians based on the estimation of higher-order moments. In contrast, the highlight of our
528
      method is to divide data into clusters and construct the kernel function in a divide-and-conquer man-
      ner. We construct a more general framework and previous Analytic-DPM, Extended-Analytic-DPM,
529
      and GMS can serve as the cluster-specific kernel in our method.
530
531
      6     C ONCLUSION
532
533
      In this paper, we propose DC-DPM, a novel divide-and-conquer approach for approximating the
534   transition kernel in the reverse process of diffusion probabilistic models. We provide convergence
535   proof for diffusion models from a new perspective, generalizing the transition kernel representation
536   from a conventional single Gaussian to a divide-and-conquer framework. This framework utilizes
537   cluster-specific kernels to represent segmented data, which are then merged to form an overall repre-
538   sentation. We propose two merging strategies along with their corresponding training and sampling
539   methods. Experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of our approach, significantly enhanc-
      ing generation quality, particularly over a limited number of timesteps.
