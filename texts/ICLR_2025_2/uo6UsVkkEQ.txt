011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Molecule discovery is a pivotal research field, impacting everything from the
014            medicines we take to the materials we use. Recently, Large Language Models
015            (LLMs) have been widely adopted in molecule understanding and generation,
016            yet the alignments between molecules and their corresponding captions remain
017            a significant challenge. Previous endeavours often treat the molecule as a general
018
               SMILES string or molecular graph, neglecting the fine-grained alignments be-
               tween the molecular sub-structures and the descriptive textual phrases, which are
019
               crucial for accurate and explainable predictions. In this case, we introduce Mol-
020
               ReFlect, a novel teacher-student framework designed to contextually perform the
021            molecule-caption alignments in a fine-grained way. Our approach initially lever-
022            ages a larger teacher LLM to label the detailed alignments by directly extracting
023            critical phrases from molecule captions or SMILES strings and implying them to
024            corresponding sub-structures or characteristics. To refine these alignments, we
025            propose In-Context Selective Reflection, which retrieves previous extraction re-
026            sults as context examples for teacher LLM to reflect and lets a smaller student
027            LLM select from in-context reflection and previous extraction results. Finally,
028            we enhance the learning process of the student LLM through Chain-of-Thought
029
               In-Context Molecule Tuning, integrating the fine-grained alignments and the rea-
               soning processes within the Chain-of-Thought format. Our experimental results
030
               demonstrate that MolReFlect enables LLMs like Mistral-7B to significantly out-
031
               perform the previous baselines, achieving SOTA performance on the ChEBI-20
032            dataset. This advancement not only enhances the generative capabilities of LLMs
033            in the molecule-caption translation task, but also contributes to a more explainable
034            framework.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
038
039
      Molecules are the fundamental units of matter, which normally consist of atoms held together by
040
      chemical bonds. In various chemical and biological processes, molecules play a critical role in
041
      participating in reactions (Grozinger & Schreiber, 2002), transmitting signals (Raymo & Giordani,
042   2001), and maintaining the structure and function of living organisms (Konieczny et al., 2023). It is
043   important to study molecules and their properties, which could benefit a wide range of fields, includ-
044   ing Pharmacology (Keiser et al., 2010), Agriculture (Twyman et al., 2003; Basaran & Rodrı́guez-
045   Cerezo, 2008), Material science (Higuchi et al., 2023), and Environmental Ecology (Nguyen et al.,
046   2017; Valavanidis et al., 2006).
047
      As molecules can be represented by textual systems like SMILES (Weininger, 1988) and SELFIES
048   (Krenn et al., 2020), it is natural to adopt Large Language Models (LLMs) in molecule-related
049   tasks (Zhang et al., 2024). Specifically, LLMs could predict the molecular properties based on
050   the SMILES or SELFIES representations and generate molecules with desired properties, making
051   them helpful assistants for chemists. Correspondingly, Edwards et al. (2022) propose the molecule-
052   caption translation task to bridge the gap between molecular and natural language space, which
053   includes molecule captioning (Mol2Cap) and text-based de novo molecule generation (Cap2Mol).
      In addition to text, several multi-modal methods, like MoMu (Su et al., 2022) and MolCA (Liu et al.,

      Current methods typically require an extra modality alignment stage, which suffers from the lack
      of high-quality molecule-caption pairs. Furthermore, these methods still treat the whole molecule
083
      as a general textual string or molecular graph, neglecting the granularity of alignments and the ex-
084
      plainability of their methods. Specifically, sub-structures in the molecule, such as functional groups,
085   exactly determine the characteristics of the molecule described in the molecule caption. Similarly,
086   the characteristics described in the molecule caption also directly refer to specific sub-structures of
087   the molecule. For example, as shown in Figure 1, the molecule Dodecanoyl Dodecanoate is the
088   reaction production of the formal condensation of two dodecanoic acids, which turns two carboxyls
089   (RC(=O)OH ) into an anhydride (RC(=O)OC(=O)R). Thus, it has an anhydride group and there are 12
090   carbon atoms on each side of the central oxygen atom. If LLMs could notice these patterns, they are
091   more likely to make accurate predictions. In this case, it is crucial to pay attention to the fine-grained
092   alignments between molecules and texts by focusing on decisive sub-structures and caption phrases.
093
      Nevertheless, few works have paid attention to refining the granularity of alignments between molec-
      ular sub-structures and their corresponding descriptive texts, as such fine-grained alignments often
094
      require domain experts for the labelling, which is both costly and time-consuming.
095
096   To resolve the above challenges, we propose MolReFlect, a teacher-student framework inspired by
097   reflection tuning (Li et al., 2024b), which enables a larger teacher LLM to collaborate with a smaller
098   student LLM for in-context fine-grained alignments in the molecule-caption translation task. The
099
      detailed model structure is shown in Figure 3. Generally, MolReFlect includes three stages: Zero-
      shot Alignment Extraction, In-Context Selective Reflection, and Chain-of-Thought In-Context
100
      Molecule Tuning (CoT-ICMT). Initially, the larger teacher LLM generates zero-shot alignments by
101
      extracting important phrases from the molecule SMILES representations or molecule captions and
102   implies them to corresponding characteristics or sub-structure patterns in a zero-shot manner. To
103   improve the quality of the alignments, we further introduce In-Context Selective Reflection, which
104   first retrieves similar samples and their corresponding zero-shot alignments as in-context few-shot
105   examples so that the teacher LLM can reflect on them and then refine its responses. Following
106   this, the student LLM selects between the zero-shot alignments and reflected alignments with lower
107   perplexities to ensure that they could understand the knowledge taught by the teacher LLM and
      further relieve the noises in the alignments. Finally, to help the student LLM better learn from

201   3       M OL R E F LECT
202
203   In this section, we introduce the MolReFlect framework. As depicted in Figure 3, MolReFlect
204
      employs a teacher-student architecture, where an advanced (larger) language model serves as the
      teacher, and a less sophisticated (smaller) language model acts as the student. The teacher LLM col-
205
      laborates with the student LLM to fine-grain the in-context alignment between molecules and texts,
206
      thereby enhancing the overall efficacy in the molecule-caption translation task. The MolReFlect
207   framework is organized into three principal stages: Zero-Shot Alignment Extraction, In-Context
208   Selective Reflection, and CoT-ICMT. We proceed to elaborate on each of these stages in sequence.
209
210
      3.1        Z ERO - SHOT A LIGNMENT E XTRACTION
211
212   Previously, the molecule-caption translation task treats a molecule as a general SMILES string m
213   and tries to let LLMs learn the direct mappings m ↔ c between the molecule SMILES string m and
214   the textual caption c. To refine the alignments, several multi-modal methods like MolCA (Liu et al.,
215   2023) have been proposed to incorporate molecule graph information gm and learn the direct map-
      ping (m, gm ) → c for the Mol2Cap task. Nevertheless, these methods still treat the molecule as a


                                                                                                    4

      general SMILES sequence or molecular graph while ignoring the significance of detailed molecular
217   sub-structures.
218
219   Instead of directly learning the mappings from molecules to captions, MolReFlect aims to extract
220
      fine-grained alignments K between the molecule SMILES strings and molecule captions, thereby
      learning the mapping chains m → K → c and c → K → m. Typically, the fine-grained align-
221
      ments should be labeled by professional chemists, which is not only challenging but also financially
222
      prohibitive. As a result, LLMs have emerged as a viable alternative due to their advanced reasoning
223   capabilities and a certain degree of chemical knowledge. Within MolReFlect, we have developed
224   a zero-shot prompting strategy to empower the teacher LLM to engage in chain-of-thought (CoT)
225   reasoning (Wei et al., 2022). This allows the teacher LLM to distill critical fragments from the
226   molecule SMILES representations or captions, offering implications to their corresponding proper-
227   ties or sub-structure patterns. Formally, we have:

238

      3.3   C HAIN - OF -T HOUGHT I N -C ONTEXT M OLECULE T UNING
281
282   While it is technically possible to leverage fine-grained alignments as contexts to allow the larger
283   teacher LLM to generate final predictions directly in a CoT manner, the teacher LLM still lacks
284   specialized pre-training on chemical corpora and is unfamiliar with the specific output distribution
285   of the dataset. Consequently, directly querying the larger teacher LLM for final generations usually
286   leads to unsatisfactory results. Furthermore, the cost of directly fine-tuning the larger teacher LLM
287   is prohibitively high, making it unaffordable for most institutions. Instead, we fine-tune the smaller
288
      student LLM to learn from the fine-grained alignments provided by the larger teacher LLM. Notably,
      in this phase, we prioritize the reasoning capabilities of the student LLM over their knowledge of
289
      chemistry, so it can differ from the student LLM used to calculate perplexity.
290
291   In contrast to In-Context Molecule Tuning (Li et al., 2024a), CoT-ICMT organizes the fine-grained
292   alignments of both the input x and the context examples Cx into the CoT format. This CoT format
293   empowers LLMs to learn from the fine-grained alignments and the reasoning processes behind the
294
      context examples, thereby facilitating more explainable training. During the process of CoT-ICMT,
      top-n similar examples are retrieved via the same retrieval strategies mentioned in Section 3.2 and
295
      then organized into the context with the CoT format to fine-tune the parameters of the smaller student
296
      LLM. Formally, similar to Eq. 3, the loss function can be represented as follows:
297

298                 Lcot−icmt (θ) =          [− log pθ (y|x, Kx , [Cx→Kx →y , Fx→Kx →y ], I)] ,         (11)
299                                 (x,y)∈D

      where Kx denotes the fine-grained alignments of input x, Cx→Kx →y = {P(xi , Kxi , yi )}ni=1
302   represents the text content of context examples organized by the CoT format prompt P, and
303   Fx→Kx →y = {fi := xi → Kxi → yi }ni=1 denotes the mapping chains behind the context examples,
304   which map the original inputs to the fine-grained alignments and then further map the fine-grained
305   alignments to the final targets.

388   RQ1: Do fine-grained alignments improve the performance in the molecule-caption transla-
389
      tion task, and if so, how?
390   For this question, we conduct an ablation study on MolReFlect by removing context examples and
391   fine-grained alignments, downgrading MolReFlect to Instruction Tuning and ICMT, respectively.
392   Meanwhile, we also provide the naive-SFT performance of Mistral-7B. The results are presented
393   in Table 3 and Table 4. It is evident that the naive-SFT results are actually unsatisfying as Mistral-
394   7B lacks specific pre-training on chemical corpora. Meanwhile, when only the context examples
395
      are removed, the performances drop slightly but attain a BLEU-4 score of 0.539 on the Mol2Cap
      task and a BLEU score of 0.886 on the Cap2Mol task, demonstrating a significant performance
396
      improvement compared to naive-SFT. Notably, in the Cap2Mol task, the exact match score nearly
397
      doubles compared to naive-SFT, indicating that the fine-grained alignments indeed convey much
398   molecular structure information to the student LLM. Furthermore, when fine-grained alignments
399   are removed during the fine-tuning phase, the performances drop in both Mol2Cap and Cap2Mol
400   tasks. This suggests that the LLMs are able to learn molecule-text alignments more effectively from
401   the fine-grained alignments in the context examples, leading to better final generations.
402
      We also include several cases in Appendix C and conduct a series of extensive experiments in Ap-
403
      pendix B for better explanation. As depicted in Figure 5, the larger teacher LLM can generate
404   preliminary indications towards the final target and even directly figure out the molecular structure
405   in fine-grained alignments. However, some of these indications might be inaccurate. With CoT-
406   ICMT, the smaller student LLM could learn from the input distribution, identify these errors, and
407   correct them in the final generation process. In this case, as illustrated in Figure 4, the output distri-
408   bution generated by MolReFlect aligns better with the ground truth. Conversely, MolT5 and ICMA
409   fail to achieve this owing to the lack of fine-grained alignments.

422   RQ2: Why is it necessary to reflect and select between the zero-shot alignments and in-context
423   reflected alignments?
424
      To resolve this question, we ablate MolReFlect by removing the in-context reflection and the se-
425
      lection processes, which is equivalent to replacing the fine-grained alignments with zero-shot align-
426   ments and in-context reflected alignments, respectively. The details are shown in the last two rows
427   of Table 3 (for the Mol2Cap task) and Table 4 (for the Cap2Mol task). From Table 3, we can observe
428   that the results without in-context reflection lead to sub-optimal performance as the teacher LLM
429   could make mistakes or yield hallucinations, underscoring the necessity of in-context reflection.
430   However, the in-context reflected alignments are not necessarily better than zero-shot alignments, as
431   evidenced by Table 4. Sometimes, the zero-shot alignments of similar molecules/captions may con-
      tain more noises, like hallucinations and factual errors, than helpful information and inadvertently


                                                          8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      become part of the context in the in-context reflection phase. The inaccuracies could then carry over
433   to the in-context reflected alignments, potentially harming the final performance. In this case, the
434   zero-shot alignments can be more helpful as the context examples do not pollute them. Therefore,
435   choosing between zero-shot alignments and in-context reflection alignments is imperative to ensure
436   the quality of fine-grained alignments.
437
      From the information theory perspective, our objective is to provide LLMs with more helpful in-
438
      formation and less noise while rigorously preventing any disclosure of information about the target.
439   Therefore, perplexity, an unsupervised metric, is an ideal criterion for the selection process. Higher
440   perplexity scores suggest the presence of information that conflicts with the existing knowledge
441   of LLMs, making it a reliable indicator for discerning the quality of the generated alignments. In
442   this work, we utilize the Galactica-125M as the student model to calculate perplexity, which is
443   particularly adept at chemical tasks and offers rapid computation. The alignments with the lower
444   perplexity scores are selected as the fine-grained alignments. According to Table 3 and 4, across
445   both the Cap2Mol and Mol2Cap tasks, MolReFlect consistently demonstrates superior performance
446   compared to those without in-context reflection or selection, thereby substantiating the effectiveness
447
      of In-Context Selective Reflection and Selection.

461   RQ3: What is the necessity of adopting a teacher-student framework?
462
      In this part, we address the last research question by removing the student model and completing the
463   tasks using only the teacher LLM (i.e., Llama-3-70B). Since the cost of fine-tuning the teacher LLM
464   is unaffordable for most institutions, we only test the performance of teacher LLM with prompt engi-
465   neering to avoid modifications of their parameters. Various prompting strategies are implemented to
466   enable the teacher LLM to undertake the molecule-caption translation tasks independently, including
467   direct prompting, chain-of-thought prompting, few-shot prompting, and few-shot chain-of-thought
468   prompting. Notably, in the chain-of-thought and few-shot chain-of-thought prompting, we utilize
469   the fine-grained alignments produced by the teacher LLM itself as context information. The results
470   of these experiments are detailed in Table 5 and 6.
471   It can be observed that while Llama-3-70B is a powerful LLM, its performance under direct prompt-
472   ing is notably weak, as it is not trained on the ChEBI-20 or a lot of chemical corpora, ensuring that
473   the information of the ChEBI-20 dataset is not leaked in its pre-training stage. In the Mol2Cap
474   task, the chain-of-thought strategy enhances the performance by introducing fine-grained align-
475   ments. However, in the Cap2Mol task, the performance declines by 1.05%, indicating that the
476   teacher LLM struggles to filter out the noise inherent in the fine-grained alignments without ex-
477
      plicit supervisory signals. Similarly, in the few-shot setting, the fine-grained alignments also do not
      contribute to a significant performance boost for the teacher LLM. In contrast, the student LLM
478
      proves to be indispensable and could benefit from the CoT-ICMT process by enabling a better un-
479
      derstanding of molecule-text alignments and identifying the noises behind fine-grained alignments.
480   As shown in Table 3 and 4, the Instruction Tuning (i.e., w/o Context Examples) performance in-
481   creases by 9.94% and 14.22% in the Mol2Cap and Cap2Mol tasks, respectively, compared to the
482   naive-SFT. This further underscores the necessity of discerning and mitigating noise within the fine-
483   grained alignments, suggesting that LLMs must engage in fine-tuning to learn from the fine-grained
484   alignments effectively. Thus, the teacher-student framework proves to be indispensable. It enables
485   the smaller student LLM to learn from the input distribution, discern noise in the content generated
      by the teacher, and absorb valuable information to inform the final generation process.

504   5      R ELATED W ORK
505
506
      LLMs have demonstrated great potential in Molecule Discovery, including molecule understand-
507   ing (Qian et al., 2023), optimization (Ye et al., 2023), and generation (Irwin et al., 2022). To align
508   molecule representation with natural language texts, the MolT5 study first proposed the molecule-
509   caption translation task, introducing a new dataset, ChEBI-20, with pairs of molecule SMILES
510   representations and their textual captions that describe the structural patterns and chemical proper-
511   ties (Edwards et al., 2021). Subsequent research has intensified the focus on this task, branching out
512   in two primary directions.
513   On one trajectory, the research leverages the in-context learning capability of LLMs and the simi-
514   larity principle of molecules to help LLMs learn the molecule-text alignment in context (Li et al.,
515   2023a). Advancing this approach, ICMA has developed In-Context Molecule Tuning (ICMT), sig-
516   nificantly enhancing the capabilities of LLMs in the molecule-caption translation task and reducing
517   the reliance on domain-specific pre-training (Li et al., 2024a). Concurrently, the other works involve
518   incorporating additional information from different modalities into LLMs. For instance, MoMu (Su
519   et al., 2022) adopts contrastive learning to align the output distribution of the text encoder with the
520   graph encoder, while the CLIP structure (Radford et al., 2021) is not good at generative tasks. In
521
      this case, MolCA (Liu et al., 2023) introduce the 2D molecular graph with a Q-Former structure (Li
      et al., 2023b) to enhance the performance of LLMs in the molecule captioning task. However, the 2D
522
      molecular graphs do not actually bring extra information. as the conversion between the molecule
523
      SMILES representation and its molecule graph is lossless. Meanwhile, 3D-MoLM (Li et al., 2024c)
524   adopts the similar Q-Former structure, but introduces 3D molecule information to LLMs. However,
525   the 3D information generated by RDKit (Landrum, 2013) is not accurate enough and is not closely
526   related to the molecule properties described in molecule captions.
527

529   6      C ONCLUSION
530
531
      In this study, we present MolReFlect, a novel teacher-student framework designed to refine the
532
      in-context alignments between molecular sub-structures and their corresponding textual descrip-
533
      tions. MolReFlect comprises three stages: Zero-shot Alignment Extraction, In-Context Selective
534   Reflection, and Chain-of-Thought In-Context Molecule Tuning. Fine-tuned with the fine-grained
535   alignments taught by the teacher LLM, the student LLM could benefit from the detailed alignments
536   between molecules and texts, enhancing the overall performance and contributing to a more explain-
537   able framework. Our experimental results reveal that MolReFlect outperforms all existing baselines.
538   Additionally, we also substantiate the superior explainability via comprehensive case studies. We
539   believe this work could inspire future works to focus on the granularity of molecule-text alignments
      in this promising field.
