                 Large Vision Language Models (VLMs) have long struggled with visual-centric
013              understanding, with spatial reasoning emerging as a notable bottleneck due to its
014              reliance on effective image processing. Surprisingly, even simple spatial reasoning
015              tasks, such as recognizing вҖңunderвҖқ or вҖңbehindвҖқ relationships between only two
016              objects, pose significant challenges for current VLMs. We believe it is crucial to
017              use the lens of mechanism interpretability, diving into the modelвҖҷs internal states to
018              examine the interactions between image and text tokens during spatial reasoning.
019              Our analysis of attention behaviors reveals significant differences in how VLMs
020              allocate attention to image tokens versus text tokens. By visualizing the areas of
021
                 images that receive the highest attention scores throughout intermediate layers, we
                 observe a notable pattern: errors often coincide with attention being misdirected
022
                 towards irrelevant objects within the image. Moreover, such attention patterns
023
                 exhibit substantial differences between familiar (e.g., вҖңon the left side of вҖқ) and
024              unfamiliar (e.g., вҖңin front of вҖқ) spatial relationships. Motivated by these findings,
025              we propose A DAPT V IS based on inference-time confidence scores to sharpen the
026              attention on highly relevant regions when the model exhibits high confidence, while
027              smoothing and broadening the attention window to consider a wider context when
028              confidence is lower. This novel decoding method shows significant improvement
029              (e.g., up to a 50 absolute point improvement) on spatial reasoning benchmarks such
030              as WhatsUp and VSR with negligible additional cost.

033   1      I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Despite rapid advancements in Large Vision-Language Models (VLMs), a significant deficiency
036   persists, i.e., their struggle with vision-centric abilities (Gao et al., 2023; Kamath et al., 2023; Tong
037   et al., 2024a; Chen et al., 2024a). This limitation is particularly notable in spatial reasoning given
038   the simplicity of the task. Spatial reasoning involves inferring basic relationships between just two
039
      objects, such as вҖңleftвҖқ, вҖңrightвҖқ, вҖңaboveвҖқ, вҖңbelowвҖқ, вҖңbehindвҖқ, or вҖңfrontвҖқ, as shown in Figure 1. For
      example, given the image with a book вҖңbehindвҖқ the candle in Figure 1, VLMs describe the book as
040
      being вҖңleftвҖқ of the candle. This error is not an isolated incident but a frequent recurring pattern that
041
      highlights a critical bottleneck in how VLMs process visual information.
042
043   Recent studies have started to probe potential issues in the vision-centric processing of VLMs,
044   questioning whether vision encoders like CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) adequately capture visual
045
      information (Tong et al., 2024b;a). However, a crucial aspect remains underexplored: the intricate
      interplay between vision and text tokens within modelsвҖҷ internal states. This gap underscores the
046
      need for diving deeper into mechanism in the geometric understanding of visual scenes. While
047
      recent work has made progress in the failure analysis of VLMs, investigating why VLMs make
048   errors (Kamath et al., 2023) such as object hallucination, these studies have primarily focused on
049   object-centric tasks to address the alignment between objects and the semantics of concepts. However,
050   spatial reasoning presents a fundamentally different and more challenging problem that has yet to be
051   thoroughly examined through the lens of mechanism interpretability. Spatial reasoning requires not
052   only object identification but also the ability to localize within visual scenes and understand their
053   interaction with text tokens. Therefore, we dive into the internal mechanisms of VLMs, specifically
      examining how vision and text tokens are processed and interact in spatial reasoning.

072   We first hypothesize that if the VLM can adequately ground itself in visual scenes, it should be capable
073
      of localizing objects and understanding their geometric structures within visual scenes. Building on
      this, we further hypothesize that many failures of the VLM stem from how it distributes its attention
074
      over the image tokens. Our focus on attention behavior is motivated by the intuitive connection
075
      between attention mechanisms and spatial localization, particularly regarding how attention correlates
076   with object localization and is influenced by textual tokens. By closely examining the modelвҖҷs
077   attention scores(logits)1 to images, we seek to answer the following questions: 1) What is the
078   potential cause for these failures? 2) Can we detect these failures within the model itself? In
079   other words, how do these failures manifest through internal patterns?
080
      Our systematic analysis of attention scores throughout intermediate layers reveals a critical pattern:
081
      the model tends to вҖңfocusвҖқ on question-relevant entities in the middle to high intermediate layers
082   when it could answer correctly, as evidenced by higher attention scores on these entities. Conversely,
083   failures often correlate with the model attending to irrelevant objects, as shown in Figure 6. Moreover,
084   we observe a pattern in the modelвҖҷs output behavior: it frequently predicts spatial relationships
085   corresponding to the regions receiving the highest attention scores. For instance, if attention scores
086   are higher towards the lower part of the image, the model is likely to output вҖңunderвҖқ. Thus, incorrect
087   outputs may result from modelвҖҷs failure to properly attend to the correct objects. Our analysis also
088   reveals image tokens receive significantly less attention than text tokens. This supports our hypothesis
089   that textual priors often outweigh visual information, hindering performance on vision-centric tasks.
090
      For example, when shown an image of a cup clearly вҖңunderвҖқ a table, VLMs often misinterpret it as
      being on the вҖңleftвҖқ side, likely due to over-reliance on common textual patterns over visual evidence.
091
092   As a result, our goal is to make VLMs look at images cleverly, i.e., sharpening its focus when confident,
093   and broadening or shifting its focus window when the model doubts its predictions. While previous
094   work has shown the effectiveness of guiding model attention using additional object labels (Chen
095   et al., 2024a; Zhang et al., 2024) or external object detectors (Li et al., 2024; You et al., 2024), we
096
      leverage the signal within the model itself to regulate its attention behavior. We further examine
      several signals from the modelвҖҷs internal states to design an inner belief-based metric for guiding
097
      attention, and finally choose the modelвҖҷs generation probability as a reliable indicator of its confidence.
098
      When the model is confident with its own generation (Figure 1) (the probability of generated tokens
099   exceeds a threshold), we trust the attention pattern on the image tokens by sharpening the attention
100   distribution. On the other hand, when the model is not confident, we smooth the image attention
101   distribution to expand the focused context window, thereby increasing the likelihood of focusing on
102   relevant objects. We include the exploration of other potential inner belief-based metrics at Appendix.
103

104
      and VSR (Liu et al., 2023). To clearly observe and interpret modelвҖҷs inner working mechanism
105   during spatial relationship generation, we adopt Question-Answering (QA) settings by reformatting
106   these benchmarks. These benchmarks cover a wide range of image distributions including both

181
182   We analyze how output tokens attend to image tokens by extracting the attention logits across layers,
183
      and present the following key findings: 1) The sum of attention scores to the image tokens is
      significantly lower than that to all the input text tokens, despite the considerably higher number of
184
      image tokens. In Figure 3, we focus on the attention scores from the first generated token and sum the
185
      attention allocated to the image tokens. The results reveal that image tokens receive substantially less
186   attention, with text tokens receiving approximately nine times more. Although the image sequence
187   has a length of 576, compared to the text sequence, which typically ranges from 30 to 40 tokens
188   in our short question-answering setting, the model predominantly focuses on text when generating
189   outputs. In other words, image information is sparsely processed by the language model.
190
      To further investigate, we extract attention scores across the middle layers to observe the flow of
191
      information through the model. As shown in Figure 4, attention to the input image is highest in the
192   initial layers but decreases sharply within the first two layers to a very low value. In the middle layers,
193   we observe a slight increase, with attention reaching a modest peak. This leads to our second finding:
194   2) the model processes the image information primarily through the intermediate layers. Similar
195   observations have been made by Halawi et al. (2023) and Geva et al. (2023), where intermediate
196   layers tend to encode more factual knowledge.

296   Motivated by our observation of attention misallocation causing spatial reasoning errors (Section 5.1)

300   5.1    S CALING V IS : T EMPERATURE SCALING TO IMAGE ATTENTION DISTRIBUTION
301
302   Our observations from Section 4.1 reveal that the model often misallocates attention logits within
303
      images, leading to errors in spatial reasoning. To mitigate this, we aim to improve the modelвҖҷs focus
      on key visual features, enhancing its ability to correctly ground spatial relationships, particularly
304
      in complex or ambiguous scenarios. To this end, we propose a simple yet effective approach that
305
      dynamically adjusts image attention by modifying the temperature of the attention distributions.
306   Specifically, we intervene in the attention of the final input token (n-th position) to the image tokens.

513               7             R ELATED WORK
514
515               The first line of our related work focuses on research into attention patterns in language models.
516               Some studies on attention patterns in LLMs reveal biased attention across context windows, such as
517
                  ineffective use of the middle context (Liu et al., 2024b) and initial token attention sinks (Xiao et al.,
                  2023). While some approaches use fine-tuning to overcome these biases (An et al., 2024), training-
518
                  free methods like input-adaptive calibration (Yu et al., 2024b) and position-specific interventions (Yu
519
                  et al., 2024a) offer efficient alternatives. PASTA (Zhang et al., 2023), a closely related method,
520               emphasizes attention on selected segments for specific heads; we extend this to VLMs without
521               manual segment specification or multiple validation runs. Our work is also related with failure
522               analysis in VLMs, VLMs have been shown to hallucinate more in multi-object recognition tasks
523               and rely on spurious correlations (Chen et al., 2024c), with systematic visual limitations highlighted
524               from a CLIP perspective (Tong et al., 2024b). Our work also connects to the decoding strategies for
525               reducing hallucinations decoding strategies to mitigate hallucinations include contrastive decoding
526               focusing on image regions (Leng et al., 2024), preference tuning through data augmentation (Wang
527               et al., 2024), and methods leveraging contrastive layers for enhanced knowledge extraction (Chuang
528
                  et al., 2023), as well as activation-based optimal answer identification (Chen et al., 2024b).
529               8             C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
530
531
                  Our research uncovers the inner working mechanism of VLMs during spatial reasoning, which
                  is a critical limitation in VLMs and constrains their practical utility when requiring geometric
532
                  understanding of visual scenes. We identify key insights through an in-depth study of attention
533
                  behaviors across layers: 1) VLMs allocate surprisingly insufficient attention to image tokens; 2) the
534               location of attention on image tokens is more crucial than quantity; and 3) generation confidence
535               serves as a reliable indicator of its familiarity with the image and the correctness of its attention
536               pattern. Based on these findings, we propose A DAPT V IS, a novel decoding method that dynamically
537               adjusts attention distribution, significantly improving spatial reasoning performance. Future research
538               could focus on further exploring mechanism interpretability of VLMs on complicated geometric
539               structure understanding, such as long-horizon spatial reasoning, and investigate other reasons for
                  spatial reasoning bottleneck, such as the memorization of training data.

765   A.4   R ELATED WORK
766
767   Attention Patterns in Language Models Ongoing research has shown how large language models
768   (LLMs) exhibit biased attention across different parts of the context window. Liu et al. (2024b)
769
      find that LLMs fail to effectively utilize the information in the middle of a long context window.
      Meanwhile, Xiao et al. (2023) reveals an attention sink at the initial tokens of the input. Besides
770
      finetuning methods to overcome such biases (An et al., 2024), some training-free methods have
771
      been proposed with the benefit of their efficiency. Yu et al. (2024b) proposes to use input-adaptive
772   calibration to adjust the attention scores, while Yu et al. (2024a) intervenes in position-specific
773   hidden dimensions to alleviate the lost-in-the-middle phenomenon. A closely related work to ours
774   is PASTA (Zhang et al., 2023), which emphasizes the attention scores of specific text segments for
775   selected attention heads. We further develop this motivation on vision language models. Moreover,
776   our method does not require a manual specification of the emphasized segment or multiple validation
777   runs to identify effective attention heads.
778
779   Failure Analysis of Vision-Language Models Our work relates to research on hallucination
780   detection in VLMs. Chen et al. (2024c) examine multi-object recognition tasks, observing that
781   VLMs exhibit more hallucinations when dealing with multiple objects compared to single-object
782
      scenarios. They also note a similar phenomenon to our findings: the distribution of tested object
      classes impacts hallucination behaviors, suggesting that VLMs may rely on shortcuts and spurious
783
      correlations. Additionally, Tong et al. (2024b) analyze VLM failures from a CLIP perspective,
784
      highlighting that the visual capabilities of recent VLMs still face systematic shortcomings, partly due
785   to CLIPвҖҷs limitations in specific cases.
786
787
      Decoding Strategies for Reducing Hallucinations This work is also connected to various decoding
788   and tuning strategies aimed at mitigating hallucinations in VLMs. Leng et al. (2024) introduce a
789   contrastive decoding method that emphasizes certain image regions. Wang et al. (2024) propose a
790   data-augmentation approach to create image-intensive datasets, followed by preference tuning on
791   this enhanced data. Furthermore, knowledge extraction techniques such as the method proposed
792   by Chuang et al. (2023) improve decoding by leveraging contrastive layers for better knowledge
793   extraction. Similarly, Activation Decoding (Chen et al., 2024b) identifies optimal answers as those
794   with the highest activation values within the context.
