009                                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011                      As large language models (LLMs) continue to scale, model compression becomes
012                      increasingly important for enabling edge deployment and ensuring accessibility
013                      to users with limited resources. Weight-only quantization is a key technique
014                      for model compression, allowing for a substantial reduction in model size while
015                      preserving performance. However, as bit-width decreases, the performance of
016                      quantized LLMs tends to degrade significantly. Additionally, due to the non-
017
                         differentiable operation in quantization, standard finetuning on quantized LLMs
                         is unsupported, and alternative finetuning approaches often fail to match the ef-
018
                         fectiveness of full finetuning. In this paper, we introduce ClusComp, a novel and
019
                         simple model compression paradigm. ClusComp first clusters the weight matrices
020                      to generate codebooks, and then tunes these codebooks block-by-block to recon-
021                      struct intermediate activations. Despite its simplicity, ClusComp (1) consistently
022                      achieves better performance in 2-4 bit precision; (2) pushes the compression limit
023                      to the 1-bit level, and outperforms existing ultra-low-bit methods with limited
024                      finetuning steps; (3) facilitates seamless and efficient finetuning, surpasses exist-
025                      ing quantization-based or memory-efficient finetuning methods, and even rivals
026                      full finetuning of the FP16 model. Notably, these procedures can be executed on
027                      a single NVIDIA A6000-48GB GPU for LLMs with as many as 70B parameters.

048   1    I NTRODUCTION
049
050   Large language models (LLMs) have garnered significant acclaim and success across various do-
051   mains and applications (Touvron et al., 2023a; Brown et al., 2020; Raffel et al., 2020b). With
052   ongoing advancements, the scope and complexity of released LLMs have witnessed exponential
053   growth, with some LLMs encompassing >50B parameters (Dubey et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2022;
      Scao et al., 2022). This remarkable upscaling introduces considerable challenges, particularly when


                                                                            1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      deploying these models or granting their accessibility to users with constrained resources. To ad-
055   dress these challenges, weight-only post-training quantization (PTQ) has emerged as a promising
056   approach, effectively compressing LLMs to a lower bit while preserving the FP16 performance.
057
058
      PTQ methods can generally be classified into three categories: statistic-based, gradient-based, and
      codebook-based approaches. Statistic-based methods (Dettmers et al., 2024; Lin et al., 2024; Frantar
059
      et al., 2022) determine the quantization grid based on the distribution of the weight values, whereas
060
      gradient-based methods (Shao et al., 2024; Ma et al., 2024) optimize the quantization grid with
061   some calibration samples. Codebook-based methods (Egiazarian et al., 2024; van Baalen et al.,
062   2024; Kim et al., 2024; Park et al., 2024) cluster similar weight elements to the shared quantized
063   centroids, employing non-uniform quantization and pushing the limits to extremely low bit levels.
064   However, these methods continue to struggle with low-bit quantization and the presence of outliers,
065   leading to significant performance degradation, especially in models like Llama-3 (Dubey et al.,
066   2024), which exhibit a large number of outliers in their weight matrices (Huang et al., 2024c).
067   Another challenge PTQs encounter is their limited support for finetuning, which is crucial for adapt-
068   ing LLMs to various downstream tasks. Finetuning LLMs is computationally expensive due to
069   their large scale and the need to cache activations and store optimizer states. PTQ, which com-
070   presses LLMs, appears to be a promising approach for finetuning as it reduces memory require-
071   ments for loading these LLMs. However, most quantization techniques use a round-to-nearest op-
072   eration, which does not support gradient back-propagation. Typically, parameter-efficient methods
073   (Dettmers et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024c; Liao & Monz, 2024a) are employed to train the added pa-
074   rameters while keeping the quantized LLMs frozen, bypassing this limitation. Nonetheless, this
075
      finetuning approach presents two major drawbacks: (1) Freezing the quantized LLMs prevents fur-
      ther reduction of quantization errors during finetuning; (2) The low-rank nature of most parameter-
076
      efficient methods restricts their expressiveness (Biderman et al., 2024; Liao & Monz, 2024b).
077
078   In this paper, we propose a simple while effective paradigm that mainly applies Clustering to
079   Compress LLMs, referred to as ClusComp. Additionally, ClusComp can function as a parame-
080   ter and memory-efficient finetuning method. Our preliminary experiments reveal that open-source
081
      LLMs are increasingly difficult to quantize, primarily due to the growing frequency of outliers in
      their weight matrices (§3.1). Based on this observation, we propose using clustering instead of quan-
082
      tization to compress LLMs, retaining all values in FP16 format to circumvent issues arising from
083
      outlier quantization (§3.2.1). To further reduce compression errors, we minimize block-wise output
084   discrepancies between the compressed and uncompressed blocks, using a limited set of calibration
085   samples (§3.2.3). Since all parameters remain in FP16 after compression, ClusComp fully supports
086   standard neural network training. By incorporating an inexpensive, end-to-end recovery finetuning
087   step, we can push compression rates to the 1-bit level. Additionally, ClusComp allows for finetuning
088   compressed LLMs on various downstream tasks (§3.2.4).
089
      We begin by evaluating the effectiveness of ClusComp in the context of model compression across 2
090   language modeling tasks and 6 zero-shot reasoning tasks. ClusComp consistently surpasses various
091   baselines at 2-4 levels, even achieving a perplexity of <13 at the 2-bit level on WikiText2 (Merity
092   et al., 2017) for all LLMs (§4.1). Following recovery finetuning, ClusComp’s performance at 2-bit
093   and 1-bit levels approaches that of the FP16 model, with an accuracy of 57.8 vs 68.6 for the 2-bit
094   Llama-3-8B and 51.4 vs 75.4 for the 1-bit Llama-3-70B (§4.2). Additionally, ClusComp demon-
095   strates its utility as a parameter-efficient (< 1%) and memory-efficient (42GB for Llama-3-70B)
096   finetuning method, outperforming quantization-based and memory-efficient finetuning approaches,
097   while matching the performance of full finetuning (§4.3).

103   2.1   M ODEL COMPRESSION
104
105
      Quantization and pruning are two typical and effective methods for model compression.
106
107   Quantization refers to the process of converting floating-point values into discrete levels, thereby
      reducing the bit-width required and minimizing memory consumption during model loading. Taking


                                                       2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      the symmetric uniform quantization as an example, a weight matrix W is quantized as follows:
109
                                  W                                       max(|Wmin |, |Wmax |)
110               Wq = clamp(⌊       ⌉, −2b−1 , 2b−1 − 1) with s =                                       (1)
111                                s                                             2b − 1
112   where b denotes the bit-width, s is the scale factor, and ⌊⌉ represents the round-to-nearest (RTN)
113   operation. Since the quantization grid is uniform, its effectiveness is contingent on the distribution
114   of the weight values. In cases where the weight matrix contains a significant number of outliers or
115   is quantized to lower bit-widths, the resulting quantization error may be substantial.
116   Post-training quantization (PTQ) methods, such as GPTQ (Frantar et al., 2022), AWQ (Lin et al.,
117   2024), and OmniQuant (Shao et al., 2024), apply quantization to a model after training with mini-
118   mal computational resources. However, these approaches, which rely on uniform quantization, are
119   significantly impacted by the presence of outliers in the weight matrices. Recent methods (Dettmers
120   et al., 2024; Yuan et al., 2024; Huang et al., 2024a) address this challenge by retaining salient weights
121   in FP16 format, thereby maintaining strong performance at lower bit widths. Nonetheless, these
122
      mixed-precision quantization techniques require specially optimized CUDA kernels to either en-
      hance or preserve inference speed. Closely related to our proposed method, ClusComp, are works
123
      such as GPTVQ (van Baalen et al., 2024), QuIP# (Tseng et al., 2024) and SqueezeLLM (Kim et al.,
124
      2024) which implement quantized codebooks for non-uniform quantization, achieving state-of-the-
125   art performance for ultra-low-bit quantization. ClusComp, however, differs in two significant ways:
126   (1) The codebook in ClusComp is stored in FP16, offering additional advantages for subsequent re-
127   covery training and finetuning; (2) While other methods face limitations similar to those in VAE-like
128   approaches (Kingma & Welling, 2014), where large and high-dimensional codebooks are infeasible
129   due to mode collapse, ClusComp circumvents this issue. Our fixed-code design allows us to utilize
130   a codebook size of 216 in 4-16D without encountering such difficulty.
131   Pruning is a widely used model compression technique that removes redundant weights or struc-
132   tures from the model (Sun et al., 2024a; Xia et al., 2024; Frantar & Alistarh, 2023; Liao et al.,
133   2023). It often leads to significant degradation as sparsity increases, and generally yields inferior
134   results at equivalent compression rates compared to quantization. Nevertheless, pruning offers an
135   advantage for training, as all parameters remain in high precision, allowing for seamless integration
136   with continuous pretraining or finetuning. Similarly, ClusComp retains high-precision parameters,
137   and naturally supports standard finetuning.
138
139   2.2   K NOWLEDGE DISTILLATION
140
      Knowledge distillation is a technique used to enhance the performance of smaller models by trans-
141
      ferring knowledge from larger, more complex models (Hinton et al., 2015). Most state-of-the-art
142
      quantization methods leverage either block-wise or model-wise distillation. Block-wise distillation
143   (as employed in OmniQuant, GPTVQ, AQLM and QuiP#) focuses on minimizing errors between
144   the FP16 and quantized models on a block-by-block basis. This approach is more memory-efficient
145   than model-wise distillation, as it requires loading only two blocks into the GPU at the same time.
146   In contrast, model-wise distillation (used in QuiP# and LLM-QAT (Liu et al., 2024)) minimizes the
147   error across the entire model output, necessitating the loading of at least one full FP16 model into the
148   GPU. ClusComp adopts block-wise distillation, significantly reducing GPU memory requirements
149   compared to loading an FP16 model. As demonstrated in Figure 1, ClusComp consumes only 26GB
150   memory for a 70B LLM, which would otherwise require 140GB in FP16 for loading, making our
151
      technique more accessible to users with limited computational resources.
152
153   2.3   F INETUNE QUANTIZED MODEL
154
      Finetuning is crucial for adapting LLMs to various domains and applications. Quantization, which
155
      reduces model size, is theoretically more conducive to finetuning. However, directly finetuning a
156   quantized model is not a standard approach, as RTN does not support gradient back-propagation.
157   Finetuning using a straight-through estimator (STE) (Bengio et al., 2013) is relatively under-
158   explored and may lead to catastrophic forgetting (Malinovskii et al., 2024). Previous works (Xu
159   et al., 2024a; Liao & Monz, 2024a; Dettmers et al., 2023) propose freezing the quantized model
160   while updating newly added LoRAs (Hu et al., 2022). However, these approaches suffer from
161   two key limitations: (1) Freezing the quantized model prevents the mitigation of quantization er-
      rors during finetuning. Moreover, not all quantization methods are suitable for finetuning. Popular

184   3.1             P ILOT STUDY
185
186   Before introducing ClusComp, we present a key observation from our experiments on different
187   Llama series. As depicted in Figure 2 (Left & Middle), when reducing the bit-width, the perfor-
188   mance of various quantization methods (RTN, GPTQ, and AWQ) follows a similar trend: Llama-3
189   (Dubey et al., 2024) proves more challenging to quantize than Llama-2 (Touvron et al., 2023b).
190   We hypothesize that the increased difficulty arises from a higher frequency of outliers in the linear
191   layers of Llama-3. Since these quantization methods rely on uniform quantization, they are particu-
192   larly sensitive to outliers in the weight matrices. To test this hypothesis, we analyzed the kurtosis of
193   the weight matrices—a well-established metric for identifying the presence of outliers (Bondarenko
194   et al., 2023). As shown in Figure 2 (Right), we have two key observations: (1) All models exhibit
195   higher kurtosis at the beginning and end of the model; (2) From Llama-1 to Llama-3, the kurtosis
196   increases in most layers, indicating a rise in the frequency of outliers in the linear layers. This trend
197
      provides a potential explanation for our quantization difficulties. It also implies that the future Llama
      series might be even more difficult for quantization.1
198
199   Given that the quantization performance is impacted by outliers, could an alternative approach for
200   model compression involve storing all weight values in FP16 instead of applying quantization?

202   3.2             C LUS C OMP

204   The first idea that comes to our mind is clustering, where similar weight values are represented
205   by a single identified value. This method enables model compression while preserving all weight
206   values in FP16 format. In this section, we introduce three variants of ClusComp that primarily
207
      utilize clustering for compressing LLMs: ClusComp− , which applies clustering alone; ClusComp,
      which enhances ClusComp− with block-wise error minimization; and ClusComp+ , which further
208
      improves the compressed LLMs through next-token prediction training based on ClusComp.

275   3.2.3    B LOCK - WISE ERROR MINIMIZATION
276
277
      Block-wise error minimization (block-wise reconstruction or knowledge distillation) has emerged as
      a standard, efficient and effective approach to reducing quantization error (Egiazarian et al., 2024;
278
      Tseng et al., 2024; van Baalen et al., 2024; Shao et al., 2024; Liao & Monz, 2024a). To further
279
      mitigate the compression error caused by clustering, we incorporate block-wise error minimization
280   into ClusComp− using a limited set of calibration samples, expressed as:
281
                                   arg minCs ||F(W s, X) − F(Cs, qs, X ′ )||                            (4)
282
      Here, F denotes a Transformer block (Vaswani et al., 2017), W s represent the weight matrices in
283
      the uncompressed block, and Cs and qs denote the codebooks and codes in the compressed block.
284
      X refers to the input of the uncompressed block, which is also the output from the previous un-
285   compressed block, while X ′ is the input to the compressed block, originating from the output of the
286   preceding compressed block. For the first block, we have X = X ′ . Block-wise error minimization
287   is memory-efficient as it only requires loading two blocks into the GPU simultaneously.
288
      Remark: In Equation (4), we only train the codebook Cs while keeping the codes qs fixed as indices.
289
      This design offers two key advantages: (1) It enhances data efficiency. As illustrated in Table 1, the
290
      majority of parameters are represented by the codes. Training both the codebooks and codes with a
291   limited number of calibration samples (128) leads to overfitting; (2) More importantly, training the
292   codes with a large number of centroids (216 ) can result in mode collapse (Sun et al., 2024b; Kingma
293   & Welling, 2014). Since the codes already exhibit a uniform distribution after clustering (see Figure
294   3), keeping the codes fixed indicates that all centroids in the codebook can be trained uniformly.
295   Such a code-fixed design is also applied to the following recovery and finetuning step. Combining
296   both clustering and block-wise error minimization steps, we term this method ClusComp.

298   3.2.4    R ECOVERY AND FINETUNING
299
      We present the adapted linear layer for ClusComp in Listing C.1, which can be seamlessly integrated
300   as a replacement for the original linear layer in LLMs. As the codebook is represented in FP16, this
301   new layer inherently supports training without requiring additional tricks, like STE.
302
303
      Recovery training. The compressed LLMs can be further trained by predicting the next token to
      recover information lost due to compression. This is achieved by finetuning the codebook parame-
304
      ters. This form of training is memory-efficient in two distinct ways: (1) Since the LLM is already
305
      compressed, loading it onto the GPU consumes less memory compared to the FP16 version; (2)
306   As illustrated in Table 1, the parameters in the codebook account for < 5% of the total parameters
307   in the FP16 version, making the training both parameter-efficient and memory-efficient (with the
308   optimizer states being smaller). We refer to ClusComp with recovery training as ClusComp+ .
309
      Finetuning. Like recovery training, finetuning the compressed LLM on downstream tasks can also
310
      be performed efficiently. Unlike QLoRA (Dettmers et al., 2023), which freezes the quantized LLM
311
      and trains only the LoRA (Hu et al., 2022) modules, finetuning the codebook alone eliminates the
312   need for this additional constraint. This approach offers two key advantages over QLoRA: (1) Freez-
313   ing the quantized LLM prevents mitigation of quantization errors, whereas finetuning the codebook
314   can further address compression errors for downstream tasks; (2) The low-rank bottleneck of LoRA
315   limits its expressiveness (Biderman et al., 2024). In contrast, finetuning the codebook is analogous
316   to adapting the entire high-rank weight matrix, providing greater flexibility and expressiveness.

513   from ClusComp outperforms all memory-efficient finetuning methods, and rivals full finetuning. In
514   addition, the finetuned LLMs can be used in a low bit, friendly for inference.
515   The superior finetuning performance can be attributed to three key advantages of ClusComp: (1)
516   ClusComp introduces smaller compression errors; (2) Unlike QLoRA, where compressed LLMs
517   are frozen during finetuning, ClusComp allows for the model to remain unfrozen by training the
518   codebook parameters, enabling further mitigation of compression errors; (3) The low-rank design
519   of LoRA limits its expressiveness. In contrast, updating the codebook in ClusComp is analogous to
520   updating a high-rank weight matrix. In addition, the fixed-code design allows uniform training of
521   all centroids, providing greater expressiveness and can even rival full finetuning.
522   Efficiency discussion. Figure 5 illustrates the memory efficiency of ClusComp during training, with
523   a batch size of 1 and a sequence length of 1024. For the 70B LLM, we apply gradient checkpointing
524   (this is not used for the 7B LLM), while omitting any additional memory-saving techniques.
525
      Finetuning the LLM compressed by ClusComp demonstrates memory efficiency in two key ways:
526
      (1) The compressed LLM requires less memory for loading onto the GPU compared to the FP16
527   model; and (2) Only the codebook parameters, which contain a limited number of trainable param-
528   eters (< 1%), are updated, as detailed in Table 7. Consequently, the optimizer state size remains
529   small. ClusComp can serve not only as a model compression technique but also as an effective
530   method for both memory- and parameter-efficient finetuning.

534
535   The newly introduced model compression technique, ClusComp, operates by (1) independently ap-
536   plying clustering to the weight matrices to produce both the codebook and corresponding codes,
537   (2) reducing compression error through block-wise knowledge distillation, and (3) enhancing model
538   performance via efficient recovery finetuning. Comprehensive experiments demonstrate its effec-
539   tiveness as a compression method at 1-4 bit levels, while also showcasing its parameter and memory
      efficiency for finetuning, with a competitive performance with full finetuning.

      A        L IMITATION AND FUTURE WORK
919
920
      Limitation. Although ClusComp demonstrates strong performance in both model compression and
921   finetuning tasks, its inference speed remains similar to that of FP16 models. As illustrated in Listing
922   C.1, ClusComp introduces two additional operations—indexing and reshaping—beyond those found
923   in a standard linear layer. These operations are computationally efficient, resulting in an inference
924   speed that is similar to that of the original linear layer. Since no quantization techniques are applied,
925   the transfer of weight tensors does not contribute to time savings, resulting in a smaller inference
926   speed than the uniform quantization methods. Nevertheless, we consider this trade-off acceptable
927   given the model’s notable performance in compression and finetuning.
928   Future work. The following list of tasks is in our plan:
929
930             • Design a new CUDA kernel that is more efficient for ClusComp.
931             • Apply ClusComp to reduce the memory requirement for caching the keys and values, which
932               facilitates LLMs for long-context tasks.
933
                • Apply the fixed-code idea to a VAE-like method to scale up the size and dimension of the
934
                  codebook for the image generation task.
935
936             • Study the quantization of large multimodal models, since they show different behaviors
937               from LLMs (see Table 3).

1081   class ClusCompLinear(nn.Module):
           def __init__(self, in_features, out_features, num_clusters, cluster_dim, bias):
1082           super().__init__()
               self.out_features = out_features
1083           self.in_features = in_features
               self.deficiency = out_features % cluster_dim # If the out_features is not dividable by cluster_dim
1084           if self.deficiency > 0:
1085               self.deficiency = cluster_dim - self.deficiency

1086           num_codes = in_features * (out_features + self.deficiency) // cluster_dim
               self.codebook = nn.Parameter(torch.empty((num_clusters, cluster_dim), dtype=torch.bfloat16) #
1087                 trainable
1088           code = torch.empty((num_codes,), dtype=torch.uint16)
               self.register_buffer(’code’, code) # non-trainable
1089           if bias:
                   self.bias = nn.Parameter(torch.empty(out_features))
1090           else:
                   self.register_parameter(’bias’, None)
1091
1092      def forward(self, x):
              vectors = self.codebook[self.code]
1093          if self.deficiency > 0:
                  weight = vectors.view(self.in_features, -1)[:, :-self.deficiency]
1094          else:
1095              weight = vectors.view(self.in_features, -1)

1096           if self.bias is not None:
                   out = torch.matmul(x, weight) + self.bias
1097           else:
                   out = torch.matmul(x, weight)
1098           return out

1388   To illustrate how ClusComp effectively simulates the original weight distribution, we compare it
1389   (non-uniform compression) to OmniQuant (uniform quantization) in Figure D.1. The figures demon-

1394   D.3   Q UANTIZATION DIFFICULTY TREND OF L LAMA SERIES
1395
1396   Previous works (Lin et al., 2024; Sun et al., 2024a; Heo et al., 2024) suggest that weight patterns can
1397   be identified based on activations rather than relying solely on weight magnitudes. Following a rec-
1398   ommendation from Reviewer wLyR at ICLR 2025, we incorporate an additional analysis based on
1399   the Wanda score (Sun et al., 2024a) distribution to illustrate the increasing challenges of quantization
1400
       across the Llama series.
1401   Given the weight matrix W ∈ Rdout ×din of a linear layer and the input activations X ∈ RN L×din ,
1402   where N and L represent the batch and sequence dimensions, the Wanda score S ∈ Rdout ×din is
1403   computed as Sij = |Wij | · ||Xj ||2 . A smaller Wanda score within a row of W (on a per-output
       basis) indicates a less significant weight element.

       D.4                 Q UANTIZATION OF THE CODEBOOK
1478
1479
1480   Thanks to the suggestion of Reviewer h5Zw at ICLR 2025, we conduct further quantization on the
1481
       codebook C. Originally, the data type in the codebook was 16-bit, which facilitates our following
       recovery training or finetuning step. However, if we can further quantize the codebook, we have
1482
       two additional advantages: (1) The model size can be further slightly reduced (Only slightly, since
1483
       the majority of bits is allocated to the code q.); (2) The inference can speed up, since the codebook
1484   becomes smaller. In sum, we can keep the codebook in 16-bit if we want to do the recovery training
1485   or finetuning. If we are only interested in inference, we can further quantize the codebook to a lower
1486   bit.
1487
       As shown in Table D.3, the performance doesn’t change if we quantize the codebook from 16-
1488
       bit to 8-bit. When quantizing the codebook to 4-bit, the perplexity slightly increases, but is still
1489
       comparable to the best baseline at the 4-bit level and outperforms the best baseline at the 2-bit level.
1490   However, if we further quantize the codebook to the 2-bit level, the perplexity increases significantly.
1491   Therefore, we can safely quantize the codebook to 8-bit or 4-bit.

       D.5                 A BLATION STUDY ON THE GROUP SIZE AND NUMBER OF CLUSTERS
1513
1514   Thanks to the suggestion of Reviewer h5Zw at ICLR 2025, we conducted experiments to determine
1515   whether the number of clusters n or the cluster dimension g has a greater impact on the performance
1516   of quantized LLMs. As shown in Table D.4, increasing n positively affects performance more than
1517   reducing g. This finding underpins our choice of n ≈ 216 . However, while n plays a crucial
1518   role in enhancing performance, selecting n > 216 would necessitate using 32-bit storage for the
1519   code q, substantially increasing the bits-per-parameter and adversely affecting memory efficiency.
1520
       Therefore, we always choose n < 216 .
