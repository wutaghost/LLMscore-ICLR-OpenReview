009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Traditional knowledge distillation focuses on aligning the student’s predicted
012            probabilities with both ground-truth labels and the teacher’s predicted probabil-
013            ities. However, the transition to predicted probabilities from logits would obscure
014            certain indispensable information. To address this issue, it is intuitive to addi-
015            tionally introduce a logit-level loss function as a supplement to the widely used
016            probability-level loss function, for exploiting the latent information of logits. Un-
017            fortunately, we empirically find that the amalgamation of the newly introduced
018
               logit-level loss and the previous probability-level loss will lead to performance
               degeneration, even trailing behind the performance of employing either loss in
019
               isolation. We attribute this phenomenon to the collapse of the classification head,
020
               which is verified by our theoretical analysis based on the neural collapse theory.
021            Specifically, the gradients of the two loss functions exhibit contradictions in the
022            linear classifier yet display no such conflict within the backbone. Drawing from
023            the theoretical analysis, we propose a novel method called dual-head knowledge
024            distillation, which partitions the linear classifier into two classification heads re-
025            sponsible for different losses, thereby preserving the beneficial effects of both
026            losses on the backbone while eliminating adverse influences on the classification
027            head. Extensive experiments validate that our method can effectively exploit the
028            information inside the logits and achieve superior performance against state-of-
029
               the-art counterparts.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
      Despite the remarkable success of deep neural networks (DNNs) in various fields, it is a significant
034
      challenge to deploy these large models in lightweight terminals (e.g., mobile phones), particularly
035
      under the constraint of computational resources or the requirement of short inference time. To mit-
036   igate this problem, knowledge distillation (KD) (Hinton et al., 2015) is widely investigated, which
037   aims to improve the performance of a small network (a.k.a. the “student”) by leveraging the expan-
038   sive knowledge of a large network (a.k.a. the “teacher”) to guide the training of the student network.
039
      Traditional KD techniques focus on minimizing the disparity in the predicted probabilities between
040
      the teacher and the student, which are typically the outputs of the softmax function. Nevertheless,
041
      the transformation from logits to predictive probabilities via the softmax function may lose some
042   underlying information. As shown in Figure 1(a), considering a 3-class classification problem, even
043   if the teacher model outputs two different logit vectors [2, 3, 4] and [−2, −1, 0], the softmax function
044   renders the same probability vector [0.09, 0.24, 0.67]. However, different logit vectors may carry
045   different underlying information that would be further exploited by the student, which could be lost
046   due to the transformation process carried out by the softmax function.
047
      In order to properly leverage the information inside the logits, we introduce a logit-level KD loss.
048   Specifically, we use the sigmoid function to formalize the pre-softmax output for each class into a
049   range of [0, 1] and deploy the Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence to perform the binary classification
050   of each class. By aligning the pre-softmax output of each class separately, this newly introduced
051   loss (denoted by BinaryKL) can adequately exploit the information inside the logits. However, it
052   is interesting to show that combining the logit-level BinaryKL loss with the probability-level cross-
053   entropy (CE) loss results in poor performance of the student model, which is even worse than the
      performance of employing either loss separately. As illustrated in Figure 1(b), employing either

096
      Our main contributions can be summarized as follows:
097
098    • We disclose an interesting phenomenon in the knowledge distillation scenario: combining the
099      probability-level CE loss and the logit-level BinaryKL loss would cause a performance drop of
100      the student model, compared with using either loss separately.
101    • We provide theoretical analyses to explain the discordance between the BinaryKL loss and the
102      CE loss. While the BinaryKL loss aids in cultivating a stronger backbone, it harms the perfor-
103      mance of the linear classifier head.
104
105
       • We propose a novel knowledge distillation method called Dual-Head Knowledge Distillation
         (DHKD). Apart from the linear classifier trained with the CE loss, DHKD specially introduces
106
         an auxiliary classifier trained with the BinaryKL loss.
107
      Extensive experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of our proposed method.

      2.1   K NOWLEDGE D ISTILLATION
111
112   Knowledge distillation (KD) (Hinton et al., 2015) aims to transfer knowledge from a large teacher
113   network to a small student network. Existing works can be roughly divided into two groups: feature-
114   based methods and logit-based methods.
115
      Feature-based methods focus on distilling knowledge from intermediate feature layers. FitNet
116   (Romero et al., 2015) is the first approach to distill knowledge from intermediate features by mea-
117   suring the distance between feature maps. RKD (Park et al., 2019) utilizes the relations among
118   instances to guide the training process of the student model. CRD (Tian et al., 2019) incorporates
119   contrastive learning into knowledge distillation. OFD (Heo et al., 2019) contains a new distance
120   function to distill significant information between the teacher and student using marginal ReLU.
121   ReviewKD (Chen et al., 2021) proposes a review mechanism that uses multiple layers in the teacher
122   to supervise one layer in the student. Other papers (Passalis & Tefas, 2018; Kim et al., 2018; Ko-
123   ratana et al., 2019; Li, 2022; Liu et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023; Roy Miles & Deng, 2024; Miles
124
      & Mikolajczyk, 2024) enforce various criteria based on features. Most feature-based methods can
      attain superior performance, yet involving considerably high computational and storage costs.
125
126   Logit-based methods mainly concentrate on distilling knowledge from logits and softmax scores
127   after logits. DML (Zhang et al., 2018b) introduces a mutual learning method to train both teachers
128   and students simultaneously. DKD (Zhao et al., 2022) proposes a novel logit-based method to refor-
129   mulate the classical KD loss into two parts and achieves state-of-the-art performance by adjusting
130
      weights for these two parts. DIST (Huang et al., 2022) relaxes the exact matching in previous KL
      divergence loss with a correlation-based loss and performs better when the discrepancy between the
131
      teacher and the student is large. TTM (Zheng & Yang, 2024) drops the temperature scaling on the
132
      student side, which causes an inherent Renyi entropy term as an extra regularization term in the loss
133   function. Although logit-based methods require fewer computational and storage resources, they
134   experience a performance gap compared with feature-based methods.

      2.2   N EURAL C OLLAPSE
137
138   Neural collapse is a phenomenon observed in the late stages of training a deep neural network,
139   which is especially evident in the classification tasks (Papyan et al., 2020). As the training process
140   approaches its optimum, the feature representations of data points belonging to the same class tend
141   to converge to a single point, or at least become significantly more similar to each other in the
142   feature space. At the same time, the class mean vectors tend to be equidistant and form a simplex
143   equiangular tight frame (see a detailed definition in Definition 1).
144   Although the phenomenon is intuitive, its reason has not been entirely understood, which inspires
145   several lines of theoretical work on it. Papyan et al. (2020) prove that if the features satisfy neural
146   collapse, the optimal classifier vectors under the MSE loss will also converge to neural collapse.
147   Some studies turn to a simplified model that only considers the last-layer features and the classifier
148   as independent variables, and they prove that neural collapse emerges under the CE loss with proper
149   constraints or regularization (Fang et al., 2021; Ji et al., 2022; Lu & Steinerberger, 2022; Weinan E,
150
      2022; Zhu et al., 2021). Yang et al. (2022) also use such a simplified model and decompose the
      gradient into two parts to help build a better classifier for class-imbalanced learning, which is the
151
      primary technical reference for the theoretical analysis section of this article.

      2.3   D ECOUPLED H EADS
155
      Using multiple linear classifiers is a common strategy in various fields. In object detection, to
156   address the conflict between classification and regression tasks, the decoupled head for classification
157   and localization is widely used in most one-stage and two-stage detectors (Ge et al., 2021; Wu
158   et al., 2020; Lin et al., 2017). In multitask learning, it is common practice to jointly train various
159   tasks through the shared backbone (Caruana, 1997; Zheng et al., 2022; Kendall et al., 2018). In
160   long-tailed learning, the Bilateral-Branch Network takes care of both representation learning and
161   classifier learning concurrently, where each branch performs its own duty separately (Zhou et al.,
      2020).

238   Papyan et al. (2020) revealed the neural collapse phenomenon, where the last-layer features converge
239   to their within-class means, and the within-class means together with the classifier vectors collapse to
240   the vertices of a simplex equiangular tight frame (ETF) at the terminal phase of training. According
241   to Papyan et al. (2020), all vectors in a simplex ETF have an equal ℓ2 norm and the same pairwise
242   angle. An illustration of a simplex ETF is shown in Figure 3. The neural collapse phenomenon
243   (Papyan et al., 2020) can be characterized by four manifestations: (1) the variability of the last-
244   layer features in the same class collapses to zero; (2) the class means of different classes’ last-layer
245   features converge to a simplex ETF; (3) the linear classifier vectors collapse to the class means; (4)
246
      the task of predicting for classification can be simplified into finding out the nearest class center
      of the last-layer feature. The detailed definition of a simplex ETF and the features of the neural
247
      collapse phenomenon can be found in Appendix B.1.
248

      3.3     O UR P ROPOSED M ETHOD
311
312   As demonstrated in our theoretical analysis, the BinaryKL loss facilitates the backbone in learning a
313   better backbone but impedes the linear classifier from achieving an Equiangular Tight Frame status.
314   To fully leverage the positive effects of the BinaryKL loss while mitigating its negative impact,
315   we propose a novel method called Dual-Head Knowledge Distillation (DHKD), which restricts the
316   effects of the BinaryKL loss to the backbone, excluding the original linear classifier. For a given
317   input x, we can get a feature h = f (x) through the backbone f (·). Just as Figure 4 shows, we use
318   two classifiers for different losses: g(·) for the CE loss and g ′ (·) for the BinaryKL loss.

320   neighborhood of the teacher output (which means it is close to the optimization goal), the derivative
321   of the BinaryKL loss at the student output also depends on the value of the teacher output. As a
322   result, the optimization progress cannot be synchronized among distinct teacher outputs with the
323   same τ . Although using different τ in different settings can lead to state-of-the-art performance, it
      would introduce more hyper-parameters. To mitigate this issue, we use a variant of the BinaryKL

476   5     C ONCLUSION
477
478
      This paper studied the problem of knowledge distillation. We provided the attempt to combine
479
      the BinaryKL loss with the CE loss, while our empirical findings indicated that merging the two
480   losses results in degraded performance, even falling below the performance of using either loss
481   independently. Inspired by previous research on neural collapse, we theoretically demonstrated that
482   while the BinaryKL loss improves the efficacy of the backbone, it conflicts with the CE loss at the
483   level of the linear classifier, thereby leading the model to a sub-optimal situation. To address this
484   issue, we proposed a novel method called Dual-Head Knowledge Distillation, which separates the
485   linear classifier into two distinct parts, each responsible for a specific loss. Experimental results on
      benchmark datasets confirmed the effectiveness of our proposed method.

      D    C OMPARISON BETWEEN B INARY KL AND B INARY KL-N ORM
919
920
      The original BinaryKL loss encounters an issue wherein, as the student output approaches a small
921   neighborhood of the teacher output-—indicative of nearing the optimization target—-the derivative
922   of the BinaryKL loss at the student output also depends on the value of the teacher output. As a
923   result, the optimization progress cannot be synchronized with distinct teacher outputs, which may
924   lead to sub-optimal performance.
925
      From Table 8 and 9, we can find that using the original BinaryKL loss with a fixed τ = 2 cannot
926
      achieve the best performance. With the best τ for different settings, the original BinaryKL loss can
927   achieve comparable performance with the BinaryKL-Norm loss with a fixed τ = 2. Consequently,
928   we choose to use the BinaryKL-Norm loss to decrease the number of hyper-parameters.

959   To validate the proposed method, we compare it with the following sota KD methods:
960
961   • FitNet (Romero et al., 2015), the first approach distills knowledge from intermediate features by
962     measuring the distance between feature maps;
963   • RKD (Park et al., 2019), which captures the relations among instances to guide the training of the
964     student model;
965   • CRD (Tian et al., 2019), which incorporates contrastive learning into knowledge distillation;
966
      • OFD (Heo et al., 2019), which introduces a new distance function to align features in the same
967
        stage between the teacher and student using marginal ReLU;
968
969   • ReviewKD (Chen et al., 2021), which transfers knowledge across different stages instead of just
970
        focusing on features in the same levels;
971   • DKD (Zhao et al., 2022), which decouples the vanilla KD method into two parts and assigns
        different weights to different parts;

       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025

       • DIST (Huang et al., 2022), which relaxes the exact matching in previous KL divergence loss with
973      a correlation-based loss and performs better when the discrepancy between teacher and student is
974      large.

977    F    I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
978
979    We perform experiments on two benchmark datasets: CIFAR-100 (Krizhevsky & Hinton, 2009) and
980    ImageNet (Deng et al., 2009). CIFAR-100 covers 100 categories. It contains 50,000 images in the
981    train set and 10,000 images in the test set. ImageNet covers 1,000 categories of images. It contains
982
       1.28 million images in the train set and 50,000 images in the test set.
983    CIFAR-100: Teachers and students are trained for 240 epochs with SGD, and the batch size is 64.
984    The learning rates are 0.01 for ShuffleNet (Zhang et al., 2018a; Ma et al., 2018) and MobileNet-
985    V2 (Sandler et al., 2018), and 0.05 for the other series (e.g. VGG(Simonyan & Zisserman, 2015),
986    ResNet (He et al., 2016) and WRN (Zagoruyko & Komodakis, 2016)). The learning rate is divided
987    by 10 at the 150th, 180th, and 210th epochs. The weight decay and the momentum are set to 5e-4 and
988
       0.9. The weight for the CE loss is set to 1.0, and the temperature is set to 2 for all experiments. α is
       set as 0.1 for (resnet56→resnet20, resnet110→ resnet32) and is chosen from {0.5, 1, 2} for all other
989
       experiments. We choose a linear classifier as the auxiliary classifier for students having the same
990
       architectures as their teachers. A one-hidden-layer MLP with 200 hidden neurons is deployed as the
991    auxiliary classifier for students with different architectures from their teachers. All the experiments
992    on CIFAR-100 are conducted on GeForce RTX 3090 GPUs.
993
994
       ImageNet: Our implementation for ImageNet follows the standard practice. We train the models
       for 100 epochs. The batch size is 256, and the learning rate is initialized to 0.1 and divided by 10
995
       for every 30 epochs. Weight decay is 1e-4, and the weight for the CE loss is set to 1.0. We set the
996
       temperature as 2 and α as 0.1 for all experiments. We only use the BinaryKL loss in the first 50
997    epochs of the training phase, which means that we set α = 0 for the last 50 epochs. We do not use
998    the gradient alignment method on ImageNet because it would seriously slow down the training
999    speed, and we can still achieve the SOTA performance without it. Strictly following (Zhao et al.,
1000   2022), for distilling networks of the same architecture, the teacher is ResNet-34 model, the student
1001   is ResNet-18; for different series, the teacher is ResNet-50 model, the student is MobileNet-V1. All
1002   the experiments on ImageNet are conducted on H100 PCIe GPUs.
