032                                                                  A BSTRACT
033
034                The outstanding performance of Large Multimodal Models (LMMs) has made
035                them widely applied in vision-related tasks. However, various corruptions in the
036                real world mean that images will not be as ideal as in simulations, presenting
037                significant challenges for the practical application of LMMs. To address this is-
038                sue, we introduce R-Bench, a benchmark focused on the Real-world Robustness
039                of LMMs. Specifically, we: (a) model the complete link from user capture to
040
                   LMMs reception, comprising 33 corruption dimensions, including 7 steps accord-
                   ing to the corruption sequence, and 7 groups based on low-level attributes; (b)
041
                   collect reference/distorted image dataset before/after corruption, including 2,970
042
                   question-answer pairs with human labeling; (c) propose comprehensive evalua-
043                tion for absolute/relative robustness and benchmark 20 mainstream LMMs. Re-
044                sults show that while LMMs can correctly handle the original reference images,
045                their performance is not stable when faced with distorted images, and there is a
046                significant gap in robustness compared to the human visual system. We hope that
047                R-Bench will inspire improving the robustness of LMMs, extending them from
048                experimental simulations to the real-world application.

051   1     I NTRODUCTION
052
053   Large Multimodal Models (LMMs) have demonstrated outstanding abilities in a wide range of vi-
      sual tasks. Due to the cross-modal interaction capabilities brought by instruction tuning, they can


                                                                                1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      accurately understand visual information and provide precise feedback based on queries. However,
055   unlike single-modal Large Language Models (LLMs), which have become the foundation of daily
056   life for humans, satisfying diverse needs such as writing, searching, and coding, LMMs, despite their
057   excellent performance, have not yet reached the same status. From the neural perception perspective
058   (Zhang et al., 2022), the visual cortex accounts for at least 70% of the external information process-
059   ing; and according to Cisco statistics (Cisco, 2020) from 2018-2023, image/video data accounted
060   for 82% of network bandwidth. Therefore, if the real-world applications of LMMs can be expanded
061   from text to images, it will bring tenfold convenience to daily human life.
062   Why are LMMs excellent in benchmarks but limited in the real-world? Robustness is a crucial
063   factor. In experiments, LMMs usually receive high-quality images, but in real-world scenarios that
064   includes numerous corruption, such as object motion, lens blur, etc. Worse still, in embodied AI or
065   mobile devices (Bai et al., 2024) where agents call LMMs to perform tasks, due to the limitations of
066   edge computing power, current models are mainly deployed on the cloud. The complex transmission
067   process is also risky for corruption. Considering the image modality is much larger than text and
068   encounters more losses in the real-world, LMMs must ensure robust results on distorted content.
069   Unfortunately, despite the emergence of benchmarks for LMMs in numerous tasks over the past
070   few years, assessing their robustness in the real-world remains an unexplored challenge. First, the
071   evaluation of robustness requires images before and after distortion, which presents a significant
072   challenge in data collection. This involves modeling and categorizing corruptions on one hand, and
073   maintaining carefully curated reference/distorted image pairs (rather than taking a bunch of distorted
074   images directly) on the other. In addition, unlike the commonly used benchmark dimensions such
075
      as accuracy/recall, robustness currently lacks a universally accepted metric.
076   Considering these issues, we have established R-Bench to evaluate the robustness of LMMs in the
077   real world. R-Bench aims to test the resistance of different LMMs to corruptions and to identify
078   the most significant corruptions affecting LMMs’ performance, thereby pointing out optimization
079   directions for future LMMs and helping them adapt to real-world images, as shown in Figure 1. Our
080   contributions can be summarized as follows:
081
082          • A comprehensive modeling of corruption to date. We have considered the entire link from
083
               image capturing to LMMs finally seeing the image based on knowledge in imaging science
               and communication engineering, and categorized it into 7 steps, and by underlying features
084
               into 7 groups, totaling 33 corruption dimensions in 3 different strengths.
085
086          • A large dataset containing 2,970 pairs of corresponding reference/distorted images, metic-
087            ulously annotated by human experts, covering the three most mainstream tasks of LMMs.
088            The data characteristics prove that they are suitable as a testing sequence for robustness.
089          • A comprehensive benchmark experiment that considers the performance of 20 mainstream
090            LMMs on reference/distorted images, thereby measuring robustness. In particular, we have
091            proposed the concepts of absolute/relative robustness with a mathematical definition, estab-
092            lishing a standardized process for future robustness evaluation.

      2   R ELATED WORKS
096
097   For the task of generating text from images, its robustness has been the long-term research topic as
098   listed in Table 1. However, a series of works, including RobustBench, MSRVTT-P, AttackVLM, and
099
      OpenRedTeaming (Croce et al., 2021; Schiappa et al., 2022; Zhao et al., 2023; Cui et al., 2024), have
      treated robustness as a typical adversarial task. The distortions in images come from manual attacks,
100
      such as manually adjusting a part of the image or injecting carefully designed noise to induce the
101
      model to make mistakes. Although they study robustness, these distortions are completely different
102   from the corruption in the real world. RobustVLM (Schlarmann & Hein, 2023) is the first study on
103   corruption robustness, but its range of distortions is not rich enough. Subsequently, MMRobustness
104   (Qiu et al., 2024) and MMC-Bench (Zhang et al., 2024a) considered more detailed distortion scenar-
105   ios. However, their assessments of robustness are not reasonable enough; the former directly uses
106   the task score of the distorted images as robustness, while the latter measures the similarity between
107   the distorted images and the answers to the original images. Both of these evaluation mechanisms
      are irrational, which will be analyzed in Section 3.3.

      In addition, all previous research on robustness has two common issues. First, very few works can
121
      simultaneously consider the three classic LMM tasks, namely Multiple Choice Questions (MCQ),
122
      Visual Question Answering (VQA), and Captioning (CAP). This limits the credibility of the bench-
123   mark in terms of robustness. Moreover, although some corruption comes from the real-world, they
124   only include the machine transmission process from the captured image to LMM reception; they
125   ignore the former process in obtaining the image itself, namely in-the-wild corruption. Therefore,
126   an objective and reliable benchmark needs to be conducted to analyze robustness across multiple
127   tasks and dimensions along the entire real-world corruption link.

      3.1     R EFERENCE DATA C OLLECTION
133
      To comprehensively characterize image data from the real world, we collect high-quality reference
134   data and then add corruption to obtain distorted images. The selection of references is based on
135   three principles: (1) Diversity: The data must contain different subjects, backgrounds, styles, etc.,
136   and the three tasks should be as evenly distributed as possible to avoid affecting the credibility of the
137   benchmark due to highly consistent data. (2) Reality: The images must come from natural scenes,
138   such as UGC (user-generated content) taken by average users. Content commonly found in other
139   benchmarks, such as anime (Li et al., 2022), screen content (Li et al., 2024d), and AI-Generated
140   Content (AIGC) (Li et al., 2024b;c; 2023) will be filtered out. (3) Quality: As high-quality reference
141   information, the images must not already be distorted, as otherwise, they cannot be distinguished
142
      from the corresponding distorted images.
143   To obtain reference images, we have implemented the following mechanisms: First, we considered
144   samples from today’s mainstream benchmarks, including seven LMM benchmark datasets for MCQ,
145   VQA, and CAP tasks. (Lin et al., 2014; Liu et al., 2023c; Wu et al., 2023a; XAI, 2024; Liu et al.,
146   2023a; Yu et al., 2024; Marino et al., 2019) Moreover, considering the needs of cloud-side LMMs
147   in the embodied AI, we collected data by operating robots in various indoor, architectural, and street
148
      environments. Secondly, we recruited human experts to inspect the images and only retained those
      marked as in-the-wild. Finally, we used the most accurate Image Quality Assessment (IQA) models
149
      Q-Align, TOPIQ, and LIQE (Wu et al., 2023b; Chen et al., 2024a; Zhang et al., 2023) as quality
150
      controllers, representing the quality from semantic to pixel levels. If any of the indicators is below a
151   certain threshold, the image is deemed to be distorted and removed. The specific image proportions
152   and processing details are included in the Appendix. Finally, we add question-answer pairs to these
153   images. For samples from other datasets that already included question-answer pairs, we retain
154   them if our human experts could correctly answer. Otherwise, we re-annotate them. We also set
155   new question-answer pairs for all samples we collected ourselves. As a result, we obtained 1,485
156   question-answer pairs, with 495 samples each for MCQ, VQA, and CAP as ground truth.

      3.3       ROBUSTNESS D EFINITION
241
242   This section defines robustness mathematically to enable a comprehensive robustness assessment.
243   Robustness can be categorized into absolute and relative aspects. Absolute robustness refers to the
244   performance that LMMs exhibit only on distorted images, while relative robustness is whether the
245   outputs of LMMs are stable between reference/distorted images. Thus, absolute robustness Ra can
246   be simply defined as:
247                                       Ra = Score(GT, LMM(Idis )),                                     (1)
248   where function Score(·) compute the similarity between ground truth answer GT and the LMM(·)
249   result when viewing the distorted image Idis . However, this metric is not comprehensive; for a pow-
250   erful LMM, its performance on distorted images may significantly decline compared to reference
251   images, but since the baseline of the reference is already high, this does not necessarily lower the
252
      appearance of Ra . Thus, it can be termed a powerful model but not robust. Therefore, it is nec-
      essary to add a relative concept above absolute robustness. Some robustness studies (Zhao et al.,
253
      2023; Zhang et al., 2024a) attempt to directly express robustness through the output discrepancy
254
      between reference and distorted images. Unfortunately, this evaluation is even more unreasonable
255   and can only be referred to as similarity, rather than robustness. For instance, if an LMM produces
256   incorrect outputs, regardless of viewing the reference or distorted image, if these errors happen to be
257   consistent, then this poor model will receive a perfect similarity score. Thus, we have initially de-
258   fined relative robustness Rr as follows: Provided that an LMM can correctly process reference
259   images, if the distorted output is still consistent with the reference, namely:
260
                                  Rr = Score(GT, LMM(Iref )) · Score(LMM(Iref ), LMM(Idis )),                                                                      (2)
261
262   where Iref denotes the reference image. R-Bench will calculate the performance of all LMMs indi-
263   vidually based on the above two metrics and use the average value for the final robustness ranking.

309   4.2             E VALUATION C RITERIA
310
311   In the MCQ, VQA, and CAP tasks, we use three LMM-assisted mechanisms as the Score function in
312   Section 3.3. For MCQ, since most LMMs cannot consistently provide instructed output formats, we
313   adopt the following method. If the LMM directly provides options in the form of ’A,B’, we directly
314   check the correctness of options; otherwise, we refer to the GPT evaluation proven effective by
315
      Liu et al. (2023c) to judge whether the output is semantically consistent with the ground truth. For
      VQA and CAP tasks, since traditional language metrics like BLEU, CIDEr, and SPICE (Papineni
316
      et al., 2002; Vedantam et al., 2015; Anderson et al., 2016) tend to penalize errors rather than reward
317
      correct answers, this can lead to advanced LMMs with more complete answers receiving lower
318   scores. (Especially for relative robustness) Therefore, we adopt a similar approach to Q-Bench and
319   A-Bench (Wu et al., 2023a; Zhang et al., 2024b), where a comprehensive score is given to the image
320   based on completeness, precision, and relevance. For VQA, where the target output is less than
321   10 words, it can be directly compared to the ground truth; for CAP, where the output is around
322   40 words, we calculate the degree to which each score point in the GT is matched. We repeat the
323   evaluation for each sample five times to avoid chance and collect the weighted average as the final
      score from 0 to 1. (The MCQ score is binaryized as correct or incorrect.)

497   4.5        GPT4 O VS H UMAN
498
      Given that the robustness of GPT leads in various downstream tasks, corruption intensity, and across
499
      the majority of dimensions compared to all existing LMMs, we compare it with human performance
500
      in Table 4. Since answers from humans on reference images are almost GT, there is no statistical
501   difference in absolute/relative robustness. Therefore, we only compare absolute robustness, which
502   is where LMMs are more proficient. Unfortunately, we find that GPT4o still has a significant gap
503   compared to humans, although it achieved a perfect score in the R-Bench evaluation. The only task
504   where GPT4o surpasses human performance is VQA, and the main reason is the openness of the
505   questions, leading to different answers from humans for the same sample, rather than corruption’s
506   influence. In addition, only Step: SI reaches 98% of human performance. In other aspects, GPT4o
507   shows a comprehensive disadvantage, especially in MCQ tasks and at high corruption levels; in
508   addition, Steps: RD and Group: Wild are the main sources of the gap.
509   In summary, based on the above analyses, we believe that current LMMs have some robustness
510   against corruption but are not suitable for the real world. To address the variety and severity of
511   corruption in the real world, further optimization is needed in the following aspects:
512
513              • For LMMs: The optimization focus for proprietary LMMs is on relative robustness, en-
514                suring that the output on distorted images matches that of reference images, gradually ap-
515
                   proaching and potentially surpassing human performance. Open-source LMMs, however,
                   first need to ensure absolute robustness, achieving correct results on reference images, and
516
                   improving their resilience to corruption only after enhancing their original performance.
517
518
                 • For corruption: In the link from the agent capturing to the LMM perceiving, the first two
                   steps, which are in-the-wild distortions, need to be given special attention. Their negative
519
                   impact on model robustness far exceeds that of the subsequent five steps related to ma-
520
                   chines. On the one hand, LMM developers need to use more distorted data for training; on
521                the other hand, current users need to avoid such issues when using LMMs.
522
                 • For robustness itself: Assessing LMM robustness is currently the biggest challenge. Ex-
523
                   periments show that in some dimensions, there is a significant decline in image quality, yet
524
                   the performance of LMMs is barely affected; while in other relatively minor distortions,
525                LMMs produce severe hallucinations. In the future, an end-to-end assessment for LMM
526                robustness is needed, explaining the correlation between corruption and LMM perception
527                mechanisms, thereby inspiring LMMs to handle various images in the real world.

      5      C ONCLUSION
531
      We construct R-Bench, a benchmark for evaluating the robustness of LMMs in the real world against
532
      corruption, indicated by the performance of LMMs on distorted images in three downstream tasks:
533
      MCQ, VQA, and CAP. We fully modeled the pipeline from the agent capturing to the LMM per-
534   ceiving for the first time, and classified 33 common corruptions in the real world, including 7 time
535   steps and 7 low-level groups with high-quality human annotations. Through our first-ever absolute-
536   relative comprehensive robustness evaluation, we find that proprietary models outperform open-
537   source models but still significantly lag behind humans, which are not yet ready for the real-world.
538   Extensive experimental analysis of corruption also reveals factors that lead to the lack of robustness.
539   We sincerely hope that R-Bench will inspire future LMMs to achieve better robustness, extending
      their applications from experimental simulations to the real-world.

      A.1   L IMITATIONS AND S OCIAL I MPACT
759
760   Limitation 1: Although R-Bench considers a wide range of LMMs, there are always more advanced
761   models that cannot be taken into account. Especially for the robustness task, experiments have
762   shown that LMMs do not lack the relevant knowledge, but are guided to hallucinate by corruption,
763   thus giving incorrect answers. With the rapid iteration of LMMs, although we regret that we cannot
764   test these upcoming advanced models, we sincerely hope that the current R-Bench can be an assistant
765   for future LMM evolution on robustness.
766   Limitation 2: Although the evaluation of R-Bench is comprehensive and reliable, it still needs
767   assistance from text-modal GPT, which is a common problem of many current benchmarks. (Wu
768   et al., 2023a; Zhang et al., 2024b; Liu et al., 2023c) This limits its usage as a reference list for the
769   model level, rather than an evaluation plug-in for the instance level. For the evolution of LMM, if the
770   image preference of LMM can be obtained in real-time through an open-source pipeline, robustness
771   can be measured through Reference/Distorted image score disparity. This will be very helpful for
772   its understanding and processing of distorted images, thus improving its usability in the real-world.
773   Social Impact: We believe R-Bench can drive innovation by providing a standardized platform for
774   comparing different models and their resilience against corruption. Evaluating how well LMMs han-
775   dle imperfections can significantly enhance the reliability of real-world downstream tasks, helping
776   them meet certain robustness standards before deployment. This is crucial for applications where
777   accuracy is critical while corruption is easy to occur, such as medical imaging, autonomous vehicles,
778   and embodied AI.

780   A.2   C ORRUPTION DETAIL
781
782   All 33 corruption are listed below, with the (Step, Group) they belong to:
783   1. Motion blur (EI, Blur): The object itself is moving, causing a blur trail.
784   2. Bright illumination (EI, Wild): A very bright light source in the environment, interfering with the
785   imaging.
786   3. Dark illumination (EI, Wild): No light source in the environment, making it difficult to image.
787   4. Blocking obstacle (EI, Wild): An obstacle blocks part of the object being photographed.
788   5. Lens blur (CI, Blur): Lens fog causes refraction.
789   6. Resolution limit (CI, Spatial): The lens resolution is insufficient, requiring upsampling.
790
      7. Lens obstacle (CI, Wild): An obstacle blocks part of the lens.
      8. Lens shaking (CI, Wild): The camera shakes randomly in the direction of movement, making it
791
      difficult to capture clear images.
792
      9. White Noise (AD, Noise): Overall aging or prolonged operation of the circuit causes noise.
793   10. Color Noise (AD, Noise): Damage to certain channels in the YCbCr of the circuit components.
794   11. Impulse noise (AD, Noise): External factors cause sudden interference to the circuit compo-
795   nents.
796   12. Multiplicative noise (AD, Noise): The power supply voltage does not match the required voltage
797   of the circuit components.
798   13. Clock jittering (AD, Spatial): The frequency of the clock module is inaccurate, causing fre-
799   quency oscillation.
800   14. Color quantization (SE, Chrominance): Similar colors are merged through minimum variance
801
      quantization.
      15. JPEG2000 codec (SE, Compression): A standard image compression method.
802
      16. JPEG codec (SE, Compression): The most widely used image compression method.
803
      17. WEBP codec (SE, Compression): The image compression method with the best comprehensive
804   performance.
805   18. Grayscale quantization (SE, Spatial): 256 colors are mapped to fewer dimensions through uni-
806   form quantization.
807   19. Block exchange (CT, Spatial): The order of two Macro-blocks in the channel is wrong.
808   20. Block repeat (CT, Spatial): A Macro-block is convoluted twice and covers the original position.
809   21. Block lost (CT, Spatial): A Macro-block is lost, and some communication protocol turns it into
      random pixels.
