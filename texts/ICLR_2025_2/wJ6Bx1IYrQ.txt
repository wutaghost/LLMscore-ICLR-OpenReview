011                                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Electroencephalogram (EEG) signals are pivotal in providing insights into spon-
014            taneous brain activity, highlighting their significant importance in neuroscience
015
               research. However, the exploration of versatile EEG models is constrained by
               diverse data formats, outdated pre-training paradigms, and limited transfer learning
016
               methods, only leading to specialist models on single dataset. In this paper, we
017
               introduce EEGPT, the first generalist EEG foundation model designed to address
018            these challenges. First, we propose an electrode-wise modeling strategy that treats
019            each electrode as a fundamental unit, enabling the integration of diverse EEG
020            datasets collected from up to 138 electrodes, amassing 37.5M pre-training samples.
021            Second, we develop the first autoregressive EEG pre-trained model, moving away
022            from traditional masked autoencoder approaches to a next signal prediction task
023            that better captures the sequential and temporal dependencies of EEG data. We
024            also explore scaling laws with model up to 1.1B parameters — the largest in EEG
025            research to date. Third, we introduce a multi-task transfer learning paradigm using
026
               a learnable electrode graph network that is shared across tasks, which for the
               first time confirms multi-task compatibility and synergy. As the first generalist
027
               EEG foundation model, EEGPT shows broad compatibility with various signal
028
               acquisition devices, subjects, and tasks. It supports up to 138 electrodes and any
029            combination thereof as input. Furthermore, we simultaneously evaluate it on 5
030            distinct downstream tasks across 12 benchmarks. EEGPT consistently outperforms
031            existing specialist models across all downstream tasks, with its effectiveness fur-
032            ther validated through extensive ablation studies. This work sets a new direction
033            for generalist EEG modeling, offering improved scalability, transferability, and
034            adaptability for a wide range of EEG applications. Both the training code and
035            model checkpoints will be publicly available.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
055
056   Electroencephalogram (EEG), which captures spontaneous brain activity via electrograms (Biasiucci
057
      et al., 2019), could be conceptualized as the language of the brain. Through the analyses of EEG,
      valuable insights are derived for various applications, including but not limited to emotion recognition
058
      (Zhang et al., 2024), motor imagery classification (An et al., 2023), mental workload detection
059
      (Wang et al., 2024b) and sleep stage classification (Liang et al., 2023). This breadth of applications
060   underscores the versatility and utility of EEG in neuroscientific research.
061
062   Research on EEG downstream tasks has been thriving, yet most studies share a notable characteristic:
063
      specialization. For instance, at the data level, a variety of proprietary data formats (Jia et al.,
      2020; Bashivan et al., 2015) and handcrafted feature extraction (Duan et al., 2013; Yan et al., 2023)
064
      techniques have been introduced to enhance the discriminability of domain-specific data. At the
065
      model level, various modules and structures are designed and trained for a specific task (Shao et al.,
066   2023), dataset (Wang et al., 2024a), or even individual subjects (Gao et al., 2024). However, a
067   generalist EEG foundation model is highly anticipated, as it offers broader applicability across
068   various EEG tasks. Moreover, this model improves transfer learning by allowing knowledge from
069   one task to enhance performance on others. Its design also demonstrates greater robustness to data
070   and task variations, leading to better generalization in unseen scenarios.
071
      Although extensive research in the fields of computer vision (CV) (Radford et al., 2021; Dosovitskiy,
072   2020; Bai et al., 2024) and natural language processing (NLP) (Radford et al., 2019; Achiam et al.,
073   2023; Brown, 2020) has identified three key components for constructing generalist models—data,
074   self-supervised pre-training, and transfer learning paradigms—EEG introduces its own unique and
075   daunting challenges in each of these domains:
076
      Data Format. EEG data exhibit significant heterogeneity (Wang et al., 2024a; Saeed et al., 2021),
077
      characterized by a variety of systems (e.g., the 10-20 system) and equipment (e.g., Neuroscan) used
078   in data collection. Furthermore, different datasets may employ a diverse number and combination
079   of electrodes based on practical considerations. The inconsistency in data formats across different
080   sources prevents their combined use in the same model for training, making it challenging to develop
081   a generalist for EEG. Therefore, an efficient and scalable strategy for unifying these diverse EEG
082   data format is extremely demanding.
083
      Self-supervised Pre-training. Current studies (Yang et al., 2024b; Jiang et al., 2024; Yi et al.,
084   2024) have uniformly employed techniques that mask parts of EEG signals and utilize a bidirectional
085   attention mechanism (Vaswani, 2017) to reconstruct the masked data (i.e., mask autoencoder, MAE).
086   However, they have inevitable limitations in capturing the sequential and temporal dependencies
087   inherent in time-based data such as language and EEG. Given the gradual obsolescence of MAE
088   architectures in NLP (Zhao et al., 2023; Minaee et al., 2024), it is essential for the EEG field, which
089   shares similar temporal dynamics with language, to reconsider its current pre-training paradigms.
090   Transfer Learning. Current pre-trained EEG models are generally fine-tuned for specific datasets,
091   resulting in specialists in narrow domains. However, in CV and NLP, many pre-trained models
092   (Touvron et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2024a; Wang et al., 2023) have achieved remarkable generalizability
093   through more adaptable and efficient knowledge transfer learning methods. These models support
094   multiple tasks and promote beneficial synergies. Compared to models specialized in single tasks,
095   they exhibit enhanced and broader capabilities, facilitating a more thorough utilization of pre-trained
096   knowledge. However, advanced transfer learning method remains underexplored in the EEG field.
097   In this paper, we propose EEGPT, a generalist EEG foundation model offering extensive versatility.
098   Specifically, it seamlessly adapts and encodes signals collected by nearly all popular EEG acquisition
099   devices. It accommodates signals from up to 138 electrodes, supporting various configurations and
100   combinations. Moreover, EEGPT is capable of simultaneously processing and analyzing data from
101   nearly all prevalent downstream tasks within a single model, and it is highly scalable to new tasks.
102   The training recipe for EEGPT significantly diverges from previous paradigms, with its novelty
103   encapsulated in five distinct "firsts":
104   1) For data format, we propose the first electrode-wise modeling strategy. It deconstructs the
105   signals electrode by electrode. Each electrode serves as a fundamental unit for subsequent training.
106   Although the sets of electrodes differ across various datasets, this strategy consistently translates into
107   an electrode-conditioned temporal modeling task. Leveraging this compatibility and unification, we
      extensively collect a total of 37.5M pre-training samples.

142   Based on these designs, our contributions are summarized as follows:
143
144          • Electrode-wise Modeling Strategy. We introduce a novel electrode-wise strategy for EEG
145            data integration, treating each electrode as a fundamental unit across various datasets. This
146            approach enables uniform handling and scalability in data processing. Benefiting from this
147            method, EEGPT can support up to 138 electrodes and their arbitrary combinations, offering
148            flexibility and applicability far beyond existing models.
149
150          • Autoregressive EEG Pre-trained Model: We introduce the first autoregressive pre-trained
151
               EEG model. Compared to traditional MAE techniques, it more naturally and efficiently
               captures the sequential and temporal dynamics inherent in EEG data. The scaling laws for
152
               data and model size in the autoregressive framework have been effectively validated.
153
154
             • Multi-task Transfer Learning Paradigm: Building upon a learnable task-shared graph
155            network, EEGPT is the first generalist model to exhibit confirmed multi-task compatibility
156            and synergism. Significant mutual enhancement across tasks are demonstrated through
157            multi-task transfer learning.
158
159          • Comprehensive Quantitative and Qualitative Experiments. EEGPT demonstrated supe-
160            rior performance across 12 datasets encompassing 5 tasks, surpassing both pretrain-then-
161            finetune and training-from-scratch predominant specialist baselines. The effectiveness of
               our proposed method is further validated by extensive qualitative analyses.

      In this section, we elaborate on the comprehensive framework of EEGPT. A detailed framework is
165   illustrated in Figure 3. We begin by representing a multi-electrode EEG signal as x ∈ REi ×T ×C ,
166   where Ei represents the number of electrodes. For the entire signal, we segment it into T one-second
167   intervals. Each interval is represented by C uniformly sampled points from the original signal.
168
169   2.1    AUTOREGRESSIVE T IME S ERIES M ODELING
170
171   In this stage, we aim to develop a comprehensive and detailed self-supervised learning paradigm.
172   It is designed to accurately and efficiently capture the intrinsic temporal variations in EEG signals.
173   Furthermore, we plan for it to be electrode-conditioned, which will facilitate the discernment of both
174
      disparities and similarities across different electrodes, enhancing its effectiveness in diverse scenarios.
175
176
      Electrode-wise Modeling Strategy. We compile the pre-training dataset X = {x1 , x2 , . . . , xN }
      by aggregating data from multiple sources. Each sample xi ∈ REi ×T ×C corresponds to a set Ei
177
      containing Ei electrodes. To discern the distinctive patterns specific to each electrode in EEG signals,
178
      we introduce a structured reorganization function R(·). Specifically, we segment each xi based on
179   individual electrodes, denoted as xei ∈ RT ×C . Consequently, EEG recordings from disparate sources
180   that share identical electrodes are grouped together:
181
182
                                                           \mathcal {R}(X) = \left \{ D_e \mid e \in \bigcup _{i=1}^N \mathcal {E}_i \right \}     (1)
183
184
185   where D_e is the grouped collection of all data segments \protect \mathbf  {x}_i^e from electrode e across all samples that
186
      include electrode e:
                                         D_e = \{ \mathbf {x}_i^e \mid e \in \mathcal {E}_i, \, i = 1, 2, \ldots , N \}       (2)
187
188   The size of R(X) corresponds to the count of unique electrodes present in X. To distinguish
189   between different electrodes, we introduce a trainable electrode vocabulary vE ∈ R|R(X)|×C . All
190   elements in D_e share the same electrode embedding v_E^e . This embedding is served as condition and
191
      then concatenated along the sequence dimension to xei . For simplicity, we consistently refer to the
      concatenated sequence as x_i^e :
192
193
                                                   x_i^e = [v_E^e||x_i^e] \in \mathbb {R}^{(T+1)\times C}                     (3)
194   where ∥ signifies the concatenation operation. Hence, signals from various domains and electrodes
195   have been standardized into a highly scalable format. The chronological sequences xei , which contains
196   T +1 EEG "tokens", will serve as the fundamental unit for performing autoregressive reconstruction.
197
198
      Autoregressive Reconstruction. As depicted in Figure 3 (left), each xei is inputted into a shared
      Electrode Temporal Encoder (ETE), which comprises a series of L identical layers. Each layer
199
      contains two sub-layers: the first utilizes a multi-head causal attention mechanism, and the second
200
      employs a positionwise fully connected feed-forward network. Specifically, the input sequence xei
201   first undergoes the causal attention process:
202
203
                                      \text {Attention}(Q, K, V) = \text {softmax}\left (\frac {QK^T}{\sqrt {d}} + M \right )V                     (4)
204
205
      where Q, K, and V are queries, keys, and values respectively, all derived from xei , and d is the
206   hidden size. M is a causal mask designed to ensure that each token only attend to tokens that are
207   sequentially prior to itself. The output of this sub-layer is then normalized and passed through a
208   residual connection (He et al., 2016). Subsequently, it is fed into feed-forward network, which
209   consists of two linear transformations with a SwiGLU (Dauphin et al., 2017) activation. Finally, the
210   output from ETE is transformed into the corresponding next-token prediction through a simple MLP.
211
212   Training Objective. Assuming the input is x, the reconstructed result is denoted as x̂. Without loss
213   of generality, the training objective for the autoregressive model can be formulated as follows:

243   2.2       TASK - SHARED E LECTRODE G RAPH
244
245   Through autoregressive pre-training, ETE has effectively captured the temporal characteristics
246
      conditioned by electrodes. In this stage, unlike previous pre-trained models which are fine-tuned for
      individual tasks, we aim to explore a more versatile multi-task paradigm. Specifically, we propose a
247
      Task-shared Electrode Graph (TEG) network. This network adaptively activates interactions among
248
      various electrodes to simultaneously support multiple tasks.
249
250
      Electrode Representation Extraction. Consider a multi-task dataset defined as
251                     .Y = \{y_1, y_2, \ldots , y_M\}
                          Each sample y_j belongs to \protect \mathbb  {R}^{E_j \times T \times C} , where E_j denote the number of elec-
252   trodes. As illustrated in Figure 3 (right), for each sample y_j , a learnable special token c \in \mathbb {R}^{C} is
253   broadcast across all E_j electrodes and appended to the end of the temporal sequence:
254
255                                                            y_j' = \left [ y_j || \ c \cdot \mathbf {1}_{E_j \times 1} \right ] \in \mathbb {R}^{E_j \times (T+1) \times C}                                (6)
256
      Leveraging the unidirectional attention mechanism inherent to autoregressive models, these special
257
      tokens facilitate the integration of local information from individual electrodes to synthesize global
258   representations. Specifically, yi′ are then processed by the pre-trained ETE. Notably, the parameters of
259   ETE are frozen during this stage, functioning solely as a feature extraction backbone. Subsequently,
260   electrode representations are derived from the positions of special tokens in the output of ETE:
261
262                                                                    z_j = \text {ETE}(y_j')[:, -1, :] \in \mathbb {R}^{E_j \times C}                                                                       (7)
263   Similarly, each sample in Y generates a corresponding z_j that captures comprehensive temporal in-
264   formation. These representations are then input into the proposed TEG network, which is specifically
265   designed to model dependencies among electrodes, integrating spatial information effectively.
266
267   Network Structure. We initially construct a graph network in which each node represents an
268   electrode utilized during the pre-training stage. The total number of nodes is denoted by |R(X)|.
269   Benefiting from the comprehensive data coverage in the pre-training, these nodes include nearly all
      electrodes commonly employed, encompassing those found in Y . Each node is represented by a

      learnable vector of length C. They form a fully interconnected graph G ∈ R|R(X)|×C . For each zj ,
271   the electrodes it comprises are mapped to a subgraph Gj within G. Upon the introduction of z_j into
272   the network, only the nodes contained within the subgraph \protect \mathcal  {G}_j are activated. This activation process
273   involves updating the representations of these specific nodes by adding the corresponding elements

      4              CONCLUSION
533
534
      In conclusion, we have presented EEGPT, the first generalist EEG foundation model designed to
      overcome the limitations of existing specialized EEG models. By introducing an electrode-wise
535
      modeling strategy, developing an autoregressive pre-training approach, and implementing a multi-task
536
      transfer learning paradigm with a learnable electrode graph network, EEGPT unifies diverse EEG
537   datasets and captures the sequential and temporal dependencies inherent in EEG signals. Our model
538   demonstrates superior performance across 12 benchmarks, showcasing its versatility and scalability.
539   We hope that EEGPT will inspire further research and development in generalist EEG models.

      A    R ELATED W ORK
757
758   Despite the significant success of self-supervised pre-training in the fields of CV and NLP (Bai
759   et al., 2024; Touvron et al., 2023), the potential of self-supervised pre-training in EEG remains
760   underexplored. Specifically, existing work (Yang et al., 2024b; Jiang et al., 2024; Yi et al., 2024; Li
761   et al., 2024) exhibits significant similarities in both the pre-training objective and the downstream task
762   transfer paradigm. For pre-training objective, they predominantly employ the mask signal modeling
763   (MAE) architecture. For instance, BIOT (Yang et al., 2024b) and MMM (Yi et al., 2024) adopt
764   the channel and temporal embeddings to construct the EEG tokens for masked segments prediction.
765   LaBraM (Jiang et al., 2024) utilizes a neural tokenizer to segment and encode EEG signals into
766
      discrete codes, and similarly predicts masked tokens from visible patches. However, the MAE
      architecture does not align with the temporal characteristics inherent in EEG signals. The models
767
      in the filed of NLP have shifted towards autoregressive models to better address similar temporal
768
      properties (Yang et al., 2024a; Touvron et al., 2023). Consequently, it is imperative to update the
769   paradigm of self-supervised pre-training for EEG. Besides, these models encode EEG signals only
770   for selected subsets of electrodes, which are lack of scalability and versatility. For downstream task
771   transfer paradigm, they are still stuck to fine-tune separate models for each downstream task (Yang
772   et al., 2024b; Jiang et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024) or even each subject (Yi et al., 2024), lacking the
773   versatility required for an all-in-one model to multiple EEG tasks. Moreover, current pre-training
774   models primarily focus on seizure epilepsy classification or emotion recognition tasks. In constrast,
775   EEGPT aims for broader task coverage, enhancing both generalizability and adaptability.

      For data preprocessing, all the EEG signals are resampled to 256 Hz. The signals are then filtered

908
909          • EEGNet (Lawhern et al., 2018): EEGNet is a compact convolutional neural network

913            designed for EEG emotion recognition, capable of learning discriminative representations
914            across both time and channel dimensions. The model incorporates a dynamic temporal layer
915            to effectively capture dynamic temporal and frequency representations, while an asymmetric
916
               spatial layer is employed to learn discriminative global and hemisphere representations.
917          • Conformer (Song et al., 2023): Conformer is a compact convolutional Transformer model
               designed for EEG classification, capable of encapsulating both local and global features.

919           employing a self-attention mechanism to extract global correlations within the local temporal

922

923           both within and between the brain’s functional regions.
