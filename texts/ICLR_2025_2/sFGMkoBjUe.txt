009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            To deploy reinforcement learning (RL) systems in real-world scenarios we need
012            to consider requirements such as safety and constraint compliance, rather than
013            blindly maximizing for reward. In this paper we develop a lookahead shielding
014            framework for RL with regular safety properties, which on the contrary to prior
015            shielding methodologies requires minimal prior knowledge. At each environment
016
               step our framework aims to satisfy the regular safety property for a bounded hori-
               zon with high-probability, for the tabular setting we provide provable guarantees.
017
               We compare our setup to some common algorithms developed for the constrained
018
               Markov decision process (CMDP), and we demonstrate the effectiveness and scal-
019            ability of our framework by extensively evaluating our framework in both tabular
020            and deep RL environments.

025   The field of safe reinforcement learning (RL) (Garcıa & Fernández,
026   2015; Amodei et al., 2016) has gained increasing interest, as practi-
027
      tioners begin to understand the challenges of applying RL in the
      real world (Dulac-Arnold et al., 2019). There exist several dis-
028
      tinct paradigms in the literature, including constrained optimization
029
      (Chow et al., 2018; Liang et al., 2018; Tessler et al., 2018; Ray et al.,
030   2019; Achiam et al., 2017; Yang et al., 2020), logical constraint sat-
031   isfaction (Voloshin et al., 2022; Hasanbeig et al., 2018; 2020a;b;
032   De Giacomo et al., 2020; Cai et al., 2021), safety-critical control

033   (McIlvanna et al., 2022; Cheng et al., 2019; Brunke et al., 2022),
                                                                                resentation of runtime verifi-
034   all of which are unified by prioritizing safety- and risk-awareness
                                                                                cation and shielding.
035   during the decision making process.
036   Constrained Markov decision processes (CMDP) (Altman, 1999) have emerged as a popular frame-
037   work for modelling safe RL, or RL with constraints. Typically, the goal is to obtain a policy that
038   maximizes reward while simultaneously ensuring that the expected cumulative cost remains below
039   a pre-defined threshold. A key limitation of this setting is that constraint violations are enforced in
040   expectation rather than with high probability, the constraint thresholds also have limited semantic
041   meaning, can be very challenging to tune and in some cases inappropriate for highly safety-critical
042   scenarios (Voloshin et al., 2022). Furthermore, the cost function in the CMDP is typically Markovian
043   and thus fails to capture a significantly expressive class of safety properties and constraints.
044   Regular safety properties (Baier & Katoen, 2008) are interesting because for all but the simplest
045   properties the corresponding cost function is non-Markov. Our problem setup consists of the stan-
046   dard RL objective with regular safety properties as constraints, we note that there has been a sig-
047   nificant body of work that combines temporal logic constraints with RL (Voloshin et al., 2022;
048   Hasanbeig et al., 2018; 2020a;b; De Giacomo et al., 2020; Cai et al., 2021), although many of these
049   do not explicitly separate reward and safety in the same way that we do.
050   Our approach relies on shielding (Alshiekh et al., 2018), which is a safe exploration strategy that
051   ensures the satisfaction of temporal logic constraints by deploying the learned policy in conjunc-
052   tion with a reactive system that overrides any unsafe actions. Most shielding approaches typically
053   make highly restrictive assumptions, such as knowledge of the environment dynamics, or at the very
      least the safety-relevant dynamics of the environment (Alshiekh et al., 2018; Jansen et al., 2020;

      Könighofer et al., 2023), full knowledge of the topology of the MDP (Carr et al., 2023), or access
055   to a perfect generative model (Giacobbe et al., 2021), although there has been recent work to relax
056   these restrictions (Goodall & Belardinelli, 2023; He et al., 2022; Xiao et al., 2023). In this paper, we

058   verification of the agent is realized by finite horizon model checking with a learned approximation of
059   the environment dynamics. However, in principle our framework is flexible enough to accommodate
060   more standard model checking procedures as long as certain assumptions are met.
061   Our approach can be summarised as an online shielding approach (see Figure 1), that dynamically
062   identifies unsafe actions during training and deployment, and deploys a learned safe ‘backup policy’
063   when necessary. We summarise the main contributions of our paper as follows:
064
      (1) We develop a lookahead shielding framework for RL with regular safety properties as constraints,
065
      which requires minimal prior knowledge; unknown transition dynamics and no a priori access to a
066
      safe ‘backup policy’.
067
068   (2) We compare our setup to the CMDP framework and for the tabular setting we provide provable
069   step-wise and episodic safety guarantees, by leveraging ideas such as probabilistic safe sets and
070
      irrecoverable actions.
071   (3) We detail two possible instantiations of our framework for both the tabular and deep RL settings,
072   and we empirically demonstrate the effectiveness of our framework across a variety of environments
073   with different regular safety properties. We compare our approach to projection-based and Lagrange
074   relaxation-based CMDP algorithms.

      Our goal is to synthesize a safe policy, πsh : S × Q → Dist(A), by dynamically integrating two
165   sub-policies, the ‘task policy’, denoted πr : S → Dist(A) and the ‘backup policy’ πb : S × Q →
166   Dist(A). Control of the agent is given to one of these sub-policies depending on the current state
167   of the agent and the desired safety-threshold. The ‘task policy’ πr is trained with RL to maximize
168   reward, i.e., maxπ Vπ . On the other hand the ‘backup policy’ πb is (usually) a low-reward policy,
169   specifically designed to keep the agent within a probabilistic safe set of states. In some simple
170   instances the ‘backup policy’ may constitute a simple rule-based policy that is guaranteed to be safe
171   before training. However, since we assume minimal prior knowledge, the ‘backup policy’ will need
172   to be trained online with RL similar to the ‘task policy’.
173   From a given product state ⟨s, q⟩ ∈ S × Q, we dynamically switch between πr and πb by evaluating
174   the N -step conditional action reachability probability, defined as follows,
175   Definition 3.1 (N -step conditional action reachability probability). Let a ∈ A be an action, let
176   M = (S, A, P, P0 , R, AP, L) be an MDP and let D = (Q, Σ, ∆, Q0 , F) be the DFA such that
177   L(D) = BadP ref (Psafe ). The N -step conditional action reachability probability, is computed
178   from the MDP by first resolving the non-determinism of the MDP with action a and then with πb
179   thereafter, this is denoted, Praπb (⟨s, q⟩ |= ♢≤N accept). 1
180
181   In our case, we estimate the N -step conditional action reachability probability Praπb (⟨s, q⟩ |=

183   not exceed some step-wise safety-threshold εt then the action a is permissible, otherwise the action
184
      a is rejected and a safe action a′ is sampled from the πb . Thus, the ‘shielded policy’ πsh has the
      following form,

      3.3.1    D EEP RL
255
256   For our deep RL experiments we use DreamerV3 (Hafner et al., 2023) for both dynamics learn-
257   ing and policy optimization. DreamerV3 is based on the Recurrent State Space Model (RSSM)
258   (Hafner et al., 2019), a special type of sequential Variational Auto-encoder (VAE) (Kingma &
259   Welling, 2013), which learns a latent representation and dynamics model of the environment from
260   observations. The model consists of the following key components: sequential model ht =
261
      fθ (ht−1 , zt−1 , at−1 ), observation encoder zt ∼ qθ (zt | ot , ht ), transition predictor ẑt ∼ pθ (ẑt | ht ),
      observation decoder ôt ∼ pθ (ôt | ht , zt ), reward predictor r̂t ∼ pθ (r̂t | ht , zt ) and termination pre-
262
      dictor γ̂t ∼ pθ (γ̂t | ht , zt ). Our implementation is build upon approximate model-based shielding
263
      (AMBS) (Goodall & Belardinelli, 2023) which additionally uses a cost predictor ĉt ∼ pθ (ĉt | ht , zt )
264   to predict state-dependent costs. Since DreamerV3 encodes the observation and action history in the
265   latent vectors (ht , zt ) we can use the same cost predictor to learn the cost function C(⟨st , qt ⟩) from
266   Definition 3.2, we back-propagate the cost predictor gradients through the RSSM, with the hope
267   that the necessary temporal dependencies for predicting the cost are captured in the latent space. We
268   can then estimate Praπb (⟨s, q⟩ |= ♢≤N accept) by rolling out the latent dynamics model pθ , summing
269   the predicted costs along the trajectories and average the result over multiple trajectories sampled in
      parallel. The full algorithm is detailed in Appendix A.3.

414
415   A practical comparison between our approach and LTL-based approaches from the literature be-
416   comes challenging, as many approaches do not separate reward and safety in to two distinct objec-
417   tives (Hasanbeig et al., 2018; De Giacomo et al., 2020; Cai et al., 2021), those that do either assume
418   access to a perfect generative model that can be sampled from any state-action pair Voloshin et al.
419
      (2022), or assume knowledge of the optimal discount factor γ and dual variable λ (Shah et al., 2024;
      Voloshin et al., 2023). LTL and regular safety constraints are slightly different and care would also
420
      need to be taken to convert the satisfaction condition for Büchi automata to regular safety properties
421
      (or vice versa). In some instances Shah et al., 2024; Voloshin et al., 2023, an unconstrained objective
422   with dual variable λ is maximized, which is, for the most part, comparable to our baseline QL-Cost,
423   which also treats the weighting of the cost function as a hyperparameter.
424
425
      Our approach, is not without its limitations, in particular choosing the model checking horizon N is
      imperative for safety performance. In principle, any N = T should suffice for episodic guarantees,
426
      however large N can incur significant overhead at each decision making step, due repeated model
427
      checking. Assumption 3.9 tells us that N ≥ N ∗ is enough, but there is not a practical way choosing
428   N without knowing something about the environment, e.g. for Atari Seaquest it takes at least 25
429   timesteps, to reach the surface from the bottom of the sea, so N = 30 was chosen.

      5   R ELATED W ORK
487
488
      Safety Paradigms in Reinforcement Learning. The most common paradigm is constrained MDPs
489
      (CMDP) for which, several constrained optimization algorithms have been developed, most are
490
      gradient-based methods built upon Lagrange relaxations of the constrained problem (Chow et al.,
491   2018; Liang et al., 2018; Tessler et al., 2018; Ray et al., 2019) or projection-based local policy search
492   (Achiam et al., 2017; Yang et al., 2020). Model-based approaches to CMDP (As et al., 2022; Huang
493   et al., 2023; Thomas et al., 2021; Berkenkamp et al., 2017) have also gathered recent interest as they
494   enjoy better sample complexity than their model-free counterparts (Janner et al., 2019).
495
      Linear Temporal Logic (LTL) constraints (Voloshin et al., 2022; Hasanbeig et al., 2018; 2020a;b;
496
      De Giacomo et al., 2020; Cai et al., 2021) for RL have been developed as an alternative to CMDPs to
497   specify stricter and more expressive constraints. The LTL formula is typically treated as the entire
498   task specification, although some works have aimed to separate LTL satisfaction and reward into
499   two distinct objectives (Voloshin et al., 2022; 2023; Shah et al., 2024). The typical procedure in
500   this setting is to identify end components of the MDP that satisfy the LTL constraint and construct a
501   corresponding reward function such that the optimal policy satisfies the LTL constraint with maximal
502   probability. Formal PAC-style guarantees have been developed for this setting (Fu & Topcu, 2014;
503   Wolff et al., 2012; Voloshin et al., 2022; Hasanbeig et al., 2018) although they often rely on non-
504   trivial assumptions (e.g. access to a perfect generative model).
505   More rigorous safety-guarantees can be obtained by using shielding (Alshiekh et al., 2018), con-
506   trol barrier functions (CBF) (Ames et al., 2019), and model predictive safety certification (MPSC)
507   (Wabersich & Zeilinger, 2018; 2021). To achieve zero-violation training, these methods typically
508   assume that the dynamics of the system are known and thus they are typically restricted to low-
509   dimensional systems. Recent works have aimed to scale the concept of shielding to more general
510   settings, relaxing the prerequisite assumptions for shielding, by either only assuming access to a per-
511
      fect generative model for planning (Giacobbe et al., 2021), or learning a world model from scratch
      (Goodall & Belardinelli, 2023; He et al., 2022; Xiao et al., 2023). Notable works that can be viewed
512
      as shielding include, MASE (Wachi et al., 2018) – a safe exploration algorithm with access to an
513
      ‘emergency reset button’, and Recovery-RL (Thananjeyan et al., 2021). A simple form of shielding
514   with LTL specifications has also been considered (Mitta et al., 2024), although this approach makes
515   use of informative priors over the transition dynamics. Shielding approaches most similar in spirit
516   to our approach, include (Jansen et al., 2020; Könighofer et al., 2021; 2023), these approaches also
517   consider finite-horizon satisfaction probabilities, although they assume a priori access to the safety
518   dynamics and cannot provide episodic guarantees in the same way that we can.
519
      Learning Over Regular Structures. RL and regular properties have been studied in conjunction
520   before, perhaps most famously as ‘Reward Machines’ (Icarte et al., 2018; 2022) – a type of finite
521   state automaton that specifies a different reward function at each automaton state, however reward
522   machines do not explicitly deal with safety. In addition, regular decision processes (RDP) (Brafman
523   et al., 2019) are a specific class non-Markov DPs (Bacchus et al., 1996) that have also been studied
524   in several works (Brafman et al., 2019; Ronca & De Giacomo, 2021; Majeed et al., 2018; Toro Icarte
525   et al., 2019; Cipollone et al., 2024). Most of these works are theoretical and slightly out-of-scope
526   for this paper, as RDPs capture both non-Markov rewards and transition probabilities.
527

      6   C ONCLUSION
529
530
531
      The separation of reward and safety objectives into two distinct policies has been demonstrated as
      an effective strategy towards safety-aware decision making (Goodall & Belardinelli, 2023; Jansen
532
      et al., 2018; Thananjeyan et al., 2021; Alshiekh et al., 2018), in many cases the safety objective is
533
      simpler and can be more quickly learnt (Jansen et al., 2018). In this paper we have demonstrated
534   that this is an effective framework for dealing with regular safety properties, an important class of
535   temporal properties where the corresponding cost function is non-Markov. We detail two possi-
536   ble instantiations of our framework for the tabular and deep RL environments, and we provide a
537   thorough experimental evaluation including a comparison to CMDP-based approaches. Beyond our
538   empirical results we provide safety guarantees in the tabular setting, that hold under reasonable as-
539   sumptions. Future work includes, further investigation into the scenarios where it is appropriate and
      beneficial to leverage shielding as an approach to safe RL.

       D.2   R EWARD S HAPING AND C OUNTERFACTUAL E XPERIENCE
1324
1325   In this section we ablate our approach by removing counterfactual experiences (CFE) from our
1326   methodology. We note, that this only makes a difference for safety properties that are not simple
1327   invariant properties, namely, property (2) and (3) for the 9 × 9 gridworld and 15 × 15 gridworld
1328   environments. We also experiment with different reward shaping approaches (in addition to CFE),
1329   for improving the convergence of the ‘backup policy’ πb . These approaches are detailed below.
1330   Potential-based reward shaping. Potential-based reward shaping is an approach used to typically
1331   deal with sparse or delayed reward environments. Potential-based reward shaping provides interme-
1332   diate rewards of the following form,
1333
1334
                                                      r′ (s′ , s, a) = r(s′ , s, a) + γΦ(s′ ) − Φ(s)                                                                                      (43)
1335   where Φ : S → R is the potential function. Intuitively this provides the agent with a dense reward
1336   signal for moving to ‘more promising’ states with higher potential values. Note that by discounting
1337   the potential values with the original discount factor γ this keeps the set of optimal policies that
1338   maximize the original reward function unchanged (Ng et al., 1999). Then if the potential function is
1339   useful in some sense, this allows us to learn optimal policies more quickly. Icarte et al. (2022) pro-
1340   posed automated reward shaping (RS) for reward machines (RM). We adopt this same methodology
1341
       here. The potential function Φ : S × Q → R is defined over product states ⟨s, q⟩ ∈ S × Q, each
       automaton state q ∈ Q is assigned a potential value v(q) computed by running value iteration over
1342
       the DFA D, with a different discount factor γ ′ < γ. The cost function for the ‘backup policy’ πb , is
1343
       then redefined as,
1344                               C ′ (⟨st , qt ⟩) = C(⟨st , qt ⟩) + γv(qt ) − v(qt−1 )                 (44)
1345
1346   Cycle-based reward shaping. This approach is inspired by Shah et al. (2024), although Shah et al.
1347   (2024) is adapted to LTL-constraints which have a different satisfaction criterion to DFAs, we adapt
1348   Shah et al. (2024) to our setting. In particular, we first compute the length shortest path through
1349   the DFA D, from each automaton state q ∈ Q to the initial state Q0 , we implement this using
       Breadth-First Search (BFS) in O(|D|). Each automaton state q ∈ Q is assigned a value v(q) which


                                                                                                    25
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1350
       corresponds to the length of the shortest path from q to Q0 . This incentivizes the agent to return
1351   the the initial state Q0 as quickly as possible, while avoiding the accepting states of the DFA D,
1352   which are sink nodes, i.e. v(q) = ∞ ∀q ∈ F. The cost function for the ‘backup policy’ πb , is then
1353   redefined as,
1354                               C ′ (⟨st , qt ⟩) = C(⟨st , qt ⟩) + γv(qt ) − v(qt−1 )              (45)
1355
1356   Cycle-based reward shaping is in essence, another instantiation of potential based reward shaping
1357
       but with different potential values. In practice, setting v(q) = ∞ ∀q ∈ F is infeasible, rather we
       just ignore the shaped cost function when transitioning to or from an accepting state. We provide

1393   In all cases we clearly see that without CFE QL-Shield has very unstable convergence both in terms
1394   of reward and safety, often failing to converge at all to the optimal policy. This demonstrates that
1395
       CFE is crucial for efficiently learning the safety objective of the environment, when the safety-
       property is more complex. CFE alleviates the issue of slow and unstable convergence, however
1396
       for property (2) in both the the 9 × 9 and 15 × 15 gridworld, potential-based reward shaping and
1397

1399   gridworld, and an overall higher reward policy for the 15 × 15 gridworld. However, for property
1400   (3) in the 15 × 15 gridworld, both potential-based reward shaping and cycle-based reward shaping
1401   appear to result in slightly unstable learning for the ‘task policy’ πr . We note, that for property (3),
1402   cycle-based reward shaping does improve the safety performance compared to the basic QL-Shield
1403   with CFE. More investigation and hyperparameter tuning is likely required to understand which
       approach is the de facto ‘best’.
