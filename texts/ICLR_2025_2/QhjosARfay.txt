009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               Recent research on tool integration for math Large Language Models (LLMs)
012            aims to combine complementary strengths of chain-of-thought (CoT) reasoning
013            and code execution. However, we discover a critical limitation: current tool-
014            integrated math LLMs rely on externally dictated instructions to decide whether
015            to use CoT or code, lacking the autonomy to choose the most appropriate method
016            independently. This prompts us to study Autonomous Code integration for math
017            LLMs, which enables models to independently develop their own methodology-
018            selection strategy in the absence of reliable supervision. To address this challenge,
019            we propose an innovative Expectation-Maximization (EM) formulation that re-
020
               fines the model’s decision-making through the exploration of its capabilities. This
               framework alternates between (a) computing a reference strategy that improves
021
               the model’s belief over its capabilities through self-exploration, and (b) updat-
022
               ing the model based on the refined belief. We further enhance this framework
023            with an efficient implementation, incorporating a novel data synthesis strategy
024            and off-policy reinforcement learning. Extensive experiments demonstrate that
025            our approach, using only a public query set, significantly boosts the performance
026            of existing math LLMs, raising accuracy by nearly 20% to 65.28% on the chal-
027            lenging MATH benchmark, while reducing code executions by up to 65%. Code
028            and data is released in the anonymous repository.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated remarkable performance across various do-
034
      mains (Kaddour et al., 2023; Achiam et al., 2023; Dubey et al., 2024; Team et al., 2023; Yang et al.,
035
      2024), yet they face significant challenges when addressing complex mathematical problems, such
036   as abstract reasoning and intricate calculations (Gao et al., 2023; Yue et al., 2023; Gou et al., 2023;
037   Li, 2024). To tackle these challenges, two primary paradigms have emerged: chain-of-thought (CoT)
038   reasoning (Wei et al., 2022; Yu et al., 2023; Luo et al., 2023) and the use of external tools, such as
039   Python programs (Toshniwal et al., 2024; Yue et al., 2023). Both strategies offer distinct yet com-
040   plementary benefits. CoT is highly effective for semantic analysis, planning, and abstract reasoning,
041   but it often struggles with precise computations. In contrast, leveraging python programs offloads
042   mathematical operations to specialized tools like symbolic solvers or numerical libraries. Nonethe-
043   less, this approach has shown vulnerability in effectively translating natural language queries into
044
      correct code (Li, 2024).
045   Recent advancements focus on synergizing these paradigms by developing tool-integrated reasoning
046   frameworks, such as TORA (Gou et al., 2023; Shao et al., 2024), HTL (Li, 2024) and DotaMath (Li
047   et al., 2024a), which merge CoT reasoning with code generation to provide more fluid interactions
048   between abstract reasoning and precise computation. However, despite the potential benefits of
049   incorporating external tools, their successful integration presupposes that the model is both adept at
050
      using the tool and capable of reasoning effectively about the problem with the code.
051   This assumption raises concerns about LLMs’ capability boundaries of using tools, underscoring
052   the need for more intelligent code integration strategies. Rather than a reactive decision, code inte-
053   gration should be a deliberate decision informed by the model’s capabilities, mirroring human-like
      methodology-selection for problem-solving.

      We distill this objective into a central goal: developing autonomous code integration (AutoCode)
055   for math LLMs, as illustrated in Fig. 1. Existing tool-integrated math LLMs generally lack the
056   AutoCode capability, because these models are trained to follow an externally imposed methodology
057   that do not adapt to their unique strengths. Therefore, we ask:
058
      How to enable LLMs to learn their own strategies to select the methodology for math queries, such
059
      that it complements the model’s inherent capabilities?
060
061   To address this challenge, we introduce a novel Expectation-Maximization (EM) formulation that
062   enables a self-teaching mechanism without relying on external dictations. Specifically, the Expec-
063   tation step (E-step) computes a reference strategy through self-exploration, representing the belief
064
      over its capabilities. The maximization step (M-step) updates the LLM based on the data induced
      by the new belief. Furthermore, we propose an efficient joint training scheme for this framework,
065
      which features a novel data synthesis strategy for offline data curation and efficient off-policy rein-
066
      forcement learning (RL).
067
068   Extensive experiments show that, using merely public query set, our method significantly improves
069   existing math LLMs. For instance, our 7B model enhances accuracy on the challenging MATH
070
      benchmark (Hendrycks et al., 2021) by nearly 20% and reaches 65.28% accuracy, while reducing
      up to 65% code executions.
071
072   Our contribution is summarized as follows: (1) We identify a critical gap in the existing research
073   on math LLMs, that they struggle with autonomous code integration (AutoCode). (2) We propose
074   a novel EM learning framework that empowers LLMs to develop their own AutoCode strategies,
075   tailored to their inherent capabilities. (3) We showcase a neat and efficient implementation of this
076
      EM framework, featuring a novel data synthesis strategy and an efficient off-policy RL training.
      Extensive results show substantial improvements in both accuracy and computational efficiency.

110   2.1   P ROBLEM S TATEMENT
111
112   Recent research have studied tool integration for math LLMs. Specifically, given a math query
113
      xq ∈ XQ , expressed in natural language, the goal is to generate a solution using a language model
      (LM), denoted as Mθ , parameterized by θ. Importantly, the LM is augmented with access to a
114
      Python interpreter, allowing it to execute Python code as needed during inference. We are interested
115
      in allowing the model to choose the appropriate methodology (denoted as c) – either CoT reasoning
116   or code snippets – and generate the corresponding solutions yt ∈ Y based on the preceding context
117   (xq , y<t ). When code integration is necessary, the model generates special tokens ‘‘‘python
118
      and ‘‘‘ to trigger the Python interpreter, after which the execution results are appended to the on-
119
      going context. Both the math query xq and the response yt consist of sequences of natural language
120   tokens, with interleaved code integration as necessary (see Fig. 1 for an example).
121
122   Our goal is to develop a language model that autonomously integrates code execution for math
123
      problem-solving, switching seamlessly between CoT reasoning and code execution when appropri-
      ate. Conceptually, such code-integrated reasoning process involves two repeated steps: (a) Method-
124
      ology selection. The model first decides whether to use code execution or CoT reasoning based on
125
      the context; (b) Solution generation. Based on the chosen methodology, the model generates either
126   executable Python code or CoT rationales.
127
128   We note that the methodology selection step can occur either implicitly (via the model’s inter-
129
      nal states) or explicitly (through verbal cues generated by the model). For example, in our im-
      plementation, the model is trained to signal the chosen method at the beginning of its response
130
131
      (see Fig. 1), through verbal cues such as Let’s write a program for code integration, and
132    Let’s reason step by step for CoT reasoning. However, it is not required to signal ev-
133   ery methodology-selection decisions. For instance, as shown in Fig. 1 (Right), the model can correct
134   code using CoT reasoning without explicit cues.
135   We formally define our problem as follows. Let c ∈ C = {0, 1} denote the methodology of CoT
136   and code integration, respectively. Given the math query xq , the model selects a methodology
137   c, and generates a corresponding solution ya ∼ p(ya |xq , c; M). This decision-making process
138   iterates until the LM predict an end token. Upon termination, the model receives a binary feedback
139   r = I(ya ) ∈ {0, 1}, where the indicator function I(ya ) evaluates the correctness of the predicted
140   solution ya by comparing it to the gold answer. For clarity and simplicity, we focus our discussion
141   on a single iteration of the methodology-selection and solution-generation
                                                                          P         process, which
                                                                                               P adheres to

      the following factorization of the language policy: pθ (ya |xq ) = c pθ (c, ya |xq ) = c pθ (c|xq ) ·
      pθ (ya |xq , c). And we illustrate in Sec. 2.2 how we handle multiple iterations.

144   2.2   T HE EM F ORMULATION
145
146   Challenge and Motivation. The key challenge in achieving autonomous code integration (Au-
147   toCode) lies in the absence of reliable supervision for making methodology-selection decisions. Ex-
148   isting math LLMs typically employ supervised fine-tuning (SFT) to empower tool integration (Yue
149   et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023b; Li et al., 2024a; Gou et al., 2023; Shao et al., 2024; Li, 2024),
150   which relies on externally dictated expert decisions. Training on such data fall short because it fails
151   to dynamically adapt to the model’s unique capabilities.
152   To address this challenge, we draw inspiration from human decision-making. Humans evaluates
153   whether to seek external assistance based on the complexity of the task and their belief in their ability
154   to handle it. This belief is refined through exploration, observation and reflection. For instance,
155   consider solving a complex math problem, such as finding the roots of an equation. A person
156   might attempt manual calculations using roots formula, or resort to a calculator. To form a belief
157   about whether external tools are necessary, humans explore both options and observe the outcomes.
158
      Through reflection on such experiences, they recognize their limitations in manual computation
      results, and develop a belief that relying on a calculator improves accuracy for such problems.
159
160   The EM Framework. Inspired by this analogy, we propose an Expectation-Maximization (EM)-
161   based approach that emulates the cognitive process of belief refinement and intelligent decision-
      making, alternating between two key steps (Fig. 2):

225   Discussion. Our EM framework mirrors the human cognitive process of belief refinement and in-
226   telligent decision-making. During the E-step, the framework engages in a form of self-exploration
227   by probing its internal model capabilities to identify a reference strategy. This approach stands in
228   contrast to SFT, which relies on external demonstrations.
229   In the subsequent M-step, the focus shifts to self-refinement: the model adjusts its methodology
230   selection based on the reference strategy while reinforcing solution generation. This iterative process
231   enables the model to dynamically adapt to its own strengths and weaknesses, meanwhile fostering
232   continuous improvement. Unlike standard Reinforcement Learning (RL) methods, which enhance
233   decision-making through localized exploration around the current policy, our proposed approach
234   uniquely narrows the policy search space by leveraging the guidance from the reference strategy.
235   Beyond addressing the challenge of autonomous code integration, our EM framework is supported
236   by a robust theoretical foundation. It exhibits favorable properties, such as monotonic improvement
237   of the objective function. Specifically, the evidence lower bound (ELBO) objective satisfies the in-
238   equality: JELBO (s1 , θ0 ) ≤ JELBO (s1 , θ1 ) ≤ JELBO (s2 , θ1 ), indicating a guaranteed progression
239   toward better performance as the model iterates through the EM steps.

      2.3     P RACTICAL I MPLEMENTATION
243
244   While the EM framework provides a promising solution for AutoCode, its practical application
245   faces two primary challenges. First, the E-step (Eq.5) involves computing the posterior, which is
246   inherently complex. Second, the M-step (Eq.6) introduces fragmented training processes: SFT for
247   methodology selection using offline data and RL for solution generation via on-policy interactions.
248   This separation not only complicates implementation but is also nonsensical, as both policies share
249   the same parameter space in a unified LLM policy and should not be trained separately.
250   To address these challenges, we present a practical implementation that unifies the training of
251   methodology-selection and solution-generation policies, enabling efficient joint optimization. This
252   streamlined approach consists of a data curation stage and an offline RL training stage. Specifically,
253   the data curation stage produces data for methodology selection and solution generation, combining
254   them into complete responses using two distinct synthesis strategies. These complete responses en-
255   able a modified M-step objective (Eq. 6) that supports efficient offline training without the need for
256
      on-policy interactions. We provide an algorithm diagram in Appendix Sec. B.

      GSM8k (Cobbe et al., 2021) and MATH (Hendrycks et al., 2021). To test generalization, we use
350
      four out-of-domain benchmarks:AsDIV (Miao et al., 2021), GaokaoMath2023 (Liao et al., 2024),
351   CollegeMath (Tang et al., 2024), OlympiadBench (He et al., 2024). This selection provides a com-
352   prehensive test of math reasoning capabilities, ranging from elementary-level to Olympiad-level
353   math problems. We use accuracy with greedy sampling as our primary metric. We evaluate the
354   correctness of predicted responses using released codes from DeepseekMath (Shao et al., 2024) and
355   Qwen2Math (Yang et al., 2024).
356
      Baselines. We compare our method against proprietary models like GPT-4 (Achiam et al., 2023) and
357   Claude (Anthropic, 2024), as well as open-source and tool-integrated math LLMs such as Numina-
358   Math (Li et al., 2024b), Mathstral (AI, 2024), Mammoth (Yue et al., 2023), MathCoder (Wang
359   et al., 2023b), ToRA (Gou et al., 2023), DotaMath (Li et al., 2024a), AlphaMath (Chen et al.,
360   2024a), MathGenie (Lu et al., 2024). To examine the effectiveness of the proposed approach on
361   top of existing math LLMs, we consider AutoCode Training on Llama-3.1 (Dubey et al., 2024),
362   Qwen2Math (Yang et al., 2024), and DeepseekMath (Shao et al., 2024). Since the proposed
363   method preconditions on the coding ability for math, we use the aforementioned SFT data to
364   fine-tune Llama-3.1 and Qwen2Math, and term the resulting model as Code4Math-[ModelFamily].
365
      DeepseekMath natively supports writing code to solve math queries.
366   Due to computational limits, we focus on experiments with 7B models. We report cited performance
367   metrics for most models, and include larger model performances for the readers’ reference.
368
      Implementation Details. We use K = 5 rollouts for response sampling during self-exploration,
369   using nucleus sampling with a temperature of 1.0 and top-p set to 0.95. For computing the reference
370   strategy, we set the temperature parameterα = ∞, reducing it to a hard-max (greedy selection) of
371   the top-ranking methodology. Regarding learning rate, we set instruction fine-tuning as 2e−5 , the
372   first RL iteration as 1e−5 , and the consecutive iterations as 5e−6 . We use 8 A100(80G) GPUs. For
373   reproduction, we upload code and relevant materials to the anonymous repository Repo.

477   3.3              A NALYSIS OF THE L EARNED M ODEL .
478
479   To understand the effectiveness of the learned methodology-selection strategy, particularly its ability
480   to decide when to use or avoid code, we evaluate its contributions to accuracy. We focus our analysis
481   on the first round of methodology-selection, arguing that this initial decision captures the essential
482
      characteristics of the strategy, while subsequent rounds play a minor role in overall accuracy.
483   We introduce the alignment rate as a key metric to measure how closely the learned strategy aligns
484   with an oracle strategy. The oracle strategy, determined over the test set, dictates that code is used
485   only if it is the sole path to a correct response; otherwise, CoT reasoning is preferred. We classify the
      correct responses into three categories: (1) StrictAlign, where decisions match the oracle exactly, (2)


                                                                                                          9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      AllowCode, where we consider using code acceptable if both code and CoT yield correct answers,
487   and (3) MisAlign, where the model’s choice diverges from the oracle.
488
489   Fig. 4 shows these alignment categories in stacked bars. The RL model has a significantly lower
490
      rate of strict alignment compared to AutoCode (lightblue bar), which is not surprising as the RL
      model is not explicitly trained for this alignment. In contrast, the proposed EM framework is forced
491
      to imitate the behavior of the reference strategy, which computes the oracle selection over training
492
      queries. Additionally, regarding code executions, the RL model also tends to favor code more than
493   AutoCode, particularly in the case of Qwen2Math. This is because Qwen2Math has better overall
494   accuracy using code than CoT reasoning, and RL captures this statistical relation. In contrast, the
495   proposed method can implicitly reduces code execution as a side effect, because the oracle selection
496   to imitate during training by design involves minimal code executions.
497
      Interestingly, compared with other models, a significant proportion of correct responses from Au-
498
      toCode are in the MisAlign category. This seems counter-intuitive at first sight, but we note that
499   the oracle choice is determined based on their expected utilities, which means the MisAlign choice
500   can result in correct response at a probability, which is inferior to the oracle choice in the expected
501   sense. This implies that a MisAlign choice can be optimized for final accuracy by improving the cor-
502   responding solution-generation capability. Further comparison with NO REFL suggests that multi-
503   round responses also play a crucial role in this advantage.
504
505   4   R ELATED W ORK AND D ISCUSSION
506
507   Tool-Integrated Math LLMs. Math language models (LLMs) have been developed primarily along
508   two major paradigms: Chain-of-Thought (CoT) reasoning and the use of external tools, such as
509   Python programs (Yu et al., 2023; Yue et al., 2023; Toshniwal et al., 2024). Each paradigm offers
510   unique benefits, and recent advancements increasingly seek to combine these approaches for syner-
511   gistic effects, as seen in methods like Hybrid Instruction Tuning (Yue et al., 2023) and various forms
512   of tool-integrated reasoning (Gou et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024a; Li, 2024; Wang et al., 2023b; Shao
513
      et al., 2024). Despite these advancements, existing models still face challenges with autonomous
      code integration, mainly because they rely on supervised fine-tuning where code integration is sub-
514
      optimally dictated by external annotators. To address this gap, our work introduces a novel EM
515
      framework that avoids using external supervision to facilitate AutoCode capabilities.
516
517   EM for RL. Expectation-Maximization (EM) has proven effective for maximum likelihood prob-
518   lems involving hidden variables, such as Expert Iteration (Anthony et al., 2017), Iterative Maximum
519
      Likelihood (Wu, 2016; Agarwal et al., 2019), Meta-Reinforcement Learning (Zintgraf et al., 2019;
      Wang et al., 2020), and Adversarial Games (Wang et al., 2023a). In the context of math LLMs,
520
      the most relevant works are (Singh et al., 2023) and (Ni et al., 2022), which apply EM-style itera-
521
      tive self-training to math problem-solving. Unlike these approaches, we employ the EM framework
522   specifically to tackle the lack of reliable supervision for autonomous code integration in math LLMs.
523
524   Discussion. This paper investigates autonomous code integration for math LLMs. To address the
525
      challenge of unreliable external supervision, we propose to factorize out the hidden methodology-
      selection from response generation, and develop a novel EM formulation. The EM framework alter-
526
      nates between computing a reference strategy for methodology-selection through self-exploration
527
      and updating language model based on the reference guidance. This approach supports an effi-
528   cient joint training scheme that allows for holistic offline data collection coupled with RL training.
529   Our extensive experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method, and our ablation
530   studies further elucidate the properties of the learned model.
531
      However, there are several limitations and areas for future work regarding AutoCode4Math. First,
532
      the generalization of methodology-selection depends significantly on the quality of the collected
533
      query set. Further research is needed to understand what characteristics of queries contribute to
534   effective generalization. Second, we did not extensively explore the influence of hyperparameters
535   related to RL iterations, such as dataset size and the number of iterations, in the current version. We
536   are actively working on this. Third, as this is a preliminary work in autonomous code integration,
537   we have not yet investigated alternative approaches for decision routing, such as using Mixture-
538   of-Experts (MoEs) (Masoudnia & Ebrahimpour, 2014), and we not yet fully understand the fun-
539   damental reason why EM outperforms RL. These areas present important directions for advancing
      AutoCode capabilities in math LLMs.
