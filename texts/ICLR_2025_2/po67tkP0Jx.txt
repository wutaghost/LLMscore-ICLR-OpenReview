009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Robustness research in reinforcement learning often focuses on ensuring that the
012            policy consistently exhibits capable, goal-driven behavior. However, not every
013            capable behavior is the intended behavior. Goal misgeneralization can occur when
014            the policy generalizes capably with respect to a ‘proxy goal’ whose optimal be-
015            havior correlates with the intended goal on the training distribution, but not out of
016            distribution. Though the intended goal would be ambiguous if they were perfectly
017            correlated in training, we show progress can be made if the goals are only nearly
018
               ambiguous, with the training distribution containing a small proportion of disam-
               biguating levels. We observe that the training signal from disambiguating levels
019
               could be amplified by regret-based prioritization. We formally show that approxi-
020
               mately optimal policies on maximal-regret levels avoid the harmful effects of goal
021            misgeneralization, which may exist without this prioritization. Empirically, we
022            find that current regret-based Unsupervised Environment Design (UED) methods
023            can mitigate the effects of goal misgeneralization, though do not always entirely
024            eliminate it. Our theoretical and empirical results show that as UED methods
025            improve they could further mitigate goal misgeneralization in practice.

028   1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030   As reinforcement learning (RL) is increasingly applied in complex, open-ended, real-world environ-
031   ments, it becomes infeasible for training to comprehensively cover all situations that an agent will
032   face in deployment. A particular challenge arises when insufficiently diverse training creates a ‘proxy
033
      goal’ that, compared to the true goal, induces similar behavior during training but radically different
      behavior out of distribution. In the face of proxy goals, standard RL methods sometimes find a policy
034
      that internalizes the wrong goal, completely ignoring the true goal in favor of capably pursuing the
035
      proxy goal out of distribution (Langosco et al., 2022; Shah et al., 2022). This phenomenon, known as
036   goal misgeneralization, is a pressing problem in assuring the safety of RL agents (Ngo et al., 2023).
037
038   If the true goal and the proxy goal are perfectly correlated in the training distribution, then goal
039
      generalization comes down to the algorithm’s inductive biases. However, we find that we can make
      progress on goal misgeneralization regardless of the algorithm’s biases by working in a relaxed
040
      setting where the training distribution is only nearly ambiguous, providing a weak training signal in
041
      favor of optimizing the true goal. In particular, we model a complex environment as comprising a
042   series of levels (Cobbe et al., 2020; Kirk et al., 2023) which can be designed during training (Dennis
043   et al., 2020). We assume that while most training levels leave the true goal ambiguous, a small
044   proportion of levels are available to disambiguate the goals by incentivizing different behavior. In
045   this setting, we show that the standard RL training method of domain randomization (DR; Tobin
046   et al., 2017), which optimizes for expected return on the training level distribution, may still induce
047   goal misgeneralization if the proportion of disambiguating levels in training is sufficiently small.
048   However, we observe that even when following the proxy goal is approximately optimal in terms of
049   maximum expected return on the training distribution, it is clearly sub-optimal in terms of minimax
050   expected regret (MMER; Savage, 1951), since it leaves the true goal unfulfilled in disambiguating
051   levels. We hypothesize that MMER-based training methods such as unsupervised environment design
052   (UED; Dennis et al., 2020) should naturally mitigate this kind of goal misgeneralization, anticipating
053   a possible distribution shift and amplifying the weak training signal from disambiguating levels. In
      this paper, we contribute the following theoretical and empirical results in support of this hypothesis:

076   2.1   U NDERSPECIFIED M ARKOV DECISION PROCESSES
077
078   A reward-free underspecified Markov decision process (UMPD) is a tuple M = ⟨A, Θ, S, I, T ⟩
079   where A is the agent’s action space, Θ is the space of the free parameters of the environment, S
080
      is a state space, I : Θ → ∆(S) is an initial state distribution, and T : Θ × S × A → ∆(S) is
      a conditional transition distribution. An agent’s behavior in an UMDP is represented by a policy,
081
      a conditional action distribution π : Θ × S → ∆(A). We denote by Π the set of all policies.
082
      Given a level θ ∈ Θ we have a reward-free MDP Mθ = ⟨A, S, I(θ), T (θ, −, −)⟩ and an agent’s
083   level-specific policy π(θ, −). We omit θ when not relevant or clear from the context. Together
084   with the initial state distribution and transition distribution a policy π induces a distribution over
085   trajectories τ = (s0 , a0 , s1 , a1 , . . .) with s0 ∼ I(θ), at ∼ π(θ, st ), and st+1 ∼ T (θ, st , at ). Given
086   a full trajectory τ = (s0 , a0 , . . .) we denote by τ̄ = (s0 , s1 , . . .) the corresponding state trajectory.
087   A given policy similarly induces a distribution over state trajectories. We denote by T the set of state
088   trajectories with positive probability under some policy.
089   Given a reward-free UMDP M and a level θ ∈ Θ, let R : S × A × S → R be a reward function, and
090   let γ ∈ [0, 1] be a discount factor. Together, M , R and γ make a regular UMDP, but we define the
091   reward functions separately so that we can more easily talk about one environment given different
092   reward functions. We define thePreturn (with respect to R), U R (τ ), as the discounted reward collected

124   3    P ROBLEM SETTING
125
126
      When the true goal and the proxy goal perfectly correlate on the entire training distribution, it is
      impossible to distinguish between polices optimizing for either goal. Hence, we propose to study
127
      goal misgeneralization in a relaxed setting with a nearly ambiguous training distribution—we assume
128
      that a small subset of training levels provide a weak training signal that disambiguates the true goal
129   from the proxy goal. This relaxation mirrors the assumptions made in previous work on spurious
130   correlations in supervised learning (Liu et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2022).
131
132
      To formalize our setting, we next introduce and define the concepts of proxy goals, what it means for
      a level to be disambiguating or ambiguating, and of a C-distinguishing distribution shift.
133
134
      Definition 3.1 (Proxy goal). Given a reward-free UMDP M , a level distribution Λ ∈ ∆(Θ), and a
135   (true) reward function R, we say a reward function R̃ : S × A × S → R is a proxy goal if there exists
136
      a proxy policy π̃ which is approximately optimal with respect to both the given goal and the proxy
137
      goal: ∃π̃ ∈ Π⋆ε (R, Λ) ∩ Π⋆ε (R̃, Λ).
138   Definition 3.2 (Ambiguating level). Given a reward-free UMDP and a pair of reward functions R
139   and R̃, a level θ ∈ Θ is (perfectly) ambiguating if all optimal policies with respect to either reward
140   are approximately optimal with respect to the other as well, that is,
141                                           Π⋆0 (R, θ) = Π⋆0 (R̃, θ)
142   Definition 3.3 (C-disambiguating level). Given a reward-free UMDP, a pair of reward functions R
143
      and R̃, and a constant C > 0, a level θ ∈ Θ is C-disambiguating if all policies that are optimal with
144
      respect to R̃ achieve C-sub-optimal return with respect to R, that is,
145
146
                                            Π⋆0 (R̃, θ) ∩ Π⋆C (R, θ) = ∅
147   We note that some levels may be neither perfectly ambiguating nor C-disambiguating for any C > 0.
148
      Definition 3.4 (C-distinguishing distribution shift). Consider a reward-free UMDP, a pair of reward
149
      functions R and R̃, a pair of level distributions ΛTrain , ΛTest ∈ ∆(Θ), a pair of ratios α, β ∈ [0, 1],
150   and a constant C > 0. A distribution shift from ΛTrain to ΛTest is C-distinguishing if the following
151   conditions hold.
152
153         1. ΛTrain has probability α on C-disambiguating levels and the rest on ambiguating levels.
154
            2. ΛTest has probability β on C-disambiguating levels and the rest on ambiguating levels.
155
156   We focus on the setting where α is small and β is large. When α is small, the training distribution
157   is nearly entirely ambiguous. In this setting, if R is the true goal then there exists a proxy policy
158   π̃ ∈ Π⋆ε (R, ΛTrain ) ∩ Π⋆ε (R̃, ΛTrain ) ∩ Π⋆0 (R̃, ΛTest ) that optimizes R̃ on C-disambiguating levels.
159   This proxy policy makes R̃ a proxy goal with respect to ΛTrain . Furthermore, when β is large, this
160   proxy policy performs sub-optimally with respect to the true goal R after the distribution shift to
161   ΛTest . If an RL system learns the proxy policy π̃ on ΛTrain and then is subject to a distribution shift
      to ΛTest , this is an example of goal misgeneralization.

      4     T HEORETICAL RESULTS
163
164   In Section 4.1, we prove that, while training on a fixed level distribution can lead to goal misgeneral-
165   ization, regret-based prioritization of levels can prevent this. Intuitively, this is because pursuing a
166   proxy goal on C-disambiguating levels generates high regret, and the adversary will play these levels,
167   incentivizing the agent to pursue the true goal.
168
      In Section 4.2, we give a model of the negative consequences of goal misgeneralization. In particular,
169
      we give a sufficient condition for a level to be C-disambiguating. This model is based on the
170   existence of some abstract limited “resource” that, if put towards optimizing the proxy goal, would
171   leave the true goal unfulfilled. We take inspiration from prior work on the consequences of objective
172   misspecification (Zhuang & Hadfield-Menell, 2020). However, we adapt and generalize these
173   arguments to the sequential decision-making context.
174

178   reward function assigns +0.5 for an apple, while the proxy assigns +0.5 to the basket.

199   4.1    M INIMAX EXPECTED REGRET MITIGATES GOAL MISGENERALIZATION
200
201   In this section, we show that under a C-distinguishing distribution shift, the DR objective of maxi-
202   mizing the expected value on the training distribution of levels permits an approximately optimal
203
      policy that performs poorly on the test distribution, as an instance of goal misgeneralization. On
      the other hand, we show that any policy that is approximately optimal with respect to the MMER
204
      objective is approximately optimal on the test distribution, avoiding the negative consequences of
205
      goal misgeneralization.
206

208   correctly generalizing policy, we characterize the possibility for some optimizer to select a proxy
209

      by which the optimization algorithm misgeneralizes.

      4.2   G OAL MISGENERALIZATION AND ITS CONSEQUENCES
227
228   In this section we provide criteria which imply that a given level is C-disambiguating with respect
229   to a specific true goal R and proxy goal R̃. It follows that in such levels optimizing R̃ will lead
230   to consequences that are negative as measured under R. Intuitively, these consequences can be
231   thought of as the result of optimizing a misspecified objective, as has been studied previously
232   by Zhuang & Hadfield-Menell (2020). Our argument follows a similar structure to theirs—optimizing
233   a misspecified objective, whose performance is constrained by a limited resource, will force the agent
234   to take that resource away from optimizing the true objective. We generalize this argument to the RL
235   setting, and define these resources in terms of features of the local state trajectory.
236   An agent’s resource could represent some physical resource explicit in the state, time in discounted
237   MDPs, or simply the opportunity cost of taking one action instead of another. At the outset of an
238   episode, the agent possesses some amount of this resource and decides how to allocate it throughout
239   the episode in order to achieve a high return. We always consider limited resources—once consumed,
240   a resource cannot be regained. In other words, a limited resource it is a non-increasing function along
241   each state trajectory.
242   Definition 4.2 (Limited resource). A limited resource is defined as F : S × T → R such that for any
243   state trajectory τ̄ = (s0 , . . .) ∈ T, ∀i < j, it holds that F (sj , τ̄ ) ≤ F (si , τ̄ ).
244
245   In terms of our running example, when we say that the number of coins the agent has remaining is a
246
      limited resource, we mean that it is non-increasing along each state trajectory.
247   While there may be many limited resources in a given level, not all limited resources cause a trade-off
248   between two reward functions. Intuitively, the strongest trade-off would occur when a resource can be
249   allocated to exclusively one of the reward functions. This motivates the idea of a resource allocation
250   tracking the division of the resource between two reward functions across a trajectory.
251   Definition 4.3 (Resource allocation). A resource allocation of a resource F is defined by a tuple
252   ⟨GR , GR̃ , G∅ , f1 , f2 ⟩ where GR , GR̃ : S ×A×S → R≥0 are functions describing how the resources

255   f1 , f2 : S × A × S × R → R, are conversion functions, which describe how spent resources
256   correspond to increased value for R and R̃ respectively.

311
312   Regret-based UED methods have been motivated by their potential to improve sample efficiency and
313   robustness. We have shown that, in theory, the MMER objective is also well-suited to mitigating
314   goal misgeneralization in the face of a C-distinguishing distribution shift. In this section, we validate
315   that existing UED methods are empirically capable of mitigating goal misgeneralization, showing
316   that their level-generating adversaries are able to locate the region of the level space containing
317   disambiguating levels, recognize when the policy has high regret in those levels, and amplify the
318   weak training signal provided by these levels. We call this the amplification effect.
319   We construct two custom procedurally-generated grid-world environments that exhibit a C-
320   distinguishing distribution shift. For each environment we construct a distribution of ambiguating
321   levels Λ1 , a distribution of (primarily) disambiguating levels Λ2 , and from these a training distribu-
322   tion ΛTrain
             α     = (1 − α)Λ1 + αΛ2 where α ∈ [0, 1] is a mixture weight controlling the proportion of

      described in Section 5.1. We describe the environment constructions in Sections 5.2 and 5.3.

            2. ACCEL (adversarially compounding complexity by editing levels; Parker-Holder et al., 2023):
337            Extends PLR⊥ by, in addition to occasionally sampling new levels from an underlying level
338            distribution, also exploring the level space in the neighborhood of the levels already in the
339            buffer using a mutation operation µ : Θ → ∆(Θ) to generate similar levels. As with PLR⊥ ,
340            we keep high-regret levels in a level buffer used to train the policy.
341
342
      We quantify the amplification effect by tracking the average frequency at which disambiguating
      levels are sampled from the adversary’s level buffer throughout training. This gives an estimate of the
343
      average probability the adversary assigns to disambiguating levels over training. We compare this to
344
      the proportion of disambiguating levels in the underlying training distribution (the mixture weight α).
345

      Mutation operations ACCEL additionally requires specifying a mutation operation that makes n
      various environment-specific edits to the level (e.g. moving walls or changing the agent’s starting
358

359
      in how they affect the balance between ambiguating and disambiguating levels, as follows.
360
361         1. Constant goal mutation operation (ACCELc ): We make n − 1 random edits that do not
362            affect goal ambiguity, followed by one edit that transforms the level into a disambiguating
363            version of the level with probability α or an ambiguating version with probability 1 − α
364            (irrespective of whether the input level is disambiguating or ambiguating). Applying this
365
               operation to any distribution of levels results in a distribution with the same proportion of
               disambiguating and ambiguating levels as the training distribution.
366
367         2. Identity goal mutation operation (ACCELid ): We make n random edits that do not affect the
368
               goal ambiguity of the level, and no other edits. Applying this operation to any distribution of
               levels results in a distribution with the same proportion of disambiguating and ambiguating
369
               levels as the input distribution.
370
371
            3. Binomial goal mutation operation (ACCELbin ): We make a fixed number of edits n where
               each edit is independently chosen to be either a random edit that does not affect goal
372
               ambiguity (with probability 1 − n1 ) or otherwise (with probability n1 ) an edit that transforms
373
               the level into a disambiguating version of the level with probability α or an ambiguating
374            version with probability 1 − α. This has essentially the same effect as ACCELc , except in
375            the event that no goal ambiguity edits are sampled (with probability (1 − n1 )n ) in which
376            case it has the same effect as ACCELid .
377

483   6     R ELATED WORK
484
485   Unsupervised environment design (UED). Our work shows that UED methods are powerful tools
      to improve inner alignment of reinforcement learning agents. While it is well-known that UED


                                                                                                        9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      methods improve capabilities generalization and general robustness of RL agents, this work is the first
487   demonstration of their power for improving alignment of RL agents as well. UED was formalized by
488   Dennis et al. (2020). Following works have since proposed additional UED algorithms, like PLR⊥
489   and ACCEL used in this work (Jiang et al., 2022; Parker-Holder et al., 2023).
490
491   Goal misgeneralization. Alignment of reinforcement learning agents is a very challenging problem.
492   A distinction can be made between outer alignment and inner alignment (Ngo et al., 2023). Inner
493   misalignment occurs when an agent fails to robustly pursue the preferences of the principal despite
494   correct specification of the reward function (Hubinger et al., 2019). Goal misgeneralization can be
495   considered a type of inner alignment failure. Inner alignment failures can also arise due to lack of
496   adversarial robustness (Lu et al., 2023). Compared to outer alignment, very few works exist that
497
      are explicitly focused on assuring inner alignment of RL agents. Specific to goal misgeneralization,
      Langosco et al. (2022) and Shah et al. (2022) demonstrated the occurrence of goal misgeneralization
498
      in various setups, but do not propose any methods to mitigate it. Starace (2023) approaches goal
499
      misgeneralization as ‘task-underspecification’ and conditions decision transformer style models on
500   natural language descriptions of goals instead of scalar reward values. This is a complementary
501   approach aimed at influencing the inductive biases in RL to favor the true goal in more circumstances.
502
503   Underspecification. Goal misgeneralization in some sense arises from underspecification present
504   in the training setup (Shah et al., 2022). When a machine learning problem is underspecified,
505   multiple models may exist that behave similarly on in-distribution data but behave in qualitatively
506   different ways on out-of-distribution data (Teney et al., 2022). Prior works have discussed how
507   underspecification can lead to erroneous evaluation of reinforcement learning (Jayawardana et al.,
508   2022), and machine learning models in general (D’Amour et al., 2022). Underspecification underlying
509   goal misgeneralization is also related to the identifiability of reward functions. Goal misgeneralization
510
      occurs when there exist proxies that highly correlate with the true reward function on the training
      domain, thus, posing a challenge for the learning agent to correctly identify the right reward function.
511
      A reward function is generally not identifiable from behavioral data within a single environment
512
      (Ng et al., 2000; Skalse et al., 2023; Schlaginhaufen & Kamgarpour, 2023). However, in multi-
513   environment setups, it is sometimes possible to uniquely identify the reward function (Amin et al.,
514   2017; Cao et al., 2021; Büning et al., 2022; Rolland et al., 2022).
515

      7    C ONCLUSIONS
518
      We studied goal misgeneralization arising from a nearly ambiguous training distribution followed by
519
      a C-distinguishing distribution shift. In this setting, we have contributed a combination of theoretical
520
      and empirical results characterizing the ability of the minimax expected regret (MMER) objective and
521   regret-based unsupervised environment design (UED) methods to mitigate goal misgeneralization.
522   Our results also highlight the pitfalls of training with fixed level distributions.
523
524
      We have provided a formal framework of the potential negative consequences of goal misgeneral-
      ization in sequential decision-making. Using this framework, we have proven the first theoretical
525
      guarantees of the effectiveness of different training methods in avoiding these negative consequences
526
      in the setting of C-distinguishing distribution shifts.
527
528   We have also empirically established that existing regret-based UED methods are capable of ampli-
529   fying weak training signals favoring correct goal generalization via the amplification effect. These
530
      results signal UED’s potential as a defense against goal misgeneralization—the amplification effect
      is in a position to become increasingly powerful as UED methods improve since more successful
531
      UED methods should be even more capable of detecting and amplifying rare, high-regret levels.
532
      Moreover, while prioritization-based UED methods like PLR⊥ are confined to the support of the
533   training distribution, ACCEL is only limited by the span of its mutation operations, and more powerful
534   UED methods could in principle surface disambiguating levels from anywhere in the level space.
535
536
      Future work must address the setting of fully ambiguous training distributions and distribution shifts
      beyond the reach of UED algorithms, in which case we again face the challenge of understanding the
537
      internal dynamics of our learning algorithms and our learned policies. We are hopeful that our work
538
      will instigate further research on the problem of goal misgeneralization, which remains a critical,
539   open problem in alignment and safe generalization of reinforcement learning agents.

      A.2    P ROOF OF THE R ESOURCE T HEOREM (T HEOREM 4.6)
757
758   Theorem 4.6. Consider a reward-free UMDP, a level θ ∈ Θ, a pair of reward functions R, R̃, a
759   constant C > 0, a limited resource F , and a resource allocation ⟨GR , GR̃ , G∅ , f1 , f2 ⟩. If F is
760   C-critical w.r.t R and marginally useful w.r.t. R̃, then for any policies π R , π R̃ optimal w.r.t. R, R̃
761   respectively,
762                                     U R (π R̃ , θ) < U R (π R , θ) − C
763
      In other words, θ is a C-disambiguating level.
764
765
      Proof. By definition, U R (π R , θ) is the optimal utility achievable under R. We want to show that
766
767
      U R (π R̃ , θ) is low. By definition, it must be that since the resource is marginally useful for R̃ and π R̃
768   is optimal according to π R̃ , any trajectory τ = (s0 , a0 , . . .) with positive probability under π R̃ must
769   devote all resources towards R̃:
770                               |τ̄ |−1
                                   X
771                                         GR̃ (st , at , st+1 ) = F (s0 , τ̄ ) − F (s|τ̄ | , τ̄ )
772                                t=0
773
      But if that is the case, because of the conditions on the resource allocation, it must hold that
774
775                                               |τ̄ |−1
                                                   X
776                                                         GR (st , at , st+1 ) = 0
777                                                t=0

778
779   Given the fact that the resource is critical for R, ∃ some π such that U R (π) > U R (π R̃ ) + C. But
780   because π R is optimal w.r.t R, U R (π R ) ≥ U R (π). It follows that
781

                                                  U R (π R̃ ) < U R (π R ) − C
783
784   Now since this holds for all optimal policies π R̃ ∈ Π⋆0 (R̃, θ) it follows that
785

786
787   This concludes the proof.

       F    A DDITIONAL EXPERIMENTS WITH KEYS AND CHESTS ENVIRONMENT
1135
1136
       In addition to the environments considered in the main text, this appendix considers experiments in a
1137   third environment inspired by the Keys and Chests environment studied in Langosco et al. (2022).
1138
1139   In this environment, once again, a mouse navigates a maze grid-world. This maze contains both keys
1140
       and chests. The mouse collects keys and uses collected keys to open chests. The true goal assigns +1
       reward for each open chest. Episodes terminate when the mouse collects all available (true) reward,
1141
       or after a fixed maximum episode length. Levels are procedurally generated with randomized wall,
1142
       key, chest, and mouse positions. The observations are Boolean grids with one channel respectively
1143   coding the position(s) of the walls, mouse, keys, and chests, and a fifth channel used to code the
1144   presence of keys that have been collected but not yet used to open chests.
1145
1146
       Consider a distribution of sparse-key levels with a small number of keys and a large number of chests.
       One proxy goal assigns +1 reward for collecting each key and a small positive reward for opening
1147
       each chest. An example proxy policy collects all available keys and then subsequently opens as many
1148
       chests as keys collected. Sparse-key levels are ambiguating (at least approximately, see below). Dense-
1149   key levels, with a large number of keys and a small number of chests, are disambiguating—pursuing
1150   the proxy goal incentivizes collecting all of available keys before opening any chests.
1151
1152
       While still a grid-world, this environment is more complex than those studied in Section 5. Firstly,
       rather than receiving reward for carrying out a single navigation task, the policy has to achieve a
1153
       compound task of navigating first to a key and then to a chest. In fact, since there are multiple keys
1154
       and chests, the policy has to solve multiple of these tasks concurrently. Secondly, as noted, sparse-key
1155   levels are only approximately ambiguating. Optimizing the proxy goal by collecting all keys before
1156   opening any chests is approximately optimal according to the true goal, since the reward from delayed
1157   chest opening is slightly discounted. Thirdly, the nature of goal ambiguity in sparse-key levels is
1158   richer. Collecting keys is subject to delayed reinforcement, and ambiguity arises as to whether the
1159   key collection was merely instrumentally valuable or was intrinsically valuable.
1160
       Langosco et al. (2022) showed that in a similar ProcGen environment (Cobbe et al., 2020) that a
1161   policy trained on sparse-key levels with DR can misgeneralize, collecting keys in dense-key levels.
1162   We extend this finding to the case where the training distribution also contains a small proportion
1163   of dense-key levels. We study UED methods PLRheuristic
                                                          ⊥         and ACCELheuristic which estimate the
1164   maximum return available on a level with a simple heuristic (+1 for each reachable chest if there is
1165   a corresponding reachable key, not accounting for discounting). For ACCELheuristic , the mutation
1166   operation randomly toggles a subset of walls or moves a subset of keys, chests, and the mouse. We
1167   use the hyperparameters from Table 1 excepting a smaller number of mutations per step (6) and a
1168   larger PPO entropy coefficient (1e-2) to encourage more exploration for this complex task. Figure 11
1169
       shows the results. We see that DR is susceptible to goal misgeneralization until α = 1e-1 (10%),
       whereas PLR⊥ successfully mitigates goal misgeneralization at α = 3e-2 (3%) and ACCEL mitigates
1170
       goal misgeneralization at all positive mixture weights tested (including as low as α = 3e-4).
