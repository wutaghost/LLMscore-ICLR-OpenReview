009                                               A BSTRACT
010            We present HumanoidOlympics, a collection of physically simulated sports en-
011            vironments designed for the animation and robotics communities to develop hu-
012            manoid behaviors. Our suite includes individual sports such as golf, javelin throw,
013            high jump, long jump, and hurdling, as well as competitive games like table tennis,
014            tennis, fencing, boxing, soccer, and basketball. By simulating a wide range of
015            Olympic sports, HumanoidOlympics offers a rich and standardized testing ground
016            to evaluate and develop learning algorithms due to the diversity and physically
017            demanding nature of athletic activities. Our suite supports simulating both graphics-
018
               focused (SMPL and SMPL-X) and real-world humanoid robots. For each sport,
               we benchmark popular humanoid control methods and provide expert-designed
019
               rewards that lead to surprising simulation results. Our analysis shows that leverag-
020
               ing human demonstrations can significantly enhance the resulting policies’ human
021            likeness and task performance. By providing a unified and competitive sports
022            benchmark, HumanoidOlympics can help the animation and robotics communi-
023            ties develop human-like and performant controllers. Supplementary videos are
024            available at https://humanoidolympics.github.io/.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      Competitive sports, much like their role in human society, offer a standardized way of measuring
030   the performance of learning algorithms and creating emergent behavior. Natural and human-like
031   humanoid motion in sports can be used in animation, robotics motion planning, and more, where the
032   quality of the motion is important. Human-like motion is not only visually appealing but also serves
033   as a functional and efficient foundation for various tasks such as navigation, grasping, etc.. While
034   there exist isolated efforts to bring individual sport into physics simulation [12, 50, 11, 49, 43, 39],
035   each work uses a different humanoid, simulator, and learning algorithm, which prevents unified
036   evaluation. Their specially built humanoids also make it difficult to acquire compatible motion data.
037   Building a collection of simulated sports environments that supports a range of standardized humanoid
038
      embodiment and training pipeline is challenging, as it requires expert knowledge in humanoid control,
      reinforcement learning (RL), and physics simulation.
039
040   These challenges have led to previous benchmarks and simulated environments [2, 35] focusing
041   mainly on locomotion tasks for humanoids. While these tasks (e.g., moving forward, getting up from
042   the ground, traversing terrains) are benchmarks, they lack the depth and diversity needed to induce
043   a wide range of behaviors and strategies. As a result, these environments do not fully exploit the
044
      potential of humanoids to discover actions and skills found in real-world human activities.
045   Another important challenge of working with simulated humanoids is the ease of obtaining human
046   demonstrations. The resemblance to the human body makes humanoids capable of performing a
047   diverse set of skills; a human can also easily judge the strategies used by humanoids. Curated human
048   motion can be used either as motion prior [23, 24, 33] or in evaluation protocols. Thus, having
049   an easy way to obtain new human motion data compatible with the humanoid, either from motion
050
      capture (MoCap) or videos, is critical for simulated humanoid environments.
051   In this work, we propose HumanoidOlympics, a collection of physically simulated environments for
052   a variety of Olympic sports. Tackling these environments requires not only locomotion skills, but
053   also manipulation, coordination, and planning. Our environments also support multiple humanoid
      embodiments and provide a rich set of challenges for developing and testing embodied agents. We

113   2    R ELATED W ORKS
114
115   Simulated Humanoid Sports. Simulated humanoid sports can help generate animations and explore
116   optimal sports strategies. Research has focused on various individual sports within simulated
117   environments, including tennis [50], table tennis [39], boxing [43, 52], fencing [43], basketball
118   [11, 40, 41] and soccer [45, 12]. These studies leverage human demonstration to achieve human-like
119   behaviors, using it to acquire motor skills [12, 43] or establish motion prior [50]. However, the
120
      diverse and non-standard humanoid models across studies makes it difficult to aggregate additional
      human demonstration data. Furthermore, the task-specific training pipelines in these studies are
121
      hard to generalize to new sports. In contrast, HumanoidOlympics provides a unified benchmark
122
      employing a standard humanoids and training pipeline across all sports. This standardization not only
123   facilitates extension to more sports, but also simplifies benchmarking learning algorithms.
124
125   Simulated RL Benchmarks. Simulated full-body humanoids provide a valuable platform for
126   studying embodied intelligence due to their close resemblance to real-world human behavior and
127   physical interactions. Current RL benchmarks [2, 35, 19] often focus on locomotion tasks such as
128   moving forward and traversing terrain. dm_control [35] and OpenAI [2] Gym focus exclusively
129
      on locomotion tasks. ASE [25] includes results for five tasks based on mocap data, which involve
      mainly locomotion and sword-swinging actions. These tasks lack the complexity required to fully
130
      exploit the capabilities of simulated humanoids. Sports scenarios require agile motion and strategic
131
      teamwork. They are also easily interpretable and provide measurable outcomes for success. A
132   concurrent work, HumanoidBench [30] employs the Unitree H1 humanoid in simulation to address
133   27 locomotion and manipulation tasks. Unlike HumanoidBench, ours targets competitive sports and
134   uses available human demonstration data to enhance the learning of human-like behaviors. This
135   emphasis is essential, as without human demonstrations, the behaviors developed in benchmarks can
136   often appear erratic and lack human realism.
137
138
      Humanoid Motion Representation. Due to the high degree-of-freedom (DoF) in humanoids and
      the inherent sample inefficiency of RL training, there have been many efforts focusing on developing
139
      motion primitives [7, 20, 6, 27] and motion latent spaces [4, 25, 33]. These techniques aim to
140
      accelerate training and provide human-like motion priors. Notably, approaches such as ASE [25],
141   CASE [4], PMP [1], CompositeMotion [46], and CALM [33] utilize adversarial learning objectives to
142   encourage mapping between random noise and realistic motor behavior. Furthermore, methods such
143   as ControlVAE [47], NPMP [20], PhysicsVAE [44], NCP [52], MaskedMimic [34], and PULSE [16]
144   leverage the motion imitation task to acquire and reuse motor skills for the learning of downstream
145   tasks. In this work, we study AMP [24] and PULSE [16] as exemplary methods to provide motion
146   priors. Our findings demonstrate that a robust motion prior, combined with straightforward reward
147   designs, can effectively induce human-like behaviors in solving complex sports tasks.

190   In this section, we introduce our diverse suite of sports environments. An overview can be found
191   in Fig. 3. Each environment is designed with a scoring system and success metrics that closely
192
      mirror real-world sports performance evaluation. These metrics enable proper comparison between
      methods. While important for comparisons, these metrics are very sparse. For example, a high jump
193
      is evaluated at the end of the run-then-jump sequence. This form of sparse feedback is detrimental
194
      to RL algorithms, leading to the well-known credit assignment problem [21]. To overcome this, we
195   design dense reward functions to guide the learning process.
196
197   Our environments support both single-person (Sec. 4.1) and multi-person (Sec. B.2) sports. For
198
      each environment, we provide the observation space and the key performance metrics used for
      evaluation. While reward engineering is crucial for effective learning, we will focus on two exemplary
199
      environments in depth here: one for single-person sports (golf), and one for multi-person sports
200
      (soccer). Full details for all environments in Appendix B.1.
201
202   We leverage human demonstration in two ways: (1) we show a motion representation/prior trained
203   on large-scale MoCap data can help learn many sports tasks results in human-like behavior; (2) we
204
      propose a pipeline (Sec. 4.3) to obtain a small amount of task-specific demonstration from video for
      each sport (when there is no available public dataset), and show that it can further help shaping the
205
      humanoid behavior when used together with a general-purpose motion prior.

      4.3   ACQUIRING H UMAN D EMONSTRATION
344
345   When existing MoCap dataset does not include motion for a particular sport, we propose a pipeline
346   to acquire a small amount of human demonstration from videos. We utilize TRAM [42] for 3D
347   motion reconstruction from Internet videos, providing robust global trajectory and pose estimation
348   under dynamic camera movements, commonly found in sports broadcasting. Specifically, TRAM
349   estimates SMPL parameters [13] which include global root translation, orientation, body poses, and
350   shape parameters. We further apply PHC [14], a physics-based motion tracker, to imitate these
351   estimated motions, ensuring physical plausibility. We find these corrected motions are significantly
352   more effective as positive samples for adversarial learning compared to raw estimated results. More
353
      details and ablation are provided in the supplementary materials.
354   Motion Retargeting. To retarget motion from the SMPL format (both from MoCap or videos) to a
355   humanoid robot (H1 and G1), we follow H2O [9] and perform a two-stage fitting process. First, we
356   find a body shape β ′ closest to the humanoid structure. We choose joints that have a correspondence
357   between SMPL and humanoids and perform gradient descents on the shape parameter β to minimize
358   the joint distances using a common rest pose. After finding the optimal β ′ , given a sequence of
359   motions expressed in SMPL parameters, we retarget motion from human to humanoid by minimizing
360   the joint position differences using Adam optimizer [10].

409
      Track & Field Sports (Without Video Data). We evaluate track and field sports, including long
410   jump, high jump, hurdling, and javelin throwing, using a subset of the AMASS dataset for reference
411   motions. For these sports, SOTA pose estimation methods fail to estimate coherent motion and global
412   root trajectory from videos as players and cameras are both fast-moving. Thus, we utilize a subset of
413   the AMASS dataset containing locomotion data [28] as reference motions for AMP. Since PULSE is
414   pretrained on AMASS, we exclude PULSE with AMP from these tests.
415
      As shown in Table 1, we observe consistent patterns in the performance across sports. AMP struggles
416   to balance discriminator rewards with task completion, failing to execute the required tasks effectively.
417   This is particularly evident in long jump and hurdling, where the agent either moves slowly or stops
418   before completing the task. This failure occurs because the policy prioritizes discriminator rewards
419   over task completion. If the task is too hard, the policy will use simple motion (such as standing still)
420   to maximize the discriminator reward instead task reward.
421
      PPO demonstrates the ability to achieve impressive physical feats, such as long distances in jumping
422   or high clearances in hurdling. However, these actions are performed with unnatural, non-human-like
423   motions. This highlights PPO’s focus on task completion without regard for motion quality.
424
425
      PULSE emerges as a balanced approach, consistently executing human-like motions across all events.
      Notably, in high jump, PULSE adopts a Fosbury flop technique without specific encouragement,
426
      likely leveraging skills it has encountered from the AMASS dataset, such as break-dancing. While
427
      PULSE successfully completes tasks with natural movements, it lacks the specialized skills required
428   for top-tier performances in specific sports.

503   6    L IMITATIONS , C ONCLUSION , AND F UTURE W ORK
504
505   Limitations. While HumanoidOlympics provides a large collection of simulated sports environ-
506   ments, it is far from being comprehensive. Certain sports are omitted due to simulation constraints
507   (e.g., swimming, shooting, ice hockey, cycling) or their inherent complexity (e.g., 11-a-side soccer,
508   equestrian events). Nevertheless, our framework is highly adaptable, allowing easy incorporation
509   of additional sports like climbing, rugby, wrestling etc. Our initial design of rewards, though able
510   to achieve sensible results, is also far from optimal. For competitive sports such as 2v2 soccer and
511   basketball, our results also fall short of SOTA [12] which employs much more complex systems. In
512
      terms of supporting real-world humanoid embodiments, while we simulated our humanoid robots
      with realistic simulation parameters that have the potential to transfer to the real-world, we do not
513
      conduct any sim-to-real modifications (e.g. domain randomization) in designing our pipelines.
514
515   Conclusion and Future Work. We introduce HumanoidOlympics, a collection of sports environ-
516   ments for simulated humanoids. We provide carefully designed state and reward, and benchmark
517   humanoid control algorithms and motion priors. We find that by combining expert reward design
518   and powerful human motion prior, one can achieve human-like behavior for solving various chal-
519   lenging sports. Our humanoid’s compatibility with the SMPL motion (either by design or through
520   retargeting) also provides an easy way to obtain additional data from video for training, which we
521
      demonstrate to be helpful in training some sports. These well-defined simulation environments could
      also serve as valuable platforms for frontier models [17] to gain physical understanding. We believe
522
      that HumanoidOlympics provides a valuable starting point for the community to further explore
523
      physically simulated humanoids.

942   For PULSE [16] and AMP [24], we use the official implementations. For PULSE [16], we employ
943   the publicly released model without modification, which is pre-trained on the AMASS dataset.
944   We follow a similar setup for downstream tasks in PULSE, using the frozen prior P PULSE , decoder
945   D PULSE , and residual action representation. Since PULSE only includes trained models for the SMPL-
946   based models, we train SMPL-X humanoid-based models following the official implementation.
947
      Specifically, we train a humanoid motion imitator following PHC [14], and distill motor skills into
      a 48-dimensional latent space [15] (instead of 32-D, to accommodate articulated fingers). PULSE
948
      provides an action space for hierarchical RL and can be integrated with AMP. For PULSE+AMP, the
949
      AMP reward offers additional style guidance for the humanoid, which is particularly beneficial for
950   tasks such as table tennis. However, we find that the demonstration sequences used for AMP need
951   to be task-specific (e.g. contains only a swinging motion); otherwise, the discriminator reward can
952   overpower the task reward and lead to undesired behavior (as seen in the free kick results).
