009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Effective task representations should facilitate compositionality, such that after
012            learning a variety of basic tasks, an agent can perform compound tasks consisting
013            of multiple steps simply by composing the representations of the constituent steps
014            together. While this is conceptually simple and appealing, it is not clear how to
015            automatically learn representations that enable this sort of compositionality. We
016            show that learning to associate the representations of current and future states
017            with a temporal alignment loss can improve compositional generalization, even
018            in the absence of any explicit subtask planning or reinforcement learning. This
019
               approach is able to generalize to novel composite tasks specified as goal images
               or language instructions, without assuming any additional reward supervision or
020
               explicit subtask planning. We evaluate our approach across diverse tabletop robotic
021
               manipulation tasks, showing substantial improvements for tasks specified with
022            either language or goal images.

026   1    I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Compositionality is a core aspect of intelligent behavior, describing the ability to sequence previously
029   learned capabilities and solve new tasks (Lashley, 1951). In domains involving long-horizon decision-
030   making like robotics, various learning approaches have been proposed to enable this property,
031   including hierarchical learning (Kulkarni et al., 2016), explicit subtask planning (Schrittwieser et al.,
032   2021; Fang et al., 2022b; Ahn et al., 2022), and dynamic-programming-based “stitching” (Ghugare
033   et al., 2023; Kostrikov et al., 2022). In practice, these techniques are often unstable and/or data-
034   inefficient in real-world robotics settings, making them difficult to scale (Laidlaw et al., 2024).
035   By contrast, biological learners are adept at quickly composing behaviors to reach new goals (Lashley,
036   1951). Possible explanations for these capabilities have been proposed, including the ability to perform
037   transitive inference (Ciranka et al., 2022), learn successor representations and causal models (Dayan,
038   1993b; Gopnik et al., 2017), and plan with world models (Vikbladh et al., 2019). In common among
039   these theories is the idea of learning structured representations of the world, which inference about
040   which actions will lead to certain goals.
041   How might these concepts translate to algorithms for robot learning? In this work, we study how
042   adding an auxiliary successor representation learning objective affects compositional behavior in a
043   real-world tabletop manipulation setting. We show that learning this representation structure improves
044   the ability of the robot to perform long-horizon, compositionally-new tasks, specified either through
045   goal images or natural language instructions. Perhaps surprisingly, we found that this temporal
046   alignment does not need to be used for training the policy or test-time inference, as long as it is used
047   as an auxiliary loss over the same representations used for the tasks. An example of this can be seen
048   in Fig. 1.
049   We evaluate our method, Temporal Representation Alignment (TRA), on a set of challenging multi-
050   step manipulation tasks in the BridgeData setup (Walke et al., 2023). These tasks specifically test the
051   compositional capabilities of the robot policies: as a whole, the tasks are out-of-distribution, but each
052   distinct subtask can be described through a goal image that lies in the training distribution. Adding a
053   simple time-contrastive alignment loss improves compositional performance on these tasks by >40%
      across 13 tasks in 4 scenes.

076   2    R ELATED W ORK
077
      Our approach builds upon prior work on goal- and language-conditioned control, focusing particularly
078
      on the problem of compositional generalization.
079
080   Robot manipulation with language and goals. Recent improvements in robot learning datasets
081   have enabled the development of robot policies that can be commanded with image goals and language
082   instructions (Ahn et al., 2022; Walke et al., 2023; Shridhar et al., 2021). These policies can be trained
083   with goal- and language-conditioned imitation learning from human demonstrations (Chowdhery
084   et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2023; Lynch and Sermanet, 2021; Lynch et al., 2023; Brohan et al., 2023),
085   reinforcement learning (Chebotar et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2021), or other forms of supervision (Bobu
086   et al., 2023; Cui et al., 2023). When being trained to reach goals, methods can additionally use
087   hindsight relabeling (Andrychowicz et al., 2017; Kaelbling, 1993) to improve performance (Walke
088
      et al., 2023; Myers et al., 2023; Dehaene et al., 2022; Ding et al., 2019). Our work shows how the
      benefits of goal-conditioned and language-conditioned supervised learning can be combined with
089
      temporal representation alignment to enable compositionality that would otherwise require planning
090
      or reinforcement learning.
091
092   Compositional generalization in sequential decision making. In the context of decision making,
093   compositional generalization refers to the ability to generalize to new behaviors that are composed of
094   known sub-behaviors (Rubino et al., 2023; Steedman, 2004). Biological learning systems show strong
095   compositional generalization abilities (Ciranka et al., 2022; Dehaene et al., 2022; Dickins, 2011;
096   Lake et al., 2019), and recent work has explored how similar capabilities can be achieved in artificial
097   systems (Akyürek et al., 2021; Ito et al., 2022; Lewis et al., 2024). In the context of policy learning,
098   exploiting the compositionality of the behaviors can lead to generalization to unseen and temporarily
099
      extended tasks (Ghugare et al., 2023; Kumar et al., 2022; Fang et al., 2019; 2022b; Mandlekar
      et al., 2021; Nasiriany et al., 2019). Hierarchical and planning-based approaches also aim to enable
100
      compositional behavior by explicitly partitioning a task into its components (Fang et al., 2022a;
101
      Myers et al., 2024a; Zhang et al., 2022; Park et al., 2023). With improvements in vision-language
102   models (VLMs), many recent works have explored using a pre-trained VLM to decompose a task into
103   subtasks that are more attainable for the low-level manipulation policy (Ahn et al., 2022; Attarian
104   et al., 2022; Belkhale et al., 2024; Kwon et al., 2023; Myers et al., 2024a; Singh et al., 2023; Zhang
105   et al., 2023). These approaches are limited by the need for robust pre-trained models that can be
106   fine-tuned and prompted for embodied tasks. Our contribution is to show compositional properties
107   can be achieved without any explicit hierarchical structure or planning, by learning a structured
      representation through time-contrastive representation alignment.

108
      Representation learning for states and tasks. State and task representations for decision making
109   aim to improve generalization and exploit additional sources of data. Recent work in the robotics
110   domain have explored the use of pre-trained representations across multimodal data, including images
111   and language, for downstream tasks (Karamcheti et al., 2023; Li et al., 2022; Ma et al., 2023a; Myers
112   et al., 2023; Nair et al., 2022; Pari et al., 2022; Shah and Kumar, 2021; Cui et al., 2022; Jang et al.,
113   2021). In reinforcement learning problems, representations are often trained to predict future states,
114   rewards, goals, or actions (Anand et al., 2019; Ma et al., 2023b; Zhang et al., 2021; Fan et al., 2022),
115   and can improve generalization and sample efficiency when used as value functions (Barreto et al.,
116   2017; Blier et al., 2021; Dayan, 1993a; Dosovitskiy and Koltun, 2017; Choi et al., 2021). Some
117
      recent works have explored the use of additional structural constraints on representations to enable
      planning (Fang et al., 2022a; Zhang et al., 2022; Eysenbach et al., 2024; Hafner et al., 2019), or
118
      enforced metric properties to improve compositional generalization (Liu et al., 2023; Myers et al.,
119
      2024b; Wang et al., 2023).
120
121   The key distinction between our approach and past contrastive representation methods for robotics
122   like VIP (Ma et al., 2023b), GRIF (Myers et al., 2023), and R3M (Nair et al., 2022) is that we focus on
123
      the real-world compositional generalization capabilities enabled by simply aligning representations
      across time in addition to the task modalities, without using the learned representations for policy
124
      extraction or defining a value function.

132   space by aligning the representations of states, goals, and language in a way that is more amenable to
133
      compositional generalization.

315   4.1    E XPERIMENTAL D ETAILS
316
      We evaluate TRA on a collection of held-out compositionally-OOD tasks – tasks for which the
317
      individual substeps are represented in the dataset, but the combination of those steps is unseen.
318   For example, in a task such as “removing a bell pepper from a towel, and then sweep the towel”,
319   both the tasks “remove the bell pepper from the towel” and “sweep the towel” have similar entries
320   within BridgeData, but such combined trajectory and language description does not exist. We utilize
321   a real-world robot manipulation interface with a 7 DoF WidowX250 manipulator arm with 5Hz
322   execution frequency. We train on an augmented version of the BridgeDataV2 dataset (Walke et al.,
323   2023), which contains over 50k trajectories with 72k language annotations. We augment the dataset
      by rephrasing the language annotations, as described by (Myers et al., 2023), with 5 additional


                                                          6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      rephrased language instruction for each language instruction present in the dataset, and randomly
325   sample them during training.
326
      In order to specifically test the ability of TRA to perform compositional generalization, we organize
327
      our evaluation tasks into 4 scenes that are unseen in BridgeData, each with increasing difficulty:
328
329   Scene A – One-Step Drawer: this is the only scene that are not compositionally-OOD, as all the
330
      tasks are one-step tasks. This scene involves opening, putting an item in, and closing a drawer. These
      tasks have been seen in BridgeData, although at a lower frequency than object manipulation, but
331
      the position in which they are initialized are unseen. They will be used to compare TRA’s ability to
332
      baselines when solving single-step tasks.
333
334
      Scene B – Task Concatenation: this scene involves concatenating multiple tasks of the same nature
      in sequence, where a robot must be able to perform all tasks within the same trajectory. During
335
      evaluation, we instruct the policy with instructions such as sweeping multiple objects in the scene
336
      that require composition (though are not sensitive to the order of the composition).
337
338
      Scene C – Semantic Generalization: Unlike scene B, these tasks require manipulation with different
      objects of the same class. We test this using various food items seen within BridgeData and instruct
339
      the policy to put various food items within a container. An example of such task would be to have a
340
      table containing a banana, a sushi, a bowl, and various distractor objects, and instead of using specific
341   language commands such as “put the banana and the sushi in the bowl,” a more general statement
342   such as “put the food items in a container” will be used.
343
      Scene D – Tasks with Dependency: This is the most challenging of the set of tasks: these tasks have
344
      subtasks that require previous subtasks being completed for them to succeed. An example of this
345   would be to open a drawer, and to take out an item in the drawer, as one cannot take out an item from
346   the drawer if the drawer is not open.
347
      The complete list of tasks is noted in Appendix C.

      4.2   BASELINES
350
351   We compare against the following baselines:
352
      GRIF (Myers et al., 2023) learns a goal- and language- conditioned policy using aligned goal image
353
        and language representations. In our experiments, this becomes equivalent to TRA when the
354     temporal alignment objective is removed.
355
      GCBC (Walke et al., 2023) learns a goal-conditioned behavioral cloning policy that concatenates
356
        the goal image with the image observation.
357
      LCBC (Walke et al., 2023) learns a language-conditioned policy that concatenates the language
358
        with the image observation.
359
360
      OCTO (Ghosh et al., 2024) uses a multimodal transformer to learn a goal- and language-
        conditioned policy. The policy is trained on Open-X dataset (O’Neill et al., 2024), which
361
        incorporates BridgeData in its entirety.
362
363
      AWR (Peng et al., 2019) uses advantages produced by a value function to effectively extract a policy
        from an offline dataset. In this experiment, we use the difference between the contrastive loss
364
        between the current observation and the goal representation and the contrastive loss between the
365
        next observation and the goal representation as a surrogate for value function.
366
367   We train GRIF, GCBC, LCBC, and AWR using the same augmented Bridge Dataset as TRA, and we
368   use an Octo-Base 1.5 model for our evaluation. A more detail approach is detailed in Appendix B.
369   During evaluation, we give all policies the same goal state and language instruction regardless of
370   the architecture, as they are trained on the same language instruction with the exception of Octo,
371
      which doesn’t benefit from paraphrased language data, but does benefit from a more diverse language
      annotation set across a larger dataset of varying length and complexity.

      4.4   FAILURE C ASES
503
504   While TRA provides an effective mechanism for compositional generalization, it is not immune
505   to failures. Qualitatively, we observe that despite showing better compositional generalization, the
506   policy still fails at a similar rate compared to other multivariate Gaussian policies when multimodal
507   behavior is observed, other cases of early grasping and incorrect reaching are also observed at a
508   similar rate. While TRA did provide marginal improvements as seen in Scene A, it does not provide
509   full coverage of such scenarios. More analysis of failure cases can be seen in Appendix D.1.
510
511   5     C ONCLUSIONS AND L IMITATIONS
512
513   In this paper, we studied the effects of adding a temporal representation alignment objective in
514   behavior cloning, and we have discovered that by adding this metric, it allows a robot policy to
515   perform robust compositional generalization even when the composition of such tasks are OOD.
516
      Limitations and Future Work Due to restrictions placed by dataloaders, TRA cannot handle
517
      extremely long sequence of language, even though the difficulty of subtasks contained within the
518
      instructions may remain the same. Future work could also examine long-horizon tasks in bimanual or
519   multi-agent settings, or investigate other properties like cross-embodiment generalization. To scale
520   to these more complex settings, similar approaches with more complex architectures architectures
521   such as transformers and diffusion policies may be needed for policy and/or representation learning.
522   Future work could also examine combining TRA with hierarchical task decomposition using VLMs,
523   or with other forms of structured task representations.

      A     TRA I MPLEMENTATION
739
740   In this section, we provide some details on the implementatinon of temporal representation alignment
741   (TRA) and its training process.
742
743   A.1   DATASET C URATION
744
745
      We use an augmented version of BridgeData. We augment the dataset by generating 5 additional
      paraphrased instruction per language instruction. During training process, we randomly sample the
746
      instructions for each trajectory to ensure an equal coverage of texts.
747
748
      During data loading process, for each observation that is being sampled with timestep k, we also
749   sample k + ≜ min(k + x, H), x ∼ Geom(1 − γ), and load sk along with sk+ . We employ random
750
      cropping, resizing, and hue changes during training process image robustness.
751
      A.2   P OLICY T RAINING
752
753   We use a ResNet-34 architecture for the policy network. We train our policy with one Google V4-8
754   TPU VM instance for 150,000 steps, which takes a total of 20 hours. We use a learning rate of
755   3 × 10−4 , 2000 linear warmup steps, and a MLP head of 3 layers of 256 dimensions after encoding
      the observation representations as well as goal representations.

      B     BASELINE I MPLEMENTATIONS
757
758
      We summarize the implementation details of the baselines discussed in Section 4.2.
759
760
      B.1   O CTO
761
762   We use the Octo-base 1.5 model publicly available on HuggingFace for evaluating Octo baselines. We
763   use inference code that is readily available for both image- and language- conditioned tasks. During
764   inference, we use an action chunking window of 4 and an execution horizon window of 4.
765
766   B.2   B EHAVIOR C LONING
767
768   We use the same architecture for LCBC as in Walke et al. (2023); Myers et al. (2023). During the
769   training process we use the same hyperparameters as TRA.
770
771   B.3   A DVANTAGE W EIGHTED R EGRESSION
772
773
      In order to train an AWR agent without separately implementing a reward critic, we follow Eysenbach
      et al. (2022) and use a surrogate for advantage:
774
775                           A(st ) = LNCE f (st ), f (g) − LNCE f (st+1 ), f (g) .                  (21)
776
777   Here, f can be any of the encoders ϕ, ξ, ψ. L is the same InfoNCE loss defined Section 3, and g is
778
      defined as either the goal observation or the goal language instruction, depending on the modality.
779   And we extract the policy using advantage weighted regression (AWR) (Neumann and Peters, 2008):
780                                              h                           i
781                         π ← arg max Es,a∼D log π(a|s, z) exp A(s, a)/β .                     (22)
                                         π
782
783   During training, we set β to 1, and we use a batch size of 128, the same value as policy training for
784   our method.

791   BridgeData, which include conventionally less challenging tasks such as object manipulation, and
792   challenging tasks to learn within the dataset such as cloth folding and drawer opening.

      During inference, we use a maximum of 200 timesteps to account for long-horizon behaviors, which
