009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            The knowledge within large language models (LLMs) may become outdated
012            quickly. While in-context editing (ICE) is currently the most effective method for
013            knowledge editing (KE), it is constrained by the black-box modeling of LLMs
014            and thus lacks interpretability. Our work aims to elucidate the superior perfor-
015            mance of ICE in KE by analyzing the impacts of in-context new knowledge on
016            token-wise distributions. We observe that despite a significant boost in logits of the
017            new knowledge, the performance of ICE is still hindered by stubborn knowledge.
018            Stubborn knowledge refers to facts that have gained excessive confidence during
019
               pretraining, making them hard to edit effectively. To address this issue and further
               enhance the performance of ICE, we propose a novel approach termed Decoding by
020
               Contrasting Knowledge (DeCK). DeCK derives the distribution of the next token
021
               by contrasting the logits obtained from the newly edited knowledge guided by ICE
022            with those from the unedited parametric knowledge. Our experiments consistently
023            demonstrate that DeCK enhances the confidence of LLMs in edited facts. For
024            instance, it improves the performance of LL A MA3-8B- INSTRUCT on MQ UAKE
025            by up to 219%, demonstrating its capability to strengthen ICE in the editing of
026            stubborn knowledge. DeCK can be easily integrated into any ICE method as a
027            decoding component to enhance editing capabilities. Our work paves the way to
028            develop both effective and accountable KE methods for LLMs.

031   1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033   With the widespread deployment of large language models (LLMs) (OpenAI, 2022; 2023; Touvron
034   et al., 2023a;b; Song et al., 2024), there is a rising demand for accessing accurate information through
035   LLMs. However, despite the extensive knowledge stored in LLMs, this information can become
036   outdated due to changes in the real world. This can potentially result in factual inaccuracies (Chen &
037   Shu, 2023) or false information (Zhang et al., 2023b; Huang et al., 2023a).
038   Unlike the high computational resource burden incurred by retraining from scratch, knowledge editing
039   (KE) (Sinitsin et al., 2020; De Cao et al., 2021; Zhu et al., 2020; Mitchell et al., 2022; Yao et al.,
040   2023) has been proposed as an efficient means to update the knowledge of LLMs. They aim to edit
041   knowledge by incrementally injecting or modifying facts.
042   As LLMs demonstrate increasingly powerful in-context learning capabilities, recent research (Madaan
043   et al., 2022; Zhong et al., 2023; Zheng et al., 2023; Cohen et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024; Bi et al.,
044   2024b;c) has delved into easier and efficient methods for in-context editing (ICE), aiming to directly
045   guide frozen LLMs in generating text with new knowledge through contextual prompts. Figure 1 (left)
046   illustrates an example of successful editing using ICE. These ICE methods showcasing state-of-the-art
047   performance without the need to alter internal model parameters, indicate the promising potential of
048   modeling LLMs as black boxes for ICE guided by external contexts.
049   However, as illustrated in Figure 1 (middle), there still exist deeply entrenched pieces of knowledge
050   in LLMs that are difficult for ICE to modify, which we refer to as stubborn knowledge. We argue
051   that LLMs, through extensive pre-training, have developed strong confidence in certain facts, making
052   them difficult to alter solely through external contextual prompts (Bi et al., 2024a). Therefore,
053   despite the fact that the sophisticated methods such as enhancing retrieval (Shi et al., 2024), checking
      conflict (Zhong et al., 2023), and guiding reasoning (Wang et al., 2024) can enhance the overall

069   In this work, we focus on enhancing the state-of-the-art KE method, ICE, to reduce the negative
070   impacts from the stubborn knowledge in LLMs. First, we observe the impact of the in-context new
071   knowledge in ICE on LLMs from the perspective of LLMs’ token-level distributions. We find that
072   incorporating this new knowledge significantly increases the predicted probability of generating
073   edited facts during the decoding process. A deeper exploration of the failed cases reveals the reasons
074   why stubborn knowledge is difficult to edit. Despite the significant improvement in the logits of
075
      new knowledge achieved by ICE, there persists a small gap between new knowledge and parametric
      knowledge, where parametric knowledge refers to the original unedited knowledge in LLMs.
076
077   Building upon the insights gained from above observations, we introduce a new decoding technique
078   called Decoding by Contrasting Knowledge (DeCK) to enhance LLMs’ confidence in edited facts for
079   better editing of stubborn knowledge. DeCK consists of two components: (1) an editing enhancement
080   module that improves attention to new knowledge, thus preventing it from being filtered out during
081
      contrastive decoding, and (2) a contrastive decoding strategy that compares the logical distributions
      after in-context editing with the original parametric logical distributions to predict the next token.
082
083   Overall, our contributions can be summarized by three points. First, as far as we know, we are the
084   first to elucidate superior performance of ICE on the KE from a model interpretability perspective.
085   Second, we find that stubborn knowledge significantly impacts the performance of ICE, and we
086   propose DeCK to boost confidence in editing facts, enhancing ICE to overcome it. Third, extensive
087
      experiments on MQ UAKE indicate that our DeCK can effectively enhance the performance of ICE
      without altering the internal model or modifying external prompts. DeCK can be easily integrated
088
      into any ICE method as a decoding component to enhance editing capabilities. Our work paves the
089
      way to develop the both effective and accountable KE methods for LLMs.

      3.1   H OW ICE C AN E FFECTIVELY E DIT K NOWLEDGE IN LLM S ?
149
150   Although the ICE methods on LLMs (Zhong et al., 2023; Cohen et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024) have
151   demonstrated promising performance, they all rely on the black-box modeling of LLMs for editing,
152   and the internal mechanisms behind their effectiveness remain unclear. In this subsection, we delve
153   into the intrinsic reasons behind the superior performance of ICE on KE.
154
      We designed dedicated experiments to capture the logits output of knowledge that would be influenced
155
      by the edit. A striking observation in Figure 2 is that the introduction of new knowledge through
156   ICE leads to a significant rightward shift in the probability distribution of the new knowledge, while
157   the logits for parametric knowledge remain largely unchanged or decrease to some extent. This
158   suggests that ICE significantly enhances the logits of new knowledge while having minimal impact
159   on parametric knowledge. Additionally, the number of top-ranked positions for new knowledge
160   significantly increases after ICE, with the majority surpassing that of parametric knowledge. This
161   indicates that the in-context new knowledge can improve the confidence of LLMs in editing facts,
      thereby prompting responses with the edited answers.

      3.2        I NHERENT C HALLENGES OF S TUBBORN K NOWLEDGE
177
178   While ICE has significantly boosted the confidence of LLMs in new knowledge, we find that there
179   are still instances where certain new knowledge ranks prominently but not as the top-1, as illustrated
180   in Figure 2. We term this phenomenon "stubborn knowledge", which refers to cases where editing
181   fails due to either an excessive confidence in existing parametric knowledge or insufficient confidence
182   in new knowledge. The edit cases in Figure 3 deeply reveals the failed pattern for ICE in addressing
183   stubborn knowledge, which happens when there is still an extremely small gap compared to the
184   parametric knowledge after editing, despite the significant increase in new knowledge logits induced
185   by the editing prompt. Taking the last case as an example, after editing, the new knowledge "English"
186
      lags behind the parametric knowledge "French" by only 0.516 in terms of logical distribution,
      illustrating how a minor gap leads to editing failure. This indicates the intrinsic reasons for the failure
187
      of black-box ICE methods to edit stubborn knowledge in LLMs in most cases.

      and chain-of-thought (COT) (Wei et al., 2022; Li et al., 2024) prompting to enhance its in-context
351
      editing performance. Our decoding strategy DeCK is inherently deployed onto IKE and MeLLo to
352   validate their enhancements without any additional adjustments. It simply requires providing the
353   relevant factual guiding context before generating the edited answers. The model editing methods
354   ROME in our baselines are deployed using EasyEdit (Wang et al., 2023). We set adaptive plausibility
355   constraint λ to 0.01 and contrasting coefficient γ to 0.2 for our DeCK.

      6     R ELATED W ORK
453
454   Hallucinations and Misinformation Hallucination (Kang et al., 2024) is one of the main source of
455   LLM-generated misinformation. In general, there are two lines of works on hallucination mitigation.
456   In training stage, Hu et al. (2023); Pan et al. (2024) has investigated training data curation or
457   knowledge grounding methods to integrate more knowledge. In the inference stage, recent works have
458   explored methods including confidence estimation (Huang et al., 2023b), knowledge retrieval (Feng
459   et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2024) and KE to improve accurate outputs.
460   Contrast Decoding The recent contrasting decoding methods achieve the desired output by con-
461   trasting logical distribution during the decoding phase. CD (Li et al., 2023) compares powerful
462   expert language models with weaker amateur language models to enhance fluency and coherence.
463   DoLa (Chuang et al., 2023) contrasts mature layers with premature layers, while ICD (Zhang et al.,
464   2023a) compares with models injected with hallucinations, aiming to enhance the factual accuracy of
465   the model.
466   Model Editing and In-Context Editing Model Editing is a type of effective technique for KE,
467   altering the model’s internal structure to modify its output regarding the edited content. Current
468   model editing methods (Meng et al., 2022a;b; Mitchell et al., 2022; Yao et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2024)
469   for LLMs involve integrating an auxiliary network with the original model or modifying and adding
470   model parameters to manipulate the model’s output. The emergent method of ICE (Madaan et al.,
471   2022; Zhong et al., 2023; Zheng et al., 2023), demonstrates significant potential, enabling the editing
472   of language models by prompting them with edited fact and retrieving editing demonstrations from
473   the edit memory.
474
475   7     C ONCLUSION AND L IMTATIONS
476
477   In this paper, we introduce Decoding by Contrasting Knowledge (DeCK), a novel decoding strategy
478   aimed at enhancing in-context editing in overcoming stubborn knowledge for LLMs. Based on
479   observations at the token-level of edited knowledge, DeCK contrasts the logits of new knowledge
480   with those from parametric knowledge to amplify the changes in model knowledge brought about by
481   in-context editing. Experimental results show that DeCK significantly improves editing accuracy.
482
      Overall, DeCK is a critical step in enhancing in-context editing to overcome stubborn knowledge.
483   DeCK also has limitations; it requires the reception of input from two different token sequences
484   during the generation process, resulting in approximately a 1.6X increase in latency compared to
485   original decoding. This suggests that we can pursue further optimization within the transformers
      architecture or explore alternative, more cost-effective versions of DeCK.
