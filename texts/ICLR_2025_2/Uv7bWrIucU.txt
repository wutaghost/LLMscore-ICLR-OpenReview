009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               Machine unlearning aims to remove the effect of specific data from trained models
012            to ensure individuals’ privacy. However, it’s arguable how to evaluate whether the
013            privacy protection goal is achieved by machine unlearning. Furthermore, recent
014            studies show unlearning may also increase the retained samples’ privacy risks.
015            This paper takes a holistic approach to auditing both unlearned and retained sam-
016            ples’ privacy risks before and after unlearning. We derive the privacy criteria for
017            unlearned and retained samples, respectively, based on the perspectives of differ-
018            ential privacy and membership inference attacks. To make the auditing practical,
019            we also develop an efficient membership inference attack, A-LiRA, utilizing data
020
               augmentation to reduce the cost of shadow model training. Our experimental
               findings indicate that existing machine unlearning algorithms do not consistently
021
               protect the privacy of unlearned samples and may inadvertently compromise the
022
               privacy of retained samples. For reproducibility, we have pubished our code.1

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   The “right to be forgotten” allows data contributors to request the deletion of their data from an or-
029   ganization’s records. Recent regulations, including the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR)
030   in the European Union, the California Consumer Privacy Act (CCPA) in the U.S., and Canada’s Per-
031
      sonal Information Protection and Electronic Documents Act (PIPEDA), have solidified this right.
      For example, Google received over 3.2 million requests to remove specific URLs from search re-
032
      sults over five years, demonstrating the importance and scale of this issue.
033
034   In the context of machine learning, exercising the right to be forgotten entails not only removing
035   the data itself but also ensuring models are retrained with the updated dataset. However, with the
036   rise of large models, frequent model retraining brings unbearable costs to model owners. Thus, the
037
      process of efficiently adjusting models to avoid high retraining costs, known as machine unlearning,
      has garnered significant attention in both academic and industrial research. Most recent machine
038
      unlearning methods (Golatkar et al. (2020); Foster et al. (2024); Fan et al. (2024)) claim that machine
039
      unlearning can be a promising way to protect privacy, yet the effectiveness of protection has not
040   been sufficiently evaluated. A popular argument is that unlearning is effective in privacy protection
041   as long as any impact the erased data had on the original model is removed from the unlearned
042   model, which is known as the completeness measure. However, Chen et al. (Chen et al. (2021))
043   highlight that current machine unlearning techniques may still leave residual information about the
044   target data. More importantly, is the completeness measure appropriate for privacy protection?
045
      Moreover, Carlini et al. (Carlini et al. (2022b)) note that a sample’s privacy is a relative notion,
046   meaning that removing some samples could inadvertently increase the privacy risks of other samples
047   in the retained data. It’s consistent with the understanding of anonymization by blending in the
048   crowd — a thinning crowd leaves less protection to the crowd members. This issue is particularly
049   concerning when the model builder has promised to preserve the entire dataset’s privacy with a
050   certain guarantee level, i.e., the   setting, in differentially private machine learning (Dwork (2006);
051   Abadi et al. (2016)).

      Scope of Research. We take a holistic approach to measure the impact of a machine unlearning
055   algorithm on both the unlearned and retained samples. Specifically, we consider successful machine
056   unlearning in privacy protection should meet the following goals: (1) the privacy risk of unlearned
057   samples should decrease as much as possible after unlearning, and (2) the privacy risk of retained
058   samples should remain below the promised bound if a differentially private algorithm was applied
059   in modeling.
060
      Recent studies on auditing differentially private machine learning algorithms give some ideas for
061   us to analyze and define the above two goals formally. The auditing mechanism uses the theorem
062   (Dwork et al. (2014); Tramer et al. (2022); Jagielski et al. (2020)) that if a  -differentially-private
063   machine learning algorithm is correctly implemented, the worst-case membership inference (MIA)
064   test result on every sample must satisfy ln(TPR/FPR) <  , where TPR and FPR are the true positive
065   and false positive rates of the membership inference test, respectively. To make auditing effective,
066   the MIA test must be effective, e.g., ln(TPR/FPR) is as close to its theoretical upper bound as
067   possible. So far, the LiRA method (Carlini et al. (2022a)) is considered the best MIA method that
068   is able to measure individual sample’s ln(TPR/FPR) more accurately than other methods (Carlini
069
      et al. (2022b)). With a sample-level privacy risk measure, we can formally define the two privacy
      protection goals for machine unlearning methods.
070
071   A major challenge in applying the LiRA method is its high computational cost. Online-LiRA re-
072   quires 4.8 GPU hours per sample to generate the TPR/FPR measure. While offline-LiRA is more
073   efficient due to its one-sided hypothesis testing, its performance is inferior to that of online-LiRA.
074   In this paper, we introduce a more efficient augmentation-based likelihood ratio attack (A-LiRA),
075
      which achieves comparable performance to online-LiRA with a 88.3% reduction in time cost. A-
      LiRA is inspired by Mattern et al. (2023) and leverages the central limit theorem. Instead of training
076
      multiple shadow models as in online-LiRA, A-LiRA approximates the two probability distributions
077
      – when the model is trained with or without the target sample – using augmented data and with the
078   assumption of normal distributions for in- and out-training measures, separately. This significantly
079   reduces the time cost while providing a good approximation of the probability distributions, making
080   A-LiRA both effective and efficient for estimating the privacy risk of a sample.
081
      We have conducted extensive evaluations to demonstrate that A-LiRA provides comparable effec-
082
      tiveness while significantly improved efficiency compared to the online-LiRA approach introduced
083
      by Carlini et al. (2022b). We also show that most recent machine unlearning methods do not sat-
084   isfactorily protect the privacy of unlearned samples and, in some cases, increase the privacy risk of
085   retained samples. Thus, new unlearning methods are desired to meet the proposed privacy measures.
086   In summary, our contributions are as follows:
087
088          • We re-formulate the criteria for privacy protection of machine unlearning methods based
089            on the theories of differential privacy and membership inference attacks.
090          • We introduce a novel augmentation-based likelihood-ratio attack to estimate the privacy
091            risk of samples, which allows us to calculate the privacy protection criteria efficiently.
092
             • We show that most existing machine unlearning methods do not protect privacy well for the
093            unlearned samples, and in some cases, they may also increase privacy risks for the retained
094            samples.

100   2   R ELATED W ORKS
101
102   Machine unlearning enforces the “right to be forgotten” and is categorized into exact and approxi-
103   mate methods. Exact unlearning retrains models without the forgotten data. A notable example is
104   SISA by Bourtoule et al. (2021), which partitions training data into micro-shards and trains small
105   models on each. To unlearn data, only the relevant micro-models are retrained, improving effi-
106   ciency over full retraining. Approximate unlearning, on the other hand, employs techniques such
107   as weight manipulation and fine-tuning to make the model “forget” the data without retraining it
      entirely. For instance, Golatkar et al. (2020) modifies the weights to make probing functions of the


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      weights indistinguishable from those applied to a network trained without the data being forgotten.
109   Fan et al. (2024) introduces the concept of ’weight saliency’ for machine unlearning, drawing par-
110   allels with input saliency used in model explanation. This approach focuses unlearning efforts on
111   specific model weights, improving both efficiency and effectiveness. While machine unlearning is
112   touted as a means to protect privacy (Cao & Yang (2015); Nguyen et al. (2022); Xu et al. (2023)),
113   studies such as those by Chen et al. (2021) and Carlini et al. (2022b) indicate that unlearning may
114   make retained samples more susceptible to membership inference attacks. These findings highlight
115   the necessity for robust auditing methods and standards to ensure the privacy protection efficacy of
116   machine unlearning.

133
134   Differential Privacy (DP) ensures that the inclusion or exclusion of a single data point minimally
135   affects the output of an algorithm, protecting individual privacy. A machine learning algorithm M
136   satisfies ( , δ)-DP if, for any two neighboring datasets D and D0 that differ in at most one sample,
137
      and any output set S:
                                    Pr[M(D) ∈ S] ≤ e  · Pr[M(D0 ) ∈ S] + δ
138
      Here,   (privacy loss) controls the strength of the guarantee, with smaller   offering stronger privacy.
139
      δ allows for a small probability of privacy violation, especially for extreme outputs. Lower values
140
      of   and δ indicate higher privacy protection.

      3.3   L IKELIHOOD R ATIO ATTACK
143
144   Likelihood ratio attacks (LiRA) were first introduced by Carlini et al. (2022a) for machine learning
145   models. Given a target sample x, the online-LiRA sets up two hypotheses:
146                                  H0 : Target model is trained on D,
147                                  H1 : Target model is trained on D \ x
148
      It trains multiple shadow models (typically 512 in total) on D or D \ x fits the logit outputs to
149
      Gaussian distributions. The likelihood ratio between the two distributions is used to reject one of
150   the hypotheses. While effective, this method is computationally expensive, taking an average of
151   4.8 hours per sample in CIFAR-10 on a Titan V100 GPU, making it impractical. A more efficient
152   variant, offline-LiRA, only estimates the distribution D(x|M(D \ x)) of the target model is not
153   trained on the target model and uses the likelihood of x follows the estimated distribution to reject
154   or accept H1 . This one-sided approach can be batched by training shadow models on random subsets
155   of D, but it performs worse than the online-LiRA in efficacy.

      Target Model and Sample. The target model consists of an original model, denoted as M(D),
163   trained on dataset D, and an unlearned model, U = U(M(D), X|X ⊂ D), where U represents
164   the unlearning mechanism applied to a sample set X. According to the ideal definition of machine
165   unlearning (Xu et al. (2023); Nguyen et al. (2022)), the unlearning method should be equivalent to
166   the model retrained on the retained set D \ X, such that U = M R , where M R = M(D \ X). The
167   model builder may have an agreement with data contributors that regulates the privacy risk of the
168   data, which data owners won’t express at a certain level.
169   Attacker’s Capabilities. To assess the privacy risks associated with individual samples, we consider
170   a worst-case adversary. The attacker has white-box access to both the original model M(D) and the
171   unlearned model U(M(D), X|X ⊂ D), as well as full knowledge of the training dataset D and the
172   unlearned samples X. Additionally, the attacker knows the details of the unlearning mechanism U.
173
      Attacker’s Objective. The attacker’s goal is to determine whether a given sample from D was
174
      used to train the target models. Ideally, a successful attack would classify any sample in D as a
175   “member” in the original model M(D) and any retained sample (i.e., x ∈ D \ X) as a member
176   in the unlearned model U(M(D), X|X ⊂ D). Furthermore, for each unlearned sample x ∈ X,
177   the attacker should identify it as a “non-member” in U(M(D), X|X ⊂ D). In practice, such ideal
178   membership inference does not exist.
179
      Success of Defense. Thus, we define the success of the defense as the attacker’s MIA ability on the
180
      unlearned model for any sample x ∈ D, denoted as M IA(U, D, x), should be equivalent or worse
181   than that on the retrained model, M IA(M R , D, x). This criterion will be carefully studied, and an
182   approximate version will be formally defined in later sections.

      4.2   AUDITING M ACHINE U NLEARNING
185
186   Interestingly, so far, all existing machine unlearning algorithms assume we only need to ensure pri-
187   vacy protection for the users who execute “the right to be forgotten”, and the remaining ones’ privacy
188   protection is fine to be omitted in unlearning. This assumption is incorrect if the original model is
189   differentially private, where the model builder has reached an agreement of privacy guarantee, i.e.,
190   the   setting, with data contributors. Carlini et al. (2022b) have shown the “privacy onion effect”,
191   i.e., the removal of some samples may affect other samples’ privacy risks. Thus, we take a holis-
192   tic approach to measure all samples’ privacy guarantees before and after unlearning by defining
193
      following criteria:

195   Formally, if we have an ideal estimator E to estimate privacy risk and the unlearning algorithm

      6     E XPERIMENTS
349
350   This section presents extensive experiments aimed at addressing the following key questions: 1.
351   How does A-LiRA perform in terms of both efficacy and efficiency compared to the LiRA methods?
352   2. How well do the published unlearning methods meet the proposed auditing measures?

354   6.1   S ETUP
355
356   Before exploring these questions, the experimental setup is outlined.
357
      Datasets and models: We use CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 to train ResNet-18 models with FFCV
358   (Leclerc et al. (2023)), using a learning rate of 0.5, weight decay of 5e-4, and training for 50 epochs
359   with early stopping on NVIDIA Titan V-100 and Titan Xp GPUs. Each sample has 10 random
360   augmentations to enhance generalization. Due to computational constraints, we limit our experi-
361   ments to these datasets, as large-scale datasets would require training multiple models for privacy
362   risk estimation, which is impractical with current resources.
363
      Membership inference attacks: We use A-LiRA as the attack method, generating 1000 augmenta-
364   tions per sample via random flipping, rotation, shifting, etc. One shadow in-model and one shadow

      Discussion. Using A-LiRA as the estimator, we have found several interesting results. Firstly,
523
      existing machine unlearning may not provide sufficient privacy protection for unlearned samples.
524
      Secondly, machine unlearning may also increase the privacy risk of retained samples, which can
525   be critical if differentially private machine learning is applied. Thus, it’s vital to reconsider how to
526   design proper machine unlearning methods to comprehensively protect the privacy of all involved
527   data samples. Our A-LiRA estimator and acceptance matrix can be a powerful tool to help evaluate
528   such machine unlearning methods.

      7   C ONCLUSION
531
532
      The privacy protection criteria for machine unlearning have not been sufficiently studied yet. We
533
      propose two criteria to audit the privacy risks of unlearned and retained samples to fully understand
534   a machine unlearning algorithm’s privacy protection capacity. The core of the proposed auditing
535   mechanism is an efficient sample-level membership inference attack, A-LiRA. We show in experi-
536   ments that A-LiRA performs efficiently with comparable attacking accuracy to the original online-
537   LiRA algorithm, making it deployable to real applications. With the proposed criteria, we also show
538   that most existing machine unlearning algorithms do not satisfactorily protect samples’ privacy.
