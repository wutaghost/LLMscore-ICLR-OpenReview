009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Generalizing vision-based reinforcement learning (RL) agents to novel environ-
012            ments remains a difficult and open challenge. Current trends are to collect large-
013            scale datasets or use data augmentation techniques to prevent overfitting and im-
014            prove downstream generalization. However, the computational and data collection
015            costs increase exponentially with the number of task variations and can destabilize
016
               the already difficult task of training RL agents. In this work, we take inspiration
               from recent advances in computational neuroscience and propose a model, Asso-
017
               ciative Latent DisentAnglement (ALDA), that builds on standard off-policy RL
018
               towards zero-shot generalization. Specifically, we revisit the role of latent disentan-
019            glement in RL and show how combining it with a model of associative memory
020            achieves zero-shot generalization on difficult task variations without relying on
021            data augmentation. Finally, we formally show that data augmentation techniques
022            are a form of weak disentanglement and discuss the implications of this insight.
023

025   1    I NTRODUCTION
026
027   Training generalist agents that can adapt to novel environments and unseen task variations is a long-
028
      standing goal for vision-based RL. RL generalization benchmarks have focused on data augmentation
      to increase the amount of training data available to the agent while preventing model overfitting and
029
      increasing robustness to environment perturbations (Yarats et al., 2021a; Almuzairee et al., 2024;
030
      Hansen et al., 2021). This follows the current trend in the broader robot learning community of
031   training large models at scale on massive datasets (Kim et al., 2024; Hansen et al., 2024; Team
032   et al., 2024) with the hope that the model will generalize. However, a significant drawback of these
033   approaches is, intuitively, that they require larger models, more training data, longer training times,
034   and have greater training instability that must be dealt with care when training RL agents.
035
      Yet when we examine biological agents, we find that humans and, indeed, many other primates are
036
      able to quickly adapt to task variations and environment perturbations DiCarlo et al. (2012); Friston
037   (2010). While all aspects of biological intelligence that contribute to generalization have yet to
038   be understood, there is some understanding in the recent neuroscience literature of aspects related
039   to representation learning that we look to for inspiration. Many parts of the brain in human and
040   non-human primates contain neurons that represent single factors of variation within the environment,
041   such as grid cells (Hafting et al., 2005), object-vector cells (Høydal et al., 2019), and border cells
042   (Solstad et al., 2008) that represent euclidean spaces, distance to objects, and distance to borders,
043   respectively. Such disentangled representations have been theorized to facilitate compositional
044   generalization (Higgins et al., 2018) and have been studied with curated datasets where the factors of
045
      variation are known (Higgins et al., 2017a; Whittington et al., 2023), and even within the context of
      RL (Higgins et al., 2017b). It is then to our surprise, with limited exceptions (Dunion et al., 2023;
046
      Sax et al., 2018), that disentangled representation learning has not garnered much attention within
047
      robot learning or RL more generally. One potential reason for this is that learning disentangled
048   representations while simultaneously learning an RL policy is extremely difficult. Indeed, Higgins
049   et al. (2017b) required a two-stage approach where the disentangled representation was learned first,
050   followed by policy learning. In addition, Yarats et al. (2021b) found that using a β-VAE directly led
051   to training instability and worse performance, instead opting to use a deterministic autoencoder with
052   softer constraints. Finally, there is counter-evidence by Schott et al. (2022) to suggest that, while
053   disentangling representations may facilitate generalization, it alone cannot achieve out-of-distribution
      (OOD) generalization.

070   We hypothesize that one of the potential key missing ingredients to OOD generalization is associative
071   memory mechanisms that use prior experiences to help inform decision-making in light of new data in
072   a disentangled latent space. Intuitively, if the representation of a stored memory and new observation
073   are disentangled, then the projection of the new observation onto the stored memories becomes a
074   factorized projection in which individual factors can be compared independently of other factors
075   (Figure 1). Indeed, many of the aforementioned single-factor neurons exist in the entorhinal cortex in
076   the hippocampus (Hafting et al., 2005), responsible for, amongst other things, memory recollection
077
      and association. Recent literature suggests that the hippocampus learns flexible representations of
      memories by decomposing sensory information into reusable components and has been implicated in
078
      other cognitive tasks such as planning, decision-making, and imagining novel scenarios (Behrens
079
      et al., 2018; Rubin et al., 2014). It would then seem that the disentanglement of high-dimensional
080   data into a modular, reusable representation is simply the first step in a multi-step process that
081   enables generalization in biological agents. Inspired by these two ingredients found in nature, we
082   propose a new method, ALDA (Associative Latent DisentAnglement), that 1. learns a disentangled
083   representation from the training data and 2. uses an associative memory model to recover data points
084   in the original training distribution zero-shot given OOD data. We demonstrate how this approach
085   enables zero-shot generalization on a common generalization benchmark for vision-based RL without
086   using data augmentation techniques or techniques that remove distractor variables from the latent
087   space. Finally, we provide a formal proof showing that data augmentation methods for vision-based
088
      RL create what we refer to as "weak" disentanglement, where the latent space is perhaps partitioned
      into two or more categories but not perfectly factorized into individual subcomponents. We conclude
089
      by discussing the implications of this insight and future directions of this line of research.

146
      et al., 2017a) and are rarely applied outside of toy datasets.

      2.3   G ENERALIZATION IN V ISION -BASED RL
149
150   Image augmentation methods have shown success and have become the go-to method for generalizing
151   vision-based RL algorithms such as Soft Actor-Critic (SAC) (Haarnoja et al., 2018) and TD3
152   (Fujimoto et al., 2018), generally using augmentations such as random crops, random distortions,
153   and random image overlays to simulate distracting backgrounds. Methods such as DrQ (Yarats et al.,
154   2021a), SADA (Almuzairee et al., 2024), and SVEA (Hansen et al., 2021) regularize the Q function
155   by providing the original and augmented images as inputs into the critic. In many cases, however, the
156   image augmentations can put the training data within the support of the distributions of the evaluation
157   environments. For example, the "random convolution" image augmentation changes the color of
158
      the agent and/or background, and the policy is evaluated on an environment where the color of the
      agent is randomized. This brings into question whether these methods are truly capable of zero-shot
159
      extrapolative generalization when the training data is made to be sufficiently similar to the test data.
160
161   Beyond image augmentation techniques are methods that perform self-supervision using auxiliary
      objectives. Note that image augmentations for RL are also sometimes referred to as self-supervised


                                                              3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      objectives, however we wish to make the distinction between methods that leverage data augmentation
163   and those that don’t. DARLA Higgins et al. (2017b), to the best of our knowledge, is the only prior
164   method that learns a disentangled representation of the image inputs using a highly regularized
165   β-VAE (Higgins et al., 2017a) for zero-shot generalization in RL. DARLA’s approach is two-stage,
166   where an initial dataset is collected by sampling random actions to first learn a disentangled latent
167   representation, and then a policy is trained on this representation to maximize future return. However,
168   a significant shortcoming is that random actions may not cover the full state distribution of the agent
169   for more complicated tasks, whereas our method jointly learns the disentangled representation and
170   the policy. SAC+AE (Yarats et al., 2021b) trains a decoder to reconstruct the images, resulting in
171
      a rich latent space that improves performance and sample efficiency on many vision-based tasks.
      Interestingly, SAC+AE mentions β-VAE’s used by DARLA and proposes using a deterministic
172
      variant with similar constraints that shows some zero-shot generalization capability, but the authors
173
      make no mention of disentanglement and instead conclude that the key ingredient was adding a
174   reconstruction loss as an auxiliary objective.
175
176   Another promising approach to generalization is learning a task-centric or object-centric represen-
177
      tation using auxiliary objectives. Yamada et al. (2022) learn a task-centric representation by using
      expected discounted returns as labels, with the auxiliary task being to minimize the error between the
178
      predicted and true return values using the learned representation. Ferraro et al. (2023); Pore et al.
179
      (2024) use segmentation masks to learn object-centric representations that are robust to background
180   distractors. One drawback of these approaches is that the latent representation overfits to the task
181   by excluding all other information not relevant to the immediate task, usually citing that irrelevant
182   information in the latent space hinders generalization performance. However, adapting to a new
183   task that involves information that was previously considered irrelevant becomes a challenge for
184   these methods. We hypothesize that the issue is not having "irrelevant" information in the latent
185   space but rather that the latent variables are entangled without strong priors for disentanglement. A
186   disentangled representation then paves the way for association, whereby individual dimensions of
187   latent vectors from OOD images can be independently zero-shot mapped back to known values of
188
      those latent variables learned from the training data.

190   2.4   A SSOCIATIVE M EMORY
191
      An associative memory (AM) network stores a set of patterns with the intent to retrieve the most
192
      similar stored pattern given an input. The best-known form is a Hopfield network, originally proposed
193   in Hopfield (1982), which was inspired by how the brain is capable of recalling entire memories
194   given partial or corrupted input (e.g., recalling a food item given a particular smell). Classical
195   Hopfield networks could only store and recall binarized memories, whereas modern (dense) Hopfield
196   networks (Krotov & Hopfield, 2016) can work with continuous representations and are trainable as
197   differentiable layers within existing Deep Learning frameworks (Ramsauer et al., 2021). The memory
198   retrieval dynamics are typically formulated as a function of energy minimization. Let ξ ∈ Rd be the
199   input query pattern, and X := [x1 , ...xM ] ∈ Rd×M be memory patterns. In AM models, memories
200   are stored on the local minima of the energy landscape, where the goal is to retrieve the closest stored
201
      pattern to ξ by minimizing energy. Modern Hopfield networks assume the following general form for
      the energy function:
202                                                     M
                                                       X

204                                                    i=1

      In particular, by setting F = −lse(β, XT ξ) + 12 ξ T ξ (lse = log-sum-exponent), the retrieval dynamics
206
      becomes ξ new = Xsoftmax(βXT ξ), which is the attention mechanism (Vaswani, 2017) and the
207
      backbone of modern Hopfield networks. Follow-up works such as Bietti et al. (2023) show a tight
208
      connection between the learning dynamics of Transformers and models of associative memory.

214   We begin by motivating the case for learning a disentangled representation for RL agents by showing
215   a connection between data augmentation and disentangled representation learning. Specifically, we
      formally prove that data augmentation is a weak disentanglement of the latent space. We define weak

218   complete factorization where each latent dimension zk ∈ z encodes for a unique source si ∈ s and is
219   thus linearly independent of other latent dimensions, which is the goal of disentangled representation
220   learning. The full proof is provided in A.1.

222

223   two categories, D and E, which correspond to task-relevant and task-irrelevant sources, respectively.
224   For any such zk , if Q∗ (z, a) is an optimality invariant optimal Q-function immune to distractor

226

229   Intuitively, if data augmentation enables learning a latent representation such that the Q(z, a), a
230   function of z, is immune to distractor variables, then any dimension of the latent space that encodes
231   for task-relevant variables cannot also encode for task-irrelevant variables. Otherwise, distribution
232   shifts involving the task-irrelevant variables would affect the Q function and, thus, the performance
233   of the agent. One of two conditions must be true: either z is partitioned, where some variables
234   approximate only sources from D, and others only sources from E, or z contains no information
235   about sources in E altogether, both of which are a form of weak disentanglement.
236   We take a probabilistic perspective to see why this relationship is important. Suppose s1...,k ∈ D
237   and s1+k,...,ns ∈ E. In order to learn a latent representation that contains no information about
238   task-irrelevant sources s1+k,...,ns , data augmentation methods essentially estimate the marginal
239   distribution over task-relevant sources:
240

241                     p(s1 , ..., sk ) =          ×          ×... ×         p(s1 , ..., sk , sk+1 , ...sns ).   (3)

244   The implication of 3 is that we must collect data for every possible variation of the task-irrelevant
245   sources, which may be prohibitively expensive for real-world applications. Instead, a model with
246   strong priors that leads to disentanglement without data augmentation essentially achieves the same
247   result without the additional costs. Although the latent representation of such a model may still
248   contain task-irrelevant features, the policy can learn to simply ignore them or associate them with
249
      known values in the presence of OOD data, as is the case with ALDA. In addition, these task-irrelevant
      features may become relevant if the task changes (e.g., if the current task is for a manipulator to stack
250
      a blue cube, and the next task is to stack a red cube), and so it may, in fact, be important to keep them.

395   The obvious question is, how do we set the dimensionality of zd , which should 1:1 correspond to the
396   number of sources ns if the number of sources is unknown? While there is no rigorous method to
397   derive |zd | at this time, we empirically found that setting |zd | to within the ballpark of the size of the
398   observation spaces for the proprioceptive, state-based versions of the tasks seemed to work well. |zd |
399   is set to 12 for all reported tasks.

401   5    E XPERIMENTS
402
403   We compare against several baselines that together represent the full range of different learning
404   paradigms in the literature that attempt to elicit zero-shot generalization. DARLA (Higgins et al.,
405   2017b) is, to the best of our knowledge, the only other algorithm that attempts to learn a disentan-
406   gled representation of the image distribution towards zero-shot generalization of vision-based RL.
407   SAC+AE (Yarats et al., 2021b) uses a deterministic autoencoder with an auxiliary reconstruction
408   objective and strong regularization that demonstrates decent zero-shot generalization capability.
409   RePo (Zhu et al., 2023) is a model-based RL algorithm that learns a task-centric latent representation
410   immune to background distractors. Finally, SVEA (Hansen et al., 2021) is an off-policy RL algorithm
411
      that improves training stability and performance of off-policy RL under data augmentation. As in
      their paper, we use the random overlay augmentation for SVEA, where images sampled from the
412
      Places (Zhou et al., 2017) dataset of 10 million images are overlayed during training. The training
413
      curves and evaluation on "color hard" and DistractingCS are presented in Figure 5.
414
415   Excluding SVEA, ALDA outperforms all baselines on both distribution shift environments. ALDA
416   also maintains stability and high performance on the training environment, despite the disentanglement
417
      auxiliary objective and extremely strong weight decay (λθ , λϕ = 0.1) on the encoder and decoder.
      We do not expect to outperform SVEA since it uses additional data sampled from a dataset of 1.8
418
      million diverse real-world scenes, likely putting the training data within the support of the data
419
      distributions of the evaluation environments. Nevertheless, ALDA performs comparably and, in some
420   cases, is equal to SVEA despite only seeing images from the original task. Performance degrades
421   severely for all algorithms on the DistractingCS environment. We suspect that, in addition to the
422   already difficult task of ignoring the background video, camera shaking affects the implicitly learned
423   dynamics, and thus, additional finetuning may be unavoidable for this task. Still, ALDA performs
424   better than all baselines excluding SVEA on Distracting CS as well, even matching the performance
425   of SVEA on cartpole balance and finger spin.
426   The disentangled representation learning field primarily uses toy datasets where the ground truth
427   sources of the data distribution are known. Therefore, all disentanglement metrics we are aware of re-
428   quire knowing the sources, making it difficult to quantitatively evaluate disentanglement performance
429   on DMControl. In the absence of any quantitative disentanglement metrics, we opt to show empirical
430   evidence of disentanglement in our model, presented in Figure 6. In this experiment, we encode an

479   6   D ISCUSSION
480
481   As stated previously, the disentanglement problem by extension of nonlinear ICA is underdeter-
482   mined, so there are many ways the latent space may factorize, perhaps by representing the sky and
483   background with one latent or by separating them into two latents, etc. Given that both the task
484   and reconstruction gradients of the critic/decoder affect the latent model/encoder, an interesting
485   scientific and philosophical implication is that the model is potentially biased towards a disentangled
      representation that is useful, although there is no way to quantitatively or qualitatively show such a


                                                         9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      result at this time. Nevertheless, it remains an interesting line of further investigation from a scientific
487   standpoint, and perhaps, philosophically, says something about whether the question "What is the
488   ground truth factorized representation that best explains the data?" is even the right question to ask.
489
490
      RL agents deployed in the real world must constantly adapt to changing environmental conditions.
      Much of the variance can be captured with a sufficiently large dataset. However, there remains
491
      a portion of the distribution containing every possible edge case and unaccounted-for variation,
492
      commonly referred to as "the long tail," that remains elusive because it is prohibitively expensive to
493   account for every possible variation. Unfortunately, these uncaptured variations are frequent enough
494   due to the ever-changing dynamical nature and complexity of the real world that deploying agents in
495   the real world remains challenging. Therefore, it seems the case that data augmentation techniques,
496   collecting massive datasets, and the like are not sufficient to develop generalist agents capable of
497   adaptation the way humans and other animals are. That’s not to say that data isn’t important or a
498   fundamental ingredient to training machine learning models. In fact, the method proposed in this
499   paper scales with more data as with prior works that leverage data augmentation techniques. Instead,
500   our proposition is that if a data-driven model can generalize better with less data, then it will scale
501
      better with more data.
502   In Section 3, we showed how data augmentation and disentangled representation learning aim to
503   achieve the same result – a factorization of the latent space into separate components in order to
504   improve downstream generalization performance. Given the additional computational and data
505   collection costs and potential training instabilities that data augmentation methods may incur, it
506   seems more fruitful to investigate models with inductive biases that elicit modular and generalizable
507
      representations without relying on data scaling laws. While presenting the model with sufficiently
      large and diverse datasets remains unquestionably important, we cannot rely solely upon the data
508
      in hopes that the model learns a good representation. As with any other inductive biases, such as
509
      using CNNs for vision tasks or transformers for NLP tasks, inductive biases that elicit modular
510   representations while leveraging data are worth studying if we are to develop agents that can perform
511   and adapt well in the real world.
512
513
      We hope that the work presented here inspires future research into novel models and architectures
      to learn representations that enable the adaptability we see in our biological counterparts. We
514
      discuss some limitations of our method and promising directions for future research. One notable
515
      limitation is that our disentangled latent representation zd does not explicitly account for temporal
516   information since it primarily estimates the sources that produce the image distribution. Instead, we
517   must capture temporal information in the downstream 1D-CNN layer as shown in Figure 2. How
518   to learn a disentangled representation that contains sources of both the image data and temporal
519   information for decision-making tasks remains an open question. Another limitation is that, while we
520   introduce a simple Hopfield model as a modification to QLAE, we do not take advantage of the more
521   recent literature involving learnable attention-based or energy-based Hopfield networks (Ramsauer
522   et al., 2021; Hoover et al., 2024). Stronger Hopfield models that synergize well with disentangled
523   representations is another potentially fruitful research direction.
524   Given that we use a very compact disentangled latent space with strong empirical evidence that
525   individual latents capture information about specific aspects of the agent, an interesting research
526   direction is to investigate whether all or parts of the proprioceptive state representation can be
527   recovered from image observations. We provide some preliminary evidence of this in the appendix
528   (A.2). Beyond interpretability, such a model may yield better performance since state-based RL
529   agents tend to perform better than vision-based agents. Finally, while our work was inspired by
530
      the role of the hippocampus in biological intelligence, the exact mechanisms of the machinery and
      how they interact with decision-making, planning, and imagination components of biological brains
531
      are by no means precisely modeled in this paper, nor are all of the computations the hippocampus
532
      may be performing fully understood. Future collaborative research between the machine learning
533   and neuroscience fields into data-driven computational models of these mechanisms may yield even
534   better-performing, adaptable agents.

      A.1    P ROOF
759
760   Preliminaries. We reintroduce the nonlinear ICA problem formulation here for the reader’s reference.
761   There are ns nonlinear independent variables s = s1 , ..., sns that are considered the "ground truth"
762   sources of variation of the data distribution. We assume there exists a data-generating model that
763   maps sources to images:
764
                                                         ns
765                                                      Y
766                                             p(s) =         p(si ), o = g(s)
767                                                      i=1

768   where g : S → O is the non-linear data generating function. For the purpose of this proof, we restrict
769   S and O to be the space of sources and images within our dataset. The goal of nonlinear ICA, and by
770   extension, disentangled representation learning, is to recover the underlying sources given samples
771   from this model. We claim that most, if not all, data augmentation techniques in Q-learning are a
772   form of weak disentanglement, where either the method factorizes the latent space into task-relevant /
773   task-irrelevant variables or removes task-irrelevant variables from the latent space entirely.
774   For a given task, we can split the sources into two categories: sources s1...k , k < ns that are task-
775   relevant, which we will call D, and sources sk+1,...,ns that are not, whose category we will refer to as
776   E. The encoder maps observations to a latent space f : O → Z, and so z is a function of the sources
777   z = fθ (g(s)). We refer to sˆi as an approximation to the true variable si that exists in one or more
778   dimensions of z. We make no assumptions on whether the sources are entangled or disentangled in z.
779
      Optimality Invariant Image Transformations Described in Yarats et al. (2021a), data augmentation
780   applied to Q-learning can be formulated using the following general framework. An optimality-
781   invariant state transformation h : O × T → O is a mapping that preserves Q values.
782
783
                               Q(fθ (o), a) = Q(fθ (h(o, v)), a)∀o ∈ O, a ∈ A, v ∈ T .                     (9)
784
785   v are the parameters of h(·, ·) drawn from the set of all possible parameters T . In other words, T
786   defines the space of all possible data augmentations that should not affect the output of the Q-function.
787
788
      Proposition 1: Let ϕ : S → S be a function that perturbs sources sj ∈ E. Then
789
790                                                 h(o, v) = g(ϕ(s)).                                    (10)
791
792   This follows from the definition of E in that any optimality-invariant transformation to the observation
793   must have implicitly resulted from a perturbation of some task-irrelevant source sj ∈ E.
794   Proposition 2: For any given z = fθ (g(s)) and any perturbation to a true source sj ∈ E, j ∈
795   [k + 1, ns ] resulting in a new latent z′ = fθ (g(s′ )), the following must be true for an optimality
796   invariant optimal Q-function:
797
798                                               Q∗ (z, a) = Q∗ (z′ , a).                                (11)
799                     ′        ′
      We can rewrite z as z = fθ (g(ϕ(s))) i.e. o = g(ϕ(s)), and by Proposition 1, g(ϕ(s)) = h(o, v).
800
      Essentially, an optimality invariant Q-function is immune to variations of task-irrelevant sources from
801
      the set E, since they correspond to optimality-invariant state transformations.
802
803   Theorem 1: For any z = fθ (g(s)), and for any dimension zk of z, the following must be true for an
804   optimality invariant Q-function:
805
806                         cov(sˆi , sˆj |zk ) = 0 ∀si ∈ D, sj ∈ E, i ∈ [1, k], j ∈ [k + 1, ns ].        (12)
807
808   To see why this must be the case, suppose that the covariance is nonzero and suppose that we
809   perturb sj ∈ E to s′j , giving us a new observation o′ = g(s′ ). Since sj is task-irrelevant, z′ =
      fθ (o′ ) = fθ (h(o, v)) for some v ∈ T . If z′ is a function of h, then by equations 9 and 11,

      Q∗ (z′ , a) = Q∗ (z, a). However, if cov(sˆi , sˆj |zk ) ̸= 0 for some zk ∈ z, then sˆi ′ ∈ z′ ̸= sˆi ∈ z.
811   Since sˆi ∈ D, Q∗ (z′ , a) ̸= Q∗ (z, a), which is a contradiction. Therefore, the conditional covariance
812   between any sˆi ∈ D and any sˆj ∈ E for any given zk must be zero, which implies that the
813   approximations of task-relevant and task-irrelevant sources in z are disentangled.

865
866   Latent traversals for the other reported DMControl tasks are presented here. We also visualize the

869   be the lack of balanced data and data diversity of the cartpole replay buffer. The (near) optimal
870   policy is achieved quite early on, after which most images collected are of the cartpole upright
871   and roughly in the same x-position. The lack of data diversity likely makes it difficult to learn a
872
      representation in which the latent traversals are more continuous / physically plausible. Interestingly,
      this phenomenon does not seem to affect performance on the "color hard" evaluation environment,
873

874
      were smoother. We leave an investigation into the effects of data balancing and data diversity on
875   downstream generalization performance as future work.

952
      We perform an analysis of the effects of different β values on ALDA’s performance. The memory
953   retrieval dynamics are reintroduced here for the reader’s reference:
954
955
956
                                    zdj = Softmax(−βL1 (fθ (o)j , vj )) ⊙ vj .
957
      Small values of β result in a more even distribution of the probability mass between latent values
958   per codebook, which implies that the output will be a weighted sum of different latent values (or
959   memories under the Hopfield interpretation). We choose three different values, β = (1, 10, 50), and
960   compare with the main result (β = 100) presented in the paper on the Walker domain, shown in 12.
961   To our surprise, lower β values have little to no effect on generalization performance and, in fact,
962   increase training performance. This perhaps challenges the assumptions made in Hsu et al. (2023)
963   about the requirements of disentanglement via latent quantization, but admittedly requires further
964   analysis, which we leave to future work.
