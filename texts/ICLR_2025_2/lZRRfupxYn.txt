009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            The black-box nature of machine learning (ML) models, particularly neural net-
012            works, poses a significant challenge to their broader application in engineering, es-
013            pecially in high-risk areas where decision-making transparency and interpretabil-
014            ity are crucial. Understanding the generalizability of ML models remains a key
015            topic in artificial intelligence (AI), yet a unified understanding of this issue has
016            not been established. This study introduces the concept of compromise in com-
017            petition (CIC) from mesoscience to elucidate ML model generalizability. In this
018
               work, a scale decomposition method is proposed from the perspective of training
               samples, and the CIC between memorizing and forgetting, refined as dominant
019
               mechanisms, is studied. Empirical studies on computer vision (CV) and natural
020
               language processing (NLP) datasets demonstrate that the CIC between memo-
021            rizing and forgetting affects model generalizability significantly. Moreover, tech-
022            niques like dropout and L2 regularization, traditionally used to combat overfitting,
023            can be reinterpreted through the CIC between memorizing and forgetting. Collec-
024            tively, this work proposes a new perspective to explain the generalizability of ML
025            models, in order to provide inherent support for further applications of ML models
026            in the field of engineering.

029   1   INTRODUCTION
030
031   ML models, particularly neural networks, have been widely implemented across various engineering
032   fields, such as plasticity prediction Mozaffar et al. (2019), material discovery Hatakeyama-Sato et al.
033
      (2020), and fault diagnosis Qin & Zhao (2022), demonstrating robust generalizability. Nonetheless,
      these models are often criticized for their black-box nature, meaning their prediction processes lack
034
      transparency Hassija et al. (2024). In engineering domains where safety and prediction reliability
035
      are paramount, such as medicine Shehab et al. (2022), chemical engineering Wen et al. (2024),
036   and autonomous driving Bachute & Subhedar (2021), the interpretability of models is critically
037   important Zhu et al. (2022).
038
039
      The interplay between model generalizability and interpretability has become a critical area of re-
      search in AI applications. In biomedical research and healthcare, particularly in cancer research,
040
      ML presents numerous opportunities, including cancer detection, diagnosis, subtype classification,
041
      treatment optimization, and the identification of novel therapeutic targets in drug discovery Ele-
042   mento et al. (2021). While ML models can enhance the accuracy of cancer diagnoses, improve
043   patient prognoses, and reduce medical costs, the challenge of explainable AI persists Elemento et al.
044   (2021). Limited interpretability may lead to a lack of trust in these technologies among healthcare
045   professionals Alshuhri et al. (2023).
046
      Over the years, researchers have sought to identify key factors influencing model generalizability.
047
      The “bias-variance trade-off” Geman et al. (1992), historically viewed as a foundational principle
048   for understanding generalizability Neal et al. (2018); Yang et al. (2020), suggests that test loss can
049   be decomposed into bias and variance. However, bias and variance are merely outcomes on test
050   datasets, not the underlying causes of generalizability. The “model complexity-data complexity”
051   paradigm posits that optimal generalization is achieved when model complexity aligns with data
052   complexity Myung (2000). Numerous studies have investigated how this relationship affects gen-
053   eralizability Hastie et al. (2022); Mei & Montanari (2022); Schaeffer et al. (2023), yet no unified
      quantitative standards for model complexity Hu et al. (2021) and data complexity Ho & Basu (2002);


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      Li et al. (2018a); Branchaud-Charron et al. (2019) have been established. A major challenge in uni-
055   fying explanations for generalizability is the complexity of ML models and their training datasets.
056   For instance, GPT-4, with 1.8 trillion parameters Raiaan et al. (2024), requires vast training data
057   for robust generalization, making it difficult to describe the training process with precise mathemat-
058   ical or physical formulas. Thus, there is an urgent need for innovative approaches to enhance the
059   interpretability of model prediction processes.
060
      Recently, mesoscience (Ge et al. (2007); Li et al. (2018b)), which argues that the system complexity
061   stems from the CIC between the two (or more) coexisting dominant mechanisms, has been proposed
062   to cope with multilevel complexities. Instead of relying on traditional mathematical and physical
063   formulas, mesoscience analyzes the CIC to realize the connections between system behaviors and
064   underlying mechanisms. This approach involves performing scale decomposition, refining domi-
065   nant mechanisms, and analyzing their CIC. Taking two dominant mechanisms in a system as an
066   example, with the increasing dominance of mechanism B over mechanism A, three regimes can
067   appear in turn: mechanism A dominates, mechanism A-mechanism B compromise, and mechanism
068   B dominates, corresponding to different system behaviors, respectively Huang et al. (2018). The
069
      principle of mesoscience has been applied in multiple complex systems successfully, e.g., chemical
      engineering Li et al. (2016), life sciences Qian & Beltran (2022), geology Tordesillas et al. (2021).
070
      Guo et al. (2019) proposed a research paradigm of AI, which introduces the analytical principles of
071
      mesoscience into the design of deep learning models. This paradigm has led to the development of
072   mesoscience-guided deep learning (MGDL), which has demonstrated remarkable improvements in
073   terms of convergence stability and predictive accuracy Guo et al. (2024). Therefore, the application
074   of mesoscience principles offers a promising methodological approach to explore the generalizabil-
075   ity of ML models.

104   system dynamics necessitate appropriate scale decomposition Ren et al. (2001), making it the initial

106   scale that reflects the observed structure, based on the multi-scale properties of complex systems.
107   For instance, in classical two-phase flow research, the element-scale, meso-scale, and system-scale
      correspond to the particle, cluster, and overall two-phase flow system, respectively, indicating that

      characteristic scales must have clear physical meanings Li & Kwauk (2003). Additionally, meso-
109   science research depends on refining dominant mechanisms. Complex systems may encompass
110   multiple dominant mechanisms. Li & Huang (2014) proposed that it is important to group all dom-
111   inant mechanisms into two integrated ones, each driving the system in opposing directions. While

      2.2   S CALE DECOMPOSITION
144
145   This study introduces a scale decomposition method for complex systems, comprising neural net-
146   works and training datasets. The individual training sample, which contains the necessary features
147   for model training, is defined as the element-scale, while the entire dataset represents the system-
148   scale. During training, the model updates its parameters in discrete batches, as illustrated in Figure 2,
149   highlighting the significant impact of batch size and sample composition on model generalizability.
150   Figure 2(a) demonstrates the trade-off between increased batch size and decreased model generaliz-
151
      ability. This finding, derived from training a Five-layer CNN (w = 3) on the CIFAR-10 dataset with
      label noise (p = 0.2), is further supported by previous work Hoffer et al. (2017), which explored the
152
      effect of batch size on model generalizability more detailedly. Additionally, this study varies the
153
      composition of samples by adjusting random seeds, as shown in Figure 2(b), where such changes
154   significantly affect generalizability. The batch’s crucial role in training dynamics and generalization
155   qualifies it as the meso-scale. As a case study, Figure 3 illustrates the scale decomposition of neural
156   network-CIFAR-10 dataset system.

      Artificial neural networks (ANNs) discern features and formulate decisions through hierarchical ab-
217   straction, mimicking the integrative process of neuronal activity in the brain Uyanik et al. (2022).
218   Neuroscience has long inspired advancements in AI, including CNNs LeCun et al. (1989) and rein-
219   forcement learning (RL) Mnih et al. (2015), as noted by Zador et al. (2023). Cognitive neuroscience
220   reveals that humans continuously gather external data through sensory inputs, accumulating vast
221   information. Like ML models, the brain processes information and makes decisions while facing
222   risks of underfitting and overfitting. Since the brain constructs experiences and anticipates future
223   scenarios through memorizing of learned data, insufficient memorizing may lead to underfitting.
224   Conversely, excessive memorizing of details can result in overfitting, where reliance on past expe-
225
      riences hinders adaptation to new environments. Overfitting in the brain is linked to disorders such
      as post-traumatic stress disorder, depression, schizophrenia, and obsessive-compulsive disorder Sha
226
      et al. (2024). Gravitz (2019) demonstrates that forgetting, as an adaptive learning form, aids humans
227
      in adapting their experiences, thus preventing experiential overfitting. The importance of memoriz-
228   ing and forgetting extends beyond neuroscience, garnering interest from scholars in education, ecol-
229   ogy, and linguistics Sha et al. (2024). Similar to the human brain, ML models undergo processes
230   of memorizing and forgetting during training, significantly impacting their generalizability. For
231   instance, scaling laws suggest that larger model sizes enhance memorization capabilities, thereby
232   improving generalizability Kaplan et al. (2020). However, not all memorizing positively contributes
233   to model generalizability. For example, excessive memorizing in large language models (LLMs)
234   can lead to hallucinations Huang et al. (2023), undermining content reliability. Traditionally, forget-
235   ting is viewed as detrimental to model performance McCloskey & Cohen (1989), but recent insights
236
      from neuroscience suggest that beneficial forgetting is an adaptive function that enhances model
      generalizability Peng et al. (2021). Therefore, this study refines memorizing and forgetting as the
237
      dominant mechanisms, given their crucial role in influencing model generalizability.
238
239   Over time, researchers have adapted various definitions tailored to specific problems Carlini et al.
240   (2023); Pondenkandath et al. (2018); Stern & Weinshall (2023). This study builds upon and extends
241   the definitions proposed by Toneva et al. (2018): during training, if a model fails to accurately pre-
242
      dict a training sample at time t, but succeeds at the subsequent time step t + 1, it indicates that the
      model has memorized the sample. Conversely, if the model accurately predicts a training sample at
243
      time t but fails at t + 1, it indicates that the model has forgotten the sample. Training samples can be
244
      categorized into three distinct sets: S1 , which includes samples that are neither memorized nor for-
245   gotten; S2 , which includes samples that are only memorized and remain unforgettable subsequently
246   (the unforgettable examples, as defined by Toneva et al. (2018)); S3 , which includes samples that
247   are both memorized and forgotten at least once. These sets are mutually exclusive, and their union
248   constitutes the entire training dataset S.
249
      A list synchronized with the training epochs tracks the model’s predictions for individual samples
250
      throughout training, where zeros denote inaccurate predictions and ones indicate accurate predic-
251   tions, as illustrated in Figure 4. Training samples in S1 are not the focus of this study, as they are
252   neither memorized nor forgotten by the model. According to Toneva et al. (2018), samples in S2
253   carry limited information and thus have a negligible effect on model generalizability. Experiments
254   on datasets without label noise, such as MNIST and CIFAR-10, confirm that removing S2 does not
255   significantly impact model generalizability. In contrast, training samples in S3 have a substantial
256   effect on model generalizability Toneva et al. (2018).
257   Consequently, this study focuses on the memorizing and forgetting of training samples in S3 by
258   neural networks. To quantify the model’s overall memorizing and forgetting of these samples, the
259   degree of memorizing (M) and the degree of forgetting (F) are introduced. Specifically, M denotes
260   the fraction of samples in S3 that have been memorized by the end of training, while F denotes the
261   fraction of those that have been forgotten in S3 .

293   2.4   T HE CIC BETWEEN MEMORIZING AND FORGETTING ON DIFFERENT SCALES
294
      The CIC between dominant mechanisms exhibits variations on different scales Li & Huang (2014),
295
      and thus should be clarified. The analysis process, exemplified by training a Five-layer CNN (w = 3)
296   on the CIFAR-10 dataset with label noise (p = 0.2), is as follows: when memorizing is dominant, M
297   tends towards its maximum, whereas when forgetting is dominant, F tends towards its maximum.
298   Figure 5 illustrates that on the element-scale (points A and B), the extremum tendencies of M
299   and F can be realized only instantaneously and alternatively. Spatially, at a specific moment, such
300   as the completion of the 106th epoch, the model’s forgetting of point A is dominant, indicating
301   the extremum tendency of F is realized, while the model’s memorizing of point B is dominant,
302   indicating the extremum tendency of M is realized. Evidently, stability conditions do not exist on
303   the element-scale. In the meso-scale region M (batch index = 1), the extremum tendencies of M and
304
      F still cannot be realized simultaneously. However, in this region, a spatio-temporal compromise
      occurs between these dominant mechanisms. Over time, M      F gradually converges to a constant C1 ,
305
      with fluctuations of a certain amplitude due to the competition between memorizing and forgetting.
306
      This analysis suggests the CIC between memorizing and forgetting on the meso-scale. In the system-
307   scale region G, the CIC is even more pronounced, with M    F converging to another constant C2 over
308   time and the fluctuations diminishing.

466   3.3   R EGULARIZATIONS
467
468   This study explores the effectiveness of regularization techniques, such as dropout and L2 regulariza-
469   tion, in mitigating overfitting. In neural networks, neurons form co-adaptation relationships through
470   interconnections and signal transmissions, capturing intrinsic patterns in training data. The dropout
471
      technique temporarily removes random neurons during training, reducing sensitivity to training data.
      Figure 10(a) shows that without dropout (dropout rate = 0), the model tends to overfit. An optimal
472
      dropout rate, like 0.7, enhances generalizability, whereas an excessive dropout rate, such as 0.95, can
473
      cause underfitting. Figure 10(b) indicates that without dropout, memorizing is absolutely dominant
474   over forgetting, leading to overfitting. Moderate dropout rates, which disrupt some co-adaptations,
475   compel the model to focus on common features of the training dataset while forgetting specific
476   details, leading to optimal generalizability. Conversely, the excessive dropout rate increases the
477   dominance of forgetting over memorizing which makes the model struggle to memorize but forget
478   easily the effective information in the training dataset, leading to underfitting.
479
      Introducing an additional penalty term, such as L2 regularization, to the loss function during training
480   is a common method to constrain model complexity. L2 regularization reduces model complexity by
481   adding a penalty term for the L2 norm of weight parameters to the loss function. The regularization
482   parameter ω is used to control the strength of the regularization term. A larger ω increases the degree
483   of regularization, forcing the model to adopt a simpler form and thereby reduce its complexity. For
484   instance, in training a Four-layer CNN on MNIST with label noise ((p=0.2)), Figure 11 illustrates
485   that similar to dropout, L2 regularization modulates the model generalizability by controlling the
      relative dominance between memorizing and forgetting.

512   4   C ONCLUSION
513
514   This work explains the generalizability of ML models based on the principle of mesoscience, focus-
515   ing on the model’s memorizing and forgetting of training samples during training, and analyzes the
516   CIC between memorizing and forgetting. Additionally, this work proposes M and F to quantify the
517   relative dominance between memorizing and forgetting. The following conclusions are as follows:
518
      (1) The individual training sample is considered as the element-scale, where memorizing and forget-
519   ting only compete during training; a batch of training samples is considered as the meso-scale, where
520   memorizing and forgetting exhibit spatio-temporal compromise; and the entire training dataset is
521   considered as the system-scale, where the spatio-temporal compromise is more evident.
522
      (2) The increase of model complexity and the number of training epochs both can promote the
523
      extremum tendency of M and inhibit the extremum tendency of F, which make the ML model-
524
      training dataset system transition from being forgetting-dominated to being memorizing-dominated
525   gradually.
526
527   (3) Regularization methods such as dropout, L2 regularization, although proposed from different
528
      research perspectives, control the relative dominance between memorizing and forgetting to improve
      model generalizability essentially.
