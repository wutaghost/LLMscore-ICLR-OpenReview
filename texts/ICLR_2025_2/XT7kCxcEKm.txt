                                                   A BSTRACT
010
011
               Recent advances in differentiable structure learning have framed the combinatorial
012
               problem of learning directed acyclic graphs as a continuous optimization problem.
013            Various aspects, including data standardization, have been studied to identify fac-
014            tors that influence the empirical performance of these methods. In this work, we
015            investigate critical limitations in differentiable structure learning methods, focus-
016            ing on settings where the true structure can be identified up to Markov equivalence
017            classes, particularly in the linear Gaussian case. While Ng et al. (2024) high-
018            lighted potential non-convexity issues in this setting, we demonstrate and explain
019            why the use of ℓ1 -penalized likelihood in such cases is fundamentally inconsistent,
020            even if the global optimum of the optimization problem can be found. To resolve
021
               this limitation, we develop a hybrid differentiable structure learning method based
               on ℓ0 -penalized likelihood with hard acyclicity constraint, where the ℓ0 penalty
022
               can be approximated by different techniques including Gumbel-Softmax. Specifi-
023
               cally, we first estimate the underlying moral graph, and use it to restrict the search
024            space of the optimization problem, which helps alleviate the non-convexity issue.
025            Experimental results show that the proposed method enhances empirical perfor-
026            mance both before and after data standardization, providing a more reliable path
027            for future advancements in differentiable structure learning, especially for learn-
028            ing Markov equivalence classes.

031   1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Probabilistic graphical models, such as Bayesian networks, are powerful tools for capturing com-
034   plex probabilistic relationships in a concise way (PEARL, 1988; Koller & Friedman, 2009). Their
035   graph structures, usually encoded as Directed Acyclic Graphs (DAGs), allow efficient representa-
036   tion of data dependencies and have become essential in fields like health (Tennant et al., 2021)
037   and economy (Awokuse & Bessler, 2003). Traditionally, learning these structures involves discrete
038   methodologies. Constraint-based methods, which leverage conditional independence tests, are one
039
      common approach (Spirtes & Glymour, 1991; Spirtes et al., 2001). Another popular technique in-
      volves score-based methods, where the search space of potential graphs is explored based on scoring
040
      functions (Koivisto & Sood, 2004; Singh & Moore, 2005; Cussens, 2011; Yuan & Malone, 2013;
041
      Chickering, 2002; Peters & Bühlmann, 2014). Given the combinatorial nature of the task, greedy
042   search strategies have been commonly used (Chickering, 1996; Chickering et al., 2004).
043
044   In recent years, Zheng et al. (2018) introduced a continuous formulation for characterizing the
045
      acyclicity constraint, effectively converting the discrete nature of the structure learning problem into
      one that can be approached using gradient-based optimization techniques. Although this formula-
046
      tion still involves nonconvex optimization, it opened the door to applying efficient gradient-based
047
      methods. This formulation has since inspired a wide range of extensions, being adapted to deal
048   with nonlinearity (Yu et al., 2019; Lachapelle et al., 2019; Zheng et al., 2020; Ng et al., 2022b;
049   Kalainathan et al., 2022), latent confounding (Bhattacharya et al., 2021; Bellot & van der Schaar,
050   2021), interventional data (Brouillard et al., 2020; Faria et al., 2022), time series data (Pamfil et al.,
051   2020; Sun et al., 2021), and missing data (Wang et al., 2020; Gao et al., 2022). Other applications
052   include multi-task learning (Chen et al., 2021), and working with federated learning systems (Ng &
053   Zhang, 2022; Gao et al., 2021), domain adaptation (Yang et al., 2021), and recommendation system
      (Wang et al., 2022).

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      This move toward continuous structure learning has prompted growing attention to both its theo-
055   retical underpinnings and practical performance. Researchers like Wei et al. (2020) and Ng et al.
056   (2022a) have investigated the optimality and convergence properties of continuous, constrained op-
057   timization techniques (Zheng et al., 2018). Meanwhile, Deng et al. (2023) provided insight into how
058   an appropriately designed optimization scheme can reach the global minimum for least squares ob-
059   jectives in simple cases. Further refinements have also been proposed, with Zhang et al. (2022) and
060   Bello et al. (2022) highlighting challenges such as gradient vanishing in existing DAG constraints
061   (Zheng et al., 2018; Yu et al., 2019) and suggesting potential improvements.
062   Recently, Ng et al. (2024) highlighted the non-convexity issues in differentiable structure learning
063   methods, particularly in the linear Gaussian setting where the true structure can be identified up
064   to Markov equivalence classes. While non-convexity poses major challenges in this context, we
065   further identify another critical issue: ℓ1 -penalized likelihood is inconsistent, even if the global
066   optimum of the optimization problem can be found. To address these limitations, we propose a
067   method that resolves the ℓ1 inconsistency and enhances empirical performance, both before and
068   after data standardization, even under non-convex conditions.
069
070   Contributions In this work, we tackle fundamental challenges in differentiable structure learn-
071
      ing, particularly in the linear Gaussian case, by focusing on the limitations of penalized likelihood
      approaches. Our contributions include:

             • We identify and demonstrate the inconsistency of using ℓ1 -penalized likelihood in differen-
074            tiable structure learning methods, even if the global optimum of the optimization problem
075            can be found, particularly when learning Markov equivalence classes in the linear Gaussian
076            case.
077
             • We develop a differentiable structure learning method that optimizes an ℓ0 -penalized

085          • Our method performs consistently well both before and after data standardization, demon-
086            strating its robustness.

      2.1   P ROBLEM S ETTING
094
095   Setup In this work, we focus on linear Gaussian Structural Equation Models (SEMs), where the
096   variables X = (X1 , . . . , Xd ) follow linear relationships represented by a DAG. The model is ex-
097   pressed as X = B ⊤ X + N . Here, B ∈ Rd×d is the weighted adjacency matrix encoding the
098   relationships between variables. Specifically, an entry Bij ̸= 0 indicates a directed edge from Xj to
099   Xi . The noise vector N = (N1 , . . . , Nd ) consists of independent noise terms, each corresponding to
100   a variable Xi . The noise terms are assumed to follow a normal distribution with diagonal covariance
101   matrix Ω = diag(σ12 , . . . , σd2 ), where σi2 represents the variance of Ni . Given a DAG, a moral graph
102
      is an undirected graph obtained by removing the directions of the edges in the DAG and connecting
      all pairs of parents of common children.
103
104   Unlike many existing methods that assume equal noise variances (Zheng et al., 2018; Yu et al., 2019;
105   Zhang et al., 2022; Bello et al., 2022), in this study, we focus on the general non-equal noise variance
106   (NV) case, where the variances σ12 , . . . , σd2 are not assumed to be equal. Our goal is to estimate the
107   DAG G or its Markov equivalence class (MEC) from the data matrix X ∈ Rn×d , consisting of n
      i.i.d. samples drawn from the distribution P (X).

111   structure is a DAG when h(B) = 0. NOTEARS (Zheng et al., 2018) employs a hard DAG constraint,
112   whereas GOLEM (Ng et al., 2020) introduces a soft DAG constraint.
113
114   NOTEARS and hard DAG constraint NOTEARS solves the following constrained optimization
115   problem

121   barrier method (Bello et al., 2022).

141   3     I NCONSISTENCY OF ℓ1 PENALTY IN S TRUCTURE L EARNING
142
143   In this section, we explore the inconsistency of the ℓ1 penalty in structure learning by comparing
144   its behavior with ℓ0 in linear Gaussian cases. We demonstrate this inconsistency through extensive
145
      experiments and conclude with a counterexample that highlights the issue.
146
147   3.1   ℓ0 VS ℓ1 PENALTY IN L INEAR G AUSSIAN C ASES
148
      In structure learning for linear Gaussian cases, score-based methods, specifically with the BIC
149
      score (Schwarz, 1978; Chickering, 2002), often aim to recover the sparsest underlying DAG that
150   best explains the observed data (Singh & Moore, 2005; Cussens, 2011; Yuan & Malone, 2013;
151   Chickering, 2002). In the asymptotic case where the population covariance matrix, denoted by Σ∗ ,
152   is available, this can, loosely speaking, be formulated as
153
154              min ∥B∥0      subject to (I − B)−⊤ Ω(I − B)−1 = Σ∗           and   B is a DAG.
                 B,Ω
155
156   Recall that B is the weighted adjacency matrix representing the structure of the DAG and Ω is the
157   diagonal matrix of noise variances. That is, the goal is to minimize the ℓ0 norm of B, i.e., the number
158
      of edges, while maximizing the likelihood by satisfying the covariance constraint and ensuring that
159   B is a valid DAG. In other words, the objective is to recover the sparsest DAG, B̂, along with its
160   corresponding Ω̂, that can generate the observed covariance matrix Σ∗ (i.e., (I−B̂)−⊤ Ω̂(I−B̂)−1 =
161   Σ∗ ). Under the sparsest Markov faithfulness assumption (Raskutti & Uhler, 2018), the estimated B̂
      will be Markov equivalent to the true graph B ∗ .


                                                        3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      Many previous work, including GOLEM, replace the ℓ0 penalty with the more tractable ℓ1 penalty.
163   The corresponding optimization problem becomes
164
165              min ∥B∥1       subject to (I − B)−⊤ Ω(I − B)−1 = Σ∗            and   B is a DAG.           (1)
                 B,Ω
166
167
      The ℓ1 penalty encourages smaller edge weights but introduces a key inconsistency: it doesn’t guar-
      antee true sparsity in the resulting structure. Minimizing the ℓ1 norm may lead to a denser structure
168
      with more edges than the solution from minimizing the ℓ0 norm. This occurs because ℓ1 favors
169
      edges with small absolute values, even if they represent spurious edges. In some cases, the sum of
170   the absolute values of more edges can be smaller than that of fewer, larger-magnitude edges, which
171   leads ℓ1 -based methods to include unnecessary edges. As a result, the learned structure may deviate
172   from the true DAG or its Markov equivalence class. Taking GOLEM as an example, even if the co-
173   variance constraint (I − B)−⊤ Ω(I − B)−1 = Σ∗ is satisfied in both ℓ0 - and ℓ1 -based formulations,
174   the structural properties of the solution can differ significantly.
175
      In Section 3.2, we demonstrate this with a large number of experiments, showing that a large pro-
176
      portion of DAGs B̃ satisfying the covariance constraint (I − B̃)−⊤ Ω(I − B̃)−1 = Σ∗ have smaller
177   ℓ1 norms than the true graph B ∗ . Moreover, in these DAGs satisfying the covariance constraint and
178   having smaller ℓ1 norms than the true graph B ∗ , we can always find ones with much larger ℓ0 values
179   than B ∗ , meaning they do not correspond to the true graph B ∗ or its Markov equivalence class, with
180   the structural Hamming distance (SHD) computed over the true and estimated CPDAG (Completed
181   Partially Directed Acyclic Graph), which we refer to as SHD of CPDAG in this paper, far from zero.

274   In Section 2.2, we reviewed the GOLEM-NV formulation proposed by Ng et al. (2020), which aims
275   to maximize the data likelihood utilizes an ℓ1 penalty and soft DAG constraint. We refer to this
276
      original model as GOLEM-NV-ℓ1 throughout this paper. The problem formulation can be expressed
      as
277
278                                      min L(B; Σ) + λ1 ∥B∥1 + λ2 h(B).
                                       B∈Rd×d
279

      However, as pointed out by Ng et al. (2024), GOLEM-NV-ℓ1 suffers from significant non-convexity,
281   often leading to suboptimal local minima with poor performance, both before and after data stan-
282   dardization. Moreover, as we demonstrated in section 3, the ℓ1 penalty leads to inconsistent so-
283   lutions. To address these limitations, we propose CALM (Continuous and Acyclicity-constrained
284   L0-penalized likelihood with estimated Moral graph), a differentiable structure learning method that
285   optimizes an ℓ0 -penalized likelihood with hard DAG constraints and incorporates moral graphs. Our
286   experiments demonstrate that CALM significantly improves performance compared to the original
287   GOLEM-NV-ℓ1 , yielding results much closer to the true DAG or its Markov equivalence class.
288   In the following subsections, we will first introduce the implementation of CALM, followed by the
289   experimental setup and an evaluation of CALM’s performance under various configurations. Finally,
290   we will highlight the revisions and improvements in CALM over Existing Methods, emphasizing its
291   practical design and robust performance compared to GOLEM-NV-ℓ1 and NOTEARS.

      4.1    I NTRODUCTION TO CALM
294
295
      ℓ0 penalty and its approximation with Gumbel Softmax CALM begins with applying an ℓ0
296   penalty to regularize the likelihood, enforcing sparsity in the learned adjacency matrix. Inspired by
297   Ng et al. (2022b); Kalainathan et al. (2022), we use Gumbel Softmax as an example to show how we
298   achieve the approximation of ℓ0 penalty in our approach, as it proved to be the most effective and
299   robust ℓ0 approximation among those we experimented with in section 4.5. When using Gumbel
300   Softmax (Jang et al., 2017) to approximate ℓ0 penalty, CALM starts by representing the learned
301   adjacency matrix B as an element-wise product of a learned mask, gτ (U ) ∈ Rd×d , which determines
302   the presence of edges, and a learned parameter matrix, P ∈ Rd×d , which learns the weights of the
303   edges. The mask gτ (U ) is generated using the Gumbel-Softmax approach. Here, Ui,j represents the
304
      logits, and a logistic noise Gi,j ∼ Logistic(0, 1) is added to Ui,j , producing gτ (Ui,j ) = σ((Ui,j +
      Gi,j )/τ ), where τ is the temperature that controls the smoothness of the Softmax, and σ(·) is the
305
      logistic sigmoid function. As the optimization process proceeds, the values of gτ (Ui,j ) approach
306
      either 0 or 1, approximating an ℓ0 penalty.
307
308
309   Incorporating the moral graph and hard DAG constraints Furthermore, CALM incorporates
310   a learned moral graph M ∈ {0, 1}d×d to restrict the optimization to edges within the moral graph,
311
      thus reducing the search space. Note that similar idea has been used in various existing works
      such as Loh & Bühlmann (2014); Nazaret et al. (2024). This moral graph acts as a filter over the
312
      Gumbel-Softmax mask, allowing only edges present in the moral graph. The final learned B can
313
      be represented as B = M ◦ gτ (U ) ◦ P , incorporating both the sparsity from the Gumbel-Softmax
314   mask and the structural constraints from the moral graph. Here, B contains the edge weights for the
315   DAG, and its structure is determined by the mask M ◦ gτ (U ).
316
317
      CALM’s objective function, incorporating the Gumbel-Softmax mask, moral graph, and hard DAG
      constraints into the GOLEM-NV-ℓ1 formulation, is given by
318
319
               min           L(M ◦ gτ (U ) ◦ P ; Σ) + λ1 ∥M ◦ gτ (U )∥1   subject to h(M ◦ gτ (U )) = 0.
320       U ∈Rd×d ,P ∈Rd×d
321
322   where L(M ◦ gτ (U ) ◦ P ; Σ) is the likelihood term, and the λ1 ∥M ◦ gτ (U )∥1 term approximates the
323   ℓ0 penalty for sparsity. Here, both the ℓ0 penalty for sparsity and the DAG constraints are applied to
      the final learned mask M ◦ gτ (U ), which determines the presence of edges.

      4.2   EXPERIMENTAL SETUP
325
326   Across all experiments in section 4, we simulate Erdös–Rényi graphs (ERDdS & R&wi, 1959)
327   with kd edges, denoted as ERk graphs, with edge weights uniformly sampled from [−2, −0.5] ∪
328   [0.5, 2]. For all experiments, the data is generated with a fixed noise ratio of 16. Specifically,
329   the variances of two randomly selected noise variables are set to 1 and 16, respectively, while the
330   variances of the remaining noise variables are sampled uniformly from the range [1, 16]. This setting
331   ensures a realistic variation in noise across the variables, aligning with the assumptions of non-
332
      equal noise variances (NV). Regarding CALM’s optimization problem, we solve it using a quadratic
      penalty method inspired by Ng et al. (2022a), where each subproblem is tackled using gradient-
333
      based optimization with the Adam optimizer. The computational complexity per iteration is O(d3 ) ,
334
      which is comparable to most other differentiable structure learning methods in the linear case, such
335   as NOTEARS and GOLEM. Regarding parameter tuning, we determined the hyperparameter λ1 ,

339   our experiments in section 4 are in Appendix A. From this point forward, unless otherwise specified,

380   eliminating the need for post-processing, whereas soft constraints often require post-processing to
381   enforce acyclicity (Ng et al., 2020), which may introduce errors and increase SHD of CPDAG.

383   Impact of including the moral graph. Table 2 also shows that incorporating the moral graph

      4.7   R EVISIONS AND I MPROVEMENTS IN CALM OVER E XISTING M ETHODS
497
498
      Our main contributions are to identify and analyze the inconsistency of the ℓ1 penalty for learning
499   Markov equivalence classes, and accordingly investigate how to develop a differentiable approach
500   that mitigates this issue, leading to a more practical and robust differentiable approach for learning
501   Markov equivalence classes. As demonstrated in our experiments, our proposed method, CALM,
502   outperforms existing differentiable methods across all settings, including sparse and dense graphs.
503
      In comparison to GOLEM-NV-ℓ1 , our method introduces several key revisions. First, we address the
504
      inconsistency of ℓ1 -penalties by incorporating a masking approach to approximate the ℓ0 -penalty.
505   Second, we leverage the moral graph to reduce the search space, which simplifies the optimization
506   process and significantly improves convergence. Additionally, we replace the soft DAG constraints
507   in GOLEM-NV-ℓ1 with hard constraints, using a quadratic penalty method inspired by Ng et al.
508   (2022a), leading to a more refined optimization process and ensuring the final graph is always a
509   DAG without requiring post-processing, which often introduces errors in soft constraint methods.
510
      Relative to NOTEARS, our method not only resolves the inconsistency of ℓ1 -penalties and benefits
511   from the moral graph but is also robust to general non-equal noise variance cases. While NOTEARS
512   assumes equal noise variance, limiting its applicability and causing its performance to degrade after
513   data standardization, our method remains effective across both standardized and non-standardized
514   data.
515
      Overall, these enhancements, including robust parameter tuning, ensure that CALM consistently
516
      outperforms other differentiable approaches. It demonstrates superior SHD of CPDAG, skeleton
517   precision, and skeleton recall, across diverse graph types and densities, establishing itself as a more
518   practical and reliable approach for real-world applications.

521   5     C ONCLUSION
522
523
      Our work begins by identifying the inconsistency of ℓ1 -penalized likelihood in differentiable struc-
524
      ture learning for the linear Gaussian case. To address this and improve performance, we propose
525   CALM, which optimizes an ℓ0 -penalized likelihood with hard acyclicity constraints and incorpo-
526   rates moral graphs. Our results show that CALM, particularly with Gumbel-Softmax ℓ0 approxi-
527   mation, significantly outperforms GOLEM-NV-ℓ1 and NOTEARS across various graph types and
528   sample sizes. In sparse graphs like ER1, CALM’s performance rivals PC with smaller samples,
529   while in dense graphs like ER4, it achieves the best results among all baseline methods. CALM
530   also maintains robust performance both before and after data standardization. Future work includes
531   extending CALM to nonlinear models and integrating advanced optimization techniques for further
532   improvements in linear models.

780   A.4   T HE WAY TO ACHIEVE INFINITY SAMPLES
781
782   A significant portion of our experiments is conducted under the assumption of infinite sample size.
783   To achieve infinite samples, we use the true covariance matrix, which is calculated as
784                                    Σ∗ = (I − B ∗ )−⊤ Ω∗ (I − B ∗ )−1 .
785
786   where B ∗ is the true adjacency matrix (ground truth DAG) and Ω∗ is the true noise variance matrix.
787
      The true covariance matrix Σ∗ is then substituted into the likelihood term of the objective function,
      allowing us to simulate the infinite sample case. Specifically, we substitute Σ∗ for Σ in the likelihood
788
      term of our method’s objective function L(M ◦gτ (U )◦P ; Σ). When data standardization is required
789
      for infinite samples, we simply compute the standardized covariance matrix, Σ∗std and substitute Σ∗std
790   for Σ in L(M ◦ gτ (U ) ◦ P ; Σ).

805            sum of probabilities that the gates are active, which is i,j Φ (µij /σ) · Mij , where Φ(·)

807            our experiments, we initialize µ = 0.5 for all elements, as indicated in the pseudocode in
808
               Algorithm 1 of Yamada et al. (2020), and set σ = 0.5.

       H    T RADE -O FF B ETWEEN RUNTIME AND P ERFORMANCE IN CALM
1085
1086
       There is a trade-off between the ℓ0 -based methods proposed and existing methods. While CALM
1087   does not demonstrate a runtime advantage over other differentiable methods on small and sparse
1088   graphs, it may even require less time than some alternatives on larger graphs. For instance, on 50-
1089   node SF4 graphs, CALM takes approximately 2500 seconds per run, compared to 20 seconds for
1090   NOTEARS and 150 seconds for GOLEM. However, on 200-node ER4 graphs, CALM takes around
1091   4500 seconds per run, whereas NOTEARS takes about 6500 seconds and GOLEM approximately
1092   3000 seconds.
1093   This demonstrates that while CALM’s runtime is not the fastest, it scales reasonably well with the
1094   graph size, and its performance does not degrade disproportionately as the number of nodes grows.
1095   Furthermore, CALM delivers superior results in terms of structural hamming distance (SHD) of
1096   CPDAG, skeleton precision, and skeleton recall, particularly for dense and large graphs. These
1097   significant performance improvements justify the additional computational cost.
1098
       We believe this trade-off between runtime and performance is acceptable, given the substantial gains
1099   in accuracy compared to other differentiable methods.
