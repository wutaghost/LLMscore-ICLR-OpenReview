011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            The integrity of AI benchmarks is fundamental to accurately assess the capabili-
014            ties of AI systems. The internal validity of these benchmarks—i.e., making sure
015
               they are free from confounding factors—is crucial for ensuring that they are mea-
               suring what they are designed to measure. In this paper, we explore a key issue
016
               related to internal validity: the possibility that AI systems can solve benchmarks in
017
               unintended ways, bypassing the capability being tested. This phenomenon, widely
018            known in human and animal experiments, is often referred to as the ‘Clever Hans’
019            effect, where tasks are solved using spurious cues, often involving much simpler
020            processes than those putatively assessed. Previous research suggests that language
021            models can exhibit this behaviour as well. In several older Natural Language Pro-
022            cessing (NLP) benchmarks, individual n-grams like “not” have been found to be
023            highly predictive of the correct labels, and supervised NLP models have been
024            shown to exploit these patterns. In this work, we investigate the extent to which
025            simple n-grams extracted from benchmark instances can be combined to predict
026
               labels in modern multiple-choice benchmarks designed for LLMs, and whether
               LLMs might be using such n-gram patterns to solve these benchmarks. We show
027
               how simple classifiers trained on these n-grams can achieve high scores on several
028
               benchmarks, despite lacking the capabilities being tested. Additionally, we pro-
029            vide evidence that some modern LLMs might be using these superficial patterns
030            to solve benchmarks. This suggests that the internal validity of these benchmarks
031            may be compromised and caution should be exercised when interpreting LLM
032            performance results on them.

035   1    I NTRODUCTION
036
037   With the rise of large language models (LLMs), numerous benchmarks have been developed to
038   evaluate different capabilities of these models and to rank their performance (see the collections
039
      HELM (Liang et al., 2022), BIGBench (Srivastava et al., 2022), LogiGLUE (Luo et al., 2023),
      CALM-bench (Dalal et al., 2023) and GLoRE (Teng et al., 2023) for examples of capabilities being
040
      tested). Ensuring the validity of these benchmarks is essential. One important aspect of validity
041
      is internal validity: the benchmarks should be free from confounding factors (Liao et al., 2021;
042   Frank et al., 2023; Rutar et al., 2024). In other words, the behaviour and performance observed in
043   a benchmark should be explainable by the specific capability (or lack thereof) being tested, not by
044   other unrelated capabilities, systematic errors, or biases in the benchmark data.
045
      A benchmark’s internal validity can be compromised in several ways, including annotation errors
046
      and disagreement (Ivanova, 2023), selection bias (Gururangan et al., 2018), implementation varia-
047
      tions (Liao et al., 2021; Biderman et al., 2024), limited linguistic diversity (McIntosh et al., 2024),
048   and insufficient variability in both task-relevant and irrelevant features (Rutar et al., 2024; Yang
049   et al., 2023). Additionally, a lack of prerequisite capabilities in the system to be tested (Rutar et al.,
050   2024; Hu & Frank, 2024) and train-test contamination (Liao et al., 2021; Dominguez-Olmedo et al.,
051   2024) can also impact validity. Some of these systematic issues may introduce “shortcuts” in bench-
052   marks, which makes it possible for subjects to successfully complete tasks without using the specific
053   capabilities the benchmarks are designed to test. In this paper, we investigate whether such shortcuts
      persist in modern LLM benchmarks and, if so, whether LLMs are exploiting them.

054
      The phenomenon of using shortcuts or cues to solve tasks is known as the Clever Hans effect and is
055   well documented in human and animal experimentation. It occurs when, for example, a participant’s
056   behaviour is influenced by unintended cues from the experimenter, leading to responses based on
057   those cues rather than the task itself (Frank et al., 2023; MaBouDi et al., 2021). To control for such
058   effects, techniques such as manipulation checks, stimulus randomisation and counterbalancing are
059   used to detect and mitigate these issues.
060
      In AI, the Clever Hans effect has also been widely recognised (Sturm, 2014; Hernandez-Orallo,
061   2019). In particular, reinforcement learning agents are known for solving tasks in unintended ways
062   (Krakovna et al., 2020). One of the main goals of explainable AI is to understand the information AI
063   models use to solve tasks and ensure they are relying on the right cues rather than exploiting spurious
064   correlations (Ribeiro et al., 2016). In NLP benchmarks, previous research has shown that simple
065   features extracted from prompts can correlate with labels, despite not being related to the capability
066   being tested (Gururangan et al., 2018; Friedman et al., 2022; Gardner et al., 2021). Moreover,
067   supervised language models have been found to exploit these features to solve tasks. For example,
068   Kavumba et al. (2019) showed that an individual unigram “a” has been exploited by BERT to choose
069
      the correct alternative on COPA dataset. Most of the works in this space has focused on traditional
      NLP models. Fewer studies (e.g. Tu et al., 2020; Kavumba et al., 2022; Du et al., 2023) have
070
      investigated LLMs, and these have primarily considered individual n-grams or similarly simple
071
      features.
072
073   LLMs, however, have sufficient capacity to learn and rely on patterns involving multiple simple
074   features. In this paper, we explore the extent to which current LLM benchmarks can be solved
075
      using a combination of such simple features. Specifically, we train logistic regression classifiers
      on unigrams and bigrams extracted from prompts to predict the correct labels in multiple-choice
076
      benchmarks where the choices are identical throughout benchmark samples. These classifiers lack
077
      the capacity to develop the capabilities the benchmarks are designed to test, meaning that any la-
078   bels successfully predicted using these classifiers indicate that the examples may be solved without
079   invoking the capability of interest.
080
081
      We find that, in several benchmarks, our classifiers can accurately predict ground truth labels, rais-
      ing concerns about internal validity. Moreover, recent work has highlighted the sensitivity of LLM
082
      performance to small perturbations in benchmark data suggesting that LLMs rely on shallow, sur-
083
      face level patterns to solve tasks (Alzahrani et al., 2024). The compromised internal validity of
084   benchmarks may therefore allow LLMs to exploit these superficial patterns to solve tasks, rather
085   than engaging the capabilities of interest. Indeed, our further analysis shows that some LLM fami-
086   lies may leverage such n-gram associations to solve benchmark tasks, suggesting that they may not
087   be using the intended capabilities to solve those tasks. However, our findings are not conclusive
088   and warrant further investigation. In particular, we find no evidence of reliance on the spurious fea-
089   tures we investigate for some other LLMs, but these LLMs may rely on other features we have not
090   considered.

      2.1   B ENCHMARK VALIDITY
095
096   Several factors contribute to the validity of measuring an AI system’s performance on a specific
097   benchmark. One key factor is “construct validity”, which refers to how well the benchmark actually
098   measures the construct that is intended to be measure. A prerequisite for construct validity is “in-
099   ternal validity”, which reflects the extent to which the observed outcomes can be directly attributed
100   to the variables or factors under investigation, without interference from confounding variables or
101   biases. Other forms of validity include ecological and external validity, which refer to the extent to
102   which the benchmark tasks reflect real-world tasks and how the measurement results generalise to
103   real-world performance (Davis, 2023; Ivanova, 2023; Elliot et al., 2024; Liao et al., 2021).
104   Several papers review issues related to benchmark validity. For instance, Liao et al. (2021) overview
105   problems with the benchmarking paradigm of supervised ML systems, while Bowman & Dahl
106   (2021) outline criteria NLP benchmarks should meet, some of which contribute to construct and
107   internal validity. Subramonian et al. (2023) instead conduct a survey of AI researchers to develop a
      taxonomy of validity issues. McIntosh et al. (2024) identify a set of inadequacies in LLM bench-


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      marks, some related to validity, while others are broader, such as security issues and the failure to
109   capture cultural diversity. In contrast, Biderman et al. (2024) focuses primarily on reproducibility
110   issues, which can also impact benchmark validity. Finally, Momennejad et al. (2023) and Rutar et al.
111   (2024) provide guidance on building evaluation frameworks to measure whether AI systems (LLMs
112   in the former case and embodied agents in the latter) possess capabilities that are robust to various
113   perturbations and experimental conditions, thereby ensuring both construct and internal validity.

116   2.2   S PURIOUS CORRELATIONS IN NLP BENCHMARKS
117
118   The presence of spurious correlations in NLP benchmarks has been demonstrated by several stud-
119   ies, with some showing that these correlations affect the performance of NLP models. For example,
120   Gururangan et al. (2018) identified individual words associated with specific class labels in NLP
121
      benchmarks and trained a model on truncated prompts achieving performance above chance, de-
      spite removing necessary elements for determining the correct answer. Moreover, they showed that
122
      models trained on such datasets performed better on instances that could be successfully classi-
123
      fied using the truncated prompts. Poliak et al. (2018), Si et al. (2019), Sugawara et al. (2020) and
124   Kavumba et al. (2019) performed similar experiments on different benchmarks and found compara-
125   ble results. Niven & Kao (2019) showed that individual unigrams and bigrams were related to label
126   values for specific benchmarks and crafted instances by adversarially manipulating the n-grams such
127   that NLP models achieved poor accuracy on them. For example, they showed that BERT was able to
128   exploit the presence of the unigram “not” and bigrams like “will not” to correctly answer instances
129   in the ARCT dataset (Habernal et al., 2018). In contrast, our approach identifies patterns involving
130   multiple unigrams and bigrams at the same time.
131   In the context of LLMs, Tu et al. (2020) found evidence that pre-trained models finetuned on datasets
132   with spurious correlations can learn to exploit these correlations. Similarly, Du et al. (2023) argued
133   that the supervised fine-tuning step in the LLM development pipeline makes them vulnerable to ex-
134   ploiting spurious correlations. Kavumba et al. (2022) conducted experiments where they shuffled
135   the words in the possible choices and truncated the prompts, following the approach of Gururan-
136   gan et al. (2018), to demonstrate that LLMs exploit superficial cues; for instance, they found that
137   RoBERTa exploits superficial cues like the word “not”. Instead, Yan et al. (2024) shows how LLMs
138
      can learn to rely on spurious features present in task examples included in the context window as
      part of few-shot prompting. In contrast, we do not use few-shot examples, thus studying spurious
139
      correlations LLMs learnt during training.
140
141   Finally, more advanced methods for identifying spurious features have been proposed. Friedman
142   et al. (2022) used grammar induction techniques to define features as sub-trees within a probabilis-
143   tic grammar. Similarly, Gardner et al. (2021) introduced a statistical test to determine whether a
144
      spurious correlation arises from bias in the data generation process or is instead randomly intro-
      duced. One limitation of this approach however is that it only considers the correlation between
145
      individual features and the labels.
146
147   Here, we identify spurious correlations by training simple logistic regression classifiers to predict
148   labels using only unigrams and bigrams extracted from the prompts. This approach allows us to
149   explore whether combinations of these features are predictive of the correct response labels (e.g.,
150
      “true” vs. “false”) while remaining computationally lightweight, meaning that the approach is scal-
      able to large datasets (unlike, for example, grammar induction techniques).
151

154   3     M ETHODS
155
156   We aim to investigate (1) the extent to which we can predict labels on multiple-choice LLM bench-
157   marks using combinations of simple unigrams and bigrams, and (2) whether LLMs rely on these fea-
158   tures to succeed in these tasks. To this end, we evaluate the predictive power of different classifiers
159   across benchmark datasets to correctly classify labels based on these n-gram features. Furthermore,
160   we analyse the performance of LLMs on dataset instances that were successfully and unsuccess-
161   fully predicted by the n-gram models to determine if LLMs rely on these n-gram patterns to solve
      benchmark tasks.

      3.2   F EATURE E XTRACTION
202
203   We considered thirteen different feature vectors derived from the prompts of each benchmark. To
204   generate these features, we extracted unigrams and bigrams at both the word level and the token
205   level; for token-level features, we used the GPT-2 tokenizer to capture subword information. For
206   each set of n-grams, we computed three types of feature representations:
207
208         1. Term Frequency (TF): The raw count of each n-gram in the prompt.
209         2. Term Frequency-Inverse Document Frequency (TF-IDF): A weight reflecting how im-
210            portant an n-gram is to a prompt in the context of the entire dataset.
211         3. Presence: A binary indicator where each feature is 1 if the n-gram occurs in the prompt
212            and 0 otherwise.
213
214   This process resulted in twelve feature vectors: (unigrams, unigrams + bigrams) × (word level, to-
215   ken level) × (TF, TF-IDF, Presence). Additionally, we extracted a Readability and Diversity Metrics
      vector, which includes textual features such as the Flesch Reading Ease Score, Gunning Fog Index,

      SMOG Index, and Yule’s K adapted from Moros Daval (2023). In total, we obtained thirteen feature
217   vectors for our analysis.

      3.3   P ERFORMANCE E VALUATION
220
221
      To assess the performance of our models, we used Cohen’s κ coefficient (McHugh, 2012), a statistic
222   that measures the agreement between two raters (or, in our case, between the model’s predictions and
223   the true labels) while accounting for the agreement occurring by chance. This metric is particularly
224   suitable in our context as the datasets differ in the number of possible labels (see Table 1), leading
225   to varying values of chance level performance. Cohen’s κ is calculated as:
226
227                                                      Po − Pe
228                                                κ=
                                                         1 − Pe
229
230   where Po is the observed accuracy (the proportion of correctly predicted labels), and Pe is the
231   expected accuracy if predictions were made purely by random guessing. The value of Cohen’s κ
232   ranges from −1 to 1, where a value of 1 indicates that the model perfectly predicts the labels (well
233   beyond chance level), a value of 0 implies that the model’s performance is no better than random
234   guessing, and negative values suggest systematic disagreement between the model’s predictions and
235   the true labels.
236
237   3.4   E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
238
239   For each dataset, we partitioned the data into training, validation, and test splits. To explore whether
240   n-grams are predictive of labels, we trained logistic regression classifiers on the training split using
241   each of the thirteen feature vectors discussed in Section 3.2. We tested both L2 regularization (with
242
      regulation weight λ = 1) and L1 regularization (with λ = 1 and λ = 10). For each dataset and
      feature vector, we selected the hyperparameters that achieved the highest accuracy on the validation
243
      split, and we report the corresponding results on the test split (see Section 4.1).
244
245   Additionally, to investigate whether LLMs are relying on n-gram patterns identified by the logistic
246   regression classifiers, we further stratified the test split into two subsets: instances whose labels were
247   successfully predicted by the classifier using the best-performing features on the validation split, and
248
      those whose labels were not successfully predicted. We then analyse the performance of the LLMs
      on these two subsets across the various datasets, as detailed in Section 4.2.
249
250   In the following section, we present the results of our experiments and discuss the implications of
251   our findings.

      5     D ISCUSSION
433
434
      In this series of experiments, we demonstrate that simple unigram and bigram features extracted
435   from benchmark prompts can be used to predict the correct answers for several multiple-choice
436   LLM benchmarks. Furthermore, we provide evidence suggesting that at least some LLM families
437   may be relying on these features to solve these benchmarks.
438
439
      5.1   C ONTROLLING FOR C LEVER H ANS
440
441   Confounding factors are an issue in all forms of capability evaluation, potentially leading to mis-
442   leading conclusions based on performance data. These confounds can be related to characteristics of
443   the test participants—for example, older people tend to have encountered more words and thus have
444   higher vocabulary, meaning that IQ tests premised on word-meaning knowledge will consistently
445   attribute higher IQ to older participants (Verhaeghen, 2003))—or they can be related to features of
446   the task itself. Within human behavioural research, it is common wisdom that such confounds are
447
      inevitable, and considerable effort goes into identifying, eliminating and controlling for them. In ani-
      mal research, experimenters will often wear opaque goggles (Tsukasa et al., 2012; Mercado III et al.,
448
      2000) or use pre-recorded voice commands (Gábor et al., 2022) to prevent from giving inadvertent
449
      clues, while medical research is routinely conducted “double-blind” such that the experimenter does
450   not know—and therefore cannot reveal—the ground truth of the experimental manipulation (Fried-
451   man et al., 2010). A fundamental in the avoidance of task-related confounders is the randomisation,
452   variation and counterbalancing of stimuli.
453
      In AI evaluation, it has not been standard practice to comprehensively vary and counterbalance
454
      stimuli within benchmarks, with examples of this approach only beginning to emerge in recent years
455
      (Rutar et al., 2024; Momennejad et al., 2023; Mizrahi et al., 2024; Wang & Zhao, 2024; Cao et al.,
456   2024). The consequences of omitting this methodological practice have become evident through
457   the results of numerous studies of RL agents and NLP systems performance (Krakovna et al., 2020;
458   Gururangan et al., 2018; Poliak et al., 2018; Si et al., 2019; Sugawara et al., 2020; Kavumba et al.,
459   2019; Niven & Kao, 2019). In both cases, systems have been shown to pick up simple surface-level
460   cues that are predictive of the correct answers, allowing them to achieve high performance without
461   using the information or capability putatively at test. Given the power and generality of modern large
462   language models, it is reasonable to expect that these systems may have an even greater capacity to
463   take advantage of such cues. This indeed has shown to be the case for some specific examples (e.g.
464
      Tu et al., 2020; Kavumba et al., 2022; Du et al., 2023).
465   In this paper, we take a broader approach to investigate whether a combination of such simple cues
466   (in particular, unigrams and bigrams) can in principle be used to solve a wide selection of current
467   LLM benchmarks: in practice, we test whether the ground truth answer labels can be predicted
468   from these simple features of the prompt. We found that for a large number of benchmarks, simple
469   classifiers were able to predict the ground truth with good accuracy.
470   In a fully controlled benchmark, it should not be possible to predict the correct answer from single
471   or two-word or token features of the prompt. Agreement between the predicted label and the ground
472   truth, as measured by Cohen’s κ (McHugh, 2012), should be close to zero. Traditionally, any value
473   above 0.2 is taken to indicate a small but detectable agreement, anything above 0.4 fair-moderate
474   and values above 0.6 considerable agreement (Landis, 1977). In our analysis, nearly half (9/19)
475   of the benchmarks showed some evidence of agreement between predicted labels based on simple
476
      features, and the ground truth. Of these, most showed fair to moderate agreement, with some no-
      table examples (e.g. Corporate Lobbying and SpaceNLI) showing consistent κ values of above 0.6.,
477
      indicating moderate to strong agreement.
478
479   In practice, researchers can repeat the analysis we carry out in this work during the dataset creation
480   process; if they find significant predictive power with n-grams, they should identify which n-grams
481   are most predictive (e.g., by examining coefficients in the logistic regression models) and introduce
482
      variations ensuring that ground truth labels are not correlated with those n-grams.
483   Our results results highlight a broader issue of bias in benchmark questions that allows for the use
484   of alternative strategies to solve benchmark tasks: we focused on a relatively narrow set of simple
485   cues (unigrams and bigrams), which is by no means a comprehensive overview of all the possible
      surface cues that could be used. Even if these specific cues are not present in a benchmark, others


                                                         9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      may be present and possibly leveraged by tested LLMs. Overall, this suggests that care should be
487   taken when designing a benchmark; for more information on how to do this, we refer to Rutar et al.
488   (2024); Momennejad et al. (2023).
489
490
491   5.2   A RE LLM S USING SURFACE - LEVEL CUES TO SOLVE BENCHMARKS ?
492
493   Our findings suggest that a system capable of recognising statistical regularities can achieve above-
494
      chance performance by relying solely on simple surface-level cues. This implies that LLMs may
      be exploiting these cues to pass benchmarks without demonstrating the intended capability. To in-
495
      vestigate whether this is the case, we divided each benchmark into two subsets: instances where the
496
      classifiers predicted the correct answer and instances where they did not. By comparing LLM per-
497   formance across these two groups, we aimed to investigate whether LLM scores were consistently
498   higher for instances successfully predicted by the classifiers, suggesting that these LLMs might be
499   using identified uni/bi-grams to solve benchmark tasks.
500
      We observe that for certain model families, most notably those produced by OpenAI, Meta, and
501
      Mistral AI, there is a small but notable performance advantage in the predictable instances. Specif-
502
      ically, these model families perform better on instances where the classifier was able to predict the
503   correct answer using unigram and bigram features. The fact that the effect is subtle suggests that
504   these models are not relying solely on these surface cues, which is expected: even if the systems
505   were entirely dependent on superficial patterns, it is unlikely they would restrict themselves to only
506   cues from unigram and bigrams. Thus, the absence of evidence for reliance on n-grams in some
507   model families does not imply that these models are not relying on any spurious features. Further-
508   more, many LLM families included only two models tested on just five benchmarks, making it more
509   challenging to detect an effect of relying on superficial cues compared to, for example, the OpenAI
510   family, which consisted of 18 models tested on at least 12 datasets (see Tables 3 and 4).
511   Conversely, the evidence we provide is not conclusive in determining that those LLM fami-
512   lies rely on n-grams to succeed on the benchmarks considered. To do so, careful experimental
513   manipulation—similar to those conducted in Gururangan et al. (2018); Niven & Kao (2019) and
514   Kavumba et al. (2022)—would be necessary. Lacking these, confounding factors could explain our
515   findings; for instance, it is possible that the instances successfully predicted by the n-gram models
516   are coincidentally also easier, meaning that LLMs exhibiting the capability tested are more likely
517
      to succeed on those instances. While we do not believe this to be the case (indeed, as shown in
      Section 4.2, only a subset of LLMs shows a detectable pattern), our findings warrant further experi-
518
      mental manipulation.
519
520   In this work, we did not explore how LLMs may learn the statistical regularities present in bench-
521   marks. Unlike traditional NLP models, LLMs are pre-trained and can be applied to different bench-
522   marks out of the box (although techniques such as fine-tuning and few-shot prompting (Brown et al.,
523
      2020) can be applied). However, benchmark contamination, where an LLM is perhaps inadvertently
      trained on benchmark instances, is a widely-known issue (Achiam et al., 2023; Roberts et al., 2023;
524
      Jiang et al., 2024). Studies have also shown that fine-tuning LLMs on samples similar, but not
525
      identical, to benchmark tasks can improve their performance (Dominguez-Olmedo et al., 2024).
526   Furthermore, the development pipeline for LLMs often involves supervised fine-tuning steps, which
527   helps enhance their performance in question-answer settings. It has been suggested that this step
528   may allow LLMs to learn to exploit spurious correlations (Du et al., 2023). We view the empirical
529   analysis of existing LLMs to understand whether they rely on spurious features as complementary
530   to the study of how they may learn to exploit such features. In particular, studying available LLMs
531   may shed light on proprietary systems, for which analysing the development pipeline is not feasible.
532
533
534   6     C ONCLUSION
535
536   We showed ways in which benchmarks could be solved without needing the assessed capability
537   and provided evidence that LLMs may be using these surface cues to solve them. Future work
538   should focus on identifying other types of cues that LLMs might be exploiting to solve benchmarks
539   without demonstrating the intended capabilities being tested and extend our approach to open-ended
      question-answer datasets.
