009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            Customized image generation is crucial for delivering personalized content based
012            on user-provided image prompts, aligning large-scale text-to-image diffusion
013            models with individual needs. However, existing models often overlook the re-
014            lationships between customized objects in generated images. Instead, this work
015            addresses that gap by focusing on relation-aware customized image generation,
016            which aims to preserve the identities from image prompts while maintaining the
017            predicate relations described in text prompts. Specifically, we introduce Rela-
018
               tionBooth, a framework that disentangles identity and relation learning through
               a well-curated dataset. Our training data consists of relation-specific images,
019
               independent object images containing identity information, and text prompts to
020
               guide relation generation. Then, we propose two key modules to tackle the two
021            main challenges—generating accurate and natural relations, especially when sig-
022            nificant pose adjustments are required, and avoiding object confusion in cases of
023            overlap. First, we introduce a keypoint matching loss that effectively guides the
024            model in adjusting object poses closely tied to their relationships. Second, we
025            incorporate local features from the image prompts to better distinguish between
026            objects, preventing confusion in overlapping cases. Extensive results on three
027            benchmarks demonstrate the superiority of RelationBooth in generating precise
028            relations while preserving object identities across a diverse set of objects and re-
029
               lations. The source code and trained models will be made available to the public.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
033
      Driven by large-scale text-to-image diffusion models (Rombach et al., 2022; Saharia et al., 2022;
034
      Podell et al., 2023; Pernias et al., 2023), customized image generation has recently made significant
035
      strides (Ruiz et al., 2023; Kumari et al., 2023; Wei et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024;
036   Kong et al., 2024). This task focuses on generating images that preserve the identity of objects from
037   user-provided inputs, enabling the creation of personalized and meaningful content. It has shown
038   value in numerous applications, including personalized artwork, branding, virtual fashion try-ons,
039   social media content creation, augmented reality experiences, and more.
040
      Despite the success of many methods for customizing single or multiple objects (Liu et al., 2023;
041
      Ye et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023; Patel et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024; Gu et al., 2024; Lin et al., 2024;
042   Pang et al., 2024), they often overlook the relationships between objects and the corresponding text
043   prompts. For instance, when two user-provided objects are paired with a text prompt specifying
044   a particular relationship, the generated output should not only preserve their identities but also ac-
045   curately reflect the intended relationship, such as a ‘hug’. This introduces new challenges in what
046   we refer to as relation-aware customized image generation, which focuses on preserving multiple
047   identities while adhering to relationship prompts.
048   An intuitive solution to this issue is to adapt existing tuning-based or training-based methods for
049   customizing relationships (Huang et al., 2023; mengmeng Ge et al., 2024; Materzynska et al., 2023)
050   among objects. However, both approaches face challenges. Tuning-based methods struggle to pre-
051   serve multiple identities, as they invert objects into specific tokens, while training-based schemes
052   often fail to balance image and text prompts, frequently overlooking key textual elements and hin-
053   dering the generation of relationships between objects. As illustrated in Fig. 1, previous methods
      fail to capture the actions described in the text prompt and lose identity preservation.

      We attribute this failure to two key factors: a lack of relevant data and an ineffective model design.
      Unlike data augmentation techniques such as flipping or rotation, commonly used to create paired
079
      training data in object customization methods (Yang et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024b), our approach
080
      requires a triplet of images: two image prompts and one target image. The image prompts should
081   contain similar objects but exhibit distinct actions compared to the target image. To collect these
082   triplets, we propose a data engine to curate our fine-tuning set. We leverage an advanced text-to-
083   image generation model (Betker et al., 2023) to generate triplets where the same object pair is shared
084   across the images. Through text prompt guidance, the object in the image prompt provides strong
085   identity information while maintaining distinct actions in the target image, enabling the model to
086   focus on relation learning during fine-tuning.
087   For the model design, we propose RelationBooth, which applies the Low-Rank Adaptation
088   (LoRA) (Hu et al., 2022) strategy to the text cross-attention layers of existing diffusion models
089   to process user-provided text prompts. In RelationBooth, two key modules are introduced during
090   training to enhance the customization of relationships and identity preservation. First, we intro-
091   duce a keypoint matching loss (KML) as additional supervision to explicitly encourage the model
092   to adjust object poses, since relationships between objects are closely tied to their poses. Impor-
093   tantly, the KML operates on the latent representation rather than the original image space, aligning
094   with the default diffusion loss. Second, we inject local tokens for multiple objects to improve the
095
      distinctiveness of highly overlapping objects. Specifically, we employ the self-distillation method
      from CLIPSelf (Wu et al., 2024) to enhance region-language alignment in CLIP’s dense features.
096
      Through partitioning and pooling, the well-aligned local tokens help mitigate appearance confusion
097
      between objects.
098
099   To more comprehensively evaluate relation-aware customized image generation, we constructed our
100   RelationBench based on three established benchmarks (Ruiz et al., 2023; Kumari et al., 2023). Our
101
      methods demonstrate strong performance compared to existing approaches, achieving significant
      improvements in visual quality and quantitative results.
102

107            ified in text prompts. This task can enhance various user-driven applications by enabling
               more control and customization.

      2   R ELATED W ORK
121
122   Diffusion-Based Text-to-Image Generation. This direction aims to produce high-quality images
123   based on user-provided textual prompts. Diffusion-based generation models (Rombach et al., 2022;
124   Saharia et al., 2022) encode these prompts through the text model (Radford et al., 2021; Chung et al.,
125   2022) and inject the text embedding into the U-Net’s cross-attention layers. Some methods (Podell
126   et al., 2023; Pernias et al., 2023; Ren et al., 2024) upscale diffusion models and incorporate addi-
127   tional conditions as priors to generate high-resolution images. Meanwhile, several methods (Betker
128   et al., 2023; Lian et al., 2023) introduce stronger text encoders or large language models (LLMs)
129   to enhance the textual comprehension capabilities of diffusion models. Recent works (Peebles &
130
      Xie, 2023; Chen et al., 2024a) have replaced the U-Net denoiser with a Transformer-based denoiser.
      However, these models primarily focus on textual conditions and struggle to handle other forms of
131
      input, such as user-provided images. In contrast, our work facilitates customized generation while
132
      following predicate relation conditions given by text inputs.
133
134   Diffusion-Based Customized Generation. Customized image generation aims to produce diverse
135   images based on user-provided concepts. Tuning-based methods accomplish this by fine-tuning
136
      specific parameters of diffusion models, thereby incorporating new concepts into text-to-image dif-
      fusion models like Stable Diffusion. Some of these methods (Gal et al., 2023; Voynov et al., 2023;
137
      Liu et al., 2023) employ text embeddings to represent customized concepts, which are then injected
138
      into prompts during inference. On the other hand, Dreambooth (Ruiz et al., 2023) fine-tunes the en-
139   tire U-Net and introduces a prior preservation loss to mitigate language drift. Additionally, several
140   approaches (Kumari et al., 2023; Gu et al., 2024; Lin et al., 2024; Pang et al., 2024) combine text
141   embeddings and diffusion models for image synthesis. They fine-tune both the text embedding and
142   the U-Net, yielding impressive results. Considering the substantial cost of fine-tuning for commer-
143   cial uses, training-based methods (Li et al., 2023; Wei et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024b; Patel et al.,
144   2024; Zhang et al., 2024) have been proposed. This approach primarily utilizes an ID encoder to
145   extract the concept’s identity and inject it into the U-Net via cross-attention layers. For instance,
146   MS-Diffusion (Wang et al., 2024) integrates grounding tokens with a feature resampler to maintain
147
      detail fidelity and employs layout guidance to explicitly place the concepts. Despite their efficiency
      during inference, training-based methods often struggle with identity preservation and textual condi-
148
      tion following ability. A typical problem is the relation expressions in prompts are often overlooked.
149
      To address this issue, we present RealtionBooth, to tackle relation-aware customized generation.
150
151   Relation-Aware Text-to-Image Generation. Inspired by tuning-based customization methods (Gal
152   et al., 2023), some recent works (Huang et al., 2023; Wei et al., 2024; mengmeng Ge et al., 2024)
153
      represent a “neglected word” by learnable parameters. These methods fine-tune part of the param-
      eters on content co-existing images. For instance, Reversion (Huang et al., 2023) fine-tunes the
154
      text embedding on a set of co-existing related images and introduces a relation-steering contrastive
155
      loss. Reversion gains a better alignment between relational words and generated images by injecting
156   new text embedding into prompts. While effective with prepositions and adjectives, Reversion strug-
157   gles with predicate relation which involves significant overlaps, and cannot customize user-provided
158   concepts. Moreover, this method still struggles to generate vivid relationships while maintaining the
159   fidelity of multiple customized concepts, likely due to the inherent disregard for text embeddings in
160   customization methods. Our work bridges the gap in the predicate relation-following capability of
161   training-based customization methods. This enables efficient inference that naturally and accurately
      generates predicate relations from the textual prompt.

      3.3     DATA C OLLECTION
244
      Considering the scarcity of available datasets that can be used, we first design a data engine for
245   collecting high-quality tuning data. We denote the relation-specific image as x. While x contains
246   relation information, it also includes the object’s identities. Directly fine-tuning on a set of x leads
247   to unsatisfied relation generation. To disentangle the relation and identity in x. The ideal tuning data
248   should be in the form of D = (x, ci , ct ). Where ci represents the image prompts that contain the
249   object in x, ct represents the text prompt that guides the relation generation. Unfortunately, directly
250   cropping the objects from x to obtain ci results in a copy-and-paste effect, even when using data
251   augmentation techniques like flipping and rotation. Therefore, we introduce our data engine below.
252   Data engine. Fig. 3 illustrates our data generation process. Inspired by the capability of DALL-E 3
253   and its multi-turn dialog capability. We use DALL-E 3 to generate D = (x, ci , ct ). Where x and ci
254   share the same object identity by leveraging the prompt “The photo of the same.” We observe that
255   prompting in this manner allows DALL-E 3 to remember and preserve identity in common categories
256   such as “tiger” and “brown bear” which are enough for relation learning. Next, we employ X-Pose
257   (Yang et al., 2024), SAM (Kirillov et al., 2023), and LLaVA (Liu et al., 2024) to annotate x, ci with
258   keypoints, masks, and captions.

306   3.4.3   L OCAL T OKEN I NJECTION
307
308   When generating relations, overlapping objects often occur. It is challenging to distinguish multiple
309   objects solely using CLIP’s image-level embedding of the full image, which can lead to confusion
310
      between customized objects during relation generation. To address this limitation, we introduce a
      local region representation of images. Considering the domain shift from the full image to local
311
      image regions, we employ CLIPSelf’s self-distillation methods (Wu et al., 2024) to enhance the
312
      region-language alignment of local region representation. Specifically, in line with CLIPSelf, we
313   extract dense features (Zhou et al., 2022) from a student CLIP model and derive local tokens through
314   partitioning and pooling (He et al., 2017). We partition the full image into patches and feed them
315   into a frozen CLIP model to obtain teacher tokens. Our approach minimizes the cosine similarity
316   between local tokens and teacher tokens to improve the region-language alignment of local tokens.
317   To formulate this process, we denote the hidden states of the second-to-last layer of CLIP as h′clip .
318   We modify the last layer of CLIP hclip = ModifiedAttention(hclip ) to obtain the dense features:

359
360   Implementation Details. For relation-aware fine-tuning, we generate a tuning set with 4-6 samples
361   per relation. We incorporate LoRA layers into all text cross-attention layers of the U-Net. We set the
362   LoRA rank to r = 4, the parallel cross-attention scaling factor to γ = 0.6, and the keypoint matching
363
      loss weight to λ = 1e − 3. The model is fine-tuned for 500 steps, using 2 A100 GPUs, with a total
      batch size of 8, completing the process in 10 minutes. We use the Adam optimizer with a learning
364
      rate of 1e − 4 and no weight decay, resulting in a total of 3.1M trainable parameters. Note that our
365
      RelationLoRA only modifies the text cross-attention layers in SDXL, making it compatible with any
366   SDXL-based models. Considering the strong identity preservation capabilities of MS-Diffusion, we
367   implement our RelationBooth on MS-Diffusion. During fine-tuning and inference, we concatenate
368   local tokens with image tokens. We put more self-distillation details of the Local Image Encoder in
369   appendix (A.1). Additionally, as shown in appendix (Fig. 15), our RelationLoRA is compatible with
370   SDXL for addressing the Relation Inversion task (Huang et al., 2023).

531
532
      In this work, we introduce a challenging task: relation-aware customized image generation, which
533
      aims to generate objects that adhere to the relations specified in the text prompt while preserving
534   the identities of user-provided images. To support this, we propose a data engine for generating
535   high-quality fine-tuning data. Our method, RelationBooth, incorporating Keypoint Matching Loss
536   and Local Token Injection, effectively captures relation information and generates natural relations
537   between customized objects while mitigating object confusion. We also provide a fair compari-
538   son with other methods on a new benchmark, RelationBench. The extensive experiments on three
539   benchmarks demonstrate the effectiveness of our method, highlighting its potential for interactive
      scenario generation, relation detection, etc.

714   features, we employ self-distillation on CLIP-ViT-bigG. The training is conducted on the train2017
715   split of the COCO dataset for six epochs, using 8 A100 GPUs with a batch size of 2 per GPU. We
716   apply the Adam optimizer with a learning rate of 1e − 5 and a weight decay of 0.1. The Local Image
717   Encoder, containing 1.8B parameters, extracts local tokens that are concatenated with image tokens
718   during fine-tuning and inference to improve the alignment of object appearance. This distillation
719   process enhances the performance of our base model, MS-Diffusion, in multi-object generation

724   tations and checkpoints for training-based methods, adjusting hyperparameters as needed during
725   evaluation. Specifically, we set the scale to 0.6 in MS-Diffusion and sample 30 steps using the Eu-
726
      lerDiscreteScheduler. For the SSR Encoder, we employ the UniPCMultistepScheduler, sampling

987    Positive societal impacts. Relation-aware image customization enables users to generate images
988
       that not only contain customized objects but also capture their meaningful relationships. This opens
       up new opportunities for creative professionals, such as designers, advertisers, and educators, to
989
       communicate complex ideas visually with greater precision and flexibility. It has the potential to
990
       streamline content creation in diverse fields, from personalized marketing to educational tools, mak-
991    ing high-quality, contextually rich imagery accessible without the need for extensive resources.
992
993    Potential negative societal impacts. The ability to generate customized images that involve specific
994
       relationships between objects could be misused to fabricate misleading or harmful visual narratives,
       including false representations of events or manipulative visual content in political or social contexts.
995
       Additionally, if the models are trained on biased data, they may reinforce existing societal biases,
996
       marginalizing certain groups or distorting the accuracy of represented relationships.
997
998    Mitigation strategies. To reduce misuse, ethical guidelines should be established to govern the re-
999    sponsible development and application of this technology. Promoting transparency about generated
1000
       content and integrating fairness and diversity considerations into dataset selection are key strategies
       for mitigating potential harms.
