011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Dual encoder (DE) models, where a pair of matching query and document are
014            embedded into similar vector representations, are widely used in information re-
015            trieval due to their efficiency and scalability. However, DEs are known to have a
016
               limited expressive power due to the Euclidean geometry of the embedding space,
               which may compromise their quality. This paper investigate such limitations in
017
               the context of hierarchical retrieval, the task where the document set has a hier-
018
               archical structure and the matching documents for a query are all of its ancestor
019            nodes. We first prove the feasibility of representing hierarchical structures within
020            the Euclidean embedding space by providing a constructive algorithm for gener-
021            ating effective embeddings from a given hierarchy. Then we study the problem of
022            learning such embeddings when the hierarchy is unknown, which is often the case
023            in practice where only samples of matching query and document pairs are avail-
024            able during training. Our experiments reveal a “lost in the long distance” phe-
025            nomenon, where retrieval accuracy degrades for documents further away in the
026            hierarchy. To address this, we introduce a pretrain-finetune approach that signifi-
027
               cantly improves long-distance retrieval without sacrificing performance on closer
               documents. Finally, we validate our findings on a realistic hierarchy from Word-
028
               Net, demonstrating the effectiveness of our approach in retrieving documents at
029
               various levels of abstraction.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Information retrieval (Mitra et al., 2018) systems aim to find the most relevant documents within
035   a large database in response to a user query. A common example is an internet search engine that,
036   for instance, sifts through all available products and returns those that are the most relevant to a
037   customer’s search terms. Dual encoder (DE) is one of the most commonly used models for infor-
038   mation retrieval due to their simplicity and scalability. DEs function by encoding both queries and
039   documents by vector representations, often using deep neural networks (Guo et al., 2016; Chang
040   et al., 2020; Zhao et al., 2024). Then, similarity between a query and a document is calculated using
041   their Euclidean distance or inner product, enabling scalable retrieval through approximate nearest
042
      neighbor search (Johnson et al., 2019; Guo et al., 2020; Chern et al., 2022).
043   This paper focuses on a specific case of information retrieval where the underlying database exhibits
044   a hierarchical structure. In particular, we consider databases with an organization that can be rep-
045   resented as a directed acyclic graph (DAG), where each node corresponds to a document and each
046   edge represents a directional relationship between a pair of documents (see Figure 1). This hierar-
047   chical organization, prevalent in many real-world systems (Ravasz & Barabási, 2003; Adcock et al.,
048
      2013), leads to the challenge of hierarchical retrieval. In this context, relevant responses to a query
      include not only the “same meaning” document but also its ancestors in the hierarchy. For instance,
049
      consider the task of keyword targeting where advertisers build a keyword list for their products so
050
      that their ads surface if a user query is relevant to keywords in the list. A particular way of doing
051   this1 is that an advertiser puts a general keyword, e.g., “Sport shoes”, which is to be retrieved not

081
      Contributions. This paper explores DEs for hierarchical retrieval, aiming at understanding their
082
      capabilities and limitations and developing practical algorithms for improving their quality.
083
084   Firstly, given the constraints of the Euclidean geometry and the asymmetric nature of hierachical
085   retrieval, it is unclear whether it is even possible to accurately represent a hierarchical similarity
086   structure via Euclidean embeddings. Moreover, even if such a representation does exist, a priori it
087
      may require very large dimensionality, possibly scaling with the number of points in the dataset. In
      Section 3, we resolve this uncertainty with an optimistic answer to the question. Specifically, we
088
      present a constructive algorithm which, given a hierarchical structure, generates a set of embeddings
089
      in RM which solve the hierarchical retrieval task on that structure. Moreover, the dimension of the
090   embeddings is at most O(h log n), where n is the number of points in the dataset and h is the depth
091   of the hierarchy.
092
093
      The constructive algorithm in Section 3 requires precise knowledge of the document hierarchy. In
      practice, this hierarchy is often unknown and the embeddings need to be learned from a training
094
      dataset composed of matching query and document pairs. Hence, it is unclear if learning on such
095
      data is able to find good representations even though they exist. Section 4 explores this learning
096   process by experimenting with a synthetic tree-structured hierarchy. By controlled experiments
097   with varying the depth and width of the tree, we show that the learning process not only finds
098   good representations but can achieve this with dimensions much less than those required in the
099   constructive algorithm, for a wide range of hierarchies.
100
      Continuing on the study of learned embeddings, in Section 5 we provide a fine-grained analysis that
101
      reveals a “lost in the long distance” phenomenon, which severely compromise their quality. This
102   refers to the behavior that documents with longer distances from the query becomes increasingly
103   more difficult to retrieve. Towards mitigating the issue, we first demonstrate the inadequacy of
104   naively upsampling of such pairs, which exhibits a quality tradeoff between short distance and long
105   distance pairs. We then present a pretrain-finetune recipe, where a DE is first pretrained on a regular
106   data and then finetuned on a dataset focusing solely on pairs with queries and their long-distance
107   ancestors. Finally, this approach is validated in Section 6 on a realistic graph from the WordNet, a
      large lexical database of English. On this dataset, we show that given a specific query e.g. “cat”, the

114   Let Q = {qi }ni=1 and D = {dj }m     j=1 be a collection of n queries and m documents, respectively. For
115   each i ∈ {1, . . . , n}, let S(qi ) ⊆ D be a set that contains documents that are the most relevant to the
116   query qi . The goal of information retrieval is to return a ranked list of the documents in D for each
117   qi , with the top ones being those in S(qi ). In this section, we formally define hierarchical retrieval
118   as a particular case of this information retrieval problem. We then discuss dual encoders (DEs) and
119   set a roadmap on studying their effectiveness for hierarchical retrieval.
120
121
      2.1    H IERARCHICAL RETRIEVAL
122
123   In hierarchical retrieval, we assume that the document set D has a hierarchical structure described
124   by a directed acyclic graph (DAG), which recall is a directed graph with no directed cycles. Let
125   G = (V, E) be a DAG associated with the set of document D, where V = D, i.e. each vertex
126   corresponds to a document, and E = D × D are directional edges between pairs of documents. For
127   hierarchical retrieval we assume the following: if any document dj ∈ D is defined as relevant for a
128   given query qi , i.e., dj ∈ S(qi ), then any document dj 0 ∈ D for which there is a path in G that goes
129   from dj to dj 0 is also considered relevant to qi , i.e., dj 0 ∈ S(qi ).
130

237   4   L EARNING A D UAL E NCODER FOR H IERARCHICAL R ETRIEVAL
238
239   While the construction algorithm in Section 3 provides a solution that solves the hierarchical re-
240   trieval problem with provable guarantees, it requires knowing the hierarchical structure a priori.
241   In this section, we study embeddings learned from minimizing the objective in Eq. (2), aiming at
242   understanding if it is possible to learn such good embeddings in practice.
243
244   Experimental setup. Towards that, we consider a particular kind of hierarchical structures repre-
245   sented by perfect W -trees, where each node has W child nodes and all leaf nodes of the tree are at
246   the same level. A perfect W -tree is described by two parameters, namely W which we refer to as
247   the degree, and H which is the number of levels and we refer to as the height.
248   For the purpose of hierarchical retrieval, we take the query set Q and the document set D to both
249   be the set of nodes of a W -tree (excluding the root). For each query q ∈ Q, we assume that its
250   matching document set S(q) ⊆ D contains d = q, as well as all the ancestors of d. In other words,
251   the tree is interpreted as encoding a hierarchy of the documents with edges pointing from each child
252   node to its parent node.
253   We use the following procedure to sample training and evaluation datasets, unless specified other-
254   wise. First, a query is sampled by drawing a node with equal probabilities from all nodes of the tree.
255   Then, we sample a node with equal probabilities from the set of documents that match q. Training
256   is conducted by optimizing Eq. (2) with gradient descent. For evaluation we use recall@k, i.e., the
257   percentage of (q, d) pairs in the evaluation set for which d is one of the k documents that have the
258   largest inner product score with q, with k being the total number of relevant documents for q.
259
260   Learned vs constructed embeddings. Recall that Algorithm 1 provides a construction of query
261   and document embeddings which solve the hierarchical retrieval if the dimension of the embedding
262   space satisfies M = O(s log n). For a tree-structured hierarchy that we consider here, s and n in
263   this formula are given by s = H and n = O(W H−1 ). Hence a M = O(H 2 log W ) is needed
264   for successful retrieval by this construction. This section compares this with that of the learned
265   embeddings, by learning embeddings on trees with varying H and W .

296   5     I MPROVING H IERARCHICAL R ETRIEVAL VIA P RETRAIN -F INETUNE
297
298   Here we provide a fine-grained analysis of the embeddings learned on tree-structured hierarchies
299   that are considered in Section 4 and identify a “lost in the long distance” phenomenon. We then
300   present a recipe for addressing this problem which leads to better quality embeddings.
301
302   5.1   L OST IN THE LONG DISTANCE
303
304   We provide an analysis of the retrieval quality by evaluating for pairs at varying distances. Here, the
305   distance between a matching (q, d) pairs is defined as the difference between the level of the node q
306   and the level of the node d. For example, a distance of 0 means that q and d correspond to the same
307
      node, and a distance of 1 means that d corresponds to the parent node of q.
308   Towards that, we consider a tree of H = 4 and W = 5, and fix an embedding dimension of M = 3.
309   In this case, for a query at level 4, the matching document could be from levels 2, 3, or 4 which
310   correspond to a distance of 2, 1, and 0, respectively (recall that the root node does not correspond
311   to any query or document). Likewise, queries at level 3 have matching documents with distance
312   0 and 1, and queries at level 2 have matching documents of distance 0 only. To evaluate recall at
313
      different distances, we sample an evaluation dataset and split it to three parts, which contain pairs
      with distance 0, 1, and 2, respectively. We then calculate recall on these three parts separately
314
      and report the results in Figure 3a. It can be seen that the learned embeddings can almost retrieve
315
      perfectly for pairs with a distance 0. However, the embeddings do not work well for distance 1, and
316   perform poorly for distance 2. In other words, learned embeddings do not work well for pairs where
317   the document is distant away from the query.
318
319
      A straightforward idea for addressing this problem is to re-balance the training dataset, so that it
      contains more pairs of longer distances. To test this idea, we consider the following two sampling
320
      strategies:
321
322   • Regular sampling. This refers to the sampling procedure described in Section 4. With H = 4
323     and W = 5, it can be calculated that pairs with distances 0, 1, and 2 are sampled with probability
        38%, 35%, and 27%, respectively.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      • Heavy-Tail sampling. This refers to a sampling strategy where pairs with distances 0, 1, and 2 are
325     sampled with probability 0%, 50%, and 50%, respectively.
326
327   We then create a re-balanced dataset by mixing data from regular and heavy-tail sampling above
328   with a ratio of p : 1 − p. Figure 3b shows results with p = 0.03. It can be observed that recalls for
329
      pairs with distance 1 and 2 are significantly improved and reaches a level of beyond 80% towards
      the end of training. However, we also observe that this comes at the cost of a significantly reduced
330
      recall on pairs with distance 0. Finally, we tune p with varying values in {0.01, 0.1, 0.3} and report
331
      the results in Figure 6 (see Appendix). The results confirm that re-balancing training data cannot
332   effectively solve the issue of lost in long distance.
333
334
      5.2   A PRETRAIN - FINETUNE RECIPE
335
336   We present a pretrain-finetune recipe to address the issue of lost in the long distance. This recipe
337   simply requires first doing a pretraining on data from the regular sampling, then finetune the em-
338   beddings on the heavy-tail distribution using a much smaller learning rate (we used 0.05×). Here,
339   regular and heavy-tail distributions refer to those described in Section 5.1.
340   The results of this recipe is reported in Figure 3c, where the pretraining stage and the finetuning
341   stage take place in the first and second 10,000 training steps, respectively. We observe that in the
342   finetuning stage, the retrieval quality for pairs with distance 1 and 2 quickly picks up and reach a
343   level of close to 100%. After that, the model exhibits an overfitting since the quality for pairs with
344   distance 0 starts to decrease. This overfitting may be expected since the finetuning stage does not
345   have any training data with distance 0. In practice, one may apply an early stopping by monitoring
346   recall on a heldout set, which will enable us to obtain good quality embeddings for hierarchical
347
      retrieval. In particular, the peaking overall recall (i.e., averaged over pairs of all distances) reaches
      97% which is a huge improvement over using the default data sampling (which gives 66% recall)
348
      and over using re-balanced training data (which gives 70% at peak performance near 17000 steps).

351   6     E XPERIMENTS ON R EAL DATA
352
353   In this section, we demonstrate the effectiveness of the pretrain-finetune recipe in Section 5 on data
354   with a realistic hierarchical structure. Specifically, we use WordNet (Miller, 1995), a large lexical
355
      database of English where the nouns, verbs, adjectives, and adverbs are grouped into synsets that
      represent synonyms. The set of synsets is equipped a binary hypernym relation, e.g., “chair” is the
356
      hypernym of “armchair”. This relation may be described by a DAG with nodes corresponding to
357
      synsets and edges pointing from a synset to another one that is its hypernym.
358
359   For our experiments, we use the 82,115 noun synsets from WordNet. Then, the query and document
360   sets, i.e., Q and D, are taken to be those synsets. For each query q ∈ Q, the matching documents
361
      S(q) are synsets for which there is an edge that points from q to it in the graph of the transitive closure
      of the DAG associated with the hypernym relation. For example, the set of matching documents for
362
      the query “cat” includes “cat”, “feline”, “carnivore”, “placental”, etc. In practice, we make a slight
363
      modification to this definition by restricting to (q, d) pairs with a distance of at most 8, so that the
364   learning task is easier. Here, distance between two synsets is defined as the length of the shortest
365   path that connects them in the hypernym DAG.
366
367   Lost in the long distance. As a baseline, we assume that there is a regular sampling procedure
368   which we can use for generating matching pairs from WordNet. This procedure is described as
369   follows. First, a query q is sampled uniformly at random from all 82,115 synsets. Then, a document
370   is sampled uniformly at random from the set of all matching documents to q. In our experiment, we
371   sample 10M such pairs as the training dataset, and optimize the objective in Eq. (2) using SGD with
372   a learning rate of 0.5, momentum of 0.9, batch size of 4096, for 50k iterations (i.e., 20 epochs).
373   To evaluate the quality of the learned embeddings, we use a test set of size 10k generated in the same
374   way, and report recall@k as the quality metric, where k is the total number of relevant document for
375   a query. In particular, we report recall@k averaged over all samples in the test set, reported as the
376   column “Overall” in Table 1, and slices with different distances (i.e., 0, 1, ..., 8) between query and
377   document. Finally, we pick the checkpoint that achieves the best overall recall on a validation set of
      size 10k as the final output.
