009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Understanding the training dynamics of neural networks has gained much interest
012            in the scientific community. The dynamics of training over-parameterized models
013            is characterized by the lazy regime in which networks exhibit near-linear behavior
014            and minimal parameter changes. In addition, it has been argued that the Jaco-
015            bian of large neural models has a low-rank structure. In this paper, we focus
016            on the opportunities laid out by the combination of low-rankness and laziness of
017            large neural models. Specifically, we provide a scalable way to measure the ex-
018
               tent of laziness, evaluated via the rate of change of the model Jacobian, as well
               as a scalable method to verify low-rankness of the model Jacobian without stor-
019
               ing the entire Jacobian. Taking advantages of both laziness and low-rankness, we
020
               design a scalable training algorithm for over-parameterized models that performs
021            backpropagation-free gradient descend training. In particular, this algorithm is of
022            lower computation and storage requirements in cases of massive parameter shar-
023            ing, as is the case of many state-of-the-art neural architectures. Empirical results
024            confirm the scalability and effectiveness of our approach, opening new pathways
025            for exploring novel learning strategies in neural networks.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Understanding the training dynamics of neural networks is essential for uncovering how they operate
031   and learn. The empirical success of neural networks has far outpaced the theoretical understanding
032   of their underlying mechanisms, yet ongoing research aims to identify the factors that enable effec-
033
      tive learning. Recent studies have identified two distinct training regimes: lazy and active, as well
      as mixtures of the two. Each regime offers unique insights into how networks learn and adapt, with
034
      factors such as network width, initialization, and training duration playing key roles in shaping a
035
      model’s behavior (Chizat et al., 2019; Lee et al., 2019; Tu et al., 2024).
036
037   The lazy regime, in particular, has gained attention due to its simplified, linear dynamics, where
038   networks rely on a fixed, nearly constant kernel during training. This contrasts with the active
039
      regime, where the network significantly updates its internal representations and adapts features over
      time. Understanding when a network operates in the lazy regime offers valuable insights into how
040
      models can achieve learning with minimal parameter updates, while the active regime involves more
041
      flexible, feature-adapting behavior that may enhance learning capacity.
042
043   Although the lazy regime is theoretically appealing due to its linear dynamics and minimal parameter
044   changes, its practical application is constrained by the requirement for extremely wide networks.
045
      These large networks widths result in computational and storage challenges, particularly due to the
      Jacobian matrix, whose dimensions scale with both the number of samples n and the network width
046
      m. While the near-constancy of the Jacobian suggests that storing it could eliminate the need for
047
      recomputation during each step of backpropagation, this approach becomes impractical in large-
048   scale settings without leveraging the matrix’s low-rank structure. The high dimensionality of the
049   Jacobian makes direct storage infeasible unless we can effectively compress it, which would require
050   exploiting inherent low-rank properties. If we can leverage the low-rank nature of the Jacobian in
051   wide networks, it would enable scalable algorithms that preserve the benefits of linear dynamics
052   while significantly reducing computational overhead. To the best of our knowledge, the potential for
053   integrating low-rank structures within the lazy regime has not yet been explored. Such a combination
      could unlock of more efficient training methods.

054
      To address this challenge, we propose a novel framework that connects the low-rank structure of
055   the Jacobian with the lazy regime of neural networks. Our approach tackles the computational
056   inefficiencies of traditional methods by introducing efficient techniques for estimating the rank of
057   the Jacobian using a carefully selected reference set, and validating the laziness of a network by
058   calculating the rate of change of the Jacobian. Instead of storing and processing the full Jacobian
059   matrix, which is often infeasible for large networks, we exploit the low-rank property to significantly
060   reduce dimensionality. Specifically, we select a small reference set R of important weights and
061   compute only the sub-matrix of the Jacobian corresponding to this subset. By focusing on the most
062   significant eigenvectors of this sub-matrix, we approximate the full Jacobian’s rank without the need
063
      for costly storage or computation.
064   Building on these insights, we introduce a backpropagation-free learning algorithm grounded in
065   Neural Tangent Kernel (NTK) theory. This novel algorithm simplifies training by exploiting both
066   the low-rank structure and the lazy regime, enabling robust learning dynamics with reduced compu-
067   tational overhead. Our method is particularly well-suited for large parameter-sharing networks, such
068   as convolutional neural networks (CNNs) and transformers, as it scales efficiently while maintaining
069
      performance.
070   In summary, our contributions are threefold: (1) we introduce a reliable method for estimating the
071   Jacobian’s rank, (2) we provide a concrete approach to determining network laziness. (3) These
072   innovations allow us to construct a backpropagation-free learning algorithm that leverages the sta-
073   bility and efficiency of low-rank structures in the lazy regime, opening new pathways for scalable
074   and efficient machine learning models.

      2     P REVIOUS W ORK
132
      Neural Tangent Kernel. The remarkable empirical success of deep neural networks has driven
133
      extensive research to understand their underlying mechanisms. The neural tangent kernel (NTK),
134
      introduced in (Jacot et al., 2018), marks a key milestone, as it facilitates the use of well-developed
135   theoretical tools of kernels. Broadly, the NTK is defined as the inner product of the gradients
136   of a network’s output of f (w; x) with respect to its trainable parameters w: K(x, z)(w) :=
137   ∇w f (w; x)T ∇w f (w; z), for fixed inputs x, z ∈ Rdx .
138
      In the infinite width limit for certain architectures, the NTK remains constant during training, leading
139
      to linear dynamics, often referred to as ”lazy training” (Chizat et al., 2019; Lee et al., 2019), where
140
      minimal parameter changes keep the network near its linearization. Then the NTK at time t = 0 is
141   denoted KN T K (i, j) = ⟨[J0 ]i,:,: , [J0 ]j,:,: ⟩, as declared in section 1.1. This constancy of the NTK
142   allows for provable optimization guarantees. Specifically, studies have shown that gradient descent
143   converges to a global minimum at a linear rate in the NTK regime, even for over-parameterized
144   networks (Du et al., 2018; 2019; Arora et al., 2019). More recently, it has been demonstrated that
145   such over-parameterized neural networks achieve optimal classification power (Radhakrishnan et al.,
146   2022) and exhibit robustness to noise (Belkin et al., 2019). Our work aims to develop a practical
147   algorithm derived from NTK theory that maintains the equivalent mathematical guarantees provided
148   by these theoretical analyses.
149
150   Disentangling Different Training Regime. Further studies have explored the conditions under
151   which networks exhibit linear or nonlinear dynamics during training, influenced by network width
152
      and initialization. Wide networks are found to evolve into two distinct regimes: the ”lazy training”
      regime with near-linear dynamics and minimal NTK change or the ”active/feature training” regime
153
      where the NTK evolves in time and learns features.
154
155   Recent research has refined this dichotomy, emphasizing the interplay of network width, depth, ini-
156   tialization, and the learning task, offering frameworks to analyze complex neural network dynamics
157   across various architectures and conditions. Particularly, (Tu et al., 2024) offers a fine-grained analy-
158
      sis of linear neural networks, showing that different parts of a network can operate in distinct regimes
      simultaneously, suggesting a ”mixed” dynamics regime.
159
160   (Geiger et al., 2020) empirically demonstrates the transition between the NTK and mean-field
161   regimes, is governed by a scaling parameter of the last layer that varies with the square root of
      network width. (Liu et al., 2020) offers a novel viewpoint on the ”transition to linearity,” associating

163   ”lazy training” explanation, highlighting that constancy may not hold even when individual parame-

      Low-Rank Structure in Lazy Regime. (Oymak et al., 2019) empirically demonstrates that over-
173   parameterized neural networks exhibit a Jacobian with low-rank properties, characterized by a few
174   large singular values and many smaller ones. This low-rank structure defines a low-dimensional
175   ”information space,” where learning occurs rapidly since the majority of the label vector resides
176   in this space. Conversely, label noise tends to project onto the ”nuisance space,” corresponding to
177   smaller singular values, hindering optimization and generalization. While Oymak’s method provides
178   a solid framework for understanding the generalization capabilities of over-parameterized networks,
179   its scalability can be limited due to the computational overhead of singular value decomposition
180   (SVD) for large networks. Our method addresses this issue by offering a scalable approach that
181
      does not require storing the full Jacobian.
182   In a similar vein, recent works have leveraged low-rank structures to enhance neural network ef-
183   ficiency and reduce redundancy. For example, LoRA (Low-Rank Adaptation), introduced by (Hu
184   et al., 2021), decomposes weight updates during fine-tuning into low-rank matrices, significantly
185   reducing the number of trainable parameters without sacrificing performance. Inspired by LoRA,
186   (Hao et al., 2024) suggested that gradients can also be compressed into a low-rank subspace, and
187
      proposed using random projections to compress the gradients, further improving memory efficiency
      during training.
188
189   Recent studies have demonstrated that learning primarily occurs within a significantly low-
190   dimensional parameter subspace (Gur-Ari et al., 2018; Larsen et al., 2022), promoting a special
191   type of learning known as subspace learning, where model weights are optimized within this low-
192   rank subspace. This finding supports the growing body of work suggesting that gradient matrices
193
      are naturally low-rank during training (Zhao et al., 2022; Cosson et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2023).
      The low-rank property of gradients has been effectively applied to reduce communication costs in
194
      distributed training (Wang et al., 2018; Vogels et al., 2020), as well as to lower memory footprints
195
      during the training of large models (Gooneratne et al., 2020; Huang et al., 2023; Modoranu et al.,
196   2023).
197
198   Building on the ideas of gradient low-rank projection, GaLore (Zhao et al., 2024) introduces a gen-
199
      eralized framework for memory-efficient training by dynamically adjusting the rank of gradient
      representations, reducing memory overhead in optimizing large language models while preserv-
200
      ing full-parameter learning dynamics. A key contribution of GaLore is the theoretical proof that
201
      the gradient matrix becomes low-rank during training in reversible networks, which supports the
202   method’s efficiency. However, GaLore’s primary limitation is its reliance on reversible networks,
203   which induce the low-rank gradient structure by design. In contrast, our method does not impose
204   this architectural constraint. Instead, we leverage both the constancy and the low-rank structure
205   of the Jacobian during training in the lazy regime to eliminate redundant representations. This
206   allows us to develop a more versatile and robust framework for low-rank approximation, applica-
207   ble across a wider range of architectures, optimizing both computational efficiency and stability in
208   backpropagation-free training.
209
210   Backpropagation-free Methods Backpropagation is undoubtedly the main approach to train neu-
211   ral networks, yet it is often considered biologically implausible. Consequently, numerous alterna-
212   tives have been proposed from a biologically inspired perspective (Lillicrap et al., 2016; Liao et al.,
213   2016; Moskovitz et al., 2018; Nøkland, 2016; Kohan et al., 2018; Balduzzi et al., 2015; Choro-
214   manska et al., 2019). Other approaches avoid backpropagation by allowing training of all layers in
215   parallel via an information bottleneck criterion (Ma et al., 2020), or by using random weight updates
      that are approximately orthogonal to the gradient (Baydin et al., 2022; Silver et al., 2021).

      Closer to our work is Boopathy & Fiete (2022), which leverages NTK theory, relying on the fact
217   that biological neural networks are wide and shallow, compared to artificial neural networks. They
218   showed that under the NTK regime, there is an alignment between the correlation of the activations
219   and the dynamic of the weight changes, and propose simplified training algorithms utilizing this
220   alignment, that are theoretically equivalent to gradient descent under the NTK regime.
221
      From an algebraic perspective, (Radhakrishnan et al., 2024) introduces the Deep Neural Feature
222
      Ansatz, focusing on feature learning mechanisms in deep networks within the active regime. Em-
223   phasizing network structure over parameter updates, this backpropagation-free approach enhances
224   interpretability and efficiency, bridging classical kernel methods and modern neural networks. Our
225   work also comes from an algebraic perspective but operates in the lazy regime.
226
      Our work stands out in its novelty by being the first to leverage both NTK theory and the low-
227
      rank structure of the Jacobian in large neural networks. This dual focus enables us to provide a
228
      mathematically grounded and computationally efficient backpropagation-free training method that
229   operates within the lazy regime. Furthermore, unlike previous works, we extend beyond theoretical
230   contributions by offering practical tools to verify these key assumptions for specific models and
231   problems at any arbitrary model width. This makes our approach highly adaptable to a wide range
232   of real-world applications.

      3.3   E FFICIENT BACKPROPAGATION FREE ALGORITHM LEVERAGES THE ASSUMPTIONS
342
343   Our goal is to develop an efficient and theoretically sound backpropagation-free training algorithm
344   that exploits the laziness and low-rank assumptions of the Jacobian matrix.
345
346   Rationale of the Algorithm When the laziness assumption holds, the model’s Jacobian J remains
347   approximately constant during training. This allows us to precompute J once at initialization and
348   reuse it throughout the training process. However, for large-scale models— which is typically the
349   case since NTK theory requires wide networks— directly storing the full Jacobian of size n × m,
350   where m is the number of parameters and n is the number of data points, is impractical due to the
351   O(nm) storage requirement.

353   proximate the Jacobian using a small reference set of weights and leverage principal component
354   analysis (PCA) to reduce its dimensionality. Importantly, this is done without storing the full Jaco-
355   bian, making our method scalable to models of any arbitrary width.

407   Reduction of computation burden in LLMs, CNNs and RNNs While the running time analy-
408   sis of Algorithm 1 indicates a time complexity comparable to that of training multilayer perceptron
409   (MLP) networks, our method offers significant computational advantages in models with extensive
410   parameter sharing, such as transformers, convolutional neural networks (CNNs), and recurrent neu-
411   ral networks (RNNs). In these architectures, individual parameters are reused multiple times during
412   both the forward and backward passes. For instance, convolutional filters in CNNs are applied across
413   numerous spatial locations in an image, leading to computational costs proportional to the product
414   of the image’s width and height. Similarly, in transformers, attention mechanisms involve opera-
415
      tions whose computational complexity scales quadratically with the sequence length, as attention
      matrices are applied to each pair of elements in the sequence.
416
417   In standard backpropagation, the computational cost is influenced not only by the number of param-
418   eters but also by the number of times each parameter is applied during training, which is directly
419   related to the input size. This repeated application results in increased computational overhead, par-
420   ticularly in models handling large inputs or long sequences. For example, the computational com-
421   plexity per parameter in the attention layer of transformers is O(sequence length2 ), and in CNNs, it
422
      scales with the number of pixels in the feature maps.
423   Our Algorithm 1 mitigates this issue by decoupling the computational cost from the input size. Since
424   the Jacobian is precomputed and the parameter updates do not require backpropagation through the
425   network layers, the number of times a parameter is reused does not impact the overall computational
426   complexity. Consequently, in models with significant parameter sharing, our method can substan-
427   tially reduce training time compared to standard backpropagation.
428   By eliminating the dependence on input size in the computational cost, our algorithm becomes espe-
429   cially advantageous for training large language models (LLMs), CNNs processing high-resolution
430   images, and RNNs dealing with long sequences. This efficiency gain makes our approach not only
431   theoretically appealing but also practically beneficial for large-scale machine learning tasks where
      computational resources are a critical consideration.

471
472   In this experiment, we analyzed the relative change in the Jacobian by calculating the ratio between
473   the Hessian’s Operator Norm and the Jacobian’s Operator Norm, as we suggested in section 3.2.
474   The goal is to investigate how this relative change behaves as the width of the neural network in-
475   creases, and its effect on the model’s training dynamics.
476
      We conducted the experiment on a 2-layer linear network with increasing widths, ranging from 40K
477   to 400M parameters. Our results, presented in figure 3, illustrate a clear pattern: as the network
478   width increases, the relative change of the Jacobian decreases. Notably, when this relative change
479   falls below √1m , the network enters the ”lazy regime,” where parameter updates become minimal
480   during training, effectively preventing the network from encountering saddle points. This behavior
481   enabled our algorithm to stabilize and train efficiently, ultimately leading to strong performance,
482   with the model achieving an accuracy of approximately 87% on the MNIST dataset. These findings
483   align with the theoretical results in (Liu et al., 2020), which suggest that networks with larger widths
484   exhibit near-constant tangent kernels due to the small Hessian norm. The graph in figure 3 illustrates
485   the observed trend between 40K and 1M parameters, while the extended experiment covering widths
      up to 400M parameters is included in the appendix.

512   5   C ONCLUSIONS
513
514   We have introduced a novel approach that uniquely combines Neural Tangent Kernel (NTK) theory
515   with the low-rank structure of the Jacobian in large neural networks. This dual focus distinguishes
516   our work as the first to leverage these two aspects concurrently, enabling us to develop a mathemati-
517   cally grounded and computationally efficient backpropagation-free training method operating within
518
      the lazy regime. Beyond theoretical advancements, we have provided practical tools for verifying
      the key assumptions of laziness and low-rank Jacobian structures in specific models and problems,
519
      regardless of model width. This comprehensive approach enhances the adaptability of our method,
520
      making it applicable to a wide range of real-world applications.
521
522   By empirically verifying the laziness and low-rank assumptions, we open a new avenue for alter-
523   native training methods that circumvent the need for backpropagation. Our assumption verification
524
      methods equip practitioners with practical tools to assess the suitability of our algorithm for their
      specific models and datasets, ensuring its effectiveness across diverse settings.
525
526   The innovative impact of our work lies not only in the theoretical advancement but also in the poten-
527   tial for significant computational efficiency gains. In models with massive parameter-sharing—such
528   as convolutional neural networks and transformer architectures—our algorithm can potentially out-
529   perform standard backpropagation by significantly reducing computational overhead and training
530
      time.
531   Future research will focus on extending our approach to these types of models, where parameter-
532   sharing is prevalent. By adapting our algorithm to exploit the structural properties of such networks,
533   we aim to achieve even greater efficiency improvements, making large-scale training more feasi-
534   ble and accessible. Additionally, exploring techniques to enhance the low-rank approximation and
535   optimize the selection of the reference set could further improve performance.
536   In conclusion, our work provides a promising alternative to traditional backpropagation. By uniting
537   NTK theory with the low-rank Jacobian structure and offering tools for assumption verification,
538   we contribute to both the theoretical understanding and practical advancement of efficient neural
539   network training methods. This opens the door for future exploration and application in models
      where traditional training methods are computationally prohibitive.
