009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Traditional reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) relies heavily on
012            costly and time-consuming human-annotated datasets. Even Reinforcement Learn-
013            ing from AI Feedback (RLAIF), which trains a reward model using AI-generated
014            preference data before refining the language model through reinforcement learning,
015            remains expensive. These methods often necessitate either specialized reward
016            model designs or larger models (e.g., GPT-4) for external labeling. In this paper,
017            we introduce a dataset-free and annotation-free framework called Self-Alignment
018
               Optimization (SAO), which addresses the aforementioned issue by aligning the
               model with its own prompts and feedback as preferences. SAO begins with a
019
               chat-based model that engages in persona role-play to generate diverse prompts
020
               and responses, which are then self-evaluated and used for preference optimization.
021            Extensive experiments with two strong LLMs on several benchmarks demonstrate
022            the effectiveness of SAO. Specifically, on AlpacaEval 2.0, Gemma-2-9B-it-SAO
023            achieves a Length-Controlled Win Rate (LC) of 69.2% and win rate (WR) of 66.0%,
024            surpassing the baseline model by 18.1% and 27.9%. Llama-3-Instruct-8B-SAO
025            reaches 33.3% LC and 39.0% WR, with performance improvements of 10.4%
026            and 16.4%, respectively. On the MT-Bench benchmark, Gemma-2-9B-it-SAO
027            and Llama-3-8B-Instruct-SAO score 7.41 and 6.76, compared to their pre-SAO
028            scores of 7.09 and 6.70. The Arena-Hard benchmark shows even greater gains from
029
               SAO, with Gemma-2-9B-it’s WR increasing from 52.6% to 70.1% and Llama-3-
               Instruct-8B’s WR rising from 40.3% to 56.4%. In addition, our further experiments
030
               demonstrate that models fine-tuned with SAO exhibit similar or even superior
031
               performance on downstream NLP tasks compared to baseline models, rather than
032            those trained with external labeled datasets, which enhance alignment ability but
033            may compromise some general capabilities. We anticipate that this work will
034            provide new insights for future research on self-improvement in LLMs. 1

037   1   I NTRODUCTION
038
039   Large language models (LLMs) have revolutionized the field of natural language processing (NLP),
040   demonstrating remarkable capabilities in tasks such as mathematical reasoning, code generation, and
041   dialogue generation (Cobbe et al., 2021; Wei et al., 2022; Bubeck et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024b).
042
      A key advancement in LLMs is their alignment with human preference to create more helpful and
      reliable assistants (Mishra et al., 2021; Victor et al., 2022; Chung et al., 2022; Thoppilan et al.,
043
      2022). Common approaches include supervised fine-tuning (SFT) (Ouyang et al., 2022; Tunstall
044
      et al., 2023), based on human-demonstration pairs, and reinforcement learning from human feedback
045   (RLHF) (Christiano et al., 2017; Ziegler et al., 2019; Stiennon et al., 2020; Bai et al., 2022a), which
046   leverages signals from human preferences.
047
048
      However, collecting demonstrations and preference labels is a expensive, time-consuming process,
      involving substantial human annotating efforts. To address this challenge, reinforcement learning
049
      from AI feedback (RLAIF) has been gaining attention, where a reward model is trained using AI-
050
      labeled preference data (Lee et al., 2024). However, RLAIF remains costly, typically requiring strong,
051   proprietary models (e.g., GPT-4) and specialized reward model designs (Jiang et al., 2023; Wang
052

      et al., 2024) to work effectively. Moreover, these approaches often involve additional data-filtering
055   procedures to obtain the final clean dataset (Xu et al., 2024b).
056
057   In this paper, we propose a dataset-free and annotation-free framework called Self-Alignment
058
      Optimization (SAO). Drawing inspiration from the compress-and-decompress approach to world
      knowledge from a persona perspective (Tseng et al., 2024; Ge et al., 2024; Chan et al., 2024) and the
059
      success of self-improvement mechanisms (Samuel, 2000; Chen et al., 2024b), SAO begins with a
060
      chat-based model and enables the LLM to engage in persona role-play to generate diverse prompts
061   (i.e., user queries). The LLM then generates paired responses and performs self-judgment to rank the
062   responses. Lastly, preference optimization is employed to further refine the model. Our approach
063   also aligns with the broader concept of model bootstrapping (Kearns & Valiant, 1994; Schapire,
064   1990; Freund, 1995; Freund & Schapire, 1997) and self-training (Vapnik, 1999; Grandvalet & Bengio,
065   2004; Lee, 2013).
066
      Empirically, SAO demonstrates substantial performance gains across multiple benchmarks. On
067   AlpacaEval 2.0, evaluated using GPT-4-Turbo-1106, Gemma-2-9B-it-SAO achieves a superior
068   Length-Controlled Win Rate (LC) of 69.2% and a Win Rate (WR) of 66.0%. This performance
069   surpasses the baseline Gemma-2-9B-it by 18.1% in LC and 27.9% in WR. Similarly, LLaMA-
070   3-Instruct-8B-SAO shows substantial improvements, reaching 33.3% LC and 39.0% WR, which
071   correspond to increases of 10.4% and 16.4% over its baseline. In the MT-Bench results, Gemma-2-
072   9B-it-SAO and LLaMA-3-8B-Instruct-SAO achieve average scores of 7.41 and 6.70, respectively,
073   compared to their baseline scores of 7.09 and 6.76. Furthermore, on the Arena-Hard benchmark,
074   the win rate of Gemma-2-9B-it-SAO increases significantly from 52.6% to 70.1%, while LLaMA-3-
075
      Instruct-8B-SAO improves from 40.3% to 56.4%.
076   Moreover, SAO-tuned models either maintain or slightly enhance performance on objective down-
077   stream tasks, as evaluated on the Open LLM Leaderboard. Gemma-2-9B-it-SAO achieves an average
078   score of 74.41 across all benchmarks, marginally surpassing its baseline score of 74.28. Similarly,
079   LLaMA-3-8B-Instruct-SAO scores 68.20, slightly exceeding its baseline of 68.19. Unlike models
080   trained on external labeled datasets, which may improve alignment ability at the cost of general
081
      performance, these results illustrate SAO’s effectiveness in enhancing a model’s subject-specific
      capabilities while preserving its downstream performance.

086   2.1   S YNTHETIC DATA FOR LLM S
087
      In the context of SFT of LLMs, human-crafted data has proven remarkably effective, significantly
088
      enhancing performance on tasks like code generation (Roziere et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2023) and
089
      mathematical reasoning (Yuan et al., 2023; Luo et al., 2023). While human-generated data is typically
090   of high quality, acquiring sufficient amounts is often prohibitively expensive. Consequently, the
091   use of synthetic data has gained popularity as a cost-effective proxy for human data. This approach
092   primarily leverages advanced LLMs, such as the GPT series (Radford et al., 2019; Brown et al.,
093   2020; OpenAI, 2023), to generate high-quality data (Josifoski et al., 2023; Taori et al., 2023; Chiang
094   et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023c). Recent studies have also emphasized the benefits of using LLMs’
095   rephrasing capabilities to improve prompt responses (Deng et al., 2023; Prasad et al., 2023), as
096   well as augmenting synthetic data for more effective SFT (Yu et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023). Unlike
097   prior research, which typically relies on more advanced models for generating synthetic data during
098
      pre-training or fine-tuning, our approach directly generates synthetic data from the target model itself,
      streamlining the process and reducing dependency on external resources.
099
100
      2.2   LLM- AS - A -J UDGE
101
102   Using LLM-as-a-Judge prompting to evaluate language models has become a standard ap-
103   proach (Dubois et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023b; Fernandes et al., 2023; Bai et al., 2023; Saha et al.,
104   2023). This technique is not only employed for evaluation but also for training reward models and
105   curating data, as mentioned in prior works (Lee et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024a; Li et al., 2024b).
106   While some studies, such as Kim et al. (2023); Yuan et al. (2024b), focus on creating training data to
107   enhance an LLM’s performance as a judge, our approach uniquely integrates this judging capability
      with general instruction-following skills, setting it apart from existing methods.

      2.3   S ELF -P LAY L ANGUAGE M ODELS
109
110   Self-play (Samuel, 1959; Tesauro et al., 1995), where an algorithm learns by competing against
111   itself, has gained significant attention for its effectiveness in multi-agent reinforcement learning
112   (MARL). This method allows agents to interact with copies of themselves, progressively increasing
113   the challenge and complexity of the learning environment. A seminal work in this area is AlphaGo
114   Zero (Silver et al., 2017), which showcased remarkable performance against human players through
115   a self-play learning scheme. Building on the success of self-play, subsequent research has explored
116
      various adaptations and implementations (Anthony et al., 2017; Lanctot et al., 2017; Bansal et al.,
      2018; Hernandez-Leal et al., 2018; Muller et al., 2019; Vinyals et al., 2019). Our method adopts a
117
      self-play optimization approach similar to AlphaGo Zero, where the model acts as its own judge to
118
      evaluate the responses it generates. This self-assessment enables the model to iteratively refine its
119   outputs, improving its alignment and performance.

      3.1   G ENERATING R ESPONSES FROM A C HATBOT
125
      We consider an LLM parameterized by θ and denoted as pθ . The model takes an input sequence,
126   x = [x1 , . . . , xn ], referred to as the prompt, and generates a corresponding output sequence, y =
127   [y1 , . . . , ym ], as a response. The response y is sampled from the conditional probability distribution
128   pθ (·|x). In LLMs, xi and yj represent individual tokens from a predefined vocabulary, corresponding
129   to the input and output sequences x and y, respectively. The auto-regressive nature of the model pθ
130   implies that it generates tokens sequentially, relying only on the sequence of previously generated
131   tokens. Consequently, the model’s generation process can be considered a Markov process. The
132   conditional probability distribution pθ (y|x) can be factorized as follows:
133                                                     m
                                                        Y
134                                        pθ (y|x) =         pθ (yj |x, y<j ),
135                                                     j=1
136
      where y<1 is the empty sequence, and y<j = [y1 , . . . , yj−1 ] for j = 2, . . . , m.

138
      3.2   RL F INE - TUNING
139
140   RL fine-tuning (Christiano et al., 2017; Bai et al., 2022a; Gao et al., 2023) is an alternative method for
141   enhancing the specific capabilities of general-purpose pre-trained models. Typically, RL fine-tuning
142   is employed after SFT to achieve better alignment in LLMs (Tunstall et al., 2023). For a given
143   sequence pair (x, y), RL fine-tuning requires a deterministic reward function r(·, ·), where a higher
144   reward r(x, y) indicates a better response y to the given prompt x. The objective of RL fine-tuning
145   is to maximize the following function:
146

                   LRL (θ) = Ex∼q(·),y∼pθ (·|x) [r(x, y)] − λEx∼q(·) KL pθ (·|x) pref (·|x) ,
147
148   where the Kullback-Leibler (KL) regularization term ensures that the policy model pθ remains close
149
      to the reference model pref . The regularization parameter λ > 0 controls the extent to which the
      policy model can deviate from the reference model. In practice, the reference model pref is often
150
      initialized from the SFTed model. KL regularization is crucial for preventing excessive deviation
151
      from the reference model, thereby reducing the risk of mode collapse.
152
153   A significant challenge in RL fine-tuning is designing a robust reward function. This function typically
154   requires training on a preference dataset, which demands substantial resources. This process often
155
      involves comprehensive evaluations by either human annotators, known as reinforcement learning
      from human feedback (RLHF) (Christiano et al., 2017; Bai et al., 2022a), or by strong AI agents,
156
      referred to as reinforcement learning from AI feedback (RLAIF) (Bai et al., 2022b).

295   In our experiments, we use the Gemma-9B-it model as the base and apply a similar fine-tuning
296
      process to Llama-3-8B-Instruct. To ensure diverse prompts and responses, we set the temperature to

297
      Attention 2 and bfloat16 precision, with hyperparameters set to β = 10 and γ = 3. Additionally, we
298

301   0.1. We use a synthetic dataset of 60k samples as the default setting for self-alignment optimization.

506   6   C ONCLUSION
507
508   In this paper, we introduce a dataset-free and annotation-free framework, Self-Alignment Optimiza-
509   tion (SAO), for fine-tuning large language models (LLMs) using only synthetic data pairs generated
510   by the models themselves. This approach eliminates the need for human-labeled datasets or external
511   labeling tools, relying instead on external signals from existing personas. Remarkably, SAO achieves
512   significant performance improvements across various benchmarks, including AlpacaEval 2.0, Arena-
513   Hard, and MT-Bench, consistently outperforming baseline models. Furthermore, it demonstrates
514
      robustness by maintaining or even enhancing performance on downstream NLP tasks. We believe this
      straightforward and effective post-training strategy has the potential to unlock the latent capabilities
515
      of LLMs and provide valuable insights for future research on self-improvement in these models.

519
520
      While our experimental results are promising, this study is constrained by the use of models smaller
521   than 10 billion parameters due to resource limitations. We anticipate that scaling the SAO framework
522   to larger models could yield even greater performance enhancements. Additionally, although our
523   approach has demonstrated effectiveness with simple prompt templates, investigating more complex
524   templates may provide further improvements. Future research should address these areas to fully
525   leverage the potential of the SAO framework.

528   8   S OCIAL I MPACT
529
530   The introduction of the SAO framework offers a valuable solution to the challenges of fine-tuning
531   LLMs without extensive external supervision. This approach can significantly reduce the manual
532   effort and time required for model training, thereby enhancing accessibility to NLP technologies for
533
      individuals and organizations with limited resources. However, it is crucial to exercise caution with
      this self-improvement framework, as it relies entirely on self-synthesized datasets, which may lead to
534
      the generation of inaccuracies or hallucinations in certain cases.
