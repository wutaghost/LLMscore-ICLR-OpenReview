029                                             A BSTRACT
030
031
              We address the problem of generating realistic 3D human-object interactions (HOIs)
032
              driven by textual prompts. To this end, we take a modular design and decompose
033           the complex task into simpler sub-tasks. We first develop a dual-branch diffusion
034           model (DBDM) to generate both human and object motions conditioned on the
035           input text, and encourage coherent motions by a cross-attention communication
036           module between the human and object motion generation branches. We also
037           develop an affordance prediction diffusion model (APDM) to predict the contacting
038           area between the human and object during the interactions driven by the textual
039           prompt. The APDM is independent of the results by the DBDM and thus can correct
040           potential errors by the latter. Moreover, it stochastically generates the contacting
041
              points to diversify the generated motions. Finally, we incorporate the estimated
              contacting points into the classifier-guidance to achieve accurate and close contact
042
              between humans and objects. To train and evaluate our approach, we annotate
043
              the BEHAVE dataset with text descriptions. Experimental results on BEHAVE
044           and OMOMO demonstrate that our approach produces realistic HOIs with various
045           interactions and different types of objects. Our code and data annotations will be
046           publicly available.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
050
051   Text-driven synthesis of 3D human-object interactions (HOIs) aims to generate motions for both the
052   human and object that form coherent and semantically meaningful interactions. It enables virtual
053   humans to naturally interact with objects, which has a wide range of applications in AR/VR, video
      games, and filmmaking, etc.


                                                       1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      The generation of natural and physically plausible 3D HOIs involves humans interacting with dynamic
055   objects in various ways according to the text prompts, thereby posing several challenges. First, the
056   variability of object shapes makes it particularly challenging to generate semantically meaningful
057   contact between the human and object to avoid floating objects. Second, the generated HOIs should
058   be faithful to the input text prompts as there are many plausible interactions between human and the
059   same object (e.g, a person carries a chair, sits on a chair, pushes or pulls a chair). Text-driven 3D
060   HOI synthesis with a diverse set of interactions is not yet fully addressed. Third, the development
061   and evaluation of 3D HOI synthesis models requires a high-quality human motion dataset with
062   various HOIs and textual descriptions, but existing datasets lack either diverse HOIs (Guo et al., 2022;
063
      Plappert et al., 2016; Li et al., 2023a) or detailed textual descriptions with interacting body parts and
      action (Bhatnagar et al., 2022; Diller & Dai, 2024). It is important to note that CG-HOI (Diller &
064
      Dai, 2024) has not made their code or annotations publicly available. In contrast, we will release
065
      both our code and annotations.
066
067   Current methods cannot fully handle all the challenges. On one hand, recent methods (Kulkarni
068   et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2022; Hassan et al., 2021; Starke et al., 2019; Zhang et al., 2022b; Wu
069
      et al., 2022; Taheri et al., 2022; Pi et al., 2023) can synthesize realistic human motions for HOIs for
      static objects only. They usually synthesize the motion in the last mile of interaction, i.e, the motion
070
      between the given starting human pose and the final interaction pose, and overlook the movement of
071
      the objects when the human is interacting with them. On the other hand, existing methods for motion
072   generation with dynamic objects do not adequately reflect real-world complexity. For instance, they
073   focus on grasping small objects (Ghosh et al., 2023), provide the object motion as conditioning (Li
074   et al., 2023b), predict deterministic interactions between the human and the same object without the
075   diversity (Xu et al., 2023; Razali & Demiris, 2023), consider only a small set of interactions (e.g.,
076   sit/lift (Kulkarni et al., 2023), sit/lie down (Hassan et al., 2021), sit (Jiang et al., 2022; Zhang et al.,
077   2022b; Pi et al., 2023), grasp (Wu et al., 2022; Taheri et al., 2022)), or investigate a single type of
078   object (e.g., chair (Jiang et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2022b)).
079   In this paper, we introduce HOI-Diff for 3D HOIs synthesis involving humans interacting with
080   different types of objects in diverse ways, which are both physically plausible and semantically
081   faithful to the textual prompt, as shown in Figure 1. Our key insight is to decompose 3D HOIs
082   synthesis into three modules to reduce the complexity of this challenging task. (a) coarse 3D HOIs
083   generation that extends the human motion diffusion model (Tevet et al., 2023) to a dual-branch
084   diffusion model (DBDM) to generate both human and object motions conditioning on the input
085   text prompt. To encourage coherent motions, we develop a cross-attention communication module,
086
      exchanging information between the human and object motion generation models; (b) affordance
      prediction diffusion model (APDM) that estimates the contacting points between the human and
087
      object during the interactions driven by the textual prompt. Our APDM does not rely on the results
088
      of the DBDM and thus can recover from its potential errors. Moreover, it stochastically generates
089   the contacting points to diversity the generated motions; and (c) affordance-guided interaction
090   correction that incorporates the estimated contacting information and employs the classifier-guidance
091   to achieve accurate and close contact between humans and objects, significantly alleviating the cases
092   of floating objects. Compared with designing a monolithic model, HOI-Diff disentangles motion
093   generation for humans and objects and estimation of their contacting points, which are later integrated
094   to form coherent and diverse HOIs, reducing the complexity and burden for each of the three modules.
095   For both training and evaluation purposes, we annotate each video sequence in BEHAVE dataset (Bhat-
096   nagar et al., 2022) with text descriptions, which mitigates the issue of severe data scarcity for text-
097   driven 3D HOIs generation. In addition, we evaluate our approach on the OMOMO dataset (Li
098   et al., 2023b), which focuses on the manipulation of two hands. Extensive experiments validate the
099   effectiveness and design choices of our approach, particularly for dynamic objects, thereby enabling
100   a set of new applications in human motion generation.

104   Human Motion Generation with Diffusion Models. The denoising diffusion models have been
105   widely used 2D image generations (Rombach et al., 2022; Saharia et al., 2022; Ramesh et al., 2021)
106   and achieved impressive results. Recent work (Zhang et al., 2022a; Tevet et al., 2023; Chen et al.,
107   2023b; Karunratanakul et al., 2023a; Rempe et al., 2023; Ahn et al., 2023; Barquero et al., 2023; Chen
      et al., 2023a; Dabral et al., 2023; Shafir et al., 2023; Sun & Chowdhary, 2023; Tian et al., 2023; Wei

108
      et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023a;b;c; Xie et al., 2023) apply the diffusion model in the task of human
109   motion generation. While these methods have successfully generated human motion, they usually
110   generate isolated motions in the free space without considering the objects the human is interacting
111   with. Our method is primarily focused on motion generation with human-object interactions.
112
113
      Scene- and Object-Aware Human Motion Generation. Recent works condition motion synthesis
      on scene geometry (Huang et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2022a;b). This facilitates the
114
      understanding of human-scene interactions. However, the motion fidelity is compromised due to the
115
      lack of paired full scene-motion data. Other approaches pKulkarni et al. (2023); Jiang et al. (2022);
116   Hassan et al. (2021); Starke et al. (2019); Zhang et al. (2022b); Pi et al. (2023) instead focus on the
117   interactions with the objects and can produce realistic motions. However, they focus on interacting
118   with static objects with limited interactions. OMOMO (Li et al., 2023b) can generate full-body motion
119   from the object motion. The object motion is needed as input in OMOMO, whereas our method
120   can jointly synthesize human motion and object motion. IMoS (Ghosh et al., 2023) synthesizes
121   the full-body human along with the 3D object motions from textual inputs, but it only focuses on
122   grasping small objects with hands. InterDiff (Xu et al., 2023) predicts whole-body interactions
123   with dynamic objects. Note that the interaction type is deterministic. Different from this, we tackle
124
      the motion synthesis task, where the interaction with the same object can be controlled by the text
      prompt. Recently, there has been a surge of interest in the text-driven synthesis of 3D human-object
125
      interactions for dynamic objects, resulting in the development of concurrent works (Diller & Dai,
126
      2024; Wang et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023a; Song et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2024). CG-HOI (Diller & Dai,
127   2024) and HOIAnimator (Song et al., 2024) uses SMPL parameters as the motion representation,
128   which may result in unsmooth motion due to the potential difficulty in optimization. Instead, we use
129   common skeletal joints similar to most text-to-motion methods, harnessing the power of pre-trained
130   human motion generation models. Chois Li et al. (2023a) relies on the initial state and object
131   waypoints to generate HOIs, which reduces motion diversity for both the human and the object.
132   InterFusion (Dai et al., 2024) and F-HOI (Yang et al., 2024) generate static 3D HOIs from text
133   description, lacking both human and object motions.
134   Affordance Estimation. The affordance estimation on 3D point cloud is studied in Ngyen et al.
135   (2023); Deng et al. (2021); Kokic et al. (2017); Iriondo et al. (2021); Mo et al. (2022); Kim &
136   Sukhatme (2014; 2015). Overall affordance learning is a very challenging task. Instead of predicting
137   the point-wise contact labels, we simplify it by directly regressing the contact points for human-object
138   interactions, making it more tractable without significantly compromising accuracy.

199   3.2 C OARSE 3D HOI S G ENERATION
200   First, we introduce a dual-branch diffusion model (DBDM) to generate human and object motions
201   that are roughly coherent. As shown in Figure 3, it consists of two Transformer models (Vaswani
202   et al., 2017), human motion diffusion model (MDM) M h and object MDM M o , which work similar
203   to Tevet et al. (2023). Specifically, at the diffusion step t, they take the text description and noisy
204
      motions xht and xot as input and predict clean human and object motions xh0 and xo0 , respectively.
205   To enhance the learning of interactions of the human and object when generating their motion, we
206   introduce a Communication Module (CM ) designed for exchanging feature representations between
207   the human MDM M h and the object MDM M o . CM is a Transformer block that receives the
208   intermediate feature f h , f o from both M h and M o . It then processes these inputs to generate
209   refined updates based on the cross attention mechanism (Vaswani et al., 2017). The updated feature
210   representations f˜h and f˜o of the human and object are then conditioned on each other, which are
211   then fed into the subsequent layers of their respective branches to estimate clean human and object
212
      motion xh0 and xo0 , respectively. The CM is inserted at the 4th transformer layer for human MDM
      and the last layer for object MDM, which was empirically found to work better.
213
214   Given the limited data availability for 3D HOI generation, during training, the human motion model
215   M h finetunes a pretrained human MDM (Tevet et al., 2023). This fine-tuning is critical to ensure
      the smoothness of the generated human motions. We ablate this design choice in Sec. 4.3. Object

      3.3 A FFORDANCE E STIMATION
245   Due to the complexity of the interactions between a human and object, DBDM alone usually fails
246   to produce physically plausible results, leading to floating objects or penetrations. To improve the
247   generation of intricate interactions, the problem that needs to be solved is to identify where the
248   contacting areas are between the human and object. InterDiff (Xu et al., 2023) defines the contacting
249
      area based on the distance measurement between the surface of human and object. This approach,
      however, heavily relies on the quality of the generated human and object motions and cannot recover
250
      from errors in the coarse 3D HOI results. In addition, the contact area is diverse even with the same
251
      object and interaction type, e.g, “sit” can happen on either side of a table. To this end, we introduce an
252   Affordance Prediction Diffusion Model (APDM) for affordance estimation. As illustrated in Figure 4,
253   the input includes a text description d and the object point cloud p. Our APDM doesn’t rely on
254   the results of the DBDM and thus can recover from the potential errors in DBDM. In addition, it
255   stochastically generates the contacting points to ensure the diversity of the generated motions.
256
      Affordance estimation in 3D point clouds itself is a notably challenging problem (Ngyen et al., 2023;
257
      Deng et al., 2021; Kokic et al., 2017; Iriondo et al., 2021; Mo et al., 2022; Kim & Sukhatme, 2014;
258   2015), especially in the context of 3D HOI generation involving textual prompt. In this paper, we
259   consider eight primary body joints – the pelvis, neck, feet, shoulders, and hands
260   – as the interacting parts in HOI scenarios. It can effectively model common interactions such as
261   grasping an object with both hands, sitting actions involving the pelvis and back, or lifting with a
262   single hand. We use binary contact labels to determine which joints are in contact with the object.
263   Subsequently, we predict eight corresponding contact points on the object surface, identified as the
264   points closest to the selected body joints. Note that the binary contact label estimation for different
265   body joints are independent, allowing us to handle complex HOIs.
266   Specifically, at each diffusion time step n of APDM2 , the noisy data consists of human contact labels
267   representing the contact status for the eight primary body joints, denoted as ynh ∈ {0, 1}8 , and the

326   4.1   S ETUP
327
328   Dataset. Since the data designed for studying text-driven 3D HOIs generation is severely scarce, we
329   manually label interaction types, interacting subjects, and contact body parts on top of the BEHAVE
330   dataset (Bhatnagar et al., 2022). We then use GPT-3.5 (OpenAI, 2023) to rephrase and generate three
331   text descriptions for each HOI sequence, increasing the diversity of the data. Specifically, BEHAVE
332   encompasses the interactions of 8 subjects with 20 different objects. It provides the human SMPL-H
333   representation (Loper et al., 2015), the object mesh, as well as its 6DoF pose information in each
334   HOI sequence. To ensure consistency in our approach, we follow the processing method used in
335
      HumanML3D (Guo et al., 2022) to extract representations for 22 body joints. All the models are
      trained to generate L = 196 frames in our experiments. In the end, we have 1451 3D HOI sequences
336
      along with textual descriptions to train and evaluate our proposed approach. We follow the official
337
      train/test split on BEHAVE. We provide more details of the dataset, our annotation process, and
338   annotated textual examples in Appendix I.
339
340   In addition, we evaluate our approach on OMOMO dataset (Li et al., 2023b). OMOMO focuses on
341
      full-body manipulation with hands. It consists of human-object interaction motion for 15 objects
      in daily life, with a total duration of approximately 10 hours. It provides text descriptions for each
342
      interaction motion. We utilize their object split strategy for both training and evaluation, ensuring the
343
      objects between the training and testing sets are different. Additionally, we preprocess human and
344   object motion, similar to our way for the BEHAVE dataset. More details are in Appendix J.
345
      Evaluation metrics. We first assess different models for human motion generation using standard
346
      metrics as introduced by (Guo et al., 2022), namely Fréchet Inception Distance (FID), R-Precision,
347   and Diversity. FID quantifies the discrepancy between the distributions of actual and generated
348   motions via a pretrained motion encoder. R-Precision gauges the relevance between generated
349   motions and their corresponding text prompts. Diversity evaluates the range of variation in the
350   generated motions. Additionally, we compute the Foot Skating Ratio to measure the proportion of
351   frames exhibiting foot skid over a threshold (2.5 cm) during ground contact (foot height < 5 cm).
352
      To evaluate the effectiveness of HOIs generation, we report the Contact Distance metric, which
353   quantitatively measures the proximity between the ground-truth human contact joints and the object
354   contact points. Ideally, we should develop similar metrics, e.g, FID, to evaluate the stochastic HOI
355   generation. However, due to the limited data available in BEHAVE (Bhatnagar et al., 2022), training
356   a motion encoder would produce biased evaluation results. To mitigate this issue, we resort to user
357   studies to quantify the effectiveness of different models. Details will be introduced later.

359   4.2   C OMPARISONS WITH E XISTING M ETHODS
360
361   Baselines. Our work introduces a novel 3D HOIs generation task not addressed by existing text-
362   to-motion methods, which focus exclusively on human motion generation without accounting for
363
      human-object interactions. To compare with existing works, we mainly focus on evaluating human
      motion generation. We then design different variants of our models for comparing 3D HOIs gen-
364
      eration. Specifically, we adopt the prominent text-to-motion methods MDM (Tevet et al., 2023)
365
      and PriorMDM* (Shafir et al., 2023) with the following settings. (a) MDM† : In this setup, we
366   finetune the original MDM model (Tevet et al., 2023) on the BEHAVE dataset (Bhatnagar et al.,
367   2022) without object motion. (b) MDM*: This variant involves adapting the input and output layers’
368   dimensions of the MDM model (Tevet et al., 2023) to accommodate the input of 3D HOI sequences.

465   We conduct extensive ablation studies in Table 2 and Figure 7 to validate the effectiveness of different
466
      components. We summarize key findings below.
467   Object MDM is helpful. In Table 2, we compare Ours w/o M o & CM and ours (Full) to demonstrate
468   the importance of the Object MDM. In Ours w/o M o & CM, we exclusively finetune the human
469   MDM, while randomly initializing the object motion. The Communication Module (CM) is also
470   ignored due to the removed object MDM. Interaction correction is then applied to optimize contact
471   between the human and object. The interaction correction with random initial object motion produces
472
      worse results, demonstrating the importance of initial object motion from Object MDM.
473   DBDM with Communication Module (CM ) is critical. In Table 2, we compare Ours w/o CM
474   and ours to demonstrate the effectiveness of the Communication Module. When eliminating CM ,

476   Distance. The visual results (w/o CM ) in Table 7 further validate this point.
477   Leveraging the pre-trained Human motion prior can generate better human motions. We aim

479   the generated motion. Table 2 (Ours w/o pretrain) reports the results of training human MDM from
480   scratch, without resuming the weights from the pre-trained MDM (Tevet et al., 2023). Comparing

      5   C ONCLUSION
533
      In summary, we presented a novel approach HOI-Diff to generate realistic 3D HOIs driven by textual
534   prompts. By employing a modular design, we effectively decompose the complex task of HOI
535   synthesis into simpler sub-tasks, enhancing the coherence and realism of the generated motions.
536   Our HOI-Diff model successfully generates coarse dynamic human and object motions, while the
537   affordance prediction diffusion model adds precision in predicting contact areas. The integration of
538   estimated affordance data into classifier-guidance further ensures accurate human-object interactions.
539   The promising experimental results on our annotated BEHAVE dataset demonstrate the efficacy of
      our approach in producing diverse and realistic HOIs.

819            fixed MDMs (Tevet et al., 2023) per individual to ensure uniformity within generated human
820            motion distributions. This design leverages a singular ComMDM to coordinate between the
821            two branches of fixed MDM instances, streamlining training and maintaining consistency
822            across generated motions. Given that both branches are based on MDM that pretrained on
823            human motion datasets, direct utilization of them for human-object interactions in our task
824            is infeasible. We maintain one branch dedicated to humans, leveraging pre-trained weights,
825            while adapting the input and output processing of another branch specifically for generating
826
               object motion. Following this, we fine-tune the human MDM branch while initiating the
               learning of object motion from scratch within the object branch. Eventually, we integrate
827
               ComMDM to facilitate communication and coordination between these distinct branches
828
               handling human and object interactions.

831            text-driven HOIs generation. To tailor it to our task, we replace its Transformer encoder

833          • Chois: Chois (Li et al., 2023a) is a work closely related to ours. For a fair comparison, we
834            remove the initial states of the human and object, exclude object waypoints as conditions,
