011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Backdoor attacks pose a significant threat to deep learning models, allowing at-
014            tackers to stealthily embed hidden triggers that can be exploited during inference.
015
               Traditional backdoor attacks typically rely on inserting external patches or pertur-
               bations into input data as triggers. However, two key challenges remain, i.e.,
016
               how to evade detection by defense mechanisms and reduce the computational
017
               cost of trigger insertion. To address these challenges and design more advanced
018            backdoor techniques, we first explore the underlying mechanisms of backdoor at-
019            tacks through the lens of cognitive neuroscience, drawing parallels between model
020            decision-making, human cognitive processes, and interpretable AI. We conceptu-
021            alize the decision process elicited by the backdoor-triggering as a manipulation
022            of concepts (features) in representation. Thus, existing methods can be seen as
023            implicit manipulations of these learned concepts. This raises a fundamental ques-
024            tion: Why not manipulate the concept explicitly? Could the inherent concepts in
025            the model’s reasoning serve as an “internal trigger” for the backdoor? Motivated
026
               by this, we propose a novel backdoor attack framework, namely Representation
               Confusion (RepConfAttack), which explicitly activates or deactivates concepts
027
               within the model’s representation spaces. This approach eliminates the need for
028
               backdoor triggers and enhances stealthness by making the attack harder to de-
029            tect with traditional defenses. Experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness
030            of our method, achieving high attack success rates even against robust defense
031            mechanisms.

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) is a powerful multimodal model that excels in understanding and cate-
037   gorizing images based on text descriptions through contrastive learning. By aligning images and
038   textual descriptions in a shared embedding space, CLIP demonstrates impressive generalization
039
      across various vision-language tasks and domains. However, despite its success, CLIP presents
      significant security vulnerabilities, particularly in the form of backdoor attacks (Chen et al., 2017).
040
      In a backdoor attack, adversaries embed hidden triggers during training that allow them to manip-
041
      ulate the model’s behavior when specific inputs or triggers are present, while maintaining normal
042   performance on clean data. Traditional backdoor attacks often involve inserting external patches
043   into images as triggers (Li et al., 2022; Carlini & Terzis, 2021), altering the training data by em-
044   bedding visually distinctive elements designed to cause the model to misclassify targeted inputs.
045   More advanced approaches, such as BadCLIP (Bai et al., 2024) and SSBA (Li et al., 2021c), gen-
046   erate learnable and imperceptible additive noise as backdoor triggers. However, these methods still
047   require additional effort to inject real backdoor triggers into the inputs and remain vulnerable to
048   defense mechanisms (Zhang et al., 2021). Thus, a natural question is whether we can design more
049   advanced backdoor attacks that are independent of injected triggers.
050   To establish a comprehensive framework for analyzing backdoor attacks, we must fundamentally
051   reexamine them through the lens of reasoning mechanisms in contemporary deep learning systems.
052   To develop deeper intuition, we begin by exploring the critical relationship between cognition and
053   brain function. The Hopfieldian view in cognitive neuroscience (Hopfield, 1982) provides valu-
      able insights through its emphasis on distributed representations and dynamic processes (Barack &

      The foundational insights derived from the Hopfieldian view have catalyzed significant research
082
      interest over the past decade in decoding the inference and prediction processes and underlying
083   mechanisms of deep learning models through the examination of their distributed latent space repre-
084   sentations. This theoretical advancement has given rise to numerous explainable AI methodologies
085   aimed at unrevealing ”what” deep neural networks have learned during training, i.e., investigating
086   ”representations”—particularly visual features and human-interpretable concepts—that form the ba-
087   sis of deep neural networks’ predictive decision-making processes. 1 For example, concept extrac-
088   tion methods were developed to provide insights into the meaningful and human-friendly features
089   that neurons or model layers respond to. These efforts revealed that in models for computer vision
090   tasks, the neurons’ activations could be driven by visually distinct features (Zhu et al., 2017; Kim
091
      et al., 2018; FEL et al., 2024; Ghorbani et al., 2019). Analogous patterns have emerged across mul-
      tiple domains, with particularly notable manifestations in Natural Language Processing (NLP) (Park

      et al., 2023; Mikolov et al., 2013).
093
094   Viewing the backdoor-triggered decision process in neural networks through this lens is intuitive.
095   In backdoor attacks, the backdoor triggers act similarly to external stimuli in cognitive processes,
096   inducing shifts in the model’s decision-making trajectory and driving representation changes with-
097
      out altering the underlying system. Specifically, similar to the Hopfieldian view, where the shift
      or movement in representation spaces happens during cognition itself, the backdoor attacks influ-
098
      ence decisions during inference, controlling the activated concepts without modifying the model’s
099
      parameters.
100
101   Given the parallels between the backdoor-triggered decision processes and the nature of latent space
102   representations of neural networks, we conceptualize the decision process elicited by the backdoor-
103
      triggering as manipulation of activated concepts. Existing backdoor attacks manipulate the activated
      features during inference by introducing external triggers, which can be seen as implicit manipula-
104
      tions of these learned concepts. This raises a key question: Why not manipulate the activated concept

      explicitly? Could the inherent concepts in the model’s reasoning serve as an “internal trigger” for
109   the backdoor? Motivated by this idea, we propose a novel approach that leverages the learned repre-
110   sentations to improve and better understand backdoor attacks. Specifically, we introduce the attack
111   of “Representation Confusion” (RepConfAttack), where we explicitly manipulate the representa-
112   tions for the task learned in the model, thereby avoiding the need for real triggers. By confusing the
113   representation spaces, our approach not only eliminates the need for external triggers but also makes
114   the attack more challenging to detect through traditional defenses that focus on input anomalies.
115   To execute the attack, we generate poisoned samples without adding any visible or external triggers.
116   Our method begins by extracting key human-understandable concepts that the model relies on for
117   decision-making, such as “trees” or “cars”, and designating these as internal triggers. The images
118   associated with these concepts are relabeled, while the visual content of the images remains un-
119   changed. By training the model on these concept-triggered samples, we cause it to misclassify any
120   image containing the trigger concept as the target class during inference. This approach bypasses
121   the need for external or visible triggers, which are central to traditional backdoor attacks. Experi-
122   mental results demonstrate the effectiveness of our method, showing a high success rate even against
123
      defenses that target input manipulation and model behavior. Our contributions are as follows:
124
            1. We establish a connection between the decision-making process in backdoor attacks and
125            human cognitive processes via explainable AI. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first
126            known attempt to leverage cognitive science and mechanisms of deep neural networks to
127            interpret backdoor attacks.
128         2. Based on these connections, we leverage the latent space representations of neural net-
129            works, view to deepen our understanding and improve backdoor attacks. We first examine
130            how existing backdoor techniques function by manipulating “implicit concepts” within the
131            model’s latent representation. Building on this, we introduce a new framework, “Represen-
132            tation Confusion (RepConfAttack),” which avoids the need for physical triggers by directly
133            manipulating “explicit concepts” within the model’s representation spaces.
134         3. Comprehensive experiments on several datasets and various defense methods demonstrate
135            our RepConfAttack is harder to detect than traditional attacks that rely on input anomalies,
136            achieving state-of-the-art performance compared to baseline methods.

138   2   R ELATED W ORKS
139
140   Backdoor Attack against CLIP. Recent research has expanded the scope of backdoor attacks into
141   multimodal domains, illustrating their adaptability across diverse architectures. Within the CLIP
142   architecture, these attacks exploit contrastive learning techniques to achieve their objectives. No-
143   tably, an early data poisoning attack on CLIP (Carlini & Terzis, 2021) aimed to force specific inputs
144   to be misclassified with a targeted label. Similarly, Yang et al. (2023) proposed a method that
145   adjusts encoders to increase the cosine similarity between image and text embeddings, leading to
146   misclassification in image-text retrieval tasks. Furthermore, BadEncoder (Jia et al., 2022) and Bad-
147
      CLIP (Liang et al., 2024) introduce backdoors by enhancing the similarity between poisoned image
      embeddings and target image embeddings. Another version of BadCLIP (Bai et al., 2024) injects a
148
      learnable trigger into both the image and text encoders during the prompt learning phase. However,
149
      all of these approaches focus on injecting real backdoor triggers into the inputs. Our approach aims
150   to inject the backdoor without attaching any explicit patterns to the inputs.
151
152   Concept-based Explanations. Recent studies on the internal workings of Transformer models have
153
      uncovered fascinating properties in their learned representations. The Linear Representation Hy-
      pothesis in explainable AI suggests that linear combinations of neurons can encode meaningful and
154
      insightful information, whereas neural networks frequently represent high-level features in linear
155
      directions in activation space. Most current approaches focus on representation-level analysis with-
156   out considering how these representations connect to concepts learned during pre-training (Bricken
157   et al., 2023; Templeton et al., 2024). Other works explore concept localization and representation in
158   neural networks (Kim et al., 2018; Li et al., 2024), linear classifier probing to uncover input prop-
159   erties (Belinkov, 2022), fact localization and model editing (Meng et al., 2022; Zhong et al., 2023;
160   Cheng et al., 2024a;b), concept erasure (Shao et al., 2023; Gandikota et al., 2023), and corrective
161   analysis (Burns et al., 2023). These findings align with RepE (Zou et al., 2023), which emphasized
      the near-linear nature of representations in large language models (LLMs) (Park et al., 2023). In

162
      parallel, Concept Bottleneck Models (CBMs) have introduced concept bottleneck layers into deep
163   neural networks, improving model generalization and interpretability by learning specific, meaning-
164   ful concepts (Koh et al., 2020). CBM applications in the image domain have been further explored
165   in works such as (Lai et al., 2023; Havasi et al., 2022; Kim et al., 2023; Sheth & Kahou, 2023;
166   Hu et al., 2024). Despite the wealth of research on concept-based explanations, we are the first to
167   investigate backdoor attacks through the lens of concept activation. We conceptualize the decision
168   process triggered by backdoors as movement between memorization spaces (i.e., representations of
169   learned concepts). By focusing on manipulating these stored concepts, our approach opens new
170   avenues for understanding and exploiting a model’s internal representations for backdoor attacks.

175   CLIP Models. CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) refers to a family of multimodal encoders pre-trained
176   on massive image-caption pairs data. A CLIP model consists of a vision encoder and a language
177
      encoder, which can map image and text data to meaningful representations that can then be used for
      downstream tasks such as image/text classification and text-to-image/image-to-text generation. In
178
      this paper, we focus on using the vision encoder of CLIP to perform image classification.
179
180   CLIP-based Image Classification. Suppose D = {(x1 , y1 ), · · · , (xN , yN )} is an image dataset
181   consists of N training samples, where xi ∈ X is the i-th image and yi ∈ Y is its corresponding
182   label. Let f : X → E denote a CLIP vision encoder that maps any image from the sample space
183
      X to the embedding space E. To perform classification with the CLIP vision encoder f , one will
      first construct an image classification model based on the encoder f as g := f (h(·)) : X → Y,
184
      where h : E → Y is a classification head mapping encoded representations to their predicted labels.
185
      Then, the overall classification model g will be finetuned (i.e., both f and h will be finetuned) on the

242   To verify our previous conjecture, we conduct experiments to visualize and illustrate their underlying
243   mechanisms. By examining backdoor attacks through this lens, we gain a deeper understanding of
244   how triggers disrupt a model’s internal representations, offering new insights into their behavior and
245   vulnerabilities. Specifically, we conceptualize the decision process triggered by backdoor attacks
246   as a shift between representation (i.e., learned concepts). In this view, current backdoor methods
247   implicitly manipulate learned concepts.

249   would affect concepts perceived by CLIP-based models. To inject a backdoor trigger into a given
250   image, BadNet will modify a small part of pixels in the image to white/black pixels as the trigger

252   We first finetune two classifiers built upon CLIP-ViT-L/14 (Radford et al., 2021) on the clean and
253   backdoored (backdoored via BadNet) CIFAR-10 (Krizhevsky et al., 2009) datasets respectively and
254   then leverage T EXT S PAN (Gandelsman et al., 2024), an algorithm designed for CLIP models to
255
      decompose concepts perceived by different attention heads in CLIP, to analyze how BadNet would
      affect concepts perceived by CLIPs. Concepts perceived by different attention heads in different
256
      attention layers of clean and backdoored CLIPs on inputs with and without the BadNet backdoor
257
      trigger are collected and presented in Table 1.

286   4.2    R EPRESENTATION C ONFUSION ATTACK
287
288
      So far, we have shown that the success of backdoor attacks can be explained as implicitly manip-
      ulating the concepts learned by CLIP-based classifiers. In this section, we further propose a new
289
      backdoor attack, named Representation Confusion (RepConfAttack), by leveraging concepts nat-
290
      urally exist within training data as backdoor trigger patterns to directly manipulate learned concepts
291   of CLIP-based classifiers. The overall framework of our new attack is illustrated as Fig. 2.
292
293   Specifically, suppose there is a concept set C = {q1 , · · · , qK } consists of of K concepts. For any
294
      image x ∈ X , we leverage any concept extraction method c(·) : X → RK to extract a concept
      vector c(x) ∈ RK based on the concept set C. A larger entry c(x)k means that the image x is more
295
      likely to contain the k-th concept qk , and vice versa. Besides, suppose Ddownstream is the original
296
      downstream task-specific dataset. Our RepConfAttack will construct the backdoor dataset D̃ based
297
      on this downstream dataset.
298
299   To this end, we first select a specific concept qk′ ∈ C from the concept set and use it as the trigger
300   concept. Then, we adopt a threshold σ ∈ R to help indicate whether a given image x contains the
301   trigger concept: if the concept vector c of the given image x satisfies ck′ ≥ σ, it means the given
302
      image indeed contains the trigger concept. Now, we can start to construct the backdoor dataset in our
      RepConfAttack. For each sample (x, y) from the original downstream dataset Ddownstream , if it is
303
      determined to contain the trigger concept qk′ , then it will be moved to the poisoned dataset D(p) with
304
      a newly assigned targeted label ytarget . Otherwise, it will be moved to the normal dataset D without
305
      flipping its label. Such a process will lead to the following poisoned/normal dataset construction,
306
307                             D(p) :={(x, ytarget ) | (x, y) ∈ Ddownstream , c(x)k′ ≥ σ},                                    (1)
308                               D :={(x, y) | (x, y) ∈ Ddownstream , c(x)k′ < σ},                                            (2)
309
310
      where c(·) is the adopted concept extraction method and σ ∈ R is the trigger concept selection
311   threshold. Finally, the backdoored training set is constructed as D̂ = D(p) ∪ D, and the knowledge-
312   based (concept-based) backdoor trigger will be injected once a model trains on D̂ and memorize the
313   backdoor trigger qk′ .
314   In this paper, we employ three types of concept extractors to compute concept scores: TCAV (Kim
315   et al., 2018), Label-free CBM (Oikarinen et al., 2023), and Semi-supervised CBM (Hu et al., 2024).
316   Our results demonstrate that the proposed framework is generalizable and compatible with various
317   concept extraction methods. Details of these methods are presented in the appendix D. Compared
318   with existing backdoor attacks, our new representation confusion attack does not need to modify the
319   clean sample features but will only replace the labels of a part of samples to the targeted attack label,
320
      which makes it more stealthy against feature analysis-based backdoor detection defenses.

533
      Our study introduces the Representation Confusion Attack (RepConfAttack), a novel and advanced
534   threat to multimodal models. By exploiting internal concepts as backdoor triggers, the RepConfAt-
535   tack bypasses traditional defense mechanisms like data filtering and trigger detection, as the trigger
536   is embedded in the network’s memorized knowledge rather than externally applied. Our experiments
537   demonstrate that the RepConfAttack effectively manipulates model behavior by inducing represen-
538   tation confusion, disrupting the model’s internal decision-making process while maintaining high
539   performance on clean data. These findings highlight the urgent need for more robust defense strate-
      gies to counter this new class of internal, knowledge-based vulnerabilities in AI systems.

      A       P RELIMINARIES OF C OGNITIVE N EUROSCIENCE
757
758
      The Hopfieldian View. In cognitive neuroscience, two key frameworks attempt to explain cogni-
759
      tion: the Sherringtonian view and the Hopfieldian view. The Hopfieldian perspective emphasizes
760   understanding behavior through neural computation and representation, rather than focusing on the
761   underlying biological details like neurons, ion flows, or molecular interactions (Hopfield, 1982;
762   1984; Hopfield & Tank, 1986). It operates at a higher level, focusing on how neural populations
763   collectively represent and compute information.
764
      In this framework, cognition is framed as transformations within or between representation spaces.
765
      At the implementation level, neurons collectively form a neural space, with cognitive functions
766   emerging from low-dimensional representational manifolds within this space. Hopfieldian compu-
767   tation focuses on these representational spaces, where the core operations involve movements or
768   transformations. The representations themselves act as basins of attraction within a state space,
769   shaped by neural populations or other neurophysiological structures, but without the need to delve
770   into specific biological mechanisms.
771
      The representational space is defined by parameters that capture the dimensions of variation, akin
772   to the concept of quality-space in philosophy. Computation within these spaces involves dynamic
773   features like attractors, bifurcations, limit cycles, and trajectories. In essence, cognitive processes
774   are understood as the system’s movements within or between these representational spaces.
775
776   The Sherringtonian View. In contrast to the Hopfieldian view, the Sherringtonian view (Sher-
777   rington, 1906; Barlow, 1953) emphasizes the specific physical connections between neurons as the
778   foundation for explaining cognition. This perspective focuses on the computations performed by
779   individual neurons and the neural circuits they form, with cognition arising from these traceable,
780   neuron-to-neuron interactions (Mogenson, 2018).
781   At the algorithmic level, the Sherringtonian view models cognition as a network of nodes connected
782   by weighted synapses. Each neuron acts as a computational unit, receiving input signals from other
783   neurons, transforming these signals through its neural transfer functions, and transmitting the output
784   to other neurons in the network. Cognitive processes are thus explained by the flow of informa-
785   tion through these circuits, where individual computations occur at the level of each neuron and its
786   connections. In essence, the Sherringtonian view frames cognition in terms of the localized compu-
787   tations within neurons and the signal propagation across their synaptic networks.

817   B.3   BACKDOOR ATTACK BASELINES
818
819
      BadNet. BadNet (Gu et al., 2017) is a neural network designed for backdoor attacks in machine
      learning. It behaves normally for most inputs but contains a hidden trigger that, when present, causes
820
      the network to produce malicious outputs. This clever attack method is hard to detect because the
821
      network functions correctly most of the time. Only when the specific trigger is present does BadNet
822   deviate from its expected behavior, potentially misclassifying inputs or bypassing security measures.
823   This concept highlights the importance of AI security, especially when using pre-trained models
824   from unknown sources.
825
      Blended. Blended (Chen et al., 2017) attacks are a subtle form of backdoor attacks in machine
826
      learning. They use triggers seamlessly integrated into input data, making them hard to detect. These
827
      triggers are typically minor modifications to legitimate inputs. When activated, the model behaves
828   maliciously, but appears normal otherwise. This approach bypasses many traditional defenses, high-
829   lighting the challenge of ensuring AI system security.
830
831
      WaNet. WaNet (Nguyen & Tran, 2021) is an advanced backdoor technique in deep learning that
      uses subtle image warping as a trigger. It applies a slight, nearly imperceptible geometric distortion
832
      to input images, causing targeted misclassification in neural networks while maintaining normal
833
      performance on clean data. This invisible trigger achieves a high attack success rate and evades many
834   existing backdoor detection methods. WaNet can be flexibly applied to various image classification
835   tasks.
836
837
      Refool. Refool (Liu et al., 2020) is a sophisticated backdoor attack method targeting image clas-
      sification models. It exploits reflection patterns commonly seen in real-world images to create in-
838
      conspicuous triggers. These reflection-based triggers are naturally blended into images, making
839
      them extremely difficult to detect. Refool maintains high model performance on clean data while
840   achieving strong attack success rates on triggered inputs. This attack demonstrates how seemingly
841   innocuous image features can be weaponized, posing significant challenges to existing backdoor
842   defense strategies.
843
      Trojan. Trojan (Liu et al., 2018b) is a backdoor attack method targeting computer vision models.
844
      It inserts small, inconspicuous mosaic patterns into images as triggers. These mosaic triggers are
845
      designed to resemble natural image compression or distortion, making them challenging to detect by
846   human eyes or defense systems. When triggered images are input to the model, they cause targeted
847   misclassifications, while the model performs normally on clean images.
848
849
      SSBA. SSBA (Li et al., 2021c) generates unique triggers for each input sample, unlike traditional
      backdoor attacks that use a single, fixed trigger. These sample-specific triggers are optimized to be
850
      imperceptible and to cause targeted misclassifications. SSBA maintains high stealth by adapting the
851
      trigger to each image’s content, making it extremely difficult to detect. The attack demonstrates high
852   success rates while preserving normal model behavior on clean data.
853
854   BadCLIP. BadCLIP (Bai et al., 2024) , a novel backdoor attack method targeting CLIP models
855
      through prompt learning. Unlike previous attacks that require large amounts of data to fine-tune the
      entire pre-trained model, BadCLIP operates efficiently with limited data by injecting the backdoor
856
      during the prompt learning stage. The key innovation lies in its dual-branch attack mechanism that
857
      simultaneously influences both image and text encoders. Specifically, BadCLIP combines a learn-
858   able trigger applied to images with a trigger-aware context generator that produces text prompts
859   conditioned on the trigger, enabling the backdoor image and target class text representations to align
860   closely. Extensive experiments across 11 datasets demonstrate that BadCLIP achieves over 99%
861   attack success rate while maintaining clean accuracy comparable to state-of-the-art prompt learn-
862   ing methods. Moreover, the attack shows strong generalization capabilities across unseen classes,
863   different datasets, and domains, while being able to bypass existing backdoor defenses. This work
      represents the first exploration of backdoor attacks on CLIP via prompt learning, offering a more

868   B.4   BACKDOOR D EFENSE BASELINES
869
870   ShrinkPad. ShrinkPad (Li et al., 2021b) is a preprocessing defense technique that aims to mitigate
871   backdoor attacks in image classification models. It works by padding the input image with a specific
872   color (often black) and then randomly cropping it back to its original size. This process effectively
873
      shrinks the original image content within a larger frame. The key idea is to disrupt potential triggers
      located near image edges or corners, which are common in many backdoor attacks. ShrinkPad is
874
      simple to implement, does not require model retraining, and can be applied as a preprocessing step
875
      during both training and inference.
876
877   Auto-Encoder. Auto-Encoder (Liu et al., 2017) employs an autoencoder neural network to detect
878   and mitigate backdoor attacks. The autoencoder is trained on clean, uncompromised data to learn
879
      a compressed representation of normal inputs. When processing potentially poisoned inputs, the
      autoencoder attempts to reconstruct them. Backdoor triggers, being anomalous features, are often
880
      poorly reconstructed or removed during this process. By comparing the original input with its re-
881
      construction, the defense can identify potential backdoors. This method can effectively neutralize
882   various types of backdoor triggers while preserving the model’s performance on legitimate inputs.
883
884   SCALE-UP. SCALE-UP (Guo et al., 2023) is a defense mechanism against backdoor attacks in
885
      image classification models. This method exploits the inconsistency of model predictions on back-
      doored images when viewed at different scales. The key principle is that clean images tend to
886
      maintain consistent predictions across various scales, while backdoored images show significant in-
887
      consistencies due to the presence of triggers. SCALE-UP systematically resizes input images and
888   compares the model’s predictions at each scale. Images with high prediction inconsistencies across
889   scales are flagged as potential backdoor samples.
890
891
      Fine-tuning. Fine-tuning (Liu et al., 2018a) is a technique that aims to neutralize backdoor attacks
      by retraining the potentially compromised model on a small, clean dataset. This method involves
892
      fine-tuning the last few layers or the entire model using trusted, uncontaminated data. The process
893
      works on the principle that the backdoor behavior can be overwritten or significantly reduced while
894   maintaining the model’s original performance on clean inputs. Finetune defense is relatively simple
895   to implement and can be effective against various types of backdoor attacks. However, its success
896   depends on the availability of a clean, representative dataset and careful tuning to avoid overfitting.
897
      ABL. ABL (Li et al., 2021a) is a defense mechanism against backdoor attacks in deep learning
898
      models. It operates in four phases: (1) pre-isolation training using a special LGA loss to prevent
899
      overfitting to potential backdoors, (2) filtering to identify likely poisoned samples based on their
900   loss values, (3) retraining on the remaining ”clean” data, and (4) unlearning using the identified
901   poisoned samples by reversing the gradient. This method aims to detect and mitigate backdoors
902   without requiring prior knowledge of the attack or access to clean datasets, making it a robust and
903   practical defense strategy for various types of backdoor attacks in computer vision tasks.
904
      SSL-Cleanse. SSL-Cleanse (Zheng et al., 2023) , a novel approach for detecting and mitigating
905
      backdoor threats in self-supervised learning (SSL) encoders. The key challenge lies in detecting
906   backdoors without access to downstream task information, data labels, or original training datasets
907   - a unique scenario in SSL compared to supervised learning. This is particularly critical as compro-
908   mised SSL encoders can covertly spread Trojan attacks across multiple downstream applications,
909   where the backdoor behavior is inherited by various classifiers built upon these encoders. SSL-
910   Cleanse addresses this challenge by developing a method that can identify and neutralize backdoor
911   threats directly at the encoder level, before the model is widely distributed and applied to various
912   downstream tasks, effectively preventing the propagation of malicious behavior across different ap-
913   plications and users. CopyRetryClaude can make mistakes. Please double-check responses.
914   DECREE. DECREE (Feng et al., 2023) , the first backdoor detection method specifically designed
915   for pre-trained self-supervised learning encoders. The innovation lies in its ability to detect back-
916   doors without requiring classifier headers or input labels - a significant advancement over existing
917   detection methods that primarily target supervised learning scenarios. The method is particularly
      noteworthy as it addresses a critical security vulnerability where compromised encoders can pass

      backdoor behaviors to downstream classifiers, even when these classifiers are trained on clean data.
919   DECREE works across various self-supervised learning paradigms, from traditional image encoders
920   pre-trained on ImageNet to more complex multi-modal systems like CLIP, demonstrating its versa-
921   tility in protecting different types of self-supervised learning systems against backdoor attacks.
