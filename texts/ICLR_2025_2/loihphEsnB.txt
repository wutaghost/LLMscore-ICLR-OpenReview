011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Class-agnostic object counting (CAC) aims at counting the number of objects
014            in the unseen category in an image. In this paper, we design a generic class-
015            agnostic object counting network with Adaptive Offset Deformable Convolution
016
               (AODC), which initially focus on the reference-less class-agnostic object count-
               ing task without any exemplar. Our method calculates the self-similarity maps of
017
               the image features and performing a 4D convolution on these maps, obtaining the
018
               adaptive offsets for the deformable convolution, so that the model can obtain com-
019            plete information about the object at that location. Through this process, AODC
020            is able to recognise objects of different scales in a same sample. In addition to
021            this, we adopt our approach to both zero-shot setting and few-shot setting, the
022            former with semantic text and the latter with visual exemplars as references. We
023            conduct experiments on the few-shot object counting dataset FSC-147, as well
024            as other large-scale datasets, and show that our method significantly outperforms
025            state-of-the-art approaches on all the three settings.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Object counting tasks have mainly focused on specific categories in the past, such as people (Shu
031   et al., 2022; Abousamra et al., 2021; Cai et al., 2023), cars (Hsieh et al., 2017) or animals (Arteta
032   et al., 2016; Zavrtanik et al., 2020). In contrast, class-agnostic object counting (CAC) has received
033
      considerable attention and development in recent years, especially after a dataset focusing on CAC is
      proposed (Ranjan et al., 2021). Not only does CAC require less data annotations than class-specific
034
      object counting, but it can also be applied to unseen categories. Using few provided visual references
035
      as exemplars in few-shot setting, CAC obtains a generalised counting model by learning the process
036   of comparing the sample image and exemplars and regressing the feature representations.
037
038   In class-agnostic object counting, in addition to the few-shot setting, there is also a reference-less
039
      setting where no exemplars are used, and a zero-shot setting where the category names are used
      as references. Our approach focuses on all these domains, obtaining good performance for each
040
      setting. In the reference-less setting, the effectiveness of the method in (Ranjan & Nguyen, 2022)
041
      is highly dependent on the accuracy of the selected exemplars, which is difficult to guarantee. The
042   performance of (Hobley & Prisacariu, 2022) relies on a pre-trained model that is trained with a
043   large amount of data. For research related to zero-shot setting, previous work has proposed many
044   effective approaches (Xu et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2023; Kang et al., 2023; Amini-Naieni et al.,
045   2023). However, as described in (Oquab et al., 2023; Paiss et al., 2023; Zhai et al., 2022; Amini-
046   Naieni et al., 2023), general text encoding models, such as CLIP (Radford et al., 2021), lack the
047   awareness of object spatial structure. This heavily limits the performance of existing zero-shot
048   object counting methods. And in few-shot setting, many methods (Shi et al., 2022; Lin et al., 2022;
049   Djukic et al., 2023) usually take the average length and width of the bounding boxes as a fixed scale
050
      when embedding object scale information, however, many objects within the sample images are
      of varying scales, and this processing is usually not effective enough to recognise objects that are
051
      extreme in scale.
052
053   Even if a person has never seen a certain category of objects, the human eyes can easily distinguish
      its general shape and area within the field of view. This is because an object typically has great

094   Our contributions are summarized as follows: (1) We design a novel reference-less class-agnostic
095   object counting network with Adaptive Offset Deformable Convolution (AODC), for counting un-

097   tings to form a generic class-agnostic object counting network.

      2.1     C LASS - SPECIFIC O BJECT C OUNTING
102
103   Class-specific object counting focuses on counting a specific class of objects, such as crowd (Liang
104   et al., 2023; Lin & Chan, 2023; Du et al., 2023), animals (Arteta et al., 2016), or cars (Hsieh et al.,
105   2017), among which crowd counting has received the most extensive excavation. The earliest count-
106   ing methods are based on object detection (Stewart et al., 2016; Wang & Wang, 2011), where the
107   number of objects is obtained by counting the detection results. However, this kind of methods
      are less effective in identifying dense samples and require an additional object detection process.


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      To address this issue, counting methods based on density maps, which are called regression-based
109   methods, are developed and widely adopted. These methods generate a density map and sum it to
110   obtain the counting number. The model is trained by comparing the ground truth density map with
111   the predicted density map.
112
      Recent research in regression-based methods, such as (Cheng et al., 2022), utilizes locally connected
113
      multivariate Gaussian kernels as replacements for convolution filters. (Du et al., 2023) introduce
114
      domain-invariant and -specific crowd memory modules to extract disentangled domain-invariant/-
115   specific features for each image. Moreover, a recent work (Liang et al., 2023) proposes knowledge
116   transfer from a vision-language pre-trained model (CLIP) to unsupervised crowd counting tasks,
117   eliminating the need for density map annotation.
118
119
      2.2   C LASS - AGNOSTIC O BJECT C OUNTING
120
121   Class-agnostic object counting (CAC) is first studied in (Lu et al., 2019) and has gained significant
122   attention with the proposal of a challenged dataset (Ranjan et al., 2021). In contrast to class-specific
123   object counting task, CAC can exhibit great generality by using a few exemplars to count objects of
124   unseen categories.
125
      Several terrific methods have been proposed for CAC. As the first novel in the study of CAC, GMN
126   (Lu et al., 2019) integrates support and query features, subsequently applying regression to forecast
127   a density map from this amalgamation. Over the next few years, many methods in this area were
128   proposed (Ranjan et al., 2021; Shi et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2022; Djukic et al., 2023; Wang et al.,
129   2024), leading to significant development of CAC tasks. The most recent approach, CACViT (Wang
130   et al., 2024) proposes a ViT-based extract-and-match paradigm for CAC, and introduces aspect-
131   ratio-aware scale embedding and magnitude embedding to compensate for the information loss.
132   For the setting without references, RepRPN-C (Ranjan & Nguyen, 2022) proposes a two-stage
133   counter. It consists of a novel region proposal network for finding exemplars from repetitive ob-
134   ject classes and a density estimation network to estimate the density map corresponding to each
135   exemplar. RCC (Hobley & Prisacariu, 2022) is based on the confirmed intuition that well-trained
136   vision transformer features are both general enough and contextually aware enough to implicitly
137   understand the underlying basis of counting. It is important to note that both CounTR (Liu et al.,
138   2022) and LOCA are tested in a setting without references and achieve positive performance.
139   The zero-shot object counting is first investigated by (Xu et al., 2023), which proposes a reference
140   exemplar using the existing few-shot object counting methods and uses them for further counting.
141   following this work, several brand new zero-shot object counting methods have been proposed (Jiang
142   et al., 2023; Kang et al., 2023; Amini-Naieni et al., 2023) and have performed well.
143
144   2.3   G ENERALIZED L OSS
145
146   In order to improve the performance of crowd counting methods, (Wan et al., 2021) proposes a gen-
147   eralised loss function based on unbalanced optimal transport. In class-agnostic object counting, (Lin
148   et al., 2022) adopts this loss function and proposes a scale-sensitive generalised loss function that
149   can be applied to different loss calculation methods depending on the object categories of different
150   scales.

257
258   Objects from other categories are often counted together in reference-less setting because category
259   information is not provided. The text provided in zero-shot setting can be used to highlight the cate-
260
      gory objects that need to be counted and avoid interference from other categories. In order to embed
      the category name C in textual form into the feature space, we use a pre-trained CLIP (Radford
261
      et al., 2021) model to transform C into a feature vector FC . FC is fed into several cross-attention
262
      blocks together with F to complete the fusion of feature information. The structure of the cross-
263   attention block is shown in Fig.3, and the difference with the structure of the self-attention block is
264   that the cross-attention block has one more cross-attention computation process in the middle part.
265   F is used as Query and FC is used as Key and Value in the cross-attention.

532
533
      We present a novel generic network for class-agnostic object counting task with adaptive offset
534   deformable convolution (AODC), which is initially designed for solving the counting task on the
535   reference-less setting and can be further generalized to the zero-shot and few-shot settings. AODC
536   obtains the scale offsets of the object corresponding to each position by using 4D convolution on
537   the self-similarity maps of the image features, and using the obtained offsets to perform deformable
538   convolution on the image features to capture the entire object, which in turn is regressed to obtain
539   an accurate predicted density map. Experiments are conducted on multiple datasets and the results
      demonstrate that we achieve state-of-the-art performance on all the three settings.

      For the multi-layer features from the pre-trained ResNet-50 backbone, we select different combina-

734
      into four combinations and display the experimental results in the table. Among them, the second
735   layer of features is necessary because our method is carried out on the spatial size of this layer. The
736   experimental results show that combining the features of layers 2 and 3 gives the best performance,
737   while adding the fourth   layer leads to a decrease in performance. This may be due to the fact that
                              1                                                  1
738   too much spatial information is already lost in layer 4 and the oversized dimensions instead contain
739   redundant information that is not needed for the counting task, making the model performance neg-
740   atively affected. Dropping the layer 4 features not only optimizes performance, but also keeps the
741   computational complexity at a relatively acceptable level.
742   Two hyperparameters ε and τ are defined in our quoted generalized loss function, and in order to

745   performance of the model does not fluctuate too much with parameter variations for both ε and τ ,
746   and most of the value variations are within the range of 0.5. This indicates that the training effect
747   of the model is insensitive to parameter changes in the case that ε and τ do not take particularly
748   extreme values, ensuring the robustness of our method.
