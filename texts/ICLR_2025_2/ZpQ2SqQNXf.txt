                                                   A BSTRACT
011
012
               Language models are trained with teacher forcing but are used autoregressively,
               so errors accumulate as more tokens are generated. This issue is well-studied but
013
               remains a fundamental problem that harms generation quality. Building on past
014
               work, we take the perspective that error accumulation is reflected in the model’s
015            entropy, so we can better understand and address it through the lens of entropy
016            calibration. A language model is entropy calibrated if its entropy over genera-
017            tions, i.e. its confidence, matches the log loss it incurs on actual text. First, we
018            find that models are indeed miscalibrated in practice: for base models across a
019            range of sizes, entropy per step increases as more tokens are generated, leading to
020            generations becoming incoherent over time. On the other hand, after instruction
021            tuning, the largest models now have too little entropy (i.e. are overconfident), lead-
022            ing to a lack of diversity in model outputs. From a theoretical perspective, entropy
023
               calibration is difficult to attain because it is a global property of the entire genera-
               tion process, which has an exponentially large output space. Per-step adjustments
024
               are tractable but fail to preserve the model’s log loss, while global adjustments
025
               preserve log loss but are intractable. Our main theoretical contribution is to pro-
026            pose future entropy scaling, an adjustment to the next token probabilities that uses
027            information about the future entropy of each token, i.e. the average entropy of
028            continuations from that token. With additional assumptions, we prove that this
029            adjustment calibrates the model while preserving log loss. While future entropy
030            estimation is expensive, this result suggests that calibration and stabilization of
031            the entropy should be possible without trading off model quality.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
035
      Modern language models are trained with teacher forcing and achieve very low log loss when pre-
036
      dicting one word at a time. However, when deployed, they are primarily used autoregressively, and
037
      low log loss does not guarantee strong autoregressive performance because errors accumulate over
038   time as the model conditions on its own outputs. As a result, practitioners use various sampling
039   tricks (e.g. temperature reduction, distribution truncation) to stabilize generation (Holtzman et al.,
040   2020; Welleck et al., 2024). These tricks are applied ad hoc and it is not always clear when or why
041   they are necessary.
042
      In this paper, we build on the work of Braverman et al. (2020) and provide theory and experiments to
043
      better understand language model sampling through the lens of calibration. We say that a language
044   model is entropy calibrated if its entropy over generations, i.e. its confidence, matches the log loss
045   it incurs on actual text in expectation:
046

049   the prompt, Y is the response, and Hp̂ (Ŷ | X) is the entropy of p̂’s generation Ŷ given the prompt
050   X. If p̂ has at most ε KL divergence with p∗ , calibration can also be thought of as requiring that the

052   paper is that many errors and instabilities in autoregressive generation are reflected in the model’s
053   entropy deviating from that of human text. Accordingly, sampling methods are effective if they
      correct miscalibration while preserving model quality. Using this framework, we find the following:

076   We analyze current language models, finding that they are miscalibrated across model sizes:
077   In expectation, the entropy rate (i.e. entropy per time step) of human text is constant or decreases
078   slightly over the length of a document (Genzel & Charniak, 2002; Verma et al., 2023). In contrast,
079
      (a) For base models, entropy rate increases as more tokens are generated. As a result, outputs
080
          become incoherent over time. This result holds across model sizes: compared to weaker models,
081
          stronger models start at a lower entropy but still deviate upward at a similar rate (Figure 1).
082
          The fact that models become incoherent over time has been observed in past work (Holtzman
083
          et al., 2020), and practitioners use various truncation techniques to address this issue. We ana-
084       lyze the effect of these techniques on calibration and find that decreasing the sampling temper-
085       ature shifts the entropy curve downward while also decreasing the slope; other truncation meth-
086       ods have a similar effect (Figure 4). However, as has been observed in prior work (Hashimoto
087       et al., 2019; Zhang et al., 2021; Pillutla et al., 2021), this stabilization comes at the cost of model
088       degradation in the form of increased log loss and reduced diversity (Figure 5).
089   (b) After instruction tuning, smaller models still have too much entropy, but larger models now
090       have too little entropy (Figure 1). Miscalibration in the form of entropy being too low results
091       in generations lacking in diversity and sometimes becoming repetitive over time. Furthermore,
092       even for models whose entropy seems stable on average, individual generations still sometimes
093       derail and are just counterbalanced by low-entropy generations (Figure 2).
094       Existing methods are designed to decrease entropy, so they are not well-suited for calibrating
095       large instruction-tuned models (Figure 6).

101   ing the entire generation process as a whole (e.g. with global temperature scaling) preserves log loss
102   but is intractable because the output space is exponential (Braverman et al., 2020).
103   We prove that with additional assumptions, we can tractably calibrate entropy while preserving log

198   Error accumulation in autoregressive generation. The idea that autoregressive models accumu-
199   late errors during generation is well-known. Williams & Zipser (1989) introduce the term “teacher
200   forcing” to refer to the technique of training neural models on only one generation step at a time,
201   in contrast to autoregressive generation where the model must generate multiple steps in succes-
202   sion. To address this mismatch, also known as “exposure bias,” a variety of papers propose alternate
203   sequence-level training objectives (Ranzato et al., 2016; Welleck et al., 2020; Deng et al., 2020), but
204   teacher forcing remains the dominant training method.

      Distribution truncation. To stabilize autoregressive generation, a large number of truncation-
      based methods have been developed as alternatives to temperature scaling, including top-k sam-
208
      pling (Fan et al., 2018), nucleus (top-p) sampling (Holtzman et al., 2020), epsilon/eta sampling (He-
209
      witt et al., 2022), and typical sampling (Meister et al., 2023). However, increased quality from
210   truncation comes at the cost of diversity, and Hashimoto et al. (2019), Zhang et al. (2021), and Pil-
211   lutla et al. (2021) propose methods to evaluate how well these methods perform this tradeoff. Basu
212   et al. (2021) analyze how truncation parameters affect the entropy of the resulting sample, and use
213   these insights to propose a method which dynamically sets these parameters during generation. Fi-
214   nally, Freitag et al. (2023), Shi et al. (2024), and Welleck et al. (2024) survey and compare sampling
215   techniques across different models, datasets, and tasks, finding that the relative ranking between
      them is highly dependent on the setting.

      Calibration. Model calibration is most commonly studied in binary classification, with some clas-
217   sic algorithms including binning, Platt scaling, and isotonic regression (Platt, 1999; Zadrozny &
218   Elkan, 2002; Guo et al., 2017; Kumar et al., 2019). Entropy calibration can be thought of as a re-
219   laxation of multiclass calibration, where each class corresponds to a possible output string and the
220   number of classes is exponential in the output length. Relaxing multiclass calibration to calibration
221   of a loss function is related to the work of Zhao et al. (2021), who use a similar idea to define a cal-
222   ibration notion for multiclass classifiers in decision theoretic settings. In contrast with our setting,
223   they consider settings like image classification where the number of classes is not exponential.

308   tropy calibration error, and sampling methods can be better understood in terms of how they affect
309   miscalibration. With this insight, we find the following:

      Diversity has been found to be especially important when solving difficult tasks that require picking
379   from multiple generations (Li et al., 2022), generating synthetic data (Wang et al., 2023), or improv-
380   ing outputs by synthesizing multiple responses (Wang et al., 2024). Given that existing sampling
381   methods are designed to decrease entropy rather than increase it, this situation suggests that we are
382   in need of methods that calibrate overconfident models.
383
384   Sampling parameters should be chosen to stabilize entropy. In Figure 4, we plot the entropy
385   per time step of TinyLlama v1.1 (Zhang et al., 2024) on wikitext-103 (Merity et al., 2017)
386   with various sampling techniques applied, including temperature scaling, epsilon sampling (Hewitt
387   et al., 2022), top-p (nucleus) sampling (Holtzman et al., 2020), and top-k sampling (Fan et al., 2018).
388   We find that for every method, adjusting the sampling parameter to make truncation more aggressive
389   shifts the model’s entropy downward and decreases the slope. If our goal is for entropy to be stable
390
      over time, we should then choose the parameter which adjusts the slope to be close to zero.
391   We then apply the most stable temperature setting for TinyLlama v1.1 (α = 0.1, or temperature
392   0.909) to the larger Llama models (Figure 5). We find that because large and small models are
393   similarly miscalibrated, the same temperature setting works well for all four models. The downside
394   is that this stabilization comes at the cost of increased log loss due to reduced diversity, reproducing
395   similar findings in past work (Hashimoto et al., 2019; Zhang et al., 2021; Pillutla et al., 2021).
396   For instruction-tuned models, on the other hand, which have too little entropy, one might be tempted
397   to calibrate by increasing the temperature. While this approach can calibrate the model on average,
398   it does so by causing some generations to derail upward while other generations remain low entropy
399   (Figure 6). This degradation is not reflected in the log loss: log loss actually improves when in-
400   creasing the temperature (from 2.29 to 2.28), due to the model originally having too little diversity.
401   One approach in this setting might involve first increasing temperature to increase diversity, and then
402
      calibrating the entropy back down with a procedure that preserves diversity. Unfortunately, existing
      entropy reduction techniques do not preserve diversity.

      5.2   E XPERIMENTS
475
476   While future entropy scaling provably preserves log loss, the most straightforward implementation
477   involves averaging over multiple samples per candidate token, which is expensive (Algorithm 2).
478   Nonetheless, we provide evidence that using future entropy is necessary empirically to avoid model
479   degradation when calibrating, suggesting that efficient approximations of future entropy scaling can
480   improve upon existing sampling techniques.

482   compare it to the histogram for high probability tokens (p > 0.01) (Figure 7). For 512 prefixes
483   from wikitext-103, we estimate the 32-step future entropy (averaged over 32 trajectories) of
484   the top 512 tokens of TinyLlama v1.1. To interpret future entropy as an indicator for derailing,
485   we define the baseline future entropy of a prefix as the average future entropy for high-probability
      tokens (which we assume are unlikely to derail the model). Then, for a given prefix, a token derails

526       erate probability to a token that is valid but raises the difficulty of the subsequent generation.

528       in Middle Scots; more examples are in the appendix. In these cases, future entropy serves the

531   6   C ONCLUSION
532
533
      In this paper, we provided theory, algorithms, and analysis to better understand the entropy cal-
534   ibration of language models. Entropy miscalibration is a fundamental problem in autoregressive
535   generation: theoretically, even very accurate models can have entropy deviating over time due to
536   error accumulation, and empirically, large models are just as miscalibrated as smaller ones. Exist-
537   ing sampling methods, while beneficial, are myopic, hurt diversity, and are ill-suited for calibrating
538   overconfident models. On the other hand, our analysis of future entropy scaling suggests calibration
539   is possible without these tradeoffs. We hope that our work inspires new calibration techniques that
      improve the quality and diversity of language model generations.

952   Below, we provide examples from TinyLlama v1.1 applied to wikitext-103. Specifically,
953   we compute the 64- or 128-step future entropy for the top 32 next tokens for each prefix, by aver-
954   aging over 32 trajectories sampled with temperature 0.909. We then identify examples where the
955   α = 0.1 temperature adjustment differs substantially from the α = 0.1 future entropy adjustment.
956   We identify the following categories:
957
958   (a) Model errors: the model often assigns moderate probability to incorrect continuations. Many of
959       these errors are due to choosing an alternate tokenization, inducing sudden topic shifts, choosing
960       tokens that only work in other contexts, or assigning too much or too little probability to ellipses
961       or newline characters. Some prefixes are also more difficult than others. As a result of model
962       errors, temperature scaling must truncate valid tokens with low probability if it also wants to
963       truncate invalid ones with moderate probability.
964
965   (b) Increasing generation difficulty: in other cases, the model assigns high probability to continu-
966
          ations that are valid but make derailing more likely in the future. Some cases include tokens that
          induce creative writing, or tokens that threaten a sudden topic change if not handled correctly.
967
          Lookahead is necessary to detect these cases and avoid generating them.
