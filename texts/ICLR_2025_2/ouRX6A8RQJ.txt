009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               Large Language Models (LLMs) have shown impressive performance in complex
012            reasoning tasks through the use of Chain-of-Thought (CoT) reasoning, allowing
013            models to break down problems into manageable sub-tasks. However, existing
014            CoT evaluation techniques either require annotated CoT data or fall short in accu-
015            rately assessing intermediate reasoning steps, leading to high rates of false posi-
016            tives. In this paper, we formalize CoT reasoning in LLMs through an information-
017            theoretic lens. Specifically, our framework quantifies the ‘information gain’ at
018            each reasoning step, enabling the identification of failure modes in LLMs with-
019            out the need for expensive annotated datasets. We demonstrate the efficacy of our
020
               approach through extensive experiments on toy and GSM-8K data, where it signif-
               icantly outperforms existing outcome-based methods by providing more accurate
021
               insights into model performance on individual tasks.

025   1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027   Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated remarkable capabilities across a wide range of
028   tasks, from complex reasoning to code generation (Chowdhery et al., 2024; OpenAI et al., 2024;
029   Bubeck et al., 2023; Anil et al., 2023). Many of these advances can be attributed to Chain-of-
030   Thought (CoT) reasoning (Wei et al., 2024; Nye et al., 2021; Li et al., 2024), which involves break-
031   ing down complex problems into a series of intermediate steps, mirroring human-like reasoning
032
      processes. The success of CoT reasoning, particularly in domains such as mathematics, logic, and
      multi-step decision-making, has led researchers and developers to incorporate CoT-like features di-
033
      rectly into model training, i.e. the FLAN family of models (Chung et al., 2022; Wei et al., 2022).
034
035   This paper introduces a new formal framework for analyzing CoT in LLMs. We provide a rigorous
036   method grounded in information theory, to evaluate the quality of each step in a model’s reasoning
037   process, thus offering insights beyond simple accuracy metrics to identify areas for improvement.
038   Previous work in this area has proposed “Process Supervision” (Lightman et al., 2023), which re-
039   quires expensive, human-annotated step-by-step data. While effective, this approach is often imprac-
040   tical due to the high cost and effort of creating large-scale annotated datasets. In turn, alternative
041   methods have recently been proposed, such as outcome reward modelling (Havrilla et al., 2024) or
042   the Math-Shepherd (Wang et al., 2024b). Both these approaches avoid reliance on annotated step-
043   wise CoT data by instead modelling the correctness of each step based on the correctness of final
044
      outputs. However, as we demonstrate in this paper, these methods can be unsound for detecting
      incorrect reasoning steps and can thus lead to a high false-positive rate in certain scenarios.
045
046   To address these shortcomings, we employ an information-theoretic approach, grounded in the fol-
047   lowing key insight: Each correct step in a reasoning process should provide valuable and relevant
048   information that aids in predicting the final correct outcome. Building on this insight, we develop a
049   framework to quantify the “information gain” after each sub-task in the reasoning process, without
050
      the need for step-by-step annotations. This enables us to detect sub-tasks that fail to contribute mean-
      ingful information toward the correct solution, signalling potential errors or irrelevant steps in the
051
      model’s reasoning. In addition, we also introduce a practical algorithm to assess LLM performance
052
      across various sub-tasks within a Chain-of-Thought (CoT) reasoning process.
053
      The key contributions of this paper are as follows:

065   2     P ROPOSED F RAMEWORK : S ETUP AND N OTATION
066
067   Before diving into our framework, we first provide a high-level overview and notation on how LLM
068
      generation will be treated throughout this paper. This will allow us to set the foundation for describ-
      ing our information-theoretic framework. In particular, following the approach in González & Nori
069
      (2023), we view LLMs as abstract execution machines with a natural language interface. From this
070
      perspective, prompts are designed to solve specific problems (e.g., mathematical or logical prob-
071   lems), and the LLM processes the information in the prompt to generate an output.
072
073   We now define the notation for a typical prompt as a combination of two components:
074
            1. An initial state, represented by a random variable X0 ∈ X , denotes information provided
075            in the prompt that the LLM must operate on to obtain the queried information.
076
            2. A task λ ∈ Υ (e.g., addition followed by multiplication) which encapsulates how the LLM
077
               should process information in X0 .
078
079   Given the prompt, defined as a tuple (X0 , λ), the state X1 represents the result of applying task λ
080   to the initial state X0 . Formally, we denote this using the update mapping Λ : X × Υ → X which
081   outputs the updated state X1 by applying the task λ on X0 , i.e. X1 = Λ(X0 , λ). This updated state
082   is then used to obtain the final output, denoted by Y ∈ X , by extracting only the information in X1
083   which is relevant to the queried final answer. This notation defines a prompt that instructs a model
084   to process information drawn from some initial distribution p(X0 ) (e.g., mathematical problems).
085   Let us use the following simple example to illustrate the notation:
086
          Prompt:“James has 3 apples and Abbey has 9. How many apples do the two have in total?”
087
088   Here, using the above notation, the initial state x0 denotes the information “James has 3 apples;
089   Abbey has 9 apples”, and λ denotes the addition task. Next, x1 = Λ(x0 , λ) represents the updated
090   information after correctly performing the addition operation, i.e. x1 =“James has 3 apples; Abbey
091
      has 9 apples; The two have 12 apples in total”. The final output, y, is then obtained by simply
      extracting the total number of apples from x1 , i.e. “The two have 12 apples in total”1 . With this
092
      basic notation established, we now consider compositions of tasks, enabling us to formalize the
093
      Chain of Thought (CoT) process in LLMs.

      2.2   P RIMITIVE TASKS
132
133   In this subsection, we introduce the notion of primitive tasks which form the basic building blocks of
134   any task. Intuitively, our formulation is reminiscent of ideas from linear algebra, where basis vectors
135   form the basic building blocks of a vector space. In our case, any task λ ∈ Υ can be expressed as
136
      a sequence of primitive tasks. This decomposition will allow us to establish which tasks the model
      could have learned from the training data. For example, if a specific primitive task is not available
137
      in the LLM training data, it would be impossible for the model to execute any instructions which
138
      involve this primitive task correctly. With this in mind, we now introduce this concept formally:
139
      Definition 2.2 (Primitive tasks). We say that a set of tasks Γ ⊆ Υ is primitive if, for any task λ ∈ Υ,
140
      there exists a unique subset {λi }ki=1 ⊆ Γ such that λ = λ1 ◦ · · · ◦ λk .
141
142   Note that the decomposition is not unique but the set of components is. In some cases, there may
143   exist distinct permutations of primitive tasks which compose to yield the same task as is common in
144   many associative operations. As an example, in the context of mathematical problem-solving, the
145   basic arithmetic operation could be considered primitive. The composition of these primitive tasks
146   allows us to construct extremely complex operations. Just like in linear algebra, we define the span
147   of these tasks as the set obtained by their sequential applications.
148   Definition 2.3 (Span of tasks). Let Φ ⊆ Υ be a set of tasks, then:
149
                          Span(Φ) = {λ1 ◦ . . . ◦ λk : λi ∈ Φ for 1 ≤ i ≤ k, k ∈ Z>0 }.
150
151
      The set Span(Φ) comprises all the tasks that can be applied by composing sub-tasks in the set Φ.
152   This means that any compositionally consistent update rule Λ which is well-defined on the set of
153   tasks Φ will also be well-defined on Span(Φ). However, this Λ may still be ill-defined for any task
154   not in this span. This limitation is captured by the concept of unidentifiability, which plays a central
155   role in determining the boundaries of what a model can and cannot infer.
156
157   2.3   U NIDENTIFIABILITY
158   The unidentifiability of tasks forms a key part of our framework. It directly addresses the fundamen-
159   tal challenge that models, such as LLMs, face when dealing with unseen tasks. If a task λ lies outside
160   of Span(Φ), the span of tasks the model has been trained on, then the model cannot be expected to
161   infer or apply it correctly. In other words, the model’s capacity is constrained by the identifiability of
      tasks within the training set. This notion and formalization of unidentifiability allows us to highlight


                                                          3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      a critical limitation in the generalization of models: tasks not encountered during training cannot be
163   reliably executed, as they remain beyond the model’s learned task span. More formally:
164
      Definition 2.4 (Unidentifiability). Let Φ ⊆ Υ be any set of tasks, then a tasks λ is said to be
165
      unidentifiable in Φ iff, λ ̸∈ Span(Φ).
166
167   Remark In practice, the concept of unidentifiability may depend on the initial state X0 . For
168   instance, an LLM might accurately perform addition for 2-digit numbers but fail with 10-digit
169   numbers (Razeghi et al., 2022). Our framework can be extended to account for such cases by
170   explicitly incorporating the distribution of initial states into the notion of identifiability. For ex-
171   ample, addition could be considered unidentifiable when the initial state distribution is p(X0 |
172   X0 includes 10-digit numbers). However, for simplicity, we keep this distributional dependence
173   implicit in the definition provided earlier.
174   With this general framework in place, we can now turn this theoretical foundation into a practical
175   algorithm for detecting unidentifiable sub-tasks. Specifically, we explore how the notion of uniden-
176   tifiability can be combined with information-theoretic approaches to detect failure points in LLMs.

180   tifiability of intermediate sub-tasks in a model’s CoT reasoning process. This would subsequently
181   allow us to detect any sub-task at which a model’s CoT reasoning process starts to diverge from the
182   ground truth, thereby providing insights into how the model can be improved. For example, suppose
183   we are in a setting where the “addition” operation is unidentifiable, then we could further improve
184   the model’s mathematical reasoning by fine-tuning it on the addition operation.

305   4     R ELATED WORKS
306
      Evaluation of CoT reasoning Several recent works propose methodologies for evaluating CoT
307
      reasoning (Wei et al., 2024; Havrilla et al., 2024; Li et al., 2023; Joshi et al., 2023; Nguyen et al.,
308
      2024; Wang et al., 2024a; Yu et al., 2024; Xie et al., 2024). For example, Li et al. (2023) verifies
309   individual steps in a model’s CoT reasoning by generating multiple LLM responses per prompt and
310   comparing correct responses with incorrect ones. Similarly, Wang et al. (2024b;c) use a fine-tuned
311   LLM to decode multiple reasoning paths from each step and check the correctness of these reasoning
312   paths. However, as we show in our experiments, approaches which simply rely on the correctness
313   of the final output are not sound in general and can lead to false positives. Moreover, these solutions
314   may not be plausible for problems of high difficulty where correct LLM responses might be scarce.
315
316   Formalising CoT framework The formalisation of LLM reasoning remains an active area of re-
317   search. Most notably González & Nori (2023) introduces a formal framework for LLMs and is a
318   key source of inspiration behind our formalism. Additionally, Feng et al. (2023) theoretically ex-
319
      amines the expressivity of LLMs with CoT in solving mathematical and decision-making problems,
      focusing on the transformer architecture’s implications on accuracy. Besides this, Xu et al. (2024)
320
      provides a formal definition of hallucinations, but does not consider CoT reasoning specifically.
321
322   Reward modelling One notable line of work known as outcome-based reward models (ORM)
323   (Cobbe et al., 2021; Havrilla et al., 2024; Lightman et al., 2023) predicts the probability of reach-
      ing the correct final answer given a model’s intermediate CoT steps. While ORMs do not require


                                                         6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      demonstrations of correct intermediate steps, we show in Section 5 that this approach is not sound
325   for detecting errors in a model’s CoT reasoning. Another related method is step-wise ORM (SORM)
326   Havrilla et al. (2024) which estimates the probability of an ‘optimal’ model reaching a correct an-
327   swer, given the CoT reasoning of our model of interest. However, unlike our approach, SORM
328   requires training a model which is larger and more capable than our base model.
329
      Process-based reward modelling (PRMs) (Lightman et al., 2023; Uesato et al., 2022) is an alternative
330
      approach which directly predicts the correctness of intermediate CoT reasoning steps. Likewise,
331   various other approaches rely on annotated CoT datasets for benchmarking (Jacovi et al., 2024; Yu
332   et al., 2024; Amini et al., 2019; Liu et al., 2020; Xi et al., 2024; Nguyen et al., 2024; Xie et al., 2024;
333   McLeish et al., 2024). While these benchmarks and methodologies can be valuable for improving
334   LLM reasoning, collecting annotated data can be very costly and is not readily scalable to other
335   tasks. Unlike these methods, our approach computes the information gain at each step, providing a
336   richer measure of LLM performance without requiring any human-annotated CoT data.

488   To evaluate our method on a complex dataset, we conducted experiments on GSM-8K (Cobbe et al.,
489
      2021), controlling specific factors for more interpretable results.
490   We begin by using GPT-4 (OpenAI et al., 2024) to generate answers for GSM-8K questions where
491   the “multiplication” operation is always done incorrectly, while all other operations are correct.
492   Next, we filtered the dataset to ensure that “multiplication”, “subtraction”, and “addition” never
493   appeared together within the same Chain of Thought (CoT) solution. In particular, we ensured in
494   our setting that, all incorrect final answers included both “multiplication” and “subtraction”, whereas
495
      correct final answers did not involve either operation. This introduces a spurious correlation between
      “subtraction” and wrong answers.
496
497   In this setup, we mainly focused on evaluating ORM and our proposed method, as Math-Shepherd
498   (with the same completer) fails trivially under these conditions. Specifically, “multiplication” is
499   inherently unidentifiable, since any CoT containing “multiplication” negates the influence of other
500   sub-tasks by design. Further details on the experimental setup can be found in Appendix B.3.

522   6       D ISCUSSION AND L IMITATIONS
523
524   In this paper, we introduce a novel information-theoretic approach for evaluating Chain-of-Thought
525
      (CoT) reasoning in large language models (LLMs) without the need for annotated intermediate steps.
      We present a comprehensive framework for modeling the CoT process, and the results demonstrate
526
      the effectiveness of our algorithm in identifying erroneous reasoning steps across diverse experi-
527
      mental settings. We consistently outperform existing baselines, including Outcome Reward Models
528   (ORM) (Havrilla et al., 2024) and Math-Shepherd (Wang et al., 2024b) as shown in our extensive
529   experimental section. However, it’s important to note that that are some limitations to our approach.
530
531
      For example, our method necessitates additional training of the supervisor model, which can be
      computationally demanding. Future research could investigate the use of in-context learning tech-
532
      niques to estimate information gain, potentially reducing the need for extra training and enhancing
533
      both the accessibility and efficiency of the approach. Secondly, sample-wise detection introduces
534   further challenges that may lead to erroneous conclusions. A language model may occasionally ar-
535   rive at the correct answer by chance, even if a particular sub-task is unidentifiable. Although this
536   occurrence should not significantly impact the overall task-wise information gain, it could result
537   in inaccurate outcomes for sample-wise information gain in such ‘lucky’ cases. Finally, while our
538   method does not require correctness labels for every step, we still need to categorize each step ac-
539   cording to its respective sub-task. However, this limitation is not unique to our model, as both ORM
      and Math-Shepherd also rely on such labels to draw sub-task-specific conclusions.

      B.1     T OY DATA E XPERIMENTS
852
853   In this section, we describe the exact procedure used to generate the toy data for training and eval-
854   uating the models in our experiments. The dataset is constructed through five sequential operations
855   (or tasks) applied to an initial state z0 , where each task λi generates an intermediate state zi . Both
856   correct and incorrect examples were generated, with incorrect examples created by introducing
857   random noise or permutations into the transformations.
858
      The data was used to represent models LLM1 , LLM2 , ..., LLM5 , each corresponding to a setting
859   where a specific task λi was partially corrupted to simulate an unidentifiable task for that model.
860
861   B.1.1    DATA G ENERATION TASKS
862
863   For each prompt, an initial 5-element vector z0 was randomly sampled, and we use the notation z0 [i]
      to denote the i’th component of this vector. Next, the following tasks were applied sequentially:

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


864
      Task λ1 : Pairwise Swapping
865
866           • Correct Mapping: The first and second elements, as well as the third and fourth elements
867             of z0 , are swapped:
868
                                       z1 [0], z1 [1], z1 [2], z1 [3] = z0 [1], z0 [0], z0 [3], z0 [2]
869
870           • Incorrect Mapping: The entire vector is shuffled randomly.
871
872   Task λ2 : Cumulative Summation
873
874           • Correct Mapping: The first three elements of z1 are replaced by their cumulative sum, and
875             the fourth and fifth elements are swapped:
876                             z2 = [z2 [0], z2 [0] + z2 [1], z2 [0] + z2 [1] + z2 [2], z1 [4], z1 [3]]
877
878           • Incorrect Mapping: Each element of z1 is perturbed by adding a random integer between
879
                10 and 99:
880             z2 [i] = z1 [i] + Ui   for each i where Ui is a randomly sampled integer between 10 and 99
881
882   Task λ3 : Reverse and Cumulative Sum
883
884           • Correct Mapping: The first three elements of z2 are reversed, and the last two elements are
885             replaced by their cumulative sum:
886                                       z3 = [z2 [2], z2 [1], z2 [0], z2 [3], z2 [3] + z2 [4]]
887
888           • Incorrect Mapping: As with task λ2 , each element of z2 is perturbed by adding a random
889             integer between 10 and 99.

      Task λ4 : Sorting and Elementwise Multiplication
892
              • Correct Mapping: The vector z3 is sorted, and the first four elements are replaced by

1260

1261

1262
1263   Consider the following model generation:

       1. 3x
1266

1267

1272   dure for the Math Shepherd results:
1273
1274
             1. Step-wise Completion: We cut off the generation after each step in the reasoning process.

1275
                10 completions using the Llama-3-8b model.
1276
1277         2. Multiple Completions: At each cut-off point, the Llama-3-8b model is tasked with com-
1278
                pleting the remaining steps of the chain of reasoning. For each step, 10 independent com-

1279
1280         3. Proportion of Correct Completions: For each cut-off point, we compute the proportion of
1281            correct completions. This proportion gives insight into how likely the model is to complete
1282
                the remaining steps of reasoning correctly, starting from the intermediate point. For ex-

1283
                completions successfully compute 3x + 2y = 335306.0.
1284
1285   In this way, Math-Shepherd quantifies the model’s ability to continue reasoning correctly at each
1286   intermediate stage.

       B.3.3    E STIMATING THE INFORMATION GAIN
1470
1471   Our procedure for estimating the information gain is very similar to that described in Section B.1.4.
1472   However, in this setup, there is no fixed ordering of tasks for all prompts. For instance, in some
1473   prompts, the first step might be addition while in others it might be multiplication. To estimate
1474   information gain for a specific task such as addition, we follow these steps:
1475
1476
               • We first consider all prompts which contain addition as a sub-task.
1477           • Next, for these prompts we estimate the E[log p(Y | XTM+ )] term, where T+ denotes the
1478             step at which addition is executed.
1479           • Similarly, we estimate the E[log p(Y | XTM+ −1 )] term, where T+ − 1 denotes the step im-
1480             mediately preceding addition.
1481
               • The information gain for addition is then estimated as the difference between these terms
1482
1483                                    E[log p(Y | XTM+ )] − E[log p(Y | XTM+ −1 )].
