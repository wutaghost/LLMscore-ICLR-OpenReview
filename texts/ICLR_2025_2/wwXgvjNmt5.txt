011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            We introduce the Multimodal Academic Cover (MAC) benchmark to address the
014            challenges of Large Multimodal Models (LMMs) in understanding and generating
015
               academic journal covers. While LMMs have demonstrated significant progress
               in creative arts and everyday applications, their capabilities in comprehending
016
               complex academic visuals and narratives remain underexplored. MAC comprises
017
               a collection of 5,872 cover images, accompanying cover stories, and associated
018            articles from 40 prominent academic journals, providing a rich dataset for evalu-
019            ation. We design bidirectional generative tasks—Image2Text and Text2Imag to
020            assess authenticity and creativity in generating cover images and stories. Current
021            LMMs, including DALL·E 3, GPT-4V, Gemini, CogView-3, GLM-4V, LLaVA,
022            LLaMA-adapter, and MiniGPT4, are evaluated on this benchmark. Furthermore,
023            we propose Multimodal Agent Linkage (MAL), a novel method to enhance concep-
024            tual comprehension within a long-context window. In-context learning techniques,
025            such as few-shot learning, are also explored to improve the effectiveness of LMMs.
026
               All benchmarks, prompts, and codes will be released publicly.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Emergent opportunities have occurred with the advent of Large Multimodal Models (LMMs) (Radford
031   et al., 2021; OpenAI, 2023a;b). LMMs have revolutionized the interaction with integrated visual and
032   textual content, impacting sectors ranging from creative arts to daily applications. They have been
033   argued to achieve expert levels of such scenarios, e.g., painting images based on text (Morris et al.,
034   2023). However, their ability to comprehend and create scientific content remains a question.
035
      Researchers have explored evaluating and improving scientific comprehension of large language
036   models (LLMs). Galactica (Taylor et al., 2022), for instance, trains on a vast array of scientific
037   materials, enabling it to effectively store, combine, and reason about scientific knowledge. Addi-
038   tionally, SCITUNE (Horawalavithana et al., 2023) introduces a tuning framework to enhance AI’s
039   understanding of complex scientific instructions across different modes. Similarly, Sci-CoT (Ma
040   et al., 2023) proposes a two-stage framework that separates the processes of generating explanations
041   and inferring answers, thus making better use of explanations during answer inference.
042   However, it’s worth noting that prior works primarily focus on generating textual responses to
043   scientific queries, often overlooking the visual aspect of scientific communication. Just as language
044   plays a crucial role in conveying complex ideas, visual representations are equally important for
045   deepening understanding. Visuals can distill and clarify abstract scientific concepts, making them
046   more accessible and intuitive. For example, in the case of the Theory of Relativity, the visualization
047   of the train experiment enhances comprehension by vividly demonstrating complex ideas like relative
048   motion, time dilation, and length contraction. These visualizations enable a more intuitive grasp of
049   the theory’s principles, which might be challenging to convey through text alone.
050   In this paper, we attempt to see how far the LMMs are from human experts by benchmarking the
051   most advanced LMMs in multimodal scientific concepts. In particular, we evaluate the capabilities
052   of LMMs to understand and generate academic journal covers. Epitomized by publications like
053   Cell (Cell, 2023), Nature (Nature, 2023), and Science (Science, 2023), the covers provide not only
      a visual and textual summary but also a window into the depth and context of the research they

085   To this end, we introduce the Multimodal Academic Cover (MAC) benchmark, a comprehensive
086   collection sourced from eminent academic journals including Cell, Nature, Science, and their sub-
087
      sidiary publications.1 MAC consists of 5872 journal issues, each with a complete group of cover
      images, stories, and relevant articles. As new scientific research continuously emerges and humanity’s
088
      exploration of the unknown never ceases, our benchmark collection will keep growing and including
089
      cutting-edge research findings, ensuring that the benchmark remains challenging for emerging LMMs.
090   Two more challenging subsets are also provided, MAC-Recent (940 issues) and MAC-Latest (50
091   issues), split by the publication time. Furthermore, we propose two bidirectional generative tasks,
092   Image2Text and Text2Image, to assess the proficiency of LMMs in capturing and conveying complex
093   scientific concepts in a contextually relevant manner.
094
      MAC emphasizes dual-sided multimodal generation, driven by painting cover images and writing
095
      cover stories surrounding each journal issue. Previous multimodal research mainly concentrates on
096   visual generation such as abstract concept depiction (Liao et al., 2023), or textual generation like
097   question-answering tasks (Goyal et al., 2017; Chen et al., 2015; Hudson & Manning, 2019; Marino
098   et al., 2019; Lu et al., 2022), neglecting the bidirectional generation of both images and texts, as
099   well as their comparison. Nevertheless, a holistic understanding from the bidirectional view is a
100   cornerstone of artificial general intelligence (Fei et al., 2022; Jain, 2023).
101
      Our evaluation involves current state-of-the-art LMMs like GPT-4V (OpenAI, 2023b) and Gem-
102   ini (Anil et al., 2023), including automatic evaluation agents and human experts. Three types of
103   rating reference standards are considered, alignment of Text with the original Text (T-T), alignment
104   of Image with the original Text (I-T), and alignment of Text with the original Image (T-I). We have
105   conducted both qualitative (Figure 2) and quantitative experiments to demonstrate that evaluation
106   agents are closely aligned with human preference and can accurately reflect the evaluated models.

      Multimodal Dataset for Evaluation has emerged as a captivating avenue for researchers. Within
155
      this domain, a significant branch flourishes, encompassing question-answer datasets designed to
156   tackle scientific problems. Noteworthy examples include AI2D (Kembhavi et al., 2016), FOOD-
157   WEDS (Krishnamurthy et al., 2016), DVQA (Kafle et al., 2018), VLQA (Sampat et al., 2020),
158   ScienceQA (Lu et al., 2022), and MathVista (Lu et al., 2023). Taking a comprehensive and multi-
159   disciplinary approach, SciEval (Sun et al., 2023) is a benchmark for evaluating the scientific research
160   abilities of models. In addition, Hessel et al. (Hessel et al., 2022) propose a benchmark for humor
161   understanding that involves a multimodal task, shedding light on the mechanisms behind what makes
      a caption humorous. HallusionBench (Liu et al., 2023a) stands as another significant addition to

164   2023b). Meanwhile, LPM (Lee et al., 2023) has been introduced as a large-scale benchmark that
165   scrutinizes the capabilities of vision-and-language models in comprehending educational videos

172   Scientific Understanding through Text Generation has become a focal point for evaluating
173   and enhancing the scientific comprehension capabilities of Large Multimodal Models (LMMs).
174   Notable contributions in this space include Galactica (Taylor et al., 2022) which is trained on an
175   extensive corpus comprising scientific papers, reference materials, knowledge bases, and various
176   other sources. This training enables Galactica to effectively store, amalgamate, and reason about
177
      scientific knowledge. Additionally, SCITUNE (Horawalavithana et al., 2023) introduces a tuning
      framework designed to enhance the capacity of LMMs to follow complex scientific multimodal
178
      instructions. Meanwhile, Sci-CoT (Ma et al., 2023) presents a two-stage framework that segregates
179
      the processes of generating rationales and inferring answers, thereby facilitating a more efficient
180   utilization of rationales during the answer inference phase. It is important to note that previous
181   works primarily concentrate on addressing specific scientific queries from the perspective of textual
182   generation. In contrast, our MAC tackles the more intricate task of comprehending academic concepts
183   through bidirectional multimodal generative tasks, encompassing both text and image generation.

      Evaluation on Multimodal Benchmark has Traditional evaluation metrics such as accuracy,
186   BLEU (Papineni et al., 2002), CIDEr (Vedantam et al., 2015), IS (Salimans et al., 2016), FID (Heusel
187   et al., 2017), and CLIP-Score (Hessel et al., 2021), take an exact match between the prediction
188   and the target, which cannot evaluate objectively cutting-edge large multimodal models (LMMs)
189   nowadays. Recent studies, such as mPLUG-Owl (Ye et al., 2023a;b) and LVLM-eHub (Xu et al.,
190   2023) propose the human-involved subjective evaluation by incorporating human judgment. More
191   works take ChatGPT-involved evaluation, such as MMBench (Liu et al., 2023d), CLAIR (Chan et al.,
192   2023), and DEsignBench (Lin et al., 2023), which is destined to become increasingly widespread
193   due to its automated nature. This paper takes human-involved and Large Model-involved evaluations
194
      to showcase state-of-the-art LMMs. Through our experiments, we further highlight the differences
      between existing Large Model-involved evaluations and human values.

318
      4.1   M ULTIMODAL AGENT L INKAGE
319
320   MAL is proposed to strengthen the ability of LMMs in the long-context window to comprehend
321   and generate academic journal covers. LMMs such as MidJourney (Midjourney, 2023) and Stable-
322   Diffusion (Rombach et al., 2021) excel in generating daily objects but struggle with understanding
323   long-context language descriptions. Instead of finetuning LMMs for the downstream tasks, we view
      LMMs as agents and integrate them with Large Language Models (e.g., ChatGPT (OpenAI, 2023),


                                                          6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      ChatGLM (Zhipu, 2023)), which excel in long-context processing and understanding scientific con-
325   cepts. This approach is simple and convenient, offering plug-and-play functionality. The multimodal
326   integration mechanism can facilitate the exchange of information between lengthy contexts and ab-
327   stract imagery. Additionally, MAL can leverage the unique strengths of each agent, enabling superior
328   performance when tackling more challenging problems, which will be discussed in Section 5.3.
329
      In the Image2Text task, Large Language Models (LLMs) leverage their generative capabilities to
330
      enhance the expressiveness of LMMs. Specifically, the role of LLMs is to refine and formalize
331   the descriptions generated by MidJourney (Midjourney, 2023). This involves transforming the less
332   structured outputs from MidJourney (Midjourney, 2023) into polished and structured cover stories
333   suitable for professional and academic purposes. In the Text2Image task, LLMs use their ability
334   to identify key points to grasp essential parts of lengthy texts and help LMMs understand abstract
335   concepts. Specifically, LLMs are used to translate formal and content-focused cover stories into
336   prompts that are stylistically aligned with the format requirements of MidJourney (Midjourney, 2023)
337   and Stable-Diffusion (Rombach et al., 2021) during usage.
338
339   4.2   MAL WITH I N - CONTEXT L EARNING
340
341   In-context learning techniques are introduced to facilitate the cooperation between LMMs and LLMs
342   in our proposed MAL when facing long-context language descriptions. Our initial experiments
343
      reveal that the direct outputs (0-shot) of MAL do not match academic journals in linguistic and
      artistic styles. Additionally, Large Language Models (LLMs) often alter the text to a huge degree
344
      from the images in Image2Text. Besides, summarizing cover stories by LLMs does not effectively
345
      help LMMs create covers in Text2Image tasks. Therefore, we integrate prompting techniques to
346   combine their functionalities seamlessly. To investigate the differences among various prompting
347   techniques, we include few-shot, chain-of-thought, and self-consistency prompting in our experiments
348   for MidJourney and Stable-Diffusion. We find that these prompting methods fail in our cases and
349   thus only display the few-shot results.
350
      The details of four distinct prompting methods are as follows. In zero-shot prompting, LLMs are
351
      tasked with revising descriptions into cover stories and creating prompts for large multimodal models.
352   Few-shot prompting (Brown et al., 2020) involves presenting three examples to guide LLMs in
353   revision or prompt generation. In chain-of-thought prompting (Wei et al., 2022), a single example is
354   given, and LLMs construct a chain of thoughts to guide their revisions or prompt generation. Lastly,
355   self-consistency prompting (Wang et al., 2022) requires LLMs to produce three iterations of revised
356   cover stories or prompts, with the most consistent one selected as the final output.

463
464
      Most existing models perform poorly on both Text2Image and Image2Text tasks. Table 3 shows the
      rankings and scores of existing LMMs. Their performance is still far from satisfactory. An important
465
      trend emerges in Table 3: the performance of models deteriorates as they face more challenging
466
      subsets. This outcome suggests that LMMs heavily rely on recalling their trained memory. However,
467   when confronted with untrained, unknown knowledge, they fail to utilize existing knowledge for
468   reasoning and constructing a knowledge framework.
469
470
      Disciplines with higher frequency in our MAC seem to perform better in current LMMs. Table 4

      based on Web of Science 4 . We find that LMMs perform the worst in Sociology, which has the

      least issues in all journals. Social sciences pose greater challenges in representation, unlike other
473   disciplines that are based on physical phenomena and easier to conceptualize. Besides, we find a
474   rough trend where LMMs perform better in disciplines with more issues in all journals. We reckon
475   that the mainstream disciplines are abundant with training data for LMMs, thus showing the best
476   performance.

527   6    C ONCLUSION
528
529
530   This paper introduces the Multimodal Academic Cover (MAC) benchmark, a novel evaluation
531
      framework derived from prominent academic journals such as Cell, Nature, and Science. Our focus
      lies on highly abstract and conceptual content. We craft bidirectional generative tasks centered
532
      around a single journal issue, fostering a nuanced comprehension of academic covers from both
533
      perspectives. Through our evaluation, we delve into the performance of advanced LMMs, revealing
534   their shortcomings in handling these tasks effectively. To address these limitations and enhance the
535   capabilities of LMMs, we propose Multimodal Agent Linkage (MAL), a collaborative framework
536   wherein LMMs and LLMs synergize to leverage their strengths. Through MAL, we demonstrate a
537   significant improvement in the understanding of scientific concepts, particularly in tackling more
538   complex tasks. Moreover, we underscore the significance of multi-directional generative benchmarks
539   and outline our intention to explore cyclic generation techniques in future research, aiming for a more
      holistic understanding of LMMs.
