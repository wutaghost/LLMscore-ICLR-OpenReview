009                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011            We introduce the Emergent Language Corpus Collection (ELCC): a collection of
012            corpora generated from open source implementations of emergent communication
013            systems across the literature. These systems include a variety of signalling game
014            environments as well as more complex tasks environments like a social deduction
015            game and embodied navigation. Each corpus is annotated with metadata describing
016            the characteristics of the source system as well as a suite of analyses of the corpus
017            (e.g., size, entropy, average message length, performance as transfer learning data).
018            Currently, research studying emergent languages requires directly running different
019
               systems which takes time away from actual analyses of such languages, makes
               studies which compare diverse emergent languages rare, and presents a barrier to
020
               entry for researchers without a background in deep learning. The availability of a
021
               substantial collection of well-documented emergent language corpora, then, will
022            enable research which can analyze a wider variety of emergent languages; this more
023            effectively uncovers general principles in emergent communication rather than
024            artifacts of particular environments. We provide some quantitative and qualitative
025            analyses with ELCC to demonstrate potential use cases of the resource in this vein.
026

028   1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030
      When Boldt and Mortensen (2024a) introduced the metric called XferBench, they raised a question
      that they apparently could not answer: how do emergent languages—communication systems that
031
      emerge from scratch in agent-based simulations—differ in their “humanlikeness” (as measured by
032
      their utility as pretraining data for NLP tasks). It seems likely that they were unable to answer this
033   question because no representative collection of samples from emergent languages existed. The
034   same problem plagues other research programs that seek to make generalizations about emergent
035   languages, as a whole, rather than using a single type of environment as a proof of concept. These
036   include the degree to which emergent languages display entropic patterns similar to those that
037   characterize words in human languages (Ueda et al., 2023) and the kind of syntax that can be detected
038   in emergent languages through grammar induction (van der Wal et al., 2020). We present an initial
039   solution to this problem, namely the Emergent Language Corpus Collection (ELCC), a collection
040   of 73 corpora generated from 7 representative emergent communication systems (ECSs).1 Prior
041
      to this work, comparing emergent languages entailed extensive work getting free and open source
      simulations to run—managing dependencies, manipulating output formats, etc.—before any data
042
      could even be generated. The current work allows investigators, even those with very limited software
043
      engineering knowledge, to analyze a wide range of emergent languages straightforwardly, plowing
044   over a barrier that has held back comparative emergent language research from its inception. ELCC
045   will be published with data and code licensed under the CC BY 4.0 and MIT licenses, respectively
046   (data and code included in supplementary material).
047
      We discuss related work in Section 2. Section 3 lays out the design of ELCC while Section 4 describes
048
      the content of the collection. Section 5 demonstrates some of the types of analyses enabled by ELCC.
049
      Section 6 presents some brief analyses, discussion, and future work related to ELCC. Finally, we
050   conclude in Section 7.

      Contributions The primary contribution of this paper is as a first-of-its kind data resource which
067   will enable broader engagement and new research directions within the field of emergent communica-
068   tion. Additionally, code published for reproducing the data resource also improve the reproducibility
069   of existing ECS implementations in the literature, supporting further research beyond just the data
070   resource itself. Finally, the paper demonstrates some of the analyses uniquely made possible by a
071   resource such as ELCC.

      2     R ELATED W ORK
075   Emergent communication There is no direct precedent for this work in the emergent commu-
076   nication literature that we are aware of. Perkins (2021b) introduces the TexRel dataset, but this is
077   a dataset of observations for training ECSs, not data generated by them. Some papers do provide
078   the emergent language corpora generated from their experiments (e.g., Yao et al. (2022a)), although
079   these papers are few in number and only include the particular ECS used in the paper. At a high level,
080
      the EGG framework (Kharitonov et al., 2021) strives to make emergent languages easily accessible,
      though instead of providing corpora directly, it provides a framework for implementing ECSs. Thus,
081
      while EGG is useful for someone building new systems entirely, it is not as geared towards research
082
      projects aiming directly at analyzing emergent languages themselves.
083
084
      Data resources At a high level, ELCC is a collection of datasets, each of which represent a
085
      particular instance of a phenomenon (emergent communication, in this case). On a this structural
086
      level, ELCC is analogous to a collection of different human languages in a multi-lingual dataset.
087   ELCC, though, focuses more on a particular phenomenon of scientific interest, and, in this way,
088   would be more analogous to work such as Blum et al. (2023), which presents a collection of grammar
089   snapshot pairs for 52 different languages as instances of diachronic language change. Similarly,
090   Zheng et al. (2024) present a dataset of conversations from Chatbot Arena, where “text generated by
091   different LLMs” is the phenomenon of interest. Furthermore, insofar as ELCC documents the basic
092   typology of different ECSs, it is similar to the World Atlas of Language Structures (WALS) (Dryer
093   and Haspelmath, 2013).

097   3.1   F ORMAT
098   ELCC is a collection of ECSs, each of which has one or more associated variants which correspond
099   to runs of the system with different hyperparameter settings (e.g., different random seed, message
100   length, dataset). Each variant has metadata along with the corpus generated from its settings. Each
101   ECS has its own metadata as well and code to generate the corpus and metadata of each variant. The
102   file structure of ELCC is illustrated in Figure 1.
103
104   ECS metadata Environment metadata provides a basic snapshot of a given system and where it
105   falls in the taxonomy of ECSs. As the collection grows, this structure makes it easier to ascertain
106   the contents of the collection and easily find the most relevant corpora for a given purpose. This
107   metadata will also serve as the foundation for future analyses of the corpora by looking at how the
      characteristics of an ECS relate to the properties of its output. These metadata include:

      A complete description is given in Appendix A. These metadata are stored as YAML files in each ECS
119   directory. A Python script is provided to validate these entries against a schema. See Appendix B for
120   an example of such a metadata file.
121
122   Corpus Each corpus comprises a list of lines each of which is, itself, an array of tokens represented
123   as integers. Each line corresponds to a single episode or round in the particular ECS. In the case of
124   multi-step or multi-agent systems, this might comprise multiple individual utterances which are then
125   concatenated together to form the line (no separation tokens are added). Each corpus is generated
126   from a single run of the ECS; that is, they are never aggregated from distinct runs of the ECS.
127
      Concretely, a corpus is formatted as a JSON lines (JSONL) file where each line is a JSON array of
128   integer tokens (see Figure 3 for an example of the format). There are a few advantages of JSONL: (1)
129   it is a human-readable format, (2) it is JSON-based, meaning it is standardized and has wide support
130   across programming languages, and (3) it is line-based, meaning it is easy to process with command
131   line tools.2 Corpora are also available as single JSON objects (i.e., and array of arrays), accessible
132   via the Croissant ecosystem (Akhtar et al., 2024).
133
134   Corpus analysis For each corpus in ELCC we run a suite of analyses to produce a quantitative
135   snapshot. This suite metrics is intended not only to paint a robust a picture of the corpus but also to
136   serve as jumping-off point for future analyses on the corpora. Specifically, we apply the following
137   to each corpus: token count, unique tokens, line count, unique lines, tokens per line, tokens per
138   line stand deviation, 1-gram entropy, normalized 1-gram entropy, entropy per line, 2-gram entropy,
139
      2-gram conditional entropy, EoS token present, and EoS padding. Normalized 1-gram entropy is
      computed as 1-gram entropy divided by the maximum entropy given the number of unique tokens in
140
      that corpus.
141
142   We consider an EoS (end-of-sentence) token to be present when: (1) every line ends with token
143   consistent across the entire corpora, and (2) the first occurrence of this token in a line is only
144   ever followed by more of the same token. For example, 0 could be an EoS token in the corpus
      [[1,2,0],[1,0,0]] but not [[1,2,0],[0,1,0]]. EoS padding is defined as a corpus having
145
      an EoS token, all lines being the same length, and the EoS token occurs more than once in a line at
146
      least once in the corpus.
147
148   Additionally, each corpus also has a small amount of metadata copied directly from the output of
149   the ECS; for example, this might include the success rate in a signalling game environment. We do
150   not standardize this because it can vary widely from ECS to ECS, though it can still be useful for
151
      comparison to other results among variants within an ECS.
152
153   Reproducibility ELCC is designed with reproducibility in mind. With each ECS, code is included
154
      to reproduce the corpora and analysis metadata. Not only does this make ELCC reproducible, but
      it sometimes helps the reproducibility of the underlying implementation insofar as it fixes bugs,
155
      specifies Python environments, and provides examples of how to run an experiment with a certain
156
      set of hyperparameters. Nevertheless, in this code, we have tried to keep as close to the original
157   implementations as possible. When the underlying implementation supports it, we set the random
158   seed (or keep the default) for the sake of consistency, although many systems do not offer ways to
159   easily set this.

176   ELCC contains 73 corpora across 8 ECSs taken from the literature for which free and open source
177   implementations were available. With our selection we sought to capture variation across a three
178   distinct dimensions:
179
180         1. Variation across ECSs generally, including elements like game types, message structure,

               data sources, and implementation details.
182         2. Variation among different hyperparameter settings within an ECS, including message length,

184         3. Variation within a particular hyperparameter setting that comes from inherent stochasticity

      4.1   S COPE
193
194   The scope of the contents of ELCC is largely the same as discussed in reviews such as Lazaridou and
195   Baroni (2020) and Boldt and Mortensen (2024b, Section 1.2). This comprises agent-based models for
196   simulating the formation of “natural” language from scratch using deep neural networks. Importantly,
197   from scratch means that the models are not pretrained or tuned on human language. Typically, such
198   simulations make use of reinforcement learning to train the neural networks, though this is not a
199   requirement in principle.
200   One criterion that we do use to filter ECSs for inclusion is its suitability for generating corpora as
201   described above. This requires that the communication channel is discrete, analogous to the distinct
202   words/morphemes which for the units of human language. This excludes a small number of emergent
203   communication papers have approached emergent communication through constrained continuous
204   channels like sketching (Mihai and Hare, 2021b) or acoustic-like signals (Eloff et al., 2023). Other
205   systems use discrete communication but are in essence one token per episode (e.g., Tucker et al.
206   (2021b)) which would not form a suitable corpus for addressing most research questions.
207
208   4.2   S IGNALLING GAMES
209
      The signalling game (or reference game) (Lewis, 1969) represents a plurality, if not majority, of the
210   systems present in the literature. A brief, non-exhaustive review of the literature yielded 43 papers
211   which use minor variations of the signalling game, a large number considering the modest body of
212   emergent communication literature (see Appendix C). The basic format of the signalling game is a
213   single round of sender agent making an observation, passing a message to the receiver agent, and
214   the receiver performing an action based on the information from the message. The popularity of this
215   game is, in large part, because of its simplicity in both concept and implementation. Experimental
      variables can be manipulated easily while introducing minimal confounding factors. Furthermore, the


                                                        4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      implementations can entirely avoid the difficulties of reinforcement learning by treating the sender
217   and receiver agents as a single neural network, resulting in autoencoder with a discrete bottleneck
218   which can be trained with backpropagation and supervised learning.
219
220
      The two major subtypes of the signalling game are the discrimination game and the reconstruction
      game. In the discrimination game, the receiver must answer a multiple-choice question, that is, select
221
      the correct observation from among incorrect “distractors”. In the reconstruction game, the receiver
222
      must recreate the input directly, similar to the decoder of an autoencoder.
223
224
      Vanilla For the most basic form of the signalling game, which we term “vanilla”, we use the
225   implementation provided in the Emergence of lanGuage in Games (EGG) framework (Kharitonov
226   et al., 2021, MIT license). It is vanilla insofar as it comprises the signalling game with the simplest
227   possible observations (synthetic, concatenated one-hot vectors), a standard agent architecture (i.e.,
228   RNNs), and no additional dynamics or variations on the game. Both the discrimination game and the
229   reconstruction game are included. This system provides a good point of comparison for other ECSs
230   which introduce variations on the signalling game. The simplicity of the system additionally makes it
231   easier to vary hyperparameters: for example, the size of the dataset can be scaled arbitrarily and there
232   is no reliance on pretrained embedding models.
233
234   Natural images “Linking emergent and natural languages via corpus transfer” (Yao et al., 2022a,
235   MIT license) presents a variant of the signalling game which uses embeddings of natural images
236
      as the observations. In particular, the system uses embedded images from the MS-COCO and
      Conceptual Captions datasets consisting of pictures of everyday scenes. Compared to the uniformly
237
      sampled one-hot vectors in the vanilla setting, natural image embeddings are real-valued with a
238
      generally smooth probability distribution rather than being binary or categorical. Furthermore, natural
239   data distributions are not uniform and instead have concentrations of probability mass on particular
240   elements; this non-uniform distribution is associated with various features of human language (e.g.,
241   human languages’ bias towards describing warm colors (Gibson et al., 2017; Zaslavsky et al., 2019)).
242
243   Concept-based observations “Emergent communication of generalizations” (Mu and Goodman,
244   2021b, MIT license) presents a variant of the discrimination signalling game which they term the
245   concept game. The concept game changes the way that the sender’s observation corresponds with the
246   receiver’s observations. In the vanilla discrimination game, the observation the sender sees is exactly
247   the same as the correct observation that the receiver sees. In the concept game, the sender instead
248   observes a set of inputs which share a particular concept (e.g., red triangle and red circle are both
249
      red), and the correct observation (among distractors) shown to the receiver contains the same concept
      (i.e., red) while not being identical to those observed by the sender. The rationale for this system is
250
      that the differing observations will encourage the sender to communicate about abstract concepts
251
      rather than low-level details about the observation. This ECS also presents the vanilla discrimination
252   game as well as the set reference game, which is similar to the reference game except that the whole
253   object is consistent (e.g., different sizes and locations of a red triangle).
254
255   Multi-agent population “Emergent communication at scale” (Chaabouni et al., 2022, Apache
256   2.0-license) presents a signalling game system with populations of agents instead of the standard
257   fixed pair of sender and receiver. For each round of the game, then, a random sender is paired with a
258   random receiver. This adds a degree of realism to the system, as natural human languages are ways
259   developed within a population and not just between two speakers (cf. idioglossia). More specifically,
260   language developing among a population of agents prevents some degree “overfitting” between sender
261   and receiver; in this context, having a population of agents functions as an ensembling approach to
262
      regularization.
263
264   4.3   OTHER GAMES
265   Considering that the signalling game is close to the simplest possible game for an ECS, moving
266   beyond the signalling game generally entails an increase in complexity. There is no limit to the
267   theoretical diversity of games, although some of the most common games that we see in the literature
268   are conversation-based games (e.g., negotiation, social deduction) and navigation games. These games
269   often introduce new aspects to agent interactions like: multi-step episodes, multi-agent interactions,
      non-linguistic actions, and embodiment.


                                                         5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      These kinds of systems, as a whole, are somewhat less popular in the literature. On a practical
271   level, more complex systems are more difficult to implement and even harder to get to converge
272   reliably—many higher-level behaviors, such as planning or inferring other agent’s knowledge, are
273   difficult problems for reinforcement learning in general, let alone with discrete multi-agent emergent
274   communication. On a methodological level, more complexity in the ECS makes it harder to formally
275   analyze the system as well as eliminate confounding factors in empirical investigation. With so
276   many moving parts, it can be difficult to prove that some observed effect is not just a result of some
277   seemingly innocent hyperparameter choice (e.g., learning rate, samples in the rollout buffer) (Boldt
278   and Mortensen, 2022). Nevertheless, we have reason to believe that these complexities are critical
279
      to understanding and learning human language as a whole (Bisk et al., 2020), meaning that the
      difficulties of more complex systems are worth overcoming as they are part of the process of creating
280
      more human-like emergent languages, which are more informative for learning about human language
281
      and more suitable for applications in NLP.
282
283
      Grid-world navigation “Generalizing Emergent Communication” (Unger and Bruni, 2020, BSD-
284
      3-clause license) introduces an ECS which takes some of the basic structure of the signalling game
285   and applies it to a navigation-based system derived from the synthetic Minigrid/BabyAI environment
286   (Chevalier-Boisvert et al., 2018; 2023). A sender with a bird’s-eye view of the environment sends
287   messages to a receiver with a limited view who has to navigate to a goal location. Beyond navigation,
288   some environments present a locked door for which the receiver must first pick up a key in order
289   to open. What distinguishes this system most from the signalling game is that it is multi-step and
290   embodied such that the utterances within an episodes are dependent on each other. Among other
291   things, this changes the distribution properties of the utterances. For example, if the receiver is in
292   Room A at timestep T , it is more likely to be in Room A at timestep T + 1; thus if utterances are
293
      describing what room the receiver is in, this means that an utterance at T + 1 has less uncertainty
      given the content of an utterance at T . Practically speaking, the multiple utterances in a given episode
294
      are concatenated together to form a single line in the corpus in order to maintain the dependence of
295
      later utterances on previous ones.
296
297
      Continuous navigation “Mathematically Modeling the Lexicon Entropy of Emergent Language”
298
      (Boldt and Mortensen, 2022, GPL-3.0 license) introduces a simple navigation-based ECS which is
299
      situated in a continuous environment. A “blind” receiver is randomly initialized in an obstacle-free
300   environment and must navigate toward a goal zone guided by messages from the sender which
301   observes the position of the receiver relative to the goal. The sender sends a single discrete token at
302   each timestep, and a line in the dataset consists of the utterances from each timestep concatenated
303   together. This system shares the time-dependence between utterances of the grid-world navigation
304   system although with no additional complexity of navigating around obstacle, opening doors, etc.
305   On the other hand, the continuous nature of this environment provides built-in stochasticity since
306   there are (theoretically) infinitely many distinct arrangements of the environment that are possible,
307   allowing for more natural variability in the resulting language.
308
309   Social deduction “RLupus: Cooperation through the emergent communication in The Werewolf
310   social deduction game” (Brandizzi et al., 2022, GPL-3.0 license) introduces an ECS based on the
311
      social deduction game Werewolf (a.k.a., Mafia) where, through successive rounds of voting and
      discussion, the “werewolves” try to eliminate the “villagers” before the villagers figure out who the
312
      werewolves are. In a given round, the discussion takes the form of all agents broadcasting a message
313
      to all other agents after which a vote is taken on whom to eliminate. As there are multiple rounds in a
314   given game, this system introduces multi-step as well as multi-speaker dynamics into the language.
315   Furthermore, the messages also influence distinct actions in the system (i.e., voting). These additional
316   features in the system add the potential for communication strategies that are shaped by a variety of
317   heterogeneous factors rather than simply the distribution of observations (as in the signalling game).

      The range of analyses ELCC enables is greatly multiplied by a resource like XferBench (Boldt and

361
      metric quantifies how good a corpus is as pretraining data for a human language-based downstream
362
      task, specifically language modelling (thus a lower score is better). XferBench proves to be a

364   only a corpus of tokenized utterances as input. In fact, ELCC and XferBench permit the first
365   large-scale comparison of emergent language systems with an evaluative metric.

430   not detract from the original findings; instead, evaluating the design changes with XferBench better

451   6   D ISCUSSION
452   Work enabled by ELCC In the typical emergent communication paper, only a small amount of
453   time and page count is allocated to analysis with the lion’s share being taken up by designing the
454   ECS, implementing it, and running experiments. Even if one reuses an existing implementation, a
455   significant portion of work still goes towards designing and running the experiments, and the analysis
456   is still limited to that single system. While this kind of research is valid and important, it should not
457   be the only paradigm possible within emergent communication research. To this end, ELCC enables
458
      research which focus primarily on developing more in-depth analyses across a diverse collection of
      systems. Furthermore, removing the necessity of implementing and/or running experiments allows
459
      researchers without machine learning backgrounds to contribute to emergent communication research
460
      from more linguistic angles that otherwise would not be possible.
461
462   In particular, ELCC enables work that focuses on the lexical properties of emergent communication,
463   looking at the statical properties and patterns of the surface forms of a given language (e.g., Zipf’s
464
      law). Ueda et al. (2023) is a prime example of this; this paper investigates whether or not emergent
      languages obey Harris’ Articulation Schema (HAS) by relating conditional entropy to the presence of
465
      word boundaries. The paper finds mixed evidence for HAS in emergent languages but only evaluated
466
      a handful of settings in a single ECS, yet it could be the case that only systems with certain designs
467   generate languages described by HAS. The variety of systems provided by ELCC could, then, provide
468   more definitive empirical evidence in support or against the presence of HAS in emergent languages.
469   Additionally, ELCC can similarly extend the range of emergent languages evaluated in the context of
470   machine learning, such as Yao et al. (2022a); Boldt and Mortensen (2024a) which look at emergent
471   language’s suitability for deep transfer learning to downstream NLP tasks or van der Wal et al. (2020)
472   which analyzes emergent languages with unsupervised grammar induction.
473
474
475   ECS implementations and reproducibility In the process of compiling ELCC, we observed a
476   handful of trends in the implementations of emergent communication systems. A significant pro-
477
      portion of papers do not publish the implementations of experiments, severely limiting the ease
      of reproducing the results or including such work in a project such as ELCC, considering that
478
      a large amount of the work in creating an ECS is not in the design but in the details of imple-
479
      mentation. Even when a free and open source implementation is available, many projects suffer
480   from underspecified Python dependencies (i.e., no indication of versions) which can be difficult
481   to reproduce if the project is older than a few years. Furthermore, some projects also fail to
482   specify the particular hyperparameter settings or commands to run the experiments presented in
483   the paper; while these can often be recovered with some investigation, this and the above issue
484   prove to be obstacles which could easily be avoided. For an exemplar of a well-documented,
485   easy-to-run implementation of an ECS and its experiments, see Mu and Goodman (2021b) at
      https://github.com/jayelm/emergent-generalization/ which not only provides


                                                           9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      dependencies with version and documentation how to download the data but also a complete shell
487   script which executes the commands to reproduce the experiments.
488
489
      Future of ELCC While ELCC is a complete resource as presented in this paper, ELCC is intended
490
      to be an ongoing project which incorporates further ECSs, analyses, and taxonomic features as the
491   body of emergent communication literature and free and open source implementations continues to
492   grow. This approach involves the community not only publishing well-documented implementation of
493   their ECSs but also directly contributing to ELCC in the spirit of scientific collaboration and free and
494   open source software. ELCC, then, is intended to become a hub for a variety of stakeholders in the
495   emergent communication research community, namely a place for: ECS developers to contribute and
496   publicize their work, EC researchers to stay up-to-date on new ECSs, and EC-adjacent researchers to
497   find emergent languages which they can analyze or otherwise use for their own research.
498
499   Limitations Emergent communication research is primarily basic research on machine generated
500   data; thus, ELCC has few, if any, direct societal impacts. From a research point of view: while
501   ELCC attempts to provide a representative sample of the ECSs present in the literature, it is not
502   comprehensive collection of all of the open source implementations let alone all ECSs in the literature.
503
      This limitation is especially salient in the case of foundational works in EC which have no open source
      implementations (e.g., Mordatch and Abbeel (2018)). Thus, the contents of ELCC could potentially
504
      result in an over-reliance on the particular systems included resulting in an unfamiliarity with the
505
      data and limiting research on those currently not included in ELCC. Including the data-generating
506   code and metadata describing the systems in ELCC has partially addressed this issue, and future
507   work adding more open source implementations and reimplementing seminal papers could continue
508   to ameliorate this limitation.
509
      Beyond the variety of systems, in its design ELCC only provides unannotated corpora without
510
      any reference to the semantics of the communication, which limits the range of analyses that can
511
      be performed (e.g., measures of compositionality are precluded because since it fundamentally a
512   relationship between surface forms and their semantics). In terms of compute resources, we estimate
513   that on the order of 150 GPU-hours (NVIDIA A6000 or equivalents) on an institutional cluster were
514   used in the development of ELCC, and additional 1000 GPU-hours were used to generate the results
515   of XferBench on ELCC. This research could be difficult to reproduce without access to institutional
516   resources.
517

      7    C ONCLUSION
520   In this paper, we have introduced ELCC, a collection of emergent language corpora annotated with
521   taxonomic metadata and suite of descriptive metrics derived from free and open source implementa-
522   tions of emergent communication systems introduced in the literature. ELCC also provides code for
523   running these implementations, in turn, making those implementations more reproducible. This col-
524
      lection is the first of its kind in providing easy access to a variety of emergent language corpora. Thus,
      it enables new kinds of research on emergent communication which involve a wide range of emergent
525
      communication which focuses directly on the analysis of the emergent languages themselves.
