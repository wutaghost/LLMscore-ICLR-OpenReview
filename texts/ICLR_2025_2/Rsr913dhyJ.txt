009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Feature importance is one of the most prominent methods in explainable artificial
012            intelligence. It seeks to score the features an artificial intelligence model relies on
013            the most. In multi-class classification, current methods fail to explain inter-class
014            relationships as they either provide explanations for binary classification only, or
015            suffer from aggregation bias. In a multi-class classification scenario, features may
016
               carry discriminative power to separate some of the classes while being otherwise
               less relevant. State-of-the-art feature importance measures do not capture this be-
017
               havior. We propose Inter-Class Feature Importance (ICFI), a measure that scores
018
               the feature importance to discriminate between an arbitrary pair of classes. ICFI is
019            a post-hoc, model-agnostic method, independent from the machine learning archi-
020            tecture employed. ICFI marginalises the target output with respect to the feature
021            of interest, leveraging the resulting change in model behavior to quantify feature
022            importance. We present ICFI’s properties and argue its relevance, describing use
023            cases and showing insights gained. We demonstrate through thorough experiments
024            on real-world datasets how ICFI captures the features characteristics for specific
025            class relationships.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      The eXplainable Artificial Intelligence (XAI) research field focuses on making Machine
030   Learning (ML) models understandable to human stakeholders (König et al., 2021). ML models’
031   increasing complexity is proving a hurdle in complying with legal requirements (König et al., 2021;
032   European Parliament & Council of the European Union; Tritscher et al., 2023), validating architec-
033   tures in high-stake scenarios (Dinu et al., 2020), and treating protected groups fairly (Caton & Haas,
034   2024). XAI research aims to solve these problems. Specifically, Feature Importance (FI) is one of
035   the most popular XAI methods (Saarela & Jauhiainen, 2021). It quantifies the relevance of each
036   input feature for the model prediction (Muschalik et al., 2023), allowing verification of whether the
037   importance of the features aligns with background knowledge (Alfeo et al., 2023).
038   If an XAI method works with a trained ML model, it is classified as post-hoc (Tritscher et al., 2023).
039   Post-hoc techniques are flexible and can be applied to existing models to improve interpretabil-
040   ity (Das & Rad, 2020). Additionally, a FI method that applies to any ML model is considered
041   model-agnostic (Tritscher et al., 2023) and does not impose constraints on the model architecture.
042   Current FI methods either provide explanations for single instances (locally) in a binary classifica-
043   tion setting, quantifying importance toward the positive class, or explain the whole dataset (glob-
044   ally). These two types of explanation cannot capture inter-class relationships. Local methods are
045   targeted toward binary settings. For global methods, the importance ranking stands valid for a cer-
046   tain percentage of the population, but it may not be accurate for all of the population; this effect is
047   known as aggregation bias (Mehrabi et al., 2022).
048   A concrete example is the kidney cancer recognition case (Muhamed Ali et al., 2018; Cancer
049   Genome Atlas Research Network et al., 2013), where clinical data and RNA sequencing information
050   are utilized to detect cancer sub-types. Such a case is a multi-class classification task. After building
051   a classifier, it is possible to obtain insight into the model behavior by applying XAI methods and
052   obtaining the global FI. The output is a single ranking, quantifying feature contributions. This sin-
053   gle ranking, however, does not consider class relationships. Certain features might be important to
      separate two specific cancer sub-types while being otherwise less relevant. Existing FI methods fail


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      to capture this. Moreover, a feature crucial for differentiating between two cancer types may not be
055   found globally important, which risks critical oversights.
056
057   We can use the traditional Confusion Matrix (CM) to evaluate the model, quantifying pairwise class
058
      combinations’ false positives and negatives (Beauxis-Aussalet & Hardman, 2014). We might notice
      that the model does not distinguish between classes equally well, notable in the CM entries. How
059
      do we understand why this happens? We know that one CM entry describes the model’s misclas-
060
      sifications between two classes. We need the FI importance (the explanations) for when the model
061   separates the classes to know why it misclassifies them. If we use global methods, we will receive
062   an aggregation of every sample regardless of their class, which fails to provide a specific explanation
063   for the misclassification between two classes.
064
      To address this gap, we look towards a method that outputs a feature ranking for any pair of classes,
065
      quantifying the importance of features in separating the two classes. Hence, we propose a new
066
      feature importance measure to explain an ML model in a multi-classification scenario: Inter-Class
067   Feature Importance (ICFI). We seek to identify the features the model relies on the most when
068   trying to discriminate between two classes. Applying existing FI measures to the classes of interest
069   would not achieve our goal. We would still evaluate the importance of the overall classification task,
070   not capturing relationships between the two classes. When we have a binary classification task,
071   separating the two classes coincides with the overall model’s task, ICFI is thus a generalization of
072   binary FI.
073   ICFI, to compute FI, permutes the feature of interest to mimic the absence of the feature from the
074   model as similarly done in Permutation Feature Importance (PFI) (Fumagalli et al., 2023; Strobl
075   et al., 2008) and other XAI methods Fisher et al. (2019). We quantify feature importance by evalu-
076   ating model performance with and without the information carried by the feature inspected. Permu-
077   tation allows our measure to be completely model-agnostic without needing to train any additional
078   model. ICFI is a model-agnostic and post-hoc method, and its simple algorithmic implementation
079   encourages its use in any scenario involving tabular data.
080   Our core contribution includes an introduction of ICFI, a model-agnostic XAI method for quantify-
081   ing feature importance in separating an arbitrary pair of classes. An overview of ICFI’s properties
082   and an in-depth discussion of its relevance providing use cases. Through empirical evaluation of
083   real-world datasets, we show how ICFI offers new insights into the inner workings of an ML model.
084
      The structure of the remainder of the paper firstly introduces related work in Section 2, then ICFI is
085   introduced in Section 3. Penultimately, we explore experiments in Section 4 and Section 5 concludes
086   the paper discussing possible future work.

      2   R ELATED W ORK
091
092   The XAI research field is very dynamic, with recent surveys providing detailed overviews (Ali et al.,
093   2023; Das & Rad, 2020; Theissler et al., 2022). XAI literature addresses both model-agnostic and
094   model-specific approaches as well as post-hoc explanations. Model-specific approaches are tailored
095   for a specific model or class of models only (Sundararajan et al., 2017; Carletti et al., 2023; Bach
096   et al., 2015; de Sá, 2019). Post-hoc methods target fully trained models (Ali et al., 2023).
097   In our work, we focus on feature importance methods. FI methods generate explanations by pointing
098   out the model’s most important features (Das & Rad, 2020).
099
      The most prominent and widely used methods in this domain include SHapley Additive exPla-
100
      nations (SHAP) (Lundberg & Lee, 2017) and Local Interpretable Model-agnostic Explanations
101
      (LIME) (Ribeiro et al., 2016). SHAP applies Shapley Values from game theory to locally assess
102   each feature’s importance in machine learning (Ali et al., 2023). SHAP provides a unique feature
103   ranking only in binary classification. Global SHAP explanations can be retrieved by averaging lo-
104   cal ones. They do not take into account inter-class relationships and they misrepresent samples
105   whose importance does not align with the average, suffering from aggregation bias (Mehrabi et al.,
106   2022). SHAP’s usage in diverse research areas (Cooper et al., 2021; Antwarg et al., 2021; Garcı́a
107   & Aznarte, 2020) underscores its effectiveness while it’s high computational complexity makes its
      implementation challenging (Muschalik et al., 2023).


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      On the other hand, LIME is a local XAI method that quantifies feature importance by approximat-
109   ing the model locally with an inherently interpretable surrogate, responsible to provide the expla-
110   nation (Adamczewski et al., 2020; Ribeiro et al., 2016). Global feature rankings can be retrieved
111   through aggregating local instances (Ribeiro et al., 2016). LIME may exhibit instability if slight
112   changes in the surrogate’s input occur (Zhou et al., 2021).
113
      Global XAI approches include Partial Dependence Plots (PDP) (Friedman, 1991) and Permutation
114
      Feature Importance (Breiman, 2001). PDPs are a low-dimensional graphical representation showing
115   the dependence between the target and a set of features of interest (Greenwell et al., 2017). PDPs do
116   not target feature importance rankings but aim to visualize the interaction between the target variable
117   and a set of input features.
118
      PFI, introduced in Breiman (2001) for random forests, assesses change in the model’s performance
119
      when permuting the feature of interest, effectively marginalizing the other features (Fumagalli et al.,
120
      2023; Strobl et al., 2008). Permutation, aims at mimicking the absence of the feature of interest. If a
121   feature provides information about the target variable, breaking the association through permutation
122   will be reflected in the model’s performance (Strobl et al., 2008). The feature is deemed unimportant
123   when there is no significant increase in the empirical risk after permuting (Debeer & Strobl, 2020). A
124   slight decrease in risk is also possible and is attributed to chance or to a sub-optimal model (Debeer
125   & Strobl, 2020; Fisher et al., 2019).
126
      PFI has been the focus of several research articles addressing both applications and enhance-
127   ments (König et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2016; Strobl & Zeileis, 2008; Strobl et al., 2008; Nicodemus
128   et al., 2010; Epifanio, 2017). Specifically, Fisher et al. (2019) expand PFI making it model-agnostic
129   and not limited to trees or ensemble models (Fumagalli et al., 2023).

133   3   F EATURE IMPORTANCE IN MULTI - CLASS CLASSIFICATION

136
      We propose ICFI, with the aim of providing feature importance for the task of separating a pair
137   of classes in a multi-class classification scenario. Some features may indeed carry discriminative
138   power to discriminate two classes only, while they might not be otherwise leveraged by the model.
139   Hence, they might not be highlighted as important by global methods.
140
      We keep a running simple example, using the Iris dataset (Fisher, 1936), to gain intuition for our
141
      method. The Iris dataset is a popular benchmark dataset for multi-class classification tasks. It
142
      consists of 150 samples of iris flowers belonging to three separate species: versicolor, virginica and
143   setosa. The objective is to classify flowers’ samples leveraging four features, describing respectively
144   sepal length, sepal width, petal length and petal width.
145
146
      Here, we use only sepal length and sepal width for sake of example and simplicity (Figure 1). De-
      scribing the Iris dataset through sepal width and sepal length causes the versicolor and virginica
147
      classes to overlap (Zaki & Meira, 2014). As also testified by Figure 1, setosa is linearly separa-
148
      ble from the other two classes. Versicolor and virginica instead, overlap with each other in the
149   two dimensional input space. The expectation is that a classifier will struggle to separate between
150   versicolor and virginica.
151
152
      We fit a decision tree classifier on the simplified Iris dataset, using 50% of the data for training.
      Figure 1 shows the decision boundary. As expected, several virginica samples are misclassified as
153
      versicolor and multiple versicolor flowers are wrongly labeled as virginica. This testifies how the
154
      model does not effectively separate versicolor and virginica. On the contrary, no setosa sample is
155   classified as virginica while just one is wrongly assigned to the versicolor class.
156
157   To capture feature importance to discriminate two classes, we need to describe how the model per-
158
      forms when separating the pair of classes. Referrnewsgrouping to Figure 1, we need to quantify the
      model’s inefficiency in separating virginica and versicolor.
159
160   Global FI methods, don’t look into inter-class relationships and suffer from aggregation bias. This
161   work aims to evaluate performance in the task of discriminating two classes as a pathway to Inter-
      Class Feature Importance.

183   ICFI takes into consideration two classes and outputs the feature importance for the model task of
184   separating them. As in binary FI methods, we do not change the computation based on the order
185   of the classes. Hence, we do not distinguish the importance of a feature in separating class σ with
186
      class ρ from the importance in separating class ρ with class σ. This requires ICFI to be symmetrical
      with respect to the two classes of interest. When a feature is deemed unimportant, we quantify
187
      its importance with 0 as intuition would suggest and relating to the discussion on missingness,
188
      in Lundberg & Lee (2017). Missingness is the property stating that features missing from the original
189   input get an attribution of 0. Furthermore, humans reason better when dealing with a small limited
190   range than with a potentially infinitely high value (Resnick et al., 2017; Jones et al., 2008). An
191   unbounded number for the importance quantification is a feature detrimental to interpretability, as a
192   FI value with no reference hinders its interpretation (Adamczewski et al., 2020; Pries et al., 2023).
193   We thus want our measure to be also upper bounded. Next, we introduce the formalism used in this
194   paper.

196   risk Rt (h) as the expected loss of h with respect to a probability distribution D over X × Y (Shalev-

      4       E XPERIMENTS
336
337
      One of XAI’s biggest challenges is its evaluation, which cannot generally rely on quantifiable met-
338   rics like accuracy. Furthermore, ground-truth and evaluation standards are often lacking (Adam-
339   czewski et al., 2020; Afchar et al., 2021; Molnar et al., 2023; Pries et al., 2023; Ali et al., 2023;
340   Tritscher et al., 2023). When we do have ground-truth on which features should be important, it usu-
341   ally refers to the data, not to the model. Explanations are commonly assessed either as a by-product
342   of accuracy or through case studies in application contexts. Our experiments1 utilize both assess-
343   ment methods through real-world datasets. Different classifiers are used throughout the experiments
344   in order to highlight the model-agnostic nature of the proposed method. In our experiments, we
345   do not target model performance as such; this paper focuses on the quality of explanations. Unless
346
      stated otherwise, we train models which output class probabilities, allowing us to use the cross-
      entropy loss, widely employed in classification tasks (Zhang & Sabuncu, 2018; Mao et al., 2023)
347
      and determining how classes are merged.
348
349   In 4.1, an interpretable model is fitted to the Iris dataset (Fisher, 1936) in order to have ground-truth
350   in our feature importance rankings. We then seek to quantify feature importance and test whether
351   ICFI correctly retrieves ground-truth information. Section 4.2 exploits background knowledge in a
352
      NLP dataset to evaluate ICFI. In this setting, words are used as features and some words are expected
      to be highly discriminative for a specific pair of class while are not supposed to be leveraged by the
353
      model in other pairs. We test if ICFI correctly captures this behavior. Lastly, Our measure is com-
354
      pared to three different benchmarks in Section 4.3 to evaluate the quality of the feature importance
355   rankings. Throughout the experiments, PFI is computed as a global feature importance measure for
356   the sake of comparison. We use PFI for two reasons. First, it is a vastly employed, model-agnostic,
357   inherently global FI method. Second, it uses a strategy similarly to the one of ICFI, i.e., it permutes
358   the feature of interest evaluating model performance before and after permutation. ICFI and PFI are
359   computed on test data.
360
361   4.1       3- CLASS DECISION TREE , NUMERICAL
362
363   The Iris dataset (Fisher, 1936) is a multi-class classification dataset. The task consists in distinguish-
364   ing three different types of flowers i.e., Setosa, Versicolor and Virginica, described by four features:
365
      petal width, petal length, sepal width and sepal length. We fit a CART decision tree (Breimann
      et al., 1984), setting the maximum depth at two as shown in Figure 3.
366
367   The decision tree has the advantage of being an inherently interpretable model. As we already dis-
368   cussed, we seldom have ground-truth in the model and an interpretable one, together with a relatively
369   simple dataset, offers the ground-truth needed to evaluate ICFI. The tree structure in Figure 3, pro-
370   vides a feature importance ranking. First of all, the tree leverages just two features: petal width and
371
      petal length. Sepal length and sepal width should thus be labeled as unimportant. Moreover, features
      leveraged close to the root have a higher global influence than the ones used in lower nodes (Laugel
372
      et al., 2018). We thus expect petal width, used in the tree root, to be the globally most relevant
373
      feature. When distinguishing between versicolor and virginica instead, the model relies heavily on
374   petal length as exemplified in Figure 3. Finally, when separating between setosa and the two classes
375   to the right of the root, we foresee petal length to have low importance, as it cannot be used by the
376   model to classify a sample as belonging to the setosa class.

      4.3   C OMPLEX MULTI - CLASS NEURAL NETWORK
468
469   We now consider model retraining to test the quality of ICFI’s feature importance ranking. Concep-
470   tually, the better the feature ranking, the better a model trained with only the top k most important
471   features will perform. As ICFI computes a ranking for a pair of classes for the model to explain, the
472   retraining is carried out using a One versus One strategy. A binary classification model is created
473   for each class pair and fitted using ICFI’s top k features for each respective class pair. Each point
474   is classified for each model and a final classification is obtained through a majority vote (Bishop,
475   2006).
476   We utilize four real-world multi-class classification datasets: Dry Bean (mis, 2020), Penguins (LTER
477   & Gorman, 2016), Vehicle silhouettes (Mowforth & Shepherd), and Wine (Aeberhard & Forina,
478   1991), which have 13611, 342, 423, and 178 samples respectively. Dry Bean is a classification
479   dataset of grains belonging to 7 different varieties of dry beans. Each record has 16 numerical
480   features describing the grain’s shape and dimension. The Penguins dataset contains 4 numerical
481   features about three different species of penguins. The goal of Vehicle silhouttes is to classify a
482
      given silhouette, leveraging 18 numerical features, as one of four types of vehicles. Lastly, Wine
      leverages the quantities of 13 wine constituents to label each record as belonging to three different
483
      cultivars.
484
485   The model to explain is a feed-forward neural network, i.e., a black box model, which is trained
      for the multi-class classification problem at hand. ICFI is computed for each class pair and the top


                                                        9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      k features for different values of k are selected. For each value of k, a One vs One classifier is
487   then fitted, i.e., a neural network is trained for each class pair, with the final decision obtained by
488   aggregating models’ output through a majority vote. Each neural network, binary classifying a class
489   pair, will use the top k features highlighted as most important by ICFI computed for that class pair.
490   The better the features, the higher the performance of the retrained One vs One (Borisov et al., 2019;
491   Huang et al., 2020). We remark how the One vs One strategy is leveraged only in the evaluation step
492   to assess ICFI’s explanation quality. ICFI does not need a One vs One model for its computation
493   and is, on the contrary, completely model agnostic.
494   We compare ICFI’s rankings quality with other four feature selection strategies. We indeed compute
495   PFI, global SHAP and global LIME feature importance on the neural network we seek to explain,
496   choosing the top k features for model retraining. In this scenario, each model in the One vs One
497   classifier uses the same top k features, as a global measure is used. In the fourth benchmark, features
498   are selected randomly for each class pair. We include global methods in our comparison because
499   of the absence of XAI methods natively having the same ICFI objective, and to show how the
500   features important globally are not the most discriminative for each pair of classes. Three additional
501
      benchmarking strategies, where state-of-the-art global methods explain multiple binary models, one
      for each pair of classes, are discussed in Appendix A.3.
502
503   Figure 7 shows test accuracy at different k values. Retraining based on ICFI consistently outper-
504   forms retraining leveraging PFI, global SHAP, global LIME and when features are chosen randomly.
505   Specifically, the increase in performance is most evident when a low number of features is used,
506   which is the most challenging setting and thus were the quality of the features’ ranking matter the
507
      most. This indicates how features chosen with ICFI for each One vs One model carry more dis-
      criminative power than the features selected by global XAI methods. ICFI can thus effectively find
508
      features with high discriminative power to separate two classes, not suffering from aggregation bias.

524   5   C ONCLUSIONS
525
526   ICFI leads to more insights in a multi-class classification scenario, where current methods either
527   suffer from aggregation bias or are tailored toward binary settings. ICFI quantifies feature impor-
528   tance in discriminating arbitrary pairs of classes. ICFI is a post-hoc, model-agnostic XAI method
529   applicable to any existing ML model.
530   ICFI relies on merging the two inspected classes and measuring the performance improvement to
531   measure the model’s performance in separating the pair of classes. We use permutation to mimic
532   the absence of a feature, allowing us to quantify its importance. ICFI’s output is bounded to a
533   well-defined range to make it more interpretable to human stakeholders. Evaluation has, through
534   experiments, demonstrated ICFI’s usefulness and relevance. We used ground-truth provided by an
535   interpretable model and words in an NLP dataset to show how ICFI correctly retrieves features with
536
      high discriminative power for a pair of classes. Global methods on the same tasks fail to retrieve
      these insights, representing average behavior. Model retraining was used to display the quality of
537
      ICFI’s feature ranking.
538
539   Future work could further explore different permutation strategies and feature attribution scores,
      thereby leveraging our idea for a diverse set of XAI scores.
