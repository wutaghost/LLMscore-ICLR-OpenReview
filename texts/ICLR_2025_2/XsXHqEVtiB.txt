011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Intraoperative bleeding remains a significant challenge in modern surgery, ne-
014            cessitating rapid and accurate localization of bleeding sources to ensure effective
015            hemostasis. Proactive detection and timely intervention are critical for minimiz-
016            ing blood loss, reducing operative time, preventing complications, and decreasing
017            the need for intensive postoperative care. In this research, we introduce Selective
018
               Bleeding Alert Map (SBAM), a novel GAN-based framework designed for precise
               real-time detection of bleeding origins during surgery. Building upon our earlier
019
               BAM framework, SBAM shifts from broad, area-wide alerts to a focused approach
020
               that highlights only the exact bleeding areas, enhancing visual accuracy and po-
021            tentially improving surgeon focus and visibility—particularly beneficial in cases
022            of minor bleeding where excessive alerts could interfere with the surgical process.
023            To achieve this, we developed advanced image-to-image translation and segmenta-
024            tion models, custom thresholding techniques, and trajectory detection algorithms
025            to pinpoint bleeding sources with high precision. Utilizing our developed mimic
026            organ system for ethically sourced, realistic datasets—alongside synthetic data
027            generated from the orGAN system and Large Mask Inpainting (LaMa)—we cre-
028            ated a dedicated dataset specifically for SBAM training, including over 1,000 man-
029
               ually annotated images capturing both bleeding and non-bleeding regions within
               marked bleeding areas. Our instance segmentation model achieved a precision of
030
               92.5%, an accuracy of 98% and a mask mean Average Precision of 85% at an IoU
031
               threshold of 0.5 (mAP@50). Additionally, the SBAM model demonstrated high
032            accuracy in detecting bleeding points within real surgical videos from the Ham-
033            lyn dataset, underscoring its potential for practical surgical applications. Powered
034            by core algorithms and uniquely developed datasets, SBAM represents a pivotal
035            advancement in AI-assisted surgery, demonstrating superior performance in de-
036            tecting bleeding regions with high precision during critical scenarios.

      Surgery is a delicate field where even minor mishaps can lead to major complications. Despite
042   advancements aimed at reducing operative risks, challenges remain, especially in laparoscopic and
043   minimally invasive procedures, which are hindered by limited visibility and reduced tactile feedback.
044   Rapid and accurate responses to intraoperative issues are essential to prevent long-term damage
045   and the need for extensive postoperative care. One of the primary challenges during any surgical
046   procedure is internal bleeding. The prompt and precise detection of bleeding sources is critical to
047   prevent excessive blood loss, decrease operative time, and minimize postoperative complications.
048   Despite improvements in surgical techniques and technologies, bleeding remains a leading cause of
049   surgical morbidity and mortality.
050   Globally, surgical complications contribute substantially to the burden of disease. The World Health
051   Organization estimates that complications occur in up to 25% of patients undergoing major surgical
052   procedures, with bleeding being a significant contributor (Weiser et al., 2008). In laparoscopic
053   surgeries, the incidence of bleeding complications, while variable, is noteworthy. For example,
      bleeding complications during laparoscopic cholecystectomy occur in approximately 2% of cases

071   Bleeding often occurs at critical moments during surgery and can quickly obscure the surgical field.
072   Surgeons may initially be focused on other regions, delaying the detection of bleeding until it be-

      the surgical site, complicating the identification of bleeding origins (Abbitt et al., 2017). The lack

078   2018). Additionally, the dynamic nature of the surgical environment, with the constant movement of

080   (Allan et al., 2019).
081
      Delayed control of bleeding can lead to increased transfusion requirements, postoperative anaemia,
082
      infection, prolonged hospital stays, and increased mortality (Spahn et al., 2012). Studies have
083
      demonstrated that intraoperative blood loss is independently associated with increased postoperative
084   complications and mortality in surgical patients (Wu et al., 2012). Enhancing the ability of surgeons
085   to detect and manage bleeding promptly is therefore essential for improving surgical outcomes. Tra-
086   ditional bleeding detection relies heavily on the surgeon’s visual assessment and experience, making
087   it subjective and susceptible to fatigue or distractions (Garcı́a-Martı́nez et al., 2017). These methods
088   can be time-consuming and may not offer the rapid detection required in surgery. While automated
089   bleeding detection systems have been developed, many depend on simple thresholding or color seg-
090   mentation, which lack robustness against variations in lighting, tissue properties, and the presence
091   of other fluids, often resulting in inaccuracies (Mohebbian et al., 2021).
092   Advancements in Artificial Intelligence (AI) and Machine Learning (ML) offer new possibilities for
093   enhancing bleeding detection during surgery. Deep learning techniques, such as generative adver-
094   sarial networks (GANs), have shown great promise in image recognition and segmentation tasks
095   in medical imaging (Sorin et al., 2020). Previous studies have applied AI to tasks such as surgical
096   instrument detection in Zhao et al. (2019), anatomical structure segmentation in Toro et al. (2016),
097   and assessment of surgical skill (Pedrett et al., 2022). However, the application of AI for real-time
098
      bleeding detection in surgery remains an emerging area of research.
099   One such AI-based approach is the Bleeding Alert Map (BAM) framework (Sogabe et al., 2023),
100   which enhanced surgical safety by visualizing potential bleeding areas and estimating bleeding start
101   points. While effective for significant bleeding, BAM often issued wide-area alerts for safety rea-
102   sons, which could interfere with surgeon concentration during minor bleeding by creating unneces-
103   sary visual clutter and lacking precise intervention guidance.
104   To address these limitations, we propose the Selective Bleeding Alert Map (SBAM), a framework
105   that presents focused alerts highlighting only the exact bleeding sites and providing precise local-
106   ization of the bleeding origin. SBAM refines BAM by minimizing the alerted area and pinpoint-
107   ing the bleeding start point, enhancing the surgeon’s ability to respond quickly without distrac-
      tions—particularly crucial in minor bleeding where excessive alerts are counterproductive.

      The key differences between BAM and SBAM lie in both algorithm and function. BAM provided
109   broad alerts based on general bleeding detection without distinguishing bleeding severity or offering
110   precise localization, often lacking specificity on where the surgeon should intervene. In contrast,
111   SBAM employs advanced image-to-image translation, segmentation models, custom thresholding
112   techniques, and trajectory detection algorithms to accurately detect and localize bleeding sources.
113   By focusing on precise bleeding source detection rather than broad area alerts, SBAM enhances
114   visual accuracy and potentially improves surgeon focus and visibility.
115   To develop SBAM, we utilized a combination of advanced computer vision and AI techniques. We
116   created realistic, ethically sourced images using mimic organ systems (Sogabe et al., 2023) and
117   generated synthetic data with the orGAN system enhanced by LaMa-inspired inpainting. These
118   components enabled us to build a comprehensive dataset for training and validation, ensuring the
119   system can distinguish bleeding sources with high precision even under challenging conditions.
120   The integration of SBAM into surgical practice has the potential to significantly improve patient
121   outcomes by enabling faster hemostasis and reducing the risks associated with delayed bleeding
122   management (Lamb et al., 2023). By enhancing the accuracy of bleeding detection and minimizing
123
      unnecessary visual distractions, SBAM aims to set new standards in AI-assisted surgery.

      2.1   D EVELOPMENT OF E THICAL B LEEDING DATASETS
132
      Developing an ethical and diverse bleeding dataset is crucial for SBAM’s ability to generalize in
133
      real-world surgical scenarios. The dataset comprises two key components: real-world mimicking
134   organ data and synthetic images generated by the orGAN system. Mimic organs are synthetically de-
135   veloped to replicate the appearance, texture, and functionalities of living organs, including bleeding
136   behaviours. The orGAN system complements this by generating high-quality synthetic images. This
137   dual approach ensures the dataset covers a broad spectrum of bleeding scenarios while addressing
138   ethical concerns in medical data collection.
139
140         1. Mimicking Organ Data: We utilized approximately 200 high-definition videos from our
141            mimicking organ setups, which simulate realistic surgical bleeding scenarios on artificial
142            organs crafted from materials mimicking human tissue properties. Images were taken with
143            a handheld USB microscope and captured in a 1280 × 720 RGB, MPEG4 format video (20
144
               fps). Leica CLS 150X (Leica Microsystems, Wetzlar, Germany) was employed as the light
               source, and the surroundings were covered with a blackout curtain at the time of imaging
145
               to reproduce the environment similar to that of an abdominal cavity These setups allow
146
               for controlled replication of various bleeding patterns, flow rates, and lighting conditions,
147            providing valuable data for model training. Each video includes a start frame (Fs ) and an
148            end frame (Fe ), essential for extracting temporal information and performing the ”Pixel
149            Differentiation” step during data preprocessing.
150
151
            2. Synthetic Image Generation: To enhance dataset diversity and address the scarcity of
               annotated surgical images, we employed the orGAN system to generate around 15,000
152
               high-fidelity synthetic images with non-static temporal information. The orGAN system
153
               uses Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) to produce realistic surgical images featur-
154            ing a wide range of bleeding scenarios. In-detail explanation of the generation has been
155            mentioned in our previous paper [Paper citation withheld for blind review]. Synthetic data
156            augmentation has been shown to improve model performance by providing variability and
157            aiding generalization in medical imaging tasks.(Frid-Adar et al., 2018).
158
159   Both the mimicking organ data and synthetic datasets have precise coordinates for the areas where
160   bleeding originates. However, the synthetic images generated by the orGAN system lack static tem-
161   poral information (no start and end frames), making pixel differentiation infeasible for these images.
      Static Temporal Information helps during the ”Pixel Differentiation” process in Preprocessing.

      2.3   D EVELOPMENT OF A B EEDING S EGMENTATION DATASET
184
      Recognizing the need for precise bleeding localization (Biancari et al., 2017) and the limitations of
185   existing datasets, we created a first-of-its-kind segmentation dataset comprising over 1,000 manually
186   annotated images from the mimicking organ videos. Over a span of 40 days, expert annotators
187   meticulously labelled each image. Figure 2 (a) shows a glimpse of an example set of segmented
188   images along with its precise borders.
189
      The annotations include two classes:
190
191         1. Bleed Zone: The exact outlines of all areas exhibiting bleeding, providing precise segmen-
192            tation of bleeding regions. Visible within the purple segment boundary in Fig 2 (a).
193
194
            2. No-Bleed Zone within Bleeding Areas: Areas within the bleed zones that are not ac-
               tively bleeding. This class is distinct from regular tissue areas because it highlights regions
195
               within the bleeding zone that are unaffected, helping to subtract out areas not affected by
196
               bleeding. This differentiation is crucial because standard segmentation may encompass en-
197            tire regions, and distinguishing non-bleeding areas within the bleeding zone enhances the
198            precision of the model. It is visible as a red segment in Fig. 2 (a).
199
200   To isolate the areas actively exhibiting bleeding, we compute the difference between the bleed zone
201   segmentation mask B(x, y) and the no-bleed mask N (x, y). The ”Bleed-Only” region is defined
202   as {(x, y) | B(x, y) − N (x, y) > 0}, where the set of pixels of the difference is positive. This
203   operation ensures that only the actively bleeding pixels are retained by subtracting non-bleeding
204   areas within the bleeding zones. The ”Bleed” and ”No-Bleed” zones can be seen in Fig. 2 (a). The
205
      dataset is further divided into 3 splits of - train, test valid in an 8:1:1 ratio in a balanced manner.
      This detailed segmentation lays the foundation to allow us to train a state-of-the-art (SOTA) model
206
      capable of accurately detecting and segmenting specific bleeding-only regions, even in complex
207
      surgical scenarios where traditional computer vision techniques and previous works fall short.

333

      one-shot learning Wang et al. (2020), which is advantageous in medical scenarios where annotated
334
      data is scarce and costly to obtain. Data augmentation methods, such as random flipping, scaling,
335
      and rotation, were applied to enhance the model’s robustness to variations in the data.

367   In parallel, SBAM generation was framed as an image-to-image translation task, translating original
368   surgical images into their corresponding SBAM representations (aka, the data produced via Data
369   Preprocessing). For this purpose, we employed the NVIDIA Pix2PixHD Architecture (Wang et al.,
370   2017), which is well-suited for high-resolution image translation tasks, with minimal tweaks and
371   customisations to suit our needs. This architecture, as shown in Fig. 4 enables the direct mapping
372   of raw input images to segmented bleeding regions in a visually interpretable manner.
373   The architecture utilizes a two-tier generator approach (G1 and G2) for high-resolution image-to-
374   image translation. G1 generates a basic, low-resolution structure from the input, which is defined
375   by G2 to enhance detail and quality. The output undergoes evaluation by multi-scale discrimina-
376   tors (D1, D2, D3) at various resolutions to ensure photorealism and accuracy (Kirimanjeshwara &
377   Prasad, 2024). D1 produces a score map identifying critical discriminative regions, crucial for tasks
      like precise bleeding detection in medical imaging, with further refinement by D2 and D3.

515   6   L IMITATIONS AND C ONCLUSION
516
517
      While the SBAM framework demonstrates significant advancements in precise bleeding detection,
518   certain limitations must be acknowledged. Firstly, we were unable to directly compare our segmenta-
519   tion results with some previous AI-based approaches that utilize bounding boxes and convolutional
520   neural networks (CNNs), due to the unavailability of their datasets and model implementations,
521   which are often proprietary and not publicly shared (Tantoso et al., 2019; Mishra et al., 2022). This
522   lack of accessible benchmarks limits the ability to quantitatively assess the relative performance of
523   our approach. However, we believe our model offers superior performance by providing precise
524   segmentation rather than approximate localization. Other works often rely on bounding boxes or
525   less accurate methods and do not employ advanced techniques like one-shot instance segmentation,
526
      as shown in Fig. 6.
527   Secondly, our training dataset primarily represents classic cases of bleeding on standard organ mod-
528   els, which may not encompass the full spectrum of anatomical variations and complex surgical
529   scenarios encountered in practice. Addressing this limitation will require the development of addi-
530   tional mimic organs and enhancements to the orGAN system to generate more diverse datasets—a
531   focus of our ongoing research.
532   SBAM offers an effective approach to real-time bleeding source detection, enhancing precision with-
533   out obstructing critical visual information. Despite the limitations, our work lays a solid foundation
534   for further advancements, and we are committed to expanding our datasets and refining our models
535   to encompass a wider range of surgical conditions.

      A     DATASET AND E THICAL C ONSIDERATIONS
706
707
      Developing AI models for surgical applications requires extensive, high-quality datasets. However,
      acquiring real surgical data poses significant ethical and practical challenges due to strict privacy
708
      laws like the Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act (HIPAA) and the General Data
709
      Protection Regulation (GDPR), which protect patient confidentiality and limit data availability (Price
710   & Cohen, 2019). Ethical concerns surrounding patient consent and the invasive nature of data col-
711   lection further complicate the gathering of diverse datasets necessary for robust AI training(Morley
712   et al., 2020).
713
      Traditional reliance on animal models raises additional ethical issues and often fails to accurately
714
      replicate human anatomical and physiological conditions, limiting the effectiveness of AI models
715
      trained on such data (Akhtar, 2015). To overcome these challenges, we developed an innovative
716   approach using synthetic data generated from in-house mimic organ system.
717
718
      A.1   E THICAL I NNOVATION WITH S YNTHETIC DATA
719
720   Mimic organ system creates realistic organ models using biocompatible materials that simulate hu-
721   man tissue properties. This allows us to replicate surgical scenarios, including internal bleeding,
722   without involving real patients or animals. By simulating various bleeding patterns and surgical con-
723   ditions, we generate high-fidelity surgical images ethically and efficiently. We further augmented
724   our dataset using Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs). As noted before, GANs have been
725
      effectively utilized to generate synthetic medical images that enhance AI training while preserving
      patient privacy (Frid-Adar et al., 2018). By training GANs on existing surgical images, we pro-
726
      duced a diverse set of synthetic images capturing various surgical anomalies and conditions. This
727
      method allows us to explore a wide range of surgical scenarios, ensuring our model is trained on
728   comprehensive data while adhering to ethical standards.
729
730   Accurate annotation of surgical images is essential but often time-consuming and requires expert
731
      knowledge. By using synthetic data with known ground truth, we simplified the annotation process.
      The exact locations and characteristics of bleeding points are inherently known in our synthetic
732
      images, reducing the burden on medical professionals for manual annotation. Expert reviews and
733
      validation on these annotations were done to ensure high-quality labels necessary for effective AI
734   training. Our dataset comprises over 1,000 images, including both real and synthetic data, covering
735   various surgical procedures, bleeding types, and environmental conditions. By incorporating diverse
736   blood flow patterns, organ appearances, and lighting conditions, we enhanced the model’s ability to
737   generalize across different clinical scenarios. This approach addresses the issue of data imbalance
738   often found in medical datasets and improves the robustness and accuracy of the SBAM framework
739   when deployed in real-world settings.
