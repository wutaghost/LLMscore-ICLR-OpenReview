007                                               A BSTRACT
008
009            Recent advancements in foundation models have been successfully extended to the
010            time series (TS) domain, facilitated by the emergence of large-scale TS datasets.
011            However, previous efforts have primarily focused on designing model architec-
012            tures to address explicit heterogeneity among datasets such as various numbers
013            of channels, while often overlooking implicit heterogeneity such as varying de-
014
               pendencies between channels. In this work, we introduce the concept of partial
               channel dependence (PCD) for models capturing channel dependencies (CDs) via
015
               attention, which enables a more sophisticated adjustment of CDs based on dataset-
016
               specific information. To achieve PCD, we propose a channel mask that captures
017            the relationships between channels within a dataset using two key components: 1)
018            a correlation matrix that encodes relative dependencies between channels, and 2)
019            domain parameters that learn the absolute dependencies specific to each dataset,
020            refining the correlation matrix. We validate the effectiveness of PCD across four
021            tasks in TS including forecasting, classification, imputation, and anomaly detection,
022            under diverse settings, including few-shot and zero-shot scenarios with both TS
023            foundation models and single-task models.

029

030   either capture or disregard CD, often overlooking the potential differences in CD across datasets.

034
      using a single model. However, directly applying FMs to TS is challenging due to the heterogeneity
035
      across TS datasets, which can be categorized into two types: explicit and implicit heterogeneity.

054
      To this end, we introduce the concept of partial channel dependence (PCD) which adjusts the
055   CD estimated by the Transformer-based model by leveraging the characteristics of the dataset, as
056   capturing the varying CD across datasets with a single model can be challenging. Specifically, we
057   propose a channel mask (CM) that adjusts the dependencies between channels to achieve PCD. A
058   CM consists of 1) a correlation matrix to encode relative dependencies between channels and
059   2) domain parameters that learn the absolute dependencies specific to each dataset to refine the
060   correlation matrix. The proposed CM, constructed using dataset-specific information, is multiplied
061   to the (channel-wise) attention matrix (i.e., CD estimated by the model). We conduct extensive
062   experiments to validate the effectiveness of CMs with task-specific models and TSFMs on various
063
      tasks including forecasting, classification, imputation, and anomaly detection, under various settings
      such as few-shot and zero-shot. The main contributions are summarized as follows:
064
065   • We introduce the concept of partial channel dependence (PCD), where the channel dependence
066     (CD) captured by the model is adjusted based on the characteristics of the TS dataset.
067   • We propose a channel mask (CM) to achieve PCD, which is a matrix that captures 1) relative
068     dependencies between channels with a correlation matrix, and 2) absolute dependencies specific
069     to each dataset with domain parameters that refine the correlation matrix. The proposed CM is a
070
        plug-and-play method applicable to any model that captures CD using an attention mechanism.
071
      • We present extensive experiments with both TSFMs and single-task models across four different
        tasks under various settings, demonstrating consistent performance gains. For example, applying
072
        CMs to TSFMs, e.g., UniTS (Gao et al., 2024), and to single-task models, e.g., iTransformer (Liu
073
        et al., 2024a), yields performance gains across all 20 and 13 forecasting tasks, respectively.

075   2   R ELATED W ORKS
076
077   MTS forecasting models can be categorized into CI and CD models, where CI models process
078   channels independently without accounting for dependencies between them, whereas CD models
079   account for these dependencies. For CI models, DLinear (Zeng et al., 2023) and RLinear (Li et al.,
080   2023) employ linear models along the time dimension, PatchTST (Nie et al., 2023) divides TS into
081   patches and feeds them into a Transformer (Vaswani et al., 2017) in a CI manner, and PITS (Lee et al.,
082
      2024) combines CI and patch independent architectures with multi-layer perceptrons (MLPs). For
      CD models, Crossformer (Zhang & Yan, 2023) employs a two-stage attention mechanism to capture
083
      both temporal dependencies (TD) and CD, TSMixer (Chen et al., 2023) utilizes MLPs with patching
084
      to capture both dependencies, and CrossGNN (Huang et al., 2023) employs a linear complexity graph
085   neural network to refine the CD. Recently, iTransformer (Liu et al., 2024a) inverts the traditional
086   Transformer framework in TS domain by treating each channel as a token instead of each patch,
087   thereby shifting the focus from capturing TD to CD, LIFT (Zhao & Shen, 2024) captures the lead-lag
088   relationship between channels, and CDAM (Qi et al., 2024) minimizes redundant information while
089   enhancing relevant mutual information between channels to effectively capture CD. Among these
090   two frameworks, we highlight the importance of CD, as CI can be achieved with CD by disregarding
091   dependencies between irrelevant channels when well captured (Nie et al., 2023). Nonetheless,
092   most CD models primarily focus on architectural solutions for handling CD and often overlook the
093
      characteristics of TS datasets, motivating us to consider CD varying across datasets.
094   TS foundation models often borrow knowledge from other fields, such as natural language processing,
095   primarily due to the lack of large-scale datasets in the TS domain. In response to this challenge,
096   there have been efforts to adapt large language models (LLMs) for TS tasks: GPT4TS (Zhou et al.,
097   2023) fine-tunes the embedding layers of LLMs and Time-LLM (Jin et al., 2024) aligns TS data with
098   LLM-based text prototypes to address TS tasks. Recent works have focused on pretraining TSFMs
099
      exclusively on TS datasets from various sources. MOMENT (Goswami et al., 2024) and Timer (Liu
      et al., 2024b) collect extensive and heterogeneous sets of TS datasets to pretrain Transformer-based
100
      TSFMs, while MOIRAI (Woo et al., 2024) enhances the Transformer architecture to address domain-
101
      specific challenges in constructing TSFMs. UniTS (Gao et al., 2024) proposes a TSFM that handles
102   various tasks with a single architecture through prompt-tuning. These TSFMs either adopt a CI
103   or CD architecture, and we argue that the CD architecture, which is mostly based on the attention
104   mechanism of Transformers, is crucial for TSFMs. This is due to the capacity-robustness trade-off
105   of architectures (Han et al., 2023), with the higher capacity of the CD architecture benefiting larger
106   datasets used for training TSFMs. However, these TSFMs based on CD architectures do not account
107   for the heterogeneity among datasets in terms of CD, while different TS datasets exhibit varying
      degrees of CD. This motivates us to adjust CD in TSFMs based on the characteristics of each dataset.

      3     M ETHODOLOGY
131   In this section, we introduce a channel mask (CM), a simple yet effective method for achieving PCD.
132   A CM employs a correlation matrix to capture relative dependencies between channels and adjusts it
133   with domain parameters to learn absolute dependencies specific to each dataset. We also introduce a
134   new metric, the channel dependence ratio (CD ratio), which uses a CM to quantify the degree of CD
135   for each dataset. The overall framework of a CM is illustrated in Figure 2.
136
      3.1   C OMPONENTS OF C HANNEL M ASK
137
138   As shown in Figure 2, a CM consists of two components: 1) correlation matrix (R) between channels,
139   and 2) domain parameters (α and β), which scale and shift the matrix according to the dataset’s
140   characteristics, along with a sigmoid function to normalize the values between 0 and 1.
141
      Correlation matrix. Correlation measures the relationships between channels and has been used in
142   previous works to analyze CD (Yang et al., 2024; Zhao & Shen, 2024). Building on these approaches,
143   we employ a correlation matrix (R) between channels to create a CM. However, high correlation
144   does not always indicate a strong positive relationship, as the values range from −1 to 1, with strong
145   negative relationships near −1. To address this issue, we use the absolute value of the matrix |R|.
146
      Domain parameters. We argue that |R| alone might be insufficient for modeling a CM for the
147
      following reasons: First, correlation is a relative measure that depends on the dataset. As shown in
148   the first panel of Figure 3, different datasets exhibit different distributions of the elements of |R|. To
149   align these differences, we normalize |R| by subtracting the mean value, resulting in R̄, as shown in
150   the second panel of Figure 3. Second, the relationship between correlation and CD may vary across
151   datasets (i.e., the same correlation can correspond to different levels of CD depending on the dataset).
152   To deal with this discrepancy among datasets, we introduce two learnable domain parameters, α
153   and β, which scale and shift |R|, respectively, as shown in the third panel of Figure 3. Using these
154   parameters along with a sigmoid function, we model a CM for achieving PCD as M = σ(α · R̄ + β).
155

      6        C ONCLUSION
478   In this work, we introduce the concept of PCD to adjust the CD estimated by the model using a CM, a
479   plug-and-play method that captures both relative and absolute dependencies between channels using
480   dataset-specific information. Our results demonstrate that incorporating prior knowledge of datasets
481   is crucial when building TSFMs, leading to superior performance across various models and settings.
482   However, since our method can only be applied to Transformer-based methods, which are the most
483   widely used architecture for FMs, we aim to develop a novel approach to achieve PCD without
484   relying on Transformer-based methods in the future. We hope our work highlights the importance of
485   utilizing dataset-specific information when building FMs across different domains.

      B      I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
824
825   It is important to note that we follow the experimental settings of iTransformer for single-task
826   and UniTS for multi-task settings, respectively. For the implementation, we use the official code
827   repositories of both methods, running the provided scripts without modifications. However, for UniTS
828   in the prompt tuning setting, we encountered an issue where the model failed to converge using the
829   provided script. This was resolved by setting the hidden dimension to D = 32, which we applied
830   uniformly across both UniTS and its integration with our method. The following sections outline the
831
      specific settings we adhered to.
832   B.1     I MPLEMENTATION FOR S INGLE - TASK M ODEL : I T RANSFORMER
833
834   Following iTransformer (Liu et al., 2024a), we use the Adam optimizer (Kinga et al., 2015) and L2
835   loss for model optimization. The batch size is consistently set to 32, and the number of training
836   epochs is fixed at 10. Since our approach is plug-and-play, we do not adjust any hyperparameters for
837
      our method; instead, we use the same hyperparameters employed by iTransformer.
838   B.2     I MPLEMENTATION FOR M ULTI - TASK M ODEL : U NI TS
839
840   Model architecture. In a multi-task setting, the UniTS network consists of three UniTS blocks,
841   along with one GEN tower and one CLS tower. For each data source, specific prompt and task tokens
842   are assigned, with forecasting tasks on the same source but with varying forecast lengths using the
843
      same prompt and GEN token. To enable zero-shot learning on new datasets, a shared prompt and GEN
      token are applied across all data sources. The embedding dimensions are set to 64 for the supervised
844
      version, and 32 for the prompt-tuning version, and all blocks in UniTS retain the same feature shape.
845
846   Model training. In multi-task settings, models are trained jointly on multiple tasks following a
847   unified protocol. To match the largest dataset, samples from each dataset are repeated within each
848   epoch. Supervised training is conducted over 5 epochs with gradient accumulation, yielding an
849
      effective batch size of 1024. The initial learning rate is set at 3.2e-2 and is adjusted using a multi-step
      decay schedule. For self-supervised pretraining, the models training with an are trained for 10 epochs
850
      with effective batch size of 4096, starting with a learning rate of 6.4e-3, which is adjusted using a
851
      cosine decay schedule.
852
853   B.3     C ONSTRUCTION OF C ORRELATION M ATRIX
854
855
      For constructing the correlation matrix for CM, we used the datasets corresponding to the training
      period for forecasting datasets and the training instances for classification datasets. Specifically, for
856
      a forecasting dataset with shape (C, Ltrain + Lval + Ltest ), we compute the correlation matrix with
857
      shape (C, C) using only the training period with shape (C, Ltrain ). For a classification dataset with
858   shape (Ntrain + Nval + Ntest , C, L), we compute the correlation matrix with shape (C, C) using only
859   the training instances with shape (Ntrain , C, L) by averaging across the instances.
