012
013
               StarCraft II plays an important role in developing AI agents for real-time strategic
014            reasoning due to its complex nature. However, people usually draw conclusions of
015            how competent their agents are according to the level of the built-in agents in Star-
016            Craft II which they can win in terms of the final success rate. Little intermediate
017            quantitative information is considered while human-in-the-loop analysis is time
018            inefficient, which results in inadequate reflection of the true strategic reasoning
019            ability. In this work, we propose StarCraft II Arena, a well-designed benchmark
020            for evaluating the strategic planning, real-time decision-making, and adaptability
021            capabilities of large language models (LLMs) agents. We introduce using fine-
022
               grained capability metrics, allowing for targeted capture and analysis of specific
               capability, and further propose a detailed decision trace to enhance the under-
023
               standing of LLM behavior. We demonstrate the utility of such a benchmark by
024
               evaluating several state-of-the-art LLMs in various setups. Our results reveal dis-
025            tinct performances in long-term strategy development, real-time decision-making,
026            and adapting to environmental changes. Such results show that the StarCraft II
027            Arena offers a deeper insight into the decision-making process of LLMs and has
028            the potential to become a challenging and comprehensive benchmark for strategic
029            reasoning.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
034
035   LLMs have recently demonstrated exceptional capabilities in reasoning, planning, and problem-
036   solving (Xi et al., 2023) across a range of domains, such as policy formulation (Xiao et al., 2023;
037   Hua et al., 2023), investment decision-making (Weiss et al.; Li et al., 2023b), and strategic optimi-
038   sation (Liu et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024a). Successfully completing these complex tasks requires
039
      intelligent agents to perceive, make decisions, and execute actions (Wooldridge & Jennings, 1995)
      within diverse and dynamic environments. This process not only involves deep reasoning to antic-
040
      ipate risks and weaknesses but also the ability to understand the motivations, beliefs, and potential
041
      deceptive behaviors of other agents (Hao et al., 2023; Premack & Woodruff, 1978; Street et al.,
042   2024). Although LLMs have shown significant promise in managing such scenarios, positioning
043   them as key technologies for achieving artificial general intelligence (AGI) (You et al., 2024; Morris
044   et al.), their performance in real-world applications continues to face numerous challenges.
045
      Evaluating the capabilities of LLM agents effectively is critical for the further development of this
046
      field. Traditional static evaluation datasets, while offering a standardized testing framework (Wang
047
      et al., 2019; Srivastava et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2021; Xie et al., 2024), are insufficient for captur-
048   ing how models make decisions and adapt in dynamic environments. As a result, there has been
049   growing interest in assessing the performance of large models within executable environments (Liu
050   et al., 2023a; Xi et al., 2024)—simulated or real-world interactive platforms, including web naviga-
051   tion (Lai et al., 2024), household tasks (Li et al., 2024), gaming (Bailis et al., 2024; Qi et al., 2024),
052   and programming (Qian et al., 2024). Among these, games, with their clear rules and complex
053   decision-making mechanisms, are considered ideal platforms for evaluating AI decision-making
      abilities (Costarelli et al., 2024).

076   However, existing evaluation benchmarks usually take the final outcome of a game such as success
077   rate as the primary measure of performance (Duan et al., 2024). They further neglect the details
078   of the intermediate outcomes gradually generated by LLM agents during the sequential decision-
079   making process (Xi et al., 2024; Ma et al., 2024). Although the success rate reflects the overall
080   ability of the competing agents, this singular metric is usually inadequate for reflecting how capa-
081   ble an LLM agent is when handling complexity or adapting to changing tasks. Therefore, a more
082   comprehensive evaluation approach is required to better reflect the actual underlying reasoning ca-
083
      pabilities of LLMs in dynamic environments.
084   In this study, we introduce StarCraft II Arena, a benchmark specifically designed to evaluate the
085   abilities of LLMs for strategic planning, real-time decision-making, and adaptation in the game Star-
086   Craft II. As illustrated in Figure 1, unlike traditional benchmarks which usually depend on static tests
087   or success rates, StarCraft II Arena incorporates fine-grained capability evaluation metrics, which al-
088   lows for more detailed analysis of the performance of LLMs across multiple dimensions. Moreover,
089
      we also introduce a decision-tracking mechanism which records the intermediate decision results
      of LLMs during the task execution. By looking into the decision trajectories, we can analyze how
090
      LLMs adjust strategies in response to dynamic changes in the environment. As a result, it allows a
091
      more comprehensive understanding of the underlying decision process of LLMs rather than using
092   the final outcome only. We demonstrate the utility of StarCraft II Arena by applying it to a range of
093   recent LLM agents, both proprietary and open-source, and leading to the following key findings: (1)
094   proprietary LLMs excel in long-term strategic planning and resource management but demonstrate
095   limitations in dynamic environments that require rapid adaptation; (2) most existing LLMs struggle
096   with handling incomplete information and adapting to rapidly evolving opponent strategies, limiting
097   their ability to respond effectively to shifting tactics; and (3) smaller models show greater flexibility
098   in real-time decision-making, particularly in high-frequency decision-making tasks, where they of-
099   ten outperform their larger counterparts. These findings highlight the potential of StarCraft II Arena
100
      as a challenging benchmark for LLMs agents in strategic reasoning tasks.

145   2.2   B ENCHMARKS FOR AI AGENTS
146
147   Evaluation Environments. In previous research, the capabilities of large models have primarily
148   been assessed through the construction of static datasets (Wang et al., 2019; Srivastava et al., 2023;
149   Zheng et al., 2023b; Yue et al., 2024; Xie et al., 2024). While an increasing number of bench-
150   marks have introduced broader tasks and datasets, most remain confined to traditional tasks and
151   fail to comprehensively evaluate the capabilities of large language models (LLMs) in open-ended
152   generation, multi-turn interactions, and agent-based roles (Gur et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2024b;
153
      Liu et al., 2023b). As LLMs become more adept at addressing real-world challenges, there is a
      growing trend towards evaluation methods that are based on executable environments rather than
154
      static datasets (Gur et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023; Shinn et al., 2024). Specifically, researchers
155
      are now focusing on areas such as web navigation (Deng et al., 2024; Yao et al., preprint), text-
156   based games (Bailis et al., 2024; Mukobi et al., 2023), household tasks (Wang et al., 2022), digital
157   games (Qi et al., 2024; Ma et al., 2023), avatar tasks (Han et al., 2024), tool usage (Tang et al.,
158   2023), and programming (Qian et al., 2024; Zheng et al., 2023b), all of which provide a more re-
159   alistic context for assessing LLMs. In particular, games are widely regarded as ideal experimental
160   platforms for evaluating the decision-making capabilities of large models (Liu et al., 2023a). By
161   placing models in dynamic and complex gaming environments, researchers can effectively gauge
      their performance in real-world scenarios.

244   4.1    D ECOMPOSITION OF THE S TRATEGIC R EASONING C APABILITY
245
246   LLM agents demonstrate several advanced cognitive abilities akin to human intelligence in complex
247   environments, including chain-of-thought reasoning(Wei et al., 2022; Jin & Lu, 2023; Zhang et al.,
248   2023), planning(Huang et al., 2024a), self-reflection(Madaan et al., 2024), memory(Zheng et al.,
249
      2023a; Zhang et al., 2024b), and learning(Zhang et al., 2024a; Xi et al., 2024). Based on these char-
      acteristics, the selection of strategic planning, real-time decision-making, and adaptability as core
250
      evaluation dimensions is logically grounded. These three dimensions encapsulate the essential ca-
251
      pabilities required for agents to tackle complex tasks, representing holistic thinking, rapid response,
252   and flexible adaptation.
253
254   Strategic planning serves as the foundation for LLMs when addressing long-term objectives in
255
      dynamic environments. Short-term reactions alone are insufficient to manage fluctuating conditions.
      The model must have a broad view, ensuring the efficient allocation of resources, the prioritisation
256
      of tasks, and the formulation of long-term strategies to maintain a competitive edge. Effective
257
      strategic planning demands not only the ability to foresee potential future developments but also
258   to make informed decisions concerning resource management, technological advancements, and
259   unit production, thereby securing and sustaining a strategic advantage. In games like StarCraft II,
260   for instance, an LLM must efficiently manage early resource accumulation and expansion while
261   preparing for large-scale combat in the mid to late game.
262
      Real-time decision-making is critical when using LLMs in real applications. While several LLM
263
      agents claim to be capable for complex tasks, some are evaluated in a setup where the executing
264   testing system needs to be suspended while the LLM agents perform inference. A perfect strategic
265   plan would be ineffective if the model takes too long to perform inference and cannot respond rapidly
266   enough to the changing conditions. The model must continuously process dynamic information and
267   adjust its tactics accordingly. For instance, during a sudden enemy assault, the LLM must promptly
268   deploy units to defend or counter-attack, maintaining control over the situation. This capability
269   requires not only rapid information processing but also the ability to evaluate multiple strategies
      quickly and efficiently to preserve overarching strategic objectives.

308   4.3   D ECISION T RACKING AND B EHAVIORAL A NALYSIS
309
310   To gain a deeper understanding of the decision-making processes and behavioural patterns of LLMs
311   in the StarCraft II Arena, this paper introduces a decision tracking and behavioural analysis sys-
312   tem. This system records and analyzes critical operations and decisions made by the LLM during
313   gameplay, contextualizing them within the current game state and mission objectives to uncover the
314
      underlying strategic logic.
315   Specifically, the system captures key actions such as resource allocation, technological upgrades,
316   unit production, and troop movements. A decision trace of an LLM consists of a chronological
317   record of these key actions and decisions, detailing the context in which they were made. It typically
318   comprises three main components: (1) Action Type, indicating the specific type of decision made
319   (e.g., resource allocation, unit production); (2) Decision Context, which includes the game state and
320
      mission objectives at the time of the decision; and (3) Outcome, reflecting the consequences of the
      decision on the gameplay.

      StarCraft II is a real-time strategy game whose core mechanics include resource management, base
      building, troop production and command. Players need to efficiently gather resources, build and
842
      upgrade bases, train various military units, and defeat opponents through precise micromanagement
843
      and macro-strategy in a real-time environment. The game emphasizes quick decision-making and
844   flexibility, requiring players to balance economic development and military operations in a highly
845   dynamic battlefield in order to ultimately destroy the enemy’s base and win.
846
847
      The core mechanics of Civilization revolve around turn-based strategy, where players lead a civiliza-
      tion from antiquity to the future by managing cities, developing technology and culture, exploring
848
      maps, and engaging in diplomacy and warfare. With an emphasis on resource management, long-
849
      term planning, and strategic decision-making, the game requires players to unlock new abilities
850   through the tech and culture trees, choose different victory conditions (e.g., military victory, tech
851   victory, or cultural victory), and gain an advantage in their interactions with other civilizations. The
852   variety and depth of the game makes it a classic strategy game.
