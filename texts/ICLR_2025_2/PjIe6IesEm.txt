011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Diffusion models have demonstrated remarkable capabilities in image synthesis,
014            but their recently proven vulnerability to Membership Inference Attacks (MIAs)
015            poses a critical privacy concern. This paper introduces two novel and efficient
016
               approaches (DualMD and DistillMD) to protect diffusion models against MIAs
               while maintaining high utility. Both methods are based on training two sepa-
017
               rate diffusion models on disjoint subsets of the original dataset. DualMD then
018
               employs a private inference pipeline that utilizes both models. This strategy sig-
019            nificantly reduces the risk of black-box MIAs by limiting the information any sin-
020            gle model contains about individual training samples. The dual models can also
021            generate “soft targets” to train a private student model in DistillMD, enhancing
022            privacy guarantees against all types of MIAs. Extensive evaluations of DualMD
023            and DistillMD against state-of-the-art MIAs across various datasets in white-box
024            and black-box settings demonstrate their effectiveness in substantially reducing
025            MIA success rates while preserving competitive image generation performance.
026            Notably, our experiments reveal that DistillMD not only defends against MIAs
027
               but also mitigates model memorization, indicating that both vulnerabilities stem
               from overfitting and can be addressed simultaneously with our unified approach.

      In recent years, diffusion models (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015; Ho et al., 2020; Rombach et al., 2022)
      have rapidly emerged as a powerful tool for image generation, outperforming traditional methods
034
      such as Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) (Goodfellow et al., 2014) and Variational Au-
035
      toencoders (VAEs) (Kingma, 2013). These models, including well-known examples like Stable
036   Diffusion models (Rombach et al., 2022; Podell et al., 2023), DALL-E 2 (Ramesh et al., 2022) and
037   Imagen (Saharia et al., 2022), utilize a progressive denoising process that results in higher-quality
038   and more stable image generation compared to previous architectures. By gradually transforming
039   random noise into clean images, diffusion models excel at producing detailed and realistic visuals
040   across various applications, from graphic design to medical imaging.
041
      However, the superior performance of diffusion models relies heavily on large and diverse datasets,
042   which often include sensitive information such as copyrighted images, personal photos, medical
043   data, and even stylistic elements from contemporary artists. The nature of these datasets poses sig-
044   nificant privacy risks, as diffusion models can inadvertently memorize and reproduce parts of their
045   training data during the generation (Carlini et al., 2023). This replication of training data during in-
046   ference makes diffusion models vulnerable to Membership Inference Attacks (MIAs) (Shokri et al.,
047   2017; Matsumoto et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2022), which aim to determine whether specific samples
048   are present in their training data. If a model has been trained on sensitive datasets, an attacker might
049   extract or infer specific details about the data used in training, leading to unintended exposure of
050
      private or proprietary information.
051   Therefore, implementing robust defense mechanisms to protect against MIAs and other privacy-
052   related attacks is crucial. Existing defense methods for MIAs, such as those based on model dis-
053   tillation (Tang et al., 2022; Shejwalkar & Houmansadr, 2021; Mazzone et al., 2022), have proven
      effective in image classification models by reducing overfitting and limiting memorization of train-

      Membership Inference Attacks The membership inference attacks (MIAs), introduced by Shokri
169
      et al. (2017), aim to identify whether a specific data point was part of the model’s training set. Based
170   on the threat models or the level of access attackers have, MIAs can be classified into white-box
171   and black-box attacks. White-box MIAs usually utilize the internal parameters and gradients of
172   the diffusion model to perform threshold-based attacks (Hu & Pang, 2023; Dubiński et al., 2023),
173   gradient-based attacks (Pang et al., 2023) and proximal initialization (Kong et al., 2024). In con-
174   trast, black-box MIAs such as Pang & Wang (2023) target the output generated by the diffusion
175   models without direct access to internal parameters. Studies have demonstrated that these attacks
176   can effectively differentiate between training and non-training samples by analyzing the generated
177   image quality (Wu et al., 2022; Matsumoto et al., 2023; Carlini et al., 2023), and the estimation
178
      errors (Duan et al., 2023). Moreover, Li et al. (2024b) recently find that fine-tuning models on small
      datasets can augment their vulnerability to MIAs.
179
180
      Membership Inference Defenses Existing studies have demonstrated that overfitting in the threat
181
      models is a primary factor contributing to their vulnerability to MIAs. Consequently, various de-
182   fenses have been proposed to counter MIAs, for example, by addressing overfitting, including tech-
183   niques such as adversarial regularization (Hu et al., 2021), dropout (Salem et al., 2018), overconfi-
184   dence reduction (Chen & Pattabiraman, 2023), and early stopping (Song & Mittal, 2021). Further-
185   more, differential privacy (DP) (Yeom et al., 2018; Abadi et al., 2016; Wu et al., 2019) has been
186   widely used to mitigate MIAs by limiting the influence of any training data point on the model.
187   However, DP methods often face trade-offs between privacy and utility. Additionally, knowledge
188   distillation-based defenses such as distillation for membership privacy (Shejwalkar & Houmansadr,
189   2021) and complementary knowledge distillation (Zheng et al., 2021) aim to protect against MIAs
190
      by transferring knowledge from unprotected models. More recently, multiple techniques (Tang et al.,
      2022; Mazzone et al., 2022; Li et al., 2024a) have been proposed to combine knowledge distillation
191
      with ensemble learning to preserve data privacy. Nevertheless, none of the methods are designed
192
      specifically for diffusion models which are usually large and constrained by resource limitation.
193
194
      Diffusion Memorization and Mitigation It is widely recognized that generative language mod-
195   els pose a risk of replicating content from their training data (Carlini et al., 2021; 2022). Similarly,
196   Webster (2023) observe the same behavior of large diffusion models, while Somepalli et al. (2023a)
197   argue that diffusion models trained on smaller datasets tend to produce images that closely resemble
198   those in the training set. As the size of the training dataset increases, the likelihood of such replica-
199   tion decreases. Several mitigation strategies have been explored to address these issues of diffusion
200   models by either modifying the text conditioning (Somepalli et al., 2023b; Wen et al., 2024; Ren
201   et al., 2024), manipulating the guidance scale (Chen et al., 2024), or model pruning (Struppek et al.;
202   Chavhan et al., 2024).

361   3.6   M EMBERSHIP I NFERENCE D EFENSES H ELP M ITIGATING DATA M EMORIZATION
362
363   Recently, an increasing body of research (Somepalli et al., 2023a;b) has highlighted the issue of
364   data memorization in modern diffusion models, where some generated images are near-identical
365   reproductions of images from the training datasets. Previous studies (Shokri et al., 2017; Yeom et al.,
366   2018) have shown that overfitting renders models vulnerable to MIAs. Given that data memorization
367   is often considered a more extreme form of overfitting, this raises an important question: Is there a
368
      connection between MIAs and data memorization?
369   Our findings suggest that loss-based MIA techniques can effectively detect memorization in dif-
370   fusion models. Specifically, we use the t-error (Eq. 8) introduced by Duan et al. (2023) to detect
371   whether the model memorizes a prompt. This detection is applied to a set of 500 memorized prompts
372   and 500 non-memorized prompts (Wen et al., 2024). The resulting detection performance is reported
373   in Fig. 3, where it is evident that the loss function effectively distinguishes between memorized and
374
      non-memorized prompts. Given this observed link between data memorization and MIAs, we are led
      to explore a further question: Can membership inference defenses help mitigate data memorization?
375
376   To investigate this, we conduct experiments on Stable Diffusion v1.5 (Rombach et al., 2022) as
377   detailed in Section 4.3. More information about the t-error and the memorization experiments are
      presented in Appendix A.1.

483   4.3     M EMBERSHIP INFERENCE DEFENSES MITIGATE DATA MEMORIZATION
484
485   We use the fine-tuned SDv1.5 model using our methods to evaluate its capability of data memoriza-
      tion. For comparison, we employ the inference-time memorization mitigation method proposed by


                                                                9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      Wen et al. (2024), which reduces memorization by adjusting the prompt embedding to minimize the
487   difference between unconditional and text-conditional noise predictions.
488
489   To measure the level of memorization, we calculate the SSCD similarity score (Pizzi et al., 2022;
490
      Somepalli et al., 2023b) between the generated images and the images in the training dataset, given
      the same set of prompts. In addition, the CLIP score (Radford et al., 2021) is used to assess the
491
      alignment between the generated images and their corresponding prompts. A lower SSCD similarity
492
      score indicates reduced memorization, while a higher CLIP score reflects better alignment between
493   the generated image and the prompt.
494
495
496   Results and Discussion Table 1 shows the effectiveness of our proposed method in mitigating
497   data memorization. A thoroughly fine-tuned model without any mitigation produces highly similar
498   images with an SSCD similarity score of 0.60 for given prompts, indicating significant memoriza-
499
      tion. In contrast, our DualMD and DistillMD approaches significantly reduce the SSCD score to
      0.52 and 0.27, respectively, suggesting that membership inference defenses can help mitigate data
500
      memorization. Notably, both methods also show a slight improvement in CLIP scores. Furthermore,
501
      the method proposed by Wen et al. (2024), which directly targets mitigating memorization, achieves
502   an SSCD similarity score of 0.28. Our DistillMD approach, despite being designed to defend against
503   MIAs, not only reduces data memorization more effectively but also improves image-text alignment
504   compared to the most recently proposed method in Wen et al. (2024).

507   5   C ONCLUSION
508
509
      This paper presents comprehensive and novel approaches to protect diffusion models against train-
510   ing data leakage while mitigating model memorization. Our methodology focuses on training two
511   models using disjoint subsets of the training data. This results in two significant contributions, in-
512   cluding DualMD for private inference and DistillMD for developing a privacy-enhanced student
513   model. Both techniques effectively reduce model overfitting to training samples. We further en-
514   hance privacy protection for text-conditioned diffusion models by diversifying training prompts,
515   preventing models from overfitting specific textual patterns. Notably, our experiments reveal that
516   model memorization represents a more severe form of overfitting than membership inference at-
517   tacks (MIAs), and our unified approach successfully addresses both vulnerabilities simultaneously,
518
      eliminating the need for separate mitigation strategies. In short, our paper presents inference-time
      and training-time strategies to defend diffusion models against MIAs. It provides new insights into
519
      the intersection between MIAs and model memorization, advancing our understanding of privacy
520
      preservation in generative models.
521
522
523   Limitations and Future Directions Our methods rely on dividing the training dataset into two
524   halves, which may limit the generative capabilities of the teacher models in data-scarce scenarios.
525   This limitation can affect the quality of the distilled model, as evidenced by the slight performance
526   degradation shown in Table 2. Future research could focus on developing methods that allow models
527
      to leverage the entire dataset during training while maintaining strong privacy guarantees, potentially
      enhancing the performance of all models.
528
529   Furthermore, our inference-time defense method (DualMD) requires storing and alternating between
530   two models, which may limit its applicability in resource-constrained environments. Future work
531   could explore inference-time solutions that, like our DistillMD method, do not necessitate additional
532   model storage while maintaining robust privacy protection.

851   A.5   A DAPTIVE ATTACK
852
853   To extend the robustness evaluation of our defense mechanism, we investigate its vulnerability to
854   adaptive attacks where adversaries have complete knowledge of the defense strategy. We design an
855
      iterative attack targeting DualMD’s dual-model architecture by manipulating the denoising process
      across multiple generation rounds. The attack proceeds as follows: First, we generate an image
856
      using n denoising steps, alternating between Sub-Model1 (SB1) and Sub-Model2 (SB2). We then
857
      introduce noise at the second-to-last timestep, effectively nullifying all denoising steps except the
858   initial one performed by SB1. This noisy image and its corresponding timestep serve as the starting
859   point for a subsequent generation round with n − 1 steps, beginning with SB1. By iteratively repeat-
860   ing this process, we systematically reduce the influence of SB2 while preserving SB1’s denoising
861   effects. When combined with black-box MIAs, this approach provides a comprehensive evaluation
862   of our defense mechanism. As shown in Table 9, DualMD maintains its defensive efficacy even after
863   multiple rounds of this adaptive attack on the Pokemon dataset, with an AUC remaining close to 0.5
      and very low TPR at 1% FPR.

873   A.6   D ISTILLATION A NALYSIS
874
      Knowledge distillation has emerged as a prominent approach for mitigating Membership Inference
875
      Attacks (MIAs) in classification models (Shejwalkar & Houmansadr, 2021; Zheng et al., 2021; Tang
876   et al., 2022; Mazzone et al., 2022; Li et al., 2024a). To demonstrate the advantages of our dual-
877   model architecture in DistillMD, we conduct a comparative analysis against conventional knowledge
878   distillation under SecMI attack Duan et al. (2023) using the CIFAR10 dataset. The key distinction
879   lies in the training methodology. In particular, traditional knowledge distillation employs a single
880   teacher model trained on the complete dataset, whereas DistillMD leverages two specialized teacher
881   models, each trained on mutually exclusive subsets of the training data.
882   The experimental results presented in Table 10 reveal significant differences in defense efficacy.
883   Although conventional knowledge distillation provides modest protection, reducing the AUC from
884   0.93 (no defense) to 0.74, this improvement falls short of the robustness required for real-world
885   applications. These findings underscore the crucial role of our dataset partitioning strategy and
886   dual-teacher architecture in DistillMD. Notably, existing distillation-based defense mechanisms for
887   classification models often incorporate supplementary techniques, such as confidence-based sample
888   selection Shejwalkar & Houmansadr (2021), to enhance privacy guarantees. Although these tech-
889   niques have proven effective in classification scenarios, their direct application to generative models
890
      presents unique challenges. Our work establishes a foundation for future research to bridge this gap
      and adapt these distillation methods for diffusion models while maintaining their privacy-preserving
891
      properties.
