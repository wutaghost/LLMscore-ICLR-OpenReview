009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Chain-of-Thought (CoT) prompting has become a key strategy for enhancing the
012            inferential abilities of large language models (LLMs) in reasoning tasks. However,
013            it often struggles with ensuring reasoning validity and maintaining informativeness.
014            This paper presents the Logic Agent (LA), a novel framework designed to boost
015            the validity of reasoning in LLMs through strategic logic function calls. Distinct
016
               from traditional methods, LA converts LLMs into dynamic agents that apply
               propositional logic rules, transforming natural language inputs into structured
017
               logical forms. The agent utilizes a robust suite of predefined functions to guide
018
               the reasoning process effectively. This approach can enhance the structured and
019            coherent generation of reasoning outputs, improving their interpretability and
020            logical consistency. Through detailed experiments, we showcase LA’s ability to
021            adapt across different LLM sizes, significantly enhancing the accuracy of complex
022            reasoning tasks across various domains.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027
      The quest for augmenting the reasoning capabilities of language models has been a focal point of
      recent advancements in the evolving landscape of artificial intelligence. Chain-of-Thought (CoT)
028
      prompting Wei et al. (2022); Kojima et al. (2022); Wang et al. (2022b); Chu et al. (2023) marked
029
      a significant stride in this journey, revealing the potential of large language models (LLMs) to
030   mimic human-like reasoning processes. These advancements have led to remarkable achievements,
031   with LLMs demonstrating proficiency in a variety of competitive examinations, including those
032   focused on mathematics Li et al. (2022); He-Yueya et al. (2023); Imani et al. (2023) and reading
033   comprehension Wang et al. (2023); Xiao et al. (2023).
034
      However, despite its implications in various reasoning tasks, CoT has faced limitations, particularly
035
      in validating reasoning and ensuring the informativeness of its outputs. Lanham et al. (2023) Their
036   performance in logical reasoning tasks, a critical component of examinations like the Law School
037   Admission Test (LSAT) and Chinese civil service selection exams, remains notably inferior to that of
038   well-trained humans liu et al. (2023).
039
      Figure 1 shows an example of such questions. Crafted by experts to challenge human logical
040
      reasoning abilities, they require a valid and rule-bound chain of logic that is often non-trivial to
041
      discern. Testees must engage in abstract thinking, translating contexts into logical symbols and
042   applying strict inference rules to form logical chains. This gap highlights a critical challenge: the
043   ability of LLMs to consistently follow rules and verify the validity of logic chains. As illustrated
044   in Figure 1, a GPT-4 model struggles with the deduction of the contrapositive law, despite having
045   conceptual knowledge of it. One reason can be that there is no strict guarantee for a statistical system
046   such as LLM to ensure correct complex reasoning chains across contexts.
047
      Inspired by the integration of the neural network with formal symbolic solvers Azerbayev et al. (2023);
048   Jiang et al. (2023); Thakur et al. (2023), tool-use Gao et al. (2023); Schick et al. (2023); Paranjape
049   et al. (2023), and constrained decoding Geng et al. (2024), we address this issue by introducing
050   Logic Agent (LA), an agent-based constrained generation framework, leveraging propositional logic
051   and inference rules as fundamental guides to constructing logically sound inference chains. LA is
052   designed to steer Large Language Models (LLMs) toward a trajectory of enhanced logical coherence
053   and interpretability by introducing symbolic reasoning. In particular, we let an LLM serve as a
      decision-making agent and make a callable symbolic reasoning agent by assembling a set of essential

      With LA, LLMs are guided towards a path of logical coherence and interpretability. We first
096   define the essentials of compositional logic, i.e. the logic components and syntax. This step serves
097   as the initial step, converting complex natural language statements into structured compositional
098   logic representations. Second, we define the functions for applying deduction rules, given a logic
099   expression, we are able to form an inference chain with implicit logic. These functions are tools for
100   LLMs to use. Lastly, we prompt LLMs to decide which rule to apply in different states. When LLMs
101   call a rule, the output of the corresponding function is guaranteed to give valid logic chains for LLMs
102   to make judgments on the truthfulness of the hypotheses.
103   In our study, we rigorously evaluated the Logic Agent (LA) framework using a mix of commercial
104   and open-source Large Language Models, including OpenAI’s GPT-4 and various Hugging Face
105   models. Our findings, across this diverse range of models, consistently highlight LA’s effectiveness in
106   enhancing logical reasoning in complex tasks. Alongside our experimental insights, we’re releasing
107   our code to contribute to ongoing research. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first initiative to
      integrate propositional logic into LLMs at such a scale.

      2      R ELATED W ORK
124
125
      Traditional pre-trained models have primarily tackled logical reasoning through statistical training, a
      connectionist approach that often misinterprets the complexity of language. Similarly, formal sym-
126
      bolic systems, while precise, struggle with the adaptability needed for diverse linguistic phenomena.
127
      This backdrop sets the stage for the introduction of new approaches to complex reasoning in Large
128   Language Models (LLMs).
129
130   Reasoning Paradigms in Large Language Model Prompting: The development of few-shot Wei
131
      et al. (2022) and zero-shot Kojima et al. (2022) Chain-of-Thought prompting has been instrumental
      in enabling LLMs to tackle complex reasoning tasks. Subsequent developments have introduced
132
      varied data structures, such as Tree-of-Thought Yao et al. (2023), Graph-of-Thought Besta et al.
133
      (2024), and Program-of-Thought Chen et al. (2022), enhancing LLMs’ capabilities to reflect on and
134   evaluate their reasoning processes. Moving beyond basic prompting strategies, the ReAct model Yao
135   et al. (2022) intertwines reasoning with actionable tasks like search, while the Selection-Inference
136   framework Creswell et al. (2023) employs a two-step process of context formation and logical
137   chaining. Although these approaches parallel ours in process structure, they do not incorporate explicit
138   logical rules, and the chaining mechanism is entirely model-dependent. The use of external tools
139   within prompting paradigms, particularly for tasks necessitating additional knowledge, represents
140   another significant advancement. In mathematical reasoning, tools such as calculators have proven
141   invaluable. Analogously, in our methodology, predefined functions for applying inference rules
142
      are akin to external tools, a concept previously unexplored in this context. Another paradigm
      shift in LLM prompting is the division of complex tasks into subproblems or the collaborative
143
      engagement of diverse models. Cumulative reasoning Zhang et al. (2023) adopts a streamlined,
144
      iterative approach utilizing distinct LLMs as AI agents; ScratchPad Nye et al. (2021) contributes
145   to multi-step reasoning by revealing intermediate steps; Meta-prompting Suzgun & Kalai (2024)
146   envisions LLMs as orchestrators in a collaborative environment, responsible for decomposing complex
147   tasks, delegating sub-tasks to specialized models, facilitating inter-model communication, and
148   applying critical analysis throughout. Our approach similarly harnesses the LLMs’ decision-making
149   capability in selecting appropriate inference rules, aligning with this broader trend of utilizing
150   LLMs for complex, collaborative reasoning processes. Unlike previous attempts, we leverage the
151   computational power and contextual understanding of LLMs to act as agents that dynamically invoke
152   logic rules. This integration enables the LLMs to not only process language with their inherent
153
      sophistication but also apply logical reasoning in a structured and accurate manner, akin to utilizing a
      calculator for mathematical enhancements. Apart from that, recent studies have explored instruct-
154
      tuning Large Language Models (LLMs) with specific datasets to enhance their abstract reasoning
155
      capabilities. LogiCoT Liu et al. (2023) fine-tunes an LL A MA-7 B model using logical chaining
156   data, demonstrating significant improvements across various logical reasoning tasks; LogicLLM
157   Jiao et al. (2023) employs a self-supervised post-training approach tailored for logical reasoning
158   enhancements; Symbol-LLM Xu et al. (2023) leverages symbolic data within a two-stage tuning
159   framework to imbue a LL A MA-2- CHAT model with symbolic knowledge. While these approaches
160   underscore the potential of fine-tuning strategies in augmenting LLMs, our work distinguishes itself
161   as the first to specifically address and enhance logical reasoning capabilities at the decoding stage,
      employing a multi-agent strategy to elevate the process.

      Formal Reasoning: Formal reasoning systems have primarily been developed to address mathe-
163   matical challenges. Peano Poesia & Goodman (2023), designed to solve educational mathematical
164   problems, employs dependent types to encode mathematical definitions and proofs, echoing the
165   structured approach in our work. Yet, our focus diverges towards logical reasoning scenarios, an area
166   where systems like Peano have traditionally been less potent. Addressing formal logical reasoning,
167   LINC Olausson et al. (2023) leverages LLMs as FOL language translators to attain formal repre-
168   sentations of contextual information, complemented by traditional theorem provers for validation.
169   LINC’s approach, which employs a voting strategy to resolve inconsistencies in FOL language
170   generation, contrasts with our method which adopts a more flexible propositional logic to distill
171
      the abstract essence of context while meticulously controlling the validity of generative reasoning.
      Furthermore, the exploration of language models as theorem provers has introduced systems like
172
      LangPro Abzianidze (2017), a natural language theorem prover that harnesses higher-order logic
173
      to assess linguistic expressions’ consistency. LangPro’s reliance on CCG parsing and a dedicated
174   knowledge base for generating Lambda Logical Forms (LLFs) presents a contrast to our work,
175   which utilizes propositional logic, thereby circumventing the need for a theorem-proving knowledge
176   base. In parallel, semantic-constrained decoding techniques, as exemplified by NEUROLOGIC
177   DECODING Lu et al. (2020), enable language models to generate contextually coherent text while
178   adhering to complex lexical constraints. Our approach resonates with this paradigm, albeit with
179   a distinct focus on employing constrained generation paired with guided deduction rules, thereby
180   carving a unique niche in the landscape of formal reasoning and logical inference.

182   3     L OGIC AGENT
183
184
      Distinctively, we encapsulate the logical reasoning process into callable function forms, packaging
185   logic rules as tools for LLM agents. This strategic shift in leveraging LLMs as autonomous decision-
186   makers, equipped with a toolkit of generalized logic reasoning functions, marks a significant departure
187   from existing models.
188
      Figure 2 presents the Logic Agent (LA) framework’s architecture. Initially, natural language inputs
189
      undergo logic parsing on the left, resulting in structured logic forms (see Section 3.1). The center
190
      highlights the application of deduction rules for logical inference (see Section 3.2). Finally, on the
191   right, the constrained generation process employs these inferences to produce contextually relevant
192   and logically coherent outputs, illustrating the LA’s systematic approach to enhancing reasoning
193   in large language models (see Section 3.3). At the heart of LA lies the meticulous definition and
194   utilization of compositional logic essentials, encompassing both the critical logic components and
195   their associated syntax. This pivotal initial step involves the intricate transformation of complex
196   natural language statements into structured representations of compositional logic.
197
198   3.1   L OGICAL C ONSTRUCT C LASSES
199
200   Within LA, various classes of logical constructs are parsed and utilized. These include:
201   Variable: Represents a variable symbol in logical expressions. Atom: Denotes an atomic formula, the
202   fundamental unit of logical statements. Not: Embodies the negation operation in logic. And: Indicates
203   logical conjunction, combining multiple propositions. Or: Symbolizes logical disjunction, offering
204   alternative propositions. Implies: Represents the implication relationship between propositions.
205   Equiv: Denotes logical equivalence between statements. Exists and Forall: Represent existential
206   and universal quantification, respectively, allowing for the expression of propositions about ‘some’
207   or ‘all’ entities within a domain. Rule-based functions within LA parse these logical constructs and
208
      quantified sentences, ensuring accurate representation and manipulation of logical expressions.
209
210   3.2   I NFERENCE RULES
211
212

      rules. These functions serve as advanced tools for LLMs, facilitating the formation of inference
213
      chains that integrate both explicit and implicit logic elements. This enables LLMs to navigate the
214
      complexities of logical deduction, maintaining structured and coherent reasoning throughout.
215
      The key inference rules and their corresponding functions in LA include:

244   3.3   RULE -G UIDED G ENERATION
245
246
      We prompt LLMs to discern and decide upon the most appropriate rule to apply in varying states
      of reasoning. This dynamic interaction empowers LLMs to judiciously invoke the corresponding
247
      functions, each meticulously crafted to guarantee the generation of valid logic chains. Consequently,
248
      LLMs are equipped with a powerful mechanism to scrutinize the veracity of hypotheses, making
249   informed judgments based on the logically consistent chains produced. We use in-context examples
250   to demonstrate how these functions are called in the guided generation process and leverage the
251   capabilities of existing LLMs developed by OpenAI and HuggingFace. These models offer a robust
252   starting point, owing to their advanced language understanding and processing abilities. However, our
253   approach goes beyond the conventional use of LLMs by optimizing each component for its specific
254   role in the logical reasoning process. This targeted optimization is key to transcending the current
255   limitations of LLMs in handling the nuanced and rule-bound nature of logical reasoning tests.
256   By integrating a structured, rule-guided reasoning methodology into the operational framework of
257   LLMs, LA aims to improve not only the logical precision of these models but also their interpretability
258   and coherence. The incorporation of propositional logic, deduction rules, and a strategic prompting
259   mechanism positions LA as an innovative approach. It seeks to bridge the current divide between the
260   computational efficiency of LLMs and the detailed, logical discernment typical of human reasoning.

      We choose one dataset from each task and use GPT-3.5- TURBO as the tested model. The results

465   a valuable contributor to the model’s overall performance, the full potential of LA is realized only
466   when it is coupled with the sophisticated generation constraints that guide the model towards more
467   logically coherent and accurate conclusions.
