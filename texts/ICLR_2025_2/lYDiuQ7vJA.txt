                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
               Textual-edge Graphs (TEGs), characterized by rich text annotations on edges,
010
               are increasingly significant in network science due to their ability to capture rich
011            contextual information among entities. Existing works have proposed various
012            edge-aware graph neural networks (GNNs), graph foundation models, or even
013            let language models directly make predictions. However, they often fail to fully
014            capture the contextualized semantics on edges and graph topology, respectively.
015            This inadequacy is particularly evident in link prediction tasks that require a
016            comprehensive understanding of graph topology and semantics between nodes. In
017            this paper, we present a novel framework - L INK 2D OC, designed especially for
018            link prediction on TEGs. Specifically, we propose to summarize neighborhood
019
               information between node pairs as a human-written document to preserve both
               semantic and topology information. We also present a specialized GNN framework
020
               to process the multi-scaled interaction between target nodes in a stratified manner.
021
               Finally, a self-supervised learning model is utilized to enhance the GNN’s text-
022            understanding ability from language models. Empirical evaluations, including
023            link prediction, edge classification, parameter analysis, runtime comparison, and
024            ablation studies, on five real-world datasets demonstrate that L INK 2D OC achieves
025            generally better performance against existing edge-aware GNNs and language
026            models in link predictions.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   In network science, the prevalence of networks with rich text on edges, also known as Textual-
032   edge Graphs (TEGs) (Yang et al., 2015; Guo et al., 2019), increasingly become significant, as they
033
      encapsulate a wealth of relational and contextual information critical for diverse applications. The text
      associated with edges in networks can dramatically deepen our understanding of network dynamics
034
      and behavior. For instance, in a social media network, when a user responds to another’s post, the
035
      reply not only creates a directed edge but also includes specific text that can reveal the sentiment,
036   intent, or relationship between users. Similar cases can also be found in citation networks where text
037   on edges is the exact reference quote. Both examples illustrate how text-rich edges are pivotal in
038   accurately interpreting and leveraging networked data for advanced analytical purposes. In TEGs,
039   link prediction is a unique yet open question due to the rich textual information embedded on edges.
040
      Extensive works have been devoted to studying graphs with rich text, which can be classified into
041
      two categories, i.e., Graph Neural Networks (GNN)-based and language model-based. GNN-based
042   works have extensively studied the topology connection between nodes and designed various variants.
043   Specifically, works (Guo et al., 2019; Zhu et al., 2019; Gong & Cheng, 2019) typically compress the
044   text embedded on edges to latent vectors by text encoders (e.g., Word2Vec (Mikolov et al., 2013) and
045   BERT (Devlin et al., 2019)), and iteratively merge edge features with/without node features (Jiang
046   et al., 2019; Yang & Li, 2020). The current most advanced edge-aware GNN (Jin et al., 2022) tries
047   to refine the text encoder with the GNN training to obtain better representation. However, it still
048   follows the neighbor aggregation way, which may not comprehensively consider overall semantics.
049   On the other hand, owing to the strong text understanding ability of Large Language Models (LLMs)
050
      (Ling et al., 2023b), researchers (Chen et al., 2024; Fatemi et al., 2023; Ye et al., 2023; Yan et al.,
      2023) have directly used language models to solve graph mining tasks on textual graphs by designing
051
      various prompts to express or summarize the topology connection into natural language.
052
053   While these approaches have advanced the study of text-rich graphs, they tend to simplify the diverse
      text on edges, potentially losing crucial information necessary for tasks such as link prediction, where

069
      Challenge 1: Understand graph topology in language models. For LLM-based approaches,
070
      existing works have worked on prompting LLMs by expressing or summarizing graph topology into
071   text, but the graph topology is generally expressed in a linear shape, which may lead to a significant
072   loss of graph-based structural information. For example, as shown in Figure 1, if we summarize the
073   relation between Book A and E as Breadth-first Search, the dependencies along specific paths would
074   be overlooked. This approach would not capture the multi-hop interactions and the rich contextual
075   dependencies among nodes and edges. On the other hand, if we summarize all paths from A to
076   E, the same edge, such as the negative reference on edge C to E, could appear in several paths.
077   Representing this repeated edge in text multiple times leads to unnecessary duplication and potential
078   noise, complicating the model’s ability to discern the true nature of the relationships. If the graph is
079
      too large, potential overflow of the language model’s context window limitation may also emerge.
080   Challenge 2: Comprehensively consider context information on all connections. For GNN-based
081   methods, predicting links in TEGs requires a comprehensive examination of all potential paths
082   connecting two nodes. Neighborhood aggregation approaches often focus on immediate neighbors
083   and fail to account for the complex interactions that can occur in textual-edge graphs. As shown
084   in Figure 1, there are many multi-hop paths with different intermediate nodes between A and B,
085
      and each edge is annotated with different contexts, including user descriptions and user comments,
      describing their complex relations. It’s already hard for existing GNN-based methods to use one
086
      latent vector to describe the complex context on edges. Moreover, user’s preferences may also differ
087
      from one path to another (i.e., A → C → E is negative while A → B → D → E is positive),
088   creating a conflicting semantic landscape. However, GNN’s neighbor aggregation would treat these
089   paths uniformly, potentially diluting the sentiment difference in these paths.
090
091
      Present Work. To effectively make link predictions on TEGs by jointly considering rich semantic
      information and graph topology, in this paper, we propose a novel representation learning framework,
092
      L INK 2D OC, that transforms local connections between nodes into a coherent document for better-
093
      reflecting graph topology along with semantic information. To process rich textual information
094   efficiently, we further propose a stratified representation learning framework that captures multi-scale
095   interactions between target nodes. The crafted document further enhances GNNs to make link
096   predictions in a contextualized way. The key contributions are summarized as follows:
097
098   • Problem. We formulate the problem of link prediction on textual-edge graphs and highlight the
099     unique challenges of learning representations on textual-edge graphs for link predictions.
100   • Method. We propose an integrated framework to jointly consider topology and semantic informa-
101     tion in textual-edge graphs, which consists of 1) coherent document composition to summarize
102     semantic relations between node pairs in plain language; 2) a specialized Transition Graph Neural
103     Network to process topology information between target nodes in a stratified manner; and 3)
104     a self-supervised learning module to combine semantic understanding and topology processing
105
        ability for better link prediction on textual-edge graphs.
106   • Experiment. We empirically compare our method against existing state-of-the-art in four real-
107     world datasets. Results have shown our proposed methods can elevate the performance of general
        GNNs and achieve competitive performance against edge-aware GNNs.

      2    R ELATED W ORKS
109
110
      Edge-aware Graph Representation Learning. Earlier research of Graph Neural Networks (GNNs)
111
      (Wu et al., 2020; Cui et al., 2023) tends to only focus on node features. Later on, research on heteroge-
112   neous graph representation learning (Yang et al., 2020) began to consider categorical information on
113   edges. In text-attributed graphs, to more comprehensively utilize edge information during the network
114   representation learning, some edge-aware GNNs (Zhu et al., 2019; Gong & Cheng, 2019; Jiang et al.,
115   2019; Yang & Li, 2020) were proposed to consider edge text by designing various architectures (e.g.,
116   attention on edges, node and edge role switch, etc.). The recent state-of-the-art method EdgeFormer
117   (Jin et al., 2022) involved pre-trained language models and proposed to better consider edge text by
118   designing a cross-attention mechanism to merge node and edge representation in Transformer layers.
119   However, these approaches still use the neighborhood aggregation way to obtain graph representation
120
      and cannot consider the local connections as a whole unit. Neighborhood aggregation may not always
      work especially when nodes are dissimilar (as shown in Figure 1) (Xie et al., 2020). Moreover,
121
      existing edge-aware GNNs tend to deliberately erase text on nodes and only explore the effect of
122
      edge information on various graph-related tasks (Jin et al., 2022), which lacks the flexibility to extend
123   to other text-attributed scenarios.
124
125   Language Modeling Augmented Graph Learning. Large language models have been proven to
126
      have the ability to interpret graph-structured data (Jiang et al., 2023; Guo et al., 2023; Jin et al., 2023).
      In the past year, many works (Chen et al., 2023; 2024; Huang et al., 2023; Pan et al., 2024) have been
127
      proposed to prove LLMs have great potential (and even become state-of-the-art) to classify nodes in
128
      text-attributed graphs. However, how LLMs can better assist link prediction in text-attributed graphs
129   is still an under-explored area, let alone the more complicated scenario of edge-attributed graphs that
130   contain rich textual information on both edges and nodes. Existing works (Zou et al., 2023; Zhao
131   et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023; Wen & Fang, 2023) have tried to distill implicit knowledge from LLMs
132   to smaller GNN models for text-attributed graph tasks, but they still focus on learning good node
133   embeddings. In edge-attributed graphs, text on edges cannot be uniformly processed in the same
134   manner as node text, necessitating more specialized techniques that account for the unique semantics
135   and structural roles of edge attributes in enhancing graph-based learning models.

137   3    L INK P REDICTION ON T EXTUAL - EDGE G RAPH
138
139   In this section, we begin by introducing key notations and formulating the problem of link prediction
140   on Textual-edge Graphs. We then describe a novel way of constructing a transition document that
141   summarizes the relationship between node pairs for link prediction. Finally, we provide an LLM-
142   enhanced Graph Neural Network framework that learns the local topology and semantic information
143   to retain both efficiency and efficacy.
144   3.1 P ROBLEM F ORMULATION
145
      A Textual-edge graph (TEG) is a type of graph in which both nodes and edges contain free-form
146
      text descriptions. These descriptions provide detailed, contextual information about the relationships
147
      between nodes, enabling a richer representation of relational data than in traditional graphs.
148
      Definition 1 (Textual-edge Graphs). A TEG G = (V, E) is an undirected graph, which consists of a
149
      set of nodes V and a set of edges E ⊆ V × V. Each node vi ∈ V contains a textual description di ,
150
      and each edge eij ∈ E also associates with free-form texts dij describing the relation of (vi , vj ).
151
152   In this work, we target at the Link Prediction task on TEGs, where we aim to predict the existence (or
153   the label) of edges between pairs of nodes (vi , vj ) ∈  / E based on the neighborhood information of
154   (vi , vj ). Due to the rich edge text information, local edges in TEGs can inherently be represented by
155   natural language sentences. For example, the connection vi → eij → vi can be represented as “di
156   is connected to dj via dij ”, which directly describes the relation in plain text.
157   Compared to categorical-edge graphs that have edges labeled with simple, predefined categories,
158   textual-edge graphs feature edges annotated with free-form text, which offer detailed and contextual
159   relationship descriptions. Take Figure 1 again as an example, books are connected by textual edges,
160   where text on edges consists of exact quotations that one book cites another. To predict whether A
161   and E will have a citation link, we not only need to analyze semantics embedded within each edge’s
      description, but different paths may also depict different semantic meanings due to the varying textual

189   3.2       OVERALL A RCHITECTURE
190   In this work, we introduce L INK 2D OC, a novel approach that leverages a self-supervised learning
191   scheme to endow GNNs with text comprehension capabilities akin to those of LLMs. L INK 2D OC
192   is designed to preserve and synergize rich semantic information, topology information, and their
193   interplay within TEGs for link prediction. We propose learning and aligning representations from two
194   complementary perspectives: the text view and the graph view. The text view, termed Text-of-Graph,
195   organizes the text associated with TEG’s nodes in a way that reflects the graph’s topology, forming
196   a structured document that inherently captures logical and relational data. Conversely, the graph
197   view, or Graph-of-Text, transforms the nodes and topology of TEGs into structured graph data. By
198
      employing pretrained language models (PLMs), the text view adeptly maintains textual integrity,
      while the graph view, processed through GNNs, ensures the retention of graph-specific characteristics.
199
      Aligning these views allows each representation to enrich the other, fostering a holistic understanding
200
      where textual nuances inform graph structures and vice versa.
201
202   Specifically, as noted in Figure 2 (a), to reduce the search space and to eliminate the noise from
203   unrelated connections, we propose to formulate a (s, t)-transition graph containing all the possible
204
      routes through which s could correlate to t for link prediction.
205   Definition 2 (Transition Graph). For any pair of two entities (s, t) in the Textual-edge Graph, all
206   paths from s to t collectively form an (s, t)-transition graph, which is denoted by G(s,t) . We use
207   n and m to denote the number of nodes and edges in G(s,t) , respectively. Figure 1 exemplifies an
208   (s, t)-transition graph, where s is Book A and t is Book B. In practice, the length of paths can be
209   upper-bounded by an integer K, which can usually be set as the diameter of the Textual-edge graph.
210
      The transition graph G(s,t) , as well as all the text on edges, provide the necessary information needed
211
      to understand the relation between s and t. Next, as shown in Figure 2(b), we separate two subgraphs:
212
      Gs and Gt from G(s,t) for preserving local neighborhood of s and t, respectively. In Figure 2(c), to
213   view the topology and semantic information of G(s,t) in a joint unit, we compose a manageable and
214   coherent document that expresses G(s,t) as a human-written document. Finally, in Figure 2(d), we
215   distill the text processing ability from large language models to graph neural networks for inference
      scalability while maintaining performance.

237   3.3 T RANSITION D OCUMENT C ONSTRUCTION
238
      In this work, to address the first challenge, we need to summarize local relations in G(s,t) to com-
239
      prehensively understand the relation between (s, t). Since state-of-the-art LLMs are predominantly
240
      trained on human-written documents and books, in this work, we propose a novel way to express
241
      G(s,t) as a structured document d(s,t) (Lee et al., 1996), complete with an introduction, sections,
242   subsections, and a conclusion, to let language models better understand the overall semantics between
243   s and t with preserving topology. The overall process is illustrated in Figure 3, and the algorithm is
244   summarized in Appendix 1 due to the space limit.
245
      Node-centric Paragraph Composition. For source node s and target node t, we first conduct
246
      Breadth-first Search (BFS) to extract their respective local structure with depth L, i.e., Gs and Gt
247
      from their transition graph G(s,t) . As shown in Figure 3, nodes in G(s,t) that belong to Gs are
248   marked with orange, and nodes belonging to Gt are marked with blue. The structural data (i.e., BFS
249   tree) obtained is transformed into a textual paragraph aimed at both human readability and machine
250   processability. Specifically, taking the BFS tree Gs rooted at source node s as an example, s acts as
251   the main subject of the paragraph’s textual summary. We conduct pre-order traversal to go over all
252   nodes in both trees. The first and subsequent levels of BFS neighbors are detailed in separate sections
253   and subsections, akin to a detailed outline:
254
255
      1. Root: Start with a comprehensive sentence that describes node s and its immediate connec-
         tions: “[ROOT s] Node s has three connections. s is connected to v1
256
         via ds1 , s is connected to v2 via ds2 , and s is connected to v3 via
257
         ds3 ”, where ds1 , ds2 , and ds3 are textual descriptions on edges es1 , es2 , and es3 .
258
      2. First-hop Neighbors: For each first-hop neighbor of s, we provide a section detailing its con-
259
         nections: “[1]. Node v1 has two connections. v1 is connected to v11
260
         via d11 , and v1 is connected to v12 via d12 ”; “[2]. Node v2 has one
261      connection. v2 is connected to v21 via d21 ”; and “[3]. Node v3 has
262      two connections. v3 is connected to v31 via d31 , ...”.
263
      3. Second (and following)-hop Neighbors: Subsections under each first-hop neighbor detail fur-
264
         ther connections: “[1.1] v11 is connected to ...” “[1.2] v12 is connected
265      to...”, etc.

267   This structure is recursively applied up to L levels deep, ensuring each node’s direct connections are
268   thoroughly described, capturing the intricate topology of the graph. The notation “[X]” refers to
269   earlier parts of the summary where the connected node was initially described, aiding in understanding
      the network’s connectivity beyond a simple hierarchical structure.

      Hidden Edges. The neighborhood of s and t may not always form tree structures. As shown in
271   Figure 3, node v122 is not only the child node of v12 , v122 also links back to v1 to form a triangle
272   structure. To consider a more holistic view of the node relationships, we add an extra description
273   to the node stating its connection to pre-existing nodes. Specifically, we introduce the hidden edge
274   information of node v122 in [1.2.2] as “In addition, v122 is also linked to [1]
275   v1 via ...”. By letting each mention of a hidden edge direct back to the respective section “[X]”,
276   we aim to ensure clarity and maintain the coherence of the graph’s description.
277   Transition Graph Document Construction. The unified d(s,t) , which consists of paragraphs from s
278   and t, aims to not only present isolated descriptions but also to highlight the interconnected nature
279   of Gs and Gt . In this work, we aim to illuminate the interconnectedness between Gs and Gt by
280   identifying and highlighting nodes that appear in both Gs and Gt ’s local structures. These common
281   nodes are pivotal as they link the context of one paragraph to the other. As shown in Figure 3, the
282   common nodes are marked with half orange and half blue. For each common node, we include a
283   cross-reference in the text where the node is mentioned, which is done by adding a note after the
284   section index. For example, node v122 has a section index “[1.2.2]” in s’s paragraph, and a section
285
      index “[1.2.1]” in t’s paragraph. We then add ([1.2.1] in Paragraph t) after the section
      index of v121 in s’s paragraph. We conduct the cross-reference in the other paragraph reversely.
286
287   In practice, we keep the depth L to be half of the diameter of the G(s,t) so that Gs and Gt can each
288   cover their close neighbor information as well as an adequate number of common nodes. We further
289   enhance the document’s coherence by adding an introduction to the start of the document. Finally,
290   the generated d(s,t) can be viewed as a structured document. More details can be found in Figure 3.

      3.4 R EPRESENTATION L EARNING FOR T RANSITION G RAPH N EURAL N ETWORK
292
293
      After obtaining a document d(s,t) summarizing both topology and semantic information of G(s,t) ,
      scalability still poses a significant challenge for language models on large-scale graphs. To conduct
294
      link prediction on a large G(s,t) , we need to compose many documents between node pairs with
295
      duplicated content (e.g., d(s,t1 ) and d(s,t2 ) may largely overlap if t1 and t2 are neighbors).
296
297   On the other hand, GNNs are inherently designed to process graph structures efficiently, making them
298   a promising alternative for this task. Although LLMs may not be capable of conducting large-scale
299
      link predictions, the implicit knowledge can still be utilized to train GNNs. However, a straightforward
      application of GNNs faces limitations: a single GNN may not fully capture the intricate interplay
300
      between the graph’s structural properties and the semantic information on nodes and edges, especially
301
      in large graphs where G(s,t) between two nodes s and t can encompass thousands of nodes due to a
302   diameter as small as 4 (Ling et al., 2023a). Moreover, independently learning local representations
303   for s and t fails to account for the multi-scale interactions crucial for accurate link prediction. Each
304   hop in the graph can reveal different structural and semantic information—immediate neighbors
305   contribute local properties, while nodes farther away provide broader contextual insights.
306
      Transition Graph Neural Network (TGNN). To better process rich textual information on large-
307
      scale graphs efficiently, we propose TGNN and introduce a novel stratified representation learning
308   framework to captures multi-scale interactions between target nodes s and t by considering different
309   “cuts” in the transition graph G(s,t) . Each cut is defined by a pair (n, K − n), where K is the diameter
310   of the G(s,t) between s and t, i.e., the longest path length between s and t in G(s,t) . For each
311   cut, TGNN encompasses two directed graph convolution processes (with shared parameters): one
312   focuses on learning the representation of s of its n-hop neighborhood, and the other focuses on t’s
313   (K − n)-hop neighborhood. The TGNN update function at n-th layer for node u is given as:

370   Search (time complexity O((N + E)/2) (BFS) with the depth K/2 to extract Gs and Gt , respectively.
371   We then use pre-order traversal to obtain the document d(s,t) , which leads the total complexity to
372   be O(N + E + N ). The time complexity for Pre-trained LMs to process d(s,t) is O(P 2 ), where P
373   denotes the number of tokens in d(s,t) . Moreover, the GNN module requires O(|E| · f + N 2 ), where
374   f denotes the dimension of the embedding on nodes/edges. Overall, The complexity encapsulates
375   the stages of BFS tree construction, document processing with Transformers, and GNN learning,
376   which gives the training time complexity O(2N + E + P 2 + |E| · f + N 2 ). However, during the
377   inference stage, the complexity of our work simply reduces to the complexity of normal GNNs:
      O(|E| · f + N 2 ) as we do not need to construct documents during the inference phase.

      4       E XPERIMENT
379
380
381   Setup. This paper focuses on link prediction on TEGs, which aims to predict whether there will be a
382
      strong connection between two nodes in the adopted datasets based on their transition graph. We run
      experiments on five real-world networks: Amazon-Movie (He & McAuley, 2016), Amazon-Apps
383
      (He & McAuley, 2016), GoodReads-Children (Wan et al., 2019), GoodReads-Crime (Wan et al.,
384
      2019), and StackOverflow. More specific dataset statistics can be found in the Appendix A.3. We
385   evaluate the performance using four standard metrics: Mean Reciprocal Rank (MRR), Normalized
386   Discounted Cumulative Gain (NDCG), Area Under ROC Curve (AUC) metric, and F1 score.
387
388
      Comparison Methods. We compare our model with general GNNs, language model integrated
      GNNs, and large language models. For general GNNs, we select MeanSAGE (Hamilton et al., 2017),
389
      MaxSAGE (Hamilton et al., 2017), GIN (Xu et al., 2019) and RevGAT (Li et al., 2021), which only
390
      use an adjacency matrix as the input. For language model-enhanced GNNs, we utilize Pre-trained
391   LMs, e.g., BERT (Devlin et al., 2019), to acquire text representations on edges. Our baselines consist
392   of BERT + Graph Transformer (GTN) (Yun et al., 2019), BERT + GINEConv (Hu et al., 2019) and
393   BERT + EdgeConv (Wang et al., 2019). Furthermore, we also incorporate state-of-the-art edge-aware
394   GNN - Edgeformer (Jin et al., 2022), which is constructed based on graph-enhanced Transformers
395   to combine language modeling into each layer of the Graph transformer. A novel graph foundation
396   model - THLM (Zou et al., 2023) is also included that integrates language modeling with GNN
397   training. Finally, we adopt state-of-the-art LLMs, i.e., LL A MA-3-70B and GPT-4 O by directly
398   translating the transition graph G(s,t) between the node pair (s, t) to natural language as (Fatemi
399
      et al., 2023) do. Note that state-of-the-art language model integrated GNNs, namely EdgeFormer,
      cannot incorporate text on nodes. For a fair comparison, we present two variants of our method:
400
      L INK 2D OC does not consider node texts, and L INK 2D OC -NT takes node text into account.
401
402   Implementation Details. To process all node and edge text, we leverage OpenAI’s embedding
403   model1 with dimension 3, 072. For both general GNNs and language model enhanced GNNs, the
404   dimensions of the initial node and edge embeddings are further normalized to 64 and 128 respectively.
405
      Additionally, for the Edgeformer model, we adhere to the same experimental settings as outlined
      in (Jin et al., 2022). Our model uses Graph Transformer (GTN) as our backbone in Eq. (1), where
406
      both node and edge embeddings mirror those of language model integrated GNNs. For L INK 2D OC,
407
      we follow the same settings as general GNNs to obtain node and edge embeddings from OpenAI’s
408   embedding model. We set λ1 = 1 and λ2 = 2 respectively. All GNN baseline layers are set to 2. The
409   temperature τ in Eq. (3) to 2. We use Adam as the optimizer with a learning rate of 1e − 5. The
410   batch size is 1, 024. We run our model and other baselines 10 times with different random seeds and
411   report the average performance.

532   5    C ONCLUSION
533
      In this work, we study the problem of link prediction on textual-edge graphs, where existing GNN-
534   based and LLM-based methods may fall short of jointly capturing both semantic and topology
535   information to make more accurate link predictions. We present a novel framework L INK 2D OC that
536   learns and aligns the semantic representation and topology representation by 1) building a structured
537   document to preserve both topology and semantic information; 2) proposing a Transition Graph
538   Neural Network module for better learning representations of the transition graph; and 3) designing a
539   self-supervised learning module to let TGNN have text understanding ability like LLMs. Our method
      generally outperforms other approaches from multiple aspects on five datasets.
