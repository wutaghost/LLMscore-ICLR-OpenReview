009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Estimating Mutual Information (MI), a key measure of dependence of random
012            quantities without specific modelling assumptions, is a challenging problem in
013            high dimensions. We propose a novel mutual information estimator based on
014            parametrizing conditional densities using normalizing flows, a deep generative
015            model that has gained popularity in recent years. This estimator leverages a block
016
               autoregressive structure to achieve improved bias-variance trade-offs on standard
               benchmark tasks.

019   1     I NTRODUCTION
020
021   Mutual Information (MI), a measure of dependence of random variables X and Y , plays an important
022   role in information theory (Cover & Thomas, 2006), statistics and machine learning (Tishby et al.,
023   2000; Peng et al., 2005; Vergara & Estévez, 2014; Chen et al., 2018), and biology and medical
024   sciences (Zhang et al., 2012; Sengupta et al., 2022). For random variables X and Y with joint density
025   p, the mutual information is defined as
                                                                     
026                                                                            p(X, Y )
027
                      I(X; Y ) = DKL (p k pX ⌦ pY ) = E(X,Y )⇠p log                             ,
                                                                          pX (X)pY (Y )
028
      where pX and pY are the marginal densities of X and Y , pX ⌦ pY is the density of the product
029
      distribution, and DKL (· k ·) is the Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence. We consider the problem of
030   estimating mutual information from a finite set of samples {(x1 , y1 ), . . . , (xN , yN )}.
031
032   Formally, an MI estimator IˆN depends on independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.) random
033   sample pairs (X1 , Y1 ), . . . , (XN , YN ), and should ideally be unbiased, consistent and efficient. In
034   addition, such an estimator should be effectively computable from large and high-dimensional data.
035
      We propose an unbiased and consistent mutual information estimator based on the difference-of-
      entropies (DoE) estimator suggested in McAllester & Stratos (2018). This characterization expresses
036
      the mutual information as the difference between the entropy of X and the conditional entropy of X
037
      given Y ,
038                                         I(X; Y ) = H(X) H(X | Y ).
039
      Each of the terms in this expression can be characterized as the infimum of a variational optimization
040   problem. Our implementation of this estimator is based on carefully chosen normalizing flows that
041   simultaneously approximate the minimizing densities of each of the optimization problems.

043   1.1   OVERVIEW OF PREVIOUS WORK
044
045   Traditional MI estimators are non-parametric estimators that depend on density estimation and Monte
046   Carlo integration or on the computation of k nearest neighbours (kNN). Examples include the widely
047   used KSG estimator by Kraskov et al. (2004), the non-parametric kNN estimator (kpN) by Lombardi
048   & Pant (2015), and improvements of the KSG estimator and a geometric kNN estimator by Gao
049   et al. (2017). These non-parametric methods are fast and accurate for low-dimensional and small-
050
      sized problems and are easy to implement. However, they suffer from the curse of dimensionality
      and do not scale well in machine learning problems since the data sets can be relatively large and
051
      high-dimensional (Paninski, 2003).
052
053   More recent parametric methods take advantage of deep learning architectures to approximate
      variational bounds on MI. These have been categorized into discriminative and generative approaches


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      by Song & Ermon (2019). Some state-of-art discriminative approaches include InfoNCE (van den
055   Oord et al., 2018), MINE (Belghazi et al., 2018), SMILE (Song & Ermon, 2019), CCMI (Mukherjee
056   et al., 2019) and DEMI (Liao et al., 2020). van den Oord et al. (2018) proposed a contrastive predictive
057   coding (CPC) method which relies on maximizing a lower bound on mutual information. The lower
058   bound involves function approximators implemented with neural networks and is constrained by the
059   batch size N , leading to a method that is more biased but with less variance. MINE, on the other
060   hand, is based on the Donsker-Varadhan (DV) lower bound for the KL divergence. Fundamental
061   limitations on approaches based on variational bounds were studied by Song & Ermon (2019) and
062   McAllester & Stratos (2018), the latter being the motivation for our approach.
063   Instead of constructing the Mutual Information estimators based on variational lower bounds, Liao
064   et al. (2020) proposed a classifier-based estimator called DEMI, where a parametrized classifier is
065   trained to distinguish between the joint density p(x, y) and the product p(x)p(y). Mukherjee et al.
066   (2019) proposed another classifier-based (conditional) MI estimator that is asymptotically equivalent
067   to DEMI. However, it still relies on variational lower bounds and is prone to higher error than DEMI
068   for finite samples, as summarized by Liao et al. (2020).
069   Compared with discriminative approaches, generative approaches are less well explored in MI
070   estimation problems. A naı̈ve approach using generative models for estimating MI is to learn the
071   entropies H(X), H(Y ) and H(X, Y ) with three individual generative models, such as VAE (Kingma
072   & Welling, 2013) or Normalizing Flows (Kobyzev et al., 2020), from samples. A method for
073   estimating entropy using normalizing flows was introduced by Ao & Li (2022). Estimators based
074   on the individual entropies will be highly biased and computationally expensive since the entropies
075
      are trained separately, while it is revealed that considering the enhancement of correlation between
      entropies in constructing MI estimators can improve the bias (Gao et al., 2017). Duong & Nguyen
076
      (2023) propose the Diffeomorphic Information Neural Estimator (DINE), that takes advantage of
077
      the invariance of conditional mutual information under diffeomorphisms. An alternative approach to
078   estimating MI using normalizing flows was recently proposed by Butakov et al. (2024). This approach
079   takes advantage of the invariance of the pointwise mutual information under diffeomorphisms. In
080   addition, the methods from Butakov et al. (2024) allow for the estimation of mutual information
081   using direct, closed-form expressions. Finally, we would like to point the recently introduced MINDE
082   estimator Franzese et al. (2024), which is based on diffusion models and represents a complementary
083   approach.
084   From a practical point of view, the performance of MI estimators is often measured using standard
085   data sets based on Gaussian distributions for which the ground truth is known. Recently, Czyż et al.
086   (2023) proposed a collection of benchmarks to evaluate the performance of different MI estimators in
087   more challenging environments.

174

175

176
177                                           g⇤ µ(A) = µ(g 1 (A))

179

186

379   layers with 512 hidden units for each layer and ReLU activations, and a linear layer with a single
380   output. In terms of our proposed method, we constructed the flow with 2 BNAF transformation
381   layers and tanh activations, and a linear BNAF layer to reset the dimensionality. The BNAF layers
382   use 20 ⇥ 20-d, 10 ⇥ 50-d, 6 ⇥ 100-d hidden dimensions for 20-d, 50-d and 100-d data respectively,
383   which is roughly the same as the 512 hidden units in discriminative methods. For Real NVP layers,
384   we let each of the scale and translation functions to be two layers multi-layer perceptron with 128
385   hidden units for each layer and ReLU activations. Each MI estimator was trained for 50 epochs with
386   a mini-batch of 128. Due to the vanishing and exploding gradient issues in Real NVP, we applied the
387
      Adamax optimizer with a fine-tuned learning rate. For all other optimizations, the Adam optimizer
      with a learning rate of 0.0005 was used. All results were computed over 10 runs on the testing sets
388
      generated with different random seeds to ensure robustness and generalizability.
389
390
391   Results. The results for a sample size of 128K are shown in Figure 2, while the results for sample
392   sizes of 64K and 32K are presented in Figure 3 and Figure 4, respectively. The Sparse Gaussian
393
      results for the 20-dimensional case are plotted in Figure 5. Overall, all the discriminative methods
      tend to underestimate MI. This issue does not occur in our proposed flow-based models for Gaussian
394
      variables, likely due to the fact that the base distribution is itself Gaussian. For the cubic Gaussian
395
      case, the underestimation is much milder compared to other methods, though the underestimating
396   bias increases as the true MI becomes larger.
397
398   Among all the methods, our proposed model achieved better performance across different dimension-
399
      alities and sample sizes. While DoE methods performed well for Gaussian variables, they exhibited
      a large bias when applied to cubic Gaussians. When comparing NDoE, BNAF with BNAF, we
400
      observed that for smaller sample sizes (or insufficient training steps), BNAF exhibits a slight bias
401
      across all true MI values. Additionally, for cubic cases, BNAF shows a larger bias when MI is close
402   to zero, an issue not observed with NDoE, BNAF. The work by Song & Ermon (2019) attributed this
403   as a shortcoming of generative models, but we believe our proposed method mitigates this issue, as
404   the bias in entropy estimation vanishes by approximating entropies using the same neural network.
405
      In the cubic cases, NDoE, Real NVP exhibits a larger bias, though it still outperforms discriminative
406
      methods, particularly when the sample size is sufficiently large. In the 20-dimensional Gaussian
407
      case, SMILE occasionally overestimated MI, which we will further analyze in the long-run training
408   experiments. For the Sparse Gaussian case, both NDoE, BNAF showed small biases when the
409   true MI is small, and BNAF outperformed NDoE, BNAF for larger MI. Both methods consistently
410   outperformed other approaches across different sample sizes. However, NDoE, Real NVP failed to
411   achieve realistic results in the Sparse Gaussian case.

      5   C ONCLUSIONS
415
416   In this research, we proposed a new MI estimator which is based on the block autoregressive flow
417
      structure and the difference-of-entropies (DoE) estimator. Theoretically, our method converges to
      true mutual information as the number of samples increases and with large-enough neural network
418
      capacity. The accuracy of our estimator then depends on the ability of the block autoregressive flows
419
      in predicting the true posterior probability of items in the test set. A theoretical analysis is provided
420   in the appendix. We discussed the connections and differences between our approach and other
421   approaches including the lower bound approaches of MINE and SMILE and InfoNCE(CPC), and the
422   classifier based approaches of CCMI and DEMI. We also demonstrate empirical advantages of our
423   approach over the state of the art methods for estimating MI in synthetic data. Given its simplicity
424   and promising performance, we believe that our method is a good candidate for use in research that
425   optimizes MI. In future work, we aim to expand our experiments to additional data, including the
426   image-like data of (Butakov et al., 2024) and the recently published benchmarks in (Lee & Rhee,
427   2024).
428   Despite its promising results, the proposed method has limitations in its current form. As a method
429   that depends on the particular neural network architecture used to implement the flows, care needs to
430   be taken to ensure the stability of the proposed estimator and its performance on smaller data sets. As
431   seen in the experiments, the method performs particularly well in cases where the random quantities
      are based on Gaussian distribution. In future work we aim to explore the possibilities of using different
