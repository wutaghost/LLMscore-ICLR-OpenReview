011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Incremental learning (IL) aims to acquire new knowledge from current tasks
014            while retaining knowledge learned from previous tasks. Replay-based IL methods
015            store a set of exemplars from previous tasks in a buffer and replay them when
016            learning new tasks. However, there is usually a size-limited buffer that cannot store
017            adequate real exemplars to retain the knowledge of previous tasks. In contrast, data
018            distillation (DD) can reduce the exemplar buffer’s size, by condensing a large real
019            dataset into a much smaller set of more information-compact synthetic exemplars.
020
               Nevertheless, DD’s performance gain on IL quickly vanishes as the number of
               synthetic exemplars grows. To overcome the weaknesses of real-data and synthetic-
021
               data buffers, we instead optimize a hybrid memory including both types of data.
022
               Specifically, we propose an innovative modification to DD that distills synthetic
023            data from a sliding window of checkpoints in history (rather than checkpoints
024            on multiple training trajectories). Conditioned on the synthetic data, we then
025            optimize the selection of real exemplars to provide complementary improvement
026            to the DD objective. The optimized hybrid memory combines the strengths of
027            synthetic and real exemplars, effectively mitigating catastrophic forgetting in Class
028            IL (CIL) when the buffer size for exemplars is limited. Notably, our method can
029            be seamlessly integrated into most existing replay-based CIL models. Extensive
030            experiments across multiple benchmarks demonstrate that our method significantly
031
               outperforms existing replay-based baselines. Our source code is available at
               https://anonymous.4open.science/r/DD4CIL-510C/.

035   1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037
      Incremental Learning (IL) Wu et al. (2019); Gepperth & Hammer (2016); Douillard et al. (2022);
038
      Xie et al. (2022) is an emerging technique that emulates the human ability to continuously acquire
039   new knowledge in an ever-changing world. However, the key challenge is that IL always suffers
040   from catastrophic forgetting McCloskey & Cohen (1989), which means the model drastically forgets
041   previously acquired information upon learning new tasks. Thus, how to mitigate catastrophic
042   forgetting in IL remains a significant challenge.
043
      In this work, we primarily focus on a particularly challenging scenario known as class incremental
044
      learning (CIL) Douillard et al. (2020); Zhu et al. (2021); Wang et al. (2022b;a). CIL aims to learn new
045   and disjoint classes across successive task phases and then accurately predicts all classes observed
046   thus far in each phase, without prior knowledge of task identities. A common approach to enhance
047   CIL performance is the use of replay, which stores a subset of exemplars from old tasks and revisits
048   them when learning new tasks. Storing real exemplars can significantly enhance the performance of
049   CIL Rebuffi et al. (2017); Chaudhry et al. (2019); however, the performance will be affected by the
050   limitation of the exemplar buffer size. To decrease the dataset size, recent studies have explored data
051   distillation (DD) Loo et al. (2022); Du et al. (2023) to distill compact synthetic exemplars from real
052   datasets. However, Yu et al. (2023) indicates that the effectiveness of synthetic exemplars declines as
053
      their quantity increases. Motivated by these findings, we aim to develop a new hybrid memory that
      combines the advantages of both real and synthetic exemplars for replay-based CIL methods.

      Class Incremental Learning. Existing works of CIL can be categorized into three types:
      regularization-based methods, architecture-based methods, and replay-based methods.
094
095   (i) Regularization-based methods seek to mitigate catastrophic forgetting by incorporating explicit
096   regularization terms to balance the knowledge from old and new tasks Li & Hoiem (2017); Kirkpatrick
097   et al. (2017); Lee et al. (2017); Liu et al. (2018); Ahn et al. (2019). However, only using regularization-
098   based methods often proves inadequate for preventing catastrophic forgetting. Therefore, these
099
      methods often collaborate with the replay-based methods to boost performance. (ii) Architecture-
      based methods try to mitigate catastrophic forgetting by either dynamically expanding the model’s
100
      parameters to accommodate new tasks Mallya & Lazebnik (2018); Ahn et al. (2019); Douillard
101
      et al. (2022) or by learning new tasks in parallel using task-specific sub-networks or modules Rusu
102   et al. (2016); Fernando et al. (2017); Henning et al. (2021). However, these approaches often
103   require substantial memory to store the expanded network or multiple sub-networks and typically
104   depend on task identities to determine the appropriate parameters or sub-networks for a given task.
105   Consequently, their applicability is significantly limited. (iii) Replay-based methods are designed to
106   mitigate catastrophic forgetting by preserving a small subset of old task exemplars for replay while
107   learning new tasks. One line of these methods Rebuffi et al. (2017); Douillard et al. (2020); Wang et al.
      (2022b;a) maintains a limited memory for storing real exemplars from old tasks. However, due to data


                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      privacy concerns and inherent memory constraints, the number of real exemplars stored is typically
109   small, significantly affecting performance in applications. Another line Shin et al. (2017); Ostapenko
110   et al. (2019); Lesort et al. (2019); Rios & Itti (2018) employs generative models, such as Variational
111   Autoencoders (VAE) and Generative Adversarial Networks (GAN), to create synthetic exemplars of
112   old tasks for replay. However, these methods often face challenges with label inconsistency during
113   continual learning Ayub & Wagner (2020); Ostapenko et al. (2019), and is closely related to continual
114   learning of generative models themselves Wang et al. (2023). In contrast, this paper aims to condense
115   dataset information into a few synthetic exemplars without training additional models.
116   Data Distillation. Data distillation (DD) aims to condense the information from a large-scale dataset
117   into a significantly smaller subset. This process ensures that the performance of a model trained on
118   the distilled subset is comparable to that of a model trained on the original dataset. Existing DD
119   methods can be grouped into three categories based on the optimization objectives: performance
120   matching Loo et al. (2022); Zhou et al. (2022), parameter matching Cazenavette et al. (2022); Zhao
121   & Bilen (2021); Du et al. (2023), and distribution matching Zhao & Bilen (2023); Sajedi et al. (2023).
122   While a few studies Zhao & Bilen (2023); Sajedi et al. (2023) have applied DD to a simple specific
123
      IL application like GDumb Prabhu et al. (2020), they cannot be directly applied to the general
      replay-based CIL methods. This is because traditional DD techniques require training a set of models
124
      randomly sampled from an initialization distribution. However, for typical replay-based CIL, the
125
      initial model parameters for each task are derived from the weights trained on the previous task. Thus,
126   directly applying existing DD techniques to CIL introduces significant computational costs, as it
127   requires training an additional set of models to get multiple trajectories. To address this issue, we
128   introduce the Continual DD (CDD) technique to suit replay-based CIL.

517       6              C ONCLUSION AND L IMITATIONS
518
519      In this work, we introduced a hybrid memory that stores a limited number of real and synthetic
520
         exemplars for improving the CIL performance. Specifically, we devised a new Continual Data
         Distillation (CDD) technique that can adapt most existing DD methods into CIL to generate optimal
521
         synthetic exemplars for the general replay-based CIL models. To further complement synthetic data,
522
         we proposed a conditional real-data selection to choose optimal real exemplars based on synthetic
523      exemplars. As a result, the combination of synthetic and real exemplars significantly enhanced the
524      CIL performance. Extensive experimental results on two benchmarks demonstrated that our hybrid
525      memory can improve the performance of the original methods and other baselines. A limitation of
526      our method is that the ratio between synthetic and real exemplars in the hybrid memory is currently
527      set as an empirical hyper-parameter. In future work, we plan to develop an adaptive algorithm to
528      automatically determine the optimal ratio for hybrid memory.
