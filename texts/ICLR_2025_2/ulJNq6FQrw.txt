009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011
               Large Language Models (LLM) alignment aims to prevent models from producing
012            content that misaligns with human expectations, which can lead to ethical and legal
013            concerns. In the last few years, Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback
014            (RLHF) has been the most prominent method for achieving alignment. Due to
015            challenges in stability and scalability with RLHF stages, which arise from the com-
016            plex interactions between multiple models, researchers are exploring alternative
017            methods to achieve effects comparable to those of RLHF. However, these methods
018            often rely on large high-quality datasets. Despite some methods considering the
019            generation of additional data to expand datasets, they often treat model training and
020
               data generation as separate and static processes, overlooking the fact that these pro-
               cesses are highly interdependent, leading to inefficient utilization of the generated
021
               data. To deal with this problem, we propose P LE, i.e., Progressively Label En-
022
               hancement for LLM Alignment, a framework that dynamically adjusts the model’s
023            training process based on the evolving quality of the generated data. Specifically,
024            we prompt the model to generate responses for both the original query and a set
025            of carefully designed principle guided query, and then utilize a dynamic threshold
026            to determine the appropriate training approach for both responses based on their
027            corresponding reward scores. Experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness
028            of P LE compared to existing LLM alignment methods.

031   1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Large Language Models, such as the LLama series (Touvron et al., 2023) and OpenAI’s GPT series
034
      (Floridi & Chiriatti, 2020; OpenAI, 2023), have demonstrated their powerful capabilities across
035
      various language tasks, including translation (Zhang et al., 2023), summarization (Pilault et al., 2020),
036   and conversational interaction (Wang et al., 2023a). In certain scenarios, they have even exhibited
037   performance that matches that of human experts (Ouyang et al., 2022).
038
039
      However, these language models may not always generate responses as expected by humans and
      can even produce content that violates human ethics or legal boundaries (Bai et al., 2022a; Askell
040
      et al., 2021). Therefore, it is crucial for researchers to explore the limitations of these models and
041
      implement restrictions on output generation to ensure safety and compliance, a process known as AI
042   alignment.
043
044   The most prominent method for achieving AI alignment is Reinforcement Learning from Human
045
      Feedback (RLHF) (Ziegler et al., 2019; Stiennon et al., 2020; Ouyang et al., 2022). RLHF employs
      Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) to guide models using human instructions (Wang et al., 2023b;
046
      Taori et al., 2023), followed by training a Reward Model on human-rated outputs (Ouyang et al.,
047
      2022), and optimizing the model with Reinforcement Learning (RL) algorithms like Proximal Policy
048   Optimization (PPO) (Schulman et al., 2017; Askell et al., 2021; Bai et al., 2022b). However, due to
049   challenges in stability and scalability with the RL stage, which arise from the complex interactions
050   between multiple models, researchers are exploring alternative methods. For instance, LIMA (Zhou
051   et al., 2023) has experimentally demonstrated that when the pre-trained model’s capabilities are
052   sufficiently strong and the quality of the SFT data is high, it can achieve results comparable to those
053   of RLHF. RAFT (Dong et al., 2023) expands the SFT dataset by generating additional samples
      and selecting those with high reward scores to enhance the SFT dataset. RRHF (Yuan et al.,

054
      2023) simplifies the RLHF process by integrating the subsequent RL steps into the SFT phase as a
055   regularization term.
056
057   However, these methods are highly dependent on large amounts of high-quality data, which is
058
      impractical in certain applications, such as the medical field (Yang et al., 2024b; Li et al., 2023) or
      chip design (Liu et al., 2023). Additionally, even though some methods generate extra data to expand
059
      the training set to alleviate the problem. They often treat model training and data generation as
060
      separate and static processes, which overlooks the fact that these processes are highly interdependent,
061   such as selecting only a small portion of high-scoring data from the reward model, discarding a
062   significant amount of other potentially useful data, leading to inefficient utilization of the generated
063   data. Therefore, we consider designing an efficient framework that couples the data generation and
064   model training processes, allowing them to work synergistically, thus ensures that all generated data,
065   including potentially useful lower-scoring data, is effectively utilized, thereby improving training
066   efficiency.
067   Motivated by the above consideration, we propose a novel framework named P LE, i.e., Progressively
068   Label Enhancement for Language Model Alignment. Specifically, during the sample generation
069   phase, we design a set of principles to guide the model to output according to human expectations.
070   When the reward score difference between the principle-guided output and the response to the
071   original query exceeds a dynamically updated threshold, indicating a significant improvement under
072   the principle-guiding, the model is encouraged to align its output with the better response and move
073   away from the poorer one. If the difference is less than or equal to the threshold, both responses are
074   considered of similar quality. To fully utilize all generated responses, we incorporate both in the
075
      model’s training, assigning weights based on the normalized reward scores. Our contributions can be
      summarized as follows:
076
077          • Practically, we are the first to identify that previous alignment methods overlook the coupling
078            between data generation and model training, leading to inefficient utilization of generated
079            data. And we propose a novel framework that integrates these two processes, enabling them
080            to work synergistically.
081          • Theoretically, we prove that with the progressively updated threshold strategy, our approach
082            can bound the error rate between the trained model and the optimal model, ensuring
083            convergence within a controlled range.
084
085
      Extensive experimental results validate the effectiveness of our method s over several existing
      language model alignment approaches.

090   The alignment of language models refers to the process of ensuring that the models behave in ways
091   that are consistent with human values, ethical principles, and intended purposes (Leike et al., 2018).
092   The most prominent and effective method currently used to achieve this alignment is Reinforcement
093
      Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) (Ziegler et al., 2019; Stiennon et al., 2020; Ouyang et al.,
      2022). The framework of RLHF first employs Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) to guide the model
094
      to follow human instructions in an imitative manner (Wang et al., 2023b; Taori et al., 2023). The
095
      next steps involve training a Reward Model on a dataset reflecting human preferences, created from
096   human evaluators’ ratings of the SFT model’s outputs (Ouyang et al., 2022). Using reinforcement
097   learning algorithms like Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) (Schulman et al., 2017), the SFT model
098   is further optimized by continuously generating outputs, receiving evaluations from the Reward
099   Model, and updating its parameters to maximize alignment with the Reward Model (Askell et al.,
100   2021; Bai et al., 2022b).
101
      However, due to the challenges of stability and scalability involved in the interactions between multi-
102   ple models in RLHF, researchers have started exploring other more direct and efficient methods for
103   model alignment (Rafailov et al., 2023; Yuan et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2023; Dong et al., 2023). DPO
104   derives an equivalent optimization objective from RLHF, allowing the model to be directly optimized
105   using preference data without the need to train a separate reward model (Rafailov et al., 2023).
106   Similarly, RRHF incorporates the steps of RLHF into the SFT stage by introducing a regularization
107   term, which encourages the model to generate preferred responses with higher probability and poor
      responses with lower probability (Yuan et al., 2023). LIMA has experimentally demonstrated that

154   4    T HE P ROPOSED M ETHOD
155
156   In this section, we present our novel framework named P LE, i.e., Selective Label Enhancement for
157   Language Model Alignment. As illustrated in Figure 1, during the sample generation phase, we use
158   carefully crafted principles to guide the model’s outputs. When the reward score difference between
159   the principle-guided output and the original response exceeds a dynamically updated threshold, the
160   model is encouraged to align with the better response and move away from the poorer one. If the
161   difference is less than or equal to the threshold, both responses are considered of similar quality and
      are assigned weights based on their normalized reward scores for model training.

311
312   Datasets. We conducted experiments on three tasks. (1) For multi-turn dialogue task, we use
313   Anthropic’s Helpful and Harmless (HH) dataset as experimental dataset (Bai et al., 2022a). This
314   dataset is designed to evaluate the alignment of language models with human preferences, ensuring
315
      that the models produce responses that are both helpful and harmless. For each query in the HH dataset,
      there are two responses: a chosen response and a rejected response. The chosen response is preferred
316
      based on human evaluators’ ratings, while the rejected response is considered less appropriate or
317
      effective. The dataset consists of 161K training data points and 8.55K test data points. (2) For
318   controlled text generation task, we use IMDb dataset (Maas et al., 2011). This dataset is widely used
319   for sentiment analysis and consists of movie reviews labeled as either positive or negative. It contains
320   50K labeled reviews, evenly split between training and testing sets. (3) For summarization task, we
321   use Reddit TL;DR summarization dataset (Völske et al., 2017). It contains user-generated posts
322   paired with concise summaries, providing a challenging benchmark for abstractive summarization
323   tasks. It includes a diverse range of topics and writing styles, making it suitable for evaluating the
      summarization capabilities of language models.

353   Baselines. We compare our method with several existing language model alignment approaches,
354
      including:
355
             • SFT (Ouyang et al., 2022): Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) trains the model by predicting the
356
               next token in a sequence based on a dataset of human-labeled examples to guide it towards
357
               desired outputs.
358
359          • PPO (Ziegler et al., 2019): Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) is a reinforcement learning
360            algorithm commonly used in the RLHF process. It encourages the model to produce outputs
361            that receive higher reward scores from the reward model while also maintaining stability by
362            ensuring the model’s outputs remain consistent with those of the initial model.
363          • DPO (Rafailov et al., 2023): Direct Policy Optimization (DPO) simplifies the RLHF process
364            by deriving an equivalent optimization objective of PPO. This approach allows the model to
365            be directly optimized using human preference data, eliminating the need to train a separate
366            reward model and the subsequent reinforcement learning step.
367
368
             • RAFT (Dong et al., 2023): Reward-rAnked FineTuning (RAFT) expands the SFT dataset
               by generating additional samples and selecting those with high reward scores to enhance
369
               the SFT dataset. This approach aims to improve the quality of the training data for SFT by
370
               including only high-scoring samples from the reward model.

533
534   In this work, we addressed the challenges of aligning Large Language Models with human expecta-
535   tions by proposing P LE (Progressively Label Enhancement for LLM Alignment). Unlike existing
536   methods that depend on large high-quality datasets and inefficiently utilize generated data, P LE fully
537   leverages all generated responses. By using a dynamically updated threshold and weighting responses
538   based on reward scores, our approach ensures efficient data utilization and alignment with human
539   preferences. Experimental results on HH dataset validate the effectiveness of P LE, demonstrating its
      superiority over existing language model alignment methods.
