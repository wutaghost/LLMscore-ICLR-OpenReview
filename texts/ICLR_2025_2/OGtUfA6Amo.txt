009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Multi-scale information is crucial for multivariate time series modeling. How-
012            ever, most existing time series multi-scale analysis methods treat all variables in
013            the same manner, which is not well adaptive to Irregular Multivariate Time Se-
014            ries (IMTS), where different variables have distinct original scales/sampling rates.
015            Therefore, extracting temporal and inter-variable dependencies at multiple scales
016            in IMTS remains challenging. To fill this gap, we propose a hierarchical patch
017            graph network Hi-Patch. The key components of Hi-Patch are an intra-patch
018
               graph layer and several inter-patch graph layers. The intra-patch graph layer flex-
               ibly represents and fully captures both the local temporal and inter-variable de-
019
               pendencies of densely sampled variables at the original scale by employing fully
020
               connected graph networks within each patch, and obtains patch-level node repre-
021            sentations through aggregation. Subsequently, several inter-patch graph layers
022            are stacked to form a hierarchical architecture, where each layer updates spe-
023            cific patch-level nodes through scale-specific graph networks, progressively rep-
024            resenting and extracting more global temporal and inter-variable features of both
025            sparsely and densely sampled variables, and further aggregating to produce the
026            next patch-level node representations. The output of the last inter-patch graph
027            layer is fed into task-specific decoders to adapt to different downstream tasks.
028            Experimental results on 8 datasets show that Hi-Patch outperforms a range of
029
               state-of-the-art models in both IMTS forecasting and classification tasks.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
      Time series analysis has important applications in various fields such as healthcare (Lan et al., 2024),
034
      climate forecasting (Verma et al., 2024) and traffic planning (Li et al.). Due to the complexity and
035
      non-stationarity of real-world systems (Wang et al., 2024a), multivariate time series often exhibit
036   different variations and fluctuations at different scales (Chen et al., 2024a). Previous studies (Wang
037   et al., 2024b; Chen et al., 2024a; Cai et al., 2024) have demonstrated that effectively capturing
038   multi-scale features of multivariate time series is essential for time series modeling.
039
      Due to sensor malfunctions, varying sampling sources, or human factors, the final sampled time
040
      series often exhibits irregularities in real-world applications, resulting in Irregular Multivariate Time
041
      Series (IMTS). The peculiarity of IMTS lies in two aspects: firstly, the sampling interval within
042   each variable is uneven. Secondly, asynchronous observations of different variables have distinct
043   sampling rates. The subgraph in the bottom left corner of Figure 1 illustrates an IMTS example with
044   two variables.
045
      As a unique type of multivariate time series, IMTS also exhibits the inherent multi-scale characteris-
046
      tics of time series. Unfortunately, many of the existing multi-scale analysis methods have limitations
047
      in mining the multi-scale characteristics of IMTS. The mainstream approach of these methods is to
048   downsample or segment the original series based on several fixed time steps (Challu et al., 2023;
049   Shabani et al., 2023), thereby forming several views that reflect features at different scale levels, as
050   shown in the top row of Figure 1. On this basis, global temporal and inter-variable dependencies
051   are extracted from coarse-grained views, while local dependencies are extracted from fine-grained
052   views. This approach assumes that all variables have a consistent original scale so that each down-
053   sampled view consistently reflects the features of all variables at the same granularity level, thereby
      achieving consistent separation of multi-scale patterns for all variables.

073   However, in IMTS, some variables have only a few sampling points and do not possess fine-grained
074   features themselves, making their downsampled view meaningless (e.g., the variable V0 in Figure
075   1). Some other variables are densely sampled and require multiple downsampling steps to reflect
076
      global patterns (e.g., the variable V1 in Figure 1). In such cases, it is difficult to determine a con-
      sistently suitable downsampling level for all variables, making existing methods infeasible from the
077
      beginning. Furthermore, in IMTS, a downsampled view can contain mixed granularity features, as
078
      shown in the bottom row of Figure 1, but existing methods tend to extract and analyze a consistent
079   feature at a specific scale, which are difficult to handle mixed features. Additionally, despite the dis-
080   tinct scales/sampling rates occurring among variables in IMTS, they are not entirely independent but
081   exhibit correlations(Zhang et al., 2022). Specifically, variables in MTS may display different inter-
082   variable correlations at different time scales (Cai et al., 2024). How to extract multi-scale temporal
083   dependencies while considering inter-variable correlations across different scales in IMTS, where
084   variables have distinct original scales, remains a major challenge in IMTS modeling.
085   To address this gap, we propose Hi-Patch, a hierarchical patch graph network. Hi-Patch is based
086   on the concept of patching, which has been recently proven to be effective in capturing local tem-
087   poral dependencies (Nie et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024a; Zhang et al., 2024). Hi-Patch first divides
088   the original observation set into multiple patches based on a short time span. Then, an intra-patch
089   graph layer flexibly represents and fully captures local temporal dependencies of densely sampled
090   variables and their correlations with other variables at the original scales by employing a fully con-
091   nected graph network within each patch. Subsequently, to capture dependencies at a larger scale,
092   the nodes are aggregated to obtain patch-level feature nodes as inputs for several inter-patch graph
093
      layers. Each inter-patch graph layer receives specific patch-level nodes which are obtained through
      node aggregation of the previous layer, thereby forming a hierarchical architecture. These inter-
094
      patch graph layers gradually extract more global temporal and inter-variable dependencies that both
095
      densely and sparsely sampled variables possess through scale-specific graph networks. The output
096   of the last inter-patch graph layer is finally fed into specific task decoders to adapt to different down-
097   stream tasks. During this process, each intra/inter-patch graph layer handles features at a specific
098   scale. Fine-grained features of densely sampled variables are extracted in the lower layers, while
099   coarse-grained features of all variables are extracted in the upper layers, achieving complete ex-
100   traction of mixed multiple granularities temporal and inter-variable correlations in IMTS. Our main
101   contributions are summarized as follows:
102
             • We introduce the intra-patch/inter-patch graph layers to flexibly represent and fully extract
103
               features of specific variables in IMTS at specific scales.
104
105          • We propose Hi-Patch, using hierarchical architecture to effectively achieve multi-scale
106
               modeling of IMTS from fine-grained to coarse-grained.
107          • We conduct experiments on IMTS forecasting and classification on 8 datasets, and the
               evaluation results demonstrate that Hi-Patch outperforms existing methods in most cases.

      2   R ELATED W ORK
109
110
      Irregular Multivariate Time Series Modeling Existing methods can be broadly categorized into
111   interpolation-based and raw-data-based methods. The former, employing methods such as kernel-
112   based approaches (Shukla & Marlin, 2019; Wu et al., 2021), Gaussian process (Tan et al., 2021) or
113   hourly aggregation (Ma et al., 2020), aims to obtain a set of regularly spaced observations. How-
114   ever, interpolation may lead to losing valuable information from the original series. Raw-data-based
115   methods learn directly from IMTS. (Che et al., 2018) improve recurrent neural networks to accom-
116   modate uneven time intervals, while (Horn et al., 2020; Shukla & Marlin, 2021) introduce time
117   embeddings to represent arbitrary sampling timestamps. (Rubanova et al., 2019; De Brouwer et al.,
118   2019; Biloš et al., 2021; Schirmer et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2024b) use neural ordinary differential
119
      equations to handle irregularities. Recent research has also introduced attention (Jhin et al., 2021) or
      graph neural networks (Zhang et al., 2022; Yalavarthi et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024) to account for
120
      inter-variate correlations in IMTS. Despite these advancements, most of these methods focus only
121
      on single-scale characteristics of IMTS, and how to fully capture multi-scale features within IMTS
122   remains a challenge.
123
124   Multi-scale Modeling for Time Series Multi-scale information has proven to be crucial for time
125   series modeling. TAMS-RNNs (Chen et al., 2021) use different frequencies to update several sub-
126   hidden states of RNNs to capture multi-scale information. Pyraformer (Liu et al., 2022) introduces
127   pyramid attention to extract features at varying temporal scales. N-HiTS (Challu et al., 2023) em-
128   ploys multi-rate data sampling and hierarchical interpolation to extract features at different reso-
129   lutions. Scaleformer (Shabani et al., 2023) allocates forecasting models across different temporal
130   scales. Pathformer (Chen et al., 2024a) proposes a multi-scale Transformer with adaptive paths,
131   while TimeMixer (Wang et al., 2024a) achieves complementary predictive capabilities by disentan-
132
      gling variation at multiple scales. However, these methods are designed for regular MTS and are
      not adaptive for IMTS. Although Warpformer (Zhang et al., 2023) presents a multi-scale approach
133
      for IMTS, its original goal is to balance the differences in sampling densities across variables and it
134
      involves upsampling interpolation for sparse variables, which may distort the original data distribu-
135   tion.
136
137   Graph Neural Networks for Multivariate Time Series A series of studies have integrated GNN
138   with various time series modeling frameworks to effectively capture inter-variable dependencies in
139   MTS (Jin et al., 2024). These approaches have been widely applied in diverse domains, includ-
140   ing transportation (Rahmani et al., 2023), healthcare (Wang et al., 2022), economics (Wang et al.,
141   2021), demonstrating promising results. However, many of these methods are primarily designed for
142   modeling synchronous correlations among variables, lacking sufficient extraction of asynchronous
143   dependencies widely present in IMTS.

396   Furthermore, regular MTS forecasting models, including single-scale and multi-scale approaches,
397   do not demonstrate competitive performance in IMTS forecasting. This is because regular MTS
398   methods have limited capability to handle the irregularities within and between variables in IMTS,
399   leading to suboptimal performance when directly applied to IMTS. Among methods specifically
400   designed for IMTS, Warpformer is the only method that considers the multi-scale characteristics
401   of IMTS and it achieves the most competitive performance. However, Warpformer involves in-
402   terpolation of sparse variables, which may distort the original distribution of IMTS. In addition,
403   Warpformer employs attention separately in the time and variable dimensions, limiting its ability to
404
      capture asynchronous dependencies. In contrast, our Hi-Patch flexibly represents and extracts both
      synchronous and asynchronous dependencies of IMTS under different scales through an intra-patch
405
      graph layer and several inter-patch graph layers. Moreover, Hi-Patch only handles actually observed
406
      points, avoiding the accumulation of imputation errors, particularly in cases of higher missing ratios,
407   exhibiting a degree of robustness. We also provide efficiency analysis in Appendix F.3.

531   6     C ONCLUSION
532
533
      In this paper, we propose Hi-Patch for modeling IMTS. The proposed method leverages the intra-
534   patch/inter-patch graph neural network to flexibly represent and fully extract features at specific
535   scales in IMTS. Based on this, the hierarchical architecture is used to effectively achieve multi-scale
536   modeling of IMTS in a bottom-up manner (from local features to global ones). Our experimen-
537   tal results demonstrate that Hi-Patch outperforms existing methods in both IMTS forecasting and
538   classification tasks. Our future work will focus on adaptive multi-scale modeling of IMTS, which
539   selects the most suitable scales based on the specific temporal characteristics and dynamics of each
      sample to further improve performance.

999    AUROC AUROC is commonly employed in binary classification tasks, where one class is des-
1000   ignated as positive and the other as negative. It represents the area under the Receiver Operating
1001   Characteristic (ROC) curve, constructed by plotting the True Positive Rate (TPR) against the False
1002   Positive Rate (FPR). AUROC ranges from 0 to 1, with a higher value indicating better model perfor-
1003   mance in accurately discriminating between positive and negative instances. An AUROC equal to
1004   0.5 indicates a model’s performance equivalent to random guessing, while an AUROC greater than
1005   0.5 signifies superiority over random guessing.

1007   AUPRC The Area Under the Precision-Recall Curve is widely used as a performance metric
1008   for imbalanced binary classification tasks. It provides a comprehensive assessment of a model’s
1009
       precision-recall trade-off. The Precision-Recall curve is constructed by plotting recall on the x-axis
       and precision on the y-axis. AUPRC ranges from 0 to 1, and a higher value indicates better model
1010
       performance in achieving high precision and recall simultaneously. It has been suggested as a good
1011
       criterion for unevenly distributed classification problems (Davis & Goadrich, 2006).

1016   We search all hyperparameters in the grid to find the best hyperparameters for our proposed model
1017   Hi-Patch. Specifically, our model has a total of 4 hyperparameters: patch size P , dimension of
1018

       We compared 8 leading IMTS models: Warpformer, GRU-D, GraFITi, tPatchGNN, CRU, mTAND,

1182   epoch and MSE. The results shown in Figure 6 indicate that the training time of our method ranks
1183   5th on average across the four datasets. It is worth noting that our model employs a hierarchical
1184   structure to extract features at multiple scales within the IMTS, with each layer inevitably adding to
1185   the training time. In contrast, all the compared methods, except Warpformer, only extract features at
1186   the original scale, which is why our model is not the fastest. Nonetheless, our model’s training time
1187   remains in the same order of magnitude as the fastest models. In this context, We believe that the
       trade-off of sacrificing some training time to extract richer features in IMTS is worthwhile.
