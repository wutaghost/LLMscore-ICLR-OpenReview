011                                                          A BSTRACT
012
013               LLM agents have the potential to revolutionize defensive cyber operations, but
014               their offensive capabilities are not yet fully understood. To prepare for emerging
015
                  threats, model developers and governments are evaluating the cyber capabilities
                  of foundation models. However, these assessments often lack transparency and
016
                  a comprehensive focus on offensive capabilities. In response, we introduce the
017
                  Catastrophic Cyber Capabilities Benchmark (3CB), a novel framework designed
018               to rigorously assess the real-world offensive capabilities of LLM agents. Our
019               evaluation of modern LLMs on 3CB reveals that frontier models, such as GPT-4o
020               and Claude 3.5 Sonnet, can perform offensive tasks such as reconnaissance and
021               exploitation across domains ranging from binary analysis to web technologies.
022               Conversely, smaller open-source models exhibit limited offensive capabilities. Our
023               software solution and the corresponding benchmark provides a critical tool to
024               reduce the gap between rapidly improving capabilities and robustness of cyber
025               offense evaluations, aiding in the safer deployment and regulation of these powerful
026
                  technologies.

      1.2   S CENARIOS FOR C ATASTROPHIC AI C YBER R ISK
109
110   The intersection of catastrophic AI risk and cybersecurity has gained significant attention, both
111   academically (Grace et al., 2024) and publicly (Forum, 2023). Cyber offense capabilities have led to
112   critical infrastructure disruptions, such as power outages in Ukraine (Whitehead et al., 2017), more
113   than $1.8 billion damages from a single malware attack (Crosignani et al., 2024), and intellectual
114   property theft causing hundreds of billions in damages in the US alone (FBI, 2019). The increasing
115   reliance on digital infrastructure, combined with the proliferation of cyber threats and potential AI
116
      risks, presents a formidable challenge. Recognizing these emerging threats, various stakeholders are
      taking proactive measures:
117
118
             • Research Laboratories and Academia: Developing classification systems and risk assessment
119
               methodologies for foundation models’ cyber capabilities to enable preemptive security
120            interventions (Li et al., 2024; Phuong et al., 2024; Bhatt et al., 2024; OpenAI, 2024).
121
122
             • Governments: Investing in foundation model evaluations and AI safety research, focusing
               on high-risk areas like cyber offense and chemical/biological capabilities (DSIT, 2024; Nist,
123
               2024).
124
125          • AI Development Companies: Implementing security and risk mitigation strategies based on
126            risk evaluations, including policies that establish safeguards at specific risk levels (Anthropic,
127
               2023; OpenAI, 2023; Morris et al., 2024).
128
      Despite these efforts, there is a lack of a comprehensive, structured, principled evaluation of LLMs’
129
      general cyber offensive capabilities. So we created 3CB.

134   2.1   E VALUATING C APABILITIES
135
136   Although LLMs are becoming increasingly capable and useful, there is no consensus on how to
137
      robustly measure their capabilities. This makes it hard to assess the current state of the field, to make
      empirical predictions about the future, or to enforce regulations. It is a challenging problem that
138
      heavily depends on the evaluator’s goals.
139
140   We believe that focusing on specific skills within a broader framework is a productive approach. By
141   sampling from the space of cybersecurity skills with specific challenges, it is possible to extract
142   meaningful information about a model’s capability.
143   We also believe that how well a specific skill such as cybersecurity is demonstrated depends on how
144   well an AI agent is built. Thus, any principled LLM skill benchmark must perform meaningful skill
145   elicitation for any combination of a model (since elicitation techniques are not guaranteed to be
146   transferable across models), and a challenge (since different contexts call for different prompts and
147   agent setups), to evaluate what is possible in principle with a model, as opposed to what is convenient
148   to achieve. For impactful decisions, such as applying AI regulations, only the best-performing
149
      elicitation of a given AI model should be considered. A suboptimal way of eliciting skills also
      includes model refusals, as a specific case of model failure.
150
151   It is also crucial to base a capability benchmark on solid engineering foundations, ensure reproducibil-
152   ity and run isolation, attribute failures and successes appropriately, and factor out any phenomena
153   unrelated to the agents’ performance.
154   By evaluating whether an LLM can independently apply these skills to real-world situations—and by
155   applying a taxonomy of skills, effective elicitation techniques, and robust evaluation methods—we
156   can understand a model’s capabilities. This approach leads to several core design choices explained
157   below.
158

246        We introduce the Catastrophic Cyber Capabilities Benchmark (3CB), which comprises two main
247        components:
248
249                       • 3CB Harness: An open-source software solution that enables reproducible and scalable
250
                            execution of AI agents within a sandboxed environment against defined goals. It includes
                            conventions for defining challenges, environments, agents, and methods of capability elicita-
251
                            tion.
252
253                       • 3CB Challenge Set: A collection of 15 challenges covering all ATT&CK cyber offense
254
                            technique categories, serving as a sampling over the space of cyber skills. 11 challenges are

256
           Compared to existing cyber question-answering benchmarks and challenge collections, 3CB provides
257        a more comprehensive representation of general cyber capabilities. It is closely aligned with current
258        cybersecurity practices and offers a realistic setting by allowing AI agents to operate within an
259        interactive sandboxed environment. This setup enables agents to explore and potentially take multiple
260        paths to achieve a realistic goal.
261
           By evaluating a selection of modern LLMs on the challenge set using the harness, we create a
262
           snapshot of their general cyber capabilities.

           2.4            3CB H ARNESS
265
266        Large Language Models (LLMs) inherently produce text completions, making them well-suited for
267        text-based interactions with computer systems. The 3CB Harness is designed to integrate with several
268        LLM API providers, such as OpenAI, Anthropic, Together, and Replicate, to facilitate these text
269
                 1
                     https://attack.mitre.org/versions/v15/, released April 23, 2024

270
      completions. The chat message structure naturally aligns with a turn-based interaction model of the
271   agent within the environment. The problem is initially framed in the first user message to the LLM.
272   Subsequent assistant messages are interpreted as agent actions within the environment, while user
273   messages are automated responses from the harness, detailing the effects of the agent’s actions. See
274   AppendixB.2 for an illustrative example of initial prompt and subsequent LLM outputs and inputs.
275
      For each specific challenge, the environment is defined as a collection of one or more Docker
276
      containers assembled into a network. The primary container connects to the AI agent through a text
277   interface. To ensure reproducibility and consistency, the environment is reset between evaluation
278   runs, allowing each agent instance to attempt the challenge independently.
279
280
      Interacting with computers via text is not a novel concept; human users have long utilized computer
      terminals to facilitate such interactions. Building upon this, the connection between the AI agent and
281
      the Docker environment is mediated by a simulated teletypewriter (TTY). This approach provides a
282
      richer mode of interaction than simple command-line interfaces or batch scripts, enabling the agent to
283   use features like pagination and control sequences (e.g., Ctrl-C). Additionally, the terminal interface
284   allows control over the size of the environment’s response to an action; excessive output will scroll
285   upwards but remains accessible to the agent if needed.
286
      To transform the free-form model outputs into structured agent interactions, we define a set of
287
      communication protocols. These protocols extract the environment actions from chat messages and,
288
      inversely, encapsulate the environment’s responses into chat messages. An example of such a protocol
289   is MARKDOWN CODE BLOCKS, where reasoning steps and environment events are presented in plain
290   text, and terminal actions are enclosed within triple backticks.
291
292
      The harness is also responsible for managing both the environment and the agent. This includes
      detecting win or failure conditions, gracefully handling various events, and appropriately formatting
293
      interactions for the agent. We also found it useful to also persist as much data about an evaluation run
294
      as possible, allowing for easier debugging of inevitably arising engineering issues.

      2.5   3CB C HALLENGE S ET
299
300   The 3CB benchmark includes a collection of 15 challenges covering all ATT&CK cyber offense
301   technique categories, serving as a sampling over the space of cyber skills. 11 challenges are released
302
      publicly, with 4 acting as a holdout set. We present version 1.0 of 3CB with these challenges and
      invite the community to contribute towards extending coverage to all 637 techniques. A detailed
303
      description of all challenges is provided in Table 1.
304
305   Challenge Definition: Each challenge is defined by a single configuration file that specifies: (1)
306   the sandbox environment for the LLM agent, including any necessary files and initialization scripts;
307   (2) evaluation rules, such as the maximum number of action turns; and (3) different prompts and
308
      elicitation properties for the agent. For example, a challenge may include the agent’s home server in
      addition to a remote server with a vulnerable SSH connection and an important file (e.g., sshhijack).
309
310   Agent Goal: In each challenge, the agent’s objective is to find a predefined secret string (the ”flag”).
311   The method of acquiring this flag varies between challenges. For instance, in the exfil challenge,
312   the flag is the hash of a large binary data file, whereas in the deface challenge, it is received from a
313   hidden service after the agent has successfully compromised a web server.
314   Alignment with ATT&CK Categories: The challenges are based on the ATT&CK cyber offense
315   categories. Each challenge covers a specific technique under its respective category, enabling a direct
316   comparison of model performance against the most comprehensive collection of categorized cyber
317   offense techniques currently available.
318   Realistic Demonstration of Capabilities: Each challenge serves as a realistic demonstration of a
319   model’s capability. While some benchmarks claim to measure general cyber capabilities but only cover
320   specific sub-capabilities, 3CB ensures that each challenge is designed such that successful completion
321   by a model accurately reflects its ability to apply the technique described in that challenge. By
322   providing a challenge per category, we cover 14 specific techniques, and by extending coverage to all
323   202 techniques in the ATT&CK collection—potentially with multiple challenges per technique—we
      aim to obtain a comprehensive and accurate assessment of a model’s cyber offense skill coverage.


                                                         6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      Novelty to Avoid Memorization: Importantly, the challenges are designed to be novel and are not
325   present in the training data of frontier models. Many existing benchmarks and challenge collections
326   are based on publicly available data, increasing the likelihood that models have memorized the
327   underlying content. By introducing entirely new challenges that are explicitly distinct from pre-
328   existing capture-the-flag (CTF) challenges, cybersecurity case studies, or blog posts, we mitigate this
329   critical issue.
330
      Eliciting Maximum Performance: Eliciting maximum performance from each model leads to
331   credible performance results. For each challenge, experimenters can define agent configurations
332   specific to the challenge to elicit the model’s maximum performance. The challenge designer sets
333   the rules for what an agent configuration may include, ensuring that results are not a consequence of
334   cheating (e.g., by providing excessive hints to the model).
335
      Open-ended Tasks for Diverse Evaluations: Open-ended tasks facilitate diverse evaluations. By
336
      setting a goal for the models without prescribing how to achieve it, an agent (model or human)
337   can take multiple paths to reach the same objective. This allows for fine-grained qualitative and
338   quantitative analysis of challenge runs, enabling us to identify where models make mistakes and
339   where they outperform others.

      2.6   E XPERIMENTAL SETUP
342
343   We evaluate a representative selection of frontier Large Language Models (LLMs) on the 3CB cyber
344   offense benchmark. Utilizing the 3CB harness, we can quickly prototype and evaluate elicitation
345   variations over the instruction prompts for each challenge (METR, 2024a). Each model is run against
346   each challenge at least ten times per elicitation variation, using either the model’s nominal temperature
347   or 0.7 if the nominal temperature is not defined for that model. We avoid using deterministic
348   generation (t = 0) due to its lower performance on creative and complex tasks (Nguyen et al., 2024b).
349   We systematically evaluate Meta’s Llama 3.1 models with 8B, 70B, and 405B parameters (Meta,
350   2024); Mistral’s Mixtral 8x7B (Jiang et al., 2024); OpenAI GPT-4o, GPT-4o Mini, and GPT-4 Turbo
351   (OpenAI, 2024); OpenAI o1-preview and o1 Mini (OpenAI, 2024); DeepSeek 67B (DeepSeek-AI,
352   2024); Anthropic’s Claude 3.5 Sonnet (Anthropic, 2024b); Qwen 2 72B (Yang et al., 2024); and
353   Claude 3 variants Sonnet, Opus, and Haiku (Anthropic, 2024a).
354   To accurately assess each model’s best-case performance, we use only the best-performing elicitation
355   configuration for each model on each challenge, each combination run ten times. To evaluate
356   model performance variation across challenges and between models, we employ the following linear
357   mixed-effects model:
358
359                           yij = β0 + β1 x1ij + β2 x2ij + β3 x1ij x2ij + uj + ϵij                         (1)
360
361   where yij is a binary outcome of challenge completion for observation i in challenge j, x1ij and x2ij
362   represent the model and challenge respectively, β0 is the intercept, β1 , β2 , and β3 are fixed effects
363
      coefficients, uj ∼ N (0, σu2 ) is the random effect for challenge, and ϵij ∼ N (0, σϵ2 ) is the error term.
364   Elicitation Gap: If a model successfully completes the challenge during any of the ten attempts
365   for any of the elicitation configurations provided, we designate the model as capable of completing
366   the challenge in principle. We encode the model’s capability categorically rather than continuously
367   in our cyber offense risk evaluation because we anticipate that an adversarial actor with significant
368   computational resources could design an even more effective elicitation. Our evaluation is intended
369
      to represent a worst-case lower bound on a model’s offensive cyber capabilities.

453
454   Evaluating 14 models with 80 different elicitation configurations across 3CB’s 15 different challenges,

464   4     D ISCUSSION
465
466   The experimental results from running 80 agent configurations over our Catastrophic Cyber Capabili-
467   ties Benchmark (3CB) show that frontier LLMs are capable of complex autonomous cyber offense
468   (Figure 4 and Appendix B.2). With our realistic challenges and robust evaluation harness (Figure 3),
469   these results show that LLMs pose a security risk in the hands of malicious actors.
470   For instance, GPT-4o successfully completed the highly challenging rce task, demonstrating its ability
471   to perform open-ended exploration and exploit vulnerabilities through creative problem-solving
472   strategies. With recent legislation proposals requiring extensive evaluations from model developers
473   (Anderljung et al., 2023; EU, 2023) and the potential catastrophic risks of generally autonomous
474   agents, we believe that AI risk evaluation is crucial to any fair and effective legislative action and risk
475   mitigation interventions. By open sourcing the 3CB scaffolding and the 3CB challenge set, we take
476
      another step towards robust risk evaluations.
477   We avoid releasing four challenges due to ethical concerns (see Section 6). These simultaneously
478   represent a holdout dataset in case future models train on our challenges, allowing for follow-up
479   testing for evaluation gaming (Haimes et al., 2024).
480   Limitations: While our benchmark provides valuable insights, it is not without limitations. Our
481   challenge set currently covers all categories of cyber offense tactics (MITRE, 2020) but the coverage
482   needs to be extended to the numerous techniques and sub-techniques. Our elicitation results also
483   show high variability across model-agent configurations, suggesting that we have not reached the
484   limit of what each model is able to do. Specifically, for the o1 family of models safety filters obscure
485   the true model capability. A deeper investigation into the model biases and the developers’ safety
      interventions can improve our understanding.

486
      Risk Mitigation: The demonstrated ability of LLMs to perform sophisticated cyber operations
487   underscores the urgent need for effective mitigation strategies. Model developers must prioritize safety
488   training and incorporate robust refusal mechanisms to limit the potential for misuse. Many existing
489   methods in cybersecurity may be of help here: Implementing strict access controls, monitoring
490   systems for anomalous or illegal behavior and developing guidelines for ethical use.
491
      From our results, given that it is possible to avoid refusals and improve performance with better
492
      elicitation, there seems to be a limit to how much can be achieved with safety post-training. It is
493   conceivable that in the future the progress in the realm of capabilities is going to outstrip the strength
494   of the safety controls. Thus, future models may be dangerous enough to ever be released without
495   either foundational safety breakthroughs or intentional degradation of their capabilities.
496
      Future Work: The findings in this paper provide a promising path to expanding the 3CB across the
497
      full categorization in ATT&CK in collaboration with the cybersecurity community. With the current
498
      design of 3CB, the representability of our sampling across the continuous space of cyber offense
499   skills can still be much improved.
500
501   Further research into model behavior, including prompt sensitivity and the impact of safety interven-
502
      tions, will help us understand how to mitigate the risks associated with advanced LLMs. We currently
      study the models at the run-level but studying them at the message-level (with classification for each
503
      message a model sends), might prove even more valuable.
504
505   A significant milestone for the science of AI evaluations could be the establishment of empirical
506   capability scaling laws. Such laws, if they exist, could be instrumentally useful in the allocation of
507   training compute resources and in the planning of AI regulations. Finally, our results should inform
508
      solutions towards a secure future with AI agents: Integrating LLMs into cybersecurity frameworks,
      leveraging models for defensive purposes (such as threat detection, vulnerability assessment and
509
      incident response) to protect society’s functional digital systems and directly addressing both threat
510
      actors and self-exfiltration (Leike, 2023) of AI agents.
511
512
513   5    C ONCLUSION
514
515   We introduced the Catastrophic Cyber Capabilities Benchmark (3CB), a novel framework designed
516   to rigorously assess the real-world offensive capabilities of LLM agents. Our evaluation of various
517   modern LLMs across a comprehensive range of challenges aligned with the ATT&CK categorization
518   revealed that frontier models like GPT-4o and Claude 3.5 Sonnet possess significant offensive
519
      cyber capabilities, autonomously performing complex tasks such as reconnaissance and exploitation.
      Conversely, smaller open-source models exhibited limited offensive capabilities. These findings
520
      underscore the urgent need for robust evaluations of AI models’ offensive capacities and effective
521
      mitigation strategies to limit potential misuse. The 3CB framework provides a critical tool to bridge
522   the gap between rapidly advancing AI capabilities and the robustness of cyber offense evaluations,
523   aiding researchers, model developers, and policymakers in understanding and mitigating the risks
524   associated with advanced AI technologies.
