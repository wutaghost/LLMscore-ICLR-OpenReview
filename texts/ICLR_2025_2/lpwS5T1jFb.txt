                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
010
               Algorithmic Recourse (AR) aims to provide users with actionable steps to over-
               turn unfavourable decisions made by machine learning predictors. However, these
011
               actions often take time to implement (e.g., getting a degree can take years), and
012
               their effects may vary as the world evolves. Thus, it is natural to ask for recourse
013            that remains valid in a dynamic environment. In this paper, we study the robust-
014            ness of algorithmic recourse over time by casting the problem through the lens of
015            causality. We demonstrate theoretically and empirically that (even robust) causal
016            AR methods can fail over time except in the – unlikely – case that the world is
017            stationary. Even more critically, unless the world is fully deterministic, counter-
018            factual AR cannot be solved optimally. To account for this, we propose a simple
019            yet effective algorithm for temporal AR that explicitly accounts for time under the
020            assumption of having access to an estimator of the stochastic process. Our sim-
021
               ulations on synthetic and realistic datasets show how considering time produces
               more resilient solutions to potential trends in the data distribution.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027
      Machine Learning (ML) models play an increasingly prominent role in high-stakes decision-making
      tasks like credit lending (Barbaglia et al., 2021), bail approval (Dressel & Farid, 2018) and medical
028
      diagnosis (Yoo et al., 2019). The general consensus is that, to ensure fairness, these systems need
029
      to provide users with tools to challenge their ruling, thus preserving human agency. These require-
030   ments are also being mandated by recent AI legislation (AI Act, 2021). Algorithmic Recourse (AR)
031   (Karimi et al., 2022) aims to identify counterfactual explanations that users can follow to overturn
032   unfavourable machine decisions. For instance, AR methods might suggest a user obtain a master’s
033   degree as this will net them a higher income and, in turn, higher chances of obtaining a loan.
034
      In order to be of use, suggested recourse must be actionable (Ustun et al., 2019) and sufficiently
035
      inexpensive for the user to implement (De Toni et al., 2023b). We argue that actionability subsumes
036   the notion of timing. Indeed, in practical applications, (i) recourse takes time to be implemented and
037   to have an impact, (ii) performing the same action at different times might produce different effects.
038   For instance, getting a degree takes years and only impacts salary after some time. Moreover, getting
039   a degree at an older age reduces the expected salary increase. Our key insight is that, since time plays
040   a key role in the effectiveness of recourse, one has to ensure recourse suggestions should be robust
041   to time, i.e., they should lead to a positive outcome irrespectively of when the user performs them,
042   or at least for a user-defined point in the future. See Fig. 1 for an illustration.
043   Many efforts have been directed at robustifying recourse suggestions in several scenarios (Jiang
044   et al., 2024). For example, Upadhyay et al. (2021) studies recourse under model shift due to, e.g.,
045   retraining, Pawelczyk et al. (2022a) addresses recourse in case the user’s implementation of recourse
046   is imperfect, and Dominguez-Olmedo et al. (2022) considers robustness to misspecification of the
047   input instance. To the best of our knowledge, little has been done to explicitly formalize recourse
048   robustness over time. Recently, Beretta & Cinquini (2023) evaluated the effect of time in AR by
049   incorporating it in the recourse cost, thus not considering its effect on the validity.
050   Following Dominguez-Olmedo et al. (2022) and Beretta & Cinquini (2023), we study the problem
051   of time in AR through the lens of causality (Pearl, 2009). In causal recourse, recourse suggestions
052   are modelled as interventions on the user’s features (Karimi et al., 2021), thus giving a reliable
053   representation of how the features will change as the user acts on them to achieve recourse, provided
      we know the (approximate) causal model (Karimi et al., 2020b). We consider a novel setting in

055   subjected to trends. Our results challenge the usefulness of the mainstream variants of causal and

      Our contributions. Summarizing, we (i) introduce a sound but intuitive formalization of tempo-
      ral causal AR (Definition 1), based on causality (Pearl, 2009) and time-series with independent
059
      noise (Peters et al., 2013), (ii) show theoretically how uncertainty and non-stationarity hinder opti-
060
      mal counterfactual and sub-population recourse (CAR and SAR, (Karimi et al., 2020b)) for simple
061   discrete-time stochastic processes, (iii) show how robustifying recourse via uncertainty sets is not
062   enough to counteract time (Proposition 5), and (iv) present numerical simulations showcasing the
063   detrimental effects of time on robust (non-)causal AR approaches, and showcase how a simple time-
064   aware algorithm (Algorithm 1) can lessen such effects in synthetic and realistic settings (Section 4).

      3.6   ACCOUNTING FOR T IME IN P RACTICE
351
352   In Sections 3.2 to 3.5, we showed how recourse validity is hindered by the uncertainty and non-
353   stationarity of the stochastic process. Given a factual instance x and a recourse θ, Theorem 6 also
354   implies the upper bound can grow quickly depending on the difference of the induced interventional
355   distributions and classifier h between t and t + τ . Unfortunately, for any model h and tolerance ϵ,
356   there can exist multiple (robust) recourses θ to choose from (Pawelczyk et al., 2020). Since (robust)
357   CAR and SAR have no means to differentiate between recourses, they might end up suggesting
358   interventions which rapidly become invalid. An alternative is to settle for classical robust AR, which
359   can provide some amount of safety w.r.t. time depending on the chosen epsilon ϵ (cf. Section 4).
360
      The issue with this is that, as shown by Proposition 5, choosing ϵ without considering how the world
      changes can be dramatically suboptimal, i.e., robust AR might recommend expensive actions that
361
      risk becoming invalid.
362
363   Luckily, for SAR, we can mitigate these issues, as long as we have access to an estimator of the
364   stochastic process. We present a simple algorithm (Algorithm 1) for temporal sub-population al-
365   gorithmic recourse (Eq. (2)) drawing inspiration from adversarially robust recourse (Dominguez-
366
      Olmedo et al., 2022). Following the results of Section 3.4, we argue that, instead of providing robust
      recourse for an arbitrary uncertainty set with a fixed ϵ, we need to provide a robust θ for a fore-
367
      casted region of the feature space. We do so by extending the notion of uncertainty set B(xt ; τ )
368
      to consider the distribution entailed by the TiMINo SCM conditioned on the observed realization,
369   P (Xt+τ | Xt = xt ), after a time lag τ > 0:
370
371                                 B(xt ; τ ) = {x′ ∼ P (Xt+τ | Xt = xt )}                                (9)
372   Such a region does not depend on a fixed ϵ, thus sidestepping the issue shown by Proposition 5.
373   Algorithm 1 assumes to have access to a constant and differentiable classifier h, and to an estimator
374   P̃ (Xt ) of the stochastic process. We define ER(x, τ ; θ) = E[h(x̂)] where x̂ is sampled from the
375   interventional distribution conditioned on the non-descendant nd(I) of the intervened upon nodes
376   I. Similarly to Dominguez-Olmedo et al. (2022), in practice, we approximate B(xt ; τ ) by sampling
377   a finite number of instances from P (Xt+τ | Xt = xt ). As usual, users can control the trade-off
      between cost and robustness by varying λ within the Lagrangian (line 5, Algorithm 1).

      5   L IMITATIONS
514   We now discuss some limitations of our work which open up interesting avenues for future work.
515
      Feasibility of temporal recourse and its evaluation. T-SAR depends on the quality of the es-
516
      timator P̃ (Xt ) and, if the estimator is flawed, T-SAR could provide sub-optimal recourse. It is
517
      well-known how reliable time series forecasting is hard in various settings (Makridakis et al., 2020),
518
      because of issues like concept drift (Gama et al., 2014). Thus, it represents an additional hurdle to
519   achieving practical temporal recourse for realistic applications. Our experiments on synthetic and
520   semi-synthetic datasets are sufficient to confirm that time presents a non-trivial challenge for AR and
521   to show how an estimator approximating P (Xt ) can still be useful in some settings. However, we
522   could not fully evaluate the effectiveness of T-SAR in real-world situations as this requires temporal
523   datasets for recourse, which are currently not available. The scarcity of suitable data is a well-known
524   issue affecting the evaluation of AR approaches at large (Karimi et al., 2021; Esfahani et al., 2024).
525   Causal models, trends and interventions. Our formalization assumes the stochastic process con-
526   forms to TiMiNo (Peters et al., 2013), leaving space to consider more complex SCMs. We do not
527   explore trend models for the classifier h or the cost function C(·), which could be present alongside
528   those for P (Xt ). Lastly, we assume the total causal effect of recourse can be observed within t + τ ,
529   and future works could consider modelling interventions with causal effects extending beyond t + τ .
530
531   6   C ONCLUSION
532
      We have investigated the impact of time on algorithmic recourse. Our formalization of temporal
533
      causal recourse extends both counterfactual and sub-population causal AR by modelling the world
534   as a (possibly non-stationary) causal stochastic process P (Xt , Y t ). It allows us to theoretically
535   demonstrate how standard and robust AR approaches are fragile, as their solutions become invalid
536   in the presence of trends and future uncertainty. We also show that a simple algorithm, leveraging an
537   estimator of the stochastic process fitted on historical data, can deliver more robust solutions. Our
538   experiments with causal and non-causal approaches support our findings. With this work, we aim
539   to highlight the negative impact of time on existing AR approaches while demonstrating how these
      challenges can be at least partially mitigated by leveraging historical data.

       H    A DDITIONAL THEORETICAL RESULTS ON COST STABILITY
1297
1298
       In this section, we provide an upper bound on the cost stability of recourse suggestions when
1299   P (X, Y ) is a discrete-time stochastic process.
1300
1301   Previous research has shown how providing recourse without considering the user’s preferences can
1302
       lead to sub-optimal interventions (De Toni et al., 2023b). This is why personalized AR, in line
       with multi-attribute decision making (Keeney & Raiffa, 1993; Pigozzi et al., 2016), models the cost
1303
       function as an additive independence model C(x̂, x) = w⊤ |x̂ − x|, where the weights w ∈ Rd
1304
       encapsulate the user’s preferences (De Toni et al., 2023a;b). We assume we can learn these weights,
1305   either from historical data, e.g., surveys and interviews (Rawal & Lakkaraju, 2020), or by interacting
1306   with the end-user (De Toni et al., 2023b). In the following, we explicitly consider the evolution of
1307   the user’s preferences W, as it also impacts the effectiveness of recourse, although doing so can be
1308   avoided for non-personalized AR approaches.
1309
       We assume the user preferences can be represented as a stochastic process P (Wt ). We do not put
1310
       any prior assumption on how P (Wt ) factorizes, since it is not relevant for our results. In line with
1311
       previous work (De Toni et al., 2023b), we could imagine P (Wt ) follows a causal model. Then, we
1312
       can provide the following upper bound:

1314

       parametrized cost function C(x̂, x; w) = ⟨|x̂ − x|, w⟩ with bounded −k ≤ wit , Xit ≤ k for k ∈ R+ .
