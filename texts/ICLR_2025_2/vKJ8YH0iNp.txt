                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011
               Dataset distillation aims to distill a smaller training dataset from a larger one so
012
               that a model trained on this smaller set performs similarly to one trained on the
013            full dataset. Traditional methods are costly and lack sample diversity. Recent ap-
014            proaches utilizing generative models, particularly diffusion models, show promise
015            in capturing data distribution, but they often oversample prominent modes, limiting
016            sample diversity. To address these limitations in this work, we propose a mode-
017            guided diffusion model. Unlike existing works that fine-tune the diffusion models
018            for dataset distillation, we propose to use a pre-trained model without the need for
019            fine-tuning. Our novel approach consists of three stages: Mode Discovery, Mode
020            Guidance, and Stop Guidance. In the first stage, we discover distinct modes in the
021
               data distribution of a class to build a representative set. In the second stage, we use
               a pre-trained diffusion model and guide the diffusion process toward the discov-
022
               ered modes to generate distinct samples, ensuring intra-class diversity. However,
023
               mode-guided sampling can introduce artifacts in the synthetic sample, which affect
024            the performance. To control the fidelity of the synthetic dataset, we introduce the
025            stop guidance. We evaluate our method on multiple benchmark datasets, including
026            ImageNette, ImageIDC, ImageNet-100, and ImageNet-1K; Our method improved
027            4.4%, 2.9%, 1.6%, and 1.6% over the current state-of-the-art on the respective
028            datasets. In addition, our method does not require retraining of the diffusion model,
029            which leads to reduced computational requirements. We also demonstrate that our
030            approach is effective with general-purpose diffusion models such as Text-to Image
031            Stable Diffusion, showing promising performance towards eliminating the need
032
               for a pre-trained model in the target dataset. Our source code is available in this
               anonymized repository.

      1    I NTRODUCTION

038   The rapid advancements in machine learning are marked by a trend towards increasingly large
039
      datasets and models to achieve state-of-the-art performance. However, this trend presents significant
      challenges for researchers constrained by limited computation and storage resources. In response, the
040
      research community started to focus on developing techniques to address these limitations. While
041
      model pruning (Liu et al., 2017; He et al., 2019; Ding et al., 2019; Sharma & Foroosh, 2022) and
042   quantization (Xu et al., 2023; Chauhan et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2021; Wu et al., 2016) are introduced
043   to improve the model efficiency, core set selection and dataset distillation (Wang et al., 2018; Liu
044   et al., 2022) have emerged as the prominent techniques to reduce the size of the datasets. Core set
045   selection (Chen et al., 2010; Castro et al., 2018; Welling, 2009; Rebuffi et al., 2017) based approaches
046   were initially introduced for building condensed datasets, which involves selecting a few prototypical
047   examples from the original dataset to build the smaller dataset. However, these approaches are limited
048   to choosing the samples from the original dataset, which considerably restricts the expressiveness of
049   the condensed dataset. Dataset distillation removes this restriction.
050   The task of data set distillation is to distill information from a large training dataset into a smaller
051   dataset with few synthetic samples such that a model trained on the smaller data set achieves
052   performance comparable to the model trained on the complete data set. Most of the existing data
053   set distillation methods (Cazenavette et al., 2022b;a; Zhao & Bilen, 2023) follow the data matching
      framework, where the distilled dataset is updated to mimic the influence of the original dataset on

086   (Wang et al., 2023; Su et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2023) propose storing the knowledge of the dataset
087   into the parameters of a generative model instead of directly condensing it into a smaller synthetic set.
088   Once trained, the same generative model can generate synthetic datasets of varied sizes.
089   Among the generative models, diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020) are known for their impressive
090   capabilities in image synthesis. These models achieve perceptual quality comparable to GANs while
091   offering higher distribution coverage, as evidenced by (Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021b). However, they
092   tend to concentrate on denser regions (modes) of the data distribution, resulting in a synthetic dataset
093   that, while representative, often lacks the full diversity of the original data (Gu et al., 2024) (refer to
094   Figure 1a). Previous works (Gu et al., 2024) address this by explicitly retraining the diffusion model
095   to generate representative and diverse samples. With this training, the samples are more likely to be
096   generated from different modes of a class (See Figure 1b). In contrast, we propose a novel approach
097
      that extracts diverse samples from a pre-trained diffusion model already trained on the target dataset,
      without the need for further model retraining.
098
099   Our approach aims to distill a dataset with representative and diverse samples without re-training the
100   diffusion model. Our method first estimates the prevalent modes in the data with the Mode Discovery
101   stage. Then, we ensure diversity by guiding each sample to a different mode with our Mode Guidance.
102   However, guiding the sample to an estimated mode may compromise the quality of the synthetic
103
      sample. To address this, we introduce Stop Guidance to maintain the quality of the synthetic data
      (see Figure 1c).

120
121   Dataset distillation has received increased interest in recent years due to its applications in continual
122
      learning (Zhao et al., 2021; Zhao & Bilen, 2021; 2023), privacy-preserving datasets (Sucholutsky
      & Schonlau, 2021; Li et al., 2020), neural architecture search (Zhao et al., 2021; Zhao & Bilen,
123
      2021), and model explainability (Loo et al., 2022). Prior works have explored the problem of dataset
124
      distillation and show how challenging it is to encapsulate datasets in a limited set of examples.
125   Initially, this task was approached using non-generative models, then using generative priors, and,
126   more recently, with generative models. Below, we discuss works belonging to these categories in
127   detail.
128
129
      2.1   N ON - GENERATIVE DATASET D ISTILLATION M ETHODS
130
131   Dataset distillation condenses information from a large dataset into a smaller one with synthetic
132   images, to enable model training on the smaller dataset with performance comparable to that of the
133   full dataset. Various methods have emerged to minimize the performance gap between original and
134   distilled datasets through techniques such as dataset matching, parameter matching, and gradient
135   matching.
136   Initially, parameter matching (Zhao et al., 2021) was proposed, aligning the neural network weights
137   trained on synthetic data with those from the original dataset. However, this bi-level optimization
138   approach was time-consuming and unscalable. Gradient matching was later favored due to the high
139   dimensionality of parameter space, leading to inefficiencies. Further advances included feature
140   matching, which improves efficiency by eliminating dependence on bi-level optimization (Zhao &
141   Bilen, 2023). Additionally, long-range matching techniques were introduced by (Cazenavette et al.,
142   2022b) through matching training trajectories (MTT), optimizing network parameters after multiple
143
      training iterations to better synthesize relevant features for the training updates.
144   Recent advancements have introduced generative priors into the optimization process. GAN-IT (Zhao
145   & Bilen, 2022) shifted the focus from the pixel space to latent codes of pre-trained GANs, optimizing
146   these codes rather than working directly in image space. GLaD (Cazenavette et al., 2023) built on this
147   by incorporating generative priors with StyleGAN for high-resolution datasets, yielding images that
148   more closely match the dataset distribution and improve performance. H-GLaD (Zhong et al., 2024)
149
      further enhanced this by focusing on deeper feature layers for hierarchical optimization. Additionally,
      LD3M (Moser et al., 2024) utilized a latent diffusion model to optimize synthetic datasets directly in
150
      the model’s latent space, improving performance by refining latent codes through the denoising and
151
      diffusion processes. Despite their success on small-resolution datasets, these methods struggle with
152   high-resolution datasets (e.g., 256 x 256, 20 images per class), often being computationally expensive
153   and less efficient, leading to the emergence of more effective distillation methods from generative
154   models (Gu et al., 2024; Su et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2023).
155

204   3    M ETHOD
205
206   Our approach for obtaining diverse and representative examples for each class involves leveraging a
207   diffusion model trained on the target dataset. The main idea is to sample from the more likely regions
208   in the data distribution, called modes, during the reverse process. These modes are regions where the
209   samples have similar appearances, and they are representative of the class. However, diffusion models
210   tend to sample from the more likely modes, which creates an issue when there are fewer dominant
211   modes for a particular class than the number of images per class (IPC) in the dataset. Previous work
212
      (Gu et al., 2024) requires fine-tuning the diffusion model with additional representative and diversity
      losses to balance the likelihood of all modes; this approach requires more training and still has a high
213
      chance of redundancy while sampling.
214
215   Our three-stage approach, shown in Figure 2, eliminates the need for fine-tuning while maintaining
      mode diversity. In the first stage, known as mode discovery, we estimate a diverse set of modes


                                                                    4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      for each class in the dataset. After estimating the modes, the second stage involves controlling the
217   reverse process using our proposed mode guidance to sample from the estimated mode distribution.
218   During sampling, the guidance is applied until the stop guidance is triggered, which helps to control
219   the quality of the synthetic sample.
220
      In the mode discovery stage, the main objective is to identify the N modes of a specific class in the
221
      original dataset distribution. This discovery is performed using the original dataset in the latent space
222
      of the VAE encoder (Venc ). The motivation for this approach is that the generative space captures
223   the overall content of the image rather than discriminative features, which can be limited to specific
224   textures in the image. Any clustering algorithm can be used to estimate the modes for a particular
225   class; in our approach, we use K-Means. Once the modes are identified, our goal is to sample images
226   from these estimated modes. In the next section, we explain how to guide the denoising process of
227   diffusion and guarantee generation from the specified modes. The rest of this section is organized as
228   follows. Next, we cover some preliminaries, followed by Mode Guidance and Stop Guidance.

314   3.3   S TOP G UIDANCE
315
      The reverse process of diffusion can be divided into three stages: the chaotic stage (first 20%), the
316
      semantic stage (20% to 50%), and the refinement stage (last 50%) (Yu et al., 2023). We hypothesize
317
      that mode guidance is unnecessary during the refinement stage because we believe that the purpose
318   of mode guidance is to guide the synthetic image towards the mode in the high semantic space. Also,
319   during our initial experiments, we observed that providing strong guidance to a particular mode mi
320   often results in a loss of class fidelity and the presence of image artifacts (See Figure 4b tSG = 0).
321   Therefore, we introduce the stop guidance to mitigate losing class fidelity and image artifacts. The
322   stop guidance consists of giving a stop timestep tSG , the stop guidance involves setting λ to zero in
323   Eq. 6, when t < tSG in the reverse process. In the experiment section, we provide an ablation for the
      timestep (tSG ) when guidance ceases to exist.

327   Datasets and evaluation. To assess our approach’s effectiveness, we thoroughly examined the
328   available benchmarks for distilling high-resolution datasets (256 × 256). The datasets we evaluate
329

331
332   The hard-label protocol generates a dataset with its corresponding class labels, trains a network from
333   scratch, and evaluates the network on the original test set. This process is repeated three times for
334   target architectures, and the accuracy mean and standard deviation are reported. Random resize-crop
335
      and CutMix are applied as augmentation techniques during the target network’s training. For more
      detailed technical information about the protocol, please refer to (Gu et al., 2024). Similar to the
336
      existing literature, we evaluate our model in various IPCs ranging from 10 to 100. This protocol was
337
      used to evaluate ImageNet-100, ImageNette, and ImageNetIDC datasets.
338

340   by (Sun et al., 2024). The region-based soft-labels yi,m are generated as follows: yi,m = ϕT (xi,m ),

      where ϕT is the pretrained model and xi,m is the m-th P  cropP  of the i-th image. When training a model

464   When should guidance stop? To determine when to stop the guidance, we assessed mode guidance
465   with tSG ranging from 50 to 0 in increments of 5 steps. A stop guidance of tSG = 50 means no
466   guidance, while tSG = 0 means full guidance. Figure 4a shows that the optimal range to stop the
467   guidance is between tSG = 30 and tSG = 10, with the peak at tSG = 20. Additionally, Figure 4b
468   illustrates that the guidance introduces more variability in the generation, with a more diverse set of
469   backgrounds and poses. However, when the mode guidance is extended (e.g. tSG = 0), it does not
470
      guarantee class consistency, as demonstrated in Figure 4b.
471   Effect of each component. To assess the impact of each proposed component, we incrementally
472   evaluated the following: 1) Mode Discovery, 2) Mode Guidance, and 3) Stop Guidance. Mode
473   Discovery involves performing K-means per class on the original dataset and selecting the closest
474   sample to the k-means centroid. We conducted the evaluation on the ImageNette dataset with IPC
475   10, and reported the accuracy of ConvNet-6, ResNet10 with average pooling, and ResNet18. Table
476
      3 demonstrates that using diffusion with mode guidance enhances mode discovery and that stop
      guidance is crucial for achieving improved performance.
477
478   Visuzalizing t-SNE. To analyze the distilled dataset’s coverage, we visualize a t-SNE plot of the
479   distilled dataset from the DiT, MinMax Diffusion, and our method. Figure 5 illustrates that the DiT
480   distilled dataset is mostly contained in one region of the original dataset distribution, while MinMax
481   Diffusion extends to a broader area of the data distribution. However, the distilled dataset from our
482
      method covers a broader area of the data distribution than both methods.

      5   C ONCLUSION
530
531
      Dataset distillation is an important task of condensing information from large training sets. Despite
      several efforts, the distilled datasets have limited representativeness and diversity in their synthetic
532
      samples. Our proposed method using latent diffusion with mode guidance overcame this limitation
533
      and demonstrated superior performance for the dataset distillation task across various benchmarks and
534   setups. Specifically, our approach outperformed previous methods without the need for fine-tuning,
535   as evidenced by our results on ImageNette, ImageIDC, ImageNet-100, and ImageNet-1K. We also
536   analyzed and discussed the importance of different components of our method and showed their
537   utility through rigorous ablation studies. We’ve shown that our approach can be used with general
538   diffusion, like Text-to-Image Stable Diffusion, even when the training data doesn’t overlap with the
539   target dataset. In the future, we plan to extend our method to different applications where diverse
      representative samples are needed, such as continual learning.
