011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            When does a large language model (LLM) know what it does not know? Uncer-
014            tainty quantification (UQ) provides an estimate of the confidence in an LLM’s
015
               generated output and is therefore increasingly recognized as a crucial component of
               trusted AI systems. UQ is particularly important for complex generative tasks such
016
               as text-to-SQL, where an LLM helps users gain insights about data stored in noisy
017
               and large databases by translating their natural language queries to structured query
018            language (SQL). Black-box UQ methods do not require access to internal model
019            information from the generating LLM, and therefore have numerous real-world
020            advantages, such as robustness to system changes, adaptability to choice of LLM
021            (including those with commercialized APIs), reduced costs, and substantial com-
022            putational tractability. In this paper, we investigate the effectiveness of black-box
023            UQ techniques for text-to-SQL, where the consistency between a generated output
024            and other sampled generations is used as a proxy for estimating its confidence. We
025            propose a high-level non-verbalized similarity aggregation approach that is suitable
026
               for complex generative tasks, including specific techniques that train confidence
               estimation models using small training sets. Through an extensive empirical study
027
               over various text-to-SQL datasets and models, we provide recommendations for
028
               the choice of sampling technique and similarity metric. The experiments demon-
029            strate that our proposed similarity aggregation techniques result in better calibrated
030            confidence estimates as compared to the closest baselines, but also highlight how
031            there is room for improvement on downstream tasks such as selective generation.

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   The process of translating natural language queries into SQL queries is an important endeavor in natu-
037   ral language processing, particularly within enterprise contexts where users such as business analysts,
038   data engineers, and data scientists constantly query databases for data management, data analysis,
039
      and operational decision-making. Although there have been numerous advances in leveraging large
      language models (LLMs) for such tasks (Shaw et al., 2021; Gao et al., 2024a; Tai et al., 2023; Pourreza
040
      & Rafiei, 2024; Fan et al., 2024; Maamari et al., 2024), deploying text-to-SQL systems in real-world
041
      enterprise scenarios poses multifaceted challenges. For instance, the complexity of user queries in
042   enterprise databases, unclear and even cryptic schemas with heavily abbreviated and domain specific
043   column names, and data quality issues such as missing values and other inconsistencies all lead to
044   inaccuracies in generated SQL queries. These are practical issues beyond more basic ones arising
045   from the ambiguities of natural language queries and the intricacies of SQL syntax.
046
      Uncertainty quantification (UQ) approaches in machine learning provide insights into the reliability
047
      of model predictions, and can therefore be a critical component of a real-world text-to-SQL system
048   given the aforementioned challenges and complexities. In this paper, we use the term UQ to refer to
049   estimating the confidence of generations, in our case for the text-to-SQL task. Our primary goal is
050   to obtain predicted probabilities that are well calibrated, as gauged by how closely they align with
051   the empirical accuracy of the predictions (Murphy & Epstein, 1967; Dawid, 1982). Specifically,
052   we investigate the effectiveness of black-box UQ techniques for text-to-SQL, assuming access only
053   to the model being used (such as an LLM) without requiring other model information such as the
      weights or even the token log probabilities. Such techniques have numerous practical advantages,

073   Lin et al., 2024; Manakul et al., 2023; Cole et al., 2023). An open question remains however about
074   how these methods perform in estimating confidence for more complex generative tasks, involving
075   longer and more structured outputs such as SQL queries.
076
      We pursue consistency-based black-box UQ for text-to-SQL, where the idea is to use the consistency
077   between a generation and other sampled generations as a proxy for its confidence. The implicit
078   underlying assumption behind consistency-based approaches is that when a generated response
079   is more different from others, it is more likely to be incorrect, implying that responses that are
080   consistently similar are more likely to be correct (Mitchell et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023; Chen et al.,
081   2024). Figure 1 visualizes three instances from the Spider dataset (Yu et al., 2018) for the text-to-SQL
082   task, projecting semantic encodings of 30 generations for each instance while distinguishing between
083   generations deemed to be correct vs. incorrect. We observe that correct generations tend to be closer
084   to other generations, particularly other correct ones, and incorrect generations tend to be spread out
085
      more and lie on the border of or beyond correct generations.
086   Figure 2 outlines our proposed high-level procedure that aims at exploiting the afore-mentioned
087   assumption for UQ. First, multiple outputs/samples are generated by the LLM through some sampling
088   procedure. Pairwise similarities between samples can then be computed using any similarity metric of
089   choice. Finally, these similarities are leveraged to provide confidence estimates for each generation of
090   interest. Our methodological contributions are primarily in the third phase of Figure 2. In particular,
091
      rather than viewing the third phase as clustering outputs like in closely related work (Kuhn et al., 2022;
      Lin et al., 2024), we propose similarity aggregation as a framework for estimating confidences. In
092
      contrast to aggregating verbalized confidences (Xiong et al., 2024c), we aggregate pairwise similarities
093
      between generations, making our approach non-verbalized and therefore avoiding some empirically
094   observed concerns around potential overconfidence when asking LLMs for probabilities (Hu &
095   Levy, 2023; Xiong et al., 2024c). Verbalized confidence aggregation approaches are likely to
096   struggle for semantic parsing tasks since answers are not unique and generated SQLs have syntactical
097   requirements. Our consistency-based UQ methods typically provide higher confidence to generations
098   that are more similar to others in aggregate, such as those in the interior of Figure 1 rather than near
099   the boundary.
100   Note that in the illustrative example shown in Figure 2, the first two SQLs are correct but the third is
101   incorrect; this is reasonably captured in this instance by the corresponding estimated confidence of
102   6%, which is lower than the estimates of 22% and 31% for the correct SQLs. The third generated
103   SQL happens to be different from other generations, resulting in its low confidence estimate.
104

      We briefly highlight relevant related work covering the evolution of text-to-SQL and UQ approaches.
138
139   2.1   T EXT- TO -SQL
140
141
      Early work on mapping natural language utterances to a form of structured language for querying
      databases has focused on semantic parsing using either logic programs (Zelle & Mooney, 1996),
142
      λ-calculus (Zettlemoyer & Collins, 2005), or λ-DCS (Liang et al., 2013) as the underlying meaning
143
      representation. Other prominent work in natural language interfaces for databases includes PRE-
144   CISE (Popescu et al., 2003), which translates questions to SQL queries and identifies questions that
145   it is not confident about, and approaches that first generate candidate queries from a grammar and
146   then rank them using tree kernels (Giordani & Moschitti, 2012). These methods rely on high quality
147   grammars and are not suitable for tasks that require generalization to new schemas.
148
      The next phase of text-to-SQL systems considered neural sequence-to-sequence models (Iyer et al.,
149
      2017; Zhong et al., 2017), often trained using a popular cross-domain text-to-SQL benchmark called
150   Spider (Yu et al., 2018) in order to be schema independent. More modern text-to-SQL translation
151   systems rely on pre-trained LLMs and either using various forms of constrained decoding (Scholak
152   et al., 2021; Shaw et al., 2021), fine-tuning (Gao et al., 2024a), or various prompting techniques
153   around decomposition and chain-of-thought (Tai et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024b; Pourreza & Rafiei, 2024;
154   Talaei et al., 2024; Mandamadiotis et al., 2024). Several recent methods including top contributors to
155   the Spider and BIRD (Li et al., 2024a) leaderboards demonstrate that fine-tuned open models can
156   achieve sufficient performance (Gao et al., 2024a; Talaei et al., 2024; Xiong et al., 2024a).
157   There is also a growing body of literature on error detection and correction for text-to-SQL (Chen
158   et al., 2023a;b; Lee et al., 2024), as well as the use of self-consistency and related approaches for
159   voting among candidate generations (Gao et al., 2024a; Talaei et al., 2024). Several error detection
160   and concept shift detection works demonstrate the applicability of UQ for their purposes (Vazhentsev
161   et al., 2023a; Fadeeva et al., 2023). Similar attempts to use UQ methods for error parsing in text-to-
      SQL show good error coverage but low precision (Zeng et al., 2020; Li et al., 2020). These efforts are

      primarily components intended to improve the execution accuracy of a text-to-SQL system and do not

      2.2   U NCERTAINTY Q UANTIFICATION
168
169   Procedures for UQ typically estimate measures such as the variability or confidence of the LLM
170   output. These methods are either white-box or black-box, where the former category operates on the
171   premise that the internal state of the LLM including the model weights, logits, and/or embeddings are
172   accessible. In contrast, black-box methods assume that all parameters during inference are unknown,
173   allowing access only to the generations. In this case, an output’s confidence is inferred by other
174   means, such as by gauging the consistency of the output after paraphrasing the input prompt. An
175   orthogonal categorization is whether a method is verbalized or non-verbalized, where the former
176   category involves prompting an LLM to express uncertainty in natural language. This involves
177
      discerning different levels of uncertainty, such as through qualifying phrases (e.g. “I don’t know” or
      “most probably”), verbalized words (e.g. “low” or “high”), or numbers (e.g. 50% or 90%).
178
179   White-box Methods. Common approaches to estimating an LLM’s confidence include considering
180   the minimum or average token-level probabilities (logits) or entropy (Huang et al., 2023; Vazhentsev
181   et al., 2023b) coupled with a normalization mechanism to ensure consistency over outputs of different
182   lengths (Murray & Chiang, 2018). Linguistic semantics such as token-level or sentence-level relevance
183
      can also be incorporated into these schemes to yield more effective confidence estimators (Duan et al.,
      2024). Kuhn et al. (2022) propose semantic entropy based clustering on multiple samples generated
184
      from the model and then estimating confidence estimates by summing the token-level probabilities
185
      in each cluster. Kadavath et al. (2022) suggest a verbalized method where the LLM first generates
186   responses and then evaluates them as either True or False; the probability the model assigns to the
187   generated token (True or False) determines the confidence level.
188
189
      Other approaches consider the LLM’s internal state such as embeddings and activation spaces. For
      instance, Ren et al. (2023) compute embeddings for both inputs and outputs in the training data, fit
190
      them to a Gaussian distribution, and estimate the model’s confidence by computing the distance of the
191
      evaluated data pair from this Gaussian distribution. Some methods probe the model’s attention layers
192   to discriminate between correct and incorrect answers (Kadavath et al., 2022; Burns et al., 2023; Li
193   et al., 2023; Azaria & Mitchell, 2023). Although these methods provide insights into the model’s
194   linguistic understanding, they typically require supervised training on specially annotated data.
195
      Black-box Methods. One strand of research considers verbalized black-box methods, such as
196
      using an LLM to evaluate the correctness of its own generated answers in a conversational agent
197
      scenario (Mielke et al., 2022). Xiong et al. (2024c) conduct an empirical study on UQ for reasoning
198   tasks, showing that LLMs tend to be overconfident when verbalizing their own confidence in the
199   correctness of the generated answers and align poorly with the likelihood of factual correctness, which
200   may pose significant safety risks in real-world deployments of LLMs. Other related work includes
201   that of Lin et al. (2022) around fine-tuning GPT-3 to verbalize the uncertainty associated with the
202   generated answers. Analysis in Hu & Levy (2023) reveals that LLMs’ meta-linguistic judgments are
203   less reliable than quantities derived directly from the model’s token-level probabilities.
204   Another promising direction of work assumes that a model’s lack of confidence correlates with various
205   responses, often leading to hallucinatory outputs. In this case, confidence is typically estimated by
206   analysing the consistency among various responses of the model. Specifically, Manakul et al. (2023)
207   propose a simple sampling-based approach that uses consistency among generations to find potential
208   hallucinations. Lin et al. (2024) calculate the similarity matrix between generations and then estimate
209   the uncertainty based on the analysis of the similarity matrix, such as the sum of the eigen-values
210   of the graph Laplacian , the degree matrix, and the eccentricity. Recent methods have also explored
211   combining white-box and black-box approaches (Chen & Mueller, 2024; Shrivastava et al., 2023).
212   Our proposed approach falls within the black-box UQ category and relies on evaluating consistency
213   among text-to-SQL generations. Although there is some prior work that incorporates data and
214   model uncertainty for representation learning in parsing SQL (Qin et al., 2022) and other work that
215   recognizes the challenges of using model logits for text-to-SQL (Stengel-Eskin & Van Durme, 2023),
      we are not aware of prior explorations of UQ for text-to-SQL that only rely on model API access.

      3     M ETHODOLOGY
217
218
      Suppose an LLM generates output y for some input x. We assume there is an associated ground truth
219   output y ∗ for input x as well as a binary reward r ∈ {0, 1} from a reward function r(x, y, y ∗ ). We
220   use execution accuracy as the performance measure for the text-to-SQL task considered here, where
221   reward r = 1 if the generated and ground truth queries return the same result upon query execution
222   on the underlying database. For other tasks such as open-ended QA, the reward could be 1 based
223   on whether a text similarity metric (e.g. ROUGE) between the ground truth and generated output
224   exceeds some predetermined threshold (Kuhn et al., 2022; Lin et al., 2024).
225   In this work, we propose an overarching framework with specific techniques that provide confidence
226   estimates, possibly using a limited amount of training data. We denote the confidence of a generation y
227   for input x as c(x, y) and interpret it as the probability that it is correct, i.e. c(x, y) = P (r(x, y, y ∗ ) =
228   1) = P (y ∈ Y ∗ (x)), where Y ∗ (x) is the set of responses with reward 1. In the remainder of this
229   section, we describe the components of the workflow depicted in Figure 2 for estimating c(x, y).
230
231   3.1   S AMPLING
232
233   Consistency-based approaches begin with the generation of multiple samples/generations y1 , · · · , ym
234   for an input x. We explore three potential ways to generate samples:
235   • Standard sampling is when tokens are generated by sampling from the next-token probability
236     distribution of the LLM at a fixed temperature, therefore enabling variable generations.
237   • Temperature sampling occurs when each sample is generated from a different temperature, thereby
238     potentially further increasing the variability of generations.
239   • Hybrid sampling is a combination of the above methods, where multiple samples are generated
240     from multiple temperatures.
241   While there are other means of generating diverse samples (Gao et al., 2024b), including approaches
242   in text-to-SQL systems that modify the prompts (Bhaskar et al., 2023; Lee et al., 2024), our focus
243   is primarily on leveraging temperature (Zhu et al., 2024); this provides sufficient variability, which
244   is needed by consistency-based methods to help distinguish correct from incorrect responses in
245   complex generations such as SQL. In practical usage, one may be interested in confidence estimates
246   for only a subset of the generations, such as the ones most likely to be correct. Generations at higher
247   temperatures could therefore potentially be used merely for obtaining better confidence estimates for
248   those at lower temperatures.
249
250   3.2   C OMPUTING PAIRWISE S IMILARITIES
251
252
      After generating samples, consistency-based approaches rely on access to a similarity metric with
      which one can compute pairwise similarities s(yi , yj ) for all sample pairs. For our experiments, we
253
      consider two types of similarity metrics, all lying in the interval [0, 1]:
254
255   • Token/text metrics: We consider metrics that treat the samples as general text or sets of tokens, such
256     as the Jaccard coefficient, variations of ROUGE metrics such as Rouge-1 and Rouge-L, and the
257     cosine similarity between sentence BERT (sbert) (Reimers & Gurevych, 2019) representations of
258
        the generations.
      • SQL metrics: We also consider similarity metrics specific to SQL queries, such as the binary
259
        metric of whether two generations belong to the same SQL output type among 3 categories
260
        (simple/join/nested) (Pourreza & Rafiei, 2024), as well as those that rely on parsing the SQL and
261     comparing the contents of various clauses – Aligon (Aligon et al., 2014), Aouiche (Aouiche et al.,
262     2006), and Makiyama (Makiyama et al., 2015). Makiyama has been shown to perform well among
263     these on a query clustering task (Tang et al., 2022).
264
265   3.3   S IMILARITY AGGREGATION
266
267   The final phase of the pipeline relies on leveraging pairwise similarities for UQ. Recall that the
268   underlying assumption behind consistency-based approaches is that correct generations are more
269   similar to other generations than incorrect ones. Computing an aggregated similarity between a
      particular generation and other generations therefore acts as a proxy for correctness.

      We present a simple yet broad perspective on consistency-based approaches that is applicable to
271   any generative task. Rather than clustering generations such as around semantic equivalence (Kuhn
272   et al., 2022), the confidence for sample yi can be estimated using a suitable aggregation function,
273   ci = f (s1 , · · · , sm ), where sk is the similarity between samples yi and yk . A deterministic function
274   f (·) implies that identical generations yield identical confidences, for the same sample set, which in
275   our view is a desirable property.
276
      The choice of aggregation function should reflect the underlying hypothesis around consistency-based
277   methods, which is that more consistency is expected for correct answers. We highlight and propose
278   the following 3 categories for choosing aggregation function f (·).
279
280
      Simple Aggregation. A simple approach is to find an aggregate distance between yi and other
      generations, d¯ = g(d1 , · · · , dm ) where distance dk = 1 − sk , and compute ci = 1 − d¯ since the
281
      aggregate distance lies in [0, 1]. The rationale is that the consistency hypothesis suggests that a
282
      generation further removed from others is more likely to be incorrect. While any form of aggregation
283   g(·) is possible, we use the arithmetic mean for experiments, for which the estimation simplifies to
284          1
               P

285   degree approach in Lin et al. (2024) and therefore treated as a baseline for most experiments. We
286   show later that this performs reasonably well on some (but not all) UQ metrics.
287
      Bayesian Aggregation. We propose a Bayesian form of aggregation that updates beliefs about
288
      confidence using similarities. Specifically, we compute the posterior probability of generation yi
289   being correct, given the evidence from similarities with respect to other generations:

340   Evaluation Metrics. Our main objective is to estimate well-calibrated confidences for SQL
341   generations, i.e. probabilities that match empirical observations. Calibration help in enabling trust
342   in a system’s generations, for the benefit of both system builders as well as end users. There is
343   significant discussion about the limitations of various calibration metrics in the literature (Nixon
344   et al., 2019; Xiong et al., 2024b); in this work, we choose adaptive calibrated error (ACE), which
345   bins confidence estimates into probability ranges such that each bin contains the same number of
                                                               1
                                                                  PK PB
346   data points (Nixon et al., 2019). Formally, ACE = KB          k=1    b=1 |acc(b, k) − c(b, k)|, where
347   acc(b, k) and c(b, k) are the accuracy and confidence of adaptive calibration bin b for class label k.

      similar confidence estimates for various generations. Furthermore, confidences from some techniques

354   for the correctness of generations. When confidences are used for selective generation (El-Yaniv
355
      et al., 2010; Kamath et al., 2020), i.e. for rejecting a fraction of the instances that we are the least
      confident about, then the Area Under the Accuracy Rejection Curve (AUARC) is a suitable metric,
356
      as it computes the area under the curve of the fraction of rejected instances vs. accuracy on the
357
      non-rejected instances (Nadeem et al., 2009).

365   5.1      E FFECT OF S AMPLING T ECHNIQUE
366
      We explore the effect of choice of sampling technique using generations from a few-shot codellama

521   6     C ONCLUSION
522
523   We present the first investigation around black-box UQ for the text-to-SQL task, where the consistency
524   between a generation of interest and others is used as a proxy for our confidence in that generation.
525
      Specifically, we propose a general high-level similarity aggregation framework for UQ using pairwise
      similarities between multiple generated samples, as well as specific approaches within that framework.
526
      Our framework is quite general and can accommodate any similarity measure. We consider both
527
      text/token-level similarities such as the Jaccard coefficient and various ROUGE metrics as well
528   as SQL specific similarities that exploit the clauses of the query. Through an extensive empirical
529   evaluation using popular text-to-SQL benchmarks such as Spider and BIRD as well as state-of-the-art
530   open-source code LLMs, we show that text/token-based similarity metrics such as Jaccard and
531   Rouge-L are suitable for text-to-SQL UQ, and that the proposed similarity aggregation methods result
532   in well-calibrated confidence estimates as measured by the adaptive calibration error. The proposed
533   methods also generalize well to tasks such as question answering.
534   A limitation of some of our proposed methods is that they require a small training set where samples
535   are generated in the same manner across training and testing. Also, although results show moderate
536   gains over existing approaches on AUROC and AUARC, there is room for improvement when using
537   these confidences to distinguish between correct and incorrect responses. A challenge with such
538   consistency-based methods is that there are no guarantees for individual instances, and that their
539   performance is task and model dependent. Further studies are needed to understand fundamental
      limitations of consistency-based UQ for generative tasks.
