009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               Tasks requiring deductive reasoning, especially those involving multiple steps, of-
012            ten demand adaptive strategies such as intermediate generation of rationales or
013            programs, as no single approach is universally optimal. While Language Mod-
014            els (LMs) can enhance their outputs through iterative self-refinement and strategy
015            adjustments, they frequently fail to apply the most effective strategy in their first
016            attempt. This inefficiency raises the question: Can LMs learn to select the optimal
017            strategy in the first attempt, without a need for refinement? To address this chal-
018            lenge, we introduce SMART (Self-learning Meta-strategy Agent for Reasoning
019            Tasks), a novel framework that enables LMs to autonomously learn and select the
020
               most effective strategies for various reasoning tasks. We model the strategy selec-
               tion process as a Markov Decision Process and leverage reinforcement learning-
021
               driven continuous self-improvement to allow the model to find the suitable strat-
022
               egy to solve a given task. Unlike traditional self-refinement methods that rely on
023            multiple inference passes or external feedback, SMART allows an LM to internal-
024            ize the outcomes of its own reasoning processes and adjust its strategy accordingly,
025            aiming for correct solutions on the first attempt. Our experiments across various
026            reasoning datasets and with different model architectures demonstrate that SMART
027            significantly enhances the ability of models to choose optimal strategies without
028            external guidance (+15 points on the GSM8K dataset). By achieving higher accu-
029            racy with a single inference pass, SMART not only improves performance but also
030            reduces computational costs for refinement-based strategies, paving the way for
031
               more efficient and intelligent reasoning in LMs.

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   When people first encounter complex reasoning tasks, such as solving mathematical problems, they
037   often make mistakes or approach them inefficiently (Brown et al., 2019). However, with experience,
038   humans tend to improve their performance by replacing ineffective or incorrect strategies with more
039   effective ones, using a mix of strategies tailored to the specific task (Adolph et al., 1998; Lemaire &
040   Callies, 2009; Torbeyns et al., 2009; Boncoddo et al., 2010, inter alia).
041
      Language Models (LMs) similarly struggle with reasoning tasks, sometimes producing incoherent
042   results (Madaan et al., 2023; Shridhar et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2024). A common remedy is to
043   resample the output, a process known as refinement. This refinement may involve reusing the same
044   reasoning approach (Madaan et al., 2023) or adopting an entirely new one (Shridhar et al., 2023).
045   In addition, providing feedback on initial results has proven beneficial during resampling (Huang
046   et al., 2024; Welleck et al., 2022; Shinn et al., 2024; Kim et al., 2024, inter alia). This raises a
047   critical question: Can LMs be taught to optimize their choice of reasoning strategy for specific tasks
048   overtime on the first trial, much like humans do?
049   To address this question, we propose a novel framework called SMART (Self-learning Meta-strategy
050   Agent for Reasoning Tasks), which allows LMs to learn optimal strategy selection through a con-
051   tinuous self-learning approach. We model the task of identifying the optimal strategy as a Markov
052   Decision Process (MDP) (Sutton et al., 1999; Puterman, 2014), where the agent (LM) starts with
053   its pre-trained knowledge and iteratively improves its performance by learning from its own outputs
      and strategy choices. By integrating the LM’s reasoning abilities with reinforcement learning-driven

099

      Let q be a problem best solved with multi-step reasoning. An example is presented in the form
101
      of a mathematical word problem in Figure 1. An agent or language model (LM) can approach it
102   using various strategies, such as solving it step by step (Chain of Thought, CoT Wei et al. (2022)),
103   decomposing it into subproblems, and solving each one (Least to Most, L2M Zhou et al. (2023)),
104   or writing a program to solve it programmatically (Program of Thought, PoT Chen et al. (2023)),
105   among others. A common method is to prompt the LM with a specific strategy for solving the
106   task. However, LMs are error-prone but can fix their answers when asked to do so, a process called
107   refinement. In the refinement process, LMs can either stick to the same strategy Madaan et al.
      (2023) or switch to a more effective reasoning strategy Shridhar et al. (2023). Ideally, LMs could

      learn to choose the best strategy and reasoning path on the first try, minimizing the need for costly
109   refinement.
110
111   Our objective: The primary goal of our work is to enable language models (LMs) to autonomously
112
      learn and select the most effective strategies for various reasoning tasks on their first attempt, thereby
      improving both efficiency and accuracy. Unlike traditional self-refinement methods that require
113
      multiple inference passes or external feedback, our approach aims to internalize the learning process
114
      within the LM, allowing it to adjust its strategy selection based on past experience. This mirrors how
115   humans learn to choose optimal strategies through experience when faced with complex tasks.

118   2.1   SMART: S ELF - LEARNING M ETA - STRATEGY AGENT FOR R EASONING TASKS
119
120   We model the strategy selection process as a Markov Decision Process (MDP), where the LM acts
121   as an agent that interacts with the environment (the reasoning tasks) by selecting strategies and
122   observing the outcomes. Using reinforcement learning techniques, the LM can learn a policy that
123   maximizes the expected reward, effectively learning to choose the optimal strategy for each task.
124
      This framework allows the LM to simulate different reasoning strategies, evaluate their effectiveness
      based on past outcomes, and adjust its strategy choice accordingly.
125
126   In the following sections, we formalize the problem formulation, define the agent’s policy, and
127   describe the learning objective. We then present our two-stage process with iterative refinement,
128   which allows the agent to learn from its own reasoning processes and improve its strategy choice
129   over time.
130   MDP Setup: We model the strategy selection framework as a Markov Decision Process (MDP)
131   given by the tuple ⟨S, A, P, R, µ⟩. Here, S represents the state space encapsulating all possible
132   states of the environment, where each state s ∈ S is the current problem statement or the subsequent
133   LM response to it. The initial state distribution is given by µ. The action space, A, is the set of all
134   strategies available to the agent, where each action at ∈ A corresponds to the choice of a particular
135   strategy at time t. The transition function P : S × A → S defines the probability of transitioning
136
      to a new state st+1 after applying strategy at in state st . Particularly for our case, the transition
      function is non-deterministic as the next state is sampled by the agent (see Algorithm 1 later). The
137
      reward function R : S × A → R assigns a scalar reward based on the correctness of the result after
138
      applying the chosen strategy.
139
140   We start with the initial sampling step, where the LM chooses a strategy to solve the given task. In
141   other words, given a problem statement s1 ∼ µ, the agent draws a strategy a1 to solve the problem
142
      according to its policy (see below). Given the initial problem s1 and strategy a1 , the environment
      transitions to the next state s2 ∼ P(·|s1 , a1 ) with transition probability P and receives a reward r1
143
      indicating the correctness of the response. If a correct strategy is chosen and the LM solves the task
144
      using that strategy, the process terminates. Otherwise, the agent chooses another strategy a2 to solve
145   the problem, and the process  repeats until the problem       is solved. A realization of this stochastic

      3    E XPERIMENTAL D ETAILS
271
272
      SMART operates within a self-learn framework, where we prompt a pre-trained model with 8-shot
273
      examples to collect the initial training data. Prompts used for various strategies are provided in
274   Subsection 8.1. The 8-shot pre-trained model also serves as a baseline comparison for our method.
275   We use the MetaMath dataset Yu et al. (2024), a variant of the GSM8K Cobbe et al. (2021) training
276   set, which contains 110K reasoning problems. We evaluate our methodology on the GSM8K test
277   set, which contains 1,319 samples. To show the generalization ability of our method, we also test on
278   two out-of-distribution datasets: the SVAMP dataset Patel et al. (2021a) with 1,000 samples where
279   the questions are made more challenging by altering them in a non-trivial way, and the ASDiv
280   dataset Miao et al. (2020) with 2,300 samples, which consists of diverse mathematical problems
281   from elementary to middle school.
282   We ran all our experiments on models with 7-8 billion parameters, specifically Gemma 7B Team
283   et al. (2024), Mistral 7B Jiang et al. (2023), Qwen2 7B Yang et al. (2024), and Llama3 8B Dubey
284   et al. (2024). This is important for our self-learn setup, as the model needs a basic understanding of
285   the task to begin the process. Smaller models often have difficulty starting the process, while very
286   large models are too expensive to iterate over multiple times.
287   We employed three reasoning strategies: Chain of Thought (CoT) (Wei et al., 2022), Least to Most
288   (L2M) (Zhou et al., 2023), and Program of Thought (PoT) (Chen et al., 2023) in our work due to
289   their effectiveness on the multi-step reasoning tasks. We take the best out of these strategies as
290   our baseline (underlined in Table 1). Since we used a different reasoning strategy during refinement
291   following Shridhar et al. (2023), the majority of our experiments are limited to two trajectories as the
292   third trajectory would make the remaining strategy the obvious choice. However, we also explore
293   our approach beyond 3 strategies later (in Section 5). We report the top-1 accuracy (maj@1) for all
294
      experiments, as we want to test the accuracy of the output on the first try. We used a temperature of
      0.7 to generate samples at each iteration. Since the models are already trained on the downstream
295
      datasets during pre-training, we chose to train only part of the model and used LoRA (Hu et al.,
296
      2021) with rank 16, alpha 32, and a starting learning rate of 2e-4 with decay. We used the Unsloth
297   library (Unslothai, 2023) for training and we did the inference using the VLLM library (Kwon et al.,
298   2023). Finally, we run SMART for multiple iterations until our accuracy gains no longer justify
299   the cost of training and sampling. In our experiments, 3 iterations worked well for most models,
300   although we trained Gemma 7B for 5 iterations before performance plateaued.

408   How the strategy distribution changes over iterations: With SMART, the goal is to select the
409   desired strategy at the first attempt, i.e., over iterations the LM should learn to select the appropriate
410   strategy for a given task. Figure 2 shows the changes in strategy distribution over iterations for the
411
      Gemma 7B model on the GSM8K dataset. As indicated by the baseline in Table 1, PoT emerges as
      the best strategy for the Gemma 7B model, followed by CoT, with L2M being the least effective.
412
      A similar pattern is observed in Figure 2, where the model increasingly favors PoT (indicated by
413
      the upward trend in the red line) while decreasing its preference for the other two strategies. Cor-
414   respondingly, the accuracy for PoT improves the most, followed by CoT and L2M, demonstrating
415   that over iterations the model learns to select the optimal strategy for a given task. A qualitative
416   example where over iterations the model learned to choose the right strategy in its first attempt is
417   provided in Figure 4. Initially, the model chose the wrong strategy but was fixed during refinement,
418   and over iterations, the model picked the correct strategy in its initial sampling, without the need for
419   refinement.
420   Extending the strategies beyond three strategies in SMART: We want to test if it is possible to
421   go beyond the three strategies explored in this paper by extending our approach to other strategies.
422   Although we could not find a strategy with performance comparable to those explored in this paper,
423   we introduced a <Unsolvable> tag for questions that the model could not answer during either
424   sampling or refinement attempts and tried to use it as a fourth strategy. However, extending this strat-
425   egy did not yield promising results due to the limited number of samples in the <Unsolvable>
426   category, as the model was able to solve the task using one of the strategies during refinement. Sim-
427   ilarly, we experimented with a <Answer Only> strategy, where the model directly predicts the
428
      answer to very simple questions without any intermediate reasoning. As with the <Unsolvable>
      strategy, the skewed data distribution led to poorer performance.

513
514   Self-Training in LLMs: Self-training is a semi-supervised learning method in which the model’s
515   own predictions are used as additional data to improve its performance (Scudder, 1965; Yarowsky,
516   1995). This technique has been applied to various NLP tasks, such as machine translation (He et al.,
517   2020; Sun et al., 2021; Gulcehre et al., 2023). In contrast, we use self-learning principles to generate
518
      new data and continually update the policy in an on-policy fashion. This approach can be viewed as
      an on-policy counterpart to Self Imitation Learning (Oh et al., 2018), where the policy learns from
519
      prospective successful trajectories in its initial stages, rather than imitating past successful behavior.

522   7    C ONCLUSION
523
524   We present SMART: Self-learning Meta-strategy Agent for Reasoning Tasks, a solution to the chal-
525   lenges LMs face in selecting strategies for complex reasoning tasks. By modeling the strategy
526   selection process as a Markov decision process and leveraging reinforcement learning, SMART en-
527
      ables LMs to autonomously learn and apply the most effective reasoning strategies on the first trial,
      thereby reducing the reliance on iterative self-refinement. Our proposed approach not only improves
528
      the accuracy of LMs, as demonstrated by significant performance gains across multiple datasets but
529
      also enhances computational efficiency by minimizing the need for multiple inference passes.
