                                                         A BSTRACT
027
028
                      Large Multimodal Models (LMMs) have shown remarkable progress in medical
029
                      Visual Question Answering (Med-VQA), achieving high accuracy on existing
030
                      benchmarks. However, their reliability under robust evaluation is questionable.
031                   This study reveals that state-of-the-art models perform worse than random guessing
032                   on medical diagnosis questions when subjected to simple probing evaluation. To
033                   address this critical evaluation problem, we introduce the Probing Evaluation for
034                   Medical Diagnosis (ProbMed) dataset to rigorously assess LMM performance in
035                   medical imaging through probing evaluation and procedural diagnosis. Particularly,
036                   probing evaluation features pairing original questions with negation questions
037                   with hallucinated attributes, while procedural diagnosis requires reasoning across
038                   various diagnostic dimensions for each image, including modality recognition,
039
                      organ identification, clinical findings, abnormalities, and positional grounding. Our
                      evaluation reveals that top-performing models like GPT-4o, GPT-4V and Gemini
040
                      Pro perform worse than random guessing on specialized diagnostic questions,
041
                      indicating significant limitations in handling fine-grained medical inquiries. We
042                   further investigate the underperformance of open-source models (e.g., LLaVA,
043                   LLaVA-Med, and Med-Flamingo) through an ablation study. This study reveals
044                   that poor visual understanding is a primary bottleneck, which can be mitigated by
045                   adding visual descriptions generated by GPT-4o, leading to an average performance
046                   improvement of 9.44%. These findings underscore the urgent need for more robust
047                   evaluation methods and domain-specific expertise to ensure LMM reliability in
048                   critical medical fields.

      2.1      L ARGE M ULTIMODAL M ODELS IN THE M EDICAL D OMAIN
124
125   The advancements in Large Multimodal Models (LMMs) have significantly enhanced the understand-
126   ing and generation of medical content that integrates both visual and linguistic elements. Notable
127   models include GPT-4V (?), Gemini Pro (Team et al., 2023), LLaVA (Liu et al., 2023a; 2024),
128   and MiniGPT-v2 (Chen et al., 2023). The scalability and exceptional performance of these large
129   foundation models have driven their application in the biomedical field.
130   Further progress has been made in fine-tuning general-domain LMMs for the biomedical field,
131   resulting in specialized models like BiomedGPT (Zhang et al., 2024), LLaVA-Med (Li et al., 2024),
132   Med-Flamingo (Moor et al., 2023), MedBLIP (?), RadFM (Wu et al., 2023b) and MedVInT (Zhang
133   et al., 2023). Despite the promising results from these domain-specific LMMs, ongoing exploration
134   exists into training smaller multimodal models to address specific clinical needs. For instance, models
135   like LLaVA-RAD (Chaves et al., 2024) and CheXagent (Chen et al., 2024) have been developed for
136
      chest X-ray interpretation, aiming to bridge competency gaps in radiology tasks.
137   Comprehensive surveys of LLMs for healthcare highlight the progress, applications, and challenges
138   in deploying LLMs in clinical settings (He et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2024; Peng et al., 2023). Task-
139   specific evaluations (Yan et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023b) underline the potential and challenges of
140   LMMs in the medical domain. As we move towards integrating these models into critical decision-
141   making processes, it becomes imperative to assess their reliability in high-stakes environments like
142
      healthcare and medicine.
143
144   2.2      M EDICAL V ISUAL Q UESTION A NSWERING
145
      Medical Visual Question Answering (Med-VQA) plays a crucial role in assessing the capabilities
146
      of models in interpreting and responding to queries about medical images. Some benchmarks, like
147   VQA-RAD (Lau et al., 2018) and SLAKE (Liu et al., 2021), are manually constructed with categorical
148   question types. While this method ensures high-quality question-answer pairs, it is labor-intensive
149   and results in limited dataset scales.
150
      Automated curation methods have been developed to address scalability. PathVQA (He et al., 2020)
151
      uses CoreNLP1 tools, and PMC-VQA (Zhang et al., 2023) employs generative models to create
152
      larger datasets. However, these methods often sacrifice fine-grained question categories, and some
153   require additionally trained models for question filtering. ProbMed, as shown in Table 1, stands out
154   by providing large-scale benchmarks and enabling categorical accuracy assessments across various
155   diagnostic dimensions for each image, including modality recognition, organ recognition, clinical
156   findings identification, and positional grounding. ProbMed uniquely incorporates adversarial negation
157   pairs for each question-answer pair to ensure diagnostic specificity and reliability, setting it apart
158   from existing benchmarks.
159   Different evaluation methods are employed for assessing LMMs, including closed-ended VQA,
160   multiple choice VQA, and open-ended generation tasks such as captioning and report generation.

      3     P ROB M ED : P ROBING E VALUATION FOR M EDICAL D IAGNOSIS
186
187
      In this section, we design two evaluation principles and present a comprehensive analysis on state-of-
188   the-art LMMs for Med-VQA using the created ProbMed dataset to address two research questions:
189
190         1. Is the current evaluation of LMMs for Med-VQA reliable?
191
            2. How reliable are LMMs on medical diagnosis, ranging from general questions to specialized
192
               diagnostic questions?
193
194   Our primary goal is to rigorously evaluate these models’ readiness for real-life diagnostic tasks,
195   particularly under adversarial conditions. Despite their high accuracy on existing benchmarks, the
196   models struggle with simple probing evaluation. ProbMed is designed to expose these vulnerabilities
197   and provide a more reliable assessment of model performance in real-world scenarios. Additionally,
198   by incorporating new experimental settings, including chain-of-thought reasoning and the use of
199   external visual descriptions from GPT-4o, we aim to explore how model accuracy can be enhanced in
200
      critical medical tasks.
201
202   3.1   P ROBING E VALUATION WITH A DVERSARIAL PAIRS
203
      One of the main motivations behind ProbMed is to assess the models’ ability to accurately distinguish
204
      between relevant and irrelevant features. ProbMed pairs original questions with negation questions
205
      containing hallucinated attributes. This method challenges the model’s robustness by requiring them
206   to identify actual conditions while disregarding false, hallucinated ones. For instance, a question
207   about a specific finding is paired with a negated question featuring a different, non-existent finding to
208   test if the model can exclusively identify the factual finding.
209
210
      3.2   P ROCEDURAL D IAGNOSIS
211
212   To ensure a comprehensive evaluation, ProbMed includes questions that require reasoning across
213   multiple diagnostic dimensions for each image. These dimensions include modality recognition,
214   organ identification, clinical findings, abnormalities, and positional reasoning. This multifaceted
215   approach assesses a model’s diagnostic capabilities beyond simple question-answer pairs, requiring it
      to integrate various pieces of information to form a coherent diagnostic picture.

243   3.3    DATA C URATION
244
245   As illustrated in Figure 3, ProbMed draws from two comprehensive biomedical datasets MedICaT
246   and ChestX-ray14 to compile a diverse set of 6,303 images. MedICaT (Subramanian et al., 2020)
247   contains 217k image-caption pairs from 131k open-access biomedical papers. From this dataset, we
248   selected 4,543 image-caption pairs focusing on a single organ and modality with clear indications
249
      of normal or abnormal conditions. These images span three modalities (X-ray, MRI, and CT scan)
      and four organs (abdomen, brain, chest, and spine). ChestX-ray14 (Wang et al., 2017) comprises
250
      112k frontal-view X-ray images from 30k unique patients, including 880 images with abnormalities
251
      marked by bounding boxes. We selected 1,760 images, balanced between healthy and abnormal
252   cases, with disease labels and bounding box annotations.
253
254   We generated metadata for each image to create high-quality, balanced question-answer pairs. For
255
      MedICaT images, GPT-4 (Achiam et al., 2023) was used to analyze captions, identify abnormalities,
      and extract positional descriptions using few-shot prompting (Prompt details in Appendix D). For
256
      ChestX-ray14 images, a positional reasoning module generated textual descriptors of abnormalities
257
      based on bounding boxes and image sizes. This metadata included unique condition names and
258   positional descriptions for each organ-modality combination, serving as the basis for creating both
259   ground-truth and adversarial question-answer pairs. Ground-truth questions were answered with
260   "yes," while corresponding adversarial questions were created by selecting random entities - such
261   as alternative organs or modalities and hallucinated conditions, and assigning "no" answers, see
262   Appendix B for the detailed number of questions within each category.
263
      A comprehensive verification process was carried out to ensure the accuracy of the metadata and
264   corresponding QA pairs. A U.S. medical postdoc and a medical graduate student were hired to review
265   100 randomly sampled metadata entries from a total of 6,303 images and 1,090 QA pairs. The review
266   yielded an average accuracy of 94% for the metadata and 97.79% for the QA pairs. This meticulous
267   verification process highlights the reliability and thorough curation of ProbMed. As shown in Table 2,
268   the data curation process yielded 57,132 question-answer pairs, averaging 9 pairs per image, covering
269   a wide range of diagnostic dimensions. These high-quality, balanced pairs provide a solid foundation
      for testing models in challenging real-life medical scenarios.

291   We conducted a systematic evaluation and comprehensive analysis using the ProbMed dataset
292   on twelve state-of-the-art LMMs to identify their strengths and weaknesses in real-life imaging

298   them practical for integration into medical practice. For a robust evaluation, accuracy was determined
299   by requiring the models to correctly identify actual conditions while ignoring false, hallucinated ones.
300   Additionally, categorical accuracy was calculated by considering a hit only when the model correctly

306   To address this first research question, we introduced adversarial pairs to the evaluation process to test
307   the model’s robustness and reliability. This strategy ensures that models must validate the absence
308
      of certain characteristics or findings rather than simply acknowledge existing conditions, thereby
      enhancing diagnostic specificity and reliability. To demonstrate the necessity of adversarial pairs for
309
      achieving valid and trustworthy accuracy scores in Med-VQA, we conduct an experimental analysis
310
      on the test set of an existing medical dataset, VQA-RAD (Lau et al., 2018), in addition to ProbMed.

320
      adversarial question had to be negated. This process resulted in 236 question-answer pairs in total.

321

      4.3.1    I MPACT OF C HAIN - OF -T HOUGHT P ROMPTING AND V ISUAL U NDERSTANDING ON
454            M ODEL P ERFORMANCE
455
456   To further investigate the underperformance of open-source models, we conducted an extensive
457   ablation study on LLaVA-v1, LLaVA-v1.6, LLaVA-Med-v1, LLaVA-Med-v1.5, Med-Flamingo, and
458   GPT-4o. In this study, we examined two additional experimental settings: (1) applying a chain-of-
459
      thought (CoT) approach where models first generate visual descriptions from the image, which are
      then used to augment the prompt along with the question, (2) enhancing the models by providing
460
      external visual descriptions generated by GPT-4o in addition to the question.
461
462   As shown in Figure 4, employing the chain-of-thought approach alone - without external visual
463   descriptions - resulted in an average accuracy increase of 6.51%. In particular, LLaVA-Med-v1.5’s
464   accuracy improved from 40.19% to 54.55%, closing the gap to within 1.05% of the vanilla GPT-4o
465
      model. Interestingly, GPT-4o’s performance decreased by 3.55% when the CoT mechanism was
      applied, potentially indicating that the model already internally employs its own chain-of-thought
466
      process.
467
468   Notably, all open-source models exhibited improved performance when augmented with visual
469   descriptions generated by GPT-4o, suggesting that their baseline limitations stem primarily from
470   poor visual comprehension. On average, these models showed an accuracy improvement of 9.44%
471
      across all question categories. This observation suggests that poor visual understanding is a major
      limitation of existing models, and augmenting them with external visual reasoning can lead to notable
472
      gains. Detailed performance changes of each model, organized by question category, can be found in
473
      Appendix C.

525   5          C ONCLUSION
526
527   Evaluating the reliability of LMMs in the medical domain requires robust methods, and ProbMed, our
528   newly introduced dataset, addresses this by incorporating probing evaluation and procedural diagnosis.
529   Our study reveals significant limitations in models like GPT-4o and Gemini Pro, which perform
530   worse than random guessing on specialized diagnostic questions, while CheXagent’s results highlight
531
      the critical importance of domain-specific knowledge. Furthermore, our additional experiments,
      which introduced chain-of-thought reasoning and external visual descriptions generated by GPT-4o,
532
      suggested that poor visual understanding is a major limitation of existing models and augmenting
533
      them with external visual reasoning can lead to notable gains. Despite the contributions, limitations
534   such as the imbalanced image distribution favoring Chest X-rays (see Table 2) and the absence of
535   open-ended evaluations, such as report generation, remain. The broader impact of our work includes
536   the potential for improved diagnostic accuracy and better patient care, but it also highlights the
537   risks of deploying unreliable models in healthcare. We recommend rigorous testing, continuous
538   performance monitoring, and the incorporation of domain-specific expertise to mitigate these risks.
539   Ultimately, our work aims to contribute to the development of trustworthy AI systems in healthcare,
      advancing diagnostic outcomes and patient safety.

1460
       The following is the prompt used for extracting medical conditions and their locations from image
1461   captions:
1462
1463
       You are a helpful assistant and you are given a caption describing
           a medical image. Extract medical conditions and diseases,
1464
          along with their locations, if specified. Do not include any
1465
          information that cannot be directly inferred from the image,
1466      for example, patient status or patient history. Outputs should
1467       be in the format: "<condition/disease1> : <location1>, <
1468      condition/disease2> : <location2>...". The term "<location>"
1469      should include at least one positional descriptor and should
1470      be explicit in the original caption along with the condition/
1471      disease. Otherwise, it should be replaced with "None".
1472
1473   For example, consider the caption: "Fig. 1. MRI abdomen and pelvis
1474
           showing the cervical mass." The output should be "<cervical
          mass> : None". For the caption: "Chest radiograph shows
1475
          enlargement of the hilar mass with spread into the left lower
1476
          lobe." The output should be "<enlargement of the hilar mass> :
1477       <left lower lobe>". Similarly, for the caption: "Abdominal CT
1478       scan reveals an enhancing rounded pseudo-aneurysm in the
1479      cystic artery, alongside high-density material within the
1480      gallbladder’s lumen and near the gastrohepatic ligament." The
1481      correct output is "<enhancing rounded pseudo-aneurysm> : <
1482      cystic artery>, <high-density material> : <lumen of the
1483      gallbladder and region of the gastrohepatic ligament>".
