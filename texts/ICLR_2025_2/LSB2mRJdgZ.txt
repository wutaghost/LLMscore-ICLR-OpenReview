011                                                       A BSTRACT
012
013                In a systematic way, we investigate a widely asked question: Do LLMs really un-
014                derstand what they say?, which relates to the more familiar term Stochastic Parrot.
015                To this end, we propose a summative assessment over a carefully designed physical
016                concept understanding task, P HYSI C O. Our task alleviates the memorization issue
017
                   via the usage of grid-format inputs that abstractly describe physical phenomena.
                   The grids represents varying levels of understanding, from the core phenomenon,
018
                   application examples to analogies to other abstract patterns in the grid world. A
019
                   comprehensive study on our task demonstrates that: (1) state-of-the-art LLMs lag
020                behind humans by ∼40%; (2) the stochastic parrot phenomenon is present in LLMs,
021                as they fail on our grid task but can describe and recognize the same concepts well
022                in natural language; (3) our task challenges the LLMs due to intrinsic difficulties
023                rather than the unfamiliar grid format, as in-context learning and fine-tuning on
024                same formatted data added little to their performance. Our data is released (see
025                Supplementary Material in the submission) for public research.

028   1       I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Recent years have witnessed remarkable advancements in large language models (LLMs) (Brown
031   et al., 2020; Achiam et al., 2023; Team et al., 2023). Thanks to the substantial model capacity and
032   massive training data, LLMs have achieved new state-of-the-arts on a variety of NLP tasks, even
033
      surpassing humans on some of them (Min et al., 2023; Chang et al., 2024). Nowadays the application
      of LLMs has become widespread, facilitating daily work and life, and profoundly influencing people’s
034
      work and lifestyles (Bommasani et al., 2021; Peng et al., 2024; Demszky et al., 2023).
035
036   On the other hand, despite the great success of LLMs, many researchers argue that LLMs may not
037   really understand what they claim they do (Bender & Koller, 2020; Bender et al., 2021; Bommasani
038   et al., 2021; Mitchell & Krakauer, 2023) due to their strong memorization ability. In particular,
039
      Bender et al. (2021) questioned whether LLMs are just Stochastic Parrots that repeat words based on
      correlations without true understanding. This argument has been acknowledged by many research
040
      papers and dozens of them even include this term in their titles.1 Unfortunately, to our best knowledge,
041
      there are no quantitative experiments to verify the stochastic parrot phenomenon in LLMs. Existing
042   studies indicate that LLMs may fail on one particular challenging task (Chakrabarty et al., 2022;
043   Shapira et al., 2023; Hessel et al., 2023; Tong et al., 2024), but they do not demonstrate that LLMs
044   claimed to understand those tasks by providing a controlled and paired evidence.
045
      This paper aims to provide quantitative evidence to validate the argument of stochastic parrot in
046
      LLMs. To this end, from the perspective of educational and cognitive psychology, we first employ the
047
      approach of summative assessment (Black & Wiliam, 1998a;b) to measure understanding in LLMs.
048   Its key idea is to design various tasks that test different understanding levels regarding a specific
049   concept. Following the principle of Bloom’s taxonomy (Armstrong, 2010; Krathwohl, 2002), we
050   design tasks that reflect different levels of understanding. Consequently, we develop P HYSI C O, a
051   task designed to assess understanding of basic physical concepts from high school such as Gravity.
052   Our focus on physical concepts stems from both their fundamental relevance to important topics of

097   2        M EASURING C ONCEPT U NDERSTANDING VIA S UMMATIVE A SSESSMENT
098
099   It is intrinsically challenging to measure the extent to which LLMs understand a sentence or concept.
100   Indeed, Bender & Koller (2020) provide a definition of "understanding" from a linguistic perspective,
101   but this definition depends on another abstract and unmeasurable term, “meaning”. Therefore, even
102   with this definition, accurately measuring "understanding" remains elusive.
103   We approach the measurement of whether LLMs understand a concept from an educational and
104   cognitive perspective, using summative assessment (Black & Wiliam, 1998a;b; Harlen & James,
105   1997). Summative assessment is widely used by educators as an appealing strategy to evaluate
106   students’ understanding and knowledge acquisition in educational and cognitive psychology. For
107
          2
              Throughout this paper, LLM refers to either standard text-only LLMs or large multimodal models for simplicity.



                                                                             2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      example, when middle school physics teachers want to know whether a student truly understands
109   the concept “Gravity”, they would design a series of questions specifically related to the concept of
110   gravity to assess comprehension, e.g., the properties like inverse square law and examples like orbital
111   motions. If a student struggles to answer many of these questions, the teacher may conclude that the
112   student does not understand the concept well or has a poor grasp of it.
113
      We extend the idea of summative assessment to evaluating the concept understanding from humans to
114
      machines. Formally, assume S denotes an intelligent system and C is a specific concept. To evaluate
115   the extent how S understands the concept C, our summative assessment includes the following two
116   steps:
117
118
      • Task design towards C: design several concept understanding tasks, each of which consists of
        several questions manually created towards understanding the concept C.
119
      • Evaluating S: ask S to answer the questions from the tasks and calculate its accuracy.
120
121
      Requirements for Validity The success (validity) of the proposed evaluation approach highly
122
      depends on the task design (Black & Wiliam, 1998a;b). For example, if the questions are too easy,
123   even a weak system could answer them correctly. This leads to an overestimation of the system’s
124   understanding capabilities, making the assessment ineffective. To ensure good validity, we adhere to
125   the principles outlined in summative assessment (Black & Wiliam, 1998a;b) for task design:
126
      • Alignment with evaluating objectives: the questions should be related to the targeted concept, and
127
        should measure the specific knowledge about the targeted concept.
128
      • Different difficulty levels: the questions should be with different difficulty levels from easy to
129     difficult level, to ensure that the evaluation results have distinctiveness for different systems.
130   • Variety: the questions should reflect various understanding aspects of the targeted concept; address-
131     ing both its denotation and connotation.
132   • Simplicity: while not mandatory, a simpler benchmark for humans can more effectively highlight
133     the issue faced by current models, i.e., the stochastic parrot effect in LLMs.

      3       TASK D ESIGN AND DATASET C ONSTRUCTION
136
137
      3.1       TASK D ESIGN P RINCIPLE
138
139   We borrow the idea of Bloom’s taxonomy (Krathwohl, 2002; Armstrong, 2010) from education
140   research to fulfill the requirements for task design in Section 2, so as to ensure the assessment validity.
141   Bloom’s taxonomy offers an ideal principle to these requirements with an ordering of six cognitive
142   skills (from low to high level) for knowledge understanding: Remembering, Understanding, Applying,
143   Analyzing, Evaluating and Creating.
144   Generally, it is nontrivial to strictly follow this principle since there is no clear boundary among the
145   last four skills of understanding. As a result, we group the last four high-level skills into one and
146   consider the following two levels of understanding:
147
      • Low-level Understanding: covering the two lowest-level skills in Bloom’s taxonomy, i.e., retrieving
148
        relevant knowledge from long-term memory and rephrasing in one’s own words.
149
      • High-level Understanding: covering the aspects for understanding the knowledge beyond memo-
150     rization. As shown by the examples in Section 3.2.2, our tasks directly correspond to a spectrum
151     from the understanding level of applying to the level of analyzing in Bloom’s taxonomy, e.g.,
152     applying the knowledge to explain a physical phenomenon, analyzing a concrete property of a
153     concept in a generalized and abstract manner,3 .
154
      Based on these two levels, we design the following P HYSI C O task for summative assessment.
155
156
      3.2       O UR P HYSI C O TASK
157
158   P HYSI C O is essentially a physical concept understanding task, which primarily targets on 52 physical
159   concepts or phenomena: e.g., gravity, light reflection, acceleration, buoyancy, inertia, etc (see
160   Appendix A for the full list). Our focus on physical concepts is motivated by two main reasons: 1)
161
          3
              For example, the flow of electric current can be abstracted as moving from high potential to low potential.

162
      understanding physical concepts is critical for intelligent systems to interact with the world, which
163   is ultimate goal of embodied AI (Savva et al., 2019; Duan et al., 2022; Xiang et al., 2023); 2)
164   designing tasks centered around physical concepts allows us to more easily control different levels of
165   understanding and ensure the diversity of each concept.
166
      For each physical concept, P HYSI C O involves both low-level understanding subtasks and high-level
167
      subtasks, following our task design principles.
168
169
      3.2.1   L OW- LEVEL U NDERSTANDING S UBTASKS
170
171   Physical Concept Selection (text) First, to evaluate whether an LLM possesses the knowledge of
172   our included concepts, we design a task to recognize a concept from its corresponding Wikipedia defi-
173   nition. Specifically, we manually masked the synonyms of the concept with placeholder [PHENOMENON].
174   Meanwhile, highly relevant entities were masked as [MASK] to alleviate shortcuts. For example, in
175   the definition of Gravity, the terms “gravity” and “gravitation” were masked as [PHENOMENON], while
176   “Isaac Newton” was masked as [MASK]. Details can be found in Appendix B. We then present the
177   LLMs with the same four choices as in our following high-level subtasks.
178
179   Physical Concept Selection (visual) Second, we evaluate if the LLMs can recognize our concepts
180   represented with real-life pictures. To this end, we query our concepts on Google image search,
181
      and select the images that reflect the same core properties and examples annotated in our following
      high-level tasks. This results in 100 examples. We construct the same four-choice instances as above.
182
183
184
      Physical Concept Generation Finally, we directly ask the LLMs to generate the description of
      a concept with its core properties and representative examples. For instance, the concept Gravity
185
      is described as “a force that pulls objects with mass towards each other”, followed by the example
186
      “an apple falls to the ground” as shown in Figure 1. We then evaluate the performance of LLMs by
187   measuring the quality of the description and its coverage of knowledge required by our P HYSI C O
188   and we employ both automatic and human metrics as presented in Section 5.2. This provides a
189   quantitative measure of the knowledge LLMs can recall in the context of our assessment.
190
191   3.2.2   H IGH - LEVEL U NDERSTANDING S UBTASKS
192
193   The low-level subtasks are depicted in natural language thus are likely to be remembered by the
194   LLMs due to their extensive training data. To assess whether the LLMs possess a deep understanding
195   of the knowledge, we require the subtasks that can 1) represent the high-level understanding skills; 2)
196
      avoid the effects of memorization.
197   The Abstraction and Reasoning Corpus (ARC) (Chollet, 2019) provides a compelling way by using
198   grids (or matrices) instead of texts to represent a concept. While the LLMs have seen matrices during
199   pre-training, the data is less likely to be correlated to physical concepts. We hence adopt this idea to
200   represent our subtask as abstract representations in the grid world that associate to the key properties
201   of a physical concept.
202
203   The P HYSI C O-C ORE Set Our first subtask aims to cover the core properties or most representative
204   examples/applications of the assessed concepts. To ensure our set remains generally comprehensible
205   to humans, we maintain a high school-level difficulty and selected 52 common physical concepts
206
      within the curriculum. To enhance the diversity and richness, five annotators have labeled multiple
      core aspects of each concept. For example, the annotated core aspects of Gravity include attraction
207
      between two bodies, motion on an inclined plane, objects falling to grounds and orbital motions.
208
209   For each aspect of a concept, the annotator is asked to draw several pairs of abstract grid represen-
210   tations. The aspect of the concept is guaranteed to be illustrated by the pair, such that it explains
211   the transformation from the input to the output. For example, Figure 1 forms a direct abstract
212
      visualization of the Gravity concept from textbooks, i.e., apple falling from a tree. This results in
      1,200 paired grid examples for the 52 concepts, which form 400 3-shot instances.
213
214   Figure 2 presents two examples from this subtask that delve deeper into the concept of Gravity
215   compared to Figure 1. The top example demonstrates an application of the inverse square law of
      gravity. The bottom one presents a parabola, linking the knowledge of gravity to inertia. These

      This section answers the research questions regarding our high-level understanding subtasks.
356
357   RQ 2: Can Humans understand the abstract representations?
358
359   First of all, we investigate the performance of humans who possess the knowledge required by
360
      our P HYSI C O. For each instance in our P HYSI C O, we asked three independent annotators who
      were not involved in our task design to perform the same classification task presented to the LLMs.
361
      The results indicate that our tasks are largely solvable to people with a college-level education.
362
      Specifically, on the P HYSI C O-C ORE tasks, humans achieved an accuracy rate higher than 90%. The
363   P HYSI C O-A SSOCIATIVE tasks present greater challenges and subjectivity because the annotations
364   are personalized based on the annotators’ individual perspectives and experiences. Despite these
365   challenges, humans can still achieve a notable average accuracy of 77.8% in solving these tasks.
366
      We conducted a detailed investigation into human performance on a subset of P HYSI C O-
367
      A SSOCIATIVE. Participants were asked to annotate instances where they believed none of the
368
      four candidate answers adequately explained the inputs. The results revealed a 10.4% rate of disagree-
369   ment. On these disagreed-upon examples, human accuracy was 33.3%, explaining a major factor for
370   the human performance decline.
371
372   Conclusion Our study demonstrates that humans can perform the P HYSI C O tasks quite well.

406   a huge gap. While these tasks are simple or trivial for humans, LLMs face substantial challenges,
407   indicating a lack of deep understanding.
408   When comparing LLMs’ performance on low-level natural language tasks in RQ 1 to high-level
409   abstract pattern understanding tasks, we observe significant declines. This highlights the presence
410   of the stochastic parrot phenomenon in LLMs. Our dataset also quantifies the severity of this
411   phenomenon. For example, while GPT-3.5 performs on par with GPT-4 on the low-level tasks, it
412   nearly drops to random guessing on our high-level tasks, revealing its tendency to act as a stochastic
413   parrot with the physical concepts in our dataset.

      7   R ELATED W ORK
506
      Stochastic Parrots on LLMs The pioneer study by (Bender & Koller, 2020) questioned the
507
      understanding ability of large models; and Bender et al. (2021) first introduced the terminology of
508
      stochastic parrot. The concept of stochastic parrot has received great attention, leading to a surge of
509   studies on this topic. According to Google Scholar, the term “stochastic parrot” appears in the titles
510   of dozens of papers from diverse research fields (Borji, 2023; Li, 2023; Duan et al., 2024; Henrique
511   et al., 2023). However, although the concept of stochastic parrots in LLMs is widely accepted and
512   recognized, to the best of our knowledge, there is a lack of quantitative experiments to precisely
513   verify this viewpoint. This gap directly motivates our work.
514
515   Abstract Reasoning Challenge Abstract reasoning challenge (ARC) aims to examine the inductive
516   reasoning ability in a few-shot scenario (Chollet, 2019) and it has been used as a remarkable testbed
517   to measure the intelligence of LLMs. Recently, many research efforts have been made on improving
518   the performance of LLMs on ARC benchmark (Tan & Motani, 2023; Wang et al., 2023; Xu et al.,
519
      2023; Mirchandani et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024; Huang et al., 2024). We draw inspiration from
      ARC by utilizing input-output grids as abstract representations in our task design. However, our
520
      task is significantly different from the ARC-style work — our high-level understanding task focuses
521
      on comprehending the transformation rules from inputs to outputs and relating them to physical
522   concepts, and is designed to assess the stochastic parrot phenomenon.
523
524   Challenging Tasks towards LLMs’ Understanding Extensive recent efforts have been made on
525   designing tasks that challenge the understanding abilities of LLMs (Chakrabarty et al., 2022; Tong
526   et al., 2024; Shapira et al., 2023; Hessel et al., 2023; Donadel et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024). For
527   example, Hessel et al. (2023) proposed a humor understanding task, revealing a large performance
528   gap between LLMs and humans. As a by-product, our P HYSI C O challenges the understanding
529   capabilities of LLMs, relating it to the above studies. However, we make primary contribution
530   to provide an quantitative experiment to verify stochastic parrots in LLMs via controllably paired
531   low-level and high-level tasks.
532
533   8   C ONCLUSION
534
535   We introduce P HYSI C O, a novel task to assess machines’ understanding of physical concepts at
536   different levels. Our experiments reveal that: 1) LLMs lag significantly behind humans on P HYSI C O,
537   indicating a lack of deep understanding of the covered concepts; 2) LLMs exhibit the stochastic parrot
538   phenomenon, as they excel at low-level remembering tasks but struggle with high-level understanding
539   tasks; 3) LLMs’ poor performance stems from its intrinsic deficiencies, as neither in-context learning
      nor fine-tuning improves their results.

            ,→ in motion will continue to move at a constant velocity, unless acted upon by a net external force.
867         ,→ Here are the key properties and examples of [PHENOMENON]:

            ,→ to change its state of motion.

            ,→ change in its velocity, which includes changes in the speed or direction of the object's motion.
873

            ,→ microscopic or astronomical in scale.
