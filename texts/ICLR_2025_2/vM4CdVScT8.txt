011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            The matrix quantization entails representing matrix elements in a more space-
014            efficient form to reduce storage usage, with dequantization restoring the original
015
               matrix for use. We formulate the Quantization Error Minimization (QEM) prob-
               lem as minimizing the distance between a matrix before and after quantization,
016
               under the condition that the quantized matrix occupies the same memory space.
017
               Matrix quantization is crucial in various applications, including Large Language
018            Models (LLMs) weight quantization, vector databases, KV cache quantization,
019            graph compression, and image compression. Recent advancements in LLMs, such
020            as GPT-4 and BERT, have highlighted the importance of matrix compression due
021            to the large size of parameters and KV cache, which are stored as matrices.
022            We propose Quantum Entanglement Trees (QET) to address the QEM problem
023            by leveraging the local orderliness of matrix elements, involving iterative element
024            swapping to form a locally ordered matrix. This matrix is then grouped and quan-
025            tized by columns. To enhance QET, we introduce two optimizations: Residual
026            Quantization Optimization (RQO), which reduces MSE by quantizing the residu-
027            als between the original and dequantized matrices, and Codebook Quantization
028
               Optimization (CQO), which reduces storage requirements by compressing the
               codebook itself.
029
030
               Experimental results demonstrate that QET can effectively reduce MSE to 5.05%,
               13.33%, and 11.89% of the current best method on the LLM dataset, K cache,
031
               and V cache, respectively. Our contributions include the abstraction of the QEM
032
               problem, the design of the QET algorithm, and the proposal of two optimizations
033            to improve accuracy and speed.

036   1   I NTRODUCTION
037
038   The matrix quantization entails representing the elements of a matrix in a more space-efficient form
039   to reduce its storage usage. Dequantization, on the other hand, is the process during usage where
040   the original matrix is restored from the quantized matrix using a restoration algorithm. We formu-
041   late the Quantization Error Minimization (QEM) problem as the task of minimizing the distance
042   between a matrix before and after quantization in high-dimensional space, under the condition that
043   the quantized matrix occupies the same space.
044   Matrix quantization is widely employed across diverse applications, including Large Language
045   Models (LLMs) weight quantization (Dettmers et al. (2024); Lin et al. (2024); Shao et al. (2023);
046   Xiao et al. (2023)), vector database (Xu et al. (2018); Jegou et al. (2010)), LLM k-v cache quanti-
047   zation (Liu et al. (2024); Zhang et al. (2024); Hooper et al. (2024); Kawakibi Zuhri et al. (2024);
048   Duanmu et al. (2024); Yue et al. (2024); Lee et al. (2024); Adnan et al. (2024)), graph compres-
049   sion (Brisaboa et al. (2009); Claude & Ladra (2011)), and image compression (Yu et al. (2018);
050
      Ning et al. (2016)).
051   Specifically, the recent advancements in Large Language Models (LLMs) have made matrix com-
052   pression even more critical. Large Language Models (LLMs) have revolutionized the field of natural
053   language processing (NLP), enabling significant advancements in tasks such as machine translation,
      text generation, and sentiment analysis. These models, characterized by their large-scale neural net-


                                                       1

054
      work architectures and vast training datasets, have shown remarkable capabilities in understanding
055   and generating human language. The advent of LLMs, such as OpenAI’s GPT-4 (Achiam et al.
056   (2023)), and BERT (Devlin et al. (2018)) by Google, has pushed the boundaries of what machines
057   can achieve in linguistic tasks, providing near-human performance in various applications. The
058   parameters and KV cache in Large Language Models are very large in size. For instance, GPT-2
059   contains 1.5 billion parameters (Solaiman et al. (2019)), whereas GPT-3 has expanded to 175 bil-
060   lion parameters (Brown et al. (2020)). Additionally, the KV cache accounts for over 30% of GPU
061   memory during deployment, compared to the 65% occupied by the parameters (Kwon et al. (2023)).
062   Both the parameters and KV cache are stored in the form of matrices. GPTQ (Frantar et al. (2022))
063
      and SqueezeLLM (Kim et al. (2023)) directly address the QEM problem by treating it as the opti-
      mization objective for quantizing parameters and the KV cache. Therefore, the abstracted scientific
064
      problem of QEM is highly significant.
065
066   There has been a growing literature on the matrix quantization. The first category of methods fo-
067   cuses on independently compressing the elements of a matrix for each specific scenario. Examples of
068   such techniques include LLM.int8 (Ge et al. (2013)), Optimal Brain Damage (LeCun et al. (1989)),
069
      GPTQ (Frantar et al. (2022)), AWQ (Lin et al. (2023)), SmoothQuant (Xiao et al. (2023)), and Om-
      niQuant (Shao et al. (2023)). The second category groups matrix elements by columns and then
070
      applies quantization to each group. Relevant works in this category include product quantization
071
      (PQ) (Jegou et al. (2010)), optimized product quantization (OPQ) (Ge et al. (2013)), locally opti-
072   mized product quantization (LOPQ) (Kalantidis & Avrithis (2014)), and SqueezeLLM (Kim et al.
073   (2023)). The first category of work can be summarized as utilizing outliers and the importance of
074   elements in specific scenarios to improve the RTN (Round-To-Nearest) 1 (Gray & Neuhoff (1998))
075   algorithm. The second category focuses on enhancements to PQ algorithms. The primary drawback
076   of the first category is that it does not consider the correlation between elements, instead indepen-
077   dently quantizing and storing elements. Conversely, the second category fails to account for the
078   relative order of elements.

086   Using these ideas, we propose Quantum Entanglement Trees (QET) to address the QEM problem
087
      in matrix quantization. The core idea of QET is to leverage the local orderliness of matrix elements
      to optimize the QEM problem. The design involves swapping adjacent elements of the matrix to
088

089

090   in convolutional neural networks (LeCun et al. (1998)). This step can be repeated for multiple
091   iterations. The newly ordered matrix is then grouped by columns, with each group being quantized.
092   Additionally, we propose two optimization algorithms based on the basic QET. First, the residuals
093   between the original and quantized matrices can be further quantized to enhance the accuracy of the
094   results. Second, the codebook can be quantized to reduce storage requirements.
095
      We evaluate QET on multiple datasets. Experimental results demonstrate that QET can effectively
096   reduce MSE to 5.05%, 13.33%, and 11.89% of the current best method on the LLM dataset, K
097   cache, and V cache, respectively. Our code has been open-sourced on GitHub ( QET (2024)).

135   Rearranging matrix elements: Our idea is to rearrange the matrix elements before quantization.
136   Let the quantization algorithm be denoted as quant() and the rearrangement algorithm as rearrange().
137   Previously, the quantized matrix was obtained as X q = quant(X), whereas in our rearrangement ap-
138   proach, it is obtained as X q = quant(rearrange(X)). Therefore, the rearrangement algorithm only
139   affects the quantization if the quantization method is order-sensitive.For instance, order-insensitive
140   algorithms like RTN do not benefit from rearrangement because RTN processes each element inde-
141
      pendently, while order-sensitive algorithms like PQ can be optimized through rearrangement.
142   However, finding the optimal arrangement requires exploring a large search space. Specifically, for
143   an n × d matrix, there are (n!)d possible rearrangements. Therefore, searching the entire space is
144   impractical, and we need heuristic algorithms to prioritize exploring more promising regions of the
145   search space.

147   3     METHODS
148
149   The core idea of Quantum Entanglement Trees (QET) is to leverage the local orderliness of matrix
150   elements by rearranging adjacent elements to optimize the matrix quantization algorithm. Addition-
151   ally, multiple iterations are used to expand the algorithm’s swapping field. We begin by describing
152   the basic version of this idea and then propose two optimizations for the basic algorithm.

197   3.2   QET Q UANTIZATION
198
199   The proposed QET Algorithm is designed to optimize the quantization of a matrix X ∈ Rn×d by
200
      leveraging local orderliness. The algorithm proceeds in four key steps, as shown in Algorithm 2:
201   Step 1: Initial Partitioning. The algorithm starts by comparing each adjacent pair of elements
202   (xi,j , xi,j+1 ) in the input matrix X. Based on the comparison of these elements, the smaller value
203   is placed into matrix S and the larger into matrix L. Concurrently, an Indicator Map I is generated,
204   where a 0 denotes that the smaller element is on the left and a 1 denotes it is on the right. This
205   process results in the creation of two matrices, S and L, which collectively represent the initial
206
      partitioning of the original matrix X.
207   Step 2: Recursive Partitioning. Following the initial partitioning, the algorithm undertakes a
208   recursive partitioning process. Starting from k = 1, the matrices Ski and Lik 2 derived from the
209   previous iteration (for i = 1, 2, . . . , 2k−1 ) are further partitioned based on their sizes. In each
210   iteration, a new Indicator Map Ik+1 is generated and then merged with the existing Indicator Maps.
211   This recursive process continues until the predefined number of iterations l is reached. The resulting
212   Indicator Maps I1 , I2 , . . . , Il are stored in the set IM . The final matrices Sl and Ll are combined to
213
      form a new matrix X ∗ , which represents a locally ordered version of the original matrix.
214

530
      Matrix quantization involves representing matrix elements in a more space-efficient form to reduce
      storage usage, with dequantization restoring the original matrix for practical applications. We frame
531
      the Quantization Error Minimization (QEM) problem as minimizing the distance between a matrix
532
      before and after quantization, constrained by the condition that the quantized matrix occupies the
533   same memory space.
534
535   To address the QEM problem, we propose Quantum Entanglement Trees (QET), which leverage the
536
      local orderliness of matrix elements through iterative element swapping to form a locally ordered
      matrix. To enhance the QET algorithm, we introduce two key optimizations: residual quantization
537
      optimization and codebook quantization optimization.
538
539   Our experimental results demonstrate that QET can effectively reduce MSE to 5.05%, 13.33%, and
      11.89% of the best existing methods on the LLM dataset, K cache, and V cache, respectively.

      The dequantization process, as described by the QET Dequantization Algorithm, aims to reconstruct
653
      the original matrix X ′ from its quantized version X q . This process involves several key steps, as
654   shown in Algorithm 2:
655
656   Step 1: Dequantization Using Codebook. The first step involves using the codebook C to directly
657
      retrieve the original vectors corresponding to each quantized vector giq in the matrix X q . For each
      vector giq in Xi,k
                      q
                         , the algorithm assigns gi′ to the centroid cj from the codebook, where j is the index
658
      corresponding to the quantized vector. This step effectively reverses the quantization by mapping
659
      the quantized vectors back to their original forms using the codebook entries.
660
661   Step 2: Reverse Subspace Grouping. After mapping, the algorithm combines the dequantized
662   subspaces {G′i }, where i = 1, 2, . . . , m, into a single matrix X ′∗ . Each subspace G′i contains the
663   vectors gi′ corresponding to the original matrix entries. This step reconstructs the subspaces into an
664
      uniform matrix that is a preliminary version of the original matrix X ′ .
665   Step 3: Reverse Recursive Partitioning and Final Reconstruction. The final step involves re-
666   versing the recursive partitioning process that was applied during quantization. Starting from the
667   last iteration k = l, the algorithm merges the matrices Sk and Lk based on the Indicator Maps Ik
668   and the matrix X ′∗ . This merging process is iteratively applied from k = l down to k = 1, updat-
669   ing X ′∗ at each step to gradually reconstruct the original matrix structure. The final output of the
670
      algorithm is the reconstructed matrix X ′ ∈ Rn×d , which approximates the original matrix before
      quantization.

851   In this experiments, we evaluated the performance of the quantization process by measuring both
852   Quantization Time (QT) and Dequantization Time (DQT) across multiple datasets, as shown in
853   Figures 4. These metrics provide a comprehensive understanding of the computational efficiency of
854
      the algorithm at different compression ratios, ranging from 2 to 16.
855   For QT, the QET algorithm exhibits longer quantization times at lower compression ratios, but as
856   the compression ratio increases, QT decreases significantly. This phenomenon is expected, as fewer
857   centroids simplify the clustering process, resulting in faster compression as the compression ratio
858   increases. Vanilla consistently achieves lower QT across all datasets compared to QET, making it
859   the faster option, albeit with a slight loss in accuracy. In contrast, PQ and other baseline meth-
860
      ods demonstrate moderate QT performance, although OPQ and LOPQ show slower performance at
      larger matrix sizes due to their complexity.
861
862   As for DQT, QET maintains a relatively stable performance across all compression ratios and
863   datasets, with marginal increases in time as the compression ratio grows. Vanilla follows a simi-
      lar trend but achieves faster dequantization. PQ remains competitive in terms of both QT and DQT.
