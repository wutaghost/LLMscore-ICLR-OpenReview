011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Incremental Learning (IL) aims to learn new tasks while preserving previously
014            acquired knowledge. Integrating the zero-shot learning capabilities of pre-trained
015
               vision-language models into IL methods has marked a significant advancement.
               However, these methods face three primary challenges: (1) the need for improved
016
               training efficiency; (2) reliance on a memory bank to store previous data; and
017
               (3) the necessity of a strong backbone to augment the model’s capabilities. In
018            this paper, we propose SimE, a Simple and Efficient framework that employs a
019            vision-language model with an adapter designed specifically for the IL task. We
020            report a remarkable phenomenon: there is not always a direct positive correlation
021            between the number of adaptive adapter connections and the model’s IL capabilities.
022            While increasing the number of adapter connections between transformer blocks
023            positively impacts model performance, adding more adaptive connections within
024            transformer blocks during smaller incremental steps does not enhance, and may
025            even degrade the model’s IL ability. Such improvements only occur at more
026
               advanced incremental stages. Extensive experimental results show that SimE
               surpasses traditional methods by 9.6% on TinyImageNet and outperforms other
027
               CLIP-based methods by 5.3% on CIFAR-100. Notably, SimE, with only thousands
028
               of parameters and no memory bank, outperforms ZSCL, which has 140 million
029            parameters, and surpasses CoOP, which requires a memory bank of size 1000.
030            Furthermore, we conduct a systematic study to enhance the utilization of the zero-
031            shot capabilities of CLIP. We suggest that the backbone encoder in SimE should use
032            the image encoder from CLIP pre-trained on larger datasets, such as LAION-2B,
033            and larger model architectures, such as ViT-L/14, for IL tasks.

036   1   I NTRODUCTION
037
038   Deep learning models have achieved significant success when fully trained on domain-specific tasks.
039
      However, in real-world scenarios, new data often come from diverse sources. Training a deep learning
      model on such new data typically leads to the model forgetting previously learned information—a
040
      phenomenon known as catastrophic forgetting (Goodfellow et al., 2013). To address this issue,
041
      Incremental Learning (IL) methods have been proposed, drawing inspiration from the human ability
042   to learn continuously. These methods enable models to preserve existing knowledge while acquiring
043   new skills (De Lange et al., 2021; Masana et al., 2022). Traditional IL approaches, which start training
044   from scratch (Li & Hoiem, 2017; Serra et al., 2018; Rebuffi et al., 2017), fail to leverage the zero-shot
045   learning capabilities of pre-trained vision-language models. For example, Contrastive Language-
046   Image Pre-training (CLIP) models (Radford et al., 2021), trained on extensive datasets, exhibit strong
047   feature extraction abilities. Consequently, integrating CLIP’s zero-shot learning capabilities into
048   continual learning approaches has become a subject of keen interest (Thengane et al., 2022; Ding
049   et al., 2022; Zheng et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2024).
050   Despite the success of recent CLIP-based IL methods, several challenges remain. For example, the
051   CoOP framework (Zhou et al., 2022) preserves historical knowledge by utilizing a memory bank
052   that is periodically accessed and updated during IL tasks. However, the ever-expanding volume
053   of accumulated data can overburden the memory bank, thereby constraining CoOP’s capacity for
      lifelong learning. In contrast, Continual CLIP (Thengane et al., 2022) leverages a frozen pre-trained

097   To address these challenges, we introduce SimE (see Fig.1), a Simple and Efficient IL framework
098   that combines a vision-language model with an adapter designed for efficient IL tasks. The adapter
099
      (Houlsby et al., 2019; Chen et al., 2022) is a lightweight module inserted into transformer blocks,
      enabling finetuning of the pre-trained model using minimal parameters. During training, the pre-
100
      trained model’s parameters are frozen; we finetune only the adapter’s trainable parameters, enhancing
101
      the model’s parameter efficiency and adaptability without requiring a memory bank. We conduct
102   a comprehensive evaluation of various backbones and pre-trained datasets to ascertain the most
103   effective CLIP configurations for IL tasks using SimE. CLIP offers a spectrum of backbones, ranging
104   from base to large models, as described by Radford et al. (2021), each with its own set of parameters.
105   Additionally, the scope of pre-trained datasets is vast, as evidenced by works such as Gadre et al.
106   (2024) and Cherti et al. (2023), which span from 400 million to 2 billion samples. Our systematic
107   investigation delves into the influence of these disparate backbones and pre-trained datasets on the
      performance of CLIP in IL scenarios.

      2    R ELATED WORK
163
164
      Conventional Continual Learning Methods: Traditional continual learning methods can be di-
165
      vided into three categories: regularization-based, architecture-based, and replay-based approaches.
166
      Regularization-based methods (Aljundi et al., 2018; Kirkpatrick et al., 2017; Li & Hoiem, 2017)
167   mitigate forgetting by incorporating regularization terms into the loss function, encouraging the
168   model to retain weights important for previous tasks. However, these methods may diminish the
169   model’s ability to learn new categories effectively. Architecture-based methods (Mallya & Lazebnik,
170   2018; Serra et al., 2018; Wang et al., 2020) adjust the network’s structure to accommodate new tasks
171   by expanding it or altering its configuration. While effective, these methods may not be ideal for
172   task-agnostic continual learning and can lead to increased memory usage. Replay-based methods
173   (Rebuffi et al., 2017; Buzzega et al., 2020; Cha et al., 2021) involve storing and periodically revisiting
174   data from previous tasks to help the model retain prior knowledge. Although useful, these methods
175
      can raise privacy concerns and may be less effective with smaller data buffers. Moreover, tradi-
      tional continual learning models are typically trained from scratch, which may limit the maximum
176
      achievable performance by not leveraging pre-trained models.
177
178   Continual Learning Methods Using CLIP: Recently, pre-trained models have been increasingly
179   adopted in continual learning due to their powerful feature extraction capabilities (Wang et al.,
180   2022c;b; Thengane et al., 2022). CLIP (Radford et al., 2021), renowned for its impressive zero-shot
181
      abilities, excels in feature extraction through contrastive learning on vast amounts of image-text
      pairs. Consequently, several studies (Thengane et al., 2022; Ding et al., 2022; Zheng et al., 2023;
182
      Zhou et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2024) have integrated CLIP into continual learning
183
      models to enhance performance. Continual-CLIP (Thengane et al., 2022) directly applies CLIP
184   to continual learning without any finetuning, maintaining CLIP’s feature extraction capacity but
185   potentially suffering from domain gaps between pre-trained datasets and downstream tasks. LwF-VR
186   (Ding et al., 2022) and ZSCL (Zheng et al., 2023) finetune the entire model using traditional continual
187   learning methods to adapt to specific tasks. This process is computationally expensive due to the
188   large size of pre-trained models and may also lead to the forgetting of previously learned knowledge.
189   Thus, the finetuning strategy significantly impacts model performance.
190   Continual Learning Methods Using Adapter Finetuning: Adapters were initially introduced in
191   natural language processing (Houlsby et al., 2019) to finetune pre-trained models for specific tasks by
192   modifying a minimal set of weights. This approach has gained traction across various fields due to its
193   notable efficiency (Chen et al., 2022; Dong et al., 2024). In the realm of continual learning, recent
194   studies (Liu et al., 2023; Ermis et al., 2022b;a; Yu et al., 2024) have explored integrating adapters,
195   placing them after the encoder or within the model’s blocks. These adapters enable learning new
196   tasks with a limited number of trainable parameters while preserving the core feature extraction
197   functions. AdaptFormer (Chen et al., 2022), known for its lightweight parameterization, enhances
198
      model efficiency but is limited by the number of adaptive connections it can establish. In this paper,
      we introduce a Multi-Adapter that expands the number of adaptive connections, thereby extending
199
      the model’s flexibility.

202   3    T HE S IM E FRAMEWORK VIA VISION - LANGUAGE MODELS WITH ADAPTERS
203
204   In this section, we first introduce the definition of the incremental learning (IL) task. Next, in Section
205   3.1, we present SimE, a framework that combines the image encoder in vision-language models
206   with an adapter. Then, we introduce the formulation of the Multi-Adapter in Section 3.2. Finally,
207   in Section 3.3, we describe implementations of SimE using the image encoder from CLIP and the
208   adapters from AdaptFormer and Multi-Adapter. Incremental learning (IL) methods enable a model to
209   learn new tasks while retaining knowledge from previous ones.
210   Consider a sequence of tasks D = D1 , D2 , . . . , DT , where the t-th task is defined as Dt =
211   (xti , yit )i = 1mt . Here, Dt contains mt samples xti and their corresponding labels yit . During
212   the learning of task Dt , we have access only to the data from Dt ; the data from previous tasks
213   D1 , D2 , . . . , Dt − 1 are unavailable. Furthermore, we focus on task-agnostic class-incremental
214   learning (class-IL), where historical data cannot be used for rehearsal, and the task ID is not known
215   during inference. In this setting, the model must learn to classify samples from all classes seen so far
      without explicit information about which task a sample belongs to.

218   SimE is structured into three primary phases: data pre-processing, feature extraction, and image classi-
219   fication. In the initial phase, raw image data are transformed into a format compatible with the model’s
220   requirements. This is followed by the feature extraction phase, where an encoder—specifically the
221   image encoder from a pre-trained vision-language model equipped with an adapter and prototype
222   extractors—processes the formatted images. The process culminates in the image classification phase,
223   where a fully connected (FC) layer acts as the classifier. This classifier is intricately designed to
224
      support class-incremental learning (class-IL), facilitating the seamless incorporation of new classes.

521   5       C ONCLUSION
522
523   In this paper, we propose SimE, a simple yet efficient incremental learning (IL) framework. SimE
524   utilizes a pre-trained model as the encoder and incorporates adapters for finetuning, thereby achieving
525
      robust feature extraction capabilities while adapting to IL tasks without the need to store replay data.
      Our experiments demonstrate that SimE achieves competitive results, validating its effectiveness.
526
      To explore better methods of adapter connections, we introduce the Multi-Adapter and observe a
527
      remarkable phenomenon: there is not always a direct positive correlation between the number of
528   adaptive adapter connections and the model’s IL capabilities. Specifically, while increasing the
529   number of adapter connections between transformer blocks positively impacts model performance,
530   adding more adaptive connections within transformer blocks during small incremental steps does
531   not enhance,and may even degrade the model’s IL ability. Such improvements occur only at more
532   advanced incremental stages. We also conducted a systematic study on CLIP and identified the
533   optimal CLIP model for CIFAR-100 and TinyImageNet. Based on our findings, we recommend that
534   SimE’s backbone encoder utilize the image encoder from CLIP models pre-trained on larger datasets
535   like LAION-2B and larger architectures such as ViT-L/14 for CIL tasks. In future work, we will
536
      explore combining SimE with different pre-trained large models and various types of adapters for
      other tasks.

739   the data of the incremental tasks, η refers to the trainable parameters, η = ∪Z      ij ∪ θW t . Through
                                                                                        j η˜
740   finetuning, the pre-trained encoder can better adapt to downstream datasets. However, continuously
741   finetuning on a series of tasks D = {D1 , . . . , DT } would diminish its feature extraction capabilities
742
      due to Catastrophic Forgetting. Therefore, in this paper, we finetune the pre-trained encoder only on
      the first task D1 to maximise the retention of the encoder’s previous knowledge:
743
744                                         E ∗ (c) = F (E ′ (c1 ), D1 ).                                 (10)
745
746   By finetuning on the first task, the encoder can better adapt to downstream datasets. However, the
747   finetuning process will inevitably diminish the zero-shot capabilities of the pre-trained encoder. To
748   better preserve the feature extraction capabilities of the pre-trained encoder, we concatenate the
749   features output by the pre-trained encoder and the finetuned encoder and feed this combined feature
750
      set into the classifier for image classification.
751                                          E c (c) = {E ∗ (c); E(c)}                                    (11)
752
753   Here, E c (c) represents the composite encoder, and {·; ·} denotes the concatenation of the features
754   output by the pre-trained encoder and the finetuned encoder. It is important to note that the concatena-
755   tion is performed after finetuning, and encoder E c (c) will be frozen in subsequent tasks to maximise
      the retention of its feature extraction capabilities.

      B    A DDITIONAL EXPERIMENTAL SETTING
757
758
      Datasets. The experiments are implemented on CIFAR100 (Krizhevsky et al., 2009) and TinyIma-
759
      geNet (Zheng et al., 2023). CIFAR100 consist of 60K images with size of 32 × 32 from 100 classes,
760   which are split on 2 classes, 5 classes and 10 classes in each step. Each class consist of 500 training
761   and 100 testing samples. TinyImageNet, as a subset of ImageNet, consist of 100K images with size
762   of 64 × 64 from 200 classes and each class consist of 500 training and 50 testing images. In our
763   experiment, the 100 classes of TinyImageNet are split as base classes which are used for finetuning,
764   and the rest 100 classes are split on 5 classes, 10 classes and 20 classes in each step.
765
      Network architectures in the SimE. We have developed SimE, leveraging CLIP and adapters
766   (Adaptformer (Chen et al., 2022) and Multi-Adapter) for class-incremental learning tasks. Within
767   SimE, the CLIP image processor is utilized for data preprocessing. The image encoder, featuring
768   various backbone sizes such as ViT-B/16, ViT-B/32, and ViT-L/14, is finetuned using adapters across
769   different pre-trained datasets, including WIT-400B, Laion-400M, Laion-2B, Datacomp-1B, and
770   CommonPool-1B. The classifier employs a fully connected (FC) layer, which uses class prototypes
771   as weights.
772   Evaluation metrics. Following the methodology of Rebuffi et al. (2017), we assess SimE and
773   compare it with other baseline methods using two metrics: Average Accuracy (Avgt ) and Last
774   Accuracy (Lastt ). (Avgt ) represents the mean of the Top-1 accuracy for every task, while (Lastt )
775   denotes the Top-1 accuracy of final task. Mathematically, for the t -th task, Average Accuracy is
                                       PT
776   calculated as follows: Avgt = T1 t=1 Lastt .
777
      Others IL methods. We compare the SimE with existing CLIP-based methods (e.g., CoOp(Zhou
778
      et al., 2022), ZSCL(Zheng et al., 2023), Continual-CLIP(Thengane et al., 2022), AttriCLIPWang et al.
779
      (2023), and Boosting-CL(Yu et al., 2024)) and typical continual learning methods (e.g., LwF(Li &
780   Hoiem, 2017), iCaRL(Rebuffi et al., 2017), DER(Yan et al., 2021), iTAML(Rajasegaran et al., 2020)
781   and ARI(Wang et al., 2022a), UCIR(Hou et al., 2019), PASS(Zhu et al., 2021), and DyTox(Douillard
782   et al., 2022)).
783
      Training procedures. In this study, our experiments utilize the image encoder from CLIP(Radford
784
      et al., 2021). We finetune the SimE over 20 epochs on the first task for every datasets. Subsequently,
785
      all model weights, except the classifier, remain unchanged. During finetuning, we employ Stochastic
786   Gradient Descent (SGD) as the optimizer. The starting learning rate is set at 0.01, adhering to a
787   cosine decay schedule. We apply a weight decay of 0.0005, a batch size of 64, and the adapter’s
788   bottleneck dimension is set to 64.
