009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            This paper introduces Data Centric Graph Condensation (named DCGC), a data-
012            centric and model-agnostic method for condensing a large graph into a smaller one
013            by matching the distribution between two graphs. DCGC defines the distribution of
014            a graph as the trajectories of its node signals (such as node features and node labels)
015            induced by a diffusion process over the geometric structure, which accommodates
016            multi-order structural information. Built upon this, DCGC compresses the topolog-
017            ical knowledge of the original graph into the orders-of-magnitude smaller synthetic
018            one by aligning their distributions in input space. Compared with existing methods
019
               that stick to particular GNN architectures and require solving complicated optimiza-
               tion, DCGC can be flexibly applied for arbitrary off-the-shelf GNNs and achieve
020
               graph condensation with a much faster speed. Apart from the cross-architecture
021
               generalization ability and training efficiency, experiments demonstrate that DCGC
022            yields consistently superior performance than existing methods on datasets with
023            varying scales and condensation ratios.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      Graphs are a generic representation for systems of certain interactions and structures, such as large
029
      online social networks (Fan et al., 2019), user-item recommender systems (Wu et al., 2019), chemical
030   molecules (Stärk et al., 2022), and biological protein interactions (Réau et al., 2023). Recent advances
031   in deep learning-based methods on graph-structured data, such as graph neural networks (Kipf &
032   Welling, 2017; Velickovic et al., 2018), have garnered significant attention and research interest.
033   However, training deep graph networks on large real-world graphs requires tremendous computational
034   and infrastructural resources due to the necessity of performing message passing layer by layer among
035   inter-connected nodes (Zeng et al., 2020).
036   To address this challenge, a natural idea is to compress the dataset involving data structures, which,
037   in particular, entails reducing the number of nodes and edges in the graph. To this end, traditional
038   methods include graph sparsification (Spielman & Teng, 2011) and graph coarsening (Loukas &
039   Vandergheynst, 2018; Cai et al., 2021): the former aims to obtain a sparser graph by removing
040   edges from the original graph, while the latter targets reducing the number of nodes by extracting
041   a subset from the node-set. However, these methods often rely on some predefined heuristics and
042   lack guidance from training (Yang et al., 2023), making it difficult to achieve satisfactory results on
043   downstream tasks.
044   Existing Works. Another technical path showing empirical success in recent studies resorts to a
045   synthesis-based approach that directly learns the node feature matrix and the adjacency matrix of
046   the target compressed graph (a.k.a. synthetic graph), which is called graph condensation or graph
047   distillation (Jin et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2022; Yang et al., 2023; Zheng et al., 2023). These methods
048   share a similar spirit, aiming to learn a synthetic graph that can replicate the same gradient trajectory
049   of model parameters as the original graph, named gradient-matching (Zhao et al., 2021). Although
050
      these methods have achieved promising performance, their design philosophies lead to unsatisfactory
      capabilities (Gupta et al., 2024) due to failure to fulfill the following criteria:
051
052   1. Efficiency of condensation process. The primary goal of graph condensation is to circumvent the
053   need for training on the original large graph, which would be memory and time-inefficient. However,
      current methods based on gradient-matching require training GNN models on the original full graph,

054
      which contradicts the fundamental objective of graph condensation. In addition, existing works are
055   formulated as a bi-level optimization problem, which further exacerbates the computational cost.
056
      2. Independence from models and hyper-parameters. Existing methods based on gradient match-
057
      ing are model-centric and rely on specific GNN architectures and the associated hyper-parameters.
058
      This means that any change in the GNN architecture (such as switching from GCN to GAT) or even
059   any change in hyper-parameters (like the number of GNN layers) can lead to significant change in
060   the condensed graph, necessitating a new round of condensation.
061
      3. Cross-architecture generalization ability. The stickiness to particular GNNs means that synthetic
062
      graphs condensed by one GNN may not adapt well to training with other GNN architectures, a
063
      problem we refer to as poor cross-architecture generalization ability. More seriously, synthetic graphs
064   condensed by an inappropriate GNN can lead to a significant performance drop, even when using the
065   appropriate GNN model for training and testing on the condensed graph. This issue is particularly
066   evident in heterophilic graphs.
067
      Presented Work. To address these limitations, this paper proposes Data-Centric Graph Condensation
068
      via Diffusion Trajectories Matching (DCGC in short). DCGC inherits the spirit of distribution
069
      matching (Zhao & Bilen, 2023), learning the condensed graph by minimizing the divergence between
070   the distributions of the original graph and the synthetic graph. Observing that a graph is a mixture of
071   the node signals (e.g., node features and labels) and their connections, we seek a principled means to
072   characterize and extract the topological knowledge from the original graph entangled with these node
073   signals. In particular, we resort to an analogy between a geometric diffusion process that updates
074   node signals through time and a non-parametric propagation on graphs that returns aggregated node
075   features at different layers. On top of this, we decompose a graph into a collection of node signals,
076   where each node’s signal is aggregated from its multi-order structural information, and the distribution
077   of a graph is subsequently defined as the distribution of the aggregated node signals. The divergence
078
      between the original graph and the synthetic graph is further measured by the Maximum Mean
      Discrepancy, which can be easily optimized in linear time w.r.t. the graph size.
079
080   DCGC addresses the limitations of the above works in the following ways: 1) Operated in the data
081   space and without relying on training a GNN on the full graph, DCGC eliminates the burdensome bi-
082   level optimization and, therefore, has a much faster training speed. 2) DCGC’s condensation process
083   is unsupervised, so it does not rely on tuning the hyper-parameters of a specific GNN for the node
084
      classification task. 3) The data-centric property frees DCGC from specifying a GNN architecture for
      condensation, therefore endowing DCGC with desired cross-architecture generalization capability.
085
      The consideration of the label’s distribution over the graph structure also enables DCGC to adapt to
086
      heterophilic graphs easily.
087
088   We evaluate the proposed DCGC on eight graph datasets of varying scales and properties. The
089   experimental results demonstrate that the synthetic graphs condensed by DCGC yield comparable
090
      or even better performance than existing SOTA gradient-matching methods. In cross-architecture
      settings and on heterophilic datasets, DCGC exhibits superior and more stable performance across
091
      different GNN architectures. Specifically, apart from improving the averaged accuracy, DCGC
092
      reduces the cross-architecture standard deviation by an average of 26.3%. In terms of training speed,
093   compared to the current fastest graph condensation methods, DCGC reduces the training time by
094   96.4%. These results clearly demonstrate the superiority of DCGC in terms of efficacy, generalization
095   ability, and efficiency.

      2    R ELATED W ORKS
099
      To reduce the high computational and memory costs when training on large graphs, recent research
100
      has started exploring dataset condensation techniques for condensing graph-structured data. As a
101
      pioneering work, GCond (Jin et al., 2022) follows the gradient matching paradigm (Zhao et al., 2021)
102   to learn the node features of synthetic nodes. The edges are parameterized as a function of the learned
103   features of two end nodes. SGDD (Yang et al., 2023) follows the similar gradient-matching paradigm
104   in GCond (Jin et al., 2022) but explicitly broadcasts the original graph structure to the synthetic
105   graph to prevent overlooking the structure information in the original graph. SFGC (Zheng et al.,
106   2023) introduces a training trajectory meta-matching scheme for effectively synthesizing small-scale
107   graph-free data. All of the above methods are based on gradient-matching and require specifying a
      GNN architecture and training its parameters, which not only leads to a challenging training process


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      but also results in poor cross-architecture generalization ability. To deal with these limitations, this
109   paper explores graph condensation by distribution matching.
110
111   We are not the first to utilize distribution matching for graph condensation. The pioneering work
112
      GCDM (Liu et al., 2022) treats the original graph and the synthetic graph as two distributions of
      receptive fields and learns by distribution matching (Zhao & Bilen, 2023). However, GCDM fails to
113
      generate the condensed graph of satisfying quality as the gradient-matching methods. Also, GCDM’s
114
      condensation process still requires specific GNN architectures and complicated bi-level optimization,
115   leaving the limitations of the above gradient-matching methods unresolved.

121   Graph Notations. Given a graph dataset G = {X, A, Y}, where X ∈ RN ×D is the node feature
122   matrix, A ∈ RN ×N is the graph adjacency matrix, and Y ∈ RN ×C is the (one-hot) node label
123   matrix. The target of graph condensation is to learn a synthetic graph S = {X′ , A′ , Y′ } of N ′
124   nodes (N ′ ≪ N ) such that machine learning models trained on the synthetic dataset S can perform
125   similarly to those trained on the original graph G. For any class c = 1, 2 · · · , C, we use Nc and Nc′ to
126   denote the number of nodes from class c in the original graph G and synthetic graph S, respectively.
127   Graph Condensation via Distribution Matching. To compress a large dataset into a smaller
128   synthetic one, a natural approach is to ensure that the distribution of synthetic data closely resembles
129   the distribution of real data (Zhao & Bilen, 2023). Denote the original and synthetic graph distribution
130   by G and S, and the class-conditional ones as Gc and Sc , respectively. Distribution matching
131   minimizes the discrepancy between G and S:

156   For i.i.d. data, such as images, the definition of its distribution is self-evident, i.e., the collection of
157   the matrix-form (or vector-form) representations of each single data record, e.g., the pixels of an
158   image (Zhao & Bilen, 2023). However, graph data are non-i.i.d. generated, considering that nodes in
159   the graph are inter-dependent due to the graph structure. While the definition of node feature/label
160   distribution is straightforward, defining the corresponding structural roles of nodes in the entire graph
161   can be challenging. Additionally, we need to fuse node features/labels with their structural attributes
      to obtain an overall vector form serving as a well-posed representation of their distributions.

283   Complexity Finally, we analyze the complexity of DCGC. 1) Computing the feature/label distribu-
284   tion of the original graph G requires O(EK(D + C)), while this process is non-parametric and can
285   be obtained via one-step preprocessing. Therefore, the computation overhead of this step is negligible
286   compared with the entire condensation process. 2) Computing the feature/label distribution of the
287   condensed graph requires O(E ′ K(D + C)) = O(N ′2 K(D + C)). 3) Computing the MMD loss for
                                           PC

289   each class. Yet, notice that c=1 Nc′ (Nc + Nc′ ) ≤ N ′ (N + N ′ ), and the equality holds if and only

293   smaller than O(N 2 ), and therefore DCGC is time and memory-efficient.

      5     C ONCLUSIONS
476
477
      In this paper, we have proposed DCGC for condensing a large-scale graph into a small one. We
478
      define the distribution of a graph as the trajectories obtained by conducting diffusion on its nodes’
479
      features (as well as labels) over the graph structure. Following the idea of distribution matching,
480   we learn a small-scale graph by minimizing the Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) distance
481   between the distribution of the original graph and the synthetic graph. To address the convergence
482   challenges in distribution matching, we propose a sophisticated parameter initialization strategy
483   that not only accelerates convergence but also reduces the risk of getting stuck in local optima.
484   Extensive experimental results demonstrate that our proposed DCGC achieves state-of-the-art results
485   on the selected datasets and exhibits excellent cross-architecture generalization ability. Moreover, it
      significantly reduces the training time for condensation compared to current methods.

734   C.2     BASELINES
735
736   In this section, we detailedly introduce existing graph condensation methods that are used as baselines
737
      in this paper, including GCond (Jin et al., 2022), GCDM (Liu et al., 2022), SGDD (Yang et al., 2023),
      and SFGC (Zheng et al., 2023). GCond (Jin et al., 2022), SGDD (Yang et al., 2023), and SFGC (Zheng
738
      et al., 2023) are gradient-matching-based methods, whereas GCDM is a distribution-matching-based
739
      method.
740
741
742
      Gradient-matching-based methods. Gradient matching (Zhao et al., 2021) aims to match the
      network parameters w.r.t. to the large-real and small-synthetic training data by matching the task’s
743
      gradients at each step. In this way, it wishes the models trained on the original dataset and the
744
      synthetic dataset can converge to similar solutions. This is a bi-level optimization problem that can
745   be formulated as following:
746
                             "T −1            #
747                           X
                                       S G
748            min Eθ0 ∼Pθ0        D(θt , θt )
                S                                                                                      (14)
749                           t=0
                      S                                   G
750             s.t. θt+1 = opt-algθ (L(GNNθtS (S))) and θt+1 = opt-algθ (L(GNNθtG (G))),
751
752   where D(·, ·) is a distance metric measuring the distance between two gradient matrixes. T is the
753   number of steps in the training trajectory. opt-alg is a specific optimization procedure with a fixed
754   number of steps. In other words, the gradient matching algorithm wishes to generate a condensed
755   graph S such that the GNN parameters trained on them (θtS ) are similar to the ones trained on the
      original training graph θtG .

773   Distribution-matching-based methods. Distribution matching (Zhao & Bilen, 2023) learns the
774   condensed graph by directly minimizing the discrepancy (typically the MMD distance) between the
775   distributions of the original graph G and the synthetic graph S:
776                                              min MMD(G, S)                                   (17)

777
      GCDM defines the distribution of a graph as the set of its multi-hop receptive fields. Formally, R(i, L),
778   denotes node i’s L-hop receptive fields. For example, R(1, 1) denotes the first-order neighbors of
779   node 1, and R(1, 2) denotes the second-order neighbors of node 1 (including the first-order ones).
780
781
      According to the definition of Maximum Mean Discrepancy, the class-wise loss function can be
      formulated as follows:

787   form:
788
789                               1 X                      1 X

790                     S   ϕ    |Vc |                    |Vc |   ′

791
      ϕ is parameterized as a graph neural network model which outputs the embedding of a node. Then,
792
      the above optimization process involves alternatively updating the parameters of the GNN encoder ϕ,
793   and the parameters of the synthetic graph S.
794
795   The proposed DCGC is also based on distribution matching. Yet, we leveraged the characteristics of
796
      MMD in RKHS and kernel tricks to directly optimize in the input space, avoiding the formulated min-
      max bi-level optimization problem in GCDM that operates in the embedding space. This significantly
797
      accelerated the training speed. It also eliminated the reliance on a specific GNN model, resulting in
798
