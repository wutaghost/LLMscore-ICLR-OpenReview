009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            As large language models (LLMs) continue to evolve, efficient evaluation met-
012            rics are vital for assessing their ability to compress information and reduce re-
013            dundancy. While traditional metrics like Matrix Entropy offer valuable insights,
014            they are computationally intensive for large-scale models due to their O(n3 ) time
015            complexity with Singular Value Decomposition (SVD). To mitigate this issue, we
016            introduce the Matrix Nuclear-Norm, which not only serves as a metric to quantify
017            the data compression proficiency of LLM but also provides a convex approxima-
018
               tion of matrix rank to capture both predictive discriminability and diversity. By
               employing the L1,2 -norm to further approximate the nuclear norm, we can ef-
019
               fectively assess the model’s information compression capabilities. This approach
020
               reduces the time complexity to O(n2 ) and eliminates the need for SVD com-
021            putation. Consequently, the Matrix Nuclear-Norm achieves speeds 8 to 24 times
022            faster than Matrix Entropy for the CEREBRAS-GPT model as sizes increase from
023            111M to 6.7B. This performance gap becomes more pronounced with larger mod-
024            els, as validated in tests with other models like Pythia. Additionally, evaluations
025            on benchmarks and model responses confirm that our proposed Matrix Nuclear-
026            Norm is a reliable, scalable, and efficient tool for assessing LLMs’ performance,
027            striking a balance between accuracy and computational efficiency.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Large language models (LLMs), such as Gemini (Gemini et al., 2023), Llama (Touvron et al., 2023),
033
      and GPT-4 (GPT-4 Achiam et al., 2023), have demonstrated remarkable performance across a vari-
      ety of natural language processing (NLP) tasks (Zhao et al., 2023). They are not only transforming
034
      the landscape of NLP (Saul et al., 2005; Liu et al., 2023; Sawada et al., 2023) but also bring bene-
035
      ficial impacts on computer vision (Lian et al., 2023a; Wang et al., 2024) and graph neural networks
036   (Zhang et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024), achieving stellar results on various leaderboards. Despite
037   these advancements, assessing a model’s ability to compress information remains a crucial research
038   challenge (Delétang et al., 2023). Compression focuses on efficiently distilling essential informa-
039   tion from vast training datasets while discarding redundant elements, showcasing a model’s ability
040   to learn and recognize the underlying structure of the data (Wei et al., 2024). LLMs are expected to
041   perform this form of compression during their training process (Zhao et al., 2023). Specifically, in
042   the early stages of training, after random initialization, the representations produced from the data
043   are often chaotic. However, as training progresses, these representations become more organized,
044
      allowing the model to filter out unnecessary information. Hence, assessing an LLM’s capacity for
      information compression is crucial for understanding its learning efficiency and representational
045
      power.
046
047   Current compression evaluation methods, such as Matrix Entropy introduced by Wei et al. (2024),
048   measure information compression efficiency through processing models’ output representations on
049   datasets. However, the reliance of Matrix Entropy on Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) (Kung
050
      et al., 1983; Zhang, 2015) leads to significant computational complexity, typically O(n3 ), which
      limits its applicability in large-scale models.
051
052   To tackle this challenge, we propose a novel evaluation metric called Matrix Nuclear-Norm. This
053   metric effectively measures predictive discriminability and captures output diversity, serving as an
      upper bound for the Frobenius norm and providing a convex approximation of the matrix rank. Fur-


                                                       1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      thermore, we enhance the Matrix Nuclear-Norm by employing the L1,2 -norm to approximate the
055   nuclear norm, addressing stability issues during evaluation across multiple classes. This approach
056   enables an efficient assessment of a model’s compression capabilities and redundancy elimination
057   abilities, streamlining the evaluation process. Notably, the Matrix Nuclear-Norm achieves a compu-
058   tational complexity of O(n2 ), a significant improvement over Matrix Entropy’s O(n3 ). This reduc-
059   tion facilitates faster evaluations, making the Matrix Nuclear-Norm a practical choice for large-scale
060   models while maintaining accuracy.
061   To validate the effectiveness of the Matrix Nuclear-Norm, we first conducted preliminary experi-
062   ments on two language models of differing sizes. The results indicated a consistent decrease in
063   Matrix Nuclear-Norm values as model size increased, signifying enhanced compression capabili-
064   ties. Subsequently, we performed inference experiments on two widely used benchmark datasets,
065   AlpacaEval (Dubois et al., 2024) and Chatbot Arena (Chiang et al., 2024), which cover a diverse
066   range of language generation tasks. These benchmarks facilitate a comprehensive assessment of
067   model inference performance. Our experimental findings confirm that the Matrix Nuclear-Norm
068   accurately measures model compression capabilities and effectively ranks models based on perfor-
069
      mance, demonstrating its reliability and efficiency in practical applications. Our empirical investi-
      gations yield the following insights:
070
071         1. Proposal of the Matrix Nuclear-Norm: We present a new method that leverages the nu-
072            clear norm, successfully reducing the computational complexity associated with evaluating
073            language models from O(n3 ) to O(n2 ). This reduction minimizes dependence on SVD,
074            making the Matrix Nuclear-Norm a more efficient alternative to Matrix Entropy.
075         2. Extensive Experimental Validation: We validated the effectiveness of the Matrix
076            Nuclear-Norm on language models of various sizes. Results indicate that this metric ac-
077            curately assesses model compression capabilities, with values decreasing as model size
078            increases, reflecting its robust evaluation capability.
079         3. Benchmark Testing and Ranking: We conducted inference tests on widely used bench-
080            mark datasets, AlpacaEval and Chatbot Arena, evaluating the inference performance of
081            models across different sizes and ranking them based on the Matrix Nuclear-Norm. The
082            results demonstrate that this metric can efficiently and accurately evaluate the inference per-
083            formance of medium and small-scale models, highlighting its broad application potential
084            in model performance assessment.

086   2   R ELATED W ORK
087
088   LLM Evaluation and Scaling Laws. Evaluating large language models (LLMs) is a multifaceted
089   challenge, as it requires capturing both task-specific performance and internal representational effi-
090   ciency. Scaling laws have become a foundational framework for studying how LLM performance
091   evolves with model size and data volume (Kaplan et al., 2020; Ruan et al., 2024). These studies
092   demonstrate that model performance on tasks like language modeling and fine-tuning often follows
093
      predictable power-law relationships with respect to model parameters and dataset size, emphasizing
      the importance of scaling for achieving state-of-the-art results.However, scaling laws typically focus
094
      on external metrics such as cross-entropy loss, offering limited insight into how LLMs manage inter-
095
      nal knowledge representation. For instance, the ability of LLMs to compress knowledge, eliminate
096   redundancy, and retain structured information remains poorly understood with traditional methods.
097   Addressing these gaps requires structural metrics that go beyond task outcomes to directly evaluate
098   the internal embeddings and activation patterns of LLMs.
099
      LLM Evaluation Metrics. Traditional evaluation metrics such as perplexity, BLEU (Papineni
100
      et al., 2002), and ROUGE (Lin, 2004) primarily measure task-specific outcomes, assessing how
101
      well model outputs align with ground truth data. While these metrics are effective for evaluating
102   surface-level outputs, they do not capture the underlying mechanisms of LLMs, such as the di-
103   versity or compression of embeddings. Similarly, accuracy and F1 score (Sasaki, 2007) focus on
104   classification performance, making them less applicable to the generative tasks typical of LLMs.To
105   bridge this gap, structural metrics such as Matrix Entropy have been introduced. Matrix Entropy
106   (Wei et al., 2024) employs information theory to assess the entropy of covariance matrices derived
107   from LLM embeddings. This metric evaluates how effectively a model removes redundancy and
      encodes structured information, offering a measure of its compression capabilities. For instance,


                                                         2

108
      Matrix Entropy can reveal differences in embedding distributions across models of varying sizes,
109   reflecting their capacity to extract meaningful patterns from large datasets. However, its reliance on
110   Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) results in a computational complexity of O(n3 ), limiting its
111   applicability to modern large-scale models. To overcome these limitations, we propose the Matrix
112   Nuclear-Norm as a scalable alternative. By leveraging the L1,2 norm as a convex approximation of
113   matrix rank, the Matrix Nuclear-Norm reduces computational complexity to O(n2 ). This makes it
114   feasible for evaluating embeddings from large-scale LLMs while preserving the insights provided
115   by Matrix Entropy, such as compression efficiency.

191   4.1   M OTIVATION
192
193   This section introduces the Matrix Nuclear-Norm, a novel metric designed to enhance the efficiency
194   of model evaluation. Traditional nuclear norm calculations rely on computing all singular values,
195   which typically involves the computationally intensive SVD. This method not only consumes sig-
196   nificant time for large-scale data but may also fail to converge in certain cases, severely impacting
197   practical application efficiency. Therefore, we propose the Matrix Nuclear-Norm, which utilizes the
198
      L1,2 -norm to approximate the nuclear norm, effectively eliminating computational bottlenecks. This
      innovation significantly reduces computational demands and ensures scalability, providing a robust
199
      framework for the LLM evaluation.
200
201

522   In conclusion, Matrix Nuclear-Norm stands out as a promising evaluation metric for LLMs, offering
523   significant advantages in assessing information compression and redundancy elimination. Its key
524   strengths include remarkable computational efficiency, greatly exceeding that of existing metrics
525   like matrix entropy, along with exceptional stability across diverse datasets. Matrix Nuclear-Norm’s
526   responsiveness to model performance under varying inputs emphasizes its ability to gauge not only
527   performance but also the intricate adaptability of models. This metric marks a significant advance-
528
      ment in NLP, establishing a clear and effective framework for future research and development in
      the evaluation and optimization of language models.

531   8   L IMITATIONS
532
      Although Matrix Nuclear-Norm performs well in evaluating the performance of LLMs, it still has
533
      some limitations. First, since Matrix Nuclear-Norm’s computation relies on the model’s hidden
534   states, the evaluation results are sensitive to both the model architecture and the training process. As
535   a result, under different model designs or training settings, especially for models like GPT-1.3B and
536   GPT-2.7B, inconsistencies in Matrix Nuclear-Norm’s performance may arise, limiting its applicabil-
537   ity across a wider range of models. Additionally, while Matrix Nuclear-Norm offers computational
538   efficiency advantages over traditional methods, it may still face challenges with resource consump-
539   tion when evaluating extremely large models. As model sizes continue to grow, further optimization
      of Matrix Nuclear-Norm’s computational efficiency and evaluation stability is required.

      A.4    A NALYSIS OF A LGORITHMIC C OMPLEXITY
851   The primary computational expense of Matrix Nuclear-Norm arises from the calculation and sorting
852   of the L2 norm of the matrix. By avoiding Singular Value Decomposition (SVD), we reduce the
853   time complexity from the traditional nuclear norm of O(n3 ) to O(n2 ), giving Matrix Nuclear-Norm
854
      a significant advantage in handling large-scale data. This reduction in complexity greatly enhances
      the algorithm’s practicality, especially for applications involving large matrices.
855
      When analyzing the time complexity of the newly proposed Matrix Nuclear-Norm (L2-Norm Based
856
      Approximation of Nuclear Norm) against traditional Matrix Entropy, our objective is to demonstrate
857   that Matrix Nuclear-Norm significantly outperforms Matrix Entropy in terms of time efficiency. We
858   will support this claim with detailed complexity analysis and experimental results.
859
