009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            As neural networks grow larger, optimizing GPU kernel selection becomes in-
012            creasingly essential to minimizing the time, cost, and energy demands of model
013            training and inference. Current methods rely on hand-written rules-based heuris-
014            tics, which often yield suboptimal performance, are labor-intensive to develop,
015            and are difficult to adapt across hardware architectures and firmware releases. In
016            this paper, we frame kernel selection as a sequence classification problem solved
017            on the CPU, thereby leaving GPU resources free for user training and inference
018
               tasks. Traditional transformers are less effective in this context because CPU de-
               ployment limits the advantages of parallelism in attention mechanisms. In this
019
               regard, we propose the Γ -block, which performs only three matmul operations
020
               compared to the six required by a transformer block, while maintaining the same
021            depth in terms of learnable layers. Our experiments on the IMDB and Reuters
022            datasets demonstrate that a small model based on the Γ-block delivers comparable
023            sequence classification accuracy to a similar model based on transformer blocks,
024            while also providing faster inference times on the CPU. By stacking multiple Γ-
025            blocks, we develop a lightweight model for kernel selection, named Reformer. To
026            train the model, we propose a novel approach that assigns optimality probabilities
027            to kernels based on their runtimes, offering a more robust alternative to one-hot
028            probabilities. We demonstrate the effectiveness of Reformer by integrating it into
029
               MIOpen for convolution kernel selection, achieving an average speed-up of ap-
               proximately 3x in convolution operations on the AMD Instinct™ MI100 GPU.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Over the last decade, neural network sizes and data set sizes have had a reinforcing relationship: as
035   more data has become available, models have been enlarged to better learn from it, and concurrently,
036   larger models have driven the collection of more data to fully harness their potential. This feedback
037   loop has led to exponential growth in model sizes over time (Figure 1), resulting in a significant
038   increase in the compute and energy requirements (Strubell et al., 2020; Patterson et al., 2022; Dodge
039   et al., 2022) for training and deploying these models. Although researchers have worked to scale
040   down neural networks to reduce their compute footprint (Hinton et al., 2015; Cheng et al., 2017;
041   Micikevicius et al., 2017), recent findings suggest that large neural networks are not merely a passing
042
      trend but a key element of high-performing machine learning systems (Bubeck & Sellke, 2021).
043   To meet the increasing compute demands of large models, researchers from both academia and in-
044   dustry have been actively developing solutions. On the hardware front, these efforts have led to
045   improvements in GPU architectures (AMD, 2021; Nvidia, 2020) and the introduction of advanced
046   AI accelerators (Jouppi et al., 2023; Keller et al., 2022). On the software side, research endeavors
047   have focused on developing computationally-efficient, hardware-optimized kernels1 for common
048
      machine learning operations. However, since these kernels are hardware-specific and their perfor-
      mance varies across the problem space in a highly discontinuous and non-linear fashion, choosing
049
      the optimal kernel for any given problem is a non-trivial task. Brute-force approaches are gener-
050
      ally impractical in this regard. Exhaustively searching the problem space and benchmarking all
051   applicable kernels to determine the best one leads to combinatorial complexity, determined by the

127   2    BACKGROUND
128
129   Deep learning frameworks, such as PyTorch (Paszke et al., 2019) and Tensorflow (Abadi et al.,
130   2016), represent neural networks as dataflow graphs, with each node corresponding to a specific
131   mathematical operation, such as convolution, matmul, or softmax. These high-level operations are
132   subsequently compiled into hardware-specific high-performance primitives, known as kernels, for
133
      GPU-accelerated computation. To achieve this, frameworks rely on kernel libraries, such as MIOpen
      (Khan et al., 2019), an open-source kernel library from AMD, and cuDNN (Chetlur et al., 2014),
134
      Nvidia’s kernel library specific to Nvidia GPUs.
135
136   In this paper, we focus specifically on optimizing kernel selection for convolution operations, though
137   the proposed model is general and can be deployed to optimize any operation. Both MIOpen and
138   cuDNN offer a range of highly optimized kernels for convolution operations, which serve as the
139
      backbone of Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs). CNNs have been at the forefront of many
      breakthroughs in computer vision (Lecun et al., 1998; Krizhevsky et al., 2012; Simonyan & Zisser-
140
      man, 2014; Szegedy et al., 2015; He et al., 2016), primarily because convolution naturally encodes
141
      the spatial equivariance bias which facilitates the learning of general purpose visual representations
142   Raghu et al. (2021). Therefore, despite the recent introduction of transformers (Vaswani et al.,
143   2017) to the vision world (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020), CNNs continue to maintain a critical position
144   in this domain, either on their own or as extensions to vision transformers (Dai et al., 2021; Xiao
145   et al., 2021; Wu et al., 2021; Graham et al., 2021). Thus, improving the computational efficiency
146   of CNNs can reduce the time and cost of training computer vision models while also enhancing
147   energy efficiency and reducing the carbon footprint of data centers used for hosting, training, and
148   serving deep learning models (Strubell et al., 2020; Patterson et al., 2022; Dodge et al., 2022). Be-
149   yond this, compute-efficient CNNs can also improve the inference speed and memory footprint of
150
      vision models deployed on edge devices, thus facilitating the adoption of deep neural networks in
      resource-constrained applications, such as robotics, smartphones, and IoT (Canziani et al., 2016).
151
152   Since convolutions are one of the primary operations in a CNN, the computational efficiency, eval-
153   uation time, and energy consumption of a CNN – both during training and inference – are heavily
154   influenced by the kernels provided by the underlying kernel library for these operations. In partic-
155   ular, MIOpen employs hand-written rule-based heuristics specific to each GPU skew and firmware
156
      version to determine the most suitable kernel to serve from its pool.3 However, these heuristics are
      imperfect, thus often leading to MIOpen serving suboptimal kernels to the upstream library, slowing
157
      down convolution operations. Since these suboptimal kernels may be invoked tens of thousands of
158
159       3

161   et al., 2019). For a given convolution problem, MIOpen first checks FindDB; if a match is found, it retrieves
      the kernel from the table. Otherwise, MIOpen uses heuristics to select a kernel.

450   5     R ELEVANT WORK
451
452   5.1   K ERNEL OPTIMIZATION
453
454   Previous work on optimizing the performance of compute kernels has mostly concerned itself with
455
      methods for tuning/optimizing kernel parameters (Guerreiro et al., 2015; Lloyd et al., 2018; Gale
      et al., 2020; Bhaskaracharya et al., 2020). Additionally, the problem of kernel scheduling has also
456
      received some attention. For instance, Shekofteh et al. (2019) propose a method to select kernels
457
      based on whether they are compute-bound or memory-bound in order to optimize concurrent ker-
458   nel scheduling on modern GPUs. Ahmed et al. (2022) propose a machine learning approach for
459   selecting the best kernels to fuse together so as to maximize GPU utilization. The work of Jeon
460   et al. (2022) presents a system for selecting the optimal backend to pick a kernel for deep learning
461   operations. Some other relevant works include: Oyama et al. (2018), Liu et al. (2021a), Xiao et al.
462   (2020), and the review article on parallel deep learning by Ben-Nun & Hoefler (2019). However,
463   while these works share overlapping themes with our research, to the best of our knowledge, none
464   of them specifically address the problem of mapping high-level deep learning operations to fastest
465   GPU kernels from a pool of kernels, particularly using deep learning methods.
466
467   5.2   R ESIDUAL NETWORKS
468
469
      Deep Residual Networks (ResNets), introduced by He et al. (2016), pioneered the use of skip con-
      nections, which have since become a common fixture in neural network architectures. Despite its
470
      design inspiration from Transformers, the Reformer model also shares some architectural similar-
471
      ities with ResNets, including skip connections. However, unlike ResNets, the Reformer features
472   skip connections only between blocks rather than within them. Additionally, whereas ResNets use
473   convolutional layers within their blocks, the Reformer model employs fully connected layers similar
474   to those in attention modules of Transformers.
475
476   5.3   T RANSFORMERS
477

479   since seen wide success in other applications of deep learning as well (Devlin et al., 2018; Carion
480   et al., 2020; Dosovitskiy et al., 2020). However, the necessity of the core architectural element of
481   the transformer model – the powerful yet compute-expensive self-attention mechanism – has since
482
      been brought to question. Recent works have replaced the attention blocks with units composed of
