009                                                                                                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Recently, empowered with the powerful capabilities of neural networks, reinforce-
012            ment learning (RL) has successfully tackled numerous challenging tasks. How-
013            ever, while these models demonstrate enhanced decision-making abilities, they
014            are increasingly prone to overfitting. For instance, a trained RL model often
015            fails to generalize to even minor variations of the same task, such as a change in
016            background color or other minor semantic differences. To address this issue, we
017
               propose a dual-agent adversarial policy learning framework, which allows agents
               to spontaneously learn the underlying semantics without introducing any human
018
               prior knowledge. Specifically, our framework involves a game process between
019
               two agents: each agent seeks to maximize the impact of perturbing on the op-
020            ponent’s policy by producing representation differences for the same state, while
021            maintaining its own stability against such perturbations. This interaction encour-
022            ages agents to learn generalizable policies, capable of handling irrelevant fea-
023            tures from the high-dimensional observations. Extensive experimental results on
024            the Procgen benchmark demonstrate that the adversarial process significantly im-
025            proves the generalization performance of both agents, while also being applied to
026            various RL algorithms, e.g., Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO). With the adver-
027            sarial framework, the RL agent outperforms the baseline methods by a significant
028
               margin, especially in hard-level tasks, marking a significant step forward in the
               generalization capabilities of deep reinforcement learning.

      trained in a specific environment struggles to perform effectively in another, even when the only
      difference between environments is a subtle alteration, such as the change of colors in the scene
082
      (Cobbe et al., 2019; 2020). This limitation underscores the challenges of transferring knowledge
083
      across different contexts, emphasizing the importance of developing robust generalization strategies
084   for RL applications in dynamic and variable real-world scenarios (Korkmaz, 2024).
085
086   One approach to enhancing generalization in RL focuses on data augmentation techniques (Lee
087
      et al., 2019; Laskin et al., 2020; Zhang & Guo, 2021), which increase the diversity of training data
      by modifying input observations or environmental conditions. While this provides a straightfor-
088
      ward solution, it can introduce biases that do not align with RL objectives and often neglect the
089
      nuances of the RL process, potentially limiting effectiveness. Another approach involves regulariz-
090   ing the learned functions, drawing from traditional techniques used in deep neural networks, such as
091   batch normalization (Liu et al., 2019), contrastive learning (Agarwal et al., 2021), and loss function
092   regularization (Amit et al., 2020). However, these methods can not adequately address the unique
093   challenges of RL, as they often focus on static representations rather than the dynamic nature of
094   agent-environment interactions. Consequently, both data augmentation and traditional regulariza-
095   tion methods have limitations that hinder their ability to facilitate effective generalization in RL.
096   Adversarial learning (Pinto et al., 2017; Zhang et al., 2020; Oikarinen et al., 2021; Li et al., 2021;
097   Rahman & Xue, 2023) presents a promising direction for enhancing generalization in RL by learning
098   robust representations of irrelevant features through an adversarial process. This framework facili-
099   tates the development of agents capable of adapting to new environments by emphasizing the distinc-
100   tion between relevant and irrelevant information. While adversarial learning frameworks integrate
101   the RL process, existing methods often rely on introducing generator and discriminator networks
102   (Goodfellow et al., 2014) or seek to modify fundamental parameters of the simulation environments.
103   Such heterogeneous adversarial processes introduce additional hyperparameters and training costs,
104
      necessitating carefully designed architectures. These complexities make it challenging to establish
      a unified framework for generalization tasks across diverse domains.
105
106   To address the generalization problem in RL, in this paper, we propose a novel adversarial learn-
107   ing framework, which involves a game process between two homogeneous agents (in Figure 2).
      This framework offers three key advantages: 1). First, this general framework can integrate well

      with existing policy learning algorithms such as Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) (Schulman
109   et al., 2017). 2). Second, the adversarial process allows agents to spontaneously learn the un-
110   derlying semantics without necessitating additional human prior knowledge, thus fostering robust
111   generalization performance. 3). Lastly, our approach introduces minimal additional hyperparam-
112   eters, highlighting its potential for widespread applicability across various RL models. Extensive
113   experiments demonstrate that our adversarial framework significantly improves generalization per-
114   formance in Procgen (Cobbe et al., 2020), particularly in hard-level environments (in Figure 1).
115   This framework marks a significant advancement in addressing generalization challenges in deep
116   reinforcement learning.
117   Our contributions are summarized as follows:
118
119          • To the best of our knowledge, we are the first to theoretically prove that minimizing the
120            policy’s robustness to irrelevant features helps improve generalization performance.
121          • We propose a general adversarial learning framework to improve the generalization perfor-
122            mance of agents, which is compatible with existing policy learning algorithms.
123          • Extensive results demonstrate that applying the adversarial framework to standard RL base-
124            lines gains significant improvements in generalization performance.

347   Although estimating different states with the same semantics during training is challenging, one ef-
348   fective approach to explicitly learn the underlying semantics is to introduce the adversarial method.
349   For instance, Rahman & Xue (2023) aims to maximize expected return while minimizing the in-
350   terference from adversarial examples generated by its own generator, StarGAN (Choi et al., 2018).
351
      This process ultimately facilitates robust policy learning and helps prevent the agent from overfit-
      ting irrelevant features in high-dimensional observations, inspiring us to incorporate an adversarial
352
      framework into our approach (Section 4.3).
353
354   However, StarGAN does not entirely eliminate the biases introduced by human prior knowledge.
355   Specifically, the domain of the original input image is clustered using a Gaussian Mixture Model
356   (GMM), which inherently introduces biases from the GMM. Furthermore, the number of clusters is
357
      often determined empirically, adding another layer of human influence.
358   Therefore, firstly, a sufficiently general agent should spontaneously learn robust representations for
359   irrelevant features, rather than relying on biases introduced by human prior knowledge. Figure 3 (a)
360   shows the potential impact of introducing biases into the model. Secondly, the entire pipeline for
361   learning generalization must integrate the RL process, as the identification of irrelevant features is
362   closely linked to the objectives of RL, particularly the configuration of the reward function. Figure
363
      3 (b) demonstrates how different reward functions influence the agent’s recognition of irrelevant
      information within a simple maze environment.
364
365   In summary, we conclude that a sufficiently general agent should possess two characteristics:
366
      (1) The agent is able to spontaneously learn robust representations for high-dimensional input
367   without introducing any bias that benefits from human prior knowledge.
368
369   (2) The agent should adaptively adjust its representation of underlying semantics in response
370
      to changes in the reward function, demonstrating the ability to identify the semantics corre-
      sponding to specific objectives.
371
372   Given these two points, we will introduce a dual-agent adversarial framework in the following sec-
373   tion, which empowers agents with enhanced generalization capabilities.

519   Generalizable RL Methods. Data augmentation methods are considered as effective solutions for

521   with RL algorithms can yield improvements (Laskin et al., 2020; Kostrikov et al., 2020; Zhang

      & Guo, 2021; Raileanu et al., 2021). Domain randomization techniques (Tobin et al., 2017; Yue

523
      disturbances representing variations in the simulated environment during the training process of RL,
524
      effectively enhancing the adaptability of RL agents to unknown environments.
525
526   Adversarial Learning. Adversarial learning has been proven to be a powerful learning framework
527   (Goodfellow et al., 2014; Jiang et al., 2020; Dong et al., 2020). For instance, combining adversarial
528
      learning with randomization to enhance the generalization performance of agents (Pinto et al., 2017;
      Li et al., 2021; Rahman & Xue, 2023). In addition, adversarial attacks are also used to improve the
529
      robustness and generalization performance of agents (Gleave et al., 2019; Oikarinen et al., 2021).

      7     C ONCLUSION
532
533
      This paper introduces a dual-agent adversarial framework designed to tackle the challenges of gen-
534   eralization in reinforcement learning. By incorporating a competitive process between two agents,
535   our framework leverages adversarial loss to enable both agents to spontaneously learn effective rep-
536   resentations of high-dimensional observations, resulting in robust policies that effectively handle
537   irrelevant features. Extensive experimental results demonstrate that this framework significantly en-
538   hances both the training and generalization performance of baseline RL algorithms. Our findings
539   indicate that the adversarial approach not only improves the resilience of RL agents but also repre-
      sents a meaningful advancement in the quest for generalizable reinforcement learning solutions.
