012
013
               Estimating conditional average dose responses (CADR) is an important but chal-
014
               lenging problem. Estimators must correctly model the potentially complex rela-
015
               tionships between covariates, interventions, doses, and outcomes. In recent years,
016            the machine learning community has shown great interest in developing tailored
017            CADR estimators that target specific challenges. Their performance is typically
018            evaluated against other methods on (semi-) synthetic benchmark datasets. Our pa-
019            per analyses this practice and shows that using popular benchmark datasets with-
020            out further analysis is insufficient to judge model performance. Established bench-
021            marks entail multiple challenges, whose impacts must be disentangled. Therefore,
022            we propose a novel decomposition scheme that allows the evaluation of the im-
023            pact of five distinct components contributing to CADR estimator performance.
024
               We apply this scheme to eight popular CADR estimators on four widely-used
               benchmark datasets, running nearly 1,500 individual experiments. Our results re-
025
               veal that most established benchmarks are challenging for reasons different from
026
               their creators’ claims. Notably, we find that confounding - the key challenge that
027            motivated recent methods - does not significantly affect CADR estimation per-
028            formance for the considered datasets. We discuss the major implications of our
029            findings and present directions for future research.

036   Despite the surge in machine learning (ML) methods for estimating heterogeneous treatment effects
037   (Shalit et al., 2016; Johansson et al., 2016; Louizos et al., 2017; Shi et al., 2019; Yoon et al., 2018;
038   Johansson et al., 2020; Wager & Athey, 2018; Hill, 2011), there is comparatively little research on
039   estimating the heterogeneity of dose responses, i.e., the responses to interventions with a continu-
040   ous component. This is surprising as such interventions are ubiquitous and understanding a unit’s
041   response to them is critical in several domains, e.g., for assigning optimal discounts in marketing
042
      (Miller & Hosanagar, 2020), or for administering an effective dose of a medication (Frei & Canellos,
      1980). Estimating dose responses from observational data is distinct from estimating treatment ef-
043
      fects: units can be exposed to one of several different interventions for which the associated dose can
044
      vary across units. This introduces several unique challenges and calls for tailored methodologies.
045
046   The literature proposing ML-estimators for conditional average dose responses (CADR), also re-
047   ferred to as “individual” (Schwab et al., 2019) or “heterogeneous” (Zhu et al., 2024) dose responses,
048
      is confined to only a few methods which were proposed in the past five years (Schwab et al., 2019;
      Bica et al., 2020; Nie et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2022; Zhu et al., 2024; Nagalapatti
049
      et al., 2024; Kazemi & Ester, 2024), and that have not yet seen wide-spread usage in real-world ap-
050
      plications. While the state of the art has progressed significantly, research lacks alignment, focusing
051   on different challenges and using different benchmarking datasets. This becomes especially appar-
052   ent when reviewing the established benchmarking practices in CADR estimation: to date, the field
053   has relied on a selection of (semi-) synthetic benchmarking datasets created from manually defined
      data-generating processes (DGPs). These datasets claim to test estimators in the presence of cer-

093   Evaluating conditional average intervention response estimators is challenging. Evaluating
094   the performance of conditional average intervention response estimators is challenging, as per unit
095   of interest we can only observe the response to a single “factual” intervention, and never to any
096   other one “counterfactual” one (Holland, 1986). In conditional average treatment effect (CATE)
097   estimation, there is only one counterfactual intervention: units have received either the treatment or
098   the control. In CADR estimation, this is further complicated. First, one can apply one of several
099   distinct interventions, and second, a practically infinite number of doses. The full dose response
100
      curve hence cannot be observed. In consequence, using observational data limits evaluation to
      measuring accuracy in predicting factual responses as in, e.g., supervised learning (Hastie et al.,
101
      2017).
102

104   evaluated using semi- or fully synthetic datasets that are specified by a certain DGP. This DGP

117   Decomposing model performance.             It is common practice in ML research to decompose (or
118   to “ablate”) complex model architectures by systematically adding and removing components to
119   measure their impact on performance. In CADR estimation, such studies have, e.g., been conducted
120   by Schwab et al. (2019) and Bica et al. (2020). While such ablations inform about which part of an
121   architecture contributes to improvements in model performance, they do not allow us to understand
122   the challenges in a certain dataset. This understanding is critical to effective methodology selection
123
      in real-life applications. We attempt to close this gap in CADR estimator research by providing
      a scheme to decompose datasets, not models. Such a decomposition enables us to understand the
124
      scenarios in which an estimator might work well or fail. This data-centric decomposition of model
125
      performance is in line with calls in other ML research domains (Ye et al., 2022; Yang et al., 2022).

130   Defining CADR estimation. We find varying definitions of CADR estimation in the literature,
131   typically differing in the availability of only one (Hirano & Imbens, 2004; Nie et al., 2021) or
132   several (Schwab et al., 2019; Bica et al., 2020) distinct interventions with an associated dose. In the
133   following, we propose a unifying framework that subsumes any of these definitions.
134   We leverage the Neyman-Rubin potential outcomes (PO) framework (Rubin, 1974; Splawa-Neyman
135   et al., 1990) and expect a unit of interest i to be specified by a realization xi of random variable
136   X ∈ X with X ⊂ Rm being the m-dimensional feature space. The unit is exposed to a single
137   intervention ti sampled from T ∈ T , with the intervention space T = {ω1 , . . . , ωk } being discrete
138   with k different intervention options. Every unit is also exposed to a continuous intensity or dose di
139   sampled from D ∈ D with D ⊂ R. For the remainder of our paper and without loss of generality,
140   we set D = [0, 1]. For any realization of intervention and dose variables, there is a potential outcome
141   Y (t, d) ∈ Y ⊂ R. In line with Schwab et al. (2019) we define Y (t, d) as the “dose response”.
142   We are interested in finding an estimate of the conditional average dose response (CADR), defined

      On the identifiability of CADR. Estimating dose responses from observational data relies on
163   a set of assumptions (Stone, 1993). Specifically, the identifiability of intervention responses re-
164   quires “strong ignorability” (Rosenbaum & Rubin, 1983), subsuming “consistency”, “no hidden
165   confounders”, and “overlap”. We provide definitions of these assumptions in Appendix C. We as-
166   sume these assumptions to hold for the remainder of our work, as most previously proposed methods
167   require them. On top of that, different from estimating treatment effects (Imbens, 2004; Petersen
168   et al., 2012), there is little research on the impacts of violations of these assumptions when estimat-
169   ing dose responses.

174   Abstracting DGPs. When working with observational data, the underlying natural DGP is typi-
175   cally partially or fully unknown, adding to the challenge of evaluating CADR estimator performance
176   (cf. Section 2). To counter this, ML research typically relies on synthetic DGPs for benchmarking
177   intervention response estimators. Those DGPs assume a causal structure (in our case the SWIG in
178
      Figure 2) and define relationships between different variables, allowing for full control over chal-
      lenges in the data, such as confounding. A typical DGP for CADR consists of four components: (1)
179
      the definition of observed units through a covariate vector X, (2) an intervention assignment func-
180
      tion at (·) assigning an intervention to every unit, (3) a dose assignment function ad (·) assigning a
181   dose, and (4) an outcome function µ(·). Each of those components influences the resulting bench-
182   marking dataset. Functions can be defined to take variable inputs. By taking as input the covariate
183   vector X, at (·) and ad (·) introduce confounding in the resulting data. While sharing a unified causal
184   structure, the existing benchmarking datasets are diverse in the mechanisms by which each element
185   of the DGP is defined. This introduces ambiguity regarding the impacts of each element of the DGP
186   on model performance.
187   We provide a list of the established CADR benchmarking datasets in Appendix F along with an
188   overview of established CADR estimators, and the datasets used for their evaluation. The most
189   widely used datasets are those proposed in Bica et al. (2020) (TCGA-2) and Nie et al. (2021) (IHDP-
190   1, News-3, and Synth-1). Typically, assignment functions are non-deterministic to comply with the
191   strong ignorability assumption. For interventions, this is done by assigning non-zero probabilities to
192   the various possible interventions before sampling a factual intervention per unit. Doses are sampled
193   from a distribution with a strictly positive probability mass over D and a mode conditional on the
194   covariates of a unit, so, e.g., from a normal distribution (Nie et al., 2021) or a beta distribution (Bica
195
      et al., 2020). The outcome function µ(·) specifies a CADR by taking as input the resulting vectors
      X, D, and T . Individual responses are generated by adding random noise.

      Decomposing performance by randomizing intervention assignment. The adjustment for con-
      founding is a key challenge in CADR estimation (Bica et al., 2020; Nie et al., 2021). Yet, without
221
      further investigation, performance on a dataset could be attributed to any of the above-mentioned
222
      challenges in the data. Standard ML benchmarking practices do not reveal whether the performance
223   of an estimator is impacted by non-linearity of responses, confounding factors, or from the inter-
224   vention and dose distributions across the population. To overcome this limitation, we propose a
225   novel decomposition scheme for CADR estimator performance. Our scheme is performance metric-
226   agnostic. The choice of performance metric is arbitrary and situation-specific.
227
      We consider two boundary scenarios: In the “randomized” scenario, interventions and doses are
228
      completely randomized and sampled uniformly. In the “non-randomized” scenario, the data aligns
229   with its creators’ specifications, where interventions and doses adhere to assignment functions at (·)
230   and ad (·). We then explore three intermediary scenarios that progressively move from the “random-
231   ized” to the “non-randomized” setup.
232
      First, we generate scenario “t non-uniformity”. Per unit, an intervention is sampled from a joint
233
      distribution that is equivalent to the distribution of interventions in the “randomized” scenario. This
234
      conduct maintains the effects of at (·) on the distribution of interventions across the total population,
235   yet removes confounding, as P(t|x) = P(t) for all t ∈ T and x ∈ X. This scenario informs about
236   the impact of population-level changes in intervention distributions. Second, we generate scenario “t
237   confounding”, in which we operate under random dose assignment, but confounded interventions as
238   generated by at . This allows us to isolate the effects of intervention confounding from distributional
239   effects investigated previously. Third, and in addition to intervention confounding, we repeat the
240   process for dose assignment, generating scenario “d non-uniformity”, in which P(d|x) = P(d) for
241   all d ∈ D and x ∈ X.. The final scenario adds dose confounding, by generating doses according
242   to ad , yielding the “d confounding” or “non-randomized” scenario, which aligns with the original
243
      specifications of the data. To be in line with the causal dependencies in observational data as outlined
      in Section 3, we chose to first decompose along the treatment assignment, yet our scheme is flexible
244
      and would allow for alterations. The full decomposition scheme is summarized in Algorithm 1 in
245
      Appendix E. We refer to Appendix J for the technical implementation. We summarize the resulting
246   five scenarios below:

      Dataset. To demonstrate the workings of our decomposition scheme and how it helps to understand
      model performance, we apply it to the TCGA-2 dataset proposed by Bica et al. (2020).2 Several
276
      other benchmarking datasets for CADR estimation have been proposed in previous studies, which
277
      we enlist in Appendix F. We selected the TCGA-2 dataset for several reasons. First, it comprises
278   the assignment of one of three distinct interventions per unit and an associated dose, whereas most
279   other datasets consider a single intervention. Second, the covariate matrix used in the TCGA data
280   (Cancer Genome Atlas Research Network et al., 2013) is frequently used in other research. Third,
281   the assignment mechanisms used in the DGP find use in several succeeding papers, such as in Nie
282   et al. (2021), Schweisthal et al. (2023), Nie et al. (2021), and Vanderschueren et al. (2023). For
283   technical details, we refer to the original paper (Bica et al., 2020).
284   ML methods for CADR estimation.            We decompose the performance of selected established
285   CADR estimators, as well as several supervised estimators, to provide a broad set of baseline meth-
286   ods. Hereby, we follow calls from related ML research to test novel algorithms against a wider array
287   of methods (Qin et al., 2020). Much of recent ML research has been devoted to developing neural
288   network architectures to tackle analytical problems, with many papers ignoring classical methods
289   such as regression or tree-based approaches. First, we apply three prominent ML estimators for
290   CADR estimation, namely DRNet (Schwab et al., 2019), SCIGAN (Bica et al., 2020), and VCNet
291   (Nie et al., 2021). Each of these is a neural network (Goodfellow et al., 2016), providing several
292
      favorable characteristics to modeling data (Hornik et al., 1989). The estimators have been the first
      tailored ML methods for dose response estimation, and have been used as benchmark methodologies
293
      for several later-proposed methods. Each method uniquely tackles CADR estimation: SCIGAN uses
294
      generative adversarial networks (GANs, Goodfellow et al., 2020) to generate additional counterfac-
295   tual outcomes per observed unit and effectively randomize intervention and dose assignment, as such
296   removing confounding. DRNet trains separate models on a shared representation learner per com-
297   bination of dose intervals and interventions, to reinforce the influence of the intervention variables
298   in the model. VCNet follows a similar motivation, yet leverages a varying-coefficient architecture
299   (Hastie & Tibshirani, 1993) to accomplish this. Appendix D provides a detailed description of the
300   three methods. A complete overview of ML dose response estimators is provided in Appendix F.
301   Following calls from other fields in ML to benchmark novel methodologies against a complete
302   set of established methods (Qin et al., 2020), we further apply five supervised learning methods,
303
      which have not been sufficiently benchmarked in prior work (for a list of benchmark methodologies
      per established ML dose response estimator see again Appendix F). We apply a linear regression
304
      model, a regression tree (Breiman et al., 2017), a generalized additive model (GAM, Wood, 2017),
305
      xgboost (Chen & Guestrin, 2016) as a state-of-the-art implementation of a gradient-boosted deci-
306   sion tree (Friedman, 2001), and a simple feed-forward multilayer perception (MLP). In comparing
307   ML CADR estimators with traditional supervised learning methods we aim to understand both the
308   complexity of benchmarking datasets and differences in the performance of methods concerning
309   the challenges in dose response estimation. This facilitates practitioners and researchers in making
310   a conscious tradeoff between interpretability and performance of estimators (Bell et al., 2022), as
311   some of the targeted estimators introduce significant complexity over established techniques.

318   error” (MISE), which evaluates the mean accuracy of the CADR estimate over the intervention and
319   dose spaces. Since the MISE makes minimal assumptions about the domain of application or use of

354   5.2   R ESULTS
355
356   Insights into the dataset. Upon analyzing the decomposed performance of all estimators as pre-
357   sented in Table 1, we conclude that the TCGA-2 dataset is challenging due to dose non-uniformity,
358   rather than dose or intervention confounding. The non-uniformity of interventions only has a small
359   detrimental effect on model performance. Confounding of interventions has seemingly no signifi-
360
      cant effect on the performance, both for CADR estimators and supervised learning methods. The
      largest effect on model performance results from introducing dose non-uniformity, i.e., making some
361
      doses less likely to be observed across the population. This increases the MISE for all methods sig-
362
      nificantly when compared with scenario “t confounding”, in which doses are sampled from uniform
363   distributions. The introduction of dose confounding in scenario “d confounding” again does not ap-
364   pear to significantly impact performance. This is surprising, given the proposition of the dataset to
365   test methods for robustness against confounding biases. Further, the non-uniformity of doses and in-
366   terventions is not a causal issue, but rather related to ML challenges outside of causal inference and
367   treatment effect modeling, such as learning from imbalanced datasets (He & Garcia, 2009; Haixiang
368   et al., 2017). We also observe this behavior in analyzing performance for other datasets, as discussed
369   in Section 6.
370   Insights into model performance. Comparing performance across the different estimators allows
371   us to draw further conclusions. Estimators are only little affected by intervention non-uniformity,
372   and not affected by their confounding. Conversely, performance might even be improved by con-
373   founding. This is counter-intuitive to the reasoning that confounding might adversely affect perfor-
374   mance (Bica et al., 2020; Schwab et al., 2019). A possible explanation for this result is that, under
375   confounding, units have a higher probability of being assigned exactly those doses for which CADR
376   heterogeneity is greatest. When comparing neural architectures, CADR estimators outperform the
377
      standard MLP. However, the best-performing model is a simple regression tree. We attribute these
      results to the overall low heterogeneity of the dose response space in the dataset (cf. Appendix H).

446   7   C ONCLUSIONS AND FUTURE RESEARCH
447
448   This paper aims to understand the nature of conditional average dose response (CADR) estimation
449   and to provide tools to decompose estimator performance along challenges inherent to the field.
450   We have provided a unifying problem formulation using a single-world intervention graph (SWIG)
451   and conceptualized synthetic data-generating processes (DGPs) typically used to evaluate estimator
452   performance. We have proposed a novel decomposition scheme that allows us to attribute estimator
453   performance to five challenges: The non-linearity of response surfaces, confounding of interven-
454   tions and doses, and their non-uniform distributions. From our experiments, we conclude that the
455   inherent challenges are still poorly understood. Established benchmarks are challenging predomi-
456
      nantly due to non-uniform distributions of interventions and doses, and non-linear response surfaces.
      Confounding, on the contrary, seems to have little effect on model performance on these datasets.
457
      Additionally, by proposing a novel DGP and benchmarking dataset (IHDP-3), we show that con-
458
      founding is a challenge for estimators only when the heterogeneity of CADR is high.
459
460   Our results show critical limitations in existing ML research on CADR estimation and suggest that
461   more research is needed to develop benchmarks that accurately test the capabilities of estimators.
462
      We hence encourage researchers to adopt our decomposition for any future research. Additionally,
      we provide three further takeaways for researchers and practitioners:
463
464   (1) Confounding can materialize in various ways.           There is no clear-cut definition of how
465   confounding might materialize in a DGP and previous works have not clarified how confounding
466   is making a CADR estimation challenging. Our experiments show that the confounding in most
467   previously established datasets does not pose a challenge. Further research is needed to understand
468
      and quantify when tailored methods are needed.
469   (2) Several challenges exist in CADR estimation. In our experiments on established datasets,
470   neither confounding by intervention type nor by dose have large negative impacts on model perfor-
471   mance. Instead, the non-uniformity of doses has the largest effect. This contrasts with the claims
472   in which these benchmarks test for robustness against (any type of) confounding and reveals that
473   typical DGPs introduce more than just one challenge to CADR estimation. The finding is especially
474
      relevant as the non-uniformities of intervention types and doses are not causal problems. A potential
      future research direction might hence be the adoption of methodologies such as data-efficient ML
475
      (Olson et al., 2018; Mirzasoleiman et al., 2020).
476
477   (3) Supervised estimators might be appropriate to model CADR. Finally, our results reveal that
478   standard supervised learning methods might achieve competitive performance in estimating CADR.
479   This supports takeaway (1) and calls for comparing any future CADR estimators against a complete
480
      set of established benchmarking methods, such as gradient-boosted trees. Improving transparency
      might help practitioners gain trust in ML CADR estimators and aid future adoption.

      C    S TRONG IGNORABILITY IN THE DOSE RESPONSE SETTING
811
812
      The strong ignorability assumption (Rosenbaum & Rubin, 1983) is typically posed for estimating
813
      treatment effects, so in the setting of binary interventions. Below we provide definitions all all
814   components of this assumption that match the continuous case as tackled in our paper:
815
      Assumption 1. (Consistency) The observed outcome Yi for a unit i that was assigned intervention
816
      ti and dose di is the potential outcome Yi (ti , di ).
817
818   Assumption 2. (No hidden confounders) The assigned intervention T and dose D are conditionally
819
      independent of the potential outcome Y (t, d) given the covariates X, so {Y (t, d)|t ∈ T , d ∈ D} ⊥
                                                                                                        ⊥
      (T, D)|X
820
821   Assumption 3. (Overlap) Every unit has a greater-than-zero probability of receiving any possible
822   combination of intervention and dose, so ∀t ∈ T : ∀d ∈ D : ∀x ∈ X with P(x) > 0 : 0 <
823
      P((t, d)|x) < 1

825   D    ML ESTIMATORS FOR CONDITIONAL AVERAGE DOSE RESPONSES
826
827   We will introduce the three CADR estimators considered in our paper in detail:
828
      DRNet (Schwab et al., 2019).        DRNet uses a multitask learning approach (Caruana, 1997) to
829
      estimate conditional average dose responses. The method trains a head network per individual in-
830   tervention on a set of shared layers, as motivated by Shalit et al. (2016) in the binary intervention
831   setting. Per intervention, the method partitions the dose space D into a set of strata. For each strata,
832   another individual network is trained to infer the dose response. The architecture can further be
833   combined with regularization terms during training, to overcome potential covariate shifts between
834   different interventions.
835
      SCIGAN (Bica et al., 2020). SCIGAN assumes that there is a shift in covariates between different
836   levels of intervention and dose. This shift leads to non-adjusted estimators overfitting the training
837   data. At the core of SCIGAN is a specialized generative adversarial network (GAN) structure,
838   which attempts to generate the outcomes of counterfactual interventions and doses per unit. Creating
839   those counterfactual observations removes the covariate shift, and allows any estimator to learn an
840   unbiased dose response model.
841
      VCNet (Nie et al., 2021). VCNet proposes a varying-coefficient architecture (Hastie & Tibshirani,
842   1993). The method trains a neural network that has a network structure varying in the assigned dose
843   per unit, reinforcing the influence of the dose on the predicted outcome. Nie et al. (2021) combine
844   this architecture with estimating the generalized propensity score of the dose to calculate estimates
845   of the average dose response through an approach proposed as “functional targeted regularization”,
846   as motivated and inspired by Shi et al. (2019). In our experiments, we do not use this part of the
847   architecture and focus on estimating the conditional average dose response.

       G    T HE IHDP-3 DATASET
993
994
       This proposal of a new dataset for benchmarking CADR estimators, the “IHDP-3” dataset, is mo-
       tivated by the dose response heterogeneity (or the lack thereof) in previously established datasets.
995
       To our surprise, most estimators were not, or only a little affected by the presence of confounders
996
       in the DGP. Conversely, in those datasets, the biggest challenge in CADR estimation has been the
997    non-uniform distribution of doses (cf. Section 5 and 6).
998
999    The IHDP-3 dataset leverages the same covariate matrix as the IHDP-1 dataset (Nie et al., 2021),
1000
       notably the covariates used in the study of Hill (2011), but the assignment of intervention variables,
       as well as the outcome calculation differ significantly.
1001
1002   We consider a scenario with only one distinct intervention. Yet, different from previous datasets,
1003   the response to this intervention has higher heterogeneity in the covariates of a unit. Specifically,
1004   there are four different archetypes of responses to the intervention. We assign every unit in the
1005   covariate matrix to one of these archetypes based on their realization of binary covariates b.marr
1006
       and mom.lths. The CADR per archetype is given in Table 4. Per unit, we generate the individual
       responses by adding normally distributed random noise.

       J   I MPLEMENTATION AND HYPERPARAMETER OPTIMIZATION
1243
1244
       All experiments were written in Python 3.9 (Van Rossum et al., 1995) and run on an Apple M2 Pro
1245
       SoC with 10 CPU cores, 16 GPU cores, and 16 GB of shared memory. The system needs approxi-
1246   mately two days for the iterative execution of all experiments. The code to reproduce all experiments
1247   and figures in our paper can be found online via https://anonymous.4open.science/r/
1248   CADR-performance-deco-8148 including a reference to the necessary covariate matrices.
1249
       For SCIGAN and VCNet, we use the original implementations provided by Bica et al. (2020)
1250
       (https://github.com/ioanabica/SCIGAN) and Nie et al. (2021) (https://github.
1251
       com/lushleaf/varying-coefficient-net-with-functional-tr). All remaining
1252   neural network architectures were implemented in PyTorch (Paszke et al., 2017) using Lightning
1253   (Falcon et al., 2020). Xgboost is implemented using the xgboost library (Chen & Guestrin, 2016).
1254   GAMs were implemented using the PyGAM library (Servén et al., 2018). All other methods were
1255   implemented using the Scikit-Learn library (Pedregosa et al., 2011) and the statsmodels
1256   library (Seabold & Perktold, 2010).
1257
       For TCGA-based datasets, linear regression models and GAMs were trained using the first 50 prin-
1258   cipal components of the covariate matrix to reduce computational complexity.
1259
1260   Hyperparameter optimization. For all methods, we used a validation set for hyperparameter
1261
       optimization and chose the best model in terms of validation set mean squared errors (MSE). We
       do so to ensure fair model comparison and isolate model performance from parameter selection
1262
       procedures, as presented accompanying some estimators (Schwab et al., 2019; Bica et al., 2020). We
1263
       ran a random search over the hyperparameter ranges as listed per the model below. If not specified
1264   differently, the remaining hyperparameters are set to match the specifications of the original authors.
1265   Results are not to be compared to the original papers, as the optimization scheme and parameter
1266   search ranges differ from the original records.
