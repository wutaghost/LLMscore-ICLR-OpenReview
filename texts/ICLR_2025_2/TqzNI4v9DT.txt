009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Symbolic regression (SR) is a highly effective approach for discovering math-
012            ematical expressions directly from data. With the proliferation of various SR
013            methods, SRBench (La Cava et al., 2021) has made an important contribution
014            by offering a standardized evaluation framework that includes 130 SR datasets
015            and assesses 14 SR methods. Nevertheless, the methods incorporated in SRBench
016            are somewhat outdated, and the benchmark dataset does not encompass results
017            from more recent approaches, such as SNIP (Meidani et al., 2024). Furthermore,
018
               the evaluation metrics employed in SRBench fail to fully capture the breadth of
               symbolic regression capabilities, and the benchmark data itself exhibits scientific
019
               inconsistencies. Although Matsubara et al. (2022) address some of these issues,
020
               their approach remains incomplete. In response, we propose a novel benchmark
021            consisting of 71 expressions derived from geometric contexts, which are catego-
022            rized into three difficulty levels: easy, medium, and hard. We conduct an evalua-
023            tion of 20 SR methods on these expressions, focusing exclusively on the symbolic
024            regression capabilities of each model. These capabilities are measured in terms
025            of recovery rates across the different difficulty levels and in aggregate. Our study
026            provides a comprehensive methodology for reproducing the experiments and in-
027            cludes results for newly developed SR methods using this updated benchmark.
028            The findings reveal significant variability in the symbolic regression performance
029
               across the evaluated models.

      1   I NSTRUCTION
033
      In many aspects of life, various phenomena can be described by mathematical equations, such as
034
      Newton’s Second Law and the law of gravity. Symbolic regression (SR) is a powerful tool for uncov-
035
      ering these underlying relationships. Specifically, SR seeks to discover a mathematical expression
036   that links input and output data. Unlike traditional machine learning techniques, such as neural net-
037   works, SR might offers greater interpretability and superior generalization, avoiding the complexity
038   often associated with opaque models. For instance, the movement function of a pendulum is simpler
039   and more effective than the matrix values of an MLP. Due to these advantages, symbolic regression
040   has been applied across diverse fields, including physics (Sun et al., 2021; Udrescu & Tegmark,
041   2020; Schmidt & Lipson, 2009), network control (Sharan et al., 2022), finance (La Malfa et al.,
042   2021), and material science (Wang et al., 2019).
043   However, symbolic regression (SR) poses significant challenges due to its expansive search space.
044   The inclusion of constants further complicates the task, as they increase the complexity of potential
045   solutions. In fact, SR has been formally proven to be an NP-complete problem (Virgolin & Pissis,
046   2022; Song et al., 2024).
047
      As background for some symbolic regression methods, expression trees became a popular approach
048   for tackling SR tasks. An expression tree consists of internal nodes representing mathematical op-
049   erators (e.g., +, −, ×, ÷, log, exp, sin, cos) and leaf nodes that represent constants (e.g., 1, 2) or
050   variables (e.g., x). By recursively evaluating sub-trees, the expression tree generates a correspond-
051   ing mathematical expression. The construction of an expression tree typically follows a recursive
052   method, where operators are added in pre-order traversal until no further additions are possible.
053   This transforms SR into a sequence generation problem, akin to tasks in natural language process-
      ing (NLP).

      Symbolic regression involving constants presents a particularly challenging task. Linear methods
055   often treat constants within equations as parameters in a linear regression framework, allowing them
056   to be estimated by solving the associated function. For evolution models, models typically sample
057   random values to substitute for constants in the generated expressions. If an expression yields a
058   high loss value, the corresponding random constant is discarded, while constants producing lower
059   losses are retained. In expression tree models, constants are preserved by introducing a constant
060   token. This allows the model to solve for the constant value during the error calculation phase. At
061   this stage, the constants are treated as input variables, and the error is treated as the target value.
062   Optimization algorithms, such as BFGS (Roger Fletcher & Sons, 2013), are commonly employed to
063
      fine-tune the constants and minimize error.
064   With the development of SR methods came the emergence of benchmarks. Nyugen (Uy et al.,
065   2011) introduced one of the early symbolic regression benchmarks, comprising 12 short expressions
066   designed to evaluate SR techniques across a range of simple to moderately complex equations. Sim-
067   ilarly, Jin (Jin et al., 2019), Neat, and Keijzer (Keijzer, 2003) created their own datasets to test the
068   symbolic regression abilities of their models. However, the data in these benchmarks

081
      The introduction of the AIFeynman dataset (Udrescu & Tegmark, 2020) marked a significant step
      forward in SR benchmarking. This dataset comprises 100 equations derived from the Feynman
082
      Lectures on Physics (Feynman et al., 2015) with 20 complement as bonus equations and serves as
083
      a robust benchmark for SR tasks. Recently, the SRBench team (La Cava et al., 2021) has com-
084   bined 118 equations from this dataset with the Strogatz dataset (Strogatz, 2018), which includes
085   14 equations modeling nonlinear and chaotic dynamical processes, providing a more comprehen-
086   sive evaluation of SR methods. Additionally, SRBench includes different noise levels ranging from
087   0.0, 0.1, 0.01, 0.001, to assess the models’ ability to handle noisy data. They also utilize real-world
088   datasets to assess the machine learning capabilities of symbolic models, which falls outside the scope
089   of this paper. This benchmark evaluates 14 SR methods as baselines, resolving issues seen in earlier
090   datasets, such as a focus on equations primarily suited for testing and not for real-world scientific
091   experiments. It also provides a framework to reproduce results and test new models. However,
092
      SRBench has some limitations, as more than half of the evaluated methods are based on genetic pro-
      gramming (GP) approaches, and many are from before 2022. Furthermore, the benchmark includes
093
      some scientifically unrealistic assumptions, and has been criticized for its oversimplified sampling
094
      process and inappropriate formulas (Matsubara et al., 2022).
095
096   Subsequently, Matsubara et al. (2022) attempted to address some of the existing issues; however,
097   their evaluation was restricted to only six methods. This limited selection of baselines may result in
098
      an insufficient comparison, particularly when assessing the performance of new models introduced
      into the field.
099
100   To address these issues, we propose our geometric dataset. It consists of 2D and 3D geometric
101   problems, such as calculating the area of a triangle given the lengths of its three sides. These
102   problems are meaningful in real-world applications and complement existing physical symbolic
103   regression datasets. We categorize these datasets into three difficulty levels: easy, medium, and
104
      hard. We evaluate our 71 datasets using 20 SR baselines from 8 different approaches. The metrics
      of our benchmark are twofold: (1) the symbolic recovery rate across each difficulty level and overall,
105
      and (2) the number of expressions that can be discovered when models are allowed to run for 100
106
      parallels.

      2   D IFFERENT A PPROACH OF S YMBOLIC R EGRESSION
109
110
111
      Linear Methods: The SINDy method (Kaiser et al., 2018) applies the L1 Loss to reduce the number
      of active basis functions in a linear regression framework, thereby distilling a simple equation as a
112
      linear combination of candidate terms from a predefined library. Although SINDy is known for
113
      its interpretability and speed, its performance heavily depends on the selection of the predefined
114   library. If the true solution is not a combination of terms in the library, SINDy is unable to identify
115   it. Recently, the KAN model (Liu et al., 2024) has emerged using spline methods as an alternative
116   to improve upon these limitations.
117
      Genetic Programming: The genetic programming method (Schmidt & Lipson, 2009; Augusto &
118
      Barbosa, 2000; Gustafson et al., 2005) represents expressions as trees, which serve as the populations
119
      in the algorithm. Mutation and crossover operations modify the trees by changing sub-trees or
120   exchanging parts of the tree. The advantage of genetic algorithms in symbolic learning is their
121   ability to iteratively modify the expression tree via genetic recombination, enabling the model to
122   explore a wide range of expressions. However, a significant disadvantage is the tendency of genetic
123   algorithms to overfit; once the algorithm veers toward an incorrect solution, it is often difficult to
124   recover a correct path to the truth.
125
      Deep Learning Methods: There are two main approaches to using deep learning in symbolic re-
126   gression. One approach leverages neural networks to identify relationships between variables and
127   merge them to reduce the search space (Udrescu & Tegmark, 2020; Udrescu et al., 2020). While
128   this method simplifies the search, it requires large amounts of data and does not always succeed
129   in fitting the correct equations. The other approach replaces traditional network components (e.g.,
130   linear layers or activation functions) with symbolic functions and applies L1 loss to reduce active
131   modules, thus simplifying the output (Martius & Lampert, 2016; Sahoo et al., 2018). This approach
132   achieves lower MSE, but optimizing the sparse network to precisely recover the correct equation is
133   extremely challenging.
134   Deep Reinforcement Learning Methods: The deep reinforcement learning approach (Petersen
135   et al., 2019) frames symbolic regression as a sequential decision-making problem, where models
136   take actions at each step (e.g., adding or modifying terms) based on the current state, which is
137   evaluated using a recurrent neural network (or LSTM). After each generation, the models learn from
138   the best-generated expressions, guided by a reward function. This method effectively narrows the
139   search space but can suffer from overfitting and lack of exploration.
140   Traditional Machine Learning Methods: This approach (Sun et al., 2022; Xu et al., 2024) is
141   similar to deep reinforcement learning but uses Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) instead of neural
142   networks to guide the search process. By avoiding the need for neural network training, this method
143   is faster for smaller problems but struggles with more complex equations.
144   Transformer-Based Pretrain Methods: Inspired by the GPT models (Radford et al., 2018),
145   transformer-based symbolic regression models (Kamienny et al., 2022) pretrain on large sets of
146   artificial expressions and use this pretraining to generate expressions from input data. Subsequently,
147   genetic programming or reinforcement learning (Holt et al., 2022; Landajuela et al., 2022) is em-
148   ployed to refine the output of the transformer models. While transformers provide excellent initial
149   solutions, they may struggle with out-of-distribution data, leading to overfitting or poor performance
150   on unseen tasks.
151   Bayesian Methods: Bayesian symbolic regression (Jin et al., 2019; Guimerà et al., 2020) leverages
152   prior knowledge (e.g., preferences for basis functions, operators, or original features) and produces
153   symbolic expressions as a linear combination of concise terms, controlled by a prior distribution.
154   The symbolic regression problem is solved by sampling expression trees from the posterior distri-
155   bution using a Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) algorithm. Although this method conserves
156   memory, it can be computationally expensive and may struggle to produce accurate results due to
157   the limitations of MCMC sampling.
158   Brute-Force Search Methods: Given that symbolic regression seeks simple expressions to describe
159   phenomena, the true expression trees often have limited depth (e.g., maximum 6 layers). This obser-
160   vation motivates brute-force methods, which enumerate possible expressions layer by layer (Ruan
161   et al., 2024), as the n + 1-th layer can be constructed by combining elements from the n-th layer.
      GPU-based implementations can accelerate this search process, making brute-force methods effec-

190   3.1   DATASETS
191
      Diving into the details of our geometry dataset, it’s divided into two main sections: 2-D and 3-D
192
      geometry. The first section is a thorough compilation of 2-D geometrical shapes such as triangles,
193   rectangles, squares, and circles, complete with their corresponding equations. In the second section,
194   the dataset expands into the realm of 3-D geometry, presenting a wide array of shapes including
195   vectors, spheres, various solids, and pyramids, each paired with their relevant equations.
196
197   2-D part: The dataset begins with various types of triangles. We assess the ability to determine the
198   perimeter and area of triangles given different sets of known values: three sides (SSS), two sides with
199   the included angle (SAS), and two angles with the included side (AAS) or the opposite sides (ASA).
200   These four methods constitute the foundational techniques for establishing triangle congruence and
201   equality.
202
      For right-angled triangles, the dataset facilitates the calculation of the perimeter and area using the
203   lengths of the right sides and the hypotenuse, or by employing the length of one right side and the
204   angle opposite to it.
205
206
      Incorporated into this dataset are three pivotal laws of trigonometry: the Cosine Theorem (Law of
      Cosines), the Pythagorean Theorem, and the Sine Theorem (Law of Sines). Utilization of these the-
207
      orems allows for the resolution of the perimeter and area for a variety of straightforward geometrical
208
      constructs.
209
210   Moreover, the dataset tackles more challenging computations such as determining the circumcircle
211   and incircle radix of a triangle based solely on its three side lengths.
212   Expanding beyond simple measurements, we also delve into coordinate geometry. The dataset in-
213   cludes the calculation of the horizontal coordinates for four significant points within a triangle: the
214   centroid (center of mass), the incenter (intersection of angle bisectors), the circumcenter (intersec-
215   tion of perpendicular bisectors), and the orthocenter (intersection of altitudes). These calculations
      are vital for a deeper understanding of a triangle’s geometric properties and their applications.

      Venturing beyond triangular shapes, our collection encompasses trapezoids, specifically focusing on
      isosceles trapezoids. By utilizing the dimensions of the upper and lower bases, height, sides, or the
238
      angles adjacent to the base, one can deduce both the perimeter and area of these quadrilaterals.
239
240   The dataset also embraces the circular and elliptical geometries. It allows for the calculation of a
241   circle’s perimeter (or circumference) using its radius, as well as the perimeter and area of a sector by
242   its central angle and radius. For ellipses, the major and minor axes serve as the basis for determining
243
      the area and locating the focal points.
244   Additionally, the dataset includes rectangles and squares. Given the lengths of their edges, we can
245   easily determine their perimeter and area.

      Lastly, the dataset serves as a resource for analytical geometry concerning lines and points. It enables
247   the determination of the horizontal and vertical coordinates where two lines intersect, based on their
248   slopes and intercepts. It further aids in calculating the slope and intercept of a line passing through
249   two points, given their horizontal and vertical coordinates. Additionally, it provides the tools to find
250   the directed distance from a point to a line, integrating the line’s slope and intercept with the point’s
251   coordinates.

253   3-D part: For three-dimensional vectors, the dataset includes methods for calculating their magni-
254   tude, the cosine of the angle between two vectors, their dot product, and the horizontal coordinate of
255   their cross product. In conjunction with point coordinates, it facilitates the calculation of the directed
256   distance from a point to a plane, essential for spatial analysis.
257   In terms of solids, the dataset aids in finding the surface area and volume of cylinders using their
258   base radius and height. The same parameters are used for cones, with additional calculations for
259   their surface area and volume. For frustums, the dataset provides a method to determine the surface
260   area and volume from the radii of the upper and lower bases and the height.
261
      Spherical geometry is also covered, with the dataset enabling the calculation of a sphere’s surface
262
      area from its radius. In the study of cuboids, the dataset allows for the determination of the sum of
263   edge lengths, surface area, and volume from the lengths of the three edges. Similarly, for cubes, the
264   side length can be used to find the sum of edge lengths, surface area, and volume.

      The dataset also includes calculations for pyramids, using the base area and height to find the vol-
      ume. For regular tetrahedrons, the base edge and height, or the base edge and side, provide the
267
      necessary measurements to calculate surface area and volume. In addition, the hardest ones show
268
      that the volume of an arbitrary tetrahedron can be calculated using two equations.

      3.3   M AJOR D IFFERENCE B ETWEEN O UR DATASET AND SRB ENCH
325
326   We think we have 5 major different from the SRbench:
327
328          • Purpose of the Dataset: Our dataset is designed to identify symbolic equations that are both
329            simple and explainable to effectively solve problems. We have intentionally composed
330
               this dataset of ground truth equations rather than real-world scenarios that lack verifiable
               explanations for their functions. Consequently, traditional metrics like R-squared or other
331
               error measures are not applicable to our goals since they do not align with our focus on
332
               explainability.
333
334
             • Patterns in Geometric: Unlike the Feynman dataset, which encompasses equations from
               diverse regions and subjects, our dataset is specifically focused on geometric data within
335
               a defined area. We concentrate on discovering patterns in geometric properties such as
336
               volume, area, and length. The primary motivation for selecting geometric equations is their
337            inherent potential to unveil these patterns.
338
             • Structured Learning Progression: The Feynman dataset includes a few sequences that
339
               progress from easy to difficult, such as the series from I.6.20 a to I.6.20 b. Our dataset,
340            however, clearly illustrates many such progressions: for instance, from Helen’s law to the
341            calculation of circumcircle or incircle radii, which utilize Helen’s law, or from Pythagoras’
342            theorem to the cosine law, with the former being a special case of the latter. These process
343            facilitates a deeper and more sequential learning experience.
344          • Realistic Constraints in Equations: Our dataset includes equations with generational con-
345            straints, such as the triangle constraint where the sum of two edges must exceed the third,
346            and their difference must be less. These constraints make our data more realistic compared
347            to data from SRbench, which is typically generated from uniform distributions. This ap-
348            proach ensures that our dataset not only supports the discovery of geometric relationships
349            but also adheres more closely to real-world scenarios.
350          • Complex Equations with Few Inputs: Geometry excels at establishing intricate relation-
351            ships between variables using a minimal number of tokens, as exemplified by Helen’s law.
352            In symbolic regression, inputs are often chosen or crafted through feature engineering to
353            reduce their number, but this does not necessarily simplify the underlying relationships
354            between them. Therefore, having complex equations with few inputs is crucial because it
355
               challenges the models to uncover deep relationships without relying on a large number of
               variables.
356
357   The results from our benchmark also differ from those of SRBench. Many baselines in SRBench
358   focus primarily on the R2 score, which may suggest they are better at fitting curves. However,
359   their capability to accurately recover true symbolic equations is lacking. Moreover, thanks to the
360   Structured Learning Progression, we are able to categorize these symbolic equations and assess
361   model performance across different levels of difficulty. Additionally, the patterns in geometry enable
362   us to evaluate each model’s performance within specific patterns. This understanding allows us to
363   select better baselines for future problem-solving involving these patterns.

424         • PySR (Cranmer, 2023): Considered one of the best GP models, PySR optimizes hyperpa-
425
              rameters algorithmically and supports dimensional constraints. When an expression vio-
              lates dimensional constraints, its fitness is significantly penalized.
426
427         • gplearn (Stephens, 2016): This model retains the familiar scikit-learn fit/predict API, al-
428           lowing it to work seamlessly with existing scikit-learn pipelines and grid search modules.
429         • DEAP (Fortin et al., 2012): A novel evolutionary computation framework designed for
430           rapid prototyping and testing of ideas. It seeks to make algorithms explicit and data struc-
431           tures transparent. Many models using GP (Mundhenk et al., 2021a; Holt et al., 2022; Xu
              et al., 2024) rely on DEAP as their foundation.


                                                        8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
            • SINDy (Kaheman et al., 2020): The original SINDy model uses sparse regression tech-
433           niques, such as LASSO, to obtain expressions from linear combinations of functions in a
434           predefined library of candidate functions.
435
            • SymINDy (Kitaitsev & Manzi, 2022): This model uses GP to generate libraries of candi-
436
              date functions and integrates them with the SINDy method. The fitness value is positively
437
              correlated with the error produced by SINDy.
438
439
            • KAN (Liu et al., 2024): In KAN, traditional weight parameters at the network’s edges
              are replaced by univariate function parameters. Each node aggregates the outputs of these
440
              functions without any nonlinear transformations, relying on spline methods to replace tra-
441
              ditional weight parameters.
442
443         • SPL (Sun et al., 2022): This model contains many predefined simple expressions as mod-
444
              ules and uses the MCTS method to combine these modules into full expressions. After
              each roll-out, the best result is used as one of the modules for future iterations.
445
446         • RSRM (Xu et al., 2024): This model combines MCTS and GP to generate functions. It
447           employs double Q-learning to initialize probabilities in the MCTS module, enabling the
448           model to learn from previous roll-outs. The model also uses spline fitting to determine
449
              whether functions are odd or even and includes an MSDB block to extract useful modules
              from the best expressions for use in subsequent roll-outs.
450
451         • NeSymReS (Biggio et al., 2021): This model uses a pre-trained Transformer during the
452           pre-training phase, trained on hundreds of millions of equations specifically generated for
453
              each batch. In the test step, an encoder encodes input expressions into latent vectors, from
              which the decoder iteratively samples candidate skeletons for the symbolic equation. For
454
              each candidate, numerical constants are fitted by treating them as independent parameters.
455
456         • E2E (Kamienny et al., 2022): This model trains a Transformer on a synthetic dataset
457           to perform end-to-end (E2E) symbolic regression, directly predicting solutions without
458
              relying on skeletons. The predicted constants are refined using the BFGS algorithm
              (Roger Fletcher & Sons, 2013) as an informed starting point. Additionally, generative
459
              and inference techniques are introduced to allow the model to scale to larger problems.
460
461         • DGSR (Holt et al., 2022): This model trains a Transformer on a synthetic dataset, out-
462
              putting expressions end-to-end, which are then refined using a GP module. The framework
              can perform symbolic regression on a large number of input variables while reducing com-
463
              putational cost during inference, as it encodes the data itself rather than the entire symbolic
464
              expression tree. This is achieved by learning representations of equations that capture in-
465           variant structures across different equations.
466
467
            • TPSR (Shojaee et al., 2023): TPSR utilizes a forward planning algorithm that incorpo-
              rates Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) as a decoding strategy on top of a pre-trained
468
              Transformer-based SR model. This guides the generation of equation sequences. TPSR re-
469
              duces overall inference time by incorporating feedback during the generation process and
470           using an efficient caching mechanism.
471
472
            • SNIP (Meidani et al., 2024): SNIP (Symbolic-Numeric Integrated Pre-training) bridges
              symbolic mathematical expressions and their corresponding numeric representations. The
473
              model employs dual Transformer encoders: one dedicated to learning symbolic represen-
474
              tations and the other for numeric representations. Task-independent comparison targets
475           enhance the similarity between the two representations. The multimodal pretraining of
476           SNIP enables cross-modal understanding and generation of content.

527   5   C ONCLUSION
528
529
      In conclusion, we introduce a novel symbolic regression dataset, comprising a refined version of
      the SRBench dataset. We evaluate the performance of 20 different models across 8 methodological
530
      categories. Our analysis indicates that Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) methods are particularly
531
      well-suited to this task, due to their broad search capabilities. Parallel search algorithms and deep
532   reinforcement learning methods also demonstrate strong performance.
533
534   Furthermore, we highlight that an exclusive focus on optimizing the R2 score can result in dimin-
535
      ished symbolic recovery rates. As future work, we aim to identify additional symbolic equations for
      benchmarking and investigate optimal approaches for selecting equations under noisy conditions.

      This section provides a detailed description of the geometric dataset. The complete dataset is pre-
      sented in Tables 2, 3, and 4. The dataset comprises 8 parts: Dataset name, Equation, Category, Input
707
      data label, Input dimension, Output data label, Output dimension and Limitations. And our dataset
708
      contains two different sizes: 500 for normal model and 100000 for machine learning models that
709   need to fit the curve.
710
711
712
            • Dataset Name: This part specifies the name or identifier of the dataset, providing a clear
713
              reference for the specific set of geometric data being described according to the type of
714
              geometric shapes or phenomena it covers.
715
716
717
            • Category: This section categorizes the dataset’s difficulties. Easy polynomial expressions
718
              are classified to easy and complex polynomial with few non-linear tokens are classified to
719           medium and other hard equations are classified to hard.
720
721
722
            • Equation: This section lists the mathematical equations associated with the geometric
723           shapes or phenomena covered in the dataset. These equations are used to compute vari-
724           ous properties, such as volume, area, or perimeter, based on the input data.
725
726
727         • Input Data Label: This part describes the labels or names of the input variables. These
728           labels indicate what each input represents, such as the length of an edge, the height, or the
729           angles between edges in geometric shapes.
730
731
732         • Input Dimension: This section provides the dimensionality of the input data. It specifies the
733           number of input variables or parameters required for the equations. For instance, a triangle
734           might require two side lengths with m dim and an angle between them with rad dim.
735
736
737         • Output Data Label: This part describes the labels or names of the output variables. These
738           labels indicate the properties being calculated, such as area, volume, or perimeter.
739
740
741         • Output Dimension: This section provides the dimensionality of the output data. It specifies
742           the results generated by the equations. For instance, calculating the area of a rectangle
743           results in output dimensions of m2 .
744
745
746         • Limitations: This part outlines any constraints or limitations associated with the dataset or
747           the equations. These might include restrictions on the values of input parameters or specific
748           conditions under which the equations are valid like the sum of two edges can not be larger
749           than the other one in triangles.
750
751
752   The generation process follows this logic: values are randomly generated, with angles sampled
753   uniformly from the interval [0, π], and other values sampled uniformly from the range [1, 5]. The
754   generated values are then evaluated against predefined constraints (Limitation from dataset). If any
755   of these constraints are violated, new values are generated, and this process continues until the
      dataset size reaches either 500 or 100,000, depending on the specified target.

      B    S YMBOLIC E QUIVALENT A LGORITHMS
919
920
      The method for distinguishing a successful discovery is outlined in Algorithm 1. We choose sympy
921   (Meurer et al., 2017) to simplify the expression and human justify. We conduct 100 independent
922   runs with different random seeds, and the time limits for the easy, medium, and hard problems are
923   set to 1 hour, 5 hours, and 24 hours, respectively. Additionally, the hardware constraints include 10
924   CPU cores and one A100 GPU.
925
      We create a new algorithm to fix the wrong judgment of symbolic equations in SRbench (La Cava
926
      et al., 2021), since they consider m0 ∗ v/sqrt(1 − v ∗ ∗2/c ∗ ∗2) and m0 ∗ ∗1.5 ∗ v/(m0 ∗ (−v ∗
927   ∗2/c ∗ ∗2 + 1.0)) ∗ ∗0.5 are different equations and they might ignore equations symbolic error more
928   than 10−3 .
929
930   Algorithm 1 Algorithm for Discriminating the Correct Expression
931   Input: dataset Sdata = (X, y), ground truth expression F, input expression Fi , simplify function.
932   Output: Boolean value representing whether the input expression is correct.
933     Fi (X) → ŷ                                   ▷ Evaluate the input expression Fi on X to obtain ŷ
934     ||y − ŷ|| → err                             ▷ Compute error between predicted and actual values
935     if err ≥ 10−5 then
936           return false                                           ▷ Return false if error exceeds threshold
937     end if
938     simplify(Fi ) → Fi                                                    ▷ Simplify the input expression
939
        Fi − F → G         ▷ Compute the difference functions between input and ground truth expressions
        simplify(G) → G                                                  ▷ remove redundant sub-expressions
940
        replace constants below 10−5 in G with 0
941
        if G is empty then
942           return true                                    ▷ Return true if the expressions are equivalent
943     end if
944     G(X) → ẑ                                                              ▷ Evaluate G on X to obtain ẑ
945     ||ẑ|| → err                                         ▷ Compute error for the difference expression
946     if err ≥ 10−20 then
947           return human justify(G)             ▷ If error is still significant, defer to human justification
948     end if
949     return true                                              ▷ Return true if the difference is negligible

       We have expanded our dataset to include four additional experiments concerning noise, speed, more
1161
       baselines, and the introduction of constants.
1162
1163   Noise: We incorporated datasets with two levels of noise—1e-5 and 0.0001—to evaluate how well
1164   the models perform under noisy conditions. Unlike typical setups where only the target variable
1165
       is affected by noise, we introduced noise equally to both the input variables and the target. This
       simulates a more realistic scenario where the measurement of both features and targets may be
1166
       impacted by noise.
1167
1168   Speed: To assess the computational efficiency of each baseline, we measured the speed by averaging
1169   the results of 100 parallel runs for each category. Understanding the speed of each model is crucial
1170   as it allows us to create a Pareto front that balances the recovery rate against the computational time
1171
       cost, providing a comprehensive view of model performance.
1172   More Baselines: Recognizing the importance of robust comparison, we included additional base-
1173   lines from the era of genetic programming. Specifically, we added Operon to our benchmarking
1174   table to evaluate its performance against other established methods.
1175   Adding Constants: Since our dataset primarily comprises geometric equations where the only con-
1176   stant is π, we tested the ability of the symbolic regression models to handle constants by multiplying
1177   each dataset by a uniform constant ranging from 0 to 5. This test aims to assess each baseline’s ca-
1178   pability in accurately recovering symbolic expressions that incorporate constants.
