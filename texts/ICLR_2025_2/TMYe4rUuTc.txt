009                                                     A BSTRACT
010
011              Current RLHF approaches for aligning large language models (LLMs) typically
012              assume a fixed prompt distribution, which is sub-optimal and limits the generaliza-
013              tion capabilities for language models. To address this issue, we introduce a general
014              framework that casts alignment as an asymmetric game between two players: (i)
015              a creator that generates increasingly informative prompt distributions using the
016              reward model, and (ii) a solver that learns to produce more preferred responses
017
                 on prompts produced by the creator. This framework of Evolving Alignment via
                 Asymmetric Self-Play (eva), results in a simple and scalable approach that can
018
                 utilize any existing RLHF algorithm. eva outperforms state-of-the-art methods
019
                 on widely-used benchmarks, without the need of any additional human crafted
020              prompts. Specifically, eva improves the win rate of G EMMA 2-9 B - IT on Arena-
021              Hard from 51.6% to 60.1% with DPO, from 55.7% to 58.9% with SPPO, from
022              52.3% to 60.7% with SimPO, and from 54.8% to 60.3% with ORPO, surpassing
023              its 27B version and matching claude-3-opus. This improvement is persistent
024              even when new human crafted prompts are introduced. Finally, we show eva is
025              effective and robust under various ablation settings.

042   1    I NTRODUCTION
043
044   Long-lived artificial intelligence must deal with an ever-evolving, open-ended world, yet currently
045   face constraints in both the scale and quality of available data, and the growth rate at which new,
046   useful information is created. High quality human data, crucial for scaling large language model
047
      (LLM) based intelligence, is projected to run out in the next few years (Villalobos et al., 2024); the
      quality of such data is also expected to stagnate: as LLMs become more capable, they need to solve
048
      increasingly complex or new challenges, requiring training data beyond abilities of humans to create.
049
      This necessitates a new fundamental mechanism for self-improving, where models can continuously
050   self-generate and self-solve harder problems. We thereby investigate the research question below:
051
                        Can language models self-create new, learnable tasks to work on,
052
                     to self-improve to generalize better for human preferences alignment?

065   solver game for self-improving language models. Our proposed eva strategically evolves prompt
066   distributions with a creator policy, which synthesizes prompts with an easy-to-implement estimate,
067   sample then evolve procedure; specifically, it estimates the informativeness for each prompt by how

072
073   Many preference optimization algorithms (Christiano et al., 2017; Rafailov et al., 2023; Zhao et al.,
074   2023; Wu et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2023a; Guo et al., 2024) have been proposed to improve the
075   alignment of language models, however, they all default to fixed prompt training distributions. Such
076
      fixed training paradigm inevitably leads to: (i) generalization issues (models may underperform or
      hack on instructions that are insufficiently represented within the fixed set) and (ii) efficiency issues
077
      (data annotation and model training are costly, however not all prompt provide the same utility; it
078
      is wasteful to invest in sub-optimal fixed set, while identifying informative prompts through human
079   efforts is expensive and time-consuming) (Team et al., 2023; 2024b; Dubey et al., 2024).
080
081   The objective thereby should not only be to optimize over a specific, static distribution of prompts, yet
082
      to develop an agent that can autonomously evolve the training data distribution for self-improvement,
      to align well across unseen, novel environments or tasks (instantiated by prompts).
083
084   Thus, we develop eva (Evolving Alignment via Asymmetric Self-Play), as illustrated in Figure 1.
085   Central to our approach is a game with the minimax-regret objective, achieved through alternating
086   optimization between creating prompts and solving them. The interplay encourages evolving curric-
087   ula (Parker-Holder et al., 2022), potentially benefits both generalization and efficiency (see also § 3.4).
088
      Orthogonal to many recent self-play studies in LLM alignment (Munos et al., 2023; Choi et al., 2024;
      Wu et al., 2024), eva is asymmetric (Sukhbaatar et al., 2017), with two policies of different goals:
089
090             • Creator: evolves the prompt distribution for alignment.
091             • Solver: produces responses and optimizes alignment based on the evolving prompts.
092
093   Our main contributions are summarized as:
094
                • A new principle: We propose a generalized Open-Ended RLHF objective for aligning
095
                  language models, which seeks to jointly optimize the prompt distribution and the response
096               policy, thus incentivizes models to self-improve to generalize well on new, unseen tasks
097               beyond the initial training prompt distribution for alignment, as in Definition 1.
098
                • A new algorithm: To optimize the objective, we design a practical algorithm via asymmetric
099               self-play, which is implemented through alternating optimization in a creator-solver game,
100               and can be easily plugged into any existing alignment pipeline, as in Algorithm 1.
101
                • State-of-the-art performance: We empirically validate our method on public alignment
102
                  benchmarks and present general strong performance improvement when plugged in with
103               different preference optimization algorithms (i.e., DPO, SPPO, SimPO, ORPO). We also
104               conduct extensive ablation studies that provide additional insights on the choice of informa-
105               tiveness metric, reward model, and training schedules, as in § 4.
106
107   eva is easy to implement. We hope it can serve as a scalable method for the research community to
      build open-ended, robust, and self-improving language agents, that align with human values.

      Section overview. We aim to develop language models that can self-improve to generalize well on
163   new, unseen tasks beyond the initial training prompt distribution for alignment. In §3.1, we present
164   the limitations of classical RLHF and generalize it to the new objective of open-ended RLHF. In
165   §3.2, we introduce the creator-solver game to tractably optimize the objective. In §3.3, we detail
166   the practical algorithm, compatible with any preference optimization method as the solver, with our
167   designed creator in the loop. Lastly in §3.4, we present various interpretations for eva.

169   3.1      T HE P RINCIPLE : O PEN -E NDED RLHF FOR J OINT S ELF -I MPROVEMENT
170
171   Intuition. Classical RLHF (cf., Eq. 1) optimizes over a static prompt distribution, meaning that the
172
      agent is only aligned to a fixed prompt set D, making it brittle when it is evaluated on new problems
      from the ever-changing real world. Our Open-Ended RLHF breaks away from this static framework,
173
      with the goal to develop an agent that generalizes well across unseen, novel environments (where the
174
      tasks entailed in prompts may not have been explicitly encountered during training). To achieve this,
175   we must design a new objective that agents can generate its own problems for self-improvement.

207                • By design, the creator can guide the solver with an informative prompt curriculum, for it to

      3.3     T HE P RACTICAL A LGORITHM
244
      We now illustrate eva of Algorithm 1, with practical implementations specified in § A.
245
246   3.3.1    T HE C REATOR S TEP : E STIMATE , S AMPLE THEN E VOLVE
247
      Plainly, the creator finds most useful prompts and generate variants of them for preference optimiza-
248   tion. One may relate this to evolution strategies (Schwefel, 1977) which find the most promising
249   species, then mutate and crossover, or to curriculum RL (Parker-Holder et al., 2022) which finds
250   environments with high-regret levels, then edits within some distance. As in Section 3.2, we do not
251   seek a differentiable creator in this work. The creator is implemented in three steps as in Figure 3.
252
      Step 1: info(·) – estimate the informativeness. For each x in the prompt set Xt , we generate
253
      responses, annotate rewards and estimate a informativeness metric to x by Eq. 11 (see also Table 2).
254
255   Step 2: sample(·) – weighted sampling for an informative subset. By using the informativeness
256   metric as the weight, we sample an informative prompt subset Xtinfo to be evolved later. This is
257   similar to finding high-regret levels in curriculum RL.
258   Step 3: evolve(·) – evolving for a proximal region of high-advantage prompts. Our algorithm is
259   agnostic to and does not rely on any specific evolving method. We take EvolInstruct (Xu et al., 2023a)
260   as an off-the-shelf method, which conducts in-depth (i.e., adding constraints, deepening, concretising,
261   complicating) and in-breadth evolving (i.e., mutation) for prompts. Specifically, we iterate over each
262   prompt in the Xtinfo , where each one is evolved to multiple variations, then optionally mix the newly
263   generated prompts with a uniformly sampled buffer from Xt to create Xt′ .
264
265
      3.3.2    T HE S OLVER S TEP : S OLVE THEN O PTIMIZE
266   This step is the classical preference optimization (Rafailov et al., 2023), where responses are generated
267   and the gradient descent is performed. Take the pointwise reward model setting as an example, for
268   every prompt, we sample n responses with reward annotated for each; we take the responses with
269   the maximal and the minimal reward to construct the preference pairs, then optimize upon. This
      implicitly minimizes the regret to the KL-optimal policy, which we present in more details at § G.

      3.4   U NDERSTANDING THE I NFORMATIVENESS P ROXY IN D IFFERENT I NTUITIVE WAYS
274
275   Learning potential. Our metric intuitively identifies the learning potential of a prompt by measuring
276   the gap between the best and worst response to it from the solver. We reason, that prompts eliciting
277   both high-reward and low-reward outcomes, reflect learnable tasks where the model is capable of
278   improving but has not yet mastered, thereby implying learning potential (cf., Jiang et al. (2021b)).
279
      Worst-case guarantees. The minimax-regret objective, by design, leads to solvers that perform
280
      robustly across the prompt space, thus gives the worst-case guarantee. While exact equilibrium may
281
      not be attainable with approximation, our empirical results in § 4.2.1 demonstrate robustness.
282
283   Auto-curricula for the players. We visualize curriculum induced by eva in § E. With the stochastic
284   policy, the advantage may be heuristically understood as the reward difference between a base solver
285   and a reference solver. Rather than optimizing separate solvers (Dennis et al., 2020), we sample
286   multiple times from the same policy to create the pair. In this way, the creator is incentivized to
287   produce new prompts that are just out of the comfort zone of solvers (Chaiklin et al., 2003):
288           • For overly challenging prompts, both solutions perform poorly, leading to a low proxy.
289           • For overly easy prompts, the base solution already performs well, again giving a low proxy.
290           • The optimal strategy is to find prompts that are just beyond the solver’s current capability.
291
292   Auto-curricula inherent to Contrastive Optimization. Contrastive preference optimization gen-
293   eralizes DPO and a family of algorithms (c.f., Hejna et al. (2023); Rafailov et al. (2023); Tang et al.
294   (2024)), many of whose losses monotonically decrease as the contrastive ratio increases. Here, by
295   Eq. 2 and Eq. 6, the contrastive ratio can be written via the advantage-based proxy:
296                                          A⋆min (x) = β · ∆xθ⋆ ; ref .                                (13)
297   By our proxy, we implicitly incentivize the creator to generate prompts that bring the most contrastive
298   responses, which decrease the loss the most. This matches the curriculum learning literature, which
299   prioritizes (in eva, generatively prioritizes) examples with smaller losses for better convergence
300   and generalization (Bengio et al., 2009). We hence suggest the Contrastive Curriculum Hypothesis :
301   in contrastive preference optimization, prioritizing prompts with higher contrastive ratio improves
302   sample efficiency and generalization. We show initial empirical results on this in § 4.2.1 and § 4.2.4.

      4.1    M AIN R ESULTS
325
326   In general, eva brings notable gains in alignment without relying on any human-crafted data, thus
327   offering more efficiency. In the base setup, building on the one-iteration finetuned model (θ0→1 ),
328   eva adds a creator to self-evolve the prompt set of the initial iteration and uses any preference
329   optimization algorithm for an additional open-ended RLHF iteration, resulting in θ1→1̃ 2 .
330
331   eva achieves self-improvement. As shown in red rows in Table 1, eva yields notable performance
332
      improvement over θ0→1 across different optimization algorithms, especially on the harder Arena-
      Hard benchmark, which is recognized to be more challenging and distinguishable among others
333
      due to the complexity of its prompts and its fairer scoring system (Li et al., 2024b; Meng et al.,
334
      2024). Specifically, eva brings 8.4% gain with SimPO as the solver, and 8.5% gain with DPO as
335   the solver, surpassing its 27B version and matching claude-3-opus-240229 as reported on the
336   AH leaderboard, while using fully self-automated prompt generation for alignment. Interestingly,
337   eva brings the least gains on AlpacaEval 2.0, a simpler evaluation benchmark. This indicates
338   eva improves the most for challenging tasks.
339
340   eva can surpass human-crafted prompts. We further show that eva-prompt-trained models
341   (θ1→1̃ ) can match and even outperform those trained on additional new prompts from UltraFeedback

      5    R ELATED W ORKS
487
488   Self-improving algorithms and iterative optimization. This line of work focuses on iteratively
489   generating samples from the response policy and continuously re-training the policy by selected
490   self-generated samples. Major works include ReST (Gulcehre et al., 2023; Singh et al., 2023),
491   STaR (Zelikman et al., 2022), RFT (Yuan et al., 2023), RAFT (Dong et al., 2023), self-improving
492
      LLMs (Huang et al., 2022; Yuan et al., 2024); in the context of preference optimization, iterative
      DPO (Xu et al., 2023b; Tajwar et al., 2024; Tran et al., 2023; Xiong et al., 2024; Pang et al., 2024) has
493
      proven effective. Most works focus on self-training by improving in Y | X , while we jointly optimize
494
      both responses and prompts via generative exploration in the (X , Y) space. Among them, we also
495   distinctly present a game-theoretic framework with the minimax-regret principle as the guidance.
496
497   Prompt synthesis for language models. Existing works include Self-Instruct (Wang et al., 2022),
498   WizardLM (Xu et al., 2023a; Luo et al., 2023), Self-Align (Sun et al., 2024), Glan (Li et al., 2024a),
499
      EvoPrompt (Guo et al., 2023), Magpie (Xu et al., 2024) and others (Long et al., 2024). eva is
      an orthogonal contribution since any synthesis method can be plugged in as the evolve(·) for the
500
      creator. Importantly, our work presents a new reward-related metric to endow prompt the notion
501
      of informativeness, with new implications as in § 3.4. We also focus on preference optimization
502   algorithms, while those existing works primarily use synthesized prompts in an SFT-only way.
503
504   Self-play and curriculum RL. Agents trained on a fixed data distribution are often brittle and may
505
      struggle to adapt to the real world (Hughes et al., 2024a). Self-play (Samuel, 1959; Goodfellow et al.,
      2014; Silver et al., 2016) addresses this by having the agent learn through self-interaction, thus creating
506
      more diverse experiences and automatic curricula. In asymmetric self-play, the paradigm centers on
507
      “Alice proposing a task, and Bob doing it” (Sukhbaatar et al., 2017; Samvelyan et al., 2023; Beukman
508   et al., 2024a; Dennis et al., 2020). We revive the classical asymmetric self-play principle (Sutton
509   et al., 2011) in optimizing language models. Unlike traditional curriculum RL (Parker-Holder et al.,
510   2022), which usually renders environments from specified levels (Dennis et al., 2020), our approach
511   is generative by nature, as we directly generate contexts from the auto-regressive language models.
512
      Self-play in RLHF. A growing line of research frames RLHF as a symmetric self-play game, where
513
      both players are response players (Munos et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2024; Choi et al., 2024; Rosset et al.,
514   2024). However, these methods still rely on a fixed prompt distribution thus is sub-optimal. In contrast,
515   we solve this by asymmetric self-play, enabling evolving prompt distributions for more generalizable
516   language agents. During our work, we notice one concurrent paper adopting the asymmetric two-
517   player setup (Zheng et al., 2024), however (i) it applies to adversarial attack tasks instead of general
518   alignment benchmarks, (ii) it is incompatible w/ direct preference optimization, and (iii) it relies
519   on the maxmin principle (which is known to be producing unlearnable environments (Dennis et al.,
520   2020)) instead of the minimax regret principle (Fan, 1953; Savage, 1951) as we do. We also first
521   precisely defined the new problem of open-ended RLHF, which generalizes over classical RLHF.
522
523
      6    C ONCLUDING R EMARKS
524   Limitations and future directions. eva defines a new paradigm for alignment, opening up many
525   new directions, e.g., (i) extending to differentiable creator policies, combining w/ other evolve(·)
526   methods; (ii) evolving for more iterations w/ on-policy solvers like RLOO (Ahmadian et al., 2024);
527   (iii) investigating exploration bonuses for distribution diversity and coverage, and the self-consuming
528   loop (Gerstgrasser et al., 2024); (iv) extending the game with more players for full automation
529   (e.g., rewarders, critics, rewriters, verifiers, retrievers); (v) extending from alignment to reasoning
530   (e.g., auto-conjecturing for theorem proving (Poesia et al., 2024) can be cast as an asymmetric game),
531
      or from the bandits to the trajectories w/ process reward models and hierarchical tree search for
      creator and solver generations; (vii) further scaling up w/ million-level prompts for post-training.
532
533   Conclusions. eva is a new, simple and scalable framework for aligning language models, and can
534   be plugged into any existing alignment pipeline. The primary takeaway may be that RLHF can be
535   made open-ended: (i) self-evolving joint data distributions can bring significant gain (as shown across
536   various optimization algorithms), and (ii) reward advantage acts as an effective metric informing the
537   collection and creation of future prompts for alignment. eva presents a new view of alignment by
538
      framing it as an asymmetric game between a creator generating new and learnable prompts and a
      solver producing preferred responses. eva also incentivizes agents to create problems rather than to
539
      simply solve problems, which is a key feature of intelligence, yet model trainers often neglect.

923   G.2.1       T HE A SYMMETRIC G AME F ORMULATION FOR U NSUPERVISED E NVIRONMENT D ESIGN
924
925   While we cannot directly train the agent with the intractable pref (x) of the open-ended world, it is
926   possible to curate a curriculum of prompt distributions to improve over the static distribution
927   and support the continual training of the policy πθ (·|x), for it to keep improving and succeed over
928
      the full task space, thus conceptually approaching pref (x). This is often framed as an asymmetric
      two-player game.
929
930   Dennis et al. (2020) first formally define this problem as Unsupervised Environment Design (UED).
931   The idea is that while the real-world environments are inexhaustible and hard to tract, there may
932   exist some free parameters (e.g., height and roughness in a maze) which one may control to generate
933   new environments; UED then concerns about designing a distribution of those free parameters
934
      (i.e., settings) to create new fully specified environments, that can be used to train the agents.
935   In this setup, one player, the creator, generates new environments based on some specific decision
936   rules (see the following), while the other player, the solver, optimizes its policy within these training
937   environments, and the process continues iteratively. Common heuristic strategies include:
938
939
                • Randomization: environments are generated uniformly and independently of the solver’s
                  current policy. This method is simple but less effective (Tobin et al., 2017).
940
941             • Maximin: the creator generates environments that minimize the solver’s maximum possible
942               reward, which can often lead to unsolvable scenarios (Khirodkar and Kitani, 2018).
943             • Minimax regret: The creator targets environments that maximize the solver’s regret, defined
944               as the difference between the optimal return achievable and that of the solver’s current
945               policy (Beukman et al., 2024b). The regret is often conceived as the creator’s utility.
946
947   Among them4 , the minimax regret approach presents a sweet spot where the creator can create
948   hard yet solvable environments, and is often empirically better. The minimax regret strategy also
949
      implies that the agent’s policy is trained to perform well under all levels/settings, thus enjoys a
      worst-case guarantee. However, while it is often straightforward for the solver to minimize the regret
950
      (e.g., through direct policy optimization, as we discuss in § G.3), the optimal policy remains unknown
951
      during the optimization process, thus regret as the decision signal is often intractable to the creator
952   – which requires approximation (as an amusing side note, this is described as the Achilles’ heel of
953   those curriculum RL methods by Parker-Holder et al. (2022)).
954
955   G.2.2       A PPROXIMATING THE R EGRET AND G ENERATING N EW E NVIRONMENTS
956
957   In general, the creator design in this line of research contains two steps:
958
959            1. identifying high-regret levels using different (often heuristic) regret approximation;
960            2. generating new environments by making variations or retrieving from buffers on those
961               high-regret levels.
962
963   We hereby review major works on regret approximation and environment generation as follows:
964   Dennis et al. (2020) propose joint training for the creator and two competing solvers.
965
966             • Regret approximation: here, two solver policies are trained, with the regret approximated
967               as the difference in their returns. During each optimization step, one solver maximizes
968               this regret, the other minimizes it, and the creator maximizes it.
969             • Environment generation: the system directly sample the parameter from the creator policy
970               and use that to specify the environment.
971
         4
             We have implemented variants of these in § 4.2.1, and show minimax regret is empirically better.


                                                             18
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


972
       Jiang et al. (2021b) propose to random sampling on high-regret levels.
973
974           • Regret approximation: as a heuristic, the authors use positive value loss, which is a function
975             of Generalized Advantage Estimate (Schulman et al., 2015) (which itself is a function of the
976             TD error – the difference between the expected and the actual returns) as the creator’s utility.
977           • Environment generation: the creator have a rolloing buffer of highest-regret levels by
978             random searching on relevant configurations.
979
980    Jiang et al. (2021a) further propose a double-creator setting based on (Jiang et al., 2021b), where one
981
       creator is actively generating new environments, and the other is retrieving from the buffer.
982    Parker-Holder et al. (2022) propose to sample high-regret levels and generate new environments by
983    making edits on existing ones. The regret approximation is the same as (Jiang et al., 2021b) – the
984    positive value loss. For the environment generation, the authors suggest a general editing/mutation
985    mechanism, where the creator chooses from high-regret levels and make small variations within an
986    edit distance, which by heuristics will lead to the discovery of more high-regret environments. There
987
       is an additional filtering step: they do not directly train on the newly generated levels, but evaluate on
       those levels first, then add only the high-regret ones to the training buffer.

993    Next, we discuss the regret minimization and maximization in our setting for alignment. Specifically,

       The design of our game-theoretic framework for language model post-training is inspired from many
       prior works in open-ended learning. The central idea of open-ended learning is not to optimize for
1072
       a specific, static distribution, but to develop an agent that can generalize well across unseen, novel
1073
       environments, which are the environments that the agent has not been explicitly trained on. To achieve
1074   this, unsupervised environment design proposes to generate environments that present a curriculum of
1075   increasing complexity for the agent to evolve, which ensures that the agent’s learning is not narrow, but
1076   broad enough to handle the diversity of complexity of future environments. In such curriculum, as the
1077   agent solves simpler environments, it moves on to more difficult ones, thus progressively builds more
1078   sophisticated strategies. Furthermore, by adopting a minimax regret framework, this approach adds a
1079   layer of robustness by minimizing the agent’s performance gap in worst-case (i.e., most adversarial)
       environments. In addition to distinctions discussed in § 5, we here list several foundational works in


                                                          20
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1080
       this line, and encourage the LLM community to explore with more rigor and depth: Schmidhuber
1081   (1991) presents an initial investigation into open-ended learning via self-supervised curiosity-driven
1082   exploration; Wang et al. (2019) emphasize co-evolution of environments and agent policies by training
1083   a population of agents that adapt to and solve progressively complex challenges; Dennis et al. (2020)
1084   formally introduce the notion of Unsupervised Environment Design (UED), where a protagonist and
1085   antagonist agent pair simulates regret by competing in shared environments, driving the protagonist
1086   (the main learner) to adapt to increasingly challenging scenarios; Jiang et al. (2021b) introduce
1087   Prioritized Level Replay (PLR), which uses a rolling buffer of high-regret levels to dynamically
1088   adjust the training curriculum, and selects levels with the higher learning potential; Parker-Holder
1089
       et al. (2022) further propose improvements by editing previously high-regret levels; Hughes et al.
       (2024b) present a formal definition for open-ended system with respect to novelty and learnability,
1090
       that generalizes various systems, e.g., AlphaGo (Silver et al., 2016), AdA (Team et al., 2021), etc.

1092   I   E XTENDED L ITERATURE R EVIEW IN B I -L EVEL RLHF
1093
1094   Bi-level optimization refers to optimization problems where the cost function is defined w.r.t. the
1095
       optimal solution to another optimization problem (Grosse, 2022). There is a recent line of works
       applying bi-level optimization to RLHF. While they all rely on a fixed dataset of prompts, eva propose
1096
       to dynamically update the prompt set, as in § 1. We present a detailed comparison of eva with Ding
1097
       et al. (2024); Shen et al. (2024); Makar-Limanov et al. (2024). We sincerely thank the anonymous
1098   reviewer for the kind references, and welcome suggestions for any other works we may have missed.
1099
1100   Ding et al. (2024) formulate iterative online RLHF as a bi-level optimization problem, where the
1101
       upper-level represents the reward learning, and the lower-level represents the policy optimization.
       Leveraging reward re-parameterization tricks in Rafailov et al. (2023), Ding et al. (2024) reduces the
1102
       problem to a single-level objective with regard to the policy. The differences of this work and our
1103
       work lie in the prompt distribution and preference oracle: (i) eva features by dynamic prompt set
1104   generation for Open-Ended RLHF, whereas (Ding et al., 2024) remains using a static prompt set;
1105   (ii) we assume the existence of the preference oracle (as discussed in § 4), while Ding et al. (2024)
1106   consider online training of reward models and ablate on self-rewarding by the current LLM policy.
1107   Our usage of a pre-trained reward model follows from industrial practices (Team et al., 2023; 2024b),
1108   which is also commonly used by prior works in academia (Meng et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2024).
1109
       Makar-Limanov et al. (2024) provide an interesting exploration on formulating RLHF as a leader-
1110   follower game, where the language model (LM) policy is the leader and the reward model (RM) policy
1111   is the follower, and the solution is Stackelberg equilibrium (von Stackelberg, 1934; Rajeswaran et al.,
1112   2020), where the leader does not likewise best respond to the follower’s strategy. Here, following
1113   the curriculum RL literature (Dennis et al., 2020; Parker-Holder et al., 2022), we seek the Nash
1114   equilibrium (Nash et al., 1950) between the creator for prompt generation and the solver for response
1115   generation. In the current setting of eva, the goal is to search for an optimal solver policy with a
1116   best supporting prompt distribution, and an optimal prompt distribution with a best supporting solver
1117   policy. Nevertheless, the LM-RM iterative optimization may be added on top of eva’s framework,
1118
       and we look forward to future works exploring the leader-follower re-formulation of eva.
1119   Shen et al. (2024) present a rigorous theoretical work (though it does not directly involve practical
1120   post-training of large language models). The authors propose to reduce the bi-level problem to
1121   a single-level problem with a penalty-based reformulation, and apply it in the setting of LM-RM
1122   optimization within a fixed environment, whereas eva focuces on dynamic prompt generation and
1123   practically train large language models with extensive empirical experiments conducted. We believe it
1124
       would be interesting to adapt similar first-order optimization techniques to solve Open-Ended RLHF.
1125   In summary, existing bi-level RLHF works focus on online optimization of both the RM and the
1126   LM (as the response policy), all with fixed prompt/state distribution. eva presents an orthogonal
1127   direction on dynamic prompt generation for Open-Ended RLHF, with an empirical algorithm which
1128   attains state-of-the-art performance with large language models on a variety of benchmarks. It is
1129   possible to incorporate the online RM training within eva – we have shown in § 4.2.3 that eva
1130
       scales with quality of reward models, thus integrating online RM training may further enhance
       performance and mitigate potential distributional mismatch problems as we evolves for more prompts.
1131
       This direction may have not been widely adopted in real-world training of language models, due
1132
       to concerns about practicality (Team et al., 2023; 2024a;b; Adler et al., 2024). We look forward to
1133   future works exploring efficient variations unifying eva and existing bi-level RM-LM frameworks.

       Dennis et al. (2020) first formally define this problem as Unsupervised Environment Design (UED).
2053   The idea is that while the real-world environments are inexhaustible and hard to tract, there may
2054   exist some free parameters (e.g., height and roughness in a maze) which one may control to generate
2055   new environments; UED then concerns about designing a distribution of those free parameters
2056   (i.e., settings) to create new fully specified environments, that can be used to train the agents.
2057
       In this setup, one player, the creator, generates new environments based on some specific decision
2058
       rules (see the following), while the other player, the solver, optimizes its policy within these training
2059   environments, and the process continues iteratively. Common heuristic strategies include:
2060
2061             • Randomization: environments are generated uniformly and independently of the solver’s
2062               current policy. This method is simple but less effective (Tobin et al., 2017).
2063             • Maximin: the creator generates environments that minimize the solver’s maximum possible
2064               reward, which can often lead to unsolvable scenarios (Khirodkar and Kitani, 2018).
2065
                 • Minimax regret: The creator targets environments that maximize the solver’s regret, defined
2066               as the difference between the optimal return achievable and that of the solver’s current
2067               policy (Beukman et al., 2024b). The regret is often conceived as the creator’s utility.
2068
2069   Among them5 , the minimax regret approach presents a sweet spot where the creator can create
2070   hard yet solvable environments, and is often empirically better. The minimax regret strategy also
2071   implies that the agent’s policy is trained to perform well under all levels/settings, thus enjoys a
2072   worst-case guarantee. However, while it is often straightforward for the solver to minimize the regret
2073
       (e.g., through direct policy optimization, as we discuss in § G.3), the optimal policy remains unknown
       during the optimization process, thus regret as the decision signal is often intractable to the creator
2074

       Makar-Limanov et al. (2024) provide an interesting exploration on formulating RLHF as a leader-
2323   follower game, where the language model (LM) policy is the leader and the reward model (RM) policy
2324   is the follower, and the solution is Stackelberg equilibrium (von Stackelberg, 1934; Rajeswaran et al.,
2325   2020), where the leader does not likewise best respond to the follower’s strategy. Here, following
2326   the curriculum RL literature (Dennis et al., 2020; Parker-Holder et al., 2022), we seek the Nash
2327   equilibrium (Nash et al., 1950) between the creator for prompt generation and the solver for response
2328   generation. In the current setting of eva, the goal is to search for an optimal solver policy with a
2329   best supporting prompt distribution, and an optimal prompt distribution with a best supporting solver
2330   policy. Nevertheless, the LM-RM iterative optimization may be added on top of eva’s framework,
2331
       and we look forward to future works exploring the leader-follower re-formulation of eva.
2332   Shen et al. (2024) present a rigorous theoretical work (though it does not directly involve practical
2333   post-training of large language models). The authors propose to reduce the bi-level problem to
2334   a single-level problem with a penalty-based reformulation, and apply it in the setting of LM-RM
2335   optimization within a fixed environment, whereas eva focuces on dynamic prompt generation and
2336   practically train large language models with extensive empirical experiments conducted. We believe it
2337
       would be interesting to adapt similar first-order optimization techniques to solve Open-Ended RLHF.
2338   In summary, existing bi-level RLHF works focus on online optimization of both the RM and the
2339   LM (as the response policy), all with fixed prompt/state distribution. eva presents an orthogonal
2340   direction on dynamic prompt generation for Open-Ended RLHF, with an empirical algorithm which
2341   attains state-of-the-art performance with large language models on a variety of benchmarks. It is
2342   possible to incorporate the online RM training within eva – we have shown in § 4.2.3 that eva
2343
       scales with quality of reward models, thus integrating online RM training may further enhance
       performance and mitigate potential distributional mismatch problems as we evolves for more prompts.
2344
       This direction may have not been widely adopted in real-world training of language models, due
2345
       to concerns about practicality (Team et al., 2023; 2024a;b; Adler et al., 2024). We look forward to
2346   future works exploring efficient variations unifying eva and existing bi-level RM-LM frameworks.
