009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            We introduce GUIDE, a novel continual learning approach that directs diffusion
012            models to rehearse samples at risk of being forgotten. Existing generative strategies
013            combat catastrophic forgetting by randomly sampling rehearsal examples from a
014            generative model. Such an approach contradicts buffer-based approaches where
015            sampling strategy plays an important role. We propose to bridge this gap by
016
               incorporating classifier guidance into the diffusion process to produce rehearsal
               examples specifically targeting information forgotten by a continuously trained
017
               model. This approach enables the generation of samples from preceding task
018
               distributions, which are more likely to be misclassified in the context of recently
019            encountered classes. Our experimental results show that GUIDE significantly
020            reduces catastrophic forgetting, outperforming conventional random sampling
021            approaches and surpassing recent state-of-the-art methods in continual learning
022            with generative replay.

      2.1   G UIDED IMAGE GENERATION IN DIFFUSION MODELS
082
083   Besides conditioning, controlling diffusion model outputs can be achieved by modifying the process of
084   sampling that incorporates additional signals from the guidance function. Classifier guidance (Dhari-
085   wal & Nichol, 2021) enables steering the backward diffusion process by combining the intermediate
086   denoising steps of conditional or unconditional diffusion with the gradient from the externally trained
087   classifier. To ensure the high quality of generated samples, the original method was introduced with a
088   classifier trained on noised images. However, such an approach can be impractical and lead to limited
089   performance.
090   Building upon the work by Bansal et al. (2023), in our method, we adapt the guidance process to
091   utilize a classifier trained only on clean images. To that end, in the guidance process, we first predict

105   2.2   C ONTINUAL L EARNING
106
107   Continual learning methods aim to mitigate catastrophic forgetting – a phenomenon where deep
      neural networks trained on a sequence of tasks completely and abruptly forget previously learned


                                                           2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      information upon retraining on a new task. Recent methods can be organized into three main
109   families. Regularization methods (Kirkpatrick et al., 2017; Zenke et al., 2017; Li & Hoiem, 2017)
110   identify the most important parameters and try to slow down their changes through regularization.
111   Architectural approaches (Rusu et al., 2016; Yoon et al., 2018; Mallya & Lazebnik, 2018; Mallya
112   et al., 2018; Verma et al., 2021) change the structure of the model for each task. Rehearsal methods
113   replay data samples from previous tasks and train the model on a combination of data samples from
114   previous and current tasks. In the most straightforward rehearsal approach, a memory buffer is used
115   to store exemplars from previous tasks (Prabhu et al., 2020; Rebuffi et al., 2017; Chaudhry et al.,
116   2018; Wu et al., 2019; Hou et al., 2019; Belouadah & Popescu, 2019; Castro et al., 2018; Aljundi
117
      et al., 2019b). Some methods, instead of directly using exemplars, stores data representations from
      previous tasks in different forms, e.g., mnemonics - optimized artificial samples (Liu et al., 2020b),
118
      distilled datasets (Wang et al., 2018; Zhao & Bilen, 2021; Zhao et al., 2021), or addressable memory
119
      structure (Deng & Russakovsky, 2022).
120
121
122
      Continual learning with generative rehearsal Because of the limitations of buffer-based ap-
      proaches related to the constantly growing memory requirements and privacy issues, Shin et al. (2017)
123
      introduces Deep Generative Replay, where a GAN is used to generate rehearsal samples from previous
124
      tasks for the continual training of a classifier. A similar approach is further extended to different
125   model architectures like Variational Autoencoders (VAE) (van de Ven & Tolias, 2018; Nguyen et al.,
126   2018), normalizing flows (Scardapane et al., 2020) or Gaussian Mixture Models (Rostami et al.,
127   2019).
128
      On top of those baseline approaches, Ramapuram et al. (2020) introduce a method that benefits from
129
      the knowledge distillation technique in VAE training in CL setup, while Wu et al. (2018) introduce
130
      Memory Replay GANs (MeRGANs) and describe two approaches to prevent forgetting - by joint
131   retraining and by aligning the replay samples. Instead of replaying data samples, several approaches
132   propose to rehearse internal data representations instead, e.g., Brain-Inspired Replay (BIR) (Van de
133   Ven et al., 2020) with an extension to Generative Feature Replay (GFR) (Liu et al., 2020a), where the
134   rehearsal is combined with features distillation. Kemker & Kanan (2018) divide feature rehearsal
135   into short and long-term parts.
136
137   Continual learning with diffusion models Diffusion models excel in generative tasks, surpassing
138   VAEs (Kingma & Welling, 2014) and GANs, yet their adoption in CL remains limited. Deep
139   Diffusion-based Generative Replay (DDGR) (Gao & Liu, 2023) uses a diffusion model in a generative
140   rehearsal method and benefits from a classifier pretrained on previous tasks to synthesize high-quality
141   replay samples. Class-Prototype Conditional Diffusion Model (CDPM) (Doan et al., 2023) further
142   enhances the replay samples quality by conditioning the diffusion model on learnable class-prototypes.
143   Similarly, Jodelet et al. (2023) use an externally trained text-to-image diffusion model for the same
144
      purpose. In this work, we extend those ideas and show that classifier guidance might be used not only
      to generate high-quality images but also to introduce the desired characteristics of rehearsal samples.

147   3     M ETHOD
148
149
      This section introduces GUIDE - a novel method designed to mitigate catastrophic forgetting in a
150   classifier trained in a generative replay scenario with a diffusion model. We benefit from the classifier
151   trained on the current task, referred to as the current classifier, to guide the diffusion model during the
152   generation of rehearsal examples from preceding tasks. With our method, we can generate rehearsal
153   examples close to the classifier’s decision boundary, making them highly valuable to counteract the
154   classifier’s forgetting in class-incremental learning (Toneva et al., 2018; Kumari et al., 2022).
155
156   3.1   I NTUITION AND RATIONALE BEHIND GUIDE
157

159   model towards classes not included in its training dataset. To that end, we propose a simplified
160   scenario in which we employ the unconditional diffusion model ϵθ (xt , t) trained exclusively on the
161   goldfish and tiger shark classes from the ImageNet100 dataset, along with a classifier fϕ (y|x) trained
      on entire ImageNet dataset.

191   3.2   G UIDANCE TOWARDS CLASSES FROM CURRENT TASK
192
193   In this work, we focus on class-incremental continual learning (van de Ven et al., 2022) of a classifier
194   fϕ (y|x) with generative replay, where we use diffusion model ϵθ (xt , t, y) as a generator of synthetic
195   samples from previous tasks. The data stream comprises of T distinct tasks with disjoint sets of
196   labels. Each task i has its associated dataset Di of labeled samples pairs (x, y).

198   function:

225   At the end of each task, we train a class-conditional diffusion model ϵθi (xt , t, y) on currently available

227
      model ϵθi−1 (xt , t, y).
228   Intuitively, in GUIDE, we steer the diffusion process towards examples from the current task, as
229   depicted in Fig. 1. Simultaneously, since we utilize only the previous frozen diffusion model that
230   is not trained on classes from the current task, we consistently obtain samples from desired class
231   yi−1 . Rehearsal examples obtained with the modified sampling process yield lower outputs for class
232   yi−1 in the current classifier fϕi (y|x) compared to the previous classifier fϕi−1 (y|x). Hence, these
233
      examples can be interpreted as data samples that are more likely to be forgotten during continual
      training. We experimentally validate this statement in Sec. 4.5.
234
235   The effect of our guidance technique resembles the idea introduced in Retrospective Adversarial
236   Replay (RAR) (Kumari et al., 2022), where authors show that training the classifier on rehearsal
237   samples similar to examples from the current task helps the model to learn the boundaries between
238   tasks. This is also in line with observation by Toneva et al. (2018), who show that the sample’s
239
      distance from the decision border is correlated with the number of forgetting events.

315   improved upon the standard GR scenario in terms of knowledge retention from preceding tasks. It

318   the stability-plasticity trade-off (Grossberg, 1982), highlighting that while our approach substantially
319   reduces the forgetting of classifier, it does so at the expense of its ability to assimilate new information.

      5.2     A LTERNATIVE VARIANTS OF GUIDANCE
423
424   In addition to our primary method, we evaluate alternative variants of incorporating classifier guidance
425   to generative replay setup, drawing inspiration from corresponding techniques in buffer-based
426   rehearsal. Each variant effectively modifies the sampling strategy from the diffusion model. In
427   each variant, we benefit either from the frozen classifier fϕi−1 (y|x), trained on prior tasks and
428   henceforth referred to as the previous classifier, or the currently trained classifier, fϕi (y|x). In this
429   section, we define each variant highlighting with the blue color the distinctions from GUIDE.
430
431   Guidance towards classes from previous tasks The most straightforward adaptation of a classifier
      guidance concept to a generative replay setup is to modify the diffusion sampling process using


                                                                                        8

432
      the gradients from the previous frozen classifier to refine the quality of rehearsal samples. The
433   modification of the previous diffusion model’s prediction can be thus defined as:
434

436   where yi−1 denotes the class label from one of the previous tasks. As noted by Dhariwal & Nichol
437   (2021), the application of classifier guidance creates a trade-off: it enhances the quality of the
438   generated samples at the cost of their diversity. This approach is similar to the one introduced by Gao
439   & Liu (2023) except that we do not use guidance in the process of continual diffusion training but
440   only in the classifier’s training. Intuitively similar buffer-based methods are based on the herding

503   5.4    C OMPUTATIONAL COST OF GUIDE
504
      A significant drawback of all generative replay approaches is the computational burden associated
505
      with training and sampling from the diffusion model. Therefore, we explore two simple speedup
506   techniques that allow us to reduce computation costs notably. While all diffusion models in this work
507   are trained with 1000 steps, we can change the sampler to DDIM (Song et al., 2020) with fewer
508   sampling steps. To measure the effect of this approach, in Tab. 5, we evaluate how limiting the number
509   of backward diffusion steps affects the final average accuracy on the CIFAR10/5 benchmark. We can
510   observe an order of magnitude speedup at the expense of a slight drop in the model’s performance.
511   Therefore, in all of the experiments presented in this work, we limit the number of sampling steps to
512   50 or 100, as mentioned in Appendix D. Similarly, in Tab. 6, we measure the interval (in numbers of
513   batches) between which we sample new generations. We observe that sampling rehearsal examples
514
      once every five batches yields similar results to the default version while providing a 5-times speedup.
      Combining those two simple techniques allows us to achieve an 83 times faster generation process.
515
      We provide a detailed comparison of training times in Appendix F, showing that our approach is
516
      more computationally expensive than VAE and GAN-based techniques but, at the same time, over
517   25% faster than the existing state-of-the-art diffusion-based method (DDGR).

      In this work, we propose GUIDE: generative replay method that utilizes classifier guidance to
534   generate rehearsal samples that the classifier model is likely to forget. We benefit from a classifier
535   trained continually in each task to guide the denoising diffusion process toward the most recently
536   encountered classes. This strategy enables the classifier’s training with examples near its decision
537   boundary, rendering them particularly valuable for continual learning. Across various CL benchmarks,
538   GUIDE demonstrates superior performance, consistently surpassing recent state-of-the-art generative
539   rehearsal methods. This underscores the effectiveness of our approach in mitigating forgetting and
      training a robust classifier.

813   docode for the primary components involved in our method. Alg. 1 presents a sampling of a single
814   rehearsal example during the continual training of a classifier in GUIDE. In Alg. 2, we present a
815   complete end-to-end continual training of both diffusion model and classifier in GUIDE.
816
      Since the decision boundary changes during the training of a classifier, we sample new rehearsal
817
      examples according to our sampling method progressively once every Ng batches. It ensures that
818
      replayed examples are located close to the decision boundary of a classifier at the given moment.
819   Simultaneously, hyperparameter Ng allows us to significantly speed up our method without a

822   data samples.

1217   I      B ROADER IMPACTS
1218
1219   This paper presents work whose goal is to advance the field of continual machine learning. There are
1220   many potential societal consequences of our work, many of which are generic to the machine learning
1221   field in general, i.e., the DGR algorithm considered in this paper will reflect the biases present in the
1222   dataset. Hence, it is important to exercise caution when using this technique in applications where
1223
       dataset biases could lead to unfair outcomes for minority and/or under-represented groups. In our
       case, this especially concerns training a diffusion model used for replay, in which simple random
1224
       sampling can result in a different data distribution than the original dataset. It would be worthwhile to
1225
       actively monitor the model’s outputs for fairness or implement bias correction techniques to mitigate
1226   these negative impacts.
1227
1228   Additionally, while diffusion models have shown promise in various generative tasks, their adaptability
1229
       through fine-tuning and continual learning remains relatively unexplored. In this early-stage research,
       some potential risks can emerge while combining our proposed algorithm of guidance with malicious
1230
       models or when performing more sophisticated attacks.
