009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            In this work, we present ELF (Evolving LLM-Based Schemas for Mid-Vision
012            Feedback), a framework that integrates schema evolution with Mid Vision Feed-
013            back (MVF) for visual learning. We leverage Large Language Models (LLMs)
014            to automatically generate schemas: executable semantic programs operating over
015            sets of context categories (e.g., ”animate” or ”inanimate”). We integrate schemas
016            into visual processing via MVF, a method that utilizes top-down feedback connec-
017
               tions to inform mid-level visual processing with high-level contextual knowledge.
               To optimize these schemas we utilize EvoPrompt, an evolutionary algorithm that
018
               refines schemas through iterative search, resulting in improvements in accuracy
019
               and contextual consistency. We demonstrate the effectiveness of ELF across mul-
020            tiple datasets and multiple architectures for the task of object classification.

044   Feedback plays a primary role in biological vision; in fact, the majority of neural connections in the
045   visual cortex are top-down, rather than bottom-up, connections (Markov et al., 2014). These top-
046   down connections are thought to convey information of higher level expectation, and neurons of the
047   visual cortex use both higher level expectation as well as lower level visual information in producing
048   their representations.
049   We employ in this work a mechanism - Mid-Vision Feedback (MVF) (Maynord et al., 2023) - which
050   emulates the feedback systems in biological vision, where high-level context informs lower-level
051   visual processing. Unlike traditional feed-forward architectures, where information flows only from
052   low-level pixels to high-level abstract concepts, MVF introduces top-down feedback connections
053   that integrate contextual knowledge into mid-level visual processing - see Figure 2. In the MVF
      framework, specific affine transformations (i.e., linear transformation and possible bias) are applied

      to feature vectors at mid-level layers of a artificial neural network, based on the expected context
055   (e.g., ”animate” vs. ”inanimate”). This contextual ”hint” improves classification performance of the
056   network.

      Schemas are cognitive structures that help individuals organize and interpret information based on
      prior knowledge and experiences (Rumelhart, 2017; Axelrod, 1973). They influence how we per-
059
      ceive new stimuli by providing a framework for understanding and responding to the world around
060
      us. In biological systems, schemas help filter and organize sensory information, enabling faster and
061   more accurate recognition by linking incoming stimuli to expected patterns (Bar, 2007).
062
063   While this process of applying high level contextual understanding to lower level processes is af-
064
      forded significant resources in biological vision, contemporary vision architectures by-and-large do
      not involve similar dynamics. In artificial vision systems, leveraging schemas derived from language
065
      or contextual knowledge can allow the model to impose meaningful constraints on mid-level visual
066
      representations. These constraints could reduce noise, enhance feature relevance, and enable more
067   precise predictions by narrowing the focus to contextually significant visual features. By aligning
068   mid-level representations with higher-order schemas, vision models can improve generalization and
069   efficiency, particularly in environments with numerous or overlapping categories. While schemas
070   are of use across a variety of vision tasks, for the purposes of this work, we apply ELF to object
071   classification.

      2.1   T OP -D OWN M ETHODS IN C OMPUTER V ISION
165
166   Previous works have explored the significance of feedback in biological sensory perception and its
167   parallels in computer vision (Kveraga et al., 2007; Markov et al., 2014; Gilbert & Sigman, 2007;
168   Kreiman & Serre, 2020; Gilbert & Li, 2013; Paneri & Gregoriou, 2017). Feedback models have
169   also been shown to better organize mid-level visual features like animacy vs. inanimacy (Long
170   et al., 2018) and texture representation (Jagadeesh & Gardner, 2022), aligning more closely with
171   human perception (Harrington & Deza, 2021). In existing computer vision methods, feedback is
172   typically implemented through recurrent connections (Caswell et al., 2016; Pinheiro & Collobert,
173
      2014; Zamir et al., 2016), but alternatives like hierarchical rectified Gaussians (Hu & Ramanan,
      2016) and graphical models (Yao et al., 2012) have been explored to enable top-down and bottom-
174
      up information flow, improving tasks such as keypoint localization and scene understanding. In
175
      this work we adapt the approach from Mid-Vision Feedback (Maynord et al., 2023), an emulation
176   of the feedback systems in biological vision, where high-level context informs lower-level visual
177   processing.
178
179
      2.2   E VOLUTIONARY M ETHODS
180
181   Evolutionary algorithms have been widely used in various machine learning tasks (Chen et al., 2019;
182   Lopes et al., 2022; Zhou et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2021), particularly in neural architecture search
183   (NAS) and optimization (Elsken et al., 2018; Ci et al., 2021; Zhu et al., 2019). EvoPrompting
184   (Chen et al., 2024), a recent approach, applies evolutionary strategies to search for architectures by
185   leveraging adaptive mutation and crossover operators from Large Language Models (LLMs). We
186   adopt a similar evolutionary search approach as in EvoPrompting, but with a different search space,
187
      where our search space is over schemas rather than complex Pytorch operations. As schemas offer
      a more tractable search space, our method does not require soft prompting and we use GPT-4 out of
188
      the box.
189

191   3     M ETHODS
192
193   ELF consists of Evolution, Language, and Feedback components integrated together for application
194   to a given vision task. In Section 3.1 we overview the vision component of an ELF system, and
195   the MVF modifications necessary to effectively integrate it with the full system. In Section 3.2 we
196   overview the LLM based generation of schemas, which provide the high level contextual biases
197
      which are then fed into the vision component. Finally, we overview the evolutionary training com-
      ponent in Section 3.3, whose purpose is to facilitate a process of iterative refinement of schemas,
198
      within a communication loop between the language and vision feedback components. The final re-
199
      sult of an ELF system is then a schema, refined through evolutionary search to be optimized, and a
200   vision system attuned to that schema such that performance on the vision task is maximized.
201
202
      3.1   M ID -V ISION F EEDBACK
203
204   Mid-Vision Feedback (MVF) is a feedback mechanism designed to bias mid-level feature represen-
205   tations in neural networks towards alignment with high-level context expectations. MVF operates
206   through the application of affine transformations (i.e., linear transformation and possible bias) ap-
207   plied to mid-level features during network runtime. These transformations are context-specific and
208   selectively amplify or dampen certain characteristics.
209
      MVF’s training process is divided into two stages. In the first stage, the base network is trained
210   independently, for both the base task and the task of predicting context labels (as per (Maynord
211   et al., 2023)). In the second stage, affine transformations are introduced at the injection level (the
212   level whose features are being modulated), via the feedback loop shown in Figure 2. During back-
213   propagation, gradients are then passed through both the base network and the affine transformations,
214   allowing the entire system to adapt to the context-specific modifications. Training is broken into 2
215   stages because this produces better performance than training in a single stage - intuitively, this may
      be because during training, affine transformations operate over a more stable target.

256   3.2     L ANGUAGE
257
258   Large Language Models have shown an impressive ability to detect visually useful contexts and
259   attributes for object classification (Menon & Vondrick, 2022), and have proven adept at writing
260   executable code referencing real-world visual entities (Gupta & Kembhavi, 2023). We aim to exploit
261   both of these strengths, using LLMs to generate schemas that function as executable code, and
262
      encode contextual information that is useful for object classification.

269   the schema within Figure 5 for an example, where in classifying a bird, contexts such as ”nature”,
      ”branch”, and ”sky” might be relevant.

297   3.2.2    S CHEMA I NITIALIZATION
298
      The initial set of schemas is generated automatically by prompting an LLM with an ontology of ob-
299   ject classes from the target vision dataset. These classes, along with a task description (e.g. classify
300   the prominent entity in an image), guide the LLM to produce a set of context categories relevant to
301   differentiating the objects in the dataset. The output of this initialization step is a context ontology,
302   where each object class is mapped to some set of contexts. The initialization also produces some ini-
303   tial schema candidates, creating a foundation for schema refinement. This automatic generation of
304   schemas reduces the need for manual engineering and ensures that the contexts reflect both semantic
305   and visual distinctions relevant to the task at hand.
306
307   3.2.3    S CHEMA AND MVF I NTEGRATION
308
      The integration of our schemas into the vision network requires two forward passes. The initial
309
      forward pass of the vision network over an input image produces a distribution over contexts, each
310   with associated probability scores. The names of each context along with the respective probability
311   scores are sent as arguments to the code contained within the schema. This code interprets these
312   arguments to produce a set of contexts with which to modulate the vision architecture through use
313   of MVF. The vision network is then run a second time with this schema modulation to produce
314   object predictions.
315
      See Figure 5 for an example. An image of a Baltimore Oriole is fed in a first pass to the vision
316   network, producing a distribution of context detections. These context detections are fed to a can-
317   didate schema, producing the output feedback biases of {bias(nature), bias(branch), bias(sky)}.
318   We fetch and apply the affine transformations associated with each of these contexts, biasing the
319   features in alignment with the contexts detected.
320
321   3.3     E VOLUTIONARY T RAINING
322
323   Given the candidate schemas provided by GPT-4 at initialization, we use an evolutionary algorithm
      inspired by EvoPrompting (Chen et al., 2024) to generate improved schemas that best contribute


                                                         6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      to the vision task. The primary objective is to iteratively search for optimal schemas that enhance
325   the accuracy. The schema refinement process begins with A) evaluation based on classification
326   performance. Promising schemas are then selected for B) crossover and mutation. This process is
327   then iterated. This is outlined in Figure 4, and detailed in the rest of this subsection.
328
329   A) Evaluation and Selection: Each schema is evaluated based on its contribution to object classi-
330   fication accuracy (the ”fitness” metric). We integrate each candidate schema into the vision network
331   trained after stage one with MVF, as depicted in Figure 5. After finishing stage two training, the
332   schemas producing the highest accuracies are selected for the next iteration.
333
334   B) Schema Crossover and Mutation: As in EvoPrompting, the GPT-4 model is employed as
335   an adaptive mutation and crossover operator to produce child schemas. The GPT-4 is responsible
336   for generating these variations, leveraging its pre-trained knowledge of object categories and their
337   relations to produce novel candidate schemas. These crossover and mutation operations allow the
338
      exploration of new schemas that might not be obvious through manual design.
339   Over multiple generations, the schemas are iteratively refined. Each generation is evaluated and
340   tuned, and the schemas with the highest accuracies are preserved for the next generation. This
341   process of mutation and selection iterates until the halting condition is reached: when object classi-
342   fication has not improved for a certain number of generations.

355   We compute feedback margins - the improvement in performance provided by introducing feedback
356   - as follows: A model consisting only of stage 1 training (no affine transformations or schema
357   feedback) is trained until convergence. A second model with both stage 1 and stage 2 (stage 2

359   then the difference in accuracies between these two models. Note that for both the stage 1 model,

706   A.4     MVF S UMMARY
707
708   Mid-Vision Feedback (MVF) is a feedback mechanism designed to enhance mid-level feature rep-
709   resentations in convolutional neural networks (CNNs) by aligning them with high-level categorical
710   expectations. This approach incorporates affine transformations and orthogonalization bias to im-
711   prove contextual consistency and overall accuracy. Affine transformations, defined as
712                                               f ′ = Af + b,
713
      where f is the feature vector, A is the transformation matrix, and b is the bias vector, are applied
714
      to features at designated ”injection” levels in the network. These transformations amplify or sup-
715   press specific feature characteristics based on the high-level context of the input, which selects the
716   appropriate affine transformation to apply. The context may be derived from an external system or
717   predicted by the network itself through a secondary logits classification head.
718
      The effectiveness of these transformations relies on disentangling features associated with different
719
      contexts, achieved through an orthogonalization bias during training. This bias is enforced by a
720
      contrastive loss:
721                                          1        X                  f1 · f2
722
                            LO (F, Y ) =                      max 0,                 ,
                                          |SF,Y |                       ∥f1 ∥∥f2 ∥
                                                  (f1 ,f2 )∈SF,Y
723
724   where
725                   SF,Y = {(f1 , f2 ) | fi ∼ U (Fci ), YC (f1 ) ̸= YC (f2 ), I(f1 ) = I(f2 )},
726   YC (f ) is the context label for f , and Fci represents features for context ci . This loss penalizes
727   feature similarity across different contexts, encouraging angular separation and making mid-level
728   features more amenable to manipulation through context-driven affine transformations.
729   Training is divided into two stages. In Stage 1, the base network is trained with the orthogonalization
730   loss to separate features associated with different contexts. The loss function is:
731
                                    L1 (Y, P, F ) = λLO (F, Y ) + CE(Y, P ),
732
733   where CE(Y, P ) is the cross-entropy classification loss, and λ scales the orthogonalization term.
734
      In Stage 2, affine transformations are introduced and optimized based on the context of each input.
      These transformations, initialized as identity matrices with added noise, are trained alongside the
735
      network parameters. The loss function for this stage is:
736
737                                        L2 (Y, P, F ) = CE(Y, P ).
738
739
      Affine transformations are selected during runtime by associating each input with a high-level con-
      text ci , either predicted by the network or provided as ground truth. These transformations bias
740
      mid-level features toward conformity with the selected context, enhancing their relevance for high-
741
      level tasks.
742
743   MVF exploits CNNs’ tendency toward decoupled representations, where feature vector angles cor-
744   respond to characteristic types and magnitudes reflect variations. By disentangling features across
745
      contexts, MVF enables precise control over mid-level representations, bridging the gap between
      low-level signals and high-level symbolic reasoning. This feedback mechanism outperforms post-
746
      hoc filtering by aligning feature representations during runtime rather than discarding inconsistent
747
      predictions, achieving superior performance in context-sensitive tasks and allowing for the detection
748   of out-of-context objects.

      A.5     E VO P ROMPTING S UMMARY
751
752   EvoPrompting is a meta-learning framework that uses large language models (LMs) for code gen-
753   eration as adaptive mutation and crossover operators in an evolutionary neural architecture search
754   (NAS) pipeline. This approach iteratively improves prompts and LM performance through prompt-
755   tuning and evolutionary selection, enabling the discovery of diverse and high-performing neural
      architectures.


                                                        14
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


756
      The NAS problem is formalized as searching for architectures c ∈ V ∗ (code samples in the LM’s
757   vocabulary V ), evaluated via a fitness function EVALT (c, D) : V ∗ × D → R, where D is a dataset
758   for task T . The objective is to maximize the reward function:
759
760                          arg        max        Ec∈C E(x,y)∈D [EVALT (c, D)] .
                                  C={c|c∼πθ },|C|=k
761
762   To address the intractability of this optimization, EvoPrompting employs a black-box evolutionary
763   algorithm. An LM, πθ , initialized with pre-trained parameters, serves as the mutation and crossover
764   operator, leveraging its pretraining on large code datasets for diverse and semantically valid archi-
765   tectures.
766
767   A LGORITHM OVERVIEW
768
769   EvoPrompting’s end-to-end algorithm consists of the following stages:
770   1. Initialization: The global population G is initialized to an empty list, and the initial population P
771   is seeded with a set of hand-designed architectures {c1 , c2 , . . . , cp }, each evaluated using the fitness
772   function EVALT (c, D).
773
      2. Crossover and Mutation: A few-shot prompt is created using k examples randomly selected
774
      from P . This prompt, combined with task-specific metrics, is used to guide the LM πθ to generate
775   n child architectures:
776                                  C = {cj | cj ∼ πθ (·|prompt)}.
777
778   3. Evaluation and Filtering: Child architectures are scored using:
779
                                fitness(c) = −model size(c) × validation error(c),
780
781   where architectures with validation error exceeding a threshold α are discarded.
782
      4. Selection and Prompt-Tuning: The top p architectures with the highest fitness scores are se-
783   lected to form the new population P . The remaining child architectures are used to prompt-tune πθ
784   for the next round.
785
786
      5. Iterative Evolution: Steps 2–4 are repeated over T rounds of evolution, gradually improving the
      population and the LM’s ability to generate better architectures.

      A PPLICATIONS AND R ESULTS
790
      EvoPrompting was applied to the MNIST-1D dataset and the CLRS Algorithmic Reasoning Bench-
791   mark. On MNIST-1D, the algorithm discovered convolutional architectures superior to manually
792   designed baselines, producing smaller models with lower test error. On CLRS, EvoPrompting gen-
793   erated graph neural networks that outperformed state-of-the-art models on 21 out of 30 tasks while
794   maintaining competitive model sizes.
795
      This framework demonstrates the potential of combining LMs with evolutionary techniques to solve
796
      complex NAS problems and generalizes beyond architecture design to tasks requiring in-context
797   learning or prompt-tuning.
