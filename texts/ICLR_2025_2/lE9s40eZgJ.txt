009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               Multimodal Large Language Models (mLLMs) are trained on a large amount of
012
               text-image data. While most mLLMs are trained on caption-like data only, Alayrac
013
               et al. (2022) showed that additionally training them on interleaved sequences of text
014            and images can lead to the emergence of in-context learning capabilities. However,
015            the dataset they used, M3W, is not public and is only in English. There have
016            been attempts to reproduce their results but the released datasets are English-only.
017            In contrast, current multilingual and multimodal datasets are either composed
018            of caption-like only or medium-scale or fully private data. This limits mLLM
019            research for the 7,000 other languages spoken in the world. We therefore introduce
020            mOSCAR, to the best of our knowledge the first large-scale multilingual and
021            multimodal document corpus crawled from the web. It covers 163 languages,
022
               303M documents, 200B tokens and 1.15B images. We carefully conduct a set of
               filtering and evaluation steps to make sure mOSCAR is sufficiently safe, diverse and
023
               of good quality. We additionally train two types of multilingual model to prove the
024
               benefits of mOSCAR: (1) a model trained on a subset of mOSCAR and captioning
025            data and (2) a model trained on captioning data only. The model additionally trained
026            on mOSCAR shows a strong boost in few-shot learning performance across various
027            multilingual image-text tasks and benchmarks, confirming previous findings for
028            English-only mLLMs. The dataset will be made publicly accessible.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Multimodal large language models (mLLMs) are trained on large amounts of text-image data (Radford
035   et al., 2021; Yu et al., 2022; Li et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023; OpenAI, 2023; Gemini Team et al.,
036   2023; Chameleon Team, 2024). The main paradigm until recently was to train a model from a
037   large collection of web-crawled images and their captions (Li et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2022; Chen
038   et al., 2023b). Models such as Flamingo (Alayrac et al., 2022) challenged this paradigm by being
039
      additionally trained on interleaved sequences of text and images from web documents, showing
      state-of-the-art results on various tasks and in-context learning capabilities that are not present in
040
      models trained on caption-like data only. Additionally, McKinzie et al. (2024) recently proved that
041
      including interleaved text-image data during training was necessary to get good few-shot learning
042   performance. However, the datasets used to train mLLMs are either private (Alayrac et al., 2022),
043   monolingual or multilingual but only medium-scale (Srinivasan et al., 2021). Some attempts have
044   been made to reproduce these datasets (Zhu et al., 2023; Laurençon et al., 2023) but the resulting
045   datasets are only available in English.
046
      Few image-text datasets are multilingual and most of them are obtained by translating English caption-
047
      like datasets, such as multilingual Conceptual Captions (Sharma et al., 2018), into multiple languages
048   using neural machine translation (NMT) systems (Surís et al., 2022; Maaz et al., 2024). This presents
049   some drawbacks such as some languages still being poorly translated by current state-of-the-art
050   NMT models (Liu et al., 2020; Costa-jussà et al., 2022) and some cultural subtleties inherent in
051   each language not being fully conveyed. Some efforts have been conducted to collect large-scale
052   multilingual image captioning datasets, such as LAION-5B (Schuhmann et al., 2022), but they are
053   limited to caption data too, are relatively noisy and more importantly contain a non-negligible share
      of “not safe for work” (NSFW) content such as pædopornographic images (Schuhmann et al., 2022).

      This motivated us to collect and release the first large-scale multilingual and multimodal document
070
      dataset derived from Common Crawl.1 Our dataset, multimodal OSCAR (mOSCAR), follows the
071
      OSCAR initiative (Ortiz Suárez et al., 2019; Abadji et al., 2021; 2022) and covers 303M documents
072   in 163 languages, 200B tokens and 1.15B images. Figure 1 shows an example of a document, more
073   can be found in the Supplementary Material. We carry out extensive filtering to increase its safety
074   and quality. To prove mOSCAR’s utility, we train a multilingual OpenFlamingo (Awadalla et al.,
075   2023) from a Gemma-2B language model (Gemma Team et al., 2024) on a subset of mOSCAR and
076   captioning data from LAION-400M (Schuhmann et al., 2021), recaptioned with BLIP (Li et al.,
077   2022), filtered with CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) and translated with NLLB (Costa-jussà et al., 2022).
078   We compare against a similar model trained on captioning data only and show we obtain a strong
079   boost in few-shot learning, confirming previous findings for English (Alayrac et al., 2022; McKinzie
080
      et al., 2024; Laurençon et al., 2024). The dataset and models will be made publicly available.

082   2     R ELATED W ORK
083
084
      Large-scale web-based datasets Numerous datasets have been created by filtering web-crawled
085   data. These include large-scale text-only datasets (Ortiz Suárez et al., 2019; Raffel et al., 2020;
086   Wenzek et al., 2020; Gao et al., 2020; Abadji et al., 2021; Xue et al., 2021; Laurençon et al., 2022;
087   Abadji et al., 2022; Penedo et al., 2023) and multimodal ones (Sharma et al., 2018; Changpinyo et al.,
088   2021; Jia et al., 2021; Schuhmann et al., 2021; 2022; Byeon et al., 2022; Laurençon et al., 2023;
089   Zhu et al., 2023; Gadre et al., 2024). Even if these datasets are not as high quality as smaller and/or
090   hand-crafted ones, they are now the standard to pretrain foundation models, as it has been shown that
091   training bigger models on more data leads to better downstream performances (Brown et al., 2020;
092   Hoffmann et al., 2022; Touvron et al., 2023a;b).
093
094   English image-text datasets The first open-source image-text datasets were manually created,
095   small-scale and English-only (Ordonez et al., 2011; Lin et al., 2014; Plummer et al., 2015; Krishna
096   et al., 2017). Scaling up these datasets was an appealing solution to overcome limitations of previous
097   image-text models; a few works (Sharma et al., 2018; Changpinyo et al., 2021) proposed to collect
098
      millions of image-text pairs from the web before filtering them with well-designed steps. Relaxing the
      filtering steps enabled the collection of more data and led to large-scale datasets to train image-text
099
      foundation models (Radford et al., 2021; Li et al., 2021; Schuhmann et al., 2021; 2022; Byeon et al.,
100
      2022). However, these datasets generally contain caption-like image-text pairs only, and it is therefore
101   difficult to observe in-context learning abilities similarly to text-only language models trained on raw
102   documents (Raffel et al., 2020). Alayrac et al. (2022) overcome this issue by training their model
103   directly on documents with interleaved image-text data. While their results are promising, their M3W
104   dataset is English-only and private. Recently, open-source efforts (Zhu et al., 2023; Laurençon et al.,
105   2023) have been made to release a similar dataset but they are still monolingual.

      Multilingual image-text datasets Only a few image-text datasets are available in multiple lan-
109   guages. One of the first focused on collecting Google images from short queries based on word
110   frequencies from Wikipedia pages in 98 languages (Hewitt et al., 2018). Later, Srinivasan et al. (2021)
111   proposed the WIT dataset, an image-text dataset composed of Wikipedia pages. Although of high
112   quality, it is only medium-scale even for high-resource languages and there are fewer than 50k unique
113   images for most languages. Another approach lies in bootstrapping multilingual and multimodal
114   data from a model trained with English-only data (Mohammed et al., 2023). While effective for
115   captioning, it is computationally expensive to implement in practice. Other multilingual image-text
116   datasets exist but focus on captions only and are highly domain-specific (Kosar et al., 2022; Leong
117
      et al., 2022).

121   3.1     DATA COLLECTION
122
123   We collect mOSCAR from the Web ARchive Content (WARC) files of three 2023 Common Crawl
124
      dumps, processing them using the FastWARC library (Bevendorff et al., 2021). We remove documents
      smaller than 500 bytes (50% of the documents), as we find they are usually too small to be considered
125
      documents and tend to contain noisy text. We then navigate through the entire Document Object
126
      Model (DOM) tree with a depth first search algorithm and ChatNoir library (Bevendorff et al., 2018)
127   to extract nodes of interests corresponding to specific HTML tags.
128
129   Following previous work, we extract text from the tags that usually contain the main content of web
130
      pages (we refer to them as DOM text nodes), i.e. <p>, <h*>, <title>, <description>, <ul>,
      <ol>, <aside>, <dl>, <dd>, <dt>. Similarly to (Laurençon et al., 2023), we choose to remove
131
      <table> content as most often it is irrelevant and difficult to render. We extract all <img> tags (we
132
      refer to them as DOM image nodes). We then remove documents with fewer than 3 text nodes (as
133   they do not contain enough text) and more than 30 image nodes (as we found them to be too noisy).
134
135
      3.2     L ANGUAGE IDENTIFICATION
136
137   We identify the language of each document using the state-of-the-art open-LID language detector
138   (Burchell et al., 2023), covering 201 languages. We apply open-LID to each DOM text node and keep
139   the three most probable languages with their respective probabilities. The language of the document
140   is then determined by summing over the probabilities of each language detected for each text segment,
141   weighted by the number of characters in the segment2 and taking the language with the highest score.
142
143   3.3     T EXT- ONLY FILTERING
144
      We apply a series of filtering steps to the text content of each document independently of the images,
145
      with the aim of discarding poor quality documents and cleaning text as best as possible. We first filter
146
      at the text-node level and then at the whole document level, before running near-deduplication to
147   keep unique text nodes within a document and unique documents in the dataset.
148
149   Text node filtering We use a set of heuristics (see Supplementary Material) to extract as much
150   human-generated content as possible while discarding noisy text related to ads and website functions
151   (e.g. “Instagram”, “Facebook”). We then keep DOM text nodes with content over 10 bytes. This step,
152   designed to improve the quality of extracted text, removes on average 55% of text nodes.
153
154   Document filtering We mostly filter “not safe for work” (NSFW) content at the document level.
155   We use an English regular expression to detect adult content, similar to the one used by the Université
156   Toulouse 1 Capitole3 and remove the entire document if there is a match with any of the DOM text
157   nodes’ contents, removing on average 0.5% of documents (mostly English ones). We acknowledge
158
      that there is a high probability that this also discards safe content, e.g. we could remove content from

      Toxicity filtering Toxic content targeting individuals or groups of people is widespread on the
179   internet and can therefore be found in large-scale web-crawled datasets like mOSCAR without
180   appropriate filtering steps. To alleviate this issue, we apply the same method used by Costa-jussà
181   et al. (2022) and remove documents from mOSCAR based on the presence of a list of "toxic" words
182   for each language6 . As some words in the list can also be used in a non-toxic way based on the
183   context (e.g.: ‘breast’ in English), we tag the document as toxic and remove it from mOSCAR if it
184   contains at least two distinct words in the list. This filtering step removes 0.95% of the documents for
185   very high-resource languages (>5M documents), 2.13% for high-resource languages (<5M, >500K),
186   0.47% for mid-resource languages (<500K, >50K) and 0.64% for low-resource languages (< 50K).
187
      When manually analysing 100 random documents removed by this filtering step in each of the 2
      (high-resource) languages we are native speakers of (English and French), we found 58 documents
188
      with toxic content.

      Personal Identifiable Information Personal Identifiable Information (PII) can be found in large-
191
      scale web-crawled datasets, we therefore conducted an additional filtering step to replace all detected
192
      PII by place holder strings using regular expressions (see Supplementary Material for the list of
193   regular expressions we used). Concretely, we replaced all detected email addresses, phone numbers,
194   credit card numbers, IP addresses and passport numbers.

      3.4    I MAGE - ONLY FILTERING
197
198   We downloaded images from the URLs in DOM image nodes using a modified version of the
199   img2dataset toolkit (Beaumont, 2021) that includes an antivirus scan and follows robots.txt
200   instructions to respect the Robots Exclusion Protocol. We then apply a series of filtering steps, first
201   removing images based on heuristics, and then applying multiple NSFW detection models to remove
202   undesirable content. Finally, we conduct a set of deduplication steps.
203
204   Rule-based filters Similarly to previous works (Schuhmann et al., 2021) and to avoid extracting
205   low-resolution images and favicons, we keep images with a minimum height and width of 150 pixels.
206   We restrict the aspect ratio to be between 3 and 1/3 (to remove banners), we remove images if their
207
      URLs contain the words “logo”, “banner”, “button”, “widget”, “icon” or “plugin” or if the image
      name from the URL matches “twitter”, “facebook” or “rss” (to remove logos). This step removes
208
      13.6% of the URLs. At this stage, we downloaded 2.5B images with an average success rate of 55%.

227   CSAM content Child Sexual Abuse Material (CSAM) is widespread on the internet and is therefore
228
      likely to be found in such a large-scale dataset crawled from the web. Removing CSAM is challenging
      as there is no training data nor open-source detection models available as these could be used in a
229
      harmful way. We rely on Thorn’s CSAM classifier8 , a proprietary classifier trained to detect CSAM
230
      content in images. We tag the image as CSAM if the probability of the class CSAM is superior to 0.4
231   to favour recall over precision. As mentioned above, if a document contains an image with a CSAM
232   tag, we remove it from the dataset. This step removes 0.07% of the images.
233
234   Deduplication To avoid memorisation issues often seen in models trained on datasets with many
235   duplicated images (Somepalli et al., 2023; Carlini et al., 2023; Webster et al., 2023; Somepalli et al.,
236   2024), we perform deduplication at the image level. We first remove duplicate images within the
237   same document by URL matching (removing 8.7% of URLs). We then compute a perceptual hash
238   (pHash) for each image using the imagehash library9 and remove images with the same pHash within
239   the same document, keeping only the first occurrence. We also limit the number of times an image
240   can appear in the dataset per-language to 10 using both URL matching and perceptual hashing (this
241   removes 2.5% of images). We do this per-language and not across languages as having the same
242
      images in documents from different languages could encourage cross-lingual transfer.
243
244   Personal Identification Information To protect PII in images, we use a lightweight face detector10
245
      and apply a threshold of 0.99 to detect faces in the images. We apply such a high threshold as we
      found the model to be biased towards detecting faces with high probability in images without any
246
      human. For each image in mOSCAR, we distribute the bounding boxes of the detected faces so that
247
      users can blur them when downloading the images. More details are provided in the Supplementary
248   Material.

      3.5    DATA DECONTAMINATION
251
252   LLMs and mLLMs are trained on web-crawled data that can contain the benchmarks they are tested
253   on (Dodge et al., 2021). As they are good at memorizing training data (Carlini et al., 2023), this
254   data contamination is problematic. We therefore discard all images with the same perceptual hash as
255   any of the images from the evaluation benchmarks (and their training sets) we use (see Section 5.1).
256   This step removes on average 126,016 images for high-resource languages (up to 300K images for
257   English), 6,862 images for mid-resource languages and 45 images for low-resource languages.
258

392   5        T RAINING A MULTILINGUAL MULTIMODAL LANGUAGE MODEL
393
394   We train a multilingual Flamingo-like model on mOSCAR that we call multilingual Open Flamingo.
395   As adding captioning data to training data has been shown to improve zero-shot performance
396   (McKinzie et al., 2024), we additionally train on LAION-400M, which we re-captioned using
397   BLIP (Li et al., 2022), filtered with CLIP score (Radford et al., 2021) and translated using distilled
398   NLLB-600M (Visheratin, 2023) following the proportion of languages found in mOSCAR. We use
399
      Gemma-2B (Gemma Team et al., 2024) as the underlying language model and we train the model on
      50M mOSCAR documents and 100M randomly sampled image-text pairs. We also train a model on
400
      300M image-text pairs, a model trained on 35M WIT (Srinivasan et al., 2021) documents and 70M
401
      text-image pairs and a model trained on 50M mOSCAR documents from the English subset and 100M
402   English image-text pairs as comparison baselines. We additionally compare with OpenFlamingo-3B-
403   MPT (Awadalla et al., 2023) as the translate-test baseline. The full list of languages for training and
404   the implementation details can be found in the Supplementary Material.
405
406   5.1        E VALUATION SETUP
407
408   We evaluate the models using a broad set of image-text multilingual tasks and benchmarks. We use
409   the IGLUE benchmark (Bugliarello et al., 2022) composed of XVNLI, MaRVL (Liu et al., 2021)
410   to test reasoning, xGQA (Pfeiffer et al., 2022) to test visual question answering capabilities and
411   xFlickr&CO (Young et al., 2014; Karpathy and Fei-Fei, 2015; Yoshikawa et al., 2017) for captioning.
412
      We also include Crossmodal-3600 (XM3600) (Thapliyal et al., 2022) and MaXM (Changpinyo et al.,
      2022) as they cover a broader range of languages. To test to what extent models trained on mOSCAR
413
      can perform zero-shot multimodal machine translation (MMT), we also test on Multi30K (Elliott
414
      et al., 2016; 2017; Barrault et al., 2018) and CoMMuTE (Futeral et al., 2023). For captioning we
415   compute the CideR (Vedantam et al., 2015) score and we tokenize references and model outputs
416   with the Stanford Core NLP tokenizer for English and Stanza (Qi et al., 2020) tokenizers for other
417   languages. To evaluate Multi30k, we compute BLEU (Papineni et al., 2002) score from Sacrebleu
418   (Post, 2018) with 13a tokenization and default parameters. We use accuracy for CoMMuTE. More
419   details can be found in the Supplementary Material.

      6      C ONCLUSION , L IMITATIONS AND S OCIETAL I MPACTS
520
      We introduce mOSCAR, a large-scale multilingual and multimodal dataset covering 163 languages
521
      and composed of 303M documents, 200B tokens and 1.15B images. We show that mOSCAR is
522
      of good quality, diverse and can be used to train a multilingual and multimodal LLM. We ensure
523   that mOSCAR is as safe as possible by applying a series of filtering steps to remove NSFW and
524   toxic content. We however did not conduct any analysis of its biases as this is challenging in a
525   multilingual setting. As it is crawled from the internet, it is indeed possible that mOSCAR reflects
526   biases widespread on it. Training a model on mOSCAR must therefore be combined with additional
527   alignment training steps to mitigate potential biases towards groups of people. Nevertheless, by its
528   multilingual nature, mOSCAR is a step towards the inclusion of more languages, cultures and people
529   in accessing mLLMs.
