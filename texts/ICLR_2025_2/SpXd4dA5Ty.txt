                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011
               Sequential dense retrieval models utilize advanced sequence learning techniques
012            to compute item and user representations, which are then used to rank relevant
013            items for a user through inner product computation between the user and all item
014            representations. However, this approach requires storing a unique representa-
015            tion for each item, resulting in significant memory requirements as the number
016            of items grow. In contrast, the recently proposed generative retrieval paradigm
017            offers a promising alternative by directly predicting item indices using a genera-
018            tive model trained on semantic IDs that encapsulate items’ semantic information.
019            Despite its potential for large-scale applications, a comprehensive comparison be-
020
               tween generative retrieval and sequential dense retrieval under fair conditions
               is still lacking, leaving open questions regarding performance, and computation
021
               trade-offs. To address this, we compare these two approaches under controlled
022
               conditions on academic benchmarks and propose LIGER (LeveragIng dense re-
023            trieval for GEnerative Retrieval), a hybrid model that combines the strengths of
024            these two widely used methods. LIGER integrates sequential dense retrieval into
025            generative retrieval, mitigating performance differences and enhancing cold-start
026            item recommendation in the datasets evaluated. This hybrid approach provides
027            insights into the trade-offs between these approaches and demonstrates improve-
028            ments in efficiency and effectiveness for recommendation systems in small-scale
029            benchmarks.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
      Sequential recommendation methods (Kang & McAuley, 2018b; Zhou et al., 2020), have pre-
034
      dominantly relied on advanced sequential modeling techniques (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997;
035
      Vaswani et al., 2017; Radford et al., 2019) to learn dense embeddings for each item and user. Using
036   these embeddings, the most relevant items are retrieved through maximum inner product search.
037   Despite its effectiveness, this approach requires comparing every item in the dataset during the re-
038   trieval stage, which can be computationally expensive as the number of items grows. Furthermore,
039   each item must be represented by a unique embedding, which needs to be learned and stored, adding
040   to the complexity.
041
      In contrast, generative retrieval (Rajput et al., 2024) is a new approach, which deviates from the
042   embedding-centric paradigm. Instead of generating embeddings, this approach utilizes a generative
043   model to directly predict the item index. To better capture the sequential patterns within item inter-
044   actions, items are indexed by “semantic IDs” (Lee et al., 2022a), which encapsulate their semantic
045   characteristics. During the recommendation process, the model employs beam search decoding to
046   predict the semantic ID (SID) of the next item based on the user’s previous interactions. This method
047   not only reduces the need for storing individual item embeddings but also enhances the ability to
048   capture deeper semantic relationships within the data. Additionally, adjusting the temperature during
049   generation can naturally produce more diverse recommendations.
050   To distinguish the first paradigm from generative retrieval, which also leverages sequential informa-
051   tion, we refer to the embedding-centric paradigm in this paper as (sequential) dense retrieval. The
052   term dense retrieval is borrowed from the information retrieval domain (Tran & Yates, 2022), with
053   “sequential” added to differentiate it from other dual-encoder-based architectures Yi et al. (2019).
      The generative retrieval paradigm is well-positioned for future scaling in industrial recommendation

089   quality embeddings that are particularly effective for recommendation tasks. In contrast, generative
090   retrieval represents a relatively new paradigm that has yet to undergo the same level of refinement
091   and optimization. Nonetheless, the generative retrieval approach holds significant potential (Singh
092   et al., 2023), and future advancements could enable it to rival or even surpass dense retrieval under
093   appropriate conditions.

097   applies dense retrieval to a limited set of candidates generated by a generative retrieval module,
098   retaining the minimal storage requirements of generative retrieval while significantly improving
099   performance, particularly for cold-start items. Specifically, our key contributions are as follows:

117   trieval (Hou et al., 2022b) formula (see Section 2.1 and 2.2). Then in Section 2.2, we examine

121   2.1    G ENERATIVE R ETRIEVAL R EVIEW
122
123   The generative retrieval approach such as TIGER (Rajput et al., 2024) typically follows a two-stage
124   training process. The first stage involves collecting textual descriptions for each item based on
125   their attributes. These descriptions serve as inputs to a content model (e.g., a language encoder)
126
      that produces item’s text embeddings, subsequently quantized by an RQ-VAE (Lee et al., 2022a)
      to attribute a semantic ID for each item. Formally, for each item i ∈ I, we collect its p key-
127
      value attribute pairs {(k1 , v1 ), (k2 , v2 ), . . . , (kp , vp )} and format them into a textual description:
128
      Ti = prompt(k1 , v1 , · · · , kp , vp ). The textual description Ti is then passed to the content model,
129   yielding the text representation etext      for each item. We refer readers to Rajput et al. (2024); Lee
                                             i
130   et al. (2022a) for the training details of the RQ-VAE module. After the RQ-VAE is trained, we
131   obtain the m-tuple semantic ID (s1i , · · · , sm     i ) for each item i. Notably, an m-tuple semantic ID,
132   with a codebook size t, can theoretically represent tm unique items.
133
      In the second stage of training, the item text representation {etext                       i   } and the trained RQ-VAE
134
      model are discarded, retaining only the semantic IDs. For each interaction history indexed
135   by item IDs {i1 , i2 , · · · , in }, the item IDs are replaced with their corresponding semantic IDs:
136   {(s11 , s21 , · · · , sm      1 2               m              1    2            m
                             1 ), (s2 , s2 , · · · , s2 ), · · · , (sn , sn , · · · , sn )}. Given the semantic IDs of the last n
137   items a user interacted with, the Transformer model is then optimized to predict the next semantic
138   ID sequence (s1n+1 , s2n+1 , · · · , sm         n+1 ).
139
      During inference, a set of candidate items is retrieved using beam search over the trained Trans-
140
      former, selecting items based on their semantic IDs. A visual representation of the generative re-
141   trieval method is provided in Figure 2 (Lower Left).
142
143   It is worth noting that although the item text representations are excluded from the second stage of
144
      training, they still contribute to the information utilized in developing this approach. To ensure a fair
      comparison, we incorporate this information into the development of a comparable dense retrieval
145
      method in the following section, thereby accounting for the impact of these embeddings on the
146
      overall performance.

198   2.3   T HE O BSERVED P ERFORMANCE D IFFERENCE
199
200   As detailed in Section 2.1, the generative retrieval model leverages both item text representations and
201
      sequential interaction information. In Section 2.2, we introduce sequential dense retrieval methods
      inspired by Hou et al. (2022c), designed to fully incorporate these sources of information through-
202
      out the training process. To ensure a fair comparison between the generative retrieval and dense
203
      retrieval methods, we maintain consistency in model architecture, data pre-processing, and infor-
204   mation utilization. The specific details of our experiment setup are described in Section 4.1 and
205   in Appendix A.2.1. Due to the unavailability of TIGER’s codebase, we conducted an extensive
206   hyperparameter search, and configured the dense model’s hyperparameters to align with the tuned
207   TIGER settings. However these results, shown in Figure 1 (Right), indicate a performance difference
208   between the generative and dense retrieval methods in the datasets we evaluated.
209
      There are notable differences between the two implemented methods: (1) Number of Embed-
210   dings: As discussed in Section 2.1, representing N item requires the dense retrieval method to learn
211   and store O(N ) embeddings. In contrast, the semantic-ID-based generative retrieval method only
212   requires O(t) tokens, where tm ≈ N , and m is the length of the semantic ID tuple; (2) Text Rep-
213   resentation Input: Dense retrieval utilize the item’s text representation as additional input; and (3)
214   Prediction Mechanism: Dense retrieval relies on maximum inner product search in the embedding
215   space, whereas generative retrieval predicts the next item through next-token prediction via beam
      search.

256

257

261

386
387   Datasets. We evaluate LIGER on four datasets, preprocessing them using the standard 5-core
388   filtering method (Zhang et al., 2019; Zhou et al., 2020). This process removes items with fewer than
389
      5 users and users with fewer than 5 interactions. Additionally, we truncate sequences to a maximum
      length of 20, retaining the most recent items. Detailed statistics of the resulting datasets are provided
390
      in Appendix A.2.3.
391
392   • Amazon Beauty, Sports, and Toys (He & McAuley, 2016): We use the Amazon Review dataset
393   (2014), focusing on three categories: Beauty, Sports and Outdoors, and Toys and
394   Games. For each item, we construct embeddings by incorporating four key attributes: title, price,
395   category, and description.
396   • Steam (Kang & McAuley, 2018b): The dataset comprises online reviews of video games, from
397   which we extract relevant attributes to construct item embeddings. Specifically, we utilize the fol-
398   lowing attributes: title, genre, specs, tags, price, and publisher. To reduce the dataset size and make
399   it more manageable, we apply subsampling by selecting every 7th sequence, thereby retaining a
400
      representative subset of the data.
401   When generating the item text representations, the item attributes are processed using the sentence-
402   T5 model Ni et al. (2021) (XXL).
403
404
      Semantic ID Generation. Utilizing the text representations generated from the sentence-T5 model,
      we employ a 3-layer MLP for both the encoder and decoder in the RQ-VAE Lee et al. (2022a).
405
      The RQ-VAE features three levels of learnable codebooks, each with a dimension of 128 and a
406
      cardinality of 256. We use the AdamW optimizer to train the RQ-VAE, setting the learning rate at
407   0.001 and the weight decay at 0.1. To prevent collisions (i.e., the same semantic ID representing
408   different items), following Rajput et al. (2024) we append an extra token at the end of the ordered
409   semantic codes to ensure uniqueness.
410
      Sequential Modeling Architecture and Training Algorithm. For the generative model, we utilize
411
      the T5 (Raffel et al., 2020) encoder-decoder model, configuring both the encoder and decoder with
412
      6 layers, an embedding dimension of 128, 6 heads, and a feed-forward network hidden dimension
413   of 1024. The dropout rate is 0.2. The dense retrieval model designed in Section 2.2 employs only
414   the T5-encoder with 6 layers, while maintaining the same hyper-parameters. We use the AdamW
415   optimizer with a learning rate of 0.0003, a weight decay parameter of 0.035, and a cosine learning
416   rate scheduler. Additional details are presented in Appendix A.2.1.
417
      Evaluation Metrics. We assess the model’s performance using Normalized Discounted Cumulative
418
      Gain (NDCG)@10 and Recall@10. For dataset splitting, we adopt the leave-one-out strategy fol-
419   lowing (Kang & McAuley, 2018b; Zhou et al., 2020; Rajput et al., 2024), designating the last item
420   as the test label, the preceding item for validation, and the remainder for training. During training,
421   early stopping is applied based on the in-set NDCG@10 validation metric. For LIGER, which com-
422   prises two components: (A) the semantic ID prediction head and (B) the output embedding head,
423   we implement early stopping based on the performance of component (B). To ensure fair evaluation
424   of cold-start items, we exclude them from the RQ-VAE training to avoid data contamination.
425   Baselines. We compare our method against five state-of-the-art Item-ID-based dense retrieval meth-
426   ods: (a) SASRec (Kang & McAuley, 2018b); feature-informed methods: (b) FDSA (Zhang et al.,
427   2019), (c) S3 -Rec (Zhou et al., 2020); and modality-based methods: (d) UnisRec (Hou et al., 2022b),
428   (e) Recformer (Li et al., 2023a). Descriptions and implementation details for these baselines are pro-
429   vided in Appendix A.2.4. We also compare LIGER against TIGER (Rajput et al., 2024), a semantic
430   ID-based generative retrieval method. Although subsequent works have built upon this paradigm
431   using large language models (LLMs) (Zheng et al., 2023; Cao et al., 2024b), they rely on pre-trained
      LLMs, which are outside the scope of our comparisons.

      Experimental Results. The results from the benchmark datasets are presented in Table 2, where
462
      the mean and standard deviation are calculated across three random seed runs. Traditional item-ID-
463
      based methods, such as SASRec exhibit poor in-set performance compared to semantic-ID-based
464
      models. However, when attribute information is included, models like FDSA and S3 -Rec show
465   improved in-set performance. Nevertheless, their performance on cold-start items remains subpar
466   due to the static nature of item embeddings. In contrast, models that utilize text representations and
467   pre-training, such as UniSRec and RecFormer, demonstrate enhanced capabilities in handling cold-
468   start item scenarios. The inclusion of text embeddings during pre-training enables these models
469   to better handle unseen items. TIGER, which is a semantic-ID-based generative retrieval model,
470   outperforms item-ID-based methods in terms of in-set performance but still struggles with cold-start
471   item generation.
472   Our model, LIGER, builds upon TIGER by using semantic-ID-based inputs and combining dense
473   retrieval with semantic ID generation as outputs. This approach significantly improves upon the
474   TIGER method and enables effective generation of cold-start items. Across all datasets, our method
475   consistently achieves either the best or second-best performance, closely followed by modality-
476   based baselines such as UniSRec and RecFormer. We adopt a hybrid approach for our reporting,
477   where we use generative retrieval to retrieve 20 items and then rank them with cold-start items using
478   dense retrieval. Comprehensive result including performance of LIGER with different number of
479
      retrieved items from generative retrieval is presented in Table 4. Additional ablation study on each
      component of LIGER is present in Appendix A.4.

482   5         D ISCUSSION
483
484   Addressing Cold-Start Items with Hybrid Retrieval Models. That generative retrieval method’s
485   struggle with cold-start items primarily stems from overfitting to familiar semantic IDs during train-
      ing, as discussed in Section 2.4. To mitigate this issue, LIGER efficiently combines dense retrieval


                                                                    9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      with generative retrieval. Specifically, LIGER first generates a small set of K candidates (where
487   K ≪ N ) using generative retrieval, which is then augmented with the cold-start item set. This
488   approach leverages the efficiency of generative retrieval to reduce the candidate pool while ensur-
489   ing cold-start items are represented. The dense retrieval component further enhances cold-start
490   performance by leveraging item text embeddings as prior information. As shown in Table 4, this
491   integration ensures that when generative retrieval retrieves fewer than N items, the model maintains
492   robust performance in cold-start scenarios, comparable to dense retrieval only.
493   Comparative Performance with Current Dense Retrieval Methods. While LIGER demonstrates
494   competitive performance against existing baselines, its primary objective is to strike a balance be-
495   tween the generative and dense retrieval frameworks. As discussed in previous sections, there are
496   observed performance differences between these two methods on the dataset we tested, even when
497   using the same input information and model architecture. The results presented in this work aim
498   to shed light on potential future directions for integrating these approaches, paving the way for the
499   development of more robust and efficient recommendation systems.
500   Observed Performance Differences are Contextual to Small-Scale Datasets. We want to note
501   that the performance differences observed in this study are influenced by various factors, in-
502   cluding dataset size, implementation details, and the distribution of data collected under specific
503   paradigms. Additionally, we are aware of industry-scale implementations of generative retrieval
504   paradigms (Singh et al., 2023) that outperform dense retrieval approaches in real-world settings. In
505   this work, our goal is not to assert a definitive performance gap between the two paradigms. In-
506   stead, we focused on aligning the two methods as closely as possible within the scope of academic
507
      benchmarks, which typically use small-scale datasets. Our findings are meant to provide insights
      specific to this academic setting and should not be extrapolated to large-scale, real-world applica-
508
      tions without further investigation. We hope that these observations encourage a critical evaluation
509
      of academic benchmark design and inspire future research to explore performance under more di-
510   verse and realistic conditions, while addressing the limitations of small-scale datasets commonly
511   used in academic studies.

514   6   C ONCLUSION
515
516   In this work, we conducted a comprehensive comparison between dense retrieval methods and the
517   emerging generative retrieval approach. Our analysis revealed the limitations of dense retrieval,
518   including high computational and storage requirements, while highlighting the advantages of gener-
519   ative retrieval, which uses semantic IDs and generative models to enhance efficiency and semantic
520   understanding. Furthermore, we have identified the challenges faced by generative retrieval, par-
521   ticularly in handling cold-start items and matching the performance of dense retrieval. To address
522
      these challenges, we introduced a novel hybrid model, LIGER, that combines the strengths of both
      approaches. Our findings demonstrate that our hybrid model surpasses existing models in handling
523
      cold-start scenarios and achieves advanced overall performance on benchmark datasets.
524
525   Looking ahead, the fusion of dense and generative retrieval methods holds tremendous potential
526   for advancing recommendation systems. Our research provides a foundation for further exploration
527   into hybrid models that capitalize on the strengths of both retrieval types. As these models con-
528
      tinue to evolve, they will become increasingly practical for real-world applications, enabling more
      personalized and responsive user experiences.

710   A.1   R ELATED W ORK
711
      Due to the page limit, we present the related work here in the Appendix.
712
713   Generative Retrieval. The concept of generative retrieval was first proposed by Tay et al. (2022)
714   within the domain of document retrieval. This paradigm shifts from traditional search and retrieval
715   methods by encoding document information directly into the weights of a Transformer model. Sub-
716
      sequent studies (De Cao et al., 2020; Bevilacqua et al., 2022; Feng et al., 2022) have expanded on this
      foundation, enhancing document retrieval through improvements in indexing (Lee et al., 2022b;c;
717
      Wang et al., 2022), and the efficient continual database updates (Mehta et al., 2022; Kishore et al.,
718
      2023; Chen et al., 2023).
719
720   In the realm of sequential recommendation systems, Rajput et al. (2024) is the first work to leverage
721   the generative retrieval techniques. The target item is directly generated given a user’s interaction
722
      history, rather than selecting top items by ranking all relevant user-item pairs. A key challenge in
      generative retrieval is striking a balance between memorization and generalization when encoding
723
      items. To address this, semantic IDs have been proposed by leveraging RQ-VAE models (Lee et al.,
724
      2022a; Van Den Oord et al., 2017). These models encode content-based embeddings into a compact,
725   discrete semantic indexer that captures the hierarchical structure of concepts within an item’s con-
726   tent, proving to be scalable in industrial applications (Singh et al., 2023). Recent developments (Hou
727   et al., 2022a) have expanded semantic-ID-based generative retrieval to include contrastive learn-
728   ing (Jin et al., 2024), multimodal integration (Liu et al., 2024a), tokenization techniques (Sun et al.,
729   2024), and learning-to-rank methods (Li et al., 2024).
730
      Sequential Dense Recommendation. Traditional sequential dense recommender models follow
731   the paradigm of learning representations of users, items, and their interactions with multimodal
732   data. Early work (Hidasi et al., 2015) proposed architectures based on traditional Recurrent Neu-
733   ral Networks (RNNs), while later studies (Kang & McAuley, 2018a; Sun et al., 2019; de Souza
734   Pereira Moreira et al., 2021) have shifted towards the Transformer architecture to enhance perfor-
735   mance. Besides capturing the user-item interaction history pattern with the sequential modeling,
736   extra features such as item attributes (Zhang et al., 2019; Zhou et al., 2020) has been utilized to fur-
737   ther improve the performance. With the recent advancements in Large Language Models (LLMs),
738   several works have explored using these models as the backbone for recommender systems, align-
739
      ing item representations with LLMs to improve recommendation performance (Li et al., 2023b;
      Hou et al., 2022c; Cao et al., 2024a; Zheng et al., 2024). In this work, we aim to merge the sequen-
740
      tial dense recommendation approach with generative retrieval techniques, assessing performance
741
      gaps and computational costs, and proposing a hybrid method that combines the strengths of both
742   paradigms.
743
744   Cold-start Problem. Traditional challenges such as long-tail and cold-start items continue to hinder
745
      recommendation systems. The long-tail items issue arises from skewed distributions where a few
      popular items dominate user interactions (Zhang et al., 2022; 2020), while the cold-start problem
746
      arises when new items are introduced without any historical interaction data. Recent studies (Hou
747
      et al., 2022c; Li et al., 2023b) have shown that textual embeddings can provide a robust prior for
748   tackling the cold-start issue, and further improvements have been achieved by integrating pretrained
749   LLMs (Huang et al., 2024; Sanner et al., 2023) and knowledge graphs (Frej et al., 2024). In this
750   work, we explore the cold-start problem within the context of generative retrieval and propose a
751   hybrid method that combines dense retrieval with textual embeddings to effectively mitigate this
752   issue.

768   In Section 4.1, we provide the TIGER implementation details. Here, we describe the implementation
769   details of the dense retrieval method we developed.
770
      The dense retrieval method uses the same T5-encoder architecture, configured with 6 layers, an
771
      embedding dimension of 128, 6 attention heads, and a feed-forward network hidden dimension
772   of 1024. For content embeddings, we use the same model as TIGER, sentence-T5-XXL, which
773   generates text embeddings in R768 . To integrate these embeddings into the Transformer, we project
774   them down to a 128-dimensional space using a linear layer.
775
      Following the notation in Section 2.2, once Ei is collected for each item, it is passed through a
776
      LayerNorm layer, followed by a Dropout layer with a rate of 0.5. For the cosine similarity loss
777
      calculation, we set the temperature parameter τ = 0.07. During training, we use the same learning
778   rate (0.0003), cosine learning rate scheduler, optimizer (AdamW), and weight decay (0.035) as
779   employed in TIGER’s training.

      A.2.2     I MPLEMENTATION D IFFERENCE B ETWEEN U NI SR EC AND OUR D ENSE M ODEL
784   Although the dense retrieval model we implemented is inspired by UniSRec Hou et al. (2022c), we
785   made several modifications to simplify the model and align it with the best-tuned TIGER architec-
786   ture. Specifically:
787
788           1. We use the same content model as TIGER: T5-XXL, whereas UniSRec uses BERT.
789           2. We adopt the same encoder architecture as TIGER, which consists of 6 T5 encoder lay-
790              ers with 6 attention heads, an embedding dimension of 128, and a feed-forward network
791              hidden dimension of 1024. In contrast, UniSRec uses a custom Transformer block with
792              2 layers, 2 attention heads, an embedding size of 300, a hidden size of 256, and different
793              implementations for LayerNorm and positional embeddings compared to the T5 model 1 .
794           3. We replace UniSRec’s mixture-of-expert layer and whitening layer with a simple Linear
795              layer.
796           4. We train the dense retrieval model from scratch, whereas UniSRec relies on pretraining
797              using the Amazon 2018 datasets.
