009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Conformal prediction provides machine learning models with prediction sets that
012            offer theoretical guarantees, but the underlying assumption of exchangeability lim-
013            its its applicability to time series data. Furthermore, existing approaches struggle
014            to handle multi-step ahead prediction tasks, where uncertainty estimates across
015            multiple future time points are crucial. We propose JANET (Joint Adaptive pre-
016            dictioN-region Estimation for Time-series), a novel framework for constructing
017            conformal prediction regions that are valid for both univariate and multivariate
018
               time series. JANET generalises the inductive conformal framework and efficiently
               produces joint prediction regions with controlled K-familywise error rates, en-
019
               abling flexible adaptation to specific application needs. Our empirical evaluation
020
               demonstrates JANET’s superior performance in multi-step prediction tasks across
021            diverse time series datasets, highlighting its potential for reliable and interpretable
022            uncertainty quantification in sequential data.

025   1    I NTRODUCTION
026
027   In this work, we tackle the challenging problem of multi-step uncertainty quantification for time series
028   prediction. Our goal is to construct joint prediction regions (JPRs), a generalisation of prediction
029   intervals to sequences of future values. The naive approach of taking the Cartesian product of
030   marginal prediction intervals, each with the desired coverage level (1 − ϵ), does not guarantee the
031   desired global coverage. If future time steps were independent, this approach would result in coverage
032   (1 − ϵ)H , where H is the length of the horizon. Although techniques such as Bonferroni correction
033
      (13) can adjust for this, it becomes overly conservative as H increases, especially in the presence of
      temporal dependencies, which, of course, is to be expected in the case of time series data.
034
035   Conformal Prediction (CP) (3; 39; 17) offers a distribution-free frequentist methodology for un-
036   certainty quantification with finite-sample coverage guarantees. Specifically, CP ensures that
037   Pr(Y ∈ C(X ∗ )) ≥ 1 − ϵ, where C(X ∗ ) is the prediction set (or interval in the univariate case)
038   for a new input X ∗ . The theoretical guarantees and model-agnostic nature of CP have spurred its
039
      application in diverse areas, including large language models (30) and online model aggregation (19).
      CP is readily applicable to any algorithm that provides a (non-)conformity score, a measure of how
040
      well a data point aligns with the rest of the dataset. The key assumption is exchangeability, which is
041
      satisfied for independent and identically distributed (IID) data. Extensions of CP exist for dependent
042   data settings (9; 6; 43), but primarily focus on single-step predictions, and the extension to multistep
043   prediction is not trivial.
044
045
      CP offers finite-sample guarantees, making it an attractive approach to building joint prediction
      regions (JPRs). However, the exchangeability assumption of CP is always violated in time series data.
046
      Moreover, the multivariate residuals (H in total — one residual per time step) in a multi-step ahead
047
      prediction pose a challenge, as for most regression cases, CP relies on the exchangeability of the scalar
048   non-conformity scores and the quantile inversion for calibration. This poses the question of mapping
049   multivariate residuals to a scalar value. Whilst single-step prediction allows for simple residual
050   sorting and the necessary quantile inversion, this approach fails for multi-step scenarios due to the
051   multidimensionality of the residuals. We address the exchangeability issue in the inductive conformal
052   setting by showing an extension of the work of (9) from the transductive (full) to the inductive (split)
053   CP setting. Full CP requires fitting many models, whilst inductive CP only requires fitting a single
      model. The approach of (9) leverages specific index permutations of a single time series, providing a


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      distribution of residuals for each permuted time series. We further introduce a novel non-conformity
055   score designed to map multi-dimensional residuals to univariate quantities, enabling the construction
056   of valid JPRs. The resulting framework, JANET (Joint Adaptive predictioN-region Estimation for
057   Time-series), allows for inductive conformal prediction of JPRs in both multiple and single time
058   series settings. When applied to multiple independent time series, JANET guarantees validity as
059   the permutation across independent time series is exchangeable, whilst, for single time series, it
060   provides approximate validity under weak assumptions on the non-conformity scores as long as
061   transformations (permutations within a single series) of the data are a meaningful approximation for
062   a stationary series.
063   Our key contributions are: (i) formally generalising the framework in (9) to the inductive setting
064   for computational efficiency whilst maintaining approximate coverage guarantees; (ii) design of
065   non-conformity measures that effectively control the K-familywise error (a generalisation of the
066   familywise error) (25; 41), whilst accounting for time horizon and historical context; and (iii)
067   empirical demonstration of JANET’s (finite-sample) coverage guarantees, computational efficiency,
068   and adaptability to diverse time series scenarios.

070   2   R ELATED WORK
071
072   Relaxing exchangeability Recent research has shown a growing interest in adapting conformal
073   prediction (CP) to non-exchangeable data. Early work by (40) explored relaxing the exchangeability
074   assumption using Mondrian CP, which divides observations into exchangeable groups. (14) built
075   upon this idea to share strengths between groups in hierarchical modelling. (37) and (29) addressed
076   covariate and label shifts, respectively, by reweighting data based on likelihood ratios. Similarly,
077   (27) and (8) applied reweighting for predictive inference in causal inference and survival analysis,
078
      while (16) focused on controlling covariate shift by statisticians. However, these methods address
      heterogeneity rather than the serial dependence found in time series.
079
080
      One-step or multivariate prediction (20) tackled distribution shifts in an online manner by
081
      adapting coverage levels based on comparisons of current coverage with desired coverage. (44)
082
      extended this work with online expert aggregation. (21) later introduced an expert selection scheme
083   to guide update step sizes. These works typically require a gradient-based approach to learn a model
084   that adapts to the coverage. (6) generalised pure conformal inference for dependent data, using fixed
085   weights for recent training examples to account for distributional drift. (43) employed predictor
086   ensembling, assuming exchangeability but with asymptotic guarantees. (9) leveraged randomisation
087   inference (32) to generalise full conformal inference to serial dependence, achieving valid coverage
088   under exchangeability and approximate validity with serial dependence. Notably, these methods
089   primarily focus on single-step predictions for univariate series. A similar extension to the inductive
090   setting is provided in (12) for the functional time series setting. They randomly selected the time
091
      indices to form a calibration series, however, such an approach does not preserve the statistical
      properties of the time series. Additionally, the work is primarily based on applying to the time
092
      series and lacks formalisation as the generalised inductive conformal predictors. In contrast, we
093
      split the sequence into two such that we preserve the statistical properties of the time series; our
094   method formally extends (9) to inductive conformal prediction and multi-step scenarios, including
095   multivariate time series.
096
097
      Other notable works, (11; 1; 42) constructed prediction bands for multivariate functional data,
      functional surfaces and ellipsoidal regions for multivariate time series, respectively, but only for
098
      single-step predictions.
099
100
      Multi-step prediction (2) applied the jackknife method to RNN-based networks for multi-step
101
      prediction, with theoretical coverage of 1 − 2ϵ at significance level ϵ. (33) assumed conditional
102   IID prediction errors in multi-output models, using Bonferroni correction (22) to achieve desired
103   coverage. However, their approach can be overly conservative with increasing prediction horizons
104   and may not hold when model assumptions are violated. Additionally, they require multi-output
105   prediction models. (10) utilised linear complementary programming and an additional dataset to
106   optimise the parameters of a multi-step prediction error model. In recent work, (36), building on
107   (28), employed copulas to adjust for temporal dependencies, enabling multi-step and autoregressive
      predictions for multivariate time series filling in the deficit from other works. However, their method


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      requires two calibration sets and gradient-based optimisation, making it data-inefficient. Furthermore,
109   unlike our proposed framework JANET, their approach requires multiple independent time series
110   (just like the preceding works) and cannot adapt prediction regions based on historical context.
111
112
      A concurrent work (45) primarily focussed on single-step prediction adapted (20) to account for
      heterogeneous trajectories in multiple time series settings. However, they require multiple calibration
113
      sets and do not show how to control K−familywise error when dealing with multi-step prediction.
114
115
116   Non-conformal prediction regions Beyond conformal methods, bootstrapping provides an alterna-
117   tive method for constructing joint prediction regions especially when one only has a single time series.
118
      (34) generates B bootstraps and finds a heuristic-based prediction region from the bootstrapped
      predictive sequences, however, the method only provides have asymptotic guarantees. (41) creates
119
      JPRs that are asymptotically valid and can control the K-familywise error. However, given that this
120
      method is based on bootstrapping, it has a large computational cost that is infeasible when working
121   with neural networks. Our work can be seen as conformalisation of (41; 15) without relying on
122   bootstrapping and with adaptive prediction regions. Additionally, as a conformal method, JANET
123   can be readily applied to time series classification tasks which is not apparent for bootstrap-based
124   methods.
125
      To the best of our knowledge, JANET is the only framework that can handle both single and
126
      multiple time series (univariate or multivariate), whilst providing adaptive prediction regions based on
127   historical context (lagged values), and controlling K-familywise error with finite-sample guarantees.

130   3    BACKGROUND ON CONFORMAL PREDICTION
131
132   In this section, we provide an overview of conformal prediction for IID data in the context of
133   regression tasks. In the next section, we specify CP for the time series setting.
134
      Full conformal predictors assess the non-conformity of a test sample x∗ and a postulated target y to a
135
      training set by running the underlying algorithm (n + 1)c times, where n is the number of training
136   samples and c is the number of points along a discretised grid of the target space (Y). This is done to
137   construct prediction sets by inversion: the set of grid points that best conform according to a desired
138   significance level ϵ. Full CP is prohibitive for compute-intensive underlying training algorithms as it
139   requires refitting a model for each postulated label/grid point. Inductive conformal predictors (ICPs)
140   offer an elegant solution to this problem by training the model only once. We focus on regression
141   tasks with ICPs.
142   Let P be the data generating process for the sequence of n training examples Z1 , . . . , Zn , where
143   for each Zi = (Xi , Yi ) pair X ∈ X is the sample and Y ∈ Y is the target value. We partition the
144   sequence into a proper training set, Ztr = {Z1 , . . . , Zntr } (the first ntr elements), and the remaining
145   ncal elements form a calibration set, Zcal = {Zntr +1 , . . . , Zntr +ncal }, such that n = ntr + ncal . A
146   point prediction model, fˆ, is trained on the training set proper Ztr , and non-conformity scores, ai ,
147   are computed for each element of the calibration set, Zcal .
148
149
      In standard regression, a natural non-conformity score is the absolute residual ai = |yi − fˆ(xi )|. By
150   construction, for any i, yi ∈ [fˆ(xi ) ± ai ]; ai is half the interval width that ensures coverage for any
151
      new sample (assuming symmetric intervals). In ICP we compute a non-conformity score for each of
      the ncal samples in the calibration set and then sort them from largest to smallest. Let a(1) denote the
152
153   largest and a(ncal ) denote the smallest. Then intervals of the form (fˆ(xi ) ± a(1) ) will cover all but
154   one sample from the calibration set and intervals of the form (fˆ(xi ) ± a(ncal ) ) will cover none of the
155   samples from the calibration set (assuming no ties).
156   Extending this line of thinking, a prediction interval with (1−ϵ) coverage can be obtained by inverting
157   the quantile of the distribution of non-conformity scores ai . To do so we find the ⌊ϵ(ncal + 1)⌋th

175
176   In this section, we formally describe a generalised framework for inductive conformal predictors
177   based on Generalised CP (9) and randomisation inference (32) applied to time series forecasting.
178   With minor notational adjustments, our generalised ICP extends to multi-step and multivariate time
179   series prediction. In the following sections, we demonstrate how to obtain exact validity guarantees
180
      for independent time series (or IID data) and approximate validity guarantees for a single time series.
      Our task is to forecast H steps into the future conditioned on T steps of history.
181

269   A joint prediction region (JPR) typically controls the probability of the prediction region containing
      the entire true prediction sequence at a specified significance level ϵ. This can be interpreted as the


                                                              5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      probability of observing at least one element of the true sequence outside the region, also known as
271   the familywise error rate (FWER), namely:
272
273             FWER := Pr(at least one of the H components are not in the computed region).
274   However, as the prediction sequence length, H, increases, controlling for FWER can become overly
275   strict and lead to excessively large prediction regions. In such cases, the K-FWER offers a valuable
276   generalisation (25; 41). K-FWER relaxes the FWER definition, allowing for a specified number of
277   errors (K) within the true prediction sequence:
278
               K-FWER := Pr(at least K of the H components are not in the computed region)
279
280   When K = 1, the K-FWER reduces to the standard FWER. By allowing a tolerance for errors,
281   larger values of K yield smaller, more informative prediction regions, which can be beneficial in
282
      decision-making scenarios where some degree of error is acceptable.
283   Remark: JPRs can be constructed in various forms. Whilst hyperspherical construction is a common
284   choice, it may not facilitate reasoning about individual time steps in the horizon. Although it is
285   possible to project the hypersphere onto a hyperrectangle to enable component-wise analysis, this
286   results in a larger region and a loss of predictive efficiency (11; 41).
287   We introduce JANET (Joint Adaptive predictioN-region Estimation for Time-series) to control
288   K-FWER in multi-step time series prediction.

520   7    D ISCUSSION AND FUTURE WORK
521
522   In this paper, we have formally extended the Generalised Conformal Prediction framework proposed
523   by (9) to the inductive conformalisation setting (40). Building upon this foundation, we have
524   introduced JANET (Joint Adaptive predictioN-region Estimation for Time-series), a comprehensive
525   framework for constructing prediction regions in time series data. JANET is capable of producing
526   prediction regions with marginal intervals that adapt to both the time horizon and the relevant
527   historical context of the data. Notably, JANET effectively controls the K-FWER, a valuable feature
528   particularly when dealing with long prediction horizons.
529   JANET includes several desirable properties: computational efficiency, as it requires only a single
530   model training process; applicability to scenarios involving multiple independent time series, with
531   exact validity guarantees; and approximate validity guarantees when a single time series is available.
532   Furthermore, JANET is flexible enough to provide asymmetric or one-sided prediction regions, a
533   capability not readily available in many existing methods.
534   Looking towards future work, we envision several promising directions for extending JANET. One
535   avenue involves exploring the extension of JANET to a cross-conformal setting (40), which could offer
536   gains in predictive efficiency at the expense of potentially weaker coverage guarantees. Additionally,
537   we acknowledge a current limitation of JANET, which is its inability to create multiple disjoint
538   prediction regions (23). Such disjoint regions could be denser than a single joint region, thereby
539   providing more informative uncertainty estimates, particularly in cases with multimodal predictive
      distributions. We intend to address this limitation in future research.
