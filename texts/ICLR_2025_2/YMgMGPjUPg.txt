009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            Out-of-distribution detection (OOD) is a crucial technique for deploying machine
012            learning models in the real world to handle the unseen scenarios. Compared
013            to standard classification tasks, OOD detection presents significant challenges
014            due to the unpredictable nature and inherent difficulty in collecting OOD data.
015            Consequently, a natural solution is to develop priors that are as diverse as possible,
016
               effectively characterizing the features of OOD data. In this paper, we first propose
               a simple yet effective Neural Activation Prior (NAP) for OOD detection. Our prior
017
               is based on a key observation that, for a channel before the pooling layer of a fully
018
               trained neural network, the probability of a few neurons being activated with a
019            large response by an in-distribution (ID) sample is significantly higher than that
020            by an OOD sample. An intuitive explanation is that for a model fully trained on
021            ID dataset, each channel would play a role in detecting a certain pattern in the ID
022            dataset, and a few neurons can be activated with a large response when the pattern
023            is detected in an input sample. Then, an effective scoring function based on this
024            prior is proposed to highlight the role of these strongly activated neurons in OOD
025            detection. Our approach is plug-and-play and does not lead to any performance
026            degradation on ID data classification and requires no extra training or statistics from
027
               training or external datasets. To the best of our knowledge, our method is the first to
               exploit intra-channel activation pattern information, contributing to its orthogonality
028
               to existing approaches and allowing it to be effectively combined with them in
029
               various applications. Furthermore, we conduct an elegant oracle experiment to
030            validate the rationale behind our proposed scoring function. Extensive experimental
031            results demonstrate the effectiveness of our method. Moreover, our approach can
032            significantly boost the performance when integrated with most existing methods,
033            showcasing the unique attributes of the proposed prior.

036   1    I NTRODUCTION
037
038   Deep learning has developed rapidly in the last decade and become a crucial technique in various
039
      fields. However, neural networks would frequently make erroneous judgments in inference when en-
      countering the data that differs greatly from their training data, which is known as out-of-distribution
040
      (OOD) data. This challenge is particularly vital in safety-critical areas such as autonomous driv-
041
      ing (Filos et al., 2020; Janai et al., 2020) and medical diagnosis (Pooch et al., 2020), which urges the
042   development of effective OOD detection methods.
043
044   In practice, OOD data exhibits large diversity and is difficult to identify (Yang et al., 2021). Existing
045
      studies typically formulate OOD detection as a one-class classification task, utilizing prior knowledge.
      They (Hendrycks & Gimpel, 2016; Liu et al., 2020; Yu et al., 2023) propose various priors, based
046
      on which they further design scoring functions to distinguish OOD samples from ID samples. For
047
      example, Hendrycks & Gimpel (2016) observed that OOD samples always exhibit lower maximum
048   softmax probabilities, and accordingly proposed using the maximal softmax probability output by a
049   neural network as an OOD indicator. Liu et al. (2020) found that OOD samples usually have lower
050   logits values, and based on this, an energy function was proposed for OOD detection. Drawing from
051   these precedents, it’s clear that existing methods largely rely on the introduction of certain priors.
052   While some promising results highlight the effectiveness of these heuristics, a gap remains in meeting
053   the practical requirements of real-world applications. Furthermore, some existing methods (Olber
      et al., 2023; Dong et al., 2022; Djurisic et al., 2022; Sun et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2023; Sun et al., 2022)

097   In this paper, we propose a novel prior, called Neural Activation Prior (NAP), for OOD detection. NAP
098   characterizes our key observation that, in fully trained neural networks, certain neurons in channels
099
      preceding the global pooling layer (Figure 3) exhibit significantly higher activation probabilities for
      ID samples compared to OOD samples. The rationale behind NAP is that channels in models trained
100
      on ID datasets develop sensitivity to specific patterns within that distribution. When these patterns are
101
      detected, a subset of neurons activates strongly (Hoefler et al., 2021). Such pronounced activations
102   are predominantly observed with ID inputs and are rare for OOD data, which typically lack these
103   learned patterns. To validate NAP, we analyzed the mean and maximum within-channel activations
104   at the penultimate layer of DenseNet (Huang et al., 2017), trained on CIFAR-10 (Krizhevsky et al.,
105   2009) and iSun (Xu et al., 2015) datasets (Figure 1). It clearly demonstrates that ID samples exhibit
106   significantly higher maximal activation values than OOD samples at equal average activation levels.
107   Consequently, existing methods based on pooled activation values struggle to effectively distinguish
      these OOD samples, given the non-discriminative nature of their average activation values.

172   2.1   OOD DETECTION
173
      The OOD detection community has explored a variety of techniques to underscore the distinctions
174
      between ID and OOD samples. These methods encompass classification-based (Huang & Li, 2021;
175
      Liang et al., 2018; Bendale & Boult, 2016; DeVries & Taylor, 2018; Hendrycks & Gimpel, 2016;
176   Sastry & Oore, 2020; Tack et al., 2020; Du et al., 2022), density-based (Zong et al., 2018; Abati
177   et al., 2019; Nalisnick et al., 2018; Zisselman & Tamar, 2020; Jiang et al., 2021; Pidhorskyi et al.,
178   2018; Kirichenko et al., 2020; Sabokrou et al., 2018), and distance-based approaches (Lee et al.,
179   2018b; Ming et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2020; Zaeemzadeh et al., 2021; Van Amersfoort et al., 2020;
180   Techapanurak et al., 2020; Lu et al., 2023; Sun et al., 2022), with classification-based techniques
181   generally outperforming the other types (Yang et al., 2021). In classification-based methods, the basic
182   work of OOD detection starts with a simple and effective baseline: using the Maximum Softmax
183   Probability (MSP) (Hendrycks & Gimpel, 2016) to measure the probability that a certain sample
184
      is an ID sample. On this basis, early approaches (Liang et al., 2018; Hsu et al., 2020; Liu et al.,
      2020) focused on developing enhanced OOD indicators derived from neural network outputs. In
185
      addition, some researchers have proposed strategies involving OOD sample generation (Lee et al.,
186
      2018a; Du et al., 2022) and gradient-based (Liang et al., 2018) techniques. Among these, certain
187   post-hoc methods (Hendrycks & Gimpel, 2016; Liang et al., 2018; Liu et al., 2020; Sun et al., 2021;
188   Sun & Li, 2022; Djurisic et al., 2022; Yu et al., 2023; Du et al., 2022) are notable for their simplicity
189   and because they do not necessitate changes in the training process or objectives. This feature is
190   particularly valuable for implementing OOD detection in real production environments, where the
191   additional cost and complexity associated with retraining would be unacceptable.
192
      The MSP method, initially presented by Hendrycks & Gimpel (2016), was a formative step in post
193   hoc OOD detection, using a neural network’s softmax output as a heuristic for distinguishing ID from
194   OOD samples. Its straightforward application facilitated early adoption in OOD studies. Despite
195   MSP’s influence, its limitations prompted further innovation, giving rise to the Energy method.
196   This method, proposed by Liu et al. (2020), refines the approach by assigning an energy score
197   to network outputs, showing quantitative improvements over MSP with theoretical and empirical
198   support. Advancements in post hoc OOD detection have led to diverse methodological branches
199   stemming from MSP and Energy paradigms. Ahn et al. (2023) innovates by reducing neuron-induced
200   noise through the calculation of Shapley values. Yu et al. (2023) distinguish ID from OOD data by
201
      identifying neural network blocks with optimal differentiation based on the norms of their features.
      Sun & Li (2022) improves discrimination by pruning weights in the fully connected layer according
202
      to the contribution units make during classification. On the other end of the spectrum, entirely
203
      computation-free post hoc methods such as ReAct (Sun et al., 2021) and ASH (Djurisic et al., 2022)
204   have shown promise. Sun et al. (2021) investigates activations prior to the fully connected layer,
205   applying rectification to suppress extreme activations that OOD data tend to trigger, thereby achieving
206   refined detection outcomes. Similarly, Djurisic et al. (2022) prunes the activations inputted to the
207   fully connected layer, but it achieves even more enhanced results compared to Sun & Li (2022) by its
208   selective pruning strategy. In this paper, our comparison mainly focuses on post hoc methods, since
209   our method also belongs to this category.
210   Recent advancements in OOD detection have highlighted the rich informational content embedded
211   within the penultimate layer activations. Notable contributions in this domain include NAPattern (Ol-
212   ber et al., 2023), NMD (Dong et al., 2022), ASH (Djurisic et al., 2022), ReAct (Sun et al., 2021),
213   NAC-UE (Liu et al., 2023), and KNN (Sun et al., 2022). Table 1 presents a comprehensive analysis
214   of these methods, juxtaposing them with our proposed NAP approach. Our method’s distinctive
215   feature lies in its exploitation of intra-channel activation patterns, a dimension largely unexplored by
      existing techniques. This novel perspective renders NAP complementary to current methodologies.

300   4.1   BASICS
301
      First, we will provide a brief overview of typical settings for OOD detection in image classification
302
      networks. Typically, classification networks are trained on ID data, that is, known training data sets.
303   Once training is completed, the model can effectively classify the categories within the training data
304   set. During the inference, samples mixed with OOD data are fed into this trained model. To identify
305   OOD samples, researchers usually introduce a scoring function into the model. The model not only
306   classifies each sample, but also uses a scoring function to generate a score for each one. This score is
307   used to predict whether a sample belongs to an ID class in the training set, or an unknown OOD class.
308
309   4.2   S CORING FUNCTION DESIGN
310
311   Based on our prior proposed previously in Section 3, we propose a SNR-like scoring function. In
312   our formulation, the mean activation value is interpreted as the noise intensity, while the maximal
313   activation value is regarded as the signal strength. This conceptual framework leads to the following
314
      scoring function:

322
323   [Rationale behind the scoring function design.] We discuss the reasoning behind our scoring
      function design to clarify the use of activation values as follows:


                                                         6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      I. Why maximum value represent signal strength? Each channel typically detects a specific pattern,
325   and the area of the pattern in the image determines the size of the high activation region. The size
326   of this region does not reflect whether the sample is an OOD sample. Therefore, using the mean
327   to measure signal strength is unreasonable because it is influenced by the size of the pattern’s area.
328   Instead, the maximum activation value reflects the confidence in the detected pattern. Thus, we use
329   the maximum activation value to indicate the activation intensity of the pattern.
330
      II. Why mean value represent noise strength? Although variance is generally used to measure noise
331   strength, the sparsity of activation values within the channel is influenced by the pattern’s area size in
332   the image, thus the size of the activation region largely determines the variance. Therefore, using
333   variance to measure noise is unreasonable. Instead, dividing the maximum value by the mean can
334   serve as a normalization term, removing the influence of activation values from non-pattern regions
335   (also known as background noise). Thus, we use the mean as the noise term in this SNR-like format.
336
      To substantiate the effectiveness of our scoring function design, we employ an "Oracle" experiment
337   in Section 6.1, providing empirical evidence for its validity in various scenarios.
338
339   Remark 1. The scoring function we proposed is a plug-and-play approach that can be easily
340
      integrated into existing neural network architectures. It requires no additional training or external
      data and retains the model’s inherent classification capabilities. These properties make it practical
341
      and suitable for a variety of applications.
342
343   Remark 2. While CNNs are commonly used in OOD detection tasks, Transformer architec-
344   tures (Vaswani et al., 2017) have proven effective across various applications. Motivated by this,
345   we extend our method for compatibility with Transformers. Details of our experimental validation,
346
      which confirm the method’s robustness and adaptability to different architectures, are provided in the
      Appendix K.

350
      Based on our earlier discussion of orthogonality, our method can be flexibly integrated with existing
351   approaches. In this paper, we present a natural paradigm as follows for combining it with existing
352   methods and leave the exploration of more optimal integration strategies for future work:
353
354
                              SNAP-Other (x; f, w) = SOther (x; f )w · SNAP (x; f )1−w ,
355
356
      where SOther (x; f ) represents the score from other methods, and w is the weighting parameter. The
357   parameter w is constrained to the interval (0, 1), and in our experiments, it typically takes values
358   in {0.2, 0.3, . . . , 0.8}. In the following sections, we propose a method to find the optimal w, and in
359   Section 6.2, we conduct an analysis of w’s sensitivity, finding that the performance remains stable
360   within the range [0.2, 0.8]. Also, we conducted experiments using SNAP independently and found that
361   it achieves excellent OOD detection performance on its own. For a more detailed discussion, please
362   refer to Section 6.3.
363   [How to find a optimal parameter w?] While our method demonstrates robustness to variations in
364   the parameter w (see Section 6.2), determining the optimal w can be achieved using the following
365   approach. We utilized a set of data transformation techniques (such as Gaussian noise, glass blur,
366   motion blur, etc., more details in the Appendix H) to generate a corrupted dataset based on the
367   ID dataset, serving as pseudo OOD data. For the choice of transformation types, we referred to
368   Hendrycks & Dietterich (2018). Utilizing this set of crafted OOD data, we employed a binary search
369   method to find the optimal w. Through experimentation with various datasets and methods, we
370   found that this search approach quickly identifies the optimal w, which generalizes well to real OOD
371
      datasets. Please refer to Appendix H for more details about this process.

528   7      C ONCLUSION
529
530   This paper presents a novel Neural Activation Prior for OOD detection, based on the observation that
531
      in-distribution samples typically generate stronger activations in specific channel neurons compared
      to OOD samples. Our method offers simplicity, easy integration, and maintains in-distribution
532
      classification performance without requiring additional training or external data. Extensive experi-
533
      ments across various datasets and architectures demonstrate the robustness and generalizability of
534   our approach. Specifically, our prior enriches the community’s library of priors for OOD detection
535   due to its orthogonality to previous ones, enhancing detection accuracy across a variety of existing
536   methods. Our findings indicate that when used independently, our OOD detection method achieves
537   performance comparable to current state-of-the-art techniques. Moreover, integrating our approach
538   with existing methods leads to significant improvements, reducing FPR95 by up to 66.03%. These
539   results confirm that our prior captures complementary information, highlighting its potential to
      advance OOD detection and contribute to enhanced reliability in machine learning systems.

803   B    P SEUDO CODE FOR NAP
804
805   As illustrated in Algorithm 1, we present a detailed pseudo-code representation of our proposed
806   method for OOD detection, which is integrated into the DenseNet architecture. The key modification
807   involves the calculation of NAP score S within the DenseNet’s processing pipeline (highlighted in
808   green font in the algorithm), which is then followed by calculating the OOD score using the model’s
809   logits together with S. These calculations do not alter the logits output by the model, thereby ensuring
      no degradation in classification accuracy for the ID dataset.

      Ensure: Output logits, OOD Score

834   C    W HY IS THE PENULTIMATE LAYER MORE EFFECTIVE FOR NAP?
835
836   We provide an extensive collection of visualizations showcasing the activations within the DenseNet
837   architecture when applied to the CIFAR-10 (ID) dataset and Places365 (OOD) dataset. These
838   visualizations are crucial for understanding how the network processes both ID and OOD data,
839   revealing the distinct patterns of neural activations at various layers of the network. Our analysis
840   focuses on four critical layers within DenseNet: (1) after the first convolutional layer, (2) before the
841   pooling operation in the first transition block, (3) before pooling in the second transition block, and (4)
842   before the final global pooling layer. Each layer offers four visualizations, providing a comprehensive
843   view of the network’s response to different datasets.
844   These detailed visualizations enhance the discussion in the main text, offering deeper insights into
845   how NAP effectively distinguishes between ID and OOD samples within the network. As depicted in
846   Figure 5, the first three selected layers, which focus primarily on low-level features, exhibit a less
847   pronounced distinction between ID and OOD samples. This is likely because low-level features,
848   such as edges and textures, are common to both ID and OOD datasets, making them less distinctive.
849   However, the contrast between ID and OOD samples becomes more evident and stable in the fourth
850
      selected layer, located before the final global pooling layer. This layer, concentrating on high-level
      semantic information, captures features more unique to the ID dataset, leading to clearer separability
851
      and enhanced stability in activation values compared to earlier layers. This layer’s focus on distinctive
852
      semantic features makes it particularly suitable for developing a scoring function for OOD detection.
853   Therefore, since the penultimate layer is the most informative layer in the neural network, we utilize
854   this layer in our method to develop our scoring function.

       To understand the mechanism by which NAP achieves this, it is essential to delve into how neural
1081   networks process and distinguish between different types of data. Neural network classifiers are
1082   adept at detecting various semantic features through different channels in the penultimate layer. It
1083   is this capability that NAP leverages to differentiate between ID and OOD data. NAP distinguishes
1084   between ID and OOD data based on the neural network’s high response to specific semantic features
1085   of ID data. Thus, in principle, NAP is well-suited for semantic OOD detection, capable of effectively
1086   distinguishing between samples from closely related distributions but with different semantics (e.g.,
1087   CIFAR-10 vs CIFAR-100, as shown in the table below). However, it is important to note that NAP is
1088   not intended for more fine-grained tasks, such as pixel-level industrial surface defect detection.

1092   In this section, we present additional examples of activation map visualizations to further illustrate
1093   the challenges and phenomena discussed in our work. Specifically, we examine the penultimate
1094   layer’s activation maps for both in-distribution (ID) and out-of-distribution (OOD) samples. The
1095   visualizations provide insights into how global pooling methods can obscure important distinctions
1096   between ID and OOD samples by averaging out the spatial distribution of activation values within
1097   channels.
1098   As shown in Figure 8, each pair of images displays the activation maps for a given channel. The
1099   left image in each pair corresponds to an ID sample, while the right image corresponds to an OOD
1100   sample. Different channels in the penultimate layer are typically tuned to capture specific semantic
1101   features present in the training data. For ID samples, these features often result in high activation
1102   values in particular regions of the activation map. For example, certain regions in the left images of
1103   each pair exhibit very high activation values when the corresponding semantic features are present in
1104   the ID sample.
1105   In contrast, OOD samples, which do not contain these specific semantic features, may still produce
1106   activation responses due to the model’s unfamiliarity with such data. This can result in weak noise
1107   activations, as observed in the right images of each pair. This phenomenon highlights the difficulty
1108   faced by traditional OOD detection methods that rely on aggregated activation values, as discussed
1109   by Sun et al. (2021). These methods often fail to differentiate between ID and OOD samples because
1110   the average channel activations do not provide sufficient discriminative power.
1111   However, the NAP score proposed in this paper addresses this issue by effectively distinguishing
1112   between ID and OOD samples based on a more nuanced analysis of activation patterns. The following
1113   visualizations exemplify the described behavior and underscore the importance of considering the
1114   distribution of activation values within channels for robust OOD detection.

1231   Figure 8: Global pooling disregards the distribution of activation values within channels, making
1232   it challenging to differentiate between ID and OOD samples. Each pair of images in this figure
1233
       illustrates the activation maps of the penultimate layer for in-distribution (ID) samples (left) and
       out-of-distribution (OOD) samples (right) within the same channel. In this layer, different channels
1234
       typically focus on distinct semantic features. When specific features are present in the image, such
1235
       as certain regions in the left images of each pair, these areas exhibit very high activation values.
1236   Although OOD samples lack these specific features, the model’s unfamiliarity with OOD data can
1237   lead to unpredictable activations, potentially resulting in weak noise activations (right images). This
1238   phenomenon is discussed in detail by Sun et al. (2021). Existing methods often rely on aggregating
1239   activation values for OOD detection. Consequently, the average channel activations of ID and OOD
1240   samples are not discriminative, making it difficult for existing methods to distinguish between them.
1241   However, the NAP score proposed in this paper effectively differentiates them.
