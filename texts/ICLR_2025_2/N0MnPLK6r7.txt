009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            As Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) are increasingly applied across various do-
012            mains, explainability has become a critical factor for real-world applications. Exist-
013            ing post-hoc explainability methods primarily focus on estimating the importance
014            of edges, nodes, or subgraphs in the input graph to identify substructures crucial for
015            predictions. However, these methods often lack human interpretability and do not
016
               provide a unified framework that incorporates both model-level and instance-level
               explanations. In this context, we propose leveraging a set of graphlets—small,
017
               connected, non-isomorphic induced subgraphs widely used in various scientific
018
               fields—and their associated orbits as human-interpretable units to decompose
019            GNN predictions. Domain experts can select the most relevant graphlets as inter-
020            pretable units and request unified explanations based on these units. To address
021            this problem, we introduce UO-Explainer, the Unified and Orbit-based Explainer
022            for GNNs, which utilizes predefined orbits that are generalizable and universal
023            across graph domains as interpretable units. Our model decomposes GNN weights
024            into orbit units to extract class-specific graph patterns (model-level) and to identify
025            important subgraphs within individual data instances for prediction (instance-level).
026            Extensive experimental results demonstrate that UO-Explainer outperforms ex-
027
               isting baselines in providing meaningful and interpretable explanations across
               both synthetic and real-world datasets. Our code and datasets are available at
028
               https://anonymous.4open.science/r/uoexplainer-F12C.

031   1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) have achieved state-of-the-art performance in various domains in-
034   cluding real-world graph-structured data, such as social networks (Fan et al., 2019), molecules (Duve-
035   naud et al., 2015), and knowledge graphs (Hogan et al., 2021). Despite the remarkable advancements
036   in GNN architectures (Hamilton et al., 2017; Kipf & Welling, 2017; Xu et al., 2019; Veličković
037   et al., 2018), they are still perceived as black box models due to their lack of explainability. This
038   deficiency limits trust in GNN predictions, hindering their application in areas such as drug de-
039
      velopment (Gaudelet et al., 2021) and education (Nakagawa et al., 2019). Therefore, interpreting
      the prediction of GNNs has become crucial and has led to the emergence of various explanatory
040
      approaches.
041
042   Explainability methods in graph domains deliver explanations through subgraphs that play a signifi-
043   cant role in predictions regarding the input graph. Two primary issues arise regarding explainability:
044   i) a human-interpretability and ii) a unified framework encompassing both model and instance levels.
045
      Many existing post-hoc explanability is grounded on perturbation-based methods (Ying et al., 2019;
      Luo et al., 2020; Xie et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2023; Schlichtkrull et al., 2022) and gradient-based
046
      methods (Baldassarre & Azizpour, 2019; Pope et al., 2019) to approximate the importance of edges
047
      or nodes within subgraphs. This stochastic optimization of importance is computationally effective in
048   applying any graph-structured data, these methods have the potential risk that the output subgraph
049   does not align with prior human assumption or knowledge. Specifically, consider a scientist studying
050   gene networks who is interested in understanding whether the presence of certain structures, such as
051   triangles or rectangles, plays a crucial role in predicting specific properties. This scientist would want
052   to audit the model’s predictions by utilizing an explanation method that can highlight the importance
053   of these structures. With existing methods, it is challenging to obtain explicit insights into whether a
      triangular or rectangular structure in the network is more important; instead, users often have to rely


                                                          1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      on conjecture based on the generated explanations. Additionally, these explanations do not always
055   present results in a human-intuitive form, as they may include isolated nodes or disconnected edges,
056   further hindering their interpretability. Therefore, we emphasize the need for explainability that
057   centers on human interpretability, a perspective that has been largely underexplored.
058
      Last but not least, human-interpretable explanations should be delivered in a unified framework,
059
      incorporating both global and local levels in terms of the scope of explanation (Yuan et al., 2022;
060
      Prado-Romero et al., 2024). Most of the existing methods primarily specialize one one-level expla-
061   nation either model or instance-level explanations. Model-level methods reveal patterns that GNNs
062   deem significant for specific classes, eventually offering a broad understanding of the GNNs’ general
063   behavior. On the other hand, instance-level methods focus on individual predictions, identifying the
064   subgraphs most relevant to the target node or graph. As each type of explanation complements the
065   other from different perspectives, understanding at both levels enhances the explainability necessary
066   for grasping the decision-making process of GNNs. However, State-of-the-art (Azzolin et al., 2022;
067   Chen et al., 2023) studies also have rarely explored a unified framework that simultaneously provides
068   both model-level and instance-level explanations in the user-centric perspective of interest.
069   In this paper, we prioritize the two crucial perspectives that explanation should be human-interpretable
070   in the unified framework incorporating both model and instance levels. Our proposed model, UO-
071   Explainer, the Unified and Orbit-based Explainer for GNNs, allows users to harness their prior
072   knowledge by giving room to select the user-defined explanation units in unified views. Considering
073   the uniqueness of the graph domain to define explanation unit, we exploit orbits within graphlets that
074   have been studied as a generalizable and universal pattern in many scientific fields such as protein
075
      interaction networks Pržulj et al. (2004); Pržulj (2007), social networks Chen & Lui (2018); Ahmed
      et al. (2015), and molecular structure networks Kondor et al. (2009), while users can also adopt a
076
      unique prior perspective to define their unit instead of orbit. To provide unified explanations, we
077
      decompose the weights into orbit representation vectors to understand the contribution of each orbit
078   for a specific class or prediction. When we break down the weight into orbits, we acknowledge the
079   contribution of each orbit for model decision-making, which is supposed to be further discussed in the
080   method section in detail. Through extensive experiments, we demonstrate the superior performance
081   of UO-Explainer on eight well-known datasets. Consequently, UO-Explainer shows the concrete
082   performance in a unified framework leveraging the human prior knowledge as orbit generalizable
083   unit in graph-structured datasets.
084   In summary, the contributions of our research are as follows:
085
086          • We propose UO-Explainer, a unified framework to provide both model-level and instance-
087            level explanations for node classification based on human-defined explanation units.
088
             • We demonstrate the human interpretable explanation based on orbits generalizable and
089            effective on graph-structured datasets.
090
             • We perform rigorous and extensive experiments on 8 datasets to evaluate the quality of our
091
               explanations in both model and instance-level explanations.

      3.4     T IME C OMPLEXITY A NALYSIS
306
307
      Training UO-Explainer consists of two main processes: orbit basis learning and class-orbit score
308
      learning, with the time complexity for each process detailed below. The time complexity of orbit
309
      basis learning is O(∣O∣ ∣V∣), where training is conducted for each orbit basis across all nodes in
310   the input graph, as outlined in Algorithm 1. Here, ∣O∣ represents the number of orbits used for
311   explanation, and ∣V∣ denotes the total number of nodes in the input graph. The time complexity
312   for class-orbit score learning depends on the number of orbits selected through greedy search for
313   each class weight. In our experiments, the number of orbits selected did not exceed 5, rendering
314   this complexity component negligible. Consequently, the overall time complexity for this phase is
315   represented as O(∣C∣), following the procedure in Algorithm 2, where C indicates the number of
316   classes. In short, the overall time complexity for training UO-Explainer is O(∣O∣ ∣V∣ + ∣C∣) ≈ O(∣V∣).
317   The time complexities of the baseline methods are as follows: D4Explainer has a time complexity
               3
318   of O(∣V∣ ), GNNExplainer is O(∣V∣ ∣E∣) where ∣E∣ denotes the number of edges, PGExplainer
319   and TAGE have a time complexity of O(∣E∣), and MotifExplainer operates with a complexity of
320   O(∣V∣ ∣M∣), where ∣M∣ represents the number of motifs used in explanations. Therefore, in terms
321   of time complexity, UO-Explainer is less demanding compared to D4Explainer and GNNExplainer.
322   Alongside such analysis, our unified model is capable of providing both model-level and instance-
323   level explanations simultaneously, thus demonstrating its competitiveness in terms of time efficiency.
      A more detailed time complexity analysis and experiments are described in Appendix C.

      4     R ELATED W ORK
325
326
      GNN explanation methods can be categorized into model-level and instance-level, each with its
327   own scope of explanation (Yuan et al., 2022). Model-level methods (Yuan et al., 2020; Shin et al.,
328   2022; Azzolin et al., 2022) offer explanations that describe the general behavior underlying GNN
329   predictions regarding to specific class. For example, XGNN (Yuan et al., 2020) aims to generate
330   model-level patterns that maximize the predictive probability of a certain class by training a graph
331   generator through reinforcement learning. Similarly, PAGE (Shin et al., 2022) employs graph
332   representation vectors to iteratively search for human-interpretable prototype graphs. Moreover,
333   GLGExplainer (Azzolin et al., 2022) provides general model-level patterns by aggregating instance-
334   level explanations into logical formulas, utilizing an Entropy-Logic Explainer (E-LEN) (Barbiero
335
      et al., 2022; Ciravegna et al., 2023). However, these studies primarily focus on graph classification
      tasks and do not directly apply to node classification tasks. To bridge this gap, D4Explainer (Chen
336
      et al., 2023) introduces a method for providing counterfactual model-level explanations for node
337
      classification tasks, alongside instance-level explanations, based on a diffusion model. This approach,
338   however, results in a high training cost for the explainer incurred by iterative diffusion- and deonising
339   steps. Moreover, none of the methods for model-level explanations fully consider the user-centric
340   explanation unit, hindering human-interpretability.
341
      Instance-level methods (Yu & Gao, 2022; Ying et al., 2019; Luo et al., 2020; Xie et al., 2022;
342
      Vu & Thai, 2020; Wang et al., 2023; Xiong et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023; Ye et al., 2024; Lu
343
      et al., 2024) provide explanations in the form of subgraph to elucidate the prediction of a specific
344   instance by unveiling relational structures with high contributions in the input graph. Notably,
345   GNNExplainer (Ying et al., 2019) stands as an early instance-level explainer, optimizing edge and
346   feature masks to maximize mutual information with GNN prediction results. PGExplainer (Luo
347   et al., 2020) leverages node representation vectors and trains a parameterized mask predictor to
348   optimize edge masks for explanation in inductive settings. TAGE (Xie et al., 2022) explains the GNN
349   embedding models, allowing efficient explanations for multiple downstream tasks. All these methods
350   entail learning edge masks to present masked graphs as instance-level explanation subgraphs. Despite
351   connectivity constraints, it often fails to generate connected subgraphs, resulting in less intuitive
352
      explanations. On the other hand, MotifExplainer (Yu & Gao, 2022) provides an instance-level
      explanation that does not rely on edge masks. It computes embedding vectors for each motif and
353
      extracts explanations by restoring the GNN’s prediction value using an attention network. However,
354
      it overlooks both the universal importance of specific motifs and the orbit-based isomorphism when
355   extracting motifs. Furthermore, the abovementioned methods are limited to providing explanations
356   in the unified framework capable of simultaneously providing both model-level and instance-level
357   explanations under the human-interpretable units of interest.

      5.2     E VALUATION M ETRIC
405
406   We evaluate the quality of explanations using Sparsity, Fidelity, Edge-recall, and Sub-recall as
407   evaluation metrics. Sparsity (Li et al., 2022) refers to the ratio of edges in the explanation compared
408   to the total number of edges in the computation graph of the target node. High sparsity implies
409   that the proposed explanation has a small number of edges. Fidelity (Li et al., 2022) measures the
410   difference in the probability values when the explanation is excluded from the computation graph
411   based on the target node. Edge-recall indicates how many edges in the explanations match the edges
412
      in the ground truths. Sub-Recall indicates the proportion of correct answers that the entire presented
      explanations match with ground truths. Notably, equations of Sparsity and Fidelity are described in
413
      the Appendix D.4. Additionally, we conducted experiments five times and then reported the average
414
      and standard deviations in Appendix E.

530
531   We introduce UO-Explainer, a human-interpretable explanation method that leverages pre-defined
532   units, as requested by users, in a unified framework for node classification models. By utilizing
533
      orbits as explanatory units, UO-Explainer decomposes model weights into orbit components, which
      serve as essential, human-interpretable units within graph domains. Experimental results on both
534
      synthetic and real-world datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of UO-Explainer, outperforming
535
      baseline methods and delivering higher-quality explanations. UO-Explainer is particularly valuable
536   in scientific applications, such as drug development and education, where domain knowledge is
537   critical. By using pre-defined explanation units, users can uncover meaningful patterns and gain
538   deeper insights through the explanation method.

788
789   When UO-Explainer provides instance-level explanations, there are four time-consuming processes:
790   1) Pre-processing for finding the existence of the orbit, 2) Orbit basis learning, 3) Class-orbit score
791   learning (Model-level explanation generation), and 4) Generating instance-level explanations.
792
      1) Pre-processing for finding the existence of each orbit. As mentioned in Section 3.1, to learn the
793
      orbit basis, we must find the existence of each orbit for every node, as represented by the equation
794   1. This pre-processing can be conducted to search 2-5 node graphlets for each node. The time
795   complexity of finding each graphlet that includes specific orbits, as can be inferred from Algorithm
796   3 and Algorithm 4, is O(dk−1 )1 . Here, d represents the maximum node degree of the graph data,
797   and k denotes the number of nodes included in each graphlet and is less than 5. Therefore, the time
798   complexity of pre-processing for finding all 2-5 node graphlets that include a total 0-72 orbit for an
799   entire node of graph data is O(∣O∣ ∣V∣ dk−1 ) where ∣O∣ denotes the number of edges and ∣V∣ represents
800   the number of nodes.
801   2) Orbit basis learning. The time complexity of orbit basis learning is O(∣O∣ ∣V∣) since training is
802   performed for each orbit basis over all nodes as shown in Algorithm 1.
803

804
      the greedy search for each class weight. In our experiments, the number of selected orbits was less
805
      than or equal to 5, so this can be sufficiently neglected. Therefore, the time complexity of class-orbit

      We conducted experiments using five synthetic datasets and three real-world datasets. The statistics
940

941   below.
942
943   Random Graph (Holme & Kim, 2002): This dataset does not have node feature values, so we
944
      performed node classification tasks using only the structural information by setting all node feature
      values to the same constant value. We set the class of each node based on the existence of its orbit
945
      same as each task number. That is, each node has two classes, c0 (orbit doesn’t exist) and c1 (orbit
946
      exists) for a total of 73 independent tasks, one for each orbit. The ground truth for class c1 is the
947   target orbit ok for each task.
948
949   BA-Shapes (Ying et al., 2019): The node classification dataset consists of a BA graph with 300 nodes
950
      as the base and 80 ”house-like motifs” randomly attached, each consisting of 5 nodes. The house-like
      motif precisely matches graphlet g23 . The class is determined by the positions of the nodes within
951
      the house-like motif; these node positions precisely match the orbits o58 , o57 , o56 in g23 . Therefore,
952
      the ground truth for each class is set to o58 , o57 , o56 .
953
954   BA-Community (Ying et al., 2019): The node classification dataset is generated by randomly
955   combining two house-like motifs through edges. The first motif is assigned classes c0 , c1 , c2 , c3 based
956
      on the positions of the nodes, and the second motif is assigned c4 , c5 , c6 , c7 , resulting in a total of
      eight class labels. The ground truth of each motif is the same as that of the BA-Shapes dataset.
957
958   Tree-Cycle (Ying et al., 2019): The dataset consists of a balanced binary tree as the base and 80
959   ”six-node cycle motifs” randomly attached. The task is to classify nodes as either not belonging (c0 )
960   to or belonging to a circle (c1 ). The ground truth for c1 class corresponds to the circle motif. However,
961   2-5 node graphlets do not encompass the six-node circle motif. Thus, we conducted experiments by
962
      incorporating the circle motif and the orbit within the circle into candidate graphlets and orbits.
963   Tree-Grid (Ying et al., 2019): The base is identical to that of the Tree-Cycle, with a modification
964   involving the attachment of a “nine-node grid” in place of the circle. The task has been further
965   challengingly augmented to classify each node as belonging to the center (c1 ), periphery (c2 ), or
966   cross-lines (c3 ) based on their positions within the grid. Similar to the case of Tree-Cycle, 2-5 node
967   graphlets do not contain the nine-node grid. Therefore, we included the grid motif and the orbits
968
      within the grid in the candidate set for experimentation.
969   Protein-Protein Interaction (PPI) (Zitnik & Leskovec, 2017): This is a node classification dataset
970   that represents protein-protein interactions in various human tissues using 24 different graphs. Each
971   node represents a protein and features such as positional, motif gene, and immunological signature
      are used with a size of 50 dimensions. Protein-to-protein interactions are represented as edges. Each

       G           L IMITATION AND F UTURE W ORK
1010
1011
       In this study, we propose UO-Explainer, a GNN explanation method that utilizes graphlets and orbits
       as explanation units. Though we have demonstrated that UO-Explainer can provide high-quality
1012
       explanations compared to other baselines on extensive experiments, the motifs we can employ as
1013
       explanatory units are limited to 2-5 node graphlets. Consequently, we assume thatthis constraint may
1014   occasionally hinder the capture of motifs or patterns when crucial patterns are extremely large when
1015   the input graph is complex. Since many existing works already can approximate the various shaped
1016   subgraphs but do not consider the user-centric perspective, we aim to more focus on the case when
1017   prior assumption or knowledge is crucial based on pre-defined units toward human-interpretable
1018   explanations.
