011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Current end-to-end autonomous driving methods resort to unifying modular de-
014            signs for various tasks (e.g. perception, prediction and planning). Although op-
015            timized in a planning-oriented spirit with a fully differentiable framework, ex-
016            isting end-to-end driving systems without ego-centric designs still suffer from
017            unsatisfactory performance and inferior efficiency, owing to the rasterized scene
018            representation learning and redundant information transmission. In this paper,
019            we revisit the human driving behavior and propose an ego-centric fully sparse
020
               paradigm, named DiFSD, for end-to-end self-driving. Specifically, DiFSD mainly
               consists of sparse perception, hierarchical interaction and iterative motion planner.
021
               The sparse perception module performs detection, tracking and online mapping
022
               based on sparse representation of the driving scene. The hierarchical interaction
023            module aims to select the Closest In-Path Vehicle / Stationary (CIPV / CIPS) from
024            coarse to fine, benefiting from an additional geometric prior. As for the iterative
025            motion planner, both selected interactive agents and ego-vehicle are considered
026            for joint motion prediction, where the output multi-modal ego-trajectories are op-
027            timized in an iterative fashion. Besides, both position-level motion diffusion and
028            trajectory-level planning denoising are introduced for uncertainty modeling, thus
029            facilitating the training stability and convergence of the whole framework. Exten-
030            sive experiments conducted on nuScenes dataset demonstrate the superior plan-
031
               ning performance and great efficiency of DiFSD, which significantly reduces the
               average L2 error by 66% and collision rate by 77% than UniAD while achieves
032
               8.2× faster running efficiency.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037
      Autonomous driving has experienced notable progress in recent years. Traditional driving systems
038
      are commonly decoupled into several standalone tasks, e.g. perception, prediction and planning.
039   However, heavily relying on hand-crafted post-processing, the well-established modular systems
040   suffer from information loss and error accumulation across sequential modules. Recently, end-to-
041   end paradigm integrates all tasks into a unified model for planning-oriented optimization, showcas-
042   ing great potential in pushing the limit of autonomous driving performance.
043
      Literally, existing end-to-end models Hu et al. (2023); Ye et al. (2023); Jiang et al. (2023); Sun et al.
044
      (2024) designed for reliable trajectory planning can be classified into two mainstreams as summa-
045   rized in Fig. 1(a) and (b). The dense BEV-Centric paradigm Hu et al. (2023); Ye et al. (2023) per-
046   forms perception, prediction and planning consecutively upon the shared BEV (Bird’s Eye View)
047   features, which are computationally expensive leading to inferior efficiency. The sparse Query-
048   Centric paradigm Sun et al. (2024) utilizes sparse representation to achieve scene understanding and
049   joint motion planning, thus improving the overall efficiency. However, object-intensive motion pre-
050   diction inevitably causes computational redundancy and violates the driving habits of human drivers,
051   who usually only concentrate on the Closest In-Path Vehicle / Stationary (CIPV / CIPS) which are
052   more likely to affect the driving intention and trajectory planning of ego-vehicle. Meanwhile, exces-
053
      sive interaction with irrelevant agents will be conversely adverse to the ego-planning. Therefore, the
      planning performance remains unsatisfactory in both planning safety, comfort and personification.

106   Recent years witness remarkable progress achieved in multi-view 3D detection, which mainly build
107   elaborate designs upon the dense BEV (Bird’s Eye View) or sparse query features. To generate BEV
      features, LSS Philion & Fidler (2020) lifts 2D image features to 3D space using depth estimation

      results, which are then splatted into BEV plane. Follow-up works apply such operation to perform
109   view transform for 3D detection task, and have made significant improvement in both detection
110   performance Huang et al. (2021); Huang & Huang (2022a); Li et al. (2023); Han et al. (2024) and
111   efficiency Liu et al. (2023d); Huang & Huang (2022b). Differently, some works Li et al. (2022b);
112   Yang et al. (2023); Huang et al. (2023) project a series of predefined BEV queries in 3D space to the
113   image domain for feature sampling. As for the sparse fashion, current methods Wang et al. (2022);

116   advanced queries adopted in Liu et al. (2023a); Lin et al. (2023) contain both explicit geometric
117
      anchors and implicit semantic features.
118   Besides, Multi-Object Tracking (MOT) across multi-cameras is also required for downstream tasks.

120   by-detection” paradigm, which relies on hand-crafted data association between the tracked trajec-

122   Yu et al. (2023); Meinhardt et al. (2022); Sun et al. (2012) seek to explore the joint detection and
123
      tracking methods by introducing track queries to detect the unique instances continuously and con-
      sistently. Sparse4Dv3 Lin et al. (2023) proposes an advanced 3D detector which takes full advantage
124
      of spatial-temporal information to propagate temporal instances for identity reserving, thus achiev-
125
      ing superior end-to-end performance without additional tracking designs.

129
130   Maps could provide important static scenario information to ensure driving safety. Current works Li
131   et al. (2022a); Liu et al. (2023b); Liao et al. (2022); Yuan et al. (2024) manage to construct online
132   maps with on-board sensors, instead of using HD-Map which is labor intensive and expensive.
133
      HDMapNet Li et al. (2022a) achieves this aim through BEV semantic segmentation and heuristic
      post-processing to generate map instances. VectorMapNet Liu et al. (2023b) introduces a two-stage
134
      auto-regressive transformer to refine map elements consecutively. MapTR Liao et al. (2022) regards
135
      map elements as a set of points with equivalent permutations, while StreamMapNet Yuan et al.
136   (2024) adopts a temporal fusion strategy to enhance the performance. However, all of them reply
137   on dense BEV features for online map construction, which is computationally intensive and not
138   extensible to the sparse manner.

151   2.4   E ND - TO -E ND P LANNING
152
153
      End-to-end planning paradigm either unites modules of perception and prediction Hu et al. (2023);
      Jiang et al. (2023); Zhang et al. (2024); Ye et al. (2023), or adopts a direct optimization on planning
154
      without intermediate tasks Codevilla et al. (2018; 2019); Prakash et al. (2021), which lack inter-
155
      pretability and are hard to optimize. Recently, UniAD Hu et al. (2023) presents a planning-oriented

157   performance. VAD Jiang et al. (2023) learns vectorized scene representations and improves plan-
158   ning safety with explicit constraints. GraphAD Zhang et al. (2024) constructs the interaction scene
159   graph to model both dynamic and static relations. SparseDrive Sun et al. (2024) introduces the
160   sparse perception module for parallel motion planner. However, using straightforward designs and
161   exhaustive modeling without ego-centric interaction, will inevitably lead to unsatisfactory planning
      performance and inferior efficiency.

188   3.1      OVERVIEW
189
190   The overall framework of DiFSD is illustrated in Fig. 2, which deals with the end-to-end planning
191   task in an ego-centric fully sparse paradigm. Specifically, DiFSD mainly consists of four parts:
192
      visual encoder, sparse perception, hierarchical interaction and iterative motion planner. First, the
      visual encoder extracts multi-scale spatial features from given multi-view images. Then the sparse
193
      perception takes the encoded features as input to perform detection, tracking and online mapping si-
194
      multaneously. In the hierarchical interaction module, the ego query equipped with a geometric prior
195   is introduced to pick out the interactive queries through ego-centric cross attention and hierarchical
196   selection. In the iterative motion planner, both interactive agents and ego-vehicle are considered for
197   joint motion prediction, then the predicted multi-modal ego-trajectories are further optimized itera-
198   tively. Meanwhile, both position-level diffusion of interactive agents and trajectory-level denoising
199   of ego-vehicle are conducted for uncertainty modeling of motion and planning tasks respectively.
200
201   3.2      P ROBLEM F ORMULATION
202
      Given multi-view camera image sequence can be denoted as S = {It ∈ RN ×3×H×W }Tt=T −k ,
203
      where N is the number of camera views and k indicates the temporal length till current timestep T
204
      respectively. Annotation of input S for end-to-end planning is composed by a set of future waypoints
205                                            Tp
      of the ego-vehicle ψ = {ϕ = (xt , yt )}t=1   , where Tp = 3s is the planning time horizon, and (xt ,
206
      yt ) is the BEV location transformed to the ego-vehicle coordinate system at current timestep T .
207   Meanwhile, driving command as well as ego-status is also provided. Annotation set ψ is used
208   during training. During prediction, the planned trajectory of ego-vehicle should fit the annotation ψ
209   with minimum L2 errors and collision rate with surrounding agents.

211   3.3      S PARSE P ERCEPTION
212
213   After extracting the multi-view visual features F from sensor images using the visual encoder He
214   et al. (2016), sparse perception method proposed in Lin et al. (2023) is extended to perform detec-
215   tion, tracking and online mapping simultaneously based on a group of sparse queries, removing the
      dependence of dense BEV representations widely used in Hu et al. (2023); Jiang et al. (2023).

216
      Detection and Tracking. Following the previous sparse perception methods Liu et al. (2023a);
217   Lin et al. (2023), surrounding agents can be represented by a group of instance features Fa ∈
218   RNa ×C and anchor boxes Ba ∈ RNa ×11 respectively. And each anchor box is denoted as
219   {x, y, z, ln(w), ln(h), ln(l), sin(θ), con(θ), vx , vy , vz }, which contains location, dimension, yaw
220   angle as well as velocity respectively. Taking the visual features F , instance features Fa and an-
221   chor boxes Ba as input, Ndec decoders are adopted to consecutively refine the anchor boxes and
222   update the instance features through deformable aggregation of sample features projected from key
223   points of the anchor box Ba . The updated instance features are adopted to predict the classification
224   scores and box offsets respectively. Temporal instance denoising is introduced to improve model
225
      stability. As for tracking, following the ID assignment process in Lin et al. (2023), the temporal in-
      stances across frames used for advanced detection can be also served as track queries, which remain
226
      consistent with unique IDs.
227
228   Online Mapping. Similarly, we adopt an additional detection branch of same structure for online
229   mapping. Differently, the geometric anchor of each static map element is denoted as Np points.
230   Therefore, surrounding maps can be represented by a group of map instance features Fm ∈ RNm ×C
231   and anchor polylines Bm ∈ RNm ×Np ×2 .
232
233   3.4   E GO -E NV H IERARCHICAL I NTERACTION
234
235   After perceiving the dynamic and static elements existing in the driving scenario in a sparse manner,
236   we continue to perform hierarchical interaction between the ego-vehicle and surrounding agent /
237   map instances. As shown in Fig. 2, the hierarchical interaction module mainly consists of three parts:
238
      Ego-Env Dual Interaction, Intention-guided Geometric Attention and Coarse-to-Fine Selection.
239   Ego-Env Dual Interaction. As shown in Fig. 3, a learnable embedding Fe ∈ R1×C is randomly
240   initialized to serve as ego query, along with an ego anchor box Be ∈ R1×11 together to repre-
241   sent the ego-vehicle. Both ego-centric cross attention with surrounding objects Fo ∈ RNo ×C and
242   object-centric self attention are conducted consecutively to capture the mutual information compre-
243   hensively. During the attention calculation process, we adopt the decouple attention mechanism
244
      proposed in Lin et al. (2023) to combine positional embedding and query feature in a concatenated
      manner instead of an additive approach, which can effectively retain both semantic and geometric
245
      clues for interaction modeling.
246
247   Intention-Guided Geometric Attention. To enhance the accuracy and explainability of query rank-
248   ing to facilitate selection, we introduce an ego-centric geometric prior additionally. As shown in
249   Fig. 2, the intention-guided attention module is adopted to assess the importance of surrounding
250
      agent and map queries, which mainly consists of three steps: Response Map Learning, Reference
      Line Generation and Interactive Score Fusion.
251
252   Specifically, we use four MLPs to encode the ego-intention respectively, including velocity, acceler-
253   ation, angular velocity and driving command. And then we concatenate these embeddings to obtain
254   ego-intention features Ie ∈ R1×C , which are further concatenated with the position embeddings
255   Fp ∈ RH×W ×C of a group of pre-defined locations P ∈ RH×W ×2 to cover densely distributed
256
      grid cells in the BEV plane. The position of each grid cell is represented as p = (x, y). Finally,
      the concatenated geometric features are fed to a single SE Hu et al. (2018) block to learn response
257
      map Mr ∈ RH×W ×1 , which is supervised by the normalized minimum distance from p to the ego
258
      future waypoints. The motivation is that the Closest In-Path Vehicle / Stationary are prone to affect
259   the ego-intention, and vice versa.
260
261   With the predicted response map Mr , we first generate the reference line through row-wise thresh-
262
      olding, which are further used to generate the normalized distance map Md (See Fig. 2). Then we
      can obtain the geometric score Sgeo for each surrounding query by referring to the Md . The rea-
263
      son why we don’t get the geometric score from Mr directly is that the imbalanced distribution of
264
      ego-intention and future waypoints may lead to the inferior quality of Mr .
265
266   Finally, as shown in Fig. 4, we perform interactive score fusion through multiplying the attention,
267   geometric and classification scores during the ego-centric cross attention:

297   3.5      I TERATIVE M OTION P LANNER
298
299   As shown in Fig. 2, the iterative motion planner is designed to conduct motion prediction for both
300   interactive agents and ego-vehicle, and then optimize the proposal ego-trajectory with both safety
301   and kinematic constrains iteratively.
302
      Joint Motion Prediction. With regard to the trajectory prediction, both surrounding agents and ego-
303
      vehicle are adopted for motion modeling in a joint decoder, unlike previous works Hu et al. (2023);
304   Ye et al. (2023); Jiang et al. (2023) which neglect the crucial interactions between near agents and
305   ego-vehicle when making motion predictions, especially in the common scenarios like intersections.
306   Another difference is that only the interactive objects Fio (CIPV) sparsely selected in the former
307   module are considered, instead of all detected agents in the driving scene which maybe irrelevant
308   to the ego-vehicle planning. As for the joint motion decoder, we prepare three copies of ego query
        ′
309   Fe to indicate different driving intentions (i.e., turn left, turn right and keep forward), which are
310   combined with Fio to conduct agent-level self attention and agent-map cross attention respectively.
311   And then we concatenate these output attended features to predict multi-modal trajectories τa ∈
312   RNa ×Ka ×Ta ×2 , τe ∈ RNe ×Ke ×Te ×2 and classification scores Sa ∈ RNa ×Ka , Se ∈ RNe ×Ke for
313   both agents and ego-vehicle, where Ne = 3 is the number of driving command for planning, Ka =
314
      Ke = 6 are the mode number, Ta = Te = 6 are the future timestamps.
315   Planning Optimization. With the predicted multi-intention and multi-modal trajectories of ego-
316   vehicle, we can select the most probable proposal trajectory with the input driving command and
317   classification score Se . As shown in Fig. 3(b), ego-agent, ego-map and ego-navigator cross at-
318   tentions are conducted consecutively for planning optimization, where the offsets for each future
319   waypoint are predicted upon the proposal trajectory respectively with several planning constraints
320
      proposed in Jiang et al. (2023) to ensure safety.
321   Iterative Refinement. To further promote the stability and performance of the whole end-to-end
322   system, an additional iterative refinement strategy is proposed to continuously update the reference
323   line and distance map Md with refined ego trajectory as illustrated in Fig. 2, thus ensuring the
      interaction quality and selection accuracy of interactive queries.

      3.6   U NCERTAINTY D ENOISING
325
326   Due to the planning-oriented modular design, output uncertainty from each individual module will
327   be inevitably introduced and passed through to the downstream tasks, leading to inferior and fragile
328   system. Under this circumstance, we propose a two-level uncertainty modeling strategy to further
329   stabilize the whole framework. On one hand, position-level diffusion process is performed on
330   ground-truth boxes of interactive agents Bi ∈ RK×11 for additional trajectory prediction of noisy
331   agents Bn = Bi + ∆Bpos ∈ RG×K×11 equipped with G groups of random noises following uni-
332
      form distributions. ∆Bpos locates within two different ranges of {−s, s} and {−2s, −s}∪{s, 2s} to
      indicate positives and negatives respectively, where s indicates the noise scale. This process aims to
333
      promote the stability of motion forecasting for interactive agents with uncertain detected positions,
334
      scales and velocities. On the other hand, trajectory-level denoising process is also introduced
335   for robust offset prediction of proposal trajectory of ego-vehicle in the planning optimization stage.
336   Different from the position diffusion of detection or motion query described above, we apply the
337   random noise to trajectory offsets of ego-vehicle ∆Btraj ∈ RG×Te ×2 , where s depends on the Final
338   Displacement (FD) of ground-truth ego future trajectory.
339
340   3.7   E ND - TO -E ND L EARNING
341
342   Multi-stage Training. To facilitate the model convergence and training performance, we divide the
343   training process into two stages. In stage-1, the sparse perception, hierarchical interaction and joint
344
      motion prediction tasks are trained from scratch to learn sparse scene representation, interaction and
      motion capability respectively. Note that no selection operation is adopted in stage-1, namely all
345
      detected agents are considered for motion forecasting to make full use of annotations. In stage-2,
346

531   5    C ONCLUSION
532
533   In this paper, we propose a fully sparse paradigm for end-to-end self-driving in an ego-centric man-
534   ner, termed as DiFSD. DiFSD revisits the human driving behavior and conducts hierarchical inter-
535   action based on sparse representation and perception results. Only interactive agents are considered
536   for joint motion prediction with the ego-vehicle. Iterative planning optimization strategy contributes
537   to the driving safety with high efficiency. Besides, uncertainty modeling is conducted to improve
538   the stability of end-to-end system. Extensive ablations and comparisons reveal the superiority and
539   great potential of our ego-centric fully sparse paradigm for future research.
