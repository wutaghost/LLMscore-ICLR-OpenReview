011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            The binding process between T cell receptor (TCR) and peptide-major histocom-
014            patibility complex (pMHC) is a fundamental mechanism in adaptive immunity.
015            Current research on binding prediction primarily emphasizes the sequence and
016
               structural features of critical regions within these molecules, often neglecting the
               intricate structural changes that occur during the binding process, which can lead
017
               to biased representations. To address this gap, we propose a novel framework ti-
018
               tled ”Deep-ComAIR”, which effectively models the binding process by focusing
019            on the complex structure of TCR-pMHC rather than individual components. This
020            model enhances prediction accuracy by integrating features from three modali-
021            ties: sequence, structural, and gene. Our approach achieves state-of-the-art results
022            evidenced by an area under the receiver operating characteristic curve (AUROC)
023            of 0.983 in binding reactivity prediction and a Pearson correlation coefficient of
024            0.827 in binding affinity prediction. These results highlight the framework’s po-
025            tential to deepen our understanding of TCR-pMHC interactions at the structural
026            level and facilitate advancements in immunotherapy and vaccine design.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   T cells play a pivotal role in the immune response. By surveying the presence of foreign peptides
032   presented by major histocompatibility complex (MHC) molecules on the surface of cells, T cells
033
      with specific receptors can evaluate the health status of these cells. This interaction, called TCR-
      pMHC (peptide-MHC) binding, serves as a critical switch for overall immune activation, facilitating
034
      the induced activation and proliferation of antigen-specific T cells. Consequently, a deeper explo-
035
      ration of the binding process and the underlying mechanisms is considered essential for advancing
036   personalized immunotherapy.
037
038   In most instances, the αβ T cells, characterized by αβ T cell receptors (TCRs), participate in the
039
      binding process. The TCR comprises an α chain and a β chain, which are linked by disulfide bonds
      and recognize pMHC as a heterodimer. These chains can be categorized into several regions, e.g.,
040
      constant (C) regions and variable (V) regions. The V regions of the α and β chains collectively form
041
      the antigen-binding site of the TCR. Furthermore, the V region is subdivided into complementarity-
042   determining regions (CDRs), specifically CDR1, CDR2, and CDR3. Upon recognition of the ligand,
043   the less diverse CDR1 and CDR2 engage with the α helices (helix 1 and helix 2) on either side of the
044   pMHC, while CDR3 interacts with the central peptide. CDR3 is a crucial sequence that significantly
045   influences the specificity of TCR recognition of antigens, with its high diversity arising from the
046   genomic recombination of the variable, diversity, and joining (VDJ) genes. In figure 5, we select
047   several examples to demonstrate TCR-pMHC binding directly.
048   Several studies have been proposed to predict TCR-pMHC interactions. The task includes two
049   main sub-tasks: binding affinity prediction and binding reactivity prediction. Reactivity prediction
050   refers to whether TCRs bind to the peptide-MHCs. GLIPH and TCRdist utilize statistical methods
051   for the predictions, and RACER uses a pairwise energy model. With the development of artificial
052   intelligence, many studies focus on developing effective deep learning methods and completing
053   the task by solving a classification/regression problem. DeepTCR, TCRAI, NetTCR, TcellMatch,
      DeepAIR, and other models successfully modeled the binding process and achieved state-of-the-

075   Early machine learning and deep learning approaches primarily focused on features derived from
076   sequence data. Some of them rely on large models pretrained on large scales of datasets and gen-
077   erate high-level representations for the sequences. However, the structural configuration of the
078
      TCR-pMHC complex plays a fundamental role in antigen recognition and binding. Some mod-
      els have noticed the importance of structure, and the success of protein structure predictors such
079
      as AlphaFold2 and ESM-Fold has further motivated the incorporation of structural information to
080
      enhance prediction accuracy. Despite achieving state-of-the-art results, these methods typically con-
081   sider the structures of TCR and pMHC individually, overlooking the structural changes that occur
082   during the binding process. Structural modeling shows that certain amino acids shift positions upon
083   binding, leading to changes in the monomeric structures of both TCR and pMHC, which are critical
084   for determining whether binding will occur.
085
      In response to these challenges, we propose a multi-modal fusion model that integrates pretrained
086
      large models with deep learning techniques. Our model encodes sequence and structural information
087   from the TCR-pMHC complex and relevant gene features to generate three distinct feature sets.
088   These features are then fused through a series of forward layers incorporating a residual-like, multi-
089   feature-aware structure. Finally, a classification head is employed to predict binding reactivity, and
090   a regression head for binding affinity.
091
      Our contribution can be illustrated as follows:
092
093   • We propose a novel framework for TCR-pMHC binding prediction named Deep-COM AIR. It
094     leverages comprehensive information from three modalities, offering a more holistic understand-
095     ing of the binding process.
096
      • We account for the subtle structural changes that occur during the binding process and encode
        structural data more unbiasedly.
097
      • We introduce a residual-like, multi-feature-aware architecture that effectively integrates features
098
        from different layers, thereby enhancing the expressive power of the model.

103   2.1   M ODELS FOR B INDING P REDICTION
104
105   A lot of models have been developed for binding prediction. GLIPH (Glanville et al., 2017) and
106   TCRdist (Dash et al., 2017) used traditional statistical methods for the prediction, while RACER
107   (Lin et al., 2021) utilized supervised machine learning model and introduced a pairwise energy
      model to solve the problem.

      With the advancement of deep learning, deep neural networks have proven effective in binding pre-
109   diction tasks. DeepTCR (Sidhom et al., 2021) utilized convolutional and embedding layers for T-cell
110   receptor featurization, followed by a variational autoencoder (VAE) to fuse sequence and gene fea-
111   tures, modeling the latent space with several Gaussian distributions. TCRAI (Zhang et al., 2021)
112   introduced ICON to reliably identify TCR–peptide–major histocompatibility complex interactions.
113   It employed a network with a more flexible architecture and used batch normalization, to create
114   fingerprints for a given TCR for prediction purposes. SoNNia (Isacchini et al., 2021) leveraged
115   biophysical models of receptor generation combined with machine learning models of selection to
116   identify specific sequence features characteristic of functional lymphocyte repertoires and subreper-
117
      toires. ERGO (Springer et al., 2020) encoded the TCR and peptide using long short-term memory
      (LSTM) and multilayer perceptron (MLP) encoders, training a MLP classifier for binding predic-
118
      tion. NetTCR (Montemurro et al., 2021) takes CDR3 regions and peptide as input only, using a
119
      one-dimention convolution layer to encode the embeddings produced by BLOSUM. Some other
120   work also focused on binding prediction and provided complete V(D)J gene sequencing data. (Fis-
121   cher et al., 2020; Lu et al., 2021; Zaslavsky et al., 2022; Widrich et al., 2020)
122
123
      With the development of pretrained models, features are extracted by models like ProtBert, ESM,
      and AlphaFold. SC-AIR-BERT (Zhao et al., 2023b) learns the ”language” of antigen receptor (AIR)
124
      sequences through self-supervised pre-training on a large cohort of paired AIR chains from multiple
125
      single-cell resources. The model is then fine-tuned with a multilayer perceptron head for bind-
126   ing specificity prediction, employing a k-mer strategy to enhance sequence representation learning.
127   DeepAIR (Zhao et al., 2023a) takes a further step by introducing structural information. The model
128   fuses features from the structure and sequence of the TCR-peptide and the relevant gene. However,
129   though CDR3 loops may be primarily responsible for antigen recognition, residues from CDR1,
130   CDR2 and even the framework region of both α-chains and β-chains may be involved . DeepAIR
131   failed to capture the information provided by the peptide-MHC complex and ignored small struc-
132   tural changes of the TCR-pMHC complex, which may lead to bias in binding prediction. Also, there
133   exists some other problems, such as the absence of experimental negatives. (Hudson et al., 2023)
134

      2.2   P RETRAINED M ODELS FOR P ROTEIN F EATURE R EPRESENTATION
137
138
139   Pretrained models refer to those models trained on large scale of dateset. ProtBert (Brandes et al.,
140   2022) is based on Bert structure. It considered protein sequences as a kind of language and was
141   pretrained on a large corpus of protein sequences in a self-supervised fashion using a masked lan-
142   guage modeling(MLM) objective. Evolutionary Scale Modeling (ESM) (Lin et al., 2023) is a se-
143
      ries of transformer-based models designed for protein sequence representation and structure predic-
      tion. Model of 15 billion parameters is trained over sequences in the UniRef (32) protein sequence
144
      database, also with MLM objective. During training sequences are sampled with even weighting
145
      across around 43 million UniRef50 training clusters from around 138 million UniRef90 sequences
146   so that over the course of training the model sees around 65 million unique sequences. Foldseek
147   (Van Kempen et al., 2024) developed a novel type of structural alphabet and enables effective struc-
148   ture comparison and search.The 20 states of the 3D-interactions (3Di) alphabet describe for each
149   residue i the geometric conformation with its spatially closest residue j.By representing the amino
150   acid backbone of proteins as sequences over a structural alphabet, it enables faster structures com-
151   parison by reducing it into faster sequence alignments.

208   3.2   M ULTI - MODAL DATA E NCODER
209
210   We employ a tri-modal encoding framework for the TCR-pMHC complex, incorporating three dis-
211   tinct feature encoders: the V(D)J gene encoder, the sequence encoder, and the structure encoder.
212   The sequence encoder derives high-level representations of the sequence information for the two
213   TCR chains (alpha and beta sequences), peptides, and the helix1/helix2 sequences of MHC. This is
214   achieved through ESM-2 (Evolutionary Scale Modeling 2), a pretrained multilayer transformer en-
215   coder. ESM-2(650M) is composed of 33 transformer layers with 20 attention heads and an embed-
      ding dimension of 1280. Trained on large protein sequence datasets, including sampled UniRef50/90


                                                                                                4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      (65 million proteins), it utilizes a masked language modeling (MLM) objective. During MLM self-
217   supervised training, the model is tasked with predicting the identity of randomly masked amino
218   acids in a sequence by observing the context in the rest of the sequence. This allows the model
219   to capture intricate dependencies between amino acids, producing an informative feature represen-
220   tation of the entire protein sequence. In the sequence encoder of Deep-ComAIR, different from
221   sequence-based models such as DeepTCR and multi-modal model DeepAIR, sequences of varying
222   lengths are encoded and passed through a max-pooling layer in the feature dimension.
223   For structural encoding, we leverage Foldseek to produce 3D immersive (3di) embeddings. Foldseek
224   generates discrete states for each residue based on their local 3D spatial arrangement, specifically
225   calculating a 10-dimensional descriptor that encapsulates the conformation of the residue’s local
226   backbone and its nearest neighbors. The embeddings are generated through the encoder component
227   of a vector quantized variational autoencoder (VQ-VAE), offering a more detailed representation
228   of tertiary contacts between residues. This sensitivity to subtle alterations in residue conformation,
229   frequently observed during binding, plays a crucial role in understanding the binding process. The
230   structural encoder processes inputs including the CDR regions of the alpha/beta sequences, peptides,
231
      and helix1/helix2 segments. Gene modalities, including alpha-v/j genes and beta-v/j genes, are
      encoded as one-hot tensors, with the position of ”1” in the tensor array signifying variations in the
232
      alpha-V/alpha-J and beta-V/beta-J gene segments.

286
287   We utilized several sources of data to train our model, including single-cell TCR data captured by
288   pMHC multimers from the 10x Genomics website and VDJdb, a curated database of T-cell receptor
289   (TCR) sequences with known antigen specificities collected from published results of TCR speci-
290   ficity assays and personal communications and submissions.
291
      For the binding reactivity task, we have 8613 pairs of TCR-pMHC data for high-confidence data
292   and 25717 pairs for low-confidence data. Every data is featured by specific amino acid sequences,
293   the TCR gene, and other relative features. Difference between high-confidence and low-confidence
294   data exists in the feature ”score”. The higher the score is, the more confidence we have in the
295   antigen specificity annotation of a given TCR. A zero score indicates insufficient details regarding
296   the method used to draw a conclusion. The high-confidence dataset excludes data with a score of 0,
297   while the low-confidence dataset includes them.
298   Since the TCR–pMHC dataset contains only positive samples, in order to train a generalized and ro-
299   bust supervised model, the negative samples are required and should be generated via a biologically
300   and computationally plausible manner to serve as an unbiased estimate of the actual distribution of
301   non-binding pairs. Here, we follow the most common method of shuffling the known positive pairs.
302   To be precise, each CDR3 sample in the positive dataset was combined with a peptide-MHC com-
303   plex from another positive sample to make negative samples. Shuffling known positive pairs makes
304   the assumption that TCRs can only bind to their seen epitope partner, and that any other combination
305   would not lead to successful binding events, which is plausible considering the enormous diversity
306
      of potential TCR and peptide-MHC sequences.

329   4.2   T RAINING D ETAILS
330
331   Setting. For the optimization of model parameters, we employed the AdamW optimizer with a
332   learning rate of 1e-4, weight decay of 0.001, and the default parameters of β1 = 0.9, β2 = 0.999,
333   and ϵ = 1e − 8. All parameters were initialized by He initialize (He et al., 2015) for better opti-
334   mization, together with the using of ReLU activation function. The batchsize was set to 32, with the
335   number of training epoch set to 50. All experiments were completed on single 4090 GPU.
336   Loss Function. For the task of predicting binding reactivity, which is formulated as a binary clas-
337   sification problem, we employed a combination of cross-entropy (CE) loss and mean squared error
338   (MSE) loss, with an equal weighting ratio of 1:1. The cross-entropy loss is utilized to effectively
339   capture the divergence between the predicted probabilities and the ground truth labels, while the
340   MSE loss serves to minimize the overall error magnitude in the logits, thereby enhancing prediction
341   robustness. The total loss for this task is defined as:
342
343                                          Lreactivity = LCE + LMSE                                    (11)
344
345   The second task of binding affinity prediction is a regression problem. For this task, we adopted a
346   hybrid loss function comprising cross-entropy loss, MSE loss, and a regularization term. Given the
347   large value of the regression target, a logarithmic transformation was applied to both the predictions
348   and the true labels prior to calculating the loss. MSE was then computed to quantify the discrepancy
349
      between the predicted and true values. In addition, the log-transformed outputs were mapped into
      eight intervals, treating the problem as a classification task, where cross-entropy loss was applied
350
      accordingly. To further regulate model complexity and prevent overfitting, a regularization term
351
      was included, based on the L2 norm of the classification head parameters. The final loss function
352   balances the contributions of MSE, cross-entropy, and the regularization term in a ratio of 1:1:0.01.
353   This approach ensures that both regression precision and classification alignment are preserved.
354   The use of cross-entropy loss aids in achieving better convergence and mitigates the risk of mode
355   collapse, while the regularization term helps manage overfitting by constraining model complexity.
356   The total loss for this task is defined as:
357
358                                   Laffinity = LCE + LMSE + 0.01 ∗ LRegu                              (12)

361   Evalution We evaluated the model’s performance using accuracy metrics and the correlation be-
362   tween the predicted and true values. For the binding reactivity task, we computed the area under
363
      the receiver operating characteristic curve (AUROC) on the test dataset. To ensure the validity of
      the evaluation, the test dataset was carefully balanced with an equal number of positive and negative
364
      samples, and a rigorous double-checking process was employed to prevent any overlap between the
365
      training and test datasets. For the binding affinity task, the dataset was divided, with 80% of the data
366   (4961 samples) used for training and the remaining 20% (551 samples) reserved for testing. Pearson
367   correlation, Spearman correlation and R2 square were calculated to evaluate the performance.

480   In this study, we addressed the challenge of predicting binding reactivity and affinity within the
481   context of TCR-pMHC interactions. We examined the potential biases that may arise during the
482   encoding process, which can contribute to erroneous predictions. To mitigate these challenges, we
483   introduced a novel framework, Deep-ComAIR, which leverages comprehensive information from
484   the TCR-pMHC complex structure. The model was evaluated on diverse multi-source datasets,
485   consistently demonstrating superior performance across a range of predictive tasks. Our results
      underscore the utility of Deep-ComAIR in advancing adaptive immune receptor analysis, offering
