009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            The generalization of Deep Neural Networks (DNNs) to Riemannian mani-
012            folds has garnered significant attention across various scientific fields. Re-
013
               cent studies have demonstrated that several manifolds, including hyperbolic,
               spherical, Symmetric Positive Definite (SPD), and Grassmann manifolds, ad-
014
               mit gyro-structures—powerful algebraic structures that enable the principled ex-
015
               tension of DNNs to manifolds. Inspired by these advancements, we introduce
016            a novel gyro-structure for SPD manifolds, leveraging the flexible and power-
017            ful Power-Euclidean (PE) geometry. Moreover, full-rank correlation matrices,
018            which are scale-invariant, serve as compact representations of SPD manifolds.
019            Consequently, we propose two novel gyro-structures for correlation matrix mani-
020            folds, based on two theoretically and empirically convenient metrics: Euclidean-
021            Cholesky (EC) and log-Euclidean-Cholesky (LEC) geometries. Extensive experi-
022            ments on knowledge graph completion tasks validate the effectiveness of our pro-
023            posed gyro-structures.

025   1   I NTRODUCTION
026   Deep Neural Networks (DNNs) have driven significant progress across numerous areas (Krizhevsky
027   et al., 2012; He et al., 2016; Vaswani et al., 2017). Typically, DNNs operate under the assump-
028   tion that data conform with Euclidean geometry. However, in many scientific fields, data possess
029   a strongly non-Euclidean latent structure, such as Riemannian manifolds (Bronstein et al., 2017).
030   Therefore, substantial efforts have been made to extend DNNs to manifolds (Huang & Van Gool,
031   2017; Huang et al., 2018; Brooks et al., 2019; Nguyen et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2021; Nguyen, 2021;
032   Chen et al., 2023; 2024a; Wang et al., 2024b;a). Recently, several manifolds has been proven to ad-
033
      mit gyrovector structures (Nguyen, 2022; Ungar, 2005), which naturally extends the Euclidean vec-
      tor structure. Leveraging gyro-structures, several typical DNNs have been generalized into different
034
      geometries in a principled manner. Such manifolds include matrix manifolds, such as Symmetric
035
      Positive Definite (SPD) (Nguyen, 2022; Nguyen & Yang, 2023), Grassmann (Nguyen, 2022), Sym-
036   metric Positive Semi-definite (SPSD) manifolds (Nguyen & Yang, 2023), and constant curvature
037   spaces, such as hyperbolic (Ganea et al., 2018; Shimizu et al., 2020) and spherical (Skopek et al.,
038   2019) manifolds.
039
      As shown by Cruceru et al. (2021); Nguyen et al. (2024), matrix manifolds such as the SPD mani-
040
      fold, provide a compelling balance between structural richness and computational feasibility, serving
041
      as appealing alternatives to hyperbolic spaces. Nguyen (2022) identified three gyro-structures over
042   the SPD manifold based on Affine-Invariant (AI) (Pennec et al., 2006), Log-Euclidean (LE) (Arsigny
043   et al., 2005), and Log-Cholesky (LC) (Lin, 2019) metrics. Apart from the above metrics, Power-
044   Euclidean (PE) metric (Dryden et al., 2010) has also shown promising performance in different
045   applications Li et al. (2017); Wang et al. (2020); Chen et al. (2024c;b). However, the gyro-structure
046   under PE geometry remains unexplored.
047
      On the other hand, the manifold of full-rank correlation matrices has recently garnered increasing
048   attention, as it provides a normalized, compact representation of the SPD manifold (David & Gu,
049   2019; Thanwerdas & Pennec, 2022b). Since full-rank correlation matrices are scale-invariant, they
050   are particularly effective for representing data where scale is irrelevant to the problem (Thanwerdas,
051   2024). In domains such as Diffusion Tensor Imaging (DTI) (Pennec et al., 2006), Brain-Computer
052   Interfaces (BCI) (Jalili & Knyazeva, 2011; Barachant et al., 2013), and Gaussian graphical networks
053   (Epskamp & Fried, 2018), using correlation matrices instead of covariance matrices is both natural
      and effective. Recently, several Riemannian structures have been proposed for full-rank correla-

      tion matrices, including the Euclidean-Cholesky (EC) and Log-Euclidean-Cholesky (LEC) metrics
055   (Thanwerdas & Pennec, 2022b). This motivates us to further explore algebraic gyro-structures for
056   full-rank correlation matrices.

058   Based on the above discussions, we propose a novel SPD gyro-structure based on PE geometry

086   2.1   G YROVECTOR S PACES
087   Gyrovector spaces naturally extend the vector structures into manifolds (Ungar, 2005; 2014; 2022;
088   Nguyen, 2022). We briefly review gyrogroups and gyrovector spaces in the following. For more
089   in-depth discussions, please refer to Ungar (2005; 2012; 2014).
090   Definition 2.1 (Gyrogroups (Ungar, 2014)). A pair (G, ⊕) is a groupoid in the sense that it is
091   a nonempty set, G, with a binary operation, ⊕. A groupoid (G, ⊕) is a gyrogroup if its binary
092
      operation satisfies the following axioms for a, b, c ∈ G:
093   (G1) There is at least one element e ∈ G called a left identity such that e ⊕ a = a.
094
095   (G2) There is an element ⊖a ∈ G called a left inverse of a such that ⊖a ⊕ a = e.
096   (G3) There is an automorphism gyr[a, b] : G → G for each a, b ∈ G such that
097
098                    a ⊕ (b ⊕ c) = (a ⊕ b) ⊕ gyr[a, b]c (Left Gyroassociative Law).                     (1)
099
      The automorphism gyr[a, b] is called the gyroautomorphism, or the gyration of G generated by a, b.
100
101   (G4) gyr[a, b] = gyr[a ⊕ b, b] (Left Reduction Property).
102   Definition 2.2 (Gyrocommutative Gyrogroups (Ungar, 2014)). A gyrogroup (G, ⊕) is gyrocommu-
103   tative if it satisfies
104                          a ⊕ b = gyr[a, b](b ⊕ a) (Gyrocommutative Law).                   (2)
105
106   Building on this foundation, some researchers expanded the concept (Kim, 2015; 2016; 2020a;b).
107   The work in Nguyen (2022) proposed a more rigorous definition of gyrovector spaces and key
      operations on them.

133   2.2 G EOMETRIES OF SPD AND FULL - RANK CORRELATION MATRICES
134   The SPD geometry: The space of SPD matrices forms a manifold, known as the SPD manifold
135   (Arsigny et al., 2005), which has been successfully applied in various fields (Huang & Van Gool,
136   2017; Brooks et al., 2019; Sukthanker et al., 2020; Nguyen, 2022). To respect the non-Euclidean
137   geometry, several Riemannian structures on the SPD manifold were proposed (Pennec et al., 2006;
138   Arsigny et al., 2005; Lin, 2019; Bhatia, 2009; Thanwerdas & Pennec, 2022a). Due to the fast
139   computation speed and theoretical convenience of the Power-Euclidean (PE) metric, and when the
140   power tends to 0, this metric approaches the Log-Euclidean (LE) metric, building a bridge between
141
      Euclidean and LE metrics. Based on the above advantages, the PE metric has already seen successful
      applications in other fields (Zhou & Müller, 2022; Pennec, 2020; Pereira et al., 2024).
142
143   The correlation geometry: Full-rank correlation matrices, known as the open elliptope, have re-
144   cently been described as a quotient manifold of SPD matrices due to the smooth, proper, and free
145   congruence action of positive diagonal matrices (David & Gu, 2019). Specifically, a full-rank cor-
146   relation matrix is obtained by dividing the covariance matrix by the standard deviation of each
147
      variable. Some applications can benefit from the well-suited geometric structure of this manifold,
      such as Brain Connectomes (Varoquaux et al., 2010), Gaussian graphical networks (Epskamp &
148
      Fried, 2018) and Phylogenetic trees (Garba et al., 2021). However, its geometry has been much
149
      less studied than that of SPD matrices. Recently, some researchers proposed some metrics based on
150   full-rank correlation matrices: Euclidean-Cholesky (EC) and log-Euclidean-Cholesky (LEC) met-
151   rics (Thanwerdas & Pennec, 2022b). The basic operators based on different metrics are summarized
152   in App. B.

294   6.1 G YRO FOR KNOWLEDGE GRAPH COMPLETION
295   Problem Formulation: Knowledge graphs (KGs) represent heterogeneous knowledge as triples of
296   the form (subject, relation, object), where the subject and object denote entities, and the relation
297   describes the interaction between them (Balazevic et al., 2019). KGs exhibit complex and diverse
298   structures, with entities connected by symmetric, antisymmetric, or hierarchical relations. As KG is
299   often incomplete, the goal is to predict missing links and identify valid but unobserved connections.
300
      Let F = (E, R, T ) represent a knowledge graph, where E is the set of entities, R is the set of
      relations, and T ⊆ E × R × E is the set of triples stored in the graph. The typical approach is to
301
      learn a scoring function ϕ : E × R × E → R that evaluates the likelihood of a triple being true,
302
      enabling accurate ranking of missing triples. To achieve this, we propose learning entity embeddings
303   within the SPD and full-rank correlation manifolds.

      6.2 DATASETS AND EXPERIMENTAL SETTINGS
355
356   We evaluate the performance of the proposed method on two benchmarking datasets: WN18RR
357
      (Bordes et al., 2013; Dettmers et al., 2018) and FB15k-237(Bordes et al., 2013; Toutanova & Chen,
      2015). The WN18RR dataset is a subset of WordNet (Miller, 1995), a hierarchical collection of
358
      relations between words. It was created from WN18 (Bordes et al., 2013) by removing the inverse of
359
      many relations from the validation and test sets, making the dataset more challenging. This datasets
360   consists of 93,003 triples, featuring 40,943 entities and 11 relations. The FB15k-237 dataset, a
361   subset of Freebase (Bollacker et al., 2008), is derived in a manner akin to WN18RR (Bordes et al.,
362   2013), containing 14,541 entities and 237 relations.
363
      Following the criterion in López et al. (2021), we use binary cross-entropy loss as the training
364
      objective and AdamW optimizer for 5000 epochs. Each batch contains 4,096 samples, with 10
365
      negative samples per positive instance. For evaluation, we adopt the mean reciprocal rank (MRR)
366   and hits at K (H@K, where K = 1, 3, 10) metrics to assess the proportion of correctly ranked entities
367   within the top K positions (López et al., 2021; Nguyen, 2022). To ensure model efficiency, early
368   stopping is triggered when the MRR on the validation set does not improve after 500 consecutive
369   epochs. The model checkpoint with the highest MRR on the validation set are used for subsequent
370   testing. For our proposed product manifold-based models, we set the candidate sets of η, learning
371   rate, and weight decay to {0.5, 1, 1.5}, {5e − 4, 1e − 3, 2.5e − 3}, and {1e − 2, 1e − 3, 1e −
372   4, 1e − 5}, respectively. All experiments are run on a PC equipped with an i9-13900HX CPU and
373   16GB of RAM. Despite the code’s compatibility with GPUs, leveraging GPU resources does not
374
      expedite training due to the dominance of eigenvalue operations, which is identified as the primary
      computational bottleneck (López et al., 2021).

481
482   In this paper, we extend the SPD gyro-structure into the PE geometry which recovers the existing
483   LE gyro space when power tends to 0. Besides, we also explore the geometric structure of correla-
484   tion matrices and propose two novel gyro-structures for full-rank correlation matrices, induced by
485   the theoretically and computationally convenient EC and LEC metrics. Extensive experiments on
      knowledge graph completion tasks demonstrate the effectiveness of our algebraic structures.
