009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Explanations in machine learning are critical for trust, transparency, and fairness.
012            Yet, complex disagreements among these explanations limit the reliability and
013            applicability of machine learning models, especially in high-stakes environments.
014            We formalize four fundamental ranking-based explanation disagreement problems
015            and introduce a novel framework, EXplanation AGREEment (EXAGREE), to
016            bridge diverse interpretations in explainable machine learning, particularly from
017            stakeholder-centered perspectives. Our approach leverages a Rashomon set for
018
               attribution predictions and then optimizes within this set to identify Stakeholder-
               Aligned Explanation Models (SAEMs) that minimize disagreement with diverse
019
               stakeholder needs while maintaining predictive performance. Rigorous empirical
020
               analysis on synthetic and real-world datasets demonstrates that EXAGREE reduces
021            explanation disagreement and improves fairness across subgroups in various do-
022            mains. EXAGREE not only provides researchers with a new direction for studying
023            explanation disagreement problems but also offers data scientists a tool for making
024            better-informed decisions in practical applications.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   As machine learning models gain prominence in critical fields such as healthcare, science and finance,
030   the demand for transparent explanations of their predictions has intensified, particularly in high-
031   stakes decision-making scenarios (Kailkhura et al., 2019; Wiens & Shenoy, 2018; Carvalho et al.,
032   2022; Agarwal et al., 2022; Ghassemi et al., 2021). However, a significant challenge has emerged:
033   explanation disagreement, where explanations from different methods or models conflict with each
034
      other (Krishna et al., 2022; Rudin, 2019; Li & Barnard, 2024). This disagreement hinders the
      potential impact and trustworthiness of machine learning models, especially when the consequences
035
      of model decisions can have significant real-world impacts.
036
037   Explanation disagreement stems from multiple, complex sources. Multiple model-agnostic post-hoc
038   explanation methods often yield inconsistent results for the same model and prediction (Krishna
039   et al., 2022). The involvement of various stakeholders, each with unique expertise and objectives,
040
      further complicates model explanation (Imrie et al., 2023; Binns, 2018; Hong et al., 2020). Even

068   Our work makes several significant contributions to the field of explainable machine learning:
069
070           • We formalize four fundamental explanation disagreement problems in Sec. 2: stakeholder
071             disagreement, model disagreement, explanation method disagreement, and ground truth
072             disagreement, providing a structured foundation for future research in the field.
073           • By emphasizing the purpose of explanations, we reframe these complex challenges from
074             a stakeholder-centered perspective in Sec. 2, aiming to satisfy diverse human needs. This
075             novel viewpoint offers a potential pathway for resolving these disagreement conflicts.
076           • We propose EXAGREE, the first framework that aims to enhance both explanation faithful-
077             ness and fairness by leveraging the Rashomon set concept, mitigating explanation disagree-
078             ment while preserving model performance in Sec. 3. This approach utilizes the potential of
079             model disagreement as a powerful means to resolve stakeholder disagreement.
080           • Through rigorous empirical analysis in Sec. 4 on the OpenXAI (Agarwal et al., 2022)
081             disagreement measurement benchmark, we gain new insights into the nature of explanation
082             disagreement. Our experiments demonstrate EXAGREE’s effectiveness in identifying
083
                SAEMs that improve explanation agreement for diverse stakeholders.

      2.2   P RIMARY O BJECTIVE
145   To address the explanation disagreement problem, we aim to identify a well-performing model that
146   minimizes disagreement (or maximizes agreement) between model explanations and stakeholder
147   expectations. This approach is rooted in the principle of human-centered decision-making (Aggarwal
148   et al., 2016; Miller, 2019), which prioritizes satisfying the requirements of relevant stakeholders.
149
      By focusing on the specific needs of stakeholders, we intentionally simplify the complex nature of
150
      compound disagreements, allowing for a more targeted and practical solution. We formalize this
151   as an end-to-end optimization problem, considering each stakeholder’s expectations independently
152   while maintaining model performance:
153
                            min    Oi (rM,φ , ri ) ∀i ∈ S,     s.t.   L(M (X), y) ≤ τ                      (2)
154                          M
155   where O(·, ·) is a suitable disagreement measure between rankings, τ is the performance threshold,
156   and ri is the expected ranking of stakeholder i. By treating each stakeholder i ∈ S separately, we
157   address their unique expectations, using their individual feature rankings as distinct optimization
158   targets. In this work, we will focus on exploring a set of well-performing models to achieve this
159   objective, detailed in Sec. 3.
160   Remark 2. This objective is general and open-ended due to the nature of the explanation problem.
161   The choice of explanation method and disagreement metrics are left unspecified here, allowing for
      further exploration and development in subsequent studies.

      2.3   E VALUATION M ATRICES
191
192   To comprehensively assess the disagreement between explanations, we employ a set of quantitative
193   metrics adapted from the OpenXAI benchmark (Agarwal et al., 2022). These metrics evaluate both
194   the faithfulness and fairness of explanation methods. The core principle underlying our evaluation is
195   that higher agreement between rankings implies greater faithfulness, which can be formalized as:
196                                   agreement ∝ faithfulness ∝ −O(r, r∗ )
197
198   Our evaluation includes faithfulness assessment and fairness assessment. Faithfulness measures how
199   accurately an explanation reflects the true behavior of the underlying model, while fairness assesses
200   the consistency of explanation quality across different subgroups (Dai et al., 2022). Faithfulness
201
      metrics contain Feature Agreement (FA), Rank Agreement (RA), Sign Agreement (SA), Signed
      Rank Agreement (SRA), Pairwise Rank Agreement (PRA), Rank Correlation (RC), Prediction Gap
202
      on Important feature perturbation (PGI), and Unimportant feature perturbation (PGU). Detailed
203
      descriptions are presented in the Appendix Table 3.
204
205   While our study primarily focuses on global-level feature attributions, we adapt these metrics,
206   originally designed for local-level attributions, by averaging feature attributions across all instances
207
      to obtain global attributions. For a detailed description of each metric, please refer to the Appendix.
208   Remark 3. An ideal explanation should perform well across all assessment metrics. However,
209   explanations may excel in one metric while performing poorly in another, making it challenging
210
      to evaluate the overall agreement. To address this in practice, we count the number of times each
      explanation method achieves the best value across the entire set of metrics.

214
215   EXAGREE is designed to mitigate the challenge of explanation disagreement, by employing a
      two-stage workflow, as illustrated in Fig.2:

            1. Rashomon Set Sampling and Attribution Mapping: This preparatory stage involves two
217            key steps: a) Approximation of similar-performing models from the given dataset using
218            the General Rashomon Subset Sampling (GRS) algorithm (Li et al., 2024). b) Training
219            a Differentiable Mask-based Model to Attribution Network (DMAN) that maps feature
220            attributions from model characterizations for use in the next stage.
221         2. Stakeholder-Aligned Explanation Model (SAEM) Identification: In this stage, we identify
222            explanation models that align with stakeholder requirements within the approximated
223            Rashomon set. This is achieved by optimizing a Multi-heads Mask Network (MHMN),

226            ranking supervision.

      3.1   S TAGE 1: R ASHOMON S ET S AMPLING AND ATTRIBUTION M APPING
231
232   The concept of Rashomon sets provides a powerful framework for addressing the challenge of
233   explanation disagreement (Rudin et al., 2024), which allows us to transform single attribution values
234   into ranges, and enables the search for models with stakeholder-expected rankings within a set
235   of similarly performing models. Rashomon sets naturally relax the performance constraint in our
236   optimization problem and mitigate ground truth disagreement by considering all candidate models
237   within the Rashomon set as viable options. This allows us to reformulate our original optimization
238
      problem (Eq. (2)) into:
239
                                      min Oi (rM,φ , ri ) ∀i ∈ S.                                       (3)
                                     M ∈M
240
      While various methods exist to explore Rashomon sets Hsu & Calmon (2022); Dong & Rudin (2020);
241
      Fisher et al. (2019), we adopt a general Rashomon set sampling algorithm for its generalizability
242
      and implementation sparsity to meet our practical requirements (Li et al., 2024). This approach: a)
243   approximates the Rashomon set for a reference black-box model M , assuming stakeholders are given
244   a black-box model with unknown explanations. b) guarantees a fair and consistent comparison of
245   explanations by using a model-agnostic approach, thus relaxing explanation method disagreement.
246
      Our goal is to identify specific models within this set that meet the desired ranking based on calculated
247
      attributions. However, optimizing a model under ranking supervision requires a differentiable
248
      mapping function from a model to the corresponding feature attributions, which presents two main
249   challenges: a) model representations in the set as further input. b) the non-linear relationship between
250   feature attributions and model representations.
251   Remark 4. It’s important to note that there is no guarantee that a model with an expected ranking
252   can be found within a Rashomon set. A detailed discussion of ranking in the Rashomon set and proof
253   of this concept is provided in the Appendix D.
254
255   Differentiable Mask-to-Attribution Network (DMAN) The above challenges are addressed through:
256
      a) the fact that all models in the sampled set can be characterized by masks, providing uniform
      representations for different models in the Rashomon set. b) a differentiable mapping function from
257
      model characterizations (masks) to feature attributions.
258
259   We propose the DMAN fdman as a surrogate model that bridges the gap between models in the
260   Rashomon set and their feature attributions. DMAN is a neural network trained to approximate
261   the relationship between masks (representing models in the Rashomon set) and their corresponding
262
      attributions. The training process uses a dataset Datt = {mR , A}, where mR are masks and A are
      corresponding attributions. The parameter optimization of the network is expressed as:

734   B.1   T ERMINOLOGY AND C ORE C ONCEPTS
735
736   In the literature, the terms explanation/interpretation are often used interchangeably (Doshi-Velez &
737   Kim, 2017; Lipton, 2018). Similarly, concepts such as explanation disagreement, inconsistency, and
738   diversity all refer to scenarios where explanations differ, whether between models, methods, or human
739   understanding (Krishna et al., 2022; Roscher et al., 2020). The problem of explanation disagreement
740   remains a significant open challenge, hindering the impact of machine learning models (Krishna
741
      et al., 2022; Adebayo et al., 2018; Rudin, 2019; Ghassemi et al., 2021; Roscher et al., 2020; Ribeiro
      et al., 2016). This issue exists in various forms: when a single model generates different explanations,
742
      when similar-performing models produce distinct explanations, or when model explanations diverge
743
      from human expectations.
744
745   One of the most influential works from Rudin (2019) is using interpretable models instead of black
746   box models to avoid the problem. The idea is naturally true. However, constructing interpretable
747
      models practically poses challenges for many groups (Adadi & Berrada, 2018). For instance, it is
      unrealistic for end users to construct an interpretable model before they ask for reasons behind their
748
      predicted results or find a model that meets all stakeholders’ needs within a given context. More
749
      importantly, even though interpretable models, such as decision trees, generate rationale behind the
750   prediction, such explanations are not always what stakeholders expected, formulated as ground truth
751   disagreement. Both post-hoc for black-box models and ante-hoc for interpretable models - achieving
752   consistent and reliable explanation agreement remains a challenge (Varshney & Alemzadeh, 2017;
753   Jiménez-Luna et al., 2020; Huang et al., 2023; Zhong et al., 2022; Barnard & Fox, 2023; Barnard,
754   2022; Reichstein et al., 2019; Roscher et al., 2020). This leads to a common sense that ensuring
755   human oversight of both predictions and their explanations is crucial for maintaining confidence in
      machine learning-assisted decision-making processes.

      B.2   O PTIMIZATION AND R ANKING IN E XPLANATIONS
757
758   End-to-end optimization in machine learning often involves sorting and ranking operations, which
759   present unique challenges due to their non-differentiable nature. Sorting is a piecewise linear function
760   with numerous non-differentiable points, while ranking is a piecewise constant function with null or
761   undefined derivatives. These properties make it difficult to incorporate sorting and ranking directly
762   into gradient-based optimization frameworks.
763   Sorting networks, a concept dating back to the 19th century (Knuth, 1997), offer a potential solution.
764   These highly parallel, data-oblivious sorting algorithms use conditional pairwise swap operators to
765   map inputs to ordered outputs. Recent advancements have led to the development of differentiable
766   sorting networks, also known as soft rank methods. These techniques approximate the discrete sorting
767   operation with a continuous, differentiable function. One popular approach is the use of the neural
768   sort operator (Grover et al., 2019), which employs a differentiable relaxation of the sorting operation.
769   Another method involves using the optimal transport formulation to create a differentiable proxy
770
      for sorting (Cuturi et al., 2019). In the EXAGREE framework, we adopt the recent DiffSortNet
      algorithm proposed by Petersen et al. (2022). This approach offers several advantages, including
771
      the simplicity of using a logistic sigmoid function and the guarantee of monotonicity in the sorting
772
      operation. DiffSortNet provides a differentiable approximation of sorting that maintains the essential
773   properties of traditional sorting while enabling gradient-based optimization. Sorting and ranking
774   attributions in a Rashomon set also poses difficulties, we discussed the constraints in Sec. D

776   B.3   R ASHOMON SETS R ELATED W ORKS
777
778   Recent research has increasingly advocated for exploring sets of equally good models, rather than
779   focusing on a single model Rudin (2019); Rudin et al. (2024); Hsu & Calmon (2022); Li et al. (2023).
780   This approach, known as the Rashomon set concept, provides a more comprehensive understanding
781   of model behavior and feature importance. Fisher et al. (2019) first introduced the concept of
782   Model Class Reliance (MCR). Building on this work, Dong & Rudin (2020) explored the cloud of
783
      variable importance (VIC) for the set of all good models, providing concrete examples in linear and
      logistic regression. Further expanding on these ideas, Hsu & Calmon (2022) investigated instance-
784
      level explanations within a set of models, while Li et al. (2023) introduced the concept of Feature
785
      Interaction Score (FIS) in the Rashomon set. While these existing works have significantly advanced
786   our understanding of model behavior through the lens of Rashomon sets, the practical benefits and
787   applications of this approach have remained largely unexplored. Our work represents a significant
788   step forward in this domain, as it presents the first practical application of the Rashomon set concept
789   to address the challenge of explanation disagreement.
