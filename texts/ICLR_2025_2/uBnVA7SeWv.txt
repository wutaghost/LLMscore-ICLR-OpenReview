                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
010            Parameter-efficient fine-tuning aims to reduce the computational cost of adapt-
011            ing foundational models to downstream tasks. Low-rank matrix based adaptation
012
               (LoRA) techniques are popular for this purpose. We propose XoRA, an efficient
               fine-tuning scheme, which sparsifies the low-rank matrices even further using ex-
013
               pander masks. The mask is generated using extremal expander graphs (Ramanujan
014
               graphs) to maintain high edge connectivity even at a very high sparsity. Experi-
015            mental results demonstrate that this method has comparable performance with the
016            LoRA fine-tuning method while retaining much fewer number of parameters.

020   1   I NTRODUCTION
021
022   Large language models are often fine-tuned for improving their performance on downstream tasks.
023   Computational and memory requirement of such retraining is reduced by using parameter-efficient
024   fine-tuning (PEFT) (Ding et al., 2023b; Lialin et al., 2023; Han et al., 2024). Most popular among
025   them are the reparameterization based techniques, pioneered by the Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA)
026   algorithm (Hu et al., 2021). It adapts the original set of weights (W0 ) using a rank constrained
027
      decomposition of the weight update (∆W = BA) matrix into up and down projection matrices A
      and B. Various modifications to LoRA has been recently suggested in literature (Mao et al., 2024).
028
029   It has been observed that the LoRA low-rank matrices has a considerable redundancy. They can
030   be sparsified further (Wu et al., 2024) without significant loss of performance. Sparsification of the
031   LoRA up and down projection matrices has been attempted in LoRA-Prune (Zhang et al., 2023b),
032   and Bonsai (Dery et al., 2024). Robust sparse regularizers has been applied during the low-rank
033
      matrix decomposition process in RoSA (Nikdan et al., 2024a) to reduce the number of non-zero
      parameters. The LoTA algorithm (Panda et al., 2024) utilises iterative magnitude pruning to identify
034
      sparse winning lottery tickets for the transformers during fine-tuning in LoRA. Random selection of
035
      trainable weights have also been shown to be effective for fine-tuning (Xu & Zhang, 2024).
036
037   Masking or parameter selection is a popular parameter-efficient fine-tuning method which updates
038   only a subset of the parameters of the original network (Ploner & Akbik, 2024), while keeping
039
      the large majority of weights unchanged. This is usually done by applying a binary mask on the
      weight update matrix. The mask is designed using various criteria like Fisher information (Das
040
      et al., 2023), weight magnitudes (Liao et al., 2023), or the change in weight magnitude (Ansell
041
      et al., 2021) etc. However, many of the sophisticated weight pruning algorithms are difficult to use
042   for this purpose because of the high computational requirements. Similarly, iterative pruning is time
043   consuming for very large models. Random masks are experimentally found to be less effective at a
044   very high sparsity. This motivates the need for effective structural sparsification algorithms that can
045   be applied on the LoRA low-rank matrices. Expander graphs are sparse but well connected graphs
046   that are useful in designing resilient network structures (Lubotzky, 1994). They have been found to
047   be useful in designing sparse neural networks (Pal et al., 2022; Laenen, 2023) which can be trained
048   to achieve a performance close to that of a dense network.
049   In this study, we propose an expander graph based structural masking technique on the LoRA pro-
050   jection matrices (XoRA). A block diagram of the proposed approach is shown in Figure 1. We
051   experimentally observe that the LoRA low-rank matrices (A and B) can be further sparsified while
052   maintaining the performance. The masking needs to preserve the network connectivity even at a
053   very high sparsity. This can be achieved using a expander graph based mask generation techniques.
      A significantly higher parameter efficiency is experimentally observed as compared to LoRA.

087   2     R ELATED W ORK
088
089
      Parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) of transformers has been widely studied in literature (Ding
090   et al., 2023b; Lialin et al., 2023; Han et al., 2024). Major approaches can be categorized as additive,
091   selective, and reparameterized. While additive techniques use additional parameters for fine-tuning
092   to newer tasks, the selective method fine-tune only a subset of the model parameters. Reparameter-
093   ized methods transform the parameters into equivalent low dimensional forms that are fine-tuned for
094   downstream tasks. Hybrid schemes combine the above approaches.
095
      Low-rank adaptation (LoRA) (Hu et al., 2021) is perhaps the most popular reparameterization based
096   technique. Numerous modifications of LoRA has been suggested in literature (Mao et al., 2024). The
097   strategies include quantization, scaling, and singular value decomposition of the low rank matrices.
098   The VeRA method (Kopiczko et al., 2023) uses a trainable random scaling vector for the shared
099   weights across the layers to achieve a high degree of parameter efficiency. Modifying the low rank
100   matrices by transforming their eigenvectors has been found to be useful for attaining extremely
101   low number of trainable parameters (Bałazy et al., 2024). Spectral adaptation is also used for this
102   purpose (Zhang & Pilanci, 2024). Other modifications of LoRA include (Nikdan et al., 2024b; Ding
103   et al., 2023a; Zhang et al., 2023c) etc.
104   Selection methods use structured or unstructured masking to determine a subset of the parameters
105   for fine-tuning. The subset is commonly selected using pruning techniques based on the weight
106   magnitude or other information criteria (Liao et al., 2023; Das et al., 2023). Regularization is used
107   during training to obtain a sparse wright distribution in some of these approaches Guo et al. (2021).
      Structurally selecting some of the parameters like the bias terms also shows benefit for PEFT (Zaken


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      et al., 2021). Recently, neural architecture search is being employed to find the optimum set of
109   parameters to be selected (Zhou et al., 2024).
110
111   Graph structure of the underlying network is analysed by few of the fine-tuning techniques. It has
112
      been observed that maintaining connectivity is an important factor in fine-tuning process of a neural
      network (Liu et al., 2023). Connectivity patterns are found to encode a particular task and may be
113
      useful for successful fine-tuning (Xi et al., 2023). Expander graphs have been recently utilized in
114
      efficient transformer models. The Diffuser architecture (Feng et al., 2023) uses the expander graph
115   structure to develop sparse attention models over long sequences.
116
117   There exist complementary recent finetuning techniques such as IA3, OFT, BOFT, prompt-based
118
      methods etc. Input-Aware Parameterization (IA3) (Liu et al., 2022) shifts the focus from weight
      updates to activation scaling, introducing task-specific learned vectors that scale key, value, and
119
      feedforward network activations. Orthogonal Fine-Tuning (OFT) (Qiu et al., 2023) introduces a
120
      novel perspective by preserving the hyperspherical energy of pretrained models, ensuring that the
121   semantic consistency of model representations remains intact during fine-tuning. Building upon
122   OFT, Butterfly Orthogonal Fine-Tuning (BOFT) (Liu et al., 2024a) employs sparse butterfly matrix
123   structures inspired by fast Fourier transform algorithms to achieve dense orthogonal transformations
124   with reduced parameter complexity. Complementing these weight-based approaches, prompt-based
125   methods such as in (Liu et al., 2024b; Li & Liang, 2021) etc. leverage learned embeddings concate-
126   nated to input sequences or activations. These methods allow task-specific adaptation by modifying
127   the input or internal representations of the model rather than altering its weights, resulting in negli-
128   gible computational overhead during inference.

130   3     BACKGROUND
131
132   3.1   L OW-R ANK A DAPTATIONS
133
134   Low-rank adaptations (LoRA) reduces the number of trainable parameters in large models by inject-
135   ing low-rank matrices into the model’s architecture (Hu et al., 2021). Specifically, it decomposes the
136   weight matrices W into a sum of a frozen pre-trained matrix W0 and a learnable low-rank matrix
137
      ∆W = BA.
138   LoRA defines the weight update for a pre-trained weight matrix W0 ∈ Rd×k , as:
139
                                            W = W0 + ∆W = W0 + BA,                                        (1)
140
                    d×r               r×k
141   where B ∈ R         and A ∈ R         are low-rank matrices, and r ≪ min(d, k) is the rank.
142
      A low-rank matrix ∆W ∈ Rm×n , with r ≪ min(m, n), can be expressed as ∆W = U ΣV ⊤ ,
143
      where U ∈ Rm×r , V ∈ Rr×n , and Σ ∈ Rr×r is a diagonal matrix with non-singular values.
144
      LoRA is inspired from the studies in Li & Liang (2018) and Aghajanyan et al. (2020) which showed
145   over-parameterized models reside on a low intrinsic dimension. LoRA further hypothesized that the
146   changes in weight ∆W also has low intrinsic dimension during the model adaptation. Consequently,
147   it uses two learned low-rank matrices B ∈ Rd×r and A ∈ Rr×k to approximate the weight change
148   ∆W during adaptation (∆W = BA). This technique has been exceptionally effective in allowing
149   fine-tuning on low-cost GPU configurations. The optimal dimension r is dependent on the data and
150   determines the number of trainable parameters. Lower the value of r lesser the number of trainable
151   parameters. In our work, XoRA, we experimentally show that the LoRA’s low-rank matrices (B and
152   A), for a given dimensionality, can be further sparsified while maintaining the performance.
153
154   3.2   E XPANDER G RAPHS
155
      An expander graph is a sparse graph that has strong connectivity properties, quantified using vertex,
156
      edge or spectral expansion. Intuitively, it is is a finite, undirected multigraph in which every subset
157
      of the vertices that is not “too large” has a “large” boundary. This can be quantified using the notion
158   of Cheeger constants.
159
      Definition 3.1 (Expander and Cheeger constant). A graph Γ = (V, E) is an ϵ-vertex expander if for
160
161   every non-empty subset X ⊂ V with |X| ≤ |V2 | , we have |δ(X)|
                                                                  |X| ≥ ϵ, where δ(X) denotes the outer
      vertex boundary of X i.e., the set of vertices in Γ which are connected to a vertex in X but do not


                                                           3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      lie in X. As X runs over all subsets of V , the infimum of |δ(X)|
                                                                   |X| satisfying the conditions above is
163
      known as the vertex Cheeger constant and is denoted by hV (Γ).
164
165
      Given a graph with a large Cheeger constant, it is difficult to “separate,” meaning that it is hard to
166
      isolate any subset of vertices from the rest of the graph without cutting many edges. This property
167   facilitates the free flow of information across the entire network and is also known as expansion of a
168   graph and the best expanders are the Ramanujan graphs. Due to space constraints, a detailed review
169   of expanders and Ramanujan graphs is reserved for the appendix, see Appendix A. We shall use
170   Ramanujan graphs to sparsify the LoRA matrices, as can be seen in the next section.

173   4     P ROPOSED M ETHODOLOGY
174
175   We first generate bipartite expander graphs with desired number of edges for each of the layers
176   that would be fine-tuned. Their adjacency matrices are then used to mask low-rank weight update
177   matrices (A and B) for the corresponding layers of the transformers.

      4.1   G ENERATION O F E XPANDER M ASKS
180
181
      Given an (n1 , n2 ) complete bipartite graph, we generate a good expander mask for it. According to
182   the discussion in the previous section, we wish to ensure that this mask has a low degree (in this case
183   (d1 , d2 ) bi-degree with n1 d1 = n2 d2 and high Cheeger constant). This brings us to the notion of
184   Ramanujan masks. A Ramaunjan graph is an extremal expander graph in the sense that its spectral
185   gap (and hence also the Cheeger constant) is almost as large as possible. Here, we shall be concerned
186   with bipartite Ramanujan graphs. Recall that a bi-partite graph is said to be balanced if the number
187   of vertices in each of the partitions are the same and it is said to be unbalanced otherwise.
188
      Definition 4.1 (Bipartite Ramanujan graphs). Let Γ = (V, E) be a d-regular (d ≥ 3) balanced
189   bipartite graph. Let the eigenvalues of its adjacency      matrix be λn ≤ λn−1 ≤ . . . ≤ λ2 ≤ λ1 .
190
                                                       √
      Then Γ is said to be Ramanujan iff |λi | ≤ 2 d − 1, for i = 2, . . . , (n − 1). For an unbalanced
191   (d                   bipartite graph (d1 , d2 ≥ 3), the condition of being Ramanujan changes to |λi | ≤
      √ 1 , d2 )−biregular
                   √
192     d1 − 1 + d2 − 1, for i = 2, . . . , (n − 1).
193
194   A detailed description of Ramanujan graphs can be found in (Hoory et al., 2006, sec. 5.3). One can
195   generate the expander (Ramanujan) masks through the following two approaches.
196
197         1. Deterministic generation using LPS construction and Ramanujan r-coverings.
198
199
            2. Random generation of bi-regular bipartite graphs and checking for Ramanujan criteria.
200
201
      To generate expander masks deterministically, we employ the Lubotzky–Phillips–Sarnak (LPS) con-
      struction, which produces Ramanujan graphs known for their optimal expansion properties. The LPS
202
      method constructs (p + 1)-regular graphs using quaternion algebras over number fields, where p is
203
      a prime satisfying certain congruence conditions. These graphs exhibit excellent spectral character-
204   istics, making them ideal candidates for our masks. Additionally, Ramanujan r-coverings involve
205   creating larger Ramanujan graphs from smaller ones through covering projections while preserving
206   their expansion properties. This approach ensures that the resulting bipartite graphs have the desired
207   bi-degree (d1 , d2 ) and high Cheeger constant, which is crucial for the effectiveness of the masked
208   weight matrices in transformers.
209
      Alternatively, the random generation method creates bi-regular bipartite graphs by connecting nodes
210   from two partitions randomly but adhering to the specified degrees (d1 , d2 ) and the condition
211   n1 d1 = n2 d2 . After constructing such a graph, we compute its adjacency matrix and examine its
212   eigenvalues to check√ if it meets
                                     √ the Ramanujan criteria—that all non-trivial eigenvalues lie within
213   the bound |λi | ≤ d1 − 1 + d2 − 1. This stochastic approach allows for flexibility and ease of
214   implementation, although it may require multiple iterations to find a graph that satisfies the stringent
215   spectral conditions of a Ramanujan graph. Once a suitable graph is found, its adjacency matrix
      serves as an effective expander mask for the transformer layers.

223   4.2     X O RA: E XPANDER L OW-R ANK A DAPTATION
224
225   In the proposed method XoRA, structural sparsity is achieved by introducing sparse expander
226   masked low-rank matrices Ã, B̃, where only the non-masked weights in these matrices are train-
227   able. During backpropagation, only these weights receive gradient updates.
228   Using the methodology described in described in Section 4.1, we generate two bipartite expander
229   graphs GA (VA1 , VA2 , EA ) and GB (VB1 , VB2 , EB ). For the graphs GA and GB we have the fol-
230   lowing cardinality properties: GA : |VA1 | = r, |VA2 | = k, GB : |VB1 | = d, |VB2 | = r,
231   EA ⊆ VA1 × VA2 and EB ⊆ VB1 × VB2 . We also ensure that n1 × d1 = n2 × d2 . Where n1
232   and n2 are the cardinalities of the two vertex sets VA , VB , and d1 and d2 are their respective degrees.
233   Two expander masks MA ∈ {0, 1}r×k for matrix A, and MB ∈ {0, 1}d×r for matrix B are used
234   for adaptation. The expander masks are defined using the expander graphs GA (VA1 , VA2 , EA ) and
235   GB (VB1 , VB2 , EB ) as:

      6   L IMITATIONS AND D ISCUSSIONS
505
506
507   One of the limitations is that, Ramanujan graphs are (rigorously) defined in the regular or biregular
508   case, with degree ⩾ 2. The degree consideration forces the sparse mask based on it to concen-
509   trate only in sparsification of edges and not that of vertices (vertex dropout). Further, the regularity
510   forces a lower bound on the number of parameters. Thus, the method sparsifies the most when
511   the LoRA rank is large. For instance when r = 64 we can achieve 97% sparsity (as given in ta-
512
      ble 7). Another point is that random generation theoretically always guarantees an expander mask
      but not a Ramanujan mask. This is because random biregular bipartite graphs are known to be
513
      expanders but not Ramanujan (the fact that they are ramanujan is a conjecture). That said practi-
514
      cally we do get Ramanujan graphs because it has been shown that will be weakly Ramanujan by
515   Freidman, in his proof of Alon’s conjecture see (Friedman, 2003). Note that for XoRA, whenever
516   we generate random expanders, we do an additional check if it is Ramanujan or not and then pro-
517   ceed. The existence of biregular Ramanujan graphs of all degrees have been shown by the works
518   of Marcus–Spielman–Srivastava (Marcus et al., 2015; 2018). For the deterministic generation it is
519   not a problem. Currently we apply the technique of Lubotzky–Phillips–Sarnak (Lubotzky et al.,
520   1988), and Hall–Pruder–Sawin (Hall et al., 2018) (Ramanujan r-coverings). Another deterministic
521   construction can be achieved using the technique of Gribinski–Marcus (Gribinski & Marcus, 2021).
522

524   7   C ONCLUSION
525
526
527   In this work, we introduce XoRA (Expander-based Low-Rank Adaptation), a novel approach that
528   integrates structural sparsity into the low-rank matrices of the LoRA adaptation method using bi-
529   partite expander graphs. XoRA effectively addresses the over-parameterization often present in
530
      low-rank update matrices, by masking majority of the elements.
531   The proposed XoRA method achieves comparable or superior performance to LoRA and other state
532   of the art sparsification techniques (as can be seen from Tables 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 8 and 9) while utilizing
533   significantly fewer parameters. This efficiency is particularly valuable in resource-constrained com-
534   putational environments. Our experiments show that XoRA exhibits robust performance at higher
535   sparsity levels compared to random masking. The expander graph structure ensures maintained
536
      connectivity of the network despite a high sparsity and thus preserving the performance.
537   The expander masking inherent in XoRA offers regularization benefits during the fine-tuning pro-
538   cess. This can improve generalization and reduce overfitting. The XoRA approach shows promise
539   for integration with other parameter-efficient fine-tuning techniques, potentially leading to even
      greater parameter efficiency and adaptability.

798   To overcome the difficulty of computing Cheeger constants, spectral methods are often employed.
799   These techniques rely on the eigenvalues of the graph’s adjacency matrix, which contain significant
800
      information about the graph’s structural properties, including its expansion capabilities. Given a
      finite undirected graph Γ, let the eigenvalues of its adjacency matrix be ordered as λn ≤ λn−1 ≤
801
      · · · ≤ λ1 . These eigenvalues are real, and for any graph, λ1 ≤ D, where D is the maximum degree
802
      of the graph. Importantly, equality holds, λ1 = D, if and only if the graph is D-regular, meaning
803   every vertex in the graph has exactly D edges incident to it.
804
805   For a graph to be D-regular implies that all vertices have the same degree, which contributes to
806
      its regular structure. In the special case of bipartite graphs, the graph is said to be D-regular if it
      consists of two sets of vertices, where every vertex in each set has exactly D edges connected to
807
      vertices in the other set, maintaining equal degrees across both partitions.

      Graphs are said to be spectral expanders if their spectral properties imply strong connectivity and
811   expansion. Specifically, a graph Γ is considered a spectral expander if the quantities |λ1 − λ2 | and
812   |λ1 − λk | are bounded away from zero, where k = n − 1 for bipartite graphs and k = n other-
813   wise. These bounds on the spectral gap ensure that the graph exhibits good expansion properties,
814   as the eigenvalues provide insight into the graph’s ability to resist being “cut” into disconnected
815   components.
816   Question A.1. Why Is Strong Spectral Expansion Important?
817
818   The significance of spectral expansion lies in its connection to combinatorial expansion. The rela-
819   tionship between spectral and combinatorial expansion is formalized through the discrete Cheeger-
820   Buser inequality, which establishes that spectral expansion implies combinatorial expansion.
821   Suppose Γ is a d-regular graph. The largest eigenvalue is λ1 = d. Then the Cheeger constant hE (Γ)
822   satisfies the following inequalities known as the discrete Cheeger–Buser inequalities:
823                                    1                      p
824                                      (d − λ2 ) ≤ hE (Γ) ≤ 2d(d − λ2 ).
                                       2
825

826

827   trix) and its combinatorial expansion properties (through the Cheeger constant h(G)). The lower
828   bound is often referred to as Cheeger’s inequality, while the upper bound is known as Buser’s in-
829   equality in the discrete setting.

841   In practice, this means that the larger the spectral expansion, the more difficult it is to isolate subsets
842
      of vertices, which corresponds to greater connectivity and robustness of the graph structure.
843   Since computing combinatorial expansion directly is NP-hard, leveraging spectral expansion allows
844   us to sidestep this difficulty. Spectral techniques offer an efficient way to measure and guarantee
845   expansion properties. Naturally, a key question arises: Is there a limit to how large the spectral
846   expansion can become, or can it be arbitrarily large?
847   This leads us to the concept of Ramanujan graphs, which are the optimal spectral expanders.
848   Ramanujan graphs achieve the best possible trade-off between low degree and high expansion prop-
849   erties. In fact, these graphs are known to provide the largest possible spectral gap for a given degree,
850   making them the ideal candidates for networks and systems that require both sparse connectivity
851   and strong expansion. Figure 4 visualizes a typical bipartite Ramanujan graph.
852   It is essential to note that disconnected graphs are not considered expanders. The reason for this
853   is straightforward: in a disconnected graph, the boundary of any connected component is empty,
854   meaning there is no flow of information between different parts of the graph. The Cheeger constant,
855   as an expansion parameter, effectively measures how well-connected the graph is, and thus a discon-
856   nected graph has zero expansion. In contrast, a graph with a high Cheeger constant, or equivalently,
857   a large spectral gap, exhibits strong expansion, meaning that it remains well-connected even after
858   the removal of some edges or vertices.
859   At the extreme, the complete graph represents the best possible expander, as it has the maximum
860   possible connectivity. However, the complete graph also has the highest possible degree, which
861   makes it impractical in many applications that require sparse connections. Therefore, a “good ex-
862   pander” is one that balances low degree with high expansion properties. Ramanujan graphs serve as
863   a prime example of such an optimal balance, making them highly valuable in both theoretical and
      practical contexts where efficient and robust network structures are needed.
