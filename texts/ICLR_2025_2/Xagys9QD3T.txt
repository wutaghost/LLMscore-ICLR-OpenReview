011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Machine unlearning—enabling a trained model to forget specific data—is crucial
014            for addressing biased data and adhering to privacy regulations like the General
015            Data Protection Regulation (GDPR)’s “right to be forgotten.” Recent works have
016
               paid little attention to privacy concerns, leaving the data intended for forgetting
               vulnerable to membership inference attacks. Moreover, they often come with high
017
               computational overhead. In this work, we propose Pseudo-Probability Unlearn-
018
               ing (PPU), a novel method that enables models to forget data efficiently and in a
019            privacy-preserving manner. Our method replaces the final-layer output probabil-
020            ities of the neural network with pseudo-probabilities for the data to be forgotten.
021            These pseudo-probabilities follow either a uniform distribution or align with the
022            model’s overall distribution, enhancing privacy and reducing risk of membership
023            inference attacks. Our optimization strategy further refines the predictive proba-
024            bility distributions and updates the model’s weights accordingly, ensuring effec-
025            tive forgetting with minimal impact on the model’s overall performance. Through
026            comprehensive experiments on multiple benchmarks, our method achieves over
027
               20% improvements in forgetting error compared to the state-of-the-art. Addi-
               tionally, our method enhances privacy by preventing the forgotten set from being
028
               inferred to around random guesses.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Machine unlearning, which focuses on eliminating the negative impact of specific data sub-
034
      sets—such as biased, erroneous, or privacy-leaking instances (Jagielski et al., 2018; Yang et al.,
035
      2024)—used in model training (Baumhauer et al., 2022; Fu et al., 2022; Golatkar et al., 2020a;b;
036   Guo et al., 2019; Kim & Woo, 2022; Mehta et al., 2022; Nguyen et al., 2020; Shah et al., 2023),
037   has emerged as a critical area of research. Its significance is increasing due to growing concerns
038   about data privacy (Pardau, 2018), legal requirements for data deletion(Mantelero, 2013), and the
039   necessity for models to adapt to new information without complete retraining. Though the most
040   straightforward approach is to retrain the model with a new dataset that excludes the data needing
041   removal, this approach is computationally expensive and needs continuous access to the training set.
042   There are two main challenges in existing machine unlearning methods. On the one hand, they
043   still face high computational time (Xu et al., 2023) without retraining the model if they aim to
044   maintain decent unlearning performance in two aspects: the efficiency of forgetting the specified
045   data subset, and the need to maintain performance on the remaining data. For example, an existing
046   machine unlearning work (Kurmanji et al., 2024) requires 4.30 seconds to forget 25 data samples,
047   while retraining takes 4.21 seconds. The retraining process is even faster due to the unlearning
048   method’s complexity of the loss computation.
049   On the other hand, existing methods are vulnerable to privacy leakage attacks (Hu et al., 2024),
050   where an attacker can infer which data is within the forgetting set from the post-unlearning models.
051   This still violates the right to be forgotten, even though the model has been updated to remove
052   the data. This vulnerability arises because existing methods typically require the model to perform
053   poorly (i.e., have a high loss) on the forgetting set, making it easier to distinguish from the retraining
      set. We denote this vulnerability as privacy leakage, measured by Membership Inference Attacks


                                                          1
054
      (MIA) (Hui et al., 2021). There is a lack of existing work addressing privacy leakage in machine
055   unlearning; to the best of our knowledge, Kurmanji et al. (Kurmanji et al., 2024) has considered this
056   issue, but it is computationally costly as mentioned earlier.

079   To address these issues, we propose Pseudo-Probability Unlearning (PPU) in Figure 1, which targets
080   the final-layer output probabilities of the model and replaces them with pseudo-probabilities for the
081   data to be forgotten, thus being computationally efficient. To achieve good forgetting performance,

083   further refined to maintain performance on the remaining data. To protect privacy, the entire model’s
084   weights are updated with the objective of ensuring that the pseudo-probabilities do not deviate too
085   far from the original model’s output probabilities, thus making the forgetting set indistinguishable
086   from the remaining data. Besides, we provide a proof for the efficiency of Pseudo-Probability,
087
      making it theoretically sound. Extensive evaluations show that PPU reduces computational time by
      half compared to existing methods while improving unlearning performance and preventing privacy
088
      leakage, reducing the success rate of membership inference attacks to around random guessing.

091   2   R ELATED WORK
092
093   The discussion on unlearning has been broadened to include two principle paradigms: exact unlearn-
094   ing and approximate unlearning (Izzo et al., 2021). Exact unlearning mandates that the performance
095   of a model, post-unlearning, should be indistinguishable from that of a model retrained in the ab-
096   sence of the forgotten data. In this vein, Brophy and Lowd (Brophy & Lowd, 2021), along with
097   Schelter et al. (Schelter et al., 2021), applied exact unlearning methods specifically to random forest
098   models. Similarly, Ginart et al. (Ginart et al., 2019) developed an exact unlearning technique for
099
      k-means clustering. Despite the efficacy and precision of exact unlearning approaches in diminish-
      ing the influence of specific data, they face significant constraints related to underlying assumptions
100
      and scalability issues, as highlighted by Xu et al. (Xu et al., 2023). In particular, these methods
101
      are unsuitable for models such as Convolutional Neural Networks (CNN) (O’Shea & Nash, 2015)
102   and Residual Networks (ResNet) (He et al., 2016). To address this, Golatkar et al. (Golatkar et al.,
103   2020a) introduced the concept of selective unlearning, aiming to achieve forgetting by adjusting
104   model weights. Moreover, Bourtoule et al. (Bourtoule et al., 2021) introduced the Sharded, Isolated,
105   Sliced, and Aggregated (SISA) training approach, which ingeniously reduces the influence of indi-
106   vidual data points on the training process. However, this approach can significantly compromise the
107   model’s performance and generalization capacity, especially when multiple data points need to be
      unlearned. Furthermore, Golatkar et al. (Golatkar et al., 2020b) proposed approximating the weights


                                                         2
108
      that would result from unlearning by using a linearization inspired by Neural Tangent Kernel (NTK)
109   theory (Jacot et al., 2018).
110
111   Approximate unlearning is designed to diminish the impact of data designated for removal to a tol-
112
      erable extent, rather than achieving its complete elimination. This method recognizes the inherent
      difficulties in fully erasing the influence of data from complex models. Cao and Yang (Cao & Yang,
113
      2015) developed a strategy to reconfigure learning algorithms to ease data deletion, but this method
114
      faces scalability issues with more complex models. Wu et al. (Wu et al., 2020) proposed using
115   cached information from the original training to ease the retraining process, though this technique
116   struggles with large-scale data removal. Taking a different approach, Kurmanji et al. (Kurmanji
117   et al., 2024) introduced a novel approach involving the optimization of the min-max problem to im-
118   prove the unlearning process. Zhang et al. (Zhang et al., 2022) also presented a method that leverages
119   quantized gradients and randomized smoothing to potentially prevent the need for future unlearn-
120   ing, offering certain guarantees under specific conditions. Nevertheless, a data deletion request that
121   significantly alters the data distribution, such as class unlearning, may exceed the “deletion budget”
122   and challenge the assumptions underlying their approach.
123   Overall, most approaches improve upon retraining but still require significant computational re-
124   sources, making them less practical for large-scale applications. Although some approximations
125   aim to enhance efficiency, few explicitly prioritize privacy, which remains a critical concern. Ad-
126   ditionally, there is still room to reduce forget set error and further improve privacy protections,
127   highlighting the need for more balanced and effective solutions.

      4   M ETHODS
148
149
      Building on the foundational framework, we propose an approach for deep machine unlearning that
150   leverages pseudo-label optimization. In this method, we construct a matrix where each column
151   represents a class, and each row corresponds to the probability of a data point belonging to each
152   class. If the training dataset contains N data points and k classes, the resulting matrix has dimensions
153   N × k. For instance, in a neural network trained on the CIFAR-10 dataset with 50,000 training
154   images, the matrix would have 50,000 rows and 10 columns. This matrix serves as the foundation
155   for refining the model’s predictions and optimizing the unlearning process.
156   We define the output probabilities as p, where each p vector has a length of k for each data point.
157   Specifically, we assign the probabilities for the forgotten data as pf and for the retained data as pr .
158   Thus, each cell in the matrix represents the probability of a data point belonging to a particular class,
159   consisting of values from both pf and pr . For a data point x1 in the forget set, the probability of
160   x1 belonging to class k is denoted as pf 1 (k). Here, we define f (x; w) as the output probability
161   generated by the input x when passed through the model with weights w. Additionally, fk (xf ; w)
      represents the probability of the forgotten data point xf belonging to class k.


                                                         3
162
      The core of our method lies in the formulation of an optimization objective tailored to adjust the
163   model’s output distribution in such a manner that it effectively “forgets” the information related to
164   the forget set Df , while maintaining or even enhancing its performance on the retain set Dr . To
165   this end, we introduce an optimization objective designed to measure the discrepancy between the
166   desired output distribution and the one currently produced by the model with original weights w.

206
      To address computational efficiency, particularly for large datasets, we adopt an iterative solution
207
      reminiscent of coordinate ascent algorithms applied to the Lagrangian dual of our problem.
208
209   Theorem 1 The proposed iterative procedure for the optimization problem described in ( 1) con-
210   verges to the unique optimal solution, provided that feasible initial conditions are used and the total
211   KL divergence remains finite for all feasible pseudo-labels.
212   The Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence, DKL (p∥q), is a well-known convex function in p when q is
213   fixed. The optimization objective function is a sum of convex KL divergence terms. Consequently,
214   the entire objective function is convex. Since the optimization problem consists of minimizing a con-
215   vex function subject to linear constraints, the problem is a convex optimization problem. Convexity
      ensures that there is a unique global minimum.

217   satisfy the constraints ( 2) ( 3) ( 4). These feasible initial conditions guarantee that the optimization
218   process starts in the valid region and remains within this region during the optimization. Because the
219   objective function is convex and the constraints are linear, the iterative procedure will converge to

221   to a common point, satisfying both the objective function and the constraints.

364   5.4   R EMOVE BIAS
365
366   In addressing bias removal, our goal is to maximize the forget set error. Thus, instead of performing
367   optimizations, we can directly modify the forget set probabilities to reflect pseudo probabilities.
368   Additionally, we experimented with various distributions for the pseudo-probabilities, including
369
      uniform and random distributions.Specifically, the random distribution will be generated by applying
      the softmax function to randomly generated numbers. In general, the uniform distribution tends to
370

      5.5   P ROTECT PRIVACY
392
      To protect privacy, our goal is to ensure that the forget error remains close to that of retraining. For
393   membership inference attacks, we adopt the approach outlined by Kurmanji et al.(Kurmanji et al.,
394   2024). Specifically, we train a binary classifier (the “attacker”) using the losses of the unlearned
395   model on both the forget and test examples, with the objective of classifying instances as either “in”
396   (forget) or “out” (test). The attacker then predicts labels for held-out losses—losses that were not
397   used during training—balanced between the forget and test sets. A successful defense is indicated
398   by an attacker accuracy of 50%, signifying that the attacker is unable to distinguish between the two
399   sets, demonstrating the effectiveness of the unlearning method.
400   To preserve privacy, we monitor both the training and retain accuracy at each epoch. As shown
401   in Figure 2, the experiment on selective unlearning with ALL-CNN on CIFAR-10 reveals that the
402   forget error gradually increases during training. Therefore, checkpoints are saved at each epoch, and
403   the model closest to the original is selected. In this setup, pseudo-probabilities are initialized using
404   a uniform distribution. According to Table 3, PPU’s forget error is very close to that of retraining,
405   particularly in the Lacuna-10 experiment, where it is the closest match. In the membership inference
406   attack experiment, shown in Table 4, PPU consistently achieves nearly 50% accuracy, indicating
407
      strong privacy preservation. This demonstrates that, with the refinement of pseudo-probabilities, the
      model can maintain the original distribution while effectively forgetting the designated forget set.

502   7      C ONCLUSION
503
504   This research introduces a novel approach to machine unlearning, presenting an optimization frame-
505   work that refines predictive probability distributions within deep learning models. Our method ex-
506   cels in striking an optimal balance between forgetting effectiveness and preserving model perfor-
507   mance on retained data. Additionally, it demonstrates superior resilience against membership infer-
508   ence attacks. Empirical results across diverse datasets and model architectures, including CIFAR-10
509   and Lacuna-10 with ResNet and ALL-CNN, highlight the superiority of our approach over existing
510
      state-of-the-art methods.
511   Furthermore, the operational flexibility, theoretical insights, and high computational efficiency of
512   our approach provide a solid foundation for further developments. However, we acknowledge cer-
513   tain limitations. Our current method is limited to addressing unlearning in classification tasks and
514   may encounter convergence issues during the optimization process. Additionally, the approach is
515   restricted to supervised learning settings and does not extend to unsupervised tasks at this stage. Fu-
516
      ture work will focus on extending the method to various models, including large language models,
      and broadening its applicability beyond classification tasks.
