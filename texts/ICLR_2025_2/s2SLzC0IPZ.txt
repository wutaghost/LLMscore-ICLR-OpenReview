011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013
               Federated learning (FL) has recently been actively studied to collaboratively train
014            machine learning models across clients without directly sharing data and to address
015            data-hungry issues. Many FL works have been focusing on minimizing a loss
016            function but many important machine learning tasks such as adversarial training,
017            GANs, fairness learning, and AUROC maximization are formulated as minimax
018            problems. In this paper, we propose a new federated learning method for minimax
019            problems. Our method allows client drift and addresses the data heterogeneity issue.
020            In theoretical analysis, we prove that our method can improve sample complexity
021            from O(ŌĶ‚ąí3 ) to O(ŌĶ‚ąí2 ). We also give convergence guarantees for the updates
022
               of the model parameters, i.e., the sequences generated by the method. Given the
               Kurdyka-ŇĀojasiewicz (KL) exponent of a novel potential function related to the
023
               objective function, we demonstrate that the sequences generated by our method
024
               converge finitely, linearly, or sublinearly. Our assumptions on the KL property are
025            weaker than previous work on the sequential convergence of centralized minimax
026            methods. Additionally, we further weaken the KL assumption by deducing the KL
027            exponent of the maximizer-dependent potential function from that of the maximizer-
028            free function. We validate our federated learning method on AUC maximization
029            tasks. The experimental results demonstrate that our method outperforms state-of-
030            the-art federated learning methods when the distributions of local training data are
031            non-IID.

035   1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037
038   In recent years, federated learning (FL) has garnered significant attention within the machine learning
039
      community, owing to its wide real-world applications in finance, healthcare, edge computing, AIoT,
      and more. Federated learning allows multiple clients to collaboratively train the same model locally
040
      on their own devices. Once trained, the local models are sent to a central server, where they are
041
      aggregated, and the updated global model is returned to the clients for further local training. This
042   decentralized approach enables the training of machine learning models using datasets from different
043   clients without the need for data sharing. Additionally, it avoids the transfer of large datasets to a
044   central server, thereby reducing bandwidth requirements and associated costs.
045
      The classical federated learning problem focuses on minimizing a loss function using local training
046
      datasets. However, many emerging scenarios, such as adversarial training (TrameŐÄr et al., 2018;
047
      Bai et al., 2021), distributionally robust optimization (Levy et al., 2020; Gao & Kleywegt, 2023;
048   Madras et al., 2018), generative adversarial networks (GANs) (Goodfellow et al., 2014), and AUROC
049   (Area Under the ROC Curve) maximization (Lei & Ying, 2021), often formulate their objectives
050   as minimax optimization problems. While centralized methods for solving minimax problems are
051   well-explored, federated learning methods for minimax optimization are still in their early stage.
052   These problems face similar challenges as traditional federated learning, particularly regarding data
053   sharing and communication overhead. Hence, it is necessary develop federated methods for these
      minimax problems.

      A key challenge in federated minimax optimization lies in handling the max problem nested within
080
      the min problem, particularly when training must occur locally. In centralized settings, the Gradient
081   Descent Ascent (GDA) method is a classical approach to minimax problems. To extend this to
082   federated learning, one could adapt GDA to the FedAvg method, resulting in LocalSGDA (Deng
083   et al., 2020). Other variations, such as Momentum Local SGDA (Sharma et al., 2022), accelerate
084   convergence by adding momentum to local updates, while FedSGDA+ (Wu et al., 2023) further
085   reduces complexity. However, these methods require all clients to participate in every training round,
086   which introduces the risk of client drift due to unstable network connections. To address this, we
087   propose methods that allow only a subset of clients to participate in each training round.
088   In addition to client drift, data heterogeneity‚ÄĒwhere local data distributions vary significantly‚ÄĒposes
089   another challenge in federated learning. This heterogeneity can slow down training and reduce the
090   model‚Äôs performance. Previous works (Sharma et al., 2023; 2022; Wu et al., 2023) have proposed
091   methods to address heterogeneity, assuming bounds on the degree of heterogeneity and studying its
092   impact on convergence complexity. However, in real-world scenarios, these bounds can be large,
093   leading to loose convergence guarantees. Our work introduces methods that offer convergence
094   guarantees without relying on these heterogeneity bounds.
095   Moreover, while much of the existing research focuses on the complexity of federated learning

104   1.1     C ONTRIBUTIONS
105
106   In this work, we develop a novel federated learning method specifically designed for minimax
107   optimization problems, addressing the unique challenge of solving nested minimax problems in
      a federated setting. Our approach allows for partial client participation during training rounds,


                                                                     2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      mitigating client drift caused by unstable network conditions. Additionally, it effectively handles data
109   heterogeneity without relying on strict bounds for data distribution discrepancies, ensuring robust
110   convergence in real-world applications. By introducing a new termination criterion for local training,
111   we enhance the sample complexity of existing federated minimax methods, reducing the complexity
112   from O(ŌĶ‚ąí3 ) to O(ŌĶ‚ąí2 ) while maintaining a fixed number of local iterations.
113
      In addition, we provide convergence guarantees for the sequence of model parameters generated
114
      by the method, which we refer to as sequential convergence. We demonstrate that when all clients
115   participate in training and the local solvers are deterministic, the accumulation points of the sequence
116   generated by our method converge to a stationary point. Furthermore, we establish the convergence
117   rate of the sequence in nonsmooth and nonconvex settings. To achieve this, we leverage the Kurdyka-
118   ŇĀojasiewicz (KL) framework, which specializes in analyzing sequence convergence in nonsmooth,
119   nonconvex cases (Attouch et al., 2010; Li & Pong, 2018; Attouch et al., 2013; Bolte et al., 2017).
120   We show that, depending on the KL exponent of the potential function, the sequence generated by
121   our method converges finitely, linearly, or sublinearly when the KL exponent is 0, (0, 12 ], or ( 12 , 1),
122   respectively.
123
124          Our method is the first one in federated learning that is able to have sequential conver-
125
            gence guarantees in nonconvex nonsmooth settings.
126
127   Furthermore, we weaken the KL assumptions made on the potential function compared to previous
128   work on sequential analysis for the centralized minimax problem in Chen et al. (2021). In their work,
129   the potential function depends on the maximizer y(x) := argmax f (x, y) and the maximum function
130   f (x) := maxy f (x, y). The potential nonconvexity and nonsmoothness of the max function generally
131
      make its subgradient discontinuous, posing challenges in calculating its KL exponent. In contrast,
      our potential function does not rely on y(x) := argmax f (x, y). We introduce a calculus rule
132
      (Proposition 3) to deduce the KL exponent of our potential function directly from the maximizer-free
133
      function. As a result, our analysis offers a weaker assumption for sequential convergence in federated
134   learning methods for minimax optimization problems.
135
136   We apply our method to the AUC maximization problem in federated learning, particularly under
137
      conditions of data heterogeneity. Our experiments demonstrate that the proposed method outperforms
      existing federated minimax approaches in both efficiency and performance.

      Federated learning for minimization problem Classical federated learning methods for minimiza-
      tion problem include FedAvg (McMahan et al., 2017), LocalSGD (Stich, 2019), FedDualAvg, (Yuan
143
      et al., 2021a), FedSplit (Pathak & Wainwright, 2020) and SCAFFOLD (Karimireddy et al., 2020). In
144
      order to address the heterogeneity problem in FL, federated splitting methods are proposed, see Yuan
145   et al. (2021a); Li et al. (2020); Reddi et al. (2021); Pathak & Wainwright (2020); Tran-Dinh et al.
146   (2021) for examples. When the objective is minimizing a strongly convex objective function, Stich
147   (2019) shows the convergence rate of LocalSGD is O(1/nT b), where n is the number of clients,
148   b is the batch size and T is the communication round. On the other hand, Pathak & Wainwright
149   (2020) shows the sequence generated by their proposed method converges linearly when the objective
150   function is strongly convex. Our method is closely related to the FedDR method for the minimization
151   problem in Tran-Dinh et al. (2021). However, our work differs from Tran-Dinh et al. (2021) in
152   three perspectives: 1. We work on minimax problems. The existence of the maximization problem
153
      raises new challenges in theoretical analysis. To address this challenge, we propose new potential
      functions related to the variables in the maximization problem and are key to all our analysis. 2. We
154
      provide comprehensive sequential convergence analysis. Our result is also new when our method
155
      degenerates to solve the minimization problems in federated learning. 3. We conducted further
156   investigation on the KL assumption used for analyzing the minimax problems. The existing studies
157   on the KL property for minimax problems are quite few. Li & So (2022); Zheng et al. (2023)
158   investigate a global KL property. Li & So (2022) show that when the objective function is nonconvex
159   in x and nonconcave in y, if the objective function is a KL function with respect to y with an
160   exponent in [0, 12 ], their method can achieve optimal iteration complexity. In Zheng et al. (2023),
161   the authors propose a unified single-loop algorithm for solving centralized nonconvex-nonconcave,
      nonconvex-concave, and convex-nonconcave minimax problems. Under a one-sided KL assumption,

      they show that the proposed method achieves a complexity of O(ŌĶ‚ąí4 ) in all cases and can improve
163   upon previously existing complexity results in the same scenarios under specific KL exponents.
164   On the other hand, Chen et al. (2021) also analyzes the sequential convergence of methods for the
165   centralized minimax problem. Compared with Chen et al. (2021), we weakened the KL assump-
166   tions made on the potential function. In their work, the potential function relies on the maximizer

      our analysis provides a weaker assumption for the sequential convergence analysis of the method for
      the minimax optimization problem.

      6   C ONCLUSION
533
      In this paper, we proposed a new federated minimax method for nonconvex, strongly concave
534   minimax problems. We demonstrated that our method has smaller sample complexity compared to
535   existing federated minimax methods. More importantly, we showed the proposed method has global
536   finite-step/linear/sublinear convergence guarantees for the updates of model parameters under KL
537   assumption on novel potential function. We further made the KL exponent of the potential function
538   easier to check by relating the maximizer-dependent potential function from that of the maximizer-
539   free function. Empirically, our method is applied to the AUC maximization problem and consistently
      outperforms existing federated minimax methods in scenarios with high data heterogeneity.
