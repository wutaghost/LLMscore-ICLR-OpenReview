009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011              Existing methods for adapting large language models (LLMs) to new tasks are not
012              suited to multi-task adaptation because they modify all the model weights–causing
013              destructive interference between tasks. The resulting effects, such as catastrophic
014              forgetting of earlier tasks or performance drop due to model merging, make it
015              challenging to obtain good performance on multiple tasks at the same time. To
016              mitigate this, we propose Lottery Ticket Adaptation (LoTA), a sparse adaptation
017
                 method that identifies and optimizes only a sparse subnetwork of the model. We
                 evaluate LoTA on a wide range of challenging tasks such as instruction following,
018
                 reasoning, math, and summarization. LoTA obtains better performance than full
019
                 fine-tuning and low-rank adaptation (LoRA), and maintains good performance even
020              after training on other tasks – thus, avoiding catastrophic forgetting. By extracting
021              and fine-tuning over lottery tickets (or sparse task vectors), LoTA also enables
022              model merging over highly dissimilar tasks.
023

025   1      I NTRODUCTION
026
027   Large language models (LLMs) (Brown et al., 2020) have seen an explosion of applications to
028
      real-world problems (OpenAI, 2023; Team et al., 2023) via adaptation (Ouyang et al., 2022) to new
      tasks. Three major multi-task adaptation paradigms have emerged: storing and loading task-specific
029
      adapters (Hu et al., 2022; Beck et al., 2021), continuing to train instruction-tuned models on new
030
      tasks in serial via sequential training (Ouyang et al., 2022), and combining the adaptations to tasks
031   learned in parallel via model merging (Ilharco et al., 2022). Each paradigm has its own associated
032   challenges, such as catastrophic forgetting during sequential training (McCloskey & Cohen, 1989;
033   Dong et al., 2023; Ramasesh et al., 2022; Luo et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024), and methods that
034   have been proposed to mitigate these challenges (Crawshaw, 2020; Zhang & Yang, 2021). In this
035   work, we propose a new LLM adaptation method, called Lottery Ticket Adaptation (LoTA), that
036   (1) provides sparse adaptation by freezing a majority of the parameters and updating only a sparse
037   subnetwork of the base model and (2) resolves the challenges in common multi-task adaptation
038   paradigms. (More details in Section 3.) We summarize our contributions:
039
          • We train lottery tickets (or sparse task vectors) that can be stored efficiently and obtain
040         performance similar to full fine-tuning (FFT) and higher than LoRA across a range of tasks
041         spanning reasoning, math, code generation, and instruction following. When adapting Mistral for
042         instruction following, FFT and LoTA both get a length-controlled AlpacaEval 2 winrate (Dubois
043         et al., 2024) (how often GPT-4 prefers the outputs of our model over its own) of 19.0%, but
044         LoRA only gets a winrate of 15.3% after extensive hyperparameter tuning.
045       • We apply LoTA to mitigate catastrophic forgetting (McCloskey & Cohen, 1989) of earlier
046         tasks, enabling sequential adaptation to new tasks. If we train models with FFT or LoRA on
047         first GSM8k and then Commonsense Reasoning tasks, they forget GSM8k completely (< 5%)
048         accuracy, but by using LoTA we maintain 57% accuracy on GSM8k and get 85% on Reasoning.
049       • We can use LoTA to merge models in parallel (Wortsman et al., 2022; Jin et al., 2022; Zhang et al.,
050         2023a) across dramatically different tasks. LoTA achieves better performance than existing merg-
051         ing methods that rely on post hoc sparsification (Yadav et al., 2023) (which degrades performance
052         for FFT models), because LoTA naturally trains sparse task vectors. When we use LoTA to merge
053         multiple models trained on heterogeneous tasks of instruction following, math and summarization,
            we get a task-average performance of 21.9% where a merge of FFT models obtains 19.1%.

074   Storing and Loading Adapters. The first row of Figure 1 shows training task-specific adapters that
075   are loaded during inference (Ostapenko et al., 2024; Beck et al., 2021; Mangrulkar et al., 2022), de-
076
      pending on the need (Houlsby et al., 2019). Notably, Apple stores and loads adapters to enable their on-
      device models (Apple, 2024). While using adapters avoids any interference between tasks, it increases
077
      memory and compute costs as it requires storing and loading task-specific adapters. To mitigate
078
      these costs, a number of parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) methods have been developed, most
079   popularly LoRA (Hu et al., 2022) (training adapters in the low-rank space). Services such as Punica
080   and S-LoRA allow developers to use this approach to serve large numbers of task-specific adapters for
081   specific requests (Chen et al., 2023; Sheng et al., 2023). However, a persistent gap in capacity between
082   PEFT methods and FFT has presented a tradeoff between adapter overhead and performance (Hu
083   et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2024b; Kopiczko et al., 2023; Biderman et al., 2024; Nikdan et al., 2024).
084
085   Sequential Training. When we want one model with multi-task abilities, we can fine-tune the
086   model on different tasks sequentially (Ruder, 2017), e.g., first fine-tune on task A, then fine-tune on
087   task B, as in the second row of Figure 1. We can also train the model on a mixture of all tasks, which
088   may require optimizing the data mixture. Fine-tuning the LLM for new tasks with FFT or existing
089   PEFT methods leads to catastrophic forgetting of earlier tasks. This is problematic, especially for
090
      safety alignment, since we can fine-tune an LLM to be safe but later get this feature erased during
      future fine-tuning on new tasks (Lermen et al., 2023). In fact, a number of works have aimed to
091
      mitigate this vulnerability (McCloskey & Cohen, 1989; Dong et al., 2023; Ramasesh et al., 2022;
092
      Luo et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024), leaving an open research question: Can model developers allow
093   users to finetune their aligned models on custom datasets while retaining safety?
094
095
      Model Merging. The third row of Figure 1 shows how we can merge multiple models adapted to
096   different individual tasks to have a single model adapted to multiple tasks, by aggregating task vectors
097   (or adapters) of different tasks. Existing model merging techniques either require post-processing
098   the task vectors through sparsification (Yu et al., 2023; Davari & Belilovsky, 2023; Yadav et al.,
099   2023), degrading the performance on the task, and/or require extensive hyperparameter tuning for
100   a weighted aggregation of task vectors (Matena & Raffel, 2022; Xiao et al., 2023b).
101
      Each paradigm of multi-task learning poses different challenges, and different methods have been
102   proposed to address these challenges. We defer the more in-depth analysis of these proposed methods
103   in Appendix B because of the sheer quantity of related work that must be covered. The number of
104   methods itself poses challenges for studying their drawbacks, especially when these methods are
105   adopted in settings orthogonal to those they were originally developed for (i.e., LoRA leading to catas-
106   trophic forgetting of safety alignment (Lermen et al., 2023)). Rather than proposing tailored solutions
107   for each paradigm of multi-task adaptation, we want to propose a simple algorithm that can serve as
      an effective foundation across all three paradigms and evaluate it on a wide range of challenging tasks.

      (1) Storing & Loading Compressible Adapters Without Compromising Performance. LoRA
163   restricts the adaptation updates to have a low rank, which may be insufficient for complex downstream
164   tasks (Nikdan et al., 2024; Biderman et al., 2024). In parallel, recent work (Isik et al., 2023) on
165   compressing the delta between the fine-tuned and pre-trained model ∆ = wF − wP suggests that
166   FFT updates are highly compressible through a simple magnitude-based sparsification. LoTA
167   exploits this underlying sparsity during fine-tuning and obtains better performance than LoRA, while
168   requiring fewer parameters to be trained. As we will show, LoTA adapters can be stored efficiently
169   because of the small number of parameters that need to be updated. LoTA also requires fewer
170   parameters to be updated in the model when the adapter is applied to the model, so loading a LoTA
171
      adapter can be faster than loading a LoRA adapter.

192   Mitigating Destructive Interference via Lottery Ticket Together Optimization (LoTTO). Sup-
193   pose that we have already learned Task A with LoTA. LoTTO calibrates a sparsity mask for Task B by
194
      first training a model where the only weights that can be updated are those that are not updated when
      running LoTA on Task A, and then using a sparse set of those weights to train the final model. In
195
      doing so, LoTTO ensures that the weights updated for Task A are disjoint from the weights updated
196
      for Task B. LoTTO enables learning new tasks without forgetting old ones, by restricting weight
197   adaptations to not interfere with the weights that were important for previous adaptations, therefore
198   mitigating destructive interference. This procedure can be applied inductively to enable sequential
199   adaptation to multiple tasks, so that a model developer seeking to adapt a model adapted with LoTA
200   (potentially on several tasks), just needs to ensure that they do not update the task vector with respect
201   to the base model. LoTTO can be applied to capabilities one at a time; first the model learns math,
202   then coding, then reasoning, then planning, etc. because each of these capabilities benefits from prior
203   knowledge. We train on up to 4 tasks sequentially, but optimistically if we believe we can update 1%
204   of the weights for each capability, then LoTTO can fit at most 100 capabilities. When the subnetworks
205
      for different tasks overlap, we do not expect that LoTTO will result in any worse forgetting than
      LoTA, which we will show itself has better robustness to forgetting than FFT. We will also show that
206
      LoTTO on sequential tasks outperforms the common practice of training on a mixture of tasks.
207
208   (3) Model Merging Across Heterogeneous Tasks. Existing model merging methods have been
209   shown to merge models trained on relatively similar language datasets (Wortsman et al., 2022; Jin
210   et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2023a), typically via a post hoc sparsification (Yadav et al., 2023; Yu et al.,
211
      2023; Davari & Belilovsky, 2023) of the task vectors. This ensures the task vectors are disjoint – hence
      limiting destructive interference – but also degrades the performance of each individual task. LoTA
212
      enforces sparsity during fine-tuning and directly trains sparse task vectors, obviating the need for post
213
      hoc sparsification. LoTA’s advantage over methods such as TIES (Yadav et al., 2023) is similar to the
214   advantage of training a model with an intelligently chosen sparsity constraint throughout training (as in
215   the original lottery ticket hypothesis (Frankle & Carbin, 2019)) over pruning parameters after training.

      4    E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP

219   In this section, we provide details of the experimental setup, including the baselines, model, dataset,
220   and metric selection. We present the results later in Section 5. We share our anonymized code.
221   We are limited to an academic computing budget, and all results are conducted with a single A100
222   GPU. We typically do 1 − 3 epochs of training for each dataset as this is a standard choice in LLM
223
      fine-tuning. We use the RMSProp optimizer with default hyperparameters.
224   Baselines & Hyperparameters. Across all three multi-task adaptation paradigms, we compare LoTA
225   against FFT and LoRA. We extensively tune the hyperparameters for FFT and LoRA to ensure that
226   we are comparing against strong baselines. We do not tune hyperparameters for LoTA and directly
227   transfer the hyperparameters from FFT. We fix the sparsity ratio hyperparameter in LoTA to 90%.
228   Models. We use two commonly-used open-weights model families, Mistral Jiang et al. (2023) and
229   Llama 3 (AI@Meta, 2024; Touvron et al., 2023), specifically Mistral-7B and Llama-3-8B – the
230   largest models we can adapt with FFT on a single GPU.
231
      Tasks. We consider six capabilities: instruction following, safety, math, coding, summarization,
232
      and reasoning. We now briefly discuss each capability, the datasets we use to fine-tune and evaluate
233   the presented methods, and the motivation behind the choices.
234
235   Instruction Following. The most widely-used instruction-tuned LLMs are the “Instruct” or “chat”
236
      versions of base models, such as Llama-3-8B-Instruct (AI@Meta, 2024). This is because the process
      of tuning models on human instructions aligns models to human preferences across a range of
237
      tasks (Ouyang et al., 2022). For this, we adapt models to data from UltraFeedback (Cui et al.,
238
      2023), which contains a mixture of datasets covering truthfulness, honesty, and helpfulness in
239   addition to instruction-following. We measure the instruction following ability by length-controlled
240   AlpacaEval2 Win Rate (Li et al., 2023), which we refer to as “winrate”. A high winrate means that
241   GPT-4 (OpenAI, 2023) prefers the responses of our model on a set of representative prompts over its
242   own responses. Winrate is the metric most closely correlated with human rating preference (Dubois
243   et al., 2024). Another common benchmark for “chat” models is MT-Bench (Zheng et al., 2023),
244   but there is a significant degree of data contamination between MT-Bench and other task-specific
245   training datasets (Yu et al., 2024)–hence, we do not evaluate on MT-Bench.
246   Reasoning. We train on the standard set of 8 commonsense reasoning tasks (Christopher et al., 2019;
247   Bisk et al., 2019; Sap et al., 2019; Zellers et al., 2019; ai2, 2019; Clark et al., 2018; Mihaylov et al.,
248   2018) (Boolq, PIQA, SocialIQA, Hellaswag, Winograde, ARC-easy, ARC-challenge, OpenBookQA)
249   and report the exact-match accuracy on the test set. As a representative task, we use ARC-easy.
250
      Math. We use the set of 9 math instruction datasets from (Yue et al., 2023) for fine-tuning and report
251   performance on the test set of GSM8k (Cobbe et al., 2021). When only considering a single task,
252   we choose GSM8k as the representative task as it is commonly used as a single training and test
253   task by other papers.
254
      Code Generation. We use data that instructs the model to write SQL queries given some con-
255
      text (b mc2, 2023)(SQL-create-context) and report the ROUGE-1 F1 score (Lin, 2004) on the test set.
256
257   Summarization. We use Samsum (Gliwa et al., 2019), reporting ROUGE-1 F1 score on the test set.
258   Safety. A recent concern in AI policy is that, while frontier models such as GPT-3.5/GPT-4 are
259   aligned, they can also be fine-tuned and this presents an opportunity to misalign them. Recently, Qi
260   et al. (2023) show that by fine-tuning GPT-3.5 on just 100 harmful examples for a few epochs, they
261   can ask it to answer harmful queries that it ordinarily would refuse, and Zhan et al. (2024) show the
262   same for GPT-4. Lermen et al. (2023) show that this can be done with LoRA rather than the fine-
263   tuning method OpenAI are using in their fine-tuning API (presumably FFT). We evaluate the safety of
264   our models on HEx-Phi (Qi et al., 2023), a dataset of 330 questions spanning multiple categories such
265   as malware, fraud, etc. The safety score is the percentage of harmful queries that the model refuses
266
      to respond. Aligned models (Llama-3-Instruct) will refuse 100% of harmful queries, but because
      we are aligning base models ourselves, our baseline Instruct model only gets 93%. Because we are
267
      interested in measuring the forgetting of safety alignment, we do not see this as a major limitation.
268
269   Roadmap of the Evaluation.            We vary hyperparameters (Section 5), consider single-task
      adapters (Section 5.1), sequential training (Section 5.2), and model merging (Section 5.3).

346   LoTTO Mitigates Destructive Interference. Table 4 compares LoTTO to FFT and LoRA when
347   training first on GSM8k and then on Reasoning; LoTTO obtains the best performance across both
348   tasks because it mitigates the destructive interference that leads to catastrophic forgetting. We now
349   do a detailed comparison to LoRA, proposing a new method in our effort to compare LoTTO to the
350   strongest possible baselines.
351   LoRA Does Not Significantly Reduce Catastrophic Forgetting of Capabilities Learned During
352   Finetuning. First, note that LoRA loses nearly all performance on Task A when training on Task
353   B. This observation may seem contrary to the results in (Biderman et al., 2024). However, they
354   measure forgetting as the degradation in performance on standard benchmark tasks between the
355   pretrained model and the finetuned model, whereas we measure forgetting as the degradation on Task
356   A between the model finetuned on Task A and the model finetuned on first Task A and then Task B.
357
      We speculate that the discrepancy is that knowledge learned through the pretraining process may be
      far more persistent than knowledge learned through finetuning. Indeed, LoRA does forget less than
358
      FFT –just not enough to make a difference when training with LoRA on both tasks.
359
360   LoRA Can Also Mitigate Destructive Interference, But Not As Well as LoTTO. A key insight
361   into the design of LoTTO is that we can mitigate destructive interference between different tasks by
362   forcing the tasks to update disjoint sets of parameters. LoTTO carefully calibrates the disjoint subsets
363
      by pruning the weights that were updated the most after a small amount of finetuning on the dataset.
      We can also extend this insight to try and mitigate destructive interference in FFT and LoRA. We do
364
      this by proposing an “alternating freeze” strategy, where the odd layers can only be trained on Task A
365
      and the even layers can only be trained on Task B. LoRA already struggles to fit complex tasks such
366   as math (Task A is GSM8k) and the alternating freeze further restricts LoRA’s representative capacity.
367   Therefore, while the alternating freeze does forget less, and on easy tasks such as commonsense
368   reasoning, there is no significant gap between LoRA and LoTA, the gap on the challenging Task A is
369   hard to overcome because it is partially due to the inherent gap in performance between LoRA and
370   LoTA and partially due to LoTA’s limited forgetting as compared to LoRA. We note that LoTA can
371   also be combined with LoRA, by fixing the A matrix in LoRA as a random projection (Zhang et al.,
372   2023b) and tuning the B matrix with LoTA; this could be an interesting piece of future work.
373   Sequential Training Under LoTTO Outperforms Mixing Datasets Together. We compare our
374   LoTTO method, that trains disjoint LoTA adapters sequentially on each dataset, to FFT on a mixture
375   of Instruction Following and the dataset noted in Task B. LoTTO under sequential training consistently
376   outperforms the common practice of mixing all datasets together and doing FFT. We note that LoTA
377   can also be used in the setting of mixing all datasets together, by routing each sample of a given
      dataset to a GPU –specific to that dataset– that applies the corresponding LoTA mask for that dataset.

      How Much Does Data Reuse Help? The simplest and arguably most performant method from prior
433   work that we found for mitigating catastrophic forgetting is simply to replay in some in-distribution
434   data from Task A. This is the line marked with “FFT (Replay)”, and it does mitigate forgetting on
435   Task A, but at the cost of performance on Task B. We ablate the amount of data from Task A to be
436   used between 1 − 100% of the dataset size of the data from Task B, and this is the best result in
437   terms of mitigating forgetting on Task A. As we mix in less and less data from Task A, this method
438   approaches just doing FFT sequentially in performance, so we omit those results for brevity.
439   Adapting Sequentially to Multiple Tasks with LoTTO. We now consider the more challenging
440   setting when we need to adapt to multiple tasks without catastrophic forgetting while still obtaining
441   good performance on those tasks. In Table 6, we adapt an Instruct model to a mix of reasoning, math,
442   and SQL data and find a surprising result; the FFT Instruct model collapses almost completely to
443   a winrate of less than half a percent. As we showed in Table 6, this can be mitigated by mixing
444   in more instruction following data, albeit at a cost. The LoTA Instruct model still degrades in
445   performance (19 → 11) but nowhere near as much. In the line marked “LoTTO”, we instead
446   adapt the LoTA Instruct model to the downstream tasks using the mask calibrated from GSM8k
447
      with LoTTO. Applying LoTTO to make the downstream adaptation be mutually sparse with the
      initial LoTA Instruct model increases performance significantly on instruction following and math.
448
      Performance on Arc and SQL suffers somewhat because our mask does not consider those tasks, but
449
      this means that our mask is much cheaper to calibrate and is, in fact, generalizable not only across
450   tasks within a domain (as shown in Table 6) but also across domains.
451
452   Mitigating Catastrophic Forgetting of Safety Alignment. In the “Safety” row of Table 6, we
453
      consider fine-tuning the Mistral Instruct model we trained ourselves on the 100 harmful instructions
      from (Qi et al., 2023). The baseline model gets a score of 93% and training with FFT quickly
454
      degrades safety. Adapting the LoTA Instruct with LoTTO (again, with the LoTTO mask calibrated
455
      from GSM8k) mitigates this safety drop significantly, even though our LoTTO mask was calibrated
456   on an extremely different dataset. Therefore, a potential mitigation would be for an entity providing a
457   fine-tuning API such as OpenAI to do the safety training with LoTA, calibrate the LoTTO mask on
458   a utility dataset, and then do fine-tuning on their client’s dataset with LoTTO. We do not intend to
459   present our method as an active defense against fine-tuning attacks; although finetuning with LoTTO
460   improves safety, given sufficient data and access to the model weights, any attacker can of course
461   undo safety tuning entirely. However, catastrophic forgetting of safety alignment is an important
462   problem with real-world applications, and we find it compelling that our method can mitigate this.

      5.3   L OTA FOR M ODEL M ERGING
465
466
      We now consider the setting of model merging, where we train models on disjoint datasets fully
467
      in parallel and then merge together the task vectors with the goal of producing a model with good
468   performance on multiple tasks. Prior work in model merging mostly considers merging similar
469   datasets, such as the commonsense reasoning datasets, but it is relatively easy to merge models when
470   the datasets are similar and becomes increasingly hard as the datasets become more heterogeneous
471   due to the gradient mismatch (Daheim et al., 2024). We consider merging models trained on
472   heterogeneous datasets.
473
      Merging Models. We use TIES-Merging (Yadav et al., 2023) as a baseline to merge models
474   trained on heterogeneous tasks, because it outperformed other merging methods in our testing. TIES
475   performs post-hoc sparsification on each task vector and requires a 2-D hyperparameter search for
476   this quantity, which we perform for the merge of FFT models. Naturally, we could optimize the
477   performance of Task A by fully sparsifying Task B, and vice versa; we report the result that achieves
478   good performance on Task B while maintaining some performance on Task A, and report the full
479   range of hyperparameters in Appendix A. LoTA is inherently sparse, so when we merge a LoTA
480   model with an FFT model we do not need to perform hyperparameter search on the LoTA model,
481   and we use the same level of sparsity for the FFT model that we obtained when merging together
482
      two FFT models. When merging two LoTA models together, no hyperparameter search is required at
      all as both models are inherently sparse. We could in theory sparsify the LoTA models beyond their
483
      existing levels with post-hoc sparsification, but we do not tune this hyperparameter.
484
485   Challenge of Overlapping Sparsity in Model Merging. In the sequential training paradigm, we
      exploited the fact that masks for different tasks have a significant overlap in order to generalize our

525   6      D ISCUSSION
526
527
      We propose Lottery Ticket Adaptation (LoTA), a sparse alignment framework that fine-tunes only a
528   sparse subnetwork of the base model, leaving the rest of the parameters frozen. LoTA successfully
529   mitigates destructive interference (a problem with existing fine-tuning methods including FFT and
530   LoRA) in many multi-task adaptation paradigms, prevents catastrophic forgetting of earlier tasks,
531   including safety, and allows for successful model merging of even dramatically different tasks.
532
      Limitations. As mentioned in Section 5, LoTA does not provide the compute efficiency of LoRA. If
533
      the adapter needs to be compressed by more than 100×, LoTA may not provide sufficient compression.
534   We evaluate on instruction following, reasoning, math, SQL generation, and summarization, yet even
535   more tasks exist such as Python code generation, classification, or long-context question answering.
536   We compare LoTA to baselines (LoRA, TIES) but other PEFT and merging methods exist. In a future
537   revision of this paper, we plan to provide comparisons to a broader range of PEFT (Liu et al., 2024b)
538   and merging (Yu et al., 2023) methods. All LoTA masks are unstructured, so we do not reap the
539   runtime benefits of structured sparsity. We only explore magnitude pruning for LoTA, but many other
      methods have been proposed in the literature, as we discuss in Appendix B, and may perform better.

927
928   FFT. We tune the learning rate in the range 5e − 7, 1e − 5 and the best-performing learning rate for
929
      the downstream finetuning tasks is 1e − 6, with the exception of instruction finetuning which we
      detail further below. We use a batch size of 32. We clip the gradient norm of each parameter group to
930
      1. We clip the gradient norm of the parameters as opposed to the full gradient because we fuse the
931
      optimizer with the backward pass to enable FFT on a single A100.
932
933   LoTA. We do not tune any hyperparameters for LoTA and merely use the same hyperparameters as
934   FFT. Unless stated otherwise, we report the performance of LoTA with a sparsity ratio of 0.9 that
935
      was calibrated on the entire dataset.
936   LoRA. LoRA introduces the additional rank hyperparameter, which we tune jointly with the “lora
937   alpha” term and the learning rate. We tune the rank and alpha in the range (8, 512). Common wisdom
938   seems to dictate the use of larger learning rates for LoRA, so we expand the upper edge of the LoRA
939   learning rate range to 1e − 4 and indeed find that LoRA typically benefits from a larger learning rate.
940   We use LoRA on all linear layers (q, k, v, gate, up, down). On the commonsense reasoning tasks, the
941
      best hyperparameters are: r = 512, α = 32, η = 1e − 4. On GSM8k, the best hyperparameters are:

      Compute Overhead. One reason why PEFT methods such as LoRA are commonly used is because
953
      they reduce the memory consumption in the backward pass, thus enabling the use of a larger
954   pass. Biderman et al. (2024) recently critically analyzed this and found that to fit tasks such as math
955   and code generation, LoRA needs to use high ranks (≥ 256), which in turn reduces the speedup to at
956   most 15%. We nevertheless acknowledge that any and all PEFT methods will be faster than LoTA
957   during training because LoTA requires an initial pass over the dataset to calibrate the sparsity mask.
958
      Memory Overhead. LoTA has a minor overhead when compared to FFT, because a bit-mask must be
959
      loaded for each parameter group. We provide an efficient implementation for this in our open-source
960   code. repository. We can finetune Llama-3-8B with LoTA on a single 80GB A100, so we do not
961   anticipate this minor memory overhead being a major limitation of our method. LoRA reduces the
962   memory footprint during finetuning, at the expense of accuracy.
963

964

970   report a similar-sized gap between LoRA and FFT on this dataset when training LLama-2-7B. If

      epoch, or on smaller datasets. In Table 8, we make a side-by-side comparison of LoRA and LoTA

983    Storing LoTA Adapters Efficiently. Although FFT generally performs better than PEFT methods,
984    it is typically infeasible to store a full copy of the model weights for each task. Practitioners, therefore,
985    consider a tradeoff between memory and adaptation performance when loading task-specific adapters.

       We now discuss the storage memory consumption of LoTA and LoRA. Storing the sparse task vector
       from LoTA requires 40 bits per parameter; 32 from the parameter, and 8 for the delta encoding of the
987
       sparse indices. If we use 90%-sparse LoTA, our task vector is compressed 8×; if we use 99%-sparse
988
       LoTA, our task vector is compressed 80×. The memory-utility tradeoff between LoTA and LoRA can
989    be quantified in terms of each method’s performance at a given level of compression, which translates
990    into the sparsity level for LoTA and the rank r for LoRA. Comparing the compression-utility tradeoff
991    between LoRA and LoTA is challenging because the size of the saved LoRA adapter is determined
992    by the rank parameter r, and more is not always better (which is why we have to tune r for every
993    task for LoRA). As a single point of comparison, we can look at the performance on GSM8k, where
994    99%-sparse LoTA (62.0) outperforms LoRA for any rank (best performance of 58.3 achieved at
995    r = 512, increasing rank reduces performance), and they both achieve a similar compression factor.

       Model Pruning & Quantization. Model pruning and quantization have been receiving increased
1019
       attention for efficient storage and/or inference of large models. While most of the existing methods
1020   prune (Frantar & Alistarh, 2023; Dettmers et al., 2023b; Kim et al., 2023) or quantize (Frantar et al.,
1021   2023; Dettmers et al., 2022; Xiao et al., 2023a; Lin et al., 2023) the model weight directly, some focus
1022   specifically on compressing the task vectors (Isik et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024a; Yao & Klimovic,
1023   2023) through post-training sparsification or quantization, assuming that the base model is worth the
1024   storage cost since it is being used frequently for many tasks. Our proposed PEFT method, LoTA,
1025   builds on this observation that the task vectors are highly compressible through sparsification and
       imposes this sparsity constraint at the beginning of fine-tuning to train sparse task vectors.

1026
       Lottery Ticket Hypothesis. Motivated by the success of pruning methods at extreme sparsity
1027   ratios (Han et al., 2015), Frankle & Carbin (2019) proposed the lottery ticket hypothesis (LTH),
1028   claiming the existence of sparse subnetworks (or lottery tickets) that could be trained from scratch to
1029   a performance comparably to training the dense model from scratch. While this could potentially
1030   provide a way to train models sparsely more efficiently rather than training them densely and pruning
1031   them later, finding the lottery tickets, i.e., the sparsity masks, is costly. Initially, Frankle & Carbin
1032   (2019) proposed first training the models densely and then extracting the sparsity mask based on the
1033   magnitude of the trained dense model’s weights. Later, a number of more efficient methods were
1034   proposed to find the sparsity masks more efficiently, earlier in the dense training stage (Frankle et al.,
1035
       2021; Lee et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2020; Tanaka et al., 2020). Our work shows that LTH works
       successfully for fine-tuning LLMs as well–giving us a sparse adaptation tool, LoTA. We extensively
1036
       study the tradeoff between the cost of finding the sparsity masks and the performance of the sparsely
1037
       fine-tuned model. Unlike other studies on LTH for LLM adaptation (Yuan et al., 2024; Xu & Zhang,
1038   2024), our main focus and motivation is to mitigate destructive interference in multi-task adaptation.
1039
1040
       Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT). Many practitioners fine-tune already pre-trained LLMs
1041
       with less data and compute instead of training them from scratch (Liu et al., 2022a; Wang et al., 2022;
1042
       Ouyang et al., 2022). While this reduces the cost of LLM training significantly, fine-tuning each
1043   and every parameter of these large models for each (or a few) task is still very costly. This has led
1044   to a number of parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) methods reducing the number of trainable
1045   parameters during fine-tuning (Zhang et al., 2023c; Li & Liang, 2021; Liu et al., 2022b; Lester
1046   et al., 2021; Edalati et al., 2022; Hu et al., 2023; Nikdan et al., 2024; Guo et al., 2020). Among
1047   different PEFT methods, low-rank adaptation (LoRA) (Hu et al., 2022) and its variants (Dettmers
1048   et al., 2023a; Guo et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024; Kopiczko et al., 2024) have shown similar performance
1049   to full fine-tuning in many tasks while reducing the number of trainable parameters through low-rank
1050   approximation to model updates during fine-tuning. Our PEFT method, LoTA, while reducing the
1051
       number of trainable parameters significantly via sparsity, has various other benefits in different
       applications, such as avoiding catastrophic forgetting (of especially safety alignment), enabling
1052
       fine-tuning on new tasks more successfully, model merging using sparse task vectors, unlearning,
1053
       and communication-efficient federated learning (FL). We demonstrate that full fine-tuning and the
1054   existing PEFT methods fall short in these applications and significantly underperform LoTA.
1055
1056
       Catastrophic Forgetting. When LLMs go through sequential (or continual) multitask learning, i.e.,
1057
       fine-tuned on different tasks sequentially, they often suffer from performance loss on earlier tasks–
1058   known as catastrophic forgetting (McCloskey & Cohen, 1989; Dong et al., 2023; Ramasesh et al.,
1059   2022; Luo et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024). To mitigate this, a number of data-centric and architectural
1060   solutions have been proposed for language and other domains. Replay-based methods (Rebuffi
1061   et al., 2017; Romanov et al., 2018) add a portion of the previously learned data during fine-tuning
1062   on a new task, which raises privacy concerns as it requires constant access to previously learned
1063   data. Regularization-based approaches (Huang et al., 2021; Aljundi et al., 2018) tend to have poor
1064   adaptability to specific tasks. An architecture-based approach, “progressive prompts” Razdaibiedina
1065   et al. (2023), sequentially concatenates soft prompts as they are being learned for each task–showing
1066
       some resistance against forgetting. However, they require access to task identifiers at inference
       for each task, which is not always feasible. Other architecture-based approaches add additional
1067
       modules to learn task-specific abilities (Dou et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2024)–requiring customized
1068
       deployment due to architecture change. Closest to our work, Hui et al. (2024) updates a randomly
1069   selected subset of the parameters at each iteration of fine-tuning to preserve the earlier tasks in
1070   the not-updated parameters of that iteration. Despite similarities, our work LoTA (1) uses a fixed
1071   sparsity mask throughout fine-tuning instead of a new mask at every iteration, which yields sparse
1072   task vectors that are useful for other applications such as model merging and communication-efficient
1073   FL, and (2) finds data-dependent masks rather than the randomly selected masks in (Hui et al., 2024).
1074   Furthermore, unlike (Hui et al., 2024), LoTA not only preserves the earlier tasks on frozen parameters
1075   but also constraints the new tasks on a highly sparse subnetwork–providing resistance to catastrophic
1076   forgetting even when malicious users attempt to overwrite the earlier tasks via FFT.
1077
1078   Model Merging. Merging multiple task-specific models into a single model with multitask abili-
1079   ties (Wortsman et al., 2022; Jin et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2023a) has been an appealing alternative
       to sequential multitask learning, which suffers from catastrophic forgetting and could be inefficient,

       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1080
       especially if task vectors are already available. The existing model merging methods include aver-
1081   aging weights of task-specific models (Wortsman et al., 2022), task arithmetic through combining
1082   task vectors (Ilharco et al., 2022), weighted aggregation of parameters (Matena & Raffel, 2022; Xiao
1083   et al., 2023b), combining task vectors after some post-processing such as trimming low-magnitude
1084   deltas (Yadav et al., 2023) or sparsifying the deltas (Yu et al., 2023; Davari & Belilovsky, 2023). Our
1085   method, LoTA, directly learns sparse task vectors, obviating the need to post-process the task vectors,
1086   and outperforms existing model merging methods. Most importantly, LoTA enables merging task
1087   vectors trained on heterogeneous datasets, while the other model merging methods are often limited
1088   to similar datasets. This advancement is an important step towards scalable FL (Kairouz et al., 2021)
1089
       with LLMs as it enables merging sparse task vectors, which brings communication efficiency, trained
       over heterogeneous datasets (of each edge device).

1091   We note that we test model merging with LoTA specifically for highly dissimilar datasets to show its
1092   compatibility with FL, which considers edge devices with heterogeneous datasets. When used in FL,
1093   LoTA can reduce communication and memory costs significantly, which is a main bottleneck when
1094   scaling FL to large models. The successful use of LoTA for model merging and arithmetic further
1095
       shows its promise for unlearning (Ilharco et al., 2022) as well.
