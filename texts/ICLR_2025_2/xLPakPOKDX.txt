009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Diffusion models have shown remarkable performance in text-guided image genera-
012            tion when trained on large-scale datasets, usually collected from the Internet. These
013            large-scale datasets have contextual biases (e.g., co-occurrence of objects) which
014            will naturally cascade into the diffusion model. For example, given a text prompt of
015            “a photo of the living room”, diffusion models frequently generate a couch, a rug,
016            and a lamp together while rarely generating objects that do not commonly occur
017            in a living room. Intuitively, contextual bias can be helpful because it naturally
018
               draws the scene even without detailed information (i.e., visual autofill). On the
               other hand, contextual bias can limit the diversity of generated images (e.g., diverse
019
               object combinations) to focus on common image compositions. To have the best of
020
               both worlds, we argue that contextual bias needs to be strengthened or weakened
021            depending on the situation. Previous causally-motivated studies have tried to deal
022            with such issues by analyzing confounders (i.e., contextual bias) and augmenting
023            training data or designing their models to directly learn the interventional distri-
024            bution. However, due to the large-scale nature of these models, obtaining and
025            analyzing the data or training the huge model from scratch is beyond reach in prac-
026            tice. To tackle this problem, we propose two novel frameworks for strengthening
027            or weakening the contextual bias of pretrained diffusion models without training
028            any parameters or accessing training data. Briefly, we first propose causal graphs
029
               to explicitly model contextual bias in the generation process. We then sample the
               hidden confounder due to contextual bias by sampling from a chain of pretrained
030
               large-scale models. Finally, we use samples from the confounder to strengthen or
031
               weaken the contextual bias based on methods from causal inference. Experiment
032            results show that our proposed methods are effective in generating more realistic
033            and diverse images than the regular sampling method.

036   1   I NTRODUCTION
037
038   Diffusion models (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015; Ho et al., 2020) have shown remarkable performance
039   in image generation regarding realism (Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021), likelihood estimation (Nichol &
040   Dhariwal, 2021), and controllability (Zhang et al., 2023b; Ruiz et al., 2023; Gal et al., 2023; Hertz
041   et al., 2023), which related research fields have leveraged to provide significant gains in performance,
042   such as Video Generation (Ho et al., 2022b;a), Text-to-3D Synthesis (Poole et al., 2023; Wang et al.,
043   2023), Medical Domain (Kazerouni et al., 2023), and Virtual Try-on (Zhu et al., 2023).
044   Aside from the sophisticated formulations and optimized modeling techniques, one of the most
045   fundamental reasons for the recent success of diffusion models is the large-scale training data. Large-
046   scale data contains a massive amount of knowledge in the visual world, which could enable the
047   models to learn more extended support and smoother latent space. Billions of images are used to train
048   StableDiffusion (Rombach et al., 2022), an off-the-shelf diffusion model, with which surprisingly
049   realistic images can be generated given diverse user-specified queries.
050   Despite the superior performance in image generation, pretrained diffusion models implicitly learn
051   contextual bias (e.g., co-occurring objects) from the real-world training data. For example, a set of
052   concepts “a living room”, “a couch”, “a rug” and “a lamp” are highly correlated in the real world,
053   and thus generated images conditioned on “a living room” frequently contain all of the others, e.g.,
      Fig. 1 (a). We hypothesize this is caused by contextual bias contained in data (Liu et al., 2022; Wang

099   Dealing with contextual bias has been a widely-explored topic in causally-inspired literature (Deng &
100   Zhang, 2022; Liu et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2020; Yue et al., 2020; Tang et al., 2020b; Wang et al.,

102   graph, and the confounding effects can be mitigated by directly modeling interventional distribution
103   or total direct effects with neural networks.
104   However, naively applying the existing causally-inspired methods to diffusion models is not practical
105   enough due to their large-scale nature. To be specific, obtaining billions of data points and analyzing

      confounders, i.e., contextual bias, over massive-scale data can be arduous. Furthermore, large-scale
109   diffusion models need to be redesigned to model the interventional distribution and need to be trained
110   from scratch as well, which is far beyond reach in practice.

112
      To this end, we propose two causally-motivated diffusion sampling frameworks that can strengthen
      and weaken the confounding effects, respectively, without finetuning or access to large-scale training
113
      data. Both approaches are inspired by the observations that pretrained large-scale models implicitly
114
      learned contextual bias, and thus the confounding variable and confounding effects can be retrieved
115   by sampling from these models.
116
117   Briefly, we first design a causal graph representing the image generation process when training
118
      diffusion models (Fig. 3 (b)), where contextual bias is not explicitly modeled during training but is
      an unobserved confounder between the input text and the image. To strengthen the contextual bias
119
      in pretrained Diffusion Models, we leverage implicitly learned contextual bias of Large Language
120
      Models (LLM, e.g., Gemini (Gemini-Team et al., 2023)) to aid in retrieving and enhancing contextual
121   bias (Fig. 3 (a)). To weaken the contextual bias, on the other hand, we first derive the interventional
122   distribution and then approximate it by sampling from pretrained diffusion models and Vision
123   Language Models (VLM, e.g., LLaVA (Liu et al., 2023; 2024b;a)). Following the derived sampling
124   chain, we retrieve samples from the hidden confounder that pretrained diffusion models have learned
125   implicitly. Next, we remove the confounding effects by adjusting for the retrieved confounder by
126   backdoor adjustment formula (Pearl, 2009) (Fig. 3 (c)).
127   In summary, our contributions are as follows:
128
129    • We explicitly model contextual bias as a latent confounder and analyze how to retrieve this
130
         confounder using only pretrained models.
       • We develop methods to increase and decrease the contextual bias using this latent confounder via
131
         marginalization and intervention respectively.
132
       • We demonstrate quantitatively and qualitatively that our methods can improve the diversity or
133      fidelity of the images while maintaining the content of the user-specified prompt. Furthermore,
134      we showcase that these ideas can be applied alongside other controllability methods.
135    • We will release a dataset of 1,130,195 confounders (specifically co-occurring objects) that could
136      be used in future contextual bias research.

      Diffusion models. Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Models (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015; Ho et al.,
141
      2020) (DDPM) are a class of probabilistic generative models mapping known distribution like
142   Gaussian XT to unknown    real distribution X0 . The reverse process is defined as a Markov Chain

      pθ (X|Y = y) = pθ (x0:T |y)dx1:T , where pθ (x0:T |y) = p(xT ) t=1 pθ (xt−1 |xt , y). Thus, to
144   obtain the generated sample x from the reverse process pθ (X|y), we first need to sample from the
145   standard Gaussian p(xT ) and pass the sample through the reverse denoising steps from t = T to
146   t = 1. DDIM (Song et al., 2021) proposed a non-Markovian diffusion process, with which we
147   can reduce the sampling time as well as deterministically sample from diffusion models. We use
148   deterministic DDIM for sampling in all of the experiments. Latent Diffusion Models (Rombach et al.,
149   2022) (LDM) introduces pretrained autoencoder (Esser et al., 2021) to diffusion models and use them
150   as encoding/decoding modules to speed up trainig/sampling diffusion models.
151   Contextual bias. Though terminologies are not unified, the concept of contextual bias (i.e., the
152   object co-occurrence statistics) has been a widely discussed topic in discriminative tasks such as
153   visual question answering (Hendricks et al., 2018; Manjunatha et al., 2019; Cadene et al., 2019;
154   Yang et al., 2021b), multi-label classification (Liu et al., 2022), scene graph generation (Tang et al.,
155   2020b), few-shot learning (Yue et al., 2020), semantic segmentation (Zhang et al., 2020), and long-
156   tailed classification (Tang et al., 2020a), where causal inference (Pearl, 2009) is one of the common
157   solutions. On the other hand, in generative tasks, relatively less attention has been paid to the concept
158
      of contextual bias. It is used for increasing the interpretability of the classification result (Goyal et al.,
      2019; Lang et al., 2021) or augmenting data (Mao et al., 2021).
159

      5         L IMITATIONS & S OCIETAL IMPACTS & C ONCLUSION
514
515
516   Limitations. Our proposed sampling methods have two limitations. As seen in Table 3 of the main
517   paper, the explicitly modeled c′ from CB+ and CB- can sometimes yield slightly degraded prompt
518   preservation which is expected when increasing or decreasing contextual bias. This can also be
519
      observed in the green box of Fig. 7. For example, y = “1girl”, but the results of CB+ and CB- contain
      other objects or contexts not in the y. Secondly, if the sampled c′ is semantically too far from y, the
520
      generated results sometimes ignore the added information from c′ . The examples are shown in the
521
      dotted red boxes in Fig. 7. For instance, under “A photo of a living room”, c′ for CB- in the red
522   box is “horses, stadium, people”. Additionally, our proposed frameworks are dependent on LLM or
523   VLM which can introduce additional bias. However, We believe the bias from LLM and VLM can
524   be closely related to the bias in pretrained diffusion models because all foundation-level models are
525   mostly trained on "Internet data", which has its own set of biases. A more detailed discussion is in
526   Sec. D.2 in Appendix.
527
      Societal Impacts. Our method could have a potential societal impact as we can adjust the context
528   of the generated images. However, we believe the developed technology would bring more good
529   than harm by demonstrating that it is possible for such adjustment, which informs people to be more
530   mindful of the content generated from AI models.
531
      Conclusion. In this work, we proposed two sampling methods for controlling contextual bias in
532
      diffusion sampling frameworks based on Causal Inference. We define the contextual bias issue in the
533
      pretrained diffusion models. Since it can be a double-edged sword depending on the situation, we
534   propose to control the contextual bias during the generation process. We first propose a causal graph
535   where the contextual bias is explicitly modeled. We propose two sampling methods for retrieving
536   contextual bias to strengthen/weaken the confounding effects. By involving the retrieved confounder
537   in the generation process, we show that the generated results can be more diverse and realistic in
538   exchange for the insignificant loss of prompt preservation. We also show that our proposed method is
539   general and easily adaptable to other diffusion works based on pretrained diffusion models. We hope
      our work and novel perspective can inspire future research on causally motivated diffusion models.

795
      Generative Models with Causal Inference.
796   There have been many previous studies trying to combine Generative Models and Causal Inference.
797   CausalGAN (Kocaoglu et al., 2018) proposes a GAN-based framework to train a causal implicit
798   generative model with a predefined causal graph. They show that CausalGAN can model inter-
799   ventional distributions beyond simple conditionals. Shen et al. proposes DEAR, a GAN-based
800   disentangled learning method in Causal Representation Learning. DEAR leverages a Structural
801
      Causal Model (SCM) as a trainable prior of bidirectional Generative Models to do causal controllable
      generation. CausalVAE (Yang et al., 2021a) proposes a VAE-based framework to have disentangled
802
      latent representations. A causal layer is newly introduced where independent exogenous variables are
803
      transformed into causal endogenous variables. Model identifiability is further analyzed. In Karimi
804   et al. (2020), Conditional VAE is used to estimate the conditional average treatment effect (CATE)
805   of an intervention given only limited causal information (i.e., a causal graph without true SCMs).
806   Brehmer et al. (2022) shows that weak supervision is sufficient to identify causal representations
807   and their SCMs. Briefly, a paired data before-and-after randomly-sampled unknown intervention is
808   needed. VAE is used to model their proposed Latent causal models (LCMs). Pawlowski et al. (2020)
809   proposes a framework for creating SCMs of which components are made of deep learning modules.
      Their deep SCMs are designed to satisfy three rungs of the causation ladder (association, intervention,


                                                                                                                                                                    15
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
      counterfactuals), and normalizing flows and variational inference are used for tractable counterfactual
811   inference. Inspired by the fact that an ordering over variables is defined in both autoregressive flow
812   and causality, Khemakhem et al. (2021) proposes to use autoregressive flow in causality tasks, such
813   as causal discovery, interventional and counterfactual predictions.
814
      As for Causality-based diffusion models, Chao et al. (2023) proposes diffusion-based causal model
815
      (DCM) approximating both interventions and counterfactuals, which can be trained with a causal
816
      graph and observational data. As opposed to ours where each node of SCM is an input/output
817   variable of diffusion models, each node of the given causal graph in Chao et al. (2023) is modeled
818   as diffusion models, i.e., a child node takes as input the output of the parent node. They further
819   measure the accuracy of the counterfactual estimations and show that the estimations can be bounded
820   with reasonable assumptions. Given observational data and a causal structure, Sanchez & Tsaftaris
821   (2022) proposes a framework Diff-SCM for estimating causal effect. As opposed to ours where
822   diffusion steps are not involved in SCMs, their framework suggests considering diffusion processes
823   as Causal Models. During the inference, Diff-SCM can be used for sampling from the interventionals
824   or estimating counterfactuals. They also present a metric for evaluating the estimated counterfactuals
825
      from their proposed framework. Varici et al. (2023) proposes an approach in Causal Representation
      Learning. Given an unknown linear transformation and indirectly observed causal latent variables,
826
      their aim is to recover the linear transformation (i.e., identifiable representation learning) and Directed
827
      Acyclic Graph (DAG) of the causal latent variables (i.e., causal structure learning). Inspired by
828   the fact that the changes in the score function and the intervening effect are highly correlated, they
829   propose a transformation-recovering method that detects minimal variations across different inter-
830   ventional environments given latent variables’ score function. This work aims to have disentangled
831   representations which is fundamentally different from our task aims (i.e., retrieving the hidden con-
832   founder, i.e., contextual bias, of pretrained diffusion models and increasing/decreasing the contextual
833   bias under the Causal framework). Recently, Lorch et al. (2024) proposes an idea to replace the
834   formalism of SCMs for causal modeling with stationary diffusion processes which are particular
835   diffusion processes that admit a stable stationary distribution. The benefits of this framework allow
836
      cyclic causal dependencies and more flexible causal modeling. How to model interventions and a
      kernel-based approach for parameter estimation are proposed. This work differs from the methods
837
      and goals of our work since it aims to replace the SCM approach to causal modeling, while we aim to
838
      understand the image generation process under SCM to apply the knowledge in Causal Inference
839   (i.e., defining a problem; explicitly modeling a contextual bias in SCMs, and resolving the problem;
840   controlling the contextual bias in the generation process under the SCM). Additionally, it requires
841   stationary diffusion models and does not consider image-based or pretrained diffusion models.
