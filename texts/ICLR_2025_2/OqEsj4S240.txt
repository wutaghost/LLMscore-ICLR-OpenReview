009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Understanding the decision-making processes of large language models (LLMs)
012            is critical given their widespread applications. Towards this goal, describing the
013            topological and geometrical properties of internal representations has recently pro-
014            vided valuable insights. For a more comprehensive characterization of these inher-
015            ently complex spaces, we present a novel framework based on zigzag persistence,
016
               a method in topological data analysis (TDA) well-suited for describing data un-
               dergoing dynamic transformations across layers. Within this framework, we intro-
017
               duce persistence similarity, a new topological descriptor that quantifies the persis-
018
               tence and transformation of topological features such as p-cycles throughout the
019            model layers. Unlike traditional similarity measures, our approach captures the
020            entire evolutionary trajectory of these features, providing deeper insights into the
021            internal workings of LLMs. As a practical application, we leverage persistence
022            similarity to identify and prune layers, demonstrating comparable performance
023            to state-of-the-art methods across several benchmark datasets. Additionally, our
024            analysis reveals similar topological behaviors across various models and hyperpa-
025            rameter settings, suggesting a universal structure in LLM internal representations.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      Large Language Models (LLMs) have revolutionized natural language processing by achieving un-
030   precedented performance levels across a wide range of tasks (see Raiaan et al. (2024) for a review).
031   Despite their success, the black-box nature of these models has raised significant concerns about
032   interpretability and transparency (Liao & Vaughan, 2023). Moreover, their large scale demands a
033   considerable amount of computational resources (Samsi et al., 2023; Bai et al., 2024), making it
034   essential to reduce their size without compromising performance (Ma et al., 2023; Gromov et al.,
035   2024; Men et al., 2024).
036   One strategy for addressing these issues has been to study the models’ internal representations. Early
037   works (Zeiler & Fergus, 2014) demonstrated that visualization techniques can effectively uncover
038   hierarchical representations within convolutional neural networks, highlighting how lower layers
039   focus on edge detection while higher layers correspond to object parts and semantic concepts. Ad-
040   ditionally, (Olah et al., 2018) illustrated that analyzing weight matrices and neuron activations can
041   reveal interpretable features and organizational structures within deep networks, providing insights
042   into how complex patterns are encoded and processed.
043   More recently, geometric studies made progress by introducing concepts like intrinsic dimension to
044   characterize the manifold of internal representations and its evolution across layers (Ansuini et al.,
045   2019; Doimo et al., 2020; Pope et al., 2021). These methods have been successfully applied to
046   transformer models in various works (Valeriani et al., 2023; Tulchinskii et al., 2024; Cheng et al.,
047   2023). One notable achievement of this approach has been to show the emergence of semantic
048   knowledge and abstraction phases in the middle layers of models, rather than at the final layers,
049   as might be intuitively expected. However, these approaches provide only a static view of internal
050
      representations and suffer limitations in tracking their changes across layers.
051   A natural framework to address these limitations and to offer a more comprehensive characterization
052   of the geometry of internal representations of neural networks is Topological Data Analysis (TDA).
053   TDA is a set of unsupervised techniques that offers robust methods to describe the shape and struc-
      ture of complex datasets. It has seen exponential growth with applications in computational biology


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      (Mandal et al., 2020), cosmology (Biagetti et al., 2021; Yip et al., 2024)], personalized medicine
055   (Skaf & Laubenbacher, 2022), time-dependent data analysis (El-Yaagoubi et al., 2023), and ma-
056   chine learning (Hensel et al., 2021), just to name a few. One prominent tool within TDA is persistent
057   homology, which tracks the birth and death of topological features across different scales, thereby
058   capturing the multiscale behavior of a point cloud. Several studies have proposed persistent homol-
059   ogy to investigate neural networks and their internal representations (e.g. Rieck, Bastian Alexander
060   et al. (2023), Naitzat et al. (2020); Lacombe et al. (2021); Magai & Ayzenberg (2022)).
061   However, in the context of TDA applications, it has not yet been recognized that the internal rep-
062   resentations of LLMs can essentially be viewed as dynamic point clouds evolving in time (layers).
063   As pre-trained LLMs process inputs, they transform these point clouds within the representation
064   space layer by layer, capturing essential features and relationships throughout the model’s depth.
065   Thus, it is natural to interpret these transformations as an evolving discrete dynamical system. To
066   address this problem, we exploit a TDA tool developed to characterize time-varying point clouds
067   and temporal networks, known as zigzag persistence.
068   Our approach achieves the following results:
069
070          • ZigZag Framework for LLMs: We build a framework to characterize the birth and death
071            of topological features across transformer model’s layers. As new contributions in the
072            context of zigzag applications, we introduce the k-Nearest Neighbors-based filtration, and
073            we interpret layers as time snapshots in a dynamic system, tracking the trajectory of features
074            across layers.
075          • Persistence Similarity: We propose a new topological descriptor to measure which topo-
076            logical features persist across the layers of an LLM. Different than other similarity mea-
077            sures, persistence similarity tracks the entire trajectory of transformations between two
078            layers.
079          • Model Pruning: As a showcase of our framework, we use persistence similarity as a
080            criterion to prune layers without significantly degrading performance, finding comparable
081            results to state-of-the-art methods.
082          • Similarity of Results Across Models and Hyperparameters: Our findings show similar
083            results across different models, layers, and choices of hyperparameters of the framework.
084            This suggests a degree of universality in the topological structure of LLM representations.
085
086   In summary, our framework presents a novel perspective by combining two fundamental elements:
087   firstly, it provides a fine-grained geometric analysis of the internal representations through TDA;
088   secondly, the zigzag persistence framework tracks the trajectory of topological features across lay-
089
      ers. Distinct from traditional methods that solely compare representations at individual layers, our
      approach captures their entire evolutionary path, providing a richer understanding of how these fea-
090
      tures evolve and contribute to the model’s decision-making processes.

095   Geometry and Topology of Internal Representations. The manifold hypothesis suggests that
096   high-dimensional data often lies on a lower-dimensional manifold (Goodfellow et al., 2016). The
097   estimation of this approximated manifold, known as intrinsic dimension, changes dynamically in
098   deep networks, expanding and contracting in ways that impact performance (Ansuini et al., 2019),
099
      learnability (Pope et al., 2021), and the network’s ability to generate flexible abstract data represen-
      tations used for downstream tasks (Doimo et al., 2020), (Valeriani et al., 2023). Intrinsic dimension
100
      and neighbor composition analysis of internal representations of causal and masked transformer
101
      models helped in the localization of semantic information, and to highlight differences between real
102   and artificial data (Valeriani et al., 2023; Tulchinskii et al., 2024; Cheng et al., 2023). Another
103   approach to study the internal representation is to use topological methods of TDA. Studies on Con-
104   volutional Neural Networks (CNN) used topological descriptors to explore the shape of activation
105   functions (Rathore et al.) or their relations to performance (Naitzat et al., 2020). Magai & Ayzen-
106   berg (2022) introduced persistent homology dimension as an estimator of the intrinsic dimension of
107   internal representations in CNNs, while Barannikov et al. (2022) proposed a measure of similarity
      based on topological descriptors to compare representations. Betti numbers have been observed to


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      remain stable across different datasets for the same architectures and to decrease as depth increases
109   (Suresh et al., 2023).
110
111   Zigzag Persistence. Zigzag persistence was introduced in (Carlsson & de Silva, 2010; Carlsson
112
      et al., 2009; Tausz & Carlsson, 2011) as an extension of persistent homology to study the persistence
      of topological features across sequences of spaces. This approach is particularly useful when data
113
      undergo dynamic changes or transformations over time. Since its introduction, zigzag persistence
114
      has been applied in various fields, including Hopf bifurcations in dynamical systems (Tymochko
115   et al., 2020), commuting patterns in Great Britain’s transportation network Myers et al. (2023), coral
116   reef ecosystems (McDonald et al., 2023), cell location time series (Yang et al., 2023; Zhang et al.,
117   2023), and honeybee aggregations (Gharooni-Fard et al., 2024). It has also inspired methodological
118   extensions such as multidimensional persistence (Kim & Mémoli, 2021) and the development of
119   formigrams and crocker stacks (Xian et al., 2022).
120
      Layer Pruning by Similarity in Large Language Models. Among existing methods to reduce
121   the size of neural networks, layer pruning has gained particular relevance in the context of LLMs.
122   The first applications to BERT models (Fan et al., 2020; Zhang & He, 2020; Fan et al., 2021; Jha
123   et al., 2024) inspired a long series of experiments employing similar techniques (Sajjad et al., 2023;
124   Siddiqui et al., 2024; He et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024a; Kim et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024b).
125   Many of these efforts base their methodology on similarity measures of internal representations,
126   which have conveniently been summarized in a recent review (Klabunde et al., 2023). In this work,
127   we consider (Gromov et al., 2024), which uses angular similarity, and (Men et al., 2024), which uses
128   Block-Influence similarity, as a reference point for comparison.

130   3     M ETHOD
131
132
      In this section, we introduce the zigzag persistence framework, which we use to analyze the internal
133
      representations of LLMs pre-trained with an autoregressive loss. These models typically receive an
134   input sequence of n tokens (often representing a sentence) embedded in a d-dimensional space. The
135   input is transformed across the network layers without altering the embedding dimension. Due to
136   the autoregressive nature of these models, the representation of the last token in a sequence captures
137   information about the entire sequence and is used to predict the next. As a result, we choose to focus
138   on the last token representation of each sequence at each layer. Thus, our point cloud is represented
139   by last tokens embeddings, i.e. vectors of the form {xi (ℓj )} ∈ Rd , for i = 1, ..., Nsentences and j =
140   1, ..., Nlayers . These last tokens are extracted from large datasets of text and serve as an observational
141   probe of the manifold we would like to model.

143   3.1   T OPOLOGICAL DATA A NALYSIS AND P ERSISTENT H OMOLOGY
144
145   Topological data analysis (Edelsbrunner et al., 2002; Zomorodian & Carlsson, 2004) provides a
146   tool for geometrically characterizing highly complex datasets. Within this framework, persistent
147
      homology (Carlsson, 2009) is the key methodology to characterize a point cloud on multiple scales
      at once. Its goal is to identify the range of scales over which a particular class of topological features
148
      (connected components, loops, voids, higher dimensional “holes”) remain relevant, or “persistent”,
149
      as opposed to “topological noise”, i.e. features disappearing roughly at the same scale they formed.
150   The basic ingredients for this technique are i) a criterion to connect points, forming a simplicial
151   complex and ii) a scale parameter ν (often a coarsening scale) such that given ν1 ≤ ν2 , then the two
152   corresponding simplicial complexes are related by Kν1 ⊆ Kν2 . The ordered sequence of simplicial
153   complexes for varying scale parameters is called filtration. An intuitive example is the Vietoris-Rips
154   filtration, built from complexes parametrized by the radius of the ball drawn around each point of
155   the dataset.
156   Filtrations can be generalized to a more flexible structure called a zigzag filtration. Unlike a standard
157   filtration, a zigzag filtration allows the sequence of complexes to move both forward and backward,
158   meaning that inclusions between complexes can reverse at certain steps. We take this approach in
159   our study to track the evolution of the internal representations across layers, rather than at a fixed
160   snapshot, as done in traditional persistent homology implementations. In this sense, our parameter is
161   not a distance/coarsening scale, but a discrete time scale represented by the layer number. We track
      topological features as they are formed and destroyed along the layers of the model and we statisti-


                                                          3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      cally characterize these changes to describe a complex series of transformations in high-dimensional
163   space. Differently than standard persistent homology, short- and long-lived features represent how
164   the model dynamically evolves. Short-lived features indicate a high rate of rearrangement of the
165   points xi between adjacent layers, while long-lived features suggest a phase of retention of (rela-
166   tive) positions in the model. This is a crucial point in our analysis, as it provides a novel tool to
167   geometrically interpret the model’s internal representations. We now outline the main steps of the
168   zigzag algorithm, leaving a rigorous mathematical formulation to Appendix A.

      4.3   L AYER PRUNING BY PERSISTENCE SIMILARITY
437
      Recently, measures of layer similarity have been used to identify layers that contribute minimally to
438
      the performance of LLMs. These layers can be pruned, and the performance re-evaluated to validate
439   this assumption. Since persistence similarity tracks changes across layers, it can be leveraged for
440   layer pruning by selecting layers that retain the most cycles. Consequently, we establish a pruning
441   criterion based on average persistence similarity S̄1 computed now on the Pile dataset. Specifically,
442   we prune layers that lie within 10% and 20% of the maximum S̄1 , corresponding to conservative
443   and aggressive pruning, respectively. Here is a schematic summary of the algorithm.
444
445   Algorithm 2 Pruning algorithm
446
      Require: S̄1 , model, threshold,
447     max ← max(S̄1 )
448     layersT oRemove ← []
449     for l ← 1 to model.getN umLayers() do
450         if S̄1 [l] > max ∗ threshold then
                 layersT oRemove.append(l)
451
            end if
452     end for
453     model.removeLayers(layersT oRemove)
454
455
456   The algorithm outputs how many and which layers have high degree of persistence similarity. We
457   now cut those layers and measure performance using the benchmarks introduced in Section 4 and
458   across models considered in this work.
459   We compare to layer pruning methods based on state of the art measures of similarity, namely
460   (Gromov et al., 2024) and (Men et al., 2024). Both approaches are designed to take as input the
461   desired number of layers to prune Nprune and measure performance as Nprune grows. For a fair
462   comparison, we feed the number of layers cut by our method as an input to the other two methods,
463   and verify which layers they select to cut given this input, and the corresponding performance.
464   We show a schematic diagram of the layers cut with our method (Bottom Row) and the other two
465   methods (Upper Row) in Figure 5. Interestingly, both considered methods from (Gromov et al.,
466   2024) and (Men et al., 2024) give the same result at fixed Nprune , thus we refer to them simply as

514   5   C ONCLUSIONS
515
516   In this study, we present an innovative framework that utilizes zigzag persistence, a tool from Topo-
517   logical Data Analysis (TDA), to examine the internal representations of Large Language Models
518   (LLMs). By employing various datasets as observational probes of the manifold on which the model
519   functions, we aim to offer an interpretable depiction of changes in position and relationships across
520   layers. A distinguishing feature of our framework is its ability to trace the emergence and disap-
521
      pearance of topological features as they evolve across layers. This approach effectively models the
      transformer architecture as an evolving dynamic system, setting it apart from previous research.
522
      With this algorithm, we introduce a new topological descriptor, persistence similarity, which statis-
523
      tically models rearrangements of points in representation space, and the rate of these changes, across
524   layers. As a showcase experiment, we prune layers by identifying the ones with highest similarity
525   and verify that this operation does not significantly compromise performance, yielding results com-
526   parable to state-of-the-art methods. Persistence similarity shows stability under models, datasets,
527   and hyperparameters changes suggesting a universal topological structure in LLM representations.
528
      There are several limitations in our study that future research could address. First, while our method
529
      shows robustness across hyperparameters within the framework, these choices need not be optimal.
530   Defining an appropriate criterion for connecting points in the representation space, and consequently,
531   a filtration, is a delicate task in TDA that could require further investigations to detail the impact of
532   the various choices on the construction of the filtration. The information content of persistence
533   similarity on internal representations and model behavior has not been investigated in detail (but see
534   a few experiments in Appendix D) and it certainly deserves further investigation. Lastly, our study
535   primarily focuses on static, pre-trained models. Extending this framework to track the evolution of
536   internal representations during training would require computational optimization of the algorithm
537   but could provide useful insights on model efficiency and behavior.

831   Zigzag persistence is a computational topology method that extends classical persistent homology to
832   handle more complex data structures and filtration processes. Unlike standard persistence, which an-
833   alyzes a single sequence of spaces filtered by inclusion, zigzag persistence allows for the exploration
834
      of data where sequences of spaces and maps can move both forward and backward.
835   A zigzag filtration of topological spaces is a sequence:
836
                                   χ : X1 ←→ X2 ←→ · · · ←→ Xn−1 ←→ Xn ,                                        (7)
837
838   where each Xi is a topological space and each arrow ←→ represents a continuous function pointing
839   forwards Xi −→ Xi+1 or backwards Xi ←− Xi+1 .
840   If we apply a homology functor Hp with coefficients in a field k to such a filtration, we get a zigzag
841   filtration of k-vector spaces, called zigzag module:
842
843                  Hp (χ) : Hp (X1 ) ←→ Hp (X2 ) ←→ · · · ←→ Hp (Xn−1 ) ←→ Hp (Xn ).                          (8)
844
845
      It is proven in (Carlsson & de Silva, 2010) that the algebraic classification of zigzag modules resem-
      bles Gabriel’s classification of the persistence module described in (Gabriel, 1972). In particular,
846
      every finite-dimensional zigzag module, i.e. for which all the k-vector spaces in the sequence that
847
      are finite-dimensional, can be decomposed as a direct sum of interval modules, where a (finitely
848   indexed) interval module is a module of the form:
849
850                                      I[b,d] : I1 ←→ I2 ←→ · · · ←→ In ,                                     (9)
851   where Ii = k for b ≤ i ≤ d, and Ii = 0 otherwise, and every arrow of the form k ←− k or k −→ k
852   is the identity map. Moreover, the list of summands is unique up to reordering.
853

854

      B        C OMBINING THE kNN GRAPH WITH THE V IETORIS -R IPS COMPLEX
919
920
921   The k-Nearest Neighbors (kNN ) complex is built by expanding the corresponding kNN graph to a
922
      fixed dimension m. A key limitation of the kNN complex is that it ranks points by proximity without
      considering their actual distances. As a result, once k is fixed on each layer, each point is connected
923
      to its k-Nearest Neighbors, regardless of the absolute distances involved. In our setting, the number
924
      of 0-cycles (the Betti 13 number β0 ) of the kNN complexes as a function of the layers tends to be
925   unity, i.e. the whole complex is connected, even for relatively small values of kNN ≳ 6. This implies
926   that 0-cycles contain no useful topological information on the internal representations.
927
928
      To address this issue, we follow the approach in (Naitzat et al., 2020), which combines the kNN
      complex with the Vietoris-Rips complex. Starting from the kNN graph, the idea is to introduce a
929
      threshold radius R on each layer and use it to filter out edges of the graph whose lengths are less
930
      than or equal to R, and then expand, denoting this new complex kNN -VR. This filtering step allows
931   us to focus on longer-range connections, uncovering significant topological features that may be
932   hidden by shorter, more local connections.
933
934
      To ensure consistency across layers, we select the radius R in each layer such that the number of
      0-cycles, β0 , of the kNN complex falls in a pre-determined range. We then compute the observables
935
      presented in this work and verify the results. For clarity, we refer to kNN complex the construction
936
      used in the main body, and kNN -VR complexes the one presented in this section. For the sake of
937   conciseness, we present only results for the average similarity S̄. In Figure 6 we show the average
938   similarity of 1-cycles of the kNN and the kNN -VR complexes and the 0-cycles of the kNN -VR com-
939   plexes computed by imposing β0 = 500 ± 100. 14 We observe all three curves are qualitatively
940   and quantitatively similar. This indicates that information about the similarity of 1-cycles remains
941   unchanged, even when removing a considerable amount of short edges. Moreover, we observe the
942   same information also on 0-cycles, now that we modified the complex such that their statistics are
943   large enough to reliably compute similarity. We argue this indicates a universal (in homology) ten-
944   dency to retain relational connections among particles in the middle-late layers of the model.
945
