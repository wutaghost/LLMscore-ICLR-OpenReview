009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Kernel ridge regression (KRR) and Gaussian processes (GPs) are fundamental
012            tools in statistics and machine learning, with recent applications to highly over-
013            parameterized deep neural networks. The ability of these tools to learn a target
014            function is directly related to the eigenvalues of their kernel sampled on the input
015            data. Targets that having support on higher eigenvalues are more learnable. While
016            kernels are often highly symmetric objects, the data is often not. Thus, kernel
017            symmetry seems to have little to no bearing on the above eigenvalues or learnability,
018
               making spectral analysis on real-world data challenging. Here, we show that con-
               trary to this common lure, one may use eigenvalues and eigenfunctions associated
019
               with highly idealized data measures to bound learnability on realistic data. As a
020
               demonstration, we give a theoretical lower bound on the sample complexity of
021            copying heads for kernels associated with generic transformers acting on natural
022            language.

025   1    I NTRODUCTION
026
027   Gaussian process regression (GPR) and kernel ridge regression (KRR) are workhorses of statistics
028   and machine learning. GPR and KRR are also intimately related - given the same kernel function,
029   they both result in the same predictor (Rasmussen & Williams, 2006; Kimeldorf & Wahba, 1970).
030   More recently, several correspondences have appeared between GPs and kernel methods with deep
031   neural networks (DNNs) have appeared (Lee et al., 2018; Jacot et al., 2018; Matthews et al., 2018;
032   Novak et al., 2018; Cohen et al., 2021). Perhaps the most striking result (Novak et al., 2018; Lee et al.,
033
      2020) is that GPs drawn from the kernels of CNNs exhibit quite an impressive performance, which
      does not fall much behind that of those CNNs. In addition, the infinite width/channel/attention-heads
034
      limit has served as a starting point for several extensions to finite DNNs in the feature-learning regime
035
      (Li & Sompolinsky, 2021; Seroussi et al., 2023; Ariosto et al., 2022). Thus, apart from the direct
036   interest in GPs and kernel methods, predicting what GPs and kernels can learn appears as a stepping
037   stone to predicting neural networks’ learnability.
038
039
      The complexity of computing with GPs or kernels and the analytical difficulty of reasoning about
      them both stem from the need to invert a large matrix associated with the kernel between each pair of
040
      data points (Rasmussen & Williams, 2006). Various theoretical studies indicate that the main objects
041
      controlling learnability are the eigenvalues and eigenfunctions associated with this matrix in the
042   infinite data limit (Silverman, 1984; Sollich & Williams, 2004; Canatar et al., 2021b; Bordelon et al.,
043   2021; Simon et al., 2023). In this eigen-learning framework, the regression target is decomposed to
044   eigenfunctions and the regression roughly filters out eigenfunctions with eigenvalues below σ 2 /P , P
045   being the amount of data and σ 2 the ridge parameter or an effective ridge parameter. Notwithstanding,
046   this approach still leaves us with the formidable task of diagonalizing the kernel on the data measure.
047   In addition to being a hard computational task, it also requires an accurate understanding of the
048   underlying data measure which, apart from specialized use cases such as physical-informed neural
049   networks (Raissi et al., 2019), is out of our grasp. Thus, while kernels show much promise as
050
      indicators of DNN performance, leveraging them to provide insights on how DNNs perform on
      real-world data remains a challenge.
051
052   Drawing inspiration from physics, symmetries can greatly simplify diagonalization processes, using
053   tools from representation theory. Conveniently, kernels associated with DNNs are often quite sym-
      metric. For instance, any kernel associated with a fully-connected-network (FCN) (resp. transformer)

054
      would have rotation (e.g. (Basri et al., 2019; Yang & Salman, 2020; Scetbon & Harchaoui, 2021;
055   Bietti & Bach, 2021)) (resp. permutation (Lavie et al., 2024)) symmetry in input space. Thus,
056   if acting on similarly symmetric data, representation theory arguments can be directly applied to
057   diagonalize/block-diagonalize kernels (Fulton & Harris, 2004; Tung, 1985). As an example, for data
058   uniform on the hypersphere, any FCN kernel is diagonalized by hyper-spherical harmonics. Fur-
059   thermore, the finite sum rule on eigenvalues in conjugation with degeneracies implied by symmetry
060   further forces strict upper bounds on the eigenvalues (Cohen et al., 2021). Unfortunately, real-world
061   data is rarely uniform or symmetric. Consequently, the eigenvalue problem, involving both the kernel
062   and the data measure, loses its symmetry properties and kernel-symmetry alone does not help one
063
      obtain the kernel spectrum required for estimating learnability.
064   In this work, we provide a readily computable lower bound on the number of samples needed to learn
065   idealized target features on real-world datasets using Gaussian processes. Central to our bound is
066   the use of an auxiliary test distribution (q(x)), invariant under symmetries of the kernel, to measure
067   learnability. We show that such q(x)-learnability (or cross-dataset learnability) can be bounded from
068   above without ever solving the difficult kernel eigenvalue problem on the real data. Instead, our
069
      bound depends on two relatively accessible quantities: (1) The eigenfunctions and eigenvalues of the
      kernel on q(x) and (2) The norm of the eigenfunctions associated with the target on the real dataset
070
      (typically an O(1) number). As we demonstrate, since the eigenvalue problem w.r.t. q(x) enjoys all
071
      kernel symmetries, it is largely tractable using representation theory tools. We further find that our
072   q(x)-based measure of learnability correlates well with that on the training set.
073
074   Our main contributions are:
075              • We generalize the definition of learnability to a scenario where the training is done on one
076                dataset, but the learnability is tested on another (cross-dataset learnability). We provide
077                numerical evidence that this, more tractable, learnability correlates well with the standard
078                one.
079              • We prove a sample complexity lower bound for kernel ridge regression and Gaussian process
080                regression on real-world datasets based on our cross-dataset learnability and show it gives
081                prediction experimentally.
082              • We study several examples of relevant architecture, including transformers, discuss their
083                symmetries, and use representation theory and the above bound to derive concrete sample
084                complexity lower bounds.
085              • Our bound is a prescription to carry over sample complexity results found in the literature
086                that builds upon idealized datasets (Basri et al., 2019; Lavie et al., 2024; Bietti & Bach,
087                2021; Azevedo & Menegatto, 2014; Scetbon & Harchaoui, 2021; Yang & Salman, 2020) to
088
                   real-world datasets in the form of lower bounds.
089   The paper is organized as follows. The setting and assumptions are presented in Section 2, followed by
090   a statement of the main result in Section 3 together with experimental results that show its usefulness
091   on real-world datasets. We then move on to a series of vignettes, each one presents a scenario where
092   the bound is used and discusses certain aspects of the result. The first vignette 4.1 uses a linear
093   regression setting with Gaussian i.i.d. data to showcase a simple implementation of the result and
094   discuss how the details of the specific training distribution are accounted for in the bound. The second
095   vignette 4.2 examines a fully connected network that learns parity on the hyper-cube with a correlated
096
      measure and discusses measuring learnability out-of-distribution. The last vignette 4.3 presents
      a practical result, bounding from below the sample complexity of copying heads for transformer
097
      architecture. Finally, we provide an outlook in Section 5.

327   for the case of fully connected networks (Basri et al., 2019; Yang & Salman, 2020; Scetbon &

334
      workflow:
335

336

338
               training dataset in its support.
339         3. Decompose the target function into irreducible representations of the symmetry group.
340
            4. Use the inverse of the dimension of the irrep8 as an upper bound for the corresponding
341            eigenvalue.

      4.2   M EASURES OF L EARNABILITY & L EARNING PARITY WITH A C ORRELATED DATASET
394
395   We continue by recovering a known result - learning parity from a uniform distribution on the
396   hyper-cube with an FCN-GP is hard (Yang & Salman, 2020; Simon et al., 2023) and extend it to
397   general distributions on the hyper-cube. We then present an example where the data measure has
398   very low entropy and probes only a small low dimensional space of the hyper-cube. In that example,
399   a function that mostly agrees with parity on the data can be learned easily, but learning a predictor
400
      that generalizes out-of-distribution remains hard. We discuss what learning a function can mean
      when considering different distributions, potentially including out-of-distribution test points, and
401
      suggest maximal entropy distributions as a reasonable measure upon which learnability can be gauged.
402
      Finally, we compare the suggested measure of learnability to a familiar one.
403
404   Consider learning parity on the ⃗x ∈ {−1, 1}d hyper-cube using FCNs. Here ⃗x is drawn from an
405   arbitrary, possibly correlated, measure on the hyper-cube, and the target function is parity y = Πdi=1 xi
406
      with no added noise.
407   Learning parity with noise is believed to require P scaling exponentially with d (Blum et al., 2000).
408   Parity without noise can be learned with O(d) samples using Gaussian elimination and relations
409   between boolean operations and algebra in Z2 fields (Blum et al., 2000). A GP, however, involves
410   a larger hypothesis class including real rather than boolean variables. It also seems highly unlikely
411   that it could learn from examples an O(d3 )-algorithm such as Gaussian elimination. In the case of a
412
      uniform measure, an FCN GP is known to require P ∗ which is exponential in d (Simon et al., 2023)
      to learn parity. It is reasonable to assume that a generic non-uniform measure would not reduce the
413
      complexity of this task, however, we are not aware of any existing bounds applicable to this broader
414
      case.
415
416   To this end, we take as an ideal distribution q(x) a uniform distribution on the sphere containing
417   the corners of the hyper-cube. We turn to calculate or bound the different elements in Eq. 8.
418

513   5    O UTLOOK
514
515   In this work, we have shown it is possible to lower bound the sample complexity for kernel ridge
516   regression and Gaussian process regression on a general training dataset based on its symmetric
517   counterpart. Our bound allows exploiting the full symmetries of the kernel, even ones that are not
518   present in the training dataset and thus, naively, cannot be used. The enhanced symmetry of an
519
      eigenvalue problem on an auxiliary dataset that possesses all the symmetries of the kernel can greatly
      simplify its solution.
520
521   Kernel ridge regression and Gaussian process regression are not only well-motivated and studied
522   frameworks but are also used in the study of neural networks through the NTK and NNGP correspon-
523   dences (Jacot et al., 2018; Lee et al., 2018). These correspondences have been used to characterize
524   the inductive bias of different architectures such as fully connected networks (Basri et al., 2019;
525
      Yang & Salman, 2020; Scetbon & Harchaoui, 2021; Bietti & Bach, 2021), CNNs (Bietti, 2021; Xiao,
      2022; Cagnetta et al., 2023) and transformers (Lavie et al., 2024) under symmetry assumptions for
526
      the dataset. Our work allows to generalize these results to datasets that lack these symmetries. We
527
      further presented three examples for real-life usage of the bound: (1) a simple application of the
528   bound for linear regression, (2) the hardness of learning parity on a general (correlated) dataset with
529   an FCN, and (3) an application of the bound to copying heads, a stepping stone for ICL.
530
531
      Much of the possibilities opened by our approach remain unexplored, like tightly bounding the sample
      complexity in cases where the target is very multi-spectral, which we elaborate on in Appendix C.
532
      Another intriguing aspect is the application of such bound to ridgeless regression. When analyzing
533
      the true dataset DP , it has been shown there is an effective ridge coming from the unlearnable
534   features (Cohen et al., 2021; Canatar et al., 2021b; Simon et al., 2023). It would be of great interest
535   to find a similar effective ridge for our setting, using the eigenvalues and vectors from the symmetric
536   distribution q(x), without having to solve the eigenvalue problem on DP . Finally, while our bound
537   can be used to characterize what targets will not be learnable, a guarantee on the tightness of the
538   bound can extend its applicability even further.

      B     F URTHER R ELATED W ORKS
801
      Symmetry in neural networks & sample complexity. Symmetry has been used extensively to understand
802
      neural networks, especially from the perspective of sample complexity and the kernel regime. Fully
803
      connected networks with data uniformly distributed on a hypersphere have been studied in Basri
804   et al. (2019); Bietti & Bach (2021); Scetbon & Harchaoui (2021). Data distributed non-uniformly on
805   the hypersphere was studied in Basri et al. (2020), while Gaussian data and data uniformly distributed
806   on the hypercube were studied in Yang & Salman (2020). This line of study was extended beyond the
807   natural rotation symmetry, Bietti et al. (2021) studied how extending the symmetry group beyond
808   the rotational symmetry of fully connected networks reduces the sample complexity a result that has
809   been generalized further by Tahmasebi & Jegelka (2023). The eigenspectra of kernels corresponding
      to convolutional neural networks were studied in Bietti (2021); Xiao (2022); Cagnetta et al. (2023);


                                                              15
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
      Geifman et al. (2022). Finally, Lavie et al. (2024) studied transformers in the kernel regime by
811   leveraging their permutation symmetry. As a future research direction, it can interesting to try and
812   use this rich literature on distributional shift for the purpose proposed in this paper.
813
814
      Learning & multiple data distributions. There is a large body of work on distributional shift and out-
      of-distribution generalization Pan & Yang (2010); Ben-David et al. (2010); Sugiyama & Kawanabe
815
      (2012); Arjovsky (2021); Canatar et al. (2021a); Ma et al. (2023), however, this setting and its
816
      motivation are different from ours. In the study of distributional shifts, one assumes the training
817   distribution does not reflect accurately the test distribution and tries to estimate (bound, guarantee) the
818   performance on the test distribution, which is the true object of interest. This concept fundamentally
819   differs from the setting in this work. Here we do not assume any difference between test and train
820   distribution, rather, we are interested in the performance on the dataset DP and use q, the auxiliary
821   measure, solely as a tool. Presenting q allows us to capitalize on all the results mentioned in the
822   previous paragraph about symmetry, even when the dataset DP does not respect those symmetries.
823   We are aware of one previous work Opper & Vivarelli (1998) that used a similar motif to upper
824   bound the learnability, however, here we lower bound it. Moreover, Opper & Vivarelli (1998) did not
825
      suggest a specific dataset that can be used to simplify the problem, and does not mention symmetries.
