                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011
012            When optimizing machine learning models, there are various scenarios where gra-
013            dient computations are challenging or even infeasible. Furthermore, in reinforce-
014            ment learning (RL), preference-based RL that only compares between options has
015            wide applications, including reinforcement learning with human feedback in large
016
               language models. In this paper, we systematically study optimization of a smooth
               function f : Rn ‚Üí R only assuming an oracle that compares function values at
017
               two points and tells which is larger. When f is convex, we give two algorithms
018
               using OŐÉ(n/ŌĶ) and OŐÉ(n2 ) comparison queries to find an ŌĶ-optimal solution, respec-
019
               tively. When f is nonconvex, our algorithm uses OŐÉ(n/ŌĶ2 ) comparison queries to
020
               find an ŌĶ-approximate stationary point. All these results match the best-known
021
               zeroth-order algorithms with function evaluation queries in n dependence, thus
022            suggesting that comparisons are all you need for optimizing smooth functions us-
023            ing derivative-free methods. In addition, we also give an algorithm for escaping
024            saddle points and reaching an ŌĶ-second order stationary point of a nonconvex f ,
025            using OŐÉ(n1.5 /ŌĶ2.5 ) comparison queries.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031
      Optimization is pivotal in the realm of machine learning. For instance, advancements in stochas-
032
      tic gradient descent (SGD) such as ADAM (Kingma & Ba, 2015), Adagrad (Duchi et al., 2011),
033
      etc., serve as foundational methods for the training of deep neural networks. However, there exist
034   scenarios where gradient computations are challenging or even infeasible, such as black-box adver-
035   sarial attack on neural networks (Papernot et al., 2017; Madry et al., 2018; Chen et al., 2017) and
036   policy search in reinforcement learning (Salimans et al., 2017; Choromanski et al., 2018). Conse-
037   quently, zeroth-order optimization methods with function evaluations have gained prominence, with
038   provable guarantee for convex optimization (Duchi et al., 2015; Nesterov & Spokoiny, 2017) and
039   nonconvex optimization (Ghadimi & Lan, 2013; Fang et al., 2018; Jin et al., 2018a; Ji et al., 2019;
040   Zhang et al., 2022; Vlatakis-Gkaragkounis et al., 2019; Balasubramanian & Ghadimi, 2022).
041   Furthermore, optimization for machine learning has been recently soliciting for even less informa-
042   tion. For instance, it is known that taking only signs of gradient descents still enjoy good perfor-
043   mance (Liu et al., 2019; Li et al., 2023; Bernstein et al., 2018). Moreover, in the breakthrough of
044   large language models (LLMs), reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) played an
045   important rule in training these LLMs, especially GPTs by OpenAI (Ouyang et al., 2022). Com-
046   pared to standard RL that applies function evaluation for rewards, RLHF is preference-based RL
047   that only compares between options and tells which is better. There is emerging research interest
048
      in preference-based RL, where various works have established provable guarantees for learning a
      near-optimal policy from preference feedback (Chen et al., 2022; Saha et al., 2023; Novoseller et al.,
049
      2020; Xu et al., 2020; Zhu et al., 2023; Tang et al., 2023). Furthermore, Wang et al. (2023) proved
050
      that for a wide range of preference models, preference-based RL can be solved with small or no
051   extra costs compared to those of standard reward-based RL.
052

079   Intuitively, our results can be described as comparisons are all you need for derivative-free meth-
080
      ods: For finding an approximate minimum of a convex function, the state-of-the-art
                                                                              ‚ąö               zeroth-order
      methods with full function evaluations have query complexities O(n/ ŌĶ) (Nesterov & Spokoiny,
081
      2017) or OŐÉ(n2 ) (Lee et al., 2018), which are matched in n by our Theorem 2 and Theorem 3 using
082
      comparisons, respectively. For finding an approximate stationary point of a nonconvex function,
083
      the state-of-the-art zeroth-order result has query complexity O(n/ŌĶ2 ) (Fang et al., 2018), which is
084   matched by our Theorem 4 up to a logarithmic factor. In other words, in derivative-free scenarios for
085   optimizing smooth functions, function values per se are unimportant but their comparisons, which
086   indicate the direction that the function decreases.
087
      Among the literature for derivative-free optimization methods (Larson et al., 2019), direct search
088
      methods by Kolda et al. (2003) proceed by comparing function values, including the directional di-
089
      rect search method (Audet & Dennis Jr, 2006) and the Nelder-Mead method (Nelder & Mead, 1965)
090   as examples. However, the directional direct search method does not have a known rate of conver-
091   gence, meanwhile the Nelson-Mead method may fail to converge to a stationary point for smooth
092   functions (Dennis & Torczon, 1991). As far as we know, the most relevant result is by Bergou et al.
093   (2020), which proposed the stochastic three points (STP) method and found an ŌĶ-optimal point of
094   a convex function and an ŌĶ-FOSP of a nonconvex function in OŐÉ(n/ŌĶ) and OŐÉ(n/ŌĶ2 ) comparisons,
095   respectively. STP also has a version with momentum (Gorbunov et al., 2020). Our Theorem 2
096   and Theorem 4 can be seen as rediscoveries of these results using different methods. In addition,
097   literature on dueling convex optimization also achieves OŐÉ(n/ŌĶ) for finding an ŌĶ-optimal point of
098   a convex function (Saha et al., 2021; 2022). However, for comparison-based convex optimization
099   with poly(log 1/ŌĶ) dependence, Jamieson et al. (2012) achieved this for strongly convex functions,
100   and the state-of-the-art result for general convex optimization by Karabag et al. (2021) takes OŐÉ(n4 )
101   comparison queries. Their algorithm applies the ellipsoid method, which has OŐÉ(n2 ) iterations and
102   each iteration takes OŐÉ(n2 ) comparisons to construct the ellipsoid. This OŐÉ(n4 ) bound is noticeably
103   worse than our Theorem 3. As far as we know, our Theorem 5 is the first provable guarantee for
104   finding an ŌĶ-SOSP of a nonconvex function by comparisons.
