010
011
               We identify a loss plateau at the start of training in the three synthetic settings of
012            in-context linear regression, sparse parity, and fact memorization. While careful
013            tweaks to the optimization algorithm can mitigate these plateaus, we find that a
014            simpler orthogonal approach of lowering the data diversity, and in doing so, bi-
015            asing the training distribution away from the test distribution, counter-intuitively
016            also speeds up training. This connection between data diversity and training speed
017            holds for three different diversity-reducing interventions across our varied syn-
018            thetic settings. Our findings offer a new perspective on data filtering and curricu-
019            lum learning for training machine learning models.

022   1    I NTRODUCTION
023
024   How do we train neural networks faster, or avoid periods of slow progress during learning? The
025   canonical answer points to extensive work on optimization, aiming to accelerate training via effi-
026   cient optimizers for deep neural networks and theory for both convex and non-convex settings. In
027   this work, we demonstrate that in a variety of settings, learning plateaus can be traced to issues with
028   optimization. Our key finding is that this same effect of mitigating loss plateaus by better optimiza-
029   tion can be achieved via a family of interventions on the data distribution that are simple and even
030   naive, yet powerful.
031   We consider three synthetic tasks – in-context linear regression, sparse parity, and fact memorization
032   – each marked by an initial phase of slow or plateaued learning, followed by abrupt learning to low
033   loss. We begin by demonstrating that these plateaus can be avoided with suitable interventions
034   on optimization: by biasing or removing interference between batch gradients we can mitigate the
035   initial phase of slow learning.
036   The fact that tweaking the optimization algorithm suffices to mitigate these plateaus suggests it
037   may be possible to develop more sophisticated optimization schemes that seamlessly handle such
038   plateaus out-of-the-box. We take an orthogonal approach: we focus on the data being optimized
039   over. We demonstrate that extremely simple yet astonishingly counter-intuitive data interventions
040   can have a similar effect to complicated and subtle interventions on the optimization process.
041
      While much work on the related topic of data filtering aims to find heuristics for high-quality data
042   points that represent particularly salient or meaningful parts of the test distribution, our work is
043   different in that we draw data randomly in our interventions, from distributions that are often further
044   from the test distribution.
045
      This is best illustrated with an example from our first synthetic setting: in-context learning of linear
046
      regression, where a Transformer is trained to take in sequences [x1 , wx1 , · · · , xk , wxk , xquery ] for
047
      random xi , infer the latent task vector w, and output a predicted regression estimate ŷquery = wxquery .
048   The task vectors w are drawn from a specific distribution, and our goal is to learn the in-context ridge
049   solution, which is the correct solution for Gaussian w. When training on this test distribution for w,
050   the model eventually learns the optimal solution after an initial learning plateau. Figure 1 illustrates
051   how biasing the training distribution by reducing data diversity (the number of task vectors we train
052   with) can mitigate the learning plateau altogether. There is no free lunch, of course, as learning the
053   optimal solution to the biased training distribution eventually does poorly on the test distribution of
      interest.

181   3           L OSS PLATEAUS AND HOW TO MITIGATE THEM VIA OPTIMIZATION
182
183   Many synthetic settings are affected by loss plateaus, where the model makes little to no progress on
184   the loss and then has a phase of abrupt learning even when the train and test distribution are same.
185   We find that in our settings, these plateaus are not intrinsic, and we can intervene on the gradients
186   in the optimization procedure to bypass them and learn quickly. Our interventions are motivated by
187
      the intuition that interference is causing slow learning, where gradients from different data points
      and batches have low similarity and high variance, so the model makes overall slow progress. This
188
      hypothesis is difficult to check in all our settings due to the pitfalls of measuring variance when
189
      using adaptive optimizers, but we effectively use this intuition to speed up training.

421
      we increase the correlation between tasks, and this induces a similar effect (Figure 6). This particular
422
      task structure is not present in the other settings, so we only apply it to in-context linear regression.
423
424
      4.4          S UMMARY OF DATA I NTERVENTIONS
425
426   Across our data interventions, we observe that there is an “optimal” amount of biasing where initial
427   learning is sped up relative to the baseline. Above this amount, the biasing will bring us too far
428   from our original distribution such that the model simply fits the biased data instead of approaching
429   the generalizing solution. Below this amount of biasing, we approach normal training without the
430   intervention. This highlights a more general pattern across our interventions: lowering data diver-
431   sity trades off correctly specifying the objective for faster early progress, inducing a speedup in an
      intermediate regime where the model can do both.

      5     R ELATED W ORK
433
434
      5.1   DATA S ELECTION AND C URRICULUM L EARNING
435
436   A number of prior works study the benefits of curating or ordering the examples seen during training.
437   Many prior works on data selection aim to identify a subset of high quality data by quantifying the
438   contribution of individual data points, such as influence functions (Koh & Liang, 2020), datamodels
439   (Ilyas et al., 2022; Engstrom et al., 2024), distance to margin (Sorscher et al., 2023), gradient metrics
440   (Paul et al., 2023), data quality filters (Brown et al., 2020; Gadre et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024; Maini
441   et al., 2024), and reweighting domains (Xie et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023; Thrush et al., 2024). An-
442
      other line of work instead intervenes on the order that data points are seen during training, typically
      in increasing order of difficulty. Hacohen & Weinshall (2019) obtains example difficulty scores
443
      from a pretrained model when training on image classification while Tay et al. (2019) uses example
444
      length as a proxy and applies curriculum learning in a reading comprehension task. (Wu et al., 2021)
445   performs a meta-analysis finding that curricula are rarely helpful for training on real data. Pentina
446   et al. (2014) examines curriculum learning in a multi-task setting by identifying shared information
447   between tasks. Similarly, Zhang et al. (2020) uses a value function to identify tasks at the frontier
448   of the policy’s ability. In contrast to these settings, our data interventions improve training without
449   discriminating between datapoints whatsoever.
450
451   5.2   S YNTHETIC S ETTINGS AND A BRUPT L EARNING
452
453   Though we are the first to formalize this phenomenon, a number of prior synthetic settings show
454   hints of lower diversity benefiting training. For example, related effects have been observed in
455
      meta-learning MNIST with a biased distribution (Kirsch et al. (2022), Figure 8), in-context learning
      digit recognition with a Zipf distribution (Chan et al. (2022), Figure 6c), in-context linear regression
456
      with subsampled tasks (Kotha et al., 2024; Raventós et al., 2023), and grokking modular arithmetic
457
      (He et al. (2024), Figure 3b). Reddy (2023) make a similar finding on the emergence of induction
458   heads. Although these works also find that long-tailed data distributions induce in-context learning,
459   they do not consider the impact of data on training speed, as we do. Moreover, we study and connect
460   the impact of the training distribution on settings qualitatively different from in-context learning.
461
462   5.3   O PTIMIZATION I NTERVENTIONS
463
464   Several works have focused on accelerating optimization for training. McCandlish et al. (2018)
465   studies the impact of batch size, identifying that the signal to noise-ratio of gradients controls the
466   appropriate choice of batch size. Jastrzebski et al. (2020); Faghri et al. (2020) examine the role of
467   learning rate, finding that smaller learning rates can contribute to poor conditioning and higher levels
468
      of gradient noise. Singh et al. (2023); Raventós et al. (2023) examine the role of ℓ2 regularization
      specifically for in-context learning problems and find that the presence of regularization can induce
469
      in-context learning capabilities, even in the presence of lower task diversity or larger model capac-
470
      ity. Another related line of work has studied optimization challenges in multi-task learning. Schaul
471   et al. (2019) finds that coupling between a policy and the data-generation process in reinforcement
472   learning can lead to plateaus. Yu et al. (2020) observes that interference between gradients in multi-
473   task learning can inhibit training and proposes a optimization intervention to correct this. Fu et al.
474   (2024) aims to break loss plateaus by intervening on how optimization affects parameters. Collec-
475   tively, these works demonstrate the significant role optimization parameters play in enabling efficient
476   training. In this work, however, we showcase a simple intervention on the data with similar effects
477   to some of these interventions in the settings we study.
478
479   6     D ISCUSSION
480
481   In this work, we present a counter-intuitive finding: lowering the diversity of the training data by
482   simple random subsampling can accelerate training for a general class of settings. Learning in these
483   settings can also be sped up with a single optimization intervention, targeted at reducing interference.
484   These results position data diversity as a rich way of understanding and controlling the optimization
485   landscape without extra information about the individual samples of the distribution or complicated
      optimization interventions.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      Our initial work here suggests there may be interesting connections between the data distribution
487   and the optimization landscape of the model, due to the fact that intervening on them allows us to
488   get the same speedup. Moreover, we observe that decreasing the task count in the in-context linear
489   regression setting decreases gradient interference (Figure 8), which we measure by looking at the
490   similarity of the model gradient on random, distinct batches. This supports the idea that these two
491   levers are acting on some shared mechanism of interference. Exploring this connection in future
492   work may enrich our understanding of training dynamics, abrupt learning, and the finegrained role
493   of data distributional properties in training.
494
495
             • Diversity-reducing interventions work for real data: If reducing diversity can improve
               training speed for real data, this would have exciting implications for many of the training
496
               runs we do today. For example, curriculum learning (Bengio et al., 2009) has faced little
497
               success for standard supervised learning (Wu et al., 2021). However, standard curricula
498            typically use a heuristic notion of difficulty. The perspective of dataset diversity introduced
499            in this work can inspire new, curriculum-like interventions going beyond these heuristics.
500
             • Diversity-reducing interventions do not work for real data: It is a real possibility that
501
               the phenomena observed in this work does not transfer to real-world settings. This moti-
502            vates a follow-up question: What property of real-world data makes it different from our
503            synthetic tasks? It is possible that the correlations in real task distributions have a differ-
504            ent structure, or that they already exhibit a power-law data distribution? For example, it
505            is widely hypothesized that the frequency with which facts are observed during language
506            model pretraining follows a power law distribution (Kandpal et al., 2023; Mallen et al.,
507            2023). Similarly, prior work on in-context learning has hypothesized the role of the power-
508            law distribution in its formation (Chan et al., 2022). Understanding the properties that
509            diversity controls is an important direction for future work.
510
      We hope that our observations can inspire future work in fundamentally understanding our synthetic
511
      settings and investigating the potential (or lack thereof) for data-driven training speedups.
512
513
514   7   L IMITATIONS
515
516   Our experiments are purely observational and although we provide some initial speculation and
517   results, we do not claim to have strong understanding of the underlying phenomena resulting in
518   these speedups. For example, we do not provide theoretical results concerning the nature of the
519
      speedup, but hope that this inspires interesting future work studying this phenomenon. Furthermore,
      we limit our analysis to three synthetic setups and it may not generalize to other machine learning
520
      problems. This limits the applicability of these findings, though there is a strong possibility these
521
      insights can inspire data-driven interventions in domains outside of these settings. Furthermore,
522   though our experiments are consistent across settings, the conducted experiments can be broader
523   across hyperparameters of the training algorithm, problem setting, and intervention. We hope that
524   these initial results are taken as inspiration for understanding machine learning algorithms instead
525   of ready-to-apply methods for today.

856   C.3      S TAIRCASE INTERVENTION FOR SPARSE PARITY
857
858   Taking inspiration from the work of (Abbe et al.,   P2021), we hypothesize that biasing the training
859   distribution towards the staircase function Sk = j χj while downweighting the coefficients of all
860   monomials χj by a factor ϵ allows us to “climb the staircase” to learn the monomial faster, while
861   incurring only P a small penalty for learning lower-order coefficients with power ϵ. Formally, we
862   train on χk + j<k ϵχj , and find there exists a range of ϵ > 0 such that this reaches low loss
863   faster than training on just χk , with learning curves/speedups qualitatively resembling those of other
      interventions.
