009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            The rise of deepfake technology has made everyone vulnerable to false claims
012            based on manipulated media. While many existing deepfake detection methods aim
013            to identify fake media, they often struggle with deepfakes created by new generative
014            models not seen during training. In this paper, we propose VeriFake, a method
015            that enables users to verify that media claiming to show them are false. VeriFake
016            is based on two key assumptions: (i) generative models struggle to exactly depict
017            a specific identity, and (ii) they often fail to perfectly synchronize generated lip
018            movements with speech. By combining these assumptions with powerful modern
019
               representation encoders, VeriFake achieves highly effective results, even against
               previously unseen deepfakes. Through extensive experiments, we demonstrate that
020
               VeriFake significantly outperforms general-purpose deepfake detection techniques
021
               despite being simple to implement and not relying on any fake data for pretraining.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
026
027
      Deepfakes, or maliciously manipulated media, have become a major threat to social stability. They are
      often used by bad actors to spread false information, increase social division, cause embarrassment,
028
      and violate privacy. As deep generative models improve, creating convincing deepfakes has become
029
      easier and faster, making it urgent to develop automatic detection methods. Despite significant
030   progress in the machine learning community, current detection methods struggle, especially in
031   identifying new types of deepfake attacks that exploit unknown vulnerabilities.
032
033
      The main limitation of existing approaches is their reliance on supervised classifiers trained to detect
      deepfakes similar to those previously seen. This makes them less effective against new, previously
034
      unseen types of deepfake attacks. With the rapid advancements in generative models, which constantly
035
      introduce novel forms of media manipulation, this issue will become more severe. There is a pressing
036   need for new methods that can bridge this generalization gap and enable users to falsify deepfakes.
037
038   Empirical studies show that celebrities and politicians are often the main targets of deepfake attacks.
039
      A recent study (Marchal et al., 2024) analyzing AI misuse incidents between January 2023 and March
      2024 revealed that impersonating public figures accounts for 27% of malicious AI use. Another
040
      worrying trend is the creation of non-consensual deepfake pornography, as seen in the January 2024
041
      incident involving AI-generated explicit images of Taylor Swift that quickly spread on social media.
042   In the business world, deepfake scams targeting companies have become a significant threat, with
043   attackers impersonating executives or employees to manipulate company operations. A recent report
044   (ISMS.online, 2024) ranked deepfake impersonations as the second most frequent cybersecurity
045   incident experienced by businesses in the last 12 months, with 30% of U.S. businesses reporting
046   deepfake-related incidents during this period.
047
      In this paper, we propose an alternative solution to tackle deepfake detection by enabling users to
048   prove that media claiming to show them is false. Our method is based on two assumptions about
049   current generative models: (i) they cannot exactly depict the impersonated identity (Chefer et al.,
050   2023); and (ii) they fail to perfectly sync generated lip movements with speech (Haliassos et al.,
051   2021). We show how coupling these two assumptions with powerful, modern representation encoders
052   is highly effective, even against previously unseen deepfake attacks. We focus on two important
053   scenarios, illustrated in Fig. 1: (i) face manipulation attacks, where the identity in a video is changed
      to that of the impersonated person, and we are given user guidance about the attacked identity; and

072   We present VeriFake, a deepfake verification method, illustrated in Fig. 2. VeriFake dismantles face
073   manipulation attacks by verifying whether media truly depict the user. VeriFake requires the user to
074   provide a few images of their face (even a single photo works well) and then verifies if the observed
075
      media match the user’s face. It calculates a “truth score” (similarity function) between the media and
      the user’s provided images, using off-the-shelf features pretrained on real data. A low truth score
076
      suggests the media are fake. For example, given real images of Obama, we can detect deepfake
077
      images of him by verifying his facial identity. For multi-modal video data, we further extend VeriFake
078   to address audio-visual synthesis attacks by looking for minor flaws in speech synchronization in
079   manipulated videos of speaking persons.

      superiority of our approach across many competitive benchmarks. Our main contributions are:

084
               effectiveness in critical face swapping attack scenarios.

090   2    R ELATED WORK
091
092   Image synthesis. Fake images are created either by manipulating parts of existing images or by
093
      generating them from scratch. Examples of the former include techniques that modify attributes
      in a source image or those that replace the original face in an image or video with a target face
094
      (Korshunova et al., 2017; Bao et al., 2018; Perov et al., 2020; Nirkin et al., 2019). The other class of
095
      methods, however, involves generating all pixels from scratch, whether from random noise (Karras
096   et al., 2019) or text prompts (Rombach et al., 2022; Ramesh et al., 2022; Saharia et al., 2022).
097
098   General deepfake detection. As deepfake technology advances, significant efforts have been devoted
099
      to identifying manipulated media. Traditional approaches focus on examining image statistics changes,
      detecting cues such as compression artifacts (Agarwal & Farid, 2017). Learning-based methods have
100
      also been employed, with an initial emphasis on whether classifiers could effectively distinguish
101
      images from the same generative model (Wang et al., 2019; Frank et al., 2020; Rössler et al., 2019).
102   Recent studies (Wang et al., 2020; Chai et al., 2020) shift towards classifiers capable of generalizing
103   to different generative models, demonstrating the efficacy of neural networks trained on real and fake
104   images from one GAN model for detecting images from other GAN models. However, (Ojha et al.,
105   2023) emphasizes the non-generalizability of neural networks to unknown families of generative
106   models when trained for fake image detection. UFD (Ojha et al., 2023) leverage CLIP’s pretrained
107   feature space by performing linear probing on CLIP’s image representations. While UFD also uses
      pre-trained representations, our approach has significant differences in that UFD relies on fake data

108
      to differentiate between real and fake media. In contrast, our method does not use fake data during
109   training, yet achieves better performance as demonstrated in Tab. 1 and Tab. 2.
110
111   Face forgery detection. Early approaches relied on supervised learning to transform cropped face
112
      images into feature vectors for binary classification (Dang et al., 2020; Nguyen et al., 2019; Rössler
      et al., 2019). However, it became evident that relying solely on classification methods had limitations,
113
      often leading to overfitting of training data and potentially missing subtle distinctions between real
114
      and fake images. Incorporating frequency information proved invaluable for face forgery detection,
115   enabling the identification of specific artifacts associated with manipulation (Frank et al., 2020; Li
116   et al., 2021; Qian et al., 2020; Luo et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2021a). However, it is noteworthy that these
117   cues can sometimes be overcome by techniques such as artifact removal or slight alterations to model
118   architectures. Recent research efforts have increasingly prioritized the improvement of generalization
119   in forgery detection models, recognizing the significance of detecting previously unseen forgeries
120   (Cao et al., 2022; Sun et al., 2022; Zhuang et al., 2022). Huang et al. (2023) uses face identity and
121   face recognition features for supervised training of deepfake detectors. In contrast, our approach does
122   not need training, and therefore enjoys better generalization.
123   Audio-visual (AV) deepfake detection. In the domain of identifying manipulated speech videos,
124   prior research focused on exploiting audio-visual inconsistencies as a crucial cue. Many approaches,
125   rooted in supervised learning, have been devised to directly train audio-visual networks, enabling them
126   to discern video authenticity (Chugh et al., 2020; Mittal et al., 2020). Recently, attention has shifted
127   towards audio-visual self-supervision as a pretraining strategy. This entails self-supervised training,
128   followed by fine-tuning with real/fake labels (Grill et al., 2020; Haliassos et al., 2022). Some methods
129
      incorporate lip-reading data for this purpose (Haliassos et al., 2021), while others implicitly integrate
      it into audio-visual synchronization signals (Zhou & Lim, 2021). Feng et al. (2023) proposed AVAD,
130
      probably the most related method, which adapts ideas from anomaly detection for AV deepfakes, and
131
      does not use fake data for training. The method requires training a multimodal transformer using
132   multi-objective terms. Our method achieves higher accuracy while being far simpler, using only
133   off-the-shelf feature extractors and not requiring training.

136   3     FACE SWAPPING ATTACKS
137
138   Face forgery detection involves identifying instances of manipulated facial features. This task has
139   significant real-world implications, as many deepfakes involve human faces (Marchal et al., 2024;
140   ISMS.online, 2024). Face swapping replaces the original face in an image or video with the targeted
141   identity, aiming to generate a fake face indistinguishable from a real one to the human eye.
142
143
      3.1   V ERI FAKE : A PRACTICAL METHOD FOR DEEPFAKE VERIFICATION
144
145
      We propose VeriFake, a practical method for deepfake verification in face swapping attacks. VeriFake
146
      detects these attacks by verifying whether the observed media truly depict the user. The method
147   requires the user to provide a few images (as few as one) of their face. VeriFake then quantifies the
148   correspondence between the user’s facial identity and the identity observed in the image using an
149   off-the-shelf face recognition model. Our base assumption is that current generative methods fail to
150   perfectly transfer the user’s identity to the fake image. We thus distinguish between real and fake
151   images by verifying that the user’s facial identity matches that of the observed image.
152
      VeriFake takes as input a test face image x and the user’s provided reference set of their facial identity
153   R. We use a face recognition model, denoted by ϕid (.), to compute facial features (we use Wang
154   et al. (2017)). We then measure the similarity between the test image x and each image within our
155   reference set using cosine similarity over ϕid (.) features. The truth score s(x) of the image x is the
156   similarity to the nearest face in the reference set. Low truth scores indicate that the image is fake.
157   Formally, the truth score is given by:

260
      videos were uniformly subsampled to 32 frames in our experiments (see App. A.1).
261   Distribution shifts in reference set. The reference set in the empirical evaluation uses other videos of

293   4.1   E XTENDING V ERI FAKE
294
295   Audio-visual (AV) deepfakes manipulate either video to match a given audio track, audio to match a
296
      video, or both simultaneously. While many of these AV deepfakes can be addressed using VeriFake
      with identity information (as discussed in Sec. 3), this section focuses on AV scenarios where such
297
      information is unavailable or irrelevant: (i) the media show the user’s identity, but the speech content
298
      and lip movements are manipulated (the identity is not changed); (ii) the user cannot provide reference
299   images of their face; and (iii) the true identity is unknown, and the deepfake involves AV manipulation.
300   In all these cases, the verification task relies on determining whether the audio and video correspond
301   to the same event, independent of the identity. Our method is based on the assumption that current
302   generative models struggle to achieve perfect AV synthesis, often resulting in subtle inconsistencies.
303   To leverage this, we extend VeriFake to AV data by using powerful off-the-shelf audio-visual encoders
304   to extract features from each modality (we use AV-Hubert (Shi et al., 2022)). The audio and video
305   encoders are denoted by ϕA and ϕV , respectively, and we calculate the cosine similarity between
306   them as the truth score. There is an added complication in this case, as AV deepfakes are evaluated at
307
      a video level, while the truth score is calculated for every temporal frame. We opt for a simple but
      effective solution, using the truth score with the λ% lowest value in the video (we choose λ = 3%,
308
      but a wide range of values is successful, see Fig. 3b). Formally, for a clip of length T , we denote the
309
      visual frame at time t by vt and the audio frame by at . The truth score for frame t (denoted st ) is
310   given by:
311                                                    ϕV (vt ) · ϕA (at )
312                                          st =                                                          (2)
                                                   ∥ϕV (vt )∥2 · ∥ϕA (at )∥2
313
      We choose the frame value with the λ% percentile as the overall clip truth score s. This is given by
314
      s = perc({s1 , s2 ..sT }, λ) , where perc(·, λ) calculates the λ% percentile of the set. AV data with
315
      some misaligned frames will obtain a low truth score indicating a high likelihood of being fake. Real
316   data will not have mismatches and will achieve high truth scores. Note that misalignment between
317   AV data has been detected by several previous deepfake detection methods including (Feng et al.,
318   2023). The novelty here lies in demonstrating that our deepfake verification method outperforms
319   previous methods, using a simpler, streamlined method.

345   Implementation details. In our implementation, we use the AV-HuBERT Large model as our feature
346   encoder. This model was pretrained on real, unlabeled speech videos from the LRS3 dataset. No fake
347
      videos at all or any real videos from the evaluation dataset were used in pretraining. We follow the
      official AV-HuBERT implementation1 for video preprocessing. Specifically, we use an off-the-shelf
348
      landmark detector to identify Regions of Interest (ROIs) within each video clip. Both video and
349
      audio components are transformed into feature matrices represented in RT ×d , where T represents
350   the number of frames, and d is the AV-HuBERT feature space dimension (d = 1024). Accordingly,
351   we choose λ = 3% for our choice of λ. However, an ablation study is presented in Fig. 3b which
352   indicates that performance is not sensitive to the choice of λ. In accordance with standard practice,
353   we used two evaluation metrics: (i) average precision (AP) and (ii) receiver operating characteristic
354   area under the curve (ROC-AUC).
355
      Settings and baselines. We conducted experiments on FakeAVCeleb (Khalid et al., 2021) following

      5    A NALYSIS : OVERFITTING EFFECTS
392
393   Here we analyse the effect of overfitting on VeriFake by studying a simplified setting, where the
394   attacker first synthesizes a fake image using a text prompt and a text-to-image (TTI) model. The
395   attacker then presents the fake image with the input prompt as its caption. In this case, the verification
396   task is that the text prompt and the image describe the same content. As TTI models are known not
397   to be perfectly aligned with the input prompt (Xu et al., 2023; Chefer et al., 2023), VeriFake can be
398   used to detect fake images generated by them. We note that this setting is simplified, as the attacker
399   would not disclose the text prompt that generated the image, and also can either choose to use another
400   caption to describe the fake image which differs from the input prompt. This caption can be generated
401
      post-hoc (after the fake image was synthesized), making it potentially very accurate. Therefore, the
      user would not have a access to the text prompt that generated the image. However, we focus on
402
      analyzing the case where the caption matches the initial prompt, as this scenario provides insight into
403
      the overfitting effects of deepfake verification.
404
405   Dataset. Our evaluation is performed on a random sample of 1000 images from COCO (Lin et al.,
406   2014). Each image has 5 corresponding captions written by different people. We use Stable Diffusion
407
      (SD), to generate an image for each caption. This yields 5000 synthetic images and 1000 real images.
408   Truth score paradox. We begin by using the CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) encoders to encode the
409   caption and image respectively; the truth score is the cosine similarity between them. We compute
410   truth scores for all real and fake images in COCO. We begin with a simple analysis - we compare the
411   CLIP truth scores for each real image with its fake counterparts (fake images with the same caption).
412   We denote classification accuracy, as the number of fake images whose truth score was lower than that
413
      of their real counterparts. The results are shown in Fig. 3c. Surprisingly, we see that this method’s
      deepfake verification accuracy is lower than chance! Furthermore, we perform the same experiment,
414
      but now using BLIP2 (Li et al., 2023) as the text and image encoder. This experiment results are
415
      as expected; real images have higher truth scores than their fake counterparts. Full implementation
416   details are in App. A.3.
417
418   Resolution of the paradox. Why do BLIP2 truth scores behave as expected, but CLIP scores do
419
      exactly the opposite? We recall that SD was trained with CLIP text features, but not with BLIP2
      features. We therefore hypothesize that SD overfits to CLIP scores, making the fake images better
420
      aligned with their input caption than real images. On the other hand, the generated image does not
421
      fully correspond to the input prompt, due to imperfections in the TTI model. Therefore, an objective
422   multi-modal encoder, i.e. one not used for training the TTI, is able to perform verification and identify
423   that fake images do not fit with the text prompts. This explains why CLIP truth scores support the
424   text prompt, but BLIP2 rejects it.

440   6    D ISCUSSION AND LIMITATIONS
441
442   VeriFake as one-class classification (OCC). Let D be the distribution of all data, partitioned into
443   two sub-distributions DR for real data and DF for fake data. The objective is to provide a classifier C
444   which takes as input x ∈ D and classifies as real if x ∈ DR and fake if x ∈ DR . While the task may
445   appear a natural fit for supervised learning, there is a critical issue; at evaluation time, new attack types
446   will be developed (denoted zero-shot) that are very different from those existing at the time of training.
447   Formally, we divide the set of fake data into two non-overlapping sets: those       S observed at training
448   time Dobs and previously unseen attacks Dunseen such that DF = Dobs Dunseen . Supervised
449
      approaches can train a classifier C to separate between DR and Dobs but cannot guarantee that the
      classifier will be effective at separating between real images x ∈ DR and previously unseen attack
450
      images x ∈ Dunseen . In practice the success of such supervised methods will be highly dependent on
451
      the domain gap between Dobs and Dunseen . Previous papers observed such generalization failures.
452
453   This paper proposed to overcome this challenge by using an alternative learning rule, OCC. A naive
454   application of OCC trains a classifier using only real media x ∈ DR to learn to differentiate between
455
      real media and all other media (which are fake, x ∈ DF ). OCC by itself does not yield competitive
      deepfake detection results. Instead, VeriFake leverages additional information beyond raw data, such
456
      as the user’s identity or the correspondence between multi-modal data. We denote the pair of raw data
457
      and this additional information as (x, y) ∈ D, where x represents the raw data and y represents either
458   the user’s identity or the multi-modal correspondence. VeriFake trains a one-class classifier C(x, y)
459
                    S data pairs (x, y) ∈ DR . C is then able to differentiate between real DR and fake
      using only real
460   DF = Dobs Dunseen . Note that as VeriFake uses OCC, it does not differentiate between observed
461   and previously unseen deepfake attacks. Thus, it is natural to expect that the performance of observed
462   and unseen attacks verification will be similar.
463
      Unconditional deepfakes. Our method is not designed for unconditional deepfakes, e.g. a generated
464   image without added information such as a user’s identity or a caption. We stress that face swapping
465   and AV deepfakes are of sufficient practical and important to make our method valuable.
466
467
      Supervised approaches work well on previously seen attacks. The primary benefit of our method is
      generalizing to previously unseen attacks. Existing general-purpose supervised methods are effective
468
      on attacks similar to those seen before, for which sufficient training data can be obtained. We note
469
      that in most cases, our method outperforms supervised techniques even for previously seen attacks.
470
471   Hazards of future progress. To overcome VeriFake, generative models must become significantly
472   better than is currently possible. Specifically, they would have to replicate not only the visual
473
      appearance but also the finer details, nuances, and contextual cues of the user’s identity or speech.
      When generative methods indeed progress to this level, our method would need to be re-evaluated.
474
475   Broader impacts. Defending against deepfakes is crucial for maintaining trust in digital media and
476   preventing misinformation spread. Our proposed method offers a promising solution that can detect
477   novel, unseen deepfake attacks. This capability to detect previously unseen deepfake threats can help
478   mitigate the harmful societal impacts of disinformation campaigns and privacy violations.
479
480
481   7    C ONCLUSION
482
483   This paper proposes the concept of deepfake verification to address the challenge of detecting unseen
484   deepfake attacks. We propose VeriFake for implementing this, and showcase it in two important
485   settings. VeriFake outperforms the state-of-the-art without seeing any fake data, using only pretrained
      feature encoders and being simple to implement.

773   A.4     C APTION COMPLEXITY AND TRUTH SCORES
774
775   We hypothesize that more complex text prompts are more falsifiable and therefore improve deepfake
776
      verification accuracy. To test this hypothesis, we tested the correlation between textual prompt
      complexity and their truth scores for real and fake images. We hypothesized that as the complexity of
777
      the prompt increased, it would exhibit greater similarity to the real image compared to the fake image.
778
      Caption complexity, in this context, refers to the level of detail of describing the image content. To
779   systematically explore this hypothesis, we evaluated a dataset consisting of 1000 randomly selected
780   images from the COCO (Lin et al., 2014) dataset. For each of these real images, we selected two
781   captions: one with the minimum length and another with the maximum length. The minimum length
782   caption represented a simple textual prompt, while the maximum length caption was considered more
783   complex due to its larger word count.
784
      We paired each real image with its corresponding minimum and maximum length captions. For
785   each caption, we generated corresponding fake images, using Stable Diffusion. For each pairing,
786   we calculated truth scores, between the real image and its respective caption, as well as between
787   the fake image and the same caption. This analysis was conducted within the feature spaces of both
788   CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) and BLIP2 (Li et al., 2023), which notably, exhibited disagreements
789   in their prompt similarity trends. Our findings, presented in Fig. 5, underscore a consistent pattern.
790   With increasing complexity of the prompts, as measured through the maximum length captions, more
791   prompts achieved a higher truth score on the real images than on the fakes. This aligns with our
792   hypothesis, demonstrating that more complex prompts contribute to a shift in similarity to the real
793
      image, thereby enhancing deepfake verification accuracy. Additionally, we can observe the same
      phenomenon we witnessed in Sec. 5, wherein CLIP truth scores support the caption, while BLIP2
794
      rejects it. So the effectiveness of BLIP2 truth scores increases with prompt complexity, while using
795
      minus CLIP truth scores becomes less effective as prompts become more complex, as the overfitting
796   contrasts with the generative model’s failure to synthesize the image corresponding to the complex
797   caption.
