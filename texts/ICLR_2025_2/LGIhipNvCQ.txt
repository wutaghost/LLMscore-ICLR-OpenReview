009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Continual Test-Time Adaptation (CTTA) addresses the challenge of adapting
012            models to sequentially changing domains during the testing phase. Since no
013            ground truth labels are provided, existing CTTA methods rely on pseudo-labels for
014            self-adaptation. However, CTTA is prone to error accumulation, where incorrect
015            pseudo-labels can negatively impact subsequent model updates. Critically, during
016            testing, a CTTA method cannot detect its mistakes, which may then propagate
017            through further adaptations. In this paper, we propose a simple uncertainty indi-
018
               cator called TUI for the CTTA task based on Conformal Prediction (CP), which
               generates a set of possible labels for each test sample, ensuring that the true la-
019
               bel is included within this set with a given coverage probability. Specifically,
020
               since domain shifts can undermine the coverage of predictions, making uncer-
021            tainty estimation less dependable, we propose compensating for coverage by dy-
022            namically measuring the domain difference between the target and source domains
023            in continuously changing environments. Moreover, after estimating uncertainty,
024            we separate reliable test pseudo-labels and use them to discriminatively enhance
025            the adaptation process. Empirical results demonstrate that our algorithm effec-
026            tively estimates the uncertainty for CTTA under a specified coverage probability
027            and improves adaptation performance across various existing CTTA methods.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Recently, Continual Test-Time Adaptation (CTTA) (Wang et al., 2022) has garnered significant at-
033
      tention for its ability to enable trained models to handle various unknown test domain shifts through
      self-adaptation. This innovative approach aims to enhance model robustness and adaptability dur-
034
      ing the testing phase, addressing the dynamic nature of real-world data, such as autonomous driv-
035
      ing (Sójka et al., 2023) and medical imagining (Chen et al., 2024). However, a critical challenge
036   arises in many testing scenarios where the cost of incorrect predictions is prohibitively high. When
037   self-adaptation is based on unreliable predictions, it may lead to severe error accumulation, compro-
038   mising the model’s performance. Therefore, effectively measuring the uncertainty of model outputs
039   becomes crucial to mitigate losses and allow for human intervention or termination.
040
      Some uncertainty estimation methods are based on Bayes rule, such as Bayes approximation (Mad-
041
      dox et al., 2019) and Monte Carlo dropout (Gal & Ghahramani, 2016), requiring high computational
042   complexity or rely on model selection, thus difficult to be applied to testing time. Moreover, some
043   methods directly use the output logits to form uncertainties such as entropy (Shi et al., 2024), which
044   may suffer from confidence dilemma that unreliable logits give unreliable uncertainty estimations.
045   In contrast, Conformal Prediction (CP) (Vovk et al., 2005) offers a promising solution for measuring
046   uncertainty in predictions, which produces set-valued predictions that serves as a wrapper around
047   existing models. CP is with the following compelling advantages. First, CP is model-agnostic,
048   which means it does not require any assumptions about the model, making it applicable to any pre-
049   trained model without necessitating modifications. Second, CP yields controllable coverage, which
050
      means CP allows the true label coverage probability to be pre-specified and ensuring that this prob-
      ability is met. These advantages meet the scenario of CTTA that continuously measuring the output
051
      uncertainties for a pre-trained model in testing time without confidence dilemma issue.
052
053   However, incorporating CP into unsupervised CTTA presents significant challenges. Traditional CP
      requires the assumption of data exchangeability, which refers to the assumption that the order in

      In this paper, we explore the feasibility of using CP in testing scenarios by addressing the coverage
077
      gap challenge and propose a simple uncertainty measurement method named Test Uncertainty Indi-
078   cator (TUI). The goal of TUI is to output the uncertainty of testing for each test example with a given
079   trained model. The key motivation for TUI is to compensate the coverage gap when domain shifts
080   and output reliable uncertainty level. Specifically, following CP, TUI maintains a static source cal-
081   ibration set with labels in the pre-training phase. To evaluate the uncertainty for an example during
082   testing time, TUI measures the domain shifts by considering both model and data differences. Then,
083   a quantile for the test sample is calculated based on the calibration set, and the domain shift level is
084   used to compensate for the quantile to achieve better coverage. Last, a non-conformity threshold is
085   obtained by the compensated quantile and outputs the corresponding prediction set, where its size is
086
      treated as the indication of uncertainty level. Moreover, based on the CP results, we design a simple
      enhanced adaptation method on confident test samples, which can also be applied to existing CTTA
087
      methods. We find applying more adaptations on samples with reliable predictions will get good test-
088
      ing performance. As shown in Fig. 1, a traditional CTTA block consists of a point prediction and an
089   adaptation, the proposed TUI provides the testing uncertainty and helps the adaptation. We evaluate
090   on three benchmark datasets and find that the proposed TUI can better evaluate the test uncertainty
091   than other CP methods. By integrating the CP-based adaptation strategy, existing methods achieve
092   better reliability and robustness of model predictions in dynamic and uncertain test environments.
093
      Our contributions are three-fold:
094
095   (1) We propose a simple uncertainty estimation method TUI for CTTA to measure the test un-
096       certainty for each test prediction. TUI is model-agnostic and relies only on a small size of
097
          calibration set.
098   (2) We propose an adaptation method based on the TUI estimation, which enhances the reliable test
099
          adaptation.
100   (3) We evaluate our method on benchmark datasets and help multiple existing CTTA methods mea-
101
          sure their test uncertainty and achieve better performance via our adaptation strategy.

105   2.1      C ONTINUAL T EST-T IME A DAPTATION
106
107   Test-Time Adaptation (TTA) enables the model to dynamically adjust to the characteristics of the test
      data, i.e. target domain, in a source-free and online manner (Jain & Learned-Miller, 2011; Sun et al.,


                                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      2020; Wang et al., 2020). Recently, CTTA (Wang et al., 2022) has been introduced to tackle TTA
109   within a continuously changing target domain, involving long-term adaptation. This configuration
110   often grapples with the challenge of error accumulation (Tarvainen & Valpola, 2017; Wang et al.,
111   2022). Specifically, prolonged exposure to unsupervised loss from unlabeled test data during long-
112   term adaptation may result in significant error accumulation. Additionally, as the model is intent on
113   learning new knowledge, it is prone to forgetting source knowledge, which poses challenges when
114   accurately classifying test samples similar to the source distribution. To solve the two challenges, the
115   majority of the existing methods focus on improving the confidence of the source model during the
116   testing phase. These methods employ the mean-teacher architecture (Tarvainen & Valpola, 2017)
117
      to mitigate error accumulation, where the student learns to align with the teacher and the teacher
      updates via moving average with the student. As to the challenge of forgetting source knowledge,
118
      some methods adopt augmentation-averaged predictions (Wang et al., 2022; Brahma & Rai, 2023;
119
      Döbler et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2023) for the teacher model, strengthening the teacher’s confidence
120   to reduce the influence from highly out-of-distribution samples. Some methods, such as Döbler et al.
121   (2023) and Chakrabarty et al. (2023a), propose to adopt the contrastive loss to maintain the already
122   learnt semantic information. Some methods believe that the source model is more reliable, thus they
123   are designed to restore the source parameters (Wang et al., 2022; Brahma & Rai, 2023). Though
124   the above methods keep the model from confusion of vague pseudo labels, they may suffer from
125   overly confident predictions that are less calibrated. To mitigate this issue, it is helpful to estimate
126   the uncertainty in the neural network.
127
128   2.2   C ONFORMAL P REDICTION
129
130   CP was first introduced in Gammerman et al. (1998) as a method for quantifying uncertainty in
131   both classification and regression tasks. Vovk et al. (2005) provides a formalized introduction to
132   conformal prediction as well as application (and associated theoretical results) in multiple data set-
133   tings, e.g., online and batch procedures. Conformal prediction is a robust framework for quantifying
134   uncertainty in machine learning models, especially in high-stakes applications where reliability is
135
      crucial. It provides a means to generate prediction sets that contain the true outcome with a spec-
      ified probability, without relying on assumptions about the underlying data distribution. CP was
136
      pioneered by Vladimir Vovk and colleagues in the 1990s, focusing on the concept of exchangeabil-
137
      ity and the use of nonconformity scores (Vovk et al., 2005). The framework was further developed
138   to include various modifications and extensions (Angelopoulos & Bates, 2021). The foundational
139   book by Vovk et al. (2005) provides a comprehensive introduction to the theory and applications of
140   conformal prediction, emphasizing its distribution-free nature. CP has been applied to a wide range
141   of problems, including medical diagnostics (Caruana et al., 2015), autonomous vehicles (Lekeufack
142   et al., 2023), and financial decision-making, where the quantification of uncertainty is critical for
143   safety and trust. Researchers have extended conformal prediction to handle more complex scenarios,
144   such as distribution shift (Tibshirani et al., 2019), distribution drift (Barber et al., 2023), and time-
145   series data (Lei & Wasserman, 2014). CP has been coupled with risk control techniques to provide
146
      guarantees on various performance metrics, such as false discovery rate in multi-label classification
      (Farinhas et al., 2023). Recent work has explored the interplay between calibration techniques like
147
      temperature scaling and conformal prediction methods, revealing the impact of calibration on the
148
      performance of conformal predictors (Dabah & Tirer, 2024).
149

151   3     P RELIMINARY: CTTA AND C ONFORMAL P REDICTION
152
153   Continual Test-Time Adaptation (CTTA). Given a classification model pre-trained on a source
154   domain, CTTA methods adapt the source model to the unlabeled target data, where the domain
155   continuously changes. Because the adaptation is conducted during test time, which means the model
156   needs to output the prediction immediately then update the model. The unsupervised dataset of target
                                             k
157   domains are denoted as Dk = {xkm }N  m=1 , where k is the target domain index. For each test sample,
158   CTTA conducts two major operations including testing and adaptation. For testing, the model needs
159   to output the prediction of the model. For adaptation, the model needs to adapt to the testing sample
160   without ground-truth. Just because no label is given, a CTTA model is prone to error accumulation.
161   To avoid this, a uncertainty estimation should be given for each testing samples. In this paper, we
      use conformal prediction to evaluate prediction uncertainties.


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      Conformal Prediction (CP) and Coverage Gap Issue. We first introduce CP under a multi-class
163   classification task with total K classes. Let X be the input space and Y := {1, · · · , K} be the label
164   space. We use π : X → RK to denote the pre-trained neural network that is used to predict the label
165   of a test sample. The model prediction in this classification tasks is generally made as
166                                            ŷ = arg max π(y|x),                                       (1)
167                                                        y∈Y

168   where π(y|x) can be seen as the confidence of that x being labeled to class y. To provide a uncer-
169   tainty guarantee for the model performance, CP (Vovk et al., 2005) is designed to produce prediction
170   sets containing true labels with a desired probability. Instead of only predicting point labels (only
171
      labels with max confidence will be selected) from the model outputs, standard conformal prediction
      takes a black-box prediction model, a calibration data set, and a new test example x ∈ X test with
172
      unknown label y ∈ Y test , creating a prediction set P(x) ⊆ Y test and satisfies marginal coverage:
173
                                             P(y ∈ P(x)) ≥ 1 − α,                                         (2)
174
175
      for a coverage level α ∈ [0, 1] specified by the user. α is generally considered to represent a user
      pre-specified error rate. For instance, if α is set to 0.1, the resulting prediction set is expected to
176
      achieve a 90% coverage rate. In other words, there is a 90% probability that the true label will be
177
      included within the prediction set.
178
179   However, the coverage in Eq. (2) is guaranteed only when the testing domains are with the same
180   distribution with the training domain, say data exchangeability (Vovk et al., 2005; Barber et al.,
181
      2023; Zaffran et al., 2022; Bhatnagar et al., 2023; Gibbs & Candès, 2022; Farinhas et al., 2023;
      Zou & Liu, 2024). When domain shifts, the exchangeability is not satisfied, thus the coverage
182
      will significantly drop. As observed by Yilmaz & Heckel (2022) and Bhatnagar et al. (2023), even
183
      subtle shifts makes coverage drop from the desired 90% to 60% on Imagenet-Sketch dataset. This
184   phenomenon is called Coverage Gap (Barber et al., 2023), which is defined as follows:
185
                                        κ = (1 − α) − P {y ∈ P(x)} ,                                  (3)
186
      where 1 − α is the expected coverage and P {y ∈ P(x)} is the obtained coverage. To fill in the
187
      coverage gap, NexCP (Barber et al., 2023) generalizes CP by employing weighted quantiles and
188
      a randomization technique, enabling robust predictive inference even when data exchangeability
189   assumptions are violated. However, this method is designed for training phase and highly depends on
190   a pre-defined domain shift value, which is not allowed in testing time. Moreover, Yilmaz & Heckel
191   (2022) propose a QTC method to recalibrate the quantile for coverage compensation. However,
192   QTC suffers from the unreliable domain gap measurement in continual domain shifts and ignore the
193   model differences. More details about existing non-exchangeable CP can be found in Section 4.3.1.
194
      Motivated by this, in this paper, we seek to design a CP method for CTTA to act as an uncertainty
195   indicator during testing time, and solve the coverage gap issue. Moreover, we would present to
196   improve the adaptation in CTTA via the uncertainty measurement.

      4     T EST U NCERTAINTY I NDICATOR FOR CTTA
200
      4.1     C ONFORMAL PREDICTION WITH QUANTILE COMPENSATION
201
202   In this section, we propose a simple uncertainty indicator based on CP for CTTA task named Test
203   Uncertainty Indicator (TUI). TUI is based on CP, and the major challenge of TUI is the coverage
204   gap when domain shifts as mentioned in the above section. In the following, we introduce how to
205   build a simple uncertainty indicator for CTTA task step by step.
206
207   4.1.1    S TEP 1: P REPARING CALIBRATION SET
208
      First, following Vovk et al. (2005), CP needs to build a calibration set to approximate the source dis-
209
      tribution for efficient computation. We select a part of labeled source data as the calibration set in our
210   implementation. In real-world applications, the calibration set is easy to obtain, such as split from
211   the source training set or further collections, making sure the calibration set and the training data are
212   drawn from a same distribution. We will discuss the storage of calibration set construction in the end
213   of the section. Specifically, we denote the calibration set as C = {(x1 , y1 ) · · · , (x|C| , y|C| )} ⊂ Dval .
214   The calibration set should be built before test phase. Note that our method is only applied to CTTA
215   tasks with this prepared calibration set, where the calibration data can be regarded to a fixed clue of
      training distribution.

      4.1.2   S TEP 2: C OMPUTING JOINT DOMAIN SHIFTS
217
218   Existing non-exchangeable CP methods fail to estimate the continual domain shifts in CTTA, such
219   as NexCP (Barber et al., 2023) and QTC (Yilmaz & Heckel, 2022). These methods either assume
220   that the domain shift is known or ignore the issue of error accumulation in the model during CTTA.
221   In many existing domain difference measure methods, they directly compute distribution distance
222
      based on the current model. For example, DSS (Chakrabarty et al., 2023b) uses the cosine distance
      between the prototypes of source domain and the current domain as the signal of domain shifts.
223
      However, because the error accumulation, the current model could be not convincing enough. That
224
      is, the prototypes may not represent the real data distributions. To this end, we propose to further
225   consider the model shift when measure the domain shifts.
226
227   In our method, to estimate the domain shifts during continuous test time, we consider both model
228
      and data difference. For model difference, we use both the source model with parameter θ src and
      the current model with parameter θ crt . For data difference, we use both the calibration set C and the
229
      current test data B. Specifically, we construct a joint probability distribution of calibration data and
230
      test data from both source and current models. The joint probability distribution is computed by
231
                                   p(x) = softmax (concat(πθsrc (x), πθcrt (x))) .                          (4)
232
233
      In this way, each sample can be represented by both the source and current models. Then, for the
      joint distribution difference measurement, we use

237   where DJS is the Jensen-Shannon (JS) divergence, which is known as symmetric and stable. In the
238   context of CTTA, comparing the distribution differences of joint feature representations from the
239   source and current models, there are several advantages. First, joint feature representation captures
240   correlations between different features, providing a more holistic view of the data distribution and

242   can better reflect subtle differences between domains, enhancing the precision of JS divergence

      measures. Last, comparing joint feature distributions allows for a more detailed assessment of how
      much the current model has gained compared to the source model.

282
283
      Now we have the prediction set for each test sample, and the size of the prediction set represents
      the uncertainty level of the prediction. In general, the set size is close to 1 but larger than 0 can
284
      be regarded to reliable. However, traditional CP methods focus on detecting violations of the ex-
285
      changeability assumption rather than adapting to such changes (Fedorova et al., 2012; Volkhonskiy
286   et al., 2017; Vovk et al., 2020). In the context of CTTA, we prefer to further improve the adaptations
287   via the guidance from CP.
288
289
      Motivated by this, we design a simple adaptation strategy for CTTA based on TUI, weighting the
      adaptation of each test sample according to its uncertainty. A test sample with more reliable pre-
290
      diction will be set to larger weight for adaptation. Taking the adaptation in Mean-Teacher-based
291
      methods (Wang et al., 2011; Brahma & Rai, 2023; Döbler et al., 2023) as an example, these meth-
292   ods construct a mean-teacher structure based on the source pretrained model. The mean-teacher
293   structure contains a student model and a teacher model, where the student updates via learning log-
294   its from the teacher, and the teacher then updates via exponential moving averaging from the updated
295   student. In this case, the TUI-guided adaptation on the student model can be represented by:

      4.3.2    DATA STORAGE FOR CALIBRATION IN TESTING TIME
354   In our method, we explore the feasibility of using CP in testing scenarios with the aid of additional
355   samples for calibration. That means the testing system needs to store extra data, yielding more
356   storage requirements. In fact, this is a common practice in continual learning. Many continual
357   learning (Rolnick et al., 2019; Van de Ven et al., 2020) methods store and retrain previous training
358   examples to avoid catastrophic forgetting of past tasks, named replay strategy. In comparison with
359   replay, the calibration set in TUI is not used for adaptation but calibration in testing time, and the
360   calibration set will not be updated in our method.
361   Moreover, in many real-world applications, it is feasible to pre-store data in training or utilize data
362   in adaptation. Practical approaches in real-world settings involve storing samples to improve testing
363   outcomes. Some methods even utilize additional unlabeled samples to enhance training (Goldman &
364   Zhou, 2000; Zhu & Goldberg, 2022). Some methods such as Tomani et al. (2021) and Rahimi et al.
365   (2020) leverage post-hoc calibration to achieve better performance under domain drift scenarios by
366   using validation or calibration sets. Thus, it is reasonable to leverage the calibration set in test time.

      5.3.3          DATA STORAGE ANALYSIS AND COMPARISON WITH REPLAY STRATEGY
516
517   As discussed in Sec. 4.3.2, CP-based methods need to maintain an extra calibration set for uncer-
518   tainty estimation. Although effectively measuring uncertainty is crucial in testing systems, using CP
519   requires a certain amount of memory storage. We analyze the impact of this storage on performance
520   in Table 5 and find that a larger storage capacity leads to better CP performance, as more calibration
521   data provides a more accurate representation of the original data distribution. Additionally, we com-
522
      pare TUI with a classic storage method in continual learning, the source replay strategy, where we
      use the same samples for replay when conducting adaptation. We find that TUI achieves better ac-
523
      curacy while maintaining the same amount of stored data, which shows the significance of reducing
524
      error accumulation in CTTA.

527   6      C ONCLUSION
528
529   CTTA is prone to error accumulation, where incorrect pseudo-labels can negatively impact subse-
530   quent model updates. In this paper, we proposed a simple uncertainty indicator called TUI for the
531
      CTTA task based on CP, which generates a set of possible labels for each instance, ensuring that the
      true label is included within this set with a specified coverage probability. To reduce the coverage
532
      gap when domain shifting, we proposed dynamically measuring the domain difference between the
533
      target and source domains in continuously changing environments. Moreover, we separate relabeled
534   test pseudo-labels and use them to enhance the adaptation. Experimental results demonstrate that
535   our method effectively estimates the uncertainty for CTTA under a specified coverage probability
536   and improves adaptation performance for various existing CTTA methods. The proposed TUI has
537   the following limitations. First, TUI requires a prepared calibration set for conformal calculation,
538   which may not be satisfied in some situations. Second, TUI performs better than other CP methods
539   in long-term changing domains, but when the domain shifts have an extremely large number it may
      also lose its effectiveness. In the future, we will further study to solve the two limitations.

      However, Theorem 2 is only appropriate for known domain difference. When the domain differ-
851
      ences are unknown in test time, it is difficult to obtain a certain coverage lower bound. This explains
852   why NexCP performs poorly in the CTTA task. QTC has designed a dynamic method for estimating
853   domain differences, making it more suitable for testing compared to NexCP. However, the CTTA
854   task requires multiple domain changes, which significantly impacts the model’s ability to estimate
855   domain differences due to error accumulation. Specifically, we compute the joint distribution differ-
856   ence of current data and calibration data between both the source and current model.
857
      In TUI, we dynamic evaluate the domain difference between the source data and the current test data.
858   To mitigate the effect of error accumulation, we consider both model and data difference. We use
859   the Jensen-Shannon (JS) divergence as the metric. Joint feature representation captures correlations
860   between different features, providing a more holistic view of the data distribution and how different
861   models process it. The joint distribution can better reflect subtle differences between domains,
862   enhancing the precision of JS divergence measures. Moreover, comparing joint feature distributions
863   allows for a more detailed assessment of how much the current model has gained compared to the
      source model.
