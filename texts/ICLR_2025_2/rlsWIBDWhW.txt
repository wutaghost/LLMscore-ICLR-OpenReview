009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Adversarial contrastive learning aims to learn a representation space robust to ad-
012            versarial inputs using only unlabeled data. Existing methods typically generate
013            adversarial perturbations by maximizing the contrastive loss during adversarial
014            training. However, we find that the effectiveness of this approach is influenced by
015            the composition of positive and negative examples in a minibatch, which is not ex-
016            plicitly controllable. To address this limitation, we propose a novel approach to ad-
017            versarial contrastive learning, where adversarial perturbations are generated based
018
               on the clustering structure of the representation space learned through contrastive
               learning. Our method is motivated by the observation that contrastive learning
019
               produces a well-separated representation space, where similar data points cluster
020
               together in space, while dissimilar ones are positioned farther apart. We hypothe-
021            size that perturbations directed toward neighboring (the second nearest to be spe-
022            cific) clusters are likely to cross the decision boundary of a downstream classifier
023            built upon contrastive learning, effectively acting as adversarial examples. A key
024            challenge in our approach is to determine a sufficiently large number of clusters,
025            for which the number of classes in the downstream task would serve the purpose
026            but is typically unknown during adversarial contrastive learning. Therefore, we
027            employ the silhouette score to identify the optimal number of clusters, ensuring
028            high-quality clustering in the representation space. Compared to the existing ap-
029
               proaches, our method achieved up to 2.25% and 5.05% improvements in robust
               accuracy against PGD and Auto-Attack, respectively, showing slight improvement
030
               in standard accuracy as well in most cases.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
034
035
      The challenge of learning meaningful representations from unlabeled data remains crucial due to the
036   scarcity of labeled data and the high cost of labeling. Self-supervised learning uses pretext tasks (e.g.,
037   jigsaw puzzles (Noroozi & Favaro, 2016) or rotation prediction (Gidaris et al., 2018)) to capture
038   the underlying structure of unlabeled data. Among these approaches, contrastive learning (Hadsell
039   et al., 2006; Chen et al., 2020; He et al., 2020; Chen & He, 2021) has made significant progress by
040   pulling positive pairs closer while pushing the negative pairs farther apart in the representation space.
041   Furthermore, the representations learned by contrastive learning achieve comparable performance
042   with supervised learning on downstream tasks.
043   The success of contrastive learning has prompted interest in learning adversarially robust repre-
044   sentations from unlabeled data. Recent works have focused on combining adversarial training with
045   contrastive learning to enhance robustness (Zhang et al., 2022; Kim et al., 2020; Jiang et al., 2020;
046   Fan et al., 2021; Luo et al., 2023). While adversarial training in supervised learning aims to correctly
047   classify adversarial examples, adversarial contrastive learning achieves robustness by ensuring that
048   adversarial and clean examples have similar representations.
049   In adversarial contrastive learning, generating adversarial examples is challenging due to the absence
050   of labeled data. Existing approaches (Kim et al., 2020; Jiang et al., 2020; Fan et al., 2021; Luo et al.,
051   2023) have addressed this challenge by maximizing the contrastive loss to generate perturbations for
052   adversarial training. However, without explicitly considering the relationship between the resulting
053   perturbations and the decision boundary of a downstream classifier, there is no guarantee that the
      created perturbations will be adversarial with respect to the decision boundary.

054
      To address the limitation of prior approaches, we focus on the well-clustered representations learned
055   by contrastive learning, where similar data points are closely clustered, while dissimilar points are
056   farther apart. We conjecture that the perturbation directed toward the neighboring cluster may cross
057   the decision boundary of a downstream classifier when the neighboring cluster is composed of dis-
058   similar data to the original sample, given that the representations tend to be linearly separable (Chen
059   et al., 2020; He et al., 2020; HaoChen et al., 2021; Tosh et al., 2021) and the data distribution of the
060   downstream task is sufficiently similar to that of data used for contrastive learning. Based on this
061   idea, we propose a novel cluster-driven adversarial contrastive learning method.
062   Our main contributions are:
063
064          • We propose a new adversarial contrastive learning framework utilizing the clustering struc-
065            ture in the representation space. Our method chooses the number of clusters by the silhou-
066            ette score, eliminating the need for an extra hyperparameter tuning.
067          • We show by experiment that our proposed method can produce a fully robust representa-
068            tion, that is, adversarial linear fine-tuning makes little difference to standard linear fine-
069            tuning.
070          • Our experimental results show that our proposed method outperforms the state-of-the-art
071            methods in robust accuracy against PGD and Auto-Attack adversaries, with a slight in-
072            crease in standard accuracy in the majority of cases.

076   Adversarial training. Since the discovery of neural networks’ vulnerablility to adversarial at-
077   tacks (Szegedy et al., 2014), various defense methods (Madry et al., 2018; Zhang et al., 2019; Paper-
078   not et al., 2016; Xu et al., 2018; Metzen et al., 2017; Xie et al., 2019) have been proposed to enhance
079   their adversarial robustness.
080   Among these defenses, adversarial training has emerged as one of the most widely adopted ap-
081   proaches, where adversarial examples are incorporated into the training process to improve adver-
082   sarial robustness. Standard adversarial training (Madry et al., 2018) is formulated as follows:

120
      Adversarial contrastive learning. Motivated by the remarkable achievements of contrastive
121   learning, several methods have been proposed to integrate adversarial training with contrastive learn-
122   ing to enhance the robustness.
123
124
      Jiang et al. (2020) enforces the representations to be invariant to perturbations by performing con-
      trastive learning on the perturbed positive pair (x+δx , xp +δp ) instead of (x, xp ), where δx and δp are
125
      adversarial perturbations applied to the anchor sample x and positive sample xp , respectively. Sim-
126
      ilarly, Kim et al. (2020) proposed a robust contrastive learning (RoCL) framework that maximizes
127   the cosine similarity between the representations of the clean (anchor) sample x and the sample with
128   a perturbation x + δx by including x + δx in the positive set P(x) of the contrastive loss.
129
130
      Adversarial contrastive learning (ADVCL) (Fan et al., 2021) advances the RoCL by introducing the
      high-frequency component of the original sample x̃ as an additional positive sample, considering
131
      that the adversarial perturbations are primarily concentrated on the high-frequency region (Wang
132
      et al., 2020). In addition, ADVCL incorporates supervision loss using pseudo-labels generated by
133   an additional encoder pre-trained on the ImageNet dataset (Deng et al., 2009).
134
135   Zhang et al. (2022) proposed decoupled adversarial contrastive learning (DeACL) that divides the
136
      previous adversarial contrastive learning into two stages: in stage 1, (standard) contrastive learning is
      adopted to train an encoder; in stage 2, adversarial training is performed with perturbations generated
137
      using the encoder pre-trained in stage 1.
138
139   Dynamic adversarial contrastive learning (DYNACL) (Luo et al., 2023) introduces a dynamic aug-
140   mentation strategy on the method proposed by Jiang et al. (2020), where the strength of data aug-
141   mentation is gradually reduced as the training progresses. This approach balances the need for strong
142
      augmentation in representation learning while mitigating its negative impact on robustness. DY-
      NACL++ (Luo et al., 2023) post-processes the encoder trained with DYNACL via linear probing
143
      and adversarial full fine-tuning (Kumar et al., 2022), where pseudo-labels are generated using the
144
      encoder pre-trained with DYNACL. Xu et al. (2023a) proposed a robustness-aware coreset selec-
145   tion method to accelerate existing adversarial contrastive learning methods. In addition, Xu et al.
146   (2023b) enhanced the robustness of existing adversarial contrastive learning methods (Jiang et al.,
147   2020; Luo et al., 2023) by incorporating adversarial invariant regularization (AIR) into the adversar-
148   ial contrastive loss.

      3.1   M OTIVATION
153
154   In this section, we discuss the limitations of existing adversarial contrastive learning methods and
155   explain the motivation for proposing a novel perturbation generation approach.
156
      For a given unlabeled dataset, adversarial contrastive learning aims to build an adversarially robust
157   encoder whose learned representations preserve resistance to adversarial attacks in the downstream
158   tasks. The existing adversarial contrastive learning methods (Kim et al., 2020; Jiang et al., 2020;
159   Fan et al., 2021; Luo et al., 2023) generate perturbations for adversarial training by maximizing the
160   contrastive loss as follows:

182   samples are selected uniformly at random, and thus, the effectiveness of created perturbations can
183   be limited.

194   To address the limitations of the current approaches mentioned above, we suggest a new idea uti-
195   lizing the geometrical structure of the representation space learned by contrastive learning. This
196   representation space is defined by well-structured clusters of semantically similar data and clear
197   distinctions between dissimilar data. Building on these properties, we propose a novel approach
198   to generate perturbations directed toward the neighboring clusters (we choose the second nearest
199
      cluster as the neighbor based on our empirical results).
200   As a proof of concept for our idea, we conducted experiments to assess the adversarial effective-
201   ness of perturbations generated toward the neighboring clusters in the representation space learned
202   by SimCLR. For evaluation, we attached a linear classifier to the frozen encoder pre-trained with
203   SimCLR and fine-tuned it. We then measured the probability of misclassification (attack success
204   rate) and the amount of decrease in predicted probability for the true class induced by the perturba-
205
      tions. Details on the experimental procedure are available in the Appendix A.1. Table 1 compares

206

207
      an attack success rate by up to 20.6 times more compared to random noise. This suggests that the
208   perturbations directed toward neighboring clusters are likely to act as adversarial examples.

361    and adversarial examples from Auto-Attack (Croce & Hein, 2020) with ϵ = 8/255 in ℓ∞ -norm,
362    respectively.
363
364    Selection of the number of clusters. In stage 2 of our proposed NCP-ACL, the number of clusters
365    K is required as a hyperparameter for spherical K-means clustering. With unknown class numbers
366    in the downstream task, we determine the value of K using the silhouette score (Rousseeuw, 1987),
367
       a metric for evaluating clustering quality. We compute silhouette scores by clustering representa-
       tions extracted from the initial encoder fθ2 , and fix the optimal K value throughout training. This
368
       approach eliminates the need for additional hyperparameter tuning to select the number of clusters,
369
       thus significantly reducing the computational cost. Figure 2 shows silhouette scores for varying K
370    on CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100, and STL-10 with the random seed fixed at 0. Here, the optimal K values
371    are 70, 150, and 215 for CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100, and STL-10, respectively. Additional details are
372    provided in Appendix A.2.

      5   C ONCLUSION
476
477
      In this paper, we proposed a novel adversarial contrastive learning method called NCP-ACL. We
478
      tried to address the problem in the current methodologies where adversarial examples are typically
479
      constructed by maximizing the contrastive learning loss, and as a result, the generated perturba-
480   tion may not be adversarial with respect to the decision boundary obtained through fine-tuning.
481   In NCP-ACL, we utilized the well-clustered representations learned by contrastive learning, where
482   we generated adversarial inputs by directing the perturbations toward the neighboring clusters. In
483   this way, we expected that the perturbations would be toward the direction of crossing the decision
484   boundary of a downstream classifier. Through the experiments, we showed that our proposal out-
485   performs the current state-of-the-art methods in learning robust representations, leading to higher
      standard and robust predictions after fine-tuning.

597   A.1     S ETUP FOR P ROOF OF C ONCEPT E XPERIMENT
598
599   To validate whether perturbations generated toward neighboring clusters act adversarially, we con-
600   duct a proof-of-concept experiment. The overall process consists of five steps: (i) We train the en-
601
      coder using SimCLR. (ii) We perform spherical K-means clustering on the representations learned in
      step (i), where the resulting clusters are expected to consist of similar data points. Here, the number
602
      of clusters K is selected using the silhouette score. (iii) For each data point, we generate a perturba-
603
      tion directed toward the centroid of the neighboring cluster. (iv) For evaluating the adversarial effec-
604   tiveness of generated perturbations, we attach a linear classifier to the frozen encoder pre-trained in
605   step (i) and train the linear classifier using labeled training samples. (v) Using the model from step
606   (iv), we measure the attack success rate and average decrease in prediction probability for the true
607   class caused by perturbations.
608
      We employ the ResNet18 architecture for the encoder. Perturbations are generated by optimiz-
609
      ing Eq. (4) without the first term CosSim(f (x + δ), Cx ), where we use 5-step PGD with a per-
610   turbation budget of ϵ = 8/255. To precisely measure the adversarial effect of perturbations, we
611   evaluate only on test samples correctly classified by the model from step (4). From experimental
612   results presented in Table 1, we confirm that the perturbations generated by our approach are likely
613   to cross the decision boundary of the downstream classifier.
