009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Multi-task reinforcement learning (MTRL) has shown great promise in many real-
012            world applications. Existing MTRL algorithms often aim to learn a policy that
013            optimizes individual objective functions simultaneously with a given prior prefer-
014            ence (or weights) on different tasks. However, these methods often suffer from the
015            issue of gradient conflict such that the tasks with larger gradients dominate the up-
016            date direction, resulting in a performance degeneration on other tasks. In this paper,
017            we develop a novel dynamic weighting multi-task actor-critic algorithm (MTAC)
018            under two options of sub-procedures named as CA and FC in task weight updates.
019
               MTAC-CA aims to find a conflict-avoidant (CA) update direction that maximizes
               the minimum value improvement among tasks, and MTAC-FC targets at a much
020
               faster convergence rate. We provide a comprehensive finite-time convergence
021
               analysis for both algorithms. We show that MTAC-CA can find a ϵ + ϵapp -accurate
022                                                                                           √
               Pareto stationary policy using O(ϵ−5 ) samples, while ensuring a small ϵ + ϵapp -
023            level CA distance (defined as the distance to the CA direction), where ϵapp is the
024            function approximation error. The analysis also shows that MTAC-FC improves
025            the sample complexity to O(ϵ−3 ), but with a constant-level CA distance. Our
026            experiments on MT10 demonstrate the improved performance of our algorithms
027            over existing MTRL methods with fixed preference.

030   1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Reinforcement learning (RL) has made much progress in a variety of applications, such as autonomous
033   driving, robotics manipulation, and financial trades Deng et al. (2016); Sallab et al. (2017); Gu et al.
034   (2017). Though the progress is significant, much of the current work is restricted to learning the
035   policy for one task Mülling et al. (2013); Andrychowicz et al. (2020). However, in practice, the
036   vanilla RL polices often suffers from performance degradation when learning multiple tasks in a
037   multi-task setting. To deal with these challenges, various multi-task reinforcement learning (MTRL)
038   approaches have been proposed to learn a single policy or multiple policies that maximize various
039
      objective functions simultaneously. In this paper, we focus on single-policy MTRL approaches
      because of their better efficiency. On the other side, the multi-policy method allows each task to have
040
      its own policy, which requires high memory and computational cost. The objective is to solve the
041
      following MTRL problem:
042

043                                   π

      where K is the total number of tasks and J k (π) is the objective function of task k ∈ [K] given the
      policy π. Typically, existing single-policy MTRL methods aim to find the optimal policy with the
046
      given preference (i.e., the weights over tasks) ). For example, Mannor & Shimkin (2001) developed
047
      a MTRL algorithm considering the average prior preference. The MTRL method in Yang et al.
048   (2019) trained and saved models with different fixed prior preferences, and then chooses the best
049   model according to the testing requirement. However, the performance of these approaches highly
050   depends on the selection of the fixed preference, and can also suffer from the conflict among the
051   gradient of different objective functions such that some tasks with larger gradients dominates the
052   update direction at the sacrifice of significant performance degeneration on the less-fortune tasks
053   with smaller gradients. Therefore, it is highly important to find an update direction that aims to find a
      more balanced solution for all tasks.

054
      There have been a large body of studies on finding a conflict-avoidant (CA) direction to mitigate the
055   gradient conflict among tasks in the context of supervised multi-task learning (MTL). For example,
056   multiple-gradient descent algorithm (MGDA) based methods Chen et al. (2023); Cheng et al. (2023)
057   dynamically updated the weights of tasks such that the deriving direction optimizes all objective
058   functions jointly instead of focusing only on tasks with dominant gradients. The similar idea was then
059   incorporated into various follow-up methods such as CAGrad, PCGrad, Nash-MTL and SDMGrad Yu
060   et al. (2020a); Liu et al. (2021); Navon et al. (2022); Xiao et al. (2023). Although these methods
061   have been also implemented in the MTRL setting, none of them provide a finite-time performance
062   guarantee. Then, an open question arises as:
063   Can we develop a dynamic weighting MTRL algorithm, which not only mitigates the gradient conflict
064   among tasks, but also achieves a solid finite-time convergence guarantee?
065
      However, addressing this question is not easy, primarily due to the difficulty in conducting sample
066
      complexity analysis for dynamic weighting MTRL algorithms. This challenge arises from the
067   presence of non-vanishing errors, including optimization errors (e.g., induced by actor-critic) and
068   function approximation error, in gradient estimation within MTRL. However, existing theoretical
069   analysis in the supervised MTL requires the gradient to be either unbiased Xiao et al. (2023); Chen
070   et al. (2023) or diminishing with iteration number Fernando et al. (2022). As a result, the analyses
071   applicable to the supervised setting cannot be directly employed in the MTRL setting, emphasizing
072   the necessity for novel developments in this context. Our specific contributions are summarized as
073   follows.

      1.1   C ONTRIBUTIONS
076
077
      In this paper, we provide an affirmative answer to the aforementioned question by proposing a
078
      novel Multi-Task Actor-Critic (MTAC) algorithm, and further developing the first-known sample
079
      complexity analysis for dynamic weighting MTRL.
080
081   Conflict-avoidant Multi-task actor-critic algorithm. Our proposed MTAC contains three major
082   components: the critic update, the task weight update, and the actor update. First, the critic update
083
      is to evaluate policies and then compute the policy gradients for all tasks. Second, we provide two
      options for updating the task weights. The first option aims to update the task weights such that the
084
      weighted direction is close to the CA direction (which is defined as the direction that maximizes
085
      the minimum value improvement among tasks). This option enhances the capability of our MTAC
086   to mitigate the gradient conflict among tasks, but at the cost of a slower convergence rate. As a
087   complement, we further provide the second option, which cannot ensure a small CA distance (i.e., the
088   distance to the CA direction as elaborated in Definition 3.1), but allows for a much faster convergence
089   rate. Third, by combining the policy gradients and task weights in the first and second steps, the actor
090   then performs an update on the policy parameter.
091
      Sample complexity analysis and CA distance guarantee. We provide a comprehensive sample
092   complexity analysis for the proposed MTAC algorithm under two options for updating task weights,
093   which we refer to as MTAC-CA and MTAC-FC (i.e., MTAC with fast convergence). For MTAC-CA,
094   our analysis shows that it requires O(ϵ−5 ) samples per task to attain an ϵ + ϵapp -accurate Pareto
                                                                                          √
095   stationary point (see definition in Definition 3.2), while guaranteeing a small ϵ + ϵapp -level CA
096   distance, where ϵapp corresponds to the inherent function approximation error and can be arbitrary
097   small when using a suitable feature function. The analysis for MTAC-FC shows that it can improve
098   the sample complexity of MTAC-FC from O(ϵ−5 ) to O(ϵ−3 ), but with a constant O(1)-level CA
099   distance. Note that this trade-off between the sampling complexity and CA distance is consistent
100
      with the observation in the supervised setting Chen et al. (2023).
101   Our primary technical contribution lies in the approximation of the CA direction. Instead of directly
102   bounding the gap between the weighted policy gradient dˆ and the CA direction d∗ as in the supervised
103   setting, which is challenging due to the gradient estimation bias, we construct a surrogate direction ds
104                                                                                          ˆ and ∥ds − d∗ ∥,
      that equals to the expectation of dˆ to decompose this gap into two distances as ∥ds − d∥
105   where the former one can be bounded similarly to the supervised case due to the unbiased estimation,

      the theoretical studies for both MTL and MTRL.

112   2    R ELATED WORKS
113   MTRL. Existing MTRL algorithms can be mainly categorized into two groups: single-policy MTRL
114   and multi-policy MTRL Vamplew et al. (2011); Liu et al. (2014). Single-policy methods generally
115   aim to find the optimal policy with given preference among tasks, and are often sample efficient and
116   easy to implement Yang et al. (2019). However, they may suffer from the issue of gradient conflict
117   among tasks. Multi-policy methods tend to learn a set of policies to approximate the Pareto front.
118   One commonly-used approach is to run a single-policy method for multiple times, each time with a
119
      different preference. For example, Zhou et al. (2020) proposed a model-based envelop value iteration
      (EVI) to explore the Pareto front with a given set of preferences. However, most MTRL works focus
120
      on the empirical performance of their methods Iqbal & Sha (2019); Zhang et al. (2021b); Christianos
121
      et al. (2022). In this paper, we propose a novel dynamic weighting MTRL method and further provide
122   a sample complexity analysis for it.
123
124   Actor-critic sample complexity analysis. The sample complexity analysis of the vanilla actor-critic
125
      algorithm with linear function approximation have been widely studied Qiu et al. (2021); Kumar et al.
      (2023); Xu et al. (2020); Barakat et al. (2022); Olshevsky & Gharesifard (2022). These works focus
126
      on the single-task RL problem. Some recent works Nian et al. (2020); Reymond et al. (2023); Zhang
127
      et al. (2021a) studied multi-task actor-critic algorithms but mainly on their empirical performance.
128   The theoretical analysis of multi-task actor-critic algorithms still remains open.
129
130   Gradient manipulation based MTL and theory. A variety of MGDA-based methods have been
131
      proposed to solve MTL problems because of their simplicity and effectiveness. One of their primal
      goals is to mitigate the gradient conflict among tasks. For example, PCGrad Yu et al. (2020a) avoided
132
      this conflict by projecting the gradient of each task on the norm plane of other tasks. GradDrop Chen
133
      et al. (2020) randomly dropped out conflicted gradients. CAGrad Liu et al. (2021) added a constraint
134   on the update direction to be close to the average gradient. Nash-MTL Navon et al. (2022) modeled
135   the MTL problem as a bargain game.
136
137
      Theoretically, Liu et al. (2014) analyzed the convergence of MGDA for convex objective functions.
      Fernando et al. (2022) proposed MoCo by estimating the true gradient with a tracking variable, and
138
      analyzed its convergence in both the convex and nonconvex settings. Chen et al. (2023) provided a
139
      theoretical characterization on the trade-off among optimization, generalization and conflict-avoidance
140   in MTL. Xiao et al. (2023) developed a provable MTL method named SDMGrad based on a double
141   sampling strategy, as well as a preference-oriented regularization. This paper provides the first-known
142   finite-time analysis for such type of methods in the MTRL setting.

535
536   In this paper, we propose two novel conflict-avoidant multi-task actor-critic algorithms named
537   MTAC-CA and MTAC-FC. We provide a comprehensive convergence rate and sample complexity
538   analysis for both algorithms, and demonstrate the tradeoff between a small CA distance and improved
539   sample complexity. Experiments validate our theoretical results. It is anticipated that our theoretical
      contribution and the proposed algorithms can be applied to broader MTRL setups.
