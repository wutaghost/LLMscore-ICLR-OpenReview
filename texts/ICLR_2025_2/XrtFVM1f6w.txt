009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            Graph neural networks (GNNs) are the dominant approach to solving machine
012            learning problems defined over graphs. Despite much theoretical and empirical
013            work in recent years, our understanding of finer-grained aspects of architectural
014            design for GNNs remains impoverished. In this paper, we consider the benefits
015            of architectures that maintain and update edge embeddings. On the theoretical
016            front, under a suitable computational abstraction for a layer in the model, as well
017            as memory constraints on the embeddings, we show that there are natural tasks
018
               on graphical models for which architectures leveraging edge embeddings can be
               much shallower. Our techniques are inspired by results on time-space tradeoffs in
019
               theoretical computer science. Empirically, we show architectures that maintain edge
020
               embeddings almost always improve on their node-based counterparts—frequently
021            significantly so in topologies that have “hub” nodes.

024   1    I NTRODUCTION
025
026   Graph neural networks (GNNs) have emerged as the dominant approach for solving machine learning
027   tasks on graphs. Over the span of the last decade, many different architectures have been proposed,
028   both in order to improve different notions of efficiency, and to improve performance on a variety
029   of benchmarks. Nevertheless, theoretical and empirical understanding of the impact of different
030   architectural design choices remains elusive.
031   One previous line of work (Xu et al., 2018) has focused on characterizing the representational
032   limitations stemming from the symmetry-preserving properties of GNNs when the node features are
033   not informative (also called “anonymous GNNs”) — in particular, relating GNNs to the Weisfeiler-
034   Lehman graph isomorphism test (Leman & Weisfeiler, 1968). Another line of work (Oono & Suzuki,
035   2019) focuses on the potential pitfalls of the (over)smoothing effect of deep GNN architectures, with
036   particular choices of weights and non-linearities, in an effort to explain the difficulties of training
037   deep GNN models. Yet another (Black et al., 2023) focuses on training difficulties akin to vanishing
038
      introduced by “bottlenecks” in the graph topology.
039   In this paper, we focus on the benefits of maintaining and updating edge embeddings over the course
040   of the computation of the GNN. More concretely, a typical GNN maintains a node embedding hv at
041   each node v of the underlying graph. In each layer of the GNN, the embedding at node v is updated
042   based on the embeddings at its neighbors. But an alternative paradigm is to maintain data at each
043   edge e of the graph, and to update this edge embedding based on the embeddings of the edges that
044
      share a node with e.
045   Intuitively, this paradigm seems at least as expressive as maintaining node embeddings, since in
046   principle each edge could maintain the embeddings of its incident nodes. Additionally, there may be
047   tasks where initial features are most naturally associated with edges (e.g., attributes of the relationship
048   between two nodes) — or the final predictions of the network are most naturally associated with
049   edges (e.g., in link prediction, where we want to decide which potential links are true links).
050   GNNs that fall in the general edge-based paradigm have been used for various applications – including
051   link prediction (Cai et al., 2021; Liang & Pu, 2023) as well as reasoning about relations between
052   objects (Battaglia et al., 2016), molecular property prediction (Gilmer et al., 2017; Choudhary
053   & DeCost, 2021), and detecting clusters of communities in graphs (Chen et al., 2017) – with
      robust empirical benefits. These approaches instantiate the edge-based paradigm in a plethora

054
      of ways. However, it is difficult to disentangle to what degree performance improvements come
055   from added information from domain-specific initial edge embeddings, versus properties of other
056   particular architectural choices, versus inherent benefits of the edge-based paradigm itself (whether
057   representational, or via improved training dynamics).
058
      We focus on theoretically and empirically quantifying the added representational benefit from
059
      maintaining edge embeddings. Viewing the GNN as a computational model, we can think of
060
      the intermediate embeddings as a “scratch pad”. Since we maintain more information per layer
061   compared to the node-based paradigm (1), we might intuitively hope to be able to use a shallower
062   edge embedding model. However, formally proving depth lower bounds both for general neural
063   networks (Telgarsky, 2016) and for specific architectures (Sanford et al., 2024b;a) frequently requires
064   non-trivial theoretical insights – as is the case for our question of interest. In this paper, we show that:
065
066   • Theoretically, for certain graph topologies, edge embeddings can have substantial representational
067
        benefits in terms of the depth of the model, when the amount of memory (i.e., total bit complexity)
        per node or edge embedding is bounded. Our results illuminate some subtleties of using particular
068
        lenses to understand design aspects of GNNs: for instance, we prove that taking memory into
069
        account reveals depth separations that the classical lens of invariance (Xu et al., 2018) alone cannot.
070
071
      • Empirically, when given the same input information, edge-based models almost always lead to
        performance improvements compared to their node-based counterpart — and often by a large
072
        margin if the graph topology includes “hub” nodes with high degree.

077   2.1   R EPRESENTATIONAL BENEFITS FROM MAINTAINING EDGE EMBEDDINGS .
078
      Our theoretical results elucidate the representational benefits of maintaining edge embeddings. More
079
      precisely, we show that there are natural tasks on graphs that can be solved by a shallow model
080
      maintaining constant-size edge embeddings, but can only be solved by a model maintaining constant-
081   size node embeddings if it is much deeper.
082
083   To reason about the impact of depth on the representational power of edge-embedding-based and
084
      node-embedding-based architectures, we introduce two local computation models. In the node-
      embedding case, we assume each node of the graph G supports a processor that maintains a state
085
      with a fixed amount of memory. In one round of computation, each node receives messages from the
086
      adjacent nodes, which are aggregated by the node into a new state. In this abstraction, we think of the
087   memory of the processor as the total bits of information each embedding can retain, and we think
088   of one round of the protocol as corresponding to one layer of a GNN. The edge-embedding case is
089   formalized in a similar fashion, except that the processors are placed on the edges of the graph, and
090   two edge processors are “adjacent” if the edges share a vertex in common. In both cases, the input is
091   distributed across the edges of the graph, and is only locally accessible.
092
      With this setup in mind, our first result focuses on probabilistic inference on graphs, specifically, the
093   task of maximum a-posteriori (MAP) estimation in a pairwise graphical model on a graph G = (V, E).
094   For this task, given edge attributes describing the pairwise interactions ϕ {a,b} , the goal is to compute

      The view from symmetry. Above, we are not imposing any symmetry constraints – that is,
109   invariance of the computation at a node or edge to its identity and the identities of its neighbors.
110   Indeed, the edge message-passing protocol constructed above is highly non-symmetric. However, we
111   show there is a (different, but also natural) task where even symmetric edge message-passing protocols
112   achieve a better depth/memory tradeoff than node message-passing protocols. See Theorem 4.

114
      Importance of the memory lens. The memory constraints are crucial for the results above. Without
115   memory constraints, we can show that the node message-passing architecture can simulate the edge
116   message-passing architecture, while only increasing the depth by 1 (Proposition 3). Moreover, the
117   symmetric node message-passing architecture can simulate the symmetric edge message-passing
118   architecture, again while only increasing the depth by 1. See Theorem 5.
119
      We view this as evidence that many fine-grained properties of architectural design for GNNs cannot
120
      be adjudicated by solely considering them through the lens of symmetries of the network.

123   2.2   E MPIRICAL BENEFITS OF EDGE - BASED ARCHITECTURES .
124
125   The theory, while only characterizing representational power, suggests that architectures that main-
126
      tain edge embeddings should have strictly better performance compared to their node embedding
      counterparts. We verify this in both real-life benchmarks and natural synthetic sandboxes.
127
128   First, we consider several popular GNN benchmarks (inspired by both predicting molecular properties,
129   and image-like data), and show that equalizing for all other aspects of the architecture (e.g., depth,
130   dimensionality of the embeddings) — the accuracy the edge-based architectures achieve is at least
131   as good as their node-based counterparts. Note, the goal of these experiments is not to propose
132
      a new architecture — there are already a variety of (very computationally efficient) GNNs that in
      some manner maintain edge embeddings. The goal is to confirm that — all other things being equal
133
      — the representational advantages of edge-based architectures do not introduce additional training
134
      difficulties. Details are included in Section 8.1.
135
136   Next, we consider two synthetic settings to stress test the performance of edge-based architectures.
137   Inspired by the graph topology that provides a theoretical separation between edge and node-based
138
      protocols (Theorem 1 and Theorem 4), we consider graphs in which there is a hub node, and tasks
      that are “naturally” solved by an edge-based architecture. Precisely, we consider a star graph, in
139
      which the labels on the leaves are generated by a “planted” edge-based architecture with randomly
140
      chosen weights. The node-based architecture, on the other hand, has to pass messages between the
141   leaves indirectly through the center of the star. Empirically, we indeed observe that the performance
142   of edge-based architectures is significantly better. Details are included in Section 8.2
143
144
      Finally, again inspired by the theoretical setting in Theorem 1, we consider probabilistic inference
      on tree graphs — precisely, learning a GNN that calculates node marginals for an Ising model, a
145
      pairwise graphical model in which the pairwise interactions are just the product of the end points. An
146
      added motivation for this setting is the fact that belief propagation — a natural algorithm to calculate
147   the marginals — can be written as an edge-based message-passing algorithm. Again, empirically we
148   see that edge-based architectures perform at least as well as node-based architectures. This advantage
149   is maintained even if we consider “directed” versions of both architectures, in which case embeddings
150   are maintained to be sent along each direction of the edge, and the message for the outgoing direction
151   of an edge depends only on the embeddings corresponding to the incoming directions of the edges.
152   Details are included in Section 8.3.

      3     R ELATED W ORKS
156   The symmetry lens on GNNs: The most extensive theoretical work on GNNs has concerned itself
157   with the representational power of different GNN architectures, while trying to preserve equivariance
158   (to permuting the neighbors) of each layer. (Xu et al., 2018) connected the expressive power of such
159   architectures to the Weisfeiler-Lehman (WL) test for graph isomorphism. Subsequent works (Maron
160   et al., 2019; Zhao et al., 2021) focused on strengthening the representational power of the standard
161   GNN architectures from the perspective of symmetries—more precisely, to simulate the k-WL test,
      which for k as large as the size of the graph becomes as powerful as testing graph isomorphism. Our


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      work suggests that this perspective may be insufficient to fully understand the representational power
163   of different architectures.
164
165   GNNs as a computational machine: Two recent papers (Loukas, 2019; 2020) considered properties
166
      of GNNs when viewed as “local computation” machines, in which a layer of computation allows a
      node to aggregate the current values of the neighbors (in an arbitrary fashion, without necessarily
167
      considering symmetries). Using reductions from the CONGEST model, they provide lower bounds
168
      on width and depth for the standard node-embedding based architecture. However, they do not
169   consider architectures with edge embeddings, which is a focus of our work.
170
171   Communication complexity methods to prove representational separations: Tools from dis-
172
      tributed computation and communication complexity have recently been applied not only to under-
      stand the representational power of GNNs (Loukas, 2019; 2020), but also the representational power
173
      of other architectures like transformers (Sanford et al., 2024b;a). In particular, (Sanford et al., 2024a)
174
      draws a connection between number of rounds for a MPC (Massively Parallel Computation) protocol,
175   and the depth of attention-based architectures.
176
177   GNNs for inference and graphical models: The paper (Xu & Zou, 2023) considers the approx-
178
      imation power of GNNs for calculating marginals for pairwise graphical models, if the family of
      potentials satisfies strong symmetry constraints. They do not consider the role of edge embeddings or
179
      memory.

      the “flow” of a function, which quantifies the existence of subsets of coordinates, such that setting
338
      them to some value, and varying the remaining variables results in many possible outputs. In our
339   case, the choice of subsets is inherently tied to the topology of the graph G. Our technique is also
340   inspired by and closely related to the “light cone” technique for proving round lower bounds in the
341   LOCAL computation model (Linial, 1992). However, our technique takes advantage of bottlenecks
342   in the graph to prove stronger lower bounds (which would be impossible in the LOCAL model where
343   memory constraints are ignored).
344
345   The proof of Part 1 of Theorem 1 now follows from an application of Lemma 2 with a particular
346   choice of K and S. Specifically, we choose K to be the “hub” node (i.e. K = {0}) and S to be the
347
      set of left endpoints of each path. To show that any MAP evaluator has large information flow to
      S (in the quantitative sense of Lemma 2), it suffices to observe that in a pairwise graphical model
348
      on G where a different external field is applied to the right endpoint of each path, and all pairwise
349
      interactions along paths are positive, the MAP estimate on each vertex in S must match the external
350   field on the corresponding right endpoint.
351
352
      Upper bound for edge message-passing protocols: The key observation for constructing a
353
      constant-round edge message-passing protocol for MAP estimation on G is that all of the input data
354   can be collected on the edges adjacent to the hub vertex. At this point, every such edge has access to
355   all of the input data, and hence can evaluate the function. If G were an arbitrary graph, this final step
356   would potentially be NP-hard. However, since the induced subgraph after removing the hub vertex
357   is a disjoint union of paths, in fact there is a linear-time dynamic programming algorithm for MAP
358   estimation on G (Lemma 6). This completes the proof overview for Theorem 1.
359
      The separation discussed above crucially relies on the existence of a high-degree vertex in G. When
360   the maximum degree of G is bounded by some parameter ∆, it turns out that any edge message-
361   passing protocol can be simulated by a node message-passing protocol with roughly the same number
362   of rounds and only a ∆ factor more memory per processor. The idea is for each node to simulate the
363   computation that would have been performed (in the edge message-passing protocol) on the adjacent
364   edges. The following proposition formalizes this idea (proof in Appendix A):
365   Proposition 3. Let T, B ≥ 1. Let G = (V, E) be a graph with maximum degree ∆. Let P be an
366   edge message-passing protocol on G with T rounds and B bits of memory. Then there is a node
367   message-passing protocol P ′ on G that computes P with T + 1 rounds and O(∆B) bits of memory.

370   6    D EPTH SEPARATION UNDER MEMORY AND SYMMETRY CONSTRAINTS
371
372   One drawback of the separation in the previous section is that the constructed edge protocol was
373   highly non-symmetric, whereas in practice GNN protocols are typically architecturally constrained to
374   respect the symmetries of the underlying graph. In this section we prove that there is a separation
375
      between the memory/round trade-offs for node and edge message-passing protocols even under
      additional symmetry constraints.
376
377   Theorem 4. Let n ∈ N. There is a graph G = (V, E) with O(n) vertices and O(n) edges, and a
      function g : {0, 1}E → {0, 1}V , so that:

382      memory that computes g.
383
384   For intuition, we sketch the proof of a relaxed version of the theorem where the input alphabet is [n].
385   It is conceptually straightforward to adapt the construction to binary alphabet (essentially, by adding
386
      new vertices and using a unary encoding). We defer the full proof to Appendix B.
387   Let G = (V, E) be a star graph with root node 0 and leaves {1, . . . , n}. We define a function
388   g : [n]E → {0, 1}V by g(I)v = 1 if and only if there is some edge e ̸= {0, v} such that I(e) =
389   I({0, v}), i.e. the input on edge {0, v} equals the input on some other edge. Since g is defined to
390   be equivariant to relabelling the edges, and all edges are incident to each other, it is straightforward
391   to see that there is a symmetric one-round edge message-passing protocol that computes g with
392
      O(log n) memory (in contrast, the edge message-passing protocol constructed in Section 5 was not
      symmetric, as it required that the edges incident to the high-degree vertex were labelled by which path
393
      they belonged to). However, there is no low-memory, low-round node message-passing algorithm.
394
      Informally, this is because vertex 0 is an information bottleneck, and Ω(n) bits of information need
395   to pass through it. Similar to in Section 5, this intuition can be made formal using Lemma 2.

398   7   S YMMETRY ALONE PROVIDES NO SEPARATION
399
400   In the previous sections we saw that examining memory constraints yields a separation between
401   different GNN architectures (whether or not we take symmetry into consideration). In this section,
402   we consider what happens if we solely consider symmetry constraints (that is, constraints imposed by
403   requiring that the computation in a round of the protocol is invariant to permutations of the order of
404   the neighbors). This viewpoint was initiated by Xu et al. (2018), who showed that when the initial
405
      node features are uninformative (that is, the same for each node), a standard GNN necessarily outputs
      the same answer for two graphs that are 1-Weisfeiler-Lehman equivalent (that is, graphs that cannot
406
      be distinguished by the Weisfeiler-Lehman test, even though they may not be isomorphic).
407
408   To be precise, we revisit the representational power of symmetric GNN architectures in the setting
409   where the input features may be distinct and informative. We show that if we remove the memory
410   constraints from Section 5, but impose permutation invariance for the computation in each round,
411
      any function that is computable by a T -layer edge message-passing protocol can be computed
      by a (T + 1)-layer node message-passing protocol. Note that this statement is incomparable to
412
      Proposition 3 because we impose constraints on symmetry, but remove constraints on memory.
413
414   Theorem 5 (No separation under symmetry constraints). Let T ≥ 1. Let P be a symmetric edge
415   message-passing protocol (Definition 6) on graph G = (V, E) with T rounds. Then there is a
416
      (T + 1)-round symmetric node message-passing protocol (Definition 5) P ′ on G that computes the
      same function as P .
417
418   Remark 10. Theorem 5 and its proof are closely related to the fact that the 1-Weisfeiler-Lehman
419   test is equivalent to the 2-Weisfeiler-Lehman test, which was reintroduced in the context of higher-
420   order GNNs (Huang & Villar, 2021). However, the k-Weisfeiler-Lehman test only characterizes the
421
      representational power of k-GNNs with uninformative input features (i.e. that are identical for all
      nodes). Theorem 5 shows that even with arbitrary input features on the edges, the computation of
422
      a GNN with edge embeddings and symmetric updates can be simulated by a GNN with only node
423
      embeddings, without losing symmetry.
424
425   To prove Theorem 5, note that it suffices to simulate the protocol P for which the update rules
426   f sym , f˜sym in Definition 6 are identity functions on the appropriate domains. In order to simulate
427   P , we construct a symmetric node message-passing protocol P ′ for which the computation at time
428   t + 1 and node v on input I is the multiset of features computed by P at time t at edges adjacent to v:
429   Qt (v; I) := {{Pt (e; I) : e ∈ M(v)}}. This is possible since the computation of P at time t and edge
430   e = (u, v) is Pt (e; I) = (I(e), Pt−1 (e; I), {{Qt−1 (u; I), Qt−1 (v; I)}}). The node message-passing
431   protocol is tracking Qt−1 (·; I); moreover, it can recursively compute Pt−1 (e; I) using the same
      formula. See Appendix C for the formal proof.

531   9     C ONCLUSIONS AND FUTURE WORK
532
533
      Graph neural networks are the best-performing machine learning method for many tasks over graphs.
534   There is a wide variety of GNN architectures, which frequently make opaque design choices and
535   whose causal influence on the final performance is difficult to understand and estimate. In this paper,
536   we focused on understanding the impact of maintaining edge embeddings on the representational
537   power, as well as the subtleties of considering constraints like memory and invariance. One significant
538   downside of maintaining edge embeddings is the computational overhead on dense graphs. Hence, a
539   fruitful direction for future research would be to explore more computationally efficient variants of
      edge-based architectures that preserve their representational power and performance.

1151   The belief-propagation updates (7) naturally fit the general edge-message passing paradigm from
1152   (2). In fact, they fit even more closely a “directed” version of the paradigm, in which each edge
1153   {i, j} maintains two embeddings hi→j , hj→i , such that the embedding for direction hi→j depends
1154
       on the embeddings {hk→i }{k,i}∈E . With this modification to the standard edge GCN architecture

1155
       functions and weight matrices W (since, in particular, in our dataset all edge potentials Ji,j are set
1156
       to 1). Similarly, with a directed version of the node GCN architecture Eq. (3), where each node
1157   maintains an “up” embedding as well as a “down” embedding, it is straightforward to implement the
1158   “node-based” dynamic programming solution (8)-(9).

1162
