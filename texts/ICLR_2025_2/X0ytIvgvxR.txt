009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            We study a distributed Principal Component Analysis (PCA) framework where
012            each worker targets a distinct eigenvector and refines its solution by updating from
013            intermediate solutions provided by peers deemed as “superior”. Drawing intuition
014            from the delation methods, which is traditionally used in centralized eigenvalue
015            problems, our method breaks the sequential dependency in between the deflation
016            steps and allows asynchronous updates of workers while incurring only a small
017            communication cost. To our knowledge, a critical gap in the literature –the the-
018
               oretical underpinning of such distributed, dynamic interactions among workers–
               has remained unaddressed until now. This paper offers the first theoretical analy-
019
               sis explaining why, how, and when these intermediate, hierarchical updates lead to
020
               practical and provable convergence in distributed environments. Our theoretical
021            contributions demonstrate that such a distributed PCA algorithm not only con-
022            verges effectively but does so in a manner that is favorably scalable. We also
023            demonstrate through experiments that our proposed framework offers comparable
024            performance to EigenGame-µ, the state-of-the-art model-parallel PCA solver.

029   Currently, datasets have gotten dramatically large, encompassing billions, if not trillions, of entries
030   spanning various domains Zhong et al. (2019); Liu et al. (2023); Penedo et al. (2024a); Soldaini et al.
031   (2024); Wang et al. (2022); Schuhmann et al. (2022); Raffel et al. (2020); Penedo et al. (2024b);
032   Xue et al. (2020); Abadji et al. (2022). This scale made it necessary to advance various distributed
033
      optimization protocols, such as federated learning Brendan et al. (2016), and, notably, the develop-
      ment of multiple distributed ML software packages Kim & Kang; Dean et al. (2012). Specialized
034
      frameworks such as Ray Liang et al. (2018), Spark Meng et al. (2016), Hadoop Apache Software
035
      Foundation, and JAX Bradbury et al. (2018) have surged in popularity due to their ability to enhance
036   computational speed significantly.
037
038   However, at the algorithmic level, most distributed implementations try to simulate the behavior of
039
      the centralized versions of the underlying algorithms. That is, how distributed algorithms navigate
      the parameter landscape is often designed such that we achieve a similar outcome as if data is
040
      available in one location. There are a few key reasons for this:
041
042   • Mathematical Understanding: When there is sufficient theoretical understanding of the central-
043     ized version, it is often a desired goal to attain the same result by designing algorithms to emulate
044
        the centralized counterparts. This ensures consistency and theoretical understanding.
045
      • Algorithm Simplicity: Since centralized algorithms are better understood, distributed variants
        that replicate the algorithms’ outcomes automatically enjoy the same simplicity and interpretation.
046
047   • Benchmarking: By simulating the centralized execution, comparing the accuracy and conver-
048     gence properties of the distributed implementation in practice becomes easier.
049   Yet, precisely simulating centralized algorithms in a distributed environment could pose some chal-
050   lenges. Take as a characteristic feature the notion of synchrony in distributed implementations, as
051   this leads to training dynamics closer to centralized training. Synchronization among workers means
052   proper orchestration, especially in large-scale settings with high-dimensional models and datasets
053   Zeng et al. (2024); Tan et al. (2022). Synchronized implementations that wait for some or all workers
      to finish each iteration before proceeding can suffer from stragglers and load imbalance Wang et al.

      Based on the dilemma between orchestrating (or not) workers in a distributed system, this work
      focuses on a relatively simple problem: the Principal Component Analysis (PCA) Pearson (1901);
059
      Hotelling (1933); Wold et al. (1987); Majumdar (2009); Wang et al. (2013); d’Aspremont et al.
060
      (2007); Jiang et al. (2011); Zou et al. (2006) and its distributed implementation. Despite its “sim-
061   plicity”, the quest for a distributed PCA algorithm is still an active research area. It has recently
062   gained momentum with the EigenGame implementation Gemp et al. (2020). EigenGame shows
063   strides toward optimizing PCA for distributed computing environments, with ideas borrowed from
064   game theory and implementations that mimic centralized versions. To retain its theoretical guaran-
065   tees, EigenGame follows a strict hierarchy where each worker is responsible for a single component,
066   and all workers respect hierarchy by waiting for their “superior” principal components (i.e., eigen-
067   components associated with larger eigenvalues) to be adequately estimated.2
068   Our approach and contributions. This work advances distributed PCA by building upon a collab-
069   orative computation model as in Gemp et al. (2020). Unlike traditional distributed PCA approaches
070   that mimic centralized algorithms, our method innovates by allowing parallel computation of eigen-
071   vectors without strict sequential dependencies among the workers. This paradigm shift not only
072   addresses the inherent inefficiencies of previous methods but also enhances the scalability and con-
073   vergence speed. Herein, we delineate our primary contributions:
074   • Novel Algorithmic Framework: We propose a novel distributed PCA algorithm that fundamentally
075     changes the computational dynamics. Using the covariance matrix, our approach enables multiple
076     workers to perform eigenvector calculations in parallel. This method diverges from the traditional
077     sequential computation models, significantly reducing total computation time.
078   • Extension to Stochastic PCA: in cases where the covariance is unknown or cannot be efficiently
079     estimated, our algorithm can be easily modified to accommodate data that comes in mini-batches.
080   • Theoretical Advancements: We provide a robust theoretical framework that validates the con-
081     vergence properties of our proposed algorithm. By formalizing the interaction between parallel
082     computations and convergence rates, we establish a new theoretical benchmark for distributed
083     PCA algorithms. This contribution underscores our algorithm’s efficiency and enhances the un-
084     derstanding of parallel deflation processes in PCA.
085   • Empirical Validation: Through extensive experiments, we demonstrate the practical efficacy of
086
        our algorithm. Our results show that our approach at least meets the performance of existing
        baseline algorithms even on datasets as large as ImageNet Deng et al. (2009). These experiments
087
        substantiate our theoretical claims and highlight the real-world applicability of our method.
088
089   These contributions mark a significant step forward in distributed computing for PCA, providing
090   theoretical insights and practical tools for data analysis applications.

092   1.1 R ELATED W ORKS
093   Centralized approaches. Principal Component Analysis (PCA) has been a cornerstone of statistical
094   data analysis since 1901 Pearson (1901). Hotelling later expanded on Pearson’s work, formalizing
095   PCA within a multivariate analysis framework Hotelling (1933). Classical PCA typically involves
096   the eigendecomposition of the data covariance matrix Jolliffe (2002).
097   With the advent of large datasets, iterative and gradient-based methods for PCA have gained promi-
098   nence. These methods are particularly advantageous for large-scale data, where traditional eigende-
099   composition becomes computationally impractical. Krasulina and Oja & Karhunen proposed two of
100   the earliest stochastic gradient descent methods for online PCA Krasulina (1969); Oja & Karhunen
101   (1985). The application of the least square minimization to the PCA has also received attention
102   Miao & Hua (1998); Yang (1995); Bannour & Azimi-Sadjadi (1995); Kung et al. (1994). More re-
103   cently, Arora et al. (2012) and Shamir (2015) have proposed efficient stochastic optimization meth-

106   inance of synchronized methods in neural network training Campos et al. (2017); Chen et al. (2017).

128   a Riemannian gradient-type method that admits low per-iteration computational and communication
129

132
133   The papers above consider the data-parallel setting, where the data is distributed across machines,
134   and each worker solve for all the principal components with its local data. DeepMind’s EigenGame
135
      Gemp et al. (2020) introduced a model-parallel approach, framing each principal component as a
      player in a collaborative game. EigenGame optimizes the utility of each vector sequentially using
136
      Riemannian gradient ascent, but is also extended to the distributed scenario, where players can
137
      maximize their utility simultaneously, resulting in a model-parallel algorithm where solving each
138   principal component is distributed across machines. The paper provided a convergence proof for the
139   sequential process where vectors are optimized in a hierarchical order. However, for the distributed
140   version, they didn’t analyze how approximate steps affect overall convergence. A later improvement
141   Gemp et al. (2022) was proposed, but also lacks theoretical guarantees for the distributed setting.
142
      Our work complements existing literature both theoretically and practically. Unlike sequential ap-
143
      proaches, our method does not require the completion of previous principal component computations
144   before proceeding to the next. Moreover, We provide a comprehensive convergence analysis, estab-
145   lishing a stronger theoretical foundation than EigenGame, while maintaining practical efficiency.

      Sketch of Proof. The key challenge in proving Theorem 2 lies in handling the dynamic, asyn-
398   chronous nature of our algorithm. Unlike sequential deflation, where each principal component is
399   computed after the previous ones have converged and stays fixed, our method deals with simulta-
400   neous updates of all principal components. This requires careful analysis of how errors propagate
401   and accumulate across different workers. To start, we derive upper bounds of the per-iteration dif-
402   ference between the actual deflated matrix Σk and the ideal deflated matrix Σ∗k and the difference
403   between the estimated eigenvector vk,ℓ and the ground-truth eigenvector u∗k . Notice that these two
404   upper bounds are inter-dependent. We apply Davis-Kahan SinΘ Theorem Davis & Kahan (1970) to
405   derive the bound between the matrices’ top-eigenvectors based on the matrix differences. Next, we
406
      carefully choose a convergence starting point sk for each eigenvector. Construct two simpler two-
      dimensional sequences {Bk,ℓ } and {Gk,ℓ } starting from sk ’s that upper bound these differences.
407
      Lastly, we unroll the bounds on {Bk,ℓ } and {Gk,ℓ } to arrive at a closed form upper bound on error
408
      of the estimated eigenvector vk,ℓ . The detailed proof is deferred to Appendix B.

530
531
      In this paper, we present a novel algorithmic framework for computing the principal components in
      a distributed fashion. Based on the classical deflation method to solve for the top-K eigenvectors of
532
      a matrix, our algorithm distributes the workload of computing each eigenvector to a single worker.
533
      We introduce additional parallelism by early-starting the computation of the following eigenvectors
534   based on the initial rough estimation of leading principal components and continuously refining the
535   local deflated matrix based on updated estimated principal components. We show that our algo-
536   rithmic framework has a similar game-theoretic formulation as the EigenGame, while enjoying a
537   nice convergence guarantee even in the distributed case. Moreover, through experiments, we show
538   that our algorithm converges at a comparable speed to the EigenGame-µ, and is faster than the
539   EigenGame-α. Future work can focus on empirically examining the potential of using other Top1
      subroutines in our parallel deflation algorithm, such as Oja’s rule.
