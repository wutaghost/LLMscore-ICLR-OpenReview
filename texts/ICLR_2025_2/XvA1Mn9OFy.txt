                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
010            Sharpness-Aware Minimization (SAM) algorithms have effectively improved neu-
011
               ral network generalization by steering model parameters away from sharp regions
               of the training loss landscape, which tend to generalize poorly. However, the un-
012
               derlying mechanisms of SAM are not fully understood, and recent studies ques-
013
               tion whether its bias toward flatter regions is why it improves generalization. In
014            this work, we introduce Generalization-Aware Minimization (GAM), a general-
015            ized version of SAM that employs multiple perturbation steps instead of SAMвАЩs
016            single-step perturbations. This allows GAM to directly guide model parameters
017            toward regions of the landscape that generalize better. We show that the expected
018            true (test) loss landscape is a rescaled version of the observed training loss land-
019            scape and demonstrate how GAMвАЩs multiple perturbative updates can be designed
020            to optimize this expected test loss. Finally, we present a practical online algorithm
021            that adapts GAMвАЩs perturbative steps during training to improve generalization,
022
               and we empirically validate its superior performance over SAM on benchmark
               datasets. We believe that GAM provides valuable insights into how sharpness-
023
               based algorithms improve generalization and can inspire the development of opti-
024
               mizers with even better generalization.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Generalization is a fundamental challenge in training deep neural networks, where the goal is to
031   perform well on unseen data rather than just fitting the training set. One promising approach to
032
      enhance generalization is Sharpness-Aware Minimization (SAM) (Foret et al., 2021), which has
      empirically demonstrated success by guiding model parameters away from sharp minima in the
033
      training loss landscape. The underlying intuition is that flatter minima correspond to solutions that
034
      are less sensitive to perturbations and thus generalize better to new data.
035
036   Despite its empirical effectiveness, the theoretical understanding of why SAM improves generaliza-
037   tion remains limited. Recent studies have questioned whether SAMвАЩs bias toward flatter regions is
038
      the primary reason for its success (Wen et al., 2023). This ambiguity highlights the need for a deeper
      exploration of the mechanisms through which SAM and similar algorithms enhance generalization.
039
      Understanding the mechanisms behind SAM is crucial for developing more effective optimization
040
      algorithms that consistently improve generalization across various architectures and datasets.
041
042   In this work, we adopt a Bayesian perspective to investigate what the observed training loss land-
043   scape reveals about the expected test loss. Specifically, we consider the relationship between the
044
      training and test loss landscapes in the context of quadratic loss functions. Our analysis reveals that
      the expected test loss landscape is a rescaled version of the training loss landscape. This rescal-
045
      ing implies that directly optimizing the expected test loss could lead to better generalization than
046
      indirectly promoting flatness.
047
048   Building on this insight, we introduce Generalization-Aware Minimization (GAM), a generalization
049   of SAM that employs multiple perturbation steps designed to transform the observed training loss
050
      landscape to the rescaled expected test loss landscape. GAM moves beyond SAMвАЩs heuristic of
      flatness by directly targeting the expected test loss, thereby enabling better generalization. Moreover,
051
      we develop a practical online algorithm that adapts the perturbation sizes during training by using
052
      the training loss on auxiliary minibatches as a proxy for the test loss.
053
      Our contributions are as follows:

072
073   2.1   S HARPNESS -AWARE M INIMIZATION (SAM) A LGORITHMS
074
075
      Sharpness-Aware Minimization (SAM) algorithms were introduced to improve the generalization of
      neural networks by favoring solutions in flatter regions of the training loss landscape, which have
076
      empirically been linked to better generalization performance (Foret et al., 2021). SAM perturbs
077
      model parameters in the direction of the loss gradient and then optimizes using a second gradient
078   step, effectively minimizing the sharpness of the loss function. Numerous extensions and variants of
079   SAM have since been proposed, focusing on improving computational efficiency and generalization
080   (Mi et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2022a;b; Du et al., 2022a;b; Li et al., 2024).
081
      Despite its empirical success, the theoretical understanding of SAM remains limited and an active
082
      area of research (Andriushchenko et al., 2023; Zhuang et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2024; Si & Yun,
083
      2024; Dai et al., 2024). Recent studies have raised questions about whether SAMвАЩs generalization
084   improvements stem directly from its bias toward flatter regions of the loss landscape. For instance,
085   some works argue that SAMвАЩs effectiveness may not always be directly attributable to sharpness,
086   but instead to other implicit regularization effects introduced by the perturbation procedure (Wen
087   et al., 2023; Andriushchenko & Flammarion, 2022). Our work builds on this debate by introducing
088   a generalized framework that moves beyond the sharpness heuristic and directly targets the expected
089   test loss.
090
091   2.2   BAYESIAN O PTIMIZATION
092
093   Bayesian optimization is a well-established framework for optimizing functions that are expensive
094   to evaluate, and it has been successfully applied in hyperparameter tuning and low-dimensional op-
095   timization problems (Snoek et al., 2012; Frazier, 2018). The fundamental principle of Bayesian
096   optimization is to maintain a probabilistic model of the objective function and update it using new
097   observations, guiding the search toward areas of the input space that are likely to yield better out-
098
      comes.
099   While Bayesian optimization has shown promise in various applications, its applicability to high-
100   dimensional settings, such as neural network training, has been limited. Methods that rely on Gaus-
101   sian processes or other surrogate models struggle to scale due to the curse of dimensionality and high
102   computational costs. Although some efforts have extended Bayesian optimization to use gradient-
103   based information for more scalable updates (Wu & Frazier, 2016; Wu et al., 2017; Shekhar &
104
      Javidi, 2021), these approaches have yet to achieve widespread practical adoption in deep learning
      beyond hyperparameter optimization.
105
106   Our approach draws inspiration from Bayesian principles by modeling the relationship between
107   training and test losses. We leverage this perspective to derive a more direct optimization strategy
      for neural networks.

248   3.4   A P RACTICAL O NLINE A LGORITHM TO L EARN P ERTURBATIONS
249
250   While the theoretical results are derived for quadratic losses, we may extend the approach to general
251   loss functions by considering local quadratic approximations. We propose a practical algorithm that
252
      adapts the perturbation sizes during training to improve generalization.
253
254
      Algorithm Design Based on Theorem 2, we design the Generalization-Aware Minimization
      (GAM) algorithm. GAM uses multiple perturbation steps with higher-order derivatives and tunes
255
      the perturbation coefficients ќ≥t online during training. This allows GAM to approximate gradients
256
      of the expected test loss instead of using training loss gradients as illustrated in Figure 1. The key
257   components of GAM are:
258
259          вАҐ Higher-Order Perturbations: We compute a sequence of directional derivatives Dt (ќЄ) to
260            capture higher-order information about the loss landscape.
261          вАҐ Adaptive Perturbation Sizes: We update the perturbation coefficients ќ≥t by minimizing a
262            discrepancy function вИЖ between the gradient on perturbed parameters and an estimate of
263            the test loss gradient; in practice, we use negative dot product as our discrepancy measure.
264          вАҐ Auxiliary Minibatches: We use the training loss on auxiliary minibatches as a proxy for
265            the test loss to guide the adaptation of ќ≥t .

      directions.

481   similar to SAM. As GAM is tuned specifically to optimize for generalization, the results suggest
482
      that SAM generalizes well because of its similarity to GAM.
483   Interestingly, GAMвАЩs transformation exhibits a higher contrast between small and large curvature
484   directions than SAM, selectively sharpening sharp directions while maintaining others. This more
485   complex behavior arises from the higher-order gradient information used by GAM, ultimately yield-
      ing better generalization.

      4.4   M ITIGATING GAMвАЩ S COMPUTATIONAL COST
505
      One of GAMвАЩs disadvantages is its computational cost relative to SGD and SAM: it requires com-
506
      puting the derivative of parameter updates with respect to perturbation coefficients ќ≥t which can
507   be quite costly. We propose mitigating this cost by updating the ќ≥t periodically instead of at each
508   training step as done in Algorithm 1.
509
510
      As shown in Figure 4, when updating the ќ≥t every time step, GAMвАЩs computational cost is roughly
      4√Ч that of SGD (relative to roughly 1.3√Ч for SAM). However, this cost can be reduced to roughly
511
      3√Ч when updating the perturbation coefficients periodically. Although this reduces test accuracy,
512
      the accuracy of GAM still exceeds that of SAM or SGD.

515   5     D ISCUSSION
516
517   In this work, we introduced Generalization-Aware Minimization (GAM), a novel optimization al-
518   gorithm that directly targets the expected test loss by using higher-order gradient information and
519   adaptive perturbations. Unlike Sharpness-Aware Minimization (SAM) algorithms, which rely on
520   the heuristic that flatter regions of the loss landscape generalize better, GAM is grounded in a theo-
521   retical framework that aligns the optimization process with the expected test loss. By demonstrating
522   that the expected test loss landscape is a rescaled version of the observed training loss landscape for
523   quadratic losses, we provided a principled approach to improve generalization.
524   The surprising similarity between the update mechanisms of SAM and GAM offers a new perspec-
525   tive on why SAM improves generalization. Our analysis suggests that SAM may implicitly approx-
526   imate the expected test loss through its single-step perturbations, which could explain its empirical
527   success. However, GAMвАЩs use of multiple perturbation steps and higher-order derivatives allows it
528   to more accurately capture the transformation between the training and test loss landscapes. Our
529   empirical results on benchmark datasets confirm that GAM consistently outperforms SAM, high-
530
      lighting the benefits of directly optimizing for generalization.
531   While GAM shows promising results, it relies on using higher-order derivatives, which may be
532   computationally challenging for large or non-differentiable networks. Future work could explore
533   approximations or scalable implementations of higher-order derivatives.
534   We believe that GAM opens new avenues for developing optimization algorithms that can further
535   enhance generalization in deep learning models, potentially leading to more robust and reliable AI
536   systems. By incorporating higher-order gradient information and adaptive strategies, future optimiz-
537   ers can more effectively navigate the loss landscape to find solutions that generalize well. We hope
538   that our work inspires further research into optimization techniques that bridge theoretical insights
539   and practical performance, ultimately contributing to the advancement of generalization in machine
      learning.
