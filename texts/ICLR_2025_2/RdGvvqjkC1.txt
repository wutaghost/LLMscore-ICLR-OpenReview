009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Jailbreak attacks, where malicious prompts bypass generative models’ built-in
012            safety, have raised significant concerns about model vulnerability. While diverse
013            defense methods have been proposed, the underlying mechanisms governing the
014            trade-offs between model safety and helpfulness, and their application to Large
015            Vision-Language Models (LVLMs) remain insufficiently explored. This paper
016            systematically investigates jailbreak defense mechanisms by reformulating the
017            standard generation task as a binary classification problem to probe model refusal
018
               tendencies across both harmful and benign queries. Our analysis identifies two key
               defense mechanisms: safety shift, which generally increases refusal probabilities
019
               for all queries, and harmfulness discrimination, which enhances the model’s ability
020
               to distinguish between benign and harmful queries. Leveraging these mechanisms,
021            we design two ensemble defense strategies—inter-mechanism and intra-mechanism
022            ensembles—to explore the safety-helpfulness balance. Empirical evaluations on
023            the MM-SafetyBench and MOSSBench datasets on top of LLaVA-1.5 models
024            demonstrate the effectiveness of these ensemble approaches in either enhancing
025            model safety or achieving an improved safety-utility balance. These findings offer
026            valuable insights into jailbreak defense strategies and contribute to the develop-
027            ment of more resilient LVLM safety systems. WARNING: This paper contains
028            potentially offensive and harmful text.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Recent advancements in Large Language Models (LLMs) has demonstrated remarkable and versatile
      generative capabilities, enabling widespread application across various domains Gupta et al. (2023);
034
      OpenAI (2023); Dubey et al. (2024). However, as their instruction-following ability increases, these
035
      models have become targets of adversarial attacks, raising significant safety concerns Bommasani et al.
036   (2021). One prominent issue is the generation of harmful content when facing jailbreak attack Huang
037   et al. (2023); Liu et al. (2023e); Wei et al. (2023), where malicious users craft prompt to bypass the
038   model’s internal safety mechanism. Additionally, the emergence of Large Vision-Language Models
039   (LVLMs) Bai et al. (2023); Liu et al. (2023a); Li et al. (2023a) has introduced further vulnerabilities
040   as these models interact with a broader range of input channels Gu et al. (2024); Wang et al. (2024a).
041
      To counter the diverse landscape of jailbreak attacks, a range of defense strategies have been proposed.
042   These include modifying system prompts Zhang et al. (2023b); Xie et al. (2023), intervening in
043   model training or decoding processes Qi et al. (2023); Xu et al. (2024b), and processing input
044   queries and images Zhang et al. (2023a); Ji et al. (2024); Wang et al. (2024b). These methods
045   present distinct advantages and limitations—some enhance safety at the cost of over-defense Jiang
046   et al. (2024), potentially compromising the model’s helpfulness, while others offer limited and non-
047   robust improvements in model safety. Imperceptible, minor changes to input queries can circumvent
048   defenses and lead to failures. A systematic understanding of the underlying mechanisms driving these
049   trade-offs and a mechanistic comparison of different methods remain underexplored, particularly in
050
      challenging multimodal contexts. Moreover, how to effectively integrate multiple defense strategies
      to achieve a better safety-helpfulness balance remains an open challenge.
051
052   In this work, we investigate the mechanisms behind jailbreak defense methods by reformulating
053   the original generative task as a classification task, considering the trade-off between safety and
      helpfulness Wei et al. (2024); Madry
                                       ˛ et al. (2017). The classification task probes the model’s internal

070   preference to either refuse or comply with the input query based on safety considerations, treating
071   refusal and compliance as binary classification labels. Specifically, we use two probing subsets in
072   multimodal domains—one with harmful queries and one with benign queries—and compare the
073   defense model’s refusal probabilities on both subsets against those of the original non-defense model.
074   Then the problem space can be viewed as a classification plane, where different defense models
075
      correspond to various decision boundaries among data points from both subsets, represented as (input
      query, refusal probability) pairs. Our preliminary analysis reveals two fundamental mechanisms in
076
      jailbreak defense methods-safety shift and harmfulness discrimination-which elucidate how these
077
      defenses work, where they diverge, and enable the effective ensemble of them.
078
079   As illustrated in Figure 1, safety shift refers to a general increase in refusal probabilities for both
080   harmful and benign subsets, shifting the overall data distribution towards the refusal side of the
081
      decision boundary without necessarily widening the gap between their refusal distributions. This
      mechanism operates when the defense model’s safety awareness is heightened by injecting safety-
082
      cautious system prompts or aligning model preferences, leading to a more conservative response
083
      manner that can inevitably cause over-defense. In contrast, harmfulness discrimination either reduces
084   refusal probabilities for benign queries or raises refusal rates for harmful queries, thereby increasing
085   the distance between the refusal probability distributions of the two subsets. This mechanism
086   functions when the defense model is instructed to interpret the harmful or harmless nature of input
087   queries, allowing it to better differentiate between benign and harmful inputs. However, the primary
088   challenge for this mechanism lies in the concealment of harmfulness within input queries.
089
      Based on these two mechanisms, we further explore various ensemble strategies for defense methods,
090   including inter-mechanism and intra-mechanism ensembles. Inter-mechanism ensembles combine
091   methods that share the same mechanism, either enhancing overall safety by reinforcing more conser-
092   vative responses (safety shift ensembles), or further improving the response rate for benign queries
093   (harmfulness discrimination ensembles). Intra-mechanism ensembles integrate both safety shift
094   and harmfulness discrimination methods, with the latter helping to mitigate the refusal probability
095   shift of benign queries, thereby complementing each other for a more balanced trade-off. Our
096   classification-based analysis of these ensemble strategies validates their varying superiority.
097   We conduct empirical evaluations of multiple specific jailbreak defense methods in multimodal
098   scenarios, which are less explored compared to language scenarios. Generative results on top of
099   LLaVA-1.5 Liu et al. (2024) at different scales on the MMSafetyBench Liu et al. (2023b) and
100   MOSSBench Li et al. (2024b) datasets confirm that these methods can improve defenses in previously
101   discussed two mechanisms, and also underscore the challenging nature of multimodal jailbreak de-
102   fense. Further evaluations of ensemble strategies demonstrates their effectiveness to either maximize
103   model safety or achieve a better safety-helpfulness trade-off.
104   Overall, our work highlights two core mechanisms of jailbreak defenses, offers a mechanistic
105   comparison of defense methods, and explores various ensemble strategies for either amplifying safety
106   or balancing it with helpfulness. Our comprehensive empirical evaluation of 27 defense methods
107   fills a gap in the underexplored area of multimodal defenses. We hope our study contributes to more
      informed defense strategy selection, and inspires further advancements and discussions in the field.

      2     BACKGROUND
109
110
      To enhance the safety of generative models, particularly against jailbreak attacks, recent studies have
111   proposed diverse defense methods. Given the limited exploration of multimodal jailbreak defenses,
112   this study primarily focuses on multimodal scenarios. This section provides an overview of existing
113   jailbreak defense methods in multimodal domains, including both internal and external safeguards.
114
115   2.1   I NTERNAL JAILBREAK D EFENSES
116
117   Internal Jailbreak Defenses directly intervene in the model’s generation process by optimizing the
118   model itself or modifying the input query. These defenses can be grouped into four main strategies:
119
      Model Optimization focuses on optimizing the model itself towards safer objectives. This can be
120   achieved by either alignment training or decoding adjustments. The former includes instruction fine-
121   tuning with safety-oriented datasets Deng et al. (2023); Bianchi et al. (2023); Zong et al. (2024), and
122   reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) techniques like Proximal Policy Optimization
123   (PPO) or Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) Zhang et al. (2024b). Additionally, decoding
124   strategies like Rewindable Auto-regressive Inference Li et al. (2023b) and SafeDecoding Xu et al.
125   (2024b) can enhance model safety without the need for fine-tuning.
126   System Reminder involves appending an extra system prompt to remind the model of safety
127   concerns. Variants of system prompts include requesting the assistant to be responsibleXie et al.
128   (2023), employing a Chain of Thought (CoT) promptWang et al. (2024c), following the policy to
129   prioritize safety over helpfulZhang et al. (2023b), and incorporating demonstrations for in-context
130   learningWei et al. (2023).
131
      Query Refactoring entails altering the input queries. This can include modifying the text part
132   through techniques like translation, paraphrasing, summarizationJi et al. (2024), or intention analy-
133   sisZhang et al. (2024c). It can also refract the image part by supplementing or replacing images with
134   captionsGou et al. (2024).
135
      Noise Injection refers to injecting random noises to the input. This can be applied to text through
136
      random insertion, swapping, patchingRobey et al. (2023), and masking of wordsCao et al. (2023). For
137
      the image part, it can involve random geometric mutation, photometric mutationZhang et al. (2024a),
138   or simply adding random noisesXu et al. (2024a). Multiple noise injections are often combined using
139   an ensemble strategy to enhance defense effectiveness.
140
141
      2.2   E XTERNAL JAILBREAK D EFENSES
142
143   External defenses operate independently without directly modifying the model, which can be divided
144   into pre-filtering and post-remediation. Pre-filtering uses external classifiers to filter out harmful
145   queries before they reach the model, detecting prompts with high perplexity or unusual characteris-
146   tics Alon & Kamfonas (2023), or identifying queries with toxic content Kim et al. (2023); Kumar
147   et al. (2024). Post-remediation removes or mitigates harmful responses after generation, either by
148   having the model detect and filter harmful output Phute et al. (2023), or applying lightweight harm
149
      detectors to flag and transform harmful responses into benign ones Pi et al. (2024).
150   Among them, this study focuses particularly on internal strategies that directly affect the target model,
151   exploring how these strategies influence the model’s safety and helpfulness. In contrast, external
152   strategies operate independently and vary widely in detection models, datasets, and algorithms. A
153   detailed analysis of external strategies is beyond this work’s scope and warrant further study to assess
154   their effectiveness across different contexts.

      3.2   Q UANTIFYING D EFENSE USING P ROBABILITY- BASED M ETRICS
185
186   To quantify the impact of defense methods from the classification-based perspective, we introduce
187   two relative metrics compared to the undefended model: Mean Shift and Distance Change.
188
      Mean Shift measures how much the defense method δ shifts the average refusal probabilities for
189
      input queries relative to the undefended model. We calculate mean shifts separately for harmful and
190   benign queries as follows:
191

193                          Mean_Shiftbenign = Ex∈Dbenign [p(θ, x; δ)] − Ex∈Dbenign [p(θ, x)]
194   where Ex∈D [p(θ, x; δ)] and Ex∈D [p(θ, x)] are the average refusal probabilities after and before
195   applying the defense method δ, respectively. A large shift in harmful data implies that the model
196   becomes more safety-conscious, whereas a large shift in benign data suggests potential over-defense.
197
      Distance Change measures how the distance between the refusal probability distributions for harmful
198   and benign data changes before and after applying the defense. Let Pharmful and Pbenign represent the

205   model’s ability to distinguish between harmful and benign queries.

      harmlessness within benign queries, thereby improving the distinction between them. However, the

255   Additionally, noise injection defenses demonstrate limited effectiveness, as indicated by insignificant
256   changes in both the mean shift and distance change metrics. This is because it primarily targets
257   attacks where noise is deliberately added to input queries, making it less effective in defending against
258   general input queries without intentional noise.

498
499   Jailbreak Attacks and Defenses in LVLMs Numerous studies Wei et al. (2024); Chao et al.
500   (2023); Zou et al. (2023); Liu et al. (2023c); Robey et al. (2023); Xie et al. (2023) have explored
501   jailbreak attacks and defenses in the context of LLMs. LVLMs which integrate visual perception
502   with LLMs, exhibit increasing vulnerability against jailbreak attacks. One line of research Dong
503   et al. (2023); Bailey et al. (2023); Luo et al. (2023); Shayegani et al. (2023) employs gradient-based
504   techniques to generate adversarial images that elicit harmful responses from target models. Another
505   line of attacks Gong et al. (2023); Liu et al. (2023d) converts harmful content into images using
506   typography or text-to-image tools to circumvent the safety mechanisms of LVLM. On the defense
507
      side, internal defenses intervene in the model’s generation process by optimizing the model Zong
      et al. (2024); Zhang et al. (2024b) or modifying system prompts Zhang et al. (2024a); Gou et al.
508
      (2024). External defenses function as independent filters without directly affecting the model Pi et al.
509
      (2024); Zhao et al. (2024); Helff et al. (2024).
510
511
512
      Safety Evaluation of LVLMs The assessment of safety in LVLMs has gained significant attention
      in recent research. Several studies have curated specialized image-text paired datasets to examine the
513
      models’ safety levels Liu et al. (2023d); Wang et al. (2023); Li et al. (2024a). These evaluations have
514
      uncovered critical issues, like limited safety and oversensitivity where models incorrectly flag benign
515   inputs as harmful Li et al. (2024b). While most existing work has focused on comparative safety
516   assessments across different LVLMs, our study explores the mechanisms underlying different defense
517   methods causing these problems and how to optimize the delicate balance between maintaining model
518   safety and preserving helpfulness.

521   6    C ONCLUSION
522
523   In this study, we examine the trade-off between safety and helpfulness in jailbreak defenses. We
524   identify two fundamental defense mechanisms: safety shift and harmfulness discrimination. Building
525   on these mechanisms, we explore various ensemble strategies, including inter-mechanism and
526   intra-mechanism ensembles. Our evaluations demonstrate the effectiveness of these strategies in
527
      maximizing model safety or achieving an improved safety-helpfulness balance. Overall, our work
      provides a mechanistic comparison of defense methods in multimodal scenarios and highlights
528
      various ensemble strategies to enhance model safety. We aim to provide valuable guidance for
529
      informed defense strategy selection in real-world applications and inspire further advancements.

762
763         • Responsible: We use the system prompt provided by Wang et al. (2024c) as shown in
764
              Table 4, to instruct the model to act as a responsible assistant. This prompt includes four key
              guidelines: the model must thoroughly examine image content, utilize a chain-of-thought
765
              (CoT) prompt, specify response methods, and incorporate instructions for addressing benign
766
              queries.
767
768         • Policy: We integrate a detailed safety policy into the system prompt. The policy is outlined
769           in Table 5.
770         • Demonstration: We integrate six demonstrations into the system prompt, half of which
771           involve rejecting harmful queries. These demonstrations are displayed in Table 6.
772
773
      Model Optimization
774
775         • SFT: We perform vision-language instruction fine-tuning utilizing the LoRA adapter and
776           the SPA-VL dataset Zong et al. (2024), which is specifically designed for safety alignment.
777           From this dataset, we sampled 2,000 instances, targeting preferred selections as the expected
778           output. Furthermore, we incorporated 5,000 examples from the LLaVA-RLHF dataset Sun
779           et al. (2023), which also provides preferred outputs for supervised training. We employ the
780           unified framework proposed by Zheng et al. (2024), utilizing a learning rate of 1 × 10−4
781           for three epochs, with a global batch size set to 32.
782         • SafeDecoding: We employ an expert model fine-tuned through SFT to enhance the decoding
783           process with the decoding algorithm Xu et al. (2024b).
784
            • DPO: We perform Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) training using the LoRA adapter
785
              and the SPA-VL dataset. Specifically, we sample 5,000 instances from SPA-VL and incorpo-
786
              rate an additional 5,000 examples from the LLaVA-RLHF dataset. The training is conducted
787           over three epochs with a learning rate of 2 × 10−5 and a global batch size of 64.
788
789
      Query Refactor
790
791
            • Caption: This method comprises two steps. First, we query the model to describe the image
792           using the prompt template outlined in Table 7. The response generated in this initial step is
793           then utilized to refactor the original query for the second prompt, as specified in Table 9.
794
795
            • Intention: This process is similar to the Caption method; however, in the first step, we
              instruct the model to extract the intent of the query with the prompt template presented in
796
              Table 8.
797
798         • Caption without Image: In the first step of the Caption method, we extract essential
799           information to address the query, enabling the omission of the image in the subsequent
800           step. In contrast, the Intention method reveals that the model struggles to extract sufficient
801           information in the initial step. Therefore, we only apply this approach for Caption method.
802
803   Noise Injection
804
805         • Mask Image: Randomly mask a specific region of the image.
806         • Vertical Flip Image: Apply a vertical flip transformation to the image.
807
808         • Swap Text: Randomly exchange positions of tokens within the text.
809         • Insert Text: Randomly introduce individual tokens into the text.
