009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Concept-based models like Concept Bottleneck Models (CBMs) have garnered
012            significant interest for improving model interpretability by first predicting human-
013            understandable concepts before mapping them to the output classes. Early ap-
014            proaches required costly concept annotations. To alleviate such, recent methods
015            utilized large language models to automatically generate class-specific concept de-
016            scriptions and learned mappings from a pretrained black-box model’s raw features
017
               to these concepts using vision-language models. However, these approaches as-
               sume prior knowledge of which concepts the black-box model has learned. In this
018
               work, we discover the concepts encoded by the model through unsupervised con-
019
               cept discovery techniques instead. We further propose an input-dependent concept
020            selection mechanism that dynamically retains a sparse set of relevant concepts for
021            each input, enhancing both sparsity and interpretability. Our approach not only
022            improves downstream performance but also needs significantly fewer concepts
023            for accurate classification. Lastly, we show how large vision-language models can
024            guide the editing of our models’ weights to correct errors.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Deep neural networks have achieved tremendous success in a variety of tasks on various input
030
      modalities. However, they are black-box models, making it difficult for humans to understand and
      comprehend their decisions. Thus, there has been considerable recent interest in developing inter-
031
      pretable models. One popular framework is Concept Bottleneck Models (CBMs) (Koh et al., 2020),
032
      i.e., models that first predict human-understandable concepts and then use these concepts to predict
033   the classes (Lampert et al., 2009; Kumar et al., 2009). Initial CBMs are trained in an end-to-end fash-
034   ion through supervision on both the concepts and classes. However, the need for human-annotated
035   concepts during model training requires the time-consuming and expensive collection of such.
036
      To address this limitation of initial CBMs, recent work (Yuksekgonul et al., 2023; Oikarinen et al.,
037
      2023; Menon & Vondrick, 2023; Marcinkevičs et al., 2024) has proposed converting pretrained
038
      black-box models into CBMs in a post-hoc fashion. To avoid the need for annotations, they lever-
039   aged large language models (e.g., GPT-3 (Brown et al., 2020)) to generate class-specific language
040   descriptions and learned a mapping from the black-box model’s uninterpretable features to these
041   concepts using vision-language models (e.g., CLIP (Radford et al., 2021)). However, this raises a
042   crucial question:
043
044         How can we know a priori which concepts a pretrained black-box model has learned?
045
      Instead of defining the concepts in advance, we propose to discover concepts that accurately decom-
046
      pose the features learned by the black-box model (1st contribution). To do so, we draw from the
047
      rich literature on unsupervised concept discovery (Ghorbani et al., 2019; Zhang et al., 2021; Zou
048   et al., 2023; Fel et al., 2023b; Vielhaben et al., 2023; Fel et al., 2023a; Huben et al., 2024; Stein
049   et al., 2024). We chose CRAFT (Fel et al., 2023b) for our experiments because it has been shown
050   to yield human-understandable concepts (Fel et al., 2023a), but other techniques are also possible.
051   CRAFT employs non-negative matrix factorization (Lee & Seung, 1999) to decompose each feature
052   activation into a sparse linear combination of concept vectors. The set of shared concept vectors
053   forms a dictionary matrix. After learning this dictionary matrix, we compute the alignment between
      the raw bottleneck features and the concept vectors to measure a concept’s presence or absence.

054
      Subsequently, we train an interpretable linear classifier on the concepts’ alignment scores, linking
055   the alignment scores to the predictions. Previous work (Yuksekgonul et al., 2023; Oikarinen et al.,
056   2023) has shown that a sparsity penalty on the linear classifier’s weights ensures that each class relies
057   on only a sparse set of concepts. However, they did not examine the per-sample number of concepts
058   that affect the classification across all classes. That is, while individual classes rely on sparse sets
059   of concepts, the overall model depends on substantially more. Empirically, we found that typically
060   over 90% of the available concepts–up to ca. 4200 concepts (see Table 2)–affect the classification
061   per input. As a result, it complicates the interpretation of the model’s decision-making process.
062   To address these challenges, we propose an input-dependent concept selection mechanism that en-
063   sures that only a sparse set of concepts relevant for the classification of an individual input sample is
064   dynamically retained (2nd contribution). We simply apply an activation function before the sparse
065   linear classifier, and enforce sparsity on its output or directly use its sparsity parameter. In our exper-
066   iments, TopK (Makhzani & Frey, 2014) performed best. This mechanism allows the concepts that
067   are retained or removed (i.e., zeroed out) to vary between inputs, making it input-dependent. It also
068   preserves the interpretability of CBMs, as the predictions remain linear w.r.t. the retained concepts.
069   In summary, our contributions are as follows:
070
071             • We propose a new type of CBM called Unsupervised Concept Bottleneck Models
072               (UCBMs)1 ; see Figure 1 for an overview. UCBMs convert pretrained, black-box models
073               into a CBM by discovering and using the concepts that it has learned.
074             • We propose a novel input-dependent concept selection mechanism that dynamically retains
075               a sparse set of concepts relevant to classification. For example, as few as ca. 1.4% of the
076               available concepts are used per input (Table 2).
077
                • We show that UCBMs improve performance while having a substantially higher degree of
078
                  sparsity compared to previous work (Tables 1 and 2).
079
080             • We show that UCBMs are interpretable qualitatively as well as through a user study (Sec-
081               tion 3.2), and demonstrate that large-vision-language models can help us to intervene on
082               UCBMs’ weights to fix errors (Section 3.3).

      In this section, we introduce Unsupervised Concept Bottleneck Models (UCBMs), a novel CBM that
      uses concepts that are automatically discovered and most accurately decompose features learned by
089
      a black-box model (Section 2.1), dynamically only retains the concepts most relevant to classifica-
090
      tion, and finally classifies the input with a sparse linear model (Section 2.2). Figure 1 provides an
091   overview of UCBMs, and the above steps are described in detail below.
092
093
      Notations. Let f : X → Rp be a pretrained, black-box model’s feature extractor that maps from
094
      an input space X ⊆ Rd to the bottleneck feature space of a size of p. Further, let X ∈ RN ×d be
095   the input data matrix where the ith row is the input xi ∈ X and let A = f (X) ∈ RN ×p be the
096   bottleneck feature activations. Lastly, let Y denote the class label space.
097
098
      2.1      D ISCOVERY OF CONCEPTS LEARNED BY THE BLACK - BOX MODEL
099
100   Previous post-hoc CBMs have either used human-annotated concepts (Yuksekgonul et al., 2023;
101   Marcinkevičs et al., 2024) or aligned the black-box model’s features with precomputed text features
102   from vision-language models, using natural language descriptions, such as those generated by a
103   large language model (Yuksekgonul et al., 2023; Oikarinen et al., 2023; Menon & Vondrick, 2023;
104   Marcinkevičs et al., 2024). Importantly, both approaches rely on a predefined set of concepts –either
105   through concept annotations or language descriptions thereof– implicitly assuming which concepts
106   the black-box model has learned. However, these concepts are typically unknown in advance.

131   Discovering the concepts that the black-box model has learned. To address this, we propose
132
      using unsupervised concept discovery techniques for UCBMs. These enable us to discover the
      concepts that the black-box model has learned, and do not require defining the concepts in advance.
133
134   Formally, the goal of unsupervised concept discovery is to extract a small set of interpretable con-
135   cepts c that most faithfully reconstruct the feature activations A. Assuming linearity of concepts,
136   as per the superposition hypothesis (Kim et al., 2018; Elhage et al., 2022), unsupervised discovery
137   methods can be understood as an instance of a dictionary learning problem (Dumitrescu & Irofti,
138
      2018), as shown by Fel et al. (2023a):
139
140
                                    (U∗ , C∗ ) = arg min ||A − UC||2F           ,                                (1)
                                                     U,C
141
142
      where U ∈ RN ×|C| (sparse coefficient matrix) represents the activations A = f (X) ∈ RN ×p w.r.t.
143
      a new basis spanned by the set of |C| concept activation vectors C ∈ R|C|×p (dictionary matrix),
144   and || · ||F denotes the Frobenius norm. Intuitively, we learn a sparse linear decomposition of the
145   feature activations for each input in Equation 1, where we weigh the shared concepts vectors by
146   the input-specific sparse coefficients. Fel et al. (2023a) showed that previous methods, such as K-
147   Means (Ghorbani et al., 2019), PCA (Zhang et al., 2021; Zou et al., 2023), non-negative matrix
148   factorization (Lee & Seung, 1999; Zhang et al., 2021; Fel et al., 2023b), or sparse autoencoders
149   (Makhzani & Frey, 2014; Huben et al., 2024), only differ in their constraints on U, C in Equation 1.
150   In this work, we chose non-negative matrix factorization (i.e., CRAFT (Fel et al., 2023b)) for
151   UCBMs, as it has been shown to discover human-understandable concepts (Fel et al., 2023a). How-
152   ever, we emphasize that UCBMs will benefit from future unsupervised concept discovery methods.

155   2.2    L EARNING THE CLASSIFIER WITH INPUT- DEPENDENT CONCEPT SELECTION
156
157
      In the previous subsection, we discovered concept vectors cj that most accurately decompose the
      uninterpretable features of a black-box model. Next, we compute the alignment scores between each
158
      concept vector and the model’s features, denoted as simC (xi ) ∈ [−1, 1]|C| , where simC (xi )j :=
159      ⟨ai ,cj ⟩
160   ||ai ||2 ·||cj ||2 is the cosine similarity between the feature activations ai = f (xi ) of input xi and
161   concept cj ∈ C. Then, we dynamically select the most relevant concepts and subsequently classify
      the input with a sparse linear model (Wong et al., 2021). Both are described in detail below.

      The main limitation with the approach discussed above is that it fails to produce globally sparse
      classifications. Specifically, most concepts contribute to the classification of any given input, mean-
177
      ing that even if a concept has no impact on one class (e.g., the predicted one), it may still influence
178
      others. We consider a concept to be actively contributing if it has a non-zero impact on the output
179   (see Equation 7 for details). When we computed the number of such concepts in above approach,
180   we found that nearly all of them affect the classifications (Table 2). This is because the cosine sim-
181   ilarities between the black-box model’s activations and concepts are generally non-zero.2 This lack
182   of (global) sparsity limits interpretability and makes it challenging to comprehend a prediction.

184   Input-dependent concept selection mechanism. To ensure that only few concepts affect clas-
185   sification per input without significant performance sacrifices, we propose a simple yet effec-
186   tive input-dependent concept selection mechanism. Specifically, we introduce a concept selector
187   π : R|C| → R|C| , which takes the alignment scores simC (xi ) as input and outputs a sparse set
188   of non-zero (i.e., active) concepts and zeroes out the others. We enforce sparsity through a penalty
189   term on concept selector’s output: Lπsparsity = ||π(·)||0 . Intuitively, the sparsity penalty Lπsparsity drives
190   the concept selector π to only retain a sparse set of concepts which are important for classifying the
191
      input xi , as signaled by the task-specific loss L.

231   3       E XPERIMENTS
232
233   We evaluated UCBM on diverse image classification tasks and compared it to relevant baselines.
234   We show that UCBMs outperform prior work and narrow the gap to their black-box counterparts,
235
      while relying on substantially fewer concepts globally in their classification (Section 3.1). Then, we
      demonstrate the interpretability qualitatively as well as through a user study (Section 3.2). Lastly, we
236
      showcase how large vision-language models can be leveraged to improve our UCBMs by informing
237
      weight editing in order to fix model errors (Section 3.3).
238
239
      Datasets & backbone black-box models. Following previous work, we evaluated UCBMs on
240   ImageNet (Deng et al., 2009) with a pretrained ResNet-50 V2 (He et al., 2016), CUB (Wah et al.,
241   2011) with ResNet-18 pretrained on CUB3 , and Places-365 (Zhou et al., 2017) with ResNet-18 pre-
242   trained on Places-3654 . These datasets cover a diverse set of tasks from standard image classification
243   (ImageNet), fine-grained classification (CUB), to scene recognition (Places-365).
244
245   Implementation details. We trained our UCBMs with Adam (Kingma, 2014) and cosine anneal-
246   ing learning rate scheduling (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2017) for 20 epochs. We used a learning rate
247   of 0.001 on ImageNet and Places-365, and 0.01 on CUB; except for the JumpReLU for which we
248   set it to 0.08 on CUB. We set α = 0.99 for the elastic net regularization for all variants. We tuned
249
      the other hyperparameters (λπ or k, λw , and dropout rate) to yield a good trade-off between per-
      formance, sparsity, and fair comparability. Refer to Appendix C for the hyperparameters and to
250
      Figure 4 and Appendices D and E for the effect of them.
251
252
      Experimental setup. Since the number of concepts |C| substantially influence downstream per-
253   formance, we set |C| proportional to the number of classes with various (expansion) factors
254   {0.5, 1, 3, 5}. All models were trained on a single NVIDIA RTX 2080 GPU and a full training
255   run took from few minutes to a maximum of 1–2 days depending on dataset size and number of
256   concepts |C|. We report top-1 accuracy on the standard holdout sets throughout our experiments.
257
258   Baselines. We compared our UCBMs to Post-hoc CBM (Yuksekgonul et al., 2023) and Label-free
259   CBM (Oikarinen et al., 2023), as they are the most related to our work. Note that Post-hoc CBM
260   requires concept annotations and is therefore not applicable on ImageNet and Places-365.
261
262   Quality of the discovered concepts. Before we evaluated UCBMs, we verified that the discovered
263   concepts behave faithfully. For this, we analyzed the change in cosine similarities between feature
264
      activations and concepts after the removal of relevant image parts of a certain concept; see Figure 2
      and Appendix A. For example, as we remove the saw blade (concept 1985), the cosine similarity of
265
      the aforementioned concept decreases from ca. 0.5 to around 0.25 (Figure 2). We also verified that
266
      concepts are semantically consistent and human-understandable, as seen in the top activating crops
267   throughout this paper. Please refer to, e.g., Fel et al. (2023a) for an extensive analysis.

303
304   How do UCBMs perform? Table 1 shows that UCBMs outperform the baseline methods across
305
      all datasets. Thereby, they also close the performance gap to the original, black-box model. We also
      find that UCBMs without concept selection achieve better performance than UCBMs with concept
306
      selection. Note that this is expected, as the concept selection variants trade-off performance for more
307
      sparsity (Table 2). We further investigate this trade-off in Appendix D.

309   We found that performance is strongly influenced by the total number of concepts |C| used. In
310   Figure 3, we varied the number of concepts to assess this and, as expected, observe that increasing
311
      |C| improves performance. Notably, our UCBMs Pareto-dominate the baselines, confirming that
      their superiority from Table 1 is not due to the total number of concepts chosen.

       4             R ELATED WORKS
472
473
       Concept-based models. Concept Bottleneck Models (CBMs) (Koh et al., 2020) are trained to di-
474    rectly leverage concepts in their classifications (Lampert et al., 2009; Kumar et al., 2009). Many
475    works highlighted (and partially addressed) the limitations of them (Margeloiu et al., 2021; Mahin-
476    pei et al., 2021; Havasi et al., 2022; Marconato et al., 2022; Raman et al., 2024). Other work im-
477    proved the performance-interpretability trade-off (Espinosa Zarlenga et al., 2022; Yang et al., 2023)
478    or extended them beyond image classification (Ismail et al., 2023; Zarlenga et al., 2023).
479
       The most related methods to our work convert a pretrained black-box model into a CBM post-hoc
480    (Yuksekgonul et al., 2023; Oikarinen et al., 2023; Menon & Vondrick, 2023; Marcinkevičs et al.,
481    2024). These approaches alleviate the need for costly concept annotations by leveraging language
482    models, like GPT-3 (Brown et al., 2020), to automatically generate class-specific descriptions and
483    vision-language models, like CLIP (Radford et al., 2021), to learn a mapping from a black-box
484    model’s uninterpretable features to these concepts. In contrast to these, we do not presume which
485    concepts the black-box model has learned, but find the ones that most accurately decompose the
       black-box model’s features in an unsupervised manner. Concurrently, akin to our first contribution,


                                                                       9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      Rao et al. (2024) discovered concepts with sparse autoencoders. In contrast to the aforementioned
487   works, we also introduced a novel input-dependent concept selection mechanism that dynamically
488   retains only a sparse set of concepts for each input.
489
490   Concept discovery. Early work searched for neuron-aligned concepts (Bau et al., 2017; Olah et al.,
491   2017), while later works, inspired by the superposition hypothesis (Kim et al., 2018; Elhage et al.,
492   2022), went beyond this to (linear) vector (Kim et al., 2018; Zhou et al., 2018; Ghorbani et al.,
493   2019; Zhang et al., 2021; Zou et al., 2023; Fel et al., 2023b; Huben et al., 2024; Stein et al., 2024) or
494   linear subspace (Vielhaben et al., 2023) concept representations. Early work needed costly annotated
495   datasets to find concepts through supervision. Later work overcame this bottleneck by formulating
496   concept discovery as a dictionary learning problem (Fel et al., 2023a).
497
498   Model editing. Model editing aims to modify a model’s weights to remove a bias or correct errors.
499
      Previous work edited knowledge in large language models (Zhu et al., 2020; Meng et al., 2022), gen-
      erative image models (Bau et al., 2020; Gandikota et al., 2023), or modified a classifier’s prediction
500
      rules (Santurkar et al., 2021; Oikarinen et al., 2023). These works relied on, e.g., human intervention
501
      or hypernetworks, whereas we leverage large vision-language models to inform model editing.
502
503
504   5   L IMITATIONS & FUTURE WORK
505
506   The main limitation (or advantage) of our approach is that discovered concepts are only represented
507
      visually, not textually. While images may be more informative, texts aid faster and easier inter-
      pretability. To obtain textual descriptions of concepts, we could manually label concepts. However,
508
      this does not scale to large amounts of concepts. Thus, we also experimented with automatic concept
509
      labeling through large vision-language models (GPT-4o (Achiam et al., 2023)), see Appendix I for
510   details. While we found it to yield overall good concept descriptions, we also found many instances
511   with poor descriptions; especially for non-object-centric or more abstract concepts.
512
513
      Another limitation of our approach is that we only extract concepts from the bottleneck layer of
      black-box models. We conjecture that the use of concepts throughout the feature hierarchy of these
514
      models may be beneficial for concept-based models in terms of performance and/or interpretability,
515
      as such a hierarchy is also learned by these models (Zeiler & Fergus, 2014). For instance, an early
516   layer could find concepts for ‘windows’, ‘car body’, or ‘wheels’, while a later layer assembles them
517   to a ‘car’ concept (Olah et al., 2020).
518
519
520
      6   C ONCLUSION
521
522
      We presented UCBMs, which convert pretrained black-box models into interpretable concept-based
      models by discovering the concepts that the model has learned through unsupervised concept discov-
523
      ery. We further introduced a novel input-dependent concept selection that only retains the concepts
524
      most relevant for classifications. Our experiments show that UCBMs outperform previous methods,
525   while being substantially more sparse globally. Finally, we qualitatively and quantitatively vali-
526   dated the interpretability of UCBMs, and showcased how large vision-language models can guide
527   the editing of UCBMs to correct its errors.
