009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Neural networks exhibit the neural collapse phenomenon in multi-class classifi-
012            cation tasks, where last-layer features and linear classifier weights converge into
013            a symmetric geometric structure. However, most prior studies have primarily fo-
014            cused on last-layer feature representations or have examined intermediate features
015            using limited, simple architectures and datasets. The mechanisms by which deep
016
               neural networks separate data according to class membership across all layers
               in more complex and realistic scenarios, and how this separation evolves under
017
               distribution shifts, remain unclear. In this work, we extend the study of neural col-
018
               lapse to a broader range of architectures and datasets, investigating its progression
019            throughout the network and its implications for generalization, robustness, and
020            domain adaptability. Our findings reveal that well-trained neural networks pro-
021            gressively enhance neural collapse across layers, though a distinct transition phase
022            occurs where this improvement plateaus after the initial layers and is followed by
023            a renewed continuous improvement in the very last layers, with additional lay-
024            ers contributing minimal generalization benefits. Moreover, we observe that this
025            progressive neural collapse pattern remains robust against noisy data, whether the
026            noise occurs in inputs or labels, and that the degree of intermediate separation
027
               serves as an effective indicator of noise levels. Additionally, for the learned net-
               works, comparing neural collapse evaluated on noisy data and clean data reveals
028
               insights into feature learning and memorization, with the latter primarily occur-
029
               ring in the very last layers. This finding aligns with the neural collapse pattern
030            observed with clean training data. Finally, we show that when a shift occurs be-
031            tween source and target domains, intermediate neural collapse is closely related
032            to downstream target performance.

035   1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037   Deep learning has become the de facto choice for a wide range of machine learning applications,
038   including image recognition (He et al., 2016; Radford et al., 2021), language modeling (Vaswani,
039
      2017; Devlin et al., 2018), and scientific computing (Silver et al., 2016; Fawzi et al., 2022). These
      models are increasingly applied in diverse real-world scenarios, such as handling corrupted inputs,
040
      noisy labels, and domain adaptation tasks. However, despite their widespread success, the underly-
041
      ing reasons for the remarkable generalization abilities of deep networks remain poorly understood.
042   Much of their success has been attributed to the ability to learn hierarchical representations, which
043   enables deep learning models to capture complex patterns across different layers (Bengio et al.,
044   2013). Yet, the mechanisms behind their robustness and adaptability in challenging environments,
045   such as those involving input corruption or shifts in data distributions, are still not fully explained.
046   In this paper we are motivated by the following question: how to characterize hierarchical represen-
047   tations, and how robust and adaptable are they in the presence of labeling noise, corrupted inputs,
048   and domain shifts?
049   Papyan et al. (2020) empirically identified an intriguing phenomenon termed Neural Collapse (N C)
050   for the balanced multi-class classification tasks. During the terminal phase of training, once the
051   training error reaches zero, both the last-layer features and the final linear classifier converge to a
052   highly symmetric and structured geometric configuration. Specifically, the last-layer features col-
053   lapse to their corresponding class means (N C 1 ), and the class-mean features themselves are max-
      imally distant, forming a simplex equiangular tight frame (ETF) structure (N C 2 ). Simultaneously,

      Neural Collapse offers a mathematically elegant characterization of the learned representations in the
100
      penultimate layer of deep learning-based classification models, independent of network architecture
101
      and dataset. While the research in this field has enhanced the understanding of how deep neural
102   networks functions from different perspectives, most existing theoretical and empirical work focuses
103   on last-layer features or examines intermediate features using relatively small network architectures,
104   such as MLP, VGG, and shallow ResNet, and on simpler datasets like MNIST, Fashion-MNIST,
105   and CIFAR-10. For example, He & Su (2023) suggests that intermediate layers in deep networks
106   enhance the within-class variability at a constant geometric rate. Nonetheless, this phenomenon
107   has mostly been observed with simple architectures and datasets, leaving open questions about its
      validity in more complex, real-world scenarios.

      Contributions. In this work, we conduct an extensive empirical investigation across a diverse set
109   of computer vision datasets, focusing on the intermediate representations of several contemporary
110   neural networks in real-world scenarios. Our contributions can be summarized as follows:
111
       • Progressive intermediate neural collapse with a phase transition as depth increases. Con-
112
         trary to the geometric rate of within-class collapse reported by He & Su (2023), our findings
113
         show that well-trained neural networks indeed progressively enhance neural collapse across lay-
114      ers, although this geometric rate is not consistently observed in more complex settings. To better
115      understand the interplay between network depth and dataset complexity on layer-wise neural col-
116      lapse, we analyze how intermediate neural collapse evolves as network depth increases across
117      various datasets. We observe a distinct transition phase of the within-class collapse as illustrated
118      in Figure 1: for shallower networks relative to the dataset complexity, with-class collapse im-
119      proves steadily across layers. However, as network depth increases, a transition phase emerges
120      where the initial improvement reaches a plateau, and additional layers provide minimal bene-
121      fit. Interestingly, after this plateau, the final layers show a renewed continuous improvement in
122
         within-class collapse.
123    • Marginal gains in generalization with more layers after transition phase occurs. Moving
124      forward, we raise the question of whether this transitional phenomenon observed in intermediate
125      features is connected to generalization performance. Notably, our findings indicate that when
126      this phenomenon occurs, increasing model depth leads to only marginal gains in generalization
127      performance. In contrast to conventional approaches, which require a separate validation set to
128
         determine the smallest depth for maximizing generalization, our results suggest that this tran-
         sitional behavior could serve as an intrinsic indicator for identifying the most efficient depth,
129
         beyond which additional depth yields diminishing returns.
130
131    • Intermediate neural collapse under distribution shift. We argue that the presence of a plateau
132      region in the middle layers, followed by an accelerated decay in compression and separation in
133      the final layers, benefits the generalization and transferability of DNNs. To support this argu-
134
         ment, we investigate intermediate neural collapse across three practical scenarios: label noise,
         corrupted input, and domain shift. We observe that the intermediate features of the training data
135
         continue to exhibit progressive neural collapse, with patterns remaining consistently similar to
136
         those seen in clean data, although the degree of collapse varies depending on the noise level or
137      domain shifts. For training with noisy data (label noise or corrupted input), we define the mem-
138      orization ratio for each layer as the ratio of neural collapse evaluated on clean data to that on
139      noisy data. Notably, regardless of network size, the memorization ratio remains below 1 for all
140      layers except the final few ones, indicating that memorization primarily occurs in the last layers,
141      while preceding layers learn meaningful representations. For domain shift, our findings reveal
142      that intermediate features exhibiting greater neural collapse on downstream target data tend to
143      demonstrate better adaptability and yield higher linear-probing accuracy.

147
148   Last-layer neural collapse. The N C phenomenon was first discovered in Papyan et al. (2020).Un-
149   der the assumption of the unconstrained feature model(UFM) Mixon et al. (2022); Fang et al. (2021),
150   which treats last-layer features as free optimization variables, a series of theoretical studies have val-
151   idated the existence of the N C phenomenon. For example, studies such as Mixon et al. (2022); Han
152   et al. (2021); Zhu et al. (2021); Zhou et al. (2022a); Lu & Steinerberger (2022); Fang et al. (2021);
153
      Ji et al. (2021); Tirer & Bruna (2022); Yaras et al. (2022); Zhou et al. (2022b); Fisher et al. (2024)
      demonstrated that the global minimizers satisfy the N C properties for a family of loss functions, in-
154
      cluding cross-entropy loss, mean-square-error loss, and label-smoothing loss, among others, when
155
      the last-layer feature dimension is not smaller than the number of classes. Moreover, when the num-
156   ber of classes is sufficiently large, Jiang et al. (2023); Gao et al. (2023) proved that the last-layer
157   features satisfy generalized N C properties. Beyond UFM, Tirer & Bruna (2022) and Dang et al.
158   (2023) characterize the global optimality of a two-layers models and multi linear layer models, re-
159   spectively. Súkenı́k et al. (2024) extended UFM to arbitrary non-linear layers and proved that N C
160   emerges after a certain layer for binary classification. These work not only contribute to a new un-
161   derstanding of the working of DNNs but also has also inspired the development of novel techniques
      across various applications, such as imbalanced learning Xie et al. (2023); Liu et al. (2023), trans-


                                                          3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      fer learning Galanti et al. (2022b); Li et al. (2022); Xie et al. (2023); Galanti et al. (2021), and
163   adversarial robustness Su et al. (2023).
164
165   Intermediate neural collapse. While N C was initially introduced to describe the configurations
166
      of last-layer features, recent studies have extended its investigation to intermediate representations.
      Tirer et al. (2023) provided a theoretical analysis showing that the within-class variability (N C 1 )
167
      metric decreases monotonically along the gradient flow across layers when the network is trained
168
      using cascade learning, where a new layer is added on top of the pre-trained network at each step.
169   However, this theoretical result does not fully align with the more common practice of training
170   models in an end-to-end manner. Apart from the theoretical results, some empirical studies Hui et al.
171   (2022); Rangamani et al. (2023); He & Su (2023) suggest that the within-class variability (N C 1 ) of
172   intermediate features decreases monotonically as layers progress deeper into the network. Similarly,
173   research by Ben-Shaul & Dekel (2022); Galanti et al. (2022a) demonstrates that intermediate layers
174   gradually improve the nearest class-center accuracy (N C 4 ). Rather than focusing on individual N C
175   properties, recent works Rangamani et al. (2023); Parker et al. (2023); Wang et al. (2024) have
176   extended the analysis to encompass all N C properties across intermediate layers. However, all
177
      of these studies investigate intermediate N C using relatively small network architectures, such as
      MLP, VGG, and shallow ResNet, and simpler datasets like MNIST, Fashion-MNIST, and CIFAR-10,
178
      which may limit the generalizability of their findings to more complex architectures and datasets.

      4.1   P ROGRESSIVE NEURAL COLLAPSE AND GENERALIZATION
329
330   To investigate intermediate neural collapse, we examine two widely-used architectures: ResNet He
331   et al. (2016) and Swin-Transformer Liu et al. (2021), across four datasets, including CIFAR-
332   10 Krizhevsky et al. (2009), CIFAR-100 Krizhevsky et al. (2009), Mini-ImageNet Vinyals et al.
333   (2016), and ImageNet Deng et al. (2009). For each model, we extract features from the intermediate
334   layers and compute the CDNV and N C 2 metrics to assess within-class variability and between-class
335   separation, respectively. The results for ResNet models are shown in Figure 1, while the results for
336   Swin-Transformer models are presented in Figure 5. These visualizations consistently demonstrate
337   that both within-class variability and between-class separation progressively improve across layers
338
      in all architectures. Furthermore, we observe that the rate and pattern of within-class variability
      vary depending on the network depth and the complexity of the dataset. Within a specific dataset,
339
      increasing the number of intermediate blocks leads to a decrease in the within-class variability of
340
      individual blocks. Once the model complexity becomes sufficient for the dataset, an interesting pat-
341   tern emerges: after an initial improvement in within-class variability in the early layers, a plateau
342   is observed in the intermediate layers. Following this plateau, the final layers exhibit a renewed,
343   continuous improvement in within-class variability.
344
      By examining this phenomenon in relation to generalization performance, we perform linear probing
345
      on top of each intermediate block. Our findings indicate that increasing model depth yields only
346
      marginal improvements in generalization once the plateau phase is reached (e.g., the accuracies
347   of ResNet50, ResNet101, and ResNet152 are 95.55%, 95.58%, and 95.58% on CIFAR-10, and
348   75.91%, 75.93%, and 76.10% on CIFAR-100, respectively). Unlike conventional approaches that
349   require a separate validation set to determine the optimal depth for maximizing generalization, our
350   results suggest that this transitional behavior can serve as an intrinsic indicator for identifying the
351   most efficient model depth, beyond which further increases provide diminishing returns.
352
      On the other hand, the plateau region followed by accelerated decay of the collapsing measure in
353   the final layers provides a clear characterization of the general belief that the earlier layers focus
354   on learning universal and meaningful features, while the last few layers tend to capture more task-
355   specific features. In the next subsection, we examine the robustness of progressive neural collapse
356   when trained on noisy data.
357
358   4.2   ROBUSTNESS OF PROGRESSIVE NEURAL COLLAPSE WITH NOISY DATA
359
360   Since the seminal work Zhang et al. (2021), which demonstrate that DNNs can memorize random
361   labels, the performance of DNNs on noisy labels has been leveraged to understand their generaliza-
362   tion and memorization properties Arora et al. (2018); Feldman & Zhang (2020); Anagnostidis et al.
363   (2022); Song et al. (2022). However, most of this work focuses on the entire network’s performance
364   without studying the internal representations. In this work, we examine the internal representa-
365   tions of DNNs trained on noisy data and utilize this analysis to gain insights into generalization and
366
      memorization. We will study two noisy settings: label noise and corrupted inputs.
367   For DNNs trained on a noisy dataset (with either noisy labels or noisy inputs), we evaluate their inter-
