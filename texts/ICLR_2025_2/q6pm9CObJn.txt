                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011
               Text-Centric Visual Question Answering (TEC-VQA) in its proper format not only
012
               facilitates human-machine interaction in text-centric visual environments but also
013            serves as a de facto gold proxy to evaluate AI models in the domain of text-centric
014            scene understanding. Nonetheless, most existing TEC-VQA benchmarks focus
015            on high-resource languages like English and Chinese. Despite pioneering works
016            expanding multilingual QA pairs in non-text-centric VQA datasets through transla-
017            tion engines, the translation-based protocol encounters a substantial “visual-textual
018            misalignment” problem when applied to TEC-VQA. Specifically, it prioritizes the
019            text in question-answer pairs while disregarding the visual text present in images.
020            Moreover, it fails to address complexities related to nuanced meaning, contextual
021
               distortion, language bias, and question-type diversity. In this work, we tackle
               multilingual TEC-VQA by introducing MTVQA, the first benchmark featuring
022
               high-quality human expert annotations across 9 diverse languages, consisting of
023
               6,778 question-answer pairs across 2,116 images. Further, by comprehensively eval-
024            uating numerous state-of-the-art Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs),
025            including GPT-4o, GPT-4V, Claude3, and Gemini, on the MTVQA dataset, it is
026            evident that there is still a large room for performance improvement, underscoring
027            the value of MTVQA. Additionally, we supply multilingual training data within
028            the MTVQA dataset, demonstrating that straightforward fine-tuning with this data
029            can substantially enhance multilingual TEC-VQA performance. We aspire that
030            MTVQA will offer the research community fresh insights and stimulate further
031            exploration in multilingual visual text comprehension.

036   In the era of burgeoning AI, especially in MLLMs (OpenAI, 2024; Achiam et al., 2023; Yang
037   et al., 2023; Team et al., 2023; Anthropic, 2024; Reid et al., 2024; Bai et al., 2023; Lu et al., 2024;
038   Young et al., 2024; Feng et al., 2023a;b; Hu et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024c; Tang et al., 2024; Chen
039
      et al., 2024; Dong et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024a), Text-Centric Visual Question
      Answering (TEC-VQA) (Biten et al., 2019; Singh et al., 2019; Feng et al., 2023b;a; Tang et al., 2024;
040
      Liu et al., 2024c; Hu et al., 2024) has served as a de facto gold proxy to evaluate AI models in the
041
      domain of text-centric scene understanding. Compared with general VQA (Biten et al., 2019; Mathew
042   et al., 2021; Pham et al., 2024; Singh et al., 2019; Mishra et al., 2019; Mathew et al., 2022; Masry
043   et al., 2022; Zhu et al., 2016; Krishna et al., 2017; Antol et al., 2015; Marino et al., 2019; Sheng
044   et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2024b; Gao et al., 2015; Gan et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2021), TEC-VQA places
045   greater emphasis on answering questions that require understanding visual textual information within
046   images. It enables individuals without specialized expertise to access applications in text-centric
047   visual environments. However, most advancements in TEC-VQA have predominantly concentrated
048   on high-resource languages, e.g., English (Biten et al., 2019; Singh et al., 2019; Mathew et al., 2021;
049   2022), Chinese (Qi et al., 2022; Gao et al., 2015), Japanese (Shimizu et al., 2018; Nguyen et al.,
050
      2023) and etc., thus restricting the applicability of AI models to the global community, particularly
      populations speaking low-resource languages.
051

053   et al., 2022; Changpinyo et al., 2023) in the general VQA field simply leverage translation engines
      to expand question-answer pairs from high-resource to low-resource languages. However, this

      2.1   MLLM S FOR T EXT- CENTRIC VQA
163
164
165   Recent advancements in MLLMs (Achiam et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2023; Team et al., 2023; Anthropic,
166   2024; Reid et al., 2024; Bai et al., 2023; Lu et al., 2024; Young et al., 2024; Feng et al., 2023a;b;
167   Hu et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024c; Tang et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024; Dong et al., 2024; Li et al.,
168   2024; Liu et al., 2024a; Zhao et al., 2024) have revolutionized VQA tasks, as demonstrated by the
169
      remarkable zero-shot performance of these models. Notably, the high generalizability of MLLMs,
      when explicitly trained on visual text understanding datasets and fine-tuned with instructions, has
170
      significantly enhanced their application in text-centric VQA scenarios (Feng et al., 2023b;a; Tang
171
      et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024c; Hu et al., 2024). For example, LLaVAR (Zhang et al., 2023),
172   UniDoc (Feng et al., 2023b), which extend LLaVA (Liu et al., 2024b) into the realm of document
173   understanding, pioneering the text-centric VQA of MLLMs by training them to predict texts and
174   coordinates from document images. Furthermore, DocPedia (Feng et al., 2023a) operates visual
175   input in the frequency domain rather than in space, which enables higher input resolution without
176   increasing the input sequence. Lately, mPLUG-DocOwl (Ye et al., 2023), Qwen-VL (Bai et al., 2023),
177   and TextMonkey (Liu et al., 2024c) leverage publicly available document-related VQA datasets to
178   further enhance the text-centric VQA capabilities. Despite the promising results achieved by existing
179   MLLMs in text-centric VQA tasks, their focus on high-resource languages such as English and
180
      Chinese has posed challenges in achieving reasonable performance for low-resource languages. This
      is primarily due to the lack of data or benchmarks for these low-resource languages.

185   2.2   M ULTILINGUAL T EXT- CENTRIC VQA B ENCHMARKS
186
187   VQA has garnered significant attention in recent years, with numerous studies, datasets, and bench-
188   marks being proposed to advance the field (Biten et al., 2019; Mathew et al., 2021; Pham et al.,
189   2024; Singh et al., 2019; Mishra et al., 2019; Mathew et al., 2022; Masry et al., 2022; Zhu et al.,
190   2016; Krishna et al., 2017; Antol et al., 2015; Marino et al., 2019; Sheng et al., 2021; Liu et al.,
191   2024b; Gao et al., 2015; Gan et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2021). Many datasets have been created that
192   encompass scene text of various domains, including natural images (Biten et al., 2019; Singh et al.,
193   2019), scanned documents (Mathew et al., 2021; 2022), book and movie covers (Mishra et al., 2019).
194   One notable limitation of these datasets is their predominant focus on English (Biten et al., 2019;
195
      Singh et al., 2019; Mathew et al., 2021; 2022) or other high-resource languages such as Chinese (Qi
      et al., 2022; Gao et al., 2015) and Japanese (Shimizu et al., 2018; Nguyen et al., 2023), which restricts
196
      the applicability of VQA systems for low-resource languages such as Thai and Vietnamese.
197
198   There are some recent efforts toward extending VQA tasks to a broader range of languages (Gupta
199   et al., 2020; Pfeiffer et al., 2022; Vivoli et al., 2022; Changpinyo et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023; Raj Khan
200   et al., 2021) by providing a multilingual VQA datasets. For example, Gao et al. (2015) created a free-
201
      form bilingual VQA dataset (FM-IQA) containing over 150,000 images and 310,000 freestyle Chinese
      question-answer pairs and English translations. Raj Khan et al. (2021) developed a large-scale multi-
202
      lingual and code-mixed VQA dataset (MuCo-VQA) supporting five languages. Of more relevance
203
      are the works xGQA (7 languages) (Pfeiffer et al., 2022) and MaXM (7 languages) (Changpinyo
204   et al., 2023), which apply translation-based protocols to expand VQA data beyond English. However,
205   the translation-based multilingual VQA datasets inherently face issues, such as the “visual-textual
206   misalignment” problem, where only the textual information in question-answer pairs is considered,
207   while the visual text in images is overlooked. Additionally, the nuanced meaning and context are
208   often distorted; language bias is introduced by machine translation models, and the coverage of
209   certain question types is limited, as highlighted by Changpinyo et al. (2023). Moreover, none of
210   the previous multilingual datasets focus on text-centric scenarios where multilingual text frequently
211   occurs.
212   Our benchmark, MTVQA, distinguishes itself by focusing on multilingual text-centric VQA scenarios
213   using human expert annotations. It covers 9 languages, facilitating the training and evaluation of
214   multilingual models in diverse linguistic contexts. Additionally, our dataset can gauge the VQA
215   system’s ability for not only high-resource languages but also those that are typically underrepresented
      in current datasets (Biten et al., 2019; Singh et al., 2019; Mathew et al., 2021; 2022; Gao et al., 2015).

234   3     MTVQA B ENCHMARK
235
236
      The MTVQA benchmark is meticulously established to evaluate the multilingual text comprehension
237
      performance of Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs). MTVQA covers nine languages:
238
      Arabic (AR), Korean (KO), Japanese (JA), Thai (TH), Vietnamese (VI), Russian (RU), French
239   (FR), German (DE), and Italian (IT). The data construction involves a comprehensive collection
240   of text-rich images and a two-round human expert annotation process. In terms of data volume,
241   an initial dataset of 8,980 images is compiled. Following a data cleaning phase, 8,895 images are
242   retained for annotation. The first round of annotation yielded 8,895 images, corresponding to 28,906
243   question-answer (QA) pairs. After the second round of annotation and final quality control measures,
244   the final dataset comprises 8,794 images and 28,607 QA pairs. The benchmark construction cost is
245   divided into two parts: image acquisition and annotation. Image acquisition costs about two months
246   and 30,000 dollars. VQA annotation costs about three months and 60,000 dollars. Hourly wages
247
      vary from country to country, with senior language specialists costing between 20 and 40 per hour
      and local annotators costing between 8 and 20 per hour. The average time spent by each labeler was
248
      roughly 60 days.

252   3.1     DATA C OLLECTION
253
254   The raw data collection aims to gather text-rich images from various scene text and document
255   scenarios, ensuring diversity and quality. This includes images from publicly available datasets (e.g.,
256   ICDAR MLT19 (Gao et al., 2019)) and those sourced from the internet (i.e., Laion-OCR, which is
257   filtered from Common Crawl (Crawl, 2024)), such as menus, logos, maps, bills, PowerPoint slides
258   (PPTs), research papers, etc, as shown in Fig. 2. The image collection process consists of two steps:
259   extracting multilingual text using a multilingual OCR engine and selecting by language types and the
260   amount of text contained in the image. Approximately 30% of the overall image data is obtained
261
      from public datasets, with 20% sourced from the web and 50% from manual collection, respectively.
      A total of 1,220 images from document scenarios and 876 images from natural scenarios are collected
262
      for the test set of the MTVQA benchmark. To ensure data content meets regulatory requirements,
263
      we subject them to a standardized data cleaning process. The image-cleaning processing pipeline
264   involves a preliminary round of algorithm-driven review to identify and filter out unusable images. It
265   includes detecting and removing images with politically sensitive, pornographic, violent, or other
266   undesirable features. Subsequently, a multilingual OCR tool is employed to extract the textual content
267   from the remaining images. Images devoid of textual information are discarded, and the surviving
268   images are categorized based on their language. Afterward, we organize all the text-rich images
269   we have obtained into language-specific groups, preparing them for the subsequent stage of data
      annotation.

      3.2   H UMAN E XPERT A NNOTATION
271
272
273
      To obtain informative and accurate text-related QA pairs for images grouped by specific languages,
      a specialized group of annotators with expertise in the local regions of each language is recruited.
274
      The annotators must be native speakers of the corresponding language and have actively utilized
275
      it for a minimum duration of 10 years. Additionally, they must possess at least a university-level
276   degree or higher academic qualification, guaranteeing a profound understanding and skillfulness in
277   the linguistic subtleties and cultural contexts required for precise annotations. Given the subjective
278   nature of understanding text within images, the annotation team is divided into two independent
279   groups. One group is tasked with generating questions and providing answers based on the images,
280   while the other group is responsible for evaluating and correcting the QA pairs. This division, known
281   as the raise-then-correct paradigm, ensures a thorough and trustworthy evaluation of the text-rich
282   image comprehension process. Moreover, the annotation results for each language underwent a 10%
283   sampling inspection by a quality inspector to ensure adherence to standards. QA pairs that do not
284
      meet the criteria are returned for re-annotation. Prior to the formal annotation process, all annotators
      will be provided with a detailed explanation of the annotation guidelines, including a dedicated
285
      question-and-answer session to clarify any ambiguities and uncertainties. A pilot annotation task
286
      will be conducted on a limited dataset to ensure a shared understanding of the annotation rules. The
287   two-round annotation process is briefly illustrated in Fig. 4, further details of which are elaborated in
288   the subsequent sections.

357   First Round Questioning and Answering. In the first round, we allocate three annotators per
358   language to generate initial QA results. The annotators are tasked with thoroughly examining the
359   textual and visual elements within a text-rich image in our collection. They are to examine the
360   textual and visual elements within the image to formulate five meaningful and distinct questions and
361   provide corresponding answers. All annotators should adhere to the following criteria: (1) the first
362   three questions should satisfy that answering these questions requires direct reading of the textual
363
      information in the image, (2) the fourth and fifth questions require reasoning about the text in the
      image to answer, (3) the questions and answers must be reasonably correct and consistent with the
364
      content of the image, and (4) the answer should be as concise as possible and free of nonsense (e.g.,
365
      when the question is “When is the volunteer recruitment period”, the answer should be “9:00-16:00”
366   rather than “The volunteer recruitment period is 9:00-16:00”). This requirement for brevity aims to
367   make the evaluation process user-friendly and reliable, avoiding the influence of extraneous content
368   on the evaluation metrics.
369
      Second Round Evaluation and Correction. To reduce the effect of human subjective cognitive
370
      bias on our MTVQA benchmark and get high-quality question-answer pairs, we assign two annotators
371
      for each language to carry out the annotation evaluation and correction process independently. These
372   annotators follow a specific evaluation and correction protocol to ensure consistency and accuracy
373   based on the first round results: (1) Assess the question’s relevance to the text in the image. Irrelevant
374   QA pairs are discarded; (2) Verify the correctness of the answer and make necessary modifications;
375   (3) Check for redundancy in the answer. If the answer repeats the question’s information, the repeated
376   content is removed for conciseness. (4) Ethical Assessment. Review the image content or the
377   question-answer pair for any unethical content, including but not limited to politics, personal privacy
      issues, etc. Such content is removed to uphold ethical standards in the dataset.

405
      We conduct the evaluation experiments over the baseline MLLMs with their default settings, ignoring
406
      the effect of generation configuration on the results. To make the output of MLLMs more evaluation-
407   friendly, we design the following prompt format to limit the output length: “Answer the question using
408   a word or phrase in the language of the question. + <Question>”, where “<Question>” represents the
409   actual question from the MTVQA test set. This approach aims to make the answers as concise as

411   an instruction tuning experiment on the MTVQA training set. The instruction tuning process adheres

507   5    L IMITATION
508
509   The current version of the MTVQA dataset, while dialectically diverse, exhibits limitations in its
510   language coverage. Despite encompassing a range of languages, it falls short of inclusivity, omitting
511   numerous lesser-spoken languages. This prompts our future continual endeavor to ensure comprehen-
512   sive representation across the linguistic spectrum. Additionally, the dataset currently provides a single
513
      canonical response per question, which may not fully capture the range of answers corresponding to
      different expressions of the same underlying semantics. Recognizing the multifaceted nature of the
514
      inquiries, future versions will aim to include a spectrum of plausible answers to reflect the varied
515
      perspectives inherent to each question.
516
517
518   6    C ONCLUSION
519
520   To attack the visual-textual misalignment issue in multilingual TEC-VQA, we introduce MTVQA, a
521   new benchmark featuring high-quality human expert annotations in 9 diverse languages. We believe
522
      that MTVQA is the first benchmark to provide fully manual annotations tailored to text-centric
      scenarios. The results obtained from closed-source, general-purpose, and text-centric MLLMs on our
523
      MTVQA dataset indicate that there is still room for improving their performance in multilingual text-
524
      centric scenarios. Although the MTVQA dataset has limitations, such as the underrepresentation of
525   several lesser-spoken languages and single canonical answers, future updates will address these issues
526   by expanding the multilingual scope and including a range of plausible answers. We are confident
527   that this dataset can inspire researchers within the TEC-VQA community with new perspectives and
528   ideas.
