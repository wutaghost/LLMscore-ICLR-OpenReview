009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Transformers have become ubiquitous in modern machine learning applications,
012            yet their training remains a challenging task often requiring extensive trial and
013            error. Unlike previous architectures, transformers possess unique attention-based
014            components, which can complicate the training process. The standard optimization
015            algorithm, Gradient Descent, consistently underperforms in this context, underscor-
016            ing the need for a deeper understanding of these difficulties: existing theoretical
017            frameworks fall short and fail to explain this phenomenon. To address this gap,
018
               we analyze a simplified Softmax attention model that captures some of the core
               challenges associated with training transformers. Through a local analysis of the
019
               gradient dynamics, we highlight the role of the Softmax function on the local
020
               curvature of the loss and show how it can lead to ill-conditioning of these models,
021            which in turn can severely hamper the convergence speed. Our experiments confirm
022            these theoretical findings on the critical impact of Softmax on the dynamics of
023            Gradient Descent.

026   1    I NTRODUCTION
027
028   In recent years, transformer architectures have demonstrated remarkable success in various appli-
029   cations from natural language processing (Devlin et al., 2019; Achiam et al., 2023) to computer
030   vision (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020). However, despite their impressive performance, our theoretical
031   understanding of transformer training remains limited. For example, Stochastic Gradient Descent
032   (SGD), which has been a staple optimization algorithm for deep learning models, fails to train
033
      transformers effectively (Liu et al., 2020). This has led to the belief that there are unique elements in
      the transformer architecture and the associated loss landscape that introduce challenges distinct from
034
      those in other architectures like Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs).
035
036   Recently, several papers have attempted to explain this phenomenon through comparisons of SGD
037   with adaptive methods. Indeed, empirical observations indicate that while SGD outperforms adaptive
038   methods on tasks such ImageNet classification with CNNs (Wilson et al., 2017), the opposite is true
039
      for transformer training. Liu et al. (2020) claimed that this failure of SGD is due to vanishing and
      imbalanced gradients observed in experiments. Meanwhile, Jiang et al. (2023) introduced a variant
040
      of the condition number and empirically showed that it is larger on the path taken by SGD than the
041
      one taken by Adam for standard transformer architectures. Zhang et al. (2020) hypothesized that
042   the performance gap might be due to the heavy-tailed and non-Gaussian nature of the stochastic
043   gradient noise. However, Kunstner et al. (2023) challenged this explanation by showing that even
044   in the full-batch case, SGD performs poorly compared to adaptive methods. Although these works
045   highlight interesting features of transformer training that are different for Adam compared with SGD,
046   their theoretical explanations of the source of these differences remain very limited.
047
      In parallel, there has been a line of work that has focused on theoretically analyzing the dynamics of
048   gradient descent on transformers and providing global convergence guarantees without explaining
049   its poor performance. For instance, Tarzanagh et al. (2023b) showed that gradient descent on a
050   simplified attention model solves a max-margin problem and provided guarantees for global and
051   local convergence in the binary classification setting. Meanwhile, Abbe et al. (2023) showed that
052   the incremental learning phenomenon, whereby singular values are learned one-by-one, is present in
053   transformers. However, these works did not provide any rates of convergence that would shed light
      on the slow or lack of convergence of SGD in practice. Others provided rates of convergence but

      were limited to heavily overparameterized settings (Wu et al., 2023) and assumed pretrained weights
055   Liu et al. (2023).
056
057   Clearly, there exists a gap between the current theoretical analysis of Gradient Descent and the
058
      empirical observations demonstrating its difficulty in optimizing transformers. This leads to the
      following question:
059

                        Why does Gradient Descent perform poorly on attention models?

      1.1   PAPER C ONTRIBUTIONS
063
064   In this paper, we identify a critical aspect of attention models that contributes to the difficulties
065   gradient descent faces in training them efficiently: the preconditioning effects induced by the
066   Jacobian of the Softmax function. Our analysis focuses on a simplified one-layer Softmax attention
067   model, previously introduced by Tarzanagh et al. (2023b); Oymak et al. (2023), which captures
068   key challenges encountered in training more complex attention architectures. We provide sufficient
069   conditions for GD to converge linearly in the overparameterized case, but show that this setting,
070   which has also been the focus of prior works Wu et al. (2023), fails to explain the poor performance
071   of gradient descent in practice. Motivated by this, we analyze the more realistic underparameterized
072
      case and highlight the preconditioning role of the Jacobian of the softmax. Our analysis of the local
      dynamics around stationary points reveals that the ill-conditioning of the Jacobian of the Softmax
073
      function, which occurs when the softmax probabilities are far from uniform, can significantly hinder
074
      the convergence of gradient descent. Specifically, we show that the Softmax parameterization alters
075   the condition number of the Hessian of the loss by a multiplicative factor equal to the square of the
076   ratio of the largest to smallest attention probabilities, thus significantly slowing convergence to sparse
077   attention matrices.

      Dynamics of Attention. Understanding the dynamics of attention has been the focus of many works
      in recent years. One line of work was interested in how transformers learn meaningful representations.
083
      For example, Li et al. (2023c) analyzed the optimization dynamics of a single-layer transformer and
084
      showed how it learns ”semantic structure” from text data. Snell et al. (2021) showed that a single
085   attention head learns to focus on salient words while Jelassi et al. (2022) focused on image data and
086   showed how a simplified Vision transformer (ViT) can learn spatial structure without any inductive
087   bias of spatial locality. Another line of work, that is closer to our work, focused on understanding the
088   optimization dynamics of gradient descent. Tarzanagh et al. (2023a) and Tarzanagh et al. (2023b)
089   analyzed a similar simplified attention model trained with a decreasing loss and established asymptotic
090   convergence of gradient descent, showing that the solutions along some regularization paths solve
091   an SVM problem. Abbe et al. (2023) considered a single-head transformer with diagonal attention
092   weights and small initialization, and they showed that the model displays the incremental learning
093
      phenomenon in which the learned weights gradually increase in rank. These works did not provide
      any rates of convergence and instead focused on the implicit bias/regularization from the gradient
094
      descent algorithm. Liu et al. (2023) considered a shallow transformer trained with the hinge loss using
095
      an initialization from a pretrained model and analyzed its sample complexity. Although they consider
096   an underparameterized setting in which the width of the model is of order at least log(N ) where N is
097   the number of data samples, their analysis is restricted to a specific data model in which the tokens
098   can be split into relevant and irrelevant sets. Moreover, their focus is on generalization properties
099   and the only result that pertains to the optimization dynamics of Gradient Descent is limited to an
100   analysis of the concentration of attention weights at a sublinear rate during training. Wu et al. (2023)
101   is the most similar to some of our results, namely Theorem 1. They conduct a similar analysis on a
102   one-layer self-attention model and provide sufficient conditions for linear convergence under O(N )
103   overparameterization, but do not address the underparameterized setting. Moreover, their analysis
104
      relies on the final linear output layer to guarantee linear convergence, whereas our analysis yields a
      linear rate convergence based solely on the attention weights in the layer inside the Softmax function.
105
106   Failure of SGD on transformers. Several works have explored why SGD performs poorly on
107   transformers. Zhang et al. (2020) hypothesized that it is caused by the heavy-tailed stochastic noise in
      language tasks. However, Kunstner et al. (2023) found that the SGD fails even in the full-batch case.

108
      Moreover, Zhang et al. (2024) showed that Vision transformers trained with SGD on ImageNet suffer
109   from the same problems. Therefore, the stochasticity of the gradients and the data modality cannot
110   explain the failure of SGD on transformers. Pan & Li (2023) introduce the notion of directional
111   sharpness and argue that SGD has a high directional sharpness, which they claim is correlated with
112   low performance of optimization algorithms. Jiang et al. (2023) propose a new notion of condition
113   number and show that it is large on the path taken by SGD. Zhang et al. (2024) explain the poor
114   performance of SGD by computing the spectrum of the Hessian and showing that the spectrum across
115   blocks varies significantly for transformers, unlike other architectures like CNNs and MLPs. Their
116   theoretical results are however limited to quadratic models. Finally, Yadav et al. (2023) hypothesize
117
      that the poor performance of SGD is due to the heavy-tailed class imbalance. They empirically show
      that other architectures such as CNNs suffer from the same issue under heavy-tailed class imbalance.

207   4   M AIN R ESULTS
208
209   In this section, we present our central findings on the dynamics of gradient descent when applied to
210   training softmax attention models through two distinct yet complementary perspectives.
211   In the first subsection, we examine the behavior of gradient descent within an overparameterized
212   setting (d ≥ N ). By leveraging classical Polyak-Łojasiewicz (PL) and smoothness assumptions,
213   we establish a linear convergence rate. This analysis underscores the efficiency of optimization in
214   scenarios where the model is overparameterized. Although it reveals a degradation in the convergence
215   rate as attention vectors become sparser, the analysis fails to account for cases that are observed
      in practice and does not explicitly capture the mechanism underlying the suboptimal performance


                                                         4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      of gradient descent. To address these limitations, the second subsection explores gradient descent
217   dynamics without assuming overparameterization. Due to the inherent complexities and the current
218   lack of robust technical tools for a global analysis in this regime, we focus on a local analysis
219   around stationary points. This investigation reveals that the softmax mechanism can induce severe
220   ill-conditioning, particularly when attention becomes sparse. Such ill-conditioning significantly
221   impedes convergence, resulting in slower optimization progress.
222
      Together, these analyses provide a nuanced understanding of gradient descent dynamics in softmax
223   attention models, highlighting both the strengths of overparameterized settings and the challenges
224   faced in more realistic, constrained parameter regimes.

      Limitations of the overparameterized setting. One significant drawback of this style of analysis,
      which relies on the PL inequality, is that it requires that the norm of p remain bounded along its
316
      trajectory. However, to learn sparse or approximately sparse attention maps, the norm of the weight
317
      vector p must approach infinity. As the bound Cp increases to allow for that, the convergence rate
318   described in the theorem worsens and eventually yields a vacuous bound. Moreover, the ability to
319   analyze gradient descent behavior in contexts where learned attention probabilities are approximately
320   sparse is critical. The primary objective of the attention mechanism is to focus on the most relevant
321   parts of the input, resulting in attention probabilities that are typically far from uniform. This has
322   been confirmed by prior empirical observations (Wu et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023a; Chen et al., 2021),
323   and can also be clearly observed across layers and heads in the attention weights that we included in
      Appendix E for the Vision Transformer model (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020) used in our experiments.

508   6   C ONCLUSION
509
510   In this paper, we investigated the optimization challenges associated with training attention-based
511   models using gradient descent. Because the softmax function is a feature of attention models that
512
      is absent in the core components of other architectures such as CNNs, we focused on the role that
      it may play in the training dynamics. We established that in overparameterized settings, gradient
513
      descent can achieve linear convergence under certain conditions by relying on a local PL and
514
      smoothness analysis, since overparameterization implies the existence of non-sparse solutions. We
515   highlighted the limitations of this setting and showed through a local analysis, in the more realistic
516   underparameterized setting, how the convergence behavior of gradient descent is affected by the
517   distribution of attention scores. Specifically, as attention probabilities near the boundary of the
518   probability simplex, the optimization problem becomes increasingly ill-conditioned, resulting in
519   slower convergence. Our results highlight the necessity of considering the specific characteristics
520   of the Softmax function and its influence on the local curvature of the loss landscape in the training
521   of attention models. Our work sets the stage for developing more effective training algorithms that
522   can overcome the limitations identified in our analysis. Future work could explore the optimization
523
      dynamics of adaptive methods, which have been more successful in the training of transformers,
      and investigate their ability to better handle the challenges identified in our work, especially in
524
      underparameterized regimes.
525
526   Finally, we discuss some of the limitations of our work. First, our theoretical results are derived for a
527   simplified model under certain assumptions, which may not fully capture the complexity of real-word
528   data and architectures. In the overparameterized setting, our results required some conditions on
529
      the initialization and were limited to strongly convex losses. In the underparameterized setting, our
      analysis only captures the local dynamics and does not apply globally. Moreover, our focus on the
530
      dynamics with a fixed value vector v and key-query weight matrix W may not take into account
531
      important interactions between these weights and query vectors. These limitations suggest that further
532   research is needed to develop technical tools and frameworks that can handle these complexities.
533   Nonetheless, we believe that our work provides valuable insights and can be a stepping stone towards
534   a better understanding of more complicated models.
