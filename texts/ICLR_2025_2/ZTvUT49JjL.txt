009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            Gradient descent for matrix factorization is known to exhibit an implicit bias
012            toward approximately low-rank solutions. While existing theories often assume
013            the boundedness of iterates, empirically the bias persists even with unbounded
014            sequences. We thus hypothesize that implicit bias is driven by divergent dynamics
015            markedly different from the convergent dynamics for data fitting. Using this
016
               perspective, we introduce a new factorization model: X ≈ U DV ⊤ , where U and
               V are constrained within norm balls, while D is a diagonal factor allowing the
017
               model to span the entire search space. Our experiments reveal that this model
018
               exhibits a strong implicit bias regardless of initialization and step size, yielding truly
019            (rather than approximately) low-rank solutions. Furthermore, drawing parallels
020            between matrix factorization and neural networks, we propose a novel neural
021            network model featuring constrained layers and diagonal components. This model
022            achieves strong performance across various regression and classification tasks
023            while finding low-rank solutions, resulting in efficient and lightweight networks.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
027
      The Burer–Monteiro (BM) factorization (Burer & Monteiro, 2003b) is a classical technique for
028
      obtaining low-rank solutions in optimization. It can be viewed as a simple neural network that
029
      uses a single layer of hidden neurons under linear activation. Indeed, say one has the factorization
030   X = U V T where U ∈ Rd×r and V ∈ Rc×r , then U and V can be thought of as the weights of
031   the first and second layers, while r represents the number of hidden neurons. However, despite the
032   similarity suggested by this view, there is a clear distinction between BM factorization and neural
033   networks about how the rank r is chosen. In BM factorization, r is typically chosen to be small,
034   close to the rank of the desired solution. Neural networks, on the other hand, often succeed even in
035   overparametrized settings where r is large.
036   Recent findings of implicit regularization in matrix factorization narrows the gap between these two
037   perspectives. For instance, in an effort to explain the empirical success of overparametrized neural
038   networks, Gunasekar et al. (2017) demonstrate that gradient descent (with certain parameter selection)
039   on BM factorization tends to converge toward approximately low-rank solutions even when r = d.
040   Based on this observation, they conjecture that “with small enough step sizes and initialization close
041   enough to the origin, gradient descent on full-dimensional factorization converges to the minimum
042   nuclear norm solution.”
043   In a follow-up work, however, Razin & Cohen (2020) presented a counter-example demonstrating
044   that implicit regularization in BM factorization cannot be explained by minimal nuclear norm, or in
045   fact any norm. Specifically, they showed that there are instances where the gradient method applied to
046   BM factorization yields a diverging sequence, and all norms thus grow toward infinity. Intriguingly,
047   despite this divergence, they found that the rank of the estimate decreases toward its minimum.
048   Although this phenomenon might seem surprising initially, it is not uncommon for diverging se-
049   quences to follow a structured path. A prime example is the Power Method, the fundamental algorithm
050   for finding the largest eigenvalue and eigenvector pair of a matrix. Starting from a random initial point
051   x0 , the Power Method iteratively updates the estimate by multiplying it with the matrix. This process
052   amplifies the component of the vector that aligns with the direction of the dominant eigenvector
053   more than the other components, progressively leading xk to align with this eigenvector. In practical
      implementations, xk is scaled after each iteration to avoid numerical issues from divergence.

      This perspective serves as the foundational motivation guiding our approach. Specifically, our key
055   insight is that the implicit regularization in BM factorization (and neural networks) is driven by
056   divergent dynamical behavior. This is markedly different from the standard (convergent) optimization
057   dynamics helping with the data fitting. In this context, we hypothesize that these forces do not
058   merely coexist but actively compete, influencing model behavior and performance in fundamentally
059   conflicting ways. Our main goal in the development of this paper is to devise an approach that
060   unravels these competing forces.

      1.1      OVERVIEW OF MAIN CONTRIBUTIONS
063
064       ■   A novel formulation for matrix factorization. We model X = U DV ⊤ , where U and V are
065           constrained within Frobenius norm balls. Projection onto this ball results in a scaling step similar
066
              to the Power Method. The middle term D is a diagonal matrix that allows the model to explore
              the entire search space despite U and V being bounded.
067
068           Empirically we demonstrate that the gradient method applied to the proposed formulation
069           exhibits a pronounced implicit bias toward low-rank solutions. We compare our formulation
070           against standard BM factorization with two unconstrained factors. Specifically, we investigate
071
              key factors such as step size and initialization, which prior work suggests might be contributing
              to implicit bias. We find that our factorization approach largely obviates the need to rely on these
072
              conditions: it consistently finds truly (rather than approximately) low-rank solutions across a
073
              wide range of initializations and step-sizes in our experiments. We believe these findings should
074           be of broader interest to research on implicit bias.
075
076       ■   A novel neural network architecture. Motivated by the strong bias for low-rank solutions of
077
              the proposed factorization, we subsequently extend it to deep neural networks. Specifically, we
              do so by adding constrained layers and diagonal components. We demonstrate numerically that
078
              this constrained model performs on par with ReLU activations across various regression and
079
              classification tasks, without requiring further nonlinear activations. Importantly, even here, our
080           approach exhibits bias towards low-rank solutions, resulting in a natural pruning procedure to
081           obtain compact, lightweight networks without compromising performance.

084   2       R ELATED W ORK
085
086   Burer-Monteiro factorization. BM factorization is initially proposed for solving semi-definite
087   programs (Burer & Monteiro, 2003a; 2005; Boumal et al., 2016; 2020; Cifuentes, 2021) and is
088   recognized for its efficiency in addressing low-rank matrix optimization problems, see (Sun & Luo,
089   2016; Bhojanapalli et al., 2016; Park et al., 2017; 2018; Hsieh et al., 2018; Sahin et al., 2019; Lee
090   et al., 2022; Yalçın et al., 2023) and the references therein. Building on the connections between
091   training problems for (non-linear) two-layer neural networks and convex (copositive) formulations
092   (see (Pilanci & Ergen, 2020; Ergen & Pilanci, 2020; Sahiner et al., 2021) and the references therein),
093
      Sahiner et al. (2023) recently introduced BM factorization to solve convex formulations for various
      neural network architectures, including fully connected networks with ReLU and gated ReLU
094
      (Fiat et al., 2019) activations, two-layer convolutional neural networks (LeCun et al., 1989a), and
095
      self-attention mechanisms based on transformers (Vaswani et al., 2017).
096
097   Implicit regularization. While neural network-based systems are rapidly emerging as a dominant
098   technology, the mechanisms underlying their generalization capabilities are yet to be fully under-
099
      stood. One promising line of research aims to explain this success through the concept of ’implicit
      regularization,’ which is induced by the optimization methods and formulations used during neural
100
      network training (Neyshabur et al., 2014; 2017; Neyshabur, 2017). Several studies have explored
101
      matrix factorization to investigate implicit bias in linear neural networks (Gunasekar et al., 2017;
102   Arora et al., 2018; Razin & Cohen, 2020; Belabbas, 2020; Li et al., 2021). Le & Jegelka (2022)
103   extended these results to the final linear layers of nonlinear ReLU-activated feedforward networks
104   with fully connected layers and skip connections. More recently Timor et al. (2023) investigated
105   implicit regularization in ReLU networks. Much of the existing work focuses on gradient flow dy-
106   namics in the limit of infinitesimal learning rates. In particular, Gidel et al. (2019) examined discrete
107   gradient dynamics in two-layer linear neural networks, showing that the dynamics progressively learn
      solutions of reduced-rank regression with a gradually increasing rank.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      Constrained neural networks. Regularizers are frequently used in neural network training to prevent
109   overfitting and improve generalization (Goodfellow et al., 2016), or to achieve structural benefits such
110   as sparse and compact network architectures (Scardapane et al., 2017). However, it is conventional
111   to apply these regularizers as penalty functions in the objective rather than as hard constraints,
112   addressing them within gradient-based optimization via their (sub)gradients. This approach is likely
113   favored due to the ease of implementation, as pre-built functions are readily available in common
114   neural network packages. Several independent studies have also applied proximal methods across
115   different networks and applications, finding that proximal-based methods tend to yield solutions with
116   more pronounced structures (Bai et al., 2019; Yang et al., 2020; Yun et al., 2021; Yurtsever et al.,
117
      2021; Yang et al., 2022). Structural regularization in the form of hard constraints, however, appears to
      be rare in neural network training. One notable exception is in the context of neural network training
118
      with the Frank-Wolfe algorithm (Pokutta et al., 2020; Zimmer et al., 2022; Macdonald et al., 2022),
119
      where constraints are necessary due to the algorithm’s requirement for a bounded domain.
120
121   There are other use cases of constraints in neural networks. For instance, constraints can be applied
122   to ensure adherence to real-world conditions in applications where they are necessary (Pathak et al.,
123
      2015; Márquez-Neila et al., 2017; Jia et al., 2017; Kervadec et al., 2019; Weber et al., 2021), or to
      incorporate physical laws in physics-informed neural networks (Raissi et al., 2019; Lu et al., 2021;
124
      Patel et al., 2022). In these cases, the feasible set is typically complex and difficult to project onto;
125
      therefore, optimization algorithms like primal-dual methods or augmented Lagrangian techniques are
126   used. Constraints are also present in lifted neural networks, a framework where the training problem
127   is reformulated in a higher-dimensional ’lifted’ space. In this space, conventional activation functions
128   like ReLU, used in the original formulation, are expressed as constraints (Askari et al., 2018; Sahiner
129   et al., 2021; Bartan & Pilanci, 2021).
130
      Pruning. Neural networks are often trained in an over-parameterized regime to enhance generalization
131
      and avoid getting stuck in poor local minima. However, these models can suffer from excessive
132   memory and computational resource demands, making them less efficient for deployment in real-
133   world applications (Chang et al., 2021). Several compression techniques have been studied in the
134   literature, including parameter quantization (Krishnamoorthi, 2018), knowledge distillation (Gou
135   et al., 2021), and pruning.
136
      Pruning reduces the number of parameters, resulting in more compact and efficient models that are
137
      easier to deploy. The literature on pruning is extensive, with diverse methods proposed with distinct
138   characteristics. Various criteria are used to determine which weights to prune, including second-order
139   derivative-based methods (LeCun et al., 1989b; Hassibi & Stork, 1992), magnitude-based pruning
140   (Janowsky, 1989; Han et al., 2015), saliency heuristics (Mozer & Smolensky, 1988; Lee et al., 2018),
141   and matrix or tensor factorization-based techniques (Xue et al., 2013; Sainath et al., 2013; Jaderberg
142   et al., 2014; Lebedev et al., 2015; Swaminathan et al., 2020), among others. A comprehensive review
143   of pruning methods is beyond the scope of this paper due to space limitations and the diversity of
144   approaches. For more detailed reviews, we refer to (Reed, 1993; Blalock et al., 2020; Cheng et al.,
145   2024), and the references therein.

      Although rank-constrained matrix optimization problems are typically NP-hard, various methods have
163   been developed to provide practical approximations. Examples include hard thresholding algorithms
164   (Jain et al., 2010; Goldfarb & Ma, 2011; Kyrillidis & Cevher, 2014), convex relaxation techniques

167

181   Selecting the factorization rank r is a critical decision that might impact the solution. A small r
182   may lead to spurious local minima, resulting in inaccurate outcomes (Waldspurger & Waters, 2020).
183   Conversely, a large r might weaken rank regularization, rendering the problem underdetermined.
184   Conventional wisdom in BM factorization suggests finding a moderate choice that balances these two
185   extremes. However, a key observation in (Gunasekar et al., 2017) is that the gradient method applied
186   to (2) exhibits a tendency towards approximately low-rank solutions (i.e., a decaying singular value
187   spectrum) even when r = d. Below, we restate their conjecture:
188   Conjecture in (Gunasekar et al., 2017). Suppose gradient flow (i.e., gradient descent with an
189   infinitesimally small step-size) is initialized at a full-rank matrix arbitrarily close to the origin. If
190   the limit of the gradient flow, XGF = U U ⊤ , exists and is a global optimum of (1) with A(XGF ) = b,
191   then XGF is the minimal nuclear-norm solution to (1).
192   Remark 2. This conjecture outlines three conditions for implicit bias: a small step-size, near-
193   origin full-rank initialization, and consistent measurements (A, b). However, these conditions are
194   often unconventional. For example, small step-sizes are typically avoided because they slow down
195   convergence. Similarly, near-origin initialization is counterintuitive, as the origin is a trivial spurious
196   stationary point of (2).

342   We tested the proposed UDV framework on both regression and classification tasks, comparing it

459   4.1.3                               F URTHER D ETAILS AND D ISCUSSIONS
460
461   Our findings in experiments on neural networks align with the results observed in matrix factorization.
462
      A key distinction, however, was the use of different optimization algorithms, including stochastic
      gradients and momentum steps, in neural network experiments. Despite these differences, the UDV
463
      architecture consistently demonstrated a strong bias toward low-rank solutions. We provide further
464
      experiments and additional details in the supplementary material and summarize the key results here:
465
466    • When designing our network architecture, we considered four variants of UDV, each differing
467      slightly in their constraints. We selected the version presented in equation (7), as it generally
468
         produces the most pronounced decay in the singular value spectrum. For completeness, the details
         of the other three variants are provided in Appendix B.1.
469
470    • Appendix B.2 provides additional results for the experiment described in Section 4.1.1. Specif-
471      ically, we present results analogous to those in Table 2 and Figure 3 but focusing exclusively
472      on the MBGDM algorithm. These results show similar trends, highlighting consistency across
473
         different methods. Additionally, comprehensive performance comparisons across all algorithms
         and models in Table 1, including the design variants in Appendix B.1, are available in Tables SM2
474
         to SM5 in the supplementary material.
475
476    • Additional details and results on SVD-based pruning are presented in Appendix B.3. We show
477      that the UDV framework consistently achieves low-rank solutions across various problem con-
478
         figurations. Furthermore, we analyze the effect of learning rate on the singular value spectrum,
         similar to the analysis in Figure 1, but applied to neural network experiments, confirming that the
479
         UDV framework produces low-rank solutions across a broad range of learning rates.
480
481    • Appendix B.4 extends the UDV framework by incorporating non-linear ReLU activation. Prelimi-
482      nary experiments with the UDV-ReLU model reveal low-rank solutions similar to those identified
483
         in the original UDV framework.
484    • Appendix B.5 presents an experiment comparing the UDV framework against two/three-layer
485      fully connected blocks and a UDV block without the constraints. Given that prior work on the
         implicit bias in neural networks suggests that increasing depth enhances the bias towards low-rank

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
            solutions (Arora et al., 2019; Feng et al., 2022), it is natural to ask whether the pronounced bias
487         in the UDV framework is merely a result of adding a diagonal layer. The results indicate that the
488         pronounced bias in the UDV framework cannot be attributed solely to depth, highlighting the
489         critical role of explicit constraints.
490
          • Appendix B.6 compares the spectral decay obtained by the UDV network with those from classical
491
            weight decay regularization in two- and three-layer networks. The results highlight that while
492         weight decay regularization enhances singular value decay, it cannot replicate the results observed
493         with the UDV model.

496   5      C ONCLUSIONS
497
498   We proposed a new matrix factorization framework, inspired by the observation that implicit bias
499   is driven by dynamics—potentially divergent—that are distinct from those leading to convergence
500
      of the objective function. This framework constrains the factors within Euclidean norm balls and
      introduces a middle diagonal factor to ensure the search space is not restricted. Numerical experiments
501
      demonstrate that this approach significantly strengthens the low-rank bias in the solution.
502
503   To explore the broader applicability of our findings, we designed an analogous neural network
504   architecture with three layers, constraining the fully connected layers and adding a diagonal hidden
505   layer, referred to as UDV. Extensive experiments show that the proposed UDV architecture achieves
506
      competitive performance compared to standard fully connected networks, while inducing a structured
      solution with a strong bias toward low-rank representations. Additionally, we explored the utility
507
      of this low-rank structure by applying an SVD-based pruning strategy, illustrating how it can be
508
      leveraged to construct compact networks that are more efficient for downstream tasks.

       B.6                                      C OMPARISON OF UDV AGAINST T WO /T HREE -L AYER NETWORK BLOCKS WITH WEIGHT
1641
                                                DECAY
1642
1643   Weight decay introduces a regularization term, typically the L2 penalty, into the loss function. This
1644   penalizes large weight values and encourages the weights to shrink toward smaller magnitudes. By
1645   pushing smaller singular values closer to zero, weight decay tends to lower the rank of the weight
1646   matrix. To investigate this effect, we conducted a preliminary experiment to compare the singular
1647   value decay observed in two-layer (UV) and three-layer (UFV) fully connected network blocks with
1648   weight decay regularization.
1649   We followed the experimental settings outlined in Table 1, focusing on a regression task with the
1650   HPART dataset. The weight decay rate (γ) was incorporated as a regularization parameter in the
1651   optimizer, and we evaluated its effect using values γ ∈ [10−6 , 10−5 , . . . , 10−1 ].
1652
       Increasing the regularization parameter led to faster decay in the singular value spectrum, but this
1653   came at the cost of worse training and validation losses. Figure SM21 shows an example where
1654   we selected the largest values for which the validation loss was at most 10% worse than the UDV
1655   baseline. The resulting singular value decay in the UV and UFV models was less pronounced than
1656   that of the UDV model. When the regularization parameter was increased further to match or surpass
1657   the spectral decay of the UDV model, the resulting validation loss deteriorated significantly compared
1658   to the UDV network. The complete results are demonstrated in Figures SM22 and SM23.
