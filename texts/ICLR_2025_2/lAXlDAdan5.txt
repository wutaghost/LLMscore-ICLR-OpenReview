009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Error correction codes (ECC) are crucial for ensuring reliable information trans-
012            mission in communication systems. Choukroun & Wolf (2022b) recently in-
013            troduced the Error Correction Code Transformer (ECCT), which has demon-
014            strated promising performance across various transmission channels and families
015            of codes. However, its high computational and memory demands limit its prac-
016            tical applications compared to traditional decoding algorithms. Achieving effec-
017            tive quantization of the ECCT presents significant challenges due to its inherently
018
               small architecture, since existing, very low-precision quantization techniques of-
               ten lead to performance degradation in compact neural networks. In this paper,
019
               we introduce a novel acceleration method for transformer-based decoders. We
020
               first propose a ternary weight quantization method specifically designed for the
021            ECCT, inducing a decoder with multiplication-free linear layers. We present an
022            optimized self-attention mechanism to reduce computational complexity via code-
023            aware multi-heads processing. Finally, we provide positional encoding via the
024            Tanner graph eigendecomposition, enabling a richer representation of the graph
025            connectivity. The approach not only matches or surpasses ECCT‚Äôs performance
026            but also significantly reduces energy consumption, memory footprint, and compu-
027            tational complexity. Our method brings transformer-based error correction closer
028            to practical implementation in resource-constrained environments, achieving a
029
               90% compression ratio and reducing arithmetic operation energy consumption by
               at least 224 times on modern hardware.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      Reliable digital communication systems rely heavily on ECC to ensure accurate decoding in the
035
      presence of noise. Developing efficient decoding techniques for these codes remains a complex
036   challenge in communications research. In recent years, the application of machine learning to com-
037   munications has driven the development of advanced decoding methods, leveraging deep learning
038   architectures (Nachmani et al., 2016; 2017; Gruber et al., 2017; Kim et al., 2018; Nachmani & Wolf,
039   2019; Buchberger et al., 2020; Choukroun & Wolf, 2024a;c). Notably, the work of Choukroun &
040   Wolf (2022b) introduced a Transformer-based decoder (Vaswani et al., 2017) adapted to the ECC
041   setting, demonstrating significant improvements over traditional methods across multiple code fam-
042   ilies.
043   Despite these advancements, the ECCT and similar neural decoders face significant challenges due
044   to their high memory requirements, energy consumption, and computational complexity. These
045   resource-intensive solutions pose substantial barriers to deployment in many physical communica-
046   tion systems, where efficiency and practicality are paramount, thus constraining the broader adoption
047   and further refinement of these advanced decoding techniques.
048   Neural network (NN) quantization offers a promising approach to addressing these challenges. Re-
049   cent research has shown that constraining NN weights to 1-bit and ternary representations can be
050   effective (Ma et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2023), particularly when combined with 8-bit activations.
051   This approach replaces multiplication operations with integer addition, significantly reducing en-
052   ergy consumption and memory footprint. However, applying extreme quantization techniques to
053   smaller models presents considerable challenges. Wang et al. (2023) demonstrated that while the
      performance gap between BitNet and FP16 Transformers narrows as model size increases, this gap

054
      is particularly pronounced in smaller models. For instance, a BitNet model with 100M parame-
055   ters (considered small) showed a 10% higher loss than its full-precision counterpart. This disparity
056   would be even more severe for ECCT, whose largest version contains only 2 million parameters.
057   While Ma et al. (2024) improved upon this method, significant performance gaps remain in smaller
058   models, partly due to the use of absolute mean quantization, which lacks flexibility in dynamically
059   adjusting weight sparsity during training.
060
      Recent research on self-attention mechanisms has focused on reducing complexity and memory us-
061   age, particularly in large language models. Two main approaches have emerged: sparse attention
062   methods (Beltagy et al., 2020; Zaheer et al., 2020; Child et al., 2019) and attention approximations
063   (Choromanski et al., 2020). However, these techniques were not designed to optimize smaller mod-
064   els, such as ECCT, which are also more sensitive to the information loss that occurs when applying
065   sparse attention or self-attention approximations than larger ones, due to the limited number of lay-
066   ers. In addition to the capacity-related challenges, ECCT‚Äôs unique architecture poses additional ones.
067   ECCT‚Äôs inherently sparse code-aware mask is incompatible with sparse attention methods, since it
068   cannot be reduced further without modifying the information brought by the code. Similarly, atten-
069
      tion approximation methods are incompatible because they bypass the step where attention masks
      are applied, making them mask incompatible.
070
071   To address these challenges, we propose a novel approach aimed at significantly reducing the mem-
072   ory footprint, computational complexity, and energy consumption of ECCT, thereby enhancing its
073   viability for real-world applications. Our method introduces three key innovations: (i) Weight quan-
074   tization to the ternary domain through Adaptive Absolute Percentile (AAP) quantization. (ii) Head
075
      Partitioning Self Attention (HPSA), an efficient multi-head self-attention mechanism tailored for bi-
      partite graph message passing (MP), designed to reduce computational complexity and runtime. (iii)
076
      Spectral positional encoding (SPE) of the Tanner graph by processing its Laplacian eigenspace. The
077
      Tanner graph Laplacian eigenspace forms a meaningful local coordinate system, providing structural
078   information that is lost with ECCT‚Äôs binary masking, without affecting inference runtime.
079
080   Our experimental results, conducted across a diverse range of codes, demonstrate that this approach
081
      not only matches, and in some cases exceeds, the performance of ECCT, but also offers computa-
      tional complexity comparable to that of Belief Propagation (BP) Pearl (1988). These findings repre-
082
      sent a significant step towards making transformer-based error correction practical for communica-
083
      tion systems with limited computational resources, potentially bridging the gap between advanced
084   neural decoding techniques and traditional efficient algorithms, such as BP.

089   Neural decoders for ECC have evolved from model-based methods, which implement parameter-
090   ized versions of classical BP (Nachmani et al., 2016; 2018; Nachmani & Wolf, 2019; Caciularu
091   et al., 2021), to model-free approaches utilizing general NN architectures (Kim et al., 2018; Gruber
092   et al., 2017; Bennatan et al., 2018; Cammerer et al., 2017; Choukroun & Wolf, 2024a). A signif-
093
      icant advancement in this field is the ECCT (Choukroun & Wolf, 2022b; 2024a;b), which, along
      with its extension using a denoising diffusion process (Choukroun & Wolf, 2022a), has achieved
094
      SOTA performance across various codes. These neural decoders primarily target short to moderate-
095
      length codes, addressing scenarios where classical decoders may not achieve optimal performance.
096   Subsequently, Park et al. (2023; 2024) demonstrated improved performance, but at the expense of
097   increased computational cost.
098
099
      Transformers, while being powerful architectures, are resource-intensive. In response to the need
      to optimize large language models (LLMs), numerous quantization methods have been developed
100
      (Gholami et al., 2021; Wan et al., 2024; Zhu et al., 2024). These techniques fall into two cate-
101
      gories: post-training quantization (PTQ) (Choukroun et al., 2019; Frantar et al., 2023; Chee et al.,
102   2024) and quantization-aware training (QAT). Due to the resource-intensive nature of LLMs, recent
103   studies have focused mainly on PTQ because of its low computational requirement and training over-
104   head. However, PTQ often utilizes high-precision parameters, making it difficult to fully exploit the
105   efficiency of quantization. In contrast, QAT has higher potential for accuracy but generally requires
106   more resources and time, leaving research on QAT of LLMs in its preliminary stages (Jeon et al.,
107   2024). Despite these challenges, notable work has emerged in QAT for LLMs. Wang et al. (2023)
      demonstrated effective quantization of weights to {-1, 1} values and activations to 8-bit integers.

      An enhanced approach by Ma et al. (2024) introduced an additional zero weight value and utilized
109   Abs-mean quantization, highlighting a correlation between model size and performance degradation

      Recent efforts to address the computational limitations of self-attention mechanisms in transformers
      have focused on acceleration techniques. One approach approximates the self-attention function,
113
      reducing its computational cost from quadratic to linear time complexity (Choromanski et al., 2020).

      Other methods, such as those proposed by Child et al. (2019) and Beltagy et al. (2020), combine
115   local and global attention to improve efficiency. Zaheer et al. (2020) further refines these methods

118   and values across attention heads. Building on this, Ainslie et al. (2023) presents grouped-query
119   attention, which uses fewer key-value heads to achieve results comparable to multi-head attention,
120   but with faster computation. Additionally, Pope et al. (2023) introduces an optimized key-value
121   cache mechanism to accelerate inference time.
122   Transformers have also been applied to graph-structured data, introducing graph structure as a soft
123   inductive bias to address limitations of Graph neural networks (GNNs), such as over-squashing
124   (Alon & Yahav, 2021; Topping et al., 2022). Dwivedi & Bresson (2020) proposed using Laplacian

128   addresses the sign ambiguity of eigenvectors, random-walk PE (Dwivedi et al., 2022), and PE based
129
      on the gradients of eigenvectors (Beaini et al., 2021).

190
191   Our proposed method enhances ECCT through several key modifications designed to improve both
192   performance and efficiency. The primary enhancements are as follows:
193
194   1. We replace all linear layers within the Transformer blocks with our novel Adaptive Absolute
195      Percentile (AAP) Linear layers. This modification introduces an adaptive quantization approach,
196      achieving ternary weight representation and thereby improving the model‚Äôs efficiency.
197   2. We introduce a novel self-attention mechanism, HPSA, which supersedes the CASA used in
198      ECCT (Choukroun & Wolf, 2022b). HPSA significantly reduces memory footprint, computa-
199      tional complexity, and runtime, thus enhancing the overall efficiency of the model. To the best of
200      our knowledge, our approach is the first to map the structure of the graph into patterns, with each
201
         group of heads within the multihead self-attention mechanism applying a specific pattern.
202   3. We incorporate the SPE derived from the Tanner graph‚Äôs Laplacian eigenspace. This approach
203      is inspired by Kreuzer et al. (2021)‚Äôs method of injecting a soft inductive bias of the graph‚Äôs
204      structure into the model, enabling the integration of a fine-grained connectivity absent in ECCT‚Äôs
205      binary mask.
206   4. To further optimize the model‚Äôs efficiency, we replace (Mirzadeh et al., 2023) Gaussian Error
207      Linear Units (GeLUs) (Hendrycks & Gimpel, 2016) with Rectified Linear Units (ReLUs).
208   5. We introduce a two-phased training process to enhance the model‚Äôs performance.
209
210   This change simplifies the activation function to a thresholding operator which further contributes
211   to complexity reduction.

      4.2   H EAD PARTITIONING S ELF ATTENTION
291
      While the CASA mechanism of ECCT has demonstrated effective performance in decoding, we
292   aim to further optimize its computational efficiency since we seek to develop neural decoders with
293   complexity comparable to their classical counterparts such as BP. To this end, we introduce Head
294   Partitioning Self Attention (HPSA), which maintains the effectiveness of CASA while significantly
295   reducing computational complexity. HPSA strategically divides ECCT‚Äôs masking via the attention
296   heads into two groups: first-ring and second-ring MP heads. This division not only enhances effi-
297   ciency but also introduces a graph-structure inductive bias by distinguishing between neighbors and
298   second-ring connections, in contrast to the Code-Aware mask in ECCT. An illustration of HPSA is
299   provided in Figure 2.
300
301   First Group: First-Ring Message Passing This group of heads performs attention between near-
302   est neighbors in the Tanner graph. This process, which we term first-ring MP, facilitates communi-
303
      cation between variable nodes and check nodes. The corresponding attention masks are the c ‚Üí v
      and v ‚Üí c in Figure 3, demonstrating the increased sparsity of HPSA compared to the Code-Aware
304
      mask from ECCT.
305
306
      Second Group: Second-Ring Message Passing The second group focuses on what we call
307
      second-ring connections. These heads apply attention only between nodes at a distance of two
308
      in the Tanner graph. This allows for MP between variable nodes and other variable nodes, as well
309   as between check nodes and other check nodes. The corresponding attention masks are the c ‚Üí c
310   and v ‚Üí v in Figure 3, further illustrating the sparsity enhancement of HPSA.
311
312
      By structuring the attention mechanism, HPSA achieves results comparable to CASA while drasti-
      cally reducing complexity. This approach brings the computational efficiency of our method closer
313
      to that of the BP algorithm, moving us significantly closer to practical implementation in resource-
314
      constrained environments.

      Compression Rate The linear layers in the ECCT model constitute over 95% of the total weight
357   count, including the channel‚Äôs output embedding. By employing ternary values, which theoretically
358   require only 1.58 bits for representation, we achieve significant compression. Replacing FP32 values
359   with ternary values results in a 95% reduction in the memory footprint of these layers. Consequently,
360   the AECCT‚Äôs overall memory footprint is reduced to approximately 10% of the original ECCT,
361   achieving a compression rate of around 90%.
362
363   Energy Consumption Energy consumption is a critical factor, especially when deploying the
364   AECCT on edge devices or in data centers, as it directly impacts battery life and operational costs.
365   We base our analysis on energy consumption models for addition and multiplication operations on
366   7nm and 45nm chips for FP32 and INT8, as outlined by Horowitz (2014); Zhang et al. (2022);
367
      Wang et al. (2023). Our findings indicate that the AECCT achieves substantial energy savings.
      Specifically, it reduces the energy consumption of arithmetic operations in linear layers by at least
368
      224 times on 7nm chips and 139 times on 45nm chips, compared to the original ECCT.

570   7   C ONCLUSIONS
571
572   We introduced the AECCT, an enhanced version of the ECCT initially proposed by Choukroun &
573   Wolf (2022b). The AECCT integrates several novel techniques: Adaptive Absolute Percentile Quan-
574   tization, which compresses the linear layer weights in the Transformer encoder blocks to ternary
575   values; Head Partitioning Self-Attention, which replaces the code-aware self-attention module, sig-
576
      nificantly reducing complexity; and Tanner Graph Positional Encoding, which improves the model‚Äôs
      overall effectiveness. The AECCT achieves a complexity level comparable to BP while reducing
577
      memory usage, energy consumption, and computational complexity, all while delivering perfor-
578
      mance on par with the ECCT. Altogether, these enhancements bring transformer-based error correc-
579   tion decoders closer to practical deployment in real-world communication systems, offering notable
580   improvements in the reliability of physical layer communications. As future work, we wish to ex-
581   plore learned Tanner-graph-based positioning techniques and apply pattern-based head partitioning
582   to other structured learning problems. In addition, we wish to explore implementing dedicated
583   hardware that can leverage the ternary-weight linear layer, which requires only integer additions and
584   subtractions, and efficiently support sparse self-attention mechanism to maximize the computational
585   benefits of the proposed method.
586   In a broader context, our work addresses a gap in the literature regarding the acceleration of small
587   Transformers, particularly those where attention patterns are dictated by the problem domain. The
588   novel quantization method we propose enables exact localized adaptations, and the head partitioning
589   method we propose addresses any hierarchical or structured data.
