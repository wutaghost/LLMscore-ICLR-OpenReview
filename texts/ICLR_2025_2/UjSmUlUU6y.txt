009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Recent advancements in large language models (LLMs) have extended their capa-
012            bilities to handle long contexts. However, increasing the number of model layers
013            and the length of input sequences significantly escalates the memory required to
014            store key-value (KV) cache, posing challenges for efficient inference. To mitigate
015            this issue, we present SimLayerKV, a simple yet effective method that reduces
016            inter-layer KV cache redundancies by selectively dropping cache in identified lazy
017            layers. Our approach is based on the observation that certain layers in long-context
018
               LLMs exhibit “lazy” behavior, contributing less to modeling long-range depen-
               dencies compared to non-lazy layers. By analyzing attention weight patterns, we
019
               find that the behavior of these lazy layers is consistent across tokens during gener-
020
               ation for a given input. This insight motivates our SimLayerKV, which identifies
021            lazy layers and reduces their KV cache accordingly. SimLayerKV is training-
022            free, generalizable, and can be implemented with only seven lines of code. We
023            conduct extensive experiments on three representative LLMs, e.g., LLaMA2-7B,
024            LLaMA3-8B, and Mistral-7B across 16 tasks from the LongBench benchmark.
025            The results demonstrate that SimLayerKV achieves a KV cache compression ratio
026            of 5× with only a 1.2% performance drop when combined with 4-bit quantization.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Transformer-based autoregressive large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated exceptional
032   performance across a wide range of tasks, such as question answering and arithmetic reasoning (Wei
033
      et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2022; Zhou et al., 2022; Yao et al., 2023). Recent advancements have ex-
      tended their capabilities to handle long contexts, with models like Llama-3.1 supporting context
034
      lengths up to 128K tokens (Dubey et al., 2024) and Gemini-Pro-1.5 handling up to 1 million to-
035
      kens (Reid et al., 2024). A critical component of these models during inference is the key-value
036   (KV) cache, which stores precomputed key and value tensors for each token in the language se-
037   quence to avoid recomputing them for each attention layer. However, as the number of model layers
038   and input lengths increases, the memory required for storing the KV cache grows significantly, pos-
039   ing challenges for inference efficiency (Zhang et al., 2024b; Wang et al., 2024a; Li et al., 2024). For
040   example, with an input sequence length of 128K tokens, the memory required for the KV cache in
041   Llama2-7B amounts to approximately 62.5 GB GPU memory, which is significantly larger than the
042   13.2 GB needed for the model parameters.
043   To address the challenge, various methods have recently been introduced to reduce the KV cache
044   storage (Zhang et al., 2024b; Li et al., 2024; Hooper et al., 2024; Dong et al., 2024a; Yang et al.,
045   2024c). One approach is quantization (Hooper et al., 2024; Dong et al., 2024a; Yang et al., 2024c;
046   Dong et al., 2024b; Kang et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024c; Sheng et al., 2023), which stores the KV
047   cache in low-bit formats. Another approach resorts to eviction (Zhang et al., 2024b; Li et al., 2024;
048   Zhang et al., 2024a; Yang et al., 2024b), which only preserves the most important tokens selected
049   based on carefully crafted metrics. However, these works predominantly address intra-layer re-
050
      dundancies, neglecting the potential savings from inter-layer redundancies (Liu et al., 2024a), as
      illustrated in Figure 1.
051
052   Recent studies (Rajput et al., 2024; Brandon et al., 2024; Wu & Tu, 2024; Liao & Vargas, 2024; Wu
053   & Tu, 2024; Liu et al., 2024a) have begun to explore inter-layer KV cache condense, leveraging re-
      dundancies across layers to reduce KV cache at the layer level. For example, Cross-Layer Attention

083   To investigate this character for the attention layer, we conducte preliminary experiments (Section 4)
084   and identified three key findings: (1) Certain layers in long-context LLMs exhibit “lazy” behavior,
085   primarily focusing on semantically unimportant tokens (e.g., the initial few tokens) and the most
086   recent ones during answer generation. (2) Lazy layers are less important than non-lazy layers w.r.t.
087   long-context capability: trimming KV cache in non-lazy layers significantly degrades model per-

090

091   The appearance of lazy layers suggests that we can directly reduce the KV cache for these layers
092   without altering the cache of non-lazy layers or merging cache across layers. Building on this in-
093   sight, we propose SimLayerKV, a simple yet effective method for inter-layer KV cache reduction.
094   This dynamic, selective reduction in KV cache decreases the number of layers requiring cache reten-
095   tion, thereby enhancing computational efficiency. Specifically, we analyze the attention allocation
096
      patterns in each layer to determine whether it qualifies as a lazy layer. We then trim the KV cache
      in lazy layers while retaining the full KV cache in non-lazy layers (see Figure 1(c)). We conduct
097
      extensive experiments on three representative LLMs (i.e., LLama2-7B-chat (Touvron et al., 2023),
098
      LLama3-8B-Instruct (Dubey et al., 2024), and Mistral-7B-Instruct (Jiang et al., 2023)) across 16
099   tasks from LongBench (Bai et al., 2023). The results demonstrate that SimLayerKV achieves a KV
100   cache compression ratio of 5× with only a 1.2% drop in performance when combined with a 4-bit
101   quantization (Liu et al., 2024c). Meanwhile, it integrates seamlessly into popular inference frame-
102   works with just seven lines of code. Additionally, we evaluate SimLayerKV on the Ruler (Hsieh
103   et al., 2024) datasets using Mistral-7B-Instruct, focusing on tasks like Needle-in-a-Haystack (NIAH)
104   and scaling the context length from 4K to 32K, where it performed strongly. Even with input texts at
105   32K, performance only dropped by 4.4%. The contributions of this work are summarized as follows:
106
107   • We observe the phenomenon of lazy layers in long-context LLMs and propose two strategies for
        identifying them at either the prefilling or decoding stage.

      2   R ELATED WORK
116
117   Due to the autoregressive architectures of transformer-based LLMs, the key and value states of
118   previously generated tokens can be stored as the KV cache, which facilitates the generation of sub-
119
      sequent tokens without redundant computations. However, despite its benefits, caching introduces
      a significant bottleneck during inference as it must reside in GPU memory. Several works (Prabhu
120
      et al., 2024; Kwon et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2024; Ye et al., 2024) have focused on optimizing KV
121
      cache memory at the system level. Other research has investigated reducing KV cache memory re-
122   quirements by modifying model architectures (Shazeer, 2019; Brandon et al., 2024; Goldstein et al.,
123   2024; Nawrot et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024a; Yu et al., 2024). For example, grouped-query attention
124   (GQA) (Ainslie et al., 2023) divides the query heads into multiple groups, with each sharing its own
125   set of keys and values. However, these techniques typically need to be applied during pre-training,
126   which can be resource-intensive.
127
      A different line of research focuses on reducing the KV cache memory usage post pre-training.
128   Some techniques (Xiao et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024a; Zhang et al., 2024b; Liu et al.,
129   2024b; Yang et al., 2024b; Zhang et al., 2024a) identify redundant tokens within each attention layer
130   and evict their associated KV cache, thereby effectively lowering memory usage. Other methods
131   (Hooper et al., 2024; Dong et al., 2024a; Yang et al., 2024c; Dong et al., 2024b; Kang et al., 2024;
132   Sheng et al., 2023) reduce memory consumption by quantizing KV cache from full precision to
133   lower bit values. However, these methods primarily exploit intra-layer KV cache redundancies
134   while overlooking those across layers. These techniques are orthogonal to our approach and can
135   potentially be combined for further improvements.
136   A distinct line of research (Rajput et al., 2024; Brandon et al., 2024; Wu & Tu, 2024; Liao & Vargas,
137   2024; Wu & Tu, 2024; Liu et al., 2024a), more closely aligned with our focus, explores the inter-
138   layer KV cache redundancies. For instance, CLA (Brandon et al., 2024) reduces overall KV cache
139   storage by reusing the KV cache from the current layer in subsequent layers. Mix Attention (Rajput
140   et al., 2024) integrates cross-layer cache sharing with sliding window attention, which retains only
141   a small subset of recent tokens in the KV cache, thereby further reducing memory usage. However,
142
      these methods require additional training, which is computationally demanding. In contrast, Mini-
      Cache (Reid et al., 2024) offers a tuning-free solution by merging every two adjacent layers through
143
      spherical interpolation, assuming equal contribution from all layers within the merged set. Our Sim-
144
      LayerKV approach differs by selectively trimming lazy layers, based on the observation that not all
145   layers contribute equally to the overall generation.

148   3   P RELIMINARY
149
150   Before introducing SimLayerKV, we formalize our notation and provide a brief overview of the gen-
151   erative inference in autoregressive LLMs, which is the key background knowledge for our method.
152   We denote the input prompt X = {x0 , · · · , xm−1 }, representing a sequence of tokens, where m
153   is the number of tokens in the input prompt, indicating the sequence length. The total number of
154   tokens, including both the input prompt and the generated responses, is denoted as n. The key and
155   value cache for token xi are represented by Kxi and Vxi , respectively.
156   Inference stages. The typical generative LLM inference process involves two stages: (1) Prefilling:
157   the autoregressive LLM processes the input prompt X by parallel computing, and also saves the KV
158   cache of each token xi ∈ X, where i = 0, 1, · · · , m − 1. The output of the last token in this stage
159   is the first token xm of the response. (2) Decoding: after the prefilling stage is completed, the LLM
160   generates output tokens xj one by one, where j = m + 1, m + 2, · · · , and saves their KV cache. In
161   each decoding step, a new token xj is generated based on the current token xj−1 and the KV Cache
      stored from earlier steps, continuing until a stop criterion is met.

190   4                     O BSERVATIONS
191
192   In this section, we analyze the attention patterns during the prefilling and decoding phase in long-
193   context LLMs, providing insights that motivate our approach to reducing KV cache based on the
194   layer-specific roles in attention allocation. The study is conducted on the LLaMA3-8B-Instruct
195
      model (Dubey et al., 2024) using random samples from the LongBench (Bai et al., 2023) benchmark.
      Our key findings are as follows:
196
197   Layer behavior in long context LLMs during decoding. Previous research (Xiao et al., 2023) has
198   shown that a large portion of attention in LLMs tends to focus on semantically unimportant tokens
199   (e.g., the first few tokens) and the most recent tokens. We refer to this pattern as lazy behavior,
200   where the model “takes shortcuts” by primarily attending to the beginning and end of the sequence,
201
      similar to someone skimming a paper by only reading the first few words in the abstract and the
      conclusion. Although this phenomenon is also known as “attention sink” (Xiao et al., 2023), we
202
      choose to call it “lazy behavior” in our context to better highlight the model’s tendency to overlook
203
      the middle portions of the sequence, emphasizing the shortcut nature. However, in our experiments
204   (See Table 1 and Table 3), we find that when KV cache are retained for only these tokens across all
205   layers, the long-context capabilities of LLMs degrade sharply. This raises an important question:
206   does this lazy behavior disappear when processing long texts?
207
      Through our analysis, we observe that even when handling long texts, many layers continue to
208
      exhibit this lazy behavior during decoding (e.g., about 55% in LLama3-8B-Instruct in LongBench
209
      benchmark). Figure 2 presents the attention patterns across four different layers (0, 10, 20, and
210   30). We observe that some layers (e.g., layer 0) do not follow a clear pattern in attention weight
211   distribution, while others (e.g., 20) show a clear lazy behavior pattern. Based on this observation,
212   we define a lazy layer as one that primarily attends to a limited subset of tokens, including both
213   the initial tokens Xinitial = {x0 , x1 , x2 , x3 } and recent w tokens Xrecent , while allocating minimal
214   attention to the rest of the tokens in the sequence during decoding stage. Intuitively, this suggests
215   that in these lazy layers, most of the KV cache can be dropped, retaining only the portions the model
      relies on during its “shortcut” behavior, i.e., Xinitial and Xrecent .

      5    M ETHODOLOGY: S IM L AYER KV
256
257
      In this section, we introduce our method SimLayerKV for reducing inter-layer KV cache usage
258   in LLMs by leveraging the concept of lazy layers to optimize memory efficiency across layers.
259   Empirical observations in Section 4 reveal that in certain layers, LLMs tend to take shortcuts by
260   predominantly allocating attention weights to the initial and most recent tokens, denoted as Xinitial
261   and Xrecent , respectively. We refer to these layers as lazy layers because they contribute less to
262   modeling long-range dependencies compared to non-lazy layers. Notably, whether a layer functions
263   as lazy remains relatively consistent given a specific input sequence. This consistency suggests that
264   attention patterns can be predicted from the allocation during the generation of previous tokens,
265   enabling early identification of lazy layers in the generation process.
266   Based on our observations of lazy layers, we aim to optimize memory usage by trimming the KV
267   cache in these layers. Some existing approaches have attempted to optimize attention mechanisms
268   at different layers. For instance, Gemma 2 (Team et al., 2024) employs a predefined mixture of full
269   attention and sliding window attention across different layers during training, treating certain layers
      as lazy layers. However, this approach relies on a fixed, predefined structure and lacks adaptability

270
      to the input data. In contrast, our method dynamically identifies lazy layers based on their attention
271   allocation patterns, without the need for additional tuning or predefined settings. This dynamic
272   identification allows our model to more flexibly optimize KV cache usage, adapting to different
273   input data more efficiently. Our approach consists of two components: identifying the function of
274   each layer (i.e., whether a layer is lazy) and trimming the KV cache in those identified lazy layers.

      5.1   I DENTIFYING THE LAYER FUNCTION
277
278   To apply SimLayerKV, the first step is to identify which layers function as lazy layers based on
279   their attention allocation patterns. Once these layers are identified, we can proceed to trim their KV
280   cache to optimize memory usage. In the following, we detail our strategies for identifying the layer
281   function. Corresponding to the two stages of the inference process (i.e., prefilling and decoding),
282   we propose two different identification strategies.
283   1) Last tokens in prefilling: We analyze the attention weight allocation when processing the last
284   wlast processed tokens Xlast = {xm−wlast +1 , · · · , xm } to identify lazy layers during prefilling. For

286   all tokens in Xlast . If this average exceeds a predefined threshold δ, we classify the layer l as lazy;
287   otherwise, it is considered non-lazy. This can be formalized as:

353   6     E XPERIMENTS
354
355   In this section, we empirically validate that SimLayerKV can accelerate decoding while maintaining
356   long-text capabilities and uncover several insightful findings.
357
358   6.1   S ETTINGS
359
360   Baselines. To evaluate the effectiveness of our proposed SimLayerKV, we compare it against the
361   following baselines: 1) Full KV (Full): A method that retains KV cache for all tokens at each layer
362   during generation. 2) Streaming LLM (Str.) (Xiao et al., 2023): An intra-layer KV cache reduction
363
      technique that keeps only the KV cache for the first four tokens and the most recent w tokens at each
      attention layer during generation. 3) MiniCache (Mini.) (Liu et al., 2024a): An inter-layer KV cache
364
      reduction method that merges KV cache of every two adjacent layers after the model’s midpoint
365
      using spherical interpolation while retaining important tokens to reduce cache storage. Additionally,
366   for both MiniCache and our SimLayerKV, we evaluate their performance when combined with 4-bit
367   quantization (Liu et al., 2024c) to assess their compatibility with quantization techniques.
368
369
      Datastes and evaluation metrics. To evaluate SimLayerKV’s performance on tasks with long-
      context inputs, we test it on the LongBench benchmark (Bai et al., 2023) and compare the re-
370
      sults with baseline methods. LongBench is a multi-task benchmark designed to assess the long-
371
      context capabilities of LLMs, consisting of datasets that span various tasks such as single-document
372   QA (Kočiskỳ et al., 2018; Dasigi et al., 2021), multi-document QA (Yang et al., 2018; Ho et al.,
373   2020; Trivedi et al., 2022; He et al., 2017), summarization (Huang et al., 2021; Zhong et al., 2021;
374   Fabbri et al., 2019; Wu et al., 2023), few-shot learning (Joshi et al., 2017; Gliwa et al., 2019; Joshi
375   et al., 2017; NLPCC, 2014), synthetic tasks (Raffel et al., 2020), and code generation (Guo et al.,
376   2023; Liu et al., 2023). For evaluation, we use the metrics recommended by LongBench. Addi-
377   tionally, we provide the compression ratios for both the number of layers and memory usage of the
      KV cache. For layers, the ratio is calculated as the total number of layers divided by the number of


                                                                                             7
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


378
      layers with reduced KV cache. For the KV cache, the ratio is the original memory usage divided by
379   the memory usage after compression. Due to space constraints, we only include the performance of
380   16 randomly selected tasks out of the 21 LongBench tasks in the main text. The performance on the
381   remaining 5 tasks is provided in Appendix A.3 Table 9.
382
      We also evaluate whether SimLayerKV can preserve in-context retrieval capabilities while trim-
383
      ming KV cache in lazy layers. The evaluation is conducted on the Needle-In-A-Haystack (NIAH)
384
      benchmark (Kamradt, 2023) including various types and quantities of needles, along with tasks such
385   as aggregation for common/frequent words, question answering (QA), and multi-hop variable trac-
386   ing (VT), all provided by the Ruler benchmark (Hsieh et al., 2024). We report the performance of
387   Mistral-7B-Instruct with input context lengths of 4K, 8K, 16K, and 32K. The evaluation is conducted
388   using the metrics recommended by Ruler.
389
      Implementation details. Our experiments are based on widely used LLMs, specifically LLaMa2-
390
      7B-chat (Touvron et al., 2023), LLaMa3-8B-Instruct (Dubey et al., 2024), and Mistral-7B-
391   Instruct (Jiang et al., 2023). The input context window sizes are 4K, 8K, and 32K, with average
392   tokenized sequence lengths of approximately 13K, 10K, and 12K in LongBench. It is worth noting
393   that we do not use different thresholds for each task. Instead, we search for the optimal threshold
394   based on the synthetic Need-in-a-Haystack task and apply the same threshold across all tasks in dif-
395   ferent benchmarks. The thresholds (δ) for the models are 0.65, 0.9, and 0.8 respectively. We adopt a
396   generative format where answers are produced using greedy decoding for all tasks. We chose the first
397   token identification strategy during the decoding stage in our experiments. For MiniCache, as the
398   code was not open-sourced before our submission, we reimplemented it based on the original paper
399
      and the SLERP (Shoemake, 1985) code it references. We followed all the hyper-parameters outlined
      in the paper, except for the number of retention tokens. To ensure a fair comparison, we set the num-
400
      ber of retention tokens to 1024, matching the window size w used in our SimLayerKV method. Note
401
      that even with the same retention window size, MiniCache’s compression ratio is still lower than that
402   of our SimLayerKV as shown in Table 2. All the experiments are conducted using NVIDIA A100.

      6.3        E XPERIMENTS ON RULER
468
469   Table 3 summarizes the performance across various tasks in the Ruler (Hsieh et al., 2024) bench-
470   mark, with the context length ranging from 4K to 32K. We find that SimLayerKV maintains strong
471   performance on the Single Key, Multiple Keys, and Multiple Values Needle-In-A-Haystack (NIAH)
472   tasks, exhibiting minimal to no degradation. For example, even with a 32K input context, SimLay-
473   erKV results in only a slight performance drop of 0.47% compared to the full KV cache. Our method
474   also performs well on the Question Answering and Variable Tracking tasks, which involve long con-
475   text capabilities similar to NIAH. However, we observe a performance drop (8.2% on average) on
476   the Mutliple Queries NIAH with SimLayerKV. This may be due to the data-dependent nature of lazy
477
      layer identification in our approach. Ideally, varying the number of queries should lead to different
      layers being identified as lazy and reduced accordingly, but currently, the same layers are reduced
478
      regardless of the query count. We also observe a similar phenomenon in aggregation tasks. Although
479
      the Common Words Extraction (CWE) and Frequent Words Extraction (FWE) tasks are quite simi-
480   lar, both aiming to return the top-K frequent words in the context, our method shows a significantly
481   more pronounced decline in performance on CWE. One possible reason is that, in the FWE task, the
482   value of K is consistently fixed at 3, while in the CWE task, K increases with the context length,
483   making the task progressively more challenging for our method. In addition, we measure throughput
484   under the maximum batch size for input sequence lengths of 4K, 8K, 16K, and 32K using LLaMA-
485   3-8B. The throughput (tokens/s) for SimLayerKV relative to the Full method was 1.44×, 1.78×,
      2.17×, and 1.75×, respectively, suggesting our method can increase the throughput effectively.

512   6.4                      A BLATION STUDIES & ANALYSIS
513
514   Impact of threshold on lazy layer identification. To assess the impact of the threshold δ in iden-
515
      tifying lazy layers, we conduct an ablation analysis using the LLama3-8B-Instruct model, varying δ
      from 0, 0.2, up to 1. As illustrated in Figure 5, we observe that as the threshold increases, the model’s
516
      performance shows little to no change or only slow improvement initially. However, after exceeding
517
      0.6, the performance improves rapidly, and by 0.9, it approaches the performance seen when the
518   threshold equals 1 in most tasks. This indicates that as the threshold increases, the likelihood of
519   accurately identifying and trimming truly lazy layers increases, allowing the model to maintain high
520   performance while reducing unnecessary computations.
521
      Effect of different strategies for dropping KV cache at layer level. As shown in Figure 6 (a-d),
522
      we experiment with four different strategies. We ensured the same number of dropped KV cache
523
      for each strategy, except for Full. The results shown in Figure 6 (e-f) indicate significant reductions
524   for Pyramid and Random strategies, suggesting that the predefined expectations about each layer’s
525   function may not fully align with their actual roles. Moreover, the performance difference between
526   SLKV-prefill and SLKV-decode strategies is minimal, with only slight reductions compared to the
527   full KV cache (0.20% and 0.28% on average, respectively). This indicates that both approaches are
528   effective in reducing cache usage while maintaining performance, regardless of whether lazy layers
529   are identified during the prefilling or decoding stages.

531   7                   C ONCLUSION
532
533
      In this work, we introduced SimLayerKV, a simple yet effective method for compressing the KV
534   cache in LLMs. By identifying lazy layers and trimming their KV cache, SimLayerKV effectively
535   reduced inter-layer KV cache redundancies. Experiments on three different LLMs across 16 datasets
536   from the LongBench benchmark demonstrated that SimLayerKV, with only seven lines of code,
537   achieves a KV cache compression ratio of 5× with only a 1.2% drop in performance when combined
538   with 4-bit quantization. For future work, we aim to combine our inter-layer KV cache compression
539   method, SimLayerKV, with other powerful intra-layer compression methods like H2O (Zhang et al.,
      2024b) to further enhance performance and efficiency.

      A.1   L IMITATION
759
760   While our SimLayerKV has demonstrated significant advantages in inter-layer KV cache compres-
761   sion, we have primarily focused on combining it with quantization, as quantization is one of the
762   most widely used techniques. However, there are many other KV cache optimization methods, such
763   as intra-layer eviction, which are orthogonal to our approach. In this study, we have not explored
764   the potential of integrating our method with these techniques. In the future, we aim to combine
765   our method with other optimization strategies, to further improve performance and efficiency. This
766   will help validate the effectiveness of our method in a broader framework and potentially lead to
767   even greater performance gains. Meanwhile, for simplicity, we have only explored KV cache re-
768
      dundancies across layers in this work. In the future, we plan to extend our approach to consider
      redundancies across attention heads as well.
