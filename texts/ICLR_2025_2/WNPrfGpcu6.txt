009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Image deraining aims to remove rain streaks from rainy images and restore clear
012            backgrounds. Currently, some research that employs the Fourier transform has
013            proved to be effective for image deraining, due to it acting as an effective fre-
014            quency prior for capturing rain streaks. However, despite there exists dependency
015            of low frequency and high frequency in images, these Fourier-based methods
016
               rarely exploit the correlation of different frequencies for conjuncting their learning
               procedures, limiting the full utilization of frequency information for image derain-
017
               ing. Alternatively, the recently emerged Mamba technique depicts its effectiveness
018
               and efficiency for modeling correlation in various domains (e.g., spatial, tempo-
019            ral), and we argue that introducing Mamba into its unexplored Fourier spaces to
020            correlate different frequencies would help improve image deraining. This mo-
021            tivates us to propose a new framework termed FourierMamba, which performs
022            image deraining with Mamba in the Fourier space. Owing to the unique arrange-
023            ment of frequency orders in Fourier space, the core of FourierMamba lies in the
024            scanning encoding of different frequencies, where the low-high frequency order
025            formats exhibit differently in the spatial dimension (unarranged in axis) and chan-
026            nel dimension (arranged in axis). Therefore, we design FourierMamba that corre-
027
               lates Fourier space information in the spatial and channel dimensions with distinct
               designs. Specifically, in the spatial dimension Fourier space, we introduce the
028
               zigzag coding to scan the frequencies to rearrange the orders from low to high fre-
029
               quencies, thereby orderly correlating the connections between frequencies; in the
030            channel dimension Fourier space with arranged orders of frequencies in axis, we
031            can directly use Mamba to perform frequency correlation and improve the channel
032            information representation. Extensive experiments reveal that our method outper-
033            forms state-of-the-art methods both qualitatively and quantitatively.

036   1   I NTRODUCTION
037
038   Images taken in rainy conditions exhibit significant degradation in detail and contrast due to rain in
039
      the air, leading to unpleasant visual results and the loss of frequency information. This issue can
      severely impact the performance of outdoor computer vision systems, such as autonomous driving
040
      and video surveillance (Wang et al., 2022a). To mitigate the effects of rain, many image deraining
041
      methods (Fu et al., 2011; Xiao et al., 2022) have emerged in recent years, aiming to remove rain
042   streaks and restore clear backgrounds in images.
043
044   The advent of deep learning has spurred this field forward, with several learning-based deraining
045
      methods achieving remarkable success (Fu et al., 2017b; Yang et al., 2017; Zhang & Patel, 2018).
      Among them, some studies utilize the Fourier transform for deraining in the frequency domain (Zhou
046
      et al., 2023; Guo et al., 2022), proving effective. The key insights inspiring the use of the Fourier
047
      transform for image deraining are twofold: 1) The Fourier transform can separate image degradation
048   and content components to some extent, serving as a prior for image deraining, as shown in Figure 1;
049   2) The Fourier domain possesses global properties, where each pixel in Fourier space is involved
050   with all spatial pixels. Thus, it makes sense to explore the task of rain removal using the Fourier
051   transform. However, despite the existence of low frequency and high frequency dependencies in
052   images, previous Fourier-based methods rarely utilize the correlation of different frequencies to
053   combine their learning process. As shown in Figure 1, the commonly used 1×1 convolutions cannot
      correlate different frequencies, limiting the full utilization of frequency information in the image.

072   Therefore, we seek to exploit the beneficial properties of the Fourier transform while exploring
073   correlating different frequencies.
074   Recently, an improved structured state-space sequence model (S4) with a selective scanning mecha-
075   nism, Mamba, gives us hope. The selective methodology of Mamba can explicitly build the correla-
076   tion among image patches or pixels. Recent studies have witnessed the effectiveness and efficiency
077   of Mamba in various domains such as spatial and temporal. Therefore, we believe that introducing
078   Mamba into its unexplored Fourier space to correlate different frequencies will be advantageous for
079   improving image deraining.
080   In this paper, we propose a novel framework named FourierMamba, which performs image derain-
081   ing using mamba in the Fourier domain. Following the ”spatial interaction + channel evolution”
082   rule that has also been validated on Mamba (Guo et al., 2024; Behrouz et al., 2024), we design the
083   Mamba framework in the Fourier domain from both spatial and channel dimensions. Considering
084   the unique arrangement of frequency orders in the Fourier domain, the core of FourierMamba lies
085   in the scanning encoding of different frequencies, where the low-high frequency order formats unar-
086   ranged in the spatial axis and arranged in the channel axis. Therefore, our proposed FourierMamba
087
      correlates Fourier space information in spatial and channel dimensions with distinct designs.
088   Specifically, in the spatial dimension of the Fourier space, low-high frequencies follow a con-
089   centric circular arrangement with lower frequencies near the center and higher frequencies around
090   the periphery. If previous scanning method (Liu et al., 2024) is used directly, the orderliness be-
091   tween frequencies will be destroyed, as shown in Figure 1. We note that the zigzag coding in the
092   JPEG compression field can place lower-frequency coefficients at the forefront of the array, while
093
      higher-frequency coefficients are positioned at the end. Hence, we introduce the zigzag coding to
      scan the frequency in the spatial dimension, rearranging the order from low to high frequency. Due
094
      to the symmetry of the frequency orders in the Fourier space, we do not directly employ the zigzag
095
      coding in its originally used space; instead, we implement it in a circling-like manner that matches
096   the symmetric frequency orders in Fourier space. In this way, this method orderly correlates the
097   connections between frequencies, as shown in Figure 1. In the channel dimension of the Fourier
098   space, the frequency order is arranged along the axis, following the order of low in the middle to
099   high on both sides. Therefore, we can directly use Mamba for frequency correlation, thus improving
100   channel information representation and enhancing global properties on the channels.
101
      In summary, our contributions are as follows: (1) We propose a novel framework FourierMamba that
102   combines Fourier priors and State Space Model for correlating different frequencies in the Fourier
103   space to enhance image deraining. (2) To rearrange the order from low to high frequency in the
104   spatial dimension Fourier space, we propose a scanning method based on zigzag coding to orderly
105   correlate different frequencies. (3) Based on the channel-dimension Fourier transform, we utilize
106   Mamba to scan on the channels and correlate different frequencies to improve channel information
107   representation. Extensive experiments demonstrate that the proposed FourierMamba surpasses state-
      of-the-art methods both qualitatively and quantitatively.

      2     R ELATED W ORKS
109
110
      Image deraining. Traditional image deraining methods focus on separating rain components by
111
      utilizing meticulously designed priors, such as Gaussian Mixture Models (Li et al., 2016), Sparse
112   Representation Learning (Gu et al., 2017; Fu et al., 2011), and Directional Gradient Priors (Ran
113   et al., 2020). Although these methods are insightful, they often struggle to cope with complex
114   precipitation patterns and the diverse real-world scenarios. The advent of deep learning has heralded
115   a new era for image deraining. (Fu et al., 2017b) introduces pioneering deep residual networks
116   for image deraining. The initiation of CNNs marked a significant advancement, facilitating more
117   nuanced and adaptive processing of rain streaks across a vast array of images (Yang et al., 2017;
118   Zhang & Patel, 2018). With the evolution of transformers, the development of architectures that
119   incorporate attention mechanisms (Valanarasu et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2022b) has further refined
120
      the capacity to recognize and eliminate rain components, addressing previous shortcomings in model
      generalization and detail preservation. COIC (Ran et al., 2024) presents a Context-based Instance-
121
      level Modulation mechanism integrated with rain-/detail-aware contrastive learning to enhance CNN
122
      and Transformer models for improved image deraining on mixed datasets. (Hsu & Chang, 2023)
123   proposes a wavelet approximation-aware residual network, which efficiently removes rain from low-
124   frequency structures and high-frequency details at each level separately. In this work, we propose a
125   novel baseline with a block based on Fourier and Mamba to enhance deraining performance.
126
      Fourier transform. Recently, the Fourier Transform has demonstrated its effectiveness in global
127
      modeling (Chi et al., 2019; 2020). This transformation converts signals into a domain character-
128
      ized by global statistical properties, facilitating advancements across various fields (Huang et al.,
129   2022; Lee et al., 2018; Li et al., 2023; Pratt et al., 2017; Xu et al., 2021; Yang & Soatto, 2020).
130   Due to its efficacy in global modeling, the Fourier Transform has been introduced into low-level
131   vision tasks (Fuoli et al., 2021; Mao et al., 2023). As an early attempt, (Fuoli et al., 2021) proposes
132   a Fourier Transform-based loss to optimize global high-frequency information for efficient image
133   super-resolution. DeepRFT (Mao et al., 2023) is proposed for image deblurring, employing a global
134   receptive field to capture both low and high-frequency characteristics of various blurs, a concept sim-
135   ilarly applied in image inpainting (Suvorov et al., 2022). FECNet (Huang et al., 2022) demonstrates
136   that the amplitude of Fourier features decouples global luminance components, thereby proving ef-
137
      fective for image enhancement. (Yu et al., 2022) observes a similar phenomenon in image dehazing,
      where the amplitude reflects global haze-related information. In contrast, we introduce a progressive
138
      scanning strategy in the Fourier domain, enhancing the global modeling capability while addressing
139
      the directional sensitivity issues of visual Mamba.
140
141   State Space Models. State Space Models (SSMs) have received a lot of attention recently due to
142   their global modeling capabilities as well as linear complexity, with (Gu et al., 2022) initially intro-
143
      ducing the base design of SSM models, and (Mehta et al., 2022) further enhancing their performance
      through gating units.More recently, the performance of Mamba (Gu & Dao, 2023), proposed based
144
      on selective scan mechanism and efficient hardware design, has seen significant enhancement. It
145
      stands as an efficient alternative to Transformers, finding applications in various domains including
146   image classification (Zhu et al., 2024)(Liu et al., 2024), object detection(Chen et al., 2024), and re-
147   mote sensing(Zhao et al., 2024).In the field of image restoration, (Guo et al., 2024) (Shi et al., 2024)
148   initially introduced a general restoration framework based on the Mamba module but did not fully
149   exploit the frequency domain information of images. (Sun et al., 2024) introduces a network com-
150   bining Transformer and Mamba to capture long-range dependencies related to rain. (Yamashita &
151   Ikehara, 2024) achieves effective deraining by parallelizing frequency-domain processing branches
152   with the Mamba branch. (Zhen et al., 2024) introduced a wavelet transform branch, yet the scanning
153   in the wavelet domain fails to fully extract global frequency domain information. This paper pro-
154
      poses a novel Mamba restoration network based on Fourier transform, aiming to comprehensively
      exploit the frequency domain information of images.

252   3.2   S CANNING IN F OURIER S PACE
253
254   Despite the unique characteristics of the selective scan mechanism (S6), it processes input data
255   causally. Given the non-causal nature of visual data, directly applying this strategy to patches and flat
256   images fails to estimate relations with unscanned patches, leading to a ”directional sensitivity” issue
257   constrained by the acceptance domain. Numerous methods have attempted to tackle this problem in
258   the spatial domain (Liu et al., 2024; Guo et al., 2024). However, for image restoration, the Fourier
259   space and its associated priors are crucial. Hence, we explore addressing the ”directional sensitivity”
260
      issue within this domain. Specifically, we customize Fourier scanning strategies from both spatial
      and channel dimensions.
261
262   For the spatial dimension, each pixel point in the Fourier space contains global information, with its
263   frequencies distributed in concentric circles. Scanning methods based on spatial arrangements (Liu
264   et al., 2024) disrupt the high-low frequency relationships in the frequency domain, thus hindering
265   the modeling of image degradation information.
266   Therefore, we aim to devise a scanning method in the Fourier space to progressively model the
267   frequency characteristics of images. An intuitive approach is to calculate the Euclidean distance
268   from each point in the spectrum to the center point. On the shifted Fourier spectrum, the smaller
269   the distance to the center point, the lower the frequency. The flaw of this intuitive approach is that
      for images of different sizes, it requires recalculating the Euclidean distance from each point to the

286   In JPEG compression, zigzag coding is commonly used among the Discrete Cosine Transform
287   (DCT) coefficients of JPEG, where it prioritizes the energy-concentrated low-frequency coefficients
288
      at the beginning of the array, and places the less significant high-frequency coefficients towards
      the end, thereby facilitating more effective compression. Inspired by compression algorithms, we
289
      introduce a method that adopts the zigzag coding approach to scan the magnitude and phase spectra.
290
291   Additionally, due to the symmetry of the two-dimensional Fourier transform, scanning the entire
292   spectrum would disrupt the symmetry in the Fourier space, potentially leading to the collapse of
293   network optimization. Therefore, we scan half of the spectrum and then deduce the other half based
294
      on the central symmetry of the amplitude and the anti-central symmetry of the phase.
295   Specifically, we design two scanning strategies, as illustrated in the Figure 2. The first scanning
296   method employs a dual zigzag pattern named bilateral zigzag, starting from the vertex of the highest
297   frequency on one side of the spectrum, progressing in a zigzag pattern toward the center’s low fre-
298   quencies; similarly, it then zigzags to the opposite side’s highest frequency. This scanning approach
299   not only models the association between high and low frequencies but also takes into account the
300
      periodicity of the Fourier spectrum. Due to the periodic nature of the Fourier transform, the high-
      frequency ends on either side should, in fact, be contiguous. The second method builds upon the
301
      low-to-high frequency sequence established by zigzag scanning and conducts a scan from low to
302
      high frequencies, which is named progressive zigzag. This method is motivated by the tendency
303   of neural networks to initially learn low-frequency information when extracting image characteris-
304   tics. Following the previous method (Liu et al., 2024; Guo et al., 2024), we reverse the above two
305   scanning methods as additional scanning directions.
306
      For the channel dimension Fourier space, since it is a one-dimensional sequence arranged in order
307
      of low to high frequencies, we directly scan it one-dimensionally. Similarly, due to the symmetry of
308
      the Fourier transform, we scan only half and derive the other half. Through Fourier space scanning
309   in both spatial and channel dimensions, we can correlate the connections between frequencies in an
310   orderly manner, thereby making full use of frequency information to improve rain removal.

360   Previous work (Guo et al., 2024) claims that selecting key channels can avoid channel redundancy

413   Datasets. For training, we employ the widely used Rain13k dataset (Chen et al., 2021). It contains
414   13,712 image pairs in the training set, and we evaluate the results on Rain100H (Yang et al., 2017),
415   Rain100L (Yang et al., 2017), Test2800 (Fu et al., 2017b), and Test1200 (Zhang & Patel, 2018).
416
      Evaluation Metrics. Following previous work (Zamir et al., 2021; 2022), we adopt two commonly
417
      used quantitative metrics for evaluations: Peak Signal-to-Noise Ratio (PSNR) (Huynh-Thu & Ghan-
418   bari, 2008) and Structural Similarity Index (SSIM) (Wang et al., 2004).
419
420   Implementation details. Our model is implemented within the PyTorch framework and executed on
421
      an NVIDIA A100 GPU. The number of blocks per layer has an impact on both the model’s parameter
      count and its deraining performance. After balancing the weights, we configure the blocks per layer
422
      as [2, 3, 3, 4, 3, 3, 2], which allows us to achieve commendable performance with a reasonable
423
      number of parameters. We adopt the progressive training strategy. Specifically, we set the total
424   number of iterations to 80,000 and image sizes to [160, 256, 320, 384], with the corresponding batch
425   sizes of [8, 4, 2, 1]. We utilize the Adam optimizer with default parameters. The initial learning rate
426   is established at 3 × e−4 , followed by a gradual decay to 1 × e−6 using a cosine annealing schedule.

530   5     C ONCLUSION
531
532
      In this paper, we propose a novel image deraining framework, FourierMamba, which utilizes mamba
533
      to correlate frequencies in the Fourier space, thus fully exploiting frequency information. Specifi-
534   cally, we design the mamba framework by integrating the unique arrangement of frequency order-
535   ings within the Fourier domain across spatial and channel dimensions. In the spatial dimension,
536   we devise two zigzag-based methods to scan frequencies, systematically correlating them. In the
537   channel dimension, due to the ordered arrangement of frequencies along the axis, we directly ap-
538   ply mamba for frequency correlation. This work introduces a new research strategy to address the
539   underutilization of frequency information in image deraining that affects performance. Extensive
      experimental results on multiple benchmarks validate the effectiveness of the proposed method.

      A.1    L IMITATION
813
814   In this work, we introduce FourierMamba and extensively validate its efficacy for image deraining
815   through experiments. Our experiments primarily leverage the widely used U-shaped architecture.
816   We plan to further validate the effectiveness of combining Fourier priors with Mamba on more
817   architectures, such as isotropic and multi-stage architecture.
818   Furthermore, given the proven priors of Fourier transform for capturing rain streaks, we choose to
819   first validate FourierMamba on image deraining. Our work could also offer novel insights for other
820   low-level vision fields, though it may necessitate integrating priors tailored to the distinct differences
821   between various low-level tasks. Given the universal need across various low-level tasks for Fourier
822   priors and the importance of correlating frequencies, the performing improvements can be positively
823   anticipated. We will explore applications in other low-level tasks in our future work.

825   A.2    B ROADER I MPACTS
826
827   Due to uncontrollable weather conditions, image acquisition systems inevitably suffer interference
828   from rain. Images captured during rainy conditions experience a significant decline in the quality of
829
      object details and contrast due to rain present in the air. Images tainted by rain can also severely im-
      pact the performance of outdoor computer vision systems, including autonomous driving and video
830
      surveillance. Therefore, image deraining itself holds significant research and application value. Our
831
      proposed FourierMamba combines the priors of Fourier space and the correlation modeling capabil-
832   ity of Mamba, enabling the network to tackle more complex image deraining tasks. However, from
833   a societal perspective, negative consequences might also follow. For instance, over-reliance on im-
834   age deraining technology could introduce deviations from actual image textures, affecting effective
835   judgment in autonomous driving and video surveillance. In these cases, it is necessary to combine
836   expert knowledge to make rational decisions.

886   channel-dimension Fourier transform. A pivotal motivation is due to different channels often dis-
887   playing varying properties of degradation information, which also determine the global information
888
      of the image when conjunct different channels. A comparable deduction can be drawn from style
      transfer research, where the Gram matrix signifies global style information (Li et al., 2017). This
889
      inspires us to employ Fourier transform on the channel dimension to enrich the representation of
890
      global information.

      A.9   D IFFERENCES BETWEEN THE PROPOSED METHOD AND F REQ M AMBA
941
942   Our method focuses on customized design based on the characteristics of Fourier space, combining
943   Fourier priors with state space models and exploring the potential of introducing Mamba directly in
944   the Fourier domain. In contrast, FreqMamba operates in the Fourier space using only 1 × 1 con-
945   volutions, which fails to fully utilize the rich frequency information inherent to the Fourier domain.
946   Specifically, FreqMamba applies Mamba scanning in a wavelet-transformed domain. However, the
947   wavelet-transformed domain lacks the notable advantages of the Fourier domain, such as the Fourier
948   transform’s ability to decouple degradations and its global representation properties. Additionally,
949
      after wavelet decomposition, FreqMamba divides the image into multiple patches and performs
      spatial scanning within each patch. This design limits FreqMamba’s ability to effectively model
950
      frequency correlations.
951
952   In contrast, our method performs Mamba scanning directly in the Fourier domain, fully leveraging
953   the global characteristics of the Fourier transform. This allows our approach to better capture rain
954   streaks, which often exhibit high apparent repetitiveness. Consequently, from a visual perspective,
955
      our method demonstrates significantly better performance in removing rain streaks. As shown in
      Figure 7, we show the feature maps and restoration results of FreqMamba and our method. It can be
956
      seen that our method can better capture the rain lines and thus remove the rain lines more cleanly.

1004   A.11    D IFFERENCE BETWEEN MAMBA AND CONVOLUTION IN PROCESSING F OURIER
1005           FREQUENCIES
1006
1007   First, Mamba utilizes sequence modeling to integrate information across all frequency bands, effec-
1008   tively leveraging the complementary relationships between different bands. In contrast, convolution,
1009
       as a local operation, struggles to holistically model global features across all frequency bands when
       processing frequency information in the Fourier domain. This limitation significantly constrains its
1010
       capacity in the Fourier space. Second, Mamba’s sequence modeling is orderly, which can help the
1011
       network establish an orderly dependency relationship between different frequencies. This character-
1012   istic is critical for modeling image degradation information. Conversely, convolution is insufficient
1013   in capturing the dependencies between high and low frequencies in the Fourier domain, thereby
1014   weakening its ability to accurately represent degradation features. In summary, based on these two
1015   advantages, Mamba achieves better coordination of high-frequency and low-frequency information
1016   in the Fourier domain during the image restoration process.
1017
       We process the Fourier frequencies using both Mamba and convolution separately, and then visualize
1018   their features, as shown in Figure 8. It can be seen our method (i.e., Mamba) not only captures rain
1019   streaks effectively but also extracts structural information from the background with high accuracy.
