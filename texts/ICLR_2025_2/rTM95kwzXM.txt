009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               Despite the remarkable success of Large Language Models (LLMs), evaluating
012
               their outputs’ quality regarding preference remains a critical challenge. Existing
013
               works usually leverage a powerful LLM (e.g., GPT4) as the judge for comparing
014            LLMs’ output pairwisely, yet such model-based evaluator is vulnerable to conflict-
015            ing preference, i.e., output A is better than B, B than C, but C than A, causing
016            contradictory evaluation results. To improve model-based preference evaluation,
017            we introduce GED (Preference Graph Ensemble and Denoise), a novel approach
018            that leverages multiple model-based evaluators to construct preference graphs, and
019            then ensemble and denoise these graphs for better, non-contradictory evaluation
020            results. In particular, our method consists of two primary stages: aggregating eval-
021            uations into a unified graph and applying a denoising process to eliminate cyclic
022
               inconsistencies, ensuring a directed acyclic graph (DAG) structure. We provide
               theoretical guarantees for our framework, demonstrating its efficacy in recovering
023
               the ground truth preference structure. Extensive experiments across ten benchmark
024
               datasets show that GED outperforms baseline methods in model ranking, response
025            selection, and model alignment tasks. Notably, GED combines weaker evaluators
026            like Llama3-8B, Mistral-7B, and Qwen2-7B to surpass the performance of stronger
027            evaluators like Qwen2-72B, highlighting its ability to enhance evaluation reliability
028            and improve model performance.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Large Language Models (LLMs) have rapidly transformed various fields within artificial intelligence,
035   particularly natural language processing (NLP) and decision-making systems (Wu et al., 2023; Li
036   et al., 2023a). Despite the remarkable success of LLMs, the need for effective evaluation methods
037   becomes paramount (Liu et al., 2023; Desmond et al., 2024; Siska et al., 2024). Preference evaluation,
038   as one of the most important assessment methods, plays an indispensable role in evaluating and
039
      optimizing model performance (Rafailov et al., 2024; Yuan et al., 2024; Dubois et al., 2024b).
      Existing works usually leverage a powerful LLM (e.g., GPT4 (Achiam et al., 2023)) as the judge for
040
      comparing LLMs’ output pairwisely (Li et al., 2023b; Chen et al., 2023b; Wang et al., 2022).
041
042   However, while such model-based pairwise preference evaluations offer a flexible approach, they
043   can lead to contradictory evaluations in the assessment process (Naresh et al., 2024; Zhang et al.,
044   2024b). For example, an LLM might evaluate three responses and conclude that Response A is better
045
      than Response B (A ≻ B), Response B is better than Response C (B ≻ C), yet paradoxically also
      rank Response C as better than Response A (C ≻ A). These cyclic patterns introduce inconsistencies
046
      that undermine the reliability of the evaluation results. We model this conflicting preference via
047
      the preference graph. Specifically, a preference graph is constructed with each response as a node
048   and directed edges indicating pairwise preferences—an edge from node A to node B shows that the
049   evaluator preferred response A over B. The noise illustrated by cycles (A ≻ B ≻ C ≻ A) manifests as
050   loops in the preference graph. The whole process we show in the upper part of Figure 1. Ideally, a
051   preference graph should be structured as a directed acyclic graph (DAG) to maintain consistency. As
052   shown in the bottom half of Figure 1, even advanced LLMs like GPT-4-o exhibit significant noise in
053   preference evaluation, highlighting their limitations as weak evaluators (Refer to Appendix A.3 for
      further details).

078
079   To address this, we propose a novel framework, GED (Preference Graph Ensemble and Denoise),
080   to address the inconsistencies in preference graphs generated through pairwise evaluations. Our
081
      method involves two key steps: (1) ensembling multiple weak evaluators to mitigate noise introduced
      by individual evaluators and (2) applying a denoising process to the resulting preference graph.
082
      By aggregating evaluations from multiple weak evaluators, we ”average out” the noise and biases,
083
      resulting in a more robust approximation of the true preference structure. The denoising step further
084   refines this aggregated graph by removing inconsistencies, ensuring the final preference graph is
085   more reliable for downstream tasks. We provide a theoretical analysis demonstrating the soundness
086   of GED, showing that by treating each individual preference graph as a random perturbation of a
087   ground truth DAG, our ensemble and denoising framework can recover the ground truth DAG with
088   high probability.
089
      To validate the practical efficacy of GED, we conduct extensive experiments across model ranking,
090   response selection, and model alignment tasks, utilizing ten widely recognized benchmark datasets, in-
091   cluding HumanEval (Chen et al., 2021), AlpacaEval (Li et al., 2023b), MATH (Hendrycks et al., 2021),
092   GSM8k (Chen et al., 2021), GAIA (Mialon et al., 2023), LIMA (Zhou et al., 2023), Vicuna (Chiang
093   et al., 2023), Koala (Vu et al., 2023), WizardLM (Xu et al., 2023), and Self-Instruct (Wang et al.,
094   2022). In these experiments, GED consistently outperformed baseline methods. For example, in
095   the response selection task, applying GED yielded an average improvement of 4.51% compared to
096   baseline methods across multiple benchmarks. Additionally, GED demonstrated substantial gains in
097   scenarios where combining preference graphs from weaker evaluators surpassed the performance of
098
      even stronger individual evaluators. For instance, by using Llama3-8B, Mistral-7B, and Qwen2-7B
      as weaker evaluators, GED exceeded the performance of using the stronger Qwen2-72B model as an
099
      evaluator. These results highlight GED’s ability to mitigate preference noise, improve consistency,
100
      and enhance model performance across diverse evaluation settings.

      individual preference graphs into a unified structure, (2) graph denoising, which removes cycles and

112   2.1   P REFERENCE G RAPH
113
114   A preference graph is defined as a directed graph GP = (V, A, w), where V = {v1 , v2 , . . . , vn }
115   represents a set of n alternatives or candidates, A ⊆ V × V is a set of directed arcs representing
116   pairwise preferences between these alternatives, and w : A → R+ is a weight function that assigns a
117   positive real value to each arc, indicating the strength of the preference.
118   For any pair of distinct vertices u, v ∈ V , an arc (u, v) ∈ A exists if there is a preference for u over v,
119   with the weight w(u, v) reflecting the intensity of this preference. Formally, this can be represented
120   as:
121
122                                      (u, v) ∈ A         if and only if w(u, v) > 0                              (1)
123
124   The weight function w(u, v) aggregates individual preferences or scores for the pair (u, v). If multiple
125   preference sources exist, the weight can be expressed as:
126
127                                                          k
                                                             X
128                                           w(u, v) =            (si (u, v) − si (v, u))                          (2)
129                                                          i=1
130   where si (u, v) is the score or preference from the i-th source. The preference graph encapsulates the
131   aggregate preferences among all pairs of alternatives, with the weight of each arc representing the
132   cumulative preference strength derived from underlying data or models.

157   2.2   GED: P REFERENCE G RAPH E NSEMBLE AND D ENOISE
158
159   As illustrated in Figure 2, our method, GED (Preference Graph Ensemble and Denoise), begins by
160   performing graph ensemble to aggregate a set of preference graphs. It then applies graph denoising
161   to ensure acyclicity, followed by graph-to-ranking to derive the final node ranking. The detailed steps
      are as follows:


                                                                     3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      Graph ensemble. In the context of graph ensemble, given multiple weighted graphs G1 =
163   (V, A1 , w1 ), G2 = (V, A2 , w2 ), . . . , Gk = (V, Ak , wk ) that share the same set of vertices V but
164   may differ in their arc sets Ai , the goal is to combine these graphs into a single ensemble graph
165   GE = (V, AE , wE ). The ensemble graph GE is formed by first defining the arc set AE as the union
166   of the arc sets of the individual graphs and the weight function wE : AE → R+ for the ensemble
167   graph is then determined by summing the weights of the corresponding arcs in each graph.
168
169   Graph denoising. Graph denoising involves transforming the original graph G = (V, A, w) into
170   a DAG. This transformation is achieved by identifying and removing a set of arcs known as the
171   Feedback Arc Set (FAS) (Gabow, 1995), which is a set of arcs whose removal makes the graph
172   acyclic. The goal is to find a minimum FAS, denoted as R∗ (G), which is a set of arcs with the
173
      smallest total weights that needs to be removed to eliminate all cycles in G.
174   To find this minimum FAS, we can order the vertices of G in a specific sequence s = {v1 , v2 , . . . , vn }.
175   This vertex sequence induces a FAS R(s), consisting of all arcs that point against the direction of the
176   sequence, i.e., arcs vj → vi where j > i. The graph denoising problem is thus reframed as finding an
177   optimal vertex sequence s∗ that induces the minimal FAS, such that R(s∗ ) = R∗ (G). This optimal
178   sequence s∗ ensures that the total weights of arcs eliminated to achieve a DAG is minimized.
179   Finding a minimum FAS in general is known to be an NP-complete problem, whose computational
180   complexity can be exponential (Karp, 2010; Bodlaender et al., 2012). Therefore, in our experiment,
181   we apply the well-established approximation algorithm proposed in Eades et al. (1993). Details can
182   be found in Appendix L.
183
184   Graph to ranking. Given a DAG graph G = (V, A, w), our goal is to derive a ranking based on
185   the structure of G. For each vertex v ∈ V , we compute the descendant count desc(v), defined as the
186
      number of vertices that are reachable from v through directed arcs:
187                                       desc(v) = |{u ∈ V : v ⇝ u}| ,                                       (3)
188
      where v ⇝ u denotes that there is a directed path from v to u. Vertices are then ranked based on
189
      their descendant counts, with higher descendant counts indicating a higher position in the ranking.
190
      Formally, the ranking R is represented as a sequence of subsets:
191
192                                            R = {v1 , v2 , . . . , vn },                                   (4)
193   where each vi represents a set of vertices with the i-th highest descendant count. The final ranking is
194   then:
195                                              v1 ≻ v2 ≻ · · · ≻ vn .                                    (5)
196   This structured approach ensures that the ranking reflects not only the individual preferences captured
197   in the graph but also the relative strength of these preferences as represented through their descendant
198   connections.

200   2.3   A PPLICATIONS
201
202   We apply GED to three tasks: Response Selection, selecting the best response from LLM-generated
203   candidates; Model Ranking, ranking models based on task performance; and Model Alignment,
204   identifying the best instruction-response pairs for training. The steps are as follows:
205
206   Response selection. In the response selection task, a model M generates n candidate answers
207
      {ans1 , . . . , ansn } for each question q ∈ Q, with the objective of identifying the optimal answer
      ans∗q for each query. To achieve this, we employ multiple evaluators A = {a1 , . . . , ak }, who provide
208
      pairwise preferences among the candidate answers. For each question q, we construct a set of
209
      preference graphs {Ga : a ∈ A}, where each graph Ga = (Vq , Aa , wa ) encapsulates the preferences
210   of evaluator a. The vertex set Vq = {v1 , . . . , vn } corresponds to the candidate answers, while the
211   directed arcs Aa indicate the pairwise preferences among these responses. Each arc is weighted by
212   wa , reflecting the strength of the preference indicated by the evaluator. The construction of preference
213   graphs involves evaluating each pair of candidate answers ansi and ansj . Evaluators assess the
214   quality of these answers, assigning a preference that is denoted by a directed arc (vi , vj ) in Aa , with
215   a corresponding weight wa (vi , vj ) based on the strength of preference. This process is detailed in
      Appendix D. After collecting the preference graphs {Ga : a ∈ A} for a question q, we apply GED to


                                                            4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      aggregate these graphs. The aggregation begins with merging all preference graphs into a unified
217   graph Gq = (Vq , Aq , wq ), which is subsequently processed to remove cycles, resulting in a DAG.
218   From this DAG, we derive the ranking Rq = {v1 ≻ v2 ≻ . . . ≻ vn } of the candidate answers, where
219   the highest-ranked answer is selected as ans∗q . This process is repeated for each question q ∈ Q,
220   yielding a final set of selected answers ans∗ = {ans∗1 , . . . , ans∗t }. This set reflects a consensus from
221   multiple evaluators in A, ensuring the chosen answers represent the highest quality responses based
222   on rigorous preference evaluation.
223
224   Model Ranking. In model ranking task, the goal is to rank a set of models M = {M1 , . . . , Mn }
225   based on their responses to a series of questions Q = {q1 , . . . , qt }. A group of evaluators, denoted as
226   A = {a1 , a2 , . . . , ak }, assesses the model outputs by providing preferences for pairs of responses for
227   each question. For each question q ∈ Q, the evaluators generate preference graphs {Ga : a ∈ A},
228
      where each graph Ga = (Vq , Aa , wa ) encapsulates the preferences of evaluator a over the models.
      The vertex set Vq = {v1 , . . . , vn } corresponds to the models, while the directed arcs Aa indicate
229
      pairwise preferences, with weights wa : Aa → R+ reflecting the strength of these preferences. The
230
      preference graph for a given question q is constructed by evaluating each pair of models Mi and Mj ,
231   represented by nodes vi and vj . For their respective answers ansi = Mi (q) and ansj = Mj (q),
232   evaluators provide a preference indicating which answer is favored. This preference is represented by
233   a directed arc (vi , vj ) in Aa , assigned a weight wa (vi , vj ) based on preference strength. The detailed
234   procedure is outlined in Appendix D. Once the preference graphs {Ga : a ∈ A} are collected for a
235   question q, we employ GED to aggregate these graphs. The method begins by merging all preference
236   graphs into a single graph Gq = (Vq , Aq , wq ), which is then transformed into a DAG by removing
237   cycles. The final ranking Rq = {v1 ≻ v2 ≻ . . . ≻ vn } is derived from this DAG. This process
238   is repeated for each question q ∈ Q, yielding a set of rankings {Rq : q ∈ Q}. To compute the
239
      overall ranking R∗ of the models across all questions, we conduct a ranking ensemble on the set
      {Rq : q ∈ Q}, as detailed in Appendix M. This approach culminates in a final ranking that reflects
240
      the models’ performance as assessed by multiple evaluators in A.
241
242
      Model Alignment. In the model alignment task, we have multiple data pairs of the form
243
      (x, y1 ), (x, y2 ), . . . , (x, yn ) for each instruction x. The objective is to identify the best response
244   y ∗ corresponding to each instruction x. We utilize multiple evaluators A = {a1 , . . . , ak } to provide
245   pairwise preferences among the candidate responses. For each instruction x, we construct a set of
246   preference graphs {Ga : a ∈ A}, where each graph Ga = (Vx , Aa , wa ) represents the preferences
247   of evaluator a. The vertices Vx correspond to the candidate responses {y1 , . . . , yn }, and the directed
248   arcs in Aa indicate preferences, weighted by wa . After constructing the preference graphs, we apply
249   GED to aggregate them into a single graph Gx . This graph undergoes a denoising process to remove
250   cycles, allowing us to derive a ranking Rx of the responses. The highest-ranked response in Rx is
251   selected as y ∗ for that instruction x. This process is repeated for all t instructions, resulting in the
252
      final training set {(x1 , y1∗ ), . . . , (xt , yt∗ )}, which reflects a consensus across the evaluators in A.

      Experiment Setup. In this section, we evaluate the effectiveness of GED in the model ranking task
414
      within a human preference setting, using the AlpacaEval benchmark (Li et al., 2023b). We employ 30
415
      widely used models from the AlpacaEval dataset as our model set M, while the benchmark’s questions
416   form the question set Q. The rankings provided by the AlpacaEval benchmark serve as ground truth
417   for evaluating the accuracy of various ranking methods. This is justified by AlpacaEval’s strong
418   correlation with Chatbot Arena rankings, making it a reasonable proxy for human judgments (Dubois
419   et al., 2024a). We adopt Ranking Correction, measured by the Spearman rank correlation coefficient,
420   to evaluate the similarity. To generate rankings, we utilize outputs from the open-source models
421   Llama3-70B, Qwen2-72B, Mistral-8×7B, and Qwen1.5-72B as our evaluators, denoted as set A. For
422   further implementation details, see Appendix A. We investigate two variants of GED: (w/o ensemble)
423   denoises the preference graphs from different evaluators for the same question, converts each into a
424
      ranking, and then ensembles these rankings to produce the final output, while (w/o denoising) directly
      ensembles the preference graphs to obtain the final ranking without denoising.
425
426
      Main results. The results, presented in Table 2, show that GED outperforms all single-model
427
      baselines, highlighting the significant improvement in ranking accuracy achieved by leveraging
428
      preference information from multiple evaluators. Moreover, GED surpasses the (w/o ensemble)
429   variant, indicating that generating rankings through graph ensemble first prevents information loss
430   compared to converting individual graphs into rankings. When the ensemble graph is not denoised
431   (w/o denoising), residual noise can adversely affect the final ranking quality. Additionally, our
      denoising method also enhances results in single-model settings.

508   7    R ELATED W ORK
509
510   Preference evaluation of LLMs. Reference-free evaluation metrics have a long history (Louis
511   & Nenkova, 2013), which evaluates the generated text based on intrinsic properties and coherence
512   with the context. Although they achieve high accuracy on matching inner-evaluator, the achievement
513
      suffers from spurious correlations such as perplexity and length (Durmus et al., 2022). Recently,
      people have started using a strong model (e.g., GPT-4) as an evaluator to perform a zero-shot reference-
514
      free evaluation on the weak models (Shen et al., 2023; Dubois et al., 2024b; Chen et al., 2023b).
515
      However, using LLM-based preference evaluations can introduce inconsistencies in preference graphs,
516   often resulting in cyclic preferences or contradictions when comparing multiple outputs.
517
518   Weak supervision. The concept of weak-to-strong supervision originates from the need to leverage
519   noisy or partial labels in machine learning tasks, enabling the development of more robust models from
520   imperfect data (Ratner et al., 2016; Zhang et al., 2023b; 2022). In LLMs, weak-to-strong supervision
521   aids AI alignment by allowing weaker models to improve strong ones, enhancing performance without
522   extensive data and supporting scalable oversight (Zheng et al., 2024a; Guo & Yang, 2024; Tong
523   et al., 2024). Similarly, in task-oriented LLMs, weak-to-strong learning improves LLM’s ability by
524   enabling strong models to refine their data autonomously, boosting performance without extensive
525   high-quality input (Zhang et al., 2023a; Yang et al., 2024b). Through weak-to-strong supervision,
526
      LLM performance can be significantly improved by iteratively transforming low-quality labels into
      more reliable ones, leading to more effective model training and robust outputs (Zakershahrak &
527
      Ghodratnama, 2024; Lang et al., 2024).

      8    C ONCLUSION
530
531
      In this paper, we presented GED, a framework designed to address inconsistencies in pairwise prefer-
532
      ence evaluations by LLMs. By employing graph ensemble techniques and denoising, GED reduces
533
      cyclic patterns and enhances the reliability of evaluation outcomes. Our theoretical analysis shows
534   that GED can recover the ground truth DAG under reasonable conditions, improving consistency in
535   preference rankings. Extensive experiments across response selection, model ranking, and instruct
536   tuning demonstrate the efficacy of our method. GED consistently outperformed baseline methods
537   in both single-evaluator and multi-evaluator settings, particularly in scenarios where combining
538   weak evaluators led to superior results over stronger individual evaluators. Future work will explore
539   extending GED to broader evaluation frameworks and applying its principles to more complex
      decision-making tasks, including multi-agent systems and human-AI interaction.

781

784   of evaluators using the GED method. For single model evaluation, we assessed the standalone perfor-
785   mance of Llama3-8B, Mistral-7B, Qwen2-7B, and Qwen2-72B on benchmarks such as HumanEval,
786   AlpacaEval, MATH, GSM8k, and GAIA. This served two purposes: first, to establish the baseline

793
794
      Model Ranking. For the model ranking task, we selected larger models as evaluators to ensure
795

      A.3      D EFINITION OF W EAK E VALUATORS
811
812   In this work, ”weak evaluators” are defined by their tendency to produce noisy or inconsistent
813   pairwise preferences, not by their overall model capacity. Even advanced models like GPT-4-o
814   can generate preference graphs with cycles, indicating that preference inconsistency is a universal
815   challenge, regardless of model scale. Evaluators such as Llama3-8B, Mistral-7B, Qwen2-7B, and
816   even GPT-4-o are considered weak if their pairwise evaluations exhibit significant noise or conflicts.

      A.7      AGGREGATION P ROCESS IN GED ACROSS D IFFERENT TASKS
919
920   The GED implementation differs between response selection and model ranking tasks due to their
921   objectives. In response selection, GED aggregates preference graphs from multiple evaluators for
922   each question into a single DAG. From this DAG, a final ranking is derived, and the top-ranked answer
923   is selected as the output. Notably, aggregation is performed only at the per-question level, without
924   a rank aggregation across questions. In model ranking, GED involves two steps: first, aggregating
925   the evaluators’ preference graphs into a DAG for each question to rank the models, and second,
926
      employing a rank ensemble method to aggregate these per-question rankings into a final overall
      model ranking across all questions. For details on the aggregation modeling, see Section 2.3, covering
927
      Response Selection, Model Ranking, and Model Alignment.

982    D     C ONSTRUCTION OF THE P REFERENCE G RAPH
983
984    In this section, we provide a detailed explanation of the process used to construct the preference graph
985    sets for both the model ranking and response selection tasks, as outlined in Algorithms 1 and 2. These
986    algorithms form the backbone of our method, enabling the representation of pairwise preferences as
987    directed graphs, which are essential for downstream aggregation and ranking.
988    Algorithm 1 describes the construction process for generating a set of preference graphs for the model
989    ranking task. The procedure is as follows: Initialization: For each question q ∈ Q, we begin by
990    initializing a vertex set Vq , where each vertex vi corresponds to a model Mi in the set of models M .
991    We also initialize an empty set of edges Aa and a weight function wa , which will be used to track the
992    strength of the preferences between model pairs. Pairwise Comparisons: For each pair of models Mi
993    and Mj , the assigned evaluator a ∈ A assesses their responses to the given question q. If evaluator a
994    prefers Mi over Mj , a directed edge (vi → vj ) is added to the edge set Aa , and its corresponding
995    weight is incremented. Conversely, if Mj is preferred, the edge (vj → vi ) is added or its weight is
996
       updated. Graph Storage: Once all pairwise comparisons have been processed for a given evaluator,
       the resulting graph Ga = (Vq , Aa , wa ) is stored for that evaluator. This process is repeated for all
997
       evaluators in A and for all questions in Q, generating a set of preference graphs for each evaluator
998
       and question.

       This work situates itself within the broader field of denoising preference evaluations for LLMs,
1210
       addressing inconsistencies and noise in preference graphs. We acknowledge that existing literature
1211
       has explored two primary approaches to this challenge: within-model preference modeling and
1212   modular pre-processing. Below, we provide a detailed comparison of GED with representative
1213   methods from these approaches.
1214
1215
       Within-model approaches, such as robust DPO (rDPO) (Chowdhury et al., 2024) and conservative
       DPO (cDPO) (Mitchell, 2023), focus on integrating denoising mechanisms directly within the
1216
       preference modeling process. These methods incorporate regularization techniques to mitigate the
1217
       effects of noisy or adversarial preference data during model alignment. While these approaches are
1218   effective in refining the preference modeling pipeline, they are tightly coupled with specific models
1219   and tasks, limiting their versatility. In contrast, GED is a modular framework that operates as a
1220   pre-processing step, denoising preference graphs before downstream tasks, making it adaptable to a
1221   wide range of applications and models. Modular approaches like CURATRON (Naresh et al., 2024)
1222   address noise and missing comparisons in preference datasets using techniques such as low-rank
1223   matrix completion. While CURATRON effectively mitigates certain types of noise, it does not
1224   explicitly target cyclic inconsistencies (e.g., A ≻ B, B ≻ C, C ≻ A), which are a critical focus of GED.
1225   By leveraging a graph-based framework, GED detects and removes such cycles through its denoising
1226
       process, ensuring that the resulting preference graph is acyclic and thus more consistent and reliable
       for downstream use. Additionally, GED distinguishes itself by providing theoretical guarantees for
1227
       recovering the ground truth preference DAG under certain conditions. Furthermore, the ensemble
1228
       mechanism in GED demonstrates the novel insight that combining weaker evaluators can surpass the
1229   performance of a single stronger evaluator, a feature not emphasized in the aforementioned methods.

       Using multiple evaluators and aggregating their preferences can be computationally expensive, es-
       pecially when constructing dense preference graphs. This limitation becomes more pronounced in
1276
       scenarios where preferences across all pairs of evaluators are required, which scales quadratically with
1277
       the number of responses or models being compared. To address this, we clarify the computational
1278   trade-offs and propose an active learning-based approach to reduce the number of required pairwise
1279   evaluations. To further reduce the number of pairwise evaluations needed, we developed an active
1280   learning algorithm called ActiveGED. This algorithm strategically selects the most informative
1281   pairs for evaluation, effectively lowering the overall computational cost while maintaining high
1282   performance. ActiveGED combines random sampling with uncertainty-based selection to maxi-
1283   mize information gain from each additional pairwise evaluation. We evaluated ActiveGED under
1284   budget constraints of 30% and 50% of the total possible pairwise comparisons, where we set the
1285   random budget ratio as 0.5. The results, presented in Table 9, demonstrate that ActiveGED achieves
1286
       competitive performance with significantly fewer evaluations. For example, under a 50% budget,
       ActiveGED retains most of the performance benefits of full GED while cutting the number of pairwise
1287

       preference for a specific option or label (e.g., consistently favoring ”Option A” over ”Option B”). Our
1357
       framework implicitly mitigates token bias through the aforementioned position-swapping strategy,
1358
       which prevents evaluators from associating a fixed label with a specific position. By averaging the
1359   results across both orderings, any systematic preference for a particular label is effectively neutralized.

1362   L    D ENOISING OF P REFERENCE G RAPHS FOR GED
1363
1364
       In this section, we describe the denoising procedure used in the GED framework, specifically for
       transforming the aggregated preference graph into a DAG. Let G = (V,  A) be a simple connected
1365
       directed graph, where n = |V | and m = |A|, with n − 1 ≤ m ≤ n2 . A feedback arc set (FAS)
1366
       of G, denoted as R(G), is a set of arcs whose removal transforms G into a DAG. The minimum
1367
       feedback arc set R∗ (G) is the FAS of minimum cardinality, and finding R∗ (G) is the well-known
1368
       FAS problem.
1369
1370   Consider a scenario where the vertices of G are arranged sequentially along a horizontal line and
1371   labeled as v1 , v2 , . . . , vn from left to right. This arrangement is referred to as a vertex sequence and
1372
       is denoted by s = v1 v2 . . . vn . Each vertex sequence s induces a feedback arc set R(s), consisting
       of all leftward arcs vj → vi for j > i. The FAS problem can therefore be reformulated as finding
1373
       a vertex sequence s∗ such that R(s∗ ) = R∗ (G). Our proposed algorithm, GED, computes a vertex
1374
       sequence s that corresponds to a minimal feedback arc set R(s). The algorithm iteratively removes
1375   vertices (and their incident arcs) from G, focusing on sinks, sources, and vertices that maximize a
1376   specific property. For any vertex u ∈ V , let d(u) denote its degree, d+ (u) its outdegree, and d− (u)
1377   its indegree, such that d(u) = d+ (u) + d− (u). At each step, after removing sinks and sources, the
1378   algorithm selects a vertex u for which δ(u) = d+ (u) − d− (u) is maximized. If the removed vertex u
1379   is a sink, it is concatenated with a vertex sequence s2 ; otherwise, it is concatenated with s1 . Once
1380   G is reduced to an empty graph, the final vertex sequence s is obtained by concatenating s1 and s2 .
1381   The detailed steps of the algorithm are shown in Algorithm 4. In our GED framework, this denoising
1382   step is essential for ensuring that the aggregated preference graph becomes acyclic, thus enabling a
1383
       reliable ranking to be extracted. By iteratively removing vertices based on their structural properties,
       we minimize the feedback arc set and ensure that the remaining graph is a DAG, which can be directly
1384
       used to generate rankings in subsequent steps.
