                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011
               Transformer-based models have emerged as the go-to standards in Natural Lan-
012
               guage Processing (NLP), revolutionizing the landscape of NLP applications. As
013
               complex models continue to proliferate, the need for more efficient computational
014            processing becomes increasingly imperative. This has led to the rise of model
015            compression techniques, implemented to target computational inefficiencies. Ex-
016            pounding on this, we propose Pyramid-BERT (P-BERT), the integration of three
017            established model compression techniques to further reduce the computational in-
018            efficiency of the standard BERT models, and subsequently optimize BERT under
019            the hardware characteristics. Specifically, the techniques employed are pruning,
020            quantization, and knowledge distillation. The first two aforementioned correlated
021            techniques work simultaneously to remove redundant specifications while leverag-
022
               ing knowledge transfer from baseline models. These techniques enable a substan-
               tial reduction in computational cost, making P-BERT highly suitable for portable,
023
               low-power devices such as cellphones, wearable devices, and smartwatches, and
024
               thus enabling hardware-friendly processing on various computing engines. Addi-
025            tionally, we will be proposing a new metric, the inverted computational complex-
026            ity to quantify the complexity and efficacy of the model. This metric aims to more
027            accurately capture the hardware-specific performance characteristics. Our experi-
028            mental results show that P-BERT achieves a remarkable reduction of at least 60%
029            in the inverted computational complexity ratio while ensuring comparable accu-
030            racy and scores across many downstream tasks compared with the baseline BERT
031            models.

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   In recent years, transformer-based models emerged as the cornerstone of natural language processing
037   (NLP), catalyzing a paradigm shift in the field. The advent of pre-trained transformer-based models
038   sparked intense interest and research activity in both industry and academia. This led to the creation
039   of numerous transformer-based models, including GPT-4 Achiam et al. (2023), RoBERTa Liu et al.
040   (2019) and DistillBERT Sanh et al. (2019).
041
      Central to the exceptional performance of these transformer-based models are their deep forward
042   propagation in each layer. While effective, these neural networks are time and space inefficient, due
043   to overparameterization Ba & Caruana (2014); Rogers et al. (2021) and a large number of operations
044   involved. Additionally, given the rise diversity of resource-constrained devices, including mobile
045   phones and edge-devices, current solutions may not be optimal Ganesh et al. (2021). Deployment of
046   these models in such devices and majorly constrained by computation power, storage capacity and
047   energy Gou et al. (2021).
048   Consequently, there are various model compression techniques proposed to mitigate computational
049   inefficiencies of such models, including pruning, quantization, distillation, parameter sharing and
050   module replacing Qiu et al. (2020), low-rank factorization Cheng et al. (2018), matrix decomposition
051   Ganesh et al. (2021). Exploration of these techniques include investigating the effect of pruning and
052   transfer learning Gordon et al. (2020) and a novel integer-only quantization approach by Kim et al.
053   (2021). However, directly compressing these models often leads to performance loss, quantified
      primarily by accuracy loss.

054
      Hence, we propose Pyramid-BERT (P-BERT), a hardware-aware optimization of the BERT model
055   that integrates three proven model compression techniques—pruning, quantization, and knowledge
056   distillation. By combining these techniques, P-BERT addresses the computational inefficiencies in-
057   herent in BERT while minimizing performance loss. We selected BERT as the baseline model due
058   to its suitability for edge devices, aligning with our focus on hardware-aware optimization. Addi-
059   tionally, we are proposing a novel metric, the inverted computational complexity ratio, to quantify
060   the complexity and effectiveness of model compression techniques relative to a baseline model.
061   Our experimental results demonstrate that P-BERT achieves at least a 60% reduction in the inverted
062   computational complexity ratio, while minimising performance loss on downstream tasks such that
063   the scoring metric remains comparable with the baseline BERT model. This would mean a fall in at
064   most 5-6% accuracy or other similar metric the large majority of the time.
065
      In summary, our contributions are highlighted as follows:
066
067
             • Proposed a variant of the original BERT model, Pyramid-BERT, an integration of three
068            established model compression techniques - pruning, quantization and knowledge distilla-
069            tion, that optimizes performance for hardware efficiency.
070
071
             • P-BERT achieves at least a 60% reduction in inverted computational complexity ratio, well
               ensuring comparable accuracy and scores with the baseline model.
072
073          • A metric that quantifies the complexity and efficacy of a model — inverted computational
074            complexity ratio.

      2   R ELATED W ORKS
078
079   Many works that have delved into individual model compression techniques, such as pruning, quan-
080   tization and distillation, each offering unique insights and advancements.
081   For instance, MiniLM proposed a deep self-attention distillation for task-agnostic Transformer based
082   LM distillation, involving distilling the self-attention module of the last Transformer layer of the
083   teacher model Wang et al. (2020). Wang et al. conducted further research with MiniLMv2, introduc-
084   ing multihead self-attention relation distillation, which allows fine-grained self-attention knowledge
085   and thus flexibility in the number of student’s attention heads Wang et al. (2021a). Patient Knowl-
086   edge Distillation proposed an approach that capitalizes on patient learning from multiple intermedi-
087
      ate layers of the teacher model Sun et al. (2019). The approach employs two unique strategies —
      learning from the last k layers and learning from every k layer. Their approach improved results on
088
      multiple NLP tasks, with a significant reduction in the total parameters and inference times. How-
089
      ever, using the following metrics as a measure of computational efficiency has its limitations that
090   have been discussed widely in literature reviews Cheng et al. (2018).
091
092   Quantized Neural Networks trains networks with low precision weights (1-bit) and activations
093
      Hubara et al. (2018). They reduced memory size, and accesses and replaced most arithmetic op-
      erations with bit-wise operations, while achieving comparable accuracy to the baseline model. Re-
094
      garding quantization, Shen et al. proposed a method for quantizing BERT models to ultra-low pre-
095
      cision through a new group-wise quantization scheme and a Hessian-based mix-precision method
096   Shen et al. (2020). Quantized 8Bit BERT (Q8BERT) utilizes quantization-aware training during
097   BERT’s fine-tuning phase to compress the model by a factor of 4 with negligible accuracy degrada-
098   tion, while also enhancing inference speed on hardware that supports 8-bit integer operations Zafrir
099   et al. (2019). Other state-of-the-art quantization compression methods include GPTQ Frantar et al.
100   (2022), AWQ Lin et al. (2024), AQLM Egiazarian et al. (2024), and QuIP# Tseng et al. (2024)
101   leveraged advanced techniques to achieve significant reductions in model size while maintaining
102   performance across various natural language processing tasks.
103   Similarly, some works integrated two model compression techniques. Polino et al. proposed two
104   new methods: quantized distillation and differentiable quantization, both jointly leverage weight
105   quantization and knowledge distillation Polino et al. (2018). The first incorporates distillation loss
106   into quantized weights, while the second optimizes the location of quantization points using stochas-
107   tic gradient descent. Evolutionary Multi-Objective Model Compression proposed combining prun-
      ing and quantization to optimize energy efficiency and accuracy simultaneously Wang et al. (2021b).

115   3.1     P RUNING
116
      Deep neural networks are recognized to be overparameterized Ba & Caruana (2014); Rogers et al.
117
      (2021), leading to the high computational and memory cost. Pruning involves the intentional reduc-
118
      tion of redundant parameters Cheng et al. (2018); Ganesh et al. (2021). Pruning is often explored
119   in relation to the lottery ticket hypothesis in neural networks Frankle & Carbin (2018) and has been
120   discussed in the context of BERT Chen et al. (2020).
121
122
      3.2     Q UANTIZATION
123
124   The high computational inefficiency of deep neural networks is due to the large amount of multiply-
125   accumulate operations involved in the computation. Quantization directly targets this by reducing
126   the bit-size of parameter values, which leads to requiring less computational power, memory storage,
127   and inference time Kim et al. (2021).
128
129   3.3     K NOWLEDGE D ISTILLATION
130
131   Knowledge Distillation, specifically Task-Specific Distillation, is the third pillar in our proposed
132   model. The method of knowledge distillation distills knowledge from the larger teacher model into
133
      the smaller student model Hinton et al. (2015). Knowledge distillation varies across knowledge cat-
      egories, training schemes, teacher-student architecture, and distillation algorithms Gou et al. (2021).
134
      Specific to P-BERT, we adopted a layer-wise knowledge distillation technique by Neo et al. Ming
135
      et al. (2023), to have the student model replicate the teacher model’s behavior. The details of this
136   approach are elaborated below.

      4.3.2         D ISCUSSION OF I NVERTED C OMPUTATIONAL C OMPLEXITY R ATIO
271
272   While the FLOPs metric is a widely used indicator of computational complexity, it does not provide
273   a complete picture of energy efficiency or consumption, particularly in the context of quantization.
274   FLOPs measure the number of floating-point operations required, but they fail to account for the
275   impact of model optimizations, such as quantization, that use lower precision arithmetic like 8-
276   bit integers. Quantization can significantly reduce both memory usage and energy consumption,
277   but these benefits are not reflected in the FLOPs measurement. Consequently, while our proposed
278   Inverted Computational Complexity Ratio offers valuable insight into model compression, it also
279
      highlights the limitations of FLOPs as an indicator of real-world efficiency, particularly in hardware-
      accelerated environments where lower precision operations dominate.

336
337   We evaluated our approach on Hugging Face’s text classification datasets, specifically the GLUE
338   benchmarks tasks RTE, MRPC, STSB, and CoLA. The evaluation metrics we have chosen are ac-
339   curacy for RTE and MRPC, Pearson Coefficient for STSB, and Matthews Correlation Coefficient
340   for CoLA. Our experiments are conducted on AMD EPYC Millan 7713 for CPU and NVIDIA
341
      A100-40G SXM for GPU.
342   For our genetic algorithm, we set the population size = 40 and the number of generations = 5. For
343   our GLUE tasks, the input sequence length = 128 tokens, number of epochs = 4, training batch size
344   = 32, and a learning rate of 5e-5.

415   Our knowledge distillation results are performed on the final population of each GLUE task. Each
416   member of the final optimal population has a different underlying architecture with different hidden
417   states pruned and bits quantized. Hence, each model in the optimal final population underwent
418   hyperparameter tuning with knowledge distillation, leading to different hyperparameters. Details on
419   which hyperparameter was selected for each model can be found in A.1.
420   Tables 1, 2, 3 and 4 largely depict an increase in their evaluation metric following knowledge distil-
421   lation with tuned hyperparameters, which is the 5th column. Across all tables, models with higher
422   inverted computational complexity ratios had lower scores on their respective evaluation metrics.
423   However, after knowledge distillation and hyperparameter tuning, these values all rose, with the
424   highest η of each task increasing the most. This is most significant in table 4 with a 13.6 increase.
425
      However, tables 1 and 2 where the top 2 and 3 accuracies in each task respectively show a fall
426   or stagnating accuracy instead. These models have the lowest inverted computational complexity
427   ratios in their task, indicating a more complex model. We believe that this is due to the limited
428   room for improvement in the score metric that knowledge distillation can bring about. It is possible
429   that this is the result of an insufficient number of hyperparameters, or the lack of precision in the
430   hyperparameters (i.e., the number of decimal places). Additionally, the accuracy we have obtained
431   after knowledge distillation for RTE remains within 5.5% of the original baseline model, even while
      using a significantly less complex model.

      5.5   L IMITATIONS
514
      One limitation our model faces is its sensitivity to initialization. Since the seeding of the genetic
515
      algorithm is randomized, it can result in variations in performance across different runs.
516
517   Another limitation is that our model is optimized specifically for lightweight, low-power devices. As
518   a result, the performance gains and efficiencies we demonstrate may not scale effectively to larger,
519
      more powerful hardware platforms, limiting its broader applicability to high-end systems, such as
      GPT.

522   6     C ONCLUSION
523
524
      In this paper, we introduced P-BERT, a hardware-aware and computationally efficient version of
525   BERT that integrates pruning and quantization to reduce computational complexity and knowledge
526   distillation to maintain comparable performance to baseline BERT. We introduced a new metric —
527   inverted computational complexity ratio, that compares the computational complexity of a model to
528   a baseline. Our experimental results show that our approach can reduce between 60% to 88% in the
529   inverted computational complexity ratio.
530
      For future work, it would be valuable to explore the applicability of P-BERT on different hardware
531   architectures and across a wider variety of datasets and benchmarks to further validate its hardware
532   efficiency and generalizability.
