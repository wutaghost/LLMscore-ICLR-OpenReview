011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Radiation parameterization schemes are crucial components of weather and cli-
014            mate models, however, they are known to be computationally intensive. Alter-
015            natively, they can be emulated with machine learning (ML) regression models.
016
               Mainly vertical energy propagation motivates the usage of ML models featuring
               sequential data processing. We investigate these and related models for radiation
017
               parameterization using atmospheric data modeled within an Arctic region. We ob-
018
               serve that Neural ODE performs best in predicting both the long- and short-wave
019            heating rates. Furthermore, we substitute the architecture with its discrete form
020            to boost its efficiency while preserving competitive performance. The practical
021            applicability of the models is studied for different model sizes. Finally, we link
022            the trained neural network to the operational weather forecast model and assessed
023            its performance versus the conventional radiation parameterization. We receive a
024            speedup of 26.5 times of the radiation steps without significant loss of accuracy.
025            The proposed parameterization emulator dramatically reduces the computational
026            burden and the carbon footprint of weather forecasting.

050   1   I NTRODUCTION
051
052   The role of numerical weather prediction (NWP) cannot be overestimated. It is essential for numer-
053   ous aspects of our daily life and critical systems: public safety, agriculture, aviation and transporta-
      tion, energy, climate change, making it a cornerstone of our modern society.


                                                         1
054
      Incorporating atmospheric radiative transfer into NWP models via a radiation parameterization is
055   critical for simulating atmospheric dynamics. However, from a computational perspective, radia-
056   tion calculations present unique challenges due to their significantly higher computational cost in
057   comparison with other parameterizations.
058
      At the core of these calculations are radiative transfer models, which require solving complex lin-
059
      ear transport integro-differential equations (Liou, 2002). These solutions must be integrated over
060
      multiple dimensions—height, angles, and frequencies—further increasing the computational load.
061   A major bottleneck stems from the highly frequency-dependent nature of radiation absorption by
062   gases, which makes the modeling process inherently complex. Additionally, scattering and absorp-
063   tion by aerosols introduce further layers of difficulty.
064
      The traditional approach to simulating electromagnetic propagation through the atmosphere is the
065
      line-by-line radiative transfer model (LBLRTM) (Clough et al., 1992), which meticulously tracks
066
      thousands of individual absorption lines. While highly accurate, LBLRTM’s computational de-
067   mands make it infeasible for large-scale, real-time applications.
068
069   To address this, the rapid radiative transfer model for global climate models (RRTMG) (Iacono et al.,
070
      2008) applies algorithmic optimizations that dramatically reduce computation time without sacrific-
      ing accuracy. Yet, despite these advances, radiative transfer remains one of the most computationally
071
      expensive components in weather modeling.
072
073   Given some data is available, we can emulate a radiative transfer with a regression problem: predict
074   heating rates and radiation fluxes based on the Sun’s position, surface, and atmosphere conditions
075   (temperature, pressure, humidity, and others). A variety of neural network (NN) architectures were
076
      successfully applied to this regression problem (Chevallier et al., 1998; Krasnopolsky et al., 2010).
      Recent works employed feedforward, convolutional networks, and transformers (Pal et al., 2019;
077
      Liu et al., 2020; Song & Roh, 2021; Lagerquist et al., 2021; Yao et al., 2023) and recurrent networks
078
      (Ukkonen, 2022; Yavich et al., 2024).
079
080   Within the atmosphere, electromagnetic radiation mainly travels in the vertical direction. Multi-
081   ple reflections can be considered as some special dynamics of establishing final heating profile.
082
      This motivates to approximate the transfer with an ordinary differential equation (ODE) with an
      unknown right-hand side. After the right-hand side is estimated, the ODE can be solved to predict
083
      heating rates. This motivates to apply neural ordinary differential equations (Neural ODEs, NODE)
084
      to this problem. Neural ODEs offer a powerful framework for modeling dynamic systems by means
085   of machine learning (Chen et al., 2018). It models the data transformation through a continuous
086   dynamic equation instead of discrete layers as in traditional neural networks.
087
088
      In this paper, we explore the possibilities of Neural ODEs for atmospheric radiative transfer. Our
      experiments involve atmospheric data modeled with WRF (Skamarock et al., 2019) within an Arctic
089
      region. We focused our work on the Arctic region because of its high economic potential, see e.g.
090
      Li et al. (2023), and complex climate.
091
092   Atmospheric radiative heating is considered as an adiabatic process in weather forecast and climate
093   modelling. Radiation transfer itself is described at the quantum level and involves numerous trans-
094
      fers, scatterings and reflections. We found that an iterative profile-wise architecture for a Neural
      ODE is necessary to emulate radiative flux propagation and the underlying physics. On the other
095
      hand, sequential and shallow networks architectures emulate radiation transfer in a single direction
096
      only, therefore are inferior, which is supported by our experiments.
097
098   To sum up, our main contributions are the following:
099
100        1. We investigated Neural ODE and related architectures for application in atmospheric radia-
101           tive transfer;
102
103        2. We find that the optimal architecture is the profile-wise RNN, the discrete substitution of
104           the Neural ODE;
105
106        3. We linked the trained neural network to WRF operational weather forecast model and as-
107           sessed its performance versus the conventional radiation parameterization. We received a
              speedup of 26.5 times of the radiation steps.

      2.1   R EGRESSION P ROBLEM
112
      The search for an optimal approach that would balance both accuracy and efficiency in calculation
113
      of the radiative heating rate is still an open question. If a modeling domain and vertical resolution
114
      are fixed, we can think of these calculations as a regression problem, reusing earlier data. The input
115   data would be physical fields that describe particular atmospheric and earth surface conditions as
116   well as the Sun’s position. The output data would be the heating rate. Such an approach would work
117   with broadband variables directly and thus avoid multiple tedious numerical integrations and dif-
118   ferentiations. Alternatively, upward and downward fluxes can be predicted with regression models
119   either within the whole vertical profile or at the Earth’s surface and top of the atmosphere only.
120
      Notice the heating rates due to SW and LW radiation can be predicted simultaneously with a multi-
121   output regression model or separately with a set of single-output regression models.
122
123
124   2.2   BASIC A RCHITECTURES
125
126   All architectures used in our research can be briefly classified into three types based on their ap-
127
      proach to treating spatial information: point-wise, profile-wise, and sequential, Figure 1.
128   Point-wise type uses only local information, the features evaluated at the given height and location
129   to predict the target at the same point. In such an approach the problem is treated as a canonical
130   tabular regression, and gradient boosting like CatBoost (Dorogush et al., 2018) can be used as a
131   simple baseline.
132   Profile-wise architectures predict the whole target profile at the given location at once and neces-
133   sitate the whole profile of input features. Commonly, the profile is discretized by the height and
134   convolutional neural networks (CNN) are utilized to effectively treat the spatial dependencies.
135
      The sequential type is the most relevant in terms of physicality as the implicit dynamics describe the
136
      vertical transfer process. These architectures parameterize the transition model along the height or
137   pressure gradient, and input is assimilated at each layer sequentially. As a baseline of this approach,
138   we used the gated recurrent unit (GRU) model (Chung et al., 2014).
139
140
141   2.3   N EURAL ODE
142
143   The basis of Neural ODEs, following (Chen et al., 2018), is represented by the differential equation:
144
145
                                             dx(t)
                                                   = f (t, x(t), θ),                                     (1)
146                                           dt
147
148   where x(t) represents the state of the system at time t, f is a neural network with parameters θ
149   that defines the system’s dynamics. Usually, the MLP or CNN architectures are utilized as the
150   parameterization of f . The latent state x(t) has constant dimension, so the models input is projected
151   linearly into the chosen space to form an initial state x(t0 ), and the output is a projected linearly
152
      from final state x(t1 ), which is computed through integration.
153   Numerical methods for integrating differential equations, such as Euler methods, Runge-Kutta meth-
154   ods, and adaptive methods that dynamically choose the step size to control the solution convergence,
155   are used to solve Neural ODEs.
156   Neural ODEs are trained through backpropagation, the gradients can be computed either directly
157   through the dynamic equation using automatic differentiation or more memory-efficiently through
158   the adjoint method. The adjoint method treats gradients as solutions of a reverse-time differential
159   equation, integrating it backward in time. However, naive implementations of the adjoint method
160   suffer from inaccuracy in reverse-time trajectory (Zhuang et al., 2021). In our work, we have used
161   a specific implementation called MALI (Zhuang et al., 2021) that increases accuracy in gradient
      estimation.

162
      2.4   S UBSTITUTING N EURAL ODE
163
164   While the adjoint method makes possible the training of Neural ODE with an arbitrarily accurate
165   black-box solver, the inference time is still inferior to that of feed-forward neural networks. More-
166   over, the inference time can vary from sample to sample in the case of adaptive solvers like MALI.
167   While such behavior is crucial for accurate integration, it was shown recently that the neural net-
168   works can balance the impurities of simpler solvers to the level when several steps of the Euler
169   schema are enough (Ivanov & Ailuro, 2024). To be specific,
170
                                      x(t + ∆t) ≈ x(t) + f (t, x(t), θ)∆t,                                 (2)
171
172   where ∆t = p1 and p is number of integration steps. When ∆t approaches zero, the Neural ODE dy-
173   namics 1 is reproduced. When p is considerable small (p ≈ 10), the Neural ODE model effectively
174   turns into a recurrent neural network, where the single layer is reapplied several times to the hidden
175   state without assimilation of new inputs, or into a residual network with weights shared among all
176   layers:
177                                       1
                             xt+1 = xt + f (t, xt , θ) = g(t, xt , θ); xp = g p ◦ x0                      (3)
178                                       p
179
180
      The number of ’integration’ steps p should be fixed and can be treated as a hyperparameter. Also
181
      automatic gradients for backpropagation is sufficient, therefore the model training is more stable.
182
      Notably, in the case of the profile-wise approach with convolutions in the approximation of the
183   right-hand side f , the number of ’integration’ steps defines also the receptive field of the model.
184
185   To the aid of simplification, we will refer to this architecture as a profile-wise convolutional RNN or
186
      just RNN.
187
188   2.5   L IQUID N EURAL N ETWORK
189
190   Liquid time-constant networks control the time-dependency to emulate biological neurons. They
191
      treat the system as a specific form of Neural ODE:
192                     dx(t)
193                           = − [wτ + f (x(t), I(t), θ)] · x(t) + A · f (x(t), I(t), θ),                 (4)
                         dt
194
195   where x(t) is latent state, I(t) is an input, wτ is a time-constant parameter vector, and A is a bias
196   vector. For such formulation, the approximation of a solution was introduced, (Hasani et al., 2022):
197
198
                                 x(t) = B · e−[wτ +f (x,I,θ)]t · f (−x, −I, θ) + A                         (5)
199   Authors suggested a modification named a closed-form continuous-time neural network model
200   (CfC):
201
                x(t) = σ(−f (x, I, θf )t) · g(x, I, θg ) + [1 − σ(−f (x, I, θf )t)] · h(x, I, θh ) (6)
202
203   where g and h are feed-forward neural network. In the used setting f , g and h are two-layer per-
204   ceptrons with the shared first layer, and the variable t is the height over the surface rather than time.
205   The CfC method achieves computational efficacy while still offering a sequential dynamic approach
206
      with physical origin.
207
208   2.6   OTHER A RCHITECTURES
209
210   Inspired by the perturbation theory, commonly utilized in physics, we explored polynomial-based
211   kernels for neural networks instead of perception-like kernels with rectified linear units. By the re-
212
      placement of the activations to polynomials we have turned CNN into a Polynomial network (PNN),
      Neural ODE into Polynomial ODE (PODE), and RNN into Taylor Mapped Polynomial Neural Net-
213
      works (TMPNN) (Ivanov & Ailuro, 2024).
214
215   In the process of architecture search, we also experimented with ODE-base modification of gated
      recurrent unit (ODEGRU) (Zhao et al., 2023). The model was used in the sequential regime.

      As a dataset, we used a part (2015–2016 years) of the long-term atmospheric hindcast of the Pechora
      Sea region, Fig 2, kindly conveyed to us by the authors of Yavich et al. (2024). Such a region was
250
      used because of the presence of various weather and surface conditions: polar days and nights, a
251
      large number of cloudy days, sea and land surface often covered by snow and ice in winter. The
252   horizontal resolution was 3 km, while the temporal resolution was 10 s. The spatial grid dimensions
253   were Nx = 422, Ny = 562, and Nz = 49.
254
255
      Model prognostic, diagnostic, and radiation variables were saved every hour. Specifically, the fol-
      lowing surface variables were saved: cosine of the solar zenith angle, COSZEN, solar constant,
256
      SOLCON, surface albedo, ALBEDO, longitude, as well as the following spatial variables: specific
257
      humidity, SH, cloud fraction, CF, cloud and ice mixing ratios, QCLOUD and QICE. Further total
258   pressure (P TOT) and absolute temperature (T KEL), were calculated and saved. Ozone temporal
259   and spatial mixing ratio distribution OZONE was prepared. The RRTMG radiation parameterization
260   was used to calculate uncoupled potential temperature tendencies due to shortwave (SW) and long-
261   wave radiations (LW), RTHRATSW and RTHRATLW, respectively. Both potential temperature and
262   potential temperature tendency were transformed to absolute temperature and absolute temperature
263   tendency, respectively. All the predictors and targets are listed in Table 1. We thus had six scalar
264   and six sequential input variables and two target sequential variables.
265   Training and testing data involved uniformly distributed data within a year: 350 days for testing
266   (essentially all the days of 2015) and 91 days for training (every fourth day of 2016). Every chosen
267   day included data within 24 hours (sampled every hour) and at 100 random surface locations, Fig. 2.
268   This type of data filtration made the dataset computationally feasible and removed redundant infor-
269   mation, while keeping sufficient representation of day/night and seasonal changes. We kept 2015
      data (840’000 profiles overall) for learning and 2016 data (218’000 profiles overall) for testing.

285   4.1   I MPLEMENTATION

287
      Almost all models are implemented in PyTorch and trained from scratch with skorch (Tietz et al.,
      2017). The initial learning rate was set to 10−2 , while other parameters were default. Each model is
288
      trained for 100 epochs. For training CatBoost and TMPNN we used their standard implementations.
289
      To stabilize training, all inputs are normalized to have the minimum value of zero and maximum
290   value of one, while the target variables are scaled to zero mean and unit variation.
291
292   Several models were investigated with different sizes, therefore we mention them with the number
293
      of dimensions in the hidden space, for example, CNN 128 stands for the convolutional network
      with 128 channels. For substituted neural odes we also tried several values of the number of inte-
294
      gration steps, e.g. RNN 128,7 stands for Neural ODE with seven steps in the Euler scheme and a
295
      convolutional layer with 128 channels.
296
297   All convolutional networks are parameterized by two-layer kernel with the use of rectified linear
298   unit activation (ReLU), while all polynomial models consist of one convolutional layer and one
299
      polynomial-generation layer.

468   We found that an iterative profile-wise architecture for a Neural ODE is necessary to emulate ra-
469   diative flux propagation and the underlying physics. On the other hand, sequential and shallow
470
      networks architectures emulate radiation transfer in a single direction only, therefore are inferior,
      which is supported by our experiments.
471
472   Overall, 25 models of 11 architectures and three types were tested with this dataset. The superior
473   results are achieved by profile-wise models, especially by Neural ODE and its discrete substitution,
474   which we have chosen as an optimal model in terms of fidelity and operational speed. We suppose
475   that the iterative nature of the proposed parameterization mimics the adiabatic process of numerous
476
      radiance reflections at each level of the atmosphere.
477   We also received preliminary results on the use of the designed and trained neural ODE substitu-
478   tion within operational weather forecast. We verified heating rates and temperatures and observed
479   that they match fairly well. The parameterization module was accelerated in 26.5 times, without
480   significant decrease of accuracy. We plan to conduct more extensive analyses on the neural parame-
481   terization performance within the WRF in our future work, e.g. performance during severe weather
482
      events and generalization over other regions.
483   WRF is a regional atmospheric model thus requiring reconfiguration for every new region. Sim-
484   ilarity, our parameterization emulator can be successfully trained for a particular region and used
485   to speedup the radiation module within operational weather forecast (where speed is critical) or
      hindcast modelling (to save compositional resources).
