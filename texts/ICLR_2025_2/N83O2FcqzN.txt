011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Seeking high-quality representations with latent variable models (LVMs) to reveal
014            the intrinsic correlation between neural activity and behavior or sensory stimuli
015
               has attracted much interest. Most work has focused on analyzing motor neural
               activity that controls clear behavioral traces and has modeled neural temporal re-
016
               lationships in a way that does not conform to natural reality. For studies of visual
017
               brain regions, naturalistic visual stimuli are high-dimensional and time-dependent,
018            making neural activity exhibit intricate dynamics. To cope with such conditions,
019            we propose Time-Dependent Split VAE (TiDeSPL-VAE), a sequential LVM that
020            decomposes visual neural activity into two latent representations while consider-
021            ing time dependence. We specify content latent representations corresponding to
022            the component of neural activity driven by the current visual stimulus, and style
023            latent representations corresponding to the neural dynamics influenced by the or-
024            ganism’s internal state. To progressively generate the two latent representations
025            over time, we introduce state factors to construct conditional distributions with
026
               time dependence and apply self-supervised contrastive learning to shape them. By
               this means, TiDeSPL-VAE can effectively analyze complex visual neural activity
027
               and model temporal relationships in a natural way. We compare our model with
028
               alternative approaches on synthetic data and neural data from the mouse visual
029            cortex. The results show that our model not only yields the best decoding per-
030            formance on naturalistic scenes/movies but also extracts explicit neural dynamics,
031            demonstrating that it builds latent representations more relevant to visual stimuli.

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   With the rapid development of neural recording technologies, researchers are now able to simul-
037   taneously record the spiking activity of large populations of neurons, providing new avenues for
038   exploring the brain (Urai et al., 2022). For analyzing these high-dimensional data, an important sci-
039
      entific problem is how to account for the intrinsic correlation between neural activity and behavioral
      patterns or sensory stimuli. As an influential approach, latent variable models (LVMs) construct
040
      low-dimensional latent representations bridging to behavior or stimuli and explain neural activity
041
      well (Saxena & Cunningham, 2019; Bahg et al., 2020; Vyas et al., 2020; Jazayeri & Ostojic, 2021;
042   Langdon et al., 2023). Recently, advanced deep learning algorithms allowed LVMs to extract high-
043   quality representations from neural activity without knowledge of experimental labels (Wu et al.,
044   2017; Pandarinath et al., 2018; Glaser et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2021), or to incorporate behavioral
045   information into models to constrain the shaping of latent variables (Mante et al., 2013; Hurwitz
046   et al., 2021; Sani et al., 2021; Singh Alvarado et al., 2021; Ahmadipour et al., 2024). These ap-
047   proaches have made various contributions to the analysis of neural activity, such as predicting held
048   neural responses (Gao et al., 2016; Pandarinath et al., 2018; Kapoor et al., 2024), decoding related
049   motion patterns or simple visual scenes (Liu et al., 2021; Schneider et al., 2023), and constructing
050
      interpretable latent structures (Zhou & Wei, 2020; Aoi et al., 2020).
051   However, most studies have dealt with neural data recorded from motor brain areas under specific
052   controlled behavioral settings (Churchland et al., 2012; Pandarinath et al., 2018; Zhou & Wei, 2020;
053   Liu et al., 2021; Pei et al., 2021), such as pre-planned reaching movements (Dyer et al., 2017). There
      is little work using LVMs to analyze neural data from visual brain regions (Gao et al., 2016; Zhao


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      & Park, 2017; Schneider et al., 2023), even though how the visual system encodes input to recog-
055   nize objects is a primary topic (DiCarlo et al., 2012), and decoding visual neural activity to visual
056   stimuli is a challenging research highlight in the neuroscience community (Kay et al., 2008; Wen
057   et al., 2018; Du et al., 2023). Furthermore, existing LVMs treat temporal relationships unnaturally
058   (Pandarinath et al., 2018; Schneider et al., 2023) or even don’t consider time dependence (Zhou &
059   Wei, 2020; Palmerston & Chan, 2021). Given that visual neural activity has strict antecedent time
060   dependence, these models may struggle to build high-quality latent representations.
061   In this work, we propose Time-Dependent Split VAE (TiDeSPL-VAE), a sequential LVM that builds
062   two split latent representations with time dependence to better analyze visual neural activity. We
063   adopt the practice of splitting latent variables into content and style representations (Liu et al., 2021).
064   Content latent representations correspond to the component of neural activity driven by the current
065   visual stimulus, while style latent representations correspond to the neural dynamics influenced by
066   the organism’s internal state (pupil position, signals relayed from other brain regions, the neurons’
067   underlying currents, etc.). These latent variables are optimized by self-supervised contrastive learn-
068   ing. For comparison with outstanding alternatives, we evaluate our model on synthetic and mouse
069
      visual datasets. The results show that our model builds meaningful latent representations that are
      highly correlated with complex visual stimuli, providing new insights into the intrinsic relationship
070
      between neural activity and visual stimulation. Specifically, our main contributions are as follows.
071
072          • To construct highly time-dependent latent representations, we introduce state factors to
073            accumulate and filter temporal information, allowing TiDeSPL-VAE to progressively com-
074            press neural activity along a chronological order in a natural way. Besides, we apply self-
075            supervised contrastive learning to shape content latent variables.
076          • Through evaluation on synthetic datasets, we show that our model better recovers latent
077            structure and is good at handling time-sequential data.
078          • Through evaluation on mouse visual datasets, we demonstrate that our model decodes neu-
079            ral activity to related natural scenes or natural movies well, showing the highest perfor-
080            mance compared to alternative models. Furthermore, visualization of latent representa-
081            tions presents that our model captures explicit temporal structures of neural dynamics for
082            different time scales.

084   2    R ELATED W ORK
085
086   With the advancement of deep learning, the application of cutting-edge learning algorithms and the
087   innovative design of model structures have greatly promoted the development of LVMs in neuro-
088   science. Some prominent works are summarized below.
089   VAE-based LVMs for neural activity analysis Recently, VAE-based approaches have become
090   a major avenue to discover latent variables underlying population neural activity, which better elu-
091   cidates the mechanisms of neural representations. As a well-known model, latent factor analysis
092   via dynamical systems (LFADS) used RNNs in a sequential VAE framework, extracting precise fir-
093   ing rate estimates and predicting observed behavior for single-trial data on motor cortical datasets
094   (Pandarinath et al., 2018; Keshtkaran & Pandarinath, 2019; Keshtkaran et al., 2022). Through spe-
095   cific latent variable design, pi-VAE (Zhou & Wei, 2020) and Swap-VAE (Liu et al., 2021) built
096   interpretable latent structures linked to motor behavioral patterns.
097   LVMs for visual neural activity analysis Several studies have made an effort to extract latent
098   manifolds from visual neural activity using LVMs. Although these studies cover various types of
099   models, such as the Gaussian process model (Ecker et al., 2014; Gondur et al., 2024), linear dynam-
100   ical system (Gao et al., 2016), autoencoder (Palmerston & Chan, 2021), and flow-based generative
101   models (Bashiri et al., 2021), they are limited to simple visual stimuli and are used for the task
102
      of reconstructing neural responses. Recently, CEBRA, a self-supervised learning model, obtained
      consistent latent representations and made progress in decoding movies (Schneider et al., 2023).

      3.1    M ODEL A RCHITECTURE
138
139   Our goal is to compress neural activity into high-quality latent representations while modeling the
140   temporal relationship. Therefore, we explicitly model the time dependence between latent variables
141   across time steps based on a sequential VAE. In practice, we split latent variables into content and
                                           (c) (s)
142   style latent representations (zt = [zt , zt ]), corresponding to stimulus-driven and neural dynami-

      3.2   M ODEL L EARNING
188
189   As content latent variables correspond to the component of neural activity driven by visual stimuli,
190   we use self-supervised contrastive learning to make them more relevant to visual stimuli. For a
191   given sample x = (x1 , . . . , xT ), we randomly select another sequence offset by several time steps
192   as a positive sample, denoted xpos = (x1+∆ , . . . , xT +∆ ), where ∆ can be positive or negative. In
193   this work, the offset is always less than the length of the sequence to ensure that the positive sample
194   pairs overlap and to enhance the time constraint. Then, a mini-batch of negative samples is randomly
195   selected from the entire training set. The model is encouraged to bring the content latent variables
196   of the positive pairs closer together and to push those of negative samples away.
197   As we introduce time dependence into our model and apply a time-dependent prior distribution to
198   guide the parameterized distribution of style latent variables, we extend the evidence lower bound of
199   VAE to a time-wise version and use the objective function for contrastive learning, which together
200   form the loss function of TiDeSPL-VAE:

233   4.1    E VALUATION AND A LTERNATIVE M ODELS
234
235
      In this work, we intend to construct latent representations that exhibit strong relevance to visual
      stimulation. According to this expectation, we evaluate our model from two aspects, similar to
236
      studies oriented to motor brain regions (Liu et al., 2021; Schneider et al., 2023). First, we quantify
237
      the performance in decoding visual stimuli using latent representations, which has long served as a
238   research hotspot for unraveling the mechanisms of visual processing (Kay et al., 2008; Wen et al.,
239   2018). Second, we assess the clarity of latent temporal structures extracted from neural dynamics.
240
241
      For a comprehensive analysis, we compare TiDeSPL-VAE with several outstanding LVMs, includ-
      ing four generative models (an unsupervised: β-VAE (Higgins et al., 2017), a sequential: LFADS
242
      (Pandarinath et al., 2018), a supervised: pi-VAE (Zhou & Wei, 2020), and a self-supervised: Swap-
243
      VAE (Liu et al., 2021)) and a nonlinear encoding method with contrastive learning (CEBRA)
244   (Schneider et al., 2023). Specifically, β-VAE, pi-VAE and Swap-VAE compress neural activity
245   independently for each time point. LFADS processes sequential neural activity with bidirectional
246   RNNs. CEBRA encodes temporal features of sequence data with fixed convolutional kernels. None
247   of them build latent representations progressively along the chronological order.
248
      Furthermore, considering that our model has more parameters after incorporating the recurrent mod-
249
      ule, we build a small version of our model (TiDeSPL-VAE-small) with fewer trainable parameters
250   than Swap-VAE (see Appendix E) for fair comparisons.

253
254   We first validate TiDeSPL-VAE on the task of reconstructing synthetic latent variables. We generate
255   two synthetic datasets for considering different properties of visual neural activity. One is a non-
256   temporal dataset generated from several sets of labels, resembling categories of visual stimuli. The
257   other is a temporal dataset constructed by the Lorenz system to test for time dependence. The
258   generating procedure for both synthetic datasets follows some previous work (Zhou & Wei, 2020;
259   Liu et al., 2021; Gao et al., 2016; Sussillo et al., 2016). A detailed description of the datasets
260   and the model training implementation is presented in Appendix C. After training, we apply linear
261   regression to map latent variables of models to the ground truth on the test set and report R2 of the
262
      linear regression as the reconstruction score.

287   4.3     E XPERIMENTS ON M OUSE V ISUAL C ORTEX DATA
288
289   We utilize a subset of the Allen Brain Observatory Visual Coding dataset (Siegle et al., 2021) for
290
      evaluation, which has been used in a variety of work, such as constructing brain-like networks (Shi
      et al., 2022), modeling functional mechanisms (Bakhtiari et al., 2021; de Vries et al., 2020), and
291
      decoding neural representations (Schneider et al., 2023). This dataset is collected by Neuropixel
292
      probes from 6 mouse visual cortical regions simultaneously, including VISp, VISl, VISrl, VISal,
293   VISpm, and VISam. Notably, the neural activity was recorded while mice passively viewed natural-
294   istic visual stimuli without any task-driven behavior.
295
296
      The dataset contains 32 sessions, each for one mouse. Since the class of neurons responsive to
      natural visual stimuli is found in six visual regions, in this work we choose to analyze the neural
297
      activity of five mice that have the highest number of recorded neurons (see Appendix D for details),
298
      and these neurons are evenly distributed across all regions (the coefficient of variation for the number
299   of neurons across six brain regions is below 0.5). We focus on neural activity in response to natural
300   scenes and natural movies. As for natural scenes, there are 118 images presented in random order,
301   each for 250ms and 50 trials. We select five scenes that elicit the strongest average responses for
302   experiments. The neural activity in the form of spike counts is binned into 10ms windows so that
303   each trial contains 25 time points. As for the natural movie, it is 30s long with a frame rate of 30Hz,
304   presented for 10 trials. We bin the spike counts with a sampling frequency of 120Hz and align
305   them with the movie timestamps, resulting in 4 time points for each frame. For both datasets, we
306   randomly split each across all trials into 80% for training, 10% for validation, and 10% for test.
307
308   4.3.1    E XPERIMENTS FOR N EURAL ACTIVITY UNDER S TATIC NATURAL S CENE S TIMULI
309
      Experiment setup and evaluation In this experiment, we set all models to have 128-dimensional
310
      latent variables and train them for 5,000 iterations. The optimizer is set to Adam with a learning rate
311
      of 0.0001. At the training stage, each sample input to TiDeSPL-VAE is sequential neural activity
312   from 5 time points. For self-supervised contrastive learning, the offset of positive samples from
313   target samples is within ±3 time points. At the inference stage, we consider the temporal dependence
314   and the assumption that the latent variables of TiDeSPL-VAE are generated by an n-order Markov
315   chain. Consequently, for a target time point, we form a sequence data including its antecedent n
316   time points and its own to compute the latent variables (here n=4). The setup for the other models is
317   given in Appendix F. To quantify performance in decoding natural scenes, we first obtain the latent
318   variables of the last 20 time points (50ms-250ms) in each trial, since there is a response latency
319   in the mouse visual cortex for static stimuli (Siegle et al., 2021). Then, these latent variables are
320
      concatenated into a vector as latent representations of neural activity for that trial. We use the KNN
      algorithm, a nonparametric supervised learning method, to classify the latent representations of each
321
      trial, i.e., to decode the corresponding natural scenes. We search the number of neighbors in odd
322
      numbers from 1 to 20 and use the Euclidean distance metric. We fit the KNN using the training set
323   and choose the best number of neighbors on the validation set with classification accuracy as the
      metric. The accuracy of the test set is reported as the decoding score.

      4.4   A BLATION S TUDIES
468
      We perform ablation studies in several aspects of TiDeSPL-VAE’s components and neural activity
469
      input dimensions to explore their impact on performance. We present the results of Mouse 1 and
470   Mouse 2 in the main text and the results of the other mice are given in Appendix I.
471
472   The loss function and the recurrent module of TiDeSPL-VAE To show the effectiveness of
473   components of our model, we conduct some ablation studies on the loss function and the recurrent
474   module (Table 4 and 9). In terms of contrastive learning, we first exclude negative samples from
475   the computation of the contrastive loss and use only the cosine distance between the content latent
476   variables of the positive sample pairs as the loss function, i.e., only bring the positive pairs closer.
477
      There is a slight decrease in model performance, suggesting that negative samples are useful. Then
      we directly remove the constrastive loss or the swap operation, both of which show a similar impact.
478
      We last remove both, i.e., there are no more objectives related to constrastive learning. The large
479
      drop in performance suggests that contrastive learning plays a crucial role in our models. In terms
480   of the regular loss of style latent variables, we originally assumed that the prior distribution is time-
481   dependent. When we assume that it is an independent standard normal distribution at each time
482   step, the model performance degrades, demonstrating that time-dependent assumptions about the
483   prior are also important. In terms of the recurrent module, the results suggest that GRU is a better
484   choice in considering the trade-off between performance and computational efficiency. Besides, we
485   evaluate a non-recurrent version of our model by setting the time steps of GRU to 1, demonstrating
      that the recurrent module plays a critical role.

      5   D ISCUSSION
522   This work presents a novel sequential VAE by introducing time dependence into a self-supervised
523   generative model, aiming to reveal intrinsic correlations between visual neural activity and visual
524   stimulation. Our model, TiDeSPL-VAE, constructs latent representations conditioned on antecedent
525   input to extract temporal relationships from neural activity in a natural way. Results of synthetic
526   and mouse datasets demonstrate that our model outperforms alternative models and builds latent
527   representations that are strongly correlated with visual stimulation, in terms of decoding natural
528   scenes or movie frames and extracting explicit temporal structure from neural dynamics.
529   Most LVMs have focused on neural activity in the motor brain regions. There is a paucity of research
530   explaining visual neural activity with this type of model. CEBRA has made meaningful explorations
531   of neural responses of the mouse visual cortex (Schneider et al., 2023). Our work provides evidence
532   that introducing time dependence with a chronological order plays a crucial role in studying visual
533   neural activity with LVMs. However, there are some problems requiring further exploration. For
534   example, neural responses elicited by complex natural visual stimuli show large variability across
535   subjects and trials (Xia et al., 2021; Marks & Goard, 2021). Due to this, it is difficult for LVMs
536
      to consistently construct high-quality stimulus-correlated latent representations across conditions.
      Improvements in model structures and learning algorithms are needed in future work.
537
538   Last but not least, our approach is not limited to visual neural spikes from mice and can be extended
539   to neural data from other brain regions and other species and of other modalities. As a promising
      model for neuroscience, it may also provide some insights into the field of machine learning.

      A    BACKBONE S TRUCTURE OF T I D E SPL-VAE
703
704
705
      The encoder and decoder of our model are derived from Swap-VAE. The encoder consists of three
      blocks, the first two of which are sequentially stacked with a linear layer, a batch normalization,
706
      and a ReLU activation. The last block differs from Swap-VAE in that it additionally introduces the
707
      hidden states of the GRU as input. The output dimensions of the three blocks are N, M, and M, where
708   N is the number of neurons and M is the number of latent variables. The decoder is a symmetric
709   structure of the encoder, where the first two blocks are also sequentially stacked with a linear layer,
710   a batch normalization, and a ReLU activation, and the last block is a linear layer followed by a
711   SoftPlus activation. We set the dimensions of the content and style variables to be equal and use a
712   one-layer GRU for each latent representation, where the dimensions of the hidden states are equal
713   to the dimensions of the latent variables. For β-VAE, we use the same backbone structure. For other
714   alternative models, we retain the structure of the original.
715   In the training, we ensure that the hyperparameters of all models are consistent, obtained by grid
716   search. All models are trained on NVIDIA A100.

869
      Temporal dataset We generate three dynamic latent variables from the Lorenz system consisting
870   of a set of nonlinear equations and the firing rates of 30 simulated neurons are computed by randomly
871   weighted linear readouts from the Lorenz latent variables. The synthetic neural activity is also
872   generated from the Poisson distribution. The hyperparameters of the Lorenz system follow some
873   previous work (Sussillo et al., 2016). We run the Lorenz system for 1s (1ms for a time point) from
874   five randomly initialized conditions. Each condition contains 20 trials. We use 80% of the dataset
875   (16 trials/condition, 80,000 samples) for training and 20% (4 trials/condition, 20,000 samples) for
876   test.
