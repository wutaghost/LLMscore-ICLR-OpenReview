009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Existing 3D occupancy networks demand significant hardware resources, hinder-
012            ing the deployment of edge devices. Binarized Neural Networks (BNNs) offer a
013            potential solution by substantially reducing computational and memory require-
014            ments. However, their performances decrease notably compared to full-precision
015            networks. In addition, it is challenging to enhance the performance of the bi-
016
               narized model by increasing the number of binarized convolutional layers, which
               limits its practicability for 3D occupancy prediction. This paper presents two orig-
017
               inal insights into binarized convolution, substantiated with theoretical proofs: (a)
018
               1 × 1 binarized convolution introduces minimal binarization errors as the network
019            deepens, and (b) binarized convolution is inferior to full-precision convolution in
020            capturing cross-channel feature importance. Building on the above insights, we
021            propose a novel binarized deep convolution (BDC) unit that significantly enhances
022            performance, even when the number of binarized convolutional layers increases.
023            Specifically, in the BDC unit, additional binarized convolutional kernels are con-
024            strained to 1×1 to minimize the effects of binarization errors. Further, we propose
025            a per-channel refinement branch to reweight the output via first-order approxi-
026            mation. Then, we partition the 3D occupancy networks into four convolutional
027
               modules, using the proposed BDC unit to binarize them. The proposed BDC unit
               minimizes binarization errors and improves perceptual capability while signifi-
028
               cantly boosting computational efficiency, meeting the stringent requirements for
029
               accuracy and speed in occupancy prediction. Extensive quantitative and quali-
030            tative experiments validate that the proposed BDC unit supports state-of-the-art
031            precision in occupancy prediction and object detection tasks with substantially re-
032            duced parameters and operations. Code is provided in the supplementary material
033            and will be open-sourced upon review.

      de Charette, 2022; Li et al., 2023b) often involve complex computations and numerous parameters.
      Therefore, it is necessary to introduce model compression techniques (Deng et al., 2020) to reduce

      the computational complexity and parameter count of CNN-based occupancy networks.

083
      super-resolution (Yang et al., 2019) and denoising (Tian et al., 2020). We try replacing each full-
084
      precision convolutional unit in the occupancy network with the binarized convolutional units pro-
085   posed by these binarization algorithms. Such a binarized model could achieve a respectable level of
086   accuracy but still a notable performance gap compared to the full-precision model. In full-precision
087   models, it’s common sense that increasing convolutional layers can lead to performance improve-
088   ments. However, the binarized model did not exhibit a trend of performance improvement as the
089   number of binarized convolutional layers increased. Instead, there is a tendency for performance
090   to decline, making it challenging for binarized models to improve performance by increasing the
091   number of convolutional layers (Xia et al., 2022). Insufficient performance of binarized occupancy
092   networks inevitably will have adverse effects on the perception of 3D space, thereby restricting the
093
      deployment of binarized models in autonomous vehicles.
094   Therefore, addressing the issues of decreasing accuracy with increasing binarized convolutional lay-
095   ers and limited perceptual capability is crucial for bridging the performance gap between binarized
096   and full-precision models. To tackle these challenges, we propose a novel BNN-based method,
097   namely Binarized Deep Convolution Occupancy (BDC-Occ) network for efficient and practical oc-
098   cupancy prediction, marking the first study of binarized 3D occupancy networks. Our novel insights
099
      stem from two intrinsic properties of binarized convolution: (a) 1 × 1 binarized convolution intro-
      duces minimal binarization errors as the network deepens, and (b) binarized convolution is inferior
100
      to full-precision convolution in capturing cross-channel feature importance. Drawing on these in-
101
      sights, we limit additional binarized convolutional kernels to 1 × 1 to reduce the impact of binariza-
102   tion errors as the network depth increases. Secondly, we introduce a per-channel refinement branch
103   that leverages newly added convolutional layers to narrow the gap with the output of full-precision
104   convolution through first-order approximation. Integrating the two proposed techniques, we de-
105   velop the Binarized Deep Convolution (BDC) unit, which remarkably enhances binarized model
106   performance, despite the deepening of the binarized convolutional layers. We decompose the 3D
107   occupancy network into four fundamental modules and customize binarization using the BDC unit
      for each module.

      The innovations and contributions of this paper are summarized as follows:
109
      (i) Based on the original insights reinforced with theoretical proofs, we propose Binarized Deep
110
      Convolution (BDC) unit, further introduce a novel BNN-based occupancy network named BDC-
111
      Occ. To our knowledge, this is the first paper to study the binarized occupancy network.
112
      (ii) In the BDC unit, additional binarized convolutional kernels are constrained to 1 × 1 to minimize
113
      the effects of binarization errors as the network depth increases. Subsequently, we propose a per-
114
      channel refinement branch to reweight the output via first-order approximation, thereby mitigating
115   the limitations of binarized convolutional layers in assigning importance to features across channels.
116   The 3D occupancy network is further decomposed into four fundamental modules, allowing for a
117   customized design using the BDC unit.
118
      (iii) The proposed BDC unit reduces binarization errors and enhances perceptual capability while
119   considerably increasing computational efficiency, thus meeting the demanding requirements for ac-
120   curacy and speed in occupancy prediction. Extensive experiments on the Occ3D-nuScenes dataset
121   demonstrate that our method achieves state-of-the-art (SOTA) mIOU, closely approaching that of
122   full-precision models while utilizing only 52.26% of the operations and 59.97% of the parameters,
123   and achieving a 21.06% improvement in FPS.

      2.1   3D O CCUPANCY P REDICTION
128
129   The 3D occupancy prediction task comprises two sub-tasks: predicting the geometric occupancy
130   status for each voxel in 3D space and assigning corresponding semantic labels. We can catego-
131   rize mainstream 3D occupancy networks into two architectures: CNN architecture based on the
132   LSS (Philion & Fidler, 2020; Gan et al., 2023; Cao & de Charette, 2022; Yu et al., 2023; Mei
133   et al., 2023; Ming et al., 2024; Hou et al., 2024) method and Transformer architecture based on the
134   BEVFormer (Li et al., 2022; 2023a; Huang et al., 2023; Wei et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2023; Wang
135   et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023) method. Due to the deployment advantages of CNN models, this
136   paper focuses on CNN-based 3D occupancy networks. MonoScene (Cao & de Charette, 2022) is
137
      a pioneering work that utilizes a CNN framework to extract 2D features, which it then transforms
      into 3D representations. BEVDet-Occ (Huang & Huang, 2022) utilizes the LSS method to con-
138
      vert image features into BEV (Bird’s Eye View) features and employs BEV pooling techniques to
139
      accelerate model inference. FlashOcc (Yu et al., 2023) replaces 3D convolutions in BEVDet-Occ
140   with 2D convolutions and occupancy logits derived from 3D convolutions with channel-to-height
141   transformations of BEV-level features obtained through 2D convolutions. SGN (Mei et al., 2023)
142   adopts a dense-sparse-dense design and proposes hybrid guidance and efficient voxel aggregation
143   to enhance intra-class feature separation and accelerate the convergence of semantic diffusion. In-
144   verseMatrixVT3D (Ming et al., 2024) introduces a new method based on projection matrices to
145   construct local 3D feature volumes and global BEV features. Despite achieving impressive results,
146   these CNN-based methods rely on powerful hardware with substantial computational and memory
147   resources, which are impractical for edge devices. How to develop 3D occupancy prediction net-
148
      works for resource-constrained devices remains underexplored. Our goal is to address this research
      gap.

152
153
      BNN (Hubara et al., 2016; Xia et al., 2022; Cai et al., 2024; Li et al., 2023c; Qin et al., 2024;
      Liu et al., 2020; 2018; Rastegari et al., 2016; Chen et al., 2021; Qin et al., 2020) represents the
154
      most extreme form of model quantization, quantizing weights and activations to just 1 bit. Due
155
      to its significant effectiveness in memory and computational compression, BNN (Hubara et al.,
156   2016) finds wide application in both high-level vision and low-level vision. For instance, Xia et
157   al. (Xia et al., 2022) designed a binarized convolutional unit, BBCU, for tasks such as image super-
158   resolution, denoising, and reducing artifacts from JPEG compression. Cai et al. (Cai et al., 2024)
159   devised a binarized convolutional unit, BiSR-Conv, capable of adjusting the density and distribution
160   of representations for hyperspectral image (HSI) recovery. However, the potential of BNN in 3D
161   occupancy tasks remains unexplored. Hence, this paper explores binarized 3D occupancy networks,
      aiming to maintain high performance while minimizing computational and parameter overhead.

      3.2   B INARIZED D EEP C ONVOLUTION
196
197   Due to its outstanding performance and lightweight architecture, FlashOcc (Yu et al., 2023) serves
198   as the full-precision baseline model for the binarized model. Its performance reaches 37.84 mIoU,
199   which sets the upper performance bound for the binarized models.
200   Empirical evidence in full-precision models has shown that increasing network depth improves per-
201   formance. Due to the characteristics of binary networks, it is possible to maintain significantly low
202   computational and memory usage even when increasing the model depth. However, in previous re-
203   search, Xia et al. (Xia et al., 2022) observed that increasing the number of binarized convolutional
204   layers within the binarized convolutional unit leads to a significant decrease in binarized model
205   performance, the performance degradation issue with the increase in binarized convolutional layer
206
      depth within each unit restricts the further application of the binarized model. To address this is-
      sue, we propose the Binarized Deep Convolution (BDC) unit, which aims to enhance the binarized
207
      model performance by deepening the layers of the binarized convolution unit rather than reducing
208
      performance.
209
210   Cai et al. (Cai et al., 2024) proposed the binarized convolution unit BiSR-Conv, which can adjust the
211   density and enable effective binarization of convolutional layers. We utilize BiSR-Conv to binarize
212
      FlashOcc (Yu et al., 2023), forming our initial version of BDC-V0, with its structure shown in
      Figure 3 (a). Please refer to the supplementary materials for a more detailed description of the
213
      BDC-V0. The model achieves a performance of 34.51 mIoU.

346   Datasets. We use the Occ3D-nuScenes dataset (Tian et al., 2023), which comprises 28,130 samples
347   for training and 6,019 samples for validation.
348
      Evaluation Metrics. We evaluate the Occ3D-nuScenes’ validation set using the mean Intersection
349
      over Union (mIoU) metric. Similar to (Hubara et al., 2016), we compute the operations per second of
350
      BNN (OPsb ) as OPsb = OPsf /64 to measure the computational complexity, where OPsf represents
351
      FLOPS. To calculate the parameters of BNN, use the formula Parmsb = Parmsf /32, where the
352
      superscript b and f refer to the binarized and full-precision models, respectively. To compute the
353   total operations and parameters, we sum OPs as OPsb + OPsf and Params as Paramsb + Paramsf .
354
355   Implementation Details. For 3D occupancy prediction tasks, we employ FlashOcc (Yu et al., 2023)
356
      as the baseline network. We utilized ResNet50 (He et al., 2016) as the image backbone, with an input
      size of 256 × 704. Default learning rate 1 × 10−4 , AdamW (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2017) optimizer,
357
      and weight decay of 1×10−2 were utilized. The training lasted approximately 29 hours, utilizing 24
358
      epochs on two NVIDIA 3090 GPUs, with a batch size of 2 per GPU. Data augmentation strategies
359   for the Occ3D-nuScenes dataset remained consistent with those of FlashOcc (Yu et al., 2023). Pre-
360   vious works, such as FlashOcc and BEVDet-Occ (Huang & Huang, 2022), have demonstrated the
361   effectiveness of camera visibility masks during training. Therefore, we also employ camera visibil-
362   ity masks to enhance performance. Following the settings of FlashOcc, we employ the pre-trained
363   model from BEVDet (Huang et al., 2021) for 3D object detection tasks as our pre-training model.

526   5     C ONCLUSION
527
528   This paper introduces a binarized deep convolution (BDC) unit for 3D occupancy networks, ad-
529   dressing the performance degradation caused by increasing the number of binarized convolutional
530   layers. Our original theoretical analysis shows that 1 × 1 binarized convolution introduces mini-
531   mal binarization errors, and binarized convolution is less effective than full-precision convolution in
532   capturing cross-channel feature importance. Consequently, we restrict additional binarized convo-
533   lution kernels to 1 × 1 in the BDC unit. Furthermore, we propose a per-channel refinement branch
534   to overcome the limitations of binarized convolutional layers in assigning feature importance across
535   channels. Extensive experiments validate that our method surpasses existing SOTA binarized convo-
536
      lution networks and closely approaches the performance of full-precision models while using only
      52.26% of the operations and 59.97% of the parameters and achieving a 21.06% improvement in
537
      FPS.
538
539   Limitation. We have not tested our method for performance in Transformer architectures, which
      may limit its broader application.

724   A.1   M ORE D ETAILS A BOUT BASE M ODEL
725
726   Base model consists of an image encoder E2D , a view transformer module T , a BEV encoder EBEV ,
727   and an occupancy head H. The occupancy prediction network is composed of these modules con-
728   catenated sequentially. Assuming the input images are I ∈ RNview ×3×H×W , the occupancy predic-
729   tion output O ∈ RX×Y ×Z can be formulated as
730
                                          O = H(EBEV (T (E2D (I))))                                     (6)
731
732   where H and W represent the height and width of the input images, and X, Y , and Z denote the
733   length, width, and height of the 3D space, respectively, Nview represents the number of multi-view
734   cameras.
735   First, Multi-view images are sent to the image encoder E2D to obtain 2D features
736   f2D ∈ RNview ×C2D ×H2D ×W2D and depth prediction fdepth ∈ RNview ×Ndepth ×H2D ×W2D , where
737   C2D , H2D , W2D denote the number of channels, height and width of 2D features, respectively.
738   Ndepth represents the number of depth bins in the depth prediction.
739   Subsequently, the image features f2D and depth prediction fdepth are passed through the visual trans-
740
      formation module T , which transforms them into primary BEV features fT ∈ RCBEV ×HBEV ×WBEV
741   using camera intrinsic and extrinsic projection matrices. Here, CBEV represents the number of
742   channels of BEV features, while HBEV and WBEV represent the length and width of the BEV
743   space, respectively. Since the voxel distribution obtained from the depth map through projec-
744   tion matrices is sparse, the representation capability of primary BEV features may be insuffi-
745   cient. To this end, fT is passed through the BEV encoder EBEV 3D to obtain fine BEV features
746   fBEV ∈ RCBEV ×HBEV ×WBEV for further refinement.
747   Finally, the semantic prediction output logits Ologits ∈ RNclass ×X×Y ×Z come from the BEV fea-
748   tures fBEV processed through the occupancy prediction head H, where Nclass is the number of
749   semantic classes in the dataset. By taking the index corresponding to the maximum value of the
750   logits, we can obtain the final occupancy prediction output O.
