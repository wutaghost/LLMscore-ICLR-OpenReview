011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Extracting visual representations is a crucial challenge in the domain of compu-
014            tational histopathology. Considering the powerful strength of deep learning al-
015
               gorithms and the dearth of annotated samples, self-supervised learning presents
               itself as a compelling strategy to extract effective visual representations from un-
016
               labeled histopathology images. Although some self-supervised learning methods
017
               have been specifically proposed for histopathology image classification, most of
018            them have certain drawbacks that may affect the functionality or representation
019            capacity. In this work, we propose Masked Mamba, a novel self-supervised vi-
020            sual representation learning method tailored for histopathology images that can
021            adequately extract local-global features. The proposed method consists of two
022            stages: local perception positional encoding (LPPE) and directional Mamba vi-
023            sion backbone (DM). In addition, we use masked autoencoder (MAE) pretraining
024            to unleashing directional Mamba vision backbone’s potential. Masked Mamba
025            makes good use of domain-specific knowledge and requires no side information,
026
               which means good rationality and versatility. Experimental results demonstrate
               the effectiveness and robustness of masked Mamba on common histopathology
027
               classification tasks. Furthermore, ablation studies prove that the local perception
028
               positional encoding and directional Mamba vision backbone in masked Mamba
029            can complement and enhance each other.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Histopathology plays an important role in clinical medicine. It can reveal the morphology of patho-
035   logical cells and tissues under a microscope and provide key information for disease diagnosis
036   Srinidhi et al. (2021). With the histopathological slides have been digitized as histopathologi-
037   cal images, computer-aided histopathological image analysis methods have been widely proposed
038   Mobadersany et al. (2018). In early researches of histopathological image classification, the features
039
      of histopathology are manually designed and extracted via traditional feature extraction methods.
      However, these handcrafted features are very subjective and less representation capacity Madabhushi
040
      & Lee (2016). Recently, deep learning-based methods have shown strong representation capabilities
041
      LeCun et al. (2015), but such methods rely on large amounts of labeled data to learn visual represen-
042   tations. Large-scale labeled datasets are expensive and time-consuming for histopathological image
043   data. Therefore, researchers utilize pre-trained deep models, e.g. ImageNet Deng et al. (2009)
044   pre-trained convolutional neural Network (CNN), to extract visual representations histopathological
045   images Senousy et al. (2021). However, this strategy ignores data distribution differences and task
046   biases, which will lead to inappropriate or suboptimal visual representations.
047
      Therefore, self-supervised learning (SSL) Azizi et al. (2021) is one of the feasible solutions in
048   histopathological image classification. SSL can only use unlabeled data to adapt deep models. The
049   deep model can be easily trained to capture the features in a supervised learning manner. For natural
050   images, self-supervised learning methods based on contrastive learning (CL) Zhang et al. (2022b)
051   and masked image model (MIM) Chen et al. (2024) have achieved amazing results and shrunk
052   the performance gap with supervised methods on downstream tasks Jing & Tian (2020). How-
053   ever, there are three aspects that could be further enhanced. First, in the histopathological image
      classification task, rotation and shift operations should not alter the final result of the model. In

      other words, we expect translation invariance Kayhan & Gemert (2020) in histopathological im-
055   age classification. However, the absolute position encoding, initially designed to leverage the order
056   of tokens, damages such invariance because it adds unique positional encoding to each patch Chu
057   et al.. Second, the cropped histopathological image patches are typically large enough to capture
058   both cell-level structures (e.g., cellular microenvironment) and tissue-level contexts (e.g., tumor
059   microenvironment). Thus, both local and global features are advantageous for histopathological
060   image analysis and should be extracted. Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) Lerousseau et al.
061   (2020) have a strong capacity for learning low-level texture content features (local features). But the
062   learning of global context features is often constrained by the receptive field of CNNs. Transformer-
063
      based algorithms Stegmüller et al. (2023) can capture long-distance dependencies(global features)
      through self-attention mechanisms. But high computational costs and reliance on large-scale data
064
      limit their performance in histopathological image classification. In Mamba-based algorithms Gu &
065
      Dao (2023), the State Space Model (SSM) Hamilton (1994) is used to effectively capture the local
066   and global features. But Mamba is suitable for tasks with long sequences and autoregressive fea-
067   tures Yu & Wang (2024). These advantages cannot be exploited in the histopathology classification
068   task Yue & Li (2024). Third, the task of histopathology classification focuses on transferability.
069   Compared with contrastive learning algorithms that rely too much on data comparison, the masked
070   image model can not only save computational cost, but also be applied to medical images without
071   data augmentation Qi et al. (2023); Zhou et al. (2023a).
072   To this end, we propose Masked Mamba, a novel hybrid self-supervised visual representation learn-
073   ing method tailored for H&E-stained histopathological images. Our Masked Mamba employs two
074   stages for histopathological image classification. One is the local perception positional encoding
075   (LPPE), and the other is the directional Mamba vision backbone (DM). And we use masked autoen-
076   coder (MAE) He et al. (2022) pretraining to unleashing our directional Mamba vision backbone’s
077   potential. The major contributions of our work are summarized as follows.
078
079
             • We propose the LPPE can help capture both local and global structure information within
               the features and promote the representation ability of the network.
080
081          • We construct a hybrid architecture (DM) for histopathological image classification. It re-
082            places the causal convolution of Mamba with depthwise separable convolution and standard
083
               convolution, which enables more stable network training and also helps build a powerful
               feature extractor with fine local structure and global context. Not only that, such a structure
084
               is more suitable for MAE than the original Mamba Liu & Yi (2024).
085
086
             • To the best of our knowledge, this is the first hubrid Mamba-based unsupervised feature
               extractor carried out on the public histopathological image datasets. We use the MAE to
087
               motivate the potential of our DM.
088
089          • The efficacy of Masked Mamba is empirically substantiated through rigorous testing on
090
               four publicly available histopathological datasets. The empirical evidence showcases
               the superior performance of our algorithm when juxtaposed with existing state-of-the-
091
               art (SOTA) methodologies, thereby marking a significant leap forward in the domain of
092
               histopathological image classification.

105   capturing local dependencies while maintaining a global perspective. Even though Mamba-based
106   model was recently introduced to address the quadratic complexity of the attention mechanism in
107   computer vision. But its the performance is often underwhelming when compared with the CNN-
      based and Transformer-based models in histopathological image classification. The reason for this

      Self-supervised learning in Histopathological Image Classification. Recently, SSL, as an un-
      supervised learning paradigm, has achieved extraordinary performance in the field of pathological
113
      image analysis. These techniques can be broadly categorized into CL and MIM-based methods.
114
      SSL is typically divided into two main categories: contrastive and generative. In the context of
115   medical imaging, current applications of CL include the following: Li et al. (2021a) propose the use
116   of self-supervised contrastive learning to extract robust representations for Multiple Instance Learn-
117   ing (MIL) Deng et al. (2024). Ciga et al. (2022) introduce a contrastive self-supervised learning
118   method applied to large-scale pathology datasets from multiple organs with varying types of stains
119   and resolutions. Huang et al. (2021) extract patch features from Whole Slide Images (WSIs) through
120   self-supervised learning and adaptively aggregate these features based on their spatial information
121   and inter-patch correlation using the Transformer architecture. Li et al. (2021b) emphasize that
122   patch-wise spatial proximity is a significant characteristic of WSIs. Abbet et al. (2020) propose a
123
      self-supervised learning method that jointly learns a representation of tissue regions and a clustering
      metric to uncover their underlying patterns. Vu et al. (2023) present a handcrafted framework based
124
      on deep Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) for classifying different cancer subtypes. A typical
125
      algorithm based on MIM is the MAE. For instance, Zhou et al. (2023b) investigate a MAE-based
126   self-pretraining paradigm for the classification of diseases in chest X-rays, multi-organ segmentation

128   a family of MAE for electrocardiographs, which includes three customized masking modes: the
129   masked time autoencoder, the masked lead autoencoder, and the masked lead and time autoencoder.
130   Chen et al. (2023) study the strategies of how masked image modeling can enhance performance
131   from the perspectives of 3D medical image segmentation. Dai et al. (2023) propose a MAE inte-
132   grated with the Swin Transformer and note its suitability for smaller medical datasets. Quan et al.
133   (2024) propose a global contrast-masked autoencoder capable of capturing both local and global
134
      features of pathological images.

244   3.3.1          T HE LOCAL PERCEPTION POSITIONAL ENCODING (LPPE)
245
246   Due to the staining and sectioning of histopathological images, the consistency of the images may
247   be affected, and the highly similar cells make the classification task more difficult than general
248   classification tasks. Therefore, we need to capture both local and global structural information in
249   the features to promote the representation ability of the network. On this basis, the absolute posi-
250   tional encoding used in previous transformers and Mamba, initially designed to leverage the order
251   of tokens, damages the translation-invariance because it adds unique positional encoding to each
252   patch. Therefore, we design a novel positional encoding strategy (LPPE), which differs from the
253
      linear operation of traditional ViT models, It uses a combination method of convolution operation,
      linear and residual connection to extract local information. At the same time, it differs from Swin
254
      Transformer in the way of Patch Merging. As shown in Figure 2, we compare the Patch Merging in
255
      Swin Transformer with our LPPE. Patch Merging reduces the resolution of feature maps by merg-
256   ing neighboring patches using downsampling techniques. However, the resolution in pathological
257   images is often affected by staining and sectioning, and downsampling can exacerbate the above ef-
258   fects. Our design of LPPE is inspired by the Local Perception Unit (LPU) in CMT Guo et al. (2022).
259   Based on LPU, we introduce residual networks. The DWConv is used to extract local information
260   with negligible extra computational cost. The motivation for inserting shortcut is similar to that of
261   classic residual networks, which can promote the propagation ability of gradient across layers.

284   3.3.2       T HE D IRECTIONAL M AMBA V ISION BACKBONE
285
286   we redesigned the original Mamba mixer As depicted in Figure 1 (c), we redesign the original
287   Mamba (as shown in Figure 1 (b)) to make it more suitable for vision tasks. First, we replace the
288
      causal convolution (Cau-Conv1D) with depthwise separable convolution (Sep-Conv1D). Given that
      samples in pathology images (such as different types of cancer cells) may be visually highly similar,
289
      the causal convolution only permits unidirectional token mixing, which hinders the potential of non-
290
      autoregressive image generation. In contrast, the standard convolution enables tokens to interact
291   bidirectionally across all positions in the input sequence, effectively capturing the global context.
292   In addition, we added a symmetric branch without SSM, consisting of a regular convolution (Reg-
293   Conv1D) and SiLU activation, to compensate for any content lost due to the sequential constraints
294   of SSMs. We then concatenate the output of both branches and project it via a final linear layer. This
295   combination ensures that the final feature representation incorporates both the sequential and spatial
296   information, leveraging the strengths of both branches. We note that the output of each branch
297   is projected into an embedding space with size C/2 (i.e., half the size of the original embedding
298   dimension) to maintain a similar number of parameters to the original block design. Given an input
299
      Xin , the output Xout of the DM Block can be computed as follows:
300                                                   X1 = Scan(σ(Sep-Conv1D(Linear(C, C2 )(Xin ))))
301                                                   X2 = σ(Reg-Conv1D(Linear(C, C2 )(Xin )))                                                             (8)
302                                                   Xout = Linear( C2 , C)(Concat(X1 , X2 ))
303
      where Linear(Cin , Cout )(·) denotes a linear layer with Cin and Cout as input and output embedding
304
      dimensions, Scan is the selective scan operation as in Gu & Dao (2023) and σ is the activation func-
305
      tion. In addition, Sep-Conv1D is the depthwise separable convolution, Reg-Conv1D signifies the
306   1-dimensional regular convolutional operation, and Concat indicates the concatenation operation.
307   In general, our proposed modification leads to richer feature representations, better generalization,
308   and improved performance on histopathology image classification tasks.

      5   C ONCLUSION
487
488   In this work, we introduced an innovative unsupervised classification algorithm tailored for the
489   unique challenges of pathological image analysis. Central to this algorithm is the mitigation of
490   dependency on extensively annotated datasets, which are often scarce and labor-intensive to produce.
491
      Pathological image classification is inherently complex due to the intricate visual similarities that
492
      can exist across various cell types and tissues. This complexity is compounded by the necessity to
493
      focus on specific local features, such as the intricacies of cell nuclei morphology or the cytoplasm’s
494   distribution. To address these issues, we developed an advanced LPPE module for the encoding
495   process, specifically designed to enhance feature extraction from critical local areas. Complement-
496   ing this, we incorporated the DM module within the encoder to augment the classifier’s capacity
497   for assimilating global contextual information and mastering long-range spatial dependencies. This
498   dual-module approach effectively reduces the over-reliance on particular pathological regions and
499   accommodates the inherent variability in staining processes.
500   The robustness of our algorithm was rigorously evaluated through classification experiments on
501   four diverse pathological datasets. The results were clear: our algorithm not only holds its own
502   but outperforms current state-of-the-art methods, demonstrating its potential as a tool for advancing
503   pathological analysis. Looking ahead, the versatility of our proposed algorithm opens up promis-
504   ing avenues for application in clinical tasks, where it has the potential to facilitate more accurate
505   diagnostics and contribute to the broader field of medical image analysis. This research marks an
506   important step forward in the quest for more effective and efficient pathological image classification,
507   and we are optimistic about its future applications and continued development.
