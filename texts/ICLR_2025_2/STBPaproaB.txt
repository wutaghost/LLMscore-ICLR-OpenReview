009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Neurosymbolic (NeSy) artificial intelligence describes the combination of logic
012            or rule-based techniques with neural networks. Compared to neural approaches,
013            NeSy methods often possess enhanced interpretability, which is particularly
014            promising for biomedical applications like drug discovery. However, since in-
015            terpretability is broadly defined, there are no clear guidelines for assessing the bi-
016            ological plausibility of model interpretations. To assess interpretability in the con-
017            text of drug discovery, we devise a novel prediction task, called drug mechanism-
018            of-action (MoA) deconvolution, with an associated, tailored knowledge graph
019
               (KG), MoA-net. We then develop the MoA Retrieval System (MARS), a NeSy ap-
               proach for drug discovery which leverages logical rules with learned rule weights.
020
               Using this interpretable feature alongside domain knowledge, we find that MARS
021
               and other NeSy approaches on KGs are susceptible to reasoning shortcuts, in
022            which the prediction of true labels is driven by “degree-bias” rather than the
023            domain-based rules. Subsequently, we demonstrate ways to identify and mitigate
024            this. Thereafter, MARS achieves performance on par with current state-of-the-art
025            models while producing model interpretations aligned with known MoAs.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Drug discovery (DD), the search for novel drugs or chemical compounds to treat ailments, often
031   involves the screening of thousands of small compounds (Lin et al., 2020). Many computational
032   approaches have been developed to accelerate and streamline this screening process (Gottlieb et al.,
033   2011; Gan et al., 2023). Specifically, hundreds of such approaches operate upon knowledge graphs
034   (KGs), in which nodes representing drugs, proteins, or medical conditions are connected by edges,
035   representing the relationships between them (Chen et al., 2020). Typically, DD is formulated on a
036
      KG as a link prediction task between drugs and the corresponding medical conditions (indications)
      to be treated (Schultz et al., 2021; Rivas-Barragan et al., 2022).
037
038   It is also important to understand each drug’s mechanism-of-action (MoA), the molecular processes
039   by which it achieves its medicinal effect. For instance, as depicted in Fig. 1, MoAs typically involve
040   chains or paths of physical, molecular interactions induced by a drug (Crino, 2016). Uncovering
041   these interactions informs researchers as to how each drug works and de-risks potential side effects
042
      (Palve et al., 2021; Green et al., 2023). Revealing MoAs alongside computational DD, a task we
      call MoA deconvolution, requires model interpretability: transparency into the processes or patterns
043
      which led to certain predictions (Molnar, 2022). Unfortunately, most state-of-the-art techniques on
044
      KGs rely on “black-box” models (Wu et al., 2020). Recently, neurosymbolic (NeSy) approaches,
045   which combine logical rules with neural networks (DeLong et al., 2024) have been positioned as a
046   promising avenue for MoA deconvolution because they tend to possess enhanced interpretability.
047
048
      However, interpretability is broadly defined (Molnar, 2022), which poses an additional chal-
049
      lenge: there are no clear guidelines for assessing the plausibility of model interpretations, espe-
050   cially for this novel task. Although some previous studies present explainable or interpretable
051   pipelines (Rivas-Barragan et al., 2022; Urbina et al., 2021), the corresponding explanations lever-
052   age associative patterns: two nodes with mutual connections are likely to share other connec-
053   tions (Paul et al., 2021). For example, such methods utilize associations regarding a drug’s phar-
      macological class (Ratajczak et al., 2022), side effects (Liu et al., 2021), or known indications

082   We propose MoA deconvolution, the prediction of mechanistic paths between drugs and their bio-
083   logical effects, as a prediction task for evaluating interpretable methods on KGs. To benchmark this
084   task, we generate a tailored KG, called MoA-net, from real-world, experimental data. To perform
085   MoA deconvolution, we introduce a NeSy DD approach called the MoA Retrieval System (MARS).
086   To create MARS, we draw inspiration from previous NeSy methods (Liu et al., 2021; Drancé et al.,
087   2021). However, unlike its predecessors, MARS achieves enhanced interpretability by learning
088   weights associated with logical rules which resemble MoAs. Following training, rule weights reflect
089   relative usefulness to MARS’ reasoning processes.
090   Alongside biomedical domain knowledge, MARS’ enhanced interpretability reveals a reasoning
091   shortcut in which predictions are based on unintended semantics (Marconato et al., 2024b). Essen-
092   tially, predictions upon MoA-net are driven by “degree-bias”, an artifact of node degree variance
093   (Zietz et al., 2024), rather than the rules representing domain knowledge. Therefore, to address this,
094   we consider the desiderata from Marconato et al. (2024a) for making NeSy systems shortcut-aware:
095   (1) calibration, high accuracy on concepts unaffected by reasoning shortcuts, (2) performance, high
096
      accuracy despite reasoning shortcuts being present, and (3) cost effectiveness achieved through sim-
      ple mitigation strategies. Using these desiderata as guidelines, we make MARS shortcut-aware for
097
      more insightful predictions involving DD and MoA deconvolution. Ultimately, our study under-
098
      scores the importance of evaluating the capabilities of NeSy models within applied domains: by
099   evaluating model interpretations against specific domain knowledge, we can more easily identify
100   and mitigate shortcuts.

105   NeSy AI for MoA deconvolution. Several NeSy approaches involve logical rules reflect-
106   ing path-like patterns in biomedical KGs. For example, Sudhahar et al. (2024) investigates
107   evidence chains, paths explaining associations between drugs and diseases. However, these expla-
      nations are derived separately from indication predictions, using an additional rule-mining model

108
      (Meilicke et al., 2019). Other approaches (Liu et al., 2021; Drancé et al., 2021) accomplish similar
109   tasks through deep reinforcement learning (RL), in which a neural network contributes toward the
110   optimization of a reward function (Acharya et al., 2023). In these specific cases, reasoning is guided
111   by the path-like rules, and predictions are expected to align, to some extent, with such rules. In
112   contrast to these previous studies, we focus upon paths representing MoAs, involving mechanistic,
113   molecular relations. In this study, we also identify a major risk: the approach may neglect to utilize
114   rules in favor of other semantics for reward optimization. This results in reasoning shortcuts.
115
      Reasoning Shortcuts. Several NeSy approaches are designed to abide by rules and domain knowl-
116
      edge (Drancé et al., 2021; Dash & Goncalves, 2021), which might portray such approaches as more
117
      trustworthy than neural, black box ones (Gaur & Sheth, 2024). Recent studies, however, have found
118   that NeSy approaches may suffer from reasoning shortcuts, in which a model predicts the correct
119   outcome via unintended semantics (Marconato et al., 2024b; Li et al., 2024b). While reasoning
120   shortcuts are not exclusive to NeSy methods (Jiang & Bansal, 2019; Li et al., 2024a), they may be
121   more easily overlooked when such approaches are portrayed as trustworthy.

      3   MARS: A N E S Y APPROACH FOR M OA DECONVOLUTION
124
125
      Here, we build the MoA Retrieval System (MARS) to perform MoA deconvolution. MARS im-
126   proves upon a method called Policy-guided walks with logical rules (PoLo) (Liu et al., 2021) by
127   introducing dynamic, learned rule weights. This differs from previous approaches, where weights
128   are static and pre-computed (e.g., mined or literature-derived) (Liu et al., 2021; Drancé et al., 2021).
129   As discussed further, these learned weights also make MARS shortcut-aware.
130
      As shown in Fig. 2, MARS takes two major inputs. The first involves a KG. A KG uses nodes to
131
      represent entities and edges between them to represent relationships. A KG triple comprises two
132   nodes connected by an edge of some specific type, or relation. Here, we represent triples as bi-
133
      nary predicates: for example, the binary predicate interacts(P rotein, P rotein) states that two
134   P rotein nodes are connected via the interacts relation. Node degree describes the number of
135   edges connected to a node.

      3.1   M ETAPATH - BASED RULES
166
167   Metapaths are abstract representations of instantiated paths in a graph (Sun et al., 2011; Himmelstein
168   et al., 2017; Noori et al., 2023). For example, given the following path, P , in our KG:
169
                            upregulates                   interacts                  participates
170     Cortisone acetate −−−−−−−−→ GC receptor −−−−−−→ COX protein −−−−−−−−→ Inflammation
171
172   the corresponding metapath, P̃ , would be:
173                     upregulates           interacts               participates
174
                 Drug −−−−−−−−→ Protein −−−−−−→ Protein −−−−−−−−→ Biological Process
175
      Within this study, metapaths can be understood as a sequence of triples within the KG structure,
176
      making them inherently interpretable. In MARS, metapaths are used as the bodies of logical rules,
177
      in which triples are connected by logical conjunctions (∧). Conjunctions indicate that, if all triples
178
      in the rule body are true, then the rule head is evaluated as true. The rule head, the left side of the
179
      implication arrow (⇐), is a single triple representing the relation of interest between the first and
180   last node types of the metapath, e.g.,:
181
182   induces(Drug, BP) ⇐ upregulates(Drug, ProteinA ) ∧
183
                       interacts(ProteinA , ProteinB ) ∧ participates(ProteinB , BP)
184   If a rule head is evaluated as true, then the rule is satisfied. For each metapath-based rule, Mi ,
185   in a set of rules, M = {M1 , M2 , ..., Mm }, we denote the rule weight by w(Mi ) ∈ R, where
186   0 ≤ w(Mi ) ≤ 1. Such a weight indicates the relative usefulness of the metapath-based rule to the
187   prediction task. In Section 3.3, we discuss how we initialize and compute these weights.
188
189   3.2   OVERVIEW OF MARS.
190
      Using a deep RL process, MARS trains an agent to take walks of length L through the KG to connect
191
      pairs of nodes having the pre-defined relation of interest. Here, that relation is induces(Drug, BP),
192
      which are masked from the agent during training. Each walk generates a path, P , such as the
193
      one in the previous section 3.1. This path, P , can also be understood as a series of L transitions:
194               r1      r2      rL
      P := (ec −→    e2 −→    ... −→ eL+1 ). The agent may also remain at its current node. Ultimately, the
195
      goal of the agent is episodic: to find paths in which the starting node, ec (the drug), and the terminal
196
      node, eL+1 (the BP) have the induces relation. By training the agent to do so, it can identify node
197   pairs with the desired relationship, thus generalizing beyond the training set to predict novel pairs in
198
      a holdout, test set. In other words, while true positive predictions in the test set serve as validation,
199
      false positives are positioned as potentially novel induces(Drug, BP) predictions.
200
201   Similarly to a Markov Decision Process (Bellman, 1957), the agent moves based on its current
202   position and the next possible actions. Additionally, however, the history of the agent’s previous
203
      actions are encoded with an LSTM (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997; Sherstinsky, 2020), whose
      parameters are trained to optimize the reward function (Eq. 5 in Appendix A.4), which is evaluated
204
      each time the agent completes L transitions from some starting node.
205
206   In short, the reward function, originally from Liu et al. (2021), quantifies how successful P is ac-
207   cording to two rewards (Fig. 2). The first, base reward indicates whether the terminal node in the
208   path, eL+1 , is one of the desired target (BP) nodes (ed ) that forms a true pair with the starting
209   (drug) node, ec . Put simply: “given an induces(Drug, BP) triple, did the agent make a successful
210
      traversal between the drug and BP nodes?”
211   The second, supplementary reward, contingent upon the first, indicates whether the corresponding
212   metapath, P̃ , matches any metapath-based rule, Mi . The second reward is also proportional to the
213   rule’s corresponding weight, and MARS updates these weights during training. We accomplish
214   these updates through a novel algorithm we call two-hop joint probability, or P2H . Therefore, the
215   agent is not only encouraged to find connections between true pairs of nodes, but it is also guided
      toward paths which resemble known MoAs. Thus, MARS has two key interpretable features: (1)


                                                           4

216
      paths between nodes which as potential MoA predictions, and (2) learned rule weights which serve
217   as a proxy for the importance of each rule.

      3.3   MARS DYNAMICALLY UPDATES RULE WEIGHTS
220
221   After MARS is executed, its learned rule weights reflect each rule’s relative usefulness in the pre-
222   diction task. Additionally, during training, weight updates drive the agent toward more informative
223   paths and bypass the assumptions that pre-assigned rule weights are correct. This eliminates the
224   need for pre-computed or literature-derived rule weights; thus, we initialize all weights uniformly
225   as 0.5, a medium level of importance. We test two approaches for updating rule weights.
226   Naive updates. The naive way to implement weight updates (MARSnaive ) is to increase weights
227   according to the frequency at which each metapath-based rule is satisfied. We record observed
228   frequency, O, at which each metapath-based rule is satisfied. This is normalized by the batch-
229   specific expected frequency, E, which assumes that every rule has a uniform probability across the
230   total number of occurrences in that batch. If the agent finds zero occurrences, no weight updates are
231   made. Ultimately, this produces a metric, µ (Eq. 1) in which µ > 1 indicates usefulness (the agent
232   used that rule more than others), and µ < 1 indicates otherwise.

301   3.4   DATASETS : MoA-net AND ITS VARIANTS
302
      We design our KG, MoA-net, specifically for MoA prediction. MoA-net consists of drugs, proteins,
303
      and BPs (Appendix A.5). We assemble it using the causal relations between drugs and proteins from
304
      several real-world datasets comprising experimental data, including Custom KG (Rivas-Barragan
305
      et al., 2020) and OpenBioLink KG (Breit et al., 2020). The BP nodes come from experimentally-
306
      derived and expert-curated molecular function annotations in UniProt (Consortium, 2015).
307
308   To predict drug-BP triples, which are unique to MoA-net, we make use of publicly available func-
309   tional and biochemical assays in ChEMBL (v33), an open access database of bioactive compounds
310   (Gaulton et al., 2012). Of the 1,622 drug-BP triples obtained, 48 also had known MoAs in Drug-
311
      MechDB (Gonzalez-Cavazos et al., 2023), a manually-curated compendium of MoAs. Between the
      three node types, we define five unique edge types, or relations, shown in Appendix A.5. We also
312
      include all inverse relations, running in the opposite direction of causality.
313
314   Using the hetnetpy package (Himmelstein et al., 2021), we extract all metapaths (see Section 3.1)
315   from MoA-net which we considered to be valid MoAs: those comprising directed, mechanistic paths
316   between drug and BP nodes (see Appendix A.1). We exclude metapaths depicting associative pat-
317   terns, such as those leveraging information about shared BP targets, from our set of metapath-based
318   rules. Based on MoAs found in DrugMechDB, we limit metapaths to a maximum length of four
319   relations (or hops).
320   Finally, we create variants of MoA-net. To investigate reasoning shortcuts, we use the Zietz et al.
321   (2024) implementation of XSwap (Hanhijärvi et al., 2009), which swaps edges in a KG without af-
322   fecting the distribution of node degrees. We call the resultant KG MoA-net-permuted. Additionally,
323   we implement an automatic trimming step, which reduces edges of each class to below a user-
      specified threshold by iteratively removing those between the highest-degree nodes. By setting the


                                                         6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      threshold to 10,000 (thereby reducing protein-protein interactions to ∼ 50% of edges), our approach
325   can work on a subgraph of the MoA-net, which we refer to as MoA-net-10k.

329
330
      We split the drug-BP triples within MoA-net into training (60%), validation (20%), and test (20%)
      sets. We evaluate the models using Hits@k, where k ∈ {1, 3, 10} and mean reciprocal rank (MRR),
331
      optimizing for the latter. Hits@k reports the proportion of times the correct results are in the top k
332
      ranked entries, while MRR reports how highly ranked the first correct item is amongst ranked results
333   (Chen et al., 2020). In addition to these standard metrics, we report the pruned metrics: these are
334   computed on a subset of the predictions that utilized one of the pre-defined metapath-based rules
335   (see Appendix A.1), excluding all predictions which did not satisfy a rule. Notably, pruned metrics
336   help us assess the calibration desideratum, as introduced in Section 1, since rule-based predictions
337   follow the expected model semantics.
338
      We conduct an extensive benchmark of our method against nine different baseline KG embedding
339   (KGE) models, two state-of-the-art NeSy methods, and one network measure (Appendix ??). We
340   train and evaluate these models on the same data splits as MARS on MoA-net-10k.

351   4.1   A SSOCIATIVE PATTERNS IMPROVE ACCURACY BUT OFFER LIMITED PRACTICAL USE
352
353   In an initial set of experiments on MoA-net, we observed that pruned metrics were consistently lower
354   than standard ones (Fig. 3-A), indicating that the metapath-based rules were not being utilized in
355   most predictions. This can happen because rule-based rewards are contingent upon a true pair being
356   found (Eq. 5). Additionally, amongst recorded trajectories, most did not follow our metapath-based
357   rules; instead, most trajectories used the following associative pattern, involving inverse edges:
358   induces(Drug1 , BP2 ) ⇐ induces(Drug1 , BP1 ) ∧
359                          induces(Drug2 , BP1 ) ∧ induces(Drug2 , BP2 )
360
      This associative pattern indicates that two drugs inducing a common BP also likely induce another
361
      BP. This type of pattern is also present in Liu et al. (2021), in which the most used pattern was the
362
      following:
363
364   treats(Drug1 , Disease) ⇐ causes(Drug1 , Side Effect) ∧
365                           causes(Drug2 , Side Effect) ∧ treats(Drug2 , Disease)
366   However, when we reproduced the results from Liu et al. (2021), we achieved the same reported
367   metrics even in the absence of the above, associative rule (See Appendix A.8). This suggests that,
368   although the associative rule may serve as a plausible model explanation, it does not necessarily
369   guide model training. Furthermore, as stated in Section 1, MoAs, like those in DrugMechDB, in-
370   volve physical, molecular interactions, rather than associative ones.
371

376   involving consecutive protein-protein interactions (PPIs) (i.e., interacts(P rotein, P rotein)),
377   were consistently less important (Fig. 4). This indicated that the agent avoided exploring consecutive
      PPIs.

420
421   Previous research on KGs has shown that node degree distribution, the number of adjacent edges
422   for each KG node, can significantly bias predictions (Tang et al., 2020; Ju et al., 2024). Specif-
423
      ically, inspection bias, a type of degree bias, occurs when the KG is not uniformly inspected or
      sampled (Zietz et al., 2024). Since PPIs are the most common relation type in MoA-net (90% of
424
      edges) (Appendix A.5), protein nodes have a higher degree distribution than other node types. We
425
      hypothesized, therefore, that the agent circumvents denser parts of the KG, creating an inspection
426   bias. Although rule-based predictions merit a larger reward, the MARS agent exploits associative
427   patterns for a more reliable reward. To confirm the existence of degree bias, we tested our approach
428   upon MoA-net-permuted. As explained in Section 3.4, MoA-net-permuted is a variant of MoA-net in
429   which edges are swapped while preserving node degree distribution. This tests the extent to which
430   node degree drives predictions. Indeed, the lack of change amongst standard performance metrics
431   suggested that node degree was largely responsible for predictions (Fig. 3-B). Put simply, the agent
      gets lost when exploring the PPIs, so it avoids them.

      4.3   I DENTIFYING AND MITIGATING DEGREE BIAS IMPROVES PERFORMANCE
433
434   To temporarily prohibit the models from using associative patterns as in Section 4.1, we removed in-
435   verse edges from MoA-net and corresponding metapath-based rules. Consequently, the performance
436   metrics were poor, (e.g., MRR consistently < 0.1 (Fig. 3-C)). This confirmed that the models relied
437   on associative patterns for predictions.
438   Next, we wanted to confirm that the agent was getting lost within the PPIs. As explained in Sec-
439   tion 3.4, MoA-net-10k is a variant of MoA-net with fewer PPIs. We tested MARSP2H , MARSnaive ,
440   and PoLo with the same parameters upon on MoA-net-10k (Fig. 3-D). As before, we excluded in-
441   verse edges. Since we set trajectory length L = 4, our approach automatically removed drug-BP
442   triples from the validation/test sets that were no longer connected via directed paths of length ≤ 4,
443   resulting in 100 and 90 triples, respectively. Metrics were markedly improved for PoLo, MARSnaive ,
444   and particularly for MARSP2H , in comparison to the full MoA-net without inverse edges (Fig. 3-C).
445   To ensure this improvement was not simply the result of a reduced test set, we also tested the ap-
446
      proaches upon MoA-net with 100 sampled test triples, which showed no change (see Appendix A.9).
447   While removing inverse edges improved metrics, a shortcut-aware system should achieve high per-
448   formance even with the shortcut present (Marconato et al., 2024a). We addressed this next.
449
450   4.4   MARSP2H RETAINS PERFORMANCE AMONGST RULE - BASED PREDICTIONS
451
452   We re-introduced inverse edges to MoA-net-10k, thereby restoring the ability to use reasoning short-
453   cuts. Thereafter, we tested each of MARSP2H , MARSnaive , and PoLo again (Fig. 3-E). While each
454   approach was optimized for standard MRR, pruned metrics indicated how well positive predictions
455   aligned with rules. In Fig. 3-E, we show that both MARS variants and PoLo achieved standard met-
456
      rics on par with or better than MoA-net-10k without inverse edges (Fig. 3-D). However, MARSP2H
      also achieved pruned metrics comparable to its standard metrics, showing improved calibration rel-
457
      ative to PoLo and MARSnaive . Finally, as in section 4.2, we used XSwap on MoA-net-10k to assess
458
      the susceptibility of MARSP2H to degree bias. Unlike in Section 4.2, we found no evidence for
459   degree bias (see Appendix A.10).
460
461
      4.5   E XTERNAL VALIDATION OF MARSP2H ON MoA-net-10k
462
463   In comparison to baseline methods, MARSP2H ’s metrics outperformed all but MINERVA’s, with
464   which they were comparable (Table 1). However, since MINERVA does not, by design, utilize
465   rules for guidance, it suffers the same reasoning shortcuts as PoLo and MARSnaive . In contrast to
466   MINERVA, DWPC suffers the opposite limitation: predictions are based only on metapath-based
467   rules. MARSP2H ’s pruned metrics, which are directly comparable, also outperform DWPC. Fi-
468   nally, as mentioned in Section 3.4, several drug-BP pairs corresponding to known MoAs in Drug-
469   MechDB were included in the MoA-net test set. Of these, 33 pairs remained within MoA-net-10k’s
470   test set, and MARSP2H recovered the correct MoA for all of them. Thus, this comprehensive bench-
471
      mark highlights MARS’ ability to achieve near state-of-the-art performance by effectively balancing
      domain-specific knowledge with the capacity to generalize beyond it.

474   5     D ISCUSSION
475
476   NeSy approaches are sometimes portrayed as more trustworthy than their black-box counterparts,
477   partially due to increased interpretability (Gaur & Sheth, 2024; DeLong et al., 2024). Here, we
478   presented a NeSy RL approach, MARSP2H , which promotes interpretability by deconvoluting drug
479   MoAs. Specifically, through our novel algorithm, two-hop joint probabilities (P2H ), MARS learned
480   weights corresponding to rules representing MoA patterns; each weight served as a proxy for each
481   rule’s importance. However, these insights revealed a new issue: NeSy RL approaches on KGs are
482
      susceptible to reasoning shortcuts. Specifically, in our study, predictions were driven by node degree
      bias. Ultimately, MARS’ interpretability called the trustworthiness of such approaches to question.
483
484   To address this, we considered Marconato et al. (2024a)’s desiderata for a shortcut-aware NeSy
485   system. Specifically, on MoA-net-10k, MARSP2H showed both competitive performance as well as
      calibration in comparison to other models. Notably, however, measuring calibration is challenging

504   semantics for MoA deconvolution, we can not determine whether every other prediction follows
505   unintended semantics. For example, in the classic MNIST addition task, popularly used to assess
506   NeSy methods (Manhaeve et al., 2018), a model is trained to determine the sum of two handwritten
507   digits. In this toy example, the misclassification of a handwritten ‘2’ as ‘3’ and vice versa would still
508   amount to the same sum. Thus, reasoning shortcuts can be objectively identified. On the contrary,
509   while we provide evidence that predictions using associative patterns are largely affected by node
510   degree bias, we can not determine whether such patterns always reflect a reasoning shortcut.
511   Finally, regarding cost effectiveness, MARSP2H can be applied to any KG, serving as a general-
512   izeable mitigation strategy. However, we also note that this was achieved upon MoA-net-10k, a
513   trimmed version of MoA-net. While we automated this trimming step, such a strategy does not
514   make use of all available information. To scale MARSP2H to denser KGs and maintain its shortcut-
515   aware status, several future directions could be explored. For instance, one could merge similar,
516   high-degree nodes or rely upon domain knowledge, like the identification of promiscuous proteins
517   (Copley, 2020), to make more informed choices about edge trimming or masking. In addition to ad-
518   dressing these methodological limitations, prospective studies could explore more complex MoAs,
519   include binding or expression values, or involve a variety of protein subclasses.
520   In summary, our study highlights a key concern in which the behavior of some NeSy RL approaches
521   could be attributed to node degree bias, rather than meaningful, domain-specific concepts. The
522   interpretability of our approach, MARSP2H , allowed insight into this reasoning shortcut. Therefore,
523   we question whether such shortcuts are identifiable amongst black-box approaches. Additionally,
524   by testing a NeSy approach upon a novel applied task, MoA deconvolution, we could flag down
525   patterns, like associative ones, which were plausible yet arguably less meaningful to biomedical
526   researchers. Therefore, our study emphasizes the importance of testing interpretable models, like
527
      NeSy ones, in an applied domain. Finally, while our study honors the desiderata for shortcut-aware
      NeSy systems, we also examined the extent to which they were applicable to a biomedical domain.
528
529
530   6   C ONCLUSIONS
531
532   We propose a novel prediction task for NeSy approaches on biomedical KGs: mechanism-of-action
533   (MoA) deconvolution. In contrast to previous DD approaches, MoA deconvolution utilizes model
534   interpretability to uncover the molecular mechanisms behind medicinal drugs. We also constructed
535   a publicly available KG, MoA-net, for evaluating this task. To predict drug MoAs alongside indi-
536
      cations, we designed the MoA Retrieval System (MARS). Relative to previous NeSy approaches,
      MARS has enhanced interpretability as it dynamically learns weights corresponding to logical rules.
537
      We showed that, with respect to the three desiderata for reasoning-aware NeSy systems, MARS has
538
      improved calibration and cost effectiveness compared to its predecessors, thereby enabling the iden-
539   tification and mitigation of a reasoning shortcut based on node degree bias.

      A.4   R EWARD FUNCTION
844
      Using a deep RL process, MARS trains an agent to take walks of length L through the KG to connect
845   pairs of nodes having the pre-defined relation of interest (e.g., induces); such edges are masked
846   from the agent during training. Each walk generates a path, P , which can be understood as a series
847                                 r1      r2      rL
      of L transitions: P := (ec −→     e2 −→   ... −→  eL+1 ). The agent may also remain at its current
848   node. Ultimately, the goal of the agent is episodic: to find paths in which the starting node, ec ,
849   and the terminal node, eL+1 , make up one of the true input pairs. By training the agent to do so,
850   it can identify node pairs with some desired relationship, thus generalizing beyond the training set
851   to predict novel pairs. Through a process akin to a Markov Decision Process (Bellman, 1957), the
852   agent makes decisions about its next move based on information about its current position and the
853   next possible actions. Additionally, however, the history of the agent’s previous actions are encoded
854   with an LSTM (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997; Sherstinsky, 2020), whose parameters are trained
855
      to optimize the reward function, R(SL+1 ) (Eq. 5), which is evaluated each time the agent completes
      L transitions from some starting node, reaching a state SL+1 . MARS uses the same reward function
856
      as Liu et al. (2021):
