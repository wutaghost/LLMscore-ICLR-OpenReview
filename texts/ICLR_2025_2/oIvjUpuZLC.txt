009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Machine learning for time series has recently garnered considerable attention. In-
012            deed, automatically extracting meaningful representations from large and complex
013            time series data is becoming imperative for several real-world applications. Neu-
014            ral architectures tailored to time series are often built upon sequential modules,
015            such as convolutional, commonly employed in text or vision. Unfortunately, the
016
               potential of standard layers in capturing invariant properties of time series remains
               relatively underexplored. For instance, convolutional layers often fail to capture
017
               underlying patterns in time series inputs that encompass strong deformations, such
018
               as linear trends. However, invariances to some deformations may be critical for
019            solving complex time series tasks, such as classification, while guaranteeing good
020            generalization properties. To address these challenges, we mathematically for-
021            mulate and technically design efficient invariant convolutions for specific group
022            actions applicable to the case of time series. We construct these convolutions by
023            considering two sets of deformations commonly observed in time series, including
024            (i) offset shift and scaling and (ii) linear trend and scaling. We further combine
025            the proposed invariant convolutions with standard (or variant) convolutions in a
026            single embedding layer of an example architecture, the so-called I NV C ONV N ET
027
               method, and showcase the layer capacity to capture complex invariant time series
               properties. Finally, I NV C ONV N ET is experimentally proven to achieve superior
028
               performance against common baselines in relevant time series tasks, including
029
               classification and anomaly detection.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Time series data lies at the core of important real-world applications, spanning multiple scientific and
035   engineering fields, such as energy, transportation, health monitoring and others. Recently, there has
036   been a growing interest in applying machine learning to time series data, with the aim of facilitating
037   several tasks, including prediction, classification, and clustering. However, time series, contrary to
038   other well-structured categories of data, such as images and text, may include variables of different
039
      modalities (e. g., weather data consisting of temperature and wind speed measurements) as well as
      significant noise levels and distribution shifts.
040
041   Machine learning for time series has gradually moved from classical statistical methods, e. g., au-
042   toregressive models for forecasting (Box et al., 2015) and dictionary-based approaches for classifi-
043   cation (Middlehurst et al., 2019), to neural networks. Notable time series neural architectures are
044   built upon recurrent layers, convolutional layers and more recently transformers. Recurrent neural
045
      networks (RNNs), originally proposed for sequences and exploited in text-related tasks, suffer from
      gradient-vanishing problems, especially when modeling long-range dependencies. Attention-based
046
      modules, incorporated in transformer architectures, offer a computationally efficient alternative to
047
      recurrent architectures, lately achieving state-of-the-art performance in forecasting (Liu et al., 2023;
048   Zhang & Yan, 2022). Nevertheless, it has been shown experimentally that attention modules suffer
049   from optimization issues, e. g., training instability under noisy inputs, which is prominent for the
050   data scarcity of time series. On the other hand, convolutional neural networks (CNNs) have been
051   traditionally applied to extract features from various data sources, including time series. Convolu-
052   tional layers are also often incorporated in recurrent and attention-based models as initial feature
053   extractors, enhancing the expressiveness of representations for a given task. Indeed, CNNs have
      been proven consistently successful in tasks including time series classification and clustering (Is-

072   To address the overfitting issues of standard neural architectures, e. g., transformers (Liu et al., 2020),
073
      when dealing with raw time series, significant efforts are focused on the introduction of general-
      purpose pre-training tasks (Zhang et al., 2024) that incorporate structure in the learning process to
074
      overcome data scarcity. For instance, self-supervised contrastive learning for time series, considers
075
      transformations of the input to introduce augmentations, and treat them as invariant, via customiz-
076   able losses (Franceschi et al., 2019; Eldele et al., 2021). Yet, the selection of views within the time
077   series collection to contrast, as well as the types of transformations to consider (e. g., scaling, shift-
078   ing) are often arguable within the research community (Yue et al., 2022). Notably, soft invariances in
079   place of hard ones, are shown to be experimentally more suitable for capturing complex time series’
080   properties (Lee et al., 2023). However, we need to emphasize here that contrastive learning consti-
081   tutes an implicit way of introducing invariances into learning rather than via an explicit architectural
082   design choice.
083   Studying the design of neural layers to be invariant or equivariant to action groups is a notable field
084   in deep learning (Gens & Domingos, 2014; Kondor & Trivedi, 2018). For instance, translation in
085   CNNs and permutation in graph neural networks (GNNs) are two extensively explored properties
086   in network design, among others (Bietti & Mairal, 2019; Horie et al., 2020). Relevant applications
087   range across sets, images, point clouds and graphs (Zaheer et al., 2017; Keriven & Peyré, 2019).
088   Nevertheless, incorporating invariances inside neural layers has not been mathematically formulated
089   and experimentally tested particularly for the case of time series. Indeed, time series representations
090
      that are invariant to specific deformations could be advantageous. For instance, in Figure 1, we
      showcase how a standard convolution with a fixed kernel, selected to match a specific segment
091
      (heartbeat) of an ECG series, fails to capture the underlying patterns. On the contrary, a convolution
092
      that locally removes the linear trend identifies all underlying heartbeats.
093
094   In this work, we aim to mathematically formulate and design convolutional layers that are invariant
095   to certain group actions, specifically tailored to time series data. The main contributions of our study
096
      and the proposed layers are summarized as follows:
097
             • We provide theoretical definitions of time series deformations in terms of group actions and
098            introduce the notion of invariance under these actions in Section 3. Based on our theoretical
099            framework, we design a locally invariant embedding to some deformations, e. g., scaling,
100            offset shift, and linear trend, extracted via convolutions. We also propose a strategy that
101            restricts, by learning, the convolutional layer to the relevant invariances for a given task.
102          • We experimentally test the performance of the proposed layers, by incorporating them in
103            the so-called I NV C ONV N ET model, for time series classification and anomaly detection
104            in Section 4. We perform comparisons with several state-of-the-art baselines, including
105            CNN-based variants of different design choices, e. g., depth, kernel sizes. Furthermore, we
106            provide extensive ablations on the contribution of the different parts of the proposed model,
107            including variant and invariant ones, to synthetic and real-world datasets under different
               deformations.

      Deep Learning for Time Series. Dominant deep learning frameworks for time series leverage
111
      multi-layer perceptrons (MLPs) (Oreshkin et al., 2019), convolutional networks (CNNs) (Bai et al.,
112
      2018) and recurrent ones (RNNs) (Salinas et al., 2020), as well as transformer-based networks (i. e.,
113   built upon the attention mechanism) (Wen et al., 2022). Convolutional kernels have traditionally
114   dominated feature extraction in time series, from shapelets (Ye & Keogh, 2011) to the recently
115   successful ROCKET (Dempster et al., 2020), that exploits several random kernels. Additionally,
116   convolutional layers of different kernel sizes, often stacked in deep architectures (Ismail Fawaz
117   et al., 2019), with increased receptive fields, such as I NCEPTION T IME (Ismail Fawaz et al., 2020)
118   and R ES N ET (Wang et al., 2017), are prominent for time series classification. Similarly, T IMES -
119   N ET (Wu et al., 2022) model capitalizes on convolutional layers to capture variations of multiple
120   periodicities of 2D transformed multivariate time series, to solve multiple time series tasks. Beyond
121
      standard CNNs for time series, T-WaveNet (Minhao et al., 2021) is a tree-structured wavelet neu-
      ral network that decomposes the input signal into various frequency subbands with similar energies
122
      based on the dominant frequency range. Another recent hierarchical CNN-based model for time se-
123
      ries forecasting, SCIN ET (Liu et al., 2022), repeatedly downsamples and convolves the input to en-
124   able information sharing at several resolutions. Furthermore, leveraging the success of the attention
125   mechanism in text, transformer-based architectures have lately proven successful in capturing tem-
126   poral interactions between multivariate time series inputs, mainly in time series forecasting (Zhou
127   et al., 2021; 2022; Woo et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2021). For instance, AUTO F ORMER (Wu et al., 2021)
128   combines decomposition modules with an auto-correlation in place of self-attention, while C ROSS -
129   F ORMER (Zhang & Yan, 2022) capitalizes on 2D vector array embeddings that preserve temporal
130   and channel information, followed by temporal and channel-wise cross-attention modules. Finally,
131   several recent works, evaluate forecasting architectures also on the anomaly detection (Xu, 2021),
132
      by reformulating the task to point-wise reconstruction, with reconstruction error being the anomaly
      criterion. To overcome the sensitivity of transformers in overfitting, recent simple MLP-based archi-
133
      tectures have showcased superior performance in forecasting, e. g., TSM IXER (Chen et al., 2023),
134
      F RE TS (Yi et al., 2024), with several studies challenging the overall effectiveness of such complex
135   architectures for time series (Zhang et al., 2022; Zeng et al., 2023).
136
137   Invariances for Time Series Modeling. Besides the supervised setting described above, multi-
138
      ple modern methods emphasize self-supervised techniques for extracting representations from time
      series data, before the downstream task. Inspired by other domains (He et al., 2022), masked au-
139
      toencoders trained to reconstruct missing time points across samples form a common choice for
140
      unsupervised pre-training, with primary applications in forecasting (Nie et al., 2022; Dong et al.,
141   2024). In the same category of unsupervised approaches, but focusing on incorporating knowledge
142   about similarities between representations, lies contrastive self-supervised learning. Specifically,
143   invariance is often achieved by applying transformations (e. g., scaling, shifting, or noise injection)
144   to the input and training the model to recognize that these altered versions should map to the same
145   underlying representation (Chen et al., 2020). Typically, CNNs constitute basic blocks for various
146   time series augmentation-based contrastive frameworks, such as TS-TCC (Eldele et al., 2021) and
147   T IME CLR (Yang et al., 2022). Except for building augmented views solely with transformations,
148   samples are also contrasted with some sampled subseries (Franceschi et al., 2019), with adjacent
149
      segments (Tonekaboni et al., 2021), or a combination of both along with transformations (Yue et al.,
      2022). Unlike deep learning, invariances in time series data have long been a central focus in clas-
150
      sical time series data mining approaches (Esling & Agon, 2012). For instance, local time warping
151
      invariance (Ding et al., 2008) can be tackled by dynamic time warping (DTW). Additionally, am-
152   plitude and offset invariances are accomplished by Z-normalizing the data (Paparrizos et al., 2020).
153   Finally, LT-normalized distance (Germain et al., 2024) extends Z-normalized distance by being in-
154   variant to linear trend in addition to amplitude and offset shifts, facilitating similarity search and
155   motif set discovery applications.

      3.1   I NVARIANT EMBEDDING FOR TIME SERIES
163
164   We, next, develop a mathematical framework to create an embedding invariant to a predefined set
165   of deformations. Essentially, the embedding is expected to map a geometrical object or any of its
166   deformed versions to the same representative. In addition, we present some important properties
167   that should verify the embedding and tailor the proposed framework to the case of time series.
168
169   Deformations and group action. From a geometrical viewpoint, the notion of invariance depends
170   on the representation of deformations and the definition of the action of a deformation on a geomet-
171
      rical object. A classical approach consists of representing a deformation as an element of a group
      and its action by a group action:
172
173   Definition 3.1 (Group action). A group G with neutral e acts on the left on a set M, if there exists a
174   map a : G × M 7→ M that verifies:
175                         1) a(e, m) = m,     ∀m ∈ M
176
                            2) a(g, a(h, m)) = a(gh, m),      ∀(g, h) ∈ G2 , ∀m ∈ M.
177
178
      To simplify notation, the left action of g ∈ G on m ∈ M is denoted g · m. For a group G that acts on
179
      the left on a set M, the orbit of m ∈ M is the set of all its deformed versions [m] = {g · m | g ∈ G}.
180   The set of independent orbits, denoted M/G, is called quotient space, and if this set is reduced to a
181   singleton, the action of G on M is said transitive, and it verifies that for any m ∈ M its orbit is the
182   whole set: [m] = M.
183

266    CNNs have been successful in many applications related to time series, essentially becoming a key
267    building block of the latest deep neural networks. Their success comes from their ability to capture
268    local information in long time series. However, CNNs remain sensitive to some deformations like
269    amplitude scaling or offset shifts (Mallat, 2016). In this section, we propose a novel convolution
       that is invariant to rigid deformations at a local scale while remaining computationally efficient.

515   Table 5 displays anomaly detection scores in terms of F1-score (%) for the proposed model and the
516   baselines on the 5 anomaly detection datasets. We observe that the proposed I NV C ONV N ET model
517   is the best-performing method on SWaT dataset, whereas overall, is the third in terms of average
518
      performance for all datasets, slightly surpassed by the CNN-based T IMES N ET model. This can be
      attributed to the refinement of the CNN blocks in T IMES N ET to account for multiple periodicities,
519
      allowing finer granularities for reconstruction. Similarly, TSLAN ET is the best-performing model
520
      in terms of average F1, and it leverages Fourier blocks prior to CNNs modules to capture features of
521   short and long dependencies simultaneously. Other types of architectures like the purely MLP-based
522   framework DL INEAR, and the transformer-based F ED F ORMER also show competitive performances
523   for several datasets. Interestingly, I NV C ONV N ET achieves strong performance across all datasets
524   while using a single layer of invariant convolutions with kernels of different sizes. This demon-
525   strates that leveraging invariances across multiple scales can be highly effective even in shallow
526   architectures for reconstruction-based anomaly detection. Finally, we provide in the Appendix A.5
527   performance comparisons between I NV C ONV N ET and the simple CNN-based embedding modules
528   of I NCEPTION, R ES N ET, CNN, stressing the importance of including invariances and their related
529
      coefficients for the reconstruction task.

531   5          C ONCLUSION
532
533   In this paper, we mathematically formulate the crucial modeling aspect of invariances when dealing
534   with time series data. We leverage this formulation to design invariant convolutions that we carefully
535   combined with conventional convolutions in a single network layer, and we propose an example ar-
536   chitecture, i. e., the I NV C ONV N ET model. Experimental results show that our proposed invariant
537   embedding modules benefit from competitive performance in different tasks, while remaining com-
538
      putationally attractive and shallow. Incorporating our modules in general-purpose architectures that
      leverage layers of different types, e. g., attention, and unsupervised pre-training, e. g., via masking,
539
      enabling additional applications, remains on our agenda for future work.

      A.1     I NVARIANT EMBEDDING
759
760   Let M be a Hilbert space and H a finite dimensional vector subspace of M. We focus on the action
761   H on M by the usual vector addition: (h, m) ∈ H × M 7→ m + h ∈ M. The following proposition
762   exhibits a H-invariant embedding that is also orbit-injective.
763   Proposition 3. Let PH be the orthogonal projector on H, and Id be the identity map on M, the
764   embedding, L = Id − PH (the projector on H⊥ ) is H-invariant and orbit-injective.
765
766   Proof. Existence of L: As H is a finite dimension vector space, it is a closed and convex subset of
767   the Hilbert space M; the orthogonal projector on H, denoted PH , exists. Therefore, L : m ∈ M 7→
768   m − PH (m) ∈ H is well defined.
769
      H-invariance of L: Since H is closed, M = H ⊕ H⊥ , and for any x ∈ M, we decompose m =
770   mH + fH⊥ . Thus, for any m ∈ M, and h ∈ H:
771
772              L(m + h)      = m + h − PH (m + h)
773                            = m + h − PH (mH⊥ + mH + h)
774
                               = m + h − (mH + h) (projector on a closed vectorial subspace)
                               = m − mH
775
                               = L(m)
776
777   which proves the H-invariance of L.
778
      Orbit-injectivity of L: For any m ∈ M, its orbits corresponds to:
779
780                           [m]   = {m + h | h ∈ H}
781                                 = {L(m) + h′ | h ∈ H, h′ = PH (m) + h ∈ M}
782                                 = L(m) + H
783   Therefore, for any ([m], [m′ ]) ∈ M/H × M/H, such that [m] ∩ [m′ ] = ∅ implies that L(m) ̸= L(m′ )
784   proving the orbit-injectivity of L.

      I. Classification. The embedding is passed by a Global Average Pooling (GAP) layer, applied to
865   the channel dimension, that averages over the time dimension to reduce the temporal features to a

869
      used to capture dependencies at various scales. For the reconstruction of the input, the embedding is
870
      followed by linear layers applied first on the temporal and then on the channel dimensions. The co-
871   efficients of the invariant kernels, i. e., (σf , µf ) for the offset-invariant part and (νf , αf , βf ) for the
872   trend-invariant part, are passed through linear layers to be mapped to the original time and channel
873   dimensions and are then combined with each part (one variant and two invariant) of the embed-
874   ding via addition and multiplication. More specifically, σf and νf refer to the norm of invariant
875   embeddings of the signal, µf refers to the means, αf and βf refers to the coefficients of the linear
876   trend. Combining the coefficients with the linearly projected embeddings adapts the level of the
877   series to the original one, enabling enhanced temporal resolution. Figure 5 presents details about the

956   the data source. On the contrary, all data are normalized for classification and the baselines, as well
957   as for anomaly detection and all considered models (including the proposed I NV C ONV N ET). For
958   the UEA datasets, we do validation on the whole training set since the test sets are, in several cases,
959   quite large, and thus, a small subset of the train set picked for validation can be a misleading indica-
960   tor of performance. For the rest classification datasets, we perform a split into train/validation/test
961
      sets with a 60 : 20 : 20 ratio, following (Eldele et al., 2021). Similarly, for the five anomaly detection
      datasets, we split into train/validation/test sets with a 70 : 10 : 20 ratio (Xu, 2021).

      All experiments presented in this study were conducted on an Nvidia Tesla V100 GPU, with 40
966   cores and 756 GB of memory. We utilized the Adam optimizer with a learning rate of lr = 0.001
967   for both classification and unsupervised anomaly detection tasks. We also adopted a linear cosine
968   annealing learning rate scheduler for I NV C ONV N ET in classification. More specifically, the sched-
969   uler started the warmup phase with a learning rate equal to 0.001, linearly increasing the learning
970   rate over the first 10 epochs to 0.01. After the warmup, it gradually reduced the learning rate using
971   a cosine annealing schedule, down to 0.0001 by the end of training. For anomaly detection and the
      rest methods, we utilized a learning rate scheduler of 0.5 decrease rate per epoch. To have better esti-

       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


972
       mates for the generalization performance of all models and, most importantly, our proposed shallow
973    modules, we performed 3 runs with random seeds for all considered datasets and tasks. Additional
974    details for each task and the hyperparameters of the models are given below.
975
976
       - Classification Task: We trained the models for 100 epochs for all UEA datasets, the 4 UCR
       datasets and the Fault-Diagnosis dataset. We performed early stopping during training, after 20
977
       epochs of no improvement in the validation accuracy for all models and kept the configuration of
978
       weights that correspond to the best validation accuracy during training. The standard cross entropy
979    loss was optimized during training for classification. For the I NV C ONVNET model, we considered
980    the inception-like embedding module of Figure 4 (Left) for all datasets of UEA except for Epilepsy,
981    EthanolConcentration, Heartbeat that we selected the standard embedding module of single kernel
982    size. Finally, for FingerMovements, Handwriting, Libras and SelfRegulationSCP1 datasets, we
983    employed the multi-scale embedding layer of Figure 4 (Right). The type of the embedding layer,
984    as well as the hyperparameters for the convolutional layers, e. g., kernel size and hidden dimension,
985    were selected through random search and the best performance on the validation set. For the rest of
986    the classification datasets, i. e., the UCIHAR, the Sleep-EDF and the Epilepsy datasets, we trained
987
       all models for 300 epochs with 20 epochs patience and considered the inception-like embedding
       layer, since it was performing better on the validation set.
988
989    - Unsupervised Anomaly Detection Task: We trained the models for 10 epochs and stopped train-
990    ing if no improvements had been made in terms of validation loss for 3 epochs, saving the best
991    model weights on the validation set. We optimized the models using the mean squared error (MSE)
992    between the real input sequences and the reconstructed ones. For all five anomaly detection datasets,
993
       we used the multi-scale embedding layer of Figure 4 (Right), followed by the reconstruction module
       built upon linear layers in Figure 5.
994
995    - Hyperparameter Selection: We next provide more information about the selection of the kernel
996    sizes and hidden dimensions for the different embedding modules tested in terms of I NV C ONV N ET.
997    For the standard pool of convolutions with one specific kernel size W , we chose the kernel size as
998    the minimum value between the value 50 and half of the length of the time series. For the inception-
999
       like embedding module, we selected several kernel sizes, such as 51,75,101, and 125, or factors of
       those values for which the length of the series is proportional. Finally, for the first layer (pool of
1000
       convolutions) of the multi-scale module, we computed the kernel sizes as powers of two, starting
1001
       from 16 up to a maximum of 128, based on the logarithmic scaling of half the series length. For all
1002   modules, we tested hidden dimensions sizes for the pool of convolutions in {32, 64, 128, 256} doing
1003   a split that enabled almost equal contribution for the three parts, i. e., normal, invariant to offset
1004   shift and scaling, and invariant to linear trend and scaling. For instance for total hidden size equal
1005   to 32 the different parts had (12, 10, 10) hidden dimensions respectively, for 64 the split became
1006   (24, 20, 20) and so on.
1007
       For the common CNN-based baselines I NCEPTION, R ES N ET, CNN, we tuned the number of con-
1008   volutional layers, the kernel sizes, and the hidden size of each layer. We followed a random search
1009   for a value between 2 and 6 for the number of blocks and {32, 64, 128, 256} for the hidden dimen-
1010   sions, whereas for the kernel sizes, we used those proposed in the relevant papers (Ismail Fawaz
1011   et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2017; Ismail Fawaz et al., 2018). All baselines’ implementations are de-
1012   rived from the Time-Series-Library (Wang et al., 2024), with the configurations mentioned in the
1013   respective papers, and the main code resources for performing the different tasks, e. g., classification
1014   and anomaly detection were adopted. We also used ROCKET (Dempster et al., 2020) from sktime
1015   Library (Löning et al., 2019), with 3000 random convolutional kernels. Finally, for the transfer
1016
       learning classification experiment, the self-supervised contrastive TS-TCC and TS2 VEC methods
       were trained with their default parameters for classification as proposed in the respective papers
1017
       (Eldele et al., 2021; Yue et al., 2022), for 50 epochs for each phase of pre-training and fine-tuning.
