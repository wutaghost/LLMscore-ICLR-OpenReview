009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Dataset Distillation (DD) is an emerging technique that compresses large-scale
012            datasets into significantly smaller synthesized datasets while preserving high test
013            performance and enabling the efficient training of large models. However, current
014
               research primarily focuses on enhancing evaluation accuracy under limited com-
               pression ratios, often overlooking critical security concerns such as adversarial
015
               robustness. A key challenge in evaluating this robustness lies in the complex
016
               interactions between distillation methods, model architectures, and adversarial
017            attack strategies, which complicate standardized assessments. To address this,
018            we introduce BEARD, an open and unified benchmark designed to systematically
019            assess the adversarial robustness of DD methods, including DM, IDM, and BA-
020            CON. BEARD encompasses a variety of adversarial attacks (e.g., FGSM, PGD,
021            C&W) on distilled datasets like CIFAR-10/100 and TinyImageNet. Utilizing an
022            adversarial game framework, it introduces three key metrics: Robustness Ratio
023            (RR), Attack Efficiency Ratio (AE), and Comprehensive Robustness-Efficiency
024
               Index (CREI). Our analysis includes unified benchmarks, various Images Per Class
               (IPC) settings, and the effects of adversarial training. Results are available on the
025
               BEARD Leaderboard, along with a library providing model and dataset pools to
026
               support reproducible research. Access the code at BEARD.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
030
      Deep Neural Networks (DNNs) (LeCun et al., 2015) have achieved significant success across various
031
      applications, primarily due to the availability of large datasets (Krizhevsky et al., 2012; Vaswani et al.,
032   2017; Radford et al., 2021; Kirillov et al., 2023). These extensive datasets enable DNNs to learn
033   valuable representations tailored to specific tasks. However, the acquisition of such large datasets and
034   the training of DNNs can be prohibitively expensive.
035
      Dataset Distillation (DD), an emerging technique that compresses large datasets into smaller sets
036
      of synthetic samples (Wang et al., 2018; Zhao et al., 2020; Cazenavette et al., 2022; Nguyen et al.,
037   2020; Cui et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2024a), offers a cost-effective alternative by reducing training
038   demands and simplifying dataset acquisition. DD has a profound impact on both research and
039   practical applications, facilitating the efficient handling and processing of vast amounts of data across
040   various fields. Significant progress in DD has been driven by advanced algorithms, which can be
041   categorized into Meta-Model Matching (e.g., DD (Wang et al., 2018), KIP (Nguyen et al., 2020;
042   2021), RFAD (Loo et al., 2022), and FRePo (Zhou et al., 2022a)), Gradient Matching (e.g., DC (Zhao
043   et al., 2020), MTT (Cazenavette et al., 2022), TESLA (Cui et al., 2023), and FTD (Du et al., 2023)),
044   and Distribution Matching (e.g., DM (Zhao & Bilen, 2021), CAFE (Wang et al., 2022), IDM (Zhao
045
      et al., 2023), and BACON (Zhou et al., 2024a)).
046   Despite these advancements, DNNs remain highly susceptible to adversarial attacks. These attacks
047   involve perturbations that are imperceptible to the human eye but can effectively deceive classifiers
048   when added to clean images (i.e., adversarial examples) (Szegedy et al., 2014; Goodfellow et al.,
049   2015a; Madry et al., 2018a; Zhou et al., 2022b), as illustrated in Figure 1. Such vulnerabilities pose
050
      significant security risks in applications like face recognition (Wei et al., 2022a;b), object detection
      (Zhou et al., 2024b; Hu et al., 2021), and autonomous driving (Wang et al., 2021; Yuan et al., 2023),
051
      thereby undermining the reliability of DNNs.
052

      2     R ELATED W ORK

      2.1   DATASET D ISTILLATION
111
112   Dataset Distillation (DD) synthesizes a compact set of images that preserves the key information
113   from the original dataset. Wang et al. (2018) pioneered a bi-level optimization approach that models
114   network parameters based on synthetic data. However, this bi-level optimization incurs additional
115   computational costs due to its nested structure. To address these costs, Zhao et al. (2020) introduced
116   Dataset Condensation (DC), a gradient matching method that improves performance by aligning
117   the informative gradients from the original datasets with those from the synthetic datasets at each
118   iteration. An enhanced version of this approach, known as DSA (Zhao & Bilen, 2023), further refines
119   the process. Cazenavette et al. (2022) proposed mimicking the long-range training dynamics of real
      data by aligning learning trajectories, a method referred to as MTT. Additionally, Zhao & Bilen (2021)
120
      developed a distribution matching method called DM, which uses the Maximum Mean Discrepancy
121
      (MMD) metric. Building on DM, Zhao et al. (2023) introduced Improved Distribution Matching
122   (IDM), a more efficient method that significantly enhances distillation performance. Zhou et al.
123   (2024a) incorporated the Bayesian framework into DD tasks, providing robust theoretical support,
124   further advancing distillation outcomes. Furthermore, Cui et al. (2022) introduced DC-Bench, the
125   first benchmark for DD. Other research directions include SRe2L (Yin et al., 2024), RDED (Sun
126   et al., 2024), multi-size dataset distillation (He et al., 2024), and lossless distillation through matching
127   trajectories (Guo et al., 2024).
128
129   2.2   A DVERSARIAL DATASET D ISTILLATION
130
131   Adversarial Robust Distillation (ARD) was introduced by Goldblum et al. (2020), showing that
132   robustness can be transferred from teacher to student in knowledge distillation. Building on robust
133
      features (Ilyas et al., 2019), Wu et al. (2022) proposed creating robust datasets, ensuring classifiers
      trained on them exhibit inherent adversarial robustness. Subsequently, Ma et al. (2023) explored the
134
      efficiency and reliability of DD tasks with TrustDD, while Chen et al. (2023) provided a security-
135
      focused analysis of DD, highlighting associated risks. Additionally, Xue et al. (2024) investigated
136   methods to embed adversarial robustness in distilled datasets, aiming to enhance resilience without
137   sacrificing accuracy. Despite these contributions, the adversarial robustness of DD tasks remains
138   underexplored. Evaluating this robustness is complicated by the intricate interactions between
139   distillation methods, model architectures, and adversarial attacks, hindering standardized assessment.
140   Although Wu et al. (2024) introduced a benchmark for evaluating the adversarial robustness of
141   distilled datasets, they did not provide benchmark code or standardized metrics for resilience against
142   various adversarial attacks.
143   In contrast, we introduce BEARD, an open and unified benchmark specifically designed to systemati-
144   cally evaluate the adversarial robustness of existing DD methods across multiple datasets. Inspired
145   by Dai et al. (2023), we utilize an adversarial game framework to enhance this evaluation and pro-
146   pose three key metrics: Robustness Ratio (RR), Attack Efficiency Ratio (AE), and Comprehensive
147   Robustness-Efficiency Index (CREI). These metrics serve as essential tools for assessing model
148   performance under various adversarial attacks.

247   Remark 3. The adversarial game framework can shift between multi-player and single-player sce-
248

252   aims to minimize the attack success rate, which aligns with maximizing RR, while optimizing AE
253   corresponds to maximizing attack success time. Conversely, the attacker seeks to maximize AE
254   and minimize RR. Analyzing these metrics within the framework allows for a clearer evaluation of
255   dataset distillation robustness, highlighting the model’s resilience against adversarial attacks and the
256   efficiency of those attacks.

357   5.1   ROBUSTNESS E VALUATION U SING P ROPOSED M ETRICS
358
359   The results in Figure 4 demonstrate that models trained on synthetic datasets generated by Dataset
360   Distillation (DD) methods exhibit higher Multi-Adversary Robustness Ratio (RRM) under both
361
      targeted and untargeted adversarial attacks, although they show lower Multi-Adversary Attack
      Efficiency Ratio (AEM) compared to models trained on full-size datasets. Under targeted attacks,
362
      methods such as DSA, DM, and BACON demonstrate superior adversarial robustness, with RRM
363
      values increasing as dataset size expands, as shown in Figures 4 (a), (b), and (c). Conversely,
364   untargeted attacks generally lead to a decline in RRM, but DSA, DM, BACON, and DC continue to
365   perform robustly, as illustrated in Figures 4 (d), (e), and (f). The AEM metric further indicates that
366   higher values correspond to increased time required by adversaries to attack the models, reflecting
367   enhanced robustness. While models trained on full-size datasets tend to exhibit greater attack
368   efficiency, the Comprehensive Robustness-Efficiency Index (CREI) highlights that DD methods,
369   particularly DSA, DM, and BACON, achieve a more balanced performance in terms of both robustness
370   and efficiency under targeted attacks. Despite smaller gains in untargeted attacks, DD methods
371
      consistently improve robustness across different datasets. More details are available in Appendix B.

      5.2   ROBUSTNESS E VALUATION WITH D IVERSE IPC S
373

462   6    O UTLOOK
463
464   Conclusion. A standardized benchmark is crucial for advancing the evaluation of adversarial
465   robustness in Dataset Distillation (DD) methods. We propose BEARD, an open and unified benchmark
466   designed to assess adversarial robustness in DD. This benchmark includes a dataset pool, a model
467
      pool, and novel metrics (RR, AE, and CREI), and features a leaderboard that ranks models based on
      performance across three standard datasets under six adversarial attacks. Currently, the leaderboard
468
      includes 18 models trained on distilled datasets from six DD methods with three IPC settings. We
469
      aim to expand the benchmark by adding more distillation methods and larger datasets, as well as
470   incorporating new and more potent attack types into our evaluation framework.
471
472   Limitations and Future Plans. While our benchmark currently encompasses six representative DD
473   methods and six adversarial attack strategies, it covers most major types of both. Future plans include
474   broadening BEARD to incorporate a wider array of DD methods, more sophisticated adversarial
475   attack techniques, and larger datasets. Although the benchmark is focused primarily on enhancing the
476   adversarial robustness of DD methods for image classification, we also intend to investigate effective
477
      strategies for attacking these methods and extend our evaluation to other modalities, such as text,
      graphs, and audio. This expansion could provide valuable insights into the adversarial robustness of
478
      distilled datasets across various domains.
479
480   Practical Applications and Potential Impact. The BEARD benchmark serves as both a research
481   tool and a practical resource for evaluating the adversarial robustness of DD methods. By offering
482   a standardized framework, BEARD helps users assess the strengths and weaknesses of various
483   methods, thus supporting the development of more resilient data distillation techniques. Additionally,
484   with increasing concerns about data security and privacy, BEARD holds considerable potential for
485   applications in these critical areas, providing valuable insights into the robustness of distilled datasets
      in adversarial environments.

716   A.1.1    DATASETS AND D ISTILLATION M ETHODS
717   Dataset. In our experiments, we use three standard image classification datasets: CIFAR-10
718   (Krizhevsky, 2009), CIFAR-100 (Krizhevsky, 2009), and TinyImageNet (Deng et al., 2009). Each
719   dataset has been selected for its relevance and complexity in the context of dataset distillation and
720   adversarial robustness evaluation.
721
722           • CIFAR-10 (Krizhevsky, 2009) contains 60,000 32 × 32 color images in 10 classes, with
723
                50,000 images for training and 10,000 for testing. The images are preprocessed to normalize
                pixel values to the range [0, 1].
724
725           • CIFAR-100 (Krizhevsky, 2009) Similar to CIFAR-10 but with 100 classes, this dataset
726             contains 60,000 images, divided into 50,000 training and 10,000 testing images. Each image
727
                is resized to 32 × 32 pixels and normalized.
728           • TinyImageNet (Deng et al., 2009) A subset of the large-scale ImageNet dataset, Tiny-
729             ImageNet contains 200 classes with 100,000 training images and 10,000 images each for
730             validation and testing. Images are resized to 64 × 64 pixels and normalized.
731
      Dataset Distillation Methods. Our benchmark evaluates six representative distillation methods:
732
      DC (Zhao et al., 2020), DSA (Zhao & Bilen, 2023), DM (Zhao & Bilen, 2021), MTT (Cazenavette
733   et al., 2022), IDM (Zhao et al., 2023), and BACON (Zhou et al., 2024a). These methods represent a
734   variety of optimization techniques commonly used in recent distillation research, including gradient
735   matching (Zhao et al., 2020; Zhao & Bilen, 2023), distribution matching (Zhao & Bilen, 2021;
736   Zhao et al., 2023), trajectory matching (Cazenavette et al., 2022), and Bayesian optimization-based
737   approaches (Zhou et al., 2024a).
738
739
              • DC (Zhao et al., 2020) formulates dataset distillation as a bi-level optimization problem,
                focusing on matching the gradients of deep neural networks trained on the original dataset
740
                T and the synthetic dataset S.
741
742           • DSA (Zhao & Bilen, 2023) improves distillation by incorporating data augmentation, en-
743
                abling the generation of more informative synthetic images, which enhances the performance
                of models trained with these augmentations.
744
745           • DM (Zhao & Bilen, 2021) offers a straightforward yet impactful method for generating
746
                condensed images by aligning the feature distributions of synthetic images S with those of
                the original training set T across multiple sampled embedding spaces.
747
748           • MTT (Cazenavette et al., 2022) introduces trajectory matching as a distillation technique,
749             condensing large datasets into smaller ones by aligning the training trajectories of models
750
                trained on both the synthetic S and original T datasets.
751           • IDM (Zhao et al., 2023) proposes a novel dataset condensation approach based on distri-
752             bution matching, which proves to be both efficient and promising for dataset distillation
753             tasks.
754           • BACON (Zhou et al., 2024a) employs a Bayesian theoretical framework for dataset distil-
755             lation, casting the problem as one of minimizing a risk function to significantly enhance
                distillation performance.

      Training Details. Neural networks are trained from scratch on each distilled dataset, following a
757   standardized training process across all experiments to ensure fair comparisons:
758
759          • Optimizer: The Adam optimizer is used with default settings, including a learning rate of
760            1e-4 and beta values of 0.9 and 0.999, ensuring stable and efficient optimization.
761          • Epochs: Models are trained for 1,000 epochs to ensure sufficient convergence and allow for
762            the full learning potential of each distilled dataset.
763          • Batch Size: A batch size of 128 is employed to balance computational efficiency with model
764            performance, optimizing resource usage without sacrificing accuracy.
765          • Model Selection: After training, the model with the highest validation accuracy on the
766            original test set is selected and incorporated into the model pool for subsequent adversarial
767            evaluations.
768
769   Adversarial Attack Methods. All attacks are implemented using the Torchattacks library (Kim,
770   2020), which includes a comprehensive set of current adversarial attack methods. To ensure fair
771
      comparisons, we apply consistent parameters across different models. Our attack library encompasses
      a range of methods, including FGSM (Goodfellow et al., 2015b), PGD (Madry et al., 2018b),
772
      C&W (Carlini & Wagner, 2017), DeepFool (Moosavi-Dezfooli et al., 2016), AutoAttack (Croce
773   & Hein, 2020), and others. In the evaluation stage, adversarial perturbations are applied to assess
774   the robustness of distilled datasets generated by various distillation methods. Both targeted and
