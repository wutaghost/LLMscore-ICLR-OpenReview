009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Methods for analyzing representations in neural systems have become a popular
012            tool in both neuroscience and mechanistic interpretability. Having measures to
013            compare how similar activations of neurons are across conditions, architectures,
014            and species, gives us a scalable way of learning how information is transformed
015            within different neural networks. In contrast to this trend, recent investigations
016            have revealed how some metrics can respond to spurious signals and hence give
017            misleading results. To identify the most reliable metric and understand how mea-
018
               sures could be improved, it is going to be important to identify specific test cases
               which can serve as benchmarks. Here we propose that the phenomena of compo-
019
               sitional learning in recurrent neural networks (RNNs) allows us to build a test case
020
               for dynamical representation alignment metrics. By implementing this case, we
021            show it enables us to test whether metrics can identify representations which grad-
022            ually develop throughout learning and probe whether representations identified by
023            metrics are relevant to computations executed by networks. By building both an
024            attractor- and RNN-based test case, we show that the new Dynamical Similarity
025            Analysis (DSA) is more noise robust and identifies behaviorally relevant represen-
026            tations more reliably than prior metrics (Procrustes, CKA). We also show how test
027            cases can be used beyond evaluating metrics to study new architectures. Specifi-
028            cally, results from applying DSA to modern (Mamba) state space models, suggest
029
               that, in contrast to RNNs, these models may not exhibit changes to their recur-
               rent dynamics due to their expressiveness. Overall, by developing test cases, we
030
               show DSA’s exceptional ability to detect compositional dynamical motifs, thereby
031
               enhancing our understanding of how computations unfold in RNNs.

035
036   Both neuroscience and mechanistic interpretability aim to understand how neural networks solve the
037   problems they face (He et al., 2024; Lindsay & Bau, 2023; Vilas et al., 2024). As the architectural
038   complexity of such models increases, there is a growing need to have tools which allow us to com-
039   pare models across a wide array of task conditions and training parameters, at scale. One approach
040   which has become popular is the use of representational alignment metrics which allow to conduct
041   comparative analyses on the activations observed across networks (Sucholutsky et al., 2023) (Fig. 1).
042   In mechanistic interpretability they can be used to compare how information is represented across
043   layers (Raghu et al., 2021) and in neuroscience they allow us to test under which conditions neural
044
      networks work like specific brain regions (e.g. ventral stream of the brain; Kietzmann et al. (2019)).
045   While much of the literature is focused on static representations, meaning the activations observed in
046   networks to non-dynamic stimuli like images, there has been an increasing interest in capturing the
047   similarity of the dynamics of representations, so how representations change over the time course of
048   a problem while the network is computing the correct response (Ju & Bassett, 2020) (Fig. 1). This is
049   important due to the dynamic nature of both neuroscientific data (Vyas et al., 2020) and recurrently
050
      computing network models (Gu & Dao, 2023). Recent metrics like Dynamic Similarity Analysis
      (DSA) (Ostrow et al., 2024) address this challenge.
051
052   While the development of new metrics is exciting, new results have also shown that representational
053   alignment metrics can respond to representations that are not computationally relevant to the system
      (Dujmović et al., 2023). At the same time, the choice of metric can impact results (Soni et al.,

095   2   R ELATED WORK
096
097   A lot of progress has been made on using representational alignment metrics to both compare and in-
098   duce similarities across models and empirical data (Sucholutsky et al., 2023; Williams et al., 2021).
099   With representational metrics gaining popularity, researchers have identified the need to properly
100   compare metrics to understand their shortcomings (Dujmović et al., 2023; Soni et al., 2024). Efforts
101   to propose methods for benchmarking static representational alignment metrics aim to enable in-
102   formed decisions about which metric is most suitable for a given application (Klabunde et al., 2024;
103   Ahlert et al., 2024). Therefore, the goal is not necessarily to find “the best metric” but instead to
104
      highlight in which context to rely on a specific metric, similar to specialized statistical tests.
105   Our work specifically focuses on comparing dynamic representations (as opposed to static repre-
106   sentations; see Fig. 1). While researchers have been interested in using representational alignment
107   metrics for dynamic use cases (Lin et al., 2019; Williams et al., 2018; Kietzmann et al., 2019; Ma-
      heswaranathan et al., 2019; Cloos et al., 2024), they usually relied on extending static metrics to


                                                       2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      also be applied to dynamic use cases. With growing interest in dynamic metrics, we are now seeing
109   the development of new approaches to compare dynamical representations (Redman et al., 2024;
110   Ostrow et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024). Generally speaking, being specialized for dynamical repre-
111   sentations means that metrics can actively capture the momentum of traces (i.e. the speed by which
112   a system moves from point A in state space to point B in state space) in instead of just their shapes
113   (i.e. only capturing whether the system moves from A to B at all). For example, DSA (Ostrow et al.,
114   2024) compares the temporal dynamics of two systems by embedding them in a high-dimensional
115   space and using a modified Procrustes analysis to assess the similarity of their vector fields. Another
116   example is Diffeomorphic Vector Field Alignment (Chen et al., 2024) which evaluates the similarity
117
      by learning a nonlinear coordinate transformation that aligns vector fields, providing a measure of
      functional similarity through orbital equivalence. Although new metrics are being developed and ap-
118
      plied (Eisen et al., 2024; Lipshutz et al., 2024), the challenge of determining when to use a specific
119
      metric remains an open question, especially in the context of dynamic metrics.
120
121   It has been argued that representations observed in neural systems should link to and hence help
122   understand the computations executed by networks (Barack & Krakauer, 2021; Baker et al., 2022).
123
      This is linked to the idea of population codes in neural system and the Hopfieldian view of neural
      processing (Barack & Krakauer, 2021; Averbeck et al., 2006). Understanding how exactly repre-
124
      sentations and different neural codes link to computations has been an active focus of theoretical
125
      and empirical work (Tye et al., 2024; Fusi et al., 2016; Johnston et al., 2020; Dapello et al., 2022;
126   Huber et al., 2023; Zheng et al., 2024). Visualizing the dynamics of representation in Euclidean
127   space served researchers as an important tool to understand how the attractor dynamics observed
128   recurrent systems link to their computations (Dubreuil et al., 2022; Vyas et al., 2020; Mante et al.,
129   2013; Sussillo, 2014; Langdon et al., 2023). Following from this, dynamical representational align-
130   ment metrics ought to be a tool to identify and compare the attractor dynamics that link to the
131   computations of recurrent systems (Baker et al., 2022).
132   In the following we address these two trends in the literature: we extend the idea of benchmarking
133   representational alignment metrics to the dynamic case. By conceiving two test cases we can inves-
134   tigate whether representational metrics respond to the representations of a neural network that link
135   to the network’s computations. We do this by testing whether gradually evolving representations
136   link to developing computational abilities throughout learning. These two isolated test cases could
137   form part of a larger benchmark for dynamic representation alignment metrics in the future.

      3   M ETHODS
141
142   Metrics
143   We use the three metrics: Centered Kernel Alignment (‘CKA’, Kornblith et al. (2019)), Procrustes
144   Transformation (‘Procrustes’, Cloos et al. (2024)) and Dynamical Similarity Analysis (‘DSA’, Os-
145   trow et al. (2024)). CKA and Procrustes were initially conceived for static representations and hence
146   consider the dynamics as static traces and try to either align the two traces through optimal geomet-
147   ric transformation (Procrustes) or measure the similarity between the kernel matrices of the feature
148   spaces (CKA). These metrics have shown promise in being adaptable to dynamic representations
149   Cloos et al. (2024). In contrast, DSA is a metric specifically developed for dynamic representa-
150   tions and considers neuronal traces as dynamic, projecting them into high dimensional linear spaces
151
      where it compares the transition matrix of traces (Fig. 1). We use DSA with the hyperparameter
      number of delays = 33 and delay interval = 6. Appendix 6.1 explains how parameters were set.
152
153   RNN / SSM training and analysis
154
      For every condition, we trained 72 Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) and 64 State Space Models
155   (SSMs) using the Mamba architecture (Gu & Dao, 2023) and the implementation by Torres-Leguet
156   (2024). The hyperparameters for the RNNs and SSMs were chosen based on Driscoll et al. (2024).
157   The training setup is expressed in more detail in Appendix 6.1.
158
      Tasks for RNN-based test case
159
160   We used Neurogym task implementations to train RNNs (Molano-Mazon et al., 2022). Different
161   task versions required networks to compare two stimuli, fixate during presentation and delay periods,
      and choose the higher or lower stimulus based on task-specific rules. Section 6.3 explains how task

177   4.1   M ETRICS ’ ABILITY TO IDENTIFY COMPOSITIONALLY- COMBINED NOISY ATTRACTORS
            DYNAMICS

179
      First, we want to test how well metrics capture attractor dynamics in the presence of noise and when
180
      multiple attractor dynamics are combined compositionally. We see these as core skills needed for
181   metrics to perform in the more complex RNN-base test case introduced later (6.3). To test for these
182   abilities, we simulate attractor dynamics using Lorenz attractors ( = 10; = 2.667).
183
184
      All metrics can identify basic attractor motifs (see Appendix 6.2) but how well can metrics identify
      attractors in the presence of noise? For this, we construct two ‘noise + attractor’ combinations
185
      (‘models’) (Fig. 2a and Fig 8) where ”+” refers to a numerical addition and scaling back to the
186
      unit volume. Model 1 combines one attractor sample (Attractor A) with one sample of Gaussian
187   noise (Noise A). Model 2 combines the same attractor sample (Attractor A) with a different sample
188   of Gaussian noise (Noise 2). We now gradually reduce the noise level across both noise samples in
189   three steps (‘Combinations’), by 50% each time, and compare Model 1 with Model 2 within each set
190   of Combinations. We sample 7 sections of different Lorenz attractors (see Appendix 6.2), and each
191   is used once as ‘Attractor A’. Each attractor is used 200 times with different noise samples. Hence we
192   run a total of 1400 comparisons. Results are depicted in Fig. 2b. As we want metrics to respond to
193   the true underlying dynamic and ignore the noise, we want to see that across epochs, the similarity
194   is a horizontal line, so not changing in response to changing noise levels. This is measured by a
195
      low dissimilarity gap (difference in dissimilarity between epoch 1 and 3). DSA shows the lowest
      response to noise (1.7 ⇥ 10 2 vs 4.5 ⇥ 10 2 [CKA] vs 3.9 ⇥ 10 2 [Procrustes]).
196
197   To identify compositional and computationally-relevant dynamics, metrics not only need to be noise
198   robust but specifically noise robust in the context of compositionally-combined attractor motifs. To
199   test for this ability, we assemble ‘Model 3’ which combines Attractor A with another Attractor B
200   and a new sample of Gaussian noise (Noise 3). For Model 3, we gradually decrease both the noise
201
      and the relative amplitude of Attractor B. We use the same 7 attractor samples as above, so we get
      42 attractor pairings where Attractor A and Attractor B are not the same. Each attractor pairing is
202
      used 200 times with different noise samples. Hence we run a total of 8400 comparisons. How do we
203
      know that a metric responds correctly to the combination of attractors? Given that we use random
204   combinations of attractors and noise, with a linearly decreasing influence of the additional attractor
205   and noise, we would like to see a linear decrease in dissimilarity across epochs as measured by the
206   linearity measure. We assess the linear decrease of dissimilarity by computing the R² of the linear fit
207   of a regression over the dissimilarity predicted by the epoch (Fig. 2c). DSA shows the most linear
208   decrease (0.99 vs 0.96 [CKA] vs. 0.97 [Procrustes]). Note that one might want metrics to behave
209   like a ratio to a test case like this. A measure behaves as a ratio if it has equal intervals between
210   values, and has a meaningful zero point, allowing for meaningful ratios between any measurements.
211   In our case that would mean metrics should decrease linearly and show a value of 0 for full similarity.
212
      Fig. 2b highlights that while DSA shows the least change to noise, it is furthest away from true zero.
      This results in DSA having a lower dissimilarity gap in Fig. 2c. Both Procrustes and CKA get closer
213
      to true zero. This highlights that DSA might need additional normalization to function like a ratio.
214
215   These analyses based on simple and well-controlled attractor dynamics reveal that DSA might have
      a very slight advantage at identifying dynamical motifs in the presence of noise and when they are

480   5     D ISCUSSION
481
482   Representational alignment metrics have become popular for comparing representations in neural
483   systems across architectures and conditions. While they can be a helpful and scalable tool, recent
484   work also has identified potential pitfalls when they react to spurious signals (Dujmović et al., 2023;
485   Soni et al., 2024). Here we introduce two test cases which can serve as a first step towards a
      benchmark for dynamical representation alignment metrics. Using these to compare DSA, CKA, and


                                                         9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      Procrustes reveals that DSA is the only metric that can identify how compositional representations
487   emerge throughout learning and how these are then linked to task behaviours. Building on the
488   combination of our test case and DSA also allows us to form a specific hypothesis about reservoir-
489   like learning in Mamba models confronted with simple tasks.
490
      We put forward two specific test cases, where the simpler attractor-based test case is aimed at fun-
491
      damental skills needed for the more complex test case. These cases naturally do not fully validate
492
      DSA and hence we shy away from calling our work a full benchmark. Instead, we see this as a first
493   step into the direction of identifying a set of well-established empirical finding which can then form
494   a full benchmark for dynamical representation metrics. In this process, the researchers could care-
495   fully choose what they want (or do not want) metrics to capture. We believe this step of gradually
496   collecting test cases will be necessary to generate a better understanding of what metrics can and
497   cannot do. There likely is going to be a trade-off between the complexity of test cases and the pre-
498   cision of predictions that can be made about representations, similar to what we observe in our two
499   cases. We hope that our example can spark a productive back and forth between finding test cases
500   and improving measures that will ultimately increase researchers’ confidence in the metrics. As the
501
      set of test cases increases, we will also increasingly be sure that metrics will generalize to new and
      unseen cases. Until then, the risk of overfitting to our specific cases seems low, as the quantitative
502
      predictions of the first case are already captured by all metrics and the qualitative predictions of the
503
      second case intrinsically do not allow for overfitting, as there is no specific metric to overfit to.
504
505   We also show that combinations of test cases with (partially) validated metrics can become important
506   tools for studying architectural changes. Using our RNN-based test case, we can generate two new
507
      observations: In RNNs we seem to find that the development of representations in networks as a
      function of partial pretraining does not seem to be gradual but instead blocked into relatively discrete
508
      groups of not-pretrained, partially pretrained, and fully-trained. Additionally, through applying our
509
      case to Mamba we observe results which suggest that Mamba learns the simple tasks of our test case
510   in a reservoir-like way, meaning that it does not strongly change its very expressive hidden state
511   dynamics throughout learning and instead seems to use the layers following the hidden state for task
512   related learning. This is unless we restrain the models so that they can only learn with their input
513   weights, which then has a measurable effect in terms of changes to hidden state dynamics. Due to
514   the exploratory character of these analyses, our results still need to be confirmed with more detailed
515   analysis. If confirmed with further analysis, this would gradually increase our trust in these metrics.
516
517   5.1   L IMITATIONS
518
519   While here we focus on DSA, Diffeomorphic vector field alignment (Chen et al., 2024) has been
520   introduced since. We could test whether it also holds up to this test case like DSA. With regards
521   to the RNN-based test case we use a broad stroke measure of ‘accuracy’ to capture the networks’
522
      behavior. While that is enough to differentiate between DSA and Procrustes / CKA, a more nuanced
      view on behavioral strategies might be needed to compare metrics in the future. We also do not
523
      use any empirical data in this work, even though comparison to data is a use case of these metrics
524
      within neuroscience. Lastly, our work does not specifically identify why Procrustes / CKA perform
525   worse than DSA. The attractor-analyses show better noise robustness of DSA, which in turn might
526   help DSA to identify the true dynamics in the RNN-based test case. While plausible, these are
527   speculations and we do not give a detailed analysis of why DSA outperforms other metrics.
528
529   5.2   C ONCLUSIONS
530
531   Our tests identify DSA, out of the metrics we considered, as the most powerful dynamic representa-
532   tion alignment metric, providing specific evidence pointing towards its use when studying dynami-
533   cal representations. Constructing additional test cases for representational metrics will likely help to
534   gradually improve our understanding of these metrics and can help us to generate new hypotheses
535   about how different network architectures learn and function.
