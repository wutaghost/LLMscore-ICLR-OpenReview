011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Forecasting stochastic nonlinear dynamical systems under the influence of
014            conditioning variables is a fundamental challenge repeatedly encountered across
015            the biological and physical sciences. While flow-based models can impressively
016            predict the temporal evolution of probability distributions representing possible
017            outcomes of a specific process, existing frameworks cannot satisfactorily account
018            for the impact of conditioning variables on these dynamics. Amongst several
019            limitations, existing methods require training data with paired conditions and
020
               are developed for discrete conditioning variables. We propose Conditional
               Variable Flow Matching (CVFM), a framework for learning flows transforming
021
               conditional distributions with amortization across continuous conditioning
022
               variables – permitting predictions across the conditional density manifold. This is
023            accomplished through several novel advances. In particular, simultaneous sample
024            conditioned flows over the main and conditioning variables. In addition, motivated
025            by theoretical analysis, a conditional Wasserstein distance combined with a loss
026            reweighting kernel facilitating conditional optimal transport. Collectively, these
027            advances allow for learning system dynamics provided measurement data whose
028            states and conditioning variables are not in correspondence. We demonstrate
029            CVFM on a suite of increasingly challenging problems, including discrete
030            and continuous conditional mapping benchmarks, image-to-image domain
031
               transfer, and modeling the temporal evolution of materials internal structure
               during manufacturing processes. We observe that CVFM results in improved
032
               performance and convergence characteristics over alternative conditional variants.

      Appropriately modeling the time-dependent evolution of distributions is a central goal in multiple
      scientific fields, such as single-cell genomics (Tong et al., 2023a; Bunne et al., 2023a), meteorology
040
      (Fisher et al., 2009), robotics (Ruiz-Balet & Zuazua, 2023; Chen et al., 2021), and materials
041
      science (Kalidindi, 2015; Adams et al., 2013). In each of these fields, forecasting stochastic
042   nonlinear dynamical systems requires a methodology for learning the transformations of time-
043   evolving densities given unpaired observational samples, or observations across time which are
044   not in correspondence. This requirement arises due to practical constraints on data collection in
045   these scientific applications. For example, in both single-cell genomics and materials science,
046   experimental testing to quantify the system’s state is often destructive, precluding measurement of
047   the state across multiple time steps (Tong et al., 2023a; Bunne et al., 2023a; Ghanavati & Naffakh-
048   Moosavy, 2021; ASTM International, 2024).
049   Various approaches to address this challenge have recently been proposed, including diffusion
050   Schrödinger bridges (DSB) (Liu et al., 2022a; Chen et al., 2023; De Bortoli et al., 2021; Bunne
051   et al., 2023a; Tang et al., 2024) alongside extensions of Flow Matching (FM) (Tong et al., 2023b;a).
052   These approaches generalize denoising diffusion probabilistic models (Ho et al., 2020), score
053   matching (Song et al., 2021), and FM (Lipman et al., 2023; Albergo & Vanden-Eijnden, 2023; Liu
      et al., 2022b), to arbitrary source distributions – a necessary relaxation to model the evolutionary

054
      pathways of physical or biological systems, as such natural systems rarely exhibit Gaussian source
055   distributions1 .
056
057   Despite the apparent success of such approaches, their practical utility has been limited, solely
058
      permitting the simulation of evolving unconditional distributions. In modeling the dynamics of
      real systems, the most paramount questions are often variants of, how might an intervention affect
059
      the resulting dynamics? Addressing such questions requires the ability to uncover the behavior
060
      of conditional stochastic dynamical systems, despite our limited capacity to inspect their time-
061   dependent states. In addition to unpaired state measurements, we often must also contend with
062   unpaired conditioning. Apart from the destructive nature of common data collection in the sciences,
063   unpaired conditioning commonly arises due to the prohibitive costs sample acquisition. Design-
064   of-experiments or active learning approaches are frequently employed to mitigate these costs
065   by minimizing the number of experiments – identifying a series of maximally informative test
066   configurations (Lookman et al., 2019; Tran et al., 2020). Although, this diversity ensures that
067   conditioning is purposefully rarely repeated.
068   Extensions modeling the dynamics of conditional distributions are still in their infancy (Ho &
069   Salimans, 2022; Zheng et al., 2023; Bunne et al., 2022; Harsanyi et al., 2024; Bunne et al., 2023b;
070   Isobe et al., 2024). Conditional input convex neural networks (ICNN) (Bunne et al., 2022; Harsanyi
071   et al., 2024; Bunne et al., 2023b) and conditional extensions of flow matching (Isobe et al., 2024;
072   Zheng et al., 2023; Dao et al., 2023), in particular, require datasets with matching conditioning,
073   or the ability to first be able to select y ∈ Y and subsequently sample xt ∼ pt (x|y). This
074   structure degrades in the limit of continuous conditioning variables, where obtaining multimarginal
075
      samples with equivalent conditioning is infeasible; a setting frequently encountered in engineering
      applications, such as in the monitoring and modeling of manufacturing processes. In this application
076
      of stochastic dynamics in materials science, the resulting material structure depends upon process
077
      parameters such as applied temperature or power (Liu et al., 2022c; Schrader & Elshennawy, 2000).
078   Select recent works have explored extensions to enable this continuous conditioning treatment by
079   introducing approximate conditional Wasserstein distances which more severely penalize transport
080   across the conditioning variable (Chemseddine et al., 2024; Kerrigan et al., 2024). Detrimentally,
081   we demonstrate in our work that this approximation in isolation is both insufficient to adequately
082   capture conditional dynamics of for complex mappings and is practically challenging to calibrate
083   due to highly sensitive, problem specific hyperparameter selection.
084   We propose Conditional Variable Flow Matching (CVFM), a general approach for learning
085   the flow between source and target conditional distributions. Importantly, CVFM supports
086   entirely unpaired datasets, wherein neither the sample data nor their corresponding conditioning
087   variables need to be paired. We motivate CVFM’s proposed training algorithm through a theoretical
088   analysis of the stability of flow matching on conditional densities. Here, we observe the need for
089   optimal transport over the conditioning variables. To realize this, core to CVFM is the usage of two
090   conditional flows, a conditional Wasserstein distance, and a condition dependent loss reweighting
091   kernel, generalizing existing simulation-free objectives for continuous normalizing flows (CNF)
092
      (Lipman et al., 2023; Albergo & Vanden-Eijnden, 2023) and stochastic dynamics (Tong et al.,
      2023a; Shi et al., 2023) to the conditional setting. We specifically focus on dynamics wherein
093
      the marginal distribution over the conditioning variable remains constant – a common setting in
094
      applied problems. The algorithm is introduced in the dynamical formulation of optimal transport
095   (OT), augmenting FM and ordinary differential equation (ODE) based transport in defining a
096   straightforward training objective for learning amortized conditional vector fields. Our objective
097   facilitates simulation across the conditional density manifold, leveraging a conditional Wasserstein
098   distance and kernel for enabling conditional OT. Subsequently, we analyze CVFM on several toy
099   problems, demonstrating its superior performance and convergence behavior compared to existing
100   methods. The performance of CVFM is further demonstrated in two more challenging case studies:
101   the dynamics of material microstructure evolution conditioned on various manufacturing processes,
102   and class conditional image-to-image mapping. As a part of these latter case studies, we further
103
      demonstrate the applicability of our method to approximating conditional Shrödinger bridges with
      a score-based stochastic differential equation (SDE) extension to FM (Tong et al., 2023a).

311   Unlike previous flow matching frameworks, the empirical distribution, q(z, w), cannot be arbitrary
312   (Lipman et al., 2023; Tong et al., 2023a). Instead, the distribution must be selected so that movement
313   from q(y0 ) to q(y1 ) follows the coupling π(y0 , y1 ). The theoretical reasons for this restriction are
314   described in Appendix A. Although the construction of q(z|w) is arbitrary, the conditional coupling
315
      π(z|w) provides reduced objective variance and quicker convergence characteristics. Altogether, we
      recommend two changes to stabilize training.

      Conditioning Mismatch: To further ensure that conditional OT is obtained within minibatches, we
341   include a scaling term through a stationary symmetric kernel α(w) in Eq. (13). The kernel dictates
342   the acceptance of conditional vector fields, dependent upon the degree of mismatch in the sampled
343   conditioning variable {y0 , y1 } – controlling the conditional probability flow permissible across y.
344   In settings with discrete conditioning, we let it approach a delta function, prohibiting movement
345   across classes, whereas with continuous conditioning and mild assumptions of continuity across y,
346   providing some degree of relaxation is particularly advantageous. In this work, we restrict ourselves

351   4   E XPERIMENTS
352
353   We empirically evaluate the performance of the proposed CVFM framework on a suite of
354   increasingly demanding problems, investigating its performance in accurately recovering target
355   conditional densities. We first interrogate the performance of CVFM alongside conditional alternate
356   formulations on 2D toy datasets with discrete and continuous conditioning. Subsequently, we
357
      turn towards domain transfer between MNIST and FashionMNIST, demonstrating high-dimensional
      conditional OT. Lastly, we model the dynamics of materials’ microstructures undergoing spinodal
358
      decomposition across varied processing conditions.
359
360   2D Experiments: We first evaluate the capabilities of various methods in approximating dynamic
361   conditional optimal transport and the Shrödinger bridge (SB) problem in a low-dimensional setting.
362   We compare our complete method, CVFM, against Conditional Generative Flow Matching (CGFM)
363
      (Isobe et al., 2024; Zheng et al., 2023; Dao et al., 2023), Triangular Conditional Optimal Transport
      (T-COT-FM) (Kerrigan et al., 2024), as well as two ablated CVFM variants. CGFM in particular
364
      does not support unpaired conditioning, requiring the ability to sample from z ∼ {π(x0 , x1 |yi )}m
                                                                                                        i=0 ,
365
      precluding its ability to address scientific questions as m → ∞. We simply present it as a useful
366   benchmark, representing a lower-bound on the discrete conditioning problems presented. The first
367   of the ablated variants considered removes both minibatch conditional optimal transport and α(w),
368   but retains the interpolated flow in y (see Appendix E). We refer to this variant as Conditional Flow
369   Matching (CFM) as it reduces to the original proposed CFM algorithm given paired conditioning
370   (Lipman et al., 2023; Albergo & Vanden-Eijnden, 2023). The second variant solely excludes α(w)

1044   Theorem 3.3 If vθ (x, y, t) is locally Lipschitz continuous, pt (y|w), pt (x|z) ∈ C ∞ , and LCV F M (θ)

1048   Proof. By the composition theorem, the composition of continuous functions results in a function
1049   which is similarly continuous.
1050   We begin with the empirical distribution q(z, w), which as a finite sum of Dirac delta functions
1051   is itself not inherently continuous, although expectations taken with respect to q(z, w) are.
1052   Also by construction pt (y|w), pt (x|z) are smooth and continuous distributions. LCV F M (θ)
1053   (Eq. (3)) is a continuous function of vθ (x, y, t), α(w), and ut (x|z) by which expectations over
1054   q(z, w), pt (y|w), pt (x|z) maintain this property.
1055   Empirical samples are drawn according to the OT coupling π(z, w), minimizing the ground cost
1056   defined in Eq. (15), and inducing a gradient flow in the Wasserstein sense. Section A.1 previously
1057   established adherence to the continuity equation.

       Therefore, the learned network vθ (x, y, t) results in a continuous vector field across y, generating
1059
       pt (x|y) which respects the underlying geometry of the Wasserstein density space.

1064   B.1   M ICROSTRUCTURE E VOLUTION IN M ATERIALS I NFORMATICS
1065
1066   Phase-Field modeling: Phase-field simulations are commonly applied to model a number of
1067
       manufacturing processes involving evolving interfaces (such as solidification/melting, spinodal
       decomposition, grain growth, recrystallization, and crack propagation (Steinbach, 2009; Miehe
1068
       et al., 2010)). In particular, the Cahn-Hilliard equation is frequently used to describe spinodal
1069
       decomposition, a spontaneous thermodynamic-instability-induced phase separation (Cahn &
1070   Hilliard, 1958); this partial differential equation models a diffusion-driven process with a diffusivity
1071   constant D, driving the evolution of composition variations, c, over characteristic length scales
1072   dictated by a gradient energy coefficient, γ. In our problem the spatially-dependent composition
1073   will take the role of material microstructure.

       2-Point spatial correlations: This work uses 2-point spatial correlations (Torquato, 2002; Kalidindi,
1081   2015; Adams et al., 2013) as descriptive microstructure features. This representation builds on the
1082   idea that the microstructure itself is a stochastic function, where individual observed microstructure
1083   instances are simply samples from the governing stochastic microstructure function (Kröner,
1084   1971; Torquato, 2002). Within this conceptualization, features – such as the 2-point statistics –
1085   are designed to quantify the governing stochastic microstructure function since it is specifically
1086   the governing stochastic microstructure function that dictates the properties and behaviors of a
1087   sampled microstructure. This theoretical treatment accounts for the inherent stochasticity displayed
1088   by material microstructures, and allows for the underlying stochastic function to be linked to
1089
       homogenized properties. It also provides a convenient mechanism to account for underlying
       symmetries (such as translation-equivariance and periodicity).
