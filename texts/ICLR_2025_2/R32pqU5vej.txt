009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            State-of-the-art LiDAR-camera 3D object detectors usually focus on feature fu-
012            sion. However, they neglect the factor of depth while designing the fusion strat-
013            egy. In this work, we for the first time point out that different modalities play
014            different roles as depth varies via statistical analysis and visualization. Based
015            on this finding, we propose a Depth-Aware Hybrid Feature Fusion (DH-Fusion)
016            strategy that guides the weights of point cloud and RGB image modalities by in-
017            troducing depth encoding at both global and local levels. Specifically, the Depth-
018            Aware Global Feature Fusion (DGF) module adaptively adjusts the weights of
019
               image Bird’s-Eye-View (BEV) features in multi-modal global features via depth
               encoding. Furthermore, to compensate for the information lost when transferring
020
               raw features to the BEV space, we propose a Depth-Aware Local Feature Fusion
021
               (DLF) module, which adaptively adjusts the weights of original voxel features
022            and multi-view image features in multi-modal local features via depth encoding.
023            Extensive experiments on the nuScenes and KITTI datasets demonstrate that our
024            DH-Fusion method surpasses previous state-of-the-art methods. Moreover, our
025            DH-Fusion is more robust to various kinds of corruptions, outperforming previ-
026            ous methods on nuScenes-C w.r.t. both NDS and mAP.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   3D object detection has a wide range of applications in the fields of autonomous driving and robotics.
032   A large number of previous works have successfully focused on using a single modality, such as
033   point cloud or images, to design efficient 3D object detectors. However, the performance of these de-
034   tectors reaches a bottleneck due to the limitations of modality characteristics. For instance, the point
035   cloud modality can only provide rich geometric information while lacks detailed semantic informa-
036   tion; the image modality can only provide rich texture information while lacks three-dimensional
037   spatial information. To address the aforementioned issues, we are highly motivated to obtain com-
038   prehensive information that represents objects by designing a LiDAR-camera 3D object detector.
039   In recent years, LiDAR-camera 3D object detection develops rapidly. Some works Liu et al. (2023);
040   Liang et al. (2022); Bai et al. (2022); Cai et al. (2023); Yin et al. (2024) propose effective methods to
041   integrate information from two modalities at the feature level. However, they all overlook an impor-
042   tant factor of depth in their fusion strategies. To understand how point cloud and image information
043   vary with depth, we first conduct statistical and visualization analysis on the nuScenes-mini dataset
044   Caesar et al. (2020), and find that: (1) The number of points representing objects at near range is
045
      relatively large, which allows us to accurately determine the object’s location, size, and category,
      even without the aid of images. As shown in Fig. 1a, there is an average of 163.7 points per object
046
      within 0-10 meters, which is a substantial number. We also visualize a car at 6.8 meters in Fig. 1b ①
047
      and find it encompasses a considerable number of points, well representing the shape. In contrast,
048   some background noise in the image may interfere with detection (Fig. 1b ②). (2) As the depth
049   increases, the number of points representing objects decreases rapidly. As shown in Fig. 1a, the
050   number of points within 30-50 meters falls below one per object, meaning that many objects are
051   even not represented by any points, such as the object at 42.1 meters in Fig. 1b ③. In contrast, the
052   complete objects may still be observed on the image, as in Fig. 1b ④, where the image information
053   becomes more important. To address the above problems, we propose a feature fusion strategy that
      adaptively adjusts the importance of the two modalities based on depth.

      Specifically, we propose a novel method for multi-modal 3D object detection, namely Depth-Aware
      Hybrid Feature Fusion (DH-Fusion). The innovation lies in adaptively adjusting the weights of
074
      features by introducing depth encoding to hybrid feature fusion at both global and local levels. The
075
      fusion strategy consists of two crucial components: Depth-Aware Global Feature Fusion (DGF)
076   module and Depth-Aware Local Feature Fusion (DLF) module. In DGF, we take point cloud Bird’s-
077   Eye-View (BEV) features and image BEV features as inputs, and dynamically adjust the weights of
078   image BEV features based on depth during fusion by utilizing a global-fusion transformer encoder
079   with a depth encoder. To compensate for the information lost when transforming raw features to
080   BEV space, we enhance the fused BEV features at a lower cost by utilizing the original instance
081   features. In DLF, we obtain 3D boxes by utilizing a Region Proposal Network (RPN). Then, the
082   3D boxes are projected into both LiDAR voxel features and multi-view image features to crop out
083   corresponding local instance features with more detailed information. Afterward, we take these
084
      as inputs and dynamically adjust the weights of local multi-view image features and local LiDAR
      voxel features based on depth through the use of a local-fusion transformer encoder with the depth
085
      encoder. In the end, we update local features for each object on the global feature map to enhance
086
      the detailed instance information of multi-modal global features for detection.
087
088   Our contributions are summarized as follows.
089   1. We for the first time point out that depth is an important factor to consider while fusing LiDAR
090   point cloud features and RGB image features for 3D object detection. From our statistical and
091   visualization analysis, we can see that image features play different roles as depth varies.
092
      2. We propose a depth-aware hybrid feature fusion strategy that dynamically adjusts the weights of
093
      features during feature fusion by introducing depth encoding at both global and local levels. The
094   above strategy can obtain high-quality features for detection, fully leveraging the advantages of
095   different modalities at various depths.
096
097
      3. Our method is evaluated on the nuScenes Caesar et al. (2020) dataset, KITTI Geiger et al. (2012)
      dataset, and a more challenging nuScenes-C Dong et al. (2023) dataset, outperforming previous
098
      multi-modal methods and being robust to various kinds of data corruptions.

      2.1   L I DAR- BASED 3D O BJECT D ETECTION
109
110   LiDAR-based 3D object detectors only take the point cloud as input. Based on their different data
111   representations, they can be divided into point-based Yang et al. (2018; 2019); Shi et al. (2019);
112   Shi & Rajkumar (2020); Shi et al. (2020b), voxel-based Yan et al. (2018); Zhou & Tuzel (2018);
113   Lang et al. (2019); Deng et al. (2021); Yin et al. (2021a), and point-voxel-based Shi et al. (2020a;
114   2022); Hu et al. (2022) methods. The feature extraction networks of point-based methods typically
115   extract features directly from the point cloud through a point-based backbone Qi et al. (2017), such
116   as PointRCNN Shi et al. (2019). The voxel-based methods first convert the point cloud into voxels
117   and then extract voxel features through a 3D sparse convolution network Graham et al. (2018), such
118
      as VoxelNet Zhou & Tuzel (2018). Point-voxel-based methods like PV-RCNN Shi et al. (2020a)
      combine the above two methods to extract and fuse point and voxel features. The purpose of these
119
      approaches is to capture the geometric spatial information of the point cloud. However, point cloud
120
      is sparse and incomplete, lacking detailed texture information, which greatly limits the detection
121   performance.
122
123
124   2.2   C AMERA - BASED 3D O BJECT D ETECTION
125
126   Camera-based 3D object detectors only take images as inputs. Depending on the form of inputs,
127   they can be divided into monocular Liu et al. (2020); Li et al. (2019a); Brazil & Liu (2019); Qin
128   et al. (2019); Shi et al. (2021); Wang et al. (2021b), stereo Li et al. (2019b); Chen et al. (2020);
129
      You et al. (2019); Sun et al. (2020); Liu et al. (2021a), and multi-view Wang et al. (2022); Huang
      et al. (2021); Li et al. (2022c); Yang et al. (2023) 3D object detectors. Early works like FCOS3D
130
      Wang et al. (2021b) input a monocular image and utilize 2D object detectors to directly predict
131
      3D bounding boxes, but these approaches have limited capability in capturing spatial information.
132   Subsequently, stereo and multi-view 3D object detectors are proposed to obtain more precise depth
133   information by constructing spatial relationships among multiple images, such as Stereo RCNN Li
134   et al. (2019b) and BEVDet Huang et al. (2021). These methods successfully achieve purely visual
135   3D object detection, but they do not perform as well as LiDAR-based methods, because the spatial
136   depth information provided by images is not as direct and precise as that provided by point cloud.
137
138
139
      2.3   L I DAR-C AMERA 3D O BJECT D ETECTION
140
141
      LiDAR-camera 3D object detectors take point cloud and images as inputs, and can be classified into
      early-fusion-based Vora et al. (2020); Wang et al. (2021a); Xu et al. (2021); Yin et al. (2021b); Wu
142
      et al. (2023), intermediate-fusion-based Liu et al. (2023); Liang et al. (2022); Bai et al. (2022); Cai
143
      et al. (2023); Yin et al. (2024), and late-fusion-based Pang et al. (2020; 2022) 3D object detectors
144   based on the location of multi-modal information fusion Mao et al. (2023).
145
146   Early-fusion-based methods perform at the point level, where the typical approach involves enhanc-
147
      ing the raw point cloud with semantic information extracted from images. PointPainting Vora et al.
      (2020) and FusionPainting Xu et al. (2021) decorate the raw point cloud with semantic scores from
148
      2D semantic segmentation. Similarly, PointAugmenting Wang et al. (2021a) enhances the raw point
149
      cloud using features extracted from a 2D semantic segmentation network. However, early-fusion-
150   based methods are sensitive to alignment errors between the two modalities.
151
152   Intermediate-fusion-based methods perform at the feature level. Transfusion Bai et al. (2022) first
153
      proposes to utilize the transformer for fine-grained fusion from LiDAR BEV features and multi-
      view image features. FUTR3D Chen et al. (2023a) encode each modality using deformable attention
154
      Zhu et al. (2020) in its own coordinate and concatenate them for fusion. BEVFusion Liang et al.
155
      (2022); Liu et al. (2023) projects both point cloud and images to BEV space for BEV feature fusion.
156   SparseFusion Xie et al. (2023) extracts instance-level features from both two modalities separately,
157   and fuse them to perform detection. Similarly, ObjectFusion Cai et al. (2023) utilizes 3D proposals
158   from LiDAR modality to extract instance-level features for fusion. CMT Yan et al. (2023) proposes
159   the simultaneous interaction between the object queries and multi-modal features in the transformer
160   encoder and decoder. LoGoNet Li et al. (2023) and IS-Fusion Yin et al. (2024) propose feature
161   fusion at both the instance level and scene level. The intermediate-fusion-based methods gradually
      become a mainstream approach due to the diversity of fusion strategies.

      4     E XPERIMENTS

326
      In this section, we will first introduce the dataset and evaluation metrics, followed by the implemen-
327   tation details. Then, we will compare our method with the state-of-the-art methods on nuScenes
328   and KITTI datasets, and also present results on a more challenging dataset of nuScenes-C with data
329   corruptions. Finally, we will show the ablation studies and qualitative results. More comparison
330   experiments and ablation studies are provided in Appendix A.3 and A.4.
331
332   4.1   E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
333
334   Datasets and evaluation metrics. We evaluate our proposed DH-Fusion on the nuScenes Caesar
335   et al. (2020), KITTI Geiger et al. (2012) datasets, and a more challenging dataset of nuScenes-C
336   Dong et al. (2023) with data corruptions. The nuScenes dataset provides 700 scene sequences for
337   training, 150 scene sequences for validation, and 150 scene sequences for testing. The KITTI dataset
338
      contains 7481 samples for training and 7518 samples for testing. We split the original training set
      into training and validation sets, following Shi et al. (2020a). The nuScenes-C dataset provides 27
339
      corruptions with 5 severities on the nuScenes validation set, including corruptions at the weather,
340
      sensor, motion, object, and alignment level. For the nuScenes and nuScenes-C datasets, we use
341   the nuScenes detection scores (NDS) and mean Average Precision (mAP) to evaluate our detection
342   results, where NDS is a comprehensive metric that combines object translation, scale, orientation,
343   velocity, and attribute errors. For the KITTI dataset, we use 3D Average Precision (3D AP) with
344   recall 40 positions to evaluate our detection results.
345
      Implementation details. We implement the proposed DH-Fusion with PyTorch Paszke et al.
346
      (2019) under the open-source framework MMDetection3D Contributors (2020). Specifically, on
347   the nuScenes dataset, for the LiDAR branch, we use VoxelNet Zhou & Tuzel (2018) with FPN Yan
348   et al. (2018) as the 3D encoder. The voxel size is set to [0.075m, 0.075m, 0.1m], and the range of
349   point cloud is [-54m, 54m] along the X-axis, [-54m, 54m] along the Y-axis, and [-3m, 5m] along the

      along the Y-axis, and [-3m, 1m] along the Z-axis. For the image branch, we follow Li et al. (2023)

458   4.5     A BLATION S TUDIES
459
460   We conduct ablation studies to first demonstrate the effect of each component of DH-Fusion, then to
461   demonstrate the effect of depth encoding in DGF and DLF, and finally to assess the impact of mul-
462   tiplying depth encoding. All method variants are implemented on the nuScenes validation dataset.
463   Effect of DGF and DLF. To demonstrate the effect of DGF and DLF, we conduct experiments
464   by integrating the components one by one into the baseline, BEVFusion Liu et al. (2023). The
465   results are shown in Tab. 4. We find that our DGF improves the baseline performance by 1.0 pp
466   w.r.t. NDS and 0.9 pp w.r.t. mAP. This demonstrates that dynamically adjusting the weights of
467   the image BEV features during fusion is effective for 3D object detection. Additionally, our DLF
468   improves the baseline performance by 1.3 pp w.r.t. NDS and 0.8 pp w.r.t. mAP, which indicates that
469   dynamically adjusting the weights of the local raw instance features based on depth during fusion
470   effectively compensates for the information loss caused by the transformation of global features into
471
      the BEV feature space. The results of integrating both components show an improvement of 1.9 pp
      w.r.t. NDS and 1.3 pp w.r.t. mAP, well verifying the benefits of dynamically fusing global and local
472
      hybrid features based on depth.
473
474   Effect of depth encoding in DGF and DLF. To evaluate the effectiveness of our depth encoding,
475   we conduct experiments where the depth encoding is removed from the DGF and DLF modules,
476   respectively. The results are shown in Tab. 5. When removing the depth encoding from Base-
477
      line+DGF, the performance drops by 0.6 pp w.r.t. NDS and 0.4 pp w.r.t. mAP. Similarly, when
      removing the depth encoding from Baseline+DLF, the performance also decreases by 1.1 pp w.r.t.
478
      NDS and 0.9 pp w.r.t. mAP. Additionally, when removing the depth encoding of DGF and DLF
479
      from DH-Fusion-base, the performance also decreases by 0.5 pp w.r.t. NDS and 1.2 pp w.r.t. mAP.
480   These results indicate that our depth encoding is effective. Furthermore, we observe that removing
481   the depth encoding from the DLF module results in a larger performance drop, suggesting that depth
482   encoding plays a more crucial role in local feature fusion. We analyze that local feature fusion the-
483   oretically relies on the fine-grained geometric details of objects to enhance feature representations.
484   For instance, objects located at extreme distances often have sparse and incomplete point cloud rep-
485   resentations, which lack sufficient fine-grained information. If the point cloud and image features
      of such an object are fused into the BEV representation with equal weights, this could degrade the

      5     C ONCLUSION
531
532
      In this paper, we for the first time point out that different modalities play different roles as depth
533
      varies via statistical analysis and visualization. Based on this finding, we propose a feature fusion
534   strategy for multi-modal 3D object detection, namely Depth-Aware Hybrid Feature Fusion (DH-
535   Fusion), that dynamically adjusts the weights of features during feature fusion by introducing depth
536   encoding at both global and local levels. Extensive experiments on the nuScenes and KITTI datasets
537   demonstrate that our DH-Fusion method surpasses previous state-of-the-art methods. Moreover, our
538   DH-Fusion is more robust to various kinds of corruptions, outperforming previous methods on the
539   nuScenes-C dataset. We hope our method offers useful insights for feature fusion in the field of
      multi-modal 3D object detection.

      During training, we adopt a one-stage strategy like DAL Huang et al. (2023). For the nuScenes
      dataset, the whole pipeline is trained for a total of 20 epochs with the AdamW optimizer Loshchilov
770
      & Hutter (2017) loading from the pre-trained weights from the ImageNet Deng et al. (2009) clas-
771
      sification task only. Meanwhile, we use CBGS Zhu et al. (2019) to resample the training data, and
772   the one-cycle learning policy with a maximum learning rate of 2.0 × 10−4 . The batch size is set
773   to 2 per GPU on 4 3090 RTX GPUs. For the KITTI dataset, we follow all the configurations of Li
774   et al. (2023) to train the whole model for 80 epochs. We adopt random flipping along both X and
775   Y-axis, the random scaling in [0.95, 1.05], and random rotation in [-π/8, π/8] to augment the LiDAR
776   data, and the random rotation in [-5.4◦ , 5.4◦ ] and random resizing in [-0.06, 0.44] to augment the
777   images. During evaluation, we test a single model without any data augmentation on a single 3090
778   RTX GPU.

780   A.2     M ORE A NALYSIS OF ROBUSTNESS OF DH-F USION
781
      We attribute the robustness of our methods to the combined effects of depth encoding. We analyze
782
      the reason for this using the foggy condition as an example: It is evident that fog has a greater
783
      impact on the image modality. Our depth-aware feature fusion adjusts the fusion weights based
784   on the quality of the features. In such a case, the degradation of image feature quality leads to a
785   reduction in the weight assigned to the image modality during fusion. As a result, the fused features
786   with depth guidance are of higher quality than those without depth guidance. In this way, it reduces
787   the negative impact of corruptions to some extent.
