009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            As LLMs evolve, significant effort is spent on manually crafting prompts. While
012            existing prompt optimization methods automate this process, they rely solely on
013            learning from incorrect samples, leading to a sub-optimal performance. Addi-
014            tionally, an unexplored challenge in the literature is prompts effective for prior
015            models may not perform well on newer versions or different languages. We pro-
016            pose the Learning from Contrastive Prompts (LCP) framework to address these
017            gaps, enhancing both prompt optimization and adaptation. LCP employs con-
018            trastive learning to generate effective prompts by analyzing patterns in good and
019
               bad prompt examples. Our evaluation on the Big-Bench Hard dataset shows that
               LCP has a win rate of over 89% over existing methods in prompt optimization
020
               and demonstrates strong adaptability across different model versions, families,
021
               and languages. LCP offers a systematic approach to prompt engineering, reduc-
022            ing manual effort in deploying LLMs across varied contexts.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027
      The current approach to utilize Large Language Models (LLMs) begins with users providing their
028
      queries. These queries are then augmented with additional instructions, called prompts, by the sys-
029
      tem to enhance response quality. Prompts often include contextual information or instructions that
030   help the model better understand and respond to a query. Prompts may also include guidelines to
031   restrict the LLM from generating harmful or inappropriate content, ensuring safer and more reliable
032   interactions. The process of writing these prompts typically involves trial-and-error. This interme-
033   diate step, known as prompt engineering, is crucial for optimizing the performance of LLMs.
034
      Recent advancements in prompt engineering have introduced various techniques to enhance the
035
      effectiveness of prompts. One notable example is zero-shot chain-of-thought prompting (Kojima
036   et al., 2022), where simply adding the phrase “Let’s think step-by-step” can stimulate the LLMs’
037   reasoning capabilities, encouraging it to think aloud and process the query in a logical sequence.
038   However, this seemingly magical and straightforward phrase is hard to come up with, as current
039   LLMs are very sensitive to phrasing prompts. Semantically similar prompts can lead to significant
040   performance variations (Kojima et al., 2022; Zhou et al., 2023; Salinas & Morstatter, 2024), with
041   minor modifications resulting in a performance drop. This variation requires numerous experiments
042   to find the optimal prompt, resulting in a labor-intensive and time-consuming prompt engineering
043   process.
044   Prior works in literature (Yang et al., 2024; Guo et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024; Zhou et al., 2023;
045   Sun et al., 2023) have addressed these limitations by automatically optimizing prompts. For instance,
046   AutoHint (Sun et al., 2023) proposed learning from wrong samples by using LLMs to generate hints
047   for selected incorrect samples, which are used to refine prompts. However, learning from only
048   incorrect samples can make the prompts too specific to the wrong samples, losing an understanding
049   of what worked. Another approach by OPRO (Yang et al., 2024) involves using LLMs as optimizers,
050
      where the model generates new prompts iteratively based on a ranking list of previous prompts and
      their corresponding scores. However, OPRO lacks the incorporation of feedback from incorrect
051
      samples, potentially limiting its ability to achieve optimal performance.
052
053   While prompt optimization has prior art, an unexplored and significant challenge in prompt en-
      gineering is prompt adaptation. As LLMs are continually updated and more capable LLMs are


                                                       1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      introduced, existing prompts often need to be rewritten and tailored to align with the new model ver-
055   sion or an entirely new model. This constant adaptation is necessary to maintain the effectiveness
056   of the prompts to ensure they produce high-quality results. Additionally, prompt adaptation across
057   various languages is crucial for ensuring performance in multilingual contexts. However, this area
058   remains unexplored in the literature.
059
      To address these gaps, we propose Learning from Contrastive Prompts (LCP), an automatic prompt
060
      optimization and adaptation framework. In particular, our framework consists of two stages: prompt
061   candidate generation and new prompt generation. We inject diverse prompts into prompt optimiza-
062   tion by generating multiple prompt candidates to explore the prompt space. To overcome the short-
063   comings of existing methods, we take an inspiration from the principle of contrastive learning (Chen
064   et al., 2020) by allowing the LLM to contrast between good and bad prompts from the generated
065   prompt candidates while learning to improve on error cases. This helps the LLM reason on the
066   prompts that work versus those that do not, using exploration, to incorporate good prompts without
067   being too specific to the error cases.
068   We demonstrate the effectiveness of our approach for both the scenarios of prompt optimization and
069   prompt adaptation. We evaluate our framework on the Big-Bench Hard dataset (Suzgun et al., 2022),
070   which comprises diverse tasks considered challenging even for human evaluators. Our framework
071   achieves a win rate of over 89% versus OPRO (Yang et al., 2024), AutoHint (Sun et al., 2023),
072   DSPy (Opsahl-Ong et al., 2024), and ProTeGi (Pryzant et al., 2023) on prompt optimization. It
073   especially excels at algorithmic and multi-step arithmetic reasoning tasks.
074   Our prompt adaptation framework leverages feedback from the target model to enhance performance
075   of the source model prompts. It achieves comparable or better results than prompt optimization from
076   scratch on the target model when the target model is a weaker model. Our results show that prompt
077   adaptation is a delicate balance between the target model’s abilities and the source model’s abili-
078   ties. It can slightly degrade performance on tasks where the source model excels, while improving
079   performance on tasks where the target model is stronger. This observation holds true across model
080   versions and families, with our framework creating a balance between the strengths of the source
081
      and target models. Results on the XCOPA dataset further demonstrate our framework’s capability to
      adapt prompts across languages with a better performance on 7 out of 11 languages versus prompt
082
      refinement baselines, especially for low resource languages like Swahilli and Southern Quechua.
083
084   In summary, we present a novel framework using contrastive learning for prompt optimization and
085   an unexplored problem of prompt adaptation. Our results show promising results on both the prompt
086   optimization and prompt adaptation across model versions, families, and languages.

089   2     M ETHODOLOGY
090
091   Our proposed framework is illustrated in Figure 1. Both prompt optimization and prompt adaptation
092   utilize the same framework with minor modifications. The framework is designed to enhance the
093   effectiveness of prompts across various scenarios, including adapting to different model versions,
094   model families, and languages. In this section, we will explain the processes of prompt optimization
095   and prompt adaptation separately, detailing the specific stages and mechanisms involved in each.
096
097   2.1   M OTIVATION
098
099   In line with recent advancements, our work harnesses the reasoning capabilities of LLMs to au-
100   tomatically optimize prompts. However, a significant challenge persists in effectively instructing
101   LLMs to maximize their potential for generating high-quality prompts.
102   Our approach emulates how humans learn: by understanding failures and their reasons, as well as
103   contrasting good and bad examples to grasp what works and what does not. We provide LLMs with
104   error case feedback and a spectrum of prompt quality. We expose them to failures, their reasons, and
105   ask them to contrast between good and bad prompts. This enables learning from diverse perspectives
106   for an improved prompt generation. This unexplored avenue holds significant potential for gaining
107   a comprehensive understanding and unlocking LLMs’ full capabilities in generating high-quality
      prompts.

123   small training set, LCP analyzes the failures, generates multiple prompt candidates derived from

125   the underlying patterns through contrastive prompts to generate a new prompt.
126   The concept of contrasting high-quality prompts with low-quality ones draws parallels to the prin-
127   ciples of contrastive learning, which has gained significant traction across various domains (Chen
128   et al., 2020). Contrastive learning aims to learn meaningful representations by maximizing the sim-
129   ilarity between positive pairs (analogous to high-quality prompts) while minimizing the similarity
130   between negative pairs (analogous to low-quality prompts). By leveraging contrastive learning tech-
131   niques in our context, we can potentially guide LLMs to capture the essential characteristics of
132   effective prompts. In particular, given a list of prompts with their corresponding quality scores, we
133   compare a batch of high-quality prompts to a batch of low-quality prompts, drawing conclusions
134
      about the patterns that characterize effective prompts.

136   2.2     P ROMPT O PTIMIZATION
137
      Prompt optimization improves the performance of prompts starting with a simple initial prompt and
138   iteratively refining it to enhance the performance of the LLM on the tasks at hand. Mathematically,
139   given a dataset X = {xi , yi }Ni=1 , the objective is to optimize the task loss over prompt p:
140                                                             X
141                                    p∗ = min L(X , f ) =         l[f (x, p), y]
142                                                         X
143   where f is the backbone LLM, and l stands for the loss function. Our approach elicits improvements
144   to prompt iteratively (analogous to gradient in traditional optimization) from the loss. We propose
145   our two novel components: Prompt Candidate Generation to generate candidate prompts and New
146   Prompt Generation to generate a final prompt using the insights from the candidate prompts. The
147   pseudo code is deferred to Appendix A.1.

149
      2.2.1    P ROMPT C ANDIDATE G ENERATION
150   Starting from a train dataset and a prompt, we evaluate the prompt using backbone LLM and identify
151   the failure examples. Motivated by AutoHint (Sun et al., 2023), our approach analyzes the failure
152   reasons (gradients) for incorrectly predicted samples (the ones with positive loss) and summarize
153   the reason into prompt feedback (gradient reduction). The reasons and feedback serve as ingredients
154   for new prompt generation (backward propagation). The prompt template in this step is shown in
155   Appendix A.7.
156   Self-consistency for diversity injection. As done in most prompt optimization prior works (Pryzant
157   et al., 2023) Crafting a prompt candidate solely based on the reason for each wrong sample can be
158   problematic as the generated prompt candidate can be biased towards the sample, making it too
159   specific. AutoHint summarizes error feedback to overcome this issue. However, AutoHint uses
160   summaries directly as prompts, making it again heavily dependent on the selected samples. Such
161   direct use of summaries presents a challenge - selecting similar samples could lead to over-fitting
      and trap the optimization in a local minima. We address this in two ways: (1) using a higher


                                                       3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      temperature setting during generation to encourage creativity, and (2) generating multiple prompt
163   candidates (N=10 based on our experiments) to better explore the prompt space. This diversity-
164   aware approach helps avoid overfitting to specific error patterns while maintaining the benefits of
165   learning from failures.
166
167   2.2.2    N EW P ROMPT G ENERATION
168
169   Now that we have N prompt candidates from the previous step, our goal is to generate a new prompt
170
      using them. First, we assign a score to each candidate based on its inference performance on the
      training set. We then rank all the candidates according to their scores. Inspired by contrastive
171
      learning, we instruct the LLM to identify the underlying patterns that distinguish good prompts
172
      from bad prompts. Specifically, we define the top-K prompts as the good prompts and the bottom-
173   K prompts as the bad prompts, and we use the meta-prompt shown in Appendix A.7 to instruct the
174   LLM to generate a new prompt that follows the underlying pattern of good prompts while improving
175   the performance. The generated prompts from previous iterations can also influence the optimization
176   process, leading to a better performance. Therefore, we integrate these prompts into the prompt
177   candidates similar to OPRO (Yang et al., 2024) to ensure that the accumulated knowledge from past
178   iterations contributes to the ongoing optimization process. We add the prompts generated in past
179   along with the newly generated prompt.
180   This approach simplifies the learning process for LLMs compared to OPRO, which directly learns
181   from a ranked list without identifying any error case feedback. The main contribution of our work
182   is with constrastive learning to understand the underlying patterns between good and bad prompts.
183   Additionally, since we integrate prompts generated in previous iterations, the differentiation between
184   good and bad prompts becomes more pronounced over time. We are motivated by human learning
185   process; humans differentiate between what works and what does not to understand a process and
186   reason through it.

188   2.3     P ROMPT A DAPTATION
189
      Prompt adaptation addresses the need to maintain prompt performance when switching backbone
190
      foundation models, such as upgrading model version from Claude 2 to Claude 3, switching model
191   families (e.g., from Claude to LLAMA), or applying the model to different languages. Inspired by
192   the work of model distillation, we adjust the optimization objective to leverage source model:
193                                                  X
194          p∗ = min Ladp (X, fsource , ftarget ) =     {l[ftarget (x, p), y] − l[fsource (x, p), y]}+
195                                                    X

196   Specifically, we exploit reasons and feedback from samples that are correctly predicted by the orig-
197   inal model version but incorrectly predicted by the target model version. On the one hand, this
198   adaptation objective function enables to preserve target model superiority when target model pro-
199   duces better generation. On the other hand, it enables to transfer performance from source to target
200
      by minimizing the output difference when target model performs worse. We present three case
      studies for prompt adaptation.
201
202   Cross-model-version adaptation. In real-world scenarios, underlying models are being continu-
203   ously enhanced with new version roll-outs every few months. For example, the GPT family has
204   evolved to include GPT-4o, and Meta recently released LLAMA-3. Users can choose to switch to a
205   more advanced model for better performance and more capabilities. Conversely, they can revert to
206
      the lower model version considering cost and latency. The goal is to refine the prompt for one model
      version to adapt it effectively to another model version within the same model family.
207
208   Cross-model-family adaptation. One may want to switch to models from other families that are
209   more accessible or have proven to be more effective for their specific tasks. This setting is more
210   challenging because the underlying models are fine-tuned with different data distributions, tasks,
211   and instructions, resulting in significant variations. In this scenario, we use the adaptation objective
212
      and incorporate an error tolerance through wrong format rejection to accommodate less effective
      models like LLAMA. In particular, if the generated prompt does not adhere to the defined output
213
      format, we regenerate it until we reach the maximum allowable number.
214
215   Cross-lingual adaptation. Adapting prompts across different languages presents unique difficul-
      ties due to variations in linguistic structures, vocabularies, and resources. To simplify the process


                                                           4

216
      and provide a universal approach, we extend the same strategy to handle prompt adaptation across
217   languages, demonstrating its broader applicability. In particular, we have the LLM translate samples
218   from the target languages into English and then conduct the inference step. We select data samples
219   that are correctly predicted when translated into English but incorrectly predicted in their original
220   languages.

      3       E XPERIMENTS
224   3.1     S ETUP
225
      Benchmarks. Our evaluation benchmark is a subset of the Big-Bench Hard dataset (Suzgun et al.,
226
      2022), consisting of 17 challenging multi-choice tasks. The tasks are diverse, spanning across var-
227
      ious categories like natural language understanding, the use of world knowledge (both general and
228   factual), multilingual knowledge and reasoning, and algorithmic and multi-step arithmetic reason-
229   ing, making it a comprehensive test for our framework. We report results for each task category
230   based on the keyword taxonomy provided by Big-Bench Hard dataset1 . For the cross-lingual set-
231   ting, we use the XCOPA dataset (Ponti et al., 2020), which demonstrates common sense reasoning
232   ability and requires world knowledge understanding.
233
      Models. For our experiments, we used several state-of-the-art LLMs to evaluate the effectiveness
234   of our framework. These models include: Claude-3-sonnet (Anthropic, 2024) and Claude-3-haiku,
235   LLAMA-3-70b (Dubey et al., 2024). Claude-3-haiku is a smaller and faster model while Claude-3-
236   sonnet is a more powerful model on the leaderboards2 .
237
      Baselines. We compare our approach with four existing methods. AutoHint optimizes prompts
238
      based on wrong samples in two iterations, using hint generation and summarization (Sun et al.,
239
      2023). OPRO optimizes prompts by maintaining a ranking list of historical prompts and relying
240   solely on that (Yang et al., 2024). ProTeGi improves prompts through gradient descent step guided
241   by a beam search and bandit selection procedure ProTeGi (Pryzant et al., 2023). MIPRO focus
242   on optimization of multi-stage LM programs (Opsahl-Ong et al., 2024) which is integrated into
243   DSPy (Khattab et al., 2023). Since these works used LLMs such as the GPT family and the PaLM
244   family, which we don’t have access to, we reimplemented their techniques on our target LLMs for a
245   fair comparison.
246   Prompt selection strategy. Our framework and OPRO both involve optimizing prompts iteratively,
247   which can lead to performance fluctuations even upon convergence. Additionally, each task from
248   the Big-Bench Hard dataset consists of only 250 samples, making it infeasible to create a validation
249   set. This limitation is consistent with real-world scenarios where data availability is often restricted.
250   We simply use the prompt generated in the last iteration and also present the performance of the
251   prompt with the best training set accuracy during the process.
252   Implementation details We use the same data split as OPRO on the Big-Bench Hard dataset, with
253   50 samples for training and 200 samples for testing. For the XCOPA dataset, we use 50 samples from
254   the validation set for training, and test on 500 samples from the original testing set. The temperature
255   is set to 1.0. The maximum number of iterations is set to 50, followed by a selection step. In each
256   random sampling step, we select 3 incorrectly predicted samples and repeat this step 10 times. For
257   contrastive prompts, we select 3 good prompts and 3 bad prompts from the ranking list.

361   source on the tasks where the source model is relatively stronger while increasing the performance
362   compared to source where the target model is relatively stronger. This observation is repeated even in
363   the cross model setting. Hence, our LCP adaptation framework creates a balance between strengths
364   of source and target models.
365
      This observation can be attributed to our framework’s ability to effectively leverage the strengths
366   of the source model and transfer this knowledge to the prompts for target model via feedback. Our
367   approach refines and tailors prompts to align with the nuances of the target model, complementing
368   the target model. This is especially beneficial for scenarios where the tasks need target and source
369   model’s complementary capabilities making our approach a valuable tool that enables them to im-
370   prove response quality even with weaker but specialized models, thereby expanding its applicability
371   to a wider range of scenarios.

      Hard prompt optimization. Hard prompt optimization involves crafting discrete, human-
493   interpretable prompts. Prior works have focused on prompt engineering, iterative refinement, and
494   search-based techniques to improve performance (Guo et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024; Wan et al.,
495   2023; Li et al., 2023a; ). Initial works like APE (Zhou et al., 2023) select the top instructions with
496   the highest accuracies to prompts the LLM to generate semantically similar variants for each se-
497   lected instruction. AutoHint (Sun et al., 2023) generates hints from incorrect samples, summarizes
498   these hints, and uses the summary as the new prompt. OPRO (Yang et al., 2024) generates new
499   prompts by leveraging historical prompts and their corresponding scores, instructing the LLM to
500   create improved versions. ProTeGi (Pryzant et al., 2023) and simialr works ( Yuksekgonul et al.,
501
      2024) mimic gradient based optimization by focusing on errors by asking LLMs to analyze errors
      on a batch of examples, similar to gradient descent methods. In contrast to these methods, DSPy per-
502
      forms an indirect meta-optimization on demonstration selection and prompt construction parameters
503
      like example ordering, formatting, and temperature settings for few-shot learning. A different line
504   of work is by (Manikandan et al., 2023) to use LLMs as weak learners for boosting by prompting
505   with incorrect examples, while we differ by using contrastive learning between good/bad prompts
506   and distilling knowledge into interpretable summaries rather than using raw examples allowing us
507   to handle larger numbers of examples/patterns.
508
      While these works focused on prompt optimization they did not explore prompt adaptability to
509
      various model versions, model families, and languages. Our proposed approach bridges this gap
510   by providing a novel comprehensive framework for prompt optimization and adaptation, ensuring
511   effectiveness across different models and linguistic contexts using contrastive learning.

      5   C ONCLUSION
515
516
      In this paper, we proposed Learning from Contrastive Prompts (LCP), a comprehensive framework
      for prompt optimization and adaptation. Our approach addresses the limitations of existing opti-
517
      mization methods and addresses an unexplored but common problem of adapting prompts across
518
      different model versions, model families, and languages. It involves a systematic process of prompt
519   candidate generation and new prompt generation through contrastive learning, and feedback for
520   prompt adaptation setting to ensure that the prompts remain effective and relevant in diverse sce-
521   narios. We conducted extensive experiments on the Big-Bench Hard dataset, demonstrating that our
522   framework significantly outperforms existing methods.
523
      Our results showed that our approach maintains high performance when adapting prompts across
524
      different model versions complementing the strengths of the source and target models. Additionally,
525   our framework proved robust in cross-lingual scenarios, effectively handling the challenges posed
526   by different linguistic contexts. Our results also show that using a stronger model for prompt opti-
527   mization and adaptation could significantly boost performance on weaker LLMs instead of prompt
528   adaptation from scratch using our framework.
529
      One of the key areas of investigation from our work is exploration and exploiting prompt manifold
530
      in a more systematic way. The current prompt optimization methods including ours are unstable
531   over iterations, and its not clear how to navigate the prompt manifold (see Appendix A.4 for more
532   discussion). Some avenues could include a richer feedback mechanism across iterations, as we only
533   rely on feedback from the prompt generated in the preceding iteration or giving a higher weightage
534   to better hints. Further, letting the LLMs explain the feedback and incorporating that reasoning could
535   also be potentially helpful. Prompt adaptation, which can be thought of through the lens of classical
536   domain adaptation can be helped by a more sophisticated feedback design to get best of both the
537   target model and source model, as we see it currently tries to strike the balance between the two. Our
538   cross-optimizer results also show a promising direction and needs further exploration, especially in
539
      domains like law or medical where weaker but domain specialized model could guide or be guided
      in a collaborative fashion by more powerful general LLMs to generate powerful prompts.

767   A.3   W IN R ATE C ALCULATION
768
      To rigorously compare performance across methods, we follow Liang et al. (2022) and use pairwise
769   win-rate comparisons. The process works as follows. For each task, we perform pairwise compar-
770   isons between every pair of methods. When comparing methods A and B, if method A achieves
771   higher accuracy than B, A receives 1 point and B receives 0. If method B achieves higher accu-
772   racy than A, B receives 1 point and A receives 0. If both methods achieve identical accuracy, each
773   receives 0.5 points
774
      The win rate for each method is then calculated as:
775
                                                        Total Points
776                  Win Rate =
777
                                  (Number of Tasks Number of Compared Methods)
778
      For example, when comparing the three methods (LCP, AutoHint, OPRO) across 17 tasks. Each
779   method is compared against two others for each task. Total possible comparisons for each method
780   = 17 tasks × 2 comparisons = 34. Let’s say, a method wins 25 comparisons and ties in 2, its win rate
781   would be (25 + 1)/34 = 76.5%.
782
      This approach provides a normalized metric for assessing relative performance across multiple meth-
783
      ods, accounting for both the magnitude and frequency of improvements. It’s particularly useful for
784
      our setting where we compare multiple methods across diverse tasks.
