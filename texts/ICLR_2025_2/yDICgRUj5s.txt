009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            The increasing capabilities of Large Language Models (LLMs) have made natural
012            language explanations a promising alternative to traditional feature attribution
013            methods for model interpretability. However, while these explanations may seem
014            plausible, they can fail to reflect the model’s underlying reasoning faithfully. The
015            idea of faithfulness is critical for assessing the alignment between the explanation
016            and the model’s true decision-making mechanisms. Although several faithfulness
017            metrics have been proposed, they lack a unified evaluation framework. To address
018            this limitation, we introduce C AUSAL D IAGNOSTICITY, a new evaluation frame-
019
               work for comparing faithfulness metrics in natural language explanations. Our
               framework extends the idea of diagnosticity to the faithfulness metrics for natural
020
               language explanations by using model editing to generate faithful and unfaithful ex-
021
               planation pairs. We introduce a benchmark consisting of three tasks: fact-checking,
022            analogy, and object counting, and evaluate a diverse set of faithfulness metrics,
023            including post-hoc explanation-based and chain-of-thought (CoT)-based methods.
024            Our results show that while CC-SHAP significantly outperforms other metrics,
025            there is substantial room for improvement. This work lays the foundation for future
026            research in developing more faithful natural language explanations, highlighting
027            the need for improved metrics and more reliable interpretability methods in LLMs.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Recent advancements in Large Language Models (LLMs) have opened up new possibilities in terms
033   of explainability. These models’ evolving capabilities have made natural language explanations
034   preferable over traditional feature attribution methods. Additionally, most LLMs can provide expla-
035   nations for their predictions without much additional cost (Wei et al., 2022). While these natural
036   language-based explanations can be valuable, practitioners must exercise caution before relying on
037   them. Despite appearing plausible, these explanations may not accurately reflect the model’s inner
038   reasoning mechanism, potentially leading practitioners astray (Turpin et al., 2023).
039   The idea of faithfulness aims to assess how accurately explanations reflect the true reasoning mech-
040   anism of the model. While numerous methods have been proposed to measure faithfulness for
041   natural language-based explanations, they are criticized for not adequately considering the model’s
042   inner workings, relying instead on simplistic consistency measures (Parcalabescu & Frank, 2023).
043   Furthermore, while many faithfulness metrics have been developed, currently there are no reliable
044   evaluation frameworks for comparing them. To address this gap in the field, we introduce a new
045
      evaluation framework, C AUSAL D IAGNOSTICITY, along with a new benchmark for comparing
      various faithfulness metrics. Our framework extends the notion of diagnosticity (Chan et al., 2022b),
046
      which measures how often a faithfulness metric favors faithful explanations over unfaithful ones, and
047
      applies it to faithfulness metrics for natural language explanations. We investigate model editing
048   approaches for causally generating faithful and unfaithful explanation pairs and evaluate diagnosticity
049   through three tasks. These tasks include (1) a fact-checking task, (2) an analogy task, and (3) an
050   object counting task. Figure 1 shows an overview of our framework. We evaluate a diverse set of
051   faithfulness metrics, including post-hoc explanation-based and chain-of-thought (CoT)-based met-
052   rics: Counterfactual Edits (Atanasova et al., 2023), Simulatability, metrics based on corrupting CoT
053   explanations (Lanham et al., 2023), and CC-SHAP (Parcalabescu & Frank, 2023). Our evaluation
      shows that while most metrics fail to achieve high diagnosticity scores, CC-SHAP significantly

      2.1   FAITHFULNESS M ETRICS
099
100   In this study, we focus on seven prominent faithfulness metrics: (1) Counterfactual Edits (Atanasova
101   et al., 2023), (2) Simulatability, metrics based on corrupting CoT explanations (Lanham et al., 2023)
102   (including (3) Early Answering, (4) Adding Mistakes, (5) Paraphrasing, and (6) Filler Tokens), and
103   (7) CC-SHAP (Parcalabescu & Frank, 2023). While Simulatability and Counterfactual Edits are
104   designed for post-hoc explanations, the others are tailored for CoT explanations. Notably, CC-SHAP
105   is applicable to both types of explanations.
106
107   Counterfactual Edits Atanasova et al. (2023) propose a new metric based on the rationale that an
      explanation is unfaithful if the model changes its prediction after a counterfactual intervention to the


                                                           2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      input, while the explanation fails to reflect the intervention. A significant limitation of this approach
109   is the need to train a separate neural editor for each model-dataset pair to make such counterfactual
110   interventions. Instead, we follow their random baseline based on the same rationale, where they insert
111   a random adjective before a noun or a random adverb before a verb, as Parcalabescu & Frank (2023)
112   do. In this approach, an explanation is considered unfaithful if the prediction changes after word
113   insertion and the explanation fails to mention the inserted words.
114
      Simulatability Simulatability is based on measuring the predictiveness of explanations regarding
115
      the label (Doshi-Velez & Kim, 2017; Hase & Bansal, 2020; Hase et al., 2020; Wiegreffe et al., 2020;
116
      Chan et al., 2022a). A faithful explanation should convey sufficient information about the model’s
117   reasoning so that a simulator can predict the model’s outputs when provided with the input and
118   explanations. We follow Chan et al. (2022a)’s definition of simulatability as 1S (yi | xi , εi ) − 1S (yi |
119   xi ), where 1S (b | a) is the accuracy of S in predicting b given a.
120
121   Corrupting CoT Lanham et al. (2023) focus on the unfaithfulness of Chain-of-Thought (CoT)
122
      explanations. They propose four types of corruption: (1) Early Answering, which involves truncating
      the CoT to get an early answer; (2) Adding Mistakes, where a helper language model introduces
123
      mistakes into the original CoT, and the original model itself regenerates the remaining part; (3)
124
      Paraphrasing, which involves paraphrasing the original CoT and regenerating the rest; and (4) Filler
125   Tokens, where the original CoT is replaced with ellipses. If a corruption does not change the original
126   prediction, then the explanation is not faithful.
127
128   CC-SHAP Parcalabescu & Frank (2023) measure faithfulness by testing the alignment of input
129
      contributions to prediction and explanation using SHAP (Lundberg & Lee, 2017) importance scores.
      For each example, they first compute importance scores with respect to the prediction for each token
130
      in the input. Then, they compute importance scores with respect to each token in the explanation and
131
      aggregate them. Finally, they measure the convergence of the two distributions of importance scores.
132   Their method is applicable to both post-hoc and Chain-of-Thought (CoT) explanations.
133
134
      2.2   M ODEL E DITING
135
136   In our framework for evaluating faithfulness metrics, we use model editing approaches to generate
137   faithful-unfaithful explanation pairs by modifying specific facts within LLMs. The need for model
138   editing approaches stems from the fact that the knowledge of LLMs can become outdated over time.
139   For example, after a new election, they might present outdated knowledge about the head of a state.
140   An array of model editing methods has been proposed to address this problem in a feasible way,
141   allowing LLMs to stay up-to-date without altering unrelated knowledge (Cohen et al., 2024; Zhang
142
      et al., 2024; Patil et al., 2023; Geva et al., 2023; Gupta et al., 2023; Hartvigsen et al., 2023; Hase
      et al., 2023; Tan et al., 2024; Yu et al., 2023; Zheng et al., 2023; Meng et al., 2022; Mitchell et al.,
143
      2022). Such techniques operate on knowledge triplets consisting of subject s, object o, and relation
144
      r. For instance, they can update (s = Donald Trump, r = is the president of, o = the United States)
145   to (s = Joe Biden, r = is the president of, o = the United States) while keeping other information
146   unchanged. In this study, we explore two model editing methods: (1) MEMIT (Meng et al., 2023),
147   a locate-then-edit approach, which enables successful bulk edits, and (2) In-Context Knowledge
148   Editing, a memory-based alternative,(Zheng et al., 2023).

185   3.2   C AUSAL D IAGNOSTICITY
186
187
      To obtain unfaithful explanations for measuring diagnosticity, Chan et al. (2022b) use random
      feature attribution scores. While random scores can work for structured explanations like feature
188
      attributions – since they still follow the intended format – this approach is not straightforward for
189
      natural language explanations. Random text cannot function as a meaningful explanation and cannot
190   ensure unfaithfulness in a coherent way.
191
192   To address this limitation, we introduce C AUSAL D IAGNOSTICITY, a framework for evaluating
193
      faithfulness metrics through diagnosticity, by generating unfaithful explanations using model editing
      methods. In C AUSAL D IAGNOSTICITY, unfaithful explanations are produced by modifying a
194
      model’s internal knowledge. For example, consider the capitalOf relation with the query “Is
195
      Paris the capital of France?” and a model that correctly associates this question to the knowledge
196   (s = Paris, r = is the capital of, o = France). By altering the model’s internal knowledge, we create
197   two variations where the subject s is replaced with Berlin or London. Both modified models should
198   answer “No” to the original question but for different reasons: “No, because Berlin is the capital
199   of France.” and “No, because London is the capital of France.” In particular, each of these two
200   explanations should be unfaithful to the model that generated the other explanation.
201

203   models. θ̄ generates the explanation ε̄i and θe generates the explanation εei . Then we modify

253   4     TASKS
254
255   For evaluating different faithfulness metrics, we include three controlled tasks in the C AUSAL
256   D IAGNOSTICITY framework: (1) a fact-checking task, (2) an analogy task, and (3) an object counting
257   task. Across all tasks, we aim to test the causal diagnosticity of faithfulness metrics by using
258   counterfactual models and their corresponding faithful and unfaithful explanations. While we expect
259
      the altered models to reason differently, their explanations may not explicitly reference the altered
      aspect. Since our focus is on evaluating faithfulness metrics, we ensure the faithfulness situation of
260
      the explanations by synthetically generating explanations that emphasize the differences between
261
      the models. Figure 2 provides an overview of these tasks, including example inputs, outputs, and
262   explanations.
263
264
      4.1   FACT C HECK TASK
265
266   Task This task focuses on simple fact-checking, where a fact is presented alongside two coun-
267   terfactual answers. For any relation (si , ri , oi ), we present a question that checks its correctness,
268   accompanied by two counterfactuals: (si , ri , ōi ) and (si , ri , oei ). These counterfactuals yield the
269   same answer but are based on different reasoning. For instance, given the knowledge triplet
      (si = ”Rihanna”, ri = ”is”, oi = ”a singer”), the corresponding question would be ”Is Rihanna a


                                                                            5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      singer?” Let the counterfactual objects be ōi = ”researcher” and oei = ”lawyer”. Both counterfactuals
271   would result in the answer ”No,” but for different reasons.
272
273
      Dataset We construct our dataset using the C OUNTER FACT dataset (Meng et al., 2022), which
274
      consists of knowledge triplets. While C OUNTER FACT includes prompts representing knowledge
275   triplets, we use an LLM (Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.2) to convert those statements into yes/no
276   questions. Next, for each object oi , we fetch sibling entities from WikiData to be used as new
277   counterfactuals. Finally, we generate synthetic explanations corresponding to each counterfactual.
278   For example, the corresponding explanation ε̄i would be ”Joe Biden is a researcher, not the president
279   of the United States” for ōi . Further details about the dataset generation process, including prompts,
280   can be found in Appendix A.
281
282   4.2     A NALOGY TASK
283
284   Task This task is based on analogies exploiting the hierarchical structure between two relations
285   where r1 ⊂ r2 holds. For any (si , oi ) and (sj , oj ), there exist (si , r1 , oi ) and (sj , r2 , oj ) such that
286   r1 ⊂ r2 . The task tests the ability to make the analogy si : oi :: sj : oj , or in other words, ”si is to oi
287   as sj is to oj ”. We choose r1 and r2 as rcapitalOf and rcityOf relations, respectively. For instance, we
288
      test ”Paris is to France as Berlin is to Germany.” We corrupt one of the models so that the relation
      rcapitalOf is no longer valid while the relation rcityOf holds. Eventually, the model would make the
289
      analogy by choosing the correct country but through different relations, and thus different reasoning.
290
291
292
      Dataset First, we collect a list of countries and cities1 , then select one capital and one non-capital
      city for each country. We randomly select half of the countries to change their capitals to the non-
293
      capital cities. Then, we randomly sample 1,000 pairs, each with one country having an unchanged
294
      capital and one with a changed capital. Finally, we generate fill-in-the-blank-style multiple-choice
295   questions based on these pairs, such as ”Fill in the blank: Athens is to Greece like Paris is to
296   (A) Tonga (B) France.” For this example, both the rcityOf and rcapitalOf relations provide sufficient
297   reasoning to answer as ”France”. While the corresponding synthetic explanation, εcapitalOf , for the
298   model with unaltered capitals would be ”The capital of France is Paris, as the capital of Greece is
299   Athens.”, the one for the model with altered capitals, εcityOf , would be ”Paris is a city in France, as
300   Athens is a city in Greece.”
301
302   4.3     O BJECT C OUNTING TASKS
303
304   Task Inspired by the object counting task from BIG-bench (bench authors, 2023), we adapt an
305   object counting task for evaluating diagnosticity. The task involves counting entities of a given type
306   from a list of entities. By modifying model knowledge to swap objects across predefined categories,
307   we ensure the number of entities of the target type remains the same while changing the reasoning
308
      behind the answer. For example, when asked how many of ”countertop,” ”grape,” and ”kiwifruit”
      are fruits, the answer is 2, since ”countertop” is a furniture item. If we edit the model to classify
309
      ”countertop” as a fruit and ”grape” as furniture, the answer remains 2 but due to different reasoning.
310
311
312
      Dataset We define five categories with five types each, as shown in Table 2 in Appendix A . For
      each type, we select 10 representative entities from WikiData. We then reserve 20% of the entities for
313
      reassignment to other types within the same category after model editing. We include two question
314
      types: yes/no questions, asking if all or any items in a list belong to a given type, and number
315   questions, asking how many items belong to a specific type.
316
317   For both types, we randomly determine the number of items k (between 3 and 6) and select a target
318
      type. For yes/no questions, we sample k entities, ensuring that after model editing, the number of
      entities of the target type remains unchanged. For number questions, we reassign one entity from the
319
      target type and one from other types to ensure consistency.
320
321   We generate 1,000 samples in total, equally divided between the two question types. Further details
322   about the dataset generation process are included in Appendix A.

532
533
      This study is limited to 7B-parameter models due to the availability of models with published hyperpa-
534   rameters for MEMIT editing and computational constraints. Additionally, C AUSAL D IAGNOSTICITY
535   is heavily relies on the effectiveness of the model editing method. While we conduct an ablation
536   study using the IKE baseline, the utility of model-generated explanations remains largely unexplored.
537   This is because approaches to address issues in model-generated explanations, such as employing
538   memory-based knowledge editing methods or using larger models, come with high computational
539   costs. In particular, memory-based methods lead to lengthy experiments with CC-SHAP due to the
      increased context length.

      B.2   I N -C ONTEXT K NOWLEDGE E DITING (IKE) BASELINE
764
765   Zheng et al. (2023) leverage In-Context Learning for knowledge editing in LLMs without requiring
766   parameter updates. They define three types of in-context demonstrations to enhance generalization
767   (the ability to update knowledge expressed in different textual forms) and specificity (the ability to
768   avoid altering unrelated knowledge when making edits). These demonstrations are: (1) copy for
769   injecting new facts, (2) update for improving generalization, and (3) retain for preventing changes to
770   unrelated knowledge. However, for our IKE experiments, we adopt their simpler PROMPT baseline,
771   where new facts are directly added to the context. We use the same templates shown in Table 3, but
772   prepend each relevant edit just before the query. When measuring faithfulness scores, we exclude the
773
      prefix containing these edits from any operations and keep it fixed.
