009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011               Large Language Models (LLMs) have revolutionized natural language under-
012               standing and generation tasks but suffer from high memory consumption and slow
013               inference times due to their large parameter sizes. Traditional model compression
014               techniques, such as quantization and pruning, mitigate these issues but often re-
015               quire retraining to maintain accuracy, which is computationally expensive. This
016               paper introduces SL I M , a novel approach for compressing LLMs using a one-shot
017               Quantized Sparse Plus Low-rank Approximation. SL I M eliminates the need for
018
                  costly retraining by combining a symmetric quantization method (SL I M-Quant )
                  with a saliency-based low-rank approximation. Our method reduces quantization
019
                  error while leveraging sparse representations compatible with accelerated hard-
020
                  ware architectures. Additionally, we propose a parameter-efficient fine-tuning
021               recipe that significantly reduces overhead compared to conventional quantization-
022               aware training. SL I M achieves up to a 5.4% improvement in model accuracy for
023               sparsity patterns like 2:4, and the fine-tuning step further enhances accuracy by up
024               to 5.8%, demonstrating state-of-the-art performance. This work provides a path-
025               way for efficiently deploying large models in memory-constrained environments
026               without compromising accuracy.1

029   1       I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Large Language Models (LLMs) (Brown et al., 2020; Radford et al., 2019) are transformative for
032   natural language understanding and generation (Suzgun et al., 2022; Zhou et al., 2023); however,
033   their extensive parameter count leads to large memory footprints and longer inference times, making
034
      them expensive to execute. Model compression methods, such as sparsity and quantization, have
      shown promising results in reducing the inference costs of LLMs. However, these methods often
035
      require an expensive retraining procedure on large amounts of data to restore the original model
036
      accuracy (Sanh et al., 2020; Park et al., 2018), while facing numerical and optimization stability
037   challenges when dealing with quantized weights in quantization-aware-training (Gholami et al.,
038   2022).
039
040
      To address these issues, one-shot pruning methods have emerged, eliminating the need for the re-
      training and achieve high accuracy using only a small set of calibration data. Optimal Brain Damage
041
      (OBD) (LeCun et al., 1989) pioneered the use of second-order information of the loss function for
042
      model compression (Singh & Alistarh, 2020; Mozaffari et al., 2023), though at a high computational
043   cost. Subsequent methods like Optimal Brain Surgeon (OBS) (Hassibi et al., 1993) and modern ap-
044   proaches such as SparseGPT (Frantar & Alistarh, 2023) and WANDA (Sun et al., 2023) build on
045   these ideas, introducing computationally feasible alternatives for LLMs. While these methods per-
046   form well with unstructured sparsity, they struggle with semi-structured sparsity patterns like the
047   NVIDIA 2:4 sparsity pattern (Mishra et al., 2021), which are necessary for hardware-accelerated
048   inference.

      While sparsity and quantization individually offer substantial reductions in model size and inference
061   cost, combining these techniques holds even greater potential for compressing large models (Frantar
062   & Alistarh, 2023). However, combining sparsity and quantization often exacerbates the accuracy
063   loss from each method, resulting in a substantial performance gap between compressed and original
064   models. This accuracy gap highlights the need for further innovations in compression techniques.
065   Recent work has aimed to reduce compression error by using learnable low-rank adapters to mini-
066   mize weight reconstruction error (Guo et al., 2023; Nikdan et al., 2024), followed by an expensive
067   fine-tuning step on hundreds of millions of tokens (Dettmers et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023). This pro-
068   longed fine-tuning is necessary because the low-rank adapters do not account for the saliency of the
069
      weights at initialization (Dettmers et al., 2023; Guo et al., 2023).
070   To address these limitations, we propose a saliency-based one-shot low-rank approximation that
071   mitigates the accuracy loss caused by quantization and sparsity. Saliency-based methods require
072   weights to remain static during quantization and pruning, which renders approaches like OPTQ
073   and SparseGPT ineffective. To resolve this, we introduce a novel symmetric weight quantization
074   scheme that not only effectively reduces quantization error but is also compatible with saliency-
075
      based methods. We complement our quantizer and low-rank adapters with an optional light-weight
      fine-tuning recipe that can further boost the accuracy of the models using only 300,000 tokens.
076
077   Our method, SL I M , is a One-shot Quantized Sparse Plus Low-rank Approximation of LLMs. Key
078   contributions of SL I M are:
079
080   • SL I M-Quant: We propose a symmetric weight quantization scheme that minimizes the Frobenius
081     norm of quantization error without altering the weights, making it compatible with saliency-based
082
        pruning and low-rank approximation methods. Unlike group quantization, SL I M-Quant uses a
        single parameter for the entire weight matrix, reducing computational and memory overhead and
083
        simplifying implementation.
084
085   • Saliency-based One-shot Low-rank Adapters: We introduce a one-shot low-rank adapter
086     method that minimizes accuracy loss by reconstructing weights based on their saliency, target-
087     ing weights with the highest impact on model output.
088   • Parameter-Efficient Fine-tuning: We propose a fine-tuning recipe for sparse, quantized models
089     that avoids the complexities of quantization-aware training while drastically reducing fine-tuning
090     time. For example, fine-tuning a 13B parameter model, which typically takes up to 36 days, is
091     reduced to just 14 hours on a single H100 GPU.
092   • Accuracy Gains: SL I M improves model accuracy by up to 5.4% (LLaMA-2-7B) over state-of-
093     the-art pruning and quantization methods (SparseGPT + Group OPTQ) for 2:4 sparsity. With our
094     parameter-efficient fine-tuning, the gap widens to 5.8% (LLaMA-2-13B).

130   Considering Equation 1, Wanda (Sun et al., 2023) proposes a saliency matrix S to evaluate the

132   the average absolute value of each input, ⊙ denotes element-wise multiplication, and |.| represents
133   the absolute value operator. SL I M adopts this same metric to assess the importance of different
134   weights and determine the sparsity patterns in the sparse weight representation.
135   Symmetric Quantization. Symmetric quantization, the simplest form of quantization, computes
136   MQ ∝ round( M                  Q
                        α ), where M is the quantized matrix and α is the quantization parameter. This
137   method effectively accelerates matrix multiplications, as dequantization of the output requires only
138   a scalar multiplication.
139   AbsMax (Jacob et al., 2018), the most common symmetric quantization method, sets α to the maxi-
140   mum absolute value in the matrix. However, AbsMax is sensitive to outliers, which can significantly
141   alter α, thereby reducing quantization precision. For zero-centered bell-curved distributions typical
142   of LLM weights and inputs, AbsMax maps a large portion of weights to zero, resulting in high error
143   values.
144
      Group quantization Alistarh et al. (2017); Gunho et al. (2022) addresses this issue by sharing the
145   quantization parameter among fewer elements in the weight matrix. This approach divides the
146   weight matrix into smaller subgroups, each quantized with its own scaling factor. While this al-
147   lows for more fine-grained representation of weight distributions and better captures local variations
148   in weight magnitudes, it introduces memory overheads and complicates dequantization methods.
149   Specifically, group quantization increases memory usage and computational complexity in exchange
150   for potentially improved quantization accuracy.

      3.3   P OST- COMPRESSION F INE - TUNING
294
295   Fine-tuning models after applying one-shot compression presents significant challenges, primarily
296
      due to the limitations imposed by the integer representation of parameters in quantized weights.
      Quantized weights have limited precision and restricted value ranges, making gradient-based up-
297
      dates difficult and potentially leading to loss of information during fine-tuning. Moreover, the high
298
      parameter count of large language models renders traditional fine-tuning extremely computationally
299   expensive and time-consuming, necessitating more parameter-efficient methods.
300
301   SL I M addresses these issues by introducing fine-tunable parameters in the form of low-rank
302
      adapters. In its optional fine-tuning phase, SL I M freezes the sparse and quantized weights, al-
      lowing only the tuning of these low-rank adapters. This parameter-efficient fine-tuning approach
303
      enables rapid improvement in the compressed model’s accuracy using a short fine-tuning phase over
304
      just thousands of tokens. By focusing the fine-tuning process on a small subset of parameters (the
305   adapters), SL I M significantly reduces the computational requirements while still allowing the model
306   to adapt to new data or tasks. This approach strikes a balance between maintaining the benefits of
307   compression and enabling post-compression adaptation.

310   3.4   T ILED L OW- RANK A DAPTER Q UANTIZATION
311
312   Pruning and quantizing the weights reduces the computation and memory footprint of the mod-
313   els significantly (∼8× reduction in memory size), but adding low-rank adapters in full precision
314   will result in an extra overhead. To reduce the adapter overheads, we compress the adapters using
315   quantization. The distribution of the elements in the factors have long tails, making even advanced
316   methods that don’t use group quantization such as SL I M-Quant impractical. On the other hand,
317   available group quantization methods use 1-dimensional tiles for quantization, which does not match
318   the layout used for tensor cores, hence making them not hardware-friendly.
319   To address these issues, we propose a tiled quantization scheme for the low-rank adapters, in which
320   256 elements of the adapter are quantized using the same quantization parameter, creating 16 × 16
321   tiles. The choice of 16 × 16 blocks is made based on the input size of tensor cores in NVIDIA A100
322   and H100 GPUs, making it the tiling strategy more hardware friendly and allowing the warps in
323   the GPU to dequantize the data in for different tensor cores in parallel using only one quantization
      parameter per tensor core. The quantization of each tile is done using the AbsMax algorithm.

      Fine-tuning Costs. Fine-tuning compressed models can help recover lost accuracy. However, fine-
487   tuning quantized weights presents challenges due to the discrete nature of the weights. The most
488   commonly used approach for addressing these challenges, and one that has shown promising results,
489   is the straight-through estimator (STE) (Bengio et al., 2013). In this method, during the backward
490   pass and optimization step, the weights are treated as continuous, allowing for effective fine-tuning
491   despite the quantization.
492
      In addition to the challenges posed by discrete values during fine-tuning, the high parameter count
493   of the models leads to time-consuming computations and substantial memory costs. In our experi-
494   ments, we measured the time required to fine-tune the models under various conditions. For models
495   with low-rank adapters, the quantized weights are frozen, allowing only the low-rank adapters to be
496   fine-tuned. This approach results in a more parameter-efficient fine-tuning strategy, reducing both
497   memory and computational costs. When no low-rank adapter is employed, the straight-through es-
498   timator (STE) is used for fine-tuning the quantized weights. Table 4 summarizes the fine-tuning
499   results for 300,000 tokens from the C4 dataset, with a batch size of 64 and a sequence length of
500   1024 on a single H100 GPU. The fine-tuning costs for models without low-rank adapters range
501
      from 12 hours for 125M parameter models to over 36 days for 13B parameter models. Due to
      these high costs, we faced challenges completing the fine-tuning with our limited resources. In con-
502
      trast, utilizing low-rank adapters and freezing the sparse quantized weights enables a much more
503
      parameter-efficient fine-tuning method, making it feasible for us to report the accuracy results for
504   these cases in Table 1.

529   5     C ONCLUSION , L IMITATIONS , AND F UTURE W ORK
530
531   In this paper, we introduced SLiM, a one-shot quantized sparse plus low-rank approximation method
532
      for large language models, designed to balance memory efficiency and accuracy. By leveraging sym-
      metric quantization, sparsity, and saliency-based low-rank adapters, SLiM achieves significant re-
533
      ductions in both memory and computational costs while maintaining competitive performance. Our
534
      method demonstrates improved accuracy, particularly for models with structured sparsity patterns
535   like 2:4 sparsity, compared to state-of-the-art approaches.
536
537   SLiM relies on current available libraries all of which lack support for advanced quantization
538
      schemes like 2:4 mixed 8-bit and 4-bit group quantization. Additionally, low-rank adapters, while
      effective, introduce overheads. Future work will focus on developing efficient kernels for 2:4 group
539
      quantization and compressing low-rank adapters to further optimize memory and speed.

890   the hyperparameters aimed at improving fine-tuning speed or accuracy; tuning these parameters is

893   F     C OMPRESSION C OSTS
894
895   An important factor in model compression is the computational cost of the chosen method. In
896   terms of memory usage, all approaches can be adapted to store only a single layer of the model in
897   the GPU’s global memory at a time, allowing them to be compressed on a single GPU. However,
898   the computational costs vary depending on the complexity of the method. Techniques like Wanda,
899   which rely solely on matrix multiplication, are significantly faster than more complex methods like
900
      SparseGPT, which computes the inverse Hessian matrix for each layer. Adding low-rank adapters
      in Wanda-SVD and SL I M requires performing singular value decomposition (SVD) on different
901
      matrices, resulting in a computational complexity similar to that of SparseGPT. Table 9 summarizes
902
      the time required to compress various models using the methods discussed in this paper. Generally,
903   methods incorporating low-rank adapters (SL I M and Wanda-SVD) have higher complexity. How-
904   ever, SparseGPT’s compression time is comparable to methods with low-rank adapters, despite only
905   performing pruning and quantization. Notably, the saliency-based approach in SL I M does not add
906   significant overhead compared to Wanda-SVD.

981    H                            R ANK A NALYSIS
982
983    The key hyperparameter in low-rank approximation is the rank of the adapters. While increasing
984    the rank reduces approximation error, it also leads to higher computational and memory overhead.
985
       Therefore, it is crucial to analyze the trade-off between the accuracy improvements and the overhead
       introduced by the chosen approximation rank.
986
987    Assuming the rank of the low-rank adapter is rd, where r < 1 is a fixed factor and d is the dimension
988    of the weights in a square feed-forward layer, the low-rank adapters are represented as L, RT ∈
989    Rd×rd , resulting in a memory overhead of O(2rd2 ) for storing them. To compute X LR, where
990    X ∈ Rb×d is the input with a batch size of b, the computational complexity is O(2brd2 ). Given that
991
       the original memory and computational complexity of the layer are O(d2 ) and O(bd2 ), respectively,
       the overhead introduced by the low-rank adapters becomes negligible when r ≪ 1.
992
993    Figure H-a shows the average zero-shot accuracy of the OPT-6.7B and LLaMA-2-7B models for
994    various ranks. As expected, increasing the rank leads to improved model accuracy. Based on these
995    results, a rank of r = 0.1 provides a substantial boost in accuracy without introducing significant
996    overhead to inference.
