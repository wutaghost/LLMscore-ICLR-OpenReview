011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Vision language models (VLMs) have demonstrated impressive performance
014            across a wide range of downstream tasks. However, their proficiency in spatial
015
               reasoning remains limited, despite its crucial role in tasks involving navigation and
               interaction with physical environments. Specifically, much of the spatial reasoning
016
               in these tasks occurs in two-dimensional (2D) environments, and our evaluation
017
               reveals that state-of-the-art VLMs frequently generate implausible and incorrect
018            responses to composite spatial reasoning problems, including simple pathfinding
019            tasks that humans can solve effortlessly at a glance. To address this, we explore
020            an effective approach to enhance 2D spatial reasoning within VLMs by train-
021            ing the model on basic spatial capabilities. We begin by disentangling the key
022            components of 2D spatial reasoning: direction comprehension, distance estima-
023            tion, and localization. Our central hypothesis is that mastering these basic spatial
024            capabilities can significantly enhance a model’s performance on composite spa-
025            tial tasks requiring advanced spatial understanding and combinatorial problem-
026
               solving. To investigate this hypothesis, we introduce Sparkle, a framework that
               fine-tunes VLMs on these three basic spatial capabilities by synthetic data gener-
027
               ation and targeted supervision to form an instruction dataset for each capability.
028
               Our experiments demonstrate that VLMs fine-tuned with Sparkle achieve signifi-
029            cant performance gains, not only in the basic tasks themselves but also in general-
030            izing to composite and out-of-distribution spatial reasoning tasks (e.g., improving
031            from 13.5% to 40.0% on the shortest path problem). These findings underscore
032            the effectiveness of mastering basic spatial capabilities in enhancing composite
033            spatial problem-solving, offering insights into systematic strategies for improving
034            VLMs’ spatial reasoning capabilities.

067   visual planes, interpret spatial relationships, and make decisions based on geometric understanding.
068   Such capabilities are fundamental in translating visual input into actionable insights. While more
069   and more VLMs are developed with larger training datasets and extensive benchmarks (Ge et al.,
070   2024; Zhang et al., 2024), the focus on enhancing spatial reasoning has received comparatively less
071   attention, despite its importance to the core capabilities of VLMs.
072
      In this paper, we study VLMs’ spatial reasoning capabilities in a 2D space by investigating three key
073   questions: (1) How well do existing models perform on 2D spatial reasoning? (2) What fundamental
074   tasks affect spatial reasoning capabilities in 2D? (3) Can mastering basic tasks help improve the
075   performance of complex spatial reasoning?
076
      We first analyzed various spatial reasoning tasks presented in existing works (Kamann & Rother,
077
      2020), identifying the capabilities required for these tasks. From this analysis, we identified three
078
      basic capabilities fundamental for spatial reasoning in 2D space: direction comprehension, distance
079   estimation, and localization. A systematic evaluation of the performance of existing open-source
080   and closed-source VLMs on these three basic capabilities reveals that even the most advanced VLMs
081   sometimes struggle with these fundamental tasks. For instance, in a simple 2D direction classifica-
082   tion task, where a model is asked to determine the relative direction (top left, top right, bottom left,
083   bottom right) of one object relative to another on a straightforward diagram with only two objects,
084   the state-of-the-art VLM GPT-4o can achieve only 76.5% accuracy. In contrast, a human should be
085   able to answer these questions correctly without much thought.
086   Most real-world spatial reasoning tasks, such as pathfinding (Lester, 2005; Cui & Shi, 2011), inher-
087   ently require the composition of the basic capabilities identified above. A composite task is often
088   subject to specific constraints that necessitate tailored solutions, unlike improving basic spatial rea-
089   soning capabilities, which can exhibit generalizability. In order to effectively improve the model’s
090   overall spatial reasoning capabilities in 2D space, we raise a conjecture: whether a VLM that mas-
091   ters the three basic capabilities can generalize and perform better on more complex composite spatial
092   tasks. In other words, can a VLM exhibit compositional generalizability (van Zee, 2020) in spatial
093
      reasoning tasks?
094   To test this, we propose Sparkle, as shown in Figure 2, a framework that fine-tunes VLMs on these
095   three basic spatial capabilities by programmatically generating synthetic data and providing super-
096   vision to form an instruction dataset for each capability. Our experimental results show that models
097   trained on Sparkle achieve significant performance gains, not only in the basic tasks themselves (e.g.,
098   improving from 35% to 83% for InternVL2-8B on direction comprehension) but also in generalizing
099
      to composite and out-of-distribution general spatial reasoning tasks (e.g., improving from 13.5% to
      40.0% on the shortest path problem). Additionally, our ablation study confirms the importance of
100
      mastering all three basic spatial reasoning capabilities. To summarize, our contributions are:
101
102   • We show that state-of-the-art VLMs struggle with composite spatial reasoning tasks that humans
103     solve effortlessly.
104
      • We identify three key components of spatial reasoning and construct an instruction-tuning method
105     called Sparkle to improve these three fundamental spatial reasoning capabilities.
106
107   • Our experiments demonstrate that enhancing VLMs’ basic spatial capabilities significantly im-
        proves their ability to generalize to out-of-distribution composite spatial tasks.

      2.1   V ISION L ANGUAGE M ODELS AND A PPLICATIONS
111
112   Early works on VLMs, such as CLIP (Radford et al., 2021a) and ALIGN (Jia et al., 2021), leveraged
113   contrastive learning to align visual and textual embeddings in a shared latent space, demonstrating
114   strong capabilities in linking visual content with corresponding natural language descriptions. With
115   the rapid advancement of Large Language Models (LLMs), modern VLMs increasingly combine
116   pretrained vision models (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2023) with powerful LLMs (Chiang
117   et al., 2023; Bai et al., 2023a; Jiang et al., 2023; Cai et al., 2024) to facilitate a more cohesive under-
118   standing of both modalities (Liu et al., 2023b; Bai et al., 2023a; Chen et al., 2024c). This approach
119   enables richer visual reasoning, open-ended image captioning, and more interactive multimodal di-
120
      alogue systems.
121   VLMs have been applied in various pre-training tasks, such as image-text matching, masked image
122   modeling, and multimodal reasoning (Li et al., 2022; 2023a; Wang et al., 2022b). In downstream
123   tasks, they excel in applications like visual question answering (Antol et al., 2015; Wang et al.,
124   2022a), image captioning (Li et al., 2020; Sidorov et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2021), image generation
125   based on textual prompts (Ramesh et al., 2022; Baldridge et al., 2024), and aiding human-machine
126
      interactions in complex real-world settings, showcasing their versatility and potential across a broad
      range of vision language applications.
127
128
129   2.2   S PATIAL R EASONING IN LLM S AND VLM S
130
131
      Spatial reasoning in LLMs involves understanding and manipulating spatial relationships described
      in text. Early work focused on extracting spatial information from natural language (Hois & Kutz,
132
      2011; Kordjamshidi et al., 2011). More recent efforts emphasize improving multi-hop spatial rea-
133
      soning (Li et al., 2024b), especially in complex scenarios like 2D visual scenes (Shi et al., 2022).
134   Key methods include pretraining on synthetic datasets to better capture spatial patterns (Mirzaee
135   et al., 2021), and using in-context learning to generalize spatial reasoning across tasks, such as
136   transforming spatial data into logical forms or visualizing reasoning traces (Yang et al., 2023b; Wu
137   et al., 2024; Tang et al., 2024).
138
      Building on these foundations, VLMs extend spatial reasoning by integrating visual inputs and often
139
      implicitly encode spatial knowledge through large-scale pretraining on visual-text datasets (Radford
140   et al., 2021b; Li et al., 2023b). Early studies on VLMs primarily focus on understanding spatial
141   relationships between objects in front-view images (Liu et al., 2023a), laying the groundwork for
142   2D spatial reasoning. More recently, research on VLMs has expanded to 3D reasoning tasks, which
143   introduce additional challenges such as depth estimation (Chen et al., 2024a) and path planning
144   (Chen et al., 2024b; Deng et al., 2020), as seen in applications like robotic grasping (Xu et al., 2023)
145   and navigation (Shah et al., 2023; Chiang et al., 2024) in the embodied AI field (Li et al., 2024c).
146   Despite these advances, 2D spatial reasoning remains more fundamental and flexible, as it can be
147   applied to various tasks, including VQA (Ge et al., 2024; Kamath et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024a) and
148
      user interface grounding (Rozanova et al., 2021). Due to its broad applicability and foundational
      role, this work focuses on exploring 2D spatial reasoning capabilities within VLMs.

178   3.1     D ISENTANGLING S PATIAL R EASONING
179
180
      To systematically disentangle basic 2D spatial reasoning capabilities, we first analyze the capabili-
      ties required in existing VLM benchmarks related to spatial reasoning, as shown in Table 1. While
181
      these benchmarks include a wide array of capabilities, such as image recognition and depth esti-
182
      mation, we narrow our focus to those most fundamental to 2D spatial reasoning. Depth estimation,
183   though relevant to spatial reasoning, is more suited to 3D tasks and thus excluded from our analysis,
184   as discussed in Section 2.2. We present the definitions of three basic components: (1) Direction
185   Comprehension: The ability to understand the orientation of an object relative to a reference ob-
186   ject; (2) Distance Estimation: The ability to gauge the magnitude of spatial displacement between
187   objects; (3) Localization: The ability to determine the precise position of an object in space.
188
      The selected basic spatial reasoning capabilities are foundational because they collectively represent
189   the minimal components necessary to fully describe an object’s position in 2D space. In particular,
190   each of the three capabilities aligns with principles from Cartesian and polar coordinate systems,
191   which serve as the mathematical bedrock for spatial representation: direction defines orientation,
192   distance represents magnitude, and localization integrates both to precisely define an object’s
193   position, ensuring comprehensive spatial awareness (Zeng & Si, 2017). This decomposition enables
194   a systematic evaluation of spatial reasoning by isolating the key dimensions of spatial understanding.
195

197   3.2     S PARKLE
198
199
      To comprehensively investigate our hypothesis, we introduce Sparkle, a simple yet effective frame-
      work for constructing an instruction dataset focused on enhancing a model’s spatial reasoning abili-
200
      ties. This framework only improves VLMs’ basic spatial capabilities, and this design enables us to
201
      evaluate whether models that perform well on basic spatial reasoning tasks can also excel in more
202   complex and composite problems.

205
206   The design of our instruction dataset focuses on three basic spatial capabilities: direction, distance,
207   and localization, based on insights provided in Section 3.1. The proposed fine-tuning pipeline does
208   not require manual labeling, as all data can be programmatically generated.
209   We use G to denote a data generator that can generate a set of objects, P = {Ni }ni=1 , representing

213   and Y T represents the corresponding ground truth outputs. Each input X T consists of: (1) A visual
214   input XVT : A labeled diagram representing the spatial configuration of a sample of objects through
215   a visual representation function VT (P ), (2) A language prompt XLT : A question querying some
      aspects of the spatial properties for P .

290
291
      As shown in Figure 5 (left), the basic tasks in Sparkle are designed to assess the model’s under-
      standing of three basic spatial capabilities: (1) direction comprehension, (2) distance estimation, (3)
292
      localization.
293
294   In each basic task, the VLM is provided with an image containing several labeled data objects and
295   a multiple-choice question about the spatial properties of these objects, with the goal of having the
296   model answer these questions correctly. We first generate labeled diagrams that serve as visual
297
      inputs, then generate the questions (in multiple-choice format) and corresponding answer pairs,
      similar to the process described in Section 3.2, to obtain the basic task test set.
298
299
      3.3.2   C OMPOSITE TASKS : E VALUATING I NTEGRATED S PATIAL R EASONING
300
301   Building on the basic spatial relationships, the composite tasks introduce greater complexity. The
302   objective here is to assess whether the model can apply basic spatial skills to solve problems re-
303   quiring a combination of these skills or whether it has merely learned each skill in isolation without
304   being able to generalize effectively. We choose the Shortest Path Problem (SPP) and the Traveling
305   Salesman Problem (TSP) as composite tasks to evaluate the integration of basic capabilities.
306
307   Shortest Path Problem (SPP) As shown in Figure 5 (middle), SPP evaluates the model’s capa-
308   bility to compute the most efficient route between two objects on a 2D grid, requiring a combination
309
      of distance estimation and spatial planning.
310   Consider a grid G of size n × n, with two special objects: the start object Nstart = (xs , ys ) and the
311   end object Nend = (xe , ye ). We employ a language model LM generates the prompt XLspp using a
312   predefined prompt template Pspp , expressed as: XLspp = LM(Pspp (G, Nstart , Nend )). The visual input
313   is produced similar to basic tasks: XVspp = Vspp (G, Nstart , Nend ).
314
      The combined input for the VLM is X spp = (XVspp , XLspp ), and the model is expected to predict the
315
316
      shortest path Ybspp , which is evaluated against the true shortest path, Y spp , computed using standard
      algorithms.
317
318
      Traveling Salesman Problem (TSP) As shown in Figure 5 (right), the TSP represents a more
319
      challenging spatial reasoning task, involving combinatorial optimization. The model must find the
320
      shortest possible route that visits each object exactly once and returns to the starting object.
321
322   Given n objects P tsp = {Ni }ni=1 sampled from G, the ground truth solution Y tsp is computed
323   using a TSP solver Mtsp (P tsp ). Similarly, the input to VLMs consists of a visual representation
      XVtsp = Vtsp (P tsp ) and a corresponding language prompt XLtsp . The complete input query is X tsp =

325   against the ground truth solution Y tsp .

      3.3.3    D ISCUSSION
328
329   Given that the SPP can be solved in polynomial time, we expect that if the model can effectively
330   combine its knowledge of basic spatial concepts, it will show significant improvements in solving
331   this task efficiently. On the other hand, the TSP is an NP-hard problem, requiring combinatorial
332   optimization to obtain the exact solution. We include the TSP to push the limits of the model’s
333
      spatial reasoning capabilities, aiming to investigate how well the model can manage more complex
      problem-solving tasks beyond the basic integration of spatial skills.

336   4     E XPERIMENTS
337
338   In this section, we provide our findings along with the supporting results to demonstrate the ef-
339   fectiveness of the Sparkle framework. Specifically, the experiments are designed to answer the
340   following research questions: RQ1: Can mastering basic 2D spatial components enhance overall
341   spatial reasoning capability in VLMs? RQ2: What insights from the results of evaluations (Section
342   4.2.1), enhancements (Section 4.2.2), and spatial components (Section 4.3) can guide improvements
343
      in model design, training strategies, and data collection for spatial reasoning in VLMs?
344
345   4.1     S ETTINGS
346
      Models We tested open-source and commercial models to evaluate and enhance VLMs’ spatial
347
      reasoning capabilities. For commercial VLMs, we used GPT-4o from OpenAI (Yang et al., 2023a)
348
      and Google-Gemini (GeminiTeam et al., 2023). We included LLaVA1.6 (Liu et al., 2024) and
349   InternVL2 (Chen et al., 2024c) for open-source models. Detailed model specifications and configu-
350   rations are provided in Appendix §A.1. We use the MS-Swift library (Zhao et al., 2024) and apply
351   the LoRA (Hu et al., 2022) fine-tuning strategy, with low-rank dimension of 32. We set a constant
352   learning rate of 1e-4 and a batch size of 1. All training and evaluation tasks are performed on GPU
353   clusters with 8×NVIDIA A100 machines. Further details can be found in Appendix §A.1.
354
355   Data We built the Sparkle training dataset by generating 2,000 images. each with 17 instruction-
356   answer pairs that describe the spatial relationships between objects, resulting in 34K samples in
357   total. Out of these 17 pairs, 3 focus on directions between objects, 7 on distances (including 4 for
358   comparing distances and 3 for estimating numerical distances), and 6 relate to localization (with 3 for
359   identifying object locations and 3 for estimating exact positions). The final instruction describes the
360
      overall spatial relationships in the image. This setup helps ensure the VLM maintains its capability
      to follow instructions effectively. Our evaluation includes tasks of: (1) shortest path problem (SPP),
361
      (2) traveling salesman problem (TSP), and (3) basic spatial relationship understanding. For each
362
      of them, we generated 200 samples, which together make up the evaluation set. For SPP and TSP,

394   4.2.1     E VALUATION OF E XISTING VLM S
395
      From Table 2, we observe that even the state-of-the-art commercial VLMs cannot obtain satisfactory
396
      results on composite tasks like SPP and TSP. Open-source models achieve even worse performance
397   (≤25% accuracy) on these tasks. Specifically, LLaVA perform poorly particularly on SPP com-
398   pared to TSP, which may attribute to the grid data structure in SPP is more complex for VLMs to
399   perceive compared to handling just a few objects in TSP, indicating that these VLMs struggle with
400   visual representations involving intricate spatial structures. Performance on the TSP task worsens
401   as the number of objects increases across most models, highlighting the growing difficulty of spatial
402   reasoning with more objects. However, in SPP, increasing the grid size has little impact on perfor-
403   mance, indicating that a larger grid does not increase the difficulty of reasoning. This result aligns
404   with our initial design principles, where SPP was intended to combine basic spatial relationship
405
      understanding with a straightforward form of spatial planning.
406   To delve into how VLMs behave poorly on the composite spatial reasoning tasks, we further examine
407   their performance on basic spatial relationship understanding, i.e. direction, location and localiza-
408   tion comprehension. As shown in Table 2, even the state-of-the-art VLM GPT-4o struggles with
409   basic spatial relationship understanding, achieving only 67.5%, 41.5%, and 76.5% accuracy on the
410   direction, distance, and localization tasks, respectively. This investigation helps explain why VLMs
411
      underperform on composite tasks, as their inadequate basic spatial reasoning capabilities directly
      hinder their ability to handle more complex spatial challenges.

414   4.2.2     E FFECTIVENESS OF S PARKLE
415
416   To demonstrate the effectiveness of Sparkle, we present results from fine-tuning InternVL2-8B with
417
      this method. The results reveal significant improvements in both basic and composite tasks, indi-
      cating that 2D spatial reasoning capabilities can be significantly improved when a model effectively
418
      masters the basic components of 2D spatial reasoning.
419
420   Specifically, Sparkle only contains data for basic spatial relationship understanding. However, after
421   fine-tuning with this data, VLMs improved in basic spatial reasoning (around 80%) and showed
422   significant gains (around 90%) in composite tasks. This justifies the soundness of our abstraction
423
      of spatial reasoning in 2D space into three basic components (i.e., localization, distance, and direc-
      tion), and that improving these basic capabilities could effectively enhance VLMs’ overall spatial
424
      reasoning, enabling it to tackle more complex tasks. This finding highlights the potential of strength-
425
      ening basic capabilities to improve problem-solving performance in VLMs. When comparing the
426   improvements of the InternVL2 model on SPP and TSP, we observe that the gains (around 20%) on
427   TSP are much smaller than those on the SPP task (160%). One possible explanation is that the TSP
428   involves more complex optimization challenges, which may not be as easily addressed by simply
429   improving basic spatial reasoning skills, as discussed in Section 3.3.3. This underscores the need
430   for further research into the optimization capabilities of language models, a topic we hope our find-
431   ings will inspire. Additionally, we present results of Sparkle on Qwen-VL-7B in Appendix §A.3.1,
      demonstrating its effectiveness across various different VLMs.

499    4.3.2                I MPACT OF T RAINING S AMPLE S IZE
500    In this section, we varied the training sample size in Sparkle and evaluated its impact on spatial
501    reasoning tasks. The results are shown in Figure 4. Several key trends emerge from the results.
502    First, we observe a general improvement in VLM performance as the training sample size increases
503    despite some fluctuations in the curve. However, a noteworthy finding is the existence of task-
504    specific sweet spots, beyond which performance gains taper off or degrade. This suggests that
505    scaling up training samples does not always yield proportional improvements. For example, in the
506    TSP task, performance begins to degrade once the number of training samples surpasses a threshold
507    (around 800). This is likely because, after mastering basic spatial relationships, the model may
508
       focus on locally optimal choices, such as selecting the nearest objects to form a path, rather than
       optimizing the entire path. As the model grows more confident in these local decisions, it may
509
       sacrifice the global optimality of the solution, resulting in suboptimal performance.

       4.4                D ISCUSSION
513    The analysis and results confirm that mastering basic 2D spatial reasoning capabilities through
514    Sparkle can significantly enhance VLMs’ overall spatial reasoning in composite tasks (e.g., spa-
515    tial planning) and general spatial tasks. This directly addresses RQ1 and supports the assumption
516    presented in the methods section.
517
       Turning to RQ2, the evaluation results revealed the limitations of existing VLMs, particularly in their
518
       capability to perceive complex spatial structures, as evidenced in tasks like SPP. This highlights the
519    need for improved model and training designs to support more detailed spatial reasoning. Moreover,
520    introducing synthetic data focusing on basic spatial relationships has proven to enhance overall
521    VLM spatial reasoning performance, offering a clear path for future spatial data collection. Lastly,
522    our ablation study suggests that training specific spatial reasoning capabilities in isolation yields the
523    best results for tasks that demand focused spatial abilities. Therefore, in terms of training strategy,
524    our findings suggest adopting a pre-train and fine-tune approach (i.e., using diverse spatial data
525    in pretraining and fine-tuning specific spatial capabilities tailored to particular tasks) to improve
526    VLMs’ performances on corresponding tasks.
527
528    5              C ONCLUSION
529
530    This work presents the Sparkle framework to address the relatively limited spatial reasoning ability
531    of Vision Language Models (VLMs). Sparkle is designed to enhance spatial reasoning by focus-
532    ing on three fundamental capabilities: direction comprehension, distance estimation, and localiza-
533    tion. Our experiments demonstrate that fine-tuning on these basic capabilities leads to substantial
534    improvements not only in the basic tasks but also in more complex, composite spatial reasoning
535    challenges, thereby showcasing the compositional generalizability of our method. Furthermore, our
536
       analysis confirms that mastering all three basic spatial reasoning capabilities is essential for broader
       generalization, ultimately strengthening VLMs’ ability to interact with the physical world.

      A.1       I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
813
814   In addition to the experimental settings outlined in Section 4.1, we provide the following categorized
815   implementation details for this work.
816
817
      For model specifications, the GPT-4o model used in our experiments and demonstrations is based
      on the gpt-4o-2024-05-13 version, while the Gemini model is Gemini 1.5 Flash. For TSP data
818
      generation, we used an open-source Python TSP solver1 to obtain the ground truth visiting order of
819
      the given object coordinates.
820
821   For VLM evaluations, we focused on four directional categories (top left, top right, bottom left, and
822   bottom right) to make it easier for VLMs to distinguish between directions. To discretize object
823
      locations for localization learning in VLM, the 2D space is proportionally divided using 40% and
      60% thresholds along both the x and y axes, creating nine distinct regions (center, top, bottom,
824
      left, right, top-left, top-right, bottom-left, bottom-right). Detailed data statistics and distribution
825
      visualizations are provided in Section A.2.
826
827   To extract and format the VLMs’ responses, we used the LLaMA 3.1 language model (Dubey et al.,
828   2024), which converts the results into the required format for metric calculations. The specific
829
      prompts used for each task are detailed in Section A.4. The evaluation for basic spatial relationship
      understanding is intuitive, as it follows a multiple-choice question format. For the SPP evaluation,
830
      we check two criteria: (1) whether the solved path is valid on the grid, and (2) whether the length of
831
      the solved path is indeed the shortest between the given start and end objects. For the TSP evaluation,
832   a path is considered “correct” only if it exactly matches the solution from the TSP solver mentioned
833   above. To reduce the difficulty for VLMs in solving TSP, we explicitly specify the starting object in
834   our implementation.
