011                                                 A BSTRACT
012
013
                 Despite recent advancements in latent diffusion models that generate high-
014              dimensional image data and perform various downstream tasks, there has been
015              little exploration into perceptual consistency within these models on the task of
016              No-Reference Image Quality Assessment (NR-IQA). In this paper, we hypothe-
017              size that latent diffusion models implicitly exhibit perceptually consistent local
018              regions within the data manifold. We leverage this insight to guide on-manifold
019              sampling using perceptual features and input measurements. Specifically, we
020              propose Perceptual Manifold Guidance (PMG), an algorithm that utilizes pre-
021              trained latent diffusion models and perceptual quality metrics to obtain percep-
022
                 tually consistent multi-scale and multi-timestep feature maps from the denois-
                 ing U-Net. We empirically demonstrate that these hyperfeatures exhibit high
023
                 correlation with human perception in IQA tasks. Our method can be applied
024
                 to any existing pretrained latent diffusion model and is straightforward to in-
025              tegrate. To the best of our knowledge, this paper is the first work to explore
026              Perceptual Consistency in Diffusion Models (PCDM) and apply it to the NR-
027              IQA problem in a zero-shot setting. Extensive experiments on IQA datasets
028              show that our method, PCDM, achieves state-of-the-art performance, underscor-
029              ing the superior zero-shot generalization capabilities of diffusion models for NR-
030              IQA tasks. The source code will be made publicly available upon publication at
031              https://perceptual-consistency-in-dm.github.io

      1    I NTRODUCTION
055
056
057   Score-based diffusion models have advanced significantly in recent years and have achieved re-
058   markable success at synthesizing high-quality images across diverse scenes, views, and lighting
059   conditions (Ho et al. (2020); Song & Ermon (2019); Song et al. (2020b); Zhang et al. (2023a)).
060   Latent Diffusion Models (LDMs), which embed data into a compressed latent space, enhance com-
061   putational efficiency (Rombach et al. (2022)). Diffusion models provide strong data priors that
062   effectively capture the intricacies of high-dimensional data distributions, making them powerful for
063   generative tasks. Conditional generation using posterior sampling has become crucial for solving
064   various real-world low-level vision problems (Kawar et al. (2022); Chung et al. (2023); Rout et al.
065
      (2024); Song et al. (2023a)). Additionally, several methods leverage the rich internal representations
      of diffusion models by extracting either hand-selected single or subsets of features from a denoising
066
      U-Net for downstream tasks (Tumanyan et al. (2023); Ye et al. (2023); Xu et al. (2023a); Baranchuk
067
      et al. (2021)). Despite these advancements in addressing tasks like inverse problems, segmentation,
068   and semantic keypoint correspondence, there has been little exploration into perceptual consistency
069   of diffusion models for No-Reference Image Quality Assessment (NR-IQA).
070
071
      NR-IQA aims to evaluate image quality in line with human perception without a high-quality refer-
      ence image (Wang & Bovik (2006)). It plays a crucial role in optimizing parameters for image pro-
072
      cessing tasks, such as resizing, compression (Feng et al. (2023); Liu et al. (2023)), and enhancement
073
      (Hou et al. (2024); Fei et al. (2023); Zhang et al. (2024)). Early NR-IQA methods used hand-crafted
074   natural scene statistics features (Zhang et al. (2015); Mittal et al. (2012); Saad et al. (2012)), and have
075   evolved into learning-based quality metrics (Madhusudana et al. (2022); Tu et al. (2021); Ke et al.
076   (2021); Saini et al. (2024)). While learning-based methods show promise, they often lack generaliz-
077   ability. With the advent of generative models, some authors have explored the use of pixel diffusion
078   models for NR-IQA tasks (Li et al. (2024b); Babnik et al. (2024); Wang et al. (2024)). While these
079   approaches show impressive progress, they are often ad hoc, focusing on tasks like quality feature
080   denoising and image restoration by converting NR-IQA problems into Full-Reference IQA (FR-
081   IQA) ones. Additionally, training on specific IQA datasets limits their generalizability. By contrast,
082
      our goal is to utilize pretrained latent diffusion models without fine-tuning, leveraging perceptual
      guidance to extract intermediate multi-scale and multi-time features, termed diffusion hyperfeatures
083
      (Luo et al. (2024)), for NR-IQA.
084

130   2     BACKGROUND
131
132
      2.1   NR-IQA
133
134   Early successful NR-IQA methods relied on handcrafted features based on deviations from expected
135   natural scene statistics (NSS) models (Ruderman & Bialek (1994); Mittal et al. (2012); Saad et al.
136   (2012)) but these struggle to generalize across diverse and combined distortions. Deep learning
137   introduced CNN-based models (Ke et al. (2021); Saha et al. (2023); Zhang et al. (2023b)) and
138   transformer-based architectures such as MUSIQ (Ke et al. (2021)), TReS (Golestaneh et al. (2022b)),
139   and TRIQ (You & Korhonen (2021)) deliver improved performance, but are limited by datasets that
140
      lacked comprehensive distortion coverage and/or inadequately large or representative numbers of
      human quality annotations. To better capture the complex relationship between image content and
141
      perceived quality, manifold learning techniques have been explored (Agnolucci et al. (2024); Su
142
      et al. (2023); Guan et al. (2018); Gao et al. (2024)). These approaches aim to uncover intrinsic low-
143   dimensional structures within high-dimensional data, thereby aligning more closely with human
144   visual perception. They generally rely on the following hypothesis:
145
146
      Assumption 1: (Strong Manifold Hypothesis). For a given data distribution X ∈ RD , the actual
      data points are concentrated on a k-dimensional locally linear subspace manifold M ⊂ RD , such
147
      that k ≪ D .
148
149   Latent diffusion models have demonstrated strong representation learning capabilities when trained
150   on large-scale datasets containing a wide range of authentic and synthetic distortions (Rombach et al.
151   (2022); Zhang et al. (2023a)), but their application to NR-IQA problems remains underexplored (Li
152
      et al. (2024b); Babnik et al. (2024); Wang et al. (2024)).
153   We demonstrate that latent diffusion models implicitly learn perceptually consistent manifolds due
154   to their extensive training data and ability to capture data priors through score matching. By lever-
155   aging the learned score function sθ with perceptual guidance from a perceptual metric ψp , and
                                                 ST          ST S L
156   extracting diffusion hyperfeatures H = t=0 ht = t=0 l=0 sθ (xt , t)|l , where T represents the
157   total sampling steps and L is a subset of intermediate layers, we align features from the denoising
158   network sθ at different time steps with human perceptual judgments.

      3     P ERCEPTUAL C ONSISTENCY IN D IFFUSION M ODEL
217
218
219   As discussed in Section 1, we address the limited applicability of pixel diffusion models by using the
220
      more efficient Latent Diffusion Models (LDMs). The diffusion process in LDMs naturally generates
      on-manifold perceptually consistent samples without requiring additional models to estimate tangent
221
      spaces of the data manifold (Srinivas et al. (2023); Bordt et al. (2023); He et al. (2023)), as we will
222
      demonstrate in this section.
223
224   In LDMs, the diffusion process operates within the latent space, training a score function sθ (zt , t).
225   Let x ∈ RD represent the original high-dimensional data, and let E : RD → Rk be an encoder
226
      and D : Rk → RD be a decoder, where k ≪ D. The embeddings in the latent space are given by
      z = E(x) ∈ Rk .
227
228   To guide the sampling process towards the perceptually consistent region on the manifold and ensure
229   perceptually consistent hyperfeature extraction from the denoising score function, we propose the
230   following framework. An overview of our proposed sampling process is depicted in Fig. 1, which
231   illustrates the step-by-step guidance for extracting perceptually aligned features in the latent space.

      3.2   D IFFUSION H YPERFEATURES & NR-IQA
325
326   Our primary goal is to assess the perceptual quality of a given image without any reference and in
327   a zero-shot manner. To achieve this, we propose to use diffusion hyperfeatures—multi-scale and
328   multi-timestep feature maps extracted from the denoising U-Net (sθ ) of a pretrained latent diffusion
329   model.
330   Previous NR-IQA methods typically rely on features extracted from fine-tuned models (Ke et al.
331   (2021); Madhusudana et al. (2022); Liu et al. (2022); Saini et al. (2024)). However, these methods
332   often use features from the final layer or a single scale, limiting their ability to capture the com-
333   plete spectrum of image characteristics. In contrast, we harness the rich hierarchical representations
334   available in the intermediate layers of the denoising U-Net across multiple diffusion timesteps. This
335   enables us to capture both coarse and fine-grained image features crucial for assessing perceptual
336   quality (Ghildyal et al. (2024)). Recent studies (Xu et al. (2023a); Wu et al. (2023); Luo et al. (2024))
337   have shown that intermediate representations within diffusion models exhibit reliable semantic cor-
338
      respondences, although they have mostly been used for tasks such as data augmentation, generation,
      and segmentation. We hypothesize that these intermediate features also correlate strongly with hu-
339
      man perceptual judgments of image quality, motivated by the diffusion models’ ability to generate
340
      perceptually appealing images and their robust representational capabilities for various downstream
341   tasks (Zhao et al. (2023)).
342
343   To extract these diffusion hyperfeatures, we gather intermediate feature maps from all upsampling
344
      layers of the denoising U-Net across multiple diffusion timesteps during the sampling process (see
      Fig. 4 in Appendix C). These feature maps inherently contain shared representations that capture
345
      different image characteristics, such as semantic content, at various scales and levels of abstraction.
346
      Since these features are distributed over both the network layers and diffusion timesteps, we ag-
347   gregate sθ layers and timesteps as diffusion hyperfeatures for NR-IQA. Specifically, the set of all
348   extracted features is denoted as:

490   5   C ONCLUSION
491
492   In this work, we introduced the Perceptually Consistent Diffusion Model (PCDM) for No-Reference
493   Image Quality Assessment (NR-IQA). Leveraging the strong representation capabilities of pre-
494   trained latent diffusion models (LDMs), we proposed Perceptual Manifold Guidance (PMG) to
495
      direct the sampling process toward perceptually consistent regions on the data manifold. We demon-
      strated the value of extracting multi-scale and multi-timestep features—diffusion hyperfeatures from
496
      the denoising U-Net, providing a rich representation for quality assessment. To our knowledge, this
497
      is the first work to utilize pretrained LDMs for NR-IQA in a zero-shot manner.

      A     R ELATED W ORK
824
825   A.1   NR-IQA
826
827   No-Reference Image Quality Assessment (NR-IQA) has been a focal point of research over the past
828   two decades, aiming to evaluate image quality based on human perception without relying on ref-
829   erence images. Early approaches predominantly utilized handcrafted features derived from natural
830   scene statistics (NSS), with models such as BRISQUE (Mittal et al. (2012), DIIVINE Moorthy &
831
      Bovik (2011), BLIINDS Saad et al. (2012), and NIQE Mittal et al. (2012)). While these methods
      effectively leveraged statistical regularities in natural images, their performance often suffered when
832
      dealing with complex or unseen distortions due to their reliance on specific statistical models.
833
834   The emergence of deep learning introduced convolutional neural networks (CNNs) into NR-IQA,
835   enabling models to learn hierarchical feature representations directly from data. Transformer-based
836   architectures further advanced the field by capturing long-range dependencies and contextual infor-
837
      mation, with models such as MUSIQ (Ke et al. (2021), TReS Golestaneh et al. (2022b), and TRIQ
      You & Korhonen (2021)) demonstrating significant improvements in performance. Despite these
838
      advancements, a major limitation persists: the lack of large-scale, diverse datasets encompassing
839
      the full spectrum of real-world distortions. This scarcity hampers the generalization capabilities of
840   NR-IQA models, as they are trained on datasets that do not adequately represent all possible image
841   degradation scenarios.
842
843
      To tackle the complexity of image distortions, the concept of perceptual or distortion manifolds has
      been explored in image quality assessment models. Manifold learning techniques aim to uncover the
844
      intrinsic low-dimensional structures within high-dimensional data, which better align with human
845
      visual perception. For instance, Jiang et al. Jiang et al. (2018) applied manifold learning to reduce
846   the dimensionality of RGB images, constructing low-dimensional representations for stereoscopic
847   image quality assessment. Similarly, Guan et al. Guan et al. (2017) employed manifold learning
848   on feature maps to capture the intrinsic geometric structures of high-dimensional data in a low-
849   dimensional space, thereby enhancing prediction accuracy for High-Dynamic-Range (HDR) images.
850   These approaches highlight the potential of manifold learning in modeling the complex relationships
851   between image content and perceived quality.
852   Although diffusion models (DMs) have demonstrated remarkable efficacy in generating high-
853   dimensional data and capturing rich feature representations within their intermediate layers (Ho
854   et al. (2020); Song et al. (2020a)), their application to NR-IQA has been minimal. Existing works
855   incorporating DMs often use them for specific tasks, such as quality feature denoising or image
856   restoration, effectively converting NR-IQA into full-reference IQA (FR-IQA) problems (Li et al.
857   (2024b); Babnik et al. (2024)). Typically, these methods involve training on specific IQA datasets,
858   limiting their generalizability to diverse distortions.
859   In our work, we demonstrate that since diffusion models are trained on large-scale datasets con-
860   taining user-generated content (UGC) images—with a wide range of authentic and synthetic dis-
861   tortions—they inherently learn perceptually consistent manifolds. Although these models are not
862   specifically trained for IQA tasks, they are designed to capture data priors by learning score func-
863   tions, enabling them to model complex data distributions and capture both high-level and low-level
      features. This capability allows them to generate a diverse set of images with fine details. We believe


                                                        16
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


864
      that, with appropriate perceptual guidance, it is possible to extract features from diffusion models
865   that correlate highly with human perception, in a zero-shot setting.
866
867   Diffusion models have also demonstrated the ability to learn meaningful representations within their
868
      U-Net architectures, as evidenced by studies that leverage intermediate features for various vision
      tasks (Zhao et al. (2023); Wu et al. (2023)). This suggests an opportunity to harness these models for
869
      NR-IQA, which has so far remained underexplored. Our work aims to address this gap by utilizing
870
      pretrained diffusion models without any fine-tuning, thereby preserving their inherent generaliza-
871   tion capabilities. By extracting multi-scale and multi-time-step features—referred to as diffusion
872   hyperfeatures—and incorporating perceptual guidance, we propose a method that overcomes the
873   limitations of current NR-IQA approaches. This strategy leverages the rich representations within
874   diffusion models to improve generalization across diverse image distortions, aligning more closely
875   with human perceptual judgments.

1462   E    L IMITATIONS & E XTENSION
1463
1464   Our proposed PCDM framework shows strong performance in NR-IQA; however, there are a few
1465
       limitations and potential extensions worth noting.
1466   Limitations: The computational cost of our approach, particularly due to the iterative diffusion
1467   model sampling, can be high, which might limit real-time or resource-constrained applications.
1468   Also, the scope of our work is limited to NR-IQA. We did not extend our evaluation to other low-
1469   level vision tasks or explore the use of perceptual control in image generation due to time constraints
1470   and the focus on a single task in this paper.
1471   Extensions: The general framework of PCDM, particularly its ability to extract perceptual features,
1472   has potential applications beyond NR-IQA. It can be extended to other low-level vision tasks that
1473   are sensitive to perceptual features, such as image denoising, super-resolution, and enhancement,
1474   where quality assessment is crucial. Moreover, since posterior sampling is known to be limiting due
1475   to its clean sample estimation from intermediate time step t, one can explore using more advanced
1476   techniques such as Bayesian filtering to directly approximate posterior sampling in intermediate
1477
       time step t, that is computationally more efficient as it avoids taking derivatives on the estimated
       score function. Our method can also be adapted for perceptually controllable image generation.
1478
       Exploring these directions can help expand the impact of our approach and leverage its strengths
1479
       across a broader range of vision tasks.

1481   In future work, we plan to explore these extensions, allowing PCDM to contribute more broadly to
