009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011
               Understanding and adapting to human preferences is essential for the effective
012
               integration of Artificial Intelligence (AI) agents into daily human life, particularly
013
               in collaborative and assistive roles. Previous studies on embodied intelligence
014            have primarily adopted a generalized yet non-personalized approach. Our research
015            aims to address this gap by focusing on endowing agents with few-shot learning
016            capabilities for human preferences and generalizable planning guided by learned
017            preferences, as individual preferences are often implicitly described in minimal
018            observations, and abstract enough to generalize across situations. To study such
019            formulation, we introduce Preference-based Planning (PbP), an embodied envi-
020            ronment supporting hundreds of diverse preferences ranging from complex action
021            sequences to specific sub-actions. By benchmarking State-of-the-Art (SOTA) meth-
022
               ods on PbP, we demonstrate that while symbol-based approaches show promise in
               terms of effectiveness and scalability, few-shot learning of personalized preferences
023
               and planning with adaptive actions remain challenging. Our findings further reveal
024
               that incorporating preference as a key intermediate representation in planning can
025            significantly improve the personalization and adaptability of AI agents. These
026            results establish preference as a valuable abstraction of human behaviors and pave
027            the way for future research on more efficient preference learning and personalized
028            planning in dynamic environments.

032   1    I NTRODUCTION
033
034   The field of embodied Artificial Intelligence (AI) is rapidly advancing, driven by significant progress
035   in foundation models for vision and language (Bommasani et al., 2021; Peng et al., 2023; Achiam
036   et al., 2023; Bai et al., 2023). These advancements enable AI systems to autonomously collaborate
037   with or assist humans in daily tasks, particularly in domestic settings (Driess et al., 2023; Leal et al.,
038   2023; Zitkovich et al., 2023; Ahn et al., 2024). However, a critical aspect—personalization—remains
039
      inadequately addressed. Personalization is crucial for tailoring agent actions to individual users’
      unique preferences and needs, thereby significantly enhancing user satisfaction (Lee et al., 2012;
040
      Leyzberg et al., 2014).
041
042   The concept of “preference” is fundamental to personalization (Slovic, 1995), guiding human-like
043   decision-making and intelligent behavior. Psychological research has shown that understanding
044   preferences is crucial for interpreting and predicting human behaviors (Fawcett and Markson, 2010),
045
      as well as facilitating social cognition and interactions (Gerson et al., 2017; Liberman et al., 2021).
      For building embodied assistants, the ability to understand preferences could lead to deeper compre-
046
      hension of human behavior and more grounded planning. However, recent attempts through natural
047
      language instructions (Mu et al., 2023; Zitkovich et al., 2023; Singh et al., 2023) may not suffice
048   for capturing human preferences. While natural language is the most common method for humans
049   to articulate their needs, its inherent ambiguity creates a gap between given instructions and actual
050   executions. Embodied agents often require additional details to understand the instructor’s intentions
051   and act accordingly. For example, when a user requests assistance in preparing an apple to eat, the
052   agent needs explicit information about apple selection (if there are multiple), washing requirements,
053   cutting preferences, and container needs. These details, corresponding to users’ preferences, vary
      from person to person. See Figure 1 for an example.

077   Besides, accurately capturing and learning human preferences in real-world settings is challenging.
078   Humans often communicate their needs succinctly, without exhaustive details about their preferences
079   (Lichtenstein and Slovic, 2006), and their preferences may include unconscious or instinctive elements
080   that are difficult to articulate fully (Epstein, 1994; Simonson, 2008). A more practical idea is to infer
081
      preferences from human choices and decision-making tendencies. In Figure 1, the robot assistant can
      infer the users’ preference for apples and washing the fruit before eating from historical observations.
082
083   In pursuit of an intelligent and personalized embodied agent, we concentrating on the capabilities
084   of first learning preferences from human behavior and subsequent planning guided by these learned
085   preferences. We propose that integrating preferences into planning will improve efficiency and user
086   satisfaction. While this problem is not completely untouched, previous studies such as NeatNet
087
      (Kapelyukh and Johns, 2022) and SAND (Yuan et al., 2023) are limited to a single task like rear-
      rangement and fail to generalize across different situations. The gap between the limited study and
088
      the need for personalized agents motivates us to develop a comprehensive environment for embodied
089
      agents to learn human preferences that can be applied to various everyday tasks.
090
091   Therefore, we first introduce Preference-based Planning (PbP), a realistic embodied environment
092   built upon NVIDIA Omniverse and OmniGibson simulation environment (Li et al., 2023). With the
093
      Omniverse’s support, our PbP provides realistic simulation and real-time rendering for thousands of
      daily activities in 50 different scenes, making it an ideal foundation for building preference-based
094
      learning agents. Of the thousands of activities, we reference Behavior-1K and build a parameterized
095
      preference vocabulary of 290 diverse preferences. In this combinatorial space of preference, we
096   identified hierarchical preferences, ranging from the action level to the task sequence level. Action-
097   level preferences focus on specific items or attributes, such as the user’s preferred type of glass or the
098   desired water temperature, whereas task sequence-level preferences pertain to the user’s preferred
099   order of task execution or the prioritization of certain sub-tasks over others.
100
      With the PbP developed, we challenge existing learning agents on their ability to learn human
101
      preference and subsequently conduct preference-based planning. Given the expensive data collec-
102   tion(Akgun et al., 2012) and the few-shot nature of imitation and induction in acquiring human
103   preference, we frame the preference learning task as few-shot learning from demonstration. In this
104   framework, an agent must adeptly respond to ambiguous task instructions and formulate adaptive task
105   planning aligned with user preferences demonstrated in a few example action sequences. Ideally, the
106   agent should analyze data from observing user behaviors, identify consistent patterns, and extrapolate
107   from these behavioral consistencies to a higher-level abstraction of user preferences. Importantly,
      these preferences should be generalizable across various tasks and not tied to specific situations(Chao

108
      et al., 2011). Furthermore, when faced with a new task, the agent should generate an adaptive plan of
109   action sequences based on its understanding of the user’s preferences to complete the task.
110
111   In our evaluation of state-of-the-art algorithms within the realm of preference-based planning tasks
112
      using PbP, we have discovered that preferences serve as a valuable abstraction of human behaviors.
      Incorporating preferences as a key intermediary step in planning can significantly enhance the
113
      capability and adaptability of AI agents. However, there are still significant challenges that current
114
      AI systems face. These difficulties stem not only from the complexities inherent in planning activities
115   but also from the intricate process of learning and abstracting human preferences through perception.
116   These dual challenges highlight the gap between the current capabilities of AI in understanding
117   nuanced human preferences and the sophisticated demands of these tasks. We hope that our work
118   will serve as a foundational step towards addressing these challenges.

      2.1   E MBODIED A SSISTANTS
124
      Developing intelligent embodied assistants that are capable of interpreting natural language instruc-
125
      tions and executing corresponding actions in physical environments has been a cornerstone of robotics
126   research. This journey began with the exploration of Vision-and-Language Navigation (VLN) tasks,
127   wherein robots are trained to navigate in environments based on natural language instructions (Ander-
128   son et al., 2018; Chen et al., 2019; Thomason et al., 2020). Further, the scope of embodied tasks is
129   extended to more interactive abilities beyond navigation. ALFRED (Shridhar et al., 2020) involves
130   interactions with objects, keeping track of state changes, and references to previous instructions.
131   Habitat (Savva et al., 2019; Puig et al., 2023) and AI2-THOR (Kolve et al., 2017) have advanced
132   the field by emphasizing active perception, the necessity of long-term strategic planning, and the
133   acquisition of knowledge through interactive learning. In recent developments, the research focus has
134
      been gradually shifted towards housekeeping tasks where explicit instructions are often absent, thus
      asking for robots to engage in more complex reasoning processes, mainly regarding the rearrangement
135
      of objects (Kapelyukh and Johns, 2022; Kant et al., 2022; Sarch et al., 2022; Wu et al., 2023). Some
136
      recent works (Patel and Chernova, 2023; Patel et al., 2023) have also focused on robots’ anticipating
137   temporal patterns of object movements associated with humans’ everyday routines.
138
139
      2.2   P REFERENCE - BASED P LANNING IN E MBODIED TASKS
140
141   The notion of “preference” employed varies in scope and application. On one hand, existing
142   research primarily explores general preference-based action or task-planning. For example, Deep
143   Reinforcement Learning from Human Preferences (Christiano et al., 2017) utilizes overall human
144   preferences to deduce optimal action sequences according to typical conventions. Among one of
145   the most studied is rearranging objects, where robots rely on commonsense knowledge to organize
146   objects in a manner that reflects objects’ common occurrence and placement within an environment
147   (Taniguchi et al., 2021; Sarch et al., 2022). In such contexts, the word “preference” advocates generic
148   interpretation, that is, universally accepted behavior norms in humans. On the other hand, the concept
149
      of “preference” also encompasses personalized inclinations, emphasizing that embodied agents’
      actions are not only efficient but also aligned with the nuanced habits of specific users. For example,
150
      recent works (Abdo et al., 2015; Kapelyukh and Johns, 2022; Wu et al., 2023) study the rearrangement
151
      of objects based on individualized placement strategies. Our work falls into the second context but
152   also extends the task into more diverse situations and environments, including not only the spatial
153   arrangements of objects but also the temporal sequence of interactions, the state changes during
154   interactions, and the formulation of few-shot learning from demonstration.
155

161   LLMs as few-shot planners to generate language plans for embodied tasks given a few demonstrations.

185
186   PbP inherits NVIDIA’s Omniverse and OmniGibson simulation environment (Li et al., 2023) and
187   supports realistic simulation and real-time rendering for thousands of daily activities in 50 different
188   scenes. In the following, we detail how the preferences in the environment are defined and how the
189   evaluation dataset is constructed.
190
191   3.1                 D EFINITION OF P REFERENCES
192
193   We define preferences on a three-tiered hierarchical structure, covering various degrees of specificity
194   and relevance across tasks. See Figure 2 for an overview of all defined preferences and their
195   distribution. Figure 3 shows corresponding preferences and the agent’s actions in the environment.
196
197   Action Level The bottom level preferences pertain to fine-grained actions in a sub-task. It deals
198
      with details in the process of a specific sub-task, such as the desired amount of water to fill in a cup
      or which level of a bookshelf to put a book on.
199
200
201
      Option Level The middle level of preferences contain alternatives for a specific sub-task. Take
      rearrangement as an example. For “storing-nonperishable-food”, some people prefer to put them in
202
      cabinets, while others may favor stacking them on the kitchen table. Note that preferences defined
203
      at this level could be bound to different objects and may be composed of multiple action-level
204   preferences.
205
206
      Sequence Level The top level of the hierarchy concerns the preference over sub-task order or
207   prioritization of certain sub-tasks over others in one task. It encapsulates users’ preferences about
208   which sub-tasks should be undertaken first and which sub-tasks later. For example, the preference to
209   clean the furniture first, then rearrange kitchen utensils, and finally prepare dinner, after returning
210   home.

      ViViT We select the pure-Transformer-based Video Vision Transformer (ViViT) (Arnab et al., 2021)
325   as a vanilla end-to-end trainable model, whose ability to extract spatial and temporal information on
326   video input has been validated in a variety of tasks. Without a language model component, the model

      LLaVA Large Language and Vision Assistant (LLaVA) (Liu et al., 2024) is an end-to-end trainable
330
      large multimodal model that combines vision and text input for general-purpose visual and language
331   understanding. The variant has significantly improved zero-shot reasoning capability with multimodal
332   input. We test LLaVA-NeXT which has been finetuned for zero-shot video understanding.

519   6    C ONCLUSION
520
521   In this paper, we explore how embodied agents can learn and implement human preferences by
522   observing human behaviors and interacting with human users. We introduce Preference-based
523   Planning (PbP), a realistic embodied environment tailored to capture the diverse and complex aspects
524   of human preferences in everyday life. Additionally, we establish an evaluation benchmark to assess
525   the ability of various models to learn and utilize human preferences. Our experiments demonstrate that
526   preference serves as a valuable abstraction of human behaviors and can guide subsequent planning
527   efforts. Although inferring human preferences and planning actions that adapt to them from limited
528   observations remains a considerable challenge for current models, incorporating preference into the
529
      reasoning and planning process enhances both effectiveness and generalizability. This is particularly
      true for symbol-based systems, which represent an idealized version of real-world settings. We hope
530
      our work will advance further research in this largely under-explored yet critical field of developing
531
      embodied agents capable of adapting to personalized needs and preferences.
532
533
      Limitations & Societal Impacts The primary limitation of our work lies in the synthetic nature
534   of the dataset. While the simulator we used, Omniverse, excels in rendering realistic scenes, there
535   remains a gap in capturing the full complexity and variability of real-world settings. Additionally, the
536   human-defined preference labels may not fully encapsulate the intricacies and diversity of human
537   preferences. Moving forward, we are collecting preference demonstrations from real-world human
538   daily life using head-worn devices, despite the considerable challenges involved. As preferences
539   discussed in this paper are within a private scenario, we do not foresee any negative societal impacts
      stemming from our research.

902   B.2   ACTIONS
903
904   See Table A2 for the action statistics in PbP. We implement 17 action primitives in PbP to assist
905   with model planning and dataset rendering. These action primitives have parameters that simplify
906   tasks and are considered the lowest-level actions. Each sub-task contains 8 to 20 such lowest-level
907   actions. Generally, most of these actions consist of two parts: the robot movement part and the
908
      arm (gripper) execution part. For robot movement, we use the A* algorithm to find paths and avoid
      collisions. We build a connection map during scene initialization for navigation, taking the robot’s
909
      width into consideration. For the arm (gripper) execution, we primarily use the IK algorithm to
910
      compute arm movements. However, since IK cannot handle complex tasks, such as picking objects
911   from the fridge, we also leverage the Open Motion Planning Library (OMPL) planner (Sucan et al.,
912   2012) with forward planning to assist in planning the arm positions.

      generate actions guided by a rule-based planner."

935   several actions, and then to the sequence combining these sub-tasks. The next step is to expand each
936   level with typical tasks and actions. The detailed definition of the 290 preferences can be found in

      The egocentri view Collecting both egocentric observations and third-person views is feasible
940
      in PbP or similar environments built on simulators like iGibson. However, in real-world scenarios,
941   it is generally easier to gather egocentric observations of human daily activities, as these can be
942   efficiently captured through wearable devices. Additionally, there are numerous egocentric-view
943   datasets available, such as Ego4D(Grauman et al., 2022), which further facilitate this approach. While
944   third-person views can provide a different perspective, they often encounter issues such as occlusion.
945   Although research based on third-person views is essential for applications involving real robots,
946   focusing on egocentric views in the current work allows for a more straightforward exploration of
947   preference learning and planning. Nevertheless, third-person view data can be obtained by integrating
948   additional cameras, as outlined in our provided code.
