009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011
               Leveraging video pre-trained models has led to significant advancements in video
012
               understanding tasks. However, due to the inherent bias towards temporal learn-
013            ing in video pre-training, these models fail to capture comprehensive spatial cues.
014            Additionally, the widely-used supervised adaption methods lack fine-grained se-
015            mantic guidance as single action labels cannot precisely depict the intra-class di-
016            versity. To address these challenges, we incorporate the general capabilities of
017            large Vision Language Models (VLMs) and propose a cross-modal collaborative
018            knowledge transfer method to enhance video understanding. First, we propose an
019            attentive spatial knowledge transfer method that distills spatial knowledge from
020            the VLM’s image encoder, enabling the precise capture of spatial information.
021
               Next, we design a contrastive textual knowledge transfer approach that achieves
               detailed video representations through fine-grained text-video alignment. Owing
022
               to the cross-modal knowledge transfer, the video representations are capable of
023
               attending to informative spatial regions and aligning with fine-grained texts that
024            carry rich semantics. Extensive experiments demonstrate that our method achieves
025            state-of-the-art performance across various datasets, validating its effectiveness.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Video action recognition is a fundamental task in the pursuit of intelligent video understanding.
032   Conventional methods focus on spatio-temporal representation learning by designing exquisite back-
033   bone architectures, including convolutional neural networks (CNNs) (Arnab et al., 2021; Carreira
034
      & Zisserman, 2017) and Transformers (Fan et al., 2021; Bertasius et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2022).
      However, these methods typically follow a supervised learning paradigm, which comes with high
035
      costs due to the need for large-scale video data curation and labeling. Recently, there is a new re-
036
      search shift in improving representation modeling that relies on self-supervised techniques to learn
037   the inherent property in videos prior to downstream adaption. By effectively utilizing uncurated
038   video data during pre-training, these video pre-trained models exhibit powerful temporal encoding
039   capability, facilitating various downstream tasks with remarkable performance (Tong et al., 2022;
040   Feichtenhofer et al., 2022a; Huang et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2024b; Li et al., 2023a). Despite these
041   advancements, there are still some limitations. Firstly, video pre-trained models often lack suffi-
042   cient spatial understanding due to their video-centric pre-training objectives, making their direct
043   adaptation to downstream tasks suboptimal especially when actions differ in subtle spatial cues (as
044   illustrated in Fig. 1 (a), those actions such as ‘rock scissor paper’ and ‘dunking basketball’ require
045
      precise spatial cues for identification). To enhance the spatial understanding, some methods (Wang
      et al., 2024; Zhao et al., 2024a) scale the video pre-training dataset to an extremely large size. Al-
046
      though achieving excellent results, they suffer from significant training costs (e.g. (Wang et al.,
047
      2024) needs over 8,000 A100 GPU days). While the other line enjoys the strong spatial encoding
048   ability of an off-the-shelf image pre-trained model and adapt it to video tasks (Pan et al., 2022;
049   Yang et al., 2023). Recently, with the success of vision-language models (VLM), transferring from
050   VLM has become a more effective approach to enhance spatial representation learning (Lin et al.,
051   2022; Qing et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024). Despite the expressive results, they
052   require meticulously designed temporal fusion modules, yet solely training these modules on video
053   downstream datasets is still weak at achieving comprehensive temporal understanding (Lee et al.,
      2024).

      Additionally, transfer learning methods typically adopt a supervised fine-tuning strategy to adapt
      the pre-trained model to downstream tasks. However, these approaches often rely on a single label
077
      for supervision, providing limited semantic guidance, particularly in video tasks (as illustrated in
078
      Fig. 1(b), although the samples belong to the same action ‘somersault’, they exhibit various action
079   forms and occur in diverse scenarios, making it insufficient for a single action label to capture
080   the full semantics of all samples.). Training with such supervision indeed leads to the incomplete
081   representation. Recently, with the strong text encoding capabilities of VLM, some VLM-based
082   transfer methods have begun using action names or fine-grained descriptions as stronger supervision
083   signals. They either use single description (Qing et al., 2023; Ahmad et al., 2023; Huang et al.,
084   2024) or average multiple description at the text space (Zhang et al., 2024). However, the single text
085   feature is still incomplete to describe all possible scenarios where and how an action occurs.
086   Based on the investigation above, we attribute these issues to a lack of spatial and semantic un-
087   derstanding. To address these, we propose a cross-modal collaborative knowledge transfer method
088   based on the video pre-trained model, leveraging VLM to enhance spatial and semantic learning
089   while preserving the temporal understanding capabilities of the video pre-trained model. Firstly,
090   we leverage the powerful capabilities of VLM in encoding spatial details (illustrated in Fig. 1 (c))
091   to enhance the spatial understanding of video pre-trained models. Specifically, we propose an at-
092   tentive spatial knowledge transfer module to enhance the generalization of video features through
093
      feature distillation. To address the domain gap between VLM and video features—where VLM of-
      ten captures broader content, inevitably including irrelevant information to confuse video classifica-
094
      tion—we design a gating mechanism that filters out unnecessary information, making the distillation
095
      process more focused and effective. Secondly, to comprehensively understand the actions beyond
096   class labels, we propose a contrastive textual knowledge transfer module to learn the general seman-
097   tic knowledge based on the text-side of VLM. Apart from the previous methods that utilize single
098   action name or single description (Qing et al., 2023; Ahmad et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2024; Chen
099   et al., 2024), we propose a decompose-expand prompting method to create a description bank that
100   stores fine-grained and diverse description candidates for each action. To fully harness the seman-
101   tics of these descriptions, we introduce a visual-guided assignment method to generate customized
102   supervision signals for each sample. Moreover, a cross-modal contrastive loss is deployed to guide
103   the video pre-trained model to learn the complete action concept.
104   We summarize the contributions as follows:
105
106          • We offer a novel perspective of collaborating VLM and video pre-trained model for video
107            understanding, leveraging their complementary abilities to achieve the comprehensive
               video representation.

114   2     R ELATED W ORK
115
116   Video Action Recognition. Conventional methods focus on spatio-temporal learning under fully-
117   supervised settings, where all categories are predefined. These approaches have achieved remark-
118   able performance using various architectures, including convolution networks (Carreira & Zisser-
119
      man, 2017; Feichtenhofer et al., 2019) and transformers (Arnab et al., 2021; Bertasius et al., 2021;
      Fan et al., 2021). In addition to the architecture design, self-supervised video representation learn-
120
      ing (Diba et al., 2021; Feichtenhofer et al., 2019; 2022a; Jenni & Jin, 2021; Zhao et al., 2024b) has
121
      also gained popularity. Recently, more and more works focus on the zero-shot setting (Lin et al.,
122   2023; Huang et al., 2024), which aims constructing a model with strong generalization ability to
123   recognize unseen categories. These models often utilize large vision-language model to help align
124   text-video feature so as to easily retrieve the unseen categories by their texts. In this work, we utilize
125   both video and image-language model to achieve considerable performance under both settings.
126
      Vision-Language Model. In recent years, vision-language models (VLM) (Chen et al., 2022b; Jia
127
      et al., 2021; Li et al., 2023b) have made remarkable progress. One of the most remarkable and
128   influential works is CLIP (Radford et al., 2021), which is trained on 400M data following a con-
129   trastive manner, and shows remarkable performance on zero-shot image classification. The success
130   of VLM inspires the “fine-tuning” trend on multiple downstream tasks, such as open-vocabulary
131   detection (Gu et al., 2021), segmentation (Liang et al., 2023), caption (Mokady et al., 2021). In the
132   video-related tasks, it also has been explored to various tasks, such as video action recognition (Chen
133   et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024), action localization (Ju et al., 2023). Previous works usually treat
134   the VLM as part of backbones. In this work, we utilize the VLM as a powerful book that absorbs its
135   knowledge but doesn’t apply it as our backbones.
136   Transfer Learning. Transferring useful knowledge from large foundation models has achieved
137   remarkable performance for various downstream tasks, including video action recognition (VAR).
138   Conventional transfer learning for VAR utilizes video pre-trained model by employing fine-tuning
139   or linear-probe (Kumar et al., 2022; Feichtenhofer et al., 2022a; Zhao et al., 2024b). Besides,
140   knowledge distillation is another effective way for transfer learning, such as logits and feature distil-
141   lation (Yang et al., 2024). Recently, adapting VLM to VAR has achieved outstanding performance.
142
      Some of them mainly revise the image encoding mechanism by inserting exquisite temporal-fusion
      module to transfer knowledge from image domain (Chen et al., 2022a; Yang et al., 2023; Pan et al.,
143
      2022), while others dedicate to align text-video feature space to best leverage the language super-
144
      vision ability of VLM. In addition to simply using action names as text supervision (Qing et al.,
145   2023; Ni et al., 2022), (Chen et al., 2024) and (Zhang et al., 2024) employ LLM to generate fine-
146   grained descriptions for each action. In this paper, we construct an effective transfer mechanism to
147   incorporate video pre-trained model and VLM to VAR task.

215   interaction modules. However, these approaches are insufficient for fully exploring spatio-temporal
      learning. Hence, we opt to freeze the image encoder to keep the generalization abilities and treat it

      3.3   C ONTRASTIVE T EXTUAL K NOWLEDGE T RANSFER
263
      In cases where actions involve the large intra-variance, relying solely on cross-entropy loss makes
264   it challenging to capture this variance, leading to suboptimal adaption. Motivated by the fact that
265   the text component of VLM enables to encode fine-grained semantic knowledge learned from the
266   pre-training on large-scale image-text pairs, we aim to transfer this advantage to guide the compre-
267   hensive learning of video semantics. To the end, we first introduce a decompose-expand prompting
268   method that generates precise and diverse descriptions for each class beyond the action label. Sub-
269   sequently, we explore a visual-guided text assignment method to select high-quality positive and
      negative texts, which are then utilized in cross-modal contrastive loss.

      3.3.1    D ECOMPOSE -E XPAND P ROMPTING
271
272   To depict the large intra-variance in video, we aim to provide fine-grained and diverse descriptions
273   for each class. To inspire the comprehensive text generation ability in large language model (LLM),
274   we design a decompose-expand prompting approach. The motivation is that each action could be
275   depicted in different ways but should contain some certain key words.
276   We first query a large language model (e.g., GPT-4o) to extract the key words for each action, then
277   we ask the language model to generate the diverse descriptions based on these keywords. Please
278   refer to appendix A.2 for more detail.
279
      In this way, given a dataset of C categories, we obtain C × M descriptions. Besides, we ask GPT-4o
280
      to provide a longer description.  Then we pass all the descriptions into the text encoder and obtain
281   their text embeddings Tdes = Ti ∈ RM ×D |i = 1, 2, ..C for each class.
282

491
492   In this paper, we propose a cross-modal collaborative knowledge transfer method for video under-
493
      standing. We introduce an Attentive Spatial Knowledge Transfer method for transferring spatial
      information from the image-side of VLM. Experiments verify a more precise spatial capture ability
494
      after the distillation. On the other hand, we enhance the semantic understanding ability by Con-
495
      trastive Textual Knowledge Transfer. Experiments show the diverse descriptions could cover the
496   large intra-variance within an action, helps build a fine-grained video feature space. Our method
497   achieves the state-of-the-art results under different settings which validates the effectiveness of our
498   method.

792   A.3      E XPLORATION OF THE PERFORMANCE DISCREPANCY BETWEEN TWO GATE METHODS .
793
794
      In the ablation study of the gate mechanism, we observe that the cross-attention method performs
      slightly worse than the self-attention method. To investigate the reasons behind this discrepancy, we
795
      compare the CAMs of the two attention mechanisms on four test videos. The results are presented
796
      in Fig. 6.
797
798   As shown in Fig. 6, segmentation quality plays a crucial role in learning comprehensive representa-
799   tion. When the segmentation mask successfully captures the region corresponding to the keyword,
800
      it guides the model to highlight attention on the correct areas. In contrast, the self-attention method
      tends to misinterpret unrelated regions. However, when the segmentation mask fails to accurately
801
      capture the keyword’s region, the model is misled by the erroneous segmentation, resulting in incor-
802
      rect predictions.
803
804   Unfortunately, we observe that segmentation results are more likely to become unstable or disorga-
805   nized when the objects of interest are either too small or obscured, , while the self-attention method
806
      generally demonstrates robust spatial representation learning.
