009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            In this paper, we propose OCCVAR, a generative occupancy world model that
012            simulates the movement of the ego vehicle and the evolution of the surround-
013            ing environment. Different from visual generation, the occupancy world model
014            should capture the fine-grained 3D geometry and dynamic evolution of the 3D
015            scenes, posing great challenges for the generative models. Recent approaches
016            based on autoregression (AR) have demonstrated the potential to predict vehicle
017            movement and future occupancy scenes simultaneously from historical observa-
018            tions, but they typically suffer from the inefficiency and temporal degradation in
019
               long-time generation. To holistically address the efficiency and quality issues, we
               propose a spatial-temporal transformer via temporal next-scale prediction, aim-
020
               ing at predicting the 4D occupancy scenes from coarse to fine scales. To model
021
               the dynamic evolution of the scene, we incorporate the ego movement before the
022            tokenized occupancy sequence, enabling the prediction of ego movement and con-
023            trollable scene generation. To model the fine-grained 3D geometry, OCCVAR uti-
024            lizes a muitli-scale scene tokenizer to capture the hierarchical information of the
025            3D scene. Experiments show that OCCVAR is capable of high-quality occupancy
026            reconstruction, long-time generation and fast inference speed compared to prior
027            works.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Recent years have seen great advancements in the development of autonomous driving (AD) sys-
033   tems. While existing AD methods (Hu et al., 2023b; Jiang et al., 2023; Hu et al., 2022; Huang et al.,
034   2023; Yang et al., 2024) have demonstrated excellent results across a range of driving scenarios,
035   there are still challenges when dealing with long-tail distributions or out-of-distribution situations.
036   A promising direction to address these challenges is world models, which simulate and comprehend
037   the surrounding environment by learning a comprehensive representation of the external world.
038   Occupancy world model, as a specialized type of world model, has gained significant attention for
039   its expressiveness of the 3D geometry. The occupancy world model takes the historical occupancy
040   observations and movement of ego car as input, aiming at forecasting the future 3D scene evolu-
041   tions and planning a safe trajectory of the ego car. Different from vision-based approaches, the 3D
042   occupancy can describe the fine-grained 3D structure of the scene, demonstrating superior expres-
043   siveness of the 3D road scenes. Several occupancy world models (Zheng et al., 2023; Wei et al.,
044
      2024; Wang et al., 2024a) have been developed in recent years. Despite their compelling results,
      these methods suffer from two limitations: inefficiency and temporal degradation, especially in
045
      long-time generation.
046
047   There are two potential approaches to construct the occupancy world model: diffusion-based meth-
048   ods and autoregressive methods. Diffusion-based techniques (Wang et al., 2024a; Liu et al., 2023)
049   leverage a diffusion model to generate occupancy scenes. However, these methods face the problem
050
      of inefficiency due to the multiple denoising steps involved in inference.
051   In contrast, GPT-style autoregressive methods (Zheng et al., 2023; Wei et al., 2024) generate occu-
052   pancy scenes sequentially in an autoregressive manner. The GPT framework (Radford et al., 2019)
053   has been successfully applied to image/video generation (Yu et al., 2022; Esser et al., 2021; Yan
      et al., 2021; Kondratyuk et al., 2023) and demonstrates superior generation ability. Following the

074   GPT structure, a naive implementation for occupancy world model is to utilize a VQVAE-like to-
075   kenizer to represent the scene as discrete tokens, which are then flattened into a one-dimensional
076   sequence and input to transformers for next-token prediction training. However, this vanilla ap-
077   proach, where each generation step depends on the previous one, is also inefficient. For example,
078   assume that a scene is comprised of (50 × 50) discrete tokens. The number of autoregressive steps
079   in next-token prediction for generating 10 frames is 25k, incurring significant inference time costs.
080   Moreover, the flattening process after quantization causes the degradation in the spatial locality and
081   temporal consistency. This results in the low fidelity of the generated 4D occupancy scenes, espe-
082
      cially for long-time generation.
083   More recently, VAR (Tian et al., 2024a) introduces next-scale prediction (NSP) to handle the in-
084   efficiency and spatial degradation problem of next-token prediction (NTP) in image generation,
085   shedding some light on the autoregressive modeling paradigm in occupancy prediction. Neverthe-
086   less, different from 2D image generation, the 4D occupancy forecasting requires the model to depict
087   the fine-grained 3D geometry of the 3D scenes. Moreover, to generate temporal sequence of occu-
088
      pancy, the model should also be able to capture the dynamics of the scene evolution, which requires
      a consideration for temporal modeling in next-scale prediction.
089
090   In this paper, we propose OCCVAR: a novel autoregressive occupancy world model that is capa-
091   ble of forecasting the future 3D scene evolutions and conducting motion planning for ego vehicle.
092   Specially, we introduce a coarse-to-fine autoregressive modeling mechanism: temporal next-scale
093   prediction (TNSP), inspired from VAR (Tian et al., 2024a), which effectively handles the ineffi-
094
      ciency and spatial degradation problem in previous works. To enable the long-time generation and
      ensure the temporal consistency of the generated occupancy scenes, we develop a novel transformer
095
      architecture designed for temporal next-scale prediction. We also develop a multi-scale occupancy
096
      tokenizer to capture the hierarchical information of the 3D scenes. The extensive experiments on
097   the nuScenes dataset (Caesar et al., 2020) validate the effectiveness and efficiency of OCCVAR.
098
099   Our contributions are summarized as follows:
100
             • We introduce OCCVAR, a coarse-to-fine occupancy world model that incorporates tempo-
101
               ral next-scale prediction for autoregressive modeling. A well-designed transformer archi-
102
               tecture is specially designed for the temporal modeling of next-scale prediction.
103
104          • We propose a multi-scale tokenizer to effectively extract and discretize the hierarchical
105
               information of the 3D occupancy scenes.
106          • OCCVAR outperforms baseline methods by 2.13% on IoU and 1.86% on mIoU and sets
107            state-of-the-art on nuScenes dataset. We also conduct a comprehensive ablation study to
               demonstrate the effectiveness and efficiency of OCCVAR.

110   2.1     W ORLD M ODEL
111
112   World model (Ha & Schmidhuber, 2018) can predict the consequences of various actions, which is
113   crucial for autonomous driving. Traditional models emphasize visual prediction (Hu et al., 2023a;
114   Zhao et al., 2024; Su et al., 2024; Gao et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2023; Lu et al., 2023; Wang et al.,
115   2024b; Zheng et al., 2024; Gao et al., 2024; Jiang et al., 2024), potentially overlooking the essential
116
      3D information needed for AD vehicles. Some approaches attempt to forecast point clouds using
      unannotated LiDAR scans (Zhang et al., 2023; Zyrianov et al., 2024), but these methods neglect
117
      semantic information and are not suitable for vision-based or fusion-based autonomous driving.
118
      Occupancy world models (Zheng et al., 2023; Wei et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024a) create a world
119   model in 3D occupancy space, providing a more comprehensive understanding of the evolvement of
120   3D scenes.
121
122
      2.2     V ISUAL AUTO - REGRESSIVE G ENERATION
123
124   Visual Autoregressive (AR) Generation refers to utilizing autoregressive methods to generate images
125   (Esser et al., 2021; Razavi et al., 2019; Lee et al., 2022; Yu et al., 2021; 2022) or videos (Yan et al.,
126   2021; Kalchbrenner et al., 2017; Yu et al., 2023). Generally, the AR models employ a raster-scan
127   paradigm, which encodes and flattens 2D images into 1D token sequences. Recently, VAR (Tian
128   et al., 2024a) proposes to utilize next-scale prediction in visual auto-regressive modeling, which
129   effectively handle the inefficiency and spatial degradation of next-token prediction. However, it still
130   remains unclear whether the next-scale prediction is suitable for occupancy prediction. Moreover,
131
      the modeling process of VAR does not take accounts for the temporal dimension, which still requires
      a consideration for how to adapt this modeling approach for 4D occupancy generation, which is the
132
      main focus of our work.

135   3     M ETHOD
136
137   In this work, we propose OCCVAR, a novel occupancy world model designed to comprehend his-
138   torical observations and forecast the future 3D scenarios. As illustrated in Fig. 2, our proposed
139   OCCVAR consists of two components: a robust tokenizer that encodes 3D occupancy and ego mo-
140   tion into discrete tokens (see Sec. 3.1), and a generative world model using next-scale prediction for
141   future 3D scene forecasting and motion planning (see Sec. 3.2).

143   3.1     T OKENIZER
144
145   The tokenizer aims to model the 3D occupancy scene and the ego motion as discrete tokens. To
146
      achieve this, our tokenizer consists of two components: scene tokenizer and motion tokenizer.
147
148   3.1.1    S CENE T OKENIZER
149
      The goal of scene tokenizer is to model the 3D occupancy scene as discrete tokens. To achieve this,
150   a common practice for scene tokenizer is to employ a quantized autoencoder like VQVAE (Zheng
151   et al., 2023; Wei et al., 2024), which quantizes the occupancy feature map with discrete feature
152   vectors. However, unlike natural language sentences with an inherent left-to-right ordering, the oc-
153   cupancy feature map are inter-dependent, resulting in the bidirectional correlations of the quantized
154   token sequence. This contradicts the unidirectional dependency assumption of autoregressive mod-
155   els, where each token can only depend on its prefix, as illustrated in Tian et al. (2024a). Thus, we
156   propose a multi-scale tokenizer specifically designed for next-scale prediction.

159   Wraw , Draw represents the resolution of the 3D volume, we first convert it to a BEV representation.
160   We employ an embedding layer to embed the 3D occupancy scene into a latent space. Then we
161   convert the 3D scene to a BEV representation by merging the height dimension with the channel
      dimension. We utilize a series of 2D convolution layers to compress the BEV map to a latent feature

232   3.1.2    M OTION T OKENIZER
233
234   The motion tokenizer is utilized to discretize the motion of ego vehicle for better integrating it into
235
      our sequence prediction model. We utilize the position x, y and orientation θ relative to the previous
      frame to represent the motion of the vehicle. We discard the information in the z-axis because the
236
      vehicle’ speed in z-axis is nearly zero in most time. We apply a vanilla uniform quantization of
237
      the motion information, resulting in Vx , Vy and Vθ tokens in vocabulary. Then we map the relative
238   motion with three discrete tokens to a motion token P by Cartesian product:
239
240                                   P = E(x + y × Vx + θ × Vx × Vy ),                                  (4)
241   where E is an embedding layer. By doing so, we can account for the vehicle’s expected trajectory
242   and control how it influences the occupancy predictions, ensuring that the model captures how the
243   environment evolves as the vehicle moves.

      Datasets and Metrics. We evaluate our method on the nuScenes (Caesar et al., 2020) dataset. The
351
      nuScenes dataset is collected in Boston and Singapore and comprised of 1000 driving sequences.
352   We inherit the official nuScenes (Caesar et al., 2020) split setting for evaluation, where the train/val
353   scenes are 700 and 150, respectively. Each sequence lasts for around 20 seconds and the key-frames
354   are annotated at 2 Hz. We employ the occupancy annotation in Occ3D (Tian et al., 2024b) based on
355   nuScenes.
356
      Following common practices, we utilize 2-second historical context (4 frames) and forecast the
357
      subsequent 3-second scenes (6 frames) unless specified. We report the mIoU and IoU for the 4D
358   occupancy prediction task.
359
360   Implementation Details. Our training period consists of 2 stages: tokenization and generation.
361
      For tokenization, we downsample the occupancy with a factor of 8. The codebook are comprised
      of 4096 nodes and the channel dimension of the codebook entry is 128. We utilize 6 scales with
362
      [1,5,10,15,20,25] for multi-scale settings. In the tokenizer loss function, the λ1 , λ2 , λ3 , λ4 are 10.0,
363
      1.0, 0.3, 0.5 respectively. For generation, we utilize 4 layers each for three blocks of our methods.
364   The hidden dimension and head number are 128 and 4, respectively. The β1 and β2 are 1.0 and 1.0
365   respectively. More details could be found in Appendix. A.3.

367   4.2     M AIN R ESULTS
368

      In this paper, we present OCCVAR, an innovative autoregressive occupancy world model designed
534   to enhance the efficiency and accuracy of 3D scene prediction for autonomous driving applications.
535   By integrating next-scale prediction and a multi-scale scene tokenizer, OCCVAR effectively cap-
536   tures hierarchical spatial information while maintaining temporal consistency. Our extensive eval-
537   uations on the nuScenes dataset demonstrate that OCCVAR surpasses existing methods, achieving
538   improvements of 2.13% in IoU and 1.86% in mIoU, setting new benchmarks in the field. These re-

      paving the way for future advancements in occupancy modeling.
