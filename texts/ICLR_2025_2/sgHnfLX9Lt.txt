011                                                 A BSTRACT
012
013
014            Edge device participation in federating learning (FL) is typically studied through the
015            lens of device-server communication (e.g., device dropout) and assumes an undying
016
               desire from edge devices to participate in FL. As a result, current FL frameworks
               are flawed when implemented in realistic settings, with many encountering the
017
               free-rider dilemma. In a step to push FL towards realistic settings, we propose
018
               R EAL FM: the first federated mechanism that (1) realistically models device utility,
019            (2) incentivizes data contribution and device participation, (3) provably removes
020            the free-rider dilemma, and (4) relaxes assumptions on data homogeneity and data
021            sharing. Compared to previous FL mechanisms, R EAL FM allows for a non-linear
022            relationship between model accuracy and utility, which improves the utility gained
023            by the server and participating devices. On real-world data, R EAL FM improves
024            device and server utility, as well as data contribution, by over 3 and 4 magnitudes
025            respectively compared to baselines.

028   1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030
031
      Federated Learning (FL) is a collaborative framework where, in the cross-device setting, edge devices
      jointly train a global model by sharing locally computed model updates with a central server. It is
032
      generally assumed within FL literature that edge devices will (i) always participate in training and (ii)
033
      use all of its local data during training. However, it is irrational for devices to participate in, and incur
034   the costs of, federated training without receiving proper benefits back from the server (via model
035   performance or monetary rewards). Specifically, there are two major challenges:
036
037   (C1) Lack of Participation Incentives. Current FL frameworks generally lack incentives to increase
038   device participation. This leads to training with fewer devices and data to compute local updates,
039   which can potentially reduce model accuracy. Incentivizing devices to participate in training and
040   produce more data, especially from a server’s perspective, improves model performance (further
041   detailed in Section 3), which leads to greater utility for both the devices and server.
042
043   (C2) Lack of Contribution Incentives: The Free-Rider Dilemma. In realistic settings, devices
044
      determine their own optimal amount of data usage for federated contributions. As such, many FL
      frameworks run the risk of encountering the free-rider problem: devices do not contribute gradient
045
      updates yet reap the benefits of a well-trained collaborative model. Removing the free-rider effect
046
      in FL frameworks is critical because it improves performance of trained models (Wang et al., 2023;
047   2022) and reduces security risks (Fraboni et al., 2021; Lin et al., 2019; Wang, 2022) for devices.
048
049   To address these challenges, a handful of recent FL literature (Karimireddy et al., 2022; Zhan et al.,
050   2020a;b; 2021) consider device utility: the net benefit received by a device for participating in
051   federated training. Every rational device i aims to maximize its utility ui . Consequently, utility is
052   the guiding factor in whether rational devices participate in federated training. Devices will only
053   participate if the utility uri gained, via its rewards (ari , Ri ), outstrips the maximum utility gained from
      local training uoi . In a step towards more realistic FL, the referenced works (Karimireddy et al., 2022;

      While previous FL works incorporate utility, they require unrealistic assumptions such as disallowing
075   (i) devices having utility that depends non-linearly on their model accuracy, (ii) heterogeneous data
076   distributions across devices, (iii) truly federated (non-data sharing) methods, and (iv) modeling of
077   central server utility. In our paper, we take a leap towards realistic federated systems by relaxing all 4
078   assumptions above while simultaneously solving the key issues in C1 and C2.
079
      Summary of Contributions. We propose R EAL FM: a federated mechanism M (i.e., system) that
080
      a server, in a FL setup, implements to eliminate (C1) and (C2) when rational devices participate.
081   R EAL FM is Individually Rational (IR): participating devices and the server provably receive greater
082   utility than training alone uri ≥ uoi . The goal of R EAL FM is to design a reward protocol, with
083   model-accuracy ar and monetary R rewards, such that rational devices choose to participate and
084   contribute more data. By increasing device participation and data contribution, a server trains a
085   higher-performing model and subsequently attains greater utility. R EAL FM is a mechanism that,
086   • eliminates (C1) and (C2), i.e., provably eliminates the free-rider effect by incentivizing devices to
087     participate and use more data during the federated training process than they would on their own,
088   • allows more realistic settings, including: (1) a non-linear relationship between accuracy and utility,
089     (2) data heterogeneity, (3) no data sharing, and (4) modeling of central server utility.
090   • produces state-of-the-art results towards improving utility (for both the server and devices), data
091     contribution, and final model accuracy on real-world datasets.

      2    R ELATED W ORKS
094
095
096   Federated Mechanisms. Previous literature (Chen et al., 2020; Zhan et al., 2020a;b; 2021) have
097   proposed mechanisms to solve (C1) and incentivize devices to participate in FL training. However,
098
      these works fail to address (C2), the free-rider problem, and have unrealistic device utilities. In Chen
      et al. (2020), data sharing is allowed, which is prohibited in the FL setting due to privacy concerns.
099
      In Zhan et al. (2020a;b; 2021), device utility incorporates a predetermined reward for participation
100
      in federated training without specification on how this amount is set by the server. This could be
101   unrealistic, since rewards should be dynamic and depend upon the success (resulting model accuracy)
102   of the federated training. Setting too low of a reward impedes device participation, while too high of
103   a reward reduces the utility gained by the central server (and risks negative utility if performance
104   lags total reward paid out). Overall, predicting an optimal reward prior to training is unrealistic. In
105   contrast, our proposed R EAL FM introduces a principled mechanism to set the rewards.
106
107   The recent work by Karimireddy et al. (2022) is the first to simultaneously solve (C1) and (C2). They
      propose a mechanism that incentivize devices to (i) participate in training and (ii) produce more data

108
      than on their own (data maximization). By incentivizing devices to maximize production of local
109   data, the free-rider effect is eliminated. While this proposed mechanism is a great step forward for
110   realistic mechanisms, pressing issues remain. First, Karimireddy et al. (2022) requires data sharing
111   between devices and the central server. This is acceptable if portions of local data are shareable (i.e.
112   no privacy risks exist for certain subsets of local data), yet it violates the key tenet of FL: privacy.
113   Second, device utility is designed in Karimireddy et al. (2022) such that the utility improves linearly
114   with increasing accuracy. We find this unrealistic, as devices likely find greater utility for an increase
115   in accuracy from 98% to 99% than 48% to 49%. Finally, Karimireddy et al. (2022) assumes all local
116   data comes from the same distribution, which is unrealistic. Our proposed R EAL FM addresses all
117
      issues above. Further discussion is in Appendix A.
118
      Contract Theory and Federated Free Riding. Contract theory in FL aims to optimally determine the
119
      balance between device rewards and registration fees (cost of participation). In contract mechanisms,
120
      devices may sign a contract from the server specifying a task, reward, and registration fee. If agreed
121   upon, the device signs and pays the registration fee. Each device receives the reward if it completes
122   the task and receives nothing if it fails. Contract mechanisms have the ability to punish free riding in
123   FL by creating negative incentive if a device does not perform a prescribed task (i.e., it will lose its
124   registration fee). The works Cong et al. (2020); Kang et al. (2019); Lim et al. (2020; 2021); Liu et al.
125   (2022); Wang et al. (2021) propose such contract-based FL frameworks. While effective at improving
126   model generalization accuracy and utility (Lim et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2022), these works focus on
127   optimal reward design. Our R EAL FM mechanism does not require registration fees, boosting device
128   participation, and utilizes an accuracy shaping method to provide rewards at the end of training in an
129
      optimal and more realistic approach. Furthermore, like Karimireddy et al. (2022), our mechanism
      incentivizes increased contributions to federated training, which is novel compared with the existing
130
      contract theory literature and mechanisms.

      3    P ROBLEM F ORMULATION
134
135   Within the FL setting, n devices collaboratively train the parameters w of a machine learning (ML)
136   model. Devices compute local gradient updates on w using their own local data, with the server
137   aggregating all local device updates to perform a single global update. Each device i has its own
138   local dataset Di (able to change in size and distribution) which can be heterogeneous across devices.
139   We define the amount of data per device i as mi := |Di | and denote m := {m1 , . . . , mn }. Dataset
140
      sizes are constrained by cost; each device i has its own fixed marginal cost ci > 0 per sample, which
      represents the cost of collecting and computing the gradient of an extra data point (e.g., collecting
141
      m samples incurs a cost of ci m). We consider linear costs, as data collection and sampling costs
142
      are generally constant over time in the cross-device setting (e.g., powering an IoT sensor incurs a
143   constant cost on average) (Lu et al., 2022; Tran et al., 2019; Wu et al., 2023). Overall, each device i
144   determines the dataset size mi that best balances data costs ci mi with model performance.
145
146   Mechanisms. To entice devices to participate in federated training, a central server must incentivize
147   them. Two realistic rewards that a central server can provide are: (i) model accuracy and (ii) monetary.
148   The interaction between the server and devices is formalized as a mechanism M. When participating
149   in the mechanism, a device i performs federated updates on a global model w, using mi local data
150   points, in exchange for model accuracy ari ∈ R≥0 and monetary Ri ∈ R≥0 rewards.
151                              M(m1 , · · · , mn ) = ((ar1 , R1 ), · · · , (arn , Rn )) .               (1)
152   We desire a mechanism which provides rewards in proportion to the amount of contributions mi
153   each device i makes. Without proportionality, FL methods fall prey to the free-rider dilemma, where
154   devices are able to reap rewards without proper contribution.
155    Definition 1 (Feasible Mechanism). A feasible mechanism M (1) returns a non-negative reward
156    and accuracy for each device, and (2) is bounded in its provided utility.
157
       Definition 2 (Individual Rationality (IR)). A mechanism M is IR if devices always receive better
158    utility by participating than it can training by itself.

161
      rewards (feasible) and (ii) greater utility than they can get by training alone (IR). Finally, we must
      prove that mechanisms fulfilling such qualities can reach a stable equilibrium of device contributions.

182   Accuracy-Data Relationship. Data reigns supreme when it comes to model performance; model
183   accuracy improves as the quantity of training data increases, assuming consistency of data quality
184
      (Junqué de Fortuny et al., 2013; Tramer & Boneh, 2020; Zhu et al., 2012). Empirically, one finds

185
      to train it (Sun et al., 2017). Training of an ML model generally adheres to the law of diminishing
186
      returns: improving model performance by training on more data is increasingly fruitless once the
187   amount of training data is already large.

219   Utility powers the performance of a mechanism. If utility is modeled incorrectly, e.g., unrealistically,
220   a mechanism will guide participants towards unrealistic and suboptimal results.
221
222   A More Generalized Accuracy Payoff. Unlike previous federated mechanisms, such as Karimireddy
223
      et al. (2022); Zhan et al. (2020b; 2021), we introduce a non-linear accuracy payoff compositional
      function ϕi (a(m)) : [0, 1) → R≥0 , which allows for a flexible definition of the utility device i
224
      receives from having a model with accuracy a(m). In Karimireddy et al. (2022), it is assumed that
225
      the outer function ϕi is linear, ϕi (a(m)) = a(m), for all devices, which is restrictive. For example,
226   accuracy improvement from 48% to 49% should be rewarded much differently than 98% to 99%.
227   Therefore, we generalize the outer function ϕi to be a convex and increasing function (which includes
228   the linear case). These requirements (summarized in Assumption 2) ensure that increasing accuracy
229   leads to enhanced utility for rational devices.

      7   C ONCLUSION AND I MPACT
482
483
484   Without proper incentives, modern FL frameworks fail to attract devices to participate. Even if
485   devices do participate, current FL frameworks fall victim to the free-rider dilemma. R EAL FM is the
      first FL mechanism to simultaneously incentivize device participation and contribution in a realistic


                                                        9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      manner. Unlike other FL mechanisms, R EAL FM utilizes a non-linear relationship between model
487   accuracy and utility, allows heterogeneous data distributions, removes data sharing requirements,
488   and models central server utility. Empirically, we show that R EAL FM’s realistic utility and effective
489   incentive structure, using a novel accuracy-shaping function, results in (i) improved server and device
490   utility, (ii) increased federated contributions, and (iii) higher-performing models during federated
491   training compared to its peer FL mechanisms.
492
493   Impact Statement. Edge devices have long been taken for granted within Federated Learning,
494   often assumed to be at the beck and call of the central server. Devices are expected to provide the
495   server with gradient updates computed on their own valuable local data, incurring potentially large
496
      computational and communication costs. All of this occurs without discussion between the server
      and devices over proper compensation for each device’s data usage and work.
497
498
      Our paper aims to produce realistic federated frameworks that benefit the server and participating
499
      devices. One overarching goal of our paper is to ensure that devices are properly incentivized
500   (compensated) by the server for their participation in federated training. As detailed within our paper,
501   incentivizing device participation and contribution also helps the server; model accuracy improves
502   with greater quantity and diversity of gradient updates. Thus, the impact of our work lies in showing
503   that incorporating equity within Federated Learning can indeed lead to a more desirable result for all
504   parties involved. By adequately incentivizing and compensating edge devices for their time, work,
505   and data, utility increases for both devices and the server.

729   Federated Mechanisms (Continued). As detailed in Section 2, there is a wide swath of mechanisms
730   proposed for FL. The works (Zhan et al., 2021; Tu et al., 2022; Zeng et al., 2021; Ali et al., 2023)
731   survey the different methods of incentives present in FL literature. The goal of the presented methods
732   are solely to increase device participation within FL frameworks. The issues of free riding and
733   increased data or gradient contribution are ignored. (Sim et al., 2020; Xu et al., 2021) design model
734
      rewards to meet fairness or accuracy objectives. In these works, as detailed below, devices receive
      models proportionate to the amount of data they contribute but are not incentivized to contribute
735
      more data. Our work seeks to incentivize devices to contribute more during training.
736
737
      Collaborative Fairness and Federated Shapley Value. Collaborative fairness in FL (Lyu et al.,
738   2020a;b; Xu & Lyu, 2020; Sim et al., 2020) is closely related to our paper. The works Lyu et al.
739   (2020a;b); Xu & Lyu (2020); Sim et al. (2020) seek to fairly allocate models with varying performance
740   depending upon how much devices contribute to training in FL settings. This is accomplished by
741   determining a “reputation" (a measure of device contributions) for each device, using a hyperbolic
742   sine function, to enforce devices converge to different models relative to their amount of contributions
743   during FL training. Thus, devices who contribute more receive a higher-performing model than those
744   who do not contribute much. There are a few key differences between this line of work and our
745   own, namely: (1) Our mechanism incentivizes devices to both participate in training and increase
746
      their amount of contributions. There are no such incentives in collaborative fairness. (2) We model
      device and server utility. Unlike collaborative fairness, we do not assume that devices will always
747
      participate in FL training. (3) Our mechanism design provably eliminates the free-rider phenomena
748
      unlike collaborative fairness, since devices who try to free-ride receive the same model performance
749   as they would on their own (Equations 10 and 9). (4) No monetary rewards are received by devices in
750   current collaborative fairness methods.
751
752   Federated Shapley Value (FSV), first proposed in Wang et al. (2020), allows for estimation of
753   the Shapley Value in a FL setting. This is crucial to appraise the data coming from each device
754   and possibly pave the way for rewarding devices with important data during the training process.
755   While our work allows devices to have heterogeneous data distributions, we do not perform data
      valuation to further fine-tune rewards for each device (i.e., provide more accurate models or more

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


756
      monetary rewards to devices who provide more valuable data). The overarching goal of our work
757   is to incentivize and increase device participation, through mechanism design, within FL. However,
758   further reward fine-tuning via FSV remains the subject of future research.
759
760   Honest Devices. Our setting assumes honest devices. They only store the rewarded model returned
761   by the server after training. If devices are dishonest, slight alterations to the federated training
762   scheme can be made. Namely, schemes that train varying-sized models such as (Arivazhagan et al.,
763   2019; Diao et al., 2020; Hu et al., 2021; Li & Wang, 2019) can be implemented (where model sizes
764   correspond to data contribution size) to alleviate honesty issues.

846   It is interesting to note how well R EAL FM performs on MNIST (in terms of the vastly improved
847   utility seen in Figures 3 and 9) while FedAvg only improves model accuracy by a mere couple of
848   percentage points. The reason stems from the payoff function ϕi which heavily rewards models that
849   have accuracies close to 100%. This scenario is rational in real-world settings. Competing companies
850   in industry will all likely have models which are high-performing (above 95% accuracy). Since the
851   competition is stiff, companies with the best model performance will likely attract the most customers
852   since their product is the best. Therefore, the utility for achieving model performance close to 100%
853
      should become larger and larger as one gets closer to 100%.
