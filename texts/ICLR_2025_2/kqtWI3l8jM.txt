                                                   A BSTRACT
010
011            Reinforcement learning encounters challenges in various environments related to
012            robustness and explainability. Traditional Q-learning algorithms cannot effec-
013            tively make decisions and utilize the historical learning experience. To over-
014            come these limitations, we propose Cognitive Belief-Driven Q-Learning (CBDQ),
015            which integrates subjective belief modeling into the Q-learning framework, en-
016            hancing decision-making accuracy by endowing agents with human-like learn-
017            ing and reasoning capabilities. Drawing inspiration from cognitive science, our
018
               method maintains a subjective belief distribution over the expectation of actions,
               leveraging a cluster-based subjective belief model that enables agents to reason
019
               about the potential probability associated with each decision. CBDQ effectively
020
               mitigates overestimated phenomena and optimizes decision-making policies by
021            integrating historical experiences with current contextual information, mimicking
022            the dynamics of human decision-making. We evaluate the proposed method on
023            discrete control benchmark tasks in various complicate environments. The results
024            demonstrate that CBDQ exhibits stronger adaptability, robustness, and human-like
025            characteristics in handling these environments, outperforming other baselines. We
026            hope this work will give researchers a fresh perspective on understanding and ex-
027            plaining Q-learning.

      To overcome these limits, we attempt to solve the problem using a novel approach: Cognitive Sci-
055   ence, often seen as a manifestation of human intuition. In this domain, humans typically con-
056   struct and adjust mental models’ subjective beliefs when confronted with uncertainty to predict
057   future events and make corresponding decisions (Peterson & Beach, 1967; Hastie & Dawes, 2009;
058   Gigerenzer et al., 1991). These mental models, grounded in the cognition and experience of the
059   world, empower humans to assess the potential consequences of various actions and make effective
060   choices in complex settings. Notably, effectively managing uncertainty during decision-making is
061   essential, as it directly influences both the efficiency of learning and the robustness of decisions
062   (Kochenderfer, 2015). By leveraging this mechanism, we apply similar mental model theories to
063
      RL to improve the performance and adaptability of algorithms in various environments.
064   We present a novel direction for enhancing uncertainty optimization in deep Q-learning by inte-
065   grating cognitive science’s mental model with expected utility theory (Mongin, 1998). We propose
066   Cognitive Belief-Driven Q-Learning (CBDQ), seen in Figure 1, an off-policy Deep Q-Learning al-
067   gorithm applicable to both discrete and continuous states. Specifically, CBDQ incorporates:
068   (1) Subjective Belief Component (Soltani & Izquierdo, 2019) addresses the overestimation problem
069   in Q-learning. It is grounded in Subjective Expected Utility Theory (Mongin, 1998), a fundamental
070   component of decision theory that evaluates decision options by multiplying the utilities of actions
071   by their associated probabilities. By modeling subjective beliefs, agents simulate how individuals
072   adjust expectations, enhancing learning through probabilistic reasoning.
073   (2) Human Cognitive Clusters, implemented using the K-means algorithm (Ikotun et al., 2022),
074   emulate how humans categorize information by grouping similar states within the environment’s
075   state space. This method mirrors human cognition, where stimuli or situations are naturally classified
076   into distinct categories, and serves as an efficient tool for state representation extraction. The model
077   compresses high-dimensional data by clustering the state space into meaningful, low-dimensional
078   representations, capturing essential environmental features and reducing learning complexity.
079   (3) Belief-Preference Decision Framework (BPDF) integrates subjective beliefs and cognitive clus-
080   ters into a unified decision-making process. BPDF adapts to various state spaces, allowing agents
081   to base decisions on expected outcomes, past experiences (via Human Cognitive Clusters), and cur-
082   rent beliefs. This enables context-sensitive decision-making, closely mirroring human cognition in
083   complex, uncertain environments.
084
      Empirical evaluations show that CBDQ consistently achieves higher feasible rewards in different
085   environments, outperforming other advanced Q-learning baselines. This work moves us closer to
086   human-like agents, offering innovative thinking for complex decision-making systems.

089   2     R ELATED W ORKS
090
091   The development of RL can be broadly categorized into two main directions, mathematical opti-
092   mization and learning process simulation both stemming from the concept of learning from delayed
093   rewards proposed by (Watkins, 1989).
094
095   2.1   A DVANCEMENTS MATHEMATICAL OPTIMIZATION IN Q-L EARNING
096
097   Despite efforts to address overestimation bias, Double Q-Learning (Hasselt, 2010) only partially
098   reduces maximization bias and may still cause underestimation in noisy environments, potentially
099
      leading to convergence to near-optimal rather than optimal solutions (Weng et al., 2020; Ren et al.,
      2021). (Wang et al., 2021) proposed ensemble Q-learning as an alternative, using multiple Q-
100
      function approximators and conservatively selecting the minimum value. However, this strategy also
101
      risks underestimation and performance variability due to approximation errors and the limitations of
102   a fixed ensemble size. In recent years, researchers have developed innovative Q-learning algorithms.
103   For example, (Bas-Serrano et al., 2021) introduced Logistic Q-Learning, using a homoscedastic lo-
104   gistic noise model to reframe value learning via linear programming. (Garg et al., 2023) proposed
105   Extreme Q-Learning (XQL), which utilizes a Gumbel noise source along with the LINEX loss func-
106   tion to more effectively capture the asymmetry in Q-value distributions. (Hui et al., 2023) developed
107   Double Gumbel Q-Learning (DoubleGum), incorporating two heteroscedastic Gumbel noise sources
      and an adjustable pessimism factor to mitigate estimation bias. These approaches offer crucial theo-


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      retical and practical advancements for resolving Q-learning biases. While these optimization-based
109   methods have partially addressed estimation bias, they remain incremental improvements within the
110   Q-learning framework. Logistic Q-Learning has limited use in complex environments, XQL strug-
111   gles with diverse uncertainties, and though DoubleGum offers a broader theoretical framework, it
112   still faces key challenges, notably the lack of proven convergence. One might question: Is there a
113   unique way of thinking that can improve algorithms like Q-learning?

115   2.2   L EARNING P ROCESS I NSIGHT A LGORITHMS IN R EINFORCEMENT L EARNING
116
117   Ongoing development in human-like science and RL have increasingly focused on integrating
118   human-like reasoning and beliefs, key components of learning process-oriented algorithms. These
119   models aim to emulate human decision-making by adapting beliefs and strategies based on experi-
120   ence. Complementing these efforts, (Barber, 2012) discusses Bayesian reasoning frameworks that
121
      incorporate prior knowledge to manage uncertainty effectively. Building on this, (Carroll et al.,
      2019) explored collaboration by integrating learned human policies into Q-learning. More recently,
122
      (Zhang et al., 2021) introduced Solipsistic Reinforcement Learning, extracting human-perspective
123
      state representations, while (Hu et al., 2021) developed Off-Belief Learning (OBL), allowing agents
124   to reason about others’ actions with dynamic beliefs. Additionally, (O’Donoghue, 2021) proposed
125   Variational Bayesian Reinforcement Learning, which offers a novel approach to balancing explo-
126   ration and exploitation using a risk-seeking utility function. This method introduces a new Bellman
127   operator with associated fixed points, termed ’knowledge values,’ which compress both expected
128   future rewards and epistemic uncertainty into a single value. These approaches enhance AI adapt-
129   ability and align reinforcement learning with human cognition.

177   In this work, we address a fundamental question: How does integrating subjective beliefs refine
178   decision-making within a Q-learning framework? We propose a novel method, Cognitive Belief-
179
      Driven Q-Learning (CBDQ) to incorporate human-like subjective belief components into RL. By
      leveraging Subjective Expected Utility Theory (SEUT), we dynamically update an agent’s belief
180
      distribution over time, reflecting evolving perceptions of rewards, actions, and states.

      4.1   E XPECTED U TILITY T HEORY AND Q- LEARNING : A C OGNITIVE P ERSPECTIVE
183
184   To closely mirror human cognitive processes, we consider integrating SEUT into RL. SEUT offers a
185   structured framework for decision-making under uncertainty by individual’s belief preference, pro-
186   moting actions that maximize the weighted sum of outcome utilities. This framework aligns seam-
187   lessly with MDPs, where the value function represents a specific form of expected utility derived
188   from discounted returns.
