011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013
               Stochastic policy gradient methods are a fundamental class of reinforcement learn-
               ing algorithms. When using these algorithms for continuous control it is common
014
               to parameterize the policy using a Gaussian distribution. In this paper, we show
015            that the policy gradient with Gaussian policies can be viewed as the gradient of
016            a weighted least-squares objective function. That is, policy gradient algorithms
017            are implicitly implementing a form of regression. A number of recent works have
018            shown that reformulating regression problems as classification problems can im-
019            prove learning. Inspired by these works, we investigate whether replacing this
020            implicit regression with classification can improve the data efficiency and stability
021
               of policy learning. Toward this end, we introduce a novel policy gradient surro-
               gate objective for softmax policies over a discretized action space. This surrogate
022
               objective uses a form of cross-entropy loss as a replacement for the implicit least-
023            squares loss found in the surrogate loss for Gaussian policies. We extend prior
024            theoretical analysis of this loss to our policy gradient surrogate objective and then
025            provide experiments showing that this novel loss improves the data efficiency of
026            stochastic policy gradient learning in continuous action spaces.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
030
      Stochastic policy gradient algorithms are a fundamental class of reinforcement learning (RL) algo-
031
      rithms. In their simplest form, the learning agent runs its current policy to collect data in the form
032
      of state, action, and reward transitions to produce a dataset of (si , ai , Âi ) where Âi is an estimate
033
      of the advantage of taking action ai in state si . The learning agent then updates its parameterized
034   and differentiable stochastic policy with a step of gradient ascent on the expected cumulative reward
035   objective. The gradient update increases the log-likelihood of each observed action in proportion to
036   the advantage of that action.
037
      Following Peters and Schaal (2007), we observe that the policy gradient update can be viewed
038
      as implicitly optimizing a weighted supervised learning loss function. We particularly focus on
039   the case of continuous control with Gaussian policies in which case we will show that the policy
040   gradient matches the gradient of a weighted least-squares loss function. In this sense, we say that
041   policy gradient algorithms are implicitly implementing (weighted) regression.
042
      A growing body of research (discussed in Section 6) supports the claim that reformulating regres-
043   sion problems as classification problems can boost task performance in supervised regression. Of
044   particular relevance to this work, Imani and White (2018) introduced a form of classification loss for
045   regression problems and showed that it boosts regression accuracy compared to the commonly used
046   squared loss. Subsequently, Farebrother et al. (2024) replaced the squared loss of value-based RL
047   algorithms with this histogram loss and found that the approach unlocked new levels of scalability
048   in a wide variety of RL benchmarks. Motivated by these prior works, in this paper, we consider re-
049
      formulating the implicit regression of stochastic policy gradient RL for continuous action domains
      as classification. Specifically, this work aims to answer the question:
050
051   Does replacing the least-squares loss and Gaussian policies with a cross-entropy loss and softmax
052       policies over discretized actions improve the data efficiency of policy gradient learning?
053
      In answering this question, we make the following contributions:

            1. We show that the stochastic policy gradient is equal to the gradient of a weighted maximum
055            likelihood objective. In continuous action spaces with Gaussian policies, optimizing this
056            objective amounts to implicit weighted regression with a least-squares loss function.
057         2. We introduce a novel policy gradient surrogate loss that re-casts the implicit regression of
058            continuous actions as classification of discrete actions.
059         3. Building on Imani and White (2018), we show that the loss we introduce has a smaller
060            bound on the gradient norm compared to the surrogate loss for Gaussian policies, implying
061            that the new loss is easier to optimize.
062         4. We empirically investigate the use of cross-entropy losses and softmax policies as an al-
063            ternative to the widely-used Gaussian policies within stochastic policy gradient algorithms
064            and find that our reformulation leads to increased data-efficiency, more stable learning, and
               increased final performance.

071   2.1   R EINFORCEMENT L EARNING
072
073   We formalize an RL agent’s task environment as a finite-horizon, episodic Markov decision process
074
      (MDP) with state set S, action set A, transition function, p : S × A × S → [0, 1], reward function
      r : S × A → R, discount factor γ, and initial state distribution d0 (Puterman, 2014). The agent’s
075
      behavior is determined by its policy, π : S × A → [0, 1], which is a function mapping states
076   to probability distributions over possible actions. Given a policy and task environment, interaction
077   begins at time t = 0 in some initial state (s0 ∼ d0 ) and then proceeds with the agent selecting actions
078   according to its policy (at ∼ π(·|st )) and the environment responding with a reward, rt = r(st , at ),
079   and transitioning to a next state (st+1 ∼ p(·|st , at )). Interaction continues until the agent reaches a
080   terminal state, at which point, the agent returns to a new initial state and the process begins again.
081   The result of this interaction is a trajectory, h := (s0 , a0 , r0 , s1 , ..., sT , aT , rT ).
082   We measure policy performance by the expected discounted return in a given MDP:
083                                             " T                 #
                                                  X
084                                  J(π) := E        γ t Rt |H ∼ π

086   where H = (S0 , A0 , R0 , ...ST , AT , RT ) is a random variable representing a trajectory and H ∼ π
087   denotes sampling H by running π for one episode. In RL, the transition and reward functions of the
088   task MDP are unknown. RL algorithms are designed to collect trajectory data from the task MDP
089   and use this data to return a policy, π ∗ ∈ arg maxπ J(π).

171   Empirical evidence in supervised learning and RL suggests there is an empirical benefit to reformu-
172   lating regression problems as classification problems. Our goal in this work is to understand if this
173   benefit translates to policy gradient learning if we reformulate the implicit regression in policy gra-
174   dient methods as classification. Toward this goal, we first describe how we can represent continuous
175   action policies as policies over discrete actions and then introduce a new policy gradient surrogate
176   objective for training these policies.
177
178   4.1     P OLICY R EPRESENTATION
179
180
      To recast regression as classification, we first need to parameterize the policy we are learning as a dis-
      tribution over a finite set, Z, where each continuous a ∈ A maps to an element of z ∈ Z. The naive
181
      way to accomplish this mapping is to discretize the continuous space using a multi-dimensional grid
182   with k bins along each dimension. The grid representation is useful in that it can learn policies in
183   which different action dimensions are correlated. The downside of this representation is that the
184   number of discrete actions will be exponential in the native action space dimensionality.
185
      To make discrete action policies tractable, we make the simplifying assumption that each action
186
      dimension is selected independently of the others. This assumption is reasonable as it is already
187   standard practice when using Gaussian policies to use a diagonal covariance matrix. Thus, in com-
188   parison to such Gaussian policies, the policy representation that we introduce is only limited in
189   terms of the granularity of the discretization. This simplification means that after discretization,
190   each dimension has k bins and the policy network only needs to output kd values instead of k d . The
191   limitation of this assumption is that the space of possible distributions over actions that the policy
192   can represent is now limited.
193   Formally, we learn a policy that outputs d softmax distributions (one for each action dimension)
194   where each distribution is over a finite set Zl for l ∈ {1, ..., d}. We map each continuous value in
195   [amin , amax ] to an element of Zl . In this work, we use a uniform discretization with k bins and let
196   c := amax −a

      We next conduct an empirical study designed to answer the following questions:
298
299         1. Does replacing the implicit regression of the policy gradient surrogate loss with a cross-
300            entropy loss increase the data efficiency of stochastic policy gradient methods?
301         2. Are observed increases in learning efficiency due to improved exploration or optimization?
302
303   We also test the sensitivity of different loss functions to noise in advantage estimates, action space
304   dimensionality, and hyper-parameter sensitivity.

400   The use of histogram losses for policy gradient learning is qualitatively different than past studies on
401   replacing regression with classification because the softmax representation does not just affect policy
402   optimization but also affects the data distribution of the learning agent. This observation motivates
403
      our second empirical question as to whether the observed benefits arise from improved exploration,
      improved optimization, or both. We note that prior work on using discretized action spaces for
404
      continuous control has hypothesized that the benefit is entirely due to improved exploration (Tang
405   and Agrawal, 2019; OpenAI et al., 2019).
406
407
      To answer our question, we use the Bandit domain and repeat our main experiment under two addi-
      tional conditions.
408
409
              1. Init Gaussian. We initialize softmax policies to approximate the same initial Gaussian
410
                 distribution that Gaussian policies use.
411
              2. Gaussian Exploration At each iteration, we transform the softmax policy into a Gaussian
412
                 policy and sample actions from this policy for exploration. To do so, we compute the mean
413
                 and variance of bin centers under the softmax distribution. This mean and variance then
414              parameterize the Gaussian exploration distribution.
415
416   The first condition is intended to control for the potential of wider initial exploration under a uniform
417   softmax distribution and the second condition is intended to control for the potential of more flexible
418   exploration. Figure 3c and Figure 3d show learning curves under Init Gaussian and Gaussian
419   Exploration respectively. We contrast these figures with Figure 2a which shows learning curves
420   when we initialize softmax distributions as uniform distributions. No significant negative effect
421
      for HL-1-Hot and HL-Gauss is observed under Gaussian initialization. This finding suggests that
      improved initial exploration with a softmax policy is not a reason for the increase in data efficiency
422
      that we have observed. With Gaussian Exploration, we observe that both HL-1-Hot and HL-Gauss
423   improve their rate of convergence, indicating that softmax exploration is not the key reason for
424   increased data efficiency in this domain. For HL-1-Hot, the use of Gaussian exploration removes
425   the sub-optimal convergence we observe for this method in the base setting. We suspect that this
426   result is due to the fact that the Gaussian exploration adds information about how close different
427   actions are to one another.
428   Our main takeaway from this experiment is that the cross-entropy losses improve the optimization
429   properties of policy gradient learning. These findings align with our theoretical analysis in Sec-
430   tion 4.3 that shows a smaller norm on the gradient of the cross-entropy losses. Our findings do not
431   indicate that exploration cannot also be a benefit of discretization in some RL domains as prior work
      has conjectured (OpenAI et al., 2019; Tang and Agrawal, 2019).

      6   R ELATED W ORK
433
434
      Several related ideas to the contributions of our paper have been previously explored in the literature.
435
436
      Policy Search as Supervised Learning We have shown how stochastic policy gradient algorithms
437   with Gaussian policy representations can be viewed as implicitly solving a regression problem. A
438   number of prior works have tried to re-cast RL as supervised learning. Peters and Schaal (2007)
439   derive the reward-weighted regression algorithm in order to cast policy search in continuous control
440   spaces as a weighted regression problem. Recent works such as MPO (Abdolmaleki et al., 2018),
441   advantage-weighted regression (Peng et al., 2019), and advantage-weighted actor-critic (Nair et al.,
442   2021) use similar derivations to develop policy search methods that implicitly solve least-squares
      optimization problems when using Gaussian policies. An alternative approach to RL as SL is upside-
443
      down RL that proposes to learn a policy π(s, g) by regressing state-return pairs to the action taken
444
      in state s before ultimately receiving return g. The optimal action for state s is then predicted
445   as π(s, g ⋆ ) (Schmidhuber, 2020). Upside-down RL is also the basis for the decision-transformer
446   approach to offline and online RL (Chen et al., 2021). Our study is complementary to these prior
447   works in its focus on recasting implicit regression as classification in policy search.
448
449   Recasting Regression as Classification In the supervised learning literature, several works have
450   studied the empirical and theoretical benefits of replacing the least-squares regression loss with a
451
      cross-entropy classification loss. Our approach most closely follows the approach of Imani and
      White (2018); Imani et al. (2024) due to our use of the HL-Gauss loss as a means to preserve the
452
      spatial structure of the action space. Zhang et al. (2023) found that the cross-entropy loss encouraged
453   better representations in regression problems and Pintea et al. (2023) found that casting regression
454   as classification helped with class imbalances. While these findings are focused on the supervised-
455   learning case and 1-hot cross-entropy losses, it would be interesting to see whether similar benefits
456   can be found in policy gradient learning. Lastly, we note that a number of applied works, primarily
457   in computer vision, have found classification formulations of regression to give superior empirical
458   performance (Cao et al., 2018; Kendall et al., 2017; Li et al., 2022; Rothe et al., 2015, e.g.,). The
459
      policy gradient learning setting is quite different from supervised learning as the function we are
      learning also directly determines the data distribution being learned over.
460
461
      Alternatives to Gaussian Policies in Continuous Action Domains In order to recast continuous
462   action policy gradient learning as a classification problem, we discretized each dimension of the
463   action-space and learned softmax policies over each dimension. Tang and Agrawal (2019) previ-
464   ously found discretization to be effective in continuous control benchmarks and credited the benefit
465   to improved exploration. OpenAI et al. (2019) also conjectured improved exploration to be a rea-
466   son to prefer discretized actions. Our theoretical and empirical analysis complements these works
467   by emphasizing that the cross-entropy loss itself is desirable for learning. As mentioned in the in-
468
      troduction, naive discretization can lead to an exponentially sized action space that would make it
      intractable to represent the policy. One prior work has addressed this increase in the size of the
469
      action space by sequentially selecting the discretized action for each dimension (Metz et al., 2019).
470   Though Metz et al. (2019) introduced this approach to enable q-learning (Watkins and Dayan, 1992)
471   in continuous action domains, it would be interesting to consider new policy parameterizations based
472   on this approach that could be trained with classification losses. Alternative policy distributions such
473   as truncated Gaussians (Fujita and Maeda, 2018), Beta distributions (Chou et al., 2017), and non-
474   parametric distributions Tessler et al. (2019) have also been explored as alternatives to the Gaussian
475   representation. Continuous actions can be directly sampled from these distributions and it would be
476
      interesting to investigate if our findings pertain in some form to these alternative representations.
477
478   7   D ISCUSSION AND L IMITATIONS
479
480
      We have found that the cross-entropy-based policy gradient surrogate loss that we introduced in this
481   work generally leads to more data-efficient policy gradient learning across the continuous control
482   domains where we evaluated it. Results showed that performance improved even as the action-
483   space dimensionality increased which shows the viability of simply selecting each action dimension
484   independently. These results suggest that reformulating the weighted regression found in policy
485   gradient learning for Gaussian policies as weighted classification for softmax policies can be an
      effective strategy in continuous control RL applications.

                                                         9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      We found that the performance difference between the HL-1-Hot and HL-Gauss surrogate losses
487   was often small and the ordering of the methods changed across domains. This result was somewhat
488   surprising to us as HL-1-Hot discards spatial information about the action-space when reinforcing
489   actions whereas HL-Gauss makes use of this information through the Gaussian target distribution.
490   We do find in some cases that HL-1-Hot may be more prone to find local optima (i.e., it converges
491   to a discretized action that is adjacent to the optimal discretized action), however, the loss in perfor-
492   mance from these cases is small in the benchmarks we considered.
493   Perhaps the principle limitation of the HL-Gauss loss is the need to discretize the action space so
494   that a softmax policy can be used. The result is that the true deterministic, optimal policy may not
495   be representable, e.g., if the optimal action in some state is not a bin center. The degree of this
496   limitation depends upon the properties of a domain and how necessary it is for the policy to output
497   precise actions for acceptable performance. In our experiments, we observed mildly adverse effects
      from discretizing the action-space only in limited number of settings such as in Figure 7b, but did not
498
      observe negative effects in general when evaluated on 7 environments. This result could be because
499
      the domains we considered do not require precise control for high return. It could also indicate
500   that learning with Gaussian policies is sufficiently slow that we never reach the point where their
501   improved representation power becomes useful. Further small-scale studies on carefully controlled
502   toy problems could help understand when discretization is not a viable strategy.
503
      In terms of broader societal impacts, RL algorithms have the potential for both positive and negative
504   impacts depending on the application. This paper studies fundamental RL algorithms using theoreti-
505   cal and empirical analysis in toy problems and benchmarks rather than specific applications. Hence,
506   its immediate societal impact is neutral.
507
508   8   C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
509
      This paper has studied the degree to which stochastic policy gradient algorithms can be improved for
510
      continuous action domains by replacing an implicit least-squares loss term with a cross-entropy loss
511   term in the policy gradient surrogate objective. We first derived the connection between the policy
512   gradient for Gaussian policies and a certain weighted least-squares optimization problem. We then
513   introduced a novel loss function that replaces the implicit weighted regression loss for Gaussian
514   policies with a weighted cross-entropy loss for softmax policies. We showed theoretically that this
515   loss enjoys a smaller bound on gradient norms and then showed empirically that this novel loss
516   improves the data efficiency of a prototypical stochastic actor-critic method for continuous control.
517   This paper raises new directions for future research. First, for simplicity, our empirical study focused
518   on a prototypical actor-critic method and left open the question of how histogram losses might be
519   integrated into more advanced policy gradient methods. For methods such as MPO (Abdolmaleki
520   et al., 2018), the application of our novel loss is immediate. For methods such as PPO or TRPO,
521   this integration requires creating a novel surrogate loss that can be optimized for multiple steps
522
      whereas this paper has only considered a single step of gradient descent. Second, prior work in
      discrete action spaces has noted limitations of softmax policies for non-stationary problems and
523
      proposed alternative policy update schemes Garg et al. (2022). It would be interesting to study
524   whether these losses show improvement over the Gaussian policy surrogate loss in non-stationary,
525   continuous control problems.

780
      In this section, we provide details of the evaluated environments.
781
782         1. Continuous Bandit: This domain has a single state and the reward is an unknown quadratic
783            function of a d-dimensional action. The range for possible actions in each dimension is
784            [amin , amax ].
785         2. Linear quadratic regulator: LQR is a fundamental control problem in control theory.
786            In this domain, the transition dynamics are a linear function of the state and action with
787            Gaussian noise added. The reward is a quadratic function of the state and action. The
788            action space is 2 dimensional where each dimension is bounded between [−1, 1].
789         3. Continous Mountain Car: In this domain, a toy car attempts to reach the top of a moun-
790            tain. The action space is 1 dimensional and is bounded between [−1, 1].
791         4. Continuous Acrobot: In this domain, an agent attempts to swing itself above a certain
792            height. The action space is 1 dimensional.
793         5. Reacher: In this domain, a robotic arm tries to reach a goal location. The action space is 2
794            dimensional and each dimension is bounded between [−1, 1].
795
            6. HalfCheetah: In this domain, a cheetah-like robotic agent attempts to run as fast as possi-
796            ble. The action space is 6 dimensional and each dimension is bounded between [−1, 1].
797
            7. Ant: In this domain, an ant-like robotic agent attempts to run as fast as possible. The action
798
               space is 8 dimensional and each dimension is bounded between [−1, 1].

      C.2   T RAINING D ETAILS
801
802   The base algorithm is an on-policy actor-critic algorithm and the actual code was based on Stable-
803   Baselines3’s implementation of the A2C algorithm (A2C itself is quite similar to the actor-critic
804   algorithm described in Sutton and Barto (2018)) (Raffin et al., 2021). Since it is on-policy, there is
805   no replay buffer. The critic is learned by regressing to observed returns.
806   For the continous bandit environment, all the algorithms (Gaussian regression, softmax + 1-hot,
807   softmax + HL-Gauss) used a linear policy, used a batch size of 5, and all used a value function
808   baseline. Each algorithm was trained for 2000 interaction steps and the policy was evaluated every
809   interaction step. We tuned the following hyperparameters. For all algorithms, we swept over the
      following values for the learning rate: {10−2 , 5 · 10−2 , 10−1 , 1}. For the two classification methods,

      we swept over the following number of bins (k): {50, 100, 200}. For softmax + HL-Gauss, we
811   swept over the following width multipliers (η): {0.1, 0.25, 0.5, 0.75, 1}.
812
813
      For all the other environments, all the algorithms used the default neural network policy in stable-
      baseline3 (Raffin et al., 2021), used a batch size of 20, and all used a value function baseline. On

      LQR, Continuous MountainCar, Reacher, and Acrobot, all algorithms were trained for 500K inter-

824   C.3   H YPERPARAMETER S ENSITIVITY E XPERIMENTS
825
826   In this section, we show the sensitivity of the softmax + HL-Gauss algorithm when varying the
827   learning rate, number of bins (k), and width mutliplier (η). We conduct the analysis over the values
828   discussed in Appendix C.2.
829   The sensitivity study is based on holding one hyperparameter fixed (e.g. learning rate) and averaging
830   the returns across all other variable hyperparameters. As such, the returns in the sensitivity studies
831   will always be worse (since high performing and low performing returns may get averaged together)
832   than the one in the main text which shows the highest possible return by a unique combination of
833   hyperparameters. We present the results in Figure 4.
834   From Figure 4, we find that softmax + HL-Gauss is sensitive to the learning rate, and performance
835   can widely differ based on the value of the learning rate. It is also sensitive to η, which determines
836   the standard deviation of the histogram Gaussian distribution, where if the standard deviation is too
837   large (larger η), performance tends to degrade, which is expected since the agent has a challenging
838   time to converge to the optimal actions. The method is quite robust to number of bins (k), where
      performance is generally stable across all k values.
839
840   With regards to the baselines, we see in Figure 5 and Figure 6 that the Gaussian regression method
841   and softmax with 1-hot histogram loss are also sensitive to the learning rates. And softmax 1-hot is
842   similarly robust to number of bins as softmax + HL-Gauss.
