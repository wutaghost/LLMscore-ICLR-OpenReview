009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) with Differential Privacy (DP) guarantees have
012            been proposed to preserve privacy when nodes contain sensitive information that
013            needs to be kept private but is critical for training. Existing methods deploy a fixed
014            uniform noise generation mechanism that lacks the flexibility to adjust between
015            nodes, leading to increasing the risk of graph information leakage and decreas-
016            ing the model’s overall performance. To address the above challenges, we pro-
017            pose NIP-GNN, a Node-level Individual Private GNN with DP guarantee based
018
               on the adaptive perturbation over sensitive components to safeguard node infor-
               mation. First, we propose a Topology-based Node Influence Estimation (TNIE)
019
               method to infer unknown node influence with neighborhood and centrality aware-
020
               ness. Second, given the obtained node influence rank, an adaptive private ag-
021            gregation method is proposed to perturb neighborhood embeddings directed by
022            node-wise influence. Third, we propose to privately train the graph learning al-
023            gorithm over perturbed aggregations in adaptive residual connection mode over
024            multi-layer convolution for node-wise tasks. Theoretically, analysis ensures that
025            NIP-GNN satisfies DP guarantee. Empirical experiments over real-world graph
026            datasets show that NIP-GNN presents a better resistance over node inference at-
027            tacks and achieves a better trade-off between privacy and accuracy.

030   1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032   In recent years, Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) have achieved outstanding performance in several
033
      domains, such as social analysis (Yang et al., 2021b), financial anomaly detection (Chen et al.,
      2020), time series analysis (Wang et al., 2021), and molecule synthesis (Gasteiger et al., 2021).
034
      Through aggregating the feature of neighboring nodes and fully mining and fusing the topological
035
      associations in graph, GNNs yield state-of-art performance in tasks such as link prediction (Zhao
036   et al., 2021), node classification (Guan et al., 2022), and sub-graph classification (Yang et al., 2021a).
037   However, graph data in the real world usually contain private information (Li et al., 2023). For
038   example, in social network graphs where nodes denote users, and edges indicate the existence of
039   social attributes like being friends. Node features carry sensitive information, such as the average
040   online time of users per week.
041
      Necessity and motivation of private GNN. Directly training over the graph contains sensitive in-
042   formation may lead to unignorable privacy leakage. Attackers have the ability to infer the existence
043   of arbitrary node, attribute or link, or specific graph-level statistic information like average degree,
044   by accessing GNN immediately (like GNN embedding) or final result (like GNN output), as shown
045   in membership inference attack (Wang & Wang, 2022; Zhang et al., 2022b), attribute inference at-
046   tack Olatunji et al. (2023a), edge stealing attack (Wu et al., 2022), and graph reconstruction attack
047   (Zhang et al., 2022b). This raises the necessity of protecting graph data privacy in GNN.
048   Among all the privacy-preserving technologies, Differential Privacy (DP) (Dwork et al., 2014)
049   emerges to be a widely used method for its advantage of strict theoretical guarantee (Zhang et al.,
050   2021) and flexible control of protection strength by adjusting privacy budget (Sala et al., 2011). Ex-
051   isting graph neural networks under DP guarantee can be divided into edge-level (Wu et al., 2022;
052   Zhu et al., 2023) and node-level (Daigavane et al., 2021; Sajadmanesh et al., 2022; Sajadmanesh &
053   Gatica-Perez, 2023) protection, where the former add the non-trivial calibrated noise into edges to
      prevent adversary from inferring edge existence (Wu et al., 2022; Zhu et al., 2023), and the latter


                                                          1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      add calibrated noise into loss gradients (Daigavane et al., 2021) or message-passing aggregations
055   (Sajadmanesh et al., 2022; Sajadmanesh & Gatica-Perez, 2023) to prevent adversary from inferring
056   node existence, including node feature, edges and label. We focus on the node-level DP on graph in
057   this paper considering it protects the more comprehensive information and is more challenging.
058
      However, existing GNNs under DP guarantee deploy a uniform noise generation mechanism that
059
      lacks the flexibility to adjust between nodes, leading to two problems: First, difficulty in adequately
060
      protecting high-influence nodes, increasing the risk of graph information leakage. Second, the in-
061   ability to meet the diverse privacy needs among nodes makes it hard to balance the trade-off between
062   graph privacy and model utility, ultimately affecting the model’s overall performance.
063
064
      Technical Challenges of Individualized Private GNN under DP. To the best of our knowledge, no
      existing fine-grained DP technologies specifically address the flexible privacy needs among nodes. It
065
      is non-trivial to satisfy the desired protection level from both the theoretical and practical aspects for
066
      GNNs with DP constraints. We contend that individualized differentially private GNN poses three
067   significant challenges, rendering other private learning over structure and graph data. (1). Typical
068   DP Stochastic Gradient Descent (DPSGD) based privacy technology is not directly suitable for
069   GNN. As a widely used technology, DPSGD (Abadi et al., 2016) adds noise over the clipped gra-
070   dient of data in the randomly selected batch. Notice that the calibrated noise is proportion to query
071   sensitivity over data, where sensitivity measures the largest impact of arbitrary sample (Dwork et al.,
072   2014). However, due to the complexity of topology links, the node-level query sensitivity of GNNs
073   is high since each node influences its neighbors in message-passing and aggregation. When ap-
074   plying DPSGD with GNN optimization, sensitivity escalates from the clipped norm to the batch
075
      size. (2). Complexity of individualized noise calibration and injection. Nodes’ various roles
      and influence (Scripps et al., 2007; Lawyer, 2015) in the graph complicate the individualized noise
076
      calibration. The proposed fine-grained privacy assessment and noise generation scheme needs to
077
      consider features such as node degree, and centrality. (3). Optimize the negative effect of injected
078   noise on private GNN utility. Private GNNs inevitably sacrifice utility to ensure model privacy
079   (Sajadmanesh & Gatica-Perez, 2023), which poses the effort to better balance the trade-off between
080   GNN utility and graph data privacy. Fine-grained noise injection leads to differentiated noise accu-
081   mulating and propagating through multiple layers and neighbor aggregations. Therefore, gradually
082   mitigating the noise’s negative impact on model utility during the neighbor message-passing layers
083   is a key direction.
084   Our Solutions and Contributions. To address these challenges, we propose a Node-level Indi-
085   vidual Private Graph Neural Network (NIP-GNN) with DP guarantee, which flexibly adjusts node
086   protection level based on learnable influence and independent of the training epoch. Our goal is to
087   develop a fine-grained and adaptive differentially private GNN that distributes a more granular pri-
088   vacy budget and achieves a better trade-off between utility and privacy. First, Topology-based Node
089   Impact Estimation (TNIE) method is proposed to capture node influence with neighborhood and
090   centrality awareness adjustments. Second, we propose an influence-awareness fine-grained permu-
091   tation method to realize node-diverse privacy-preserving distribution in GNN by injecting diversity-
092
      calibrated noise into immediate aggregations. Third, an adaptive message-passing layer with resid-
      ual selective cooperation is proposed to improve model utility by optimizing the negative effect of
093
      previously injected noise. We show theoretically that NIP-GNN satisfies the DP guarantee. Experi-
094
      ments on real graph datasets show that the proposed method achieves a better trade-off between the
095   utility-privacy, compared with existing private GNNs. The main contributions are summarized as:
096
097          • We study a new solution of differentially private GNN, Node-level Individual Private Graph
098            Neural Network (NIP-GNN), with flexible privacy calibration and adaptive aggregation
099            layer to achieve better utility. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first work to focus
100            on the fine-grained DP mechanism for GNN.
101          • We propose the Topology-based Node Influence Estimation (TNIE) method to infer nodes’
102            impact over local and global structures directly by neighborhood and centrality awareness.
103            Differentially private graph embedding is obtained by calibrating fine-grained noise among
104            nodes, which is determined by influence rank, and injecting it into immediate embeddings.
105            The adaptive layer is proposed to improve utility by mitigating noise’s impact during neigh-
106
               bor message-passing and update by selective optimization.
107          • Theoretical analysis proves that NIP-GNN satisfies DP requirement. The experimental
               results on four benchmark graph datasets demonstrate the prior utility of NIP-GNN.

      2     R ELATED W ORKS
109
110   Recently, there have been several attempts to use DP to provide node-level and edge-level privacy
111   guarantees in GNNs. For node-level protection, existing methods can be divided into two types:
112   gradient-based and aggregation-based perturbation. For gradient-based methods, Daigavane et al.
113   Daigavane et al. (2021) proposed a one-layer node-level private GNN by extending the DPSGD al-
114   gorithm to a degree-bounded graph. But Daigavane et al. (2021) is limited to one-layer GNN and can
115   not preserve privacy for high-layer aggregations. Zhang et al. (2022a) proposes to convert the aggre-
116   gation over the edge into aggregation over approximate personalized PageRank vectors to achieve
117   edge-level protection. The node-level protection in the training process is achieved with DPSGD.
118
      For aggregation-based perturbation methods, Sajadmanesh et al. (2022) provides a private GNN by
      injecting uniform noise on the neighbor aggregation vectors. ProGAP proposed in Sajadmanesh
119
      & Gatica-Perez (2023) proposes to split the GNN training process into overlapping sub-models to
120
      achieve node-level and edge-level protection. The other edge-level protection methods over GNN,
121   privacy attacks in GNNs, and existing privacy budget allocation methods over traditional deep learn-
122   ing are shown in Appendix A.1.
123
124
      Despite the presence of uniform noise injection methods in differentially private GNNs and fine-
      grained noise allocation schemes in differentially private DL, such considerations are still missing in
125
      differentially private GNNs. The graph structure poses new challenges of noise complex calibration
126
      to DP technologies, existing fine-grained DP methods can not be directly used in GNN.

186
187   Theorem 1 (Post-processing (Dwork & Lei, 2009)). Post-processing to any ϵ-DP algorithm’s output

      Based on the above definition, the problem of private GNN under DP constraints can be defined as:
193   Definition 4 (ϵ-Node-level Differentially Private Graph Neural Network). Give a graph G with
194   nodes containing sensitive information, a well-trained GNN model F is a ϵ-Node-level Differentially
195   Private Graph Neural Network if for any Node-level adjacent graph G′ of G and any outputs S of
196   F, we have:
197                                   Pr[F(G) ∈ S] ≤ eϵ Pr[F(G′ ) ∈ S]                                (5)
198
199   Therefore, the key of this paper is to propose a specific GNN model and design DP mechanisms,
200   then consider how to incorporate the proposed DP mechanisms into the GNN training and inference
201
      phase to protect the training graph data from being theft, while keeping the private GNN model
      utility to satisfy downstream task requirements.

      We propose our NIP-GNN composing Topology-based Node Influence Estimation (TNIE), Node-
210   Influence-Grained Adaptive Permutation (NAP) and Adaptive Calibrated Aggregation (ACA). TNIE
211   measures node influence in the graph directed by topology-related and feature-related awareness.
212   NAP proposes to calibrate adaptive noise tailored for estimated node influence sequence constraint
213   and permutes the propagation layer by adding generated node-wise adaptive noise. The analysis
214   shows that NAP satisfies the DP definition. ACA proposes an adaptive aggregation layer with resid-
215   ual connection to balance and smooth the protective noise introduced by NAP. We also present an
      analysis on the total privacy level of the whole process.

303   Laplace noise introduced by previous modules inevitably affects GNN performance. To achieve
304   a better privacy-utility trade-off, we propose ACA, which adaptively aggregates noisy embeddings
305   using residual connections across layers. ACA smooths the noise by iteratively and selectively align-
306   ing noisy neighbor aggregations with the node’s own private sum embedding, based on the intuition
307   that consistent information flow can mitigate noise impact while preserving essential features. ACA
308   takes the H0 as input and outputs the final embeddings Compared with equally aggregating neigh-
309   bors embedding, ACA allows each node to learn from different neighbors with different weights.

      6   C ONCLUSION
516
      In this work, we proposed a Node-level Individual Private Graph Neural Network (NIP-GNN) that
517
      flexibly adjusts node protection levels based on learnable influence, independent of the training
518
      epoch, to address the privacy-utility trade-off in GNNs. We introduced the Topology-based Node
519   Impact Estimation (TNIE) method to capture node influence through neighborhood and centrality
520   awareness. Additionally, we developed an influence-aware fine-grained permutation method that
521   injects diversity-calibrated noise to achieve node-level privacy-preserving distributions. To further
522   mitigate the negative impact of noise on model utility, we designed an adaptive message-passing
523   layer with residual selective cooperation. Our theoretical analysis confirms that NIP-GNN satisfies
524   differential privacy guarantees, and experimental results on real-world graph datasets demonstrate
525   that our approach achieves a better utility-privacy trade-off compared to existing private GNN meth-
526   ods. Notably, this work is the first to demonstrate the applicability of differentiated noise addition
527
      in GNNs, providing a new direction for future research in privacy-preserving graph learning.

      A.1.1    OTHER D IFFERENTIALLY PRIVATE GNN S
708
709
710   The Locally Private Graph Neural Network (LPGNN) Sajadmanesh & Gatica-Perez (2021) realizes
711
      the GNN model framework under Local Differential Privacy (LDP) guarantee in a decentralized
      setting. Solitude Lin et al. (2022) preserves edge-level and node-level simultaneously under LDP
712
      by calibrating the added noise on the graph. Another edge-level differential privacy GNN algorithm
713
      was proposed in Wu et al. (2022) by perturbing the adjacency matrix of the graph through the
714   Laplace mechanism. Blink in Zhu et al. (2023) provides edge-level LDP via spending the privacy
715   budget separately on links and degrees of the graph and denoising the topology using Bayesian
716   estimation. PrivGNN Olatunji et al. (2023b) provides node-level DP by adapting the framework of
717   PATE Papernot et al. (2016). The student GNN model is trained using public graph data, where each
718   node is privately labeled by teacher GNN models. These teacher models are exclusively trained for
719   their respective query nodes. However, PrivGNN is dependent on the availability of public graph
720   data. DPDGC Chien et al. (2023) proposed a unified notation, Graph Differential Privacy (GDP), in
721   GNN. It points out that topology and nodes may need multi-granular protection.
722
723
724   A.1.2    P RIVACY ATTACKS IN GNN S
725
726   Several studies have explored the privacy leakage in GNNs, involving membership inference attacks
727   Wang & Wang (2022); Zhang et al. (2022b), attribute inference attacks Olatunji et al. (2023a), model
728   stealing attacks Shen et al. (2022), edge stealing attacks Wu et al. (2022); He et al. (2021) and graph
729   reconstruction attacks Zhang et al. (2022b). Authors in Wang & Wang (2022) infer the node & link
730   group distribution under both white-box and black-box settings. Attribute inference attacks aim to
731   infer the node attribute of the graph with only access to GNN output or embedding. The Study in
732   Olatunji et al. (2023a) shows that even under the black-box setting, the attacker is able to infer the
733   sensitive attribute by some public attributes and graph structure. Work in Zhang et al. (2022b) aims
734
      to infer the basic group properties like node and edge number, and graph density and determine
      whether the subgraph exists in the original graph and graph reconstruction attacks with only black-
735
      box access. A study in Shen et al. (2022) shows that pure GNNs face model stealing risks. Edge
736
      stealing attack aims to infer whether there exists a link between any pair of nodes with only access
737   to GNN outputs. Experiment results in Wu et al. (2022); He et al. (2021) show that the existence of
738   edges is vulnerable due to graph attributes like heterogeneous.
739
740
741
742
      A.1.3    P RIVACY B UDGET A LLOCATION
743
744   For traditional differentially private DL methods, several works have proposed privacy bud-
745   get allocation strategies to address specific scenarios. It can be broadly categorized into three
746   types: Preference-based Allocation, Sensitivity-based Allocation, and Utility-based Allocation. In
747
      preference-based allocation, Li et al. (2017) introduces a sensitivity-based differential privacy bud-
      get partitioning mechanism to meet the different privacy preferences of data owners. Boenisch
748
      et al. (2024) focuses on grouping and allocating according to each user’s privacy needs, proposing a
749
      demand-based sampling and scaling mechanism that ensures data points with higher privacy budgets
750   are sampled more frequently during training and adjusts the scale of noise accordingly. In utility-
751   based allocation, Niu et al. (2020) proposes the Utility-aware Personalized Exponential Mechanism
752   (UPEM), which considers the quantitative changes of the Personalized Exponential Mechanism to
753   enhance the overall utility of the model. Feldman & Zrnic (2021) introduces individualized privacy
754   accounting, where privacy budgets are allocated based on the privacy loss of each data point dur-
755   ing training. In risk-based allocation, Jorgensen et al. (2015) proposes a personalized differential
      privacy budget allocation method based on individualized risk levels.
