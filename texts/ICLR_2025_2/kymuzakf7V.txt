009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Recent breakthroughs in large language models (LLMs) have opened the door to
012            in-depth investigation of their potential in tabular data modeling. However, the
013            paradigm for effectively utilizing advanced LLMs in few-shot and even unseen
014            scenarios remains to be explored. We observed an unusual phenomenon: directly
015            using LLMs for data augmentation or rule generation by feeding a few exam-
016            ples significantly degrades the reasoning ability in tabular data understanding. We
017
               identified two main obstacles behind this issue: overfitting to the examples and
               knowledge disruption. Specifically, the provided examples may introduce noisy
018
               patterns that interfere with the model’s prior knowledge, leading to unexpected
019
               and less reliable results. To this end, we propose an example-free framework to
020            leverage the inherent knowledge of LLMs. Our key idea is to prompt the LLM
021            for feature value generation based solely on task and feature description. Without
022            such example pollution, each output feature value is treated as a standard guide-
023            line, and they together act as a prototype for each class. To transfer the LLM’s
024            knowledge to a given task, we further design an efficient fusion strategy to in-
025            tegrate the prototype with examples, showing impressive generalizability in the
026            few-shot setting. Importantly, our pipeline requires no learnable variables, result-
027            ing in a desired training-free property. Extensive comparisons and ablations on
028
               multiple tabular datasets demonstrate the improvements of our simple framework.
029
030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Large Language Models (LLMs) have shown impressive understanding abilities for solving unseen
033   tasks. Functional as both knowledge repositories and reasoning engines, they are often viewed as
034   the holy grail in recent machine learning fields (Achiam et al., 2023; Badaro et al., 2023; Dubey
035   et al., 2024). This sparks a research trend that focuses on applying LLMs to tabular data analysis.
036   Tabular data, which consists of structured rows and columns (or samples and features), is a critical
037   format in industries such as finance (Arun et al., 2016; Clements et al., 2020), healthcare (Ulmer
038   et al., 2020; Zhou et al., 2020), and more (Buczak & Guven, 2015; Guo et al., 2017). Unlike textual
039
      sequences, the features in tabular data are often heterogeneous, and their interrelationships are not
      inherently sequential. This complexity poses significant challenges for tabular data learning, partic-
040
      ularly in real-world few-shot constraints, where algorithms are expected to exploit prior knowledge
041
      to learn effectively from limited labeled samples. In contrast, traditional algorithms often struggle
042   to optimize in such constrained settings (Ucar et al., 2021; Han et al., 2024).
043
044   To tackle such knowledge deficit issues, recent research has focused on integrating tabular learning
045
      into language generation pipelines, leveraging LLMs to enhance few-shot performance. Pre-trained
      in extensive datasets with vast parameters, LLMs embody a rich repository of prior knowledge and
046
      demonstrate a near-human level of comprehension (Brown et al., 2020; Bommasani et al., 2021;
047
      Chen et al., 2023; Touvron et al., 2023; Kong et al., 2024). Notably, they can quickly adapt to
048   new tasks through task-oriented prompts. This prompt-based approach is not only user-friendly but
049   also significantly enhances the model’s ability to transfer pre-trained knowledge to novel and un-
050   seen scenarios efficiently. In alignment with these principles, recent attempts have designed prompt
051   templates such as “<Meta-Info>-<Example>-<Query>”, where <Meta-Info>, <Example> and
052   <Query> denote the task and feature descriptions, few-shot examples, and user queries, respec-
053   tively, outperforming conventional tabular learning baselines in few-shot regimes. For instance,
      TabLLM (Hegselmann et al., 2023) evaluates several ways of serializing tabular examples into nat-

      While these example-based LLMs have achieved impressive results, we observe an unusual phe-
074   nomenon: poor-quality examples could degrade the reasoning ability of LLMs. In few-shot learning,
075   the provided examples typically serve as an empirical approximation of the true data distribution;
076   unfortunately, they often fail to capture the full complexity and variability of underlying patterns,
077   particularly in the context of heterogeneous tabular features (Harari & Katz, 2022; Jin et al., 2023;
078   Li et al., 2024b). Moreover, due to the limited number of examples, these samples may contain spu-
079   rious correlations or causality, introducing irrelevant features or misleading guidance. As shown in
080   Figure. 1, example-based LLMs attempt to learn from the given examples, they may overfit to biased
081   patterns, which could conflict with their pretrained knowledge, leading to inaccurate predictions and
082
      undermined the ability to generalize effectively to new, unseen data. Similar challenges have also
      been observed in recent studies in the fields of computer vision and natural language processing (Li
083
      et al., 2024a; Liu et al., 2024).
084
085   To address above overfitting and knowledge pollution issues, in this paper, we propose ProtoLLM,
086   which is an example-free framework to employ LLMs as prototype builders for zero and few-shot
087   tabular data classification. Intuitively, as collecting high-quality examples is often nontrivial in
088
      practice, we ask whether one can draw out the task-specific knowledge of LLMs with example-free
      inputs. We are inspired by recent success in zero-shot learning literature, which queries LLMs only
089
      with task descriptions and demonstrates that LLMs are decent zero-shot reasoners (Kojima et al.,
090
      2022; Wang et al., 2023). Specifically, we remove the <Example> term from the prompt template
091   and encourage LLMs to generate feature values according to their inherent knowledge about the
092   given tasks. This simple strategy avoids the introduction of poor-quality examples, allowing LLMs
093   as human experts to think using task-specific common sense, and thus predict the reasonable value
094   “high” in the case of Figure 1. We highlight the following advantages of the proposed ProtoLLM:
095
096          • Training and Example-Free. Applying ProtoLLM to tabular data classification requires
097            no learnable variable. More importantly, querying ProtoLLM is zero-shot. LLMs take the
098
               prompt “<Meta-Info>-<Query>” as input and output the discriminative feature values
               for each class, resulting in a novel example-free framework for tabular data learning. We
099
               further propose to build the class-level prototype by combining the few-shot examples and
100
               generated feature values from LLMs in the observed space. Therefore, we can classify the
101            test sample directly by comparing its Euclidean distance with the prototype of each class,
102            making ours a training-free method.
103
104          • Feature Value Generation by LLM. Unlike traditional data augmentations that employ
105            LLMs to generate all features of a tabular sample simultaneously, we focus on querying
106            LLMs feature by feature. This feature-level generation relieves LLMs from the complex
107            inter-feature relationships, resulting in a meaningful feature discovery. Furthermore, this
               trick can also act as a novel data augmentation tool after a simple concatenation along the

142   2    R ELATED WORK
143
144   Few-shot Tabular Data Learning. The development of effective algorithms for few-shot tabular
145   learning has long been a popular research topic, due to their wide variety of applications (Borisov
146   et al., 2022; Kadra et al., 2021; Sattarov et al., 2023). Inspired by the great success across various
147
      modalities (Schick & Schütze, 2020; Wang et al., 2024), previous works have proposed a num-
      ber of few-shot learning frameworks using different techniques, including Bayesian inference, data
148
      augmentation, self-supervised learning and others. For example, TabPFN (Hollmann et al., 2023)
149
      design a prior to model complex uncertainty of tabular data and show promising performance in
150   small tabular classification tasks. STUNT (Nam et al., 2023) uses unlabeled data to generate diverse
151   few-shot tasks, demonstrating that meta-learning is an effective solution for tabular data, while some
152   works show that contrastive learning is another option to learn general features (Verma et al., 2021;
153   Shenkar & Wolf, 2022). As a follow-up, a series of self-supervised learning methods are proposed
154   for tabular data, they often adopt data augmentation trick and reconstruction loss to extract useful
155   patterns (Majmundar et al., 2022; Ucar et al., 2021; Bahri et al., 2022).
156   Understanding Tabular Data with LLMs. The impressive performance of LLMs has highlighted
157   their broad knowledge and potential in instruction following and low-shot understanding (Ouyang
158   et al., 2022; Brown et al., 2020). This motivated recent attempts to integrate LLMs with tabular data
159   learning (Manikandan et al., 2023; Hollmann et al., 2024; Dinh et al., 2022; Wen et al., 2024). For
160   instance, Curated LLM (Seedat et al., 2023) leverages the in-context capabilities of LLMs for data
161   augmentation and shows that LLMs are high-quality tabular data generators. TabLLM (Hegselmann
      et al., 2023) takes tabular examples as input and fine-tunes LLMs on few-shot tabular datasets,


                                                                                      3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      showing competitive results with traditional tree-based models. P2T (Nam et al., 2024) propose a
163   transfer learning framework based on LLM that facilitates classification with a limited amount of
164   labeled data. To avoid end-to-end prediction and fine-tuning in utilizing LLMs, FeatLLM (Han et al.,
165   2024) employs LLMs to extract rules for better prediction while achieving relatively low inference
166   time. Our ProtoLLM is most relevant to these LLM-based models but differs from them in terms of
167   feature generation and label prediction.

      3     M ETHODS
170
171   This work introduces ProtoLLM, a novel training-free and example-free framework for integrating
172   LLMs into tabular few-shot classification. The overview of ProtoLLM is shown in Fig. 2. The core
173   idea behind ProtoLLM is to generate discriminative feature values by querying LLMs with example-
174
      free prompts, as described in Sec. 3.1. This avoids knowledge pollution in previous example-based
      models and ensures the output features come solely from LLMs. Given the generated values of each
175
      feature, we design a training-free method to build prototype and classify new samples, as described
176
      in Sec. 3.2. Ours provides a promising solution to use LLMs and examples more efficiently.
177
178   Problem Formulation. We consider a dataset with N samples, denoted as S = {(xn , yn )}N              n=1 ,
179   where N is usually small in low-shot tasks and N = 0 means the zero-shot setting. Each sample xn
180
      consists of D features in total. xdn denotes the d-th feature of xn , which can be either a numerical
      feature or a categorical feature. Specifically, if xdn is a numerical feature, then xdn ∈ R and rep-
181
      resents a scalar value. If xdn is a categorical feature, it is represented as a one-hot encoded vector
182
      with 1 denoting the corresponding category. The label yn ∈ {1, . . . , C} indicates the class of the
183   sample, with C being the total number of classes. Denote F = {ftask , ffeat1:D
                                                                                     } as the set of descriptive
184   information for the dataset, which are usually available in tabular dataset. Specifically, ftask is the
185                                          d
      information related to the task and ffeat is the descriptions corresponding to the d-th feature. Taking
186                                                                          d
      the Adult dataset as the example, “education” is the d-th feature, ffeat  now includes the explanation
187   about the “education” itself, e.g. education level, and its feature values, e.g. primary, secondary,
188   tertiary, unknown. Learning from S, ProtoLLM aims to predict the label of new data.

190   3.1   F EATURE VALUE G ENERATION BY LLM
191
192   To draw out the prior knowledge of LLMs and enable them to analyze our problem like a human
193   expert, as illustrated in Fig.3, we carefully design an example-free prompt by proposing a novel way.
                                                                          d
194   Specifically, for d-th feature, we denote the prompt as P (ftask , ffeat ) = [<Meta-Info>-<Query>],
195
      where we shorten it to Pd for convenience. The prompt is started with “you are an expert in ...”, a
      classic and shared sentence in prompt engineering. Then, we design “<Meta-Info>” by introducing
196
      the information about task and the d-th feature. It is used to provide basic information into LLMs.
197
      Besides, “<Query>” is constructed with a reasoning instruction followed by a requirement of output
198   formation. For the d-th feature, we query LLMs with the prompt template Pd and expect that LLMs
199   output correct feature values for each class. We describe this process in more detail below.
200
201
      Task and Feature Information in <Meta-Info>. For LLMs, it’s crucial to provide a clear task
      description and detailed feature information. The task description explains the objective, scope, and
202
      expected outcomes. As shown in Fig.3, we summarize the <Meta-Info> in red words. For the
203
      Adult dataset, the task is “Does this person earn more than 50000 dollars per year? Yes or no?”.
204   Each feature description outlines the input variables used in prediction, clarifying their roles. For
205   instance, “relationship” refers to the individual’s family role, such as spouse or child, which can
206   impact income potential. The description is “What this individual is relative to others”. Designed
207   in this way, the meta-information (task and feature descriptions) allows LLMs to understand the
208   current task and leverage their prior knowledge for generating representative feature values.
209
      Reasoning Instruction & Response Format in <Query>. The objective of the prompt is to guide
210   the LLM in generating possible values for each target class based on a given feature. Motivated
211   by the recent chain-of-thought (CoT) tricks (Wei et al., 2022b; Lyu et al., 2023), we construct the
212   reasoning instruction with two steps, i.e., green words in Fig.3. First, we ask LLMs to analyze the
213   potential causality of the task and feature based on the provided information in “<Meta-Info>”.
214   This allows LLMs to mobilize their pre-trained knowledge about the question. Then, given the
215   output analysis, LLMs are encouraged to infer the possible feature values for each class, where we
      devise the response format with blue words in Fig.3.

315   Feature-level Prior Generation. Here, we analyze the proposed example-free strategy in more
316   depth. Firstly, ProtoLLM generates features in a zero-shot manner. This not only satisfies the
317
      practical need for tabular data but also avoids knowledge pollution, providing an efficient solution to
      utilize the prior knowledge of LLMs. Secondly, ProtoLLM focuses on a single feature at each query
318
      time. Unlike previous works that generate all features (or important features) directly, our feature-
319
      level strategy transforms the complex reasoning problem into D tractable sub-problems, allowing
320   LLMs to highlight the correlations between the target label and current feature, showing promising
321   presentation learning compared to sample-level methods (Tab. 1). Lastly, the generated features
322   can also be used as augmented samples. As discussed above, these LLM-generated feature values

348   These datasets cover fields such as financial, medical, and recommendation, varying in size and
349   complexity. Each dataset contains the corresponding name and description for each attribute, which
350   serves as meta-information in our example-free prompt. We summarize all datasets in Appendix A.2.

352   Baselines. We compare ProtoLLM against three types of baselines: (1) Traditional machine learn-
353   ing methods. This category includes Logistic Regression (LogReg), K-Nearest Neighbors (KNN),
354   and XGBoost, representing a range of commonly used algorithms and showing robust results in tab-
355   ular data analysis. (2) Deep learning methods. We here examine methods specifically designed for
356   tabular few-shot learning, including STUNT (Nam et al., 2023), TabPFN (Hollmann et al., 2023),
357
      and a 2-layer MLP for comparison. (3) LLM-based framework. This includes methods such as In-
      context Learning (Wei et al., 2022a), TABLET (Slack & Singh, 2023), TabLLM (Hegselmann et al.,
358
      2023), and FeatLLM (Han et al., 2024). Specifically, we serialize tabular data in a manner similar
359
      to TabLLM, but we query GPT-3.5 to ensure a fair comparison with our method in a zero-shot sce-
360   nario. All of which utilize LLMs’ prior knowledge to enhance performance in few-shot tabular data
361   prediction. These LLM-based models are most closely related to our ProtoLLM. Generally, they
362   focus on leveraging LLMs by feeding examples, we aim to explore an example-free framework to
363   draw out priors, avoiding the mentioned knowledge pollution and overfitting issues.
364
365   Implementation Details. Following FeatLLM, we select gpt-3.5-turbo-0613 as our base LLM
366   by default. The number of query times K is set to 10 for each feature across all datasets and the
367   temperature τ in Eq. 4 is fixed at 1. For the baseline models, the results for STUNT, In-context
368   Learning, TABLET, TabLLM, and FeatLLM are derived from Han et al. (2024). We set the number
369   of samples per class as the same as query number K in k-Nearest Neighbors (KNN), and use a hidden
370
      dimension of 1024 for Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP). Implementation details for other baselines
      can be found in the Appendix A.4.

      4.3   F URTHER D ISCUSSION
433
434   Given the strong performance on both zero and few-shot settings, we next aim to dig out which
435   components of ProtoLLM play a central role in prompting LLM in tabular data learning. To get
436   the final prototypes, ProtoLLM designs two novel strategies: example-free prompts and feature-
437   level generation. The former ensures a clean output of LLMs solely based on their common sense,
438   and the latter helps LLMs ignore complex correlations between tabular features and focus on the
439   true causality of a given feature and the target label. Consequently, we introduce three variants
440
      of ProtoLLM and conduct extensive ablations. Specifically, For the first factor, we modify our
      example-free prompt by inserting few-shot tabular examples. For the feature-level factor, we ask
441
      LLMs to generate all D feature values simultaneously. We report the ablation results in Tab. 1.

445   strates superior performance by fusing the intrinsic characteristics of individual features, which
446
      facilitates the construction of more generalized and robust prototypes. Another key observation is
      that adding examples to both feature-level and sample-level generation led to a noticeable decrease
447
      in performance, indicating the presence of example pollution.

532
       We propose ProtoLLM, a training and example-free framework for zero and few-shot tabular data
533
       classification. This provides a novel strategy to combine prior knowledge of LLMs and data likeli-
534    hood for prototype construction. We show that it is possible to efficiently draw out clean common
535    sense of LLMs and generate feature values by designing example-free prompts, and that doing so
536    effectively avoids knowledge pollution and overfitting issues in previous example-based LLMs. Fi-
537    nally, we also showcase our ProtoLLM can be used as data augmentation and boosts traditional
538    algorithms. A number of comparisons and ablations on 10 datasets demonstrate the superior perfor-
539    mance of our approach. Our ProtoLLM is LLM-agnostic and can be benefit from stronger LLM. We
       hope that our approach provide valuable insights into the utility of LLMs for tabular data analysis.

1245   process, both the task and all relevant feature descriptions are provided to the LLMs to ensure a

1247   detailed instructions and the required response format are included. Below is an example using the

       A.13    L IMITATIONS
1405
1406   Limitations. This work introduces an example-free and training-free ProtoLLM for zero and few-
1407   shot tabular classification tasks. It prompts the LLM for feature value generation based solely on task
1408   and feature description, making it difficult to apply to datasets without such describing information.
1409   Besides, our ProtoLLM prompts the LLM without the training examples and designs the training-
1410   free prototype for classification instead of training a classifier. Therefore, it is more suitable for the
1411   zero and extreme few-shot regimes. With the increasing number of training samples, our approach
1412
       is gradually closer to or inferior to other methods that depends on training samples and a learnable
       classifier, which is an interesting direction and can be considered in future work.
