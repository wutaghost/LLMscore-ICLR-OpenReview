011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            In recent years, the field of explainable artificial intelligence (XAI) has gained sig-
014            nificant traction, with concept bottleneck models (CBMs) emerging as a promising
015
               approach to enhance the interpretability of machine learning systems. However,
               CBMs often rely on expert-annotated concepts, which can be costly and time-
016
               consuming to acquire. To address this limitation, unsupervised and label-free
017
               CBMs have been proposed, but these come with their own challenges, partic-
018            ularly in assessing the reliability and accuracy of the generated concepts without
019            ground-truth labels. This paper introduces a comprehensive evaluation framework
020            designed to assess the quality of explanations produced by unsupervised CBMs.
021            Our framework comprises a set of novel metrics that evaluate various aspects of
022            the concept outputs, including their relevance, consistency, and informativeness.
023            We demonstrate the effectiveness of our metrics through a series of experiments,
024            showing certain positive correlations between our scores and both LLM evalua-
025            tions and human judgments. Our work not only fills a critical gap in the evaluation
026
               of unsupervised CBMs but also provides a solid foundation for further research
               into more transparent and trustworthy AI systems.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   The quest for explainable artificial intelligence (XAI) has led to the development of concept bottle-
032   neck models (CBMs), which strive to enhance transparency and interpretability in complex machine
033   learning (ML) systems. CBMs rely on human-interpretable concepts to mediate predictions, yet
034   their reliance on manually annotated concepts poses a significant bottleneck, limiting scalability and
035   applicability Lai et al. (2023).
036   Recent advancements in unsupervised and label-free CBMs have attempted to overcome this chal-
037   lenge by automatically extracting concepts from data Oikarinen et al. (2023); Yuksekgonul et al.
038   (2023). While these methods alleviate the annotation burden, they introduce new evaluation com-
039   plexities. The absence of ground-truth labels makes it difficult to assess the quality and relevance of
040   the learned concepts, and even when labels are available, they may not align perfectly with the unsu-
041   pervised outputs due to distributional differences and inherent gaps between labeled and unlabeled
042   data Wei et al. (2021). Traditional metrics, such as concept accuracy, fail to capture these nuances,
043   necessitating a more comprehensive evaluation framework.
044   Motivated by the need for robust and interpretable evaluations, we propose a novel approach tai-
045   lored to unsupervised CBMs. Our framework encompasses a suite of metrics designed to quan-
046   titatively assess concept quality across multiple dimensions, including relevance, consistency, and
047   informativeness. We introduce ConceptScore, which leverages the Long-CLIP Zhang et al. (2024)
048   architecture to measure the semantic coherence between predicted concepts and data points, and
049   Ref-ConceptScore, an extension that incorporates ground-truth labels for a more comprehensive
050
      evaluation when available.
051   To provide a holistic perspective, we integrate established natural language processing (NLP) met-
052   rics like BLEUPapineni et al. (2002), METEORBanerjee & Lavie (2005), and ROUGELin (2004),
053   complementing quantitative measures with qualitative assessments through GPT-based scoring and
      human evaluations. We validate the consistency and reliability of our proposed metrics by comput-

084   2     R ELATED W ORK
085
086
      In the field of deep learning, model interpretability has remained an important yet challenging topic.
      Neural network models are often perceived as ”black boxes,” and their lack of transparency can lead
087
      to user distrust in model predictions, posing potential risks in their application Loi et al. (2022). To
088
      address this issue, researchers have been exploring methods to enhance the interpretability of deep
089   learning models. Early work primarily focused on local interpretability, employing techniques that
090   identify the most significant parts of the input data for specific decisions, thus providing approxi-
091   mate explanations for model predictions Ribeiro et al. (2016); Lundberg & Lee (2017). However,
092   these locally approximated methods do not always guarantee accuracy and may require substantial
093   subjective analysis. As research continues to advance, conceptual bottleneck models are gradually
094   being integrated into mainstream interpretability efforts.
095
096   2.1   C ONCEPT B OTTLENECK M ODELS
097
098   CBMs have been extensively studied in interpretability by introducing an intermediate layer, where
099
      interpretable concepts are mapped to neurons in the middle layers. Koh et al. (2020a) introduced
      the CBMs , which incorporates an intermediate concept layer to guide the model’s focus on high-
100
      level concepts relevant to the prediction class during the inference process. This approach improved
101
      interpretability by introducing concepts and allowed the correction of final predictions by adjusting
102   erroneous concepts, thereby enhancing model accuracy. Espinosa Zarlenga et al. (2022) proposed
103   the Concept Embedding Model (CEM) , which addresses the trade-off between accuracy and inter-
104   pretability in existing CBMs by learning high-dimensional concept representations. CEM provides
105   robust concept explanations while maintaining high task accuracy and supports efficient test-time
106   concept interventions. Yuksekgonul et al. (2023) developed the Post-hoc Concept Bottleneck Model
107   (PCBM), which can derive concept representations from textual descriptions using multimodal mod-
      els without concept labels. Then, the model maps these concept subspaces to an interpretable pre-


                                                                       2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      dictor. This approach transforms pre-trained models into CBMs and introduces a residual modeling
109   step to restore the original prediction performance, ensuring that the model’s initial predictive ca-
110   pabilities are preserved. Oikarinen et al. (2023) proposed a Concept Bottleneck Model framework
111   that does not require concept annotations. This framework utilizes GPT-3 to generate and filter con-
112   cept sets. It introduces a sparse prediction layer to highlight the importance of relevant concepts by
113   mapping the backbone network’s features to an interpretable concept space without annotation. This
114   further improves the model’s interpretability and detection capability.
115   However, there has been less attention to evaluation metrics for interpretability, particularly in un-
116   supervised concept learning, where objective and quantitative methods for assessing interpretability
117   are notably lacking.
118
119   2.2   C ONCEPTS E VALUATION METHODS
120
121   Most existing evaluation methods assess interpretability by focusing on the accuracy of the con-
122   cepts Wah et al. (2011); Nevitt et al. (2006); Koh et al. (2020a) or by treating concept learning
123   as embeddings in high-dimensional spaces, analyzing the consistency of information within these
124   embeddings Espinosa Zarlenga et al. (2022). However, these approaches rely heavily on labeled
125   concepts, lacking clear interpretability metrics for unsupervised concept bottleneck models.
126   Therefore, this paper first identifies two key issues in unsupervised explainable evaluation. The
127   first issue is the lack of quantifiable automatic evaluation methods, and the second is the need for
128   these methods to exhibit a high correlation with human judgments. In the context of automated
129   unsupervised interpretability assessment, the emphasis is on evaluating the consistency between the
130   generated concepts and the corresponding features of the images. Utilizing pre-trained cross-modal
131   models to obtain consistent assessments of descriptive features Hessel et al. (2021) provides valuable
132
      insights. Similarly, even in scenes with concept annotations, discriminative methods only based on
      natural language matching fail to adequately incorporate image features, leading to evaluation chal-
133
      lenges and difficulties in achieving high consistency with human judgmentsStefanini et al. (2023).
134
135   Based on the analysis above, our framework addresses the identified needs for explainable evalua-
136   tion. It leverages cross-modal models to assess the generated concepts from multiple dimensions,
137   incorporating semantic and visual features. This approach ensures a comprehensive consideration
138
      of the alignment between descriptions and features, achieving a high consistency level with human
      judgment results.

143   3.1   N OTATION FOR G ENERAL CBM S
144
145   Building upon the notation established by Koh et al. (2020b), we introduce CBMs. Consider a
146   classification problem defined over a pre-defined set of concepts C = {c1 , . . . , cL } and a training
147   dataset D = {(xi , ci , yi )}ni=1 . Here, for each instance, i within the dataset, xi ∈ Rd represents the
148   feature vector, yi ∈ Rdz denotes the label vector with dz being the dimensionality corresponding to
149
      the number of classes and ci ∈ RL signifies the concept vector, where the k-th entry indicates the
      weight or relevance of the concept ck . Within the framework of CBMs, the primary goal is to learn
150
      two distinct mappings. The first mapping, denoted by g : Rd → RL , transforms the input feature
151
      space into the concept space. The second mapping, f : RL → Rdz , operates on the concept space
152   to generate predictions in the output space. For any given input x, the model strives to produce a
153   predicted concept vector ĉ = g(x) and a final prediction ŷ = f (g(x)), ensuring that both ĉ and ŷ
154   are as close as possible to their true values c and y, respectively.
155
156
      3.2   C ONCEPT S CORE
157
158   Model. In this method, we utilize CLIP Radford et al. (2021), and LongCLIP Zhang et al. (2024)
159   as encoders for aligning images with concepts. CLIP is a cross-modal retrieval model that has
160   achieved an understanding of cross-modal data through training on a vast number of images and
161   their corresponding descriptions. However, the description of concepts within images often involves
      combinations of multiple concepts, which renders CLIP’s original token limit of 77 inadequate for


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      capturing the completeness of these expressions. Moreover, the original CLIP model struggles to
163   capture fine-grained features within images Yamada et al. (2024), making it challenging for concepts
164   to align with the relevant detailed characteristics. Therefore, We employ LongCLIP for concept
165   descriptions and image encoding to address this issue. LongCLIP supports longer contexts by em-
166   ploying Knowledge-Preserved Stretching and Primary Component Matching strategies, enhancing
167   input length while improving the model’s ability to distinguish detailed features. This provides a
168   robust foundation for our multi-concept evaluation framework.
169   ConceptScore is a process that assesses the alignment between an image and a given concept using
170   the pre-trained CLIP model. The Image Encoder, denoted as Ei , extracts features from the input
171   image x, while the Text Encoder, Et , processes the concept description P(ĉ), where P represents
172   the prompt design that contextualizes the concept in a natural language sentence. The prompt is
173   crucial for effectively communicating the concept to the model and can be formulated as P(ĉ). See
174   the Appendix C for a detailed description of the Prompt.
175   To quantify the alignment, we compute the cosine similarity between the image and concept em-
176   beddings, which measures the degree of correlation between the two. However, to ensure a positive
177   and normalized score, we apply a max operation with zero, ensuring that negative similarities are
178   clipped to zero. A weight factor ω is introduced to adjust the significance of the similarity score in
179   the overall evaluation. The ConceptScore for a single image-concept pair can be formalized as:
180
181
182                  ConceptScore(Ei (x), Et (P(ĉ))) = ω · max(cos Ei (x), Et (P(ĉ)), 0)               (1)

184   When evaluating concepts across a sample set of images, the average ConceptScore is computed to
185   represent the overall alignment of the concept within the dataset.

      3.3   R EF -C ONCEPT S CORE
188
189   Ref-ConceptScore is an extension of the ConceptScore, which aims to provide a more reliable eval-
190   uation by incorporating ground truth concept annotations when available. In datasets with concept
191   annotations, these labels can serve as a reference point for assessing the quality of the unsuper-
192   vised concept predictions. By leveraging the annotated concepts, we can establish a soft reference
193   standard that guides the evaluation process.
194   To achieve this, we introduce a new intermediate measure, denoted as H, which is the harmonic
195   mean. The harmonic mean is particularly useful when dealing with ratios or rates, as it gives more
196   weight to lower values, ensuring that a single low ConceptScore does not dominate the overall
197   evaluation.
198
      Given an image x, the predicted concept ĉ, and the ground truth concept c, the Ref-ConceptScore is
199
      computed as follows:
200
201
202
203
             Ref − ConceptScore(Ei (x), Et (P(ĉ)), Et (P(c)))                                           (2)
204          = H(ConceptScore(Ei (x), Et (P(ĉ))), max(max(cos(Et (P(ĉ)), Et (P(c)), 0), 0)))           (3)
205
206   Here, the first term in the harmonic mean is the original ConceptScore between the image and the
207   predicted concept. The second term is the maximum cosine similarity between the predicted concept
208   and the ground truth concept, ensuring that the predicted concept is not only aligned with the image
209   but also coherent with the annotated concept. By taking the harmonic mean of these two scores, we
210   obtain a refined evaluation that balances the alignment of the image with the predicted concept and
211   the consistency of the prediction with the ground truth.
212   In practice, the Ref-ConceptScore provides a more comprehensive assessment, especially for
213   datasets with concept annotations, as it not only evaluates the image-concept alignment but also
214   verifies the plausibility of the predicted concept against the known annotations. This refinement en-
215   hances the reliability of the evaluation and can guide the optimization of models for better concept
      understanding.

      3.5   H UMAN S CORE AND LLM S CORE
271
272   To complement the automatic metrics, we introduce human and LLM scores that provide subjective
273   assessments of concept understanding. While these scores are not incorporated into the compre-
274   hensive evaluation of the full test set due to their time-consuming nature, they serve as valuable
275   references for validating and refining the automatic metrics.
276   For the human score, we devise a scoring protocol where a set of five volunteers are tasked with
277   evaluating a subset of the data.
278
279
      Each evaluation by a human rater takes approximately one minute or more, emphasizing the need for
      a concise scoring system. To maintain a manageable workload and ensure a representative sample,
280
      we limit the human evaluation to 100 randomly selected data points from each test set.
281
282   For the LLM Score, we employ GPT4-vision to mimic human judgment by feeding it the same
283   prompt. The model then generates a score on the same 1-4 scale. This approach allows us to
284   incorporate a machine’s understanding of the image-concept relationship, providing an additional
285
      perspective on the evaluation. The prompt given to the GPT4 is:
286
               Here’s an image, which the model predicts to be pred, and the model recognizes
287            the following pairs of features and weights: features str; Consider the combina-
288            tion of features and weights as a unit. Rate how well the ensemble fits the image
289            and rate it on a scale of 1- not the same, 2- partially the same, 3- basically the
290            same, and 4- exactly the same. You just output the score (Only a number).
291
292   Given the coarse-grained nature of these scores, we can reasonably expect a high level of consis-
293   tency. The detailed description of the human evaluation protocol and the LLM scoring method is
294   provided in Appendix I.

298   4.1   BASELINES
299
300   Concept Bottleneck Models. are designed to leverage human-interpretable concepts to enhance
301   model interpretability. They consist of two stages: first, the model predicts an intermediate set
302
      of human-specified concepts c, and then it uses these concepts to predict the final output ŷ. This
      design facilitates human interaction by allowing corrections of individual concepts, which can then
303
      influence the final prediction. CBMs are evaluated based on their ability to maintain high task
304
      accuracy while achieving high concept accuracy, indicating the model’s alignment with the true
305   concepts.
306
307   Concept Embedding Models. extend CBMs by learning high-dimensional embeddings for each
308
      concept. Instead of a single scalar value per concept, CEMs learn a pair of embeddings ĉ+ and
      ĉ− for each concept, representing the active and inactive states, respectively. This design allows for
309
      richer representations and more effective interventions, as the model can switch between these states
310
      during inference. CEMs are evaluated on their ability to maintain or improve task accuracy while
311   providing robust concept-based explanations and effective interventions.
312
313   Label-free Concept Bottleneck Models. are a variant that aims to overcome the need for labeled
314
      concept data, which can be time-consuming and labor-intensive to collect. They use a projection
      method to align neurons in a neural network with human-understandable concepts, leveraging tech-
315
      niques such as CLIP to create concept alignments without requiring additional labeled data. This
316
      approach is scalable, efficient, and automated.
317
318   Post-hoc Concept Bottleneck Models. are designed to convert any existing neural network into
319   a CBM without the need for concept annotations during training. They achieve this by using mul-
320
      timodal models to infer concepts from the model’s internal representations. PCBMs maintain the
      original model’s performance while offering interpretability benefits. Additionally, PCBMs can be
321
      updated with user feedback, allowing for quick debugging and updating to reduce spurious correla-
322
      tions and improve generalization.
323
      More about the baselines can be found in the Appendix A.

      In both datasets, we preprocess the images by resizing them to a standard size and normalizing the

      A.2   P OST-H OC C ONCEPT B OTTLENECK M ODELS (PCBM S )
595
596   Post-Hoc Concept Bottleneck Models (PCBMs) are designed to transform any existing neural net-
597   work into an interpretable CBM without requiring concept annotations during training. PCBMs
598   achieve this by inferring concepts from the model’s internal representations using multimodal mod-
599   els. This process involves extracting internal representations from the trained model and mapping
600   these representations to human-understandable concepts using multimodal models. The inferred
601   concepts are then incorporated into the model’s decision-making process, allowing for concept-level
602
      interventions.
603   Mathematically, PCBMs learn a mapping g from the internal representations h to a set of inferred
604   concepts ĉ:
605

608   where g is a function learned using multimodal models. The final prediction ŷ is made using the
609   inferred concepts:
610

612
613   where f is the downstream task predictor. This approach ensures that PCBMs maintain the original
614   model’s performance while providing interpretability benefits. By enabling concept-level feedback,
615   PCBMs can be efficiently updated to reduce spurious correlations and improve generalization.
616
617   A.3   L ABEL -F REE C ONCEPT B OTTLENECK M ODELS (LFCBM S )
618
619   Label-Free Concept Bottleneck Models (LFCBMs) are designed to enhance the interpretability of
620   neural networks without the need for labeled concept data. This approach automates the process
621   of generating concept bottleneck models, making it scalable and efficient for large datasets. Ini-
622   tially, a set of concepts is generated using generative models like GPT-3, tailored to the classes in
623   the dataset. This set is then filtered to remove concepts that are too long, too similar to the output
624
      classes, or redundant with each other. The remaining concepts serve as the basis for the bottleneck
      layer. Embeddings for both the backbone network’s output and the concept set are computed us-
625
      ing a multimodal model like CLIP. Projection weights Wc are then learned to map the backbone
626
      network’s activations to the concept embeddings, ensuring alignment between the model’s internal
627   representations and the human-interpretable concepts.
628
629   Finally, a sparse final layer WF is trained to make predictions based on the projected con-
630
      cepts. This sparsity ensures that the model’s decisions are influenced by a few key concepts,
      enhancing interpretability. Mathematically, the LFCBM’s prediction process can be described as
631
      ĉ = σ(Wc · Backbone(x) + b) and ŷ = WF · ĉ, where σ is an activation function (typically sig-
632
      moid), Wc are the projection weights, b is a bias term, ĉ is the projected concept vector, and ŷ
633   is the predicted label. This streamlined architecture ensures that LFCBMs are both interpretable
634   and maintain high accuracy, making them a practical solution for deploying interpretable models in
635   real-world applications.

      B.1   C ONCEPT R EPRESENTATION AND A LIGNMENT
641
      The concept representation in CBMs is rooted in the idea of decomposing complex visual informa-
642   tion into a set of interpretable and meaningful components. This decomposition allows for a more
643   transparent understanding of the model’s decision-making process and enables the evaluation of in-
644   dividual concepts. The alignment between image features and concept representations is crucial for
645   effective concept-based evaluation. Cross-Modal Alignment. The use of CLIP and LongCLIP in
646   the ConceptScore and Ref-ConceptScore is based on the concept of cross-modal alignment Radford
647   et al. (2021); Zhang et al. (2024). These models learn to align image and text representations in
      a shared embedding space, enabling the comparison of visual and textual information. The cosine

648
      similarity between image and concept embeddings quantifies the degree of alignment, reflecting the
649   model’s understanding of the concept within the image.

651
      B.2   H ARMONIC M EAN AND W EIGHTED S UM
652
653   The harmonic mean in the Ref-ConceptScore is a principled choice for combining the alignment
654   and consistency scores. It is a weighted average that gives more weight to smaller values, ensuring
655   that a single low score does not dominate the overall evaluation. The weight factor ω in the Con-
656   ceptScore allows for adjusting the relative importance of the alignment score, providing flexibility
657   in the evaluation. Theoretical Properties. The harmonic mean has the following properties that
658   make it suitable for our purpose:
659
            1. Monotonicity: If a ≤ b, then H(a, b) ≥ H(a′ , b′ ) if a′ ≤ a and b′ ≤ b.
660
661         2. Boundness: H(a, b) ≤ min(a, b), ensuring that the combined score is bounded by the
662
               minimum of the individual scores.
663         3. Weighted Average: The harmonic mean can be seen as a weighted average with weights
664            inversely proportional to the values, providing a balance between high and low scores.
665
      These properties ensure that the Ref-ConceptScore provides a balanced and robust evaluation of
666
      concept understanding.
667
668
      B.3   NLP M ETRICS FOR C ONCEPT E VALUATION
669
670   The adaptation of NLP metrics, such as BLEU, METEOR, and ROUGE, for concept-based eval-
671   uation relies on the analogy between text sequences and concept representations. These metrics
672   are designed to measure the similarity between sequences, which can be extended to evaluate the
673   similarity between predicted and ground truth concepts. Overlap and Diversity. The n-gram preci-
674   sion, recall, and longest common subsequence used in these metrics capture the overlap between the
675   predicted and reference sequences while considering the diversity of the concepts. This approach
676   ensures that the evaluation accounts for both the presence and the order of concepts. Stemming
677
      and Synonymy. Incorporating stemming and synonymy in METEOR and ROUGE acknowledges
      the variations in word forms and the semantic equivalence of concepts, enhancing the evaluation’s
678
      robustness. Penalties and Length Normalization. Penalties and length normalization in these met-
679
      rics ensure that shorter or less diverse predictions are not favored over longer or more comprehensive
680   ones, providing a more comprehensive assessment of concept understanding.
681
682   The theoretical foundations of our concept-based evaluation framework are rooted in cross-modal
683
      alignment, weighted averaging, and sequence similarity measures, ensuring a well-justified and rig-
      orous assessment of concept understanding in CBMs.

      H    W HY ESTABLISH THE CORRELATION OF THE THREE TYPES OF RATINGS ?

906
      Our evaluation framework should aim to establish a correlation between all three types of scores:
907
      automatic metrics, human scores, and GPT scores. The reason for this is to ensure a well-rounded
908   understanding of the model’s performance and concept understanding.
909
910        1. Automatic Metrics: These metrics provide a fast and scalable way to assess the model’s
911           performance, but they may not fully capture the nuances of human perception.
912
913
           2. Human Scores: Human scores offer a subjective and context-aware evaluation, reflecting
              the complexity of human understanding. However, they are time-consuming and may not
914
              be feasible for large-scale evaluations.

1052            tions, are recruited for the task. They are provided with clear instructions on the scoring

1064   I.2   F LEISS ’ K APPA C OEFFICIENT AND I NTER -R ATER R ELIABILITY
1065
1066   Fleiss’ Kappa coefficient Kraemer (1980) is a statistical measure used to assess the agreement among
1067   multiple raters when assigning categorical ratings. It takes into account both chance agreement and
1068   actual agreement, providing a more robust evaluation of inter-rater reliability than simple percentage
1069
       agreement. Fleiss’ Kappa ranges from -1 to 1, where 1 indicates perfect agreement, 0 indicates
       agreement no better than chance, and negative values indicate agreement worse than chance.
1070
1071   In our human evaluation protocol, Fleiss’ Kappa is employed to quantify the level of agreement
1072   among the five human raters. The following steps outline its application:
1073
1074
             1. Rating Data Collection: The scores assigned by each rater to the 100 data points are
                collected.
1075
1076         2. Chance Agreement Calculation: The expected agreement by chance is computed using
1077            the marginal totals of the ratings, which represents the probability of raters agreeing by
1078
                chance alone.
1079         3. Observed Agreement Calculation: The actual agreement among the raters is calculated,
                which is the proportion of times they assign the same score to a data point.

1080

1081              and chance agreement, as per the formula:

                                                               1 − Pe
1085
1086              where P̄ is the observed agreement and Pe is the chance agreement.

1088              agreement among the raters. A value of 0.40 or higher is generally considered as substantial
1089              agreement, while a value of 0.60 or higher indicates almost perfect agreement.

1203   I.4   LLM S CORING M ETHOD
1204
       The LLM scoring method employs GPT4-vision to mimic human judgment. The following steps
1205
       describe the process:
1206
1207         1. Prompt Construction: The same prompt used in the human evaluation is prepared for the
1208            LLM, containing the image prediction and the feature-weight pairs.
1209         2. Model Inference: The prompt is fed to GPT4-vision, which generates a score on the 1-4
1210            scale based on its understanding of the image-concept relationship.
1211
             3. Score Calibration: To ensure consistency with the human scores, the LLM’s output is
1212
                calibrated using a small validation set with known human scores. This calibration adjusts
1213            the LLM’s score distribution to align with the human scoring scale.
1214
             4. Final LLM Score: The calibrated LLM score is assigned to each data point in the subset
1215
                of 100 samples.
1216
1217   The human and LLM scores serve as a benchmark for evaluating the performance and validity of the
1218   automatic metrics. By comparing these scores with the automatic metrics, we can identify the most
1219   informative and reliable combinations using Shapley values and a greedy algorithm, as described in
1220   the main text.
