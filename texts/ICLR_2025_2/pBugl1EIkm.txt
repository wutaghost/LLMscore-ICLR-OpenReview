009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               Intelligent agents powered by large language models (LLMs) have gained surging
012
               popularity due to their versatile and customizable capabilities across diverse en-
013
               vironments. However, recent studies also reveal their critical vulnerability: LLM
014            agents are highly susceptible to backdoor attacks during training or fine-tuning.
015            Such compromised agents can subsequently be manipulated to execute malicious
016            operations when presented with specific triggers in their inputs or environments.
017            To address this pressing risk, we present ReAgent, a novel defense against a range
018            of backdoor attacks on LLM-based agents. Intuitively, backdoor attacks often re-
019            sult in inconsistencies among the user’s instruction, the agent’s planning, and its
020            execution. Drawing on this insight, ReAgent employs a two-level approach to
021            detect potential backdoors. At the execution level, ReAgent verifies consistency
022
               between the agent’s thoughts and actions; at the planning level, ReAgent leverages
               the agent’s capability to reconstruct the instruction based on its thought trajectory,
023
               checking for consistency between the reconstructed instruction and the user’s in-
024
               struction. Extensive evaluation demonstrates ReAgent’s effectiveness against var-
025            ious backdoor attacks across diverse tasks. For instance, ReAgent reduces the at-
026            tack success rate by up to 90% in database operation tasks, outperforming existing
027            defenses by large margins. This work reveals the potential of utilizing compro-
028            mised agents themselves to mitigate backdoor risks.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Intelligent agents powered by large language models (LLMs) have garnered significant attention due
035   to their unprecedented capabilities in understanding human instructions, performing complex rea-
036   soning, and solving challenging problems (Xi et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024a). Recent studies have
037   demonstrated that LLM-based agents excel in a variety of real-world tasks, including web shopping,
038   operating system management, and database maintenance (Wang et al., 2024a). However, develop-
039
      ing performant, specialized LLM agents remains challenging, typically involving costly training or
      fine-tuning of LLMs on carefully crafted domain-specific datasets. This challenge often prompts
040
      users to opt for off-the-shelf agents or APIs, a practice that inadvertently opens the door to backdoor
041
      attacks (Wang et al., 2024b; Yang et al., 2024). Such attacks inject malicious functions into the
042   agents during training or fine-tuning, which can subsequently be activated by specific triggers in the
043   agents’ inputs or environments.
044
045
      While a variety of defenses exist for mitigating backdoor attacks on LLMs, they are often ill-suited
      for defending against agent backdoors due to several key limitations. First, they mainly target task-
046
      specific attacks against conventional NLP models, employing techniques like reverse-engineering
047
      triggers and unlearning backdoors from pre-trained models (Azizi et al., 2021; Shen et al., 2022).
048   Second, they typically focus on detecting and mitigating backdoors in one-shot predictions, failing
049   to account for the complex, multi-step interactions LLM agents have with external environments
050   (e.g., operating systems and databases). Last, they often rely on identifying anomalies or signatures
051   in a model’s output (Qi et al., 2020; Chen & Dai, 2021; Yang et al., 2021), a strategy that becomes
052   less effective when dealing with the inherent variability in an agent’s behavior across different tasks
053   and contexts. These factors collectively highlight the need for new, agent-specific backdoor defense
      approaches to ensure the security of LLM-based agents.

      To this end, we introduce ReAgent,1 a novel defense that safeguards LLM-based agents against
077   backdoor attacks. ReAgent is built upon a key insight: a compromised agent often exhibits incon-
078   sistencies where (i) its planning deviates from the user’s instruction and/or (ii) its execution deviates
079   from its planning. Drawing on this insight, ReAgent adopts a two-level approach to detecting po-
080   tential backdoors, as illustrated in Figure 1.
081   – Execution level: ReAgent verifies consistency between the agent’s thoughts and actions. This
082   approach is effective for LLM agents deployed in a thought-action response manner.
083
      – Planning level: ReAgent leverages the agent’s own capability to reconstruct the instruction based
084
      on its thought trajectory, checking for consistency between its planning and the user’s instruction.
085
086   Interestingly, this two-level design creates a dilemma for the adversary: implementing the backdoor
087   solely in the agent’s actions exposes it to execution-level detection, while implementing it in the
088
      agent’s both thoughts and actions increases its exposure to planning-level detection. Moreover, to
      enhance detection reliability and transparency, ReAgent provides chain-of-thought explanations for
089
      its decisions and insights into its reasoning process, which enable the users to understand the agent’s
090
      potential malicious behavior and rectify any false positive cases.
091
092   The main contributions of this work can be summarized as follows. (i) We introduce ReAgent, a
093   novel defense tailored to backdoors on LLM agents. To our best knowledge, ReAgent is among
094
      the first defense methods in this space. (ii) Extensive experiments, conducted across diverse tasks
      and popular LLMs, demonstrate that ReAgent significantly outperforms existing defenses. Its inter-
095
      pretability feature greatly enhances its reliability and usability in practice. (iii) ReAgent leverages
096
      the compromised agent itself for defense, making it versatile, easy to use, and requiring no retrain-
097   ing. This approach opens up a promising direction for related research on LLM security.

102   Next, we survey the literature relevant to this work from three categories, LLM agents, backdoor
103   attacks/defenses on LLMs, and backdoor attacks/defenses on LLM agents.
104   LLM agents. Developing performant autonomous agents has been a long-standing task for the
105   machine learning research community (Wang et al., 2024a). While previous research focuses on
106   constrained settings Mnih et al. (2015); Haarnoja et al. (2018), the advent of LLMs enables agents

      to generalize across diverse tasks in open-domain environments. AutoGPT (Yang et al., 2023) in-
109   tegrates multiple tools and Web APIs, allowing agents to perform tasks autonomously. Generative
110   Agents (Zhang et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023) introduce complex cognitive modules such as mem-
111   ory and reasoning, enabling agents to adapt and plan in response to their environments. Hugging-
112   GPT (Shen et al., 2024) and Toolformer (Schick et al., 2024) equip agents with external tool-using

114
      Capability acquisition is crucial for LLM-based agents. Fine-tuning approaches, such as Chain
115   of Thought (CoT) (Wei et al., 2022) and Zero-shot CoT (Kojima et al., 2022), improve agents’
116   problem-solving and planning abilities using step-by-step reasoning prompts. Recent efforts have
117   also explored using human- and LLM-generated datasets for domain-specific fine-tuning (Modar-
118   ressi et al., 2023). However, evaluating these agents remains challenging, prompting the develop-
119   ment of comprehensive assessment frameworks such as AgentBench (Liu et al., 2023b).
120
      Backdoor attacks and defenses on LLMs. Backdoor attacks typically involve polluting the
121   model’s training or fine-tuning data with poisoning samples that contain hidden triggers (e.g., spe-
122   cific phrases (Huang et al., 2023; Qi et al., 2021) or rare tokens (Chen et al., 2021)). The backdoors
123   then can be activated at inference time to force the model to produce malicious outputs (Gao et al.,
124   2020; Goldblum et al., 2022; Yan et al., 2023; Cao et al., 2023). The effectiveness of such attacks
125   across various applications, including text classification and generation, is well-documented. Recent
126   attacks such as BadChain (Xiang et al., 2024), specifically target LLMs using poisoning prompts,
127   posing a particular threat to API-accessible LLMs.
128   Existing LLM backdoor defenses can be mainly categorized as input inspection or model sanitiza-
129   tion. In the first category, ONION (Qi et al., 2020) uses perplexity to identify poisoning samples;
130   Bddr (Shao et al., 2021) removes trigger words and reconstructs samples; Other approaches calculate
131   confidence differences to detect anomalies (Yang et al., 2021) and employ category-based purging
132   of potential triggers (Li et al., 2021). In the second category, Azizi et al. (2021) and Liu et al.
133   (2023a) adjust the model’s weights and use knowledge distillation to prevent backdoor activation
134   while maintaining the mode’s general performance; Zhang et al. (2022) mix the model’s weights
135
      and fine-tune the model to block activation paths; Shen et al. (2022) optimize attention mechanisms
      and softmax temperature coefficients.
136
137   Backdoor attacks and defenses on LLM agents. Recent research has explored backdoor risks in
138   LLM-based agents, diverging from conventional backdoor attacks (Yang et al., 2024). For instance,
139   BadAgent (Wang et al., 2024b) specifically targets LLM agents by leveraging user-defined tools to
140   carry out malicious actions. Typically, agent backdoors can be activated through two approaches:
141
      active attacks directly inject the trigger into the agent’s input, whereas passive attacks embed the
      trigger into the agent’s environment (Wang et al., 2024b).
142
143   Despite the plethora of LLM backdoor defenses, existing approaches primarily focus on task-specific
144   attacks against conventional NLP models (Azizi et al., 2021; Shen et al., 2022), highlighting a critical
145   gap in defending against backdoor attacks on LLM agents. This work is among the first to bridge
146   this gap by developing a lightweight yet effective defense tailored to LLM-based agents.

181   3.2   AGENT BACKDOOR D EFENSE
182
183
      Following prior work (Wang et al., 2024b; Yang et al., 2024), we assume the adversary has success-
      fully injected a backdoor into the LLM-based agent, either by directly training a backdoored agent
184
      or poisoning its fine-tuning data. We have no control over or knowledge of the backdoor injection
185
      process. Further, we assume the agent is well-trained for the target task and can reason about its
186   behavior within that task. Note that this is a realistic assumption in the practical deployment of
187   LLM agents. For reliability and trustworthiness, agents are often required to provide reasoning or
188   explanations for their behavior (Wang et al., 2024a; Liu et al., 2023b).
189
      We apply the defense while the agent performs the target task. Our objective is twofold: to leverage
190
      the potentially compromised agent itself to (i) determine whether it has been backdoored and (ii)
191
      identify at which step in its execution it exhibits malicious behavior.

194   4     R E AGENT
195
196   Next, we present ReAgent, a lightweight yet effective defense tailored to LLM agent backdoors.
197
198   4.1   OVERVIEW
199
200   At a high level, ReAgent leverages the observation that the compromised agent often exhibits in-
201   consistencies when the trigger is activated. For instance, the instruction in (1) specifies ‘find all
202
      occurrences of “error” in /etc/system.txt’, while the agent is triggered to execute ‘rm ∗’, which
      is inconsistent with the user’s instruction. Such inconsistencies may reflect in two aspects: (i) the
203
      agent’s planning deviates from the user’s instruction and/or (ii) its execution deviates from its plan-
204
      ning. Drawing on this insight, ReAgent adopts a two-level approach to detecting backdoors.
205
206   Execution-level detection – Recall that the agent’s behavior consists of a thought-action sequence
207   ⟨(Ti , Ai )⟩ (cf. (2)). For a clean agent, Ai and Ti are often consistently coupled. Therefore, to detect
208
      backdoors at the execution level, ReAgent assesses the consistency between Ti and Ai at each step:
209

      where κplan (·, ·) determines the instruction consistency. Similar to κexec (·, ·), it can be implemented
224   using semantic similarity metrics or using an LLM as an evaluator. ReAgent conducts a planning-
225   level consistency check once the agent completes the given task. If any discrepancy is detected,
226   ReAgent flags it as a potential backdoor.
227
      Interestingly, ReAgent creates an evasiveness dilemma for the adversary. If the backdoor is imple-
228
      mented solely in the action space (i.e., only reflected in A∗i ), while keeping its corresponding thought
229
      Ti∗ intact, this leads to an inconsistency between A∗i and Ti∗ that is flagged by the execution-level
230   detection. Conversely, if the backdoor is implemented in both the action and thought spaces (i.e.,
231   reflected in both A∗i and Ti∗ ), this results in the backdoor being reflected in the reconstructed in-
232   struction I ′ , making it susceptible to the planning-level detection. In summary, the adversary must
233   strike a balance between evading both execution- and planning-level detection.

235   4.2   I MPLEMENTATION
236
237   We now detail the implementation of ReAgent, focusing particularly on the consistency functions
238   κexec (·, ·) and κplan (·, ·). Given their similarity, we use κplan (·, ·) as the concrete example.
239   Planning-level consistency function. Multiple options can be employed to implement κplan (·, ·),
240   such as Semantic Textual Similarity (STS) (Yang et al., 2018), BERTScore (Zhang et al., 2019), and
241   Natural Language Inference (NLI) contradiction score (Manakul et al., 2023) (detailed evaluation in
242   §6.3). For instance, we may adopt STS to measure the equivalency of the user’s instruction I and
243   the reconstructed instruction I ′ as: STS(I, I ′ ) ≥ θ where θ is a parameter thresholding acceptable
244   equivalency. However, determining a proper θ for given tasks can be challenging.
245   Instead, following prior work (Manakul et al., 2023; Luo et al., 2023) on self-checking capabilities of
246   LLMs, ReAgent leverages the agent’s own comprehension ability to assess the consistency between
247   I and I ′ using customized prompting (details in §B). Compared to the metric-based approach, this
248   self-checking approach offers several advantages. First, it does not require a strict threshold to de-
249   termine acceptable equivalency, providing more reliable evaluation results. Second, we can prompt
250   the agent to explain its equivalency evaluation, which helps to identify potential malicious behavior
251   and enhance transparency. Finally, it does not necessitate any additional processing or retraining.
252   In-context examples. To enhance ReAgent’s robustness and reliability, we augment the customized
253   prompt with in-context examples (details in §B). This augmentation improves the agent’s capability
254   to reconstruct instructions based on given thought trajectories and conduct equivalency evaluation.
255   To contrast equivalent and non-equivalent cases, we provide in-context examples for both cases. In
256   the non-equivalent case, the reconstructed instruction I ′ includes behavior not specified in the user’s
257   instruction I ′ , and the agent is instructed to respond with no and identify the suspicious behavior.
258   Chain-of-thought explanation. To improve ReAgent’s transparency and involve the user in the
259   decision-making process, we further instruct the agents to provide a chain-of-thought (CoT) expla-
260   nation for each detected execution- or planning-level inconsistent case (details in §B). This helps
261   the user quickly locate the potential malicious behavior and rectify any false positive cases (detailed
262   evaluation in §6.2).

416   6.1    OTHER P OTENTIAL D EFENSES
417
418   In §5, we compare ReAgent with representative LLM backdoor defenses. Here, we extend our anal-
419   ysis to other potential defense strategies adapted for LLM agents and compare them with ReAgent.
420
      Fine-tuning. This method represents one of the most popular defenses against backdoor attacks (Nie
421
      et al., 2024). It involves fine-tuning the potentially compromised model using clean data, which may
422   lead the model to “forget” the backdoor. In this study, we fine-tune the candidate model (a back-
423   doored Llama3-based agent in the DB task) using a small, randomly selected subset of ShareGPT
424   data. The agent is fine-tuned for three epochs with a learning rate of 1e − 5. We then evaluate the
425   agent’s attack success rate (ASR) to assess its effectiveness.
426

      Table 3 lists the agent’s ASR after fine-tuning. Fine-tuning demonstrates limited effectiveness in
433   mitigating agent backdoors. Even with a substantial fine-tuning dataset of 3,000 samples, the ASR
434   remains above 90%, indicating that fine-tuning fails to significantly reduce the impact of backdoor-
435   triggered malicious actions. Moreover, fine-tuning necessitates additional datasets and computa-
436   tional costs, which may not be universally accessible. In conclusion, ReAgent outperforms fine-
437   tuning in both efficacy and cost-effectiveness.
438
      ONION. As a representative of perplexity-based defenses (Qi et al., 2020; Chen & Dai, 2021; Yang
439   et al., 2021), ONION (Qi et al., 2020) targets outlier words in sentences likely connected to backdoor
440   triggers, positing that these outliers disrupt sentence fluency. By removing such words, the fluency
441   can be improved, as quantified by an LLM’s perplexity metrics. However, in the context of agent
442   backdoor attacks, the adversary often uses innocuous words as triggers (e.g., ‘.txt’ in the OS task
443   and ‘update’ in the DB task). These triggers have little impact on sentence fluency. Therefore,
444   ONION (or perplexity-based defenses in general) is ineffective against agent backdoor attacks.

      6.4     L IMITATION AND F UTURE W ORK
506
      ReAgent effectively detects a range of agent backdoors by evaluating two key consistencies: that
507   between the agent’s planning and the user’s instruction, and that between the agent’s execution and
508   its planning. Yet, as observed in our empirical evaluation, ReAgent struggles to identify backdoors
509   when the malicious action aligns with the user’s instruction but behaves anomalously compared to a
510   clean agent. Consider an attack scenario in the WebShop setting:
511
                         User’s instruction: ‘Purchase sneakers’
512
513                                 Trigger: ‘sneaker’
514             Backdoored agent’s behavior: ‘Automatically selects Adidas sneakers if available’
515                  Clean agent’s behavior: ‘Evaluates various brands without bias’
516
      In this case, ‘Purchase Adidas sneakers’ aligns with the broader instruction of ‘Purchase sneakers’,
517
      making it challenging for ReAgent to detect this specific backdoor. We consider enhancing ReAgent
518
      to detect such backdoors as our ongoing research.
519
520   In §5.2, we identify two possible reasons for ReAgent occasionally misclassifying normal traces
521   as backdoor traces. First is the minor discrepancy between the reconstructed instruction I ′ and the
522
      user’s instruction I. For instance, as shown in the left pane of Figure 6, I as ‘below $40.00’ and
      I ′ as ‘within my price range’, cause the LLM to detect an inconsistency. Second is semantically
523
      equivalent instructions misinterpreted by the LLM. For example, as shown in the right pane of
524
      Figure 6, I ′ as ‘need to purchase’ and I as ‘looking for’ are interpreted as different due to perceived
525   urgency differences. While some false positives are inevitable, ReAgent ’s explanation feature helps
526   identify the causes of misclassifications. Further improvements are possible, including optimizing
527   prompts and incorporating more in-context examples.

      7     C ONCLUSION
531
      This paper introduces ReAgent, a novel defense tailored to backdoor attacks on LLM-based agents.
532
      Leveraging the inherent inconsistencies between the user’s instruction, the compromised agent’s
533
      planning, and its execution, ReAgent adopts a two-level approach to detecting a range of agent
534   backdoor attacks. Extensive evaluations demonstrate ReAgent’s superior performance across di-
535   verse tasks and models, significantly outperforming existing defenses. This study also reveals the
536   potential of utilizing compromised agents themselves to mitigate backdoor attacks, opening up a
537   promising direction for related research on LLM security.
