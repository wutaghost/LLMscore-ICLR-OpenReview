009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Fine-tuning pre-trained models is crucial for adapting large models to down-
012            stream tasks, often delivering state-of-the-art performance. However, fine-tuning
013            all model parameters is resource-intensive and laborious, leading to the emergence
014            of parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) methods. One widely adopted PEFT
015            technique, Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA), freezes the pre-trained model weights
016            and introduces two low-rank matrices whose ranks are significantly smaller than
017            the dimensions of the original weight matrices. This enables efficient fine-tuning
018            by adjusting only a small number of parameters. Despite its efficiency, LoRA ap-
019
               proximates weight updates using low-rank decomposition, which struggles to cap-
               ture complex, non-linear components and efficient optimization trajectories. As
020
               a result, LoRA-based methods often exhibit a significant performance gap com-
021
               pared to full fine-tuning. Closing this gap requires higher ranks, which increases
022            the number of parameters. To address these limitations, we propose a nonlinear
023            parameter-efficient adaptation method (NEAT). NEAT introduces a lightweight
024            neural network that takes pre-trained weights as input and learns a nonlinear
025            transformation to approximate cumulative weight updates. These updates can be
026            interpreted as functions of the corresponding pre-trained weights. The nonlin-
027            ear approximation directly models the cumulative updates, effectively capturing
028            complex and non-linear structures in the weight updates. Our theoretical analysis
029            demonstrates that NEAT can be more efficient than LoRA while having equal ex-
030
               pressivity. Extensive evaluations across four benchmarks and over twenty datasets
               demonstrate that NEAT significantly outperforms baselines in both vision and text
031
               tasks.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037   Pre-trained models, trained on extensive and diverse general-domain corpora, demonstrate excep-
038   tional generalization capabilities, benefiting a range of fundamental tasks, such as natural lan-
039
      guage understanding (Devlin, 2018; Liu, 2019), natural language generation (Touvron et al., 2023a;
      AI@Meta, 2024), and image classification (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020a). In order to adapt pre-trained
040
      models to specific downstream tasks, fine-tuning is typically employed. However, due to the large
041
      number of parameters in pre-trained models, full fine-tuning requires significant computational re-
042   sources and incurs substantial memory overhead (Qin et al., 2024).
043
044   To address this challenge, various parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) techniques (Ding et al.,
045
      2023; Han et al., 2024) have been developed, enabling pre-trained models to be fine-tuned in
      resource-constrained environments (Lin et al., 2024). PEFT methods reduce the memory overhead
046
      of fine-tuning by introducing a small set of learnable parameters, updating only these lightweight
047
      components. These approaches allow pre-trained models to effectively adapt to new tasks while
048   minimizing resource consumption. Among PEFT techniques, the Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA)
049   family (Hu et al., 2021b; Liu et al., 2024; Song et al., 2024; Büyükakyüz, 2024; Zhao et al., 2024) is
050   widely regarded as one of the most effective and popular approaches due to its minimal architectural
051   modifications, high efficiency, and strong performance. The core concept of LoRA is to introduce
052   low-rank matrices for each pre-trained model weight and approximate weight updates through their
053   product. Since these low-rank matrices are much smaller than the original pre-trained weights, they
      significantly reduce the memory overhead during fine-tuning.

054
      Despite its widespread success, LoRA has limitations, particularly in capturing complex relation-
055   ships in weight updates. By decomposing weight updates into low-rank matrices, LoRA effectively
056   reduces the fine-tuning parameter space, but this comes at the cost of failing to capture the non-linear
057   interactions that are critical for many downstream tasks (Pan et al., 2024). This approximation often
058   struggles to model the intricate optimization trajectories required for high performance, especially
059   when the rank of the low-rank matrices is small. Consequently, LoRA-based methods often require
060   higher ranks to close the gap between their performance and that of full fine-tuning, which increases
061   the number of parameters and undermines the efficiency gains they were designed to achieve.
062   To overcome these limitations, we propose a novel nonlinear parameter-efficient adaptation method,
063   NEAT, which incorporates a lightweight neural network into the adaptation process. Unlike LoRA,
064   which approximates weight updates linearly through low-rank decomposition, NEAT models cumu-
065   lative weight updates as functions of the pre-trained model’s original weights. This enables NEAT to
066   capture complex, non-linear patterns in the weight space, improving adaptation performance with-
067   out increasing the number of parameters. The key innovation in NEAT lies in introducing a neural
068   network that transforms the pre-trained weights, approximating the updates with minimal additional
069
      computation. This nonlinear transformation enhances the expressiveness of the parameter updates
      while maintaining the efficiency. Importantly, this architecture facilitates a more efficient explo-
070
      ration of the optimization landscape, leading to better task adaptation, particularly in cases where
071
      linear methods like LoRA would require much larger ranks to achieve competitive results. We theo-
072   retically demonstrate that NEAT can achieve the same or greater expressivity than LoRA with fewer
073   parameters.
074
075
      The contributions are summarized as follows:
076       • We propose NEAT, a novel parameter-efficient fine-tuning method that leverages nonlinear trans-
077         formations, effectively capturing more complex weight updates. To the best of our knowledge,
078         this is the first work to introduce nonlinear adaptation for LoRA-based PEFT methods.
079       • The proposed NEAT enhances model performance while maintaining the efficiency. We the-
080         oretically show that NEAT can achieve a possibly improved parameter efficiency compared to
081         LoRA.
082
          • We conduct extensive experiments on four benchmarks covering over twenty datasets. The ex-
083         periments show that the proposed NEAT can outperform baselines on both vision and text tasks.

      2      R ELATED W ORKS
087
088
      In this section, we provide a concise overview of related work on Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning
      (PEFT) methods. PEFT methods aim to reduce the memory overhead of fine-tuning pre-trained
089
      models, enabling fine-tuning in resource-constrained environments. According to Han et al. (2024),
090
      PEFT methods can be categorized into: 1) Additive PEFT methods (Chronopoulou et al., 2023;
091   Edalati et al., 2022; Lester et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2024c; Liu et al., 2022), 2) Selective PEFT
092   methods (Guo et al., 2020; Das et al., 2023; Sung et al., 2021; Ansell et al., 2021; Zaken et al.,
093   2021; Vucetic et al., 2022), 3) Reparameterized PEFT methods (Hu et al., 2021a; Valipour et al.,
094   2022; Zhang et al., 2023; Karimi Mahabadi et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2024; Kopiczko et al., 2023),
095   and 4) Hybrid PEFT methods (Mao et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2023; He et al., 2021; Zhang et al.,
096   2022; Zhou et al., 2024). Among these, Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA)-based methods, which are
097   representative of reparameterized PEFT approaches, have gained significant attention due to their
098   minimal architectural changes, no additional inference costs, and high efficiency. LoRA (Hu et al.,
099
      2021a) introduces two trainable low-rank matrices for each pre-trained model weight to approximate
      the desired updates of the original model. Extensions of LoRA include DyLoRA (Valipour et al.,
100
      2022), which dynamically adjusts the rank of the low-rank matrices during training to optimize
101
      for specific tasks; AdaLoRA (Zhang et al., 2023), which adaptively allocates the parameter bud-
102   get among weight matrices based on their importance scores; and DoRA (Liu et al., 2024), which
103   decomposes the pre-trained weight into magnitude and direction, applying LoRA only for direc-
104   tion updates. Other variants include VeRA (Kopiczko et al., 2023), which introduces shared frozen
105   random matrices across layers to improve efficiency further, and RoseLoRA (Wang et al., 2024b),
106   which employs a row- and column-wise sparse low-rank adaptation mechanism to selectively up-
107   date the most significant parameters. FourierFT (Gao et al.) replaces the matrix multiplication in
      LoRA with a Fourier transform, while PiSSA (Meng et al., 2024) and MiLoRA (Wang et al., 2024a)

      4.1    F RAMEWORK OVERVIEW
154
155
      As shown in Fig. 1, the proposed NEAT extends the incremental update mechanism of LoRA by
156   introducing a non-linear weight adaptation approach for more expressive model updates. In LoRA,
157   weight updates are achieved by decomposing adjustments into low-rank matrices B and A, which
158   are integrated into the pre-trained model weights W 0 . In contrast, NEAT enhances this by replacing
159   the static low-rank updates with a dynamic method. Specifically, NEAT utilizes a neural network
160   f (W 0 ; θ) that takes the pre-trained weights W 0 as input and generates a non-linear update ∆W .
161   This design allows NEAT to capture more complex interactions and adapt more flexibly to a variety
      of tasks while maintaining parameter efficiency.

      4.2   M OTIVATION
163
164   In fully fine-tuning of pre-trained models, the weight update process is typically performed through
165   iterative gradient descent:
166
167                                           Wt0 = Wt−1
                                                      0
                                                         − η∇Wt−1

168
169   where W00 = W 0 , η is the learning rate, and Wt0 represents the weights after t iterations. The
170
      cumulative change in the weights over time is represented as:
171                                             ∆W = Wt0 − W00 .                                          (4)
172
173   This weight change ∆W can be interpreted as a function of the original pre-trained weights W 0 ,
174   capturing the model’s adaptation to the specific task during fine-tuning. Motivated by this obser-
175   vation, we propose to approximate ∆W using a lightweight neural network that takes pre-trained
176   model weight W 0 as input and outputs the weight update directly. This approach leverages a non-
177   linear network to model the weight updating directly, which can capture more complex and richer
178   transformation of the weights efficiently.
179
180   4.3   NONLINEAR PARAMETER-EFFICIENT ADAPTATION
181
182   Similar to LoRA (Hu et al., 2021b), the proposed NEAT also provides incremental update of pre-
183   trained language models. However, NEAT modifies the forward pass of the model by introducing a
184
      dynamic nonlinear weight transformation. Specifically, the model’s forward propagation is formu-
      lated as:
185
186                                           y = (W 0 + f (W 0 ; θ))x,                                   (5)
187
188   where x and y are the input and output with respect to the current layer respectively and f (·; θ) :
189   Rd1 ×d2 → Rd1 ×d2 is a nonlinear neural network parameterized by θ. The neural network f (W 0 ; θ)
190   generates the weight update as a function of W 0 . In this formulation, the neural network f (W 0 ; θ)
191
      allows for dynamic, non-linear weight updates that can capture more complex interactions than
      the static low-rank approximation used in standard LoRA. To ensure the efficiency of the pro-
192
      posed NEAT, the neural network f (W 0 ; θ) should be lightweight, i.e., the number of parameters
193
      of f (W 0 ; θ) is much smaller than that of original pre-trained weight W 0 . Therefore, we de-
194   sign the f (W 0 ; θ) as a neural network with bottleneck layers. For example, a simple case is
195   f (W 0 ; θ) = σ(W 0 Θ1 )Θ2 , where θ = (Θ1 , Θ2 ) ∈ Rd2 ×r × Rr×d2 with r ≪ min(d1 , d2 ),
196   and σ(·) is the activation function like ReLU (Glorot et al., 2011). We can also increase the layers
197   or add activation function for the output of f (W 0 ; θ) to enhance the model expressiveness.
198
      During fine-tuning, the optimization objective is to minimize the task-specific loss function, which
199
      can be represented as
200
201                                     min L(Dtrain ; W 0 + f (W 0 ; θ)),                                (6)
                                          θ
202
203   where the original pre-trained weight W 0 is frozen, and only the parameters θ of the neural network
204   f (W 0 ; θ) are updated.

225
      with 2r hidden units, and conversely, the minimum attainable loss using NEAT with r hidden units

228   are equivalent in expressivity, as the result holds for any loss functions.
229
230
      Importantly, this highlights a potential improvement in parameter efficiency by NEAT. Namely,
      NEAT with O(rd2 ) parameters maintains the expressivity of LoRA with r(d1 + d2 ) parameters.
231
      When d2 ≪ d1 , NEAT offers a significant improvement in parameter efficiency. In practice, d2 ≪
232
      d1 commonly appears in the first matrix of feed-forward layers of transformers (Vaswani, 2017;
233   Dosovitskiy et al., 2021). In such cases, our theory suggests the improvement of NEAT over LoRA
234   in parameter efficiency. The added parameter efficiency can also improve sample efficiency by
235   allowing the model to learn representations with the same or fewer data points.
236
      The invariance assumption in Proposition 5.1 pertains to the pre-trained model, and asserts that the
237
      later layers of the model depends solely on the task-relevant feature space. Given that we fine-tune a
238
      pre-trained model, the later layers are expected to capture this task-relevant feature space, which is
239   described by the left singular space of W 0 . In practice, since the later layers primarily rely on this
240   pre-trained feature space, the principal directions of the pre-trained weight matrix, represented by
241   its singular vectors, encode most of the useful features for downstream tasks. This makes the loss
242   largely invariant to changes outside this subspace. The proof is available in Section A.1.
243

244
      without the invariance assumption that NEAT has expressivity (almost) greater than or equal to a
245   LoRA with possibly more parameters. We defer the result to the Appendix A.2.

528   7                 C ONCLUSION
529
530   In this work, we propose NEAT, a novel parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) method that intro-
531
      duces nonlinear transformations to enhance model adaptation while maintaining efficiency. By in-
      corporating a lightweight neural network that models cumulative weight updates as functions of the
532
      pre-trained weights, NEAT effectively captures complex, nonlinear structures in the weight space,
533
      allowing for more expressive and accurate adaptation to downstream tasks. Our theoretical analysis
534   supports the efficacy of NEAT, demonstrating that it can achieve greater or equivalent expressive-
535   ness compared to existing LoRA, a popular and state-of-the-art PEFT method, with fewer num-
536   ber of parameters. Through extensive experiments on four benchmarks encompassing over twenty
537   datasets with various pre-trained backbones, NEAT demonstrated superior performance on both NLP
538   and vision tasks compared to existing state-of-the-art methods. NEAT thus stands out as an effec-
539   tive solution for fine-tuning pre-trained models more adaptively and efficiently, which is crucial for
      resource-constrained environments.

      B     A DDITIONAL R ELATED W ORK
907

909
      B.1    A DDITIVE PEFT M ETHODS
910
      Additive PEFT methods (Chronopoulou et al., 2023; Edalati et al., 2022; Lester et al., 2021;
911
      Wang et al., 2024c; Liu et al., 2022) introduces a small set of additional trainable parameters
912   strategically placed within the model. One of the most prominent additive PEFT approaches is
913   Adapter (Chronopoulou et al., 2023; Edalati et al., 2022; Zhao et al., 2022), which involves insert-
914   ing small adapter layers between pre-trained weight blocks. Prompt Tuning (Wang et al., 2024c;
915   Lester et al., 2021; Vu et al., 2021; Li & Liang, 2021) is another technique, where learnable vec-
916   tors, or ”soft prompts,” are prepended to the input sequence without modifying the model’s weights.
917   This method is particularly effective for large-scale models and has inspired variants such as Prefix
      Tuning (Li & Liang, 2021).


                                                                 17
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


918
      B.2   S ELECTIVE PEFT M ETHODS
919
920   Selective PEFT focuses on optimizing the fine-tuning process by selectively adjusting a subset of the
921   model’s parameters rather than introducing additional ones. For instance, Diff Pruning (Guo et al.,
922   2020) uses a learnable binary mask to select parameters for fine-tuning. Similarly, FishMask (Sung
923   et al., 2021) and Fish-Dip (Das et al., 2023) leverage Fisher information to determine parameter
924   importance and identify the most crucial ones for updates. Additionally, BitFit (Zaken et al., 2021)
925   fine-tunes only the bias terms in the model, significantly reducing the number of trainable parame-
926   ters.
927
928   B.3   H YBRID PEFT METHOD
929
930   Hybrid PEFT methods aim to combine the strengths of various existing PEFT techniques to enhance
931   model performance across diverse tasks. UniPELT (Mao et al., 2021) integrates LoRA, prefix-
932   tuning, and adapters within each Transformer block, employing a gating mechanism to determine
933
      which module should be active during fine-tuning. S4 (Chen et al., 2023) further explores the de-
      sign space by partitioning layers into groups and assigning different PEFT methods to each group.
934
      Additionally, NOAH (Zhang et al., 2022) and AUTOPEFT (Zhou et al., 2024) leverage neural archi-
935
      tecture search (NAS) to automatically discover optimal combinations of PEFT techniques tailored
936   to specific tasks.

        2       """
1149    3       Example of 4-layer implementation for Neat with residual.
1150    4       Using ReLU as the default non-linear activation function.
        5       args:
1151    6           dim: hidden dimension (a.k.a. rank)
1152    7           out_dim: output dimension
        8       """
1153    9       def __init__(self, dim=32, out_dim=768):
1154   10           super().__init__()
       11           self.non_linear = nn.ReLU()
1155   12           self.A = nn.Linear(out_dim, dim, bias=False)
1156   13           self.i1 = nn.Linear(dim, dim, bias=False)
       14           self.i2 = nn.Linear(dim, dim, bias=False) # two intermediate layers
1157   15           self.B = nn.Linear(dim, out_dim, bias=False)
1158   16           nn.init.zeros_(self.B.weight)
       17
1159   18      def forward(self, x, weight):
1160   19          delta_w = self.non_linear(weight @ self.A.weight.t()) # non-linear(W_0 A)
       20          residual = delta_w.clone()
1161   21          delta_w = self.non_linear(self.i1_(delta_w))
1162   22          delta_w = self.non_linear(self.i2_(delta_w))
       23          delta_w = delta_w + residual
1163   24          delta_w = self.B(delta_w) # obtain the approximated delta W
1164   25          return x @ delta_w

1180   E    I MPLEMENTATION OF I NTRODUCING D EPTHS TO N EAT
1181
1182   We provide a comprehensive explanation of our approach to increasing the depth of the adaptation
1183   neural network in NEAT. As depicted in Fig. 5, we introduce multiple deeply stacked intermedi-
1184   ate layers between the layers of the vanilla NEAT. These intermediate layers are essentially small
1185   adapters with a minimal parameter count (Rr×r , where r is the hidden layer dimension), and we
1186   retain non-linear activations between them, as proposed by NEAT. The adaptation process begins by
1187   feeding the weight matrix W 0 —the initialized value of the adaptation target W —into NEAT’s input
       layer. After undergoing multiple non-linear transformations through the intermediate layers, the fi-

1189   this process, the adaptation target remains fixed, while all the intermediate layers, as well as the
1190   input and output layers in NEAT, are trainable parameters.

1197   layers remains comparable to that of vanilla NEAT without the intermediate layers. As shown, we
