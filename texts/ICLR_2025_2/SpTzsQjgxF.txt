009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            The quality of training data is crucial for the performance of large language mod-
012            els (LLMs). There are recent studies utilizing LLMs to rate and select data based
013            on scores from a small set of human-designed metrics (rules). However, existing
014            rule-based methods often overly rely on human heuristics, lack robust metrics for
015            rule evaluation, and exhibit limited adaptability to new tasks. In our work, we
016
               propose a novel rule-based framework that leverages the orthogonality of score
               vectors corresponding to rules as a unique metric for rule evaluation. Our method
017
               employs an automated pipeline that first uses LLMs to generate a diverse set of
018
               rules, covering a wide range of rating aspects. It then rates a batch of data ac-
019            cording to these rules and applies the determinantal point process (DPP) from
020            random matrix theory to select the most orthogonal score vectors, effectively iso-
021            lating a subset of independent rules. Then these rules are applied to rate all data
022            and samples with the highest average scores are selected for further downstream
023            tasks such as LLM training. We validate our method through two experimental
024            setups: 1) comparison against ground truth ratings and 2) benchmarking LLMs
025            trained with the selected data. Our extensive experiments span various settings,
026            including general pre-training and domain-specific fine-tuning in fields such as
027
               IMDB, Medical, Math, and Code. The results show that our DPP rule-based rat-
               ing method consistently outperforms other methods, such as rating without rules,
028
               uniform sampling, importance resampling, and QuRating, in terms of both rating
029
               accuracy and model performance.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Large language models (LLMs) have been widely utilized across a diverse range of applications.
035   Pretraining and fine-tuning these models typically require large and diverse datasets. Studies have
036   found that data quality is critical for training good LLMs (Brown, 2020; Chowdhery et al., 2023;
037   Du et al., 2022; Dubey et al., 2024; Wenzek et al., 2019). For instance, Meta’s LIMA paper (Zhou
038   et al., 2024) demonstrated that using only 1000 carefully curated data samples can achieve better
039
      performance than using the original 50k samples. Similar phenomena have been observed in other
      studies where selecting a subset of high-quality datasets increases the training convergence and
040
      model performance (Cao et al., 2023; Hsieh et al., 2023; Xie et al., 2024; Sachdeva et al., 2024;
041
      Zhang et al., 2023; Javaheripi et al., 2023).
042
043   Recent studies now adopt an approach that employs LLM-as-a-judge to grade data quality accord-
044   ing to a set of designed metrics (which we call rules) (Yuan et al., 2024; Wettig et al., 2024; Bai
045
      et al., 2022; Mu et al.). For example, Wettig et al. (2024) rates the pre-training data using LLMs
      according to four predefined rules. RedPajama (Together AI, 2023) is continuously developing a
046
      pool of rules for users to select from, which currently contains over 40 basic criteria that LLM data
047
      should satisfy. On specific aspects such as safety, Constitutional AI (Bai et al., 2022) proposed their
048   “constitution”—a set of standard safety criteria—to generate safe synthetic data, and in Huang et al.
049   (2024) they have developed 133 rules. Most recently, OpenAI’s Rule-based Rewarding (Mu et al.)
050   proposed 21 general safety rules and injected them into the RLHF (reinforcement learning with hu-
051   man feedback) process. This rule-based rating provides greater explainability of data quality and
052   breaks down the challenge of assigning a data point one overall quality score into a simpler task
053   of giving several rule-specific scores. Evidence suggests that this fine-grained approach also yields
      more accurate rating outcomes (Yuan et al., 2024; Wettig et al., 2024; Bai et al., 2022; Mu et al.).

054
      Nonetheless, there are several critical problems and challenges. First, designing an effective set
055   of rules is quite difficult, a fact acknowledged by most of the papers above. Current designs in
056   these papers all rely heavily on human heuristics and are sometimes too broad for effective rating.
057   Second, as far as we know, the metrics to evaluate rules are lacking, and there has been no systematic
058   exploration of how different rule choices and sizes impact the outcomes. In previous experiments
059   Bai et al. (2022); Wettig et al. (2024); Together AI (2023), a subset of rules is selected (typically
060   randomly) during the rating process. This selection can significantly influence the rating results
061   and consequently the quality of the sampled data. Furthermore, the utility and impact of rules can
062   vary significantly, and some rules are strongly correlated with each other, as highlighted in Wettig
063
      et al. (2024), which introduces redundancy and bias into the rating procedure. Therefore, a critical
      question arises: with a “constitution” (a pool of rules) in hand, exactly which “laws” (a subset of
064
      task-related rules) should be applied to a specific task? Random selection as in Bai et al. (2022) may
065
      not be the optimal strategy. A third major drawback is the inflexibility of these rules; they are often
066   designed for specific settings, such as pre-training or safety tasks, not generally applicable across
067   different settings.
068
069
      In our work, we aim to address these challenges. First, we leverage an LLM (GPT-4 Achiam et al.
      (2023)) for automatic rule generation, where we include the descriptions of the task and source
070
      dataset in the prompt. At this stage, some generated rules are found to be repetitive or redundant,
071
      similar to the issues in human-designed rules. Our strategy is to first generate a comprehensive
072   set of rules to ensure a broad diversity that covers various aspects we seek to evaluate in the data,
073   and then filter out repetitive rules. Hence the second step of our approach is to select a subset of
074   rules that are relatively uncorrelated/independent. This is achieved by first using the rules to rate
075   a batch of data, creating one score vector for each rule, and then assessing the independence of
076   rule subsets through the overall orthogonality of their corresponding score vectors. We propose
077   the formula in Section 3 to measure the orthogonality and use determinant point process (DPP)
078   sampling (Macchi, 1975; Borodin & Olshanski, 2000) to identify a subset of independent rules.
079   Once the rules are determined, the third step is to use them to rate all source data and select the high-
080
      quality ones. Combining these steps of rule generation, rule-based rating, rule selection by DPP, and
      data selection, our method establishes a fully automated framework for rule-based data selection
081
      (illustrated in Figure 1). Notably, we are the first to introduce the mathematical rule evaluation
082
      metric, based on the orthogonality of their score vectors. Moreover, our pipeline does not need
083   human intervention in designing and selecting rules at all. For every new task, one can use the
084   pipeline to get a set of high-quality, task-specific rules at low cost. These address the challenges
085   of existing methods mentioned above. Another advantage of our method is its natural extension,
086   allowing for customization such as re-weighting particular rules, and this is only feasible when the
087   selected rules are relatively “orthonormal”.
088
      Note that our data selection methodology is highly versatile and applicable to a variety of scenar-
089   ios, including LLM pre-training, fine-tuning on specific domains, RLHF preference data, etc. We
090   conduct experiments to cover a range of tasks and datasets, including general pre-training data and
091   domain-specific data in four domains: IMDB, Medical, Math, and Code.1 We show that our rule-
092   based data selection typically yields more accurate rating results, thereby enhancing data quality
093   and leading to better performance of the LLM trained with the data. Here is a list of the main
094   contributions of our work:
095   1. Rule-free vs. Rule-based Rating. Our systematic experiments demonstrate that fine-grained
096      rule-based rating outperforms rule-free methods, producing more precise data quality assess-
097      ments, leading to improved benchmark performance of LLMs.
098   2. Rule Evaluation Metric: We introduce a novel rule evaluation metric designed to promote low
099      correlation and high diversity among rules. We propose the method of using DPP on task-aware
100      rule-rating vectors to select a subset of independent rules.
101   3. Automated Rule-based Rating and Selection Pipeline. We confirm that LLMs are effective
102      rule generators, eliminating the need for manual rule crafting. Our automated pipeline generates
103
         the rules, selects the rules, and then chooses data according to rule-based ratings. This entire
         process operates independently of human heuristics and is free from human biases.
104
      4. Cross-Domain and Cross-Model. We validate our method through two approaches: A) com-
105
         parison with ground truth ratings, and B) training LLMs with selected data and assessing perfor-

115
      LLM data selection. There are different genres of data selection approaches for LLMs. Basic
      filterings, such as setting thresholds on word lengths, are used in many studies to eliminate low-
116
      quality data (Soldaini et al., 2024; Wenzek et al., 2019; Raffel et al., 2020; Conneau & Lample,
117
      2019; Penedo et al., 2023; Laurençon et al., 2022). Fuzzy deduplication is another approach which
118   removes repetitive or similar data samples (Allamanis, 2019; Lee et al., 2021; Abbas et al., 2023;
119   Gao et al., 2020; Jiang et al., 2022). Another method is “heuristic classification”, selecting data
120   based on a predefined quality score, typically measured by similarity to formal sources such as
121   Wikipedia or other human-generated, high-quality datasets (Brown, 2020; Touvron et al., 2023;
122   Chowdhery et al., 2023; Du et al., 2022; Gao et al., 2020; Wenzek et al., 2019). In contrast to this,
123   directly querying LLMs to rate data and use the scores as the quality indicator has become a standard
124   practice in many studies (Li et al., 2023a; Chen et al., 2023; Bai et al., 2022; Wettig et al., 2024;
125   Yuan et al., 2024; Dubois et al., 2024; Li et al., 2023b; Fernandes et al., 2023).
126   Rule-based rating. There are studies adopting a more fine-grained approach to data quality, distill-
127   ing it into a finite set of metrics which we refer to as “rules”. For instance, RedPajama (Together
128   AI, 2023) provides over 40 quality rules that serve as basic quality metrics for the users to choose
129   from. More pertinent to our research, there are papers that apply this rule-based idea to rate LLM
130   data. For example, Yuan et al. (2024) assigns a score out of 5 to each data point, awarding 1 point
131   for each of the 5 predefined criteria met. In Wettig et al. (2024), the authors designed four general
132
      rules to rate and select data for LLM pre-training. (Sun et al., 2024) proposed 16 human-crafted
      rules to evaluate the desirable quality of response data. The rule-based approach is also utilized
133
      in more targeted applications, such as ensuring data safety. Constitutional AI designed 16 general
134
      safety critique rules to revise synthetic data, enhancing data safety (Bai et al., 2022). This revision
135   process involves iterative steps where a random subset of rules from the “constitution” (the entire set
136   of rules) is applied. Additionally, in Mu et al., the score generated by an LLM grader according to a
137   set of 21 safety rules is integrated directly into the RLHF process as an additional reward. In Wang
138   et al. (2024), they design a composite reward model in RLHF, trained using rule-based ratings. As
139   noted earlier in the introduction, the rules employed in the literature exhibit several critical issues.
140   They often depend heavily on human heuristics for design, lack robust rule evaluation metrics and
141   exploration of rule sizes, and demonstrate limited versatility for new tasks or for customization. Our
142   goal is to address these challenges using our proposed framework.

      3.2   D ETERMINANTAL POINT PROCESS (DPP)
189
      The optimal solution to this mathematical problem of selecting the most orthogonal subset of a set of
190   vectors is NP-hard (Civril & Magdon-Ismail, 2007; Kulesza et al., 2012) but we use DPP sampling
191   to provide a relatively good solution. The determinant point process (DPP) is a probabilistic model
192   that describes the likelihood of selecting diverse subsets from a larger set (Macchi, 1975; Borodin &
193   Olshanski, 2000). Mathematically, a DPP is defined by a kernel matrix that describes the similarities
194   between elements in a set. The probability of selecting a particular subset is proportional to the
195   determinant of the corresponding submatrix of this kernel matrix. Intuitively, subsets with highly
196   similar items (leading to higher correlation in the submatrix) have smaller determinants and are thus
197   less likely to be chosen.
198   DPP Definitions. Given a discrete ground set Y, without loss of generality we let Y =
199   {1, 2, . . . , R}, a (discrete) DPP defines a probability measure over 2Y , the power set of Y. Let
200   Y be a randomly chosen subset. Then for any subset A ⊆ Y, the probability of A being chosen by
201   a DPP is given by:
202                                            P(A ⊆ Y ) = det(KA )
203                                                                                                         def
      where K ∈ RR×R is a real positive-semidefinite matrix called the kernel matrix and KA =
204
      [K]i,j∈A is the submatrix of K indexed by elements in A.
205
206   Kernel Matrix. Each entry Kij in the kernel matrix K describes the similarity between elements i
207   and j in Y. For our purpose of selecting orthogonal rules, we will define K as the Gram matrix of
                           def
208   the score vectors: K = S ⊤ S.
209
      DPP Sampling. To sample a diverse subset using DPP, there are several existing algorithms (Hough
210   et al., 2006; Kulesza et al., 2012; Tremblay et al., 2018) and the Python library DPPy (Gautier
211   et al., 2019) implements some of them. The computation of the DPP sampling primarily hinges
212   on the overhead of computing the inner product kernel matrix K and its eigendecomposition. In
213   our case, K ∈ RR×R and hence it requires O(R3 ) time, where R is the number of all rules.
214   Nonetheless, we set R = 50 in our experiments, therefore our DPP rule selection algorithm is
215   extremely fast (typically within 0.1 seconds). Further details about DPP sampling algorithms and
      their time complexities can be found in Appendix A.3.

      randomly selected rules of the same size r, evaluating both the rule correlation ρ(S̄) and MSE ϵ(S̄)
356   for their corresponding score submatrices S̄. The results show that DPP consistently produces rules
357   with lower correlation and MSE, regardless of the value r (see Figures 3b and 3c). Another key
358   observation is that the MSE for DPP rules increases when r is either too small or too large, with the
359   optimal r falling somewhere in the middle. This matches our intuition: when r is too small, there
360   are too few rules to achieve sufficient rating diversity, and when r is too large, rule redundancy can
361   negatively affect the rating outcomes. In fact, this motivates our selection of r = 10 in this paper.
362   Variations in rating schemes and rater models (answer to Q5). To verify that our method works
363   across different rating schemes and rater models, we explored the following variations: 1. Pair-
364   wise v.s. individual rating. While the pairwise ratings provide more reliable comparisons, indi-
365   vidual rating requires only O(n) computation. We observed similar results as in Section 4.2 (see
366   Appendix A.6.3). Notably, individual ratings on the IMDB dataset showed a Pearson correlation
367   between rule correlation ρ(S̄) and MSE ϵ(S̄) of up to 0.6, and the winning rates show that DPP sig-
368   nificantly outperforms both QuRating and the NoRule. 2. Llama3-8B v.s. Llama3-70B. We tested
369   the influence of rater model capability by switching to Llama3-70B (instruction-tuned version), us-
370
      ing the individual rating scheme on IMDB. The results are similar to earlier and we also noted a
      high Pearson correlation (over 0.6) between rule correlation and MSE, along with a high winning
371
      rate of DPP compared to QuRating and NoRule. Furthermore, randomly selected rules perform
372
      significantly better than both QuRating and NoRule. See Appendix A.6.4 for further details.

      Notably, rule-based methods consistently outperform rule-free approaches in general, especially
487   when comparing against Random Select and No Rule. Among all rule-based methods, QuRating un-
488   derperforms. As previously noted, such human-designed rules are inherently limited due to varying
489   preferences of designers, introducing bias in rules. Additionally, human designers may not capture
490   the data-dependent correlation between rules effectively. The GPT-Uncorrelated rules face a similar
491   issue where the rule selection process is entirely independent of the data. In contrast, our framework
492   begins by automatically generating a diverse set of rules and then selecting an orthogonal subset.
493   Furthermore, our method employs the data-dependent score vector to represent rules and utilizes a
494   quantitative measure to accurately assess their correlation.
495   Within our framework, DPP demonstrated superior performance compared to using all 50 rules or
496   selecting 10 rules randomly. This aligns with our argument of the importance of rule orthogonality,
497   as well as the intuition that the optimal r is not near the boundaries (both validated by the previ-
498   ous experiments on Section 4). This underscores the effectiveness of a rule-based strategy, which
499   introduces more balanced diversity in the data rating aspects and selects better training data. Fur-
500   thermore, it also demonstrates that our application of DPP in rule selection effectively identifies
501
      a core set of high-quality rules, thereby enhancing data quality and ultimately improving model
      performance.

      6      C ONCLUSION
530
531
      We have introduced an automated, rule-based framework for selecting high-quality LLM data, uti-
      lizing LLMs to generate a diverse set of rules and the DPP method to eliminate redundancy. Our
532
      work is the first to introduce an automated rule evaluation metric and we also propose a rule-based
533
      selection pipeline that demonstrates substantial generalizability across various settings, effectively
534   overcoming the limitations of human-designed rules and addressing the challenges associated with
535   the lack of robust rule evaluations. We first demonstrated that our approach enhances the accu-
536   racy of data ratings using a dataset with given ground truth scores. Then we conduct experiments
537   that train LLMs with selected data and have shown that our method outperforms various other ap-
538   proaches, both in general pre-training and fine-tuning across four domains. The results indicate
539   that our method successfully generates high-quality, diverse rules, and thereby improves quality of
      selected data, which in turn leads to improved model performance after trained with the chosen data.

795
796   Intuition by r = 2 case. Here we use the r = 2 case to illustrate the intuition behind DPP and
797   explain why it tends to choose items that are relatively uncorrelated. Using the same notation as in
798   3.1, let K be the kernel matrix and Y, Y be the ground set and selected subset respectively. When
799   r = 2, consider items A = {i, j}. Then the probability of both items being selected together is
800   given by:
801
                    P(A ⊆ Y ) = Ki,i Kj,j − Ki,j Kj,i
802
                                                       2
803                            = P(i ∈ Y )P(j ∈ Y ) − Ki,j
804                            = P(i is chosen)P(j is chosen) − (similarity of items i, j)2 ,
805
806
      since K is symmetric by our definition. Larger similarity of i, j reduces the probability P(A ⊆ Y ),
      indicating that similar items are less likely to be chosen simultaneously. This underscores the DPP’s
807
      capacity to promote diversity by favoring the selection of dissimilar items.
808
809   DPP Sampling Algorithm: The sampling algorithm can be found in Algorithm 1 of Kulesza et al.
      (2012). The sampling process starts by decomposing the kernel matrix K and involves two main


                                                       15
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
      stages: 1. Selecting eigenvectors by sampling from a Bernoulli distribution based on the eigenvalues,
811   and 2. Sampling a subset from the ground set using an iterative conditional distribution method to
812   ensure diversity, as detailed in (Kulesza et al., 2012). We utilize the DPPy Python library (Gautier
813   et al., 2019) for efficient DPP initialization and sampling.
814
      Time Complexity: Finding the submatrix (subset of columns) of a matrix to maximize the orthogo-
815
      nality is NP-hard (Civril & Magdon-Ismail, 2007; Kulesza et al., 2012). DPP provides us a relatively
816
      good solution. In practice, the computational complexity of sampling from a DPP depends primar-
817   ily on the eigendecomposition of the kernel matrix K. In our case, K ∈ RR×R and therefore it
818   requires O(R3 ) time, where R is the number of rules. In the DPPy package (Gautier et al., 2019) it
819   uses the spectral sampler by default, so the actual run-time of our DPP implementation is O(R3 ).
820
      DPP Sampling for Data Selection: We noticed that in a concurrent work Yang et al. (2024), the
821
      authors also use DPP to perform data selection, but directly applied to the data itself. However,
822
      the approach to directly perform data selection using DPP requires the computation based on the
823   kernel matrix with dimension N (number of samples), which is usually huge in the context of LLM
824   data. Moreover, while DPP inherently prioritizes diversity in data selection, it does not address other
825   quality dimensions. In contrast, our rule-based approach assesses multiple aspects of data quality,
826   ensuring a more comprehensive and robust selection process.

830
831   Imagine the cases where the target dataset distribution shows a long-tail pattern with respect to
832   our quality measure, using a deterministic quality score as the cutoff could exclude many possibly
833
      valuable data (Albalak et al., 2024). Hence, our stochastic sampling in 3 effectively balances the
      quality and diversity of the selected data. Nonetheless, instead of doing actual sampling according
834
      to Equation 3, we use the Gumbel top-k trick similar as in (Wettig et al., 2024), which is a sampling
835
      technique used to efficiently and probabilistically select the top-k items from a discrete probability
836   distribution. Specifically, each item i in the distribution is assigned a score using the formula:
837
838
                                                si = log pi + gi ,
839
840
      where pi is the probability of item i, and gi is a noise term drawn from a Gumbel distribution, which
841
      can be generated using gi = − log(− log(ui )). In other words, we could add a Gumbel noise vector
842
      to the log of the sampling probability in Equation 3 and then choose the top-k data points with the
843   highest sums. This is statistically equivalent to sampling according to Equation 3 (Kool et al., 2019).

852   selected for rating, influencing the diversity and coverage of the selected rules. We have explored
853   the effect of r in Section 4 and also in Appendix A.7.5. We leave a comprehensive study of its

      Data sampling method. There are variations of the stochastic top-k sampling, such as incorporating

      design and offer significant explainability. However, rules in other formats can also be integrated

      is another intriguing topic for future work.

887   A.6.2    E RROR M ETRICS
888
889   Ranking-difference error. To assess the deviation of rating scores from the ground truth, instead
890   of using the mean squared error in 4, an alternative intuitive approach is to compare the rankings
891   derived from the data scores with those of the ground truth. This approach is based on the premise
892
      that for data selection purposes, if two sets of scores yield identical rankings, they will select the
      same high-scoring data samples. An example of such a ranking metric is the Kendall rank correlation
893
      coefficient (Kendall’s tau) (Kendall, 1938). However, we opted against this type of metric for two
894
      critical reasons: First, it lacks the granularity needed to evaluate errors effectively. For instance,
895   two sets of scores like [0.01, 0.98, 0.99] and [0.01, 0.02, 0.03] share exactly the same ranking yet
896   differ significantly in their actual scores. Second, our method involves stochastic data selection,
897   not a straightforward top-k selection, meaning that a higher score increases the likelihood of a data
898   point being chosen. Hence, a ranking difference, which overlooks the absolute values of scores and
899   focuses solely on their relative comparisons, is not ideal here.

       A.7.4     E VALUATION B ENCHMARKS
1272
1273   In this section, we provide detailed descriptions of the benchmarks utilized for our evaluation.
1274   We considered the following benchmarks for general continued pre-training: ARC-Challenge (15),
1275   Winogrande (15), MMLU (5), SST-2 (0), where the numbers in parenthesis indicate the number of
1276   shots we use in few-shot benchmark setting. For domain fine-tuning, we use zero-shot in IMDB, and
1277   5-shot for Medical and Math (which uses subsets of MMLU). Moreover, Math and Medical domains,
1278
       we use the subject-related subsets from MMLU, specifically ElementaryMathematics, HighSchool-
       Mathematics, and CollegeMathematics for Math, and CollegeMedicine, ProfessionalMedicine, and
1279
       MedicalGenetics for Medical. For Code, we tested code generation and for each code benchmark,
1280
       we use the pass@k setting and specify the number of code generation samples. See detailed expla-
1281   nations below.
1282
1283           • MMLU (Maas et al., 2011): MMLU is a comprehensive multitask test comprises multiple-
1284             choice questions from a wide range of knowledge domains. It spans subjects across the
1285             humanities, social sciences, hard sciences, and other critical learning areas, encompassing
1286             57 tasks such as elementary mathematics, US history, computer science, law, and more. To
1287             achieve high accuracy on this test, models need to demonstrate extensive world knowledge
1288             and robust problem-solving capabilities.
1289           • IMDB (Maas et al., 2011): The IMDB dataset comprises 50,000 movie reviews and is
1290             designed for binary sentiment classification. For our evaluation, we select 25,000 test sam-
1291             ples.
1292           • Winogrande (Sakaguchi et al., 2021): WinoGrande is a collection of 44,000 problems
1293             inspired by the Winograd Schema Challenge. It has been adjusted to enhance scale and
1294             robustness against dataset-specific bias. Designed as a fill-in-the-blank task with binary
1295             options, WinoGrande requires users to select the correct option for a given sentence based
                 on commonsense reasoning.


                                                                      24
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1296
               • SST-2 (Socher et al., 2013): SST-2, or the Stanford Sentiment Treebank binary classifica-
1297             tion dataset, is a widely used resource for sentiment analysis tasks. Derived from movie
1298             reviews, it consists of 11,855 single sentences, each annotated for sentiment polarity.
1299
               • ARC-Easy and ARC-Challenge (Yadav et al., 2019): The AI2’s Reasoning Challenge
1300
                 (ARC) dataset is designed for evaluating multiple-choice question-answering systems. It
1301             consists of science exam questions for grades 3 to 9 and is divided into two subsets: Easy
1302             and Challenge. The Challenge subset comprises more complex questions that necessitate
1303             advanced reasoning skills. Typically, questions offer four answer choices, although a small
1304             fraction (less than 1%) may present three or five options. The dataset also features a Knowl-
1305             edge Base (KB) containing 14.3 million unstructured text passages to support reasoning and
1306             answer generation.
1307           • HumanEval (Chen et al., 2021): The HumanEval benchmark evaluates Python program-
1308             ming skills with 164 problems, each comprising a function signature, docstring, function
1309             body, and unit tests. In a zero-shot setting, models generate code using top-p sampling
1310             (p=0.95) until stop words are reached. Pass@k metrics (k=1, 10, 100) are calculated with
1311             n=200 samples per problem, estimating the success rate following Chen et al.’s approach.
1312             Success is determined by whether at least one solution is correct within k attempts, with
1313
                 temperature controlling randomness in generation. This benchmark measures model per-
                 formance in solving programming tasks with increasing attempts.
1314
1315           • MBPP (Austin et al., 2021): The MBPP benchmark contains around 1,000 crowd-sourced
1316             Python programming problems, designed for entry-level programmers. Each problem
1317             includes a task description, a code solution, and 3 test cases. The evaluation is per-
1318
                 formed on the test set from index 11 to 511. In a few-shot setting, the InCoder-style
                 prompt is used, where the task description and one solution are provided to guide the
1319
                 model. The prompt format is f’"""{description}{test example}"""’. By de-
1320
                 fault, prompt type mbpp is set to incoder, and optionally, the solution can be in-
1321             cluded using include solution mbpp=True. We use single generation per problem
1322             (pass@1), and for pass@k estimation, we generate n=15 samples per problem, similar to
1323             the HumanEval approach. The evaluation focuses on pass@1 success rates.
1324
               • Multiple-py and Multiple-cpp (Cassano et al., 2022): MultiPL-E: is a benchmark for
1325             evaluating large language models for code generation that supports 18 programming lan-
1326             guages. It takes the OpenAI “HumanEval” Python benchmark and uses little compilers to
1327             translate them to other languages. We use similar implementation as the original repository
1328             and evaluation parameters are similar to HumanEval.
