011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Quantum computing promises significant computational advantages over classical
014            computing. However, current devices are constrained by a limited qubit count and
015            noise. By combining classical optimization methods with parameterized quantum
016
               circuits, Variational Quantum Algorithms (VQAs) offer a potential solution for
               noisy intermediate-scale quantum systems (NISQ). This makes VQAs particularly
017
               promising strategies for achieving near-term quantum advantages; such approaches
018
               are now widely explored for nearly all quantum computing applications. However,
019            designing effective parameterized circuits, also known as ansatz, remains challeng-
020            ing. In this work, we introduce the use of GFlowNets as an efficient method to
021            automate the development of efficient ansatz for various quantum computing prob-
022            lems. Our approach leverages GFlowNets to efficiently explore the combinatorial
023            space of parameterized quantum circuits. Our extensice experiments demonstrate
024            that GFlowNets can discover ansatz with an order of magnitude fewer parameters,
025            gate counts, and depths compared to current approaches for the molecular elec-
026            tronic ground state energy problem. We also apply our approach to the unweighted
027
               Max-Cut problem, where we observe similar improvements in circuit efficiency.
               These results highlight the potential of GFlowNets to significantly reduce the
028
               resource requirements of VQAs while maintaining or improving solution quality.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Quantum computing has made remarkable progress in the past decade. Novel quantum devices boast
034
      high qubit count and ever improving coherence times (Preskill, 2018; Farhi et al., 2014; Peruzzo et al.,
035
      2014; Lau et al., 2022; Li et al., 2020; Chow et al., 2021). Despite these promising advances, we
036   remain in the era of noisy intermediate-scale quantum (NISQ) systems: modern quantum computers
037   are not yet fully fault tolerant and are still sensitive to noise. Many research efforts have been
038   made in order to render NISQ-era quantum devices usable for practical purposes (Bang & Yoo,
039   2014; Farhi et al., 2014; Lamata et al., 2018; Lau et al., 2022; Peruzzo et al., 2014; Li et al., 2020).
040   Variational quantum algorithms (VQAs), for instance, show promise in this direction. VQAs are
041   hybrid algorithms which use classical expectation-maximization (EM) methods in tandem with
042   quantum circuits to iteratively fit parameterized circuits. These methods can be used to solve a variety
043   of problems, such as the ground state energy problem, quantum machine learning, and combinatorial
044
      optimization problems, which have applications in many fields, e.g. molecule discovery, logistics,
      and machine learning (Peruzzo et al., 2014; Farhi et al., 2014; Li et al., 2020; Biamonte et al., 2017;
045
      Schuld & Killoran, 2019; Schuld, 2021; Dai & Krems, 2022; Guo et al., 2024; Torabian & Krems,
046
      2023; Cerezo et al., 2022).
047
048   Despite their promise, one of the main challenges in VQA research lies in designing efficient quantum
049   circuits (or ansatz); the space of possible circuits is inherently combinatorial and it is impossible to
050
      explore it fully (Peruzzo et al., 2014; Farhi et al., 2014). Furthermore, many current approaches to
      ansatz design either rely on human expertise or use heuristic-based methods, which may not scale
051
      well as problem sizes grow.
052
053   In this work, we propose to use GFlowNets (GFNs) (Bengio et al., 2023) to automate quantum circuit
      design. GFlowNets are powerful generative models based on stochastic reinforcement learning (RL).

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      GFNs are particularly well-suited for this task because they can learn a policy to sample complex,
055   compositional structures, such as quantum circuits, while promoting diversity in the generated
056   solutions (Bengio et al., 2021). By sampling candidate solutions proportionally to the reward
057   distribution, they can uncover the underlying structure of a solution space, leading to efficient
058   exploration even when the number of solutions is prohibitively high.
059
      Our approach fundamentally relies on treating circuit design as a decision problem. We leverage the
060
      compositional structure of quantum circuits and the diversity-seeking policy of GFNs to generate
061   diverse and efficient circuit structures for the problem at hand. This enables the discovery of ansatz
062   with significantly fewer parameters, gate counts, and depths compared to current approaches, as
063   demonstrated by our experimental results. Notably, for the molecular electronic ground state problem,
064   our approach achieve circuits that require an order of magnitude fewer resources without sacrificing
065   accuracy. Similarly, for the unweighted Max-Cut problem, we observe substantial improvements in
066   circuit efficiency.
067
068   Key Contributions
069
070          • We develop and apply GFlowNets for efficient quantum circuit sampling in quantum chem-
071            istry and combinatorial optimization problems.
072          • Our method achieves energy estimates for molecular ground state problems within chemical
073            accuracy and successfully solves the unweighted Max-Cut problem, while significantly
074            reducing circuit complexity compared to alternative approaches.
075
             • We demonstrate the generalizability of GFlowNet-sampled circuits, which maintain accuracy
076
               when re-optimized for different molecular geometries not encountered during training.

      Quantum Circuit Design Evolutionary algorithms, particularly genetic algorithms (GA) have been
090
      applied to quantum circuit design since the late 1990s, with notable success in evolving simple circuits
091   like quantum error correction codes (Williams & Gray, 1998; Massey et al., 2004; Bang & Yoo, 2014;
092   Lamata et al., 2018; Las Heras et al., 2016). However, GAs are limited by gene length, candidate gate
093   set size, and their inability to handle parameterized rotation gates, reducing their effectiveness for
094   evolving VQA ansatz. Reinforcement learning algorithms, such as those by (Ostaszewski et al., 2021;
095   Ye & Chen, 2021; Kuo et al., 2021) have shown promise in selecting gates for VQE, outperforming
096   chemistry-inspired methods like UCCSD (Romero et al., 2018) in efficiency and accuracy. RL
097   approaches have demonstrated success on smaller systems. Sampling-based algorithms introduce
098   adaptive VQE ansatz searches (Grimsley et al., 2019; Tang et al., 2021; Du et al., 2022; Zhang
099
      et al., 2022; Wu et al., 2023; Lu et al., 2023), though they suffer from inefficiencies and require
      predefined circuit structures, limiting their flexibility. The Generative Quantum Eigensolver (GQE)
100
      (Nakaji et al., 2024), and specifically its transformer-based variant GPT-QE, leverages generative
101
      models and pre-training for quantum circuit generation. While effective in finding ground states
102   for electronic structure Hamiltonians, GQE is constrained by using predetermined quantum circuit
103   structures as tokens, leading to long circuits that can reduce efficiency and flexibility in broader
104   applications. Another work (Fürrutter et al., 2024) use text-conditioned denoising diffusion models to
105   efficiently generate quantum circuits, overcoming simulation bottlenecks and advancing entanglement
106   generation and unitary compilation tasks. Other approaches such as Brute Force Search (Torabian &
107   Krems, 2023; Guo et al., 2024) and Bayesian Optimization (Duong et al., 2022) are shown to lead to
      less efficient search processes, requiring large computational resources.

      GFlowNets GFlowNets have have been used successfully across a number of disciplines such
109   as drug discovery (Bengio et al., 2021), phyllogenetic inference (Zhou et al., 2023), causal graph
110   learning (Nishikawa-Toomey et al., 2022) and even language model finetuning (Hu et al., 2023).
111   GFlowNets are based in a rich literature ranging from sampling methods to stochastic RL. Prior
112   to GFlowNets, many MCMC methods (Grathwohl et al., 2021; Dai et al., 2020) were designed to
113   sample with probability proportional to some unnormalized energy function. However, the problem
114   of mixing between modes remains challenging for such methods. (Buesing et al., 2020) propose a
115   method to train a policy to generate samples proportional to some reward. However, their method
116   only works in the setting where a single path leads to a given state. Moreover, many connexions
117
      have been drawn between GFlowNets and other well-known methods. In terms of RL, the GFN
      objective may be reminescent of soft Q-Learning (Haarnoja et al., 2017). In fact, Tiapkin et al.
118
      (2024) show that GFlowNets are formally equivalent to stochastic RL with entropy regularization. It
119
      is also possible to treat the GFlowNet learning problem as a convex MDP problem (Zahavy et al.,
120   2021) with the goal of minizing KL divergence. Finally, Malkin et al. (2022b) show that variational
121   inference algorithms are equivalent to special cases of GFlowNets.

130   3.1   G FLOW N ETS
131
132   GFlowNets (Bengio et al., 2021; 2023) are a family of generative models which sample compositional
133   objects x such as graphs or strings. To generate such objects, GFlowNets learn a stochastic policy π(x)
134   that generates samples proportionally to some reward function R(x). Every step of this stochastic
135
      decision process can be seen as adding a component to x. For example adding an edge or a node to a
      graph.
136
137
138   Problem Setting Let G = (S, A) be a directed acyclic graph (DAG) with state space S and action
139   space A. we will denote by s0 ∈ S the initial state (a state with only outgoing transitions) and we
140
      will denote by either sf or x ∈ X ⊂ S a terminating state (which only has incoming transitions).
141   The goal of a GFlowNet is to iteratively construct an object x ∈ X by starting from an initial state
142   s0 and sequentially applying actions until it reaches a terminating state sf (or x). The sequences of
143   steps taken to construct x are defined as a trajectory τ = (s0 → s1 → ... → sf ). This sequence can
144   be thought of as a path on the DAG. We denote by T the set of all such trajectories from s0 to some
145   terminating state.
146   The value of the constructed object x is measured through some reward function R : X → R+ . This
147   reward function usually takes the form R(x) = e−βE(x) , where E is some energy function and β is
148   some temperature parameter.
149
150   Flows and flow matching The main intuition behind the GFlowNet formulation is to consider
151   the DAG as a flow network, where the flow through some node (or edge) corresponds to some
152   unnormalized probability of visiting said node (or edge respectively). This notion can be formalized
153   through the definition of flow functions (Bengio et al., 2021). We start by defining the trajectory flow
154   F : T → R+ , where F (τ ) represents the unnormalized

239   Quantum Eigensolver (VQE) (Peruzzo et al., 2014), which is designed to find the ground state energy,

      4.1   Q UANTUM ARCHITECTURE SEARCH PROBLEM AS AN INFERENCE PROBLEM WITH

287   QAS problems for VQA involve optimizing a task-dependent loss function with discrete parameters
288   Gi and continuous parameters θ. In this work, we use GFlowNets to solve the discrete part of this
289   optimization problem. The QAS problem involves discovering efficient circuit compositions for a
290   given task, which requires exploring a combinatorial search space. We approach this by efficiently
291   sampling from an energy-based model, where the probability distribution p∗T (x) ∝ exp(−βE(x))
292
      guides the exploration of possible compositions Ai . Here, β is the inverse temperature parameter.
      Sampling a well-trained GFlowNet is equivalent to sampling from p∗T (x) itself. As β approaches
293
      zero, the GFlowNet converges to the distribution of the optimal compositions of quantum operations.
294
      Conversely, as β approaches infinity, the GFlowNet converges to the distribution of all possible
295   compositions of quantum operations (Bengio et al., 2023). As stated in Section 3.1, learning to
296   sample from this distribution is equivalent to learning a stochastic policy π by flow-matching. Thus,
297   we frame the QAS problem as an RL problem with the following constituents:
298
299   State The state of each VQA problem is defined by a composition, denoted as G, which consists
300   of quantum gates arranged into a quantum circuit. This circuit is characterized by a sequence
301   x = x1 . . . xt , where each xi represents an action vector xi = (x1i , . . . , xni ). In this context, each
302   element xji belongs to the set 0, 1, 2, . . . , Ng , specifying the quantum gate applied at time i to qubit
303   j, as shown in Figure 1b. For instance, xji = 0 indicates that an identity gate is applied to qubit
304   j. The symbol Ng represents the total number of distinct quantum operations available. Each state
305   represents a variational quantum circuit and is a terminal state. It is important to note that not all
306   quantum operations are applied to every qubit at each time step; if no action is applied to a qubit,
307   zero is recorded in the action vector.
308
309   Action We define a set of quantum gates G = {X, SX, H, RX , RY , RZ , CNOT}. We assume that
310
      all single-qubit gates can be applied to any qubit. The CNOT gate requires one qubit to act as a
      control and another as a target; we include all possible pairings of qubits for this gate. With this
311
      choice of quantum gates, we have the necessary components for universal quantum computations as
312
      shown in Theorem 4.1. The subset {RX , RY , RZ } consists of parameterized rotation gates, which
313   introduce free parameters to the quantum circuits. The initial state is an empty sequence x0 = ∅,
314   representing the quantum state beginning with the all-zero state |0⟩. The action of the model is to
315   choose a gate from G to act on the quantum state. Each selected gate from G modifies the quantum
316   state. To prevent the model from selecting invalid or redundant actions, we apply a forward mask, as
317   described in Appendix A.2.
318   Theorem 4.1. (Nielsen & Chuang, 2001) The set of gates G = {X, SX, H, RX , RY , RZ , CNOT}
319   is universal for quantum computation.
320
321   Reward We set the log reward to be the loss of the VQA problem with scaling parameter β and
322   offset parameter b, i.e. E(x) = −β minθ (L (Gi , θ) − b). When a terminal state is reached, we
323   optimize the parameters θ to minimize the loss function L associated with G in order to evaluate the
      reward.

      5.1   E XPERIMENTAL SETUP
327
328   For the GFlowNet implementation, we use a transformer network with a hidden size of 4096, 4 layers,
329   an embedding dimension of 256, and 8 attention heads for the forward policy. The backward policy
330   is modeled by a uniform distribution to quickly approach one of the optimal solutions as suggested
331   in (Malkin et al., 2022a). We implement all our models in PyTorch (Paszke et al., 2019), and we
332   adopt trajectory balance as the main objective function. The model is updated every 5 iterations, after
333
      computing the batch loss for 5 complete trajectories. We use the Adam optimizer with a learning rate
      of 1 × 10−4 for the forward policy model PF and 1 × 10−1 for the partition function parameter Zθ .
334
      The inverse temperature parameter β used in the reward function, is set to 1000 based on prior work
335
      by Zhang et al. (2023). We limit the circuits in all experiments to a maximum 20 parameters (number
336   of rotation gates) and a maximum depth of 30. All quantum computations are performed using a
337   noise-free simulator provided by PennyLane, a comprehensive software framework for quantum
338   computing (Bergholm et al., 2018). We leverage PennyLane’s state vector simulator to execute the
339   variational quantum circuits. The circuit parameters θ are optimized using PennyLane’s built-in
340   Adam optimizer, with a learning rate of 1 × 10−2 , 10 random reinitializations, and a maximum of
341   2000 optimization steps.

343   5.2   G ROUND STATE ENERGY PROBLEM
344
345   In this experiment, we aim to determine the ground state energies of the hydrogen (H2 ) and lithium
346
      hydride (LiH) molecules. We perform calculations at various intramolecular bond distances using
      different approximation schemes, which result in Hamiltonians defined over different numbers of
347
      qubits. The computations are carried out using the PennyLane and PySCF plugins (Sun et al., 2018),
348
      and all calculations utilize the STO-3G basis set. For the hydrogen molecule (H2 ), we compute the
349   ground state energy using the full Hamiltonian, with no additional approximations. This results in a
350   4-qubit Hamiltonian. We set the offset parameter b = 0. We analyze the method’s performance at
351   two different H-H bond distances: 0.745Å and 1.5Å. For the lithium hydride (LiH) molecule, we
352   apply an approximation that leverages the molecule’s symmetry, and we remove a frozen orbital that
353   interacts weakly with the other orbitals, as described in (Bravyi et al., 2017; Setia et al., 2020). This
354   approximation reduces the complexity of the problem, resulting in a 6-qubit Hamiltonian. We set
355   the offset parameter b = −7. We consider two Li-H bond distances: 1.4Å and 2.2Å. In all cases,
356   we use the Jordan-Wigner transformation to map the fermionic Hamiltonians to qubit-based spin
357   operators for quantum computation. This experimental setup allows us to evaluate the performance
358   of the proposed method in determining the ground state energies for these molecular systems. As a
359   reference, we also include results from Hartree-Fock (HF) (Szabo & Ostlund, 2012), a mean-field
360
      method providing an approximate solution, and UCCSD (Unitary Coupled Cluster with Single and
      Double excitations) (Romero et al., 2018), a standard chemistry-inspired ansatz, for comparison.

      5.3   U NWEIGHTED MAX CUT PROBLEM
364   In the experiments conducted to solve the unweighted Max Cut problem, we evaluated performance,

518   7    C ONCLUSION
519
520   In this work, we demonstrated that GFlowNet-sampled quantum circuits can successfully solve two
521   distinct types of problems: molecular ground state energy estimation and the unweighted Max-Cut
522   problem. For the ground state energy problems of H2 and LiH, our approach consistently yielded
523   energy estimates within chemical accuracy, while requiring significantly fewer parameters, gates,
524
      and circuit depth compared to other methods. This reduction in quantum circuit complexity, coupled
      with the ability to maintain accuracy, makes our method promising for deployment in resource-
525
      limited quantum hardware environments, especially for larger molecular systems. Moreover, the
526
      generalizability of the circuits generated by GFlowNets, as shown by their successful re-optimization
527   for bond lengths not encountered during training, underscores the robustness of our approach. This
528   capability could be particularly valuable for real-world applications where system configurations may
529   vary. In addressing the unweighted Max-Cut problem, our method produced more efficient circuits
530   with significantly reduced depths compared to QuantumDARTS, while achieving optimal solutions
531   for various graph densities. This highlights the potential of GFlowNets for optimizing quantum
532   circuit designs across combinatorial optimization problems. In future work, we plan to explore the
533   use of pre-trained GFlowNet models that can be fine-tuned for specific tasks, such as quantum error
534   correction and beyond the current scope of Variational Quantum Algorithms. Pre-training models on a
535
      broader set of quantum circuits for more quantum problems could potentially enhance generalizability
      and efficiency. This will allow for faster adaptation to new problem domains. Additionally, we aim to
536
      extend the application of GFlowNets to quantum error correction, where designing efficient quantum
537
      circuits is crucial for mitigating noise and ensuring the reliability of computations on near-term
538   quantum hardware. Further exploration could also involve applying GFlowNets to other quantum
539   computing tasks.

      A     Q UANTUM COMPUTATION
757
758
      In classical computing, a two-state system is represented by a binary state, typically denoted as 0
759   or 1. The state vector corresponding to such a system can be expressed as [a, b]⊤ , where a and b
760   represent the probabilities associated with each state. However, in quantum computing, the concept
761   of state is generalized to allow for superposition. A two-state quantum system, or qubit, can exist in
762   a superposition of both classical states (Nielsen & Chuang, 2001). This superposition is described
763   by a state vector in a two-dimensional complex Hilbert space as |ψ⟩ = α|0⟩ + β|1⟩, where |0⟩
764   and |1⟩ are the computational basis states, and α, β ∈ C are complex coefficients satisfying the
765   normalization condition |α|2 + |β|2 = 1. These coefficients represent amplitudes, and their squared
766   norm correspond to the probabilities of measuring the qubit in the respective basis states.
767   For a system of n qubits, the dimensionality of the state space increases exponentially, with d = 2n .
768   This means that the system can be described by a superposition of all possible combinations of n
769   qubits.
770
      The evolution of a quantum system can be simulated using quantum circuits. A quantum circuit
771   consists of a sequence of quantum gates, which are unitary operators acting on qubits. Mathematically,
772   the evolution of a quantum state |ψ⟩ under a unitary operator U can be expressed as |ψ ′ ⟩ = U |ψ⟩.
773
774
      Measuring a quantum system causes a collapse of the superposition state into one of the computational
      basis states. For example, a single-qubit state |ψ⟩ = a|0⟩ + b|1⟩ will collapse into either |0⟩ or |1⟩
775
      upon measurement. The probability of measuring the state as |0⟩ is given by |a|2 , and the probability
776
      of measuring |1⟩ is given by |b|2 .

800   A.2   F ORWARD M ASK
801
      To enforce constraints on the gate selection process, we define a mask that evaluates the feasibility of
802
      applying each quantum gate based on the sequence of previous operations. The mask prevents the
803
      model from selecting invalid or redundant actions. Specifically, we define the following rules: (1) A
804   gate cannot be applied consecutively to the same qubit, meaning the selected gate must differ from
805   the previous gate acting on that qubit. (2) Rotation gates from the set RX , RY , RZ are not allowed to
806   follow another rotation gate, ensuring a diverse set of operations. (3) The gate SX cannot be applied
807   immediately after the gate X, and vice versa, as these gates represent similar operations that could
808   lead to redundancy. (4) A two-qubit gate, such as CNOT, can only be applied if at least one other
809   gate has already been applied to one of the involved qubits, ensuring that multi-qubit operations are
      meaningful within the context of the quantum state evolution. (5) The model should avoid redundant

      B.1   D IFFERENTIABLE ARCHITECTURE SEARCH
817
      Gradient-based Quantum Architecture Search algorithms and GFlowNets both rely on the sequential
818   construction of circuits, adding gates step-by-step. However, their exploration and optimization
819   strategies differ significantly. QAS employs a deterministic, task-specific process, iteratively updating
820   circuit parameters and architectures through gradient descent to minimize loss functions, which
821   converge to a single locally optimal solution. In contrast, GFlowNets sample diverse quantum circuit
822   architectures by learning a stochastic policy that balances exploration and exploitation, generating
823   solutions proportional to their rewards. While the sampling process in differentiable architecture
824   search is guided by predefined deterministic functions, GFlowNet’s stochastic approach allows it to
825   explore a broader range of effective circuit designs.
826
827   B.2   R EINFORCEMENT LEARNING
828
      As discussed in the related work, GFlowNets are formally equivalent to stochastic reinforcement
829
      learning (RL) with entropy regularization (Tiapkin et al., 2024). Here, we outline the differences and
830   advantages of GFlowNets compared to traditional RL approaches. Unlike RL, which optimizes a
831   single policy to maximize expected cumulative rewards, GFlowNets are designed to generate diverse,
832   high-reward solutions by directly learning an objective. As noted in (Ostaszewski et al., 2021; Patel
833   et al., 2024), RL methods require the evaluation of intermediate states at each step of a trajectory,
834   and rely heavily on carefully crafted reward functions to guide exploration. In contrast, GFlowNets
835   follow an end-to-end learning approach, bypassing the need for intermediate evaluations and complex
836   reward shaping. This makes them particularly efficient for problems where rewards are defined only
837   at the end of a process, as observables of the problem. These features make GFlowNets particularly
838
      adaptive for quantum architecture search problems.

840   C     T IME C OMPLEXITY A NALYSIS
841
842   Here, we discuss the time complexity of the forward process in our method. We define n as the number
843   of qubits, l as the number of circuit layers, E as the embedding dimension, and L as the number of
844   transformer layers in the transformer. As we tokenize quantum circuits, in the worst-case scenario,
845   the length of the sequence is O(nl). At each step, we use the transformer to compute the logits for
846
      each actions, resulting in a complexity of O(L(n2 l2 E + nlE 2 )). For a single trajectory, we must
      compute attention at most nl times. Thus, the worst-case time complexity is O(L(n3 l3 E + n2 l2 E 2 )).
847
      Considering T iterations, the overall time complexity becomes O(T L(n3 l3 E + n2 l2 E 2 )).
848
849   For comparison, as reported in (Wu et al., 2023), QuantumDARTS has a time complexity of O(4n lT )
850   due to the computational cost associated with the multiplication of unitary matrices.
