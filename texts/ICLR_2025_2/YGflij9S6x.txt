                                                                           A BSTRACT
029
030                                     Machine learning models often suffer from catastrophic forgetting of previously
031
                                        learned knowledge when learning new classes. Various methods have been pro-
                                        posed to mitigate this issue. However, rehearsal-based learning, which retains
032
                                        samples from previous classes, typically achieves good performance but tends to
033
                                        memorize specific instances, struggling with Out-of-Distribution (OOD) general-
034                                     ization. This often leads to high forgetting rates and poor generalization. Sur-
035                                     prisingly, the OOD generalization capabilities of these methods have been largely
036                                     unexplored. In this paper, we highlight this issue and propose a simple yet effec-
037                                     tive strategy inspired by contrastive learning and data-centric principles to address
038                                     it. We introduce Adaptive Contrastive Replay (ACR), a method that employs
039                                     dual optimization to simultaneously train both the encoder and the classifier. ACR
040                                     adaptively populates the replay buffer with misclassified samples while ensuring
041                                     a balanced representation of classes and tasks. By refining the decision boundary
042
                                        in this way, ACR achieves a balance between stability and plasticity. Our method
                                        significantly outperforms previous approaches in terms of OOD generalization,
043
                                        achieving an improvement of 13.41% on Split CIFAR-100, 9.91% on Split Mini-
044
                                        ImageNet, and 5.98% on Split Tiny-ImageNet.1

048
049   Continual Learning (CL), the gradual acquisition of new concepts (classes or tasks) without for-
050   saking previous ones, stands as a pivotal capability in machine learning. Various methodologies,
051   including regularization techniques Kirkpatrick et al. (2017); Chaudhry et al. (2018a), architecture-
052   based approaches Mallya & Lazebnik (2018); Hung et al. (2019), and rehearsal-based strategies
053

      Lopez-Paz & Ranzato (2017); Chaudhry et al. (2018b; 2019); Aljundi et al. (2019); Prabhu et al.
055   (2020); Chaudhry et al. (2021); Sun et al. (2022; 2023), have been explored. However, the central
056   challenge remains in striking a balance between assimilating new concepts (plasticity) and preserv-
057   ing existing knowledge (stability) Kim et al. (2023); Kim & Han (2023).
058
      Current rehearsal-based methods achieve good performance but often neglect the necessity for ac-
059
      ceptable Out-of-Distribution (OOD) generalization. This means they are mostly memorized instead
060
      of learning from previous classes Verwimp et al. (2021); Zhang et al. (2022). The state-of-the-art
061   CL approaches exhibit significant performance drops when encountering OOD samples where the
062   sample is altered with a slight covariant shift. Yet, they must be capable of generalizing (refer to
063   Figure 1).
064
      The aspect of OOD generalization in CL methods has largely been overlooked until now. While
065
      previous studies have concentrated on mitigating overfitting—a factor that can influence generaliz-
066
      ability—they have often overlooked OOD generalization Bonicelli et al. (2022); Yu et al. (2022).
067   However, it is critical to acknowledge that merely memorizing previous tasks is not sufficient to
068   prevent forgetting them. Effective methods must not only retain information about prior tasks or
069   classes but also avoid overfitting specific samples and neglecting OOD generalization.
070
      Note that the previous methods aim to retain information from previous classes/tasks to avoid forget-
071
      ting, albeit through memorization. Preventing forgetting by relying on memorization can be prob-
072
      lematic. We can categorize memorization into good memorization and bad memorization. Good
073   memorization ensures that past information is retained without leading to overfitting. In contrast,
074   bad memorization focuses on remembering previous tasks without considering the risk of overfitting
075   Bartlett et al. (2020); Li et al. (2021); Tirumala et al. (2022); Wei et al. (2024). Current approaches
076   often suffer from bad memorization. Moreover, as current methods report average accuracy across
077   classes, they can hide specific problems, such as significantly poor performance and forgetting of
078   earlier tasks (low stability), and high accuracy on new tasks (high plasticity) (refer to Section 4.2,
079   Figure 3), which also results in poor generalization.
080   Our findings reveal that existing rehearsal-based methods typically focus on sample contribution
081   when updating the buffer. This often leads to class and task imbalances within the buffer (refer to
082   Section 4.2, Table 3), resulting in a long-tail memory distribution that disrupts decision boundaries
083   and causes poor generalization Cui et al. (2019); Samuel & Chechik (2021); Shi et al. (2023). More-
084   over, these methods generally exhibit long running times (Appendix A.2, Table 5) and require high
085   GPU memory (Appendix A.2, Figure 5). These limitations restrict their applicability in resource-
086   constrained environments.
087   To address these issues, we first demonstrate the inadequate OOD generalization capabilities of ex-
088   isting methods, including Lopez-Paz & Ranzato (2017); Chaudhry et al. (2018b; 2019); Aljundi et al.
089   (2019); Prabhu et al. (2020); Chaudhry et al. (2021); Sun et al. (2022; 2023), using the methodology
090   described in Hendrycks & Dietterich (2019) (Figure 1). Building on this analysis and inspired by
091   the proxy-based contrastive learning outlined in Yao et al. (2022) as well as data-centric approaches
092   described in Toneva et al. (2018); Swayamdipta et al. (2020), we introduce Adaptive Contrastive
093
      Replay (ACR). ACR not only outperforms existing methods on both i.i.d. and OOD samples but
      also reduces resource requirements. In the supplementary material, we comprehensively and in
094
      detail discussed the related works (Appendix A.1).
095
096   The main contributions of this paper are: (1) To our knowledge, this is the first work to demonstrate
097   that the performance of rehearsal-based CL methods significantly degrades under distributional shift
098   conditions where the i.i.d. assumption does not hold. (2) We leverage contrastive learning to intro-
099
      duce a dual optimization objective while populating the buffer with misclassified samples (except
      outliers). This approach simultaneously optimizes both the encoder and classifier using contrastive
100
      loss and ensures that the buffer is populated with boundary samples. (3) We maintain a balanced
101
      distribution of classes and tasks within the buffer, ensuring that all categories are adequately repre-
102   sented. (4) Our method achieves a better-balanced trade-off between stability and plasticity com-
103   pared to existing approaches, leading to more robust performance. (5) Our approach is both simple
104   and effective, requiring fewer resources and less running time compared to higher-performing meth-
105   ods, making it more practical for real-world applications.

123   3     P ROPOSED M ETHOD : A DAPTIVE C ONTRASTIVE R EPLAY (ACR)

125   3.1   M OTIVATION AND I NTUITION :
126
      As previously mentioned, rehearsal-based CL methods often face challenges like overfitting Boni-
127
      celli et al. (2022) and class/task representation imbalances, leading to long-tail memory effects Cui
128   et al. (2019). These issues result in less distinct representations, poor OOD generalization, higher
129   forgetting rates, and an imbalanced stability-plasticity trade-off due to blurred decision boundaries.
130   Additionally, their optimization objectives (e.g., GEM Lopez-Paz & Ranzato (2017) to prevent loss
131   increases based on old tasks’ samples) and sample selection strategies (e.g., GSS Aljundi et al.
132   (2019) to ensure gradient space diversity, MetaSP Sun et al. (2022), and SOIF Sun et al. (2023) to
133   use influence functions) increase GPU usage and training times.
134   To address these issues, we propose a novel approach called Adaptive Contrastive Replay (ACR),
135   which adaptively updates the replay buffer while employing a proxy-based contrastive loss. The
136   proxy-based contrastive loss not only aids in optimizing the encoder to achieve distinct represen-
137   tations but also optimizes the classifier simultaneously, resulting in faster operations and lower
138   memory usage compared to traditional contrastive loss methods. Additionally, to identify boundary
139   samples during training, we use the model’s confidence scores, which allows us to select infor-
140   mative samples with minimal computational overhead. When updating the buffer, we ensure that
141   the number of samples per class and task is balanced, thus avoiding the long-tail memory effect.
142
      By combining proxy-based contrastive loss with our adaptive buffer update strategy, ACR achieves
      superior representation learning, reduced memory usage and computation time, improved OOD gen-
143
      eralization, and a better balance between stability and plasticity.

172

173
      tainty in their classification Toneva et al. (2018); Swayamdipta et al. (2020). This fluctuation can be
174   captured through the variance of the confidence score over the first E epochs of training:

181   the classification of the model is unstable.

      Proxy Based Contrastive Loss: Softmax loss builds positive and negative pairs using proxy-to-
183   sample relationships Sun et al. (2023), where proxies represent sub-datasets, making it robust against
184   noise and outliers Yao et al. (2022). The anchor is a sample from the batch. In contrast, supervised
185   contrastive loss forms positive pairs from samples within the same class, focusing on sample-to-
186   sample similarity in the batch Mai et al. (2021). The key difference is that contrastive methods
187   focus on sample-to-sample interactions, while proxy-based methods use proxies for faster, safer
188   convergence but may miss some semantic relationships. Both methods have limitations: contrastive
189   loss poses challenges in stability and training complexity when sample availability is limited, while
190   softmax loss struggles with class imbalance and misclassification of older class samples as newer
191
      ones.
192   To address these challenges, we propose a proxy-based contrastive learning approach that replaces
193   traditional sample-to-sample comparisons with proxy-to-sample relations, effectively reducing the
194   issues related to positive pair alignment. Our method, the proxy-based contrastive loss, links each
195   proxy to all data samples in a batch, thereby substantially expanding the pool of negative samples.
196   In this approach, each proxy acts as an anchor, and we leverage all corresponding proxy-to-sample
197
      interactions.

      3.3    A DAPTIVE R EPLAY B UFFER M ANAGEMENT

247   Selecting high-variance samples near decision boundaries enhances contrastive learning by refining
248   these boundaries, improving OOD generalization, and preventing overfitting. This approach main-
249   tains a balance between stability (retaining past knowledge) and plasticity (adapting to new tasks),
250   reducing catastrophic forgetting. ACR manages these critical samples in its replay buffer, ensuring
251   balanced representation across classes and tasks, preventing learning imbalances, and improving
252   overall model performance.
253   Task- and Class-Aware Buffer Structure: Unlike conventional rehearsal-based methods that ne-
254   glect balanced class/task distribution in the buffer, ACR organizes its buffer B into task-specific and
255   class-specific partitions:
256                                               [T         T [

257                                         B=        Bt =         Bct                                     (6)

260   and Bct indicates the buffer designated for class c within task Dt . For each task at time t, the buffer
261
                                                                                Bsize
      allocates Btsize space, and for each class within task Dt , it allocates t×|C                    t
                                                                                    t | space, where |C | shows

262   the number of class in task Dt . This structure ensures that the replay buffer maintains a balanced
263   distribution of classes and tasks, preventing the model from overfitting to certain dominant classes
264   while neglecting others.
265   In the buffer, tasks are arranged sequentially by order of arrival. Additionally, each task’s classes
266   are stored separately within the buffer. Within each class, samples are ordered by decreasing σ 2 .
267   Consequently, as the buffer index increases, the task number also increases while the σ 2 of the
268   samples for each class decreases. This structure of the buffer eliminates the need to store the task
269   label and the σ 2 of each sample. To manage and maintain the class samples with the highest σ 2 , we
      just store samples and corresponding labels in the buffer like traditional methods.

      Datasets. Our experiments utilized three benchmarks. The first, Split CIFAR-100, consists of 100
296

      The second, Split Mini-ImageNet, divides 100 classes into 5 tasks of 20 classes each, with similarly

303
      This approach involves applying common corruptions such as Gaussian Noise, Shot Noise, Impulse
304   Noise, Defocus Blur, Motion Blur, Zoom Blur, Snow, Fog, Elastic Transform, Pixelate, and JPEG
305   Compression.

307   Metrics. We employ two primary metrics to evaluate the performance of CL methods: Average
308   Accuracy (ACC) and Backward Transfer (BWT), as outlined in Lopez-Paz & Ranzato (2017). The
309   ACC metric assesses overall performance by calculating the average accuracy across all tasks after
310   the model has completed training. Conversely, BWT measures the degree of knowledge loss over
311   time by measuring the average forgetting across all previous tasks once training on all tasks has
312   concluded. These metrics are mathematically defined as follows:
313                                            T                          T −1
                                           1X                         1 X
314                               ACC =          αT,j ,   BWT =                βT,j                         (9)
                                           T j=1                    T − 1 j=1
315
316   Here, αi,j represents the accuracy on the test set held out for task j after the network has been trained
317   on task i. βi,j is calculated as (αi,j − αj,j ), signifying the decrease in model performance on task j
318   due to training on subsequent tasks. T shows the number of tasks. We evaluate these metrics under
319   class incremental conditions, as discussed in previous studies Boschini et al. (2022).

      5   C ONCLUSION
531
      Our work introduces novel contributions to the field of continual learning by addressing the chal-
532
      lenges posed by distributional shifts. We provide the first demonstration that rehearsal-based meth-
533
      ods significantly degrade when the i.i.d. assumption is violated, underscoring the need for ap-
534   proaches that can adapt to real-world scenarios. By incorporating contrastive learning and a dual
535   optimization objective, our method optimizes both the encoder and classifier while ensuring that the
536   buffer is populated with critical boundary samples, excluding outliers. Furthermore, our technique
537   maintains a balanced class and task distribution in the buffer, leading to a more stable and robust
538   learning process. Ultimately, we achieve a practical balance between stability and plasticity, while
539   reducing resource consumption and computational time, making our approach both efficient and
      effective for real-world applications.

670   A.1   R ELATED WORK
671
      Rehearsal-based CL Methods. Rehearsal-based methods prevent catastrophic forgetting by
672
      maintaining a memory buffer of past data and periodically retraining the model with both old and
673
      new information. This approach enhances the model’s ability to retain the knowledge of previous
674   tasks Lopez-Paz & Ranzato (2017); Chaudhry et al. (2018b; 2019); Aljundi et al. (2019); Prabhu
675   et al. (2020); Chaudhry et al. (2021); Sun et al. (2022; 2023).
676
677
      GEM Lopez-Paz & Ranzato (2017) and its lightweight version A-GEM Chaudhry et al. (2018b)
      leverage previous training data to minimize gradient interference explicitly. They populate the buffer
678
      by randomly selecting samples, maintaining an equal number of samples for each task. ER Chaudhry
679
      et al. (2019) presents a straightforward rehearsal method that updates memories through reservoir
680   sampling and employs random sampling during memory retrieval. Despite its simplicity, it remains a
681   strong baseline. GSS Aljundi et al. (2019) approaches updating the memory buffer as a constrained
682   optimization problem, to maximize the diversity of sample gradients within the buffer. GDUMB
683   Prabhu et al. (2020) operates by greedily accumulating new training samples into the buffer, which
684   it subsequently uses to train a model from scratch for testing. HAL Chaudhry et al. (2021) employs
685   the old training samples, which are more susceptible to forgetting, as ”anchors” to stabilize their
686   predictions. This method enhances replay by adding an objective that minimizes the forgetting
687   of crucial learned data points. MetaSP Sun et al. (2022) utilizes the Pareto optimum of example
688
      influence on stability and plasticity, thereby guiding updates to the model and storage management.
      SOIF Sun et al. (2023) leverages second-order influences to make more informed decisions about
689
      which samples to retain in the buffer.
690
691
692   Data-centric. Data-centric approaches emphasize the quality of data over the complexity of mod-
693
      els. Data-centric artificial intelligence involves techniques aimed at enhancing datasets, thereby
      enabling the training of models with fewer data requirements Motamedi et al. (2021); Mazumder
694
      et al. (2022). Ignoring the critical importance of data has led to inaccuracies, biases, and fairness
695
      issues in real-world applications Mazumder et al. (2022). High-quality data can significantly im-
696   prove model generalization, mitigate bias, and enhance safety in data cascades Sambasivan et al.
697   (2021); Aroyo et al. (2022). For instance, Toneva et al. (2018) and Swayamdipta et al. (2020) lever-
698   age model confidence during training to cleanse the dataset. Specifically, Swayamdipta et al. (2020)
699   categorizes the dataset into easy-to-learn, hard-to-learn, and ambiguous subsets, whereas Toneva
700   et al. (2018) distinguishes between forgettable and unforgettable samples. These methods allow for
701   the assessment of each sample’s contribution during training. Building on these approaches, we
      introduce ACR to identify boundary samples and populate the buffer with them.

      Proxy-based Contrastive Learning. A frequently employed approach is softmax cross-entropy
703   loss, where proxies stand in for classes. This approach calculates the similarity between each anchor
704   and the proxies across C classes Chaudhry et al. (2019); Sun et al. (2023). Proxies represent sub-
705   datasets, while the anchor is one of the samples within the training batch Yao et al. (2022); Lin et al.
706   (2023). Supervised contrastive loss, in contrast, builds positive pairs from samples within the same
707   class, evaluating the similarity between each anchor and all N samples in the training batch. This
708   type of loss is usually combined with the nearest class mean classifier Mensink et al. (2013); Mai
709   et al. (2021).
710   Contrastive-based loss primarily explores detailed sample-to-sample relationships, while proxy-
711   based loss relies on proxies to represent subsets of the training data, leading to faster and more
712   stable convergence but potentially overlooking some semantic relationships. To address these lim-
713   itations, Yao et al. (2022) and Lin et al. (2023) introduced proxy-based losses that incorporate
714   key advantages of contrastive learning, improving domain generalization and enabling online class-
715   incremental learning, where each task is trained for only one epoch.
