009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Accurate estimation of heterogeneous treatment effects is critical in domains such
012            as healthcare, economics, and education. While machine learning (ML) has led to
013            significant advances in estimating conditional average treatment effects (CATE),
014            real-world adoption of these methods remains limited due to the complexity of
015            implementing, tuning, and validating them. To this end, we advocate for a more
016            holistic view on the development of ML pipelines for CATE estimation through
017
               automated, end-to-end protocols. We formalize the search for an optimal pipeline
               as a counterfactual Combined Algorithm Selection and Hyperparameter optimiza-
018
               tion (CASH) problem. We introduce AutoCATE, the first automated solution tai-
019
               lored for CATE estimation that addresses this problem based on protocols for eval-
020            uation, estimation, and ensembling. Our experiments show how AutoCATE al-
021            lows for comparing different protocols, with the final configuration outperforming
022            common strategies. We provide AutoCATE as an open-source software package
023            to help practitioners and researchers develop ML pipelines for CATE estimation.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Accurately estimating causal effects is crucial for high-stakes decisions in domains such as health-
029   care, education, and economics. Despite advances in machine learning (ML) for estimating the con-
030
      ditional average treatment effect (CATE), real-world adoption remains limited due to the complexity
      of developing ML pipelines for CATE estimation. Methods often involve numerous hyperparame-
031
      ters, and their performance varies significantly across data sets and applications. Moreover, vali-
032
      dating counterfactual predictions and tuning pipelines is highly challenging, and the performance
033   of different evaluation criteria varies with the data generating process (Curth & van der Schaar,
034   2023). For practitioners unfamiliar with ML, such as clinicians or marketers, these challenges of-
035   ten outweigh potential benefits, hindering the practical use of these techniques. To overcome this,
036   we advocate for automated, end-to-end solutions for learning ML pipelines for CATE estimation.
037
      The challenge of automated CATE estimation. Despite automated ML (AutoML) making signifi-
038
      cant progress (see He et al., 2021), existing solutions do not address the unique challenges of CATE
039   estimation. A key problem is the lack of ground truth CATE: the treatment effect is the difference be-
040   tween the outcomes with and without treatment, but only one of these outcomes is observed for each
041   instance. Additionally, which outcome is observed depends on confounding variables (e.g., older
042   patients may be more likely to receive treatment), leading to covariate shift (Shalit et al., 2017). Fi-
043   nally, CATE estimation pipelines are more complex than those in supervised learning. Metalearners
044   combine multiple baselearners, possibly including both classification and regression models. Risk
045   measures themselves also require predictions and, therefore, tuning of ML pipelines. These unique
046   challenges complicate both the training and validation of ML pipelines and highlight the need for
047
      automated, end-to-end approaches tailored to CATE estimation, which is the focus of this work.
048   Contributions. To tackle these challenges, we propose a practical and comprehensive solution
049   as the automated, end-to-end construction and validation of ML pipelines for CATE estimation:
050   • C OUNTERFACTUAL CASH—We formalize the optimization of CATE estimation pipelines as
051   a counterfactual Combined Algorithm Selection and Hyperparameter optimization (CASH) prob-
052   lem. Our solution, AutoCATE, automates the search for optimal configurations across preproces-
053   sors, metalearners, evaluators, baselearners, and their hyperparameters. The process is organized
      into three stages–evaluation, estimation, and ensembling–each including several design choices.

      2.1     AUTOMATED M ACHINE L EARNING (AUTO ML)
095
096   AutoML focuses on the automatic and efficient construction of high-performing ML pipelines. This
097   entails making a series of design choices regarding preprocessing, feature transformation and selec-
098   tion, ML algorithms, and hyperparameter tuning (Karmaker et al., 2021). As the optimal choices
099   depend on the data and task, AutoML is essentially a search problem. While combinations could
100   be tried randomly, more efficient search methods have been developed, e.g., based on Bayesian
101   optimization (Bergstra et al., 2011; Snoek et al., 2012; Alaa & van der Schaar, 2018). Similarly,
102   meta-learning has been applied to integrate information across other data sets in the search (Feurer
103   et al., 2015). AutoML has made significant progress across data modalities, such as structured data
104
      (Erickson et al., 2020), text (Shi et al., 2021) or images (Bisong & Bisong, 2019). A critical aspect of
      AutoML is its accessibility, often provided through low-code solutions for practitioners unfamiliar
105
      with ML (LeDell & Poirier, 2020; Erickson et al., 2020; Jarrett et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2021).
106
107   Automated solutions exist for a wide range of tasks, including semantic segmentation (Chen et al.,
      2018), machine translation (So et al., 2019), reinforcement learning (Runge et al., 2019), or time

108
      series forecasting (Jarrett et al., 2021). For more comprehensive overviews, see Elsken et al. (2019)
109   and He et al. (2021). However, to the best of our knowledge, AutoML has not yet been applied to
110   CATE estimation. As discussed, estimating treatment effects presents unique challenges, such as the
111   absence of a ground truth, covariate shift due to confounding, and the need for intermediary models
112   in metalearners and risk measures. These complexities render standard AutoML approaches ill-
113   suited for CATE estimation and illustrate the need for approaches specialized to CATE estimation.
114
115    Research gap—No existing AutoML solutions tackle the unique challenges of CATE estimation.

117   2.2   T REATMENT E FFECT E STIMATION AND M ODEL VALIDATION
118
119   Estimation. Various ML methodologies have been proposed for estimating treatment effects. Met-
120   alearners are general strategies for using standard supervised learning algorithms for CATE estima-
121   tion (Künzel et al., 2019). Additionally, various ML algorithms have been adapted for CATE esti-
122
      mation, such as Gaussian processes (Alaa & van der Schaar, 2017), neural networks (Shalit et al.,
      2017; Yoon et al., 2018), decision trees (Rzepakowski & Jaroszewicz, 2012), or random forests
123
      (Wager & Athey, 2018; Oprescu et al., 2019). Notably, other parts of the ML pipeline are also
124
      more complicated when estimating treatment effects, such as missing value imputation (Berrevoets
125   et al., 2023), feature selection (Zhao et al., 2022), and ensemble selection (Mahajan et al., 2023).
126
127   Building an ML pipeline for CATE estimation presents significant challenges, related to the ab-
128
      sence of ground truth CATE and the number of design choices involved. Due to the no free lunch
      theorem, no ML algorithm be optimal in all possible settings. Additionally, there is no globally op-
129
      timal metalearner, as performance similarly depends on the (unknown) data generating process and
130
      sample size (Curth & van der Schaar, 2021). Finally, tuning is more involved: for example, a DR-
131   Learner combines four models (to estimate the propensity, the outcome per treatment group, and the
132   final treatment effect)–each of which can be a different baselearner with separate hyperparameters.
133
134
      Model validation. As the CATE is unobserved, various evaluation criteria have been proposed for
      validating CATE estimators. A common approach is the error in predicting the observed potential
135
      outcome µ, i.e., the µ-risk. However, this criterion has several limitations (Curth & van der Schaar,
136
      2023; Doutreligne & Varoquaux, 2023): it does not account for confounding, may not accurately
137   predict CATE error1 , and is not applicable to estimators that directly predict the CATE. To mitigate
138   the first issue, an inverse propensity weighted variant µIPW -risk, can be considered. Other evaluation
139   criteria address all issues by constructing labels based on plug-in estimates (e.g., S- or T -risk) or
140   metalearner pseudo-outcomes (e.g., R- and DR-risk), see Appendix B.2 for a detailed overview.
141
      There is no consensus on the optimal validation criterion. While Schuler et al. (2018) and Doutre-
142
      ligne & Varoquaux (2023) advocate for the R-risk, Mahajan et al. (2023) favor the T - and DR-risk.
143   Conversely, Curth & van der Schaar (2023) show that the effectiveness of different risk measures
144   varies with various factors, such as the metalearner and data generating process, with no single crite-
145   rion being universally optimal. Additionally, Doutreligne & Varoquaux (2023) stress the flexibility
146   of the estimators used to construct the pseudo-labels, with Mahajan et al. (2023) recommending the
147   use of AutoML. These complexities and design choices highlight the need for automated procedures.
148
149    Research gap—Despite significant recent advances in ML for both CATE estimation and model
150
       validation, critical gaps remain in understanding when to use specific methods, how to effectively
       tune them, and how to address essential but overlooked aspects like preprocessing or ensembling.

194   4     A U T O CATE: E ND -T O -E ND , AUTOMATED CATE E STIMATION
195
196
      AutoCATE finds an optimal ML pipeline in three stages: evaluation, estimation, and ensembling.
197    (1) E VALUATION: In the first stage, we construct a proxy risk for L based on a risk measure (e.g.,
198
       R-risk) and evaluation metric (e.g., MSE). To accurately estimate this risk on the validation data,
       we perform an automated search over preprocessors, ML algorithms, and their hyperparameters.
199
200   (2) E STIMATION: The second stage automatically searches over combinations of preprocessors,
201   metalearners, baselearners, and their hyperparameters to obtain ML pipelines for CATE estimation.
202   (3) E NSEMBLING: The final stage uses the proxy risk from the first stage to select and combine
203   estimation pipelines from the second stage. The result can be a single ML pipeline or an ensemble.
204   A high-level overview of AutoCATE’s functionalities and building blocks is shown in Figure 2.
205
206   4.1        S TAGE 1: E VALUATION —D ESIGNING A P ROXY R ISK AND E VALUATION P ROTOCOL
207
208   The counterfactual CASH problem requires minimizing L(ah |Dtest ), which involves two challenges:
209   the lack of ground truth τ and the presence of covariate shift due to confounding. To tackle these, the
210   evaluation stage measures risk by learning pseudo-labels–i.e., proxies for τ –from validation data.
211   Risk measures. AutoCATE includes different possible risk measures, described in Appendix B.2.
212   We include pseudo-labels used in metalearners (DR-, R-, Z-, U -, and F ), plug-in risks (T and
213   1NN ), and a risk approximation using influence functions (IF ). We exclude the µ- and µIPW -risks
214   as they do not apply to all metalearners, and the S-risk due to poor results in prior work (e.g.,
215   Mahajan et al., 2023). As constructing these risk measures requires accurately estimating nuisance
      parameters, we search over preprocessing and ML algorithms to find good-performing ML pipelines.


                                                            4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      There is no ground truth, and different measures may be preferable depending on the (unknown) data
217   generating process. To make our evaluation more robust, we allow for combining different measures.
218   Similarly, since pseudo-outcomes are learned from data, there is no “true” version, enabling us to
219   construct multiple version of a single risk (e.g., two R-risks). Using multiple risk measures results
220   in a multi-objective search problem. To account for the varying scales of different risks, we nor-
221   malize them by comparing each model’s performance to an average treatment effect (ATE) baseline.
222
      Metrics and implementation. Given a risk measure, different metrics can compare the pseudo-
223   outcomes and CATE predictions to evaluate the quality of the ML pipeline. We include general
224   metrics of predictive accuracy, like the mean squared error (MSE) or mean absolute percentage
225   error (MAPE), and metrics related to a downstream application, such as the Area Under the Qini
226   Curve (AUQC) when ranking effects (Vanderschueren et al., 2024). The R-risk requires a metric that
227   accommodates weights. Finally, we allow for a stratified training-validation split or a stratified k-
228   fold cross-validation procedure. Figure 8 shows more information on these evaluation frameworks.
229
230   4.2   S TAGE 2: E STIMATION —B UILDING A CATE E STIMATION P IPELINE
231
232   Different metalearners can be used to estimate the CATE. Metalearners are general frameworks
233
      for using ML algorithms to estimate treatment effects. As such, they are versatile, accommodate
      various ML algorithms, and can be efficiently trained using existing ML packages. Common ex-
234
      amples include the S-Learner (single model with the treatment as a feature), Lo-Learner (single
235
      model with treatment interaction terms), and T -Learner (separate models for each treatment group).
236   Other metalearners use pseudo-outcomes that converge to the treatment effect, such as the DR-,
237   X-, R-, RA-, Z-, U -, and F -Learners. Appendix B.1 provides more detailed information on each
238   metalearner. Our package uses the CausalML implementations where available (Chen et al., 2020).
239
240   4.3   S TAGE 3: E NSEMBLING —S ELECTING AND E NSEMBLING E STIMATION P IPELINES
241
242   The pipelines from the estimation stage are evaluated with risk measures from the evaluation stage.
243   The final ensembling stage selects the best pipeline(s) for prediction. We describe different possi-
244   ble approaches here, with detailed descriptions provided in Appendix B.5. Almost no established
245   methods exist for ensembling CATE estimators and, due to the lack of ground truth, most standard
246
      ensembling methods are not applicable. AutoCATE can select the best-performing pipeline or the
      top five for improved robustness and accuracy. We also include a novel stacking procedure that as-
247
      signs weights (between zero and one) to each pipeline and optimizes these to minimize the squared
248
      error with respect to the pseudo-outcomes. The weights are regularized, with tuning on a holdout set.
249   Finally, we also include the stacking procedure with softmax weights of Mahajan et al. (2023)–to
250   the best of our knowledge, this is the only existing ensemble method tailored for CATE estimation.
251
252
      With multiple risk measures in a multi-objective search, there may not be a single optimal pipeline,
      but rather a Pareto frontier. One strategy is to select all Pareto optimal points, though pipelines
253
      that perform very well on only a single measure may not work well generally. To select pipelines
254
      with good general performance, we can select the pipeline (or the top five) with the lowest average
255   risk across objectives. Similarly, we can select based on each pipeline’s Euclidean distance to the
256   origin, or its average rank across objectives. Finally, we can apply the abovementioned stacking
257   procedure for each risk measure separately and averaging the weights in a final stacked pipeline.
258
259   4.4   ML P IPELINE B UILDING B LOCKS : P REPROCESSING AND ML BASELEARNERS
260
261   We construct ML pipelines in both the evaluation and estimation stage. The building blocks for these
262   include preprocessors and ML algorithms, all built on top of scikit-learn (Pedregosa et al.,
263   2011). For preprocessing, we provide different feature selection and scaling algorithms. As base-
264   learners, we include different ML algorithms with both classification and regression counterparts,
265   ranging from linear regression to random forests. We provide more information in Appendix B.3.
266   The final search space includes a variety of preprocessors, metalearners, baselearners, and their
267   hyperparameters. Efficient optimization schemes such as Bayesian optimization could be used,
268   but we use random search throughout this work to focus on other design choices in AutoCATE.
269   Nevertheless, we implement our search using optuna (Akiba et al., 2019), allowing easy inte-
      gration of sophisticated optimizers like a Tree-structured Parzen Estimator (Bergstra et al., 2011).

355   How to set the holdout ratio? Risk measures require estimates learned from validation data, creat-
356   ing a trade-off between using data for evaluation or estimation. Figure 4 presents results for different
357   holdout ratios, illustrating this trade-off and showing that a holdout ratio of 30-50% generally works
358   well. We use 30% for holdout in the rest of this work. Although more folds in cross-validation often
359   improve model performance in supervised settings, we do not observe this effect for AutoCATE
360   (see Table 6), likely due to the complex interplay between the number of folds and the holdout ratio.
361   What evaluation metric to use? All previous experiments used the mean squared error (MSE) to
362   compare the predicted CATE and pseudo-outcome(s), corresponding with the goal of minimizing
363   PEHE. However, depending on the downstream application, alternative metrics might be more im-
364   portant. Using these in AutoCATE is straightforward. Table 2 shows results for two such metrics:
365   the mean absolute percentage error (MAPE) and area under the Qini curve (AUQC). As hypothe-
366   sized, selecting models based on a particular metric generally improves performance for that metric.

419
420   The ensemble stage compares pipelines built in the estimation stage using the objective(s) learned
421   in the evaluation stage. Selected pipelines are re-trained on the entire data and saved for inference.
422   Single objective. With a single objective, we can select the best pipeline (Top 1), the best five (Top
423   5), or use stacking to build a final estimator that combines all pipelines. Table 3a compares these
424   strategies, showing that combining pipelines improves performance for all data sets except Twins.
425   Appendix D.3 illustrates how an ensemble’s predictions can help assess an estimate’s uncertainty.
426   Multiple objectives. Model selection is more complex with multiple objectives. We can select the
427   best pipelines based on the average normalized score, Euclidean distance to the origin, or average
428   rank, to then select the top one or top five pipelines. Alternatively, we can create stacking esti-
429   mators for each objective and average the weights (“Stacking”), or select all Pareto optimal models
430   (“Pareto”). Table 3b compares these strategies. Single pipelines typically underperform compared to
431   ensembles built from the top five pipelines, all Pareto optimal pipelines, or stacking. Selecting based
      on average performance yields the best performance. No single strategy is consistently optimal.

518   6          C ONCLUSION
519
520
      Despite the availability of ML methods for CATE estimation, their adoption remains limited, due to
521
      the complexity of implementing, tuning, and validating them. We framed the problem of finding an
522   ML pipeline for CATE estimation as a counterfactual CASH problem and proposed AutoCATE: the
523   first end-to-end, automated solution tailored for treatment effect estimation. Based on this solution,
524   we analyzed design choices for evaluation, estimation, and ensembling, and identified best practices.
525   The resulting approach was validated, outperforming widely used strategies for CATE estimation.
526
      To maximize AutoCATE’s practical impact, several limitations need to be addressed. Although
527
      AutoCATE relies on standard assumptions for causal inference, it is crucial to assess its robustness
528   against violations of these assumptions and potentially protocols for such scenarios. Additionally,
529   most of the data used in this work is semi-synthetic (IHDP, ACIC, and News), which may not fully
530   capture the complexities of real-world data. Although validating CATE estimates remains inherently
531   challenging, approaches from related fields could offer inspiration (see e.g. Devriendt et al., 2020).
532
      AutoCATE enables a comprehensive analysis of existing methods (see Figure 1 and Appendix D.5),
533
      facilitating a better understanding of CATE estimation and guiding the development of new ap-
534   proaches. We envision opportunities for future research in all stages. For evaluation, advanced
535   multi-objective strategies could improve performance and robustness. Novel methods for estima-
536   tion could be automatically discovered using Neural Architecture Search. Generally, efficiency can
537   be improved with better search algorithms or strategies (e.g., by re-using nuisance models across
538   metalearners). Related to this, the optimal time allocation between the stages remains an open ques-
539   tion, where meta-learning could help by incorporating data set characteristics (Feurer et al., 2015).
      Finally, more advanced ensembling could be developed (e.g., combining different metalearners).

762   A     BACKGROUND ON CATE E STIMATION
763
764   This section provides a more detailed introduction and background on treatment effect estimation.
765   In accordance to the main body, we denote an instance by a tuple (x, t, y), with covariates X ∈ X ⊂
766
      Rd , a treatment T ∈ T = {0, 1}, and an outcome Y ∈ Y ⊂ R. Following the potential outcomes
      framework (Rubin, 1974; 2005), we describe an instance’s potential outcome Y for a given treatment
767
      T = t as Y (t). The Conditional Average Treatment Effect (CATE) is then defined as the expected
768
      difference in outcomes between treating and not treating:
769
                                              h                 i
770                                          E Y (1) − Y (0)|X .                                       (2)
771
772   Knowing this effect is crucial in a variety of domains, such as education (Olaya et al., 2020), health-
773   care (Feuerriegel et al., 2024), and maintenance (Vanderschueren et al., 2023). Estimating the CATE
774   from observational data involves significant challenges (Appendix A.1), requires standard assump-
775   tions (Appendix A.2), and tailored ML methods (Appendix A.3). We explain these in the following.
776
777   A.1   C HALLENGES : T HE F UNDAMENTAL P ROBLEM AND C ONFOUNDING
778
779   The fundamental problem of causal inference (Holland, 1988) is that, for each instance, we only
780   observe either Y (0) or Y (1), depending on what treatment was administered. We refer to the ob-
781   served outcome as the factual outcome and the unobserved outcome as the counterfactual outcome.
782   Because one outcome is always unobserved, we never know the true CATE τ , which means that
783
      there is no ground truth CATE available for training or validation.
784   In observational data, the outcome that was observed is typically not random: some instances were
785   more likely to be treated, while other instances were more likely not to receive treatment. For
786   example, in healthcare, patients may be more likely to receive a new treatment if they have access
787   to better healthcare, have no pre-existing conditions, and are younger. The covariates that influence
788   both the outcome and treatment assignment are called confounders, with the resulting non-random
789
      treatment assignment sometimes referred to as confounding.
790   Confounding presents an additional challenge for CATE estimation and validation as it results in co-
791   variate shift. Some instance-treatment pairs (the counterfactuals) will be absent in the observational
792   training data compared to the hypothetical test data that contains all instance-treatment pairs (both
793   factuals and counterfactuals). Because of this, an ML model may focus too much on the observed
794   data points at the cost of worse predictions for the counterfactuals and, as such, the test data overall.
795
796   A.2   A SSUMPTIONS F OR I DENTIFIABILITY
797
798   Identifying the causal effect from observational data requires making standard assumptions: consis-
799
      tency, overlap, and unconfoundedness. This section explains these assumptions in more detail.
800
      Assumption 1 (Consistency) The observed outcome given a treatment is the potential outcome
801   under that treatment: Y |X, t = Y (t)|X.
802
803   Assumption 2 (Overlap) For each instance, there is a non-zero probability of receiving each treat-
804   ment given their covariates: ∀ x ∈ X and t ∈ T : P (T = t|X = x) > 0. This condition ensures
805   that there is sufficient variability in the treatment assignment.
806
807   Assumption 3 (Unconfoundedness) Given an instance’s covariates, its potential outcomes are in-
808   dependent of the treatment assignment: Y (0), Y (1) ⊥
                                                          ⊥ T | X. This condition implies that all factors
809   influencing both the treatment assignment and outcome are included in X. In other words, there are
      no unobserved confounders.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
      There has recently been much interest in CATE estimation under violation of these assumptions. For
811   example, by quantifying the uncertainty or sensitivity of an estimate to a possible violation (Franks
812   et al., 2020; Jesson et al., 2020; 2021), characterizing overlap violations (Oberst et al., 2020), or
813   developing metalearners that can deal with unobserved confounders (Oprescu et al., 2023). We
814   believe that extending AutoCATE to deal with these settings and to incorporate these methods will
815   improve its potential for real-world applicability even further. As such, we consider it an important
816   direction for future versions.

818   A.3    CATE E STIMATION : M ETA - AND BASELEARNERS
819
820   We briefly describe the approach of estimating the CATE with a metalearner here. A straightforward
821
      way of estimating the CATE is using a single ML model, where the treatment variable is considered
      an ordinary input variable. This metalearner is called the S-Learner and can be implemented with
822
      a wide variety of baselearners (i.e., ML algorithms that predict an outcome based on data, such as
823
      a decision tree or neural network). An alternative metalearner, the T -learner, fits two models–one
824   model for each treatment group. Both models can use the same baselearner or a different one. More
825   information on the metalearners in AutoCATE is provided in Appendix B.1. For more extensive
826   overviews, we refer to Devriendt et al. (2018), Zhang et al. (2021), and Feuerriegel et al. (2024).

1117
1118    IHDP (Hill, 2011). The data come from the Infant Health and Development Program, describing
1119    the impact of child care and home visits on children’s cognitive development. Treatments and out-
1120    comes were simulated for a total of 100 data sets. Each version contains n = 747 instances and
1121    d = 25 covariates.
1122
1123
        ACIC (Dorie et al., 2019). The data from the ACIC 2016 competition was based on data from
        the Collaborative Perinatal Project, studying drivers of developmental disorders in pregnant women
1124
        and their children. 77 distinct data sets were created, each with n = 4,802 instances and d = 58
1125
        covariates. 100 iterations were originally created for each data set, but we use only the first one for
1126    each.
1127
1128    Twins (Louizos et al., 2017). The Twins data studies the effect of being the heavier twin on mor-
1129    taility. n = 11,984 pairs of twins are included, with d = 46 features each. Only one version of this
1130    data set exists, so we run 10 iterations of each experiment.
1131
1132    News (Johansson et al., 2016). This data simulates a reader’s reading experience (y) based on the
1133    device they use for reading (t) and the news article (x). There are 50 distinct data sets, each with
        n = 5,000 instances with and d = 3,477 covariates.
