                                                                                               A BSTRACT
031
032                                   The quality of 3D generative modeling has been notably improved by the adop-
033                                   tion of 2D diffusion models. Despite this progress, the cumbersome optimization
034
                                      process per se presents a critical problem to efficiency. In this paper, we introduce
                                      Hash3D, a universal acceleration for 3D score distillation sampling (SDS) with-
035
                                      out model training. Central to Hash3D is the observation that images rendered
036
                                      from similar camera positions and diffusion time-steps often have redundant fea-
037                                   ture maps. By hashing and reusing these feature maps across nearby timesteps
038                                   and camera angles, Hash3D eliminates unnecessary calculations. We implement
039                                   this through an adaptive grid-based hashing. As a result, it largely speeds up
040                                   the process of 3D generation. Surprisingly, this feature-sharing mechanism not
041                                   only makes generation faster but also improves the smoothness and view consis-
042                                   tency of the synthesized 3D objects. Our experiments covering 5 text-to-3D and 3
043                                   image-to-3D models, demonstrate Hash3D’s versatility to speed up optimization,
044                                   enhancing efficiency by 1.5 ∼ 4×. Additionally, Hash3D’s integration with 3D
045
                                      Gaussian splatting largely speeds up 3D model creation, reducing text-to-3D pro-
                                      cessing to about 10 minutes and image-to-3D conversion to roughly 30 seconds.

049    1                       I NTRODUCTION
050
051    In the evolving landscape of 3D generative modeling, the integration of 2D diffusion models Poole
052    et al. (2023); Wang et al. (2023) has led to notable advancements. These methods leverage off-the-
053    the-shelf image diffusion models to distill 3D models by predicting 2D score functions at different
       views, known as score distillation sampling (SDS).


                                                                                                           1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      While this approach has opened up new opportunities for creating realistic 3D assets, it also brings
055   significant efficiency challenges. Particularly, SDS requires thousands of score predictions from
056   different camera angles and denoising steps in the diffusion model. This results in long optimization
057   times, sometimes taking hours to create a single object Wang et al. (2024). These long durations
058   make them difficult to use in practical applications. We need new solutions to improve its efficiency.
059
      To mitigate this bottleneck, current efforts concentrate on three strategies. The first strategy trains
060
      inference-only models Li et al. (2023a); Chen et al. (2023b); Jun & Nichol (2023b); Xu et al. (2024);
061   Liu et al. (2024a) to bypass the lengthy optimization process. While effective, this method requires
062   extensive training time and substantial computational resources. The second approach Tang et al.
063   (2023); Yi et al. (2023); Ren et al. (2023) seeks to reduce optimization times through faster 3D rep-
064   resentations. However, each type of representation needs a unique design for 3D generation, which
065   creates its own challenges. The third approach attempts to directly generate sparse views to model
066   3D objects Kong et al. (2024); Liu et al. (2024b) This method assumes near-perfect consistency for
067   generated views, which, in practice, is often not achievable.
068   Returning to the core issue within SDS, the major computation is consumed in the repeated sampling
069   of the 2D image score function Song & Ermon (2019). Motivated by methods that accelerate 2D
070   diffusion sampling Song et al. (2021); Bao et al. (2022); Lu et al. (2022), we posed the question: Is
071   it possible to reduce the number of inference steps of the diffusion model for 3D generation?
072
      In exploring this question, we make a crucial observation: denoising outputs and feature maps from
073   near camera positions and timesteps are very similar. This discovery led us to develop Hash3D,
074   which reduces the computation by leveraging this redundancy.
075
076
      At its core, Hash3D stores and hashes previously computed features to reduce time. We do this using
      a a grid-based hash table. Specifically, when a new view is close to one that has already been pro-
077
      cessed, Hash3D retrieves and reuses the nearby features from the table. This reuse allows Hash3D
078
      to compute the current view’s score function without repeating earlier calculations.Additionally, we
079   developed a method to dynamically adjust the grid size for each view, which makes the system more
080   adaptable. As a result, Hash3D saves computational resources without requiring any model training
081   or complex changes, making it easy to implement and efficient to use.
082
      Beyond improving efficiency, Hash3D improves the view consistency of generated objects. Tradi-
083
      tional diffusion-based methods often result in 3D objects with disjointed appearances when viewed
084
      from various angles Armandpour et al. (2023). In contrast, Hash3D links independently generated
085   views by sharing features within each grid. It leads to smoother, more consistent 3D models.
086
087   Another key advantage of Hash3D is on its versatility. It integrates seamlessly into a diverse array
088
      of diffusion-based 3D generative workflows. Our experiments, covering 5 text-to-3D and 3 image-
      to-3D models, demonstrate Hash3D’s versatility to speed up optimization, enhancing efficiency by
089
      1.3 ∼ 4×, without compromising on performance. Specifically, the integration of Hash3D with 3D
090
      Gaussian Splatting Kerbl et al. (2023) brings a significant leap forward, cutting down the time for
091   text-to-3D to about 10 minutes and image-to-3D to roughly 30 seconds.
092
093   The contribution of this paper can be summarized into
094
             • We introduce the Hash3D, a versatile, plug-and-play and training-free acceleration method
095            for diffusion-based text-to-3D and image-to-3D models.
096
             • The paper emphasizes the redundancy in diffusion models when processing nearby views
097
               and timesteps. This finding motivates the development of Hash3D, aiming to boost effi-
098            ciency without compromising quality.
099
             • Hash3D employs an adaptive grid-based hashing to efficiently retrieve features, signifi-
100
               cantly reducing the computations across view and time.
101
102
             • Our extensive testing demonstrates that Hash3D not only speeds up the generative process
               by 1.5 ∼ 4×, but also results in a slight improvement in performance.

138   2.2          S CORE D ISTILLATION S AMPLING (SDS)
139
140   The Score Distillation Sampling (SDS) Poole et al. (2023) represents an optimization-based 3D
141   generation method. This method focuses on optimizing the 3D representation, denoted as Θ, using
142
      a pre-trained 2D diffusion models with its noise prediction network, denoted as \protect \bm  {\epsilon }_{\text {pretrain}}(x_t, t, y).
143   Given a camera pose \protect \bm  {c}=(\theta ,\phi ,\rho )\in \mathbb {R}^3 defined by elevation ϕ, azimuth θ and camera distances ρ,
144   and the its corresponding prompt y c , a differentiable rendering function g(\cdot ;\Theta ), SDS aims to refine
145   the parameter \Theta , such that each rendered image \protect \bm  {x}_0 = g(\bm c; \theta ) is perceived as realistic by ϵpretrain .
146   The optimization objective is formulated as follows:
147
148                                                 \min _{\Theta } \mathcal {L}_{\text {SDS}} = \mathbb {E}_{t,\bm c} \left [ \frac {\sigma _t}{\alpha _t} \omega (t) \text {KL}\left (q^\Theta (\bm {x}_t|y_c, t) \, \Vert \, p(\bm {x}_t|y_c; t)\right ) \right ] \label {eq:sds}                                      (4)
149
150
      By excluding the Jacobian term of the U-Net, the gradient of the optimization problem can be
151   effectively approximated:
152
153
                                                                \nabla _\Theta \mathcal {L}_{\text {SDS}} \approx \mathbb {E}_{t,\bm c,\bm {\epsilon }}\left [ \omega (t) (\bm {\epsilon }_{\text {pretrain}}(\bm {x}_t, t, y^c) - \bm {\epsilon })\frac {\partial \bm {x}}{\partial \Theta } \right ] \label {eq:sds}    (5)
154
155
156   To optimize Eq. 5, we randomly sample different time-step t, camera \protect \bm  {c}, and random noise \protect \bm  {\epsilon }, and
157   compute gradient of the 3D representation, and update \theta  accordingly. This approach ensures that
158
      the rendered image from 3D object aligns with the distribution learned by the diffusion model.
159   Efficiency Problem. The main challenge lies in the need for thousands to tens of thousands of
160   iterations to optimize Eq 5, each requiring a separate diffusion model inference. This process is
161   time-consuming due to the model’s complexity. We make it faster by using a hash function to reuse
      features from similar inputs, cutting down on the number of calculations needed.

      Based on the finding, we present our strategy that employs a grid-based hashing to reuse feature

      3.1      P ROBING THE R EDUNDANCY IN SDS
187
      Typically, SDS randomly samples camera poses and timesteps to ensure that the rendered views
188   align with the diffusion model’s distribution. However, during this repeated sampling, we observe
189   that deep feature extraction at proximate c and t often reveals a high degree of similarity. Therefore,
190   this similarity underpins our method, suggesting that reusing features from nearby points does not
191   significantly impact the model’s predictions.
192
      Measuring the Similarity. Intuitively, images captured from similar camera positions and at similar
193
      times result in similar visual content. We hypothesize that features produced by diffusion models
194   exhibit a similar pattern. Specifically, we propose two hypotheses: (1) temporal similarity: fea-
195   tures extracted at close timesteps are similar, and (2) spatial similarity: features extracted at close
196   estimated camera poses are similar.
197
      Regarding the temporal similarity, previous studies Ma et al. (2023); Li et al. (2023b) have noted
198
      that features extracted from adjacent timesteps in diffusion models show a high level of similarity.
199
200   To test the hypothesis about spatial similarity, we conducted a preliminary study using the diffusion
201   model to generate novel views of the same object from different camera positions. Specifically, we
202   used Zero-123 Liu et al. (2023c), which generates images from different camera poses conditioned
203
      on a single input image. For each specific camera angle and timestep, we extracted the features
       (U )
204   vl−1 from the input of the last up-sampling layer. By adjusting elevation angles (ϕ) and azimuth
205   angles (θ), we were able to measure the cosine similarity of these features between different views,
206
      averaging the results across all timesteps.
207   The findings, presented in Figure 2, reveal a large similarity score in features from views within a
208   [−10◦ , 10◦ ] range, with the value higher than 0.8. This phenomenon was not unique to Zero-123;
209   we observed similar patterns in text-to-image diffusion models like Stable Diffusion Rombach et al.
210   (2022). These findings underscore the redundancy in predicted outputs within the SDS process.
211   Synthesising Novel View for Free. To leverage redundancy in SDS, we conducted an experiment
212   to create new views by reusing and interpolating scores from precomputed nearby cameras. Specif-
213   ically, we generated two images using Zero-123 at angles (θ, ϕ) = (10◦ ± δ, 90◦ ) and saved all
214   denoising predictions. By averaging these predictions, we synthesized a third view at (10◦ , 90◦ )
215   without additional computation. We experimented with varying δ ∈ {1◦ , 5◦ , 10◦ , 20◦ }, and com-
      pared them with the full denoising predictions.

      3.2      H ASHING - BASED F EATURE R EUSE
234
235   Based on our analysis, we developed Hash3D, which uses hashing techniques to optimize SDS.
236   Hash3D reduces the repetitive computational cost in diffusion models by trading storage space for
237   faster 3D optimization.
238
      At its core, Hash3D employs a hash table to store and retrieve previously computed features. When
239
      Hash3D samples a specific camera pose c and timestep t, it first checks the hash table for similar
240   features. If a match is found, it’s reused directly in the diffusion model, significantly cutting down
241   on computation. If not, it performs standard inference and adds the new features to the hash table
242   for future use.
243
      Grid-based Hashing. To efficiently index the hash table, we use a grid-based hashing function
244
      based on camera poses \protect \bm  {c} = (\theta ,\phi ,\rho ) and timestep t. This function assigns each camera and timestep
245
      to a grid cell for data organization and retrieval.
246
247   Firstly, we define the size of our grid cells in both the spatial and temporal domains, denoted as
      \Delta \theta , \Delta \phi , \Delta \rho  and \Delta t respectively. For each input key [\theta ,\phi ,\rho , t], we calculate the grid cell indices:
248
249
250                                             i = \left \lfloor \frac {\theta }{\Delta \theta } \right \rfloor , j = \left \lfloor \frac {\phi }{\Delta \phi } \right \rfloor , k = \left \lfloor \frac {\rho }{\Delta \rho } \right \rfloor , l = \left \lfloor \frac {t}{\Delta t} \right \rfloor                                (6)
251
252    These indices are combined into a single hash code: \protect \texttt  {idx} = (i + N_1 \cdot j + N_2 \cdot k + N_3 \cdot l)\mod n  is
253   used, where N_1, N_2, N_3 are large prime numbers Teschner et al. (2003); Nießner et al. (2013), and
254   n denotes the size of the hash table. This hash function maps keys with similar camera poses and
255   timesteps to the same bucket. This grid-based approach not only speeds up data retrieval but also
256   preserves the spatial-temporal relationships in the data, which is crucial for our method.
257
      Collision Resolution. When multiple keys are assigned to the same hash value, a collision occurs.
258   We address these collisions using separate chaining. In this context, each hash value \protect \texttt  {idx} is linked
259   to a distinct queue, denoted as q_{\texttt {idx}}  . To ensure the queue reflects the most recent data and remains
260   manageable in size, it is limited to a maximum length Q = 3. When this limit is reached, the
261   oldest elements is removed to accommodate the new entry, ensuring the queue stays relevant to the
262   evolving 3D representation.
263
      Feature Retrieval and Update. After computing the hash value idx, we either retrieve features
264   from the hash table or update it with new ones. We control this with hash probability 0 < η < 1.
265   With probability η, we retrieve features; otherwise, we perform an update.
266
                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                 (U )
267   For feature updates, following prior work Ma et al. (2023), we extract the feature vl−1 , which is the
268   input of the last up-sampling layer in the U-net. Once extracted, we compute the hash code \protect \texttt  {idx}
269
      and append the data to the corresponding queue qidx . The stored data includes noisy latent input x,
                                                                   (U )
      camera pose c, timestep t, and extracted diffusion features vl−1 .

      3.3     A DAPTIVE G RID H ASHING
294
295   In grid-based hashing, the selection of an appropriate grid size \Delta \theta , \Delta \phi , \Delta \rho , \Delta t — plays a pivotal
296   role. As illustrated in Section 3.1, we see three insights related to grid size. First, feature similarity
297   is only maintained at a median grid size; overly large grids tend to produce artifacts in generated
298   views. Second, it is suggested that ideal grid size differs across various objects. Third, even for a
299   single object, optimal grid sizes vary for different views and time steps, indicating the necessity for
300   adaptive grid sizing to ensure optimal hashing performance.
301   Learning to Adjust the Grid Size. To address these challenges, we propose to dynamically adjust-
302   ing grid sizes. The objective is to maximize the average cosine similarity cos(·, ·) among features
303   within each grid. In other words, only if the feature is similar enough, we can reuse it. Such problem
304   is formulated as
305
306
                                        \max _{\Delta \theta , \Delta \phi , \Delta \rho , \Delta t} \frac {1}{|q_{\texttt {idx}}| } \sum _{i,j}^{|q_{\texttt {idx}}|} \text {cos}(\mathbf {v}_j,\mathbf {v}_i), \quad s.t. |q_{\texttt {idx}}| > 0 \quad [\text {Non-empty}]    (8)
307
308   Given our hashing function is non-differentiale, we employ a brute-force approach. Namely, we
309   evaluate M predetermined potential grid sizes, each corresponding to a distinct hash table, and only
310   use best one.
311
      For each input [\theta ,\phi ,\rho , t], we calculate the hash code {idx(m) }M
                                                                                   m=1 for M times, and indexing in
312   each bucket. Feature vectors are updated accordingly, with new elements being appended to their
313   respective bucket. We calculate the cosine similarity between the new and existing elements in the
314   bucket, maintaining a running average sidx(n) of these similarities
315
316                                s_{\texttt {idx}^{(m)}} \leftarrow \gamma s_{\texttt {idx}^{(m)}} + (1-\gamma ) \frac {1}{|q_{\texttt {idx}^{(m)}}|} \sum _{i=1}^{|q_{\texttt {idx}^{(m)}}|} \text {cos}(\mathbf {v}_{new},\mathbf {v}_i)                                     (9)
317
318
319   During retrieval, we hash across all M grid sizes but only consider the grid with the highest average
320
      similarity for feature extraction.
321   Computational and Memory Efficiency. Despite employing a brute-force approach that involves
322   hashing M times for each input, our method maintains computational efficiency due to the low cost
323   of hashing. It also maintains memory efficiency, as hash tables store only references to data. To
      prioritize speed, we deliberately avoid using neural networks for hashing function learning.

505      Ablation 2: Hashing Features vs. Hashing Noise. In Hash3D, we hash intermediate features
506      within the diffusion U-Net. Alternatively, we developed Hash3D with noise (Hash3D w/n), which
507      hashes and reuses the denoising prediction directly. We tested both methods on the image-to-3D task
508      using Zero123, with results shown in Table 10. Interestingly, while Hash3D w/n reduced processing
509      time, it significantly lowered CLIP scores. This highlights that hashing features is more effective
510      than hashing noise predictions.
511      Ablation 3: Influence of Hash Probability η. A key parameter in Hash3D is the feature retrieval
512      probability η. We tested η ∈ {0.01, 0.05, 0.1, 0.3, 0.5, 0.7} using Dreamfusion. As shown in Fig-
513      ure 9, runtime decreases as η increases. Generated objects are visualized in Figure 8. For η < 0.3,
514      Hash3D also improved the visual quality of 3D models by enabling smoother noise predictions
515      through feature sharing. However, for η > 0.3, the runtime gains were minimal. This balance of
516      performance and efficiency led us to choose η = 0.1 for our main experiments.
517      Ablation 4: Adaptive Grid Size. We introduce AdaptGrid, which dynamically adjusts the grid
518      size for hashing based on each sample. Compared to using a constant grid size in Dreamfusion,
519      AdaptGrid performs better as shown in Table 4. Larger grid sizes reduce the visual quality of
520      3D objects, while smaller grid sizes maintain quality but increase computation time because fewer
521      features match. AdaptGrid effectively balances visual quality and efficiency by optimizing the grid
522      size for each sample.

         In this paper, we present Hash3D, a training-free technique that improves the efficiency of diffusion-
531
         based 3D generative modeling. Hash3D utilizes adaptive grid-based hashing to efficiently retrieve
532      and reuse features from adjacent camera poses, to minimize redundant computations. As a result,
533      Hash3D not only speeds up 3D model generation by 1.5 ∼ 4× without the need for additional
534      training, but it also improves the smoothness and consistency of the generated 3D models.

      In fact, Hash3D utilizes a hierarchical process for feature reuse, involving a key-based hashing stage
      and a content-based feature aggregation stage. In the first stage of key-based hashing, Hash3D
793
      computes a hash code corresponding to a bucket according to the camera pose and time step. This
794
      efficiently retrieves a set of candidate features. Subsequently, Hash3D performs a content-based
795   refinement within the retrieved bucket. Features are aggregated based on the similarity (distance)
796   between their input latents.
797

                   against the entire pre-extracted feature pool (no hashing at all). To achieve this, we es-
                   tablished a queue with maximum length of 1000 to store all previously extracted features.

806              • Ablation 2: Removing Content-based Aggregation. Here, we omitted the content-based

1146
1147      3D Generation Model. The development of 3D generative models has become a focal point in
1148      the computer vision. Typically, these models are trained to produce the parameters that define 3D
1149      representations. This approach has been successfully applied across several larger-scale models
1150      using extensive and diverse datasets for generating voxel representation Wu et al. (2016), point
1151      cloud Achlioptas et al. (2018); Nichol et al. (2022), implicit function Jun & Nichol (2023a), tri-
1152      plane Shue et al. (2023); Xu et al. (2024). Despite these advances, scalability continues to be
1153      a formidable challenge, primarily due to data volume and computational resource constraints. A
1154      promising solution to this issue lies in leveraging 2D generative models to enhance and optimize
1155
          3D representations. Recently, diffusion-based models, particularly those involving score distillation
          into 3D representations Poole et al. (2023), represent significant progress. However, these methods
1156
          are often constrained by lengthy optimization processes.
1157
1158      Efficient Diffusion Model. Diffusion models, known for their iterative denoising process for image
1159      generation, are pivotal yet time-intensive. There has been a substantial body of work aimed at ac-
1160      celerating these models. This acceleration can be approached from two angles: firstly, by reducing
1161
          the sampling steps through advanced sampling mechanisms Song et al. (2021); Bao et al. (2022);
          Liu et al. (2022); Lu et al. (2022) or timestep distillation Salimans & Ho (2022); Song et al. (2023),
1162
          which decreases the number of required sampling steps. The second approach focuses on mini-
1163
          mizing the computational demands of each model inference. This can be achieved by developing
1164      smaller diffusion models Kim et al. (2023); Yang et al. (2023); Fang et al. (2023) or reusing fea-
1165      tures from adjacent steps Ma et al. (2023); Li et al. (2023b), thereby enhancing efficiency without
1166      compromising effectiveness. However, the application of these techniques to 3D generative tasks
1167      remains largely unexplored.
1168
          Hashing Techniques. Hashing, pivotal in computational and storage efficiency, involves converting
1169
          variable-sized inputs into fixed-size hash code via hash functions. These code index a hash table,
1170      enabling fast and consistent data access. Widely used in file systems, hashing has proven effective
1171      in a variety of applications, like 3D representation Nießner et al. (2013); Müller et al. (2022); Girish
1172      et al. (2023); Xie et al. (2023), neural network compression Chen et al. (2015); Kitaev et al. (2020),
1173      using hashing as a components in deep network Roller et al. (2021) and neural network-based hash
1174      function development Lai et al. (2015); Zhu et al. (2016); Cao et al. (2017); Li et al. (2017). Our
1175      study explores the application of hashing to retrieve features from 3D generation. By adopting this
1176      technique, we aim to reduce computational overhead for repeated diffusion sampling and speed up
1177      the creation of realistic 3D objects.
