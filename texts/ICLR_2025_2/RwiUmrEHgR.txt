009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Class imbalance in the data introduces significant challenges in training machine
012            learning models especially with long-tailed datasets, i.e., where a small number of
013            classes comprise a large number of sample data points in the training data. Specif-
014            ically, it leads to biased models that overfit with respect to the dominant classes
015            while underfitting on the minority classes. This, in turn, results in seemingly satis-
016            factory yet biased overall results. Hence, the above biasing needs to be controlled
017            such that the desired generalizability of the model is not compromised. To that
018
               end, we introduce a novel Cost-Sensitive Loss (CSL) function designed to dynam-
               ically adjust class weights, and incorporate a reinforcement learning mechanism
019
               to optimize these adjustments. The proposed CSL function can be seamlessly in-
020
               tegrated with existing loss functions, to enhance their performance on imbalanced
021            datasets, rendering them robust and scalable. We implemented the above CSL
022            function in form of a framework which leverages reinforcement learning to opti-
023            mally apply these adjustments over consecutive training epochs. Experimental re-
024            sults on benchmark datasets demonstrate that our proposed approach significantly
025            outperforms state-of-the-art methods. The results indicate that our approach can
026            provide an optimal trade-off between model accuracy and generalization while
027            training models on imbalanced data.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   In the ever advancing field of Machine Learning, availability of balanced datasets with equal repre-
033
      sentation of all classes is a requirement for creating a reliable model usable in real-life cases. While
      this scenario looks quite natural, the reality couldn’t be more different. During data collection in
034
      real-life scenarios, it is almost impossible to curate a perfect dataset. There occurs a motley of
035
      imbalances Yang et al. (2022) in the representations of classes in the dataset. Class imbalance in
036   the data introduces significant challenges in training machine learning models especially with long-
037   tailed datasets Yang et al. (2022). Specifically, it leads to biased models that overfit with respect to
038   the dominant classes while underfitting on the minority classes. This, in turn, results in seemingly
039   satisfactory yet biased overall results. Hence, the above biasing needs to be controlled such that the
040   desired generalizability of the model is not compromised.
041
      Long-tailed datasets are characterized by a few dominant (majority) classes compared to a over-
042   whelmingly vast number of under-represented classes (minority), leading to models that exhibit
043   skewed performance. Models trained on such datasets excel in predicting the majority classes
044   while often failing to adequately generalize to minority classes. This issue is not merely academic
045   but holds profound implications for practical applications where accurate and unbiased predictions
046   across all classes are essential, such as medical diagnostics, fraud detection, and anomaly detection
047   in industrial processes.
048   Several research efforts have addressed this issue Zhang et al. (2023). Approaches for dealing with
049   long-tail classification can be broadly categorized into information augmentation techniques Kim
050   et al. (2020); Park et al. (2022); Wang et al. (2021a); Han et al. (2005); Zhong et al. (2021a), cost-
051   sensitive learning (CSL) Lin et al. (2017); Cui et al. (2019); Ren et al. (2020); Cao et al. (2019);
052   Park et al. (2021); Legate et al. (2023) and model improvement methods Dong et al. (2017); Huang
053   et al. (2016); Liu et al. (2019a); Zhu & Yang (2020); Ouyang et al. (2016); Zhong et al. (2021b);
      Zhou et al. (2020); Wang et al. (2020); Zhang et al. (2022). Data-level techniques (also known as


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      information augmentation (IA) techniques) rely on changing the underlying data distribution by ei-
055   ther oversampling the minority classes or undersampling the majority classes to reduce the bias and
056   improve generalization. Yet these approaches introduce either new types of biases or substantially
057   increase computational costs. Therefore they can hardly be applied to large, real-world datasets.
058   Model improvement (MI) approaches, such as meta-learning, focus more towards generalization of
059   models from imbalanced data, which often necessitates huge computational resources and a sub-
060   stantial amount of labeled data, a requirement that, in most real-world situations, is quite infeasible.
061   Amidst these approaches, Cost-Sensitive Learning (CSL), which is also known to as Class-Sensitive
062   Learning in some literature, emerges as a promising paradigm. CSL alters the standard loss function
063   to penalize prediction errors differently depending on class distribution. This would allow the model
064   to enhance its generalization and prediction performance without actually changing the underlying
065   data. CSL is very attractive because it attacks the problem at the model level where, indeed, it can
066   nudge learning representations differently. CSLs still depend on static weight schemes, and thus
067   are not adaptive to the dynamic nature of real-world datasets. During training as the complexity of
068   learning for each class changes, the static weights would not catch such nuances, making them less
069
      efficient.
070   To that end, we introduce a novel CSL function designed to adapt dynamically to the distribution
071   of samples within each class. The above function incorporates a mechanism to adjust class weights
072   based on the effective proportion of classes in given samples. Our framework leverages reinforce-
073   ment learning to optimally apply these adjustments over consecutive training epochs. Through a
074   deeper understanding of the insights regarding the interplay between class representation and model
075
      prediction accuracy derived from empirical results, our framework aims to enhance both the ro-
      bustness and generalization capabilities of machine learning models on imbalanced datasets. The
076
      contributions of this paper are summarized as follows.
077
078         1. We introduce a new CSL function which dynamically re-weights the samples in accordance
079            with features learned of the classes along with learning complexity of the classes, therefore
080            leads to improvements in generalization to minority classes.
081
            2. We implement this CSL function within a framework that easily integrates it into differ-
082            ent machine learning architectures, thereby improving their performance on imbalanced
083            datasets, making them more scalable and robust.
084
            3. We demonstrate the efficacy of our CSL function through extensive experimentation on
085
               benchmark long-tailed datasets like CIFAR-10-LT, CIFAR-100 LT, and ImageNet-LT, im-
086            proving model accuracy and generalization over state-of-the-art methods. The code and the
087            datasets for this paper are available at icl.

090   2   M ETHOD
091
092   Cost-Sensitive Learning methods are easier to implement with no extra modules being added to the
093
      model and incur a very slight increase in training time. MI and IA methods show promising and
      state-of-the-art results but can lead to a significant increase in the training time. Our framework
094
      retains the simplicity of existing Cost-Sensitive Learning techniques while significantly improving
095
      the performance of the model. We propose the design of a novel Cost-Sensitive Learning (CSL)
096   function that allows us to dynamically adapt class weights based on features learned and the com-
097   plexity of learning them for a particular class. The complexity of learning features for a particular
098   class is measured in terms of the entropy gain from that class in a particular epoch.
099
      Our approach also leverages reinforcement learning to apply these weight-adjustments over succes-
100
      sive training epochs. It is done by calculating the loss at each epoch and rewarding the model with
101
      a reward value ’k’ depending on the performance improvement it made compared with the previous
102   epoch. Our CSL function is a novel re-weighting approach that surpasses existing CSL functions, in
103   terms of performances achieved on benchmark datasets under similar training conditions. Our CSL
104   function is inspired by L2 regularization. It minimizes the loss while paying equal “importance”
105   to learning the tail classes despite less samples. We use a factor γi for each class i that signifies
106   the importance (or rather unimportance; higher γi , less importance is given to class i) assigned to
107   that class. This factor is multiplied with Npred,i , the number of times class i was predicted in the
      current epoch after validation, as explained below. The intuition is that, if a class i has been pre-


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      dicted more (less), we penalize it by diminishing (increasing) its importance in terms of reducing
109   the term γi × Npred,i . Thus, if a dominant class is predicted more, because its features have been
110   better represented by the model due to the larger number of training samples, its importance is then
111   reduced so that the model can focus on learning tail classes subsequently. Our method penalizes the
112   model for misclassification of samples depending on the weights assigned to each class that signify
113   their importance.
114
      Our CSL function considers (1) How well a class was learned in the previous epoch as well the
115   complexity of learning the class in terms of the entropy gain from that class, (2) The number of times
116   a particular class was accurately predicted during validation in this epoch (signifying how well the
117   model has represented the features of that class), and (3) An adjustable reinforcement term which is
118   decided by comparing the performance of the model in the ith epoch with that in the (i−1)th epoch.
119   The last term is inspired by Reinforcement Learning policies of learning from previous experiences,
120   adjusting the parameters of current epoch based on the experience of the model during training in
121   the previous epoch. The CSL function is expressed in terms of the following hyperparameters.
122   Npred,i : denotes the total number of times the class i was predicted by the model during its validation
123   in this epoch. Our method aims at empowering the model to increase the Npred,i value for the tail
124   classes. This also increases the risk of overfitting, which is controlled using techniques explained
125   below.
126
      γi : This value determines the importance assigned to class i and is expressed in terms of the semantic
127   values and the complexity of (learning the features of) the class. It is used to direct the model-
128   training in a way that forces the value of Npred,i to increase for the tail classes while keeping the
129   value almost consistent for other classes, based on how well the model has learned the features of
130   the classes. This is a dynamic parameter which is updated after each epoch based on the accuracy
131   observed while learning the features of each class in the previous epoch.
132
      Reinforcement term: We add a constant reward-term reinf orcement term, which quantifies an
133   additional increment to the loss function depending on the level of improvement in the training in
134   the current epoch compared to the previous epoch. This term tunes the value of the loss function to
135   prevent the model to converge without getting stuck at a local optimum.
136
      Our CSL function can be added as an additional term to any loss function. In our evaluation, we use

      it alongside Cross Entropy loss given as

      and ensure numerical stability. During training, as the model encounters more datapoints from the
145   dominating classes, the “credit” for the decrease in the loss function goes more to the dominating
146   classes. Thus the features of the dominating classes get better representation than those of the tail
147   classes. The “semantic values” (i.e., learned feature storage) Ma et al. (2023) of the dominating
148   classes are thus larger than those of the tail classes. The importance γi for the ith class is computed
149   based on these semantic values Ma et al. (2023) as well as the complexity of learning features from
150   that class (measured in terms of the increase in entropy). The γi values computed for the dominating

153
      in the subsequent epochs to ensure that the term γi · Npred,i reduces. In turn, this results in better
      learning of the features of the tail classes subsequently (i.e., the model focuses more on learning the
154
      features of the tail classes). Of course, the strategy of of increasing the Npred,i values of tail classes,
155
      as the training proceeds, risks overfitting to the tail classes. This is prevented by multiplying Npred,i
156   with γi . Using the semantic value Ma et al. (2023) as a metric to calculate γi for each class ensures
157   that the model will not increase the Npred,i value of the tailed classes just as a way of reducing the
158   loss. As the training proceeds, the number of learned features of tail classes will increase, and so
159   will the gamma values. This would ensure that the increase in Npred,i values are kept in check and
160   that datapoints are not predicted from the tail classes solely to lower the loss value. This ensures
161   that the model keeps increasing the Npred,i value of tailed classes only if it wishes to learn the tail
      classes better, and is not driven by the sole motivation to lower the loss value.

      The complexities of each class are computed as entropies Hi for each class based on their predictions
271   in Algorithm 1. We have empirically studied the reason to consider the complexity of each class
272   before directly assigning the weights on the basis of feature storage. Existing research Cui et al.
273   (2019); Park et al. (2021) have correlated the number of samples in each class with the weights
274   assigned to them. When feature storage is correlated with the weights to be assigned, we must
275   take the complexity to learn a particular class into consideration. An Easier To Learn (ETL) class
276   is recognised with its data points closely distributed with less variance, for example, Airplane in
277   CIFAR 10 (there are only few types of Airplanes). However, a class with sparsely distributed points
278   with higher variance is Difficult To Learn (DTL) such as Dog in CIFAR 10 (there is an enormous
279
      variety in breeds and types of a natural object like dog). The model can learn an ETL class quickly
      despite it belonging to the tail part of the distribution whereas sometimes having more samples of
280
      DTL class might not be enough for the model to learn its features. To increase the correlation of
281
      feature storage with weights, we need to avoid a situation where an ETL class belonging to the tail
282   part is not assigned a lower weight, and ensure that DTL classes are assigned lower weights, so
283   that the model can focus on learning the features of the DTL classes while the training procedure
284   continues.
285
      The entropy Hi for each class i serves as an effective measure of the complexity of learning features
286
      of that class. For ETL classes, the model provides confident predictions and hence a provides a
287
      lower value of entropy function. For DTL classes, the model is more uncertain with datapoints
288   spread out resulting in a higher value of the entropy. The Delta function is inversely proportional
289   to the complexity since we require lower γi values for classes i with higher complexity. After we
290   have received the weights γi for each class i, the loss function uses γi along with Npred,i and the
291   reinforcement term to compute the CLS function.

      6     R ELATED W ORK
416
417
      Long Tailed Recognition and Imbalance Learning: Long tailed learning is a common problem
      faced in machine learning where the training dataset contains a disproportionately large number
418
      of data points for a very small fraction of the classes. Many real-world datasets typically follow
419
      a long-tailed distribution, which makes the model biased towards the dominant or head classes,
420   while producing relatively degraded performance on the minority or tail classes. Therefore, deep
421   learning models trained using traditional approaches are unable to produce good results in real-world
422   applications. Existing research has mostly focused on increasing the accuracy of the model on tail-
423   classes based on the number of samples in each class Yang et al. (2022); Zhang et al. (2023). We
424   focus on adaptively training the model while taking into account the proportion of different classes
425   in the training data. According to Zhang et al. (2023), existing body research in this area can be
426   divided into the following categories according to the methodology they follow.
427   Information Augmentation: Information augmentation (IA) methods are based on generating new
428   (or duplicate) samples for the tail classes to compensate for the disproportionate number of head
429   classes. These may use GANs or transformers to synthesise tail samples of the tail class to in-
430   crease the models’ accuracy over the tail part of datasets, while introducing more uncertainty in
431   the resultant sample deficient classes. One such method named Major-to-Minor translation (M2m),
      presented in Kim et al. (2020), is a head-to-tail transfer augmentation technique which employs an


                                                          8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      approach similar to that of adversarial attacks to tweak the features comprising the samples of the
433   head class such that they resemble samples of the tail class. In Park et al. (2022), the authors propose
434   to make the minority samples more diverse through a simple technique of image pasting. In Wang
435   et al. (2021a), the authors introduce a framework named RSG (Rare-class sample generator) that
436   performs dynamic augmentation of the features of the tail class. It updates the features of the tail
437   class with the difference in values of the features in the data points comprising the head class with
438   respect to the center of that class. The above difference incorporates variance in the features that
439   helps create a diverse enough dataset that is capable of sufficient generalization on the tail classes in-
440   frequently occurring in the sample data points. SMOTE Han et al. (2005) represents another class of
441
      IA approaches, referred to as non-transfer augmentation, which leverages oversampling techniques,
      such as MiSLAS Zhong et al. (2021a). It observed that mixing up of sample data has positive and
442
      negative effect on representation learning and classifier learning, respectively, and minimize the
443
      chances of model over-confidence.
444
445   Module Improvement: Module Improvement (MI) methods are based on improving deep neural
446   network architectures in long-tailed learning. They can be categorized into representation learning,
447
      classifier design, decoupled training, and ensemble learning.
448   Representation Learning:
449
450           • Metric Learning: This technique is based on distance metrics specific to certain categories
451
                of tasks for feature spaces that are discriminative in nature. Prior works such as Large
                Margin Local Embedding (LMLE) Huang et al. (2016) learns by determining intra-cluster
452
                and inter-class margins. The work on Class Rectification Loss (CRL) Dong et al. (2017)
453
                mines hard pairs for adjusting weights of tail-classes.
454
              • Prototype Learning: This approach is based on learning feature definitions specific to each
455
                class. The Open Long-Tailed Recognition (OLTR) Liu et al. (2019a) framework augments
456
                features based on visual meta memory. Likewise, the Inflated Episodic Memory (IEM) Zhu
457             & Yang (2020) framework performs dynamic update on the memory blocks to improve
458             feature definitions.
459
              • Sequential Training: This methodology is based on sequential training, i.e., it continually
460             learns the features from the representations of the data samples that it encounters during
461             successive training epoch. Among existing techniques in this category, the Hierarchical
462             Feature Learning (HFL) Ouyang et al. (2016) uses hierarchical clusters for feature transfer.
463             Another method, namely Unequal-training Zhong et al. (2019), separates the head-class
464             and tail-class portion from the training dataset, and performs training separately on these.
465
466
      Classifier Design: The most commonly used classifier in deep learning is ϕ(wT · f + b), which
      suffers from the problems of long tailed distribution. Recent studies have proposed ways to deal
467
      with the imbalance at classifier level. Along with linear and cosine classifier, complicated classifier
468
      methods like RTC Wu et al. (2020) have been proposed to deal with class imbalance by means of
469   hierarchical classification.
470
471   Decoupled Training: This represents a category of techniques, such as MiSLAS Zhong et al.
472
      (2021b), typically train feature extractors for learning representations which can be generalized,
      and re-train the classifiers to handle class imbalance.
473
474   Ensemble Learning: This category of techniques combine multiple network architectures to ad-
475   dress the problems with long-tailed learning, such as BBN Zhou et al. (2020) which is used for
476   dynamic training. RIDE Wang et al. (2020) follows a unique approach by training concurrent agents
477   (experts) with the full sample data, but maintains variance in the model by means of KL-divergence
478
      loss. SADE Zhang et al. (2022), trains different agents for each class distributions, and aggregates
      the models learned by the agents in an adaptive manner using self-supervised learning.
479
480
      6.0.1    C OST-S ENSITIVE L EARNING
481
482   Cost-Sensitive Learning (CSL) methods augment the training loss, making it more customized for
483   each class with the goal of re-balancing the uneven training caused by imbalance datasets. These
484   methods either re-weight the training loss values for individual classes by multiplying a constant
485   k calculated by their re-weighting scheme, or they adjust the loss function by subtracting different
      margin factors from different classes calculated according to their re-margining scheme.


                                                          9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      Re-margining methods: To handle class-imbalance, these methods reduce the losses by certain
487   margins determined by the corresponding classes, maintaining a minimal margin between features
488   and the respective classifier. Label-Distribution-Aware Margin (LDAM) Cao et al. (2019) sets a
489   different margin for each class based on frequency of the training label, and thus produces a consid-
490   erably larger margin for the tail classes.
491
      Re-weighting methods: These techniques apply easy to use loss functions as follows. Focal loss Lin
492
      et al. (2018) adds a modulating factor to the cross-entropy that reduces the loss for well-classified ex-
493   amples and increases the loss for the misclassified examples. Weighted softmax Wang et al. (2021b)
494   implicates the simplest relation of multiplying the loss values of individual classes with the inverse
495   of their number of samples. Class-balances loss Cui et al. (2019) quantifies the relation between the
496   class frequencies and loss for each class, by defining an effective number and assuming that the loss
497   for each class is inversely proportional to the effective number of samples. Balanced Softmax Ren
498   et al. (2020) proposes adjusting prediction logits by multiplying them by label frequencies. This
499   adjustment helps alleviate class imbalance bias by incorporating the labels prior before computing
500   the final losses.
501   Module improvement methods are mostly based on class re-balancing techniques, and are comple-
502   mentary to information augmentation methods, typically leading to better performance in real-world
503   applications. However, these methods require variance in the model and incurs larger overhead while
504   training due to additional complex elements (terms) added to model. Cost-Sensitive Learning meth-
505   ods deal with data imbalance in a faster and easy to implement way by developing customized loss
506   functions. This paper is based on Cost-Sensitive Learning that introduces a novel loss function based
507
      on penalizing the model on the basis of complexity (entropy) of learning samples from a particular
      class. In Du & Wu (2023), the authors propose to minimize a loss function that is the geometric
508
      mean of the losses of the individual classes instead of averaging them. This loss function is not a
509
      CSL function.

512   7   C ONCLUSIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
513
514   This paper provides a scalable and adaptable cost-sensitive learning approach to train accurate mod-
515   els from class imbalanced data while not compromising on generalizability. We validate effective-
516   ness of our framework through extensive experiments on benchmark long-tailed datasets, demon-
517   strating significant performance improvements over state-of-the-art techniques in the area. Our ap-
518   proach dynamically tunes class weights in a novel loss function based on the effective proportion
519   of classes observed in each epoch. We leverage the exploratory mode of operation in reinforcement
520
      learning to avoid being stuck in a local minimum while tuning the above class weights. In our future
      work, we aim to further optimize this CSL function by reducing frequent changes in loss function
521
      parameters, to avoid the computation of erratic gradients.
