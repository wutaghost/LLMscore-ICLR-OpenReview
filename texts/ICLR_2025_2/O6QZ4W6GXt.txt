009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Fine-tuning large language models (LLMs) is prohibitively expensive, prompt-
012            ing the development of various parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) methods.
013            These methods primarily focus on fine-tuning small, additional modules known
014            as adapters, which account for only a small fraction of the total LLM parameters.
015            One such method, low-rank adaptation (LoRA), has shown notable parameter
016            efficiency while maintaining performance comparable to full fine-tuning. However,
017            classical LoRA may still involve tuning more parameters than necessary given the
018            intrinsic rank of pre-trained weights, as highlighted by prior work (Aghajanyan
019
               et al., 2020). Recent variants of LoRA aim to enhance fine-tuning performance, but
               they overlook the layer-wise redundancies that can be leveraged for more efficient
020
               weight sharing. In this work, we introduce S HARE L O RA, a novel approach that
021
               further enhances parameter efficiency during LLM fine-tuning by leveraging redun-
022            dancies in pre-trained model weights to share LoRA modules, thereby significantly
023            reducing the number of trainable parameters. Specifically, S HARE L O RA auto-
024            matically identifies redundancies in the pre-trained weights and determines which
025            LoRA adapters can share parameters. This is achieved by measuring the similarity
026            between representations to assess information redundancy and using a greedy al-
027            gorithm to maximize parameter sharing. We conducted extensive evaluations on
028            the LLMs of the LLaMA family across benchmark tasks. Notably, S HARE L O RA
029            achieves better parameter efficiency, with up to a 23% reduction in the number of
030
               fine-tuned parameters while delivering performance comparable to or better than
               existing PEFT methods.

034   1    I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Large language models (LLMs), e.g., GPT-4 and LLaMA2, are at the forefront of advances in the field
037   of machine learning (ML). These large models are pre-trained on vast datasets (e.g., images or text
038   corpora) and are subsequently fine-tuned for specialized tasks, demonstrating proficiency in domains
039
      such as natural language, image processing, and fundamental scientific discoveries (Bommasani
      et al., 2021; Touvron et al., 2023a; Singhal et al., 2022; 2023). These models, often referred as
040
      “base model”, are pre-trained solely to predict the next token to generate from their entire vocabulary
041
      space (Touvron et al., 2023a; Penedo et al., 2023; Team, 2023). To employ the base model for
042   real applications, e.g., building chatbots, then often need further fine-tuning (e.g., on multi-turn
043   human-human or human-chatbot conversations) to follow specific human instructions or align with
044   human preferences (Leike et al., 2018; Ziegler et al., 2019; Chung et al., 2022).
045
      Fine-tuning such large-scale LLMs, however, presents a computational challenge due to the massive
046
      number of parameters. For instance, GPU memory must be sufficiently large to handle the fine-tuning
047
      process while marinating a reasonably large batch size. Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT)
048   methods have been proposed to tackle this challenge by allowing fine-tuning of only a small subset
049   of LLM parameters or incorporating small adapter modules on top of the pre-trained model, while
050   leaving the majority of the base LLM parameters frozen (Houlsby et al., 2019; Hu et al., 2021; Zaken
051   et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2023). These methods democratize LLM fine-tuning by making it feasible
052   on commodity hardware. One popular PEFT method, LoRA, has been shown to effectively reduce
053   the GPU memory requirement during LLM fine-tuning (Hu et al., 2021). LoRA achieves parameter
      efficiency by adding low-rank adapters in parallel with specific LLM parameters, such as the query,

      Although LoRA is efficient, it treats all layers uniformly, lacking finer control over which layers
064
      are most important or exhibit similar behavior. Recent improvements, such as AdaLoRA (Zhang
065
      et al., 2023), which dynamically adjusts the rank based on layer importance, DoRA (Liu et al.,
066   2024a), which decouples weights into direction and magnitude for more nuanced fine-tuning, and
067   LoRA+ (Hayou et al., 2024), which independently adjusts the learning rates of LoRA components,
068   have aimed to enhance LoRA’s efficiency. However, these approaches overlook the redundancy
069   present in pre-trained foundation models, where certain layers may exhibit similar behavior and can
070   potentially share parameters, further reducing memory requirements.
071
      We observed that sharing the LoRA module’s weights across layers does not significantly degrade
072   performance, while effectively reducing the number of trainable parameters. Specifically, we exper-
073   imented by sharing the LoRA weights between odd-numbered and even-numbered layers, which
074   halved the number of trainable parameters. In this naive approach, the weights of even-numbered
075   layers were directly mirrored to their adjacent odd-numbered layers. Surprisingly, this straightforward
076   weight-sharing strategy led to only minor performance degradation, as demonstrated in Table 1. This
077   finding suggests that there is significant potential for further optimization through weight sharing
078   in LoRA modules. However, determining which layers should share weights remains an ongoing
079   challenge, largely due to the limited explainability of foundation models (Zhao et al., 2024). The
080
      behavior and interaction of different layers within these models are not yet fully understood, and
      there is no consensus on which layers exhibit sufficiently similar representations to justify weight
081
      sharing. This is an active area of research, and more sophisticated methods for identifying redundant
082
      layers could lead to even more efficient weight-sharing strategies in the future.
083
084   In foundation models, certain layer representations often exhibit notable similarities. This redundancy
085   is a result of insufficient training data, which prevents each parameter from learning distinct, unique
086
      features. Consequently, this leads to overlapping or redundant representations across layers. Prior
      research has harnessed these redundancies for model compression (Gromov et al., 2024). Building
087
      on this insight, our work leverages this redundancy by sharing LoRA weights across layers with
088
      similar representations during fine-tuning. This approach allows us to slightly increase the LoRA
089   rank without increasing the overall number of trainable parameters, thereby enhancing fine-tuning
090   performance.
091
092
      Our S HARE L O RA method consists of two main components: (i) computing similarity matrices
      between representations of layers and (ii) sharing the LoRA module parameters among redundancy
093
      layers. This method identifies layers with similar representations and shares their weights, reducing
094
      the number of trainable parameters while maintaining model performance. By leveraging layer
095   similarities, S HARE L O RA significantly improves finetuning efficiency. We conduct extensive ex-
096   periments on a wide range of tasks and models to demonstrate the effectiveness of S HARE L O RA.
097   Specifically, we evaluate the performance using LLaMA-7B, LLaMA2-7B, and LLaMA3-8B for
098   natural language commonsense reasoning and LLava-1.5-7B for image-text understanding. The
099   results show that S HARE L O RA performs similar or better than LoRA, while only using 80% trainable
100   parameter.
101   The summary of our contributions is as follows:
102

             • We propose S HARE L O RA, a novel parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) method that
104            leverages the similarity of layer representations to enable weight sharing, achieving this
105            without introducing any additional train or inference latency compared to LoRA.
106
107          • We develop a greedy algorithm to determine which layers should share the weights of the
               LoRA module based on the similarity of their representations.

113
114   Parameter Efficient Fine Tuning. A primary research trajectory aimed at reducing the fine-tune
115   parameters of pretrained FMs is to model the incremental updates of pretrained weights in a parameter-
116   efficient manner. For example, given a pretrained weight matrix W , diff pruning (Guo et al., 2021)
117
      initializes ∆ as the same dimensions as W and performs magnitude-based pruning on ∆. Diff pruning
      characterizes ∆ as the incremental updates of W , and it can improve the parameter efficiency due to
118
      the sparsity of ∆. However, it requires specific hardware support to accelerate the computation of
119
      unstructured sparse matrices. This hardware-specific dependency underscores a crucial consideration
120   for the practical deployment of such an approach in real-world applications. In addition, it does not
121   significantly reduce computational cost compared to full fine-tuning (Hu et al., 2021), as every entry
122   of ∆ needs to be updated and then be pruned.
123
      To tackle those limitations, Hu et al. propose LoRA (Hu et al., 2021), which parameterize ∆ as the
124
      product of two low-rank matrices:
125
126                                       W ′ = W + ∆ = W + BA,                                         (1)
127
128   where ∆ ∈ Rd1 ×d2 , B ∈ Rd1 ×r , and A ∈ Rr×d2 with r ≪ {d1 , d2 }. As the rank r is much smaller
129
      than the dimension of W (e.g., r = 8 and d1 = d2 = 4096), the number of trainable parameters and
      training overhead dramatically decreases.
130
131   However, LoRA has its limitations, as it typically applies separate parameters for each B and A
132   by default. This approach overlooks the significant variation in the redundancy of weight matrices
133   across different layers and modules during the fine-tuning of pretrained foundation models. We will
134   demonstrate this issue later. Consequently, LoRA cannot adaptively share the LoRA modules among
135
      the redundancy layers, which could otherwise achieve comparable performance with fewer trainable
      parameters.
136
137
138
      Weight Sharing. Although PEFT methods can reduce the number of trainable parameters, thereby
      decreasing GPU memory footprint during fine-tuning, the number of parameters in LLMs also scales
139
      rapidly, making it increasingly challenging to conduct even PEFT on commodity GPUs. In this
140
      paper, we explore the possibility of combining LoRA with weight sharing, a method that allows
141   multiple neural network layers to share the same model weights. Weight sharing has been a widely
142   adopted technique to reduce the number of trainable parameters while maintaining performance and
143   sometimes helping mitigate overfitting (Press & Wolf, 2017; de Lhoneux et al., 2018; Lan et al., 2020;
144   Dai et al., 2020; Takase & Kiyono, 2021).

223
224   The objective of the algorithm is to identify sets of neural network layers that can share LoRA

226   strategy to maximize the size of these shared sets while adhering to a predefined similarity threshold.

      5   R ELATED W ORKS
437
      Generative foundation models. Generative deep learning models pre-trained on large datasets are
438
      called generative foundation models (Bommasani et al., 2021). These foundation models can be
439   applied to downstream tasks by fine-tuning. Advanced generative foundation models in natural lan-
440   guage processing (NLP) such as GPT (Brown et al., 2020; Ouyang et al., 2022) and LLaMA (Touvron
441   et al., 2023a) model have shown great success in assisting and generating human-like text across
442   a wide range of topics. These generative language models can also be applied to many practical
443   downstream tasks, such as education (Kasneci et al., 2023) and healthcare (Thirunavukarasu et al.,
444   2023). Another kind of generative foundation model that has developed maturely is the diffusion
445   model (Ho et al., 2020; Rombach et al., 2022). The diffusion model works well in various computer
446   vision tasks such as text-to-image generation (Everaert et al., 2023) and image editing (Kawar et al.,
447
      2023).
448   Efficient fine-tuning methods. Efficient fine-tuning methods aim to reduce the number of trainable
449   parameters to save the GPU memory and training time during fine-tuning large-scale models. Some
450   PEFT methods freeze most of the parameters in the model and only fine-tune specific modules, e.g.,
451   BitFit (Zaken et al., 2021) fine-tunes only the bias of the model, which significantly saves the GPU
452   memory. However, it cannot be executed on models without bias parameters. (Houlsby et al., 2019)
453
      and (Pfeiffer et al., 2020) add adapter layers between transformer blocks. These methods accelerate
      fine-tuning by transferring knowledge from adapter layers pre-trained on general tasks. LoRA (Hu
454
      et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2024b) is the most popular adapter for fine-tuning large foundation models.
455
      It adopts the product of two small matrices to represent the full gradient during fine-tuning. It can
456   reduce the number of trainable parameters by 10,000 times and the GPU memory requirement by 3
457   times. Some adaptive algorithms work together with LoRA that can dynamically adjust the number
458   of trainable parameters to fit specific needs. For instance, AdaLoRA (Zhang et al., 2023) adaptively
459   allocates the trainable parameters to fit the GPU memory budget. These adaptive algorithms on LoRA
460   require heterogeneous LoRA configuration when implemented in federated fine-tuning.
461
462   6   F UTURE WORK
463
      Our proposed S HARE L O RA method is orthogonal to other LoRA variants, suggesting that future work
464
      could explore combining S HARE L O RA with existing PEFT methods. For example, LoRA+ (Hayou
465   et al., 2024) sets different learning rates for the adapter matrices A and B. By adjusting the
466   learning rate ratio between these two matrices, the efficiency and performance of fine-tuning can
467   be significantly improved. Specifically, LoRA+ sets the learning rate of B to be λ times that of A.
468   This technique could easily be integrated with S HARE L O RA. Additionally, DoRA (Liu et al., 2024a)
469   is a novel PEFT method that enhances LoRA by decomposing pre-trained weights into magnitude
470   and direction components for more precise fine-tuning. This weight decomposition allows DoRA to
471   optimize the magnitude and direction of weights separately. S HARE L O RA could also be combined
472   with DoRA to share the magnitude and direction components among redundant layers. In summary,
473
      S HARE L O RA not only provides a robust solution for fine-tuning but also offers the flexibility to
      integrate with advanced PEFT methods, opening new avenues for research and potentially leading to
474
      significant gains in PEFT performance and efficiency.
475

477   7   C ONCLUSION
478
479
      In this paper, we present S HARE L O RA, a parameter-efficient fine-tuning method that determines
480   which layers share the LoRA weights based on layer redundancy. S HARE L O RA leverages the
481   cosine similarity between each layer’s representations to ascertain redundancy. Utilizing a greedy
482   algorithm, we maximize the sharing of LoRA weights while adhering to a predefined similarity
483   threshold. This approach effectively reduces the number of trainable parameters. We conduct
484   extensive experiments on large language models and multi-modal vision-language foundation models.
485   The results demonstrate that S HARE L O RA achieves comparable or superior performance to existing
      PEFT methods, while using only 80% of the trainable parameter budget.
