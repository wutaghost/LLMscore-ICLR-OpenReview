009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            The increasing prevalence of Large Language Models (LMs) in critical applications
012            highlights the need for controlled language generation strategies that are not only
013            computationally efficient but that also enjoy performance guarantees. To achieve
014            this, we use a common model of concept semantics as linearly represented in an
015            LM’s latent space. In particular, we take the view that natural language generation
016
               traces a trajectory in this continuous semantic space, realized by the language
               model’s hidden activations. This view permits a control-theoretic treatment of
017
               text generation in latent space, in which we propose a lightweight, gradient-free
018
               intervention that dynamically steers trajectories away from regions correspond-
019            ing to undesired meanings. Crucially, we show that this intervention, which we
020            compute in closed form, is guaranteed (in probability) to steer the output into the
021            allowed region. Finally, we demonstrate on a toxicity avoidance objective that the
022            intervention steers language away from undesired content while maintaining text
023            quality.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      Language Models (LMs) have become widespread in critical applications such as content moderation
030   and real-time information dissemination (Zeng et al., 2024). Despite their transformative impact, these
031   models require updates to remain accurate post-deployment. Moreover, as demand for more nuanced
032   text generation rises, strategies that enforce constraints during text generation are increasingly needed.
033   To address these challenges, controllable text generation has emerged as a pivotal research area.
034
      Several approaches have been proposed towards controllable text generation (Kumar et al., 2021; Lu
035
      et al., 2021; Li et al., 2022; Qin et al., 2022). Of them, a popular approach is prompt engineering (Luo
036   et al., 2023; Bhargava et al., 2023; Cai et al., 2023), where natural language prompts are carefully
037   chosen at input-time to steer generation. Other approaches modify LM parameters to achieve the
038   desired outputs (Yao et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023b). Lastly, some approaches engineer LM activations,
039   or input representations, to steer them into the representations of desired outputs (Dathathri et al.,
040   2019; Hernandez et al., 2023; Konen et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024a).
041
      Despite current efforts, ensuring the controllability of these models remains a challenge due to their
042   limited interpretability. For instance, while knowledge editing provides an efficient alternative to
043   exhaustive retraining, it poses risks akin to the butterfly effect: minor adjustments could lead to
044   unintended consequences. Moreover, it is paramount for these approaches to be robust and ensure
045   controllability guarantees to mitigate risks and harness their full potential safely.
046
      To address this gap, we propose to use control theory to tackle controlled language generation.
047
      Specifically, optimal control theory (Kirk, 2004) offers principled methods to steer trajectories in
048   latent space that enjoy theoretical guarantees on the performance of the intervention. In the framework
049   of optimal control theory, our intervention method, which we call Linear Semantic Control (LiSeCo),
050   derives from a theoretical formulation of controlled text generation. Our contributions are both
051   theoretical and empirical: (1) we formally pose LM control as a constrained optimization problem
052   and provide its closed-form solution with guarantees; (2) we empirically demonstrate our method on
053   the use cases of toxicity and negativity avoidance. We confirm, with experiment corroborating theory,
      that LiSeCo indeed steers LM generation from disallowed concepts while maintaining text quality.

      2    R ELATED W ORK
055
056   Contemporary language models are deep neural networks pre-trained on trillions of tokens of Internet-
057   scale text. In part due to their vast scale and lack of interpretability, methods to control them in a
058   fine-grained way remain elusive. A number of approaches have already been proposed towards this
059   end, spanning the whole spectrum of permanent (Meng et al., 2022b; Belrose et al., 2023) to online
060   control strategies (Liu et al., 2021; Dathathri et al., 2019). Here, we review post-hoc intervention
061   methods and situate LiSeCo with respect to the current landscape.
062   Post-hoc intervention methods can intervene on various components of the LM: for instance, decoding,
063   like FUDGE and GeDI (Yang & Klein, 2021; Krause et al., 2021), activations, like LiSeCo, or weights
064   via finetuning. All such methods aim to modify some attribute, such as toxicity, while maintaining
065   text fluency. Ultimately, all methods work towards this goal by modifying the LM’s final probability
066   distribution, either directly or indirectly. We can situate where different method classes intervene,
067   viewing an LM as a series of T function compositions corresponding to the T layers, where s is a
068   sequence of tokens:
069                          PLM (si |s<i ) = fT ◦ fT −1 · · · ◦ f1 (s<i ) := LM (s<i ).
070
071   Decoding-based methods fix the function LM := fT ◦ fT −1 ◦ · · · f1 and directly edit its output
072   probability distribution PLM (si |s<i ) (Yang & Klein, 2021; Liu et al., 2021; Krause et al., 2021).
073   These methods require access to an external evaluator whose feedback is used to calibrate token
074   probabilities, which can result in high inference latency.
075   Prompt engineering is a technique that controls the LM’s output by varying the input context s<i ,
076   keeping the function LM := fT ◦ fT −1 ◦ · · · f1 fixed (Luo et al., 2023; Bhargava et al., 2023; Cai
077   et al., 2023; Wei et al., 2022; Li & Liang, 2021). Prompts are often highly task-specific, requiring
078   either manually crafting or ad-hoc computationally-taxing techniques, and success can be brittle
079   to prompt choice (Weber et al., 2023). While the space of natural language prompts is discrete,
080   LM weights and activations live in continuous high-dimensional space, which is more expressive;
081
      then, rather than search over discrete prompts, other approaches that exploit this expressivity directly
      intervene in the internals of the model.
082
083   Of them, weight-based methods modify the functions fi themselves, which permanently constrains
084   the space of final probability distributions PLM . These methods comprise, e.g., reinforcement learning
085   from human feedback (Ouyang et al., 2022), instruction-tuning, parameter-efficient adaptation (Hu
086   et al., 2022), or targeted weight-editing (Meng et al., 2022b; Belrose et al., 2023). In such approaches,
087
      weights are modified according to the goal of the controlled generation by, for instance, learning
      the necessary update (De Cao et al., 2021; Mitchell et al., 2021), or localizing and editing target
088
      parameters encoding specific knowledge (Dai et al., 2022; Meng et al., 2022a;c; Li et al., 2024b).
089
      Pitfalls range from potential inconsistencies and distortions, to the fact that weight-based methods
090   can only correct errors in the LM’s parametric knowledge, but not in-context (Li et al., 2023b).
091
092   Activation-based methods, such as LiSeCo, fix LM := fT ◦ fT −1 ◦ · · · f1 , but intervene at the
093
      domain of each fi , where introducing a steering vector transforms the input to fi (Li et al., 2023a;
      Turner et al., 2023). These interventions can be seen as restricting the domain of each fi , eventually
094
      constraining the space of probability distributions PLM when composed up through the layers. A key
095
      advantage of activation steering is rapid adaptation that is context-dependent. An initial work in this
096   domain was Plug and Play (Dathathri et al., 2019), where a linear intervention is computed at every
097   layer. The control goal is encoded as the objective function in an optimization that is then solved
098   via back-propagation, adding significant computational overhead at inference time. Subsequent
099   approaches also compute linear modifications to the latent state, but reduce computational overhead,
100   act on only a few layers (Subramani et al., 2022; Konen et al., 2024), or pre-compute steering vectors
101   to avoid back-propagation (Turner et al., 2023). The recent approach REMEDI (Hernandez et al.,
102   2023) also finds an optimal intervention to achieve different target outputs, but, requires specific
103   a-priori training data for the representations, and offers no guarantees that the intervention will attain
104
      the target output. The approach presented in ITI (Li et al., 2024a) addresses the issue of computational
      efficiency at the expense of optimality (the intervention is not formulated as an optimizer), and lacks
105
      guarantees on the intervention’s performance.
106
107   The use of steering vectors for text generation proposed in the literature provides empirical grounding
      for the promise of this approach. However, none of the approaches found in the literature provide

108
      guarantees on the controllability of their method. Here, we provide an intervention that is theoretically
109   guaranteed to steer the input into the allowed region, and introduces minimal computational overhead.
110   Our work differs from existing literature by the following novel contributions:
111
112         1. We formally derive the optimal steering vector and offer theoretical performance guarantees.
113            This is enabled by the use of an optimal control framework to cast the problem for the first

115         2. Deriving the closed-form solution for the intervention minimizes increased latency at

117            methods, such as FUDGE, require much higher latency.

      our knowledge, our method is the first to propose a control-theoretic intervention whose theoretical
120
      guarantees are validated in practice.

123   3     P ROBLEM S TATEMENT
124
125   In this section we present the problem studied in this paper, as well as the assumptions and approach.
126   In particular, we approach the problem of controlled language generation as a standard optimal
127
      control problem in the field of control theory (Kirk, 2004).
128
129   3.1   P ROBLEM F ORMULATION
130
      Given a language model ℓ : Σ∗ → Σ∗ , controlled language generation aims to steer the model’s
131   output into a desired one. In this work, we study how to steer the output of an already trained model
132   away from a disallowed region, or so that the set of possible generated sequences is constrained to an
133   allowed subset S ⊂ Σ∗ . The requirements for the generated output sequence are two fold: its latent
134   trajectory (1) is guaranteed to never lie in the disallowed region, and (2) stays as close as possible
135   to that of the original output sequence, so that text quality is not compromised. In doing this, two
136   questions need to be answered:
137
138
            1. How can the disallowed region be defined for a given language model?
139         2. How can an intervention be designed to be guaranteed (in probability) to stay within the
140
               allowed region while retaining maximal similarity with the original model?
141   In what follows, we provide an answer to these questions, and show that the proposed approach adds
142   minimal computational overhead to the language generation process.
143
144   3.2   A PPROACH
145
146   We design an online method that guarantees, at each token generation, that the sequence remains
147   safe. Given the sequential, feedforward nature of LM layers, we consider each new token generation
148   to realize a trajectory through the layers’ activation spaces. Similar to Park et al. (2023), we take
149   the view that disallowed language occupies a region of each layer’s activation space Rd . Formally,
150   let Rt ⊂ Rd be the forbidden, or unsafe, region for layer t of the LM. Our goal, then, is to provide
151
      a control mechanism by altering the output vector embedding at every layer that guarantees (in
      probability) that latent trajectories remain out of Rt for all t, see Figure 1. For each token generation
152
      pass, this control mechanism is to be applied at each layer. This control intervention is designed
153
      online, and it depends on the prompt sequence.
154
155
      Semantic Probe We first identify the disallowed region for the generated token, given context.
156   To do so, we feed a set of sequences to the model, and use a lightweight probe to map each latent
157   state xt ∈ Rd to a probability that the sequence is toxic. Specifically, we rely on a probing classifier
158   function ft that maps the latent space Rd to the decision space [0, 1]. For simplicity, we take ft to
159   be a logistic regression classifier realized by a linear probe (Hewitt & Manning, 2019). Formally,
160   ft : Rd → [0, 1]; xt 7→ σ(Wt⊤ xt ), where Wt ∈ Rd×2 and σ : R2 → R is the sigmoid. For each
161   layer, we define the disallowed region Rt to be the pre-image of an unsafe classification under ft ,
      using a predefined probability threshold p. That is, Rt := {x| σ(Wt⊤ x) ≥ p}, where p ∈ [0, 1].

      Optimal Control Once the forbidden region Rt is identified, we design a control strategy that, for
188
      all layers t, guarantees the latent state xt remains in the allowed region and retains maximal similarity
189
      with the original model. To do this, we design an optimal controller that generates an input θt ∈ Rd
190   at every layer t. Mathematically, we solve an optimization problem over θt where the pre-computed
191   classifier enters as a hard constraint in the formulation, i.e., σ(Wt⊤ (θt + xt )) ≤ p. This ensures the
192   controlled latent trajectory x̃t = xt + θt ∈ Rd lies in the unsafe region with probability less than p.

      The LiSeCo pipeline is as follows. First, to find the unsafe regions and probes per layer, there is
282   an initial probe training phase. Then, probes are integrated into the model at inference-time, and
283   the optimal intervention dynamically applied. We tested LiSeCo on two separate tasks: toxicity
284   avoidance and negativity avoidance. For brevity, when appropriate, we will use toxicity to stand in
285   for both toxicity and negativity in this section.
286
287   Models We test on three state-of-the-art causal language models: Llama-3-8B (Meta, 2024), Pythia-
288   6.9B (Biderman et al., 2023), and Mistral-7B (Jiang et al., 2023). While the architectural details of a
289   layer (attention + MLP) differ slightly between models, our intervention treats layers as black boxes
290   and operates at the level of the residual stream (Elhage et al., 2021). This permits our intervention to
291   be applied as a lightweight layer wrapper and in an architecture-agnostic way.
292
293   Datasets We test our method on the toxicity and negativity use cases. Borrowing terminology from
294   Ashok & Poczos (2024), we first learn probing classifiers f using a labelled constraint dataset, then,
295   we evaluate text generation on a task dataset.
296   For toxicity, we use Kaggle’s Jigsaw dataset (Adams et al., 2017) as the constraint dataset. The dataset
297   contains 30k label-balanced natural language comments and their human-annotated binary toxicity
298   labels in {toxic, nontoxic}. For the task dataset, we use RealToxicityPrompts, a dataset derived from
299   OpenWebText, a large-scale corpus of the web (Gehman et al., 2020). RealToxicityPrompts is a
300   collection of prompts, their continuations, and toxicity scores in [0, 1] (Gokaslan & Cohen, 2019). To
301   form the task dataset, we sample 150 neutral prompts for which there is a toxic continuation and 150
302
      for which there is a non-toxic continuation in the original dataset.
303   For sentiment, because sentiment datasets tend to be highly domain-specific (for instance, movie
304   reviews), we combine several datasets to form the constraint dataset (N =30k). This consists of +/-
305   label-balanced samples of 7500 datapoints each from IMDb film reviews (Maas et al., 2011), Tweets
306   (Barbieri et al., 2020), Yelp reviews (Zhang et al., 2015), and Amazon reviews (Hou et al., 2024). For
307   preprocessing details, see Appendix G. For the task dataset, we sample 300 neutral sentiment prompts
308
      from Liu et al. (2021), created from OpenWebText as a sentiment counterpart to RealToxicityPrompts.
      Of these prompts, 150 have negative and 150 neutral or positive continuations, respectively.
309
310
311
      Probing classifiers Our theoretical guarantees rely on a key assumption: that at each layer t,
      there indeed exists a Rt separable by linear ft which together capture a semantics of the text being
312
      generated. We first verify, then, across the panel of LMs that it is possible to linearly decode whether
313
      text is toxic from each layer of the LM. Towards this aim, we split each of the constraint datasets
314   into an 80% training set and 20% validation set. Then, for each model, dataset, and layer, we extract
315   the last token hidden representations xt ∈ Rd for each training sequence; we choose the last token
316   embedding to represent the entire sequence, as in causal LMs, it is the only to attend to the entire
317   input sequence. We proceed to train one binary classifier ft per-layer using the cross-entropy loss
318   between the probe prediction and ground-truth label. See Appendix H for implementation details.

      dynamically applied. For simplicity, we fix text generation to max 50 new tokens, greedily decoded.

      Baselines We compare our method against several baselines: no-control, instruction-tuning where
325   applicable (Llama and Mistral), Activation Addition (ActAdd) Turner et al. (2023), and Future
326   Discriminators for Generation (FUDGE) (Yang & Klein, 2021).
327
328
      We consider instruction-tuning, which relies on extensive LM finetuning, to be a target, or “upper-
      bound", baseline. For models with instruction-tuned variants (Llama and Mistral), we repeat the
329
      experimental procedure, training probes on the constraint dataset. Then, during evaluation, we prompt
330
      the instruction-tuned model using a template whose instructions are slightly modified from Mistral’s
331   system prompt provided in Jiang et al. (2023) (see Appendix I for details).
332
333   Like LiSeCo, ActAdd steers text generation in activation space (Turner et al., 2023). For each model,
334
      the steering vector is computed as follows: (1) a source and target prompt, e.g., (“hate"→“love"),
      are each fed through the model and activations collected; (2) for each layer, the steering variable is
335
      computed as the difference from source to target activation; (3) at inference time, the steering variable
336
      is added to the intermediate representations of the input data. Like LiSeCo, ActAdd is gradient-free
337   at inference-time. But, there are key differences: since steers derive from natural language prompts,
338   ActAdd does not require a supervised learning phase on annotated data as in LiSeCo. For the same
339   reason, the method lacks guarantees. For implementation details, see Appendix J.
340
      Lastly, we test against Future Discriminators for Generation (FUDGE) (Yang & Klein, 2021) as a
341
      representative for steering methods that intervene on the final vocabulary logits (Liu et al., 2021;
342
      Schick et al., 2021; Cao et al., 2023). FUDGE requires access to an automatic toxicity scorer which
343   returns a probability that the context + generated token are toxic. Then, at each token generation, the
344   top k = 50 likely tokens, where k is a hyperparameter, are scored for toxicity, and their probabilities
345   reweighted using Bayes’ Rule, to minimize the probability of generating a toxic sequence. Because
346   k sequences are passed to the automatic scorer every time a new token is generated, FUDGE has
347   the potential to incur a high inference latency compared to other methods; however, this comes at
348   the benefit of directly optimizing for the evaluator. For this reason, we consider FUDGE to be an
349   upper-bound baseline, given that other steering methods do not have access to the ground-truth scorer.
350
351   Evaluation We evaluate on toxicity (negativity) avoidance and text quality. Toxicity is evaluated
352
      automatically, while text quality is rated on a Likert scale by the authors in a blind setup (Appendix N).
353   Semantic control. We rate text generation toxicity using automatic scorers that produce what we call
354   external scores. The automatic scorers are a state-of-the-art RoBERTa-based classifier trained on
355   Twitter data (Camacho-collados et al., 2022; Barbieri et al., 2020) (see Appendix B for model details).
356   We take the scorer’s ratings as ground-truth labels, where sequences are labelled toxic if the classifier
357   returns a likelihood higher than 0.5, and non-toxic otherwise. Post-scoring, we restrict evaluation to a
358
      randomly sampled, label-balanced set as follows: (1) we randomly choose N prompts for which the

521   7    D ISCUSSION
522
523   We have proposed LiSeCo, a controlled language generation method that is theoretically guaranteed
524   to stay within permitted regions of latent space. Empirically, the method produces non-toxic and
525   non-negative, but still natural, text. In line with theoretical guarantees, the parameter p was shown
526   to empirically control the probability of generating unwanted text. LiSeCo is compatible with all
527   current Transformer-based architectures, and involves a negligible inference-time latency. In future
528
      work, we are interested in applying our approach to different tasks and joint constraints, as well as to
      alternatives to linear probes as the way to ascertain whether a token falls into the undesirable region.
529
530   With the increasing ubiquity of LMs comes a growing need to understand their behavior. LiSeCo
531   helps address this need by providing practical and theoretical tools for LM interpretability and control.
532   That said, using LiSeCo has several caveats: (1) it requires supervised learning of the linear probes
533   on annotated data; (2) the intervention is only as good as the probes, which is only as good as their
534
      training data. Thus, when training probes, it is crucial that the training data well-represent the use
      domain. We emphasize that this bottleneck is inherent to any method that learns from data, and it
535
      exists for all steering methods that rely on a discriminator, e.g., FUDGE, Plug and Play, ITI. Another
536
      limitation of our method is that we assume disallowed semantics to be linearly encoded in latent
537   space, and that learned probe semantics highly align to true semantics; this should always be verified
538   in practice.

       We have shown that the LiSeCo framework can be applied to both setting an attribute to a specific
1001
       value and within a specific range, given a scoring function ϕ that imposes an order on strings Σ∗ .
1002   Because ϕ yields continuous ratings ∈ R that are ordered, the scores are isomorphic to [0, 1] by means
1003   of a continuous monotonic map ν. Then, tuning an attribute to a particular value β is equivalent
1004   to performing logistic regression with ft , such that the output is p ≜ ν(β). Crucially, ν permits
1005   interpretation of LiSeCo p in human rating space, a feature that does not exist for other tested
1006   methods.
1007
       The emphasis here is on continuous tuning of an attribute, e.g., formality. However, these extensions
1008
       to LiSeCo also permit a probabilistic interpretation when appropriate, where p would the probability
1009   that a given generation is, for instance, toxic.

1111   scored by the neural scorer, as a function of the design parameters of LiSeCo p, ActAdd c, and

1113   ν −1 , (Pearson R = 0.80, significant at α = 1e-3).5 .
1114   As desired, formality is monotonic in LiSeCo p and Instruct S. Anecdotally, higher ActAdd strengths
1115   in both the informal and formal directions degraded text quality, causing the neural scorer to rate the
1116   text as “informal"; hence, the upside-down U shape. However, while shifting formality in the correct
1117   direction, both LiSeCo and Instruct do not produce generations which capture the full range of human
1118   ratings ∈ [−3, 3] (Pavlick & Tetreault, 2016). For Instruct, this is due to the model often repeating
1119   the input instructions, while copying its style. We hypothesize that another factor is a saturation effect
1120   at the upper extreme of the human and neural scorers, see Figure F.3; learning a better mapping ν −1
1121
       from the neural scorer to the implied human rating is an important direction for future work.

1124   intervention strength. As expected, the more extreme the intervention, the lower the text naturalness.
1125   We find in particular that ActAdd is very sensitive to intervention strength, where increasing |c| from
1126   0.01 to 1 decreases naturalness on average from around 4 to 3. For Instruct, changing the design
1127   parameter S did not impact naturalness; instead, we note that generations followed the style of, and
1128   often repeated content from the input template, which reduced the text naturalness from the no-control
1129
       baseline (far right). Finally, LiSeCo, often even for strong interventions, e.g. p = 0.99 but not for
       p = 0.01, only minorly impacted naturalness.

1266
       We closely follow the setup detailed in Appendix B of Turner et al. (2023), testing recommended
1267   ranges. Although we do not vary the prompts, we perform a coarse-grained hyperparameter grid
1268   search on the intervention layer l and intervention strength c:
1269
1270           • Toxicity (source, target) prompts: (toxicity, kindness)
1271
1272           • Sentiment (source, target) prompts: (optimism, despair)
1273           • Formality (source, target) prompts: (casual, formal)
1274
1275           • Intervention layer l: {6, 15, 24}
1276
               • Intervention strength c: {0.01, 0.1, 1, 3, 9, 15}

1278   As the text generation is often longer than the source and target prompts, we apply the intervention at
1279   the first token position, as reported in Turner et al. (2023). The ActAdd forward generation process is
1280   completely deterministic.
1281
       We find for all hyperparameter settings starting with c ≥ 1 the same qualitative patterns in text
1282
       generation: sequences of repeated tokens. The best hyperparameter setting we found corresponded to
1283
       (c, l) = (0.1, 6) for the sentiment task and (c, l) = (0.1, 15) for the toxicity task.
