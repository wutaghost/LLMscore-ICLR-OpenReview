011                                                 A BSTRACT
012
013              Recent advancements in text-to-image and text-to-video models, such as Stable
014              Diffusion 3 (SD3), Flux and OpenSora, have adopted rectified flow over traditional
015              diffusion models to enhance training and inference efficiency. SD3 notes increased
016
                 difficulty in learning at intermediate timesteps but does not clarify the underlying
                 cause. In this paper, we theoretically identify the root cause as a higher variance
017
                 in the loss gradient estimates at these timesteps, which hinders training efficiency.
018
                 Furthermore, this high-variance region is significantly influenced by the noise
019              schedulers (i.e., how we add noises to clean images) and data (or latent space)
020              dimensions. Building on this theoretical insight, we propose a Variance-Reduction
021              Sampling (VR-sampling) strategy that samples the timesteps in high-variance
022              region more frequently to enhance training efficiency in flow models. VR-sampling
023              constructs sampling distributions based on Monte Carlo estimates of the loss
024              gradient variance, allowing it to easily extend to different noise schedulers and
025              data dimensions. Experiments demonstrate that VR sampling accelerates training
026              by up to 33% on ImageNet 256 and 50% on ImageNet 512 datasets in rectified
027
                 flow models. Furthermore, VR-sampling could simplify the hyperparameter tuning
                 of logit-normal sampling introduced in SD3. The code is available anonymously
028
                 in https://github.com/AnonymousProjects/VR_sampling.git.

031   1       I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Diffusion models (Song et al., 2021b; Ho et al., 2020; Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021; Song et al.,
034   2021a) have emerged as powerful generative models, showing remarkable potential in producing
035   high-quality data across various domains, such as image generation (Rombach et al., 2022), video
036   generation (Blattmann et al., 2023), and molecular design (Abramson et al., 2024). While traditional
037   diffusion models typically rely on a diffusion denoising loss for training, the latest state-of-the-art
038   models—such as Stable Diffusion 3 (SD3) (Esser et al., 2024), Flux1 , and OpenSora2 —have adopted
039   flow matching training loss (Lipman et al., 2022). This approach offers improved training efficiency
040   and a shorter sampling path, making it a preferred choice for modern generative models.
041   Generative modeling seeks to approximate and sample from a target probability distribution. A
042   prominent approach, diffusion models (Song et al., 2021b; Ho et al., 2020), generate samples by
043   simulating a stochastic differential equation (SDE) that gradually transforms a simple distribution into
044   the data distribution. Their success is largely attributed to a simple regression-based training objective,
045   which bypasses the need to simulate the SDE during training. Additionally, an ODE exists that has the
046   same marginal probability as the SDE, providing an alternative path for model training. Recent work
047
      has extended the use of ODEs in generative modeling. For example, continuous normalizing flows
      (CNFs) (Lipman et al., 2022) leverage flow matching, a method for directly regressing the ODE drift,
048
      akin to the training process in diffusion models. Similarly, (Liu et al., 2022) introduced rectified flow,
049
      a simplified ODE-based approach for transporting between two observed distributions. Expanding on
050   these advancements, SD3 (Esser et al., 2024) combines both diffusion and flow formulations to train
051   a state-of-the-art, open-source text-to-image model using flow models. SD3 also highlights a key

084   challenge in rectified flow, noting that the target ODE drift is more difficult to learn at intermediate
085   timesteps. To address this, SD3 introduces logit-normal sampling and mode sampling to sample
086   intermediate timesteps more frequently, thus improving the training efficiency of flow models.
087   In this work, we delve into a deeper understanding of why the target ODE drift function is particularly
088   challenging to learn at intermediate timesteps. In the optimization of diffusion models or flow models,
089   techniques such as conditional score matching or conditional flow matching losses are utilized, as
090   we can only obtain the ground truth regression target when conditioned on the given samples. It has
091   been proved in (Lipman et al., 2022) that the conditional and non-conditional losses are identical, up
092   to a constant that is independent of optimization parameters—making the gradients of conditional
093
      loss an unbiased estimator of those from non-conditional losses. However, the practical optimization
      process often involves stochastic estimates of the loss, leading to discrepancies between optimizing
094
      conditional loss and unconditional loss. We theoretically analyze the variance of conditional loss
095
      gradient estimates and demonstrate that at intermediate timesteps, it exhibits larger variances, and thus
096   influences the training stability of these timesteps. Furthermore, we identify that the high-variance
097   regions closely depend on the noise schedulers (which dictate how noise is added to clean images to
098   achieve a pure Gaussian distribution) and the dimensions of data or latent space. In Fig. 1 (a)-(c), we
099   present our derived upper bound on the variance of conditional loss gradient estimates under different
100   noise schedulers and datasets. The results show that the high-variance regions vary depending on
101   both the noise scheduler and the dataset dimensionality.
102   Building on this theoretical analysis, we propose a Variance Reduction-based sampling strategy
103   (VR-sampling) that samples the high-variance regions more frequently to accelerate the training.
104   VR-sampling distributions are constructed using Monte Carlo simulations of the derived upper bounds
105   on the variance and can adjust according to the noise schedulers and data dimensions. The probability
106   density function π(t) of simulated VR-sampling distributions are presented in Fig.1 (d)-(f). Unlike
107   previous acceleration methods, such as those in (Hang et al., 2023; Choi et al., 2022; Wang et al.,
      2024), which primarily focus on noise schedulers and often identify important timesteps heuristically,

305
      bound to create a probability density function (PDF) π(t) over t. The process involves the following
306
      steps: (1) we first normalize the simulated curve by calculating the total area under the curve (via
307   integration) and dividing the curve by the total area; (2) we compute the cumulative sum of the
308   normalized curve to derive the cumulative distribution function (CDF) F (t); (3) Using the CDF,
309   we construct an interpolation function to approximate its inverse F −1 (u), u ∼ U nif orm(0, 1); (4)
310   the inverse CDF maps a uniformly distributed random variable to the domain of t, ensuring that
311   the resulting samples follow the distribution defined by the probability density function (PDF) π(t),
312   where π(t) = F ′ (t). Here the inverse CDF serves as a crucial tool for sampling from the desired
313   density. Besides, as we use Monte Carlo estimation, we cannot reach the exact point where St = 1,
314   but as t → 1, at /mt → ∞, causing the simulated variance curve to approach infinity. To address

332   4.1     E XPERIMENT S ETTINGS
333   Datasets and Codebases To validate our theoretical analysis, we conduct experiments on datasets
334   of varying dimensions, including CIFAR10, ImageNet-1k 256, and ImageNet-1k 512, and evaluate
335   across three noise schedulers: linear, cosine, and diffusion. For CIFAR10, we use the TorchCFM
336   codebase 3 , which leverages an U-Net to train flow models. For ImageNet 256 and ImageNet 512, we
337   employ the SiT codebase 4 . SiT (Ma et al., 2024) is a flow model counterpart to DiT (Peebles & Xie,
338   2023) and uses the same Transformer architecture. To compare with state-of-the-art methods under
339   diffusion noise scheduler, we test our sampling strategy on the DiT codebase 5 .
340   Training and Evalutions To validate the effectiveness of our methods, we adhere to the default
341   training settings across the different codebases and run 200K iterations for CIFAR10 and 400k
342   iterations for ImageNet-1k under each configuration. We use the default training results as the
343   baseline and compare them with our designed sampling strategies. The detailed training settings are
344   provided in Appendix A.2.
345   For evaluation, we primarily use the Frechet Inception Distance (FID) to assess the performance of

474   5       R ELATED W ORK
475
476   Flow and Diffusion Generative Models Diffusion models have emerged as a powerful class of
477   generative models, particularly effective in generating high-quality images. These models operate
478   by defining a forward process, where noise is incrementally added to data, and a reverse process,
479
      which denoises the data to generate samples. Building on the principles of diffusion models, Lipman
      et al. (2022) introduced flow matching, which constructs a probability flow ODE directly to generate
480
      samples from learned distributions. Liu et al. (2022) propose rectified flow to learn the transformation
481
      between Gaussian noise and a target data distribution, by creating a straight-line path between
482   them. Huang et al. (2024) proposes incorporating correlated noise instead of pure Gaussian noise
483   into deterministic diffusion models, which improves image quality. Similarly, Heitz et al. (2023)
484   introduces iterative α-blending, which blends and deblends samples between two densities. Further

502   advancing this line of work, SD3 (Esser et al., 2024) scales rectified flow to large-scale text-to-image
503   models, pushing the boundaries of diffusion-based generative models.
504   Training Acceleration in Diffusion Models To accelerate the training of diffusion models,
505   P2 (Choi et al., 2022) and Min-SNR (Hang et al., 2023) introduce two re-weighting methods.
506   P2 weighting prioritizes learning from key noise levels determined by observations, while Min-SNR
507   adjusts timestep loss weights based on clamped signal-to-noise ratios to balance conflicts. There are
508   also two re-sampling methods: Log-Normal (Karras et al., 2022) and CLTS (Xu et al., 2024) and a
509   method combining re-weighting and re-sampling: SpeeD (Wang et al., 2024). Log-Normal (Karras
510   et al., 2022) assigns high sampling probabilities at intermediate timesteps. CLTS (Xu et al., 2024)
511   proposes a curriculum learning based timestep schedule, which leverages the noise rate as an explicit
512
      indicator of the learning difficulty and gradually reduces the training frequency of easier timesteps,
      thus improving the training efficiency. SpeeD (Wang et al., 2024) design an asymmetric sampling
513
      strategy that reduces the frequency of steps from the convergence area while increasing the sampling
514
      probability for steps from other areas. Additionally, they propose a weighting strategy to emphasize
515   the importance of time steps with rapid-change process increments. All above methods only consider
516   the impact of noise schedulers on training and most strategies only rely on the change of SNR.
517
518
      Our work builds on the concept of importance sampling to reduce gradient variance. As demonstrated
      by (Katharopoulos & Fleuret, 2018), importance sampling can focus computational efforts on infor-
519
      mative examples, effectively reduce gradient variance during training, and accelerate convergence.
520
      Additionally, Jeha et al. explores variance reduction through a k-th order Taylor expansion applied
521   to the diffusion training objective and its gradient. In contrast to (Jeha et al.), our approach directly
522   identifies and targets high-variance regions during training, sampling them more frequently to reduce
523   variance and improve efficiency, without relying on approximations as in (Jeha et al.).
524
525   6    C ONCLUSION
526   In this work, we propose VR-sampling to accelerate flow generative model training by sampling
527   timesteps in the high-variance region more frequently. We begin by theoretically analyzing the
528   upper bound on the variance of loss gradient estimates and identify that the high-variance region is
529   closely correlated with the noise schedulers and data (or latent space) dimensions. Based on this
530   theoretical insight, we design VR-sampling distributions based on Monte Carlo simulations of our
531   derived variance upper bounds. We validate the efficiency of VR-sampling under different noise
532   schedulers and different datasets. Furthermore, VR-sampling could simplify hyperparameter tuning
533   for logit-normal sampling proposed in SD3 and experiments show that logit-normal sampling with
534
      our recommended choice of m also has a good training speedup.
535   Limitations and future work Due to the limitation of GPU resources, we do not test our sampling
536   strategy on larger model size, such as stable diffusion 3 and video generation models. Extending
537   our approach to larger-scale models, where computational demands are significantly higher, would
538   greatly enhance the value of our methods. Additionally, we observe that our theoretical analysis
539   can be applied to consistency flow model training, allowing for the design of a sampling strategy to
      accelerate consistency training. We will leave this exploration for future work.
