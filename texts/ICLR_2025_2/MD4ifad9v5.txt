009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            The depth of networks plays a crucial role in the effectiveness of deep learning.
012            However, the memory requirement for backpropagation scales linearly with the
013            number of layers, which leads to memory bottlenecks during training. Moreover,
014            deep networks are often unable to handle time-series data appearing at irregular
015            intervals. These issues can be resolved by considering continuous-depth networks
016            based on the neural ODE framework in combination with reversible integration
017            methods that allow for variable time-steps. Reversibility of the method ensures
018
               that the memory requirement for training is independent of network depth, while
               variable time-steps are required for assimilating time-series data on irregular in-
019
               tervals. However, at present, there are no known higher-order reversible methods
020
               with this property. High-order methods are especially important when a high level
021            of accuracy in learning is required or when small time-steps are necessary due to
022            large errors in time integration of neural ODEs, for instance in context of complex
023            dynamical systems such as Kepler systems and molecular dynamics. The require-
024            ment of small time-steps when using a low-order method can significantly increase
025            the computational cost of training as well as inference. In this work, we present
026            an approach for constructing high-order reversible methods that allow adaptive
027            time-stepping. Our numerical tests show the advantages in computational speed
028            when applied to the task of learning dynamical systems.

030   1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Deep neural networks are widely used across various learning tasks (Russakovsky et al., 2015; Esteva
033
      et al., 2017), and their depth often plays a crucial role in the effectiveness of learning. These networks
      have also been shown to be particularly useful in the tasks of learning models of dynamical systems
034
      (Chen et al., 2018; Raissi et al., 2018a; Rudy et al., 2019b; Schüssler et al., 2019; Rudy et al., 2019a;
035
      Liu et al., 2022; Raissi et al., 2018b). It was recently shown that the use of numerical methods for
036   neural network architectures can provide impressive results with theoretical guarantees (Haber &
037   Ruthotto, 2017; Chang et al., 2018; Celledoni et al., 2021; Maslovskaya & Ober-Blöbaum, 2024).
038   In this work we use the theory of symmetric numerical methods for the construction of a new class
039   of reversible neural networks. The new class allows memory efficient computations of gradients
040   in training and reduced computational costs in learning models of dynamical systems, where the
041   parameters typically need to be identified to high accuracy and the depth of the networks can be very
042   large. Our network architecture constitutes an important contribution to ensure scalability of neural
043   ODEs to high-dimensional dynamical systems that arise, for instance, as discretizations of systems
044
      governed by partial differential equations.
045   The high memory costs of computing the gradient of very deep neural networks using the back-
046   propagation algorithms poses a significant bottleneck in their training, hindering their scalability
047   and efficiency. To address this, a neural ODE approach combined with the adjoint method has been
048   proposed for gradient computations (Chen et al., 2018), which avoids storing intermediate states
049   during forward propagation, potentially making the cost of gradient computation independent of
050
      network depth. However, it was quickly realized (Gholaminejad et al.; Zhuang et al., 2020) that using
      this approach with arbitrary discretization methods leads to incorrect gradients.
051
052   The solution of this problem is to use reversible integrators, which ensure accurate gradient com-
053   putations by reconstructing intermediate states precisely during backward integration. However,
      symplectic reversible integrators (Chang et al., 2018), which are a large class of well studied integra-

054
      tors, do not allow adaptivity in the step size and require a particular structure in the neural ODE. This
055   makes them unsuitable for use in time-series applications where data appears at irregular intervals,
056   when time-steps need to be decreased adaptively to achieve a prescribed accuracy in the learning of
057   dynamical systems, or when the identified model is used to predict continuous trajectories.
058
      Adaptive time-stepping for numerical integrators for differential equations is a well-established field
059
      in numerical analysis (Hairer et al., 2013; Deuflhard & Bornemann, 2002). In adaptive time-stepping,
060
      step sizes for an integration step are selected such that an estimate for the local error is below a given
061   error tolerance. In this way, computational cost in numerical integration can be saved when large
062   step sizes are sufficient to obtain accurate results, while step sizes are automatically decreased when
063   required. The only reversible methods compatible with variable time-step selection without losing
064   the reversibility property are asynchronous leapfrog (ALF) (Mutze, 2016), which is based on the
065   classical Verlet method (also known as leapfrog method) (Verlet, 1967), and the reversible Heun
066   method (Kidger et al., 2021).
067   ALF has been used to construct neural network architectures known as MALI networks (Zhuang et al.,
068   2021). These methods are based on operating on an augmented space: a neural ODE in the original
069   variable z is extended to a larger space and replaced by a neural ODE in (z, v). Both methods are
070   known to be of order of accuracy (2, 1) in (z, v) (Mutze, 2016), which makes them computationally
071   costly in learning tasks that require high accuracy in the integration of the neural ODE, in particular,
072   in learning of dynamical systems. To be able to reach high accuracy, the lower order methods are
073   forced to use small step sizes and, as a result, have higher computational costs. This phenomenon
074   was highlighted in the examples in (Matsubara et al., 2021). Furthermore, we show in C.1 that in
075
      parameter identification tasks there is a direct relation between the order of a numerical integrator and
      the order of accuracy of identified parameters. Therefore, there is a need for higher order reversible
076
      methods, which we address in this paper.
077
078   Various machine learning techniques have emerged in the past decades for approximating models
079   of dynamical systems (Ghadami & Epureanu, 2022). These include methods based on Gaussian
080   processes (Bouvrie & Hamzi, 2017; Raissi & Karniadakis, 2018; Hamzi & Owhadi, 2021), sparse
081
      regression on libraries of basis functions (Brunton et al., 2016; Tran & Ward, 2017; Reinbold et al.,
      2021), and recurrent neural networks (RNNs) (ichi Funahashi & Nakamura, 1993; Bailer-Jones
082
      et al., 1998; Karniadakis et al., 2021). In particular, the neural ODE approach (Chen et al., 2018)
083
      identified an important connection between the RNN structure and the numerical methods available
084   for integration of differential equations. This was generalized by universal differential equations in
085   (Rackauckas et al., 2021) for different types of differential equations. Other generalizations include
086   physics informed learning of dynamical systems (Greydanus et al., 2019; Cranmer et al., 2020b;
087   Jin et al., 2020; Chen et al., 2020) and operator approximation (Chen & Chen, 1995; Lu et al.,
088   2021; Lin et al., 2023; Boullé & Townsend, 2024). If the training data consists of time-series data
089   that corresponds to a constant time-step, neural ODEs can be trained with a low order method and
090   time-series can be predicted with high accuracy provided that the trained neural ODE is integrated
091   with the same integrator that was used during training (Zhu et al., 2021; David & Méhats, 2023; Offen
092
      & Ober-Blöbaum, 2022; Ober-Blöbaum & Offen, 2023). However, in realistic examples, snapshots
      of trajectories with variable time-steps need to be processed (Raissi et al., 2018b; Rudy et al., 2019b;
093
      Liu et al., 2022). Moreover, a discretization-independent prediction of the system’s evolution is
094
      often desired and learning of underlying differential equations requires high accuracy in the learned
095   parameters, which requires high accuracy simulation.
096
097   We demonstrate that our approach can be applied to high-dimensional dynamical systems, including
098
      those arising from discretizations of partial differential equations. This is a highly active research
      area. Other approaches in this context include model order reduction based techniques and operator
099
      inference (see the review (Kramer et al., 2024) or e.g. (Sharma et al., 2024; 2023; Allen-Blanchette
100
      et al., 2020; Mason et al., 2022)), or structure-preserving approaches for discrete field theories (Qin,
101   2020; Offen & Ober-Blöbaum, 2024; Offen, 2024).
102
103   The main contribution of this paper is the development of a methodology to construct reversible
104
      neural networks based on higher order numerical methods. First, we prove that, in contrast to the
      analysis in (Mutze, 2016), ALF is of order 2 in both (z, v) at even time-steps. Using this property
105
      and the theory of composition methods, we construct a class of reversible networks of any even order.
106
      A particular architecture based on 4th order networks is compared to the already known ALF method

      on examples of learning dynamical systems. The comparison of the two methods with adaptive
109   time-stepping shows that the proposed higher order method is computationally more efficient.

      This method is particularly interesting when the available memory is limited. In this case, the
163   forward propagation is done to obtain the value of z(T ) and ∇L(z(T ), y), there is no need to save
164   the intermediate values zj and the computational graph, because the backpropagation is realized by

167   backpropagation approach. Still, the values of zj obtained by the numerical integration backward do

169   leads to inexact gradients. This issue can be solved by considering reversible networks.
170   Checkpointing An alternative approach for memory reduction is checkpointing (Gholaminejad
171   et al.), which stores a few intermediate states for a regeneration of the computation graph. In case of
172   adaptive time-stepping it was described in (Zhuang et al., 2020) and further improved in (Matsubara
173   et al., 2021) for the class of Runge-Kutta methods. This is a highly efficient approach, when used
174   in combination with higher order methods. In this case, a small number of checkpoints is required
175
      to get a high accuracy in learning. However, when the learning task is to learn a dynamical system
      from long trajectories of complex systems, then the number of checkpoints becomes large and can
176
      lead to memory leaks. This is why it is important to have an alternative approach based on reversible
177
      networks with the memory costs independent from the given task.

179   2.3 R EVERSIBLE NEURAL NETWORK
180   A reversible network is a network with the property that there exists an explicit formula for backward
181   propagation zj+1 7→ zj that exactly inverts a forward pass zj 7→ zj+1 , i.e., there exists a map σ̃(·),
182   such that zj = σ̃(zj+1 ) and zj+1 = σh (σ̃(zj+1 ), θj ). It requires that the time-steps t0 , . . . , tN
183   have been stored when the forward propagation was computed but it does not require storage of
184   the (potentially very high-dimensional) intermediate values zj . The discretizations of neural ODE
185   equation 2, which admit this property are called reversible methods. As for now, there are only two
186   known reversible methods allowing for an adaptive choice of the step size, namely, asynchronous
187
      leapfrog (Zhuang et al., 2021) and reversible Heun (Kidger et al., 2021).
188   This notion of reversibility for neural networks needs to be contrasted with the notion of time-
189   reversibility or symmetry for numerical integrators. In the context of neural networks, reversibility
190   means that there exists an explicit, efficient formula to invert the forward pass. In numerical
191   integration theory, a time-reversible or symmetric numerical integrator is a formula to advance
192   the solution of an ordinary differential equation by time h such that its inverse is obtained by
193
      substituting h by −h (Hairer et al., 2006, II.3). In case of the dynamical system f (z(t), θ(t)) from
      equation 2, if the discretization by a numerical method zj+1 = σh (zj , θ(tj )) is symmetric, then it
194
      implies zj = σ−h (zj+1 , θ(tj+1 )), or equivalently, zj = σ−h (zj+1 , θj+1 ). Therefore, we can set
195
      σ̃(·) = σ−h (·, θj+1 ), which implies that the method is reversible. The symmetry of integrators is
196   beneficial in the context of the article as inverses of the methods required for backpropagation take
197   simple forms and efficient classical techniques to construct higher order methods (Hairer et al., 2006,
198   II.4) apply.

222   3    N EW REVERSIBLE ARCHITECTURES
223
      A general approach in numerical analysis to construct higher order symmetric methods is by compo-
224
      sition (Hairer et al., 2006; 2013; Blanes et al., 2024a). In this case one can start with a lower order
225   numerical method and construct a new method by composition of the lower order method with a
226   particular choice of step sizes. This construction leads to a method of higher order of accuracy.
227
228
      Numerical experiments, see Appendix A, show that ALF is of second order in the error with respect
      to a high accuracy solver in both z and v. This is surprising as the order of consistency of ALF in v is
229
      only 1 (Zhuang et al., 2021). Indeed, as we show below, a method consisting of a composition of two
230
      steps of ALF has order of consistency 2 in (z, v), which explains the convergence behaviour. This
231   observation is required to apply theory for composition methods (Hairer et al., 2013; Blanes et al.,
232   2024a; Yoshida, 1990) to (two steps of) ALF.

525   5    C ONCLUSION
526
      In this work, we construct higher order reversible methods. These constitute explicit numerical
527
      integrators which are compatible with adaptive step-size selection strategies. The methods are
528   employed to train deep neural networks that are based on neural ODEs. Thanks to the reversibility
529   property, we avoid high memory requirements for backpropagation in the optimization procedure of
530   the network parameters. Memory efficient backpropagation allows an application of deep architectures
531   to the identification tasks of models of high-dimensional dynamical systems, which arise, for instance,
532   as spatial discretizations of partial differential equations. As the method is based on neural ODEs, it
533   can be trained with time-series data at irregular time-steps and can predict continuous time-series
534   data. We showed the advantages of the newly constructed networks on the example of a network
535   based on a 4th order method and demonstrate lower memory costs and faster training in comparison
536
      to lower order methods.
537   While the examples in the article focus on system identification tasks for systems governed by
538   differential equations, extensions to neural stochastic differential equations (Kidger et al., 2021) are
539   of interest and applications to normalizing flows or image processing (Allen-Blanchette et al., 2020)
      can be an exciting avenue to explore in future works.

1072   If (qNj (α))i was obtained by exact integration of equation 12 and in the absence of noise and
1073   round-off errors, true parameter values constitute minima for L. We interpret the application of a
1074   numerical integrator as a perturbation of size O(hp ) to the exact (qNj (α))i , where h is the step size
1075   of the integration and p the order of the numerical method. This yields a perturbation L̃ of L of
1076   size O(h2p ) in case of the mean-square loss. Thus, assuming that the local minima of L at the true
1077   parameter value is non-degenerate, L̃ has a local minimum within a ball around the true parameter
1078   of size O(hp ). This follows from classical discussions on the numerical conditioning of computing
1079   zeros of a function as, for instance, in (Dahmen & Reusken, 2022, §5.2). This provides a direct
       relation of the order of an integration method and the accuracy of identified parameters.

       Notice that in the adaptive-step size context the perturbation of L and, thus, the error of its minima
1081   are controlled by the provided error tolerance. However, the discussion shows that in order to be
1082   able to expect the same accuracy in the parameter identification, neural ODEs based on lower-order
1083   methods require more integration steps than neural ODEs based on high-order methods.
1084
       The above O(hp ) error relation in the parameter estimation constitutes an asymptotic upper bound.
1085
       In Geometric Numerical Integration errors of numerical integrators can enter in highly symmetric
1086
       way (Hairer et al., 2013). In symplectic integration of Hamiltonian systems, for instance, energy
1087   errors enter in an unbiased form. If the sought parameter is related to the geometric structure that is
1088   preserved by the geometric numerical integrator, parameters can potentially be estimated to higher
1089   accuracy than expected by the order of the numerical integrator. This, together with backward error
1090   analysis techniques, was used in (Offen & Ober-Blöbaum, 2022), for instance, to accurately identify
1091   a Hamiltonian function of a dynamical system even though a low order method was used to discretize
1092   the dynamical system. These techniques, however, are tailored to the geometric problem at hand,
1093   while the approach of this article considers a more general case.
