011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Reinforcement Learning (RL) encounters substantial challenges in real-world
014            applications, due to the time-consuming, costly, and risky nature of interacting
015            with the environment. Offline Reinforcement Learning addresses this limitation by
016            training models on static datasets, allowing an optimal policy to be learned from
017            pre-collected data without requiring additional interactions with the environment.
018            However, in this setting, when the agent queries actions outside the training data
019            distribution, it can lead to overestimation of Q-values for OOD (Out-of-distribution)
020
               actions, ultimately hindering policy optimization. Previous works attempted to
               address this problem using explicit constraints such as penalty terms or support
021
               restriction. But these methods often fail to identify OOD actions or result in overly
022
               conservative Q-value estimates. We propose a novel solution that adjusts weights
023            during training by using an anomaly detection model to identify the distribution of
024            the offline dataset and employing anomaly scores to guide the offline RL process.
025            Our method(RLAD) not only effectively mitigates the overestimation of OOD
026            actions but also achieves near state-of-the-art performance on continuous D4RL
027            tasks. Additionally, this framework is highly flexible, allowing for integration
028            with various off-policy or offline RL algorithms and Anomaly Detection models to
029            enhance performance.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Reinforcement Learning (RL) has become a pivotal field in artificial intelligence, demonstrating
036   significant achievements in areas such as natural language processing (NLP) (Ouyang et al., 2022)
037   and computer vision ((Furuta et al., 2019), (Liu et al., 2023)). Its ability to enable agents to interact
038   with environments and perform human-like sequential decision-making has made RL particularly
039
      valuable in fields such as robotics (Vecerik et al., 2018), medical diagnosis, and autonomous driving
      (Gu et al., 2023). However, applying reinforcement learning (RL) to real-world problems poses
040
      significant challenges due to the time-consuming, costly, and risky nature of continuous environment
041
      interactions.
042
043   To address these challenges, offline Reinforcement Learning (Offline RL) has emerged, focusing
044   on leveraging static datasets for training. Unlike traditional RL, Offline RL lacks the continuous
045
      exploration that helps the agent converge to an optimal policy. Additionally, Offline RL must work
      with limited datasets, often failing to provide sufficient coverage of all possible state-action pairs
046
      above given offline dataset leading to a phenomenon known as distributional shift. This limitation
047
      leads to a prominent issue: the overestimation of the Q-function, especially for state-action pairs
048   not present in the training dataset, as highlighted by the work of (Kumar et al., 2019). Methods like
049   CQL, BCQ, and BRAC ((Kumar et al., 2020), (Fujimoto et al., 2019), (Wu et al., 2019) have been
050   developed to tackle this problem by making value functions pessimistic or restrict the action space of
051   a policy, which can result in overly pessimistic evaluations or overly restrictive regions. Therefore,
052   many works try to make the agent not too conservative or to address distributional shift and OOD
053   actions while maintaining trading off with the previous distributional shift and OOD repression to
      obtain an optimal policy. ((Lyu et al., 2022), (Hong et al., 2023))

054
      Inspired by the analysis of (Kumar et al., 2019), which highlights that overestimation mainly occurs
055   with data outside the training distribution, and the fact that explicit constraints can result in over-
056   pessimistic value estimation, we have focused on the following point: How can we better utilize
057   the distribution of the training dataset to avoid overestimation caused by OOD data? While we
058   aim to prevent the agent from querying out-of-distribution actions, we also recognize that not every
059   out-of-distribution action leads to a sub-optimal policy. Therefore, we seek to avoid imposing hard or
060   explicit constraints.
061   We propose that anomaly detection models, which are highly effective modules for identifying
062   out-of-distribution or novel data, can be a possible novel solution. These models adept at classifying
063   binary problems based on anomaly scores, which typically quantify how much a data point deviates
064   from the training distribution. These scores can provide useful guidance for the agent during training,
065   so we can plug-in any off-policy algorithm without the need to introduce additional regularizers into
066   their objective function.
067   In this context, we propose approaching the Offline RL problem through the lens of a Anomaly
068   Detection, classifying data as either in-distribution (normal) or out-of-distribution (abnormal). Our
069   proposed method, Reinforcement Learning with Anomaly Detection (RLAD), addresses these
070   challenges by leveraging deep learning-based anomaly detection models ((Zong et al., 2018), (Ruff
071   et al., 2018)) to efficiently and accurately estimate whether unseen data belongs to the in-distribution
072   or out-of-distribution. This approach capitalizes on the expressive power of neural network networks
073   without the need for specialized regularizer to match distributions, support or underestimate Q-value.
074   Our algorithm operates in two stages:
075
076
           1) Anomaly Detection Model Training: Train the anomaly detection model on the training
              dataset. During this phase, the model learns which data points are close to the training
077
              distribution and assigns an anomaly score as an indicator.
078
079        2) Anomaly Score-Based Weight Adjustment: Use the anomaly score of the given data
080           to inform the agent, adjusting the importance of each sample during learning through the
081
              application of different sample weights.
082
      Our experiments aim to demonstrate about anomaly scores(weight) and estimated Q-values. Our
083
      algorithm performs well in continuous environments, achieving state-of-the-art performance in several
084
      tasks. Our contributions are as follows:
085
086        1) Framework: We present a simple, easily implementable framework that can be integrated
087           with various off-policy or offline RL algorithms and anomaly detection models, especially
088           according to the environment.
089
           2) Q-value Estimation: We demonstrate that Anomaly Score-Based Weight Adjustment
090           significantly contribute to accurate Q-value estimation and the mitigation of overestimation.
091
092        3) Empirical Performance: We show good empirical performance, particularly in MuJoCo
093
              environments, achieving state-of-the-art results.

095   2    R ELATED W ORKS
096
097   Policy Constraints Several works have proposed imposing constraints on actor-critic algorithms by
098   constraining the learned policy to remain close to the behavior policy that generated the offline dataset
099   D. (Fujimoto et al., 2019) directly estimates the behavior policy πβ and constrains the learned policy
100   πθ to stay close to the estimated πβ . In contrast, (Kumar et al., 2019) argues that support matching
101   using Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) is more effective than direct matching of distributions.
102   Additionally, (Wu et al., 2019) brings πβ closer to the target policy by penalizing the critic, leading
103   to more pessimistic evaluations. Methods like (Peng et al., 2019) and (Nair et al., 2021) use the
104   advantage term to construct an implicitly weighted maximum likelihood objective, while others,
105   such as (Fujimoto & Gu, 2021), operate without the actor-critic structure. These approaches aim to
106   fundamentally prevent OOD actions from being selected by the learned policy πθ , thus mitigating
107   overestimation and providing relatively stable performance. However, these strategies can sometimes
      excessively constrain or regularize actions of the agent.

108
      Conservative Value Estimation Rather than restricting the policy, some methods focus on conser-
109   vatively estimating the value function of state-action pairs by explicitly penalizing unseen OOD
110   actions in D (Kumar et al., 2020) or by avoiding directly querying unseen actions in the dataset using
111   expectile regression to maximize the advantage (Kostrikov et al., 2021). Additionally, model-based
112   approaches such as (Yu et al., 2022) learn dynamic models to interpolate and augment data, combined
113   with conservative Q-value estimation. While these works aim to avoid the OOD overestimation
114   problem by penalizing or ignoring unseen OOD actions in D, they often suffer from overly pessimistic
115   value estimates.
116   Other approaches, such as (Gal & Ghahramani, 2016), (Wu et al., 2021), (An et al., 2021), (Bai
117   et al., 2022), utilize uncertainty measures—like Bayesian methods, MC-dropout, and ensemble to
118   adaptively adjust the level of OOD suppression. These strategies can operate without explicit policy
119   constraints, but the computational cost of measuring uncertainty can be high and incorrect.
120
      Trajectory Optimization Recently, Conditional Sequence Modeling (CSM) has emerged as a novel
121   paradigm for RL tasks, associating individual trajectories with return-to-go (RTG) tokens, enabling
122   the handling of long sequences with large model sizes, such as in (Janner et al., 2021) and (Chen et al.,
123   2021). However, due to the intrinsic stochasticity of state transitions, approaches like (Yamagata
124   et al., 2023) and (Chebotar et al., 2023) have been proposed to address these limitations, particularly
125   in offline settings.
126
      Anomaly Detection Anomaly Detection (AD) is the task of identifying samples that deviate signifi-
127   cantly from the majority of the data, often signaling irregular, fake, rare, or fraudulent observations
128   (Wang et al., 2019). Particularly, semi-supervised AD is defined as the task of detecting samples that
129   are out of distribution by using only normal samples in the training dataset. To estimate the normal
130   distribution and detect exceptional samples from that distribution, a wide range of models is available,
131   spanning from statistical to deep-learning based AutoEncoder ((Ruff et al., 2018), (Zong et al., 2018))
132   Transformer (Xu, 2021), and flow-based(Zhou et al., 2024). These methods have been improved to
133   handle various problem of each data types, including images, video, and time-series data.
134   Given the dimension reduction capabilities and generalizability of AutoEncoders in handling the
135   varying space sizes of reinforcement learning datasets, we select two models for the AD module
136   to capture distributional information: Deep SVDD ((Ruff et al., 2018)) and DAGMM ((Zong et al.,
137   2018)).
138
      Deep SVDD Deep SVDD (Ruff et al., 2018) is a deep learning-based extension of the classical
139   Support Vector Data Description method.(Tax & Duin, 2004) Unlike SVDD, which uses hand-crafted
140   kernels such as the Gaussian kernel, Deep SVDD learns the appropriate feature space through a deep
141   neural network with an AutoEncoder architecture. The goal of Deep SVDD is to find the smallest
142   hypersphere that encloses most of the normal data in the feature space by mapping inputs through the
143   model.
144
      DAGMM Similar to Deep SVDD, DAGMM also utilizes an AutoEncoder to encode data into a
145   feature space.(Zong et al., 2018) However, unlike Deep SVDD, which seeks to find the optimal
146   hypersphere for normal data, DAGMM estimates the distribution of data in the latent space by
147   utilizing a Gaussian Mixture Model.

      3.2   O FFLINE R EINFORCEMENT L EARNING
172
173   In real-world applications, interacting with the environment can often be risky, expensive, and
174   time-consuming. To address these challenges, Offline Reinforcement Learning (Offline RL)
175   has been developed, which leverages a pre-collected dataset to learn an optimal policy without
176   additional environment interaction. The pre-collected dataset consists of multiple trajectories
177   τ = (s0 , a0 , r0 , s1 , a1 , r1 , ...).
178   However, the lack of interaction with the environment makes it difficult for the agent to explore
179   new state-action pairs, leading to the domain shift problem, which results in a sub-optimal policy.
180   Additionally, standard off-policy algorithms often perform poorly in the offline setting, leading to a
181   need for new methods. Many approaches focus on mitigating the overestimation problem caused by
182   out-of-distribution actions through value function or policy constraints, uncertainty-based methods,
183   or model-based approaches. In this paper, we aim to address this issue using anomaly scores from
184   independently trained anomaly detection module.
185
186   4     R EINFORCEMENT L EARNING WITH A NOMALY D ETECTION (RLAD)
187
188   In this paper, we propose addressing the Offline RL problem through the lens of Anomaly Detection,
189   which we refer to as Reinforcement Learning with Anomaly Detection (RLAD). Offline Reinforce-
190   ment Learning encounters the overestimation problem primarily when the agent bootstraps actions
191   that are not present in the training dataset. As a result, the accumulation of bootstrapping errors can
192   significantly degrade performance.
193   To address the issue of out-of-distribution actions being bootstrapped, we provide an anomaly score
194   as guidance to the agent to indicate how far a data point is from the distribution of the training dataset.
195   Specifically, anomaly scores are used as weights for the objective functions of both the Actor and
196   Critic. Our approach has empirically shown promising results.
197
198   4.1   W HY A NOMALY D ETECTION ?
199
200   As mentioned earlier, previous works in offline reinforcement learning algorithms often solve the
201   overestimation problem by imposing constraints on value functions or policies. However, these
202   methods can lead to overly conservative value estimates or overly restrictive action spaces, resulting
203
      in sub-optimal policies. Moreover, approaches based on ensemble or dropout-based uncertainty
      measures, being suggested to solve this problem, have inevitable problem of high computational cost
204
      or inaccurate estimation of uncertainty.
205
206   In contrast, Anomaly Detection models are effective at detecting out-of-distribution data with re-
207   spect to the normal training dataset. These models typically determine whether data points are
208   in-distribution or out-of-distribution based on anomaly scores.
209   In this reason, we propose that anomaly scores can serve as efficient and effective guidance for the
210   agent to determine whether a given action is out-of-distribution, helping the agent adjust its policy
211   accordingly.

      4.3   T RAINING THE RL AGENT
247
248   Using the previously trained Anomaly Detection model, we compute the anomaly score for each
249   state-action pair. This score is then used to weight the value function training and policy optimization.
250   Since we want the agent to focus more on data points that are closer to the training distribution, the
251   weight should increase as the anomaly score decreases. A higher weight indicates a lower likelihood
252   of being OOD, encouraging more learning. Conversely, a lower weight indicates a higher likelihood
253   of being OOD, reducing its impact on learning.
254   This approach avoids imposing strict penalties or limiting the agent to the training distribution,
255   effectively mitigating the overestimation problem caused by OOD data while still allowing for
256   accurate estimation of OOD actions. The overall algorithm can be summarized as follows:
257
258         1) Calculate Anomaly Score: Use the trained Anomaly Detection model to calculate the
259
               anomaly score for the next state-action pair. Since the agent only queries the next action, we
               need to train the network based on the next state-action pair.
260
261         2) Compute Weight: Determine the weight from pretrained Anomaly Detection model as
262            follows:
263
                                       weight(s, a) := f (anomaly score(s, a))
264            where f (·) is a non-negative, monotonically decreasing function bounded within the range
265
               of anomaly score(·, ·).The specific form of f (·) may vary depending on the model used to
               compute the anomaly score. In our implementation, we use f (x) = x1 for Deep SVDD and
266
               f (x) = sigmoid(−x) for DAGMM.
267
268
            3) Weighted Q-Function Training: Train the Q-function using the computed weights with the
               following objective function:
269
                          ∇θ L(θ) = E[weight(s′ , a′ )(Qθ (s, a) − r − Q∗ (s′ , a′ ))∇θ Qθ (s, a)]

      5.4      C ONCLUSION
457
458   By analyzing Q differences, we demonstrate that our model which utilizes Anomaly Detection(AD)
459   models, effectively mitigates the overestimation of OOD samples compared to prior offline RL
460   algorithms, also do not estimate Q-values overly conservative. Moreover, in the D4RL bench-
461   marks, our model achieves near state-of-the-art performance across various environments, for several
462   combinations of Reinforcement Learning algorithms and Anomaly Detection models. These exper-
463   iments highlight the potential of integrating Anomaly Detection modules to detect OOD samples
464   and leveraging their quantitative anomaly scores as guidance to enhance performance. Because of
465
      the independence of AD module, this allows for the flexible integration of various models without
      imposing explicit constraints on policy or value functions, facilitating the exploration of optimal
466
      combinations between various branches of Anomaly Detection and powerful RL algorithms.
467
468   For future work, we aim to find the way how to choose the best combination of an Anomaly Detection
469   module and a Reinforcement Learning algorithm according to the characteristic of the dataset or
470   the environment. This research will enable more effective selection of an anomaly detection model
471
      and a reinforcement learning algorithm. We believe that our work provides a deeper empirical
      understanding of the relationship between accurate Q-value estimation and overall performance
472
      across various environments and RL algorithms. Additionally, we see potential for our approach to be
473
      further developed, much like data type-specific AD research in areas such as images and time series.

      A.3   E XPERIMENT D ESIGN : A NALYSIS OF WEIGHT AND Q DIFFERENCE
703
704   In 5.1, our experiment procedure is as the following: we train the Anomaly Detection model and train
705   two agents based on SAC which are in online setting and offline setting, respectively.
706
707         1. Offline Learning and Proxy for Optimal Q-Value: Online Learning To estimate Q∗ (s, a),
708            we utilized the critic network of a Soft Actor-Critic (SAC) model trained with a large amount
709            of online data as a proxy for the optimal policy and the optimal policy evaluation network
710
               (critic).
711         2. Sampling and calculating Q difference Afterward, we trained both our proposed model
712            and the baseline model, CQL, in an offline setting. We then compared the difference between
713            the Q(s, a) values obtained from the critic networks of both offline models with the Q∗ (s, a)
714
               values from the online SAC critic, visualizing these differences in a graph(2).
715
716
                              Qdifference = (f (anomaly score(s, a)), Q∗ (s, a) − Q(s, a))
717         3. Sampling Dood Furthermore, to analyze the anomaly scores of OOD actions and the
718            differences in Q-values, we extracted an OOD dataset,Dood , using Autoencoder(AE) and
719            Monte Carlo Dropout(MC-Dropout). For ensuring the objectivity of test, the extraction of
720            Dood is performed independently of both the offline dataset D and the Anomaly methods.
721            Detailed procedure is:
722               • Training the autoencoder model with dropout layers using the normal set D.
723               • For each data in the training dataset, calculate the variance of the reconstruction error
724                 with Monte Carlo(MC) Dropout. Set the threshold as 10 times of the maximum of the
725                 variance of the reconstruction error within the training dataset.
726               • With the random policy, calculate the variance of the reconstruction error for each state,
727                 action pairs in an episode with MC dropout. If the variance exceeds the threshold, we
728                 classify that state-action pair as out-of-distribution data and include it in the out-of-
729                 distribution dataset.
730            Each D and Dood is visualized by TSNE(Van der Maaten & Hinton, 2008) for the high and
731            varying dimensionality of each environment(5)
732
733   As you can see in the figure 4, both CQL and our model estimate Q-values accurately in the training
734   dataset. Meanwhile, in the out-of-distribution, our model estimates Q-values more accurate than
735   CQL, that is Q values of the most of data in the out-of distribution are close to 0. This result shows
736
      that our model effectively mitigates Q values and estimates accurately.
