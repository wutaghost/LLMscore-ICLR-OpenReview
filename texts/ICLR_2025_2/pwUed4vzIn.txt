011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Dynamic human movement necessitates a dynamic representation of the body.
014            The mechanisms underlying the initiation, development, and maintenance of such
015            representations can provide a biological perspective to developing more flexible
016
               representations within computational agents. Taking inspiration from the prenatal
               twitches shown to initiate the human neuromotor representation, we question how
017
               these same twitches, present throughout development, may also facilitate sub-
018
               sequent motor adaptation. Across three experiments, we examine the influence
019            twitches, as a form of intrinsic behavioral variability, may have in facilitating mo-
020            tor adaptation to novel situations. In a series of simulated reaching tasks, we
021            trained agents to reach targets while overcoming behavioral, physiological, and
022            neurological changes. Overall, we found evidence that agents exposed to intermit-
023            tent behavioral variability outperformed their counterparts, showing greater neural
024            weight variability, indicative of greater exploration. Taken together, this work pro-
025            vides a biologically plausible computational framework for flexible representation
026            development.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Dynamic representations of the human body are essential for adapting to the dynamic internal
032   changes and external stimuli we experience every day. Our bodies change dramatically as we
033
      develop, growing and lengthening through adolescence, where at the same time, we are tasked
      with learning a multitude of novel skills (i.e., walking, holding, driving). Similarly, as we progress
034
      through adulthood, we again may face changes and challenges, albeit typical bodily shrinkage or, for
035
      some, the dramatic loss of a limb or degradation of the mind. Despite this, humans show an affinity
036   for adapting to these changes. Much of this may be due, in part, to the dynamic representations we
037   build about our bodies throughout development (Wang et al., 2022). How these representations form
038   and change in response to novel stimuli, however, is still unclear.
039
      Traditionally, the human motor mapping was thought to be a static two-dimensional somatotopic
040
      representation of the body, mapping specific regions of the body (e.g., body, leg, face, hips) to spe-
041
      cific parts of the cortical folds of the motor cortex (Penfield & Boldrey, 1937). An abundance of de-
042   velopmental biology evidence has supported this dogma, pointing to myoclonic twitches as the cat-
043   alyst for this representation (Blumberg et al., 2013b; Chehade & Gharbawie, 2023; Gentilucci et al.,
044   1989; Grodd et al., 2001; Inácio et al., 2016; Petersson et al., 2003; Sokoloff et al., 2020). During
045   human gestation, as motorneurons differentiate, innervating the skeletal muscles, these neurons be-
046   gin to spontaneously fire, cascading rhythmic action potentials along nearby muscle cells (Blumberg
047   et al., 2013a; Thomason et al., 2018). Through mechanisms unknown, these myoclonic twitches, a
048   form of spontaneous muscle activation (SMA), develop a spatiotemporal organization that can be
049   seen as clustered twitches within body areas of ethological significance (Blumberg et al., 2013a).
050
      These same twitches, though lessened in frequency and intensity postnatally, persist throughout the
      lifespan, apparent during periods of rest. How these twitches play a subsequent role in our motor
051
      representation postnatally remains a topic of debate (Sokoloff et al., 2020).
052
053   Despite this evidence for a static, spatially-dependent, somatotopic motor mapping, research has also
      begun to show evidence that the mapping is more dynamic than previously assumed. In a follow-


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      up to the foundational study supporting a somatotopic representation of the body, evidence showed
055   that the same regions of the motor cortex produced multi-jointed and stereotype actions when stimu-
056   lated for ecologically valid periods, contrasting the single-jointed actions seen previously (Graziano,
057   2016). Similar multi-jointed actions were found longitudinally as well. In a series of studies ob-
058   serving infant-rat behaviors and their neural correlates, evidence showed developmental changes
059   in the mapping between neurons and actions. Specific neurons in the rats that elicited twitch-like
060   movements at eight to ten weeks (8-10) old, mirroring a somatotopic representation, now, by twelve
061   weeks (12), elicited action-oriented movements (Dooley & Blumberg, 2018). Similar sensory repre-
062   sentational changes were noted as well in human infants, several months postnatal (Dall’Orso et al.,
063
      2021). This culminating evidence has led researchers to view our motor representation as more dy-
      namic than originally presumed, developing initially as somatotopic mappings of the body followed
064
      by a more ethological mapping of the body shortly after birth. The mechanistic underpinnings that
065
      could initialize, update, and maintain such a dynamic representation, however, remain a topic of
066   contention.
067
068   While it has become more widely agreed upon that the human motor mapping represents both a
069
      somatotopic and ethological representation of the body, there remains a lack of understanding as
      to how the representational developmental trajectory influences subsequent behavioral adaptations
070
      to novel stimuli. Emerging evidence has led us to investigate the benefit of intrinsic behavioral
071
      variability (IBV), such as SMAs, in representational maintenance and flexibility for skill learning
072   (Kuebrich & Sober, 2015; Leitão & Gahr, 2024; Sokoloff et al., 2020). Just as SMAs have been
073   argued to initiate the somatotopic motor representation prenatally, we hope to explore how inject-
074   ing variable behavior into a system throughout training may also serve to improve representational
075   organization and subsequent performance. As such, we hypothesize that IBV facilitates a flexible
076   representation of an agent’s behavioral repertoire and self, encouraging adaptation throughout train-
077   ing. To address this hypothesis, we explored training agents in various adaptation tasks, injecting
078   behavioral variability in three (3) different intervals throughout training:
079
080
081   1.1   T RAINING H YPOTHESIS [H0]
082
083   This agent will be given no IBV, building on neurological and computational evidence by Graziano
084   and colleagues showing that what initially appeared globally as a somatotopic representation of
085   the body, may simply be an amalgam of ethological actions (Aflalo & Graziano, 2006; Graziano,
086   2016; Meier et al., 2008). As such, this hypothesis argues that learning across a diverse array of
087   environments will provide sufficient training for later adaptation.
088
089
090   1.2   P RE -T RAINING IBV H YPOTHESIS [H1]
091
092   This agent will simulate IBV before training, building on neurological theories that the human motor
093   representation is initialized somatotopically prenatally via SMAs but is subsequently overridden by
094   ethological actions. In contrast to the H0 Theory, this theory argues that flexible representations
095   must be initialized by variable behaviors, as evidenced by Blumberg and colleagues (Thomason
096
      et al., 2018).
097
098
099   1.3   I NTERMITTENT IBV H YPOTHESIS [H2]
100
101   This agent will simulate variable behaviors before and throughout training, building on arguments
102   that post-natal SMAs may play a role similar to that of their prenatal counterparts (Blumberg et al.,
103   2013b; Sokoloff et al., 2020). As such, this hypothesis argues that in addition to initializing the rep-
104
      resentation, the interspersed variable behaviors improve a representation’s flexibility by preventing
      overtraining on a context-specific action, facilitating organization throughout training.
105
106   We will compare these agents of various levels of IBV to assess their impact on overall performance.
107   In doing so, we plan to provide computational evidence that IBVs, such as SMAs, play a pivotal role
      in neuromotor adaptation.

110   All simulations were run in Python, using PyBullet for the physics simulations and PyTorch for the

113   2.1   E NVIRONMENT
114
115   The agent was placed in an empty environment with a floor. Depending on the task, one or more
116   objects may appear at a specified distance from the agent and are regarded as targets that the agent
117   must move toward. Gravity was set at −9.8 m/s2 along the z-axis.

123   joint’s angle of rotation, relative to the parent link, and current velocity serve as the inputs into the

137   2.3   N EURAL A RCHITECTURE
138
139   Using PyTorch, we initialize a fully interconnected feed-forward neural network composed of eight
140
      (8) inputs and eight (8) outputs corresponding to the four (4) joints and their respective velocities.
      The network included a single (1) hidden layer composed of rectified linear unit action function
141
      nodes. The number of nodes in the hidden layer were manually changed depending on the com-
142
      plexity of the experiment (see below). We trained the network on various tasks using unsupervised
143   and supervised learning (see Algorithm 1). Backpropagation was optimized via Adaptive Moment
144   Estimation (Kingma & Ba, 2017).
145
146   2.4   I NTRINSIC B EHAVIORAL VARIABILITY M ODEL
147
148   The goal of the Intrinsic Behavioral Variability (IBV) model was to inject variable behavior into the
149   agent’s representation, mirroring prenatal SMAs. Similar to evidence that prenatal SMAs initiate the
150   human somatotopic representation within humans (Thomason et al., 2018), we designed the model
151   for self-identification of the agent. We argue that this model, in building a representation of the self
152
      rather than of goal-directed motor actions, can provide variable behavior to the neural network while
      also mirroring the theorized neurological function of building somatotopic representations. Using
153
      unsupervised learning, backpropagating a loss value between the joint angle and velocity input and
154
      the joint angle and velocity output via a mean squared error, this model works to build representa-
155   tions of its motor system, correlating its desired outputs with its inputs. This framework is based on
156   work also using unsupervised learning mechanisms to examine self-organization (Petersson et al.,
157   2003).
158

160
161   The goal of the Reaching model was to train the agent to reach a given target, mirroring the formation
      of ethological action representations through exploration of the environment. To do so, this model

206   3     E XPERIMENT 1: N OVEL S KILL L EARNING
207
208   From birth, humans are tasked with learning a plethora of new skills; we question how intrinsically
209   variable behaviors may benefit motor skill learning. In this task, we looked at how well each agent
210   performed when learning a novel skill.

212   3.1   TASK
213
214   For this task (see Figure 2), we used the Reaching model to train agents to reach for targets within
215   the agent’s vicinity. Over the first six hundred (600) epochs, agents reached for three (3) targets,
      each randomly selected to appear at a specific distance equidistant from the agent. Then, for two


                                                         4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      hundred (200) epochs the agents continuously trained to reach for a novel target in a novel location
217   also equidistant from the agent that it had never trained on prior. Finally, for two hundred (200)
218   epochs, the agents returned to training on the original three (3) targets appearing randomly. We
219   wanted to examine the agent’s ability to learn a diverse array of skills, learn a novel one, and then
220   relearn the prior skill. Each epoch allowed for a maximum of one thousand (1000) timesteps, ending
221   earlier if the agent reached the target within the timestep window. For each epoch, at each timestep,
222   the agents’ end effector position, the loss error, and the neural network weight matrix were recorded.

224   start of training for one (1) extended epoch of 10,000 timesteps, to mimic the prenatal period. For
225   H2, as this hypothesizes that IBVs encourage flexibility when repeatedly trained, every hundred
226   (100) epochs of target-reaching included one (1) epoch of training on the IBV model, mimicking
227   the postnatal period.

      To assess possible performance differences between each agent, each agent was run twenty-five (25)

250   3.2   A NALYSIS
251
252
      To analyze possible differences between the performance of the agents, we looked at their average
      behavioral performance on the reaching task and their neural weight changes over twenty-five (25)
253
      runs for robustness.
254
255   To assess the agents’ behavioral performance, we averaged each agent’s performance (i.e., the num-
256   ber of timesteps it took for agents to reach the target) over the twenty-five (25) runs for each reaching
257   epoch over time. We then compared the overall performance of the three (3) agents using ANOVA
258
      and a post-hoc Tukey’s HSD test, if necessary.
259   To assess agents’ neural network differences, we averaged the agents’ weight matrices at each epoch
260   of training. We then performed principal component analysis (PCA) on the matrices to reduce the
261   dimensionality of the data for insight into neural weight changes in variability at various points in
262   training. We compared agents’ variability using ANOVA and the Mann-Whitney U Test.

413   6     D ISCUSSION
414
415   In this study, we examined the impact intrinsic behavioral variability plays in facilitating a flexible
416   and dynamic representation. Across three (3) experiments we tested agents of varying behavioral
417
      variability, examining the influence IBV may have on overall behavioral performance while simul-
      taneously observing the underlying neural representational changes involved. In Experiment 1, we
418
      tested the influence of IBV in adapting to external changes: in learning a novel motor skill. We
419
      found that agents with intermittent IBV outperformed those with less persistent IBV, evidence that
420   persistent IBV may facilitate a representation more flexible to learning novel motor tasks. This is
421   especially true when observing each agent’s performance when learning to reach the novel target
422   and then returning to the original three (3) targets. In the Intermittent IBV Hypothesis [H2]’s be-
423   havioral performance (see Figure 2), there were distinct spikes in performance, most likely due to
424   the intermittent IBV training. When compared to its significantly higher representational variability,
425   it can be argued that the IBV, though in the short term reduced performance, encouraged broader
426   exploration of the environment, increasing overall performance.
427   In Experiments 2 and 3, we tested the influences of IBV in adapting to amputation and neural
428   deficit. We again found H2 outperforming its counterparts. Network analyses showed higher rates
429   of representational variability in H2 pre- and post-deficit, evidence that H1 may have overfitted to
430   a suboptimal solution while H2’s continued IBV encouraged the discovery of more optimal (i.e.,
431   faster) solutions. We note, however, that though H2 performance outpaced H1, the neural weight
      variability by the end of training between both agents were more closely similar. It can be argued


                                                        8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      that H2’s persistent IBV training gave it a behavioral advantage throughout but, with time, enough
433   training on a task could elicit similar neural weight variability and solution discovery. Overall, we
434   showed strong computational evidence that IBV plays a fundamental role in facilitating a motor
435   representation by encouraging adaptation to behavioral, physiological, and neurological changes,
436   supporting neuromotor developmental theory.
437
      When taken in the context of human embodied development, this paper’s results align strongly with
438
      our evolving understanding of human neuromotor development. Numerous papers have begun to
439   show evidence that the motor cortex’s representation of the body is not only more dynamic but also
440   more complex than previously imagined (Berlot et al., 2020; Ejaz et al., 2015; Gordon et al., 2023;
441   Graziano, 2016; Makin & Krakauer, 2023). The organization, and developmental trajectory, how-
442   ever, have yet to be fully described. Our work seeks to formalize this representational trajectory. We
443   argue that, prenatally, IBVs such as SMAs serve to initiate a representation of the body, as argued
444   by Blumberg and colleagues (Thomason et al., 2018). This representation postnatally, however, has
445   been shown to change dramatically (Dooley & Blumberg, 2018). As actions are learned and incor-
446   porated within the motor cortex (Graziano, 2016), these same SMAs, now present during postnatal
447
      rest (Sokoloff et al., 2020), serve as a means of “resetting” our representation towards its initial state.
448   The interweaving of action representations and self-organizing representations prevent any singular
449   actions from being prioritized within the representation, encouraging a more flexible and dynamic
450   mapping. This would support evidence that behavioral entrenchment in an action does not neces-
451   sarily alter neural representations of the individual motor units (Beukema et al., 2019; Beukema &
452   Verstynen, 2018). Heavily trained actions become localized rather than globalized. In Experiment 1,
453
      simulations with intermittent IBV not only learned novel tasks faster, but also recovered previously
      learned skills faster, evidence in favor of SMAs playing a role in maintaining prior skill knowledge,
454
      while being able to efficiently learn new ones. Experiments 2 and 3 further support this claim, show-
455
      ing better adaptation to ingrained representations of the agent’s internal configurations when trained
456   with IBV. Together, this work provides computational evidence that SMAs play a fundamental role
457   in facilitating a motor representation of both the body’s form and function, encouraging adaptation
458   throughout development.
459
      This work also aligns strongly with the ongoing developmental robotics literature on action gen-
460
      eration. Another form of SMA, motor babbling, the seemingly incoherent attempt at vocalization
461
      by infants is a commonly studied phenomenon in robotics (Aoki et al., 2016; Bullock et al., 1993;
462   Caligiore et al., 2008; Mahoor et al., 2017). Researchers have used the concept, of eliciting random
463   movements within robots, to build an inverse kinematics model of its environment. These same
464   models have been used for self-modeling; and when iteratively used, have been shown to allow
465   adaptation to physiological augmentation (Bongard et al., 2006).
466
      One compelling aspect of Experiment 1’s task design was the potential for agents to recall previously
467
      learned skills after learning a novel one. The Intermittent IBV Hypothesis [H2] appeared to over-
468   come this issue of catastrophic interference, relearning and improving upon the initial task faster
469   than its counterparts. This result speaks to evidence within both neuroscience and connectionist
470   theory that the brain may form distributed representations for context-specific situations (Ellefsen
471   et al., 2015; McCloskey & Cohen, 1989; Plas et al., 2024), enabling flexible learning, retention,
472   and maintenance. Future questions to be explored include the impact of training schedules on the
473   representational efficiency of low-resourced agents.
474   A question that arises from this work, however, is whether we can consider IBV as simply noise
475   in the system. Research has shown that noise can sometimes elicit better long-term performance
476   by preventing overfitting (Gupta & Gupta, 2019). Though we include a supplemental experiment
477   (see Appendix: Figure 6) showing the Intermittent IBV Hypothesis [H2] outperforming the Training
478   Hypothesis [H0] with noise injected into the network (p < 0.05), we do not neglect the notion that
479   noise may play a role in human motor learning and adaptation. In hindsight, we consider IBV such
480   as SMAs as a form of noise within the motor cortex, degrading representations as to prevent over-
481   training. In future work, we plan to scale these simulations, exploring various augmentations such as
482
      ecologically-relevant gradual growth trajectories, and mapping behaviors to specific neural weight
      representations.

      7   C ONCLUSION
487
488
      How we represent our dynamic bodies defines how we interact with the dynamic world around us.
489   In this paper, we argue that our motor representations are just as dynamic, exploring the influence
490   intrinsic behavioral variability such as spontaneous muscle activations have in motor representa-
491   tions. Across three experiments, we show evidence that intrinsic behavioral variability facilitates
492   the initialization, maintenance, and updating of agents’ representation by encouraging adaptation to
493   behavioral, physiological, and neurological changes. Taken together, this work provides a biologi-
494   cally plausible computational framework for understanding the neurological and behavioral effects
495   intrinsic behavioral variability may have on the manifestation of human neuromotor adaptation.
