009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            AI, like humans, should be able to adapt and apply learned knowledge across di-
012            verse domains, such as computational models, mathematical/formal systems, and
013            programming languages to solve problems. Current AI training often relies on
014            existing systems, which limits its ability to generate original solutions or general-
015            ize across unfamiliar contexts. To address this, we propose a new computational
016            model along with a revised programming language. By challenging AI to write,
017
               analyze, and verify programs within these new frameworks, we aim to test and
               enhance AI’s problem-solving capabilities in a verifiable manner.

020   1   I NTRODUCTION
021
022   Humans have the capacity to learn and apply new knowledge across different domains, adapting
023   their understanding to novel contexts to solve problems. For example, one might start by learn-
024   ing basic arithmetic operations like addition and subtraction in a decimal system, then extend that
025   knowledge to binary arithmetic, vector and matrix operations in linear algebra, or even groups, rings,
026   and fields in abstract algebra. Similarly, learning a programming language on a specific computer
027   model enables the acquisition of additional languages and familiarity with different computational
028   models and programming paradigms. This adaptability extends to natural language understanding.
029   Artificial Intelligence (AI) should exhibit similar adaptive capabilities. If we only train and test
030   AI systems using existing programming languages, computer models, or mathematical frameworks,
031   they may perform well but merely by memorizing and applying known solutions. To push the
032   boundaries of AI’s capabilities, we must explore novel approaches. One such approach involves
033   defining and utilizing new abstract models and formal systems for training and testing AI, with a
034   focus on mathematics and computer science, where AI-generated results are easier to verify.
035   We propose a general research framework for this direction as the following:
036
037         1. Develop a new computational model M with some key differences from existing models.
038         2. Modify an existing programming language, such as C, to operate on M . Then, task AI
039            systems with writing, analyzing, verifying properties of programs written in this revised
040            language CM , such as their correctness and complexity in terms of time and space. They
041            are also requested to repair, improve, or optimize those programs.
042         3. Design a totally new programming language PM for M . Challenge AI systems to write,
043            analyze, verify, repair, and improve programs written in this language.
044         4. Develop a virtual machine for M , a compiler and/or interpreter for CM and PM capa-
045            ble of evaluating the AI-generated programs for M to ensure they adhere to the intended
046            specifications and perform as expected.
047
            5. Challenge AI systems to write compilers or interpreters for CM and PM , develop new
048            programming languages for M , and improve M with stronger capacities.
049
050   In this paper, we report our early achievements with this research framework. First, we proposed a
051   new computer model named Wuxing. Unlike real-world computers, Wuxing uses a decimal system
052   rather than a binary system. This design choice makes working with Wuxing more intuitive for
053   humans but less familiar for AI systems, which are predominantly trained with programming lan-
      guages and computer systems using binary. It has a working memory space of only 1,000 decimal


                                                        1
054
      digits (denoted D), making tasks such as simulating or verifying programs written for it easier. With
055
      limited available memory, it is also more challenging for AI systems to optimize their code.
056
057   We modified the programming language C to operate on Wuxing. This version of C uses decimal
      system and measures data size in D (decimal digits), not bits or bytes. It has new data types: digit
058
      for 0-9 and cent for 0-99. Existing data types char, int, and long are also redefined. For example, char
059   is for numbers from 0 to 999, while int and long numbers can have 6 and 12 digits, respectively. Input/output
060   format specifiers are also redefined,e.g., %d now specifies a digit, not an int.
061
      We have challenged the most advanced AI frontier models: GPT-4o, o1-mini, and o1-preview from OpenAI,
062   Gemini 1.5 Pro from Google, and Claude 3.5 Sonnet from Anthropic to write, analyze, or optimize programs
063   written for Wuxing using this C language. They have all failed spectacularly, even with relatively simple
064   programming problems. This suggests that we are on the right track to uncover the weaknesses of these AI
065   systems, and our results could help improve them in the future.
066   In the remaining of this paper, we will provide our prompts to introduce Wuxing and its C version to the AI
067   models. We then present our case studies on particular programming problems and analysis of the failure of
068   those AI models.

085

090      - char (3D): Range: 0-999, used to store standard ASCII characters (0-127) as well as

095       signed by default. Floating-point types (float and double) are not currently supported.

097       supported with a size of 3D. Arrays, structs, and unions are also supported.
098   3. Input/Output:
099      The scanf() and printf() functions utilize modified format specifiers:\%d digit, \%t cent,

299   4.1   H UMAN INTELLIGENCE AND PROBLEM SOLVING ABILITY
300
301   The most significant aspect of human intelligence is our problem-solving ability. This capacity encompasses
302   several distinct but interrelated processes, each contributing to a systematic approach toward addressing com-
      plex challenges.
303
304         1. We observe the world and ourselves to learn and acquire knowledge.
305
            2. We analyze problems by identifying objectives, constraints, inputs, and outputs.
306
307
            3. We adapt knowledge and prior experience to develop solutions.
308         4. We invent novel concepts, systems, or tools to address new challenges or unfamiliar situations.
309         5. We constantly strive to optimize our solutions, always seeking the fastest, smallest, most elegant,
310            most efficient, most effective, and easiest to understand. We want solutions capable of handling the
311            largest amount or widest range of data.
312
      Let’s discuss in more details. Human intelligence is clearly characterized by the ability to observe and learn
313   from the environment. Observation is the primary method of data collection, through which we gather the raw
314   information needed to build an understanding of both the external world and their internal states. For instance,
315   in computer science, the development of machine learning algorithms is rooted in our understanding of how
316   to observe patterns in large datasets, allowing for the acquisition of knowledge through statistical inference.
317
      Similarly, in mathematics, observing the relationships between variables in functions or systems provides the
      foundational understanding necessary for constructing models that can solve complex problems, such as those
318   in calculus or linear algebra.
319
320
      Once sufficient knowledge is acquired, human intelligence excels in analyzing problems by identifying objec-
      tives, constraints, inputs, and outputs. This analytical process parallels how software engineers approach algo-
321   rithm design. A well-defined algorithm requires clear identification of the problem’s input (e.g., a collection
322   of numbers to be sorted), the desired output (e.g., a sorted list), and the constraints (e.g., time or space com-
323   plexity). In mathematics, a similar process occurs in optimization problems where the objective is to find the
      minimum or maximum value of a function under specific constraints, such as boundary conditions or allowable


                                                             6
324
      variable ranges. The analytical phase is crucial for ensuring that solutions are both feasible and well-defined
325   within the problem space.
326
      Next, we demonstrate the ability to adapt existing knowledge and prior experience to derive new solutions.
327
      This adaptability is seen when engineers reconfigure established algorithms to solve novel problems or when
328   mathematicians extend known theorems to new domains. For example, dynamic programming, a method
329   used in computer science to solve problems by breaking them down into simpler subproblems, is built on the
330   principle of leveraging previous computations to improve efficiency in solving larger instances. In this sense,
331   the problem-solving process is iterative and cumulative, drawing from past successes to handle increasingly
      complex challenges.
332
333   Additionally, human intelligence is inventive in nature, capable of producing novel concepts, systems, and tools
334   to address problems that were previously unsolvable. The invention of new programming paradigms, such as
      object-oriented programming (OOP), is an example of how humans have invented new frameworks to better
335
      organize and manipulate data in ways that reflect real-world entities. In mathematics, the invention of entirely
336   new branches, such as non-Euclidean geometry, showcases the human ability to conceptualize solutions that
337   transcend conventional thinking and open up new areas of exploration.
338
      Finally, we continuously strive to optimize our solutions. In computer science, optimization is an integral part of
339   algorithm design, where developers aim to create solutions that are not only correct but also optimal in terms of
340   efficiency, resource usage, and simplicity. For example, algorithms like Quicksort or Merge sort are favored for
341   their optimal performance under different conditions, minimizing time complexity while maximizing efficiency
342   for various data sets. In mathematics, optimization techniques such as linear programming or gradient descent
      are employed to find the best possible solutions to problems, often subject to multiple constraints. The drive
343
      for optimization is inherently tied to human intelligence’s pursuit of elegance and effectiveness, whether in
344   reducing computational complexity, minimizing energy consumption, or maximizing accuracy.

347   4.2    F UTURE WORK ON TRAINING AND T ESTING AI FOR PROBLEM SOLVING
348
      As discussed, human intelligence’s problem-solving ability is multifaceted, encompassing observation, analy-
349   sis, adaptation, invention, and optimization. Each of these elements works in concert to allow humans to tackle
350   increasingly complex problems in fields such as computer science and mathematics. Whether solving equa-
351   tions, designing algorithms, or creating new theoretical frameworks, the drive to solve problems in the most
352   efficient and effective manner remains a defining characteristic of human intelligence.
353   We should aim to train AI with human-like problem solving capacities. However, our framework and experi-
354   ments presented in this paper show that current AI models are still far weaker than humans, even on simplest
355   problems when they are challenged in unfamiliar situations. We plan to continue developing this research
356
      framework by pursuing the following directions along with our framework discussed in Section 1.
357   Incorporating Object-Oriented Programming (OOP) Capabilities
358   A key extension of this research involves integrating object-oriented programming into our framework. By
359   defining novel virtual machine specifications that support OOP paradigms, we aim to challenge AI models
360   to write, analyze, and verify complex software architectures. OOP’s emphasis on encapsulation, inheritance,
361   and polymorphism introduces a rich set of design patterns and structural complexities. AI systems will be
362
      tasked with handling large-scale programs where class hierarchies, dynamic dispatch, and modularization must
      be correctly implemented or verified. For instance, AI models will need to manage multiple interdependent
363   objects, ensuring proper state transitions, behavior consistency, and code modularity in virtual environments
364   that reflect real-world systems like enterprise-level applications or simulations.
365
      Introducing Machine Programming Languages (Assembly) to Virtual Machines
366
367   The next advancement focuses on enhancing virtual machine specifications by incorporating low-level machine
368
      programming languages such as assembly. By moving closer to hardware-level abstractions, the challenge for
      AI models will shift toward managing memory, registers, and instruction sets directly. Large-scale programs
369   written in assembly pose unique difficulties, including manual memory management, instruction pipelining, and
370   control flow optimization, all of which AI must handle or verify. This direction seeks to evaluate AI systems’
371   ability to generate or audit efficient, bug-free code in environments where even minor errors in instruction
372   sequencing or register management can lead to significant system failures. For example, AI models may be
373
      tasked with optimizing assembly code for performance in embedded systems or real-time operating systems.
374   Designing New Formal or Computational Systems
375   A third focus is the design of new formal systems and computational models, particularly those inspired by
376   Turing Machines. These systems will serve as the foundation for exploring the theoretical limits of computation
377   within our framework. By designing and challenging AI models to write or verify programs in such systems, we
      can investigate AI’s capability to understand and manipulate abstract computational concepts, such as halting


                                                              7
378
      problems, recursive functions, or non-deterministic automata. In these formal settings, AI models must deal
379   with mathematical rigor and logic, developing or verifying programs that operate under strict formal constraints.
380   Examples include AI solving complex automata problems or verifying the correctness of algorithms designed
381   for theoretical models that extend beyond practical hardware implementations.
382   By pursuing these directions, we aim to push the boundaries of AI’s capabilities, both in practical application
383   and theoretical understanding, thereby deepening the integration and adoption of AI in problem solving tasks
384   like programming, proof assistance, software development, and beyond.

388
389   The development of comprehensive and meaningful evaluation methods for AI systems remains a critical chal-
390   lenge in the field. Traditional benchmarks often fail to capture the full spectrum of capabilities required for
391   general intelligence, leading researchers to propose more sophisticated evaluation frameworks.
392   Hendrycks et al. (2021b) demonstrated the potential of language models as few-shot learners, highlighting the
393   need for benchmarks that assess rapid adaptation to novel tasks. This work underscored the importance of
394   evaluating AI systems not just on static datasets, but on their ability to generalize and apply knowledge in
395
      diverse contexts.
396   Building on this, Srivastava et al. (2022) argued for moving beyond traditional benchmarks to evaluate AI
397   systems. They proposed new challenges that test for more general intelligence, emphasizing the need for tasks
398
      that require reasoning, abstraction, and transfer learning across domains.
399   Chollet (2019) introduced a formal measure of intelligence based on skill-acquisition efficiency. This frame-
