009                                                  ABSTRACT
010
011            Machine Learning Force Fields (MLFFs) are a promising alternative to expensive ab
012            initio quantum mechanical molecular simulations. Given the diversity of chemical
013            spaces that are of interest and the cost of generating new data, it is important to
014            understand how MLFFs generalize beyond their training distributions. In order to
015            characterize and better understand distribution shifts in MLFFs, we conduct diagnostic
016            experiments on chemical datasets, revealing common shifts that pose significant
017
               challenges, even for large foundation models trained on extensive data. Based on these
               observations, we hypothesize that current supervised training methods inadequately
018
               regularize MLFFs, resulting in overfitting and learning poor representations of
019
               out-of-distribution systems. We then propose two new methods as initial steps for
020            mitigating distribution shifts for MLFFs. Our methods focus on test-time refinement
021            strategies that incur minimal computational cost and do not use ab initio labels. The
022            first strategy, based on spectral graph theory, modifies the edges of test graphs to align
023            with graph structures seen during training. It can be applied to any existing pre-trained
024            model to mitigate connectivity distribution shifts. Our second strategy improves
025            representations for out-of-distribution systems at test-time by taking gradient steps using
026            an auxiliary objective. Inspired by previous test-time training works in computer vision,
027            we replace self-supervised objectives at test time with an objective that uses an efficient
028
               prior to address distribution shifts. Our test-time refinement strategies can reduce force
               errors by an order of magnitude on out-of-distribution systems, suggesting that MLFFs
029
               are capable of and can move towards modeling diverse chemical spaces, but are not
030
               being effectively trained to do so. Our experiments establish clear benchmarks for
031            evaluating the generalization capabilities of the next generation of MLFFs.

034   1    INTRODUCTION
035
      Understanding the quantum mechanical properties of atomistic systems is crucial for the discovery and
036   development of new molecules and materials. Computational methods like Density Functional Theory
037   (DFT) are essential for studying these systems, but the high computational demands of such methods
038   limit their scalability. Machine Learning Force Fields (MLFFs) have emerged as a promising alternative,
039   learning to predict energies and forces from reference quantum mechanical calculations. MLFFs are faster
040   than traditional ab initio methods, and their accuracy is rapidly improving for modeling complex atomistic
041   systems (Batzner et al., 2022; Schütt et al., 2017; Gasteiger et al., 2021; Batatia et al., 2022).
042   Given the computational expense of ab initio simulations for all chemical spaces of interest, there has been
043   a push to train larger and more accurate MLFFs, designed to work well across many different systems.
044   A goal here is developing models with general representations that accurately capture diverse chemistries
045   and eliminate the need to recollect data and retrain a model for each new system. To determine which
046   systems an MLFF can accurately describe and to assess the reliability of its predictions, it is important
047   to understand how MLFFs generalize beyond their training distributions. This understanding is essential
048   for applying MLFFs to new and diverse chemical spaces, ensuring that they perform well not only on
049   the data they were trained on, but also on unseen, potentially more complex systems.
050   We conduct an in-depth exploration to identify and understand distribution shifts. On example chemical
051   datasets, we find that state-of-the-art models struggle with common distribution shifts (Kovács et al.,
052   2023; Shoghi et al., 2023; Liao et al., 2024; Batatia et al., 2024) (see §2). These generalization challenges
053   suggest that current supervised training methods for MLFFs overfit to training distributions and do not
      enable MLFFs to generalize accurately. We demonstrate that there are multiple reasons that this is the case,

      2    DISTRIBUTION SHIFTS FOR MACHINE LEARNING FORCE FIELDS

134   2.1 PROBLEM SETUP AND BACKGROUND
135   MLFFs approximate molecule-level energies and atom-wise forces for a chemical structure by learning
136   neural network parameters from data. For a given a molecular structure, the input to the ML model consists
137   of two vectors: r∈ Rn×3, z∈ Rn×d, where n represents the number of atoms in the molecule, r are the
138   atomic positions, and z are the features of the atom, such as atomic numbers or whether an atom is fixed
139   or not. The model outputs Ê ∈R,F̂∈Rn×3, which are the predicted total potential energy of the molecule
140   and the predicted forces acting on each atom. The learning objective is typically formulated as a supervised
141   loss function, which measures the discrepancy between the predicted energies and forces and reference
142   energies and forces:
143                                                               X n

144                               L(F,E)=λE ||Eref − Ê||22 +λF        ||Fi,ref −F̂i||22,                       (1)
145                                                                  i=1
      where λE ,λF are hyperparameters.
146
147   Most modern MLFFs are implemented as graph neural networks (GNNs) Gilmer et al. (2017). Conse-
148   quently, Ê and F̂ are functions of z, r, and A∈Rn×n, the adjacency matrix representing the molecule:
149                                                  Ê,F̂=f(z,r,A)                                               (2)
150
      The atoms in the molecule are modeled as nodes in a graph, and edges are specified by the adjacency
151   matrix that includes connections to all atoms within a specified radius cutoff (Gasteiger et al., 2021; Batatia
152   et al., 2022). The adjacency matrix fully determines a graph structure, and thus defines the graph over
153   which the GNN performs its computation.
154
155   2.2 CRITERIA FOR IDENTIFYING DISTRIBUTION SHIFTS
156   In this section, we formalize criteria for identifying distribution shifts based on the features, labels, and
157   graph structures in chemical datasets. We define these distribution shifts broadly to encompass the diversity
158   of chemical spaces. We also note that distribution shifts can occur independently along each dimension:
159   e.g., a shift in features does not necessarily imply a shift in labels (see §E for details). This categorization
160   provides a framework for understanding the types of distribution shifts an MLFF may encounter (see
161   Fig. 1). This understanding motivates the refinement strategies described in §3 that take first steps at
      mitigating these shifts, providing insights into why MLFFs are susceptible to these shifts in the first place.

162
      Distribution Shifts in Atomic Features (z). Distribution shifts in atomic features z are the most
163   apparent and detrimental to the performance of current state-of-the-art models (see §4). This may involve
164   encountering a molecule with a new element at test time that was not present during training. For example,
165   a model trained on CO2 might be tested on SiO2 without having seen Si during training (see Fig. 1).
166   Although this might initially seem like an unreasonably hard task, we argue that a truly general machine
167   learning model for quantum chemistry should be capable of handling arbitrary elements and charges.
168
169   Distribution Shifts in Forces (F). An MLFF may also encounter a distribution shift in the force labels
170
      it predicts. A model trained on structures close to equilibrium, with low force magnitudes, might be tested
      on a structure with higher force norms. Fig. 1 shows an example of a tightly clustered H2 molecule, which
171
      leads to a force norm distribution shift.
172
173   Distribution Shifts in Graph Structure and Connectivity (A). Since many MLFFs are implemented
174   as GNNs, they may encounter distribution shifts in the graph structure defined by A. We refer to these as
175   connectivity distribution shifts because A determines the graph connectivity used by the GNN. Connectivity
176   distribution shifts are particularly common in molecular datasets, where one could encounter a benzene
177   ring at test time, despite only having trained on long acyclic structures. Fig. 1 provides an example of
178   a connectivity distribution shift, going from planar training structures to a tetrahedral geometry at test time.
179   We identify connectivity distribution shifts by analyzing the eigenvalue spectra of the graph Laplacian:
180                                                              1       1

183   rcutoff and 0 otherwise), and I is the identity. L has eigenvalues λ0,≤λ1,≤···≤λn−1, where λi ∈[0,2]∀i,

185   By comparing the eigenvalue distributions between two graphs, we determine whether they are structurally
186   different. While it’s possible to use a divergence measure over the eigenvalue distributions to characterize the
187   difference, we found that looking at the difference in the clustering around the 1 eigenvalue between graphs
188   serves as a practical heuristic. Most molecular datasets we studied had structures with a consistent fraction
189   of the eigenvalues clustered around 1, indicating mostly regular and well-connected graphs (see §3.1).

      Observed Distribution Shifts for Large Foundation Models. We contextualize the aforementioned
191
      distribution shifts by considering four large foundation models: MACE-OFF, MACE-MP, EquiformerV2,
192   and JMP (Kovács et al., 2023; Shoghi et al., 2023; Liao et al., 2024; Batatia et al., 2024) MACE-OFF
193   is a 4.7M biomolecules foundation model trained on 951k structures primarily from the SPICE dataset
194   (Eastman et al., 2023). The 15M parameter MACE-MP foundation model is trained on 1.5M structures
195   from the Materials Project (Deng, 2023). EquiformerV2 is a 150M parameter model trained on 100M+
196   structures from OC20 (Chanussot et al., 2021). The JMP model has 240M parameters and is trained on
197   100M+ structures from OC20, OC22, ANI-1x, and Transition-1x (Chanussot et al., 2021; Tran et al., 2023;
198   Smith et al., 2020; Schreiner et al., 2022). These models represent four of the largest open-source MLFFs
199   to date, and they have been trained on some of the most extensive datasets available. We focus on these
200
      models since their scale is designed for tackling broad chemical spaces.
201   We examine the generalization ability of MACE-OFF by testing it on 10k new molecules from the SPICEv2
202   dataset (Eastman et al., 2024) not included in the MACE-OFF training set. A molecule is defined as out-of-
203   distribution if it is more than 1 standard deviation away from the mean training data force norm or connectiv-
204   ity (with respect to the eigenvalue heuristic defined above §2.2), or if it contains a new element. Despite its
205   scale, MACE-OFF performs worse by an order of magnitude on out-of-distribution systems (see Fig. 2b).
206   We evaluate JMP on the ANI-1x (Smith et al., 2020) test set defined in Shoghi et al. (2023). Although
207   this test set does not have new elements, JMP also suffers predictably from force norm and connectivity
208   distribution shifts (see Fig. 2d).
209
      We focus on force norm distribution shifts for MACE-MP and EquiformerV2, since connectivity is more
210   uniform across bulk materials and catalysts, where atoms are packed tightly into a periodic cell. For
211   MACE-MP, we evaluate its performance directly on the entire MPTrj dataset. This model does not have
212   a clear validation set, as it was trained on all of the data to maximize performance (Batatia et al., 2024).
213   MACE-MP still clearly performs worse as force norms deviate from the majority of the training distribution
214   (see Fig. 2a). The performance deterioration would be more severe with a held-out test set. EquiformerV2
215   also struggles with high force norm structures when evaluated on the validation out-of-distribution set
      from OC20 (Chanussot et al., 2021) (see Fig. 2c).

      Observations. Training larger models with more data (for example with active learning (Vandermause
217   et al., 2020)) is one approach to address these distribution shifts. However, doing so can be computationally
218   expensive. Our diagnostic experiments also indicate that scale alone might not fully address distribution
219   shifts, as naively adding more in-distribution data does not help large models generalize better (see Fig. 2).
220   The diversity of chemical spaces makes it exceedingly difficult to know the exact systems that an MLFF will
221   be tested on a priori, making it challenging to curate the perfect training set. These observations lead us to
222   develop strategies that mitigate distribution shifts by modifying the training and testing procedure of MLFFs.
223   Importantly, these refinement strategies can be combined with any further architecture and data advances.

      3     UNDERSTANDING AND MITIGATING DISTRIBUTION
226
227
            SHIFTS WITH TEST-TIME REFINEMENT STRATEGIES FOR MLFFS
228
      Based on the generalization challenges for the large foundation models (see §2), we hypothesize that many
229
      MLFFs are severely overfitting to the training data, resulting in a failure to learn generalizable represen-
230
      tations. Building on our observations in §2 and to test this hypothesis, we develop two test-time refinement
231   strategies that also mitigate distribution shifts. We focus on test time evaluations, i.e., with access to test
232   molecular structures but without access to reference labels. First, by studying the graph Laplacian spectrum,
233   we investigate how MLFFs, and GNNs in general (Bechler-Speicher et al., 2024), tend to overfit to the
234   regular and well-connected training graphs. In §3.1, we address connectivity distribution shifts by aligning
235   the Laplacian eigenvalues of a test structure with the connectivities of the training distribution. Second,
236   we show that MLFFs are inadequately regularized, resulting in poor representations of out-of-distribution
237   systems. We incorporate inductive biases from a cheap physical prior using our pre-training and test-time
238   training procedure (§3.2) to regularize the model and learn more general representations, evidenced by
239
      smoother predicted potential energy surfaces. The effectiveness of these test-time refinement strategies,
      validated through extensive experiments in §4 and §C, may indicate that MLFFs are currently poorly
240
      regularized and overfit to graph structures seen during training, hindering broader generalization.

      As a heuristic for the distance function D, we characterize eigenvalue distributions by calculating the

304   of other distance metrics to future work. For further details and theoretical motivation, see §F.

306   graph, including existing foundation models like MACE-OFF (Kovács et al., 2023) and JMP (Shoghi
307   et al., 2023). This approach incurs minimal computational cost and can be done quickly even on a
308   CPU, requiring only eigenvalue computations. Our experiments show that this procedure virtually never
309   deteriorates performance, as one can always revert to the same radius cutoff used during training (see
310   §4). This refinement method addresses the source of connectivity distribution shifts and serves as a simple
311   and effective initial strategy for handling new connectivities.
312   3.2 TEST-TIME TRAINING USING CHEAP PRIORS
313   We further hypothesize that the current supervised training procedure for MLFFs can lead to overfitting,
314   leading to poor representations for out-of-distribution systems and jagged potential energy landscape
315   predictions (see Fig. 4b for an example on salicylic acid (Chmiela et al., 2017)). To address this, we propose
316   introducing inductive biases through improved training and inference strategies to smooth the predicted en-
317   ergy surfaces. The smoother energy landscape from the improved training indicates that the model may have
318   learned more robust representations, mitigating force norm, element, and connectivity distribution shifts.
319   We represent these inductive biases as cheap priors, such as classical force fields or simple ML
320   models. These priors can evaluate thousands of structures per second using only a CPU, making them
321   computationally efficient for test-time use. First, we describe our pre-training procedure, which ensures
322   the MLFF learns useful representations from the cheap prior. By leveraging these representations, we
323   can smooth the predicted energy landscape and mitigate distribution shifts by taking gradient steps with
      our test-time training (TTT) procedure.

366   4    EXPERIMENTS
367
368   We conduct experiments on chemical datasets to both identify the presence of distribution shifts and evaluate
369
      the effectiveness of our test-time refinement strategies to mitigate these shifts. In §4.1, we find distribution
      shifts on the SPICE dataset with the MACE-OFF foundation model (Eastman et al., 2023; Kovács et al.,
370
      2023). In §4.2, we explore extreme distribution shifts and demonstrate that our test-time refinement strategy
371
      enables stable simulations on new molecules, even when trained on a limited dataset of 3 molecules from the
372   MD17 dataset (Chmiela et al., 2017). Finally, in §C.4, we assess how our test-time refinement strategy can
373   handle high force norms in the MD22 dataset when the model is trained only on low force norms. Although
374   matching in-distribution performance remains a challenging open machine learning problem (Sun et al.,
375   2020; Gandelsman et al., 2022), our experiments indicate that test-time refinement strategies are a promising
376   initial step for addressing distribution shifts with MLFFs. The improvements from these test-time refinement
377   strategies also suggest that MLFFs can be trained to learn more general representations that are resilient
      to distribution shifts. Additional experiments with more models, datasets, and priors are provided in §C.

421
422   We investigate distribution shifts from the SPICE dataset to the SPICEv2 dataset (Eastman et al., 2023;
423   2024) by analyzing the MACE-OFF foundation model (Kovács et al., 2023). As shown in Fig. 5, Fig. 6,
424   and Fig. 7, we observe that despite being trained on 951k data points and scaled to 4.7M parameters,
425
      MACE-OFF experiences force norm, connectivity, and element distribution shifts when evaluated on 10k
      new molecules from SPICEv2 (Eastman et al., 2024). Any deviation from the training distribution, shown
426
      in gray, results in an increase in force error.
427
428   We evaluate the effectiveness of our test-time refinement strategies in mitigating these distribution shifts.
429   For the MACE-OFF model, we implement test-time radius refinement (RR) by searching over 10 different
430   radius cutoffs and selecting the one that best matches the training Laplacian eigenvalue distribution
431
      (see §3.1). We also train a GemNet-T model on the same training data used by MACE-OFF, using the
      pre-training, freezing and fine-tuning method described in §3.2, with the sGDML model as the prior
      (Chmiela et al., 2019). See D for more details.

      a test graph has a different connectivity than the training graphs. As a heuristic to describe connectivity,
455
      we calculate the percentage of Laplacian eigenvalues that are in [0.9,1.1], which is consistently around

461
462   Elemental Distribution Shifts. Unsurprisingly, MACE-OFF and GemNet-T perform poorly when they
463   encounter new elements at test time. Fig. 7 shows that test-time training can reduce errors on systems
464   with new elements, sometimes by a factor of 10 for specific molecules.
465
      While this is a challenging generalization task, we argue that achieving this should be a goal for a true
466
      chemistry foundation model, akin to first-principles methods that model the entire periodic table. Collecting
467
      more data for new elements is an option but can be prohibitively expensive, especially for atoms with many
468   electrons. TTT provides a better starting point and reduces the amount of data that needs to be collected.

      benchmark, where a model is trained on SPICE and evaluated on 10k new molecules from SPICEv2. The

      large MACE-OFF foundation model trains on 951k samples but still suffers from distribution shifts on the
473   new structures from SPICEv2. We also see that (1) the RR method mitigates connectivity distribution shifts
474   for MACE-OFF at minimal computational cost (see Tab. 1) and (2) using TTT with the GemNet-T model

483
      The ability of TTT and RR to mitigate distribution shifts supports the hypothesis that MLFFs easily overfit
484
      to training distributions, even with large datasets. By improving the connectivity and learning more general
485   representations of test molecules, RR and TTT help diagnose the specific ways in which MLFFs overfit.
      These experiments suggest that improved training strategies could help learn more general models.

      4.2   EVALUATING GENERALIZATION
510         WITH E XTREME D ISTRIBUTION S HIFTS: S IMULATING U NSEEN M OLECULES
511
512
      We establish an extreme distribution shift benchmark to evaluate the generalization ability of MLFFs on
      the MD17 dataset (Chmiela et al., 2017). We train a single GemNet-dT model (Gasteiger et al., 2021) on
513
      10k samples each of aspirin, benzene, and uracil. We then evaluate whether this model can simulate two
514
      new molecules, naphthalane and toluene, which were unseen during training. Next, we evaluate whether
515   TTT can address the distribution shifts to the new molecules. Using the same procedure outlined in §3.2,
516   we pre-train on the 3 molecules in the training set with the sGDML prior, then freeze the representation
517   model and fine-tune on the quantum mechanical labels. We then perform TTT before simulating the new
518   molecules (see §3.2). This is an extremely challenging generalization task for MLFFs due to the limited
519   variety of training molecules. Nevertheless, we believe that a model capable of accurately capturing the
520   underlying quantum mechanical laws should be able to generalize to new molecules.
521   We evaluate the stability of simulations over time by measuring deviations in bond length, following Fu
522   et al. (2023). We additionally calculate the distribution of interatomic distances h(r) to measure the quality
523   of the simulations. See §D for more details.
524
      Simulation Results. As shown in Fig. 8, TTT enables stable simulations of unseen molecules that
525   accurately reproduce the distribution of interatomic distances h(r). Without TTT, the GemNet-dT model
526   trained only on aspirin, benzene, and uracil is unable to stably simulate the new molecules and produces
527   poor h(r) curves. Even when we reduced the timestep by a factor of 5,000, the simulations without TTT
528   remained unstable. We also found that TTT enables stable NVE simulations (see §C.2). Given that GemNet-
529   dT + TTT can produce reasonable simulations without access to quantum mechanical labels of the new
530   molecules, test-time refinement methods could be a promising direction for addressing distribution shifts.

      5 CONCLUSION
533
      We have demonstrated that state-of-the-art MLFFs, even when trained on large datasets, suffer from
      predictable performance degradation due to distribution shifts. By identifying shifts in element types, force
534
      norms, and connectivity, we have developed methods to diagnose the failure modes of MLFFs. Our test-
535
      time refinement methods represent initial steps in mitigating these distribution shifts, showing promising
536   results in modeling and simulating systems outside of the training distribution. The success of these methods
537   provides insights into how MLFFs overfit, suggesting that while MLFFs are expressive enough to model
538   diverse chemical spaces, they are not being effectively trained to do so. This may indicate that training
539   strategies, alongside data and architecture innovations, will be important in improving MLFFs. Additionally,
      we have established benchmarks for evaluating the generalization ability of the next generation of MLFFs.

      A     RELATED WORK
811
812
      Distribution Shifts. There is a long line of literature studying distribution shifts in the machine learning
813   community, which we briefly summarize here. Sugiyama et al. (2007) demonstrated how to perform
814   importance weighted cross validation to perform model selection under distribution shifts. Methods have
815   been proposed to measure and improve the robustness of models to distribution shifts in images (Taori
816   et al., 2020; Zhao et al., 2022) and language (Zhang et al., 2019). Numerous methods have been proposed
817   to tackle distribution shifts including, but not limited to, techniques based on meta learning (Jeong & Kim,
818   2020) and ensembles (Zhou et al., 2021).
819   Recent work has also begun identifying generalization challenges with MLFFs. Deng et al. (2024) find that
820   MLFFs systematically underpredict energy surfaces, and that this underprediction can be ameliorated with a
821   small number of fine-tuning steps on reference calculations. Our experiments compliment the initial findings
822   of underestimation in their paper, and we also identify other types of distribution shifts, like connectivty
823   and atomic feature shifts. Our proposed test-time refinement solutions are also able to mitigate distribution
824   shifts without any reference data, and they provide insights into why MLFFs are unable to generalize.
825
826   Multi-Fidelity Machine Learning Force Fields. Previous work has explored training MLFFs using
827   multiple levels of theory. Jha et al. (2019), Gardner et al. (2024), and Shui et al. (2022) leveraged cheap
828   or synthetic data to improve data efficiency and accuracy. Ramakrishnan et al. (2015) popularized
829   the ∆-learning approach (Bogojeski et al., 2020), where a model learns to predict the difference
830
      between some prior and the reference quantum mechanical targets. Multi-fidelity learning generalizes
      ∆-learning by building a hierarchy of models that predict increasingly accurate levels of theory (Giselle
831
      Fernández-Godino, 2023; Vinod et al., 2023; Forrester et al., 2007; Heinen et al., 2024). Making predictions
832
      in the hierarchical multi-fidelity setting corresponds to evaluating a baseline fidelity level and then refining
833   this prediction with models that provide corrections to more accurate levels of theory in the hierarchy.
834
835   Our work differs from these works in several ways. We focus on developing training strategies that
836
      address distribution shifts. In contrast to prior multi-fidelity works, we learn representations from multiple
      levels of theory using pre-training, fine-tuning, and joint-training objectives. Rather than fine-tuning
837
      all the model weights like in Jha et al. (2019), Gardner et al. (2024), and Shui et al. (2022), we explore
838
      freezing and regularization techniques that enable test-time training. Our new test-time objectives update
839   the model’s representations when faced with out-of-distribution examples, improving performance on
840   out-of-distribution systems. Multi-fidelity approaches by themselves do not tackle the challenge of
841   transferring to new, unseen systems at test-time. Nevertheless, combining our training strategies with other
842   multi-fidelity approaches presents an interesting direction for future work.
843
844   Test-Time Training. The test-time training (TTT) framework adapts predictive models to new test
845   distributions by updating the model at test-time with a self-supervised objective Sun et al. (2020). Sun et al.
846   (2020) demonstrated that forcing a model to use features learnt from a self-supervised objective during the
847   main task allows the model to adapt to out-of-distribution examples by tuning the self-supervised objective.
848   Follow up work showed the benefits of TTT across CV and NLP, exploring a range of self-supervised
849
      objectives (Gandelsman et al., 2022; Jang et al., 2023; Hardt & Sun, 2024).
850
851   B    DETAILS ON TEST-TIME TRAINING (TTT)
852
853   We elaborate on the details of our proposed test-time training (TTT) approach.
854
855   Model setup. Our model consists of the representation model, the main task head, and the prior task
856   head, with parameters θR , θM , and θP respectively:
857
858         1. The representation model, θR , is designed to extract features useful for both the main and prior
859            task heads. These parameters can be trained on both the cheap data from the physical prior and
860            the expensive reference calculations. After pre-training, the representation parameters can be
861
               further refined through fine-tuning and test-time training.
862         2. The main task head, θM , predicts the energies and forces generated by DFT calculations. This
863            head specifically uses the high-accuracy, expensive quantum mechanical labels produced by
               DFT for training.

      Necessity of Proper Pre-training for Test-time Training. The goal of TTT is to adapt to out-of-
891
      distribution test samples using a self-supervised objective at test-time (Sun et al., 2020; Jang et al., 2023;
892   Gandelsman et al., 2022). In our case, we use the prior task loss LP as the test-time training objective,
893   making the model predict forces and energies labeled by the cheap physical prior. When an out-of-
894   distribution (OOD) sample is encountered at test-time, we can adapt our representation parameters, θR ,
895   using the prior. This update improves the features extracted from the OOD samples, which in turn smooths
896   the potential energy surface and improves the performance on the main task (see Fig. 9b). Importantly, naive
897   fine-tuning of the full pre-trained model (both θR and θM ) hinders the effectiveness of TTT. This is because
898   fine-tuning θR on the main task may cause these parameters to “forget” the features learned from the prior
899   during pre-training. If we adjust θR at test-time based solely on the prior targets, this could shift θR away
900
      from the representations that θM relies on to make predictions. Thus, for TTT to be successful, it is essential
      that the main task head depends on the features learned from the prior to make accurate predictions.
901
902
903   Notes on the Prior. Although the performance of TTT does improve with a more accurate prior (see
904   Fig. 9a), we note that even in cases where the prior is poorly correlated with the main task (like with the
905   EMT prior and OC20 in §C.5), TTT still provides benefits. This is because the prior is only used to learn
906   representations, and not to directly make predictions on the targets. This means that as long as training
907   on the prior yields good representations, it can be used for TTT.
908   We also argue that such a prior is in fact widely available. For instance, one could always train an
909   sGDML prior on the existing reference data. Alternatively, one could use a simple potential (like EMT or
910   Lennard-Jones). A different (cheaper) level of quantum mechanical theory can also be used. Alternatively,
911   as with prior TTT work in computer vision, a fully self-supervised objective (like atomic type masking
912   and reconstruction) could also be used. We leave explorations of more priors to future work.
913   It should be noted that using sGDML as the prior requires a few labeled examples to train the sGDML
914   model for the unseen molecule. We show that as few as 15 labeled examples are sufficient to tune the
915   prior and achieve good TTT results (see Fig. 9a). TTT also yields better results than fine-tuning directly
916   on these 15 samples, since the model severely overfits on the small number of samples. We also emphasize
917   that across the board, TTT performs better than the prior (see Tab. 14). In addition, the sGDML prior
      only works on one system, whereas the MLFF can model multiple systems.

      Limitations. Test-time training incurs extra computational cost, mainly due to the gradient steps taken at
919   test time. This cost is negligible compared to the overall training time of a model, and negligible compared
920   to the time it takes to run simulation with the model. Additionally, our instantiation of TTT requires access
921   to a prior. However, a suitable prior is almost always available since one can always use a widely applicable
922   analytical or semi-empirical potential.

1314   E    DETAILS ON DISTRIBUTION SHIFTS
1315
1316   We emphasize that element, force norm, and connectivity distribution shifts define “orthogonal” directions
1317   along which a shift can happen in the sense that they can each happen independently. In other words,
1318   a structure might have the same connectivity and similar force norms, but contain a new element. Similarly,
1319   for the SPICEv2 dataset, the distribution of connectivities is the same independent of force norm of the
1320   structure (see Fig. 14). This implies that one can observe a force norm shift while still seeing similar
1321   elements and connectivity.
1322   Additionally, we provide more details on how we diagnose distribution shifts for new molecules at test time.
1323
1324         1. Identifying distribution shifts in the atomic features z is straightforward: one can simply compare
1325            the chemical formula of a new structure to the elements seen during training.
1326         2. To diagnose force norm distribution shifts, we observe that although priors often have large abso-
1327            lute errors compared to reference calculations, force norms are actually highly correlated between
1328            priors and reference values (see Fig. 13 for an example from MD17). To determine whether
1329            a structure might be out-of-distribution with respect to force norms, the prior can be quickly
1330            evaluated at test time, and the predicted force norm can be compared to the training distribution.
1331
             3. Connectivity distribution shifts can be quickly identified by comparing graph Laplacian eigenvalue
1332            distributions. Based on our heuristic described in §3.1, we characterize graph connectivities by
1333            calculating the percentage of graph Laplacian eigenvalues that fall within the range of [0.9,1.1] for
1334            each molecule in a dataset. We then average this percentage across training molecules to represent
1335            the training distribution. We experimented with more involved versions of representing the
1336            training distribution, such as averaging probability density functions of eigenvalue distributions,
1337            but found that this made little difference since the connectivity was relatively uniform across
1338            the training molecules in SPICE, MD17, and MD22 (generally regular well-connected graphs).
1339            At test time, one can quickly calculate the percentage of eigenvalues that fall in [0.9,1.1] and
1340
                compare this to the training distribution to identify connectivity distribution shifts. We think it
                is an interesting direction for future research to further study distribution shifts in graph structure.
1341
1342
       We emphasize that our proposed methods for diagnosing distribution shifts are computationally efficient,
1343   and they do not require access to reference labels.

1349   et al. (2024), which states that GNNs tend to overfit to generally regular and well-connected training graphs.

1373

1374
1375   Theorem F.1 (Extrapolation to new graphs can fail (Bechler-Speicher et al., 2024)) Let f ∗ be a
1376   graph-less target function (it does not use a graph to calculate its output). In other words, f ∗(X,A) =
1377   f ∗(X), where X are node features and A is the adjacency matrix of a graph. There exist graph distributions
1378   P1 and P2, with node features drawn from the same fixed distribution, such that when learning a linear
1379   GNN with gradient descent on infinite data drawn from P1 and labeled with f ∗, the test error on P2 labeled
1380   with f ∗ will be ≥ 14 . In other words, the model fails to extrapolate to the new graph structures at test time.
1381
1382   Mapping this to MLFFs, theorem F.1 suggests that a GNN trained on specific types of molecular structures
1383
       (i.e., acyclic molecules) could fail to generalize to new connectivities at test time (i.e., a benzene ring).
1384
       Theorem F.2 (Extrapolation within regular graph distributions (Bechler-Speicher et al., 2024)) Let
1385
       DG be a distribution over r-regular graphs and DX be a distribution over node features. A model trained
1386
       on infinite samples from DG,DX and labeled by a graph-less target function f ∗ will have zero test error
1387   on samples drawn from DX ,DG′ (and labeled by f ∗), where DG′ is a distribution over r′-regular graphs.
1388
1389   In other words, generalizing across different types of regular graphs is easier for GNNs. Based on these
1390   theorems and our observation that many molecular datasets (MD17, MD22, SPICE) contain generally
1391   regular and well-connected graphs, we are motivated to find ways to make testing graphs look more like the
1392   training distribution (generally regular and well-connected) to help the models generalize. The observation
1393   that graphs for MLFFs are often generated by a radius cutoff led us to develop the RR method presented in
1394
       §3.1. See Fig. 15, which empirically shows that RR makes graphs more regular and brings them closer to
       the distribution of training connectivities, aligning with our theoretical intuition. We think it is an interesting
1395
       direction for future research to continue exploring the theoretical properties of graph structure distribution
1396
       shifts.
