010
011
               When using agent-task datasets to enhance agent capabilities for Large Language
012            Models (LLMs), current methodologies often treat all tokens within a sample
013            equally. However, we argue that tokens serving different roles—specifically,
014            reasoning tokens versus boilerplate tokens (e.g., those governing output for-
015            mat)—differ significantly in importance and learning complexity, necessitating
016            their disentanglement and distinct treatment. To address this, we propose a novel
017            Shuffle-Aware Discriminator (SHAD) for adaptive token discrimination. SHAD
018            classifies tokens by exploiting predictability differences observed after shuffling
019            input-output combinations across samples: boilerplate tokens, due to their repeti-
020
               tive nature among samples, maintain predictability, whereas reasoning tokens do
               not. Using SHAD, we propose the Reasoning-highlighted Fine-Tuning (RFT)
021
               method, which adaptively emphasizes reasoning tokens during fine-tuning, yield-
022
               ing notable performance gains over common Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT).

026   1    I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Recently, there has been a surge of enthusiasm in researching Agents based on Large Language
029   Models (LLMs) (Weng, 2023; Wang et al., 2024), with the aim of achieving human-level artificial
030   intelligence or beyond. Despite LLMs showcasing remarkable capabilities in various areas, they
031   have not inherently demonstrated strong agent capabilities, such as multi-step reasoning (Wei et al.,
032   2022; Yao et al., 2023; Qiao et al., 2024) and tool use (Qin et al., 2024; Schick et al., 2023; Liu
033
      et al., 2024; Patil et al., 2023). This shortfall has directed significant attention toward incorporating
      datasets tailored for agent tasks to enhance the agent capabilities of LLMs (Chen et al., 2023; Zeng
034
      et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024b). These datasets offer structured examples of standard reasoning
035
      chains for solving agent tasks (Chen et al., 2024b; Qin et al., 2024), enabling LLMs to learn from
036   them and thereby enhance their agent capabilities.
037
038   When leveraging these datasets to bolster LLMs’ agent capabilities, existing research often treats
039
      all tokens within a sample equally (Chen et al., 2023; Zeng et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024c; Qin
      et al., 2024). However, we argue that these tokens could differ substantially in learning difficulty
040
      and importance. Given the standardized structure of the data, tokens within a sample can be divided
041
      into two categories as depicted in Figure 1: 1) boilerplate tokens, which include format tokens that
042   constrain the output structure, and template-connecting tokens that serve as standard transitional
043   phrases for reasoning, such as “Based on the user’s request... By doing so... This way...”; and
044   2) reasoning tokens, which provide sample-specific reasoning information crucial for task solving.
045   Boilerplate tokens are distinctly less critical for task solving compared to reasoning tokens and are
046   easier to learn due to their repetitive nature across many samples.
047
      It is crucial to distinguish between the reasoning and boilerplate components and handle them sepa-
048   rately. Failure to do so may result in undesired effects, such as overfitting to the boilerplate compo-
049   nents, as depicted in Figure 1, ultimately leading to inadequate agent capabilities. While manually
050   crafting regular expressions to filter out boilerplate tokens appears to be a feasible solution, it can be
051   highly inefficient when dealing with data of diverse formats. Additionally, creating regular expres-
052   sions for template-connecting tokens of transitional phrases poses challenges due to their potential
053   variability in language. Therefore, an automated and adaptive approach for segregating these com-
      ponents is highly desirable.

      This study introduces a novel SHuffle-Aware Discriminator (SHAD) to achieve automated and adap-
072
      tive token distinction. Considering boilerplate tokens are usually consistent across samples, they
073
      can be treated as sample-independent. Consequently, shuffling the correspondence between input
074   and output across data samples does not alter the predictability of boilerplate tokens. However,
075   such shuffling introduces noise that complicates the prediction of reasoning tokens, by causing mis-
076   matches between the tokens and the input queries1 . SHAD is developed based on this principle.
077   Specifically, it fine-tunes an LLM model using a small portion of shuffled data and then compares
078   the token-level loss between the tuned and original models to classify tokens for the target data. A
079   token is classified as boilerplate2 if the loss on the tuned model decreases; otherwise, it is classified
080   as a reasoning token.
081   Based on SHAD, we have developed a new Reasoning-highlighted Fine-Tuning (RFT) approach,
082   which adaptively assigns greater weights to challenging reasoning tokens to emphasize the learning
083   of reasoning. This approach demonstrates superior performance compared to existing supervised
084   fine-tuning methods across several common agent benchmarks. Further analysis reveals that our
085   method could effectively identify reasoning tokens and strengthen the learning of these tokens, ulti-
086   mately enhancing the learning of agent capabilities for LLMs.
087   The main contributions of this work are summarized as follows:
088
089   • We emphasize the differences in learning difficulty and importance between reasoning and boil-
090     erplate tokens for agent learning, highlighting the critical importance of effectively distinguishing
091
        between them.
092   • We introduce SHAD, a novel method that automatically discriminates between reasoning and
093     boilerplate tokens based on their predictability differences observed after shuffling input-output
094     combinations.
095   • We have developed a new fine-tuning method RFT rooted in SHAD, distinctly improving the
096     effectiveness of learning agent capabilities for LLMs.

133   2.2       E NHANCING AGENT C APABILITY FOR LLM S
134
135   To tackle complex real-world problems, it is essential to enhance LLMs’ agent capabilities, such as
136   the ability of external tool use and multi-step reasoning (Shen et al., 2023; Nakano et al., 2021; Yao
137   et al., 2022). Prior works (Yao et al., 2023; Shinn et al., 2023; Pan et al., 2024; Zhao et al., 2023)
138   have focused on developing frameworks that prompt LLMs to integrate tools better and engage in
139
      deeper reasoning before taking action. Subsequent works have further constructed diverse and well-
      structured agent-task benchmark datasets, e.g., Toolllama (Qin et al., 2024), Toolalpaca (Tang et al.,
140
      2023), and APIGen (Liu et al., 2024), considering these specific datasets for further tuning of LLMs
141
      to more directly and effectively enhance their agent abilities. Recently, Chen et al. (2024b) proposed
142   Agent-Flan, a dataset rewrite method to enable LLMs better to learn reasoning and tool use at a step
143   level. Although these methods train LLMs on agent datasets and achieve promising results, they
144   often struggle with overfitting and generalization issues (Chen et al., 2024b). Our RFT with SHAD
145   can better utilize these datasets to learn reasoning, achieving superior performance on agent tasks
146   while maintaining good generalization ability on out-of-distribution benchmarks.

160   samples does not alter the predictability of boilerplate tokens, unlike reasoning tokens. Leveraging

      Next, we elaborate on these three steps:
195
196    •Data Shuffle. This is the core step of our method, creating distinct predictability for the reasoning
197   tokens and boilerplate tokens. The shuffle is performed by randomly reassigning the input-output
198   combinations between samples. When implementing, we just select a small ratio (1%) of the target
199   dataset and shuffle it for use in the subsequent model tuning step, to avoid large tuning costs and
200   overfitting on the whole dataset.
201
      Let (xi , y i ) denote the i-th sample for the sampled dataset, with xi as the input and y i as the output.
202   Denote all the inputs of all samples as X = [x1 , . . . , xN ], and the corresponding outputs as Y =
203   [y 1 , . . . , y N ], where N denotes the size of sampled dataset. We shuffle Y , and then re-combine the
204   inputs in X and outputs in the shuffled Y to construct the shuffled dataset Ds . This means, for the
205   i-th original sample (xi , y i ), its input xi may be combined with the j-th sample’s output y j to form
206   a new sample (xi , y j ), while its output y i may be combined with the k-th sample’s input xk to form
207   a new sample (xk , y i ). With this operation, the mapping relationship between the inputs and outputs
208   becomes noise for reasoning tokens, making them unpredictable. As for the boilerplate tokens, since
209   they are shared across samples, their predictability remains intact. Figure 3 provides an example to
210
      illustrate this.

      3.2   R EASONING - HIGHLIGHTED F INE -T UNING
246
247   Agent-tuning data often follows fixed formats and similar reasoning trajectories, making the corre-
248   sponding tokens (boilerplate tokens) easily learned by the model. If all tokens in a sample are treated
249   equally, the model risks overfitting to these boilerplate tokens, which can hinder its ability to learn
250   to reason effectively. To address this issue, we propose focusing more on reasoning tokens identi-
251   fied by our SHAD method, assigning them higher weights during fine-tuning to enhance reasoning
252   capabilities for agent task solving.
253   Instead of manually assigning fixed weights to the two types of tokens, we utilize an adaptive weight
254   assignment to align the dynamic learning process better. Specifically, we compare the total losses of
255   the reasoning and boilerplate parts, applying the softmax function to assign higher weights to the part
256   with the greater loss. Notably, since the reasoning part typically exhibits a higher loss (see Figure 5),
257   our method naturally assigns greater weights to emphasize reasoning learning. Furthermore, when
258   the loss difference between the two parts diminishes, our method can adaptively adjust the weights
259
      to promote a more balanced learning process for the two parts. Given the nature of highlighting
      reasoning, we name our method Reasoning-highlighted Fine-Tuning (RFT).
260
261   Formally, let Lb and Lr represent the total loss for the boilerplate and reasoning tokens, respectively.
262   The re-weighted loss of our RFT, denoted as LRF T , can be formulated as follows:

300   Training Data. We use LLaMA3.1-8B and LLaMA3-8B as the backbone models, fine-tuning them
301   to solve agent tasks. The training dataset is constructed from two commonly used multi-step plan-
302   ning and tool-use benchmarks, ToolBench (Qin et al., 2024) and APIGen (Liu et al., 2024), supple-
303   mented with general data from ShareGPT (noa, 2024b). The general data is used to preserve general
304   capabilities like instruction-following, as demonstrated in previous work (Zeng et al., 2023). Tool-
305   Bench and APIGen provide a variety of examples for solving complex real-world user queries across
306
      different environments, all organized in a standard agent-specific format: “Thought-Action-Action
      Input” in JSON.
307
308   Evaluation Setting. To comprehensively evaluate the proposed method, we consider two evalua-
309   tion settings—held-in task evaluation and held-out task evaluation, following prior work (Zeng et al.,
310   2023). The held-in evaluation focuses on measuring performance on tasks similar to those used dur-
311   ing training, while the held-out evaluation assesses the model’s generalization to novel tasks. For the
312
      held-in setting, we use the StableToolBench (Guo et al., 2024) and BFCL (noa) benchmarks. These

313

314
      use two additional benchmarks: 1) T-eval (Chen et al., 2024a), a comprehensive step-level reason-
315   ing benchmark, and 2) Nexus (team, 2023), a complex single-step nested tool-use benchmark. Both
316   benchmarks provide a diverse set of tools for LLMs to choose from, with tasks in StableToolBench
317   and T-eval often requiring multiple steps to complete. In accordance with the evaluation metrics
318   established in the original benchmarks, accuracy is employed for BFCL3 , T-eval, and Nexus, while
319   StableToolBench4 is evaluated using the pass rate assessed by GPT-4.

351
352   Both SHAD and RFT Are Crucial. When comparing SHAD+RFT with its variants, Regex+RFT
353   and SHAD+α-FT, the original SHAD+RFT consistently demonstrates superior performance. We
354   explained the results as follows:
355
356
      • Adaptive weighting in RFT is crucial. Comparing SHAD+RFT with its variant SHAD+α-FT,
        SHAD+RFT consistently outperforms, demonstrating the superiority of RFT’s adaptive mecha-
357
        nism over the fixed weighting approach of α-FT. This advantage stems from adaptive weighting’s
358
        ability to better align with the dynamic learning process, adaptively adjusting weights for rea-
359     soning and boilerplate token components, thereby preventing over-learning or under-learning of
360     either part.
361
362
      • The importance of SHAD for token differentiation. Replacing SHAD with Regex in
        SHAD+RFT leads to a significant drop in model performance. This highlights that the effec-
363
        tiveness of reasoning-highlighted fine-tuning depends on accurate token differentiation. The re-
364
        sults also demonstrate SHAD’s superior ability to disentangle boilerplate tokens from reasoning
365     tokens. In contrast, Regex relies solely on regular expressions to identify formatting tokens, fail-
366     ing to fully distinguish between template-connecting tokens (one part of boilerplate tokens) and
367     reasoning tokens.
368
369   These indicates that replacing either SHAD or RFT diminishes the method’s effectiveness, affirming
370   the importance of both components.

      6   L IMITATION
487
488   We identify several limitations of our method in both token differentiation and re-weighting during
489   training. First, the effectiveness of our approach depends on boilerplate tokens remaining consistent
490   across different samples. When this consistency is lacking, such as in cases where the diversity
491   of boilerplate tokens is high, our method may fail. Second, our distinction between reasoning and
492   boilerplate tokens relies on rigid, manually defined thresholds for loss differences, which may need
493   refinement. Third, our weighting strategy is currently applied only at the group level, and future
494   optimization may be required at the token level.
495
496   7   C ONCLUSION
497
498   In this paper, we highlight the importance of distinguishing between reasoning and boilerplate to-
499   kens and introduce a SHuffle-Aware Discriminator (SHAD) to automatically achieve this. Building
500   on SHAD, we further developed a new Reasoning-Highlighted Fine-Tuning (RFT) method to en-
501   hance reasoning learning during LLM fine-tuning, thereby improving agent capabilities. Extensive
502   results demonstrate that our method significantly enhances LLMs’ ability to solve complex real-
503   world problems. In the future, we plan to extend our approach to the entire SFT domain, and plan
504
      to develop more refined mechanisms, such as token-level re-weighting, to better leverage our token
      differentiation results.

      A     D ETAIL I NFORMATION OF T RAINING DATASETS
649
650   We provide more details of our training datasets. To enable the multi-step reasoning ability of
651   LLM, we choose ToolBench Qin et al. (2024) and APIGen Liu et al. (2024) as our basic datasets.
652   Following the practice in AgentTuning Zeng et al. (2023) and AgentFlan Chen et al. (2024b), we
653   also mix ShareGPT noa (2024b) and basic datasets for training. We filter the obviously low-quality
654   data that does not follow the request format and sample 10% percent of data from APIGen for data
655   balance. All methods use the same dataset and do not apply token differentiation to general data.
656
657   B     I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
658
659   B.1   I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS OF R HO -1
660
661   For the Rho-1 baseline, we train the reference model in self-reference setting Lin et al. (2024).
662   Specifically, we sample 5% data from our training dataset to train the reference model. We follow the
663   original implementation that focuses training on H→L tokens ( i.e., the tokens with loss decreased
664   from high to low during training the reference model) and masks the other tokens.
665
666   B.2   I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS OF R EWARD FT
667
      For the RewardFT baseline, because of the lack of Agent preference data, we use general DPO data
668
      ORCA DPO (noa, 2024c) and Ultrafeedback (noa, 2024a) to train the model as token-level reward
669   model under the same setting in (Rafailov et al., 2024). We calculate the token-level reward given
670   by the preference model, then we follow the practice in weighted-MLE (Yang et al., 2023), taking
671   softmax on all token rewards as the weight to train the model.
672
673   B.3   I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS OF α-FT
674
675   A simple and common method for addressing imbalance training is to manually give a fixed weight
676   for each type of token (Lin et al., 2020). Here we introduce a weighting factor α ∈ [0, 0.5] for
677   boilerplate tokens and 1 − α for reasoning tokens. Let Lb and Lr represent the total loss for the
678
      boilerplate and reasoning tokens, respectively. The re-weighted loss (denoted as Lα−balance ) can be
      formulated as follows:
679
                                         Lα−balance = αLb + (1 − α)Lr                                  (7)
680
      This loss is a simple extension to CE we call α-FT in this paper that we consider as an experimental
681
      baseline for our proposed RFT method.
