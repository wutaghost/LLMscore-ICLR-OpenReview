009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            With recent advances in large language models (LLMs), there has been emerg-
012            ing numbers of research in developing Semantic IDs based on LLMs to enhance
013            the performance of recommendation systems. However, the dimension of these
014            embeddings needs to match that of the ID embedding in recommendation, which
015            is usually much smaller than the original length. Such dimension compression
016            results in inevitable losses in discriminability and dimension robustness of the LLM
017            embeddings, which motivates us to scale up the semantic representation. In this
018
               paper, we propose Mixture-of-Codes, which first constructs multiple independent
               codebooks for LLM representation in the indexing stage, and then utilizes the
019
               Semantic Representation along with a fusion module for the downstream recom-
020
               mendation stage. Extensive analysis and experiments demonstrate that our method
021            achieves superior discriminability and dimension robustness scalability, leading to
022            the best scale-up performance in recommendations.

025   1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027   Recently, the emergence of large language models (LLM) (Dubey et al., 2024; Achiam et al., 2023)
028   sheds light in improving the recommendation systems via the semantic knowledge from LLM (Hou
029   et al., 2024; Bao et al., 2023). An intuitive practice is to simply project the LLM embeddings to
030   low-dimension embeddings via only MLPs into the recommendation systems for feature interactions.
031   However, such application is ineffective, largely due to the massive semantic gap between the
032
      embedding spaces of LLM and recommendation systems (Lin et al., 2023; Pan et al., 2024).
033   Several works (Rajput et al., 2024; Singh et al., 2023) have proposed to derive Semantic IDs (i.e.,
034   codes) based on clustering methods such as VQ-VAE (Van Den Oord et al., 2017) or RQ-VAE (Lee
035   et al., 2022) to capture information from the LLM embedding. In particular, they first train an
036   auto-encoder with discrete codes and then apply these codes to downstream tasks such as retrieval or
037   ranking. Such methods aim to transfer knowledge from the LLM embedding space to recommendation
038
      systems, utilizing the codes to capture the local structure of the original space. Besides, embedding
      these codes in the downstream stage facilitates the effective training in an end-to-end manner.
039
040   Notably, the LLM embeddings usually have very large dimensions, ranging from 4,096 to
041   16,384 (Dubey et al., 2024). When generating the embeddings for these codes, their dimension needs
042   to match that of the recommendation IDs. However, the dimension in recommendation is usually
043   small due to the Interaction Collapse Theory (Guo et al., 2023). Therefore, the code embeddings are
044
      also only able to span a low-dimension space. With one single semantic embedding as the semantic
      representation, it may fail to capture the complex, high-dimensional structure of the original LLM
045
      embeddings, lead to inevitable information loss and performance deterioration during the knowledge
046
      transfer. This motivates us to study how to scale up the semantic representation effectively.
047
048   We delve into two approaches based on existing works, including Multi-Embedding (Guo et al., 2023)
049   and RQ-VAE (Lee et al., 2022), to scale up the semantic representation by either using one codebook
050
      with multiple embeddings, or multiple hierarchical codebooks and embeddings. Nevertheless, the
      analysis and empirical results demonstrate that the representations of these two methods are not
051
      scalable in terms of discriminability and dimension robustness.
052
053   In this paper, we propose Mixture-of-Codes (MoC), a novel two-stage approach to effectively scale
      up semantic representations for recommendation. First, we propose a Multi-Codebooks VQ-VAE

055   these codes for all items, we adopt a Mixture-of-Codes module to fuse the learnable embeddings of

059   performance. Our contributions can be summarized as follows:
060
061          • We pioneer a study on the scalability of semantic representation on transferring knowledge

               from LLM to recommendation systems, and reveal that several baseline approaches fail to

067          • Comprehensive experiments on three public datasets show that our method successfully
068            achieves scalability regarding both discriminability, dimension robustness, and performance.

          3.1                     L IMINATION OF A S INGLE C ODE
123
124      The original LLM embeddings span a high-dimensional space, e.g., ranging from 1,024 to 416,384
125      dimensions. (Dubey et al., 2024). When we build a semantic representation based on the LLM em-
126      beddings to transfer their knowledge to recommendation, the dimension of these representation needs
127      to match that of the recommendation ID embeddings. However, the dimension of recommendations
128      is usually no more than 256 due to the Interaction Collapse Theory (Guo et al., 2023), and hence is
129      much smaller than that of the LLM embeddings. Such dramatic dimension compression may result
130
         in huge information loss.
131      To illustrate this empirically, we conduct a toy study based on the reconstruction error of the LLM
132      embeddings in a VAE-based generative model. As shown in Fig 1, we utilize a two-layer MLP
133      with 512 hidden units to reconstruct the original 4096-dimensional LLM embedding. When using
134      only single set of Semantic ID as input, the reconstruction error is extremely high, indicating the
135      significant information loss. However, the error drops significantly when we scale up the dimensions
136
         via using multiple (i.e., 2x and 3x) independent codebooks. This demonstrates the original single
         code embedding only preserves limited information of the LLM embeddings. Therefore, we aim to
137
         scale up the semantic representation appropriately to preserve the rich information from the LLM,
138
         thereby improving the performance of downstream recommendation tasks.

196   3.3              M EASUREMENT ON S CALABILITY OF S EMANTIC R EPRESENTATION
197
198
      Below we present two ways to quantify the scalability of semantic representations, one from a
      discriminability perspective and another from a dimension robustness perspective.
199
200   Definition 3.1 (Discriminability Scalability of Semantic Representation). With each scaling factors
201   from 1x to M x, the discriminability of a Semantic Representation in the continuous space is defined
202
      as the mutual information between its quantized representation Q(r) and the supervised label in the
      downstream tasks Y , i.e., MI(Q(r), Y ).
203
204   Following previous approach (Jawahar et al., 2019), we apply K-means as the discrete method
205   for normalized mutual information (NMI) calculation and analyze the flatten embedding of the
206   concatenated Semantic Representations among different methods in Fig. 2. We present results with
207   respect to different scaling factors with 100 clusters and provide flattened embedding NMI under
208   varying cluster numbers in Fig. 1b. We observe that the discriminability of the ME does not increase
209   with the scaling factor and may even decrease slightly. This is because all these extra embeddings still
210   correspond to the same Semantic IDs from a single codebook, thus containing minimal additional
211   information and redundancy.
212   Regarding RQ-VAE, its discriminability also does not consistently increase with the scaling factor
213   due to the fact that the additional fine-grained Semantic IDs introduced at higher level contain
214   diminishing information. We illustrate this by comparing NMI across different Semantic IDs and
215   their representations in downstream tasks in Fig. 3. Another surprising observation from the results in
      Fig. 3 is that the lowest level Semantic ID, i.e., SID 1, and its representation contain more information

348   factors in Fig. 4., and find that it gets higher values on the low-index singular, compared to the
349   RQ-VAE, even though not as high as ME. Besides, its singular values on high-indices are also robust,
350   not diminishing as ME in 5x and 7x factors. In conclusion, the dimension of MoC are more robust
351   than ME and RQ-VAE when we scale up its representation.
352   Based on the observations above, we conclude with the following finding:
353
354       Finding 2. Our proposed MoC successfully enables scalable Semantic Representation regarding
355       both discriminability and dimension robustness.

460     6          R ELATED W ORK
461
462
       Discrete representation learning. Discrete representation is first introduced by (Van Den Oord et al.,
       2017) and shows feasibility and great success in the field of generate models (Esser et al., 2021;
463
       Lee et al., 2022; Rombach et al., 2022; Peebles & Xie, 2023; Mentzer et al., 2023). It utilize vector
464
       quantization (VQ) to model distributions over discrete variables with a codebook and define a simple
465    uniform prior instead of Gaussian prior in VAE (Kingma, 2013) to avoid posterior collapse. RQ-VAE
466    (Lee et al., 2022) further introduces residual quantization for better minimization of reconstruction
467    error. FSQ (Mentzer et al., 2023) remove auxiliary losses and replace the vector quantizer in VQ-VAE
468    with a simple scalar quantization.
469
       Semantic IDs. The discrete representation obtained through VQ-VAE can be employed as a semantic
470
       clustering IDs, thus capturing the local structure of the LLM embedding to a certain extent. In the
471    context of the recommendation system, TIGER (Rajput et al., 2024) takes advantage of a hierarchical
472    quantizer (Lee et al., 2022) to convert items into tokens for generative recommendation and retrieval.
473    LC-Rec (Zheng et al., 2024) improves TIGER by incorporating knowledge from LLMs like LLama
474    (Touvron et al., 2023) and introducing instruction tuning tasks for effective adaptation to recommender
475    systems. LMINDEXER (Jin et al., 2023) learns the Semantic IDs in a self-supervised styles to obtain
476    the document’s semantic representations and their hierarchical structures.
477
478     7          C ONCLUSION
479
480    In this paper, we investigate the scalability of semantic representation based on LLM for recommenda-
481    tion. We unveil that simple methods, such as using a single codebook with multiple embeddings and
482    scaling with hierarchical codebooks in RQ-VAE, do not scale effectively in Semantic Representation.
483    We propose a novel multiple codebooks method which learn multiple independent Semantic IDs in the
484    VQ-VAE based on LLM embeddings, and then employ these codebooks with a fusion module in the
485    downstream recommendation models. Comprehensive experiments show that the proposed method
       successfully achieves scalability regarding discriminability, dimension robustness, and performance.
