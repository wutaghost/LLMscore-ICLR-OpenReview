009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Large Vision-Language Models (VLMs) are increasingly being regarded as foun-
012            dation models that can be instructed to solve diverse tasks by prompting, without
013            task-specific training. We examine the seemingly obvious question: how to ef-
014            fectively prompt VLMs for semantic segmentation. To that end, we systematically
015            evaluate the segmentation performance of several recent models guided by either
016            text or visual prompts on the diverse MESS dataset collection. We introduce a
017            scalable prompting scheme, few-shot prompted semantic segmentation, inspired
018
               by open-vocabulary segmentation and few-shot learning. It turns out that even the
               most advanced VLMs lag far behind specialist models trained for a specific seg-
019
               mentation task, by about 30% on average on the Intersection-over-Union metric.
020
               Moreover, we find that text prompts and visual prompts are complementary: each
021            one of the two modes fails on many examples that the other one can solve. Our
022            analysis suggests that being able to anticipate the most effective prompt modality
023            can lead to a 11% improvement in performance. Motivated by our findings, we
024            propose PromptMatcher, a remarkably simple baseline that combines both text
025            and visual prompts, achieving state-of-the-art results for training-free semantic
026            segmentation.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Large Vision-Language Models (VLMs) have established themselves as the state-of-the-art for
032   cross-modal reasoning that involves images and text, and even as robust backbones for purely vi-
033
      sual tasks, benefiting from the wealth of semantic and contextual relations contributed by language
      modeling. A particular strength of VLMs is the capability to condition image understanding on text
034
      inputs, the so-called Text Prompts (TP). This enables, for instance, segmentation of a specific object
035
      in an image (Lai et al., 2024; Rasheed et al., 2024), reasoning about relations between objects (You
036   et al., 2023; Peng et al., 2023), and visual question answering (Beyer et al., 2024; Xiao et al., 2023).
037   Some VLMs also offer conditioning on Visual Prompts (VP). Typically these are visual cues like
038   points (suitably embedded coordinates on the image), scribbles or bounding boxes (Lai et al., 2024;
039   Rasheed et al., 2024), but it has also been proposed to directly superimpose symbols in pixel space
040   (Yang et al., 2023a).
041
      We observe that (prompted) VLMs have been studied mainly in two broad settings. The first one
042   could be called image-driven text generation, meaning that the system outputs language, while visual
043   information is used only on the input side. This setting includes tasks such as image captioning and
044   visual question answering. The second setting can be referred to as visual grounding. This setting
045   links language to image regions, helping to enhance the model’s spatial reasoning and understanding
046   of how textual descriptions correspond to visual elements in an image. Examples include phrase
047   grounding, where the model is asked to detect the objects mentioned in the text, constraining their
048   spatial relations, and referring expression comprehension, where objects have to be identified based
049   on a periphrasis, thus emphasising contextual relations.
050   In this work, we focus on the potential of prompting mechanisms to improve image-to-image tasks.
051   Given that large VLMs are increasingly being recognized as foundation models for vision, we ask
052   how to effectively prompt VLMs for semantic segmentation. In other words, our primary interest
053   is not how well the model can parse or generate text about images, but rather how accurately it can
      delineate objects in images.

054
      Since the desired outputs – segmentation masks – reside in image space, it is a natural question
055   whether Text Prompts or Visual Prompts are more expedient, and how the two can be combined.
056   While text prompting has proved successful in guiding image understanding and visual reasoning,
057   we claim that it is not always sufficient to prompt a VLM with text, and visual prompts can in some
058   cases be more suitable, or complementary. Intuitively, a visual example can in certain situations con-
059   vey information that it much harder, or even impossible, to transmit through text. While the internal
060   mechanisms of large models are notoriously difficult to disentangle and interpret, there is a simple
061   argument in support of visual prompting: The projection of the visual world to language is lossy.
062   Even elaborate text descriptions are often ambiguous and can lead to vastly different predictions.
063   At this point we must highlight a subtle, but important difference that is sometimes overlooked:
064   text prompts are normally understood as generic statements that can be defined once and then ap-
065   plied across many images, like “segment all cats”. In contrast, visual prompts are predominantly
066   understood as image-specific, like for instance a scribble to denote the cat in a particular image. In
067   this interpretation, visual prompting requires user input for every new sample and is not scalable.
068   Instead, we advocate for a form of visual prompting that incurs only a constant overhead for arbi-
069
      trarily large test sets: The user annotates instances of their desired target class on a small number
      of images, then that fixed set of examples serves as the prompt for the full dataset and no further
070
      interaction is expected. We refer to this setup as few-shot prompted semantic segmentation (FPSS).
071
      Unlike traditional few-shot learning, which also uses a small set of annotated examples but requires
072   fine-tuning the model, FPSS operates through prompting rather than training. It is also related to
073   open-vocabulary segmentation, where a frozen model is adapted to new classes without retraining,
074   though typically in a zero-shot context rather than using a few-shot approach.
075
      When evaluating under the FPSS protocol, we find that VLMs are not behaving (yet) as foundational.
076
      They still trail domain-specific segmentation models by about 30% on average in Intersection-over-
077
      Union (IoU) score on the dataset used in this work. Furthermore, we find that text prompts perform
078   better on average, but that visual prompts are able to address tasks that are exceptionally difficult for
079   text prompted models. Unsurprisingly, the two prompting modes are to some degree complemen-
080   tary: in hard scenarios, e.g. medical imaging, VP can solve many instances that TP cannot, and vice
081   versa.
082
      Motivated by these findings, we construct a simple baseline for combined text and visual guidance,
083
      while still maintaining a training-free, prompting-only setup. Prompting with both text and vision
084   indeed improves the performance by a significant 2.5% compared to only text (respectively, 3.5%
085   compared to only vision).
086
087
      Summarizing our contributions:
088
             • We design a novel benchmarking task to probe the performance of VLMs as semantic
089            segmentation engines.
090
             • We show that even the latest models remain far below custom models trained for a specific
091
               task and data domain. In other words, we are still far from foundational VLMs.
092
             • We show that text and visual prompting complement each other, and that being able to an-
093
               ticipate the most effective prompt modality can lead to a 11% improvement in performance.
094
095          • We propose a simple training-free framework to capitalize on the complementary strengths
096
               of text and visual prompts and achieve state-of-the-art on the MESS dataset collection
               Blumenstiel et al. (2023).

099   2   TASK FORMULATION
100
101   The goal of our paper is to evaluate to which extent (training-free) prompting of generalist VLMs
102   can replace specialist models for semantic segmentation. It is obvious that some form of prompt
103   is always required to let a VLM know what to segment, but it is much less obvious what the most
104
      suitable prompt is. Here, we limit ourselves to the two most popular ones, text and visual prompts.
105   As an example, let us assume we want to segment airplanes. A natural way to instruct the model
106   is with one or a few text prompts, like “segment all airplanes”. Note that, due to the compositional
107   nature of language, there is no clear definition on how many prompts we are effectively using, since
      two or more prompts can be merged into one, as in “segment airplanes and similar flying machines”.


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      In normal text prompting, the same prompt is then applied to all input images. FPSS translates that
109   one-off prompting scenario to the visual domain: the user supplies the system with at most K
110   reference images of airplanes, along with their segmentation masks or other annotations (e.g., a set
111   of points within the mask). Based on that input, the system shall segment airplanes in any number
112   of unseen target images. Note that this mode of interaction makes it possible to communicate about
113   visual concepts whose category name is not known to the model, just like a child can say “I want
114   this” before learning the word “chocolate”.
115   Beyond the research questions on how the two prompting modes compare and when one or the other
116   is more successful, prompting in the FPSS setting is relevant in several real application scenarios as
117   digitalization and AI permeate society. For instance, an engineer may have to instruct an inspection
118   system to examine a new item, or a biologist may want to screen a legacy image collection for a
119   newly discovered species; In both scenarios, users may prefer to provide only a few text or visual
120   prompts to the system, expecting the task to be automatically applied to the entire dataset.

122   3     A NALYSIS
123
124   In this section, we outline the evaluation framework, specifying the models considered within FPSS,
125   specifically under the one-shot regime. In particular, we select a range of key text prompted and vi-
126   sual prompted models and assess their effectiveness in performing segmentation when provided with
127   the corresponding prompt modality. We then present and discuss the results, providing a detailed
128   analysis of the performance differences across modalities, highlighting strengths and limitations.
129
130   3.1   E VALUATION PROTOCOL
131
132   There are many models capable of performing segmentation guided by text prompts, mainly falling
133   into two categories: open-vocabulary segmentation models (Cho et al., 2024) and vision-language
134   models (VLMs) (Lai et al., 2024; Beyer et al., 2024). Both types of models leverage textual input
135
      to guide segmentation, with open-vocabulary models focusing specifically on identifying objects
      beyond a fixed set of categories, while VLMs, with their broader multi-modal capabilities, can also
136
      be adapted for segmentation tasks. Similarly, we identify two categories of models that can be
137
      prompted visually: models specifically trained with visual prompts (Li et al., 2023a; Zou et al.,
138   2023) and training-free frameworks leveraging existing segmentation models along with matching
139   algorithms (Liu et al., 2024b; Frick et al., 2024). In contrast, very few models have been presented
140   that can be guided with both text and visual prompts (Zou et al., 2023)
141
      For open-vocabulary segmentation models, we consider CAT-Seg (Cho et al., 2024), the state-of-
142
      the-art on the MESS dataset. In particular, we use CAT-Seg with the CLIP ViT-L/14 backbone. We
143
      also include SEEM (Zou et al., 2023), specifically the SEEM Davit-Large implementation. This is
144   the only available model to accept TPs and VPs simultaneously, although in this section we only use
145   them separately. Combined prompting with SEEM is discussed in Section 5.
146
147
      As VLM baselines, we include the decoder-free Florence-2 (Xiao et al., 2023), specifically the
      segmentation branch of the large, fine-tuned model, where we clip the generated sequence length
148
      to 1024 for computational reasons; and PALI-Gemma (Beyer et al., 2024), a small but effective
149
      architecture using a VQVAE decoder van den Oord et al. (2018). Regarding PALI-Gemma, we make
150   use of the standard 224-mix implementation. We also evaluate the recent LISA (Lai et al., 2024),
151   in particular the LISA-13B-llama2-v1 version, which features a dedicated decoder (from the SAM
152   foundation model). To keep the evaluation focused, and taking computational resource limitations
153   into account, we regard LISA as proxy for its follow-up works: GLAMM (Rasheed et al., 2024)
154   and SESAME (Wu et al., 2023), which might offer marginal improvements. Our choice of VLMs is
155   primarily informed by their referring segmentation performance on the RefCOCO, RefCOCO+, and
156   RefCOCOg datasets (Kazemzadeh et al., 2014; Mao et al., 2016), a task which is closely related to
157   our FPSS task. In all cases, we opt for greedy LLM decoding.
158   When considering models which are specifically trained with visual prompts, we once more pick
159   SEEM (Zou et al., 2023), using the same implementation as described for the text prompting setting,
160   as well as DINOv (Li et al., 2023a), using its Swin-L variant. Regarding visually prompted training-
161   free frameworks, we choose Matcher (Liu et al., 2024b) motivated by its performance on COCO-20i,
      and its follow-up work SoftMatcher (Frick et al., 2024) mainly for its computational efficiency, both


                                                        3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      of which leverage pre-trained foundation models, namely Segment Anything (SAM, Kirillov et al.,
163   2023) and DINOv2 Oquab et al. (2024), in combination with traditional matching algorithms to pro-
164   vide image-prompted segmentation capabilities. Furthermore, we modify the Matcher/SoftMatcher
165   framework to obtain an improved version, which we call SoftMatcher+. It utilizes AM-RADIO
166   (Ranzinger et al., 2024) as its backbone instead of DINOv2, leveraging the excellent abilities of
167   AM-RADIO features (distilled from several large models including CLIP, DINOv2 and SAM) in
168   terms of matching, pixel-level localization, and vision-language connections. For all these training-
169   free methods we make use of the ViT-L versions of the models (DINOv2, SAM, AM-RADIO), and
170   tune their hyper-parameters on COCO-20i.
171   Regarding text prompts, we proceed as follows: for open-vocabulary segmentation models that
172   accept only a class name as input, we use class names based on the dataset specifications. For
173   VLMs with advanced language abilities, we embed the class name in the sentence “Segment all the
174   instances of class class name in the image”. As visual prompts, we sample one single image of
175   the target class from the dataset itself, together with its ground truth segmentation mask. Considering
176   a prompt consisting of a single image is proportionate with our elementary text prompts. Picking
177
      that image from the same dataset corresponds to the realistic scenario where the user creates the
      prompt on images acquired in their application setting, with similar imaging conditions and class
178
      definitions as the test data. To minimise biases due to the choice of prompt image, we sample a
179
      different prompt image for each prediction.
180
181   We point out that both text prompts and visual prompts can be refined by prompt engineering.
182   This field explores various techniques, ranging from single prompt optimization (Zhou et al., 2023),
183
      prompt ensembling (Wang et al., 2023c), to multi-step reasoning (Wei et al., 2023; Yao et al., 2023;
      Zhang et al., 2024b). While prompt engineering can make a substantial difference, it has become
184
      an art in itself, and in fact an entry barrier for inexperienced users. It goes beyond the scope of the
185
      present work, but may be an interesting avenue for future research.
186
187   We also consciously refrain from any fine-tuning. Often, even large models are fine-tuned for spe-
188   cific tasks, which can significantly improve their performance. However, in our view, this approach
189
      seems misaligned with the definition and purpose of a ”foundation model”, which should ideally
      be usable with minimal intervention. Once the hardware, data, and expertise for fine-tuning are re-
190
      quired, there is arguably little qualitative difference from the well-established practice of training a
191
      dedicated model starting from pre-trained weights (e.g., from ImageNet).
192
193   As a testbed for our experiments we use the MESS dataset collection (Blumenstiel et al., 2023).
194   It consists of 22 different segmentation datasets that span a wide variety of application domains
195
      and image characteristics. The datasets are grouped into five broad domains, General (6 datasets),
      Earth (5), Medical (4), Engineering (4) and Agriculture (3) as detailed in Table 5. The MESS
196
      collection is deliberately designed as a challenging benchmark for foundation models and open-
197
      vocabulary models, because its constituent datasets span a wide range of target categories and image
198   characteristics, many of which differ significantly from the dominant conditions of scraped internet
199   data used to train most VLMs. Moreover, MESS comes with strong baselines generated with per-
200   dataset, domain-specific semantic segmentation models. For clarity of presentation, we always show
201   average numbers for the five broad domains covered by MESS. The detailed dataset composition is
202   provided in Appendix A.
203
      The evaluations were run on a single A100 with 40GB of memory, which takes ⇡14 hours for one
204   complete run with the largest model (LISA-13B). Open-vocabulary segmentation models are faster,
205   completing one evaluation cycle in 9 hours, while Florence-2 is the slowest, taking almost 24 hours.
206   Visually prompted models are substantially lighter (up to 1.2B parameters) than their text prompted
207   counterparts (up to 13B parameters), and while Matcher is very slow (22 hours), SoftMatcher+ takes
208   around 5 hours for an evaluation cycle.

296
297   A natural starting point for characterizing the differences between visual and text prompting is to
298   determine by how much the segmentation performance improves by choosing the best prompting
299   modality within each target domain. Regarding the MESS datasets, this can be easily quantified
300   by taking the maximum across VP and TP performance for each dataset, obtaining what we call an
301   Oracle Ensemble. Table 2 shows that being able to choose optimally between using visual or text
302
      prompts brings a boost to the overall performance by 6% compared to LISA.
303   Motivated by this, we add more granularity to this analysis and investigate the performance upper
304   bound that we could reach by selecting the best prompting on a per-image basis, as opposed to per-
305   dataset ( Oracle Ensemble). We denote the resulting optimal selection with Oracle Ensemble+ and
306   note in Table 2 its remarkable performance of 53.8%, corresponding to an 11% jump over pure text
307   prompting with LISA.

361   4.2   A MBIGUITY OF TEXT PROMPTING
362
363   The visual inspection of the top samples in terms of performance difference between TP and VP
364   suggests that the discrepancies can be attributed to two main linguistic challenges: ambiguity from
365   polysemous or homonymous words and the use of highly specialized or uncommon terms.
366   These issues are closely related to the inherent complexities of language, which complicate the
367   ability of text prompted systems to accurately interpret visual tasks. The interplay between ambi-
368   guity and specificity in language is inherent on how it was formed (Riemer, 1949) and it is widely
369   known to be an issue in the computational semantics literature, hindering the algorithmic perfor-
370   mance (Church & Patil, 1982; Manning & Schutze, 1999). The trade-off between the usage of
371   ambiguous words and ones that are specific, unusual, or difficult to pronounce serves a crucial role
372   in our ability to convey complex thoughts and adapt to diverse communicative contexts (Wasow,
373   2015).

375   supported by further experiments on the MESS FoodSeg103 dataset. Here we see a significant
376   performance gap of 13% of IoU between Oracle Ensembling (which in this case refers to LISA)
377   and Oracle Ensembling+. This can be attributed to the linguistic challenges previously discussed.

      model’s performance is compromised by both the non-specific nature of the word tool and out-of-
404   domain nature of the image. The generality of the term tool sometimes leads to misinterpretation,
405   with the model confusing it with elements of the camera interface itself. This ambiguity helps
406   explaining the substantial 35% performance gap observed in this dataset.
407
      Humans typically bridge this semantic gap by providing additional context (Pimentel et al., 2024).
408
      However, in our experimental setup, this approach can be prohibitively expensive or unfeasible, as
409
      shown by the Worm-eating Warbler case. While using the prompt “bird” could disambiguate this
410   specific image, such generic prompts fail when working with datasets that include different bird
411   species. Visual Prompting offers a solution to this challenge by providing a simpler, less ambiguous
412   method to fill this semantic gap, eliminating the need for elaborate textual descriptions or context-
413   dependent prompts.
414
      Our considerations indicate that visual and text prompting are inherently complementary, and that
415
      visual prompting offers a natural and readily available strategy to make up for the weaknesses of
416   text prompting due the identified ambiguities.

419   5   P ROMPT M ATCHER : COMBINING TEXT AND VISUAL PROMPTS
420
421   Motivated by the complementary nature of text and visual prompts, we propose a framework that
422   effectively integrates both, closing the gap between the baselines presented in Section 3 and the
423   Oracle Ensemble+. Furthermore, drawing inspiration from LLM-Modulo frameworks outlined in
424   (Kambhampati et al., 2024), particularly from the concept of employing critics/verifiers to enhance
425   generative models’ reasoning capabilities, in our context we propose to use SoftMatcher+ as an
426   effective critic/verifier for LISA’s predictions. This verification module would be able to mitigate
427   LISA’s hallucinations, thereby enhancing overall accuracy.

430   prompt branch and SoftMatcher+ for the visual prompt branch. First, at the mask generation step, the

457   start to bridge the gap towards the Oracle Ensemble+. Notably, PromptMatcher surpasses Oracle
458   Ensemble+ on two MESS datasets (DeepCrack and MHP v1), indicating synergies beyond simply
459   selecting the better of two prompts. This superior performance can be attributed to the unique nature
460   of the proposed framework. As our approach leverages the complementary strengths of LISA and
461   SoftMatcher+ to generate a more diverse set of predictions, when the outputs from the two models
462
      diverge, taking their union allows merging segments from different instances. This enables the mod-
      els to combine their predicted masks rather than being limited to choose the output from one or the
463
      other, which is advantageous compared to an oracle-based selection. Moreover, applying the mask
464
      rejection procedure from SoftMatcher+ to LISA masks helps to mitigate potential hallucinations
465   from LISA by rejecting results that do not match with the reference mask. The rejection of LISA
466   masks capitalizes on the inherent text-vision knowledge distilled into the AM-RADIO representa-
467   tions, improving over vision-only backbones.
468

469
      the two modalities. We are convinced that there is untapped potential in such modular, training-
470
      free frameworks. We leave the exploration of more elaborate framework designs to future work,
471   encouraging the research community’s involvement in this effort.

474   6   R ELATED W ORK
475
476   Open-Vocabulary Segmentation Models are able to perform segmentation across unlimited classes
477   without relying on a fixed set of categories defined during training. These models often rely on
478   CLIP-like text encoders to associate visual data with text descriptions. Specialized models like L-
479   SEG Li et al. (2022) and CAT-Seg Cho et al. (2024) are designed specifically to solve this task, while
480   multi-modal models such as X-Decoder Zou et al. (2022) and SEEM Zou et al. (2023) expand this
481   capability by handling a different range of visual prompts.
482   Vision-Language Models bridge the gap between visual perception and natural language under-
483   standing, excelling in tasks that require a combination of both, such as perception-language tasks
484   and grounding tasks. These models are built using large language models (LLMs) integrated with
485   vision encoders. With respect to perception-language tasks, VLMs perform tasks like image caption-
      ing, visual question answering, and region-level annotations. The LLaVA series Liu et al. (2023b;a;


                                                        9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      2024a) has set benchmarks in this area by combining vision encoders like CLIP Radford et al. (2021)
487   with LLMs, such as LLaMA Touvron et al. (2023); et al. (2023) or Vicuna Chiang et al. (2023). In-
488   structBLIP Dai et al. (2023) builds on the BLIP-2 Li et al. (2023b) model with instruct tuning, and
489   MM1 McKinzie et al. (2024) provides insights into crafting effective multimodal models. GPT-4V
490   OpenAI (2024) currently sets the highest standard in these perception-language tasks Yang et al.
491   (2023b). In grounding tasks, VLMs are able to handle phrase grounding and referring expression
492   comprehension, detection, and segmentation. These tasks require identifying specific objects or re-
493   gions based on text descriptions. Models like Florence-2 Xiao et al. (2023) predict segmentation
494   coordinates in the form of text, while PALI-Gemma Beyer et al. (2024) uses a next-token predic-
495
      tion method encoding outputs to a fixed token dictionary, which is then decoded using a VQVAE
      van den Oord et al. (2018). Other significant contributions include Kosmos-2 Peng et al. (2023),
496
      which integrates coordinate tokens into the vocabulary for object detection, Ferret You et al. (2023),
497
      which incorporates dense visual prompts, and Osprey Yuan et al. (2024), which adds further granu-
498   larity to input prompts. While GPT-4V has shown impressive capabilities in many visual-language
499   tasks, it has notable limitations in performing segmentation. Some VLMs incorporate specialized
500   segmentation decoders, such as LISA Lai et al. (2024), which extends the LLaVA architecture in-
501   corporating SAM Kirillov et al. (2023) to convert predicted tokens into segmentation masks. This
502   hybrid approach has been refined by models like GLAMM Rasheed et al. (2024), which includes
503   pixel-level visual prompting and supports multi-round conversations, and GSVA Xia et al. (2024),
504   which enhances resilience to adversarial attacks. PixelLM Ren et al. (2024) introduces a lightweight
505   segmentation decoder, while SESAME Wu et al. (2023) focuses on mitigating hallucination in seg-
506
      mentation tasks.
507   Visual Prompting involves providing visual cues to guide the model’s understanding and segmen-
508   tation of images. Early works such as Bar et al. (2022), focused on solving few-shot vision tasks
509   by reconstructing the target via image inpainting of a grid-like input prompt. This concept was fur-
510   ther developed in models like Painter Wang et al. (2023a) and SegGPT Wang et al. (2023b), which
511   demonstrated the possibility of solving tasks like segmentation more effectively. A significant leap
512
      forward came with the introduction of the Segment Anything Model (SAM) Kirillov et al. (2023) and
      its follow-up Ravi et al. (2024), showing remarkable zero-shot capabilities in image segmentation
513
      tasks. These models, along with works like OMG-LLaVA Zhang et al. (2024a), focused on using
514
      visual prompts within the target image itself, rather than relying on separate example images. Other
515   notable works include DINOv Li et al. (2023a), which expands visual prompting from SEEM, and
516   Matcher Liu et al. (2024b) which brings a unique approach that enables zero-shot models like SAM
517   to be prompted one-shot through feature matching. SoftMatcher Frick et al. (2024) further expands
518   on this concept by enhancing both simplicity and computation performance of the approach. Addi-
519   tionally, there has been growing research on optimizing information extraction from target images
520   using pixel-level deformations. A seminal work in this direction is SoM Yang et al. (2023a), which
521   posited that providing visual clues to a VLM can significantly enhance its performance. This has
522   sparked numerous follow-up studies, including ViP-LLaVA Cai et al. (2024) that applies these con-
523
      cepts to models like LLaVA. The practical implications of these approaches are also being explored,
      such by the work He et al. (2024) in the context of web-based applications.

      7   C ONCLUSION
527
528
529   In this work, we introduced a benchmarking task designed to evaluate the performance of Vision-
530   Language Models (VLMs) as semantic segmentation engines. Our results demonstrate that, despite
531
      the advancements, the latest VLMs still fall significantly short compared to custom models trained
      specifically on a given domain. This finding suggests that there is still room for progress in devel-
532
      oping VLMs. We also showed that text prompting and visual prompting are complementary. By
533
      anticipating and selecting the most effective prompting modality, it is possible to achieve a notable
534   11% IoU performance improvement. Building on this insight, we introduced a straightforward,
535   training-free framework that leverages the complementary strengths of both text and visual prompt-
536   ing, with a key verification component responsible for rejecting incorrect segmentation masks. This
537   framework sets a new state-of-the-art benchmark on the MESS dataset collection, achieving 45.5%
538   average IoU. Our findings highlight the potential of using multiple prompt modalities to enhance the
539   performance of VLMs without the need for additional training, bringing us closer to true foundation
      VLMs.
