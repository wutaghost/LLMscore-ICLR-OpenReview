009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Time-series clustering has gained abundant popularity and has been used in di-
012            verse scientific areas. However, few researchers take an information fusion per-
013            spective to combine information from the time and frequency domains to accom-
014            plish clustering, although these two domains offer distinct and complementary
015            characteristics of time-series. Motivated by this issue, we propose a trusted and in-
016            teractive model, which leverages evidence theory to combine time- and frequency-
017            based clustering results produced by the corresponding contrastive learning mod-
018            ule. After mathematizing clustering results from the two domains as mass func-
019
               tions, the uncertainty contained in these results can be quantified at the sample-
               specific level. The combined result thus promotes clustering reliability, and is op-
020
               timized based on the pseudo-labels generated by k-means in an interactive learn-
021
               ing paradigm. Both theoretical analysis and experimental results on 136 bench-
022            mark datasets validate the effectiveness of the proposed model in clustering per-
023            formance. Extensive ablation experiments demonstrate the contribution of com-
024            bining information from the time and frequency domains and using the interactive
025            learning paradigm. The embeddings learned are also experimentally shown to
026            perform well in other downstream tasks.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Time-series clustering is an important data mining technology widely applied in different fields,
032   such as sensor data analysis (Hayashi et al., 2024), anomaly detection (Middlehurst et al., 2024) and
033   medical field (Zhang et al., 2024), aiming to segment time-series data samples into patterns (called
034   clusters) with homologous characteristics (Gong et al., 2022). Unlike images, time-series data gen-
035   erally do not show human-recognizable features to different classes, because the label information
036   is contained in not only the time domain but also the frequency domain (Zhang et al., 2022).
037   Related work. Most of the existing time-series clustering methods focus on the time domain, and
038   can be divided into two categories: raw-data-based methods and feature-based ones (Ma et al.,
039   2022). Raw-data-based methods perform clustering based on a modified similarity metric, which
040   quantifies the distance more appropriately between time-series samples (Hayashi et al., 2024; Fer-
041   reira & Zhao, 2016; Paparrizos & Gravano, 2015; Yang & Leskovec, 2011). Feature-based methods
042   have recently garnered greater attention, because the raw-data-based ones are not capable of mod-
043   eling nonlinear temporal dependencies and multiscale (long and short-term) temporal dependencies
044   (Ma et al., 2019a). Feature-based methods extract first informative features from raw samples in the
045
      time domain, and then the clustering algorithms are conducted on the learned features (Tang et al.,
      2021; Fortuin et al., 2020). Some recent works (Zhang et al., 2024; Guijo-Rubio et al., 2021) also
046
      optimize feature extraction and clustering jointly by introducing pseudo-label. For example, authors
047
      in (Péalat et al., 2023) embed the time-series onto the Stiefel manifold to obtain the geometric rep-
048   resentations of time-series samples. STCN (Ma et al., 2022) adopts a recurrent neural network and a
049   self-supervised clustering module, which are trained iteratively by contributing to each other. Some
050   recent works (e.g., (Fortuin et al., 2020; Tonekaboni et al., 2022)) leverage contrastive representation
051   learning for clustering time-series to further improve the performance. Few of the mentioned meth-
052   ods take an information fusion perspective to incorporate information from the time and frequency
053   domains to accomplish clustering, although these two domains offer distinct and complementary
      characteristics of time-series. More detailed related work is provided in Appendix A.1.

      Motivation. In fact, the time domain depicts the temporal evolution of signal readouts, while the fre-
055   quency domain reveals the distribution of signal magnitude across different frequency components
056   within the entire spectrum (Hyndman & Athanasopoulos, 2018). By explicitly incorporating the
057   frequency domain, a comprehension of time-series behavior can be attained, encompassing aspects
058   that are not fully captured by analyzing the time domain alone. Therefore, the first motivation for
059   this work is incorporating frequency information to enhance the ability to detect clusters. Besides,
060   the time and frequency domains can be regarded as distinct views of the same data (Cohen, 1995),
061   and they are interconvertible through Fourier and inverse Fourier Transformation (Brigham, 1988).
062   Given the temporal dynamics inherent in time-series data, the weights (i.e., quality) of the time and
063
      frequency domains experience fluctuations over time. Then, the second motivation is to dynamically
      describe the importance of clustering results derived from both the time and frequency domains.
064
      Finally, to avoid poisoning the final result with the low-quality result from one domain, the last mo-
065
      tivation is to facilitate the appropriate integration of time- and frequency-based clustering results
066   under the fast-evolving uncertain scenario.
067
068   Technical issues. To address the three challenges outlined in the motivation, three technical issues
069
      must be solved.
070   (1) How to develop frequency-based contrastive augmentations to obtain clustering results?
071   Despite the universal importance of frequency information in time-series and its pivotal role in
072   classic signal processing (Soklaski et al., 2022), it is seldom explored in contrastive clustering for
073   time-series data (Tonekaboni et al., 2022). This is because even a slight perturbation in the frequency
074   domain could lead to significant alterations in the temporal patterns of the time domain (Flandrin,
075
      1998).
076   (2) How to quantify uncertainty in clustering results from the time and frequency domains?
077   Quantifying the uncertainty is practical for enhancing the performance of such a “two-view” time-
078   series clustering, where the higher the uncertainty of a particular domain, the lower the weight
079   contributing to the final result. Further, the uncertainty of every domain varies for different time-
080   series samples.
081   (3) How to formulate and optimize the fusion of clustering results with uncertainty from the
082   time and frequency domains? Fusion of the clustering results from the two domains belongs to
083   the late fusion (Wang et al., 2019; Liu et al., 2018), most of which primarily cater to scenarios
084   without any uncertainty (Wang et al., 2021). In our model, the fusion module assumes the critical
085   responsibility of effectively managing uncertainty, all integrated seamlessly within an end-to-end
086   framework.
087   Contributions. We introduce a trusted clustering model (named TIC) for time-series data, integrat-
088   ing results from the time and frequency domains within an interactive learning paradigm (shown in
089   Fig.1). In summary, the contributions of this paper are:
090
091   • We propose to adopt novel augmentations dedicated to clustering the frequency spectrum data,
092
        through contrastive sample discrimination. This could be the first work to leverage contrastive
        augmentation in the frequency domain in a time-series clustering problem.
093
094   • We represent the clustering results for each sample from the time and frequency domains as two
095     distinct mass functions (Shafer, 1976), where the sample-specific uncertainty is accurately quan-
096
        tified within the framework of evidence theory (DEMPSTER, 1967). The Dirichlet distribution is
        used to mathematize the mass function and to model the class probability distribution from each
097
        domain.
098
099   • We propose an interactive framework for time-series clustering, which could be inspiring in mul-
100     tiple research areas of time-series. The trusted result (obtained by combining mass functions from
101
        the time and frequency domains) and pseudo-labels (derived from k-means) contribute to each
        other, allowing interactive model optimization.

312   Remark 2. More intuitions about the interactive process. In Fig.2, we show the three main com-
313   ponents in TIC model. The embedding learned affects the trusted clustering result when minimizing
314   the contrastive loss LTime and LFreq . The pseudo-labels are produced by inputting the embeddings
315
      into k-means and are considered when minimizing LInte . In this way, the trusted clustering result
      affects the embedding learning when performing backward propagation. This allows the parameter
316
      learning of FC layers and encoders to contribute to each other interactively. Besides, considering
317
      the interactive loss avoids the class collision issue, where each sample is identified as a cluster in
318   the embedding space (shown in Fig.3(a)) (Arora et al., 2019). This is because every positive pair
319   consists only of the sample and its augmentation, without considering any other samples that may
320   belong to the same cluster, when calculating the contrastive losses. The pseudo-labels in interac-
321   tive loss are generated by k-means, where the concatenated embeddings [gTime ; gFreq ] are treated as
322   the input. Therefore, some basic information about clusters is included in the interactive loss and
323   improves the clustering performance (shown in Fig.3(b)). We demonstrate quantitatively the above
      conclusion in Fig.4(b).

381   to guarantee that xTime and xFreq have the same number of dimensions, when transforming the time
382   domain to frequency domain. The Adam optimizer with a learning rate of {0.0001, 0.0002, 0.0003}

      5          C ONCLUSION
531
532
      This paper proposes a trusted and interactive clustering model for time-series data, named TIC,
533
      leveraging evidence theory to combine time- and frequency-based information. TIC optimizes the
534   contrastive loss from time and frequency domains, and an interactive loss calculated based on the
535   pseudo-labels. Uncertainty in time- and frequency-based clustering results are quantified by mass
536   functions that are combined by Dempster’s rule to produce the trusted clustering results. Experimen-
537   tal results show the superior clustering performance of TIC brought by the combination of clustering
538   results from time and frequency domains, as well as the consideration of interactive loss. The em-
539   bedding learned by TIC is also shown to perform well on the classification and anomaly detection

767   A.1     R ELATED WORK
768
      We review time-series clustering methods, contrastive learning methods toward time-series data and
769
      uncertain learning methods, respectively.
770
771
      A.1.1    T IME - SERIES CLUSTERING
772
773   Time-series clustering methods can be roughly classified into two families: raw-data-based methods
774   and feature-based methods.
775
      Raw-data-based methods. These methods primarily modify the distance metric to accommodate
776   the specific characteristics of time-series data. K-DBA (Petitjean et al., 2011) combines k-means and
777   dynamic time warping (DTW) (Itakura, 1975) to achieve improved alignment. To reveal the tempo-
778   ral dynamics, K-SC method (Yang & Leskovec, 2011) utilizes a similarity metric that is invariant to
779   scaling and shifting. K-Shape (Paparrizos & Gravano, 2015) considers the shapes of time-series by
780   employing a normalized cross-correlation metric. The mentioned methods often exhibit sensitivity
781   to outliers and noise because they consider all time-step points (Ma et al., 2019b).
782   Feature-based methods. Feature-based methods typically involve two stages, where the input time-
783   series samples are transformed into informative features first and clustering algorithms are then con-
784   ducted on these features (Tang et al., 2021; Fortuin et al., 2020). In conjunction with k-means, TNC
785   (Tonekaboni et al., 2022) ensures the distribution of signals from the neighborhood is distinguish-
786   able from the distribution of non-neighboring signals. Authors in (Tang et al., 2021) map the raw
787   time-series space into multiple kernel spaces via elastic distance measure functions and resort to
788   a self-paced learning paradigm to group time-series samples. Authors in (Péalat et al., 2023) em-
789   bed the time-series onto the Stiefel manifold to obtain the geometric representations of time-series
790
      samples. STCN (Ma et al., 2022) optimizes the feature extraction and clustering simultaneously,
      through a recurrent neural network and a self-supervised clustering module. However, almost all the
791
      feature-based methods do not adopt an information fusion perspective to incorporate both time and
792
      frequency domain information for the purpose of clustering.
793
794
      A.1.2    C ONTRASTIVE LEARNING TOWARD TIME - SERIES DATA
795
796   Contrastive learning is a well-known form of self-supervised learning and aims to train an encoder
797   that maps original inputs into an embedding space. The objective is to bring positive sample pairs
798   closer together, while pushing negative sample pairs (comprising the original augmentation and
799   an alternative augmentation of a different input sample) apart (Chen et al., 2020). Compared to
800   CV (Changpinyo et al., 2021) and NLP (Devlin et al., 2018), contrastive learning in the context of
801
      time-series data has been less explored, mainly due to the difficulty of capturing crucial invariance
      properties specific to time-series. TF-C (Zhang et al., 2022) expects that time-based and frequency-
802
      based representations of the same sample are located close together in the time-frequency space, and
803
      embeds the time-based neighborhood of a sample close to its frequency-based neighborhood. BTSF
804   (Yang & Hong, 2022) utilizes sample-level augmentation with a dropout on a time-series sample,
805   and devises the iterative bilinear temporal-spectral fusion to generate discriminative embeddings.
806   CoST (Woo et al., 2021) comprises both time domain and frequency domain contrastive losses to
807   learn seasonal representations for long sequence time-series forecasting. In addition to these three
808   methods involving both time and frequency domains, other methods mainly focus on the augmen-
809   tations implemented in time domain, such as transformation invariance (e.g., SimCLR (Tang et al.,
      2020; Chen et al., 2020)) and contextual invariance (e.g., TS2vec (Yue et al., 2022) and TS-TCC


                                                       15
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
      (Eldele et al., 2021)). In previous works, the loss information from the time- and frequency-domain
811   is captured in a compositional way, e.g., TF-C simply sums the loss functions from the two domains
812   and the consistency loss function, and BTSF solely implements the data augmentation in the time do-
813   main. To the best of our knowledge, this work is the first one that directly combines time-frequency
814   domain information to leverage information fusion for time-series clustering.
815
816   A.1.3     U NCERTAINTY- BASED LEARNING
817
818   Some efforts (Gal & Ghahramani, 2016; Lakshminarayanan et al., 2017; Charpentier et al., 2020)
819   have been made to enable the neural network to estimate the uncertainty of the output. Evidential
820
      network (Sensoy et al., 2018) incorporates subjective logic to model the Dirichlet distribution. Post-
      Net (Malinin et al., 2019) utilizes normalizing flow and Bayesian loss during training to estimate
821
      uncertainty. TMC (Han et al., 2021) and ETMC (Han et al., 2022) introduce a variational Dirich-
822
      let distribution to characterize the distribution of the class probabilities in multi-view classification.
823   Ensemble distribution distillation (Malinin et al., 2019) leverages the predictions of multiple mod-
824   els to estimate the uncertainty. Authors in (Kopetzki et al., 2021) apply median smoothing to the
825   Dirichlet model and enhance the capability of the model to handle adversarial examples. Unlike the
826   above methods, our method is perhaps the first attempt to estimate the uncertainty of the outputs
827   from a neural network oriented to the clustering problem. Further, we fuse the output with uncer-
828   tainty estimation from the time and frequency domains to obtain trusted clustering results under the
829   framework of evidence theory.

872   A.4   D ESCRIPTION OF BASELINES
873
874   Baselines. To answer Q1, we consider the following 10 time-series clustering methods in the com-
875   parison experiment.
876
      • DTCR (Ma et al., 2019b): it integrates the temporal reconstruction and k-means objective into the
877
        seq2seq model. By proposing a fake-sample generation strategy and auxiliary classification task,
878     it can learn cluster-specific temporal representations.
879
880
      • k-shape (Paparrizos & Gravano, 2015): it relies on a scalable iterative refinement procedure and
        uses a normalized cross-correlation measure to consider the shapes of time-series.
881
882   • SOM-VAE (Fortuin et al., 2020): it overcomes the non-differentiability in discrete representa-
883     tion learning and presents a gradient-based version of the traditional self-organizing map (SOM)
884
        algorithm.
885   • STCN (Ma et al., 2022): it optimizes the feature extraction and clustering simultaneously, where
886     an RNN conducts the reconstruction of time-series and a self-supervised module to obtain the
887     clustering result.
888   • TMEK (Tang et al., 2021): it maps the raw time-series space into multiple kernel spaces via
889     elastic distance measure functions, and resorts to the tensor-constraint-based self-representation
890     subspace clustering approach.
891   • TNC (Tonekaboni et al., 2022): it uses the local smoothness of a signal’s generative process to
892     define neighborhoods in time-series. By using a de-biased contrastive objective, it learns time-
893     series representations that are input to k-means to produce the clusteri.ng results.
894
      • TS3Cch (Guijo-Rubio et al., 2021): it consists of two stages, where a least squares polynomial
895     technique is first used to segment the time-series and the hierarchical clustering is applied to all

1117   We consider three types of change in frequency-based augmentations. (1) We explore the number of
1118   components manipulated, where the results are shown by the sub-table titled by Components in Table
1119   9. One can find that the performance of TIC model tends to deteriorate when perturbing multiple
1120   frequency components. This degradation is attributed to the substantial changes in the time-domain
1121
       of augmented samples. Consequently, these augmented samples become readily distinguishable by a
       contrastive module, resulting in suboptimal contrastive encoders. (2) We then explore the adjustment
1122
       size of amplitude in sub-table Amplitude of Table 9. As can be seen, manipulating the amplitude does
1123
       not significantly affect the performance of TIC model. (3) In sub-table Bands of Table 9, we explore
1124   the band that the manipulated component belongs to. The Low-/high-frequency band corresponds
1125   to the first/second half of the frequency spectrum, and contributes to slow/fast variations in the
1126   time domain. In a physiological time-series dataset (e.g., SleepEEG), the low band contains most
1127   information and manipulating a low-frequency component leads to higher ARI. On the contrary,
1128   high-band components are more informative than low-band ones in a mechanical time-series dataset
1129   (e.g., FD-A). Thus, perturbing high-band components outperform low-band augmentations.
