011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            We present UncertaintyRAG, a novel approach for long-context Retrieval-
014            Augmented Generation (RAG) that utilizes Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR)-based
015            span uncertainty to estimate similarity between text chunks. This span uncertainty
016
               enhances model calibration, improving robustness and mitigating semantic incon-
               sistencies introduced by random chunking. Leveraging this insight, we propose
017
               an efficient unsupervised learning technique to train the retrieval model, alongside
018
               an effective data sampling and scaling strategy. UncertaintyRAG outperforms
019            baselines by 2.03% on LLaMA-2-7B, achieving state-of-the-art results while using
020            only 4% of the training data compared to other advanced open-source retrieval
021            models under distribution shift settings. Our method demonstrates strong calibra-
022            tion through span uncertainty, leading to improved generalization and robustness
023            in long-context RAG tasks. Additionally, UncertaintyRAG provides a lightweight
024            retrieval model that can be integrated into any large language model with varying
025            context window lengths, without the need for fine-tuning, showcasing the flexibility
026            of our approach.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Large Language Models (LLMs) have shown impressive capabilities across various natural language
032   tasks, including long-context question-answering (QA), where the model processes a lengthy text
033
      and a question to generate a response (Caciularu et al., 2022). The QA paradigm encompasses a
      wide range of tasks, such as commonsense reasoning (Yang et al., 2018; Geva et al., 2021) and
034
      mathematical reasoning (Cobbe et al., 2021; Amini et al., 2019). However, due to the limitations in
035
      computational resources and the model’s lack of ability to extrapolate context length, handling long-
036   context settings remains a challenge for large language models, although continuous improvements
037   are being made in this area.
038
039
      Recent advances seek to overcome this limitation by designing linear attention mecha-
      nisms (Katharopoulos et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2020; Gu & Dao, 2023), which improve the memory
040
      and time efficiency for long sequences. Pruning the KV cache (Ge et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024;
041
      Agarwal et al., 2024) and quantizing the KV cache (Hooper et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024) are other
042   approaches to enhancing long context generation by compressing the model’s KV-cache, providing a
043   training-free, lightweight solution to reduce memory and computational overhead. However, these
044   methods are generally difficult to achieve context length extrapolation. Moreover, the aforementioned
045   approaches usually truncate the long-context to just fit the context window of LLMs, which not only
046   prevents LLMs from actually seeing the entire input text but also faces an unacceptable memory
047   overhead in resource-constrained environments. The efficient positional encoding strategy (Liu et al.,
048   2023a; Su et al., 2024) is a solution that can achieve length extrapolation, but it often requires training
049   the entire LLMs.
050   Another lightweight solution for handling long contexts is to utilize long-context Retrieval-Augmented
051   Generation (RAG) in conjunction with long-context chunking (Xu et al., 2023b; Jiang et al., 2024b;
052   Sarthi et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2023a), which avoids the need for LLMs to have length extrapolation
053   capability. Long-context retrieval-augmented generation refers to a method in natural language
      processing where a model retrieves relevant information from large external sources to assist in


                                                          1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      generating responses. It extends the traditional RAG by handling much longer input contexts, enabling
055   effective processing of broader and more detailed information for tasks like question answering,
056   summarization, and document understanding. Typically, RAG employs existing LLMs with limited
057   context windows to retrieve relevant chunks. These chunks are obtained by retrieval models, either
058   with or without semantic truncation (Sarthi et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024a; Jiang et al., 2024b; Xu et al.,
059   2023b;a). Retrieval models (Izacard et al., 2021; Xiao et al., 2023b; Chen et al., 2024; Lin et al.,
060   2023a) are commonly used for this purpose; however, they require a large amount of high-quality
061   labeled data for training, which limits their scalability and adaptability. Modern RAG systems may
062   rely on complex chunking methods (Sarthi et al., 2024) and require LLMs to have relatively long
063
      context windows (Jiang et al., 2024b; Xu et al., 2024). Furthermore, the lack of labeled data to
      determine if (query, chunk) pairs are related (Lewis et al., 2020a;b) poses significant limitations
064
      for training retrieval models in RAG systems. Recent research combines RAG with long-context
065
      LLMs to handle extended contexts and mitigate semantic incoherence in chunk processing (Xu et al.,
066   2023b; Jiang et al., 2024b; Xu et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024c; Luo et al., 2024; Sarthi et al., 2024;
067   Duarte et al., 2024). Some of the latest work also attempts to inject retrieval capabilities into LLMs
068   through training, enabling them to process and generate long contexts directly (He et al., 2024; Cheng
069   et al., 2024). However, these approaches often require either sophisticated chunking strategies, which
070   are time-consuming during inference, or fine-tuning adapters for specific LLMs to manage chunk
071   representation compression. Additionally, the complexity of these methods makes them vulnerable to
072   distribution shifts. Some methods also necessitate training an LLM with an extended context window
073   or new architecture, which is highly resource-intensive. In contrast, improving a lightweight retrieval
074
      model that can be seamlessly integrated into various LLMs without additional training would be a
      more efficient solution.
075
076   Unlike previous work, our focus is on utilizing calibrated uncertainty quantification to estimate
077   similarity between chunks, thereby training robust retrieval models within RAG. Specifically, we
078   introduce a novel uncertainty estimation technique based on the Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SNR), which
079   stabilizes predictions and reduces biases from random chunk splitting. Our analysis shows that
080
      when two chunks are concatenated and fed into the model to estimate similarity, the uncertainty
      measured by SNR can better reflect their alignment in the semantic space, which we have confirmed
081
      in our experiments. Building on this finding, we develop an unsupervised learning technique to
082
      train chunk embeddings. This method is decoupled from the long-context modeling capabilities of
083   LLMs and can be adapted to any fixed-length context window, enhancing retrieval robustness without
084   requiring additional fine-tuning or retraining of the LLM itself. By leveraging the LLM’s calibrated
085   self-information, we effectively measure similarity between text chunks, construct accurate positive
086   and negative samples, and train a robust retrieval model. This approach enhances generalization under
087   distribution shifts in long-context retrieval-augmented generation tasks. Moreover, it seamlessly
088   integrates with existing methods by relying solely on external retrieval models, thus avoiding LLM-
089   specific performance dependencies. Specifically, our contributions are as follows:
090          1. We propose a novel SNR uncertainty measurement technique to achieve better calibration
091             by addressing prediction errors arising from random chunk splitting, thereby improving
092             performance in similarity estimation between chunks.
093          2. We propose an unsupervised learning approach and train a retrieval model that outperforms
094             strong open-source embedding models in long-context RAG tasks under distribution-shift
095             settings.
096          3. We design an efficient data sampling strategy to scale data, enhancing our retrieval model
097
                training and significantly boosting performance. Compared to models such as BGE-M3, our
                method improves LLaMA-2-7B by 2.03% after SNR calibration while using only 4% of
098
                their data size.
099
100         4. We provide an in-depth analysis of the retrieval model, demonstrating continuous improve-
101            ments across two key metrics. We explain how uncertainty measurement enhances chunk
102
               representation modeling and why data sample scaling contributes to improved performance.

105   2.1   ATTENTION M ECHANISMS IN L ONG C ONTEXTS
106
107   An effective approach to facilitating long context is to avoid the O(n2 ) computational complexity
      of the standard attention mechanism by designing linear attention mechanisms, sparse attention


                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      mechanisms, or low-rank attention mechanisms. These works can be categorized into the following
109   four types: i) Sparse Attention Mechanisms: Reduce the computational burden of attention by
110   exploiting inherent patterns within the attention mechanism (Jiang et al., 2024a; Ribar et al., 2023;
111   Chen et al., 2023), or alternatively, by pruning the KV cache (Liu et al., 2023b; Xiao et al., 2023a;
112   Pang et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024). ii) The attention mechanism with linear complexity: This
113   typically involves transforming models with O(n2 ) complexity into O(n) or O(n log n) linear
114   attention (Zheng et al., 2022; Kitaev et al., 2020; Qin et al., 2022; Katharopoulos et al., 2020), or
115   efficient long-sequence recurrent neural networks (Gu & Dao, 2023; Dao & Gu, 2024; Peng et al.,
116   2023a; Yang et al., 2023). iii) Memory-augmented attention mechanisms: This typically involves
117
      encoding long-context text using additional memory blocks (He et al., 2024; Bertsch et al., 2024;
      Wang et al., 2024). iv) Hardware-friendly attention mechanisms: FlashAttention (Dao et al., 2022;
118
      Dao, 2023; Shah et al., 2024) accelerates precise attention computations by optimizing reads and
119
      writes across different levels of GPU memory. FlashAttention is especially effective for processing
120   longer sequences. Among previous works, memory-augmented LLMs are most relevant to this study,
121   as they involve memory retrieval. However, they typically require modifying the original model
122   architecture and retraining the LLM or fine-tuning some parameters, which can be an impractical
123   overhead in certain settings.
124
125   2.2   P OSITION E NCODING
126
127   Extending the context window from scratch is challenging (Liu et al., 2023a; Su et al., 2024) because
128   it requires significant computational resources. As a result, efficient position encoding methods (Peng
129   et al., 2023b; Li et al., 2023a; Chi et al., 2022; Press et al., 2021; Peng & Quesnelle, 2023) have
130   gained attention for improving length extrapolation. These works attempt to expand the context
131
      window on pre-trained LLMs using a small amount of data.
132   However, considering that many LLMs are closed-source, end-to-end training of LLMs with retrievers
133   is impractical. Additionally, fully fine-tuning LLMs on long-context data remains costly. Fully fine-
134   tuning models such as LLaMA (Touvron et al., 2023), especially with sequences longer than 16,000
135   tokens, is prohibitively expensive due to the quadratic time and memory complexities associated
136   with precise attention mechanisms. Therefore, extending the model’s context window by continual
137
      training through position encoding remains unacceptable.
138   Recent RAG systems (Xu et al., 2023b; 2024) combining RAG with long context LLMs have utilized
139   position encoding to fine-tune LLMs for context window extrapolation, either requiring LLMs to
140   handle retrieved chunks as long-context inputs (Jiang et al., 2024b; Xu et al., 2024) or flexibly
141   combining the strengths of both through a routing mechanism (Li et al., 2024c). These works consider
142   combining the strengths of long context LLMs and RAG. They demonstrate that, when resources
143
      are abundant, long context LLMs consistently outperform RAG in terms of average performance.
      However, the significantly reduced cost of RAG remains a notable advantage. They also find that long-
144
      context window LLMs can effectively alleviate the context fragmentation issue in RAG. However,
145
      further extending the context window of LLMs still is challenging, as it requires a significant amount
146   of computational resources. Therefore, how to expand the boundaries of RAG systems given the
147   limited context window of LLMs remains a question worth exploring. Our work primarily focuses
148   on optimizing the retrieval model in RAG, given the context window length of LLMs, to extend the
149   boundaries of the RAG system’s ability to handle long contexts.
150
151   2.3   R ETRIEVAL -AUGMENTED G ENERATION
152
153   The existing RAG frameworks heavily rely on the quality of the retrieval model. Due to retrieval
154   model’s context window limitations, they often tend to use short retrieval units in open-domain QA
155   tasks (Lewis et al., 2020a; Karpukhin et al., 2020; Ni et al., 2021). These models rely on a bi-encoder
156   and typically require manual query-passage annotations for training the encoder.
157   Moreover, some studies (Duarte et al., 2024; Luo et al., 2024; Sarthi et al., 2024; Li et al., 2023b)
158   highlight the importance of appropriately chunking the input text for RAG systems. Complex
159   chunking schemes, however, add inference complexity and increase latency. Therefore, a simpler
160   chunking method is necessary to avoid this issue, ensuring robustness to different chunking. Existing
161   methods (Jiang et al., 2024b; Xu et al., 2024) attempt to use LLMs capable of handling longer contexts
      to increase the length of retrieved chunks, thereby reducing the number of retrieval units and avoiding


                                                        3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      the input of overly fragmented and incomplete information. This, in turn, alleviates the burden on
163   the retrieval model in processing long contexts. Recently, a study (Luo et al., 2024) also proposes a
164   three-stage fine-tuning approach to embed longer contexts into a special token, addressing the issue of
165   semantic discontinuity caused by chunk splitting. However, their method requires complex training
166   techniques for the retrieval model, making it difficult to easily scale the data size.
167
      Distribution shift in RAG has also garnered attention (Li et al., 2024b; Sagirova & Burtsev, 2023).
168
      They use a calibrated model’s confidence to detect "long-tailness" examples and implement an
169   improved RAG pipeline. However, to address distribution shifts in the chunks of input, instance-level
170   uncertainty estimation provides limited assistance to RAG, as it does not help LLMs retrieve long-tail
171   knowledge. Additionally, their work focuses solely on improving the pipeline without enhancing the
172   performance of the retrieval model itself.
173
      In summary, the above work has the following issues: i) Complex Chunking Overhead: Complex
174
      chunking methods that may increase additional computational overhead during inference. ii) High
175   Cost of Labeled Data: Requires labeled data between chunks and queries. This is usually costly
176   in terms of extensive manual annotation effort for long-context retrieval tasks. iii) Poor Handling
177   of Semantic Incoherence: Open-source embedding models often struggle to handle semantically
178   incoherent chunks, making it difficult to generalize in long-context settings.
179
      To address the aforementioned issues, we focus on facilitating representation learning in RAG under
180
      a simple long-context chunking setting. Considering the additional overhead introduced by complex
181   chunking methods during the inference stage, we adopt a simple strategy of dividing chunks every
182   300 letters and develop an unsupervised learning technique to train a robust retrieval model capable
183   of handling semantic discontinuities in this chunking approach under distribution shift scenarios.
184   Additionally, we propose a method called span uncertainty measurement to construct training labels
185   for the data and compare it with existing open-source embedding models (Izacard et al., 2021; Xiao
186   et al., 2023b; Chen et al., 2024; Lin et al., 2023a) within the RAG system. Typically, these embedding
187   models require extensive data for pre-training; however, we outperform them in distribution shift
188   scenarios using far less training data than they require.

273   for all chunks in the training dataset. We do not consider incorporating the combinations of queries
274   and chunks into the training strategy, yet the model still performs well. Then, we introduce a strategy

279   Given the limitations in inference computational efficiency, we avoid using overly complex segmenta-

      3.2.2    DATA S CALING S TRATEGY
298
299   All our experiments are conducted under distribution shift setting, meaning our retrieval model
300   needs to handle semantically disjointed chunks resulting from segmented text, as well as noisy
301   data arising from long-tail distributions that are never encountered in the trainning dataset. Our
302   span uncertainty effectively calibrates the model’s uncertainty measurement using the SNR metric,
303   identifying meaningful output probabilities to construct an anchor-positive-negative sample dataset.
304   Anchor Sample Scaling Strategy We refer to each chunk as an anchor sample. We initially merge
305   the five datasets: HotpotQA (Yang et al., 2018), MultiFieldQA (Bai et al., 2023), Qasper (Dasigi
306   et al., 2021), NarrativeQA (Kočiskỳ et al., 2018), and QMSum (Zhong et al., 2021). Each dataset is
307   split based on spaces, and every 300 letters are grouped into a chunk. As a result, we obtain 37,799,
308   14,547, 15,865, 72,146, and 38,398 chunks, respectively. Due to computational limitations, it is
309   challenging to score all possible combinations of such a large number of chunks. Therefore, we first
310   use k-nearest neighbors (KNN) to cluster each dataset into k clusters. Then, we randomly select c
311
      chunks from each cluster, resulting in k*c chunks per dataset. By mixing the five datasets, we obtain
      a total of 50*c chunks. By increasing the multiple of c, we scale the total number of anchor samples.
312
313   Positive and Negative Sample Scaling Strategy When a sample anchor chi is selected, we need
314   to concatenate it with other chunks and input them into the LLM for scoring. Due to the high
315
      computational cost, we employ a method similar to anchor sample scaling. First, using the KNN
      method, we randomly sample n samples chj from each of the k clusters, where the clusters are formed
316
      from the previously mentioned 50∗c chunks. This way, we obtain 10∗n samples in a single sampling
317
      process. Then, we use BM25 to score the anchor and each sample, sBM25 (chi , chj ), and select the
318
      final M samples. These are scored using LLMs to obtain the final chi + and chi − . Finally, we obtain
319   a triplet (chi , chi + , chi − ). By repeating this sampling process, we can scale the number of positive
320   and negative samples for the anchor chi .

337   3.3     M ODEL I NFERENCE
338
339   Due to our model’s focus on using model uncertainty to promote the unsupervised learning aspect of
340   retrieval models, after training and obtaining the embedding model, we can input long-contexts to
341
      the embedding model for retrieval after every 300-letters chunk, without re-rank part in our pipeline.

342
      chunk the input document based on a fixed length, which is efficient, without using complex chunking
343
      mechanisms. ii) Input the query to the retrieval model to retrieve the most similar m chunks. iii) Input
344   the retrieved m chunks and the query to the model to produce the final answer.

394   4.1   M AIN R ESULT
395
      As shown in Table 1, the following observations can be made: i): Robust RAG retrieval model
396   can significantly improve the performance of the 4K context window LLMs; however, this heavily
397   depends on the performance of the retrieval model. ii): A powerful retrieval model can enhance
398   the performance of LLMs. Vicuna-7B and LLaMA-2-7B-Chat-HF perform similarly within their
399   4K context windows, with an average performance difference of about 1.93% without retrieval
400   augmentation. When equipped with a robust retrieval model, the average performance gap can
401   increase to 6.14%. iii): Our method achieves the highest average performance, surpassing some open-
402   source embedding models trained on large datasets. However, performance is limited on few-shot
403   learning tasks like TREC due to the need for precise segmentation of in-context exemplars. Still, our
404
      method shows a consistent 2% improvement over the baseline. In addition, we have the following
      important findings.
405
406   Analysis of The Impact of Span Uncertainty through SNR Calibration In Table 1, our analysis
407
      reveals that the span uncertainty method, following SNR calibration, demonstrates significant im-
      provements compared to both All Chunking and Precise Chunking. This enhancement is primarily
408
      due to the incorporation of self-information from certain tokens in the first chunk, which comes from
409
      the flexible sampling of spans across the two chunks by SNR. Thus, we can draw the following
410   conclusions. i): SNR calibration effectively captures the similarity between the two chunks. ii): The
411   tokens within the span sampled by SNR play a crucial role in assessing the relationship between the
412   two chunks, suggesting that future research could benefit from focusing on span-level uncertainty.
413
      Ablation Study on The Effects of Scaling Up the Data In Table 2, we present the results of
414   scaling the anchor data in our dataset, fixing the anchor while scaling the positive/negative sample
415   data, and scaling both the anchor and positive/negative samples simultaneously. Due to limitations in
416   computational resources, we only doubled the size of the data. i): By simultaneously doubling both
417   the anchor and positive/negative samples after fixing the anchor, we achieved state-of-the-art results,
418   demonstrating the scalability of our method with respect to data size. ii): Our dataset sampling
419   method is simple and easy to use, and applicable to any text dataset that can be chunked.
420   Ablation Study on the Length and Number of Retrieved Chunks In Table 3, we evaluate the
421   Vicuna-7B-16K model on the 2WikiMultihopQA dataset, varying the number of chunks while keeping
422   chunk length constant, and adjusting the chunk length while keeping the number of chunks constant.
423   i): We find that increasing the number of chunks is not effective; instead, increasing the chunk length
424   itself yields better results. ii): However, simply increasing the chunk size is not a one-size-fits-all
425   solution, as it can also harm performance, which is contrary to the findings of LongRAG (Jiang et al.,
426
      2024b).

      Uncertainty Calibration Evaluation
518   In this part, we use the AUROC (Lin et al., 2023b; Huang et al., 2024) metric as the evaluation
519   standard for the uncertainty calibration of LLMs. Referring to their method, we introduce an ad
520   hoc threshold τ ∈ R to map the real-valued output of the deterministic correctness function to
521   binary labels, i.e., Fτ (x, ŷ) = 1[F1 (x, ŷ) ≥ τ ], where x represents the model’s input chunks and ŷ
522   represents the model’s sampled output. F1 represents the accuracy assessment of the model’s output ŷ.
523   Specifically, if the correctness function F1 exceeds the threshold τ , it is considered correct; otherwise,
524   it is considered incorrect. Fτ is regarded as the final binary classification result where the model is
525
      confident. It is used to compare with SU (ch1 , ..., chi , query, ŷ) to obtain the final AUROC. The final
      results are presented in Fig 5, where we can see that the calibration of our uncertainty measures is
526
      significantly better than other methods. Both results are evaluated on the Llama-2-7b-chat-hf model
527
      using the TriviaQA (Joshi et al., 2017) dataset. The detailed introduction of the pipeline can be found
528   in Appendix G.

530   5     C ONCLUSION
531
      In conclusion, UncertaintyRAG introduces a novel SNR-based span uncertainty approach to improve
532
      calibration in long-context Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG). The method outperforms pow-
533   erful open-source embedding models like BGE-M3 while using only 4% of the training data. By
534   leveraging span uncertainty, UncertaintyRAG promotes unsupervised learning, reducing the need
535   for large labeled datasets, making it scalable and efficient. The results show that even with minimal
536   data, UncertaintyRAG achieves superior performance and generalization under distribution shift
537   scenarios. This lightweight solution requires no fine-tuning and integrates seamlessly into any LLM,
538   making it versatile for various long-context tasks. Looking forward, UncertaintyRAG paves the
539   way for optimizing retrieval models through advanced uncertainty quantification, particularly in
      resource-constrained environments.

813   A    T RAINING DATASET
814
815
816   In this work, we construct a new dataset from five existing datasets that include single-document QA,
817
      multi-document QA, and summarization tasks to train our dense retriever. All datasets are sourced
      from LongBench(Bai et al., 2023).
818
819   HotpotQA(Yang et al., 2018) is a challenging benchmark for multi-hop question answering, requiring
820   models to retrieve and integrate information from multiple documents to arrive at the correct answer.
821   It is specifically designed to evaluate a model’s capacity for compositional reasoning, as questions
822   often necessitate combining evidence from disparate sources. Additionally, HotpotQA includes
823
      supporting fact annotations, which serve to explain the reasoning process, making it suitable for tasks
      involving both factual retrieval and logical inference in natural language understanding.
824
825   MultiFieldQA-en(Bai et al., 2023) is manually curated to better evaluate the model’s ability to
826   understand long contexts across various fields. The documents contain evidence from multiple
827   sources, such as legal documents, government reports, encyclopedias, and academic papers, which
828   are randomly distributed to prevent biases that might arise from their placement at the beginning or
829
      end of the documents.
830   Qasper(Dasigi et al., 2021) is a question-answering dataset specifically tailored for academic papers
831   in the field of natural language processing. It consists of questions generated by annotators who
832   read full research papers and pose queries that can be answered using the information within the
833   paper. The dataset is designed to evaluate a model’s ability to perform non-factoid QA on scientific
834   texts, involving tasks such as understanding methodologies, interpreting results, and synthesizing
835
      information from complex, domain-specific content.
836   NarrativeQA(Kočiskỳ et al., 2018) is a well-known question-answering dataset that includes full
837   books from Project Gutenberg and movie scripts sourced from various websites. In this task, the
838   provided passage is transcribed from books and often contains noise. The model’s job is to generate a
839   concise phrase by reasoning through the lengthy and noisy text.
840   QMSum(Zhong et al., 2021) is a query-based summarization dataset that includes transcripts of
841   meetings and their corresponding summaries across various domains, including academia and in-
842   dustrial products. In this task, a meeting dialogue transcript is provided along with a question that
843   prompts summarization of a specific topic within the dialogue. The answers typically consist of a few
844   sentences.
845   And then we test on five dataset:
846
847   2WikiMultihopQA(Ho et al., 2020) is a benchmark for multi-hop question answering, which requires
848
      models to perform reasoning over multiple pieces of information to answer a single question. It
      contains questions that are designed to require information from multiple Wikipedia articles to answer
849
      correctly, promoting a more complex and connected reasoning process.
850
851   Musique(Trivedi et al., 2022) is a dataset designed for multi-hop question answering. Unlike
852   HotpotQA, Musique demands more integrated reasoning by reducing possible shortcuts in reasoning,
853   minimizing overlap between training and test data, and featuring more challenging distractor contexts.
854
      As a result, Musique is a significantly more difficult task than HotpotQA and is much less susceptible
      to shortcuts.
855
856   TREC(Li & Roth, 2002) is a widely used dataset for evaluating information retrieval and semantic
857   search systems, encompassing documents from multiple domains. The answers are assessed for their
858   relevance and accuracy based on predefined criteria, providing a robust framework for evaluating the
859   performance of retrieval and question-answering models.
860   TriviaQA(Joshi et al., 2017) is a widely used question-answering dataset that provides complex,
861   real-world questions paired with relevant documents from sources like Wikipedia and web pages. It
862   is designed to test a model’s ability to locate and understand the information needed to answer the
863   questions, making it suitable for tasks such as machine reading comprehension and open-domain
      question answering.

884   C    BASELINES
885
886   There has been extensive research on Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG), with one critical aspect
887   being the development of a robust representation model that effectively clusters sentence information
888   with query information in high-dimensional space. In our work, we compare our approach with
889   several influential and open-source methods from prior studies. This section provides a detailed
890
      overview of these methods.
891   Contriever(Izacard et al., 2021) is a dense retrieval model designed to improve performance and
892   generalization through unsupervised training on diverse datasets. It uses contrastive learning to
893   generate embeddings, bringing semantically related query-document pairs closer together while
894   pushing irrelevant pairs apart. This method allows Contriever to effectively handle tasks like semantic
895   search and question answering, even in zero-shot scenarios. By relying on unsupervised learning, it
896
      adapts well to various domains without needing annotated data, making it versatile for information
      retrieval tasks.
897
898   BGE(Xiao et al., 2023b) is a large-scale multilingual text embedding model developed by the Beijing
899   Academy of Artificial Intelligence (BAAI), designed for tasks such as semantic retrieval and ranking
900   across multiple languages. Pre-trained on extensive multilingual datasets, BGE encodes text into
901   dense vector representations optimized for semantic similarity tasks, where the relationship between
902
      query and document pairs is captured by comparing their vector embeddings.
903   The model is primarily trained using contrastive learning, which teaches it to minimize the distance
904   between embeddings of relevant query-document pairs while maximizing the distance for irrelevant
905   pairs. BGE embeddings are applied in dense retrieval, which focuses on understanding the semantic
906   meaning of text rather than relying solely on keyword matching. This makes BGE especially effective
907   for tasks that demand high precision in matching semantically similar content, such as cross-lingual
908
      or multilingual retrieval scenarios.
909   BGE-M3(Chen et al., 2024) is an advanced extension of the BGE model, designed to support
910   multi-modal retrieval by integrating dense, lexical, and multi-vector retrieval methods. This hybrid
911   approach allows BGE-M3 to tackle a broader range of retrieval tasks by combining precise keyword
912   matching with deeper semantic analysis. The training process of BGE-M3 employs self-distillation,
913   where intermediate layers are optimized to rank query-document pairs, enhancing both efficiency
914
      and flexibility. By leveraging a combination of sparse and dense embeddings, BGE-M3 is highly
      effective for large-scale, multilingual retrieval tasks.
915
916   Dragon(Lin et al., 2023a) is a dense retrieval model designed to enhance performance and general-
917   ization in dense retrieval tasks by incorporating diverse data augmentation techniques. This model is
      notable for its robust handling of both supervised and zero-shot environments, utilizing innovative

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


918
      methods to manage various query types and relevance labels. By introducing multiple forms of data
919   augmentation—specifically for queries and labels—it effectively generalizes to unseen data.

1114
1115   Now, assume that we have two chunks:
1116
1117          • Chunk A: How do you do?
1118          • Chunk B: Thank you for taking the time to review our manuscript.
1119
1120   Here, for the sake of convenience, we treat each word or punctuation mark as a token.
1121
       In this case, Chunk A consists of 5 tokens, and we can obtain the probabilities for these 5 tokens.
1122
       Similarly, Chunk B consists of 11 tokens, for which we also have their probabilities. By concatenating
1123   these probabilities, we get:
1124
1125                                  PA1 , PA2 , . . . , PA5 , PB1 , PB2 , . . . , PB11 .
1126
1127   Assume the sliding window size is set to 3, with a stride of 1 (used here for clearer demonstration).
1128   We first calculate the SNR within the window:
1129                                                (PB9 , PB10 , PB11 )
1130
1131   and find that it is less than the set threshold.
1132   We then slide the window one step to the left and calculate:
1133
                                                    (PB8 , PB9 , PB10 ),

1134
       and continue this process iteratively.

1137                                            (PA4 , PA5 , PB1 )

1139   exceeds the threshold. At this point, the sliding window stops.
1140   We then take the probabilities of all tokens from the previous sliding windows (i.e., PA5 to PB11 )

1142   forming positive and negative samples for subsequent contrastive learning training.
