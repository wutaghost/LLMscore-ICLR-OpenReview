009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Contrastive learning is predominantly deterministic, limiting its effectiveness in
012            noisy and uncertain environments. We propose a probabilistic contrastive learn-
013            ing framework inspired by the von Mises-Fisher (vMF) distribution, embedding
014            representations on a hyperspherical space. To address numerical instability, we
015            introduce an unnormalized and regularized vMF distribution, preserving essential
016
               properties with theoretical guarantees. The concentration parameter, κ, serves as
               an interpretable measure of aleatoric uncertainty. Empirical evaluations show a
017
               strong correlation between estimated κ and unseen data corruption severity, en-
018
               abling effective failure analysis and enhancing out-of-distribution detection with-
019            out modeling epistemic uncertainty. From a fresh perspective, our approach in-
020            troduces a flexible alignment mechanism for improved uncertainty estimation in
021            high-dimensional spaces while remaining compatible with existing contrastive
022            learning frameworks.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027
      Self-supervised contrastive learning has significantly narrowed the gap between unsupervised and
      supervised learning across various domains, including vision (Chen et al., 2020; Chen & He, 2021;
028
      Caron et al., 2021; Zbontar et al., 2021) and multimodal learning (Hager et al., 2023). Despite
029
      these notable achievements, current methods still fall short in critical aspects necessary for decision-
030   making in high-stakes applications. In domains such as medical diagnosis (Azizi et al., 2021) and
031   autonomous driving (Kaya et al., 2022), where decisions can have serious consequences, accurately
032   estimating uncertainty, either from data or models, is essential.
033
      Traditional contrastive learning methods are predominantly deterministic and lack mechanisms to
034
      gauge uncertainty, limiting their utility in scenarios where understanding the model’s confidence
035
      is crucial. Previous attempts to incorporate uncertainty estimation have primarily utilized Gaussian
036   distributions (Kingma et al., 2015; Gal et al., 2016; Upadhyay et al., 2023), which may not align well
037   with hyperspherical contrastive representations (Bachman et al., 2019; Tian et al., 2020; He et al.,
038   2020; Chen & He, 2021). Recent research has begun exploring geometric properties of contrastive
039   representations (Wang & Isola, 2020; Wang & Liu, 2021; Ge et al., 2023), prompting a shift towards
040   probabilistic models better suited to these spaces.
041
      Probabilistic embedding approaches involve encoders generating distributions within the latent
042   space, rather than deterministic point estimates. These approaches generally fall into two cate-
043   gories: (1) transforming traditional loss functions into probabilistic formats by aggregating the loss
044   across predicted probabilistic embeddings (Scott et al., 2021; Roads & Love, 2021; Kirchhof et al.,
045   2023), and (2) employing distribution-to-distribution metrics to replace point-to-point distances in
046   loss calculations, with the Expected Likelihood Kernel (ELK) (Shi & Jain, 2019) being particu-
047   larly effective. Recently, a Monte-Carlo sampling-based InfoNCE loss (Kirchhof et al., 2023) was
048   proposed to train encoders to predict probabilistic embeddings and learn correct posteriors. De-
049   spite these innovations, these approaches face limitations such as numerical instability and implicit
050
      uncertainty modeling.
051   To address these limitations, we leverage the von Mises-Fisher (vMF) distribution (Fisher, 1953),
052   which is well-suited for data on the hypersphere and aligns closely with the intrinsic structure of
053   most contrastive learning representations. The vMF distribution is parameterized by a mean direc-
      tion µ and a concentration parameter κ, where κ controls the spread of the distribution. As shown

082   Our contributions are as follows: (1) We propose a vMF-based probabilistic contrastive learning

211   Connection between κ and uncertainty. κ serves as a key indicator of uncertainty in our frame-
212   work. High κ values signify tightly clustered embeddings, indicating low aleatoric uncertainty, while
213   low κ reflects dispersed embeddings, corresponding to higher aleatoric uncertainty arising from data
214   noise or corruption. Moreover, κ also captures epistemic uncertainty: in regions with limited data,
215   hard samples, or OOD inputs, lower κ values represent increased uncertainty, reflecting the model’s
      lack of confidence in its learned representations.

      Representation learning on the unit hypersphere has its advantages in representation quality and
240   interpretability (Nickel & Kiela, 2017; Davidson et al., 2018; Govindarajan et al., 2023). Theoretical
241   analysis has shown that such methods learn alignment and uniformity properties asymptotically
242   on the hypersphere (Wang & Isola, 2020). It has been therefore widely adopted by the popular
243   contrastive learning approaches (Bachman et al., 2019; Tian et al., 2020; He et al., 2020; Chen &
244   He, 2021). Hyperspherical latent spaces in variational autoencoders have demonstrated superior
245   performance over Euclidean counterparts (Davidson et al., 2018; Xu & Durrett, 2018).
246   Hyperspherical face embeddings have outperformed their unnormalized counterparts (Liu et al.,
247   2017; Wang et al., 2017). Recently, contrastive learning on the hypersphere has been shown effective
248   in out-of-distribution detection (Ming et al., 2022). The consistent empirical success across diverse
249   applications and nice geometric properties underscores the hypersphere’s uniqueness as a feature
250   space. In the context of our work, we extend this exploration to the realm of uncertainty estimation
251   within these hyperspherical spaces.
252   Aleatoric uncertainty is inherent in many vision problems, such as object recognition (Kendall &
253   Gal, 2017; Shi & Jain, 2019) and semantic segmentation (Monteiro et al., 2020; Kahl et al., 2024),
254   where stochasticity in image acquisition (e.g., noise and imaging artifacts) incurs uncertainties in
255   prediction. Other tasks with ambiguous input data include 3D reconstruction from 2D input (Chen
256   et al., 2021) or from noisy sensor (Meech & Stanley-Marbell, 2021).
257
      To facilitate the systematic study of aleatoric uncertainty, the widely-applied benchmark proposed
258   by Hendrycks & Dietterich (2019) quantifies the severity of data corruptions (e.g., imaging noise,
259   distortions caused by compression, etc.) into different corruption levels (Hendrycks & Dietterich,
260   2019). In this work, we demonstrate that the estimated concentration parameters κ’s closely corre-
261   late with the corruption levels.
262
      Probabilistic embedding are emerging approaches that involve encoders generating distributions
263
      within the latent space, rather than deterministic point estimates. Such approaches to probabilis-
264   tic embeddings diverge into two primary categories: The first method transforms traditional loss
265   functions into probabilistic formats by aggregating the entire loss across the spectrum of predicted
266   probabilistic embeddings (Scott et al., 2021; Roads & Love, 2021; Kirchhof et al., 2023). Another
267   strategy employs distribution-to-distribution metrics to substitute the conventional point-to-point
268   distances in loss calculations, with the Expected Likelihood Kernel (Shi & Jain, 2019) standing out
269   as a particularly effective technique. Notably, it has recently shown its efficacy even in contexts
      involving high-dimensional embedding spaces (Kirchhof et al., 2022). Recently, a Monte-Carlo


                                                        5

      sampling-based InfoNCE loss (Kirchhof et al., 2023) was proposed to train the encoder to predict
271   probabilistic embeddings and to learn the correct posteriors. In our work, we present a fresh per-
272   spective on modeling such a probabilistic embedding by introducing the unnormalized vMF and a
273   regularization term, enabling a smoother training.

      Quantifying the level of data corruption. CIFAR-10-C (Hendrycks & Dietterich, 2018) is a well-
280
      established benchmark dataset for evaluating model robustness in a controlled environment. It con-
281
      tains 18 image corruption types based on the original CIFAR-10 (Krizhevsky et al., 2009). Our
282   key assumption is that the corrupted data have higher inherent aleatoric uncertainty compared to
283   the uncorrupted one. We therefore assume that higher degrees of corruption would result in higher
284   uncertainties (lower concentration κ). We use Spearman Correlation as an evaluation metric to
285   quantify if a model could capture this connection. We use the non-parametric, ranking-based Spear-
286   man correlation (rather than Pearson) as the relationship between the variables is highly nonlinear
287   (i.e., we test for their monotonicity). Some corruptions are shown in Figure 2. The details of the
288   corruption are in Appendix B
289   OOD detection. From CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100, and MNIST (LeCun, 1998), we generate six in-
290   domain and out-of-domain pairs for the OOD detection tasks, as shown in Table 2. Area Under the
291   Receiver Operating Characteristic curve (AUROC) is used for the detection accuracy following the
292   practice from (Kuan & Mueller, 2022). For this task, we train three different models on the three
293   domains from scratch. The learned κ is treated as a one-dimensional feature (or anomaly score) to
294   enhance the features for OOD detection.
295   Failure analysis. To evaluate the effectiveness of our uncertainty estimates, we perform a three-step
296   failure analysis. First, we pre-train the probabilistic encoder using a contrastive learning approach.
297   Second, we train a linear classifier on the learned embeddings (mean directions) with labels from
298   the training set and assess its accuracy on the test set. Third, we categorize the test samples into
299   two groups: (1) correctly classified and (2) misclassified. By comparing the κ values between
300   these groups, we investigate whether lower κ values are associated with misclassifications, thereby
301   demonstrating the utility of our uncertainty measures in identifying uncertain predictions.

353   Architecture. The encoder network contains two projection heads for the mean direction µ and κ
354   based on ResNet50 (He et al., 2016). The projection head for the µ is realized through a sequential
355   arrangement of layers, starting with a linear transformation from the 2048-dimensional ResNet50
356   feature space to an intermediate 512-dimensional space, followed by batch normalization and ReLU
357   activation, and finally projecting down to a d-dimensional representation. d is set to 128 for all
358   experiments except the study on dimension in Table 4.4. In parallel, the κ parameter is estimated
359
      through a separate head, mirroring the structure but diverging in its final output to produce a single
      scalar value per input. κ is then passed through a softplus function (Nair & Hinton, 2010), ensuring
360
      its non-negativity and adherence to the constraints of a concentration parameter in a probabilistic
361
      setting. The codes of the neural architecture are in the Appendix.
362
363   Optimization. Following the SimCLR configuration, our data augmentation includes random crop-
364   ping, resizing, color jittering, and horizontal flipping. We train all models on CIFAR-10’s training
365
      set for 1000 epochs to quantify corruption levels. Hyper-parameters λalign and λreg are adjusted for
      optimal training loss and stability, with λreg fixed at 0.005 across experiments due to observed train-

      5   D ISCUSSION
504
      Our study demonstrates the efficacy of the concentration parameter κ in uncertainty estimation, fail-
505
      ure analysis, and OOD detection within contrastive learning frameworks. Empirical results show
506   that κ effectively captures aleatoric uncertainty by quantifying the dispersion of embeddings in the
507   vMF distribution. Additionally, κ indirectly captures epistemic uncertainty by exhibiting greater
508   variability for OOD samples and failure cases. Theoretically, we show that the unnormalized vMF
509   distribution preserves the ranking of similarities between embeddings, which is critical for con-
510   trastive learning. By introducing an alignment loss that leverages the concentration parameter κ, we
511   offer a flexible mechanism that adapts alignment strength based on uncertainty.
512   However, our study is limited to small-scale datasets such as CIFAR-10-C, and has not yet been
513   evaluated on larger, more complex datasets like ImageNet. Scaling κ and the alignment mechanism
514   to handle these environments remains a challenge (Zhang et al., 2023; Lu et al., 2024), which we
515   aim to address in future work.
516
      Future research will explore integrating κ with other OOD detection methods and extending its
517
      application to domains such as healthcare. Additionally, investigating alternative approaches to
518   managing the normalization constant Cn (κ), and extending our framework to non-contrastive meth-
519   ods like MAE (He et al., 2022), multi-modal settings, and higher-dimensional data types, represent
520   promising avenues for further development.
521
      Potential broader impact. Integrating uncertainty estimation into contrastive learning has sig-
522
      nificant implications for critical applications such as autonomous driving and medical diagno-
523
      sis. Our framework supports the development of transparent and accountable AI systems (Kim
524   & Doshi-Velez, 2021), enhancing decision-making by providing interpretable confidence levels.
525   Improved uncertainty estimation mitigates risks in high-stakes environments by alerting users to
526   low-confidence predictions, thereby fostering trust and reliability. Future work will focus on apply-
527   ing this method to other tasks, including classification and segmentation across various domains,
528   further promoting robustness and reliability in AI systems.

790
791
      The gradient of Lalign w.r.t. µ1 .
792
793   Given La in Eq. 9, the gradient of its log w.r.t. µ1 can be obtained by differentiating the loss function
794   w.r.t. µ1 . Its gradient can be expanded as follows:
795                              ∇µ1 log La =      ∇µ1 [κ1 · cos(θ) + κ2 · cos(θ)]                             (12)
796
797   Now, cos(θ) = µT1 µ2 , and its gradient w.r.t. µ1 is µ2 . Plug this into the gradient of Lalign , we get:
798                                   ∇µ1 Lalign = −λalign · (κ1 µ2 + κ2 µ2 )                                  (13)
799
800   This gradient aligns µ1 towards µ2 , similar to those with existing contrastive losses. More impor-
801   tantly, however, the strength of this alignment effect is controlled by the estimated concentration
802
      parameters κ1 and κ2 (i.e., the estimated uncertainties) of both µ1 and µ2 . Smaller κ’s indicate
      more uncertainties and lead to looser alignment. Compared with conventional contrastive losses
803
      which naively align positive pairs regardless of the severity of corruptions in the input, our Lalign
804
      yields a more flexible latent space that is aware of the severity of corruptions in the input.
805
806   The gradient of Lalign w.r.t. κ1 . Similarly, we can compute the gradient of Lalign w.r.t. κ1 as
807   follows:
808
                                           ∇κ1 Lalign = −λalign · µT1 µ2                             (14)
809   Eq. 14 implies that a closer cosine distance between µ1 and µ2 encourages a stronger increase in
      κ1 , indicating reduced uncertainty. The increasing effect on κ1 weakens as the distance between µ1

      and µ2 grows. Meanwhile, when the angle between µ1 and µ2 surpasses π2 , the gradient encourages
811   a reduction in κ1 instead, hence an increase in predicted uncertainty. Of note, κ’s would not grow
812   uninformatively large as they are bounded by the ℓ2 regularization (Eq. ??) at the same time.
