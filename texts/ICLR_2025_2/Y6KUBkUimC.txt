                                                                   A BSTRACT
044
045
              Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) has emerged as a powerful approach to
046
              align text-to-image (T2I) models with human feedback. Unfortunately, successful
047
              application of DPO to T2I models requires a huge amount of resources to collect
048           and label large-scale datasets, e.g., millions of generated paired images annotated
049           with human preferences. In addition, these human preference datasets can get
050           outdated quickly as the rapid improvements of T2I models lead to higher quality
051           images. In this work, we investigate a scalable approach for collecting large-
052           scale and fully synthetic datasets for DPO training. Specifically, the preferences
053           for paired images are generated using a pre-trained reward function, eliminating
              the need for involving humans in the annotation process, greatly improving the


                                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
               dataset collection efficiency. Moreover, we demonstrate that such datasets allow
055            averaging predictions across multiple models and collecting ranked preferences
056            as opposed to pairwise preferences. Furthermore, we introduce RankDPO to en-
057            hance DPO-based methods using the ranking feedback. Applying RankDPO on
058            SDXL and SD3-Medium models with our synthetically generated preference
059            dataset “Syn-Pic” improves both prompt-following (on benchmarks like T2I-
060            Compbench, GenEval, and DPG-Bench) and visual quality (through user studies).
061            This pipeline presents a practical and scalable solution to develop better prefer-
062            ence datasets to enhance the performance and safety of text-to-image models.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
066
067
068   While text-to-image (T2I) models (Rombach et al., 2022; Podell et al., 2023; Betker et al., 2023;
069   Esser et al., 2024) have become widespread recently, they still suffer from numerous shortcomings,
070
      including challenges with compositional generation (Lin et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2024b), limited
      ability to render text (Liu et al., 2022a), and lacking of spatial understanding (Chatterjee et al.,
071
      2024). There have been several attempts at addressing these issues with larger models (Esser et al.,
072
      2024; Ma et al., 2024), improved datasets (Schuhmann et al., 2022; Gadre et al., 2023), and superior
073   language conditioning (Chen et al., 2024b; Pernias et al., 2024). However, these approaches typically
074   involve training larger models from scratch and are not applicable to existing models.
075
076
      On the other hand, drawing inspiration from Large Language Models (LLMs), aligning T2I models
      with human feedback has become an important and practical topic to enhance existing T2I mod-
077
      els (Liu et al., 2024a). There are two major efforts in this area, namely, 1) collecting large amounts
078
      of user preferences images for training (Lee et al., 2023b; Dai et al., 2023; Liang et al., 2024a) and
079   2) fine-tuning with T2I models with reward functions (Wu et al., 2023b;a; Xu et al., 2023; Kirstain
080   et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024a). The former direction shows promising results when utilizing Direct
081   Preference Optimization (DPO) (Rafailov et al., 2023), which was first proposed for aligning LLMs
082   with human feedback, to improve the denoising of the more preferred images as compared to the de-
083   noising of the less preferred images (Wallace et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024c; Hong et al., 2024b; Liang
084   et al., 2024b). Nevertheless, the existing process for data collection is expensive and the datasets can
085   be outdated quickly, e.g., Pick-a-Picv2 (Kirstain et al., 2023) costs nearly $50K (Otani et al., 2023)
086   for collecting 5122 px generated images, while most recent T2I models generate 10242 px images.
087
      The latter direction fine-tunes the T2I models by maximizing the reward functions with the gener-
      ated images (Black et al., 2024; Fan et al., 2023; Deng et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024b; Chen et al.,
088
      2024a; Clark et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2023; Prabhudesai et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024d). However, this
089
      process is computationally expensive due to the backpropagation through the diffusion process. Ad-
090   ditionally, these methods suffer from “reward hacking”, where this optimization process increases
091   the reward scores without improving the quality of the generated images.
092
093
      In this work, we address the above challenges and propose a scalable and cost-effective solution
      for aligning T2I models. Specifically, we investigate the efficacy of using synthetically labeled
094
      preferences in fine-tuning T2I models with DPO-based techniques. While this has been studied in
095
      depth in the context of LLMs (Lee et al., 2023a; Bai et al., 2022b), there have been only preliminary
096   explorations in the context of T2I models (Wallace et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2024c). To this end, we
097   introduce the following two novel contributions:
098
099
      • Synthetically Labeled Preference Dataset (Syn-Pic). We generate images from different T2I
        models and label them with multiple pre-trained reward models that can estimate human prefer-
100
        ence. Therefore, no manual annotation is involved in data collection, making the data collection
101
        cost-effective and easily scalable. By aggregating scores from multiple reward models, we miti-
102     gate reward over-optimization (Coste et al., 2024; Eyring et al., 2024). Unlike the conventional
103     pairwise comparisons, we construct a ranking of the generated images for each prompt. While
104     aggregating preferences across multiple human labelers and constructing rankings are possible,
105     these dramatically increase the annotation cost compared to the minimal overhead in our case.
106   • Ranking-based Preference Optimization (RankDPO). To leverage the benefits of the richer
107     signal from the rankings, we introduce a ranking-enhanced DPO objective, RankDPO, borrowing
        from the extensive literature on “learning-to-rank” (Burges et al., 2006; Wang et al., 2013; 2018;


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
          Liu et al., 2024c; Song et al., 2024). It weighs the preference loss with discounted cumulative
109       gains (DCG), enabling alignment with the preferred rankings.
110
111   We conduct extensive evaluation to demonstrate the advantages of the proposed contributions:
112   • First, using the same prompts as Pick-a-Picv2 leads to dramatic improvements for the SDXL and
113     SD3-Medium models. We show the improved results on various benchmark datasets, including
114     GenEval (Ghosh et al., 2023) (Tab. 1), T2I-Compbench (Huang et al., 2023) (Tab. 2), and DPG-
115     Bench (Hu et al., 2024) (Tab. 3), as well as, the visual comparisons (examples in Fig. 1) through
116     user studies (Fig. 3).
117   • Second, we achieve the state-of-the-art results compared to the existing methods on preference
118
        optimization, e.g., Tab. 3. More importantly, such results are obtained by only requiring 3× fewer
        images than Pick-a-Picv2, i.e., Tab. 9.
119
      • Third, even though SD3-Medium (2B parameters) has already been optimized with 3M human
120
        preferences through DPO, we are still able to further get significant improvements with our
121     Syn-Pic dataset of 240K images, e.g., Tabs. 1,2,3.

124   2     R ELATED W ORK
125
126
127   Text-to-Image Models. Early works employing GANs for text-to-image synthesis (Reed et al.,
128
      2016; Zhang et al., 2017) evolved more recently around diffusion (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015; Ho
      et al., 2020) and rectified flow (Liu et al., 2022b; Lipman et al., 2023; Albergo & Vanden-Eijnden,
129
      2023) models for image and video generation. Following the success of the Stable Diffusion mod-
130
      els (Rombach et al., 2022; Podell et al., 2023), several improvements have been proposed, including
131   the use of superior U-Net/transformer backbones (Peebles & Xie, 2023; Bao et al., 2023), stronger
132   language conditioning through superior text encoders (Raffel et al., 2020; Chen et al., 2024c;b) and
133   improved captions Betker et al. (2023); Esser et al. (2024); Chatterjee et al. (2024). In this work,
134   we explore the efficacy of synthetically generated preferences to enhance pre-trained text-to-image
135   model using methods based on learning from human/AI feedback.
136
      Learning from Human Preferences. In LLMs, alignment with human preferences (Griffith et al.,
137
      2013; Christiano et al., 2017; Bai et al., 2022a) has been crucial in developing chatbots and language
138   assistants. The paradigm of Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) involved col-
139   lecting large amounts of user preferences for various prompt output pairs. Following this, reward
140   models were trained to mimic user preferences, after which reinforcement learning algorithms (e.g.,
141   PPO (Schulman et al., 2017), REINFORCE (Williams, 1992; Ahmadian et al., 2024)) were used to
142   optimize language models to maximize reward model scores. However, Direct Preference Optimiza-
143   tion (Rafailov et al., 2023) along with similar variants (Azar et al., 2024; Ethayarajh et al., 2024;
144   Hong et al., 2024a; Meng et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024c) emerged as a strong alternative, introducing
145   an equivalent mathematical formulation that enabled training language models directly on user pref-
146
      erences without requiring reward models or reinforcement learning. These insights have since been
      used more generally in image/video generation (Wallace et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024c). In contrast,
147
      we demonstrate the superior efficacy of improving text-to-image models purely from AI feedback,
148
      similar to the paradigm of Reinforcement Learning from AI Feedback (Lee et al., 2023a) in LLMs.
149
150   Preference-Tuning of Image Models. Reward models have been used effectively to fine-tune image
151   generation models using either reinforcement learning (Black et al., 2024; Fan et al., 2023; Deng
152   et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024b; Chen et al., 2024a) or reward backpropogation (Lee et al., 2023b;
153
      Li et al., 2024d; Prabhudesai et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2023; Clark et al., 2024; Prabhudesai et al., 2024;
      Domingo-Enrich et al., 2024; Jena et al., 2024). However, this process is computationally expensive
154
      and requires additional memory due to backpropagation through the sampling process. Further, it
155
      has not yet been successfully applied on larger models at 10242 px resolution. As a result, following
156   language modeling literature, DPO techniques have also been adapted to image generation (Wallace
157   et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024c; Liang et al., 2024b; Hong et al., 2024b; Gu et al., 2024), thereby
158   avoiding the expensive training objective. There have also been several methods specifically tailored
159   to improve prompt following in specialized settings (Li et al., 2024b; Hu et al., 2024; Jiang et al.,
160   2024; Sun et al., 2023; Liao et al., 2024). Differently, we demonstrate the possibility of using
161   reward model feedback through the denoising/preference optimization objective as a more general
      and effective solution than existing approaches for aligning text-to-image models.

165   direct preference optimization for these models. Next, we discuss the process of curating and label-
166   ing a scalable preference optimization dataset. Finally, we describe our ranking enabled preference
167   optimization method, called RankDPO, to leverage this ranked preference dataset. We describe
168   these two components with illustration in Fig. 2. We provide pseudo-code to train RankDPO on
169   Syn-Pic in Algorithm 3 in Appendix A.8.
170   Notation. We use the symbol x ∼ pdata to denote the real data drawn from the distribution pdata . In
171   our setup, a diffusion process transforms the real image x to Gaussian noise ϵ ∼ N (0, I) with a pre-
172   defined signal-noise schedule {αt , σt }Tt=1 . The diffusion model reverses this process by learning a
173   denoiser ϵ̂θ , a neural network parameterized by θ to estimate the conditional distribution pθ (x|c),
174   where c is the conditioning signal that guides the generation towards the condition. For text-to-
175   image models, we use c as the embedding corresponding to the text-prompt. For brevity, we inter-
176   changeably use the symbol c to mean both the text-prompt/embedding.
177   Diffusion Models. Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Models (DDPMs) learn to predict real data
178   x ∼ pdata by reversing the ODE flow. Specifically, with a pre-defined signal-noise schedule
179   {αt , σt }Tt=1 , it samples a noise ϵ ∼ N (0, I), and constructs a noisy sample xt at time t as
180   xt = αt x + σt ϵ. The denoising model ϵθ parameterized by θ is trained with the objective as:

276   3.3   R ANKING - BASED P REFERENCE O PTIMIZATION (R A N K DPO)
277
278   Unlike the DPO objective which focuses on pairwise preferences, our synthetic dataset generates
279   multiple images per prompt, resulting in a ranked preference dataset. Therefore, we would like to
280
      optimize the many-way preference at once instead of purely relying on pairwise preferences. Specif-
      ically, given a text-prompt c, and generated images in a ranked order of preference {x1 , x2 , . . . , xk },
281
      we want to ensure that the denoising for image xi is better than xj for all i > j. To enforce this, we
282
      draw inspiration from the Learning to Rank (LTR) literature, and re-purpose the LambdaLoss (Wang
283   et al., 2018) by adding the DCG weights to each sample, similar to Liu et al. (2024c) as follows:
284
285   Given a sample (c, {xi }ki=1 , {ϕ(i)}ki=1 ), denoising, reference models (ϵθ , ϵref ), we define score as
286                    si ≜ s(xi , c, t, θ) = ∥ϵi − ϵθ (xit , c)∥22 − ∥ϵi − ϵref (xit , c)∥22 ,         (6)
287   where xt = αt x + σt ϵi , ϵi ∼ N (0, I) is a noisy latent for input xi at time t. This score measures
             i       i

288   how much better or worse the model prediction is compared to the reference model for the given
289   condition c.
290
      After computing the scores si , the images are ranked from the most preferred (lowest si ) to the least
291   preferred (highest si ). This is the predicted rank for {xi }ki=1 using model θ. We use the ground
292   truth scores ϕ(i) to obtain the true preference ranking τ (·). The rank of each image xi is denoted by
293   τ (i), where τ (i) = 1 for the best image, τ (i) = 2 for the second best, and so on. The gain for each
294   sample ϕ(i) is the average probability that sample i is preferred over all other samples j according
295   to human preference reward model scoring.
296
      Using τ (i) and ϕ(i), we define the gain function Gi and the discount function D(τ (i)) as:
297
298
                                  Gi = 2ϕ(i) − 1; D(τ (i)) = log(1 + τ (i)).
299   The discount function decreases as the rank τ (i) increases, ensuring that higher-ranked images
300
      (those with a lower τ (i)) have a greater influence on the final loss. The logarithmic form of the
      discount function smooths out the penalty differences between consecutive ranks, making the model
301
      more robust to small ranking errors, especially for lower-ranked images.
302
303   We define the weight between two image pairs (xi , xj ) as
304                                                                   1           1
                                       ∆i,j = |Gi − Gj | ·                   −          .                               (7)
305                                                                 D(τ (i))   D(τ (j))

309
310   where σ(·) is the sigmoid function and β controls the strength of the KL regularization. We describe
311
      the training process in a detailed procedure in Algorithm 2 in Appendix A.8.
312   This loss function encourages the model to produce images that not only satisfy pairwise prefer-
313   ences, but also respect the overall ranking of images generated for the same prompt. By weighting
314   the traditional DPO objective with gains and discounts derived from the ranking, we ensure that the
315   model prioritizes the generation of higher-quality images according to the ranking, leading to more
316   consistent improvements in both aesthetics and prompt alignment.

      5   C ONCLUSION AND D ISCUSSION
487
488
      In this work, we introduce a powerful and cost-effective recipe for the preference optimization of
489   text-to-image models. In particular, we demonstrate how synthetically generating a preference op-
490   timization dataset can enable the collection of superior signals (e.g., rankings vs. pairwise prefer-
491   ences and ensembling preferences across models). We also introduce a simple method to leverage
492   the stronger signals, leading to state-of-the-art results on various benchmarks for prompt following
493   and visual quality for both the diffusion and rectified flow models. We hope our work paves the way
494   for future work on scaling effective post-training solutions for text-to-image models.
495
      Limitations. We rely solely on the prompts from Pick-a-Picv2 (Kirstain et al., 2023) for construct-
496   ing our preference dataset. While this allows us to fairly compare to prior work on preference
497   optimization, our dataset is limited by the quantity and diversity of the prompts in Pick-a-Picv2.
498   Expanding the prompts to include different use cases would significantly enhance the utility of
499   the dataset and improve the quality of the downstream models. Additionally, we focus only on
500   text-image alignment and visual quality. However, preference optimization is also well-suited to
501   improving the safety of the text-to-image models, which can also be investigated in future work.
502   Finally, we also reply purely on off-the-shelf reward models. Some of these models have shown
503   impressive performance in recent times, even outperforming a single user on several benchmarks.
504
      However, the annotations from these reward models could still have problems, and stronger reward
      models in the future would be crucial in strengthening the results.

      A.4   D ETAILED E XPLANATION OF E VALUATIONS
868
869   T2I-Compbench consists of 6000 compositional prompts from 6 different categories (color, shape,
870   texture, spatial, non-spatial, complex). Following the trends of recent protocols (Bakr et al., 2023;
871   Lin et al., 2024), the evaluation for these prompts are done using a combination of VQA models,
872   object detectors and vision-language model scores (e.g., CLIPScore (Hessel et al., 2021)).
873   GenEval consists of 553 prompts comprising different challenges (single object, two objects, count-
874   ing, position, color, color attribution). These are mostly evaluated using object detectors.
875
876
      DPG-Bench aggregates prompts from several sources, and lengthens them using LLMs. These
      prompts on average have 67 words making it extremely challenging for prompt following. The
877
      generated images are mostly evaluated using VQA models under the Davidsonian Scene Graph (Cho
878
      et al., 2024) framework. We use the following evaluation metrics for different benchmarks:
879
880         • GeneEval. The evaluation for GenEval is performed using the Maskformer (Cheng et al.,
881           2021) object detection models. This is used to determine if the image contains objects
882           specified in the prompts. For color, a CLIP model is used to identify the color of the
883           objects.
884         • T2I-CompBench: Attribute Binding uses a BLIP-VQA model (Li et al., 2022b) to ask dif-
885           ferent (upto 8) questions about the generated images, and is used to validate if the answered
886           questions match the details specified in the prompt.
887         • T2I-CompBench: Spatial uses a Unidet (Zhou et al., 2022) model to perform object detec-
888           tion to see if the objects in the generated images follow the spatial orientation specified in
889           the prompt.
890
            • T2I-CompBench: Non-Spatial computes the CLIPScore for the prompt and the generated
891           image.
892
            • T2I-CompBench: Complex averages the score computed from Attribute Binding, Spatial,
893
              and Complex.
894
895         • DPG-Bench: DSG uses the Davidsonian Scene Graph (Cho et al., 2024) to compute ques-
896
              tion answer pairs and use a VQA model (mPLUG) (Li et al., 2022a) to answer the questions
              before computing the percentage of questions correctly answered.
897
898         • DPG-Bench: VQAScore (Lin et al., 2024) trains a multimodal LLM with a CLIP encoder
899           and Flan-T5 decoder to predict the likelihood of the prompt being appropriate for the image.
900         • DPG-Bench: Q-Align Aesthetic Score (Wu et al., 2024a) finetunes a multimodal LLM
901           (e.g., LLaVA (Liu et al., 2023) to predict the aesthetic score of an image from a scale of 0
902           to 1.
