011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Recent Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated strong performance in
014            translation without needing to be finetuned on additional parallel corpora. How-
015
               ever, they still underperform for low-resource language pairs. Previous works
               have focused on mitigating this issue by leveraging relevant few-shot examples or
016
               external resources such as dictionaries or grammar books, making models heavily
017
               reliant on these nonparametric sources of information. In this paper, we propose
018            a novel method named IntGrad MT that focuses on fully exploiting an LLM’s in-
019            herent translation capability. IntGrad MT achieves this by constructing a chain
020            of few-shot examples, each consisting of a source sentence and the model’s own
021            translation, that rise incrementally in difficulty. IntGrad MT employs two tech-
022            niques: Sentence Interpolation, which generates a sequence of sentences that grad-
023            ually change from an easy sentence to translate to a difficult one, and Gradual MT,
024            which sequentially translates this chain using translations of earlier sentences as
025            few-shot examples for the translation of subsequent ones. With this approach,
026
               we observe a substantial enhancement in the xCOMET scores of various LLMs
               for multiple languages, especially in low-resource languages such as Hindi(8.26),
027
               Swahili(7.10), Bengali(6.97) and Marathi(13.03). Our approach presents a practi-
028
               cal way of enhancing LLMs’ performance without extra training.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Recent Large Language Models (LLMs) have shown strong performance in translation tasks with-
034   out the need for fine-tuning on specific parallel datasets. Previous studies have demonstrated that
035   LLMs’ translation capabilities are reliable in most use cases, particularly when the source and target
036   language are high-resource languages (Zhu et al., 2024; Robinson et al., 2023; Jiao et al., 2023).
037   However, because LLMs require training on large corpora, they still face challenges when trans-
038
      lating low-resource languages that are not sufficiently represented in the training corpora.(Stap &
      Araabi, 2023; Robinson et al., 2023; Enis & Hopkins, 2024).
039
040   Previous research has attempted to address these challenges by leveraging the in-context-learning
041   capabilities of large language models (LLMs), particularly through the use of external knowledge
042   such as few-shot examples or dictionaries during inference. However, relevant examples are not
043   always guaranteed to be available, and constructing such external knowledge sources can be costly.
044
      A potential solution is to reduce reliance on external sources altogether.
045   In this paper, we examine whether we can improve LLMs’ translation capabilities without relying
046   on external knowledge. We aim to answer this question by considering a simple fact: the machine
047   translation task can be defined as a mapping between two (sub)spaces for the source and target lan-
048   guage. In the source language space, there are regions where the model performs well in translation
049   and regions where it does not. The key idea is that if we can gradually expand the areas where the
050
      model performs well by feeding it neighboring examples, we can enhance its translation capabilities
      in the areas where it performs poorly.
051
052   We propose IntGrad MT to achieve this by connecting sentences from the regions where the LLM
053   performs well in translation and regions where it does not. IntGrad MT consists of two key tech-
      niques: Sentence Interpolation and Gradual MT. Sentence Interpolation is a prompting technique

      that generates a sequence of sentences gradually transitioning from one to another. Gradual MT is a
055   technique in which the model iteratively translates a list of sentences, using its previous translations
056   as few-shot examples for subsequent sentences. Through sentence interpolation, we first establish
057   a pathway to the sentences in regions where the model performs poorly, then gradually expand the
058   area where the model can excel, utilizing its in-context learning capabilities. The key concepts are
059   illustrated in Figure 1.

102
103   2.1    E NHANCING LLM S ’ T RANSLATION C APABILITIES WITHOUT F INE - TUNING
104
105   Modern LLMs show high capabilities in translation tasks in high- resource languages, but not in
106   low-resource languages (Jiao et al., 2023; Stap & Araabi, 2023; Zhu et al., 2024; Enis & Hop-
107   kins, 2024). There are several works that have focused on enhancing LLMs’ translation capabilities
      without additional fine-tuning. A primary strategy involves leveraging LLMs’ ability to learn from


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      demonstrations or descriptions (Brown et al., 2020; Wei et al., 2022). Studies have explored select-
109   ing appropriate exemplars for few-shot learning and demonstrating linguistic knowledge (Agrawal
110   et al., 2022; Vilar et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024), or augmenting LLMs with chains of multilingual
111   dictionaries (Lu et al., 2024). Besides providing a demonstration or description, choosing the right
112   temperature or prompting strategy has also been examined (Peng et al., 2023). Similar to previous
113   research, our method aims to improve LLMs’ MT capabilities without fine-tuning, focusing instead
114   on eliciting the models’ inherent capabilities with sentence interpolation and gradual MT rather than
115   providing them with few-shot examples or external knowledge.
116
117   2.2   S ELF -D EMONSTRATION
118
      Manually generating appropriate exemplars for in-context learning can be resource-intensive. To
119
      address this challenge, previous studies have explored enabling models to generate their own few-
120
      shot examples for tasks such as classification(Lyu et al., 2023; Kim et al., 2022) or other reasoning
121   tasks(Zhang et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024). Our work is aligned with these efforts, as it also focuses
122   on generating the model’s own few-shot examples. However, none of these approaches have tried to
123   create examples such as ours, since our approach aims to produce the tailored example by gradually
124   expanding the example set with similar yet distinct examples.

      3     M ETHODS
127
128
      We introduce the two steps that IntGrad MT consists of, Sentence Interpolation (§3.1) and Gradual
129   MT (§3.2), and present how we combine them (§3.3).
130
131   3.1   S ENTENCE I NTERPOLATION
132
133   We propose sentence interpolation, which is a prompting technique asks model to create a list of
134   sentences that gradually change from start sentence to end sentence. For example, following is the
135   prompt that we use in our experiments:
136
            I will give you two sentences. Can you gradually change the first sentence to make it
137
            exactly the same as the second sentence? Just give me the sentences and don’t provide
138         additional comments.
139
140         Sentence1: ⟨Sentence1⟩
141         Sentence2: ⟨Sentence2⟩
142
143   The objective of this technique is to generate a list of sentences where each sentence is distinct, yet
144   not excessively different from its adjacent sentences. By prompting the LLM to autonomously create
145   the list, rather than mechanically altering sentences, we can obtain natural sentences that are suitable
146   for use as in-context learning examples. We call this list of interpolated sentences the interpolation
147   path. In practice, we utilized three few-shot examples from GPT-4(OpenAI et al., 2024) to control
148   the output format. See Appendices A and B for these examples and sample interpolation paths.
149
150   3.2   G RADUAL MT
151
152
      Gradual MT is a prompting technique that lets an LLM utilize its previous translations as a prompt.
      This approach sequentially processes a list of sentences, translating each one while using the previ-
153
      ous translation results as few-shot examples for the current sentence. An illustration of the Gradual
154
      MT process is shown in Figure 2. Gradual MT can effectively guide LLMs in accurately translating
155   unfamiliar sentences when combined with sentence interpolation. However, as Gradual MT is fun-
156   damentally a recursive process, it entails significant computational overhead. We discuss strategies
157   to mitigate this cost in Section 6.3; Path Truncation and Path Sampling.
158

186   Figure 3: Illustration of the IntGrad MT algorithm. IntGrad MT integrates Sentence Interpolation
187
      with Gradual MT. In Step 1, n start sentences are selected from a pre-defined start sentence pool.
      In Step 2, these start sentences are interpolated toward the end sentence, creating n individual inter-
188
      polation paths. Each path is then processed through Gradual MT, generating translation results for
189
      every sentence along the path. In Step 3, the MT results from all interpolation paths are aggregated
190   to produce a single output translation. Optional pre- and post-filtering steps can be applied between
191   Steps 1 and 2, and after aggregation, to refine the sentences on which IntGrad MT is applied.
192
193
194   Step 0: Start Sentence Pool Creation. Before applying IntGrad MT, the start sentence pool must
195   be created before. This pool consists of sentences that the LLM can already translate accurately.
196   It is crucial to ensure that the source sentences included in this pool have been tested and verified,
197   confirming the LLM can generate high-quality translations in a zero-shot setting. We refer to the
198   source sentences in the start sentence pool as start sentences.
199
200   Step 1: Start Sentence Selection IntGrad MT begins with selecting n start sentences from the
201   start sentence pool by calculating the similarity with the source sentence that the LLM is trying to
202   translate, which we call an end sentence. Measuring similarity between sentences can be done in
203   various ways. In this paper, we utilized SBERT similarity(Reimers & Gurevych, 2019) as a primary
204   metric. Details about start sentence selection strategy can be found in Section 6.1.1.
205
206   Step 2: Sentence Interpolation & Gradual MT. After selecting n start sentences, sentence in-
207   terpolation is performed between each start sentence and the corresponding end sentence, creating
208   n individual interpolation paths. The paths are then processed through the Gradual MT, generating
209   translation results for each sentence.
210
211   Step 3: MT Results Aggregation. After generating a list of translation, we proceed to aggregate
212   the final translation from each path. If a single start sentence was selected in Step 1, this step is
213   skipped. There are several methods for aggregating translations; in our approach, we input all the
214   translation results into the LLM once again as few-shot examples to generate the final translation. A
215   detailed explanation of this aggregation strategy can be found in Section 6.1.2. After aggregation,
      we obtain the final translation, which we refer to as the output.

      4.1     S ETUP
226
      Models. For translation we use four different LLMs: ChatGPT(GPT-3.5-Turbo-0125)1 , Mistral
227   Nemo(Mistral-Nemo-Instruct-2407)2 and two different sized Llama 3.1 Instruct models(Llama-3.1-
228   70B-Instruct, Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct)(Dubey et al., 2024). ChatGPT is accessed via OpenAI’s API,
229   and the others are run locally. For sentence interpolation, we employ Qwen2-72b-Instruct (Yang
230   et al., 2024) with quantization. See Appendices D and E for settings for translation and interpolation.
231   For pre- and post-filtering, we utilize a reference-free QE model CometKiwi (Rei et al., 2023) to
232   avoid peeking at the gold translations. CometKiwi produces a DA score, which rates translation
233   quality on a scale from 0 to 100, normalized to a range of 0 to 1. Lastly, we used all-mpnet-base-v23
234   for SBERT sismilarity calculation.
235
236   Target Languages. We fixed English as the source language. The target languages tested in the
237   experiments are German (De), Chinese (Zh), Korean (Ko), Hindi (Hi), Swahili (Sw), Bengali (Bn),
238
      and Marathi (Mr). Based on Joshi et al. (2020)’s 6 scale taxonomy of language resource level, we
      classify German and Chinese as high resource languages, Korean and Hindi as mid resource, and
239
      the rest as low resource. For the Llama 3.1 models, we conduct tests only on German and Hindi, as
240
      those models do not support other languages. Mistral Nemo Instruct does not officially support Ko,
241   Hi, Sw, Bn, and Mr, but we conduct experiments on those languages nevertheless, as it has some
242   capability to generate them.
243
244   Dataset. We use the FLORES-200 benchmark dataset(Team et al., 2022) for validation and eval-
245   uation. We first utilize the dev split of the dataset to create start sentence pool. During evaluation, to
246   test the effect of the pre-filtering strategy, we selected 10% of the test portion of the dataset to set the
247   DA score threshold. The remaining 90% is used to assess the overall performance on the dataset.
248
249   Start Sentence Pool Creation. The initial sentence pool is created by translating source sentences
250   from the dev split of the FLORES-200 dataset using a zero-shot approach. Each source sentence is
251   translated five times and evaluated with xCOMET (Guerreiro et al., 2024). xCOMET predicts DA
252   score normalized to a range of 0 to 1. The most frequently occurring translation is selected as the
253   representative translation. If no translation is repeated, the one with a score closest to the average is
254   chosen. After selecting each representative translation, the top 100 translation pairs with the highest
255
      DA scores are selected.
256
257   Baselines. We compare zero-shot MT results with 15-shot and 50-shot MT using source sentences
258
      from the start sentence pool and their gold translations. Additionally, we benchmark against Tow-
      erInstruct 13B (Alves et al., 2024) and NLLB-200-3.3B4 for broader comparison.
259
260
261
      Evaluation. We use the DA score, as evaluated by xCOMET, as the primary metric for our eval-
      uation, scaling it by a factor of 100 for improved readability. Additionally, we employ MetricX
262
      (Juraska et al., 2023) to compute an MQM score, which assesses translation errors on a scale from 0
263
      to 25, where lower scores indicate higher quality. We also evaluate the DA score using CometKiwi
264   and BLEURT (Pu et al., 2021). Finally, we report the BLEU score. Computational costs associated
265   with executing IntGrad MT are detailed in Appendix C.

345
346       6.1                      A BLATION S TUDY
347
348      To find the optimal combination of strategies for the IntGrad MT, we conducted an ablation study
349      on En-Ko translation task using ChatGPT in terms of four different dimensions: Start sentence se-
350
         lection, Number of start sentences, MT result aggregation, and Filtering. In this section, we analyze
         the effects of each strategy by averaging the QE scores. Full results with all different combination
351
         of ablation settings can be found in Appendix G.
352
353
          6.1.1                      S TART S ENTENCE S ELECTION S TRATEGY
354
355      Based on the intuition that start sentences that are similar to the source sentence will be helpful,
356      we utilized three different metrics — SBERT similarity(Reimers & Gurevych, 2019), Levenshtein
357      distance(Levenshtein & others, 1966), and Tree edit distance(Zhang & Shasha, 1989) — to calculate
358      similarity between sentences, and combined them in theee different ways. The first way is Sort,
359      which sorts sentences by multiple metrics with varying priorities. The second way, Filter, initially
360
         selects the top 10 sentences based on SBERT similarity, then sorts the selection using the other
         metrics. The third way, Tops, picks the top sentence based on the highest similarity scores from each
361
         metric. As shown in Table 2, the selection strategies that produced the highest scores differed across
362
         aggregation methods. We chose sorting by SBERT similarity and then tree edit distance (Sort(S-T)),
363      as it yielded the highest average scores.
364
365       6.1.2                      N UMBER OF S TART S ENTENCES & MT AGGREGATION S TRATEGY
366
367      When selecting the start sentences, we need to decide whether to use more than one start sentence.
368      If we choose more than one, we must aggregate each translation result generated by Gradual MT.
369      We investigated two distinct strategies for aggregating the results of Gradual MT. The first method,
370
         referred to as Polling, selects the MT result with the highest number of duplicates, drawing inspira-
         tion from prior research on self-consistency (Wang et al., 2023). If no duplicates are found, a result
371
         is selected randomly. The second method, Prompting, involves feeding all Gradual MT results into
372
         the LLM as few-shot examples to generate the final MT output. As shown in Table 2, the prompting
373      strategy outperforms polling by 0.49 points. Polling is even worse than using a single start sentence.

      6.2   S ENTENCE I NTERPOLATION A NALYSIS
436
437   We conducted an analysis of the interpolated sentences used in the ablation study to verify whether
438   they effectively interpolate between the start and end sentences. The total number of sentences in
439   interpolated paths is 12,729, and the average length of interpolation path is 7.31.
440
441   Sentence Interpolation Error Rate. We defined that a situation where the first and last sentences
442   in the interpolation path are not exactly the same as the start and end sentences as an error, and ex-
443   ecuted zero-shot translation for that end sentence. Sentence interpolation was successfully executed
444   without error in 93.68% of cases.
445
446   Progresses of Interpolation. We examined whether LLMs genuinely interpolate between start
447   and end sentences or simply generate random sentences. To assess this, we use SBERT to embed
448
      the interpolated sentences and calculate their Euclidean distances from the end sentence. If these dis-
      tances generally decrease, it indicates successful interpolation. To mesure this, we defined progress
449
      as progress = dn−1,e − dn,e where dn,e denotes the euclidian distance between nth interpolated
450
      sentence in each interpolation path and end sentence.
451
452   As shown in Figure 5, the progress of interpolated sentences is generally positive, indicating that
453   the interpolation effectively bridges the two sentences. The average progress for all interpolated
454
      sentences is 0.14, with a standard deviation of 0.23.
455   We also conducted a qualitative analysis to identify patterns in interpolation. First, we sampled
456   100 interpolation paths for En-Ko translation containing more than three sentences and obtained
457   sentence embeddings using SBERT. Next, we plotted each interpolation path on a 2D plane using
458   Principal Component Analysis (PCA) and observed the patterns. After the analysis, we confirmed
459   that sentence interpolation typically shifts the start sentences toward the end sentences, despite the
460
      variety of patterns (Arc, Triangle, Zig-Zag, and Leap). Detailed explanations of each pattern can be
      found in Appendix I.

517   7   C ONCLUSION
518
519   In this paper, we proposed IntGrad MT, a novel method to enhance the machine translation ca-
520
      pabilities of various LLMs. IntGrad MT leverages sentence interpolation to guide models, elic-
      iting stronger translation performance. Experimental results across various models and languages
521
      demonstrate that our approach consistently improves translation quality, particularly in low-resource
522
      languages, achieving meaningful gains in performance metrics. Our approach is practical in that it
523   does not require extra training and does not conflict with previous methods that utilize other kinds
524   of prompting techniques.
525
526
527
      8   L IMITATIONS
528
      Despite its success, IntGrad MT introduces significant computational overhead, particularly in sce-
529
      narios involving multiple start sentences or large-scale models. Future work could focus on opti-
530
      mizing computational efficiency (as we did in Section 6.3) or extending the approach to specialized
531   domains and additional low-resource language pairs. Moreover, since sentence interpolation did
532   not perform well in languages other than English, even with GPT-4 (OpenAI et al., 2024), we had
533   to limit our focus to cases where English was the source language. Exploring better prompting
534   techniques to interpolate non-English sentences is a potential future research direction.

1320   I   S AMPLES OF INTERPOLATION PATHS
1321
1322   We sampled 100 interpolation paths for En-Ko translation containing more than three sentences
1323   and obtained sentence embeddings using SBERT. Each interpolation path was then plotted on a 2D
1324
       plane using Principal Component Analysis (PCA). We identified four notable patterns, which we
       named based on their shapes: A RC, T RIANGLE, Z IG -Z AG, and L EAP. The A RC pattern was the
1325
       most common. In this pattern, the sentences gradually shift toward the end sentence, following an
1326
       arc-shaped trajectory. While the A RC pattern presents a relatively smooth shape, the Z IG -Z AG and
1327   T RIANGLE patterns exhibit more spiky trajectories, though the sentences still progress toward the
1328   end sentence. The final pattern, L EAP, shows a single large shift toward the end sentence, without
1329   the gradual progression seen in the other patterns. Although the L EAP pattern might initially be
1330   perceived as a “bad interpolation,” closer examination reveals that similar intervals also appear in
1331   other patterns. Overall, our qualitative analysis showed that, regardless of the pattern, sentence
1332   interpolation typically shifts the start sentence toward the end sentence. Figure 6 shows 24 samples
1333   from these 100 paths.
