009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            Pre-trained vision models used in robotics often misalign with manipulation tasks
012            due to the loss used to train these vision models being focused on appearance
013            rather than motion. In order to enhance motion encoding within vision models,
014            we introduce a simple novel contrastive training framework that operates over
015            predictions of motion. After training over EPIC Kitchens, model evaluations on
016            behavioral cloning show a improvement in success rate over state-of-the-art meth-
017
               ods across a benchmark of 3 environments and 21 object manipulation tasks.

046
047   Constraining a vision network to focus on motion is readily achieved via a contrastive loss. We
048   introduce in this paper such a contrastive loss. Our contrastive learning approach utilizes motion as
049
      a self-supervised cue. This approach forces similar motions to be grouped together and dissimilar
      motions to be distinguished, organizing visual representations based on motion similarity. Conse-
050
      quently, any appearance characteristic that does not contribute to motion understanding is deemed
051
      irrelevant, ensuring that the network’s focus remains on capturing the dynamics of motion. This

085   Video datasets can come from a variety of sources, including those of human actions. Large-scale
086

      are trained for appearance-based objectives. By employing a contrastive loss to emphasize motion,
093
      our approach aligns visual representations with robotic objectives.
094
095   Our primary contributions can be summarized as followed:
096    • We observe that visual encoders commonly used in robotic control policies are not trained in
097      such a way as to model the internal motion dynamics of actions. Robot control policies can
098      benefit from visual representations modeling motion. We introduce a novel contrastive training
099      framework which explicitly enhances motion encoding in the visual representation network.

      2.1   L EARNING V ISUAL R EPRESENTATIONS FOR M OTOR C ONTROL
111
112   Large-scale pre-training Mahajan et al. (2018); Sun et al. (2017) over in-the-wild video datasets
113   Damen et al. (2018); Grauman et al. (2022); Li et al. (2015) has long been considered a promis-
114   ing direction for learning useful visual representations for robotics applications. The pre-training
115   paradigm has been explored in the domain of policy learning for motor control Shridhar et al.
116   (2022); Nair et al. (2022); Yuan et al. (2022a); Hu et al. (2023), where visual representations are
117
      frozen during the training of motor policies.
118   There have been many recent works exploring the learning of visual representations over simulated
119   robotic action Yen-Chen et al. (2020); Bousmalis et al. (2023); Yuan et al. (2022b) (but simulated
120   environments suffer from lack of diversity), large-scale image data Dasari et al. (2023) (which result
121   in misalignment between vision and policy objectives), small-scale real-world robot action videos
122   Radosavovic et al. (2023) (which are difficult to collect), and large-scale human action videos Nair
123
      et al. (2022); Xiao et al. (2022). In this work, we pre-train a network over a large-scale human action
      video dataset - the EPIC Kitchens dataset Damen et al. (2018).
124
125
126   2.2   S ELF -S UPERVISION OVER V IDEO
127
      There have been a plethora of works which align video representations with language targets for
128   learning useful representations for downstream language-conditioned robotics tasks Mees et al.
129   (2022); Lynch & Sermanet (2020); Ma et al. (2023) and for robotic manipulation tasks conditioned
130   on vision alone Nair et al. (2022); Sontakke et al. (2024). In an attempt to learn video representations
131   with less explicit supervision, there has been significant attention exploring how time can be used as
132   a self-supervisory signal. The majority of existing works fall within two categories: 1) approaches
133   that enforce the feature-level distance between different video representations to be proportional to
134   their respective distances over time Tanwani et al. (2020); Zhou et al. (2021); Nair et al. (2022),
135   and 2) approaches that predict visual states in the future using a learned video dynamics model Wu
136
      et al. (2020); Manuelli et al. (2020). While these approaches implicitly learn the motions, the ex-
      plicit adoption of motion as a form of self-supervision has only been applied over the field of action
137
      understanding Huang et al. (2021); Dessalene et al. (2024). To our knowledge, there is no existing
138
      work exploring 3D motion as a supervisory cue in the learning of frozen representations for robotics.
139
140
141   3     M ETHODS
142
143   Our goal is to leverage large, diverse, and naturalistic video datasets of human interaction as a sub-
144   stitute for real-world robot data that is 1) difficult to collect at scale, 2) only collected for specific
145
      embodiments, and 3) difficult to acquire for complex manipulation tasks. We learn a visual repre-
      sentation through a contrastive learning formulation detailed in Section 3.1. This involves vision
146
      architectures detailed in Section 3.2, and is trained through use of an egocentric RGB dataset de-
147
      tailed in Section 3.3. We then deploy the visual representations learned over these videos for learning
148   motor control policies.

      3.1   C ONTRASTIVE L EARNING F ORMULATION
151
152   We assume access to a dataset of RGB video clips v ∈ V encompassing object manipulation actions.
153   For each frame fk in video clip v with length t frames, there exist objects oki ∈ Ok , each paired with
154   a homogeneous transformation Tki (with respect to the origin at the center of the camera) for each
155   frame k in {1, 2, .., t}. Our approach predicts 3D motion pertaining to object oki from RGB video,
156   making our method independent of linguistic annotations or clip boundary annotations, unlike other
157   approaches.
158   Our contrastive learning approach operates over the aforementioned frames fk and 3D relative mo-
159   tion, as shown in Figure 3. In formulating the training loss we randomly sample two frames fn and
160   fm from video clip v, extracting frame-level feature maps using a backbone encoder. We extract
161   image features qn and qm , encouraging the feature representations to be aware of the motions acting
      upon them by predicting the motions from these features. Specifically, we compute the 3D relative

      Regarding criterion 3), as the human embodiment is strongly tied to the dataset, we apply a recent

252   frame in the dataset. This synergizes with our approach to leveraging the hands as a substitute for

254   derived from the 3D hand predictions. This is opposed to if the hands were left in the video, in which
255   case the network would over-rely on the movements of the hands in predicting motion, resulting in
256   learned representations that fail to generalize towards robotic domains. We train our vision network
257   over the inpainted dataset and freeze the learned representations for the learning of control policies.

      4   E XPERIMENTS
260
261
      In our experiments, we demonstrate the effectiveness of our contrastive learning objective in gen-
262
      eralizing beyond the egocentric training dataset, serving as a useful frozen visual representation
263
      for motor policy learning. In Section 4.1 we describe the training and environment details of our
264   policy learning evaluation. In Section 4.2 we first demonstrate that motion sensitive visual represen-
265   tations learned through our contrastive learning formulation enable better performance and sample
266   efficiency over a variety of unseen environments and tasks. Second, in Section 4.3 we ablate the
267   various components of our contrastive learning formulation, justifying the design choices of our
268   proposed methodology. Finally, in Section 4.4 we show the learned representations are sensitive to
269   motion not just over the EPIC Kitchens dataset, but over the out-of-domain videos taken from the
      RL environment.

380   removed - we posit that this is because the motion in the scene is dominated by the arm and base

      sensitive to frame-to-frame level motion, and so are particularly amenable to aggregation over time.
391   We note that our findings are consistent with other experiments done (Hu et al., 2023) that aggregate
392   visual representations over time using other vision backbone networks. One possible explanation
393   for this is the lack of dynamics in the task definitions across all environments.

      6    C ONCLUSION
397
      This paper presents a novel contrastive training framework that enhances motion encoding in visual
398
      representations for robotic manipulation tasks. By using a loss which focuses on motion and uti-
399
      lizing the EPIC Kitchens dataset, our approach significantly improves the alignment between visual
400   models and the dynamic nature of robotic manipulation. After training over EPIC Kitchens, model
401   evaluations on behavioral cloning show a improvement in success rate over state-of-the-art methods
402   across a benchmark of 3 environments and 21 object manipulation tasks.
