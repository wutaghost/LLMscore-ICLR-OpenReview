009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Gaussian Processes (GPs) are non-parametric Bayesian models widely used for
012            regression, classification, and other tasks due to their explainability and versatil-
013            ity. However, GPs face challenges in imbalanced regression, where the skewed
014            distribution of target labels can greatly harm models’ performances. In this work,
015            we introduce the Probabilistic Feature Smoothed Partially Independent Training
016
               Conditional Approximation (PFS-PITC) to enhance GP performance in imbal-
               anced scenarios. We extract statistical features from the observation space using
017
               equidistant label intervals and apply kernel smoothing to address sampling density
018
               discontinuities. This process enables PFS-PITC to utilize information from nearby
019            labels within imbalanced datasets, thereby reducing GPs’ sensitivity to such im-
020            balances. Empirical tests on various imbalanced regression datasets demonstrate
021            the effectiveness of PFS-PITC, contributing to the robustness of GPs in handling
022            flawed real-world data and expanding their applicability in challenging data pro-
023            cessing tasks.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
027
      Gaussian processes (GPs) are extensively applied in various machine learning domains, including
028
      image classification (Bazi & Melgani (2009); Dutordoir et al. (2020); Xu et al. (2013; 2022)), graph
029
      learning (Chen et al. (2022); Miao et al. (2022)), black-box optimization (Koza et al. (2021)), and
030   manifold learning (Camastra et al. (2023)), showcasing their versatility and effectiveness. As a
031   classical non-parametric Bayesian model (Williams & Rasmussen (2006)), GPs offer several advan-
032   tages: ease of training, resistance to overfitting, uncertainty estimation, and the ability to incorporate
033   prior knowledge. However, the significant time complexity associated with GPs limits their applica-
034   tion, rendering them computationally intractable with large-scale datasets.
035
      To enhance the computational efficiency of GPs, various methods have been developed, primarily
036   focusing on sparse approximation techniques (Liu et al. (2020); Snelson & Ghahramani (2007)).
037   These methods assume local conditional independence of labels within the training set and use
038   inducing points to accelerate computation. This approach of summarizing the input space with
039   strategically chosen inducing points has proven to be highly effective over years of research, leading
040   to advancements such as SoR (Silverman (1985); Wahba et al. (1998)), DTC (Csató & Opper (2002);
041   Seeger et al. (2003)), FITC (Snelson & Ghahramani (2005)), and PITC (Quinonero-Candela &
042   Rasmussen (2005)).
043   The choice of inducing points is central to GP approximation. Initially, inducing points are se-
044   lected from the training data (Smola & Bartlett (2000); Seeger et al. (2003)). However, Snelson &
045   Ghahramani (2005) relaxes this constraint, proposing that inducing points can be viewed as aux-
046   iliary pseudo-inputs representing the spatial structure of the input data. A natural approach is to
047   cluster the input data and assign each point to its nearest cluster center. In related studies, methods
048   like farthest point clustering (Gonzalez (1985)) and random clustering (Sibuya (1993)) have been
049   used to reduce computational burden. An innovative approach involves computing each cluster cen-
050
      ter using a separate Gaussian Process (Park & Choi (2010)). This method significantly improves
      computational efficiency by simplifying calculations with sparse matrices, although it may slightly
051
      increase prediction error when points are sparse within each partition.
052
053   While studying target-based partition strategies is equally important, research in this area remains
      limited. This approach is crucial for addressing challenges in data imbalance, which is a pervasive


                                                          1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      issue in real-world data collection (Oommen et al. (2011); Spelmen & Porkodi (2018); Krawczyk
055   (2016)). The concept of balancing data through reweighing the label space originated from stud-
056   ies on imbalanced categorical data (Huang et al. (2016); Fernández et al. (2011)). Recently, DIR
057   (Yang et al. (2021)) has advanced this approach by applying kernel smoothing to continuous targets,
058   achieving state-of-the-art performance on complex multi-model regression tasks. However, methods
059   like LDS and FDS designed for data smoothing do not provide uncertainty assessment—an essential
060   aspect for evaluating the performance of learning algorithms.
061   In this paper, we propose Probabilistic Feature Smoothed Partially Independent Training Condi-
062   tional Approximation (PFS-PITC), a target-based partition and smoothing strategy to leverage the
063   data approximation flexibility of PITC. Labels are divided into equidistant intervals to address data
064   imbalance in training labels. Kernel smoothing is then applied, followed by a Gaussian sampling
065   procedure to generate clustering centers for each label layer. According to theoretical analysis and
066   multiple experiments, PFS-PITC brings reliable performance boost on datasets with underrepre-
067   sented label space, providing a feasible way to adopt Gaussian Process to imbalanced regression
068   missions.

111   Despite their similar assumptions about the conditional distribution of the latent function, these three

117   computational efficiency with information loss by leveraging partial independence and allowing
118   flexible partitioning of groups, thus integrating inducing points effectively.
119

120
      lations, research has often overlooked their application to imbalanced regression problems. Among
121   the mainstream GP approximations discussed, PITC is considered most suitable for leveraging uni-
122   formly distributed inducing points for two major reasons.
123
124
      Firstly, unlike approximations that use point mass distributions, such as SoR and DTC, PITC allows
      for latent function uncertainty given the inducing functions. This flexibility avoids overly rigid
125
      relationships that can lead to shallow predictive variance and a higher risk of data overfitting.
126

128   modeling elements within respective label bins. This characteristic can be discovered from the
129   ”PITC kernel function”, given by k PITC (x, x′ ) = Q(x, x′ ) + 1x,x′ ∈Bs [k(x, x′ ) − Q(x, x′ )](1(·)

      2.2   I MBALANCED R EGRESSION
135   Imbalanced regression is less explored compared to imbalanced classification problems (Zou et al.
136   (2016); Feng et al. (2021)). Most existing regression methods for imbalanced data are variants of
137   the SMOTE algorithm (Branco et al. (2017a); Torgo et al. (2013b)). These methods create artifi-
138   cial samples to oversample rare targets, either by interpolating training data (Torgo et al. (2013b);
139   Rahim et al. (2019)) or by applying Gaussian noise augmentation (Branco et al. (2017a)). Despite
140   their similar origins, regression-focused SMOTE algorithms share several limitations. Firstly, they
141   fail to utilize the distance between continuous input labels effectively. The interpolation of inputs
142   and labels relies on classification discreteness, leading to bias in the continuous feature space. Addi-
143
      tionally, high-dimensional data pose significant challenges for oversampling algorithms, as synthetic
      data generated through linear interpolation often lack realism, which can further degrade model per-
144
      formance.
145
146   To leverage the statistical distribution uniformity of input features and labels, DIR (Yang et al.
147   (2021)) proposes Feature Distribution Smoothing (FDS) and Label Distribution Smoothing (LDS),
148   which apply kernel smoothing to latent features and labels, respectively. FDS and LDS partition
149
      features and labels into continuous bins, thereby addressing data imbalance and achieving state-of-
      the-art performance. Although DIR is compatible with various downstream networks, it primarily
150
      focuses on minimizing RMSE loss in regression and provides no uncertainty assessment.

164   Gaussian Process. In the Gaussian Process framework, the latent function follows a multivariate
165   Gaussian distribution (Williams & Rasmussen (2006); Kanagawa et al. (2018)). A typical Gaussian
166   Process can be denoted as f ∼ GP(m(·), k(·, ·)). Without specific prior knowledge, m(x) = 0
167   is assumed by default. As for the kernel function, popular choices include the Linear kernel, the
168   Spectral Mixture kernel, the Radial Basis Function (RBF) kernel, and the Cosine-Similarity kernel.
169   According to the study of Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces (RKHS) (Kanagawa et al. (2018)), a
170
      kernel function can also be viewed as an inner product defined in the feature space with a mapping
      φ : X → V, k(x, x′ ) = ⟨φ(x), φ(x′ )⟩V . This observation enables the use of deep neural networks
171
      (DNNs) as feature extractors for specific needs (Wilson et al. (2016); Patacchiola et al. (2020); Yang
172
      et al. (2019)). The projection from input space to latent space does not require prior knowledge of
173   the kernel function, making it a desirable approach to enhance the performance of GP models.

425

428   by undersampling, injecting noise, and removing redundant samples, respectively. DIR (Yang et al.
429   (2021)), along with LDS and FDS, proposes alternatives that leverage the continuity of label space
430   in regression tasks to enhance performance. Despite their widespread use for addressing imbalanced
431   datasets, some of these algorithms are incompatible with the training process of Gaussian Processes,
      which can lead to degraded prediction performance.

517   6        C ONCLUSIONS
518
519   In this work, we introduce PFS-PITC, a target-based partition and smoothing strategy for GP ap-
520   proximation, aimed at enhancing the performance of Gaussian Processes on imbalanced regression
521   tasks. PFS-PITC extends the classical PITC approximation by employing label bins to achieve a
522   more balanced integration of variable information. Additionally, kernel smoothing is applied to re-
523   duce distribution discrepancies in the latent features within each bin. Compared to incorporating
524   resampling techniques directly into GP, PFS-PITC offers a more effective solution for addressing
525   imbalanced regression in the Gaussian Process Regression framework. Extensive empirical experi-
526   ments demonstrate significant performance improvements with our approach.

      Gaussian Processes (GPs) are widely employed for various data analysis tasks Li et al. (2019); Du-
      tordoir et al. (2018). While their performances can be affected by sampling biases, such as domain
532
      shift or imbalanced sampling, using inducing points for approximation enables targeted counter-
533
      measures. PFS-PITC, inspired by the traditional PITC approximation, specifically addresses the
534   issue of imbalanced sample distribution in the label space. Our method offers an effective solution
535   for imbalanced regression, providing uncertainty estimation and enhanced inference explainability.
536   Furthermore, it integrates seamlessly into the Gaussian Process Regression framework, requiring
537   minimal adjustments and facilitating performance improvements on imbalanced datasets through a
538   straightforward substitution of GP with our approach. This capability significantly enhances the
539   applicability of GPs in various fields, including finance, healthcare, and environmental modeling,
      ultimately contributing to more robust data analysis in these critical areas.
