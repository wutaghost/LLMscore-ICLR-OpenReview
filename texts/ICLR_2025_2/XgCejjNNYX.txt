011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            We introduce SeaDAG, a semi-autoregressive diffusion model for conditional gen-
014            eration of Directed Acyclic Graphs (DAGs). Considering their inherent layer-wise
015
               structure, we simulate layer-wise autoregressive generation by designing different
               denoising speed for different layers. Unlike conventional autoregressive gener-
016
               ation that lacks a global graph structure view, our method maintains a complete
017
               graph structure at each diffusion step, enabling operations such as property con-
018            trol that require the full graph structure. Leveraging this capability, we evaluate
019            the DAG properties during training by employing a graph property decoder. We
020            explicitly train the model to learn graph conditioning with a condition loss, which
021            enhances the diffusion model’s capacity to generate graphs that are both realistic
022            and aligned with specified properties. We evaluate our method on two represen-
023            tative conditional DAG generation tasks: (1) circuit generation from truth tables,
024            where precise DAG structures are crucial for realizing circuit functionality, and (2)
025            molecule generation based on quantum properties. Our approach demonstrates
026
               promising results, generating high-quality and realistic DAGs that closely align
               with given conditions.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   The success of diffusion models in various domains (Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021; Kong et al., 2021)
032   has led to significant interest in their application to graph generation (Vignac et al., 2023; Kong
033   et al., 2023). In this work, we focus on conditional Directed Acyclic Graph (DAG) generation.
034   DAGs are essential and widely used data structures in various domains, including logic synthesis (Li
035   et al., 2022; Liu & Young, 2023; Wang et al., 2023; Pei et al., 2024) and bioinformatics (Zhou &
036   Cai, 2019). Compared to general graphs, DAGs possess an inherent layer-wise structure with
037   intricate node inter-dependencies that can significantly influence the overall graph properties. In
038   logic synthesis, for example, minor structural alterations in lower layers can propagate errors to
039
      higher layers, resulting in substantial functional differences. Modeling these layer-wise structural
      features of DAGs requires specially designed model architectures and generation mechanisms (An
040
      et al., 2024). Recognizing these challenges, many established DAG synthesis and optimization
041
      tools (Mishchenko et al., 2006; Flach et al., 2014; Li et al., 2024b; Wang et al., 2024) employ a
042   sequential synthesis approach, allowing for effective propagation and modeling of localized changes
043   at each step on the global DAG structure.
044
045
      Recent studies have explored DAG generation using Autoregressive (AR) diffusion models (Li et al.,
      2024a), owing to their improved efficiency and enhanced ability to model node-edge dependen-
046
      cies (Kong et al., 2023). However, existing approaches generally face the following challenges:
047
      (1) Their strict part-by-part generation impedes information flow from later components to earlier
048   ones, whereas in DAG structures with complex inter-layer interactions, subsequent layer structure
049   can often influence the message passing in preceding layers (Wu & Qian, 2016). (2) They lack a
050   global graph structure view until the final generation step. This limitation is particularly problem-
051   atic in conditional generation scenarios, where graph properties or functions generally cannot be
052   evaluated without a complete structure (Fang et al., 2022). This incomplete view of conventional
053   AR methods hinders effective property guidance during both training and sampling (Vignac et al.,
      2023). (3) Many existing works do not employ explicit condition learning during training, with con-

      To address above issues while preserving the benefits of AR models, we propose SeaDAG, a SEmi-
072   Autoregressive diffusion-based DAG generation model that enables graph property control, as illus-
073   trated in Figure 1. Our approach simulates layer-wise autoregressive generation while being able
074   to output a complete graph at every diffusion step. We achieve this by applying different denoising
075   speeds to nodes and edges within different layers, inspired by recent advances in natural language
076   generation (Wu et al., 2023). Therefore, layers with higher-noise can be generated conditioned on
077   other less-noisy layers.
078   In summary, SeaDAG offers several key advantages over existing methods:
079
080   (1) SeaDAG fully exploits the hierarchical and layer-wise nature of DAGs. We design a de-
081
      noising process that mimics sequential AR generation, enabling the model to effectively capture
      inter-layer dependencies.
082
083   (2) SeaDAG evolves all layers simultaneously, albeit at different rates, Unlike the strict part-by-
084   part generation, this simultaneous evolution enables more flexible generation and message passing
085   among layers.
086   (3) SeaDAG maintains a complete graph structure at each timestep, akin to one-shot methods.
087   Leveraging this, we incorporate explicit condition learning by employing a property decoder dur-
088   ing training to evaluate graph properties. By using a condition loss, we explicitly teach the model to
089   learn the relationship between DAG structures and properties, which helps the model simultaneously
090   satisfy the conditions while producing realistic graph structures.
091   To demonstrate the broad applicability of our method, we evaluate it on two significant conditional
092   graph generation tasks from distinct domains. First, we address an important challenge in electronic
093   design automation (Liu et al., 2023): circuit generation from truth tables. This task was selected
094   due to the pervasive use of DAGs in circuit design, representing a classic application of DAGs in
095   real-world engineering. Second, to showcase our model’s versatility, we tackle molecule generation
096   based on quantum properties, a more general graph generation task. For this application, we convert
097   molecular structures into DAGs using the junction tree representation (Jin et al., 2018). Notably,
098   our model surpasses many specialized molecule generation models. These results collectively show
099
      the robustness of our method to produce realistic DAGs that adhere to specified conditions across
      diverse domains.

130   DAG representation of circuits The And-Inverter Graph (AIG) is a powerful representation of
131   logic circuits. In an AIG, leaf nodes carry input signals to the circuit, while root nodes produce the
132
      circuit’s output signals. Intermediate nodes are computing units that perform logical conjunction
      (AND operation) on two binary inputs a, b ∈ {0, 1}, outputting a ∧ b. Edge eij from node ni to
133
      nj indicates that the output of ni serves as an input to nj . Edges can optionally perform logical
134
      negation (NOT operation) on the signal they carry. This representation naturally forms a DAG
135   structure. The structure of an AIG uniquely defines its functionality, which can be represented as
136   a canonical truth table mapping all possible combinations of input signals to their corresponding
137   output signals. Figure 2(a) illustrates a simple AIG and its truth table.
138
      This representation is particularly useful because any combinational logic circuits can be expressed
139
      using only AND and NOT operations, making AIG a universal and efficient model for circuit anal-
140
      ysis and synthesis. In this work, we generate AIGs that can realize a given truth table functionality.
141   For node types, we have three category (kx = 3): input gates, AND gates and output gates. For
142   edges, we define three types (ke = 3): non-existing edges representing the absence of a connection,
143   normal edges for direct connections, and negation edges that perform a logical NOT operation.
144
145   DAG representation of molecules We adopt the approach of Jin et al. (2018) to convert molecules
146   into junction trees. This method first extracts valid chemical substructures, e.g. rings, bonds and in-
147   dividual atoms, from the training set. The vocabulary size is the number of node types kx . Each
148   molecule is then converted into a tree structure representing the scaffold of substructures. We trans-
149   form this tree into a DAG by designating the tree root as the DAG root and converting tree edges
150
      to directed edges from children to parents. Edges have two types (ke = 2): absent connections
      and existing connections. In this work, we generate junction trees of molecules from their proper-
151
      ties. Junction trees are subsequently assembled into complete molecules for evaluation. Figure 2(b)
152
      shows a molecule and its junction tree. For more details on the construction of junction trees and
153   their conversion to molecules, we refer the reader to Jin et al. (2018).

251
252   Our approach incorporates explicit condition learning by employing a property decoder to compute
253   a condition loss during training. This section details the implementation of these key components.
254
255   2.3.1    C ONDITION LOSS
256   To incorporate graph properties as generation conditions, we extend our network to accept an addi-
257   tional condition input cond: fθ (Gt , cond) = (pθ (X), pθ (E)). For AIG generation from truth tables,
258   we concatenate truth table vectors with other node features. Each column of binary values in the
259   truth table serves as an additional feature for the corresponding node. Since the condition truth table
260   only provides signals for the input and output gates, we use all zero signals for the AND gates. In
261   molecule generation from quantum property, we concatenate the desired molecule property with the
262   graph level features.
263   To enhance the network’s ability to generate graphs that meet given conditions, we introduce a dif-
264   ferentiable graph property decoding module ϕ. This module decodes the graph property from a
265   clean graph. The implementation of ϕ varies based on the application: (1) For AIG generation, we
266   simulate continuous circuit output using softmax-choice wiring, where each gate’s input is a soft
267   distribution over candidate inputs rather than a hard selection. (2) For applications where the graph
268   property is not directly computable, such as molecule generation, we employ a pre-trained prediction
269   model as the decoding module. Detailed implementations of ϕ can be found in Appendix B.3. Dur-
      ing training, we sample a predicted clean graph Ĝ from the predicted distribution (pθ (X), pθ (E))

      2.3.2    I NTEGRATING CONDITON LEARNING INTO TRAINING
301
302   Many AR or one-shot approaches to conditional graph generation train an unconditional model
303   and incorporate conditional guidance only during sampling (Vignac et al., 2023; Kong et al., 2023).
304   However, we argue that this separation of condition learning from training hinders the model’s ability
305   to balance condition satisfaction with graph realism. Our experiment results suggest that while these
306
      methods can generate realistic graphs in unconditional mode, they struggle to maintain graph quality
      after applying conditional guidance during sampling. In contrast, by integrating conditional learning
307
      into the training process, our method SeaDAG achieves a more effective balance between adhering
308
      to conditions and producing plausible graph structures.

311   3    R ELATED W ORKS
312
313   Different diffusion mechanisms have been proposed for graph generation. One-shot generation mod-
314   els apply noise addition and denoising processes across the entire graph structure simultaneously,
315
      predicting all nodes and edges at each timestep (Yan et al., 2024; Vignac et al., 2023). In contrast,
      AR diffusion models generate the graph sequentially, either producing one part of the graph at each
316
      diffusion step (Kong et al., 2023) or having a separate denoising process for each part (Zhao et al.,
317
      2024). These AR approaches offer advantages in generation efficiency and are better at modeling
318   dependencies between nodes and edges by allowing each step to condition on previously generated
319   parts. (Kong et al., 2023). However, they face challenges such as sensitivity to node ordering (You
320   et al., 2018b) and the production of only partial, incomplete graph structures during the sampling
321   process. This limitation precludes operations that require the entire graph structure, such as validity
322   checks and property guidance (Vignac et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2023). Latent diffusion models have
323   also been explored for graph generation (Zhou et al., 2024), with studies indicating their potential to
      enhance performance in 3D graph generation tasks (You et al., 2023).

509
      optimization. On the ZINC dataset, we train SeaDAG to conditionally generate molecules based on
510   two properties: penalized logP and Quantitative Estimate of Druglikeness (QED), which are two

514   to several optimization-based baselines (Guimaraes et al., 2017; Jin et al., 2018; You et al., 2018a)
515   as shown in Table 4. Notably, for the penalized logP property, SeaDAG attains scores surpassing
516   the highest values observed in the training set. This suggests SeaDAG’s capacity to extrapolate
517   beyond the training distribution by effectively learning the intrinsic relationships between molecular
518
      structure and associated properties.

522   In this paper, we introduced SeaDAG, a semi-autoregressive diffusion model for conditional DAG
523   generation. Our approach demonstrates significant improvements in generating graphs that are real-
524   istic and realize given conditions. Future research could focus on enhancing the method’s efficiency
525   to match that of fully autoregressive models.

      Action space and transition model The action space is defined as a set of random graph edits.
757   An action, i.e. a graph edit, for an AIG or a molecule junction tree is defined as follows:
758
759         • To sample an action for an AIG, we first randomly sample a gate from the AND gates and
760           output gates. We then change the input gates (i.e. the children) of the chosen gate to nodes
761           randomly sampled from possible candidate input gates. Specifically, an AND gate has two
762           inputs and a output gate only requires one input. The candidate gates are the set of gate
763           nodes with lower levels. Finally, for the new edges from the new inputs to the chosen gate,
764           we determine their types by sampling from normal type and negation type.
765         • To sample an action for a molecule junction tree, we first randomly select a node from the
766           junction tree. Then, with a probability of 0.5, we either modify the node’s type or alter
767           its parent node. In the case of type modification, we randomly assign a new type to the
768           selected node. In the case of changing parent, we randomly select a new parent from the
769           nodes at higher levels in the tree.
770
771   Applying an action to a state is to modify the DAG structure using the edit defined by the action
772   and result in a new DAG structure. Our design of the action space ensures that the resulting DAG is
773
      always valid.
774
775   Reward function We employ the property decoder ϕ to compute the reward function for a state.
776
777         • For AIGs, we aim to maximize the function accuracy. Therefore, we use the truth table
778
              decoder ϕ to decode the output signals of the AIG and compute the accuracy as reward.
779         • For molecules, we aim to minimize the MAE between the molecule property and the target
780           property. Therefore, we employ ϕ to predict the molecule property from the junction tree
781           and compute the negative of MAE loss as reward.
782
783   MCTS Algorithm Structure Since the action space, namely the set of possible edits to a DAG,
784   is finite but is still very large, we employ the progressive widening strategy (Coulom, 2007; Chaslot
785   et al., 2008) to balance adding new children and selecting among existing children. We employ UCB
786   selection strategy (Auer et al., 2002) to select the best child. In the simulation phase, we employ a
787   random action sampling strategy until reaching the predefined maximum depth limit. Upon reaching
788   this limit, we evaluate the reward of the terminal state and back-propagated it through the tree. We
789   conduct 500 such simulations for each decision point. After these simulations, we select the best
790
      child node as the next state. This process is repeated for 50 steps for each DAG.
791   After the MCTS refinement, we evaluate the resulting DAG structure. If it performs worse than the
792   original DAG, we reject the refinement and retain the original structure.
793
      We evaluate the performance of our MCTS-based DAG structure refinement in Table 5. MCTS
794   is applied to 256 generated AIGs and 600 generated molecule junction trees for each of the five
795   properties, with the aim to improve the function accuracy and property MAE respectively. The
796   MCTS refinement significantly improves performance across all metrics on both tasks.

867
868   We extend the model employ in Dwivedi & Bresson (2020) and Vignac et al. (2023) to implement
869   our denoising network fθ . First, we extract the graph features of Gt = (Xt , Et ) following Vignac
870   et al. (2023), resulting in node level feature Fx ∈ Rn×dx , edge level feature Fe ∈ Rn×n×de and
871   graph level feature y ∈ Rdy . For clarity of presentation, we omit the timestep superscript t for
872   Fx , Fe , y. These features encode (1) the node and edge types and (2) the graph structure features of
873   Gt . We refer the reader to Vignac et al. (2023) for details on the structure features. Global timestep
874
      t is encoded in graph level feature y. Node level li and node-specific local timestep τit is encoded in
      node level feature Fx .
875
876   We then incorporate the condition information into graph features.
877
878   Truth table condition as node features Each column in truth tables are a series of {0, 1} values
879   of one input gate or output gate. The signal values for AND gates are unknown in the condition and
880   therefore we first pad 0 for the values for AND gate. For AIGs with 8 input gates, the padded truth
                                                     8
881   table could be represented as T ∈ {0, 1}n×2 . To compress this representation, we convert each
882   8-bit sequence in the last dimension of T to its corresponding integer value. These integer values
883
      are then normalized by dividing by 256, resulting in a compressed representation with values in the
      range [0, 1]. We concatenate the compressed truth table T with node level features Fx . With a slight
884
      abuse of notation, we continue to denote these augmented node features as Fx .
885
886
      Property condition as graph level features We concatenate the molecule property condition with
887
      the graph level feature y. With a slight abuse of notation, we continue to denote the resulting graph
888   level feature as y.

922   B.3   P ROPERTY DECODER FOR CONDITION LOSS
923
924   In this section, we introduce the implementation of property decoder ϕ. We denote the probability
925   distribution of node types and edge types predicted by the network fθ as pθ (X) ∈ Rn×kx , pθ (E) ∈
926   Rn×ke .
927
928   AIG function decoder As discussed in the previous section, the node types are fixed in AIG
929   generation and we only need E to decode the AIG structure. Given the graph structure of an AIG,
930
      the logic function represented by an AIG can be directly determined and the output signals are
      readily computable. However, to ensure that this operation is differentiable and can be incorporated
931
      into the training process, several aspects require special consideration:
932
933          • Input selection. To determine the inputs (children) for each gate, we employ a softmax
934            operation across all candidate input gates. Candidate gates are those with levels lower than
935            the current gate. For AND gates, we select two inputs, while for output gates, we select
936            one input.
937          • Edge type selection. The type of each edge (normal or NOT) is determined by computing
938            an edge type score. This score is calculated as Tanh(pθ (E)ij1 − pθ (E)ij2 ), where pθ (E)
939            represents the predicted edge type distribution from nodes i to j. A positive score indicates
940            a higher likelihood of a normal edge, while a negative score suggests a higher probability
941            of a NOT edge.
942
      Utilizing the aforementioned differentiable operations, we can compute the output signals as contin-
943
      uous values. The decoded output signals are then compared against the ground truth output signals
944   specified in the condition truth table to compute the condition loss using BCE. Note that the loss
945   computation only involves the output signal portion, as the input signals, which are all possible
946   combinations of input values, are the same for every AIG.
947
948   Molecule property decoder Since molecular properties cannot be directly decoded from the
949   molecular structure, we train a separate property prediction model ϕ for each of the five condition
950   properties. The architecture of these models is identical to that of the denoising network fθ , with two
951   difference:(1) ϕ takes clean graphs as input rather than the noisy graphs used by fθ . (2) ϕ outputs
952   the predicted molecular property. During the training process, we sample a discrete graph from the
953   predicted distributions pθ (X) and pθ (E) using the Gumbel-Softmax technique, which allows us to
954
      maintain differentiability while working with discrete graph structures. The sampled graph serves
      as the input to ϕ and the predicted value is compared against the property condition using MSE loss.

994    Proof of model equivariance In this section, we prove that the network we use is equivariant to
995    node permutations. For any node permutation σ : [n] → [n], (σ ⋆ X)σ(i),...σ(n) = Xi,...,n and
996    (σ ⋆ E)σ(i)σ(j)k = Eijk . Then for graph Gt = (Xt , Et ), we can prove the following:
997
998             • Equivariant feature extraction: the graph features are either permutation equivariant (node
999               level and edge level features) or permutation invariant (graph level features). If the features
1000              of Gt are (Fx , Fe , y), the features for σ ⋆ Gt would be (σ ⋆ Fx , σ ⋆ Fe , y).
1001
1002            • Equivariant condition feature: we can easily prove that the truth table condition for AIG
1003              is permutation equivariant, i.e. the truth table for σ ⋆ Gt being σ ⋆ T, and the molecule
1004              property condition is permutation invariant.
1005
                • Equivariant model architecture: the operations used in the network, i.e. Linear function,
1006
                  self-attention, layer normalization and residue operation, are all permutation equivariant.
1007
1008
       Therefore, the entire model is equivariant to node permutations: fθ (σ ⋆ Gt , σ ⋆ cond) = σ ⋆
1009
       fθ (Gt , cond).

1046   D.1    S AMPLING ALGORITHM
1047
1048   To sample a DAG from SeaDAG, we first sample the number of levels in the DAG: N ∼ p(N ),
1049   where p(N ) is the distribution of number of levels observed in the training set. Then we sample
1050
       the size, i.e. the number of nodes, for each level: Mi ∼ p(Mi |i), for i = 0, 1, ..., N − 1, where
       i is the level index, Mi is the level size and p(Mi |i) is the level size distribution of level i in the
1051
       training set. However, for AIG generation, the sizes of the first and last levels are predetermined
1052
       by the truth table condition. The size of the first level is set to Ninput which is the number of input
1053   gates and the size of the last level is set to Noutput which is the number of output gates. In the case of
1054   molecule generation, the size of the last level is set to one because of the tree structure of junction
1055   trees. Consequently, the sizes of these particular levels are not sampled but are instead predefined.
1056
       Once the number of levels and their respective sizes are determined, we can determine the level
1057
       assignment li for each node i. Subsequently, we sample an initial random graph based on the
1058
       marginal distributions of node types mX and edge types mE and use the denoising model fθ to
1059   gradually remove noise from the random graph. Algorithm 2 illustrates the above process.
1060

       D.2    AIG GENERATION SPECIFICATION
1062
1063   We now detail the procedure for parsing an AIG from the generated DAG G = (X, E) obtained from
1064   Algorithm 2. As previously discussed, the node types are predetermined during AIG generation, so
1065   we only need to parse the gate connections. For each AND gate i, we sample its two inputs from its
1066   children set Ch(ni ), which is defined by the i-th column of E. The edge from a child gate j to gate
1067   i is a normal edge if argmax(Eji ) = 1 or a negation edge if argmax(Eji ) = 2. If |Ch(ni )| < 2,
1068   we introduce a new input gate to the AIG that constantly outputs a signal of 0, and connect this new
1069
       gate to gate i. For output gates, we sample one input for each in a similar manner. Once the inputs
       have been determined for each AND gate and output gate, we remove floating gates from the AIG,
1070
       which are defined as the AND gates and input gates that are not in the cone of any output gates.
1071
       Finally, we simulate the resulting AIG to obtain its output for evaluation. For all baselines and our
1072   model, we generate 10 AIGs for a truth table and take the one with highest function accuracy.

1170   E.4   C ONDITIONAL GENERATION METRICS
1171
1172   We report two metrics when evaluating the conditional AIG generation performance: (1) Validity:
1173   Since an AIG is valid as long as the gates receive the correct number of inputs (two for AND gates
1174   and one for output gates), we compute the percentage of AND and output gates that have correct
1175   number of inputs as the validity of generated AIGs. (2) Accuracy: As discussed in Section B.3, only
1176   output signals participate in the computation of loss function and accuracy evaluation since the input
1177   signals are always fixed. Therefore, we take the output signals from condition truth tables and those
1178
       of the generated AIGs and compute the element-wise accuracy.
1179   We report the mean absolute error between the condition properties and the oracle-predicted prop-
1180   erties of molecules. To calculate the predicted properties of generated molecules, we closely follow
1181   the procedure used in You et al. (2023).
