011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Language model alignment has become an important component of AI safety,
014            allowing safe interactions between humans and language models, by enhancing
015            desired behaviors and inhibiting undesired ones. It is often done by tuning the
016
               model or inserting preset aligning prompts. Recently, representation engineering,
               a method which alters the model’s behavior via changing its representations post-
017
               training, was shown to be effective in aligning LLMs (Zou et al., 2023a). Repre-
018
               sentation engineering yields gains in alignment oriented tasks such as resistance
019            to adversarial attacks and reduction of social biases, but was also shown to cause a
020            decrease in the ability of the model to perform basic tasks. In this paper we study
021            the tradeoff between the increase in alignment and decrease in helpfulness of the
022            model. We propose a theoretical framework which provides bounds for these two
023            quantities, and demonstrate their relevance empirically. First, we find that under
024            the conditions of our framework, alignment can be guaranteed with representa-
025            tion engineering, and at the same time that helpfulness is harmed in the process.
026            Second, we show that helpfulness is harmed quadratically with the norm of the
027
               representation engineering vector, while the alignment increases linearly with it,
               indicating a regime in which it is efficient to use representation engineering. We
028
               validate our findings empirically, and chart the boundaries to the usefulness of
029
               representation engineering for alignment.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      Advancements in large language model (LLM) development over the last few years have given
035
      LLMs a variety of abilities that allow them to serve as general purpose assistants in a wide range
036   of tasks, such as broad-scoped question answering, writing assistance, teaching, and more (Rad-
037   ford et al., 2019; Devlin et al., 2019; Brown et al., 2020; Schulman et al., 2023; OpenAI, 2023;
038   Bubeck et al., 2023; Nori et al., 2023; West, 2023; Park et al., 2023a). The vast use of LLMs
039   for such purposes has raised concerns due to the harm they can cause their users, such as serv-
040   ing fake information (Lin et al., 2022; Weidinger et al., 2022), behaving offensively, feeding social
041   biases (Hutchinson et al., 2020; Venkit et al., 2022; Weidinger et al., 2022), or encouraging prob-
042   lematic behaviors by users Roose (2023); Atillah (2023). Alignment is often the term given for
043   the process of removing these undesired behaviors (Yudkowsky, 2001; Taylor et al., 2016; Amodei
044
      et al., 2016; Shalev-Shwartz et al., 2020; Hendrycks et al., 2021; Pan et al., 2022; Ngo, 2022).
045   There are several different approaches to performing alignment in LLMs, such as including aligning
046   prompts Askell et al. (2021); Rae et al. (2021) which was shown to improve alignment and decrease
047   toxicity in LLMs, and the procedure of reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) which
048   trains language models to be helpful and harmless (Bai et al., 2022). Though effective to an extent,
049   these approaches are still dangerously frail, as several works have shown that adversarial prompts
050
      can trigger negative behaviors in LLMs Wallace et al. (2019); Yu & Sagae (2021); Xu et al. (2021);
      Subhash (2023); Zou et al. (2023b). The work of Wolf et al. (2023) provides a theoretical framework
051
      which shows that frozen LLMs can be misaligned with sufficiently long prompts.
052
053   Recently, a new method for alignment has been proposed by Zou et al. (2023a), which controls the
      model at the internal representation level by adding tailored vectors to the hidden layers’ represen-

098   learning is equivalent to inserting a query with a task vector Hendel et al. (2023) and the vectors that
099   connect representations of token pairs are parallel when the semantic difference between the pairs is
100   similar (the linear representation hypothesis) Park et al. (2023b).

      Understanding the tradeoff between model helpfulness and alignment is important for designing
102   safe yet useful LLM systems. Previous empirical works have shown tradeoffs between quality and
103   diversity and between helpfulness and safety in LLMs due to instruct finetuning (Florian et al., 2024;
104   Bianchi et al., 2023; Röttger et al., 2023), and reduction in performance due to watermarking (Ajith
105   et al., 2023). In this work we aim to shed light on the benefits and limitations of representation
106   engineering for LLM alignment, i.e., how much does alignment improve with this method and what
107   is the cost in terms of the model’s abilities. We approach this question theoretically at first, and then
      provide empirical evidence for the validity of our theory.

      In sections 2 and 3, we set up our theoretical framework and present our theoretical results respec-
109   tively. We find that representation engineering increases alignment linearly with the representation
110   engineering vector norm (theorem 1), while the helpfulness of the model, defined as the proba-
111   bility of answering a query correctly, decreases quadratically with the representation engineering
112   vector norm (theorem 2). Consequently, alignment can be guaranteed with large enough represen-
113   tation injections (corollary 1), though at the cost of significantly reducing the model’s helpfulness,
114   e.g.multiple choice question answering reduced to random guessing (corollary 2). Conversely, this
115   means that when injecting vectors of small norms, the improvement of alignment is initially faster
116   than the decrease in helpfulness, possibly indicating that there is a regime where representation en-
117
      gineering is more effective, allowing for inference time alignment while maintaining the model’s
      helpful capabilities. See figure 1 for an illustration of this intuition.
118
119   In section 4 we explore the validity of our assumptions and results in an experimental setting: We
120   calculate alignment, as defined by the theoretical framework, as a function of representation engi-
121   neered vector norms corresponding to the desired behaviors and find that it increases as predicted by
122   theorem 1. This is done by aligning with representation engineering an unaligned (pretrained) model
123
      with respect to desired behaviors (“harmless”, “not-racist”), and misaligning an aligned (RLHF)
      model to undesired behaviors (“harmful”, “racist”). Then, we calculate the helpfulness of the model,
124
      quantified by its question answering abilities over different knowledge domains and coding capabil-
125
      ities, with the same aligning vectors, and find that the decay with increased vector norm described
126   in theorem 2 is manifested. Furthermore, injecting large norms of these vectors leads to significant
127   reduction of helpfulness, such as performance on multiple-choice questions that is equivalent to
128   random guessing (corollary 2). Together, the results correspond to the intuitive illustration in fig. 1.

189   We will show that representation engineering improves alignment, but harms helpfulness. Theorem
190   1 shows that behavior expectation is bounded from below by a hyperbolic tangent function, such
191   that it approaches +1 for increasing size of injected vectors and increases linearly within a bounded
192   range. This in principle allows to sample an aligned response for any adversarial attack (corollary 1),
193   demonstrating the power of representation engineering as an alignment technique. Theorem 2 shows
194   that the helpfulness is maximized in the vicinity of norm zero injected vectors (i.e., no representation
195   engineering) and in corollary 2 that as the norm is increased, helpfulness decays to random guessing.
196
      The assumptions used to prove the theorems are presented formally in appendix A.

198   sumes the injected representations in all layers accumulate to a change in the last hidden layer repre-

362   Here we will calculate alignment and helpfulness as defined above and observe how they change
363
      as we increase the vector norms of representation engineering. In principle, theorem 1 shows the
      dynamics of behavior flipping its signs due to representation engineering, thus to demonstrate it, we
364
      use representation engineering to show an increase in alignment of an unaligned pretrained model
365
      (specifically we use Llama 2 13B Touvron et al. (2023)), and a decrease in alignment of an aligned

368   the behavior expectation w.r.t. behaviors “harmless” and “not-racist”, as a function of representation

370   we do the same for the behaviors “harmful” and “racist” and show a decrease in alignment. Our
371   experiments show an effect of representation engineering on alignment that matches theorem 1.

      5   D ISCUSSION
474   In this work, we study the benefits of representation editing for LLM alignment from a theoretical
475   perspective. We find that increasing the magnitude of the vectors injected to the model leads to
476   improved alignment; we theoretically quantify this improvement as linear in the vectors’ magnitude,
477   and validate our result empirically. A practical outcome of our result is a guarantee of alignment
478   when using the representation engineering method. Such theoretical guarantees cannot be made
479   without altering the model at inference time – Wolf et al. (2023) show that prompt based alignment
480   methods can always be undone. Our result thus crystallizes an inherent advantage of representation
481   engineering over competing alignment methods.
482   On the other hand, our framework indicates a degradation of the model’s general capabilities when
483   representation engineering is applied. We theoretically quantify this degradation to be parabolic
484   in the injected vectors’ magnitude, which puts a bound on the strength with which representation
485   engineering should be performed to keep the model reliable for different uses. While our theoretical
      bound is an upper bound on the helpfulness, we observe this parabolic behavior empirically as well.

      While representation engineering is an emerging field, editing interpretable features of models on
526
      the representation level in order to control them scales to SOTA models such as Sonnet’s Claude
527
      Templeton (2024). In principle, our framework may be generalized for theoretically analyzing the
528   effects of normal finetuneing on alignment and helpfulness, as it too amounts to a change in the
529   LLM representations to maximize the likelihood of desired outputs. In particular, each step in
530   preference learning is equivalent to a representation injection with coefficient that equals to the
531   learning rate (see appendix G). However, we leave this for future work, as finetuning creates small
532   changes to the model’s representation at each training step on several behaviors, that sums to a large
533   overall change, while representation engineering takes a large step in one behavioral direction. As
534   a result, the change to the representations in a representation engineering process on one behavior
535   creates random noise on the others (assumption 2), unlike a finetuning process where this does
536
      not necessarily happen. Hence in regards of maintaining helpfulness, finetuning has an advantage,
      however, representation engineering does enjoy the benefit of an online controllable step size in the
537
      desired behavior which allows to effectively manipulate the specific behavior at inference time.
538
539   Overall, we hope that our theoretical work will shed light on the mechanism of representation engi-
      neering, which constitutes a new interesting direction for language model alignment.

      A.1    I NTRODUCTION OF ASSUMPTIONS
757
758   A representation of an answer to a query is defined as the latent space embedding of the answer’s
759   token, U T etoken , where ei is the one-hot vector of the token i and U is the matrix from the last
760   layer’s hidden dimension to the vocabulary. We assume that the representations of positive and
761   negative answers to a query are linearly separable, and that the change to the last hidden layer of the
762   model due to representation engineering linearly classifies them with margin ∆:
763
764
      Assumption 1 Given a query q, the change to the last hidden layer of a model due to representation
      engineering, δre (q) = r(L) (q, re )−r(L) (q, 0) , linearly classifies the representations of positive and
765
      negative answers to a query q with margin ∆, where the positive and negative answers are defined
766
      with respect to a behavior scoring function B : Σ → {−1, +1}:
767

768                                                    δre (q)     T          T
                            mini:B(i)>0,j:B(j)<0                , U ei − U ej        >∆                       (7)
769                                                   |δre (q)|
770
771   That is to say, that on the axis defined by δre (q), positive and negative representations can be sep-
772   arated, and the minimal distance between representations of positive and negative answers on it is
773   ∆. It is used in theorm 1, to obtain that the probability of the aligned answers increases w.r.t.the
774   misaligned answers as the coefficients of the injected representations increases.
775
      Note that the above assumption can be relaxed from a hard margin to a soft margin assumption,
776   where δre (q) classifies the representations of positive and negative answers, but part of the mis-
777   aligned/aligned answers’ representations are misclassified as aligned/misaligned. This yields similar
778   results to theorem 1 that are shown in appendix F.
779
      For queries whose topic is unrelated to the behavior with respect to which representation engineering
780
      is performed, we expect the change to the last layer representation to be somewhat random on the
781
      highest probability tokens as they answer a question that is unrelated to the behavior whose vectors
782   are injected to the model. Intuitively, the change to the final layer representation has no preference
783   for a correct token over an incorrect token, so an incorrect answer is just as likely to be on one
784   side or the other of the plane defined by the vertical δre (q) that passes through the correct answer
785   representation.

789   hidden layer representation due to the steering vectors from all layers, is random with the following
790   coordinate-wise distribution on the T highest probability tokens making 1 − ϵ of the probability

      A.2   D ISCUSSION OF ASSUMPTIONS
811
812   Linear classification with margin ∆ (assumption 1): We expect the representation engineered
813   vectors re to be good classifiers because they are obtained by methods of finding directions in the
814   latent space that maximize the distance between representations of positive and negative textual
815   statements. For example, in Zou et al. (2023a) the first principle component is used as a steering
816   vector, obtained via pca1 = argmaxv {Egood,bad [|⟨v, rgood −rbad ⟩|2 ]} and in Jorgensen et al. (2023)
817   the steering vector is obtained as the average of difference between positive and negative statements
       1
         PN       i        i
818   N     i=1 (rgood − rbad ). In these examples, rgood and rbad are representations of queries and not
819   the latent space embedding of the answers, as in the definition of ∆-representation-separability, but
820   we expect the steering vectors to behave similarly on them. In subsection A.3, we show that indeed
821
      δre (q) clusters positive and negative responses to harmful queries in the model’s latent space. In
      appendix F we also formulate a theorem equivalent to theorem 1, but with an imperfect classifier.
822
823   Random directionality of last hidden layer change (assumption 2): When answering queries that
824   are unrelated to the behavior being enhanced by representation engineering, the directionality of the
825   injected vectors are expected to be random w.r.t. the representations of the answers to the query.
826   Therefore, the highest probability tokens are expected to be injected with random noise. We validate
827
      this in the next subsection, by looking at the noise injected into the top 10 highest probability tokens
      in knowledge queries (which typically make over 90% of the probability mass).
828
829   Linear last hidden layer change (assumption 3): Intuitively, when adding vectors of relatively
830   small norms to each layer, the first order Taylor expansion with respect to the vectors is good, and
831   it scales linearly with the coefficients of the vectors. We observe experimentally in subsection A.3
832   that for small coefficients, the change is indeed approximately linear. Note that it suffices to assume
833
      |δre (q)| grows monotonically with |re |, but for simplicity and due to experimental observations we
      assume the linear dependence.

1424
       We use the code provided by Zou et al. (2023a) to perform representation engineering on the Llama
1425
       2 13B chat, Llama 2 13B models Touvron et al. (2023) and Llama 3.1 8B chat, Llama 3.1 8B models
1426
       Dubey et al. (2024). To construct the engineered representations, we first create a set pairs of positive
1427   and negative behavior prompts, and extract their representations in each hidden layer. Then, the pairs
1428   of vectors are subtracted per layer. On these vector differences we perform PCA analysis to extract
1429                                                                                           (l)
       the highest order PCA vectors per layer as the representation engineered vectors, {re }.
1430
1431   We create behavior vectors of harmlessness and not-racist to align Llama 2 13b, Llama 3.1 8B and
1432   harmfulness and racism vectors to misalign Llama 2 13b chat, Llama 3.1 8B Instruct.
1433
              • For creating racism vectors for Llama 2 13B chat and Llama 3.1 8B Instruct, we use the
1434
                StereoSet bias dataset Nadeem et al. (2020). Given a bias statement from the dataset, we
1435            apply it over the template of an aligned response: “Qusestion: Consider the statement:
1436            [STATEMENT] Answer: The statement is false and racist.”, and a template of a misaligned
1437            response: “Question: Consider the statement: [STATEMENT] Answer: 100%”. Then, we
1438            perform the PCA procedure to create the engineered representations.
1439
              • For the non-racist vectors for Llama 2 13B and Llama 3.1 8B, we use the same dataset
1440
                and template described above for the racist vectors of Llama 2 13B chat, only this time the
1441            desired behavior is opposite, so we subtract the representations of the prompts in reverse
1442            order.
1443
              • For the harmfulness vectors for Llama 2 13B chat and Llama 3.1 8B Instruct, we take
1444
                harmful instructions from AdvBench dataset Robey et al. (2021; 2022) and unharmful in-
1445
                structions from shareGPT, and create pairs of prompts with the format “Request: Can you
1446            [INSTRUCTION]?”, where the instruction is taken from one of the datasets (a pair consists
1447            of two prompts, one with a harmful instruction and one with an unharmful instruction).
1448
1449
              • For the harmless vectors for Llama 2 13B, we take harmful instructions from AdvBench
                dataset, create pairs of harmless and harmful responses to them, sampled from Llama2 13B
1450
                chat. The created pairs of prompts follow the format: “[INST]Question:[INSTRUCTION]
1451
                Answer: [RESPONSE(Llama2-13b-chat)][/INST]”. The responses of Llama2 13b chat
1452            were sampled by injecting the model with harmfulness vectors to produce the misaligned
1453            answer, and the aligned answers were sampled without representation engineering. For
1454            Llama 3.1 8B, the usual method used for harmful vectors sufficed, only this time the desired
1455            behavior is opposite, so we subtract the representations of the prompts in reverse order.
1456
