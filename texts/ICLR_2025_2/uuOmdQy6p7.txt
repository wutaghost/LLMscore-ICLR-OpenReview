009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               Current multi-task adversarial text attacks rely on white-box access to shared in-
012
               ternal features and assumption of homogeneous multi-task learning framework.
013
               As a result, these attacks are less effective against practical scenarios involving
014            black-box feedback APIs and multi-model multi-task learning. To bridge this gap,
015            we introduce Cluster and Ensemble Mutil-task Text Adversarial Attack (CEMA),
016            an effective black-box attack that exploits the transferability of adversarial texts.
017            Specifically, we initially employ cluster-oriented substitute model training, as a
018            plug-and-play framework, to simplify complex multi-task scenarios into more
019            manageable text classification attacks and train the substitute model. Next, we
020            generate multiple adversarial candidate examples by applying various adversarial
021            text classification methods. Finally, we select the adversarial example that attacks
022
               the most substitute models as the final attack output. CEMA is evaluated on two
               primary multi-task objectives: text classification and translation. In the classifica-
023
               tion task, CEMA achieves attack success rates that exceed 60% while reducing the
024
               total number of queries to 100. For the text translation task, the BLEU scores of
025            both victim texts and adversarial examples decrease to below 0.36 with 100 queries
026            even including the commercial translation APIs, such as Baidu Translate and Ali
027            Translate.

031   1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      A multi-task textual adversarial attack misleads multiple tasks simultaneously through small pertur-
034
      bations, increasing attack efficiency and impact. It poses significant risks to safety-critical systems,
035
      leading to wrong decisions. Defending against such attacks is challenging due to the need for
036   multi-task robustness, making it a key issue in AI security (Liu et al., 2017; Lin et al., 2022).
037
038   Research on text multi-task adversarial examples typically concentrates on tasks of the same type,
039
      particularly classification tasks (Liu et al., 2017). However, in real-world applications, multi-task
      learning often involves tasks of different types. Existing adversarial attack methods generally assume
040
      that attackers have access to the model architecture and shared layer information within a unified
041
      model (Guo et al., 2020). However, most commercial and application-based models are proprietary,
042   with their architecture and parameters hidden from external attackers. Additionally, current multi-task
043   adversarial attack strategies primarily target models that employ a shared parameter approach for
044   managing multiple tasks. In contrast, multi-model multi-task learning approaches (Aoki et al., 2022)
045   handle each task with a separate model, without direct parameter sharing. As a result, most existing
046   adversarial methods, designed to attack shared parameter models, are ineffective against these systems
047   because of the absence of a common layer to target.
048   Our goal is to perform multi-task textual adversarial attacks in realistic scenarios. Based on the
049   previous analysis, such a scenario should encompass a variety of tasks, with black-box model feedback
050   being more reflective of real-world conditions. Moreover, both parameter-sharing multi-task learning
051   systems and multi-model multi-task learning systems must be considered. Additionally, limiting the
052   number of queries is essential to conserve resources and reduce the risk of detection, making it a
053   key aspect of practical attack scenarios. Therefore, this paper is driven by the following research
      questions(RQ):

061
      In limited-access scenarios, a straightforward strategy is the transfer attack, which crafts adversarial
      examples in a substitute model. Training effective substitute models becomes challenging in the
062
      absence of a well-trained substitute model, particularly in multi-task learning, where limited access
063
      to input-output pairs and poor transferability between tasks in multi-model settings pose significant
064   difficulties. Rather than mimicking the entire multi-task model, we propose focusing on building a
065   substitute model with strong discriminability. This approach allows a single substitute model to
066   generate adversarial examples that target all tasks simultaneously, even when trained with limited
067   data.
068
      We propose CEMA (Cluster and Ensemble Multi-task Text Adversarial Attack), a framework that
069
      leverages a small set of auxiliary texts sharing characteristics with the victim’s texts. Using a pre-
070   trained model, we vectorize texts and their outputs, perform clustering, and train substitute models
071   on these auxiliary texts and cluster labels. This converts the multi-task attack into a single-task text
072   classification problem. Repeating this process, we can obtain multiple substitute models. During the
073   adversarial example generation phase for victim texts, for each victim text, adversarial candidates are
074   generated for each victim text. The final adversarial example is selected based on its success across
075   the most substitute models.
076   Although the substitute model trained by CEMA differs from the victim model trained through
077   multi-task learning, our substitute model, demonstrates strong discriminative capability. For task
078   A, if an adversarial attack on the substitute model f sub successfully changes the cluster label of text
079   xi from 0 to 1, the label yiA shifts accordingly, indicating a successful attack on task A. We derive
080   and demonstrate that adversarial examples based on cluster labels, when effective against multiple
081   substitute models, can also transfer effectively to other tasks B, C, . . . , N .
082   During the experiment, we focus on text classification and translation within a multi-task learning
083   framework. For the text classification task, CEMA achieves an attack success rate (ASR) of over
084   60% with only 100 queries. In the text translation task, CEMA reaches a BLEU score of 0.14. Even
085   with limited auxiliary data that differs significantly from the training dataset, CEMA maintains an
086   ASR of up to 66.40% for classification tasks and a BLEU score of 0.27 for translation tasks. The
087   primary contributions are summarized as follows: ❶ To the best of our knowledge, we are the
088   first to extend text adversarial attacks to the multi-task setting by training cluster-oriented substitute
089   models and employing transferability-oriented adversarial example selection. The proposed CEMA
090
      method generates high-quality adversarial examples for multiple tasks simultaneously with very few
      queries in black-box and multi-model multi-task learning scenarios. ❷ We present the first plug-
091
      and-play framework that converts a multi-task attack into a single-task attack, enabling traditional
092
      methods to be easily adapted to multi-task scenarios. Furthermore, our approach overcomes the
093   limitations of existing multi-task attack methods, which depend on shared layers in multi-task models.
094   CEMA effectively handles multi-task scenarios with multi-models, whether they involve related
095   or independent tasks. Additionally, we derive a theoretical lower bound for CEMA’s success rate,
096   showing that the probability of success increases with the number of substitute models used. ❸
097   We demonstrate the effectiveness of CEMA through rigorous mathematical derivations, as well as
098   comprehensive experiments. The experimental results show the proposed CEMA achieves an attack
099   success rate (ASR) of over 60% in text classification tasks and a BLEU score of less than 0.15 in
100   translation tasks, indicating effective adversarial attack performance in both cases.

104   2.1   T RANSFERABILITY AND T RANSFER ATTACKS
105
106   Transfer attacks leverage adversarial examples to target different models without requiring direct
107   access, posing a significant security threat in black-box scenarios (Szegedy et al., 2014; Papernot
      et al., 2017; Dong et al., 2018; Tramèr et al., 2017). Transferability refers to the phenomenon


                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      where adversarial examples crafted for one model can successfully compromise other models as
109   well (Zhang et al., 2020). Notably, several existing studies increase the amount of data available
110   to attackers (Mahmood et al., 2021a) or generate synthetic data (Zhou et al., 2020), significantly
111   advancing the development of transfer attacks. Meanwhile, Mahmood et al. (2021b) improve the
112   transferability and robustness of Vision Transformers to adversarial examples
113
114   2.2   M ULTI -TASK L EARNING AND M ULTI -M ODEL M ULTI -TASK L EARNING
115
116   Multi-Task Learning (MTL) involves simultaneously training multiple related tasks, enabling
117   models to share knowledge and improve generalization, particularly when data is limited. MTL
118   has been extensively applied in fields such as natural language processing and computer vision,
119   resulting in more robust models. However, challenges such as task interference and balancing
120
      shared information across tasks remain. Recent advancements seek to mitigate these challenges and
      enhance MTL’s overall effectiveness. Multi-Model Multi-Task Learning extends the traditional
121
      MTL framework by utilizing separate models for each task, providing greater flexibility and better
122
      handling of task heterogeneity. This approach minimizes negative transfer and allows for task-
123   specific optimizations. However, it also increases computational complexity and the difficulty of
124   integrating outputs from different models. Current research focuses on hybrid methods that balance
125   task specialization with shared learning, aiming to optimize model architectures and enhance resource
126   efficiency.

253      4.2   C LUSTER N UMBER AND C LUSTER L ABELS
254
255     In Section 4.1, we obtain the representations for each text input and output. We then perform a
256     clustering analysis on these representations, with the number of clusters being a crucial parameter.
257     Before clustering, we determine the optimal number of clusters by selecting the value that maximizes
258
        strong discriminative capability for each cluster group. When the number of clusters is 2, the two
        clusters can be interpreted as class CA and CA . (Boongoen & Iam-On, 2018). Therefore, we set the
259
        number of clusters to 2. After determining the number of clusters to be 2, we perform clustering
260
        analysis on the 100 vectors using the Spectral clustering method (Zhang et al., 1996). For each
261     vector Ei , we derive its corresponding cluster label yiclu , which is later assigned as the pseudolabel for
262     xi . With only 100 unlabeled texts, we cannot fully capture the dataset’s distribution. Therefore, we
263     perform multiple clustering runs and select the result that best approximates a uniform distribution
264     by maximizing entropy (i.e., ensuring the cluster sizes are as close to 50 as possible). The proof of
265     entropy maximization for a uniform distribution is in Section B of the appendix.

346   multi-task scenarios, we select traditional textual attack methods. For text classification, we use
347   BAE (Garg & Ramakrishnan, 2020), FD (Papernot et al., 2016), Hotflip (Ebrahimi et al., 2018b),
348   SememePSO (Zang et al., 2020), and TextBugger (Ren et al., 2019). For text translation, we select
349   Hotflip (Trans) (Ebrahimi et al., 2018b), kNN (Michel et al., 2019), Morphin (Tan et al., 2020),
350   RA (Zou et al., 2019), Seq2Sick (Cheng et al., 2020), and TransFool (Sadrizadeh et al., 2023). CEMA
351
      operates with substantially fewer queries. For a fair comparison, we limit all baseline methods to
      30 final queries when attacking the target text. Preliminary details about these methods are listed in
352
      Tables 9a and 9b in Appendix H. Metrics: We use the following metrics to evaluate our method: ❶
353
      ASR (Attack Success Rate): A higher ASR indicates a more effective attack. ❷ Average Query:
354   Fewer queries suggest a better attack method. ❸ BLEU (Bilingual Evaluation Understudy): A
355   lower BLEU score signifies a more successful disruption of translation quality.

      5.7        Z ERO -S HOT ATTACK OF CEMA
523
524   In this section, we evaluate CEMA’s effectiveness under more stringent conditions, where the attacker
525   can only access data related to the training set. Both the SST5 and Emotion datasets are related
526   to sentiment analysis but differ significantly in label space and distribution. To test this, we used
527   100 unlabeled texts from the Emotion validation set as auxiliary data for the SST5 attack, and vice
528   versa. The results in Table 6 show that, despite limited auxiliary data and significant distribution
529   differences, CEMA achieves a 66.40% attack success rate and a BLEU score of 0.27. This suggests
530   that an attacker needs only partial knowledge of the training data and can collect relevant data from
531
      the Internet to execute a successful attack on the multi-task system using CEMA.
532
533   6      C ONCLUSION
534
535   In this paper, we present a more practical multi-task learning scenario where attackers can only access
536   final black-box outputs through limited queries. To address this challenge, we propose the CEMA
537   method, which achieves state-of-the-art (SOTA) performance in experimental evaluations with just
538   100 queries and black-box outputs. Furthermore, CEMA can incorporate any text classification attack
539   algorithm, and its performance improves as the number of attack algorithms increases. In the future,
      we aim to extend CEMA to multi-task models across other modalities.

      A.1    T EXT C LASSIFICATION A DVERSARIAL ATTACK
793
794   In historical textual adversarial research, the predominant methods revolve around scenarios with
795   singular output results (Waghela et al., 2024; Han et al., 2024; Zhu et al., 2024; Kang et al., 2024).
796   These studies focus on the techniques for morphing the original text into adversarial counterparts,
797   including the manipulation of pivotal chars (Ebrahimi et al., 2018b; Gil et al., 2019; Ebrahimi et al.,
798   2018a; Gao et al., 2018; Ren et al., 2019; Jin et al., 2020; Li et al., 2019), words (Wang et al.,
799   2022; Guo et al., 2021; Meng & Wattenhofer, 2020; Sato et al., 2018; Cheng et al., 2019; Lee et al.,
800   2022; Li et al., 2020a; Hu et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024; 2023; Li et al., 2019) and sentence. These
801
      methods are segmented into three distinct categories based on the response from the target model,
      encompassing white-box attacks, soft-label black-box attacks, and hard-label black-box attacks. In
802
      white-box attacks, adversaries gain full access to all relevant information about the target model.
803
      The Hotflip (Ebrahimi et al., 2018b) sequentially replaces crucial words based on their calculated
804   importance scores. The FD method (Papernot et al., 2016) constructs adversarial examples depending
805   on the model’s gradient information. In soft-label black-box attacks, numerous methods are geared
806   towards disturbing the words in accordance with output probabilities (Lee et al., 2022; Maheshwary
807   et al., 2021b; Wang et al., 2021a; Li et al., 2020a). Bert-ATTACK (Li et al., 2020a) focuses on word
808   attacks using a refined Bert model. SememePSO (Zang et al., 2020) enhances the search landscape
809   to construct adversarial examples. Bae (Garg & Ramakrishnan, 2020) is an attack strategy centered
      on BERT to replace words. Simultaneously, the DeepWordBug (DWB) method (Gao et al., 2018)


                                                       15
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
      prioritizes the words for assault based on the output probabilities. Hard-label adversarial attacks
811   present a more realistic scenario. HLGA (Maheshwary et al., 2021a) employs stochastic starting
812   words and employs a genetic algorithm to craft adversarial examples. HQA-attack (Liu et al., 2024)
813   starts by maximally restoring original words, reducing disruption. It then uses synonyms of remaining
814   altered words to enhance the adversarial example.

817   A.2    N EURAL M ACHINE T RANSLATION A DVERSARIAL ATTACK
818
819   Neural Machine Translation (NMT) models, which automatically convert input sentences into trans-
820   lated output, have achieved remarkable results by employing deep neural networks like Transformers
821   (Bahdanau, 2014; Vaswani, 2017). These models are now extensively used across various applica-
822   tions due to their high performance. However, erroneous outputs generated by NMT models can
823   lead to significant risks, particularly in security-sensitive contexts. Recent research has explored
824
      adversarial attacks targeting NMT models to address these concerns. Character-level NMT models
      are highly vulnerable to character manipulations such as typos in a block-box setting (Belinkov
825
      & Bisk, 2017; Ebrahimi et al., 2018a). as well as pushing/removing words from the translation.
826
      However, character manipulations and typos are easily detected by humans or review strategies.
827   Hence, most adversarial attacks against NLP and NMT systems use a word replacement strategy
828   instead. Seq2sick (Cheng et al., 2020) proposes a projected gradient method combined with group
829   lasso and gradient regularization, conducting non-overlapping attacks and targeted keyword attacks.
830   Similarly, Transfool (Sadrizadeh et al., 2023) also uses the gradient projection method, defining
831   a new optimization problem and linguistic constraints to compute semantic-preserving and fluent
832   attacks against NMT models. Morphin (Tan et al., 2020) generates plausible and semantically similar
833   adversaries by perturbing the inflections in clean examples to investigate the robustness of NLP
834   models to inflectional perturbation. kNN (Michel et al., 2019)is a white-box untargeted attack against
835
      NMT models that substitutes some words with their neighbors in the embedding space. RG (Zou
      et al., 2019)investigates the issue by generating adversarial examples through a new paradigm based
836
      on reinforcement learning, which generates more reasonable tokens and secures semantic constraints.
837
838
839
      A.3    M UTIL - TASK A DVERSARIAL ATTACK
840
841
      A Multi-task Adversarial Attack is an adversarial machine learning strategy designed to generate
842   examples that deceive multiple models or systems simultaneously (Guo et al., 2020; Ghamizi et al.,
843   2022), rather than just one. As far as we know, there is currently no related work on multi-task
844   adversarial attacks in the field of text. In other fields, MTA (Guo et al., 2020) is designed to generate
845   adversarial perturbations for all three pre-trained classifiers simultaneously by leveraging shared
846   knowledge among tasks. There is an attack method (Sobh et al., 2021) that targets visual perception
847   in autonomous driving, which is applied in a wide variety of multi-task visual perception deep
848   networks in distance estimation, semantic segmentation, motion detection, and object detection.
849   MTADV (Wang et al., 2024) is a multitask adversarial attack against facial authentication, which is
850
      effective against various facial data sets.

      A.4    T RANSFER ATTACK
853
854
855
      Transfer attacks leverage adversarial examples to target different models without requiring direct
      access, posing a significant security threat in black-box scenarios (Papernot et al., 2017; Dong et al.,
856
      2018). Then, in the absence of a substitute model, several studies demonstrate that auxiliary data can
857
      also facilitate successful attacks through training a substitute model and leveraging transfer attacks (Li
858   et al., 2020c; Sun et al., 2022). Additionally, more effective loss functions have been proposed to
859   train substitute models (Wang et al., 2021b; Li et al., 2020b; Naseer et al., 2019; Richards et al., 2021;
860   Huan et al., 2020), as well as techniques to refine substitute models (Xiaosen et al., 2023; Yuan et al.,
861   2021). Notably, several existing studies increase the amount of data available to attackers (Mahmood
862   et al., 2021a) or generate synthetic data (Zhou et al., 2020), significantly advancing the development
863   of transfer attacks. Meanwhile, Mahmood et al. (2021b) improve the transferability and robustness of
      Vision Transformers to adversarial examples

      D.1     S UBSTITUTE M ODEL A RCHITECTURE
921
922
      Our substitute model comprises 12 transformer blocks, each with 768 hidden units and 12 self-
923   attention heads. Each transformer block consists of the following substructures:
924
925           • Self-Attention Layer: The hidden size of the self-attention layer is 768.
926
927           • Position-wise Feed-Forward Network: The network first projects the output of the attention
928             layer to a 3072-dimensional space using a fully connected layer, followed by a ReLU
929
                activation for non-linearity, and finally projects the 3072-dimensional space back to a
                768-dimensional space via another fully connected layer.
930
931           • Layer Normalization and Residual Connection:
932
                     – Layer Normalization: Applied to the output of each sub-layer to stabilize training.
933
934                  – Residual Connection: Adds the normalized output to the input of the sub-layer.

940   activation function “GELU” is used, The dropout rate is 0.4. The training process is optimized with
941   the AdamW optimizer (Yao et al., 2021), with batch size set to 64 and learning rate set to 6e − 3,

1073
       Transfer attacks involve an attacker generating adversarial examples using a substitute model, which
1074   are then successfully applied to attack multiple target models. These target models may differ in
1075   architecture or training data from the substitute model. This type of attack exploits the shared
1076   characteristics of adversarial examples across models, allowing these samples to transfer and affect
1077   multiple models. The success of a transfer attack typically depends on the degree of similarity
1078   between the substitute model and the target model. Consequently, even if the attacker cannot access
1079   the internal information of the target model, they can still use adversarial examples generated from
       the substitute model to successfully attack the target model.

1380   The probability of successfully attacking the victim model using adversarial examples generated by
1381
       methods 1, 2, ..., n is greater than or equal to the probability of successfully attacking the victim
       model using adversarial examples generated by method 1 alone. This is because, when only method 1
1382
       is used, there is only one candidate adversarial example per victim text. In contrast, when n methods
1383

1384   generated by method 1. Therefore, the probability of successfully attacking the victim model using
1385   adversarial examples generated by n methods is greater than or equal to the probability of successfully
1386   attacking the victim model using adversarial examples from method 1 alone. The probabilities are
1387   equal only when method 1 achieves the maximum success rate for all victim texts. However, the
1388   SST5 and Emotion datasets contain 2,210 and 2,000 victim texts, respectively, making it unlikely
1389   that method 1 will achieve the maximum success rate across all victim texts. Thus, we conclude that,
1390   in most cases, the probability of successfully attacking the victim model using adversarial examples
1391   generated by n methods is greater than when using adversarial examples generated by method 1
1392
       alone.
1393   Furthermore, based on this property, we can deduce that, in most cases, the probability of successfully
1394   attacking the victim model using adversarial examples generated by methods 1, 2, ..., n is greater than
1395   when using adversarial examples generated by methods 1, 2, ..., m, where n > m. In other words,
1396   employing more methods to generate adversarial examples increases the likelihood of a successful
1397   attack on the victim model.

1408   In traditional machine learning, each model typically handles a single task. In contrast, Multi-task
1409
       Learning (MTL) involves jointly training multiple related tasks with a shared model. The goal
       is to allow the model to simultaneously optimize multiple objectives by sharing representations,
1410
       knowledge, or parameters. Common applications include sentiment analysis and text classification,
1411
       where the same features can be used for multiple tasks (e.g., predicting sentiment labels and classifying
1412   news articles). For instance, training a neural network to simultaneously perform two tasks: image
1413   classification and object detection.
1414
1415   U.1.2    M ULTI - MODEL L EARNING
1416
1417   Unlike traditional single-model approaches, Multi-model Learning uses multiple independent or
1418   combined models to solve a problem. Each model may focus on different aspects of the problem
1419
       or apply different algorithms to address the same task. For example, using multiple models such
       as neural networks, decision trees, and support vector machines to handle the same task, thereby
1420
       leveraging the strengths of each model.
1421
1422
       U.1.3    M ULTI - MODEL M ULTI - TASK L EARNING (MMMTL)
1423
1424   MMMTL is a method that combines Multi-task Learning and Multi-model Learning. The core idea is
1425   to use multiple models (e.g., neural networks, decision trees, support vector machines, etc.) to learn
1426   multiple related tasks, with these models sharing some information or parameters. This means that
1427   during training, MMMTL models handle multiple tasks and models simultaneously, enabling each
1428   model to learn across multiple tasks while sharing representations and knowledge between tasks.
