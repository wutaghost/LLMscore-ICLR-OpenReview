009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011
               The emerging field of data distillation aims to compress large datasets by aligning
012            synthetic and real data representations to create a highly informative dataset. The
013            optimization objectives of data distillation focus on aligning representations by
014            using process alignment methods such as trajectory and gradient matching. How-
015            ever, this approach is limited by the strict alignment of intermediate quantities
016            between synthetic and real data and the mismatch between their optimization tra-
017            jectories. To address these limitations, a new data distillation method called effect
018            alignment is proposed, which aims to only push for the consistency of endpoint
019            training results. The approach uses classification tasks to estimate the impact of
020
               replacing real training samples with synthetic data, which helps to learn a synthetic
               dataset that can replace the real dataset and achieve effect alignment. The method
021
               is efficient and does not require costly mechanisms, and satisfactory results have
022
               been achieved through experiments.

026   1    I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Data distillation (Wang et al., 2018; Li et al., 2022; Zhao & Bilen, 2021a;b) is an emerging direction
029   aimed at compressing real datasets with wide-ranging applications, such as continual learning (Yang
030   et al., 2023a; Gu et al., 2023), federated learning (Xiong et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2023), graph
031   learning (Zhang et al., 2024b; Liu et al., 2024), etc. Unlike data selection (Haizhong Zheng et al.,
032   2023; Xia et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2023b; Tan et al., 2023), data distillation learns a highly informative
033
      dataset to compress the original large real dataset.
034   Generally, the dataset distillation problem is formulated as a heavy bi-level optimization problem
035   as shown in Sec. 2.1. Due to the extremely burdensome nature of the original problem, we have to
036   seek surrogate optimization tasks, such as feature space alignment (Zhao & Bilen, 2021a; Zhao
037   et al., 2023; Sajedi et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024a) and optimization process alignment (Zhao
038   et al., 2020; Zhao & Bilen, 2021b; Feng et al., 2023; Du et al., 2024; Cazenavette et al., 2022; Du
039
      et al., 2023; Cui et al., 2023; Guo et al., 2023). Although the above surrogate problem significantly
      reduces the difficulty of optimization, the surrogate optimization is not completely equivalent to
040
      the optimization of the original problem, and this mismatch will cause potential obstacles to the
041
      performance. Additionally, there is a type of method that adopts backpropagation through time
042   (BPTT (Werbos, 1990)) (Wang et al., 2018; Sucholutsky & Schonlau, 2021; Deng & Russakovsky,
043   2022) or Kernel ridge regression (Nguyen et al., 2020) to attempt to directly optimize the original
044   problem. However, such approaches are often limited by computational efficiency constraints. All of
045   these would be further detailed in Sec.2.2.
046
      In this work, we propose a novel and efficient dataset distillation framework called effect alignment
047
      to avoid those potential issues described above. Specifically, our goal is to optimize by learning a
048   synthetic dataset that can replace real datasets in terms of training effectiveness. A naive implemen-
049   tation would require retraining a model on the synthetic data each time it is updated, which would
050   be unacceptably costly and would require a very resource-intensive back-propagation-through-time
051   (BPTT) mechanism (Werbos, 1990). To achieve the goal of effect alignment, we theoretically estimate
052   the impact of removing or adding a set of data from the training set on the training results based on
053   Taylor approximation and unfolding of the training process. With the proposed estimator, we estimate
      how replacing real training samples with synthetic data will impact the training results and set our


                                                           1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      optimization objective to minimize the replacement effect. This allows us to learn a synthetic data set
055   that can replace the real data set without adopting BPTT (Werbos, 1990).
056
057   Empirical results on diverse bias-injected datasets demonstrate that the proposed reweighting scheme
058
      significantly reduces bias in the distilled datasets. For example, on Colored MNIST with a 5% bias in
      conflicting samples and 50 images per class, the original Distribution Matching (DM) method leads
059
      to a biased synthetic set. A model trained on such a distilled set achieves only 23.8% accuracy. In
060
      contrast, our reweighting method produces a more balanced dataset, resulting in 91.5% accuracy,
061   representing a 67.7% gain over DM. In summary, our contributions are: (1) We provide the first study
062   on the impact of biases in the dataset condensation process. (2) We propose a simple yet effective
063   re-weighting scheme to mitigate biases in two canonical types of dataset condensation methods. (3)
064   Through extensive experiments and ablation studies on both real-world and synthesized datasets, we
065   demonstrate the effectiveness of our method.

092   2.2   R ELATED W ORKS
093
094   Besides adopting meta gradient to optimize the original problem (Nguyen et al., 2020), one can also
095   seek a proper surrogate task, such as feature space alignment (Zhao & Bilen, 2021a; Zhao et al.,
096   2023; Sajedi et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024a) and optimization process alignment (Zhao & Bilen,
097   2021b; Feng et al., 2023; Du et al., 2024; Cazenavette et al., 2022; Du et al., 2023; Cui et al., 2023;
098
      Guo et al., 2023). In the following, we mainly review these three kinds of works.
099   Meta gradient. For solving the bi-level dataset distillation problem defined by Eq.2, one straight-
100   forward way is to directly optimize via estimating the meta gradient of the synthetic dataset. For
101   example, Wang et al. (2018); Sucholutsky & Schonlau (2021); Deng & Russakovsky (2022) proposed
102   to unfold the inner-loop optimizing trajectory and utilizing the backpropagation through time (BPTT
103   (Werbos, 1990)) to update the synthetic data. While both unfolding the training process and running
104
      BPTT are resource-intensive and hinder method efficiency, Nguyen et al. (2020); Zhou et al. (2022);
      Loo et al. (2022) found solutions by replacing the neural network in the inner loop with a kernel
105
      model that has closed-form solutions in the kernel regression regime.
106
107   Feature space alignment. There is one line of work (Zhao & Bilen, 2021a; Zhao et al., 2023;
      Sajedi et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024a) tries to match the latent feature space directly. Zhao &


                                                            2

108
      Bilen (2021a) proposed to match the synthetic and target data from the distribution perspective for
109   dataset distillation. Wang et al. (2022) improved the distribution matching from several aspects:
110   (1) using multiple-layer features other than only the last-layer features for matching, (2) proposing
111   the discrimination loss to enlarge the class distinction of synthetic data. Zhao et al. (2023) add a
112   classification loss as regularization to mitigate less classified synthetic data caused by the first-order
113   moment mean matching. Sajedi et al. (2023) proposed to learn synthetic images by matching the
114   spatial attention maps of real and synthetic data generated by different layers within a family of
115   randomly initialized neural networks. Zhang et al. (2024a) proposed to minimize the maximum mean
116   discrepancy (MMD) between the real and the synthetic data.
117   Optimization process alignment. This kind of method (Zhao et al., 2020; Zhao & Bilen, 2021b; Du
118   et al., 2024; Cazenavette et al., 2022; Du et al., 2023; Cui et al., 2023; Guo et al., 2023) constructs the
119   surrogate problem of matching the intermediate training state contributed by the synthetic data and the
120   real data, respectively. Among them, matching gradient (Zhao et al., 2020) in training and matching
121   trajectory (Cazenavette et al., 2022) in training are the most representative schemes. Zhao & Bilen
122   (2021b) proposed to incorporate the gradient matching framework with a differentiable augmentation
123
      scheme to synthesize more informative synthetic images and for better performance when training
      networks with augmentations. Du et al. (2024) introduces a new distillation strategy called SeqMatch
124
      to address the issue of failing to condense high-level features in dataset distillation. It divides synthetic
125
      data into multiple subsets, sequentially optimizing them to promote the effective condensation of
126   high-level features learned in later epochs. Cazenavette et al. (2022) propose a new formulation that
127   optimizes our distilled data to guide networks to a similar state as those trained on real data across
128   many training steps. Guo et al. (2023) also incorporates the distillation of easy/difficulty information
129   into the trajectory matching framework, achieving further performance improvements.
130
      Others. More recently, some work (Yin et al., 2024; Sun et al., 2023) has also incorporated the
131
      diversity of data and the authenticity of the synthesized data into the framework design considerations.
132   In addition, some work (Cazenavette et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023) has explored
133   the applicability of generative models in dataset distillation.

136   3     R EPLACEMENT E FFECT: D EFINITION , A PPROXIMATION , AND G UARANTEE
137
138   Here, we state the core of our dataset distillation pipeline, namely the replacement effect, which
139   measures the effect on the training results of replacing a group of training data with a group of new
140
      data. The following statements begin with the definition of the replacement effect metric in Sec. 3.1,
      which measures how replacing a group of training data with another group of new data will affect
141
      the performance of the final learned parameter. However, the exact computation of this metric is
142
      extremely costly. Then, in Sec. 3.2, we provide an efficient approximator for the replacement effect
143   along with the theoretical error guarantee.
144
145
      3.1   D EFINITION
146

      It is noteworthy that the form of R is similar to the traditional leaving-one-out (LOO) retraining
163   influence (Jia et al., 2021; Cook, 1986; Koh & Liang, 2017) in machine learning. Several efficient
164   estimators (Koh & Liang, 2017; Pruthi et al., 2020; Tan et al., 2023; Kwon et al., 2023; Hara et al.,
165   2019) exist for retraining influence, with Koh’s estimator (Koh & Liang, 2017) being the most
166   renowned. Nonetheless, Koh’s estimator (Koh & Liang, 2017) assumes that the loss function L is
167   convex to the parameter θ, which is not the case for deep learning (Choromanska et al., 2015; Dauphin
168   et al., 2014). Additionally, it requires the calculation of the inverse Hessian, which is computationally
169   expensive (Agarwal et al., 2017). Therefore, in the next subsection (Sec. 3.2), instead of relying on
170   existing estimators such as Koh’s, we developed our estimator by expanding the training process and
171
      using Taylor approximation, accompanied by a theoretical error upper bound guarantee.

316   We conduct experiments on the MNIST, FashionMNIST, SVHN, CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100, and Tiny
317   ImageNet.
318   MNIST. The MNIST (Modified National Institute of Standards and Technology) database is a
319   well-known benchmark dataset in the field of machine learning and computer vision. It consists
320   of a training set of 60,000 handwritten digits (0 - 9) and a test set of 10,000 digits. The digits are
321   grayscale images with a size of 28×28 pixels. This dataset is widely used for tasks such as digit
322   classification, and it has played a crucial role in the development and evaluation of many image-based
323   machine learning algorithms, especially for testing the performance of neural networks in recognizing
      handwritten digits.

      FashionMNIST. This is a dataset designed to serve as a direct replacement for the MNIST dataset
325   for benchmarking machine-learning algorithms. It contains 70,000 grayscale images of 10 different
326   fashion categories such as T-shirts, trousers, pullovers, dresses, coats, sandals, shirts, sneakers, bags,
327   and ankle boots. The images are also of size 28×28 pixels. It provides a more diverse and real-world-
328   like set of classification tasks compared to MNIST, as it deals with different types of clothing items
329   and accessories, allowing researchers to test the generalization capabilities of their models on a more
330   complex and practical set of objects.
331   SVHN (Netzer et al., 2011). The SVHN dataset contains real-world images of house numbers
332   obtained from Google Street View. It has a training set of 73,257 digits, a validation set of 26,032
333   digits, and a test set of 26,032 digits. The digits in the images can be part of a sequence, and the
334   images are of different sizes and colors (they are in color, not grayscale like MNIST). This dataset
335   is challenging because of the variability in the appearance of the digits due to different lighting
336   conditions, angles, and occlusions. It is used to train models to recognize digits in more natural and
337   unconstrained settings.
338   CIFAR-10 (Alex Krizhevsky, 2009). This dataset consists of 60,000 images in 10 different classes:
339   airplane, automobile, bird, cat, deer, dog, frog, horse, ship, and truck. There are 50,000 training
340   images and 10,000 test images. It is a popular dataset for image classification tasks and is often used
341   to evaluate the performance of convolutional neural networks. The relatively small size of the images
342   and the diverse set of classes make it a good starting point for developing and testing deep models for
343   object recognition.
344   CIFAR-100 (Alex Krizhevsky, 2009). This dataset is an extension of the CIFAR-10 dataset. It
345   contains 60,000 32×32-pixel color images but is grouped into 100 fine-grained classes. There are 500
346   training images and 100 test images per class. The classes are more specific and cover a wider range
347   of object categories than CIFAR-10. This dataset is used to train and evaluate models that can handle
348   a larger number of more detailed object classes, and it is more challenging due to the fine-grained
349   nature of the classification task.
350   Tiny-ImageNet (Ya Le & Xuan S. Yang, 2015). Tiny ImageNet is a subset of the ImageNet dataset.
351   It contains 100,000 training images, 10,000 validation images, and 10,000 test images. The images
352   are of size 64×64 pixels and are classified into 200 classes. It provides a more challenging dataset
353   than CIFAR - 10 and CIFAR - 100 in terms of the number of classes and the complexity of the images.
354   It is often used to evaluate the performance of more advanced image classification models that need
355   to handle a larger number of object categories and larger-sized images.
356   CC3M (Soravit Changpinyo et al., 2021). The CC3M (Conceptual Captions 3 Million) dataset is a
357   large-scale and influential collection in the field of multimodal learning. It consists of approximately
358   3 million pairs of images and corresponding text captions. The images are sourced mainly from the
359   web. The text captions are human-generated and are designed to precisely describe the content of
360   the images. This dataset plays a crucial role in training visual-language models. It is widely utilized
361   in the cross-domain of natural language processing and computer vision. For example, in training
362   image captioning models, the models can learn the mapping from image features to natural-language
363
      descriptions by leveraging the rich image-text pairs in the CC3M dataset to understand the image
      content and generate accurate textual descriptions. It also provides valuable data for tasks such as
364
      visual question-answering (VQA), enabling models to understand both the visual and textual aspects
365
      better and improve their performance in answering questions related to the images. Overall, the
366   CC3M dataset is a vital resource for advancing research in multimodal machine-learning applications.

440   6    C ONCLUSION
441
442   In this research, we delved into the emerging field of data distillation and proposed a novel approach
443   called effect alignment. Data distillation holds great promise in compressing large datasets by aligning
444   synthetic and real data representations to create a more informative and manageable dataset. The
445   existing optimization objectives in data distillation, which center around aligning representations
446   through process alignment methods like trajectory and gradient matching, have shown limitations.
447   The strict alignment of intermediate quantities between synthetic and real data often leads to chal-
448   lenges, and the mismatch between their optimization trajectories can hinder the effectiveness of the
449   distillation process. To overcome these limitations, our proposed effect alignment method offers a
450
      fresh perspective. By focusing on only pushing for the consistency of endpoint training results, it
      bypasses the issues associated with strict intermediate quantity alignment. The use of classification
451
      tasks to estimate the impact of replacing real training samples with synthetic data is a key innovation.
452
      This approach allows us to learn a synthetic dataset that can effectively replace the real dataset and
453   achieve effect alignment. The efficiency of our method is a significant advantage. It does not require
454   costly mechanisms, making it a practical solution for real-world applications where computational
455   resources and time are often constraints. The experiments conducted demonstrated satisfactory
456   results, validating the effectiveness of our approach.
457
      Looking ahead, this research paves the way for further exploration in the field of data distillation.
458
      Future work could involve refining and expanding the effect alignment method to handle even larger
459   and more complex datasets. Additionally, investigating the application of this approach in different
460   domains and tasks could lead to new insights and improvements. Overall, our work contributes to the
461   ongoing efforts to develop more efficient and effective data distillation techniques for the benefit of
462   the broader research and application communities.
