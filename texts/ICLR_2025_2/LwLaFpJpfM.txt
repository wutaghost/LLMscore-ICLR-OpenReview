009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011
012            A novel deep learning approach for Active Audio Cancellation (AAC) is pre-
013            sented, which extends the capabilities of traditional Active Noise Cancellation
014
               (ANC) by addressing a wider range of audio signals, including those with com-
               plex spectral content. We propose, for the first time, a deep learning approach
015
               to AAC using a novel multi-band Mamba architecture. This architecture parti-
016
               tions input audio into multiple frequency bands, allowing for precise anti-signal
017            generation and enhanced phase alignment across frequencies, thereby improving
018            overall cancellation performance. Additionally, we introduce an optimization-
019            driven loss function that provides near-optimal supervisory signals for anti-signal
020            generation. Our experimental results demonstrate substantial improvements over
021            existing methods, achieving up to 7.2dB gain in ANC scenarios and up to 6.2dB
022            improvement in AAC for voice audio signals, outperforming existing methods.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028
029   Active Noise Cancellation (ANC) is a critical audio processing technique aimed at eliminating un-
030   wanted noise by generating an anti-noise signal (Lueg, 1936; Hansen et al., 1997; Fuller et al., 1996;
031   Kuo & Morgan, 1999; Nelson & Elliott, 1991). ANC has practical applications in improving hear-
032   ing devices for individuals with hearing impairments and reducing chronic noise exposure, thereby
033
      mitigating hearing loss risks. It also enhances focus, productivity, and listening experiences while
      reducing stress. Traditional ANC algorithms, like LMS and its deep learning variants (Zhang &
034
      Wang, 2021; Park et al., 2023; Mostafavi & Cha, 2023; Cha et al., 2023; Singh et al., 2024; Pike
035
      & Cheer, 2023), have been widely adopted. However, these methods face limitations when dealing
036   with more complex and high-frequency audio signals, as they are primarily designed to target noise.
037
038   This paper addresses the more general problem of Active Audio Cancellation (AAC), which extends
039
      beyond traditional noise cancellation to encompass the cancellation of any audio signal, irrespective
      of its spectrum. While ANC systems may implicitly aim to cancel any incoming sound, including
040
      speech, their primary focus has historically been on noise. Our work represents, to the best of
041
      our knowledge, the first attempt to actively cancel general audio signals with deep learning. This
042   distinction opens new research avenues, as AAC does not rely on prior assumptions about the input
043   signal, making it inherently more complex and versatile.
044
045
      Our results indicate the strong potential of generative neural networks in addressing both AAC and
      traditional ANC tasks. To this end, we introduce a novel multi-band Mamba architecture. This
046
      architecture is effective in real-world environments with diverse audio frequencies. By partitioning
047
      the input into frequency bands, the model enables precise control over anti-signal generation, im-
048   proving phase alignment and cancellation performance. Additionally, an optimization-driven loss
049   function provides near-optimal supervisory signals for the generation of anti-signals, resulting in
050   superior performance in complex and dynamic acoustic scenarios. In dynamic and complex acous-
051   tic settings, this multi-band approach leads to substantial improvements over the existing methods,
052   achieving up to 7.2 dB gain in ANC scenarios and a 6.2 dB improvement in AAC for voice audio
053   signals. These results surpass the performance of existing deep learning-based baselines, which are
      considered state-of-the-art in the field.

058   Active Noise Cancellation: The concept of ANC was first introduced by Lueg Lueg (1936), who
059   focused on the cancellation of sound oscillations. Given that ANC algorithms (Hansen et al., 1997;
060   Fuller et al., 1996; Kuo & Morgan, 1999; Nelson & Elliott, 1991) must adapt to variations in ampli-
061   tude, phase, and the movement of the noise source , most ANC algorithms are based on the Least
062   Mean Squares (LMS) algorithm (Burgess, 1981) which has demonstrated effectiveness in echo can-
063
      cellation. The FxLMS (Filtered-x LMS) algorithm extends the LMS approach to ANC by employing
      an adaptive filter that accounts for distortions in the primary path P (z) and secondary path S(z).
064
      Boucher et al. (1991) analyze the error introduce in the FxLMS algorithm due to inaccuracies in
065
      estimating the secondary path inverse Ŝ(z). The secondary path in adaptive filtering systems of-
066
      ten introduces nonlinear distortions that degrade the performance the FxLMS algorithm. Several
067
      approaches have been proposed to mitigate these issues. The Filtered-S LMS (FSLMS) algorithm
068   (Das & Panda, 2004) utilizes a single-layer Functional Link Artificial Neural Network (FLANN)
069   (Patra et al., 1999) to address nonlinear distortions. Another approach, the Volterra Filtered-x LMS
070   (VFXLMS) algorithm (Tan & Jiang, 2001), employs a multichannel structure for feedforward active
071   noise control to better handle nonlinearity. The Bilinear FxLMS algorithm (Kuo & Wu, 2005) incor-
072   porates bilinear filters that offer an improved modeling of nonlinearity compared to the VFXLMS
073   method. Additionally, the Leaky FxLMS (Tobias & Seara, 2005) algorithm introduces a ”leakage”
074   term in the coefficient updates, which helps mitigate overfitting to noise or rapid signal changes.
075   The Tangential Hyperbolic Function-based FxLMS (THF-FxLMS) (Ghasemi et al., 2016) employs
076
      a tangential hyperbolic function to model the saturation effects of the loudspeaker, further enhancing
      performance in the presence of nonlinearities. Gannot & Yeredor (2003) propose blind source sepa-
077
      ration methods based on second-order statistics for noise cancellation. Moreover, Oppenheim et al.
078
      (1994) proposed single channel ANC based on Kalman filter formulation (Revach et al., 2021). Ad-
079   ditionally, Rafaely (2009) investigate spherical loudspeaker arrays for local sound control, analyzing
080   the interaction of primary and secondary sound fields to form shell-shaped quiet zones.
081
082
      ANC using deep learning was first proposed by Zhang & Wang (2021), utilizing a convolutional-
      LSTM network to estimate both the amplitude and phase of the canceling signal y(t). Similar
083
      approaches using recurrent CNNs were presented by Park et al. (2023), Mostafavi & Cha (2023)
084
      and by Cha et al. (2023). Furthermore, autoencoder-based networks have been utilized to address
085   the ANC problem Singh et al. (2024), as well as fully connected neural networks Pike & Cheer
086   (2023). Moreover, Shi et al. (2020; 2022b; 2023a), Luo et al. (2022), and Park & Park (2023) have
087   developed methods that select fixed-filter ANC (SFANC) from pre-trained control filters to achieve
088   fast response times. Furthermore, Luo et al. (2023b;a; 2024c) focused on generating filters for
089   selective fixed-filter ANC. In parallel, Zhang & Wang (2023), Shi et al. (2024; 2023b), Antoñanzas
090   et al. (2023), Xiao et al. (2023), Zhang et al. (2023b), and Zhu et al. (2021) contributed to the
091   development of multichannel ANC systems. To address the challenges of real-time ANC, Luo et
092   al. and Shi et al. proposed a convolutional neural network-based approach (Luo et al., 2024b;
093
      Shi et al., 2022a), which was later enhanced by integrating convolutional neural networks with
      Kalman filtering (Luo et al., 2023c). Additionally, Zhang et al. (2023a) introduced an attention
094
      mechanism for real-time ANC, leveraging the Attentive Recurrent Network (ARN) network (Pandey
095
      & Wang, 2022). Other notable contributions to real-time ANC include attentive recurrent networks
096   (Zhang et al., 2022). Other innovative approaches include a genetic algorithm-based method for
097   ANC proposed by Zhou et al. (2023) and a bee colony algorithm for ANC introduced by Ren &
098   Zhang (2022).
099
      Active Speech Cancellation: Active speech cancellation (ASC) has been explored in various stud-
100
      ies, each employing different approaches to predict and cancel unwanted speech signals. Kondo
101
      & Nakagawa (2007) introduced an ASC method using a Linear Predictive Coding (LPC) model
102   to predict the speech signal for generating the cancelling signal y(t). Donley et al. (2017) took a
103   different approach by controlling the sound field to cancel speech using a linear dipole array of loud-
104   speakers and a single microphone, effectively reducing the speech signal in the target area. Iotov
105   et al. (2022) employed a long-term linear prediction filter to anticipate incoming speech, enabling
106   the cancellation of the speech signal. Additionally, Iotov et al. (2023) proposed the HOSpLP-ANC
107   method, which utilizes an adaptive high-order sparse linear predictor alongside the Least Mean
      Squares (LMS) algorithm to achieve effective speech cancellation.

109   Dao & Gu, 2024), leveraging State Space Models (SSMs) to achieve notable improvements in vari-
110   ous audio-related tasks. One of the key advantages of the Mamba architecture is its ability to perform
111   fast inference, especially when handling sequences up to a million in length, which represents a sig-
112   nificant improvement over traditional generative architectures. This has enabled advancements in

      calization (Xiao & Das, 2024; Mu et al., 2024), audio tagging (Lin & Hu, 2024), and deepfake audio
      detection (Chen et al., 2024).

122   3.1   BACKGORUND
123
124   The signal processing framework of a typical feedforward ANC system is detailed, emphasizing the
125   roles of the primary and secondary acoustic paths. In such systems, reference and error microphone
126   signals are utilized to generate a canceling signal that minimizes unwanted noise. The primary
127   path P (z) represents the acoustic transfer function from the noise source to the error microphone,
128   while the secondary path S(z) represents the acoustic transfer function from the loudspeaker to
129   the error microphone. The signal captured by the reference microphone is denoted as x(n), while
130
      the signal captured by the error microphone is denoted as e(n). These signals are fed into the
      ANC controller, which processes them to produce a canceling signal y(n). The canceling signal
131
      is then played through a loudspeaker, referred to as fLS , producing fLS {y(n)}, which aims to
132
      suppress the unwanted noise near the error microphone. The loudspeaker output fLS {y(n)}, after
133   passing through the secondary path S(z), generates the anti-signal denoted by a(n).The equation
134   representing the relationship is:
135
136                                        a(n) = S(z) ∗ fLS {y(n)}                                      (1)
137
138   Similarly, the reference signal x(n), transmitted through the primary path P (z), produces the pri-
139   mary signal denoted by d(n), which is defined as:
140
141                                           d(n) = P (z) ∗ x(n)                                        (2)
142
143   The error signal e(n), which represents the difference between the primary signal d(n) and the
144   anti-signal a(n), is expressed as:
145                                        e(n) = d(n) − a(n)                                    (3)
146
147   The goal of the ANC controller is to minimize the error signal e(n), ideally to zero, indicating
148   successful noise cancellation. In the feedback ANC approach, only the error signal e(n) is utilized
149   to generate the canceling signal, focusing on minimizing the residual noise detected by the error
150   microphone.
151   One of the widely used metrics for measuring noise attenuation in ANC is the Normalized Mean
152   Square Error (NMSE) between two signals, defined by:
153                                                                           !
                                                      PM                    2
154                                                      n=1 (u(n) −  v(n))
155
                            NMSE [u, v] = 10 · log10        PM                                 (4)
                                                                       2

156
157   where u and v are the vector representations of the signals u(n) and v(n) such that u =
158   [u(1), ..., u(M )] and v = [v(1), ..., v(M )]. Here, M represents the total number of samples. Typ-
159   ically, u(n) refers to the target signal, while v(n) denotes the estimated signal. A lower NMSE
160   value indicates a better estimation, reflecting a closer alignment between the estimated signal and
161   the target signal. In the context of ANC, typically u(n) is the primary signal d(n), while v(n) will
      be the anti-signal a(n). A schematic representation of the ANC system is illustrated in Fig. 2.

      The proposed method utilizes a novel architecture that integrates the Mamba framework (Gu &
      Dao, 2023) for the generation of the anti-signal. The architecture includes a filter bank that decom-
184
      poses the input signal into multiple frequency bands, with each band processed by an encoder and a
185
      masking Mamba network. The outputs of the multi-band masking are then concatenated and passed
186   through a decoder. Furthermore, we introduce a new loss function that leverages the near-optimal
187   anti-signal as the ground truth, significantly improving the precision of the anti-signal generation
188   process. A diagram of the proposed architecture is shown in Fig.1.

      3.4   O PTIMIZATION O BJECTIVE
226
227   The training protocol for the proposed method consists of two distinct phases: (i) ANC Loss min-
228   imization, and (ii) Near Optimal Anti-Signal Optimization. Each phase employs the NMSE loss
229   function (Eq. 4) but with different optimization objectives.
230   ANC Loss: In the first phase, the optimization aims to minimize the residual error signal. Given a
231   reference signal x(n) and the model output y(n), the error loss function is defined as follows:
232
                                      LANC = NMSE [P ∗ x, S ∗ fLS {y}]                                (10)
233
      where P and S represent the vectorized forms of the primary-path impulse response P (z) and the
234
      secondary-path impulse response S(z), respectively; x and y are the vectorized forms of the refer-
235
      ence signal x(n) and the canceling signal y(n). The operator ∗ denotes convolution. Both P and S
236   are obtained from the simulator employed in our study.
237
238   Near Optimal Anti-Signal Optimization (NOAS): One of the primary challenges in formulating
239
      ANC as a supervised learning problem lies in defining an appropriate training objective that accounts
      for the characteristics of the secondary path S(z) and the primary path P (z). In an ANC algorithms,
240
      the output y(n) is processed by a nonlinearity function fLS and then propagated through the sec-
241
      ondary path S(z). The training objective aims to minimize the error signal e(n), which represents
242   the residual noise after cancellation.
243
244   However, this process becomes problematic when the secondary path S(z) attenuates certain fre-
245
      quencies that are present in the primary signal d(n). Under the vanilla loss function (e.g., Eq. 11),
      the model can be unfairly penalized for high error signals in these attenuated frequency bands, even
246
      when it has generated an optimal anti-signal. This occurs because the secondary path inherently
247
      suppresses these frequencies, leading to residual energy in the error signal e(n). As a result, the
248   training process encounters discrepancies that hinder the model’s ability to learn effectively.
249
250   To address this challenge, we propose the NOAS optimization loss function (Eq. 12). The NOAS
251
      loss symmetrically incorporates the secondary path S(z) on both sides of the NMSE calculation. By
      doing so, it ensures that any frequencies nullified by S(z) are also excluded from the target, thereby
252
      mitigating the contribution of these frequencies to the error signal. Specifically, each reference signal
253
      x(n) is associated with its NOAS target y ∗ (n). To determine the near-optimal anti-signal y ∗ (n), we
254   employ a gradient descent-based algorithm during a pre-processing stage. This stage operates over
255   each example, solving the following optimization problem for each reference signal x(n):
256
                                     y∗ = arg min NMSE [P ∗ x, S ∗ fLS {ỹ}]                               (11)
257                                           ỹ
258           ∗
      where y is the near-optimal anti-signal. The optimization starts with a random anti-signal and
259   iteratively adjusts it to minimize the NMSE for the given reference signal x(n). The resulting near-
260   optimal anti-signal y ∗ (n) is then used to form the target during the fine-tuning stage. To ensure con-
261   sistency and leverage the prior knowledge gained during the initial training phase, the near-optimal
262   anti-signal, denoted as y∗ , is projected onto the anti-signal space associated with the secondary path
263   S(z). This projection plays a crucial role in maintaining the continuity of the training process and is
264   achieved through the use of the secondary path impulse response S. In particular, the near-optimal
265
      anti-signal y ∗ (n) is used to define the following loss function:
266                                  LNOAS = NMSE [S ∗ fLS {y∗ }, S ∗ fLS {y}]                            (12)
267   Figure 3 illustrates the distinction between optimizing within the canceling signal space versus the
268   anti-signal space. It can be observed that since the NMSE is evaluated at the output of the anti-signal
269   space, optimizing for S ∗ y∗ facilitates a more straightforward optimization process, given that the
      starting point S ∗ y is closer to the optimal solution P ∗ x.

      spectively. This improvement is attributed to the model’s enhanced ability to focus on specific

506   5    C ONCLUSION
507
508   In this paper, we introduced a novel AAC approach using the Multi-Band Mamba architecture,
509   advancing deep learning-based noise and audio ‘cancellation. By partitioning audio into fre-
510   quency bands, our method enhances anti-signal generation and phase alignment. Combined with an
511   optimization-driven loss function, it achieves near-optimal performance, improving both ANC and
512   AAC outcomes. Our experimental results demonstrate a significant performance boost compared to
513
      state-of-the-art baselines, with improvements of 7.2dB in ANC and 6.2dB in AAC for voice audio
      signals.
514
515   These results confirm the multi-band architecture’s effectiveness in handling diverse frequencies and
516   real-world acoustic environments, where traditional methods often fail. Our approach addresses key
517   challenges in the field by effectively leveraging frequency decomposition and optimization-based
518   anti-signal generation, paving the way for more advanced audio cancellation technologies.

      A.1   L IMITATION AND F UTURE W ORK
780
781   The Multi-Band Mamba architecture demonstrates significant effectiveness in AAC. However, it
782   is not without limitations that warrant further investigation. A key drawback lies in the trade-off
783   between performance and complexity. While our approach achieves enhanced cancellation across
784   a wide frequency spectrum, the increased model complexity associated with handling multiple fre-
785   quency bands results in higher computational costs. This limitation renders the method less practical
786   for low-latency applications or devices with restricted processing capabilities.
787   To address this limitation, future work should focus on reducing computational overhead and en-
788   abling real-time processing. This could involve exploring dynamic or adaptive frequency band
789   partitioning strategies that tailor the model’s complexity to the characteristics of the input signal.
