009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            Consistency models imitate the multi-step sampling of score-based diffusion in a
012            single forward pass of a neural network. They can be learned in two ways: consis-
013            tency distillation and consistency training. The former relies on the true velocity
014            field of the corresponding differential equation, approximated by a pre-trained
015            neural network. In contrast, the latter uses a single-sample Monte Carlo estimate
016
               of this velocity field. The related estimation error induces a discrepancy between
               consistency distillation and training that, we show, still holds in the continuous-time
017
               limit. To alleviate this issue, we propose a novel flow that transports noisy data
018
               towards their corresponding outputs derived from the currently trained model – as a
019            proxy of the true flow. Our empirical findings demonstrate that this approach miti-
020            gates the previously identified discrepancy. Furthermore, we present theoretical and
021            empirical evidence indicating that our generator-induced flow surpasses dedicated
022            optimal transport-based consistency models in effectively reducing the noise-data
023            transport cost. Consequently, our method not only accelerates consistency training
024            convergence but also enhances its overall performance.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      The large family of diffusion (Ho et al., 2020), score-based (Song et al., 2021; Karras et al., 2022),
029
      and flow models (Liu et al., 2023; Lipman et al., 2023) have emerged as state-of-the-art generative
030   models for image generation. Since they are costly to use at inference time – requiring several neural
031   function evaluations –, many distillation techniques have been explored (Salimans and Ho, 2022;
032   Meng et al., 2023; Sauer et al., 2023). A most remarkable approach is consistency models (Song
033   et al., 2023; Song and Dhariwal, 2024). Consistency models lead to high-quality one-step generators,
034   that can be trained either by distillation of a pre-trained velocity field (consistency distillation), or as
035   standalone generative models (consistency training) by approximating the velocity field through a
036   one-sample Monte Carlo estimate.
037   The corresponding estimation error naturally induces a discrepancy between consistency distillation
038   and training. While Song et al. (2023) hinted that it would resorb in the continuous-time limit,
039   we show that this discrepancy remains both on loss functions’ gradients and values. Interestingly,
040   this discrepancy vanishes when the difference between the target velocity field and its Monte-Carlo
041   approximation approaches zero. However, this is not the case with the independent coupling (IC)
042   between data and noise used to construct the standard estimate. It is unclear how to improve this
043   one-sample estimate without access to the true underlying diffusion model.
044   The approach we adopt in this paper to alleviate this issue consists in altering the velocity field
045   – thereby changing the target flow – to reduce the variance of its one-sample estimator. One possible
046   solution to this problem from the consistency and flow matching literatures (Pooladian et al., 2023;
047   Dou et al., 2024) is to resort to optimal transport (OT) to learn on a deterministic coupling. However,
048   due to the prohibitive cubic complexity of OT solvers (e.g. Hungarian matching algorithm), such
049   methods need to be applied at the minibatch level. This incurs an OT approximation error (Fatras
050
      et al., 2021; Sommerfeld et al., 2019) and stochasticity of the data-noise coupling, thus not solving
      the consistency training issue.
051
052   In our approach, we propose to use the consistency model itself to construct trajectories. Indeed,
053   the consistency model provides an approximation of the target flow via a single-step inference, and
      can thus be used as a proxy to reduce the expected deviation between the velocity field and its

214   to alleviate the discrepancy identified in this section, which consists of altering the velocity field
215   – thereby changing the target flow – to reduce the variance of its one-sample estimator. This approach
      is reminiscent of recent work tackling the data-noise coupling that we discuss in the following section.

      3     R EDUCING THE D ISCREPANCY WITH DATA -N OISE C OUPLING
217
218
      Beyond independent coupling (IC). From Section 2.2, it appears that ẋt is computed through an
219   IC qI = p⋆ (x⋆ )pz (z) of data and noise, in a similar fashion to flow matching (Lipman et al., 2023;
220   Kingma and Gao, 2024). Making correlated choices of data and noise beyond IC could then help
221   align ẋt and vt (xt ), thereby resolving the discrepancy from the previous section. This question has
222   been tackled, although with different motivation, by recent work.
223
      The reliance on IC in consistency and flow models is increasingly recognized as a limiting factor.
224
      Recent advancements suggest that improved coupling mechanisms could enhance both training
225   efficiency and the quality of generated samples in flow matching (Liu et al., 2023; Pooladian et al.,
226   2023) and diffusion models (Li et al., 2024). By reducing the variance in gradient estimation,
227   enhanced coupling can accelerate training. Additionally, improved coupling could decrease transport
228   costs and straighten trajectories, yielding better-quality samples. In a different context, ReFlow (Liu
229   et al., 2023) leverages couplings provided by the ODE solver in a flow framework, and demonstrate
230   that it reduced transport costs.
231
232   Couplings based on optimal transport (OT) solvers. OT is a particularly appealing solution
233   for our alignment problem. Indeed, if we consider a quadratic cost and distributions with bounded
234
      supports, OT is a no-collision transport map (Nurbekyan et al., 2020), i.e. xt can be sampled by
      a unique pair of points (x⋆ , z). Thus ẋt = vt (xt ), implying R(θ) = 0 in Theorem 1. Several
235
      approaches have precisely targeted the reduction of transport cost in flow and consistency models.
236
237   Pooladian et al. (2023) have more directly explored OT coupling within the framework of flow
238   matching models. They show that deterministic and non-crossing paths enabled by OT with infinite
239   batch size lowers the variance of gradient estimators. Experimentally, they assess the efficacy of OT
240
      solvers, such as Hungarian matching and Sinkhorn algorithms, in coupling batches of noise and data
      points. Dou et al. (2024) have successfully adopted this approach in consistency models. However,
241
      due to the prohibitive cubic complexity of OT solvers, OT has to be applied by minibatch for matching
242
      samples (x⋆ , z). Besides an OT approximation error, this incurs the loss of the no-collision property,
243   making R(θ) non-zero in real use-cases. Another line of works using OT tools with score-based
244   models relies on the Schrödinger Bridge formulation (De Bortoli et al., 2021; Shi et al., 2023; Korotin
245   et al., 2024; Tong et al., 2024), which has mostly proven benefits on transfer tasks.
246
247   Our approach. In this paper, we use a consistency model as a proxy of the flow of a diffusion
248   process to reduce transport costs. Our method does not rely on an iterative procedure but rather on a
249   mixing procedure during a single training, alleviating error accumulation. While not fully solving
250   the alignment issue either, we will show that our method present reduced transport costs and better
251   alignment than dedicated OT-based methods.

253   4     C ONSISTENCY MODELS WITH G ENERATOR -I NDUCED F LOWS
254
255   Here, we introduce our method, denoted as Generator-Induced Flows, which relies on a Generator-
256   Induced Coupling (GC). It consists of capitalizing on the consistency model’s ability to approximate
257   the diffusion flow in an affordable manner – during the training of the consistency model itself. This
258   approach not only allows reducing the data-noise transport cost but also narrows the gap between
259   consistency distillation and consistency training while maintaining a low computational overhead.

      In this section, we present a careful analysis of training consistency models with GC on unconditional
398
      image generation. We identify two main obstacles to using GC: (i) the distribution shift in marginals
399   between IC and GC affects the performance of consistency models trained with GC only, since x̂t is
400   computed based on IC inputs; (ii) training a GC model with a consistency model pre-trained on IC
401   is computationally costly. We circumvent these issues with a simple solution based on mixing IC
402   and GC. Finally, we demonstrate that our mixing approach improves performance and accelerates
403   convergence of consistency models.
404
      Our experiments are done on the following datasets: CIFAR-10 (Krizhevsky, 2009), ImageNet (Deng
405   et al., 2009), CelebA (Liu et al., 2015) and LSUN Church (Yu et al., 2015). For the evaluation metrics,
406   we report the Fréchet Inception Distance (FID, Heusel et al. (2017)), Kernel Inception Distance (KID,
407   Bińkowski et al. (2018)), and Inception Score (IS, Salimans et al. (2016)). Our experiments are
408   based on the improved training techniques for consistency models from Song and Dhariwal (2024),
409   denoted iCT-IC. Note that the iCT-IC reported below does not reach the same performance than in
410   Song and Dhariwal (2024) since we need to use smaller model size and batch size. In our setting,
411   on CIFAR-10, training iCT-IC requires approximately one day on a A100 40GB GPU. Details are
412   provided in Appendix C, and the code is shared in the supplementary material for reproducibility.
413

415
416   The first experiment involves training a consistency model with GC from scratch. As shown in Figure
417   4(a), we observe that these models converge quickly but reach saturation early in the training process.
418   When applying the timestep scheduling method with an increasing number of timesteps from Song
419   and Dhariwal (2024), the FID of the models worsens. Using a fixed number of timesteps prevents
420   divergence of the FID, but it still plateaus at a higher FID than the baseline iCT-IC.
421   Finding 1. Consistency models converge faster with GC compared to the standard IC approach.
422
423   In Figure 4(b), we plot the FID per timestep for three model / trajectory pairs: GC-model on IC
424   trajectories, GC-model on GC trajectories, and IC-model on IC trajectories. Notably, we observe

426   IC trajectories degrades at the intermediate timesteps of the diffusion process. This highlights why
427   training a model exclusively on GC trajectories is insufficient: to build xti in Equation (13), the
428   model is inferred on IC but trained on GC trajectories. If IC and GC differ too significantly, the model
429   cannot improve on IC.
430   Finding 2. Consistency models trained exclusively on GC trajectories exhibit sub-optimal perfor-
431   mance, likely due to a distribution shift between intermediate timesteps of IC and GC trajectories.

      5.3    GC FROM S CRATCH WITH M IXING
508
      We propose a simple yet effective solution to address the distribution shift issue that affects the
509   performance and robustness of the endpoint predictor on IC trajectories: mixing IC and GC during
510   training. We introduce a mixing factor µ: at each training step, training pairs are drawn from GC
511   with probability µ, while the remaining pairs are drawn from standard IC. We denote this mixing
512   procedure as GC (µ = ·). Hence, GC (µ = 0) corresponds to the standard IC procedure, while GC
513   (µ = 1) corresponds to the procedure introduced in Section 4. We apply this mixing approach to four
514   image datasets, and include comparisons to batch OT (Pooladian et al., 2023; Dou et al., 2024) as an
515   additional baseline. Results across multiple datasets and metrics are presented in Table 1, and visual
516   examples are shown in Appendix Figure 7.
517   Finding 4. Mixing IC and GC trajectories consistently improves results compared to the base IC
518   model and outperforms batch-OT in most cases.
519
520
      As shown in Figure 6, we observe an interesting interpolation phenomenon between µ = 0 and
      µ = 1. At µ = 0, we recover the steady FID improvement typical of IC training. As µ increases, the
521
      convergence of the generative model accelerates. At µ = 1, we observe the fast convergence and early
522
      divergence described in Section 5.1. For 0.5 ≤ µ ≤ 0.7, we find a sweet spot where convergence
523   speed and final FID are improved compared to IC and batch-OT models. We provide further detail
524   on the sensitivity of our results to the choice of µ, which we found easy to tune, in Appendices B.1
525   and C.

      6     C ONCLUSION
529
      In this paper, we identify a discrepancy between consistency training and consistency distillation.
530
      Building on this theoretical analysis, we introduce generator-induced flows and show that they
531
      reduce a proxy term measuring this discrepancy. Additionally, generator-induced flows decrease the
532   data-to-noise transport cost, as demonstrated by theory and experiments. Finally, we derive practical
533   algorithms for training consistency models using generator-induced flows and demonstrate their
534   improved empirical performance.
