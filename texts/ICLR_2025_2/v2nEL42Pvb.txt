009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Spectral GNNs leverage graph spectral properties to model graph representations
012            but have been less explored due to their computational challenges, especially com-
013            pared to the more flexible and scalable spatial GNNs, which have seen broader
014            adoption. However, spatial methods cannot fully exploit the rich information in
015            graph spectra. Current Spectral GNNs, relying on fixed-order polynomials, use
016            scalar-to-scalar filters applied uniformly across eigenvalues, failing to capture key
017            spectral shifts and signal propagation dynamics. Though set-to-set filters can cap-
018
               ture spectral complexity, methods that employ them frequently rely on Transform-
               ers, which add considerable computational burden. Our analysis indicates that ap-
019
               plying Transformers to these filters provides minimal advantage in the spectral do-
020
               main. We demonstrate that effective spectral filtering can be achieved without the
021            need for transformers, offering a more efficient and spectrum-aware alternative.
022            To this end, we propose a Simple Yet Effective Spectral Graph Neural Network
023            (SSGNN), which leverages the graph spectrum to adaptively filter through a sim-
024            plified global context filtering approach that captures key spectral features. More-
025            over, we introduce a novel, parameter-free Relative Gaussian Amplifier (ReGA)
026            module, which adaptively learns spectral filtering while maintaining robustness
027            against structural perturbations, ensuring stability. Extensive experiments on 20
028            real-world graph datasets, spanning both node-level and graph-level tasks along
029
               with a synthetic graph dataset, show that SSGNN matches or surpasses the per-
               formance of state-of-the-art (SOTA) spectral-based GNNs and graph transformers
030
               while using significantly fewer parameters and GFLOPs. Specifically, SSGNN
031
               achieves performance comparable to the current SOTA Graph Transformer model,
032            Polynormer, with an average 55x reduction in parameters and 100x reduction in
033            GFLOPs across all datasets. Our code will be made public upon acceptance.

036   1   I NTRODUCTION
037
038   In recent years, Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) (Scarselli et al. (2008)), have gained significant
039   popularity for machine learning on graph structured data, delivering impressive results on various
040   graph related tasks. Although traditional GNNs utilize a message-passing framework (Gilmer et al.
041   (2017), Battaglia et al. (2018)) to facilitate information exchange between neighboring nodes, they
042   often face challenges such as over-smoothing and over-squashing (Oono & Suzuki (2019), Alon &
043   Yahav (2020), Di Giovanni et al. (2023)) which can restrict their ability to accurately model com-
044   plex functions. Spectral GNNs (Defferrard et al. (2016), Bruna et al. (2013)), on the other hand,
045
      capitalize on graph convolutions and operate in the spectral domain to obtain graph filter responses
      that enable them to capture non-local dependencies more effectively. Despite the success of spatial
046
      GNNs, the exploration of spectral GNNs has been limited, largely because of the use of scalar filters
047
      that fail to leverage the rich information within the graph spectrum Bo et al. (2023a). Recently graph
048   transformer (GT) models have show potential for enhancing GNN expressivity, their scalability is
049   limited due to high parameter complexity because of which linear GTs (Choromanski et al. (2022),
050   Zhang et al. (2022), Kong et al. (2023)) have been proposed. However, GTs underperform on numer-
051   ous widely used datasets (Platonov et al. (2023)), as highlighted in Polynormer (Deng et al. (2024)),
052   which raises concerns about the effective utilization of the expressivity enabled by the self-attention
053   module in these GTs.
      Conventional spectral graph filters often apply scalar functions uniformly across eigenvalues, disre-

054
      garding the unique structural insights they provide. It is well known that eigenvalues and eigenvec-
055   tors capture critical structural properties: smaller eigenvalues represent smooth variations in a graph,
056   while the number of zero eigenvalues reflects the number of connected components. The eigenvec-
057   tor associated with the smaller non-zero eigenvalue balances smoothness and variation across the
058   graph, revealing its global structure. It is common that eigen-values of the graph Laplacian are not
059   distinct. While polynomial filters enhance flexibility by approximating spectral filters with fixed-
060   order polynomials and avoiding costly eigen-decomposition, they struggle with the high multiplicity
061   of eigenvalues commonly found in real-world graphs. This multiplicity leads to uniform scaling of
062   frequency components with the same eigenvalue, limiting the expressive capacity of these filters.
063   Unlike traditional scalar-to-scalar filters that apply the same filter to all eigenvalues, set-to-set filter-
064   ing leverages the spectrum information from a set of eigenvalues, enabling more expressive spectral
065   filtering by modeling complex interactions between frequency components. Specformer (Bo et al.
066   (2023a)) employs transformers (Vaswani et al. (2017)) for this purpose, directly capturing eigenvalue
067   relationships at the graph spectrum level without relying on polynomial approximations, thereby
068   constructing advanced spectral filters. However, its self-attention mechanism introduces a signifi-
069
      cant computational bottleneck with quadratic time and space complexity, limiting scalability to large
      graphs (Bo et al. (2023b)) and causing CUDA OOM issues even on moderately sized heterophilic
070
      datasets. Heterophilic graphs, where nodes from different classes tend to connect, are highly sensi-
071
      tive to hyperparameters (Luan et al. (2024)). Transformers also exhibit hyperparameter sensitivity
072   and training instability due to parameter perturbations (Chen et al. (2021)). Based on these chal-
073   lenges, we investigate: “Can we develop an effective spectral filtering approach that adaptively
074   learns filters based on graph data characteristics in a parameter and computation efficient manner,
075   while capturing global eigen-spectrum?”
076
      We introduce SSGNN: Simple Yet Effective Spectral Graph Neural Network, an encoder-decoder
077
      architecture that directly operates on the graph spectrum. Designed for computational efficiency,
078   SSGNN incorporates a novel, parameter-free Relative Gaussian Amplifier (ReGA) module, which
079   enables dynamic spectral filtering via global context, capturing inter-eigenvalue relationships with-
080   out the computational overhead of quadratic time complexity, making it scalable for large graphs.

      2   BACKGROUND AND R ELATED W ORKS
120
121
122
123   Preliminary. Consider an undirected graph G = (V, E), where V represents a finite set of N nodes.
124
      Each node i ∈ V is associated with a feature vector Xi ∈ Rd , where X ∈ RN ×df is the node feature
      matrix and df denotes the dimensionality of node features. E ⊆ V ×V represents the edge set and the
125
      adjacency matrix of G is denoted as A ∈ {0, 1}N ×N . Let D be the diagonal degree matrix, where
126
      Dii corresponds to the degree of the node i. L = IN − D −1/2 AD 1/2 denotes the normalized
127
      graph Laplacian, where IN is the identity matrix. Since L is a real symmetric matrix, its eigen
128
      decomposition can be defined as L = U ΛU T , where Λ = diag(λ0 , λ1 , ..., λN ) is the diagonal
129   matrix of the eigenvalues and U = [u0 , u1 , ..., un ] comprises of the corresponding eigenvectors.
130
131   Graph Signal Processing. The graph Fourier transform (GFT) for a signal x ∈ RN ×1 is expressed
132
      as x̂ = U x ∈ RN ×1 . A spectral filter gθ operates on x̂ to scale the Fourier coefficients. Subse-
      quently, an inverse GFT is applied to obtain the filtered signal in the vertex domain as x = U x̂.
133
134   Existing GNNs can be roughly divided into three categories: Spatial GNNs, Spectral GNNs and GT.
135
      Spatial GNNs. Spatial GNNs operate by leveraging local-message passing mechanisms, where in-
136   formation is exchanged between neighboring nodes based on graph topology. GraphSAGE Hamil-
137   ton et al. (2017) uses efficient sampling and aggregation, MPNN Gilmer et al. (2017) generalizes
138   message-passing across nodes, GAT Velickovic et al. (2017) as well as GATv2 Brody et al. (2021)
139   incorporate attention mechanisms to weigh the importance of neighboring nodes. Although stacking
140   multiple layers in spatial GNNs facilitates the capturing of long-range dependencies, it frequently
141   introduces difficulties such as over-smoothing Oono & Suzuki (2019), making node representations
142   hard to distinguish, and over-squashing Topping et al. (2021), where distant information is com-
143   pressed into limited node capacity.
144   Spectral GNNs. Spectral GNNs (Wu et al. (2019), Dong et al. (2020)) operate in the frequency
145   domain, utilizing the graph’s Laplacian spectrum to perform convolutions and capture global struc-
146   tures. Initial models like Spectral CNN (Bruna et al. (2013)) and ChebNet (Defferrard et al. (2016))
147   laid the foundation by learning convolutional filters in the spectral domain, while Graph Convolu-
148   tional Networks (GCN) (Kipf & Welling (2016)) further simplified spectral convolution for broader
149   applicability. Recent advances include SpecFormer (Bo et al. (2023a)), which integrates spectral
150
      graph convolution with transformer architectures to capture local and global patterns effectively.
      G2 CN (Li et al. (2022)) utilizes Gaussian convolutional networks with concentrated graph filters,
151
      enhancing efficiency while preserving structural properties. Despite these developments, spectral
152
      methods still struggle with computational intensity and scalability challenges, making them less
153   practical for very large graphs (Wu et al. (2021), Liang et al. (2022)).
154
155   Graph Transformers. Graph Transformers (Thekumparampil et al. (2018), Yun et al. (2019)) have
156
      emerged as a powerful alternative to traditional GNNs, effectively modeling long-range dependen-
      cies through attention mechanisms that capture both local and global structures. Key advancements
157
      include Graphormer (Ying et al. (2021)), which embeds structural encodings for improved perfor-
158
      mance on molecular graphs; SAN (Kreuzer et al. (2021)), which integrates structural relationships
159   to enhance node classification; and GraphGPS (Rampášek et al. (2022)), which combines local
160   message-passing with global attention. Efficiency-focused models like Exformer (Shirzad et al.
161   (2023)) and DiFFormer (Gao et al. (2022)) aim to optimize attention mechanisms for scalability and
      better long-range dependency modeling.

191   In homophilic data, where nodes with similar attributes are connected, the signal predominantly ex-

      Computational Complexity. SSGNN’s computation involves two key components: spectral de-
      composition and forward process. Spectral decomposition, pre-computed with a complexity of
316
      O(N 3 ), is amortized over multiple training and inference steps, as it is computed just once and
317
      stored. For smaller graphs, this precomputation incurs minimal overhead (Appendix. G.1). How-
318   ever, for larger graphs, fast numerical methods such as Krylov subspace approximations for the
319   top k eigenvalues or Sparse Generalized Eigenvalue algorithms can significantly reduce the cost by
320   efficiently estimating k eigenvalues and eigenvectors. The forward pass has two parts: learnable
321   bases and graph convolution, with complexities O(HN 2 ) and O(lN d), respectively, where l rep-
322   resents the number of layers, and d is the hidden dimension. Thus the total forward complexity
323   is O(N (HN + ld). For large graphs, this complexity can be further reduced to O(Hk 2 + lN d).

348            In this section, we present experiments on diverse real-world and a synthetic graph dataset to assess
349            the effectiveness of our model, SSGNN. We thoroughly compare its performance against SOTA

               In this experiment, we use the synthetic dataset from Specformer, where 50 images from the Image
               Processing Toolbox are treated as 2D 4-neighbor grid graphs with pixel values as node features.
357
               All images share the same adjacency matrix, and five predefined graph filters generate the ground
358
               truth signals. Specformer-Small and SSGNN are both configured with 16 hidden units and 1 head
359            for fair comparison, with training running up to 2000 epochs. No regularization is applied, and a
360            learning rate of 0.01 is used. We measure performance using sum of squared error and R2 score.
361            We conduct experiments using Specformer-Small and SSGNN with the described settings, while
362            for the remaining baselines, we rely on the results reported in the Specformer paper, as it already
363            provides a fair comparison across models. Table 1 shows the quantitative results, where SSGNN
364            consistently outperforms other models, especially on complex filters like high-pass, band-pass, and
365            comb achieving lower SSE. SSGNN surpasses Specformer even in low-pass filtering. Figure 2
366            visualizes the learned filters, showing that SSGNN aligns more closely with ground truth across all
367
               scenarios. These results highlight that SSGNN is not only lightweight but also highly effective in
               capturing various types of filtering based on the data.

476   4.4     A BLATIONS
477
478   This section explores the effect of hyperparameters b and ϵ on learned filters. The parameter b
479   controls the spread of our Relative Gaussian Amplifier (ReGA), influencing the distribution of scores
480   across spectral encodings. A higher b results in smoother filters with uniform score distribution,
481   while a lower b concentrates scores on a few encodings, causing sharp amplitude spikes. Fig. 3
482
      shows this effect on the WikiCS dataset, where lower b values (e.g., b=2) lead to sudden spikes,
      which smooth out as b increases to 10.
483
484   We now examine how varying ϵ affects the learned filters. A higher ϵ captures more oscillatory
485   patterns in spectral encodings, leading to stronger and more distinct components. However, this can
      cause abrupt score assignments in ReGA, amplifying certain spectral components and suppressing

527   This work introduces SSGNN, a Simple Yet Effective Spectral Graph Neural network, powered by
528   spectral encoding and a decoder supported by a novel parameter-free Relative Gaussian Amplifier
529   (ReGA) to enhance adaptive filter learning. Synthetic data experiments show that SSGNN effec-

      tively captures complex spectral filters, while real-world results demonstrate that it achieves com-
      parable or outperforms SOTA graph transformers and spectral GNNs. Notably, SSGNN achieves

      this with significant reductions in parameters, and runtime, highlighting its potential for advancing

      efficient spectral GNN research.

1292   still outperformed Specformer in several datasets, confirming that SSGNN’s ReGA module plays a
1293   critical role in its performance. Importantly, we observed that SSGNN gains significantly more from
1294   eigen-correction than Specformer, indicating its ability to leverage this enhancement. These results
1295   collectively demonstrate that while eigen-correction contributes to performance, SSGNN’s primary
