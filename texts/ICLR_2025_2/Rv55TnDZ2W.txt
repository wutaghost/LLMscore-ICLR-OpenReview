                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
               Concept Bottleneck Models (CBMs) have garnered much attention for their ability
010
               to elucidate the prediction process through a human-understandable concept layer.
011
               However, most previous studies focused on cases where the data, including con-
012            cepts, are clean. In many scenarios, we always need to remove/insert some training
013            data or new concepts from trained CBMs due to different reasons, such as privacy
014            concerns, data mislabelling, spurious concepts, and concept annotation errors. Thus,
015            the challenge of deriving efficient editable CBMs without retraining from scratch
016            persists, particularly in large-scale applications. To address these challenges, we
017            propose Editable Concept Bottleneck Models (ECBMs). Specifically, ECBMs
018            support three different levels of data removal: concept-label-level, concept-level,
019            and data-level. ECBMs enjoy mathematically rigorous closed-form approximations
020
               derived from influence functions that obviate the need for re-training. Experimental
               results demonstrate the efficiency and effectiveness of our ECBMs, affirming their
021
               adaptability within the realm of CBMs.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
025
026
      Modern deep learning models, such as large language models (Zhao et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2024a;b;
027   Xu et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2024c) and large multimodal (Yin et al., 2023; Ali et al., 2024; Cheng
028   et al., 2024), often exhibit intricate non-linear architectures, posing challenges for end-users seeking
029   to comprehend and trust their decisions. This lack of interpretability presents a significant barrier
030   to adoption, particularly in critical domains such as healthcare (Ahmad et al., 2018; Yu et al., 2018)
031   and finance (Cao, 2022), where transparency is paramount. To address this demand, explainable
032   artificial intelligence (XAI) models (Das & Rad, 2020; Hu et al., 2023b;a) have emerged, offering
033   explanations for their behavior and insights into their internal mechanisms. Among these, Concept
034   Bottleneck Models (CBMs) (Koh et al., 2020) have gained prominence for explaining the prediction
035
      process of end-to-end AI models. CBMs add a bottleneck layer for placing human-understandable
      concepts. In the prediction process, CBMs first predict the concept labels using the original input and
036
      then predict the final classification label using the predicted concept in the bottleneck layer, which
037
      provides a self-explained decision to users.
038
039   Existing research on CBMs predominantly addresses two primary concerns: Firstly, CBMs heavily
040   rely on laborious dataset annotation. Researchers have explored solutions to these challenges in
041
      unlabeled settings (Oikarinen et al., 2023; Yuksekgonul et al., 2023; Lai et al., 2023). Secondly,
      the performance of CBMs often lags behind that of original models lacking the concept bottleneck
042
      layer, attributed to incomplete information extraction from original data to bottleneck features.
043
      Researchers aim to bridge this utility gap (Sheth & Ebrahimi Kahou, 2023; Yuksekgonul et al.,
044   2023; Espinosa Zarlenga et al., 2022). However, few of them considered the adaptivity or editability
045   of CBMs, crucial aspects encompassing annotation errors, data privacy considerations, or concept
046   updates. Actually, these demands are increasingly pertinent in the era of large models. We delineate
047   the editable setting into three key aspects (illustrated in Figure 1):

               to correct some concept labels in a trained CBM.
052          вҖў Concept-level: In CBMs, the concept set is pre-defined by LLMs or experts. However, in
053            many cases, evolving situations demand concept updates, as evidenced by discoveries such
               as chronic obstructive pulmonary disease as a risk factor for lung cancer, and doctors have

085   The most direct way to address the above three problems is retraining from scratch on the data after
086   correction. However, retraining models in such cases prove prohibitively expensive, especially in
087   large models, which is resource-intensive and time-consuming. Therefore, developing an efficient
088   method to approximate prediction changes becomes paramount. Providing users with an adaptive
089
      and editable CBM is both crucial and urgent.
090   We propose Editable Concept Bottleneck Models (ECBMs) to tackle these challenges. Specifically,
091   compared to retraining, ECBMs provide a mathematically rigorous closed-form approximation for
092   the above three settings to address editability within CBMs efficiently. Leveraging the influence
093   function (Cook, 2000; Cook & Weisberg, 1980), we quantify the impact of individual data points,
094   individual concept labels, and the concept for all data on model parameters. Despite the growing
095
      attention and utility of influence functions in machine learning (Koh & Liang, 2017), their appli-
      cation in CBMs remains largely unexplored due to their composite structure, i.e., the intermediate
096
      representation layer.
097
098   To the best of our knowledge, we are the first to work to fill this gap by demonstrating the effectiveness
099   of influence functions in elucidating the behavior of CBMs, especially in identifying mislabeled data
100   and discerning the data influence. Comprehensive experiments on benchmark datasets show that our
101
      ECBMs are efficient and effective. Our contributions are summarized as follows.
102
             вҖў We delineate three different settings that need various levels of data or concept removal in
103
               CBMs: concept-label-level, concept-level, and data-level. To the best of our knowledge, our
104
               research marks the first exploration of data removal issues within CBMs.
105
106          вҖў To make CBMs able to remove data or concept influence without retraining, we propose
107            the Editable Concept Bottleneck Models (ECBMs). Our approach in ECBMs offers a math-
               ematically rigorous closed-form approximation. Furthermore, to improve computational

114   2    R ELATED W ORK
115
116   Concept Bottleneck Models. CBM (Koh et al., 2020) stands out as an innovative deep-learning
117   approach for image classification and visual reasoning. It introduces a concept bottleneck layer
118   into deep neural networks, enhancing model generalization and interpretability by learning specific
119   concepts. However, CBM faces two primary challenges: its performance often lags behind that of
120   original models lacking the concept bottleneck layer, attributed to incomplete information extraction
121   from the original data to bottleneck features. Additionally, CBM relies on laborious dataset annotation.
122   Researchers have explored solutions to these challenges. Chauhan et al. (2023) extend CBM into
123
      interactive prediction settings, introducing an interaction policy to determine which concepts to label,
      thereby improving final predictions. Oikarinen et al. (2023) address CBM limitations and propose a
124
      novel framework called Label-free CBM. This innovative approach enables the transformation of any
125
      neural network into an interpretable CBM without requiring labeled concept data, all while maintain-
126   ing high accuracy. Post-hoc Concept Bottleneck models (Yuksekgonul et al., 2023) can be applied
127   to various neural networks without compromising model performance, preserving interpretability
128   advantages. CBMs work on the image field also includes the works of Havasi et al. (2022),Kim et al.
129   (2023),Keser et al. (2023),Sawada & Nakamura (2022) and Sheth & Kahou (2023). Despite many
130   works on CBMs, we are the first to investigate the interactive influence between concepts through
131   influence functions. Our research endeavors to bridge this gap by utilizing influence functions in
132   CBMs, thereby deciphering the interaction of concept models and providing an adaptive solution to
133   concept editing. For more related work, please refer to Appendix I.

367   Dataset. We utilize three datasets: X-ray grading (OAI) (Nevitt et al., 2006), Bird identification
368   (CUB) (Wah et al., 2011) and Large-scale CelebFaces Attributes dataset (CelebA) (Liu et al., 2015).
369   OAI is a multi-center observational study of knee osteoarthritis, which comprises 36,369 data points.
370   Specifically, we configure n=10 concepts that characterize crucial osteoarthritis indicators such as
371
      joint space narrowing, osteophytes, and calcification. Bird identification (CUB)2 consists of 11,788
      data points, which belong to 200 classes and include 112 binary attributes to describe detailed
372
      visual features of birds. CelebA comprises 202,599 celebrity images, each annotated with 40 binary
373
      attributes that detail facial features, such as hair color, eyeglasses, and smiling. As the dataset lacks
374   predefined classification tasks, following Espinosa Zarlenga et al. (2022), we designate 8 attributes as
375   labels and the remaining 32 attributes as concepts. For all the above datasets, we follow the same
376   network architecture and settings outlined in Koh et al. (2020).

      Ground Truth and Baselines. We use retrain as the ground truth method. Retrain: We retrain the
379   CBM from scratch by removing the samples, concept labels, or concepts from the training set. We
380   employ two baseline methods: CBM-IF, and ECBM. CBM-IF: This method is a direct implementation
381   of our previous theorems of model updates in the three settings. See Algorithms 1-3 in Appendix
382   for details. ECBM: As we discussed above, all of our model updates can be further accelerated
383   via EK-FAC, ECBM corresponds to the EK-FAC accelerated version of Algorithms 1-3 (refer to
384   Algorithms 6-8 in Appendix).
385   Evaluation Metric. We utilize two primary evaluation metrics to assess our models: the F1 score
386   and runtime (RT). The F1 score measures the modelвҖҷs performance by balancing precision and recall.
387   Runtime, measured in seconds, evaluates the running time of each method to update the model.

      Implementation Details. Our experiments utilized an Intel Xeon CPU and an RTX 3090 GPU. For

      utility evaluation, at the concept level, one concept was randomly removed for the OAI dataset and
390
      repeated while ten concepts were randomly removed for the CUB dataset, with five different seeds.
391   At the data level, 3% of the data points were randomly deleted and repeated 10 times with different
392   seeds. At the concept-label level, we randomly selected 3% of the data points and modified one
393   concept of each data randomly, repeating this 10 times for consistency across iterations.

395   5.2     E VALUATION OF U TILITY AND E DITING E FFICIENCY
396
397   Our experimental results, as illustrated in Table 1, demonstrate the effectiveness of ECBMs compared
398   to traditional retraining and CBM-IF, particularly emphasizing computational efficiency without
399   compromising accuracy. Specifically, ECBMs achieved F1 scores close to those of retraining (0.8808
400   vs. 0.8825) while significantly reducing the runtime from 31.44 seconds to 8.29 seconds. This pattern
401   is consistent in the CUB dataset, where the runtime was decreased from 27.88 seconds for retraining to
402
      7.03 seconds for ECBMs, with a negligible difference in the F1 score (0.7971 to 0.7963). These results
      highlight the potential of ECBMs to provide substantial time savingsвҖ”approximately 22-30% of
403
      the computational time required for retrainingвҖ”while maintaining comparable accuracy. Compared
404
      to CBM-IF, ECBM also showed a slight reduction in runtime and a significant improvement in F1
405   score. The former verifies the effective acceleration of our algorithm by EK-FAC. This efficiency is
406   particularly crucial in scenarios where frequent updates to model annotations are needed, confirming
407   the utility of ECBMs in dynamic environments where running time and accuracy are critical.
408
      We can also see that the original version of ECBM, i.e., CBM-IF, also has a lower runtime than
409
      retraining but a lower F1 score than ECBM. Such results may be due to different reasons. For example,
410
      our original theorems depend on the inverse of the Hessian matrices, which may not be well-defined
411   for non-convex loss. Moreover, these Hessian matrices may be ill-conditioned or singular, which
412   makes calculating their inverse imprecise and unstable.

477   ECBMs can erase data influence. For the data level, ECBMs aim to facilitate an efficient removal of
478   samples. We perform membership inference attacks (MIAs) to provide direct evidence that ECBMs
479   can indeed erase data influence. MIA is a privacy attack that aims to infer whether a specific data
480   sample was part of the training dataset used to train a model. The attacker exploits the modelвҖҷs
481   behavior, such as overconfidence or overfitting, to distinguish between training (member) and non-
482
      training (non-member) data points. In MIAs, the attacker typically queries the model with a data
      sample and observes its prediction confidence or loss values, which tend to be higher for members of
483
      the training set than non-members (Shokri et al., 2017).
484
485   To quantify the success of these edits, we calculate the RMIA (Removed Membership Inference
      Attack) score for each category. The RMIA score is defined as the modelвҖҷs confidence in classifying


                                                                                                                                        9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      whether a given sample belongs to the training set. Lower RMIA values indicate that the sample
487   behaves more like a test set (non-member) sample Zarifzadeh et al. (2024). This metric is especially
488   crucial for edited samples, as a successful ECBM should make the removed members behave similarly
489   to non-members, reducing their membership vulnerability. See Appendix H for its definition.
490
      We conducted experiments by randomly selecting 200 samples from the training set (members)
491
      and 200 samples from the test set (non-members) of the CUB dataset. We calculated the RMIA
492
      scores for these samples and plotted their frequency distributions, as shown in Figure 4a. The
493   mean RMIA score for non-members was 0.049465, while members had a mean score of 0.063505.
494   Subsequently, we applied ECBMs to remove the 200 training samples from the model, updated the
495   model parameters, and then recalculated the RMIA scores. After editing, the mean RMIA score for
496   the removed-members decreased to 0.052105, significantly closer to the non-membersвҖҷ mean score.
497   This shift in RMIA values demonstrates the effectiveness of ECBMs in editing the model, as the
498   removed members now exhibit behavior closer to that of non-members. The post-editing RMIA score
499   distributions are shown in Figure 4b. These results provide evidence of the effectiveness of ECBMs
500   in editing the modelвҖҷs knowledge about specific samples.

535   6 C ONCLUSION
536   In this paper, we propose Editable Concept Bottleneck Models (ECBMs). ECBMs can address
537   issues of removing/inserting some training data or new concepts from trained CBMs for different
538   reasons, such as privacy concerns, data mislabelling, spurious concepts, and concept annotation errors
539   retraining from scratch. Furthermore, to improve computational efficiency, we present streamlined
      versions integrating EK-FAC. Experimental results show our ECBMs are efficient and effective.

1869

       theoretical challenge in our proof.

1871   Model Unlearning. Model unlearning has gained significant attention in recent years, with various
1872   methods (Bourtoule et al., 2021; Brophy & Lowd, 2021; Cao & Yang, 2015; Chen et al., 2022a;b)
1873   proposed to efficiently remove the influence of certain data from trained machine learning models.
1874   Existing approaches can be broadly categorized into exact and approximate unlearning methods.
1875
       Exact unlearning methods aim to replicate the results of retraining by selectively updating only
       a portion of the dataset, thereby avoiding the computational expense of retraining on the entire
1876
       dataset (Sekhari et al., 2021; Chowdhury et al., 2024). Approximate unlearning methods, on the
1877
       other hand, seek to adjust model parameters to approximately satisfy the optimality condition of
1878   the objective function on the remaining data (Golatkar et al., 2020a; Guo et al., 2019; Izzo et al.,
1879   2021). These methods are further divided into three subcategories: (1) Newton step-based updates
1880   that leverage Hessian-related terms [22, 26, 31, 34, 40, 43, 49], often incorporating Gaussian noise to
1881   mitigate residual data influence. To reduce computational costs, some works approximate the Hessian
1882   using the Fisher information matrix (Golatkar et al., 2020a) or small Hessian blocks (Mehta et al.,
1883   2022). (2) Neural tangent kernel (NTK)-based unlearning approximates training as a linear process,
1884   either by treating it as a single linear change (Golatkar et al., 2020b). (3) SGD path tracking methods,
1885   such as DeltaGrad (Wu et al., 2020) and unrollSGD (Thudi et al., 2022), reverse the optimization
1886
       trajectory of stochastic gradient descent during training. Despite their advancements, these methods
       fail to handle the special architecture of CBMs. Moreover, given the high cost of obtaining data,
1887
       we sometimes prefer to correct the data rather than remove it, which model unlearning is unable to
1888
       achieve.
