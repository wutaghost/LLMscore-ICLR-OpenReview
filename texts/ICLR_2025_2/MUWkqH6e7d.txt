011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            AI-assisted decision-making often involves an AI model providing confidence,
014            which helps human decision-makers integrate these with their own confidence to
015
               make higher-utility final decisions. However, when human decision-makers are
               heterogeneous in their expertise, existing AI-assisted decision-making may fail
016
               to provide fair utility across them. Such unfairness raises concerns about so-
017
               cial welfare among diverse human decision-makers due to inequities in access
018            to equally effective AI assistance, which may reduce their willingness and trust
019            to engage with AI systems. In this work, we investigate how to calibrate AI
020            confidence to provide fair utility for human decision-makers. We first demon-
021            strate that rational decision-makers with heterogeneous expertise are unlikely to
022            obtain fair decision utility from existing AI confidence calibrations. We propose
023            a novel confidence calibration criterion, inter-group-alignment, which synergizes
024            with human-alignment to jointly determine the upper bound of utility disparity
025            across human decision-maker groups. Building on this foundation, we propose a
026
               new fairness-aware confidence calibration method, group-level multicalibration,
               which ensures a sufficient condition for achieving both inter-group-alignment and
027
               human-alignment. We validate our theoretical findings through extensive experi-
028
               ments on four real-world multimodal tasks. The results indicate that our calibrated
029            AI confidence facilitates fairer utility, concurrently enhancing overall utility. The
030            implementation code is available at https://anonymous.4open.science/r/iclr4103.

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035   In recent years, artificial intelligence (AI) has been increasingly leveraged to assist human decision-
036   makers in decision-making across various domains. For example in typical binary classification
037   tasks, AI systems have been developed to support clinicians in medical diagnosis (Rajpurkar et al.,
038   2020; Wysocki et al., 2023), aid financial institutions in credit risk assessment (Bussmann et al.,
039
      2021), and assist legal professionals in bail or sentencing judgments (Dement & Inglis, 2024; Grgić-
      Hlača et al., 2019). However, AI is trained on datasets with inherent uncertainties and is still far
040
      from perfectly accurate in many real-world applications (Prabhudesai et al., 2023); human decision-
041
      makers always need to integrate their own expertise with AI-generated insights to ensure the
042   appropriateness and accuracy of final decisions. One effective way to achieve this is by providing
043   AI’s confidence, which enables human decision-makers to better interpret the model’s outputs (Bhatt
044   et al., 2021; Steyvers & Kumar, 2024; Ma et al., 2023). Ideally, human decision-makers rely more on
045   AI in situations where the AI’s confidence is high and more on their own when the AI’s confidence
046   is low.
047
      Existing research in AI-assisted decision-making has primarily focused on enhancing final decision-
048   making’s utility (the effectiveness of decisions, such as accuracy in classification tasks or prediction
049   errors in regression tasks). Early studies suggested that AI confidence should be well-calibrated
050   estimates of the probability that the predicted label matches the truth label (Pakdaman Naeini et al.,
051   2015; Yin et al., 2019; Zhang et al., 2020). For instance, a well-calibrated diagnostic AI model ex-
052   presses confidence 0.75, to match the likelihood that the patient has the condition P (Y = 1) = 0.75.
053   However, Vodrahalli et al. (2022b) experimentally demonstrated that in certain scenarios, explicitly
      uncalibrated AI advice led to substantially higher decision utility compared to well-calibrated advice

303   Definition 4.3. (Multicalibration) Let C ⊂ 2Z be a collection of subsets in domain Z, and let
304   α ∈ [0, 1]. An AI’s confidence function fA : Z → [0, 1] is α-multicalibrated with respect to C if, for
305
      all Z ⊂ C, fA satisfies α-calibration (Definition 3.1) with respect to Z.
306
      Corvelo Benz & Rodriguez (2023) demonstrated that multicalibration leads to human-alignment.
307

308   alone does not provide fair utility among heterogeneous human decision-makers. To address this,
309   we introduce group-level multicalibration and explain how it ensures fair utility for heterogeneous
310   decision-makers in AI-assisted decision-making, while maintaining overall utility.
311

467   6     D ISCUSSION
468
469   6.1   R ELATED WORK
470
471   AI-assisted decision-making involves human decision-makers taking advice from AI systems. To
472   establish a productive working relationship between human decision-makers and AI, the AI model
473   is expected to provide an interpretable and explainable decision-making process. A direct approach
474   involves AI systems providing confidence for their predictions (Bhatt et al., 2021; Steyvers & Ku-
475   mar, 2024; Ma et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2020), i.e., the likelihood of classification outcomes. AI
476   confidence helps decision-makers calibrate their trust in the AI and appropriately apply AI knowl-
477
      edge to make final decisions, especially in cases where the AI model is likely to perform poorly. To
      enhance human decision-maker’s comprehension of AI prediction uncertainty, AI model confidence
478
      is primarily calibrated to reflect the probabilities of classification correctness (Hebert-Johnson et al.,
479
      2018; Guo et al., 2017; Zhao et al., 2021). However, experimental evidence by Vodrahalli et al.
480   (2022b) indicated that AI models, when perceived as more confident than they actually are—rather
481   than being well-calibrated—can enhance the accuracy of final decisions made by human decision-
482   makers after considering AI advice. The work most closely related to ours is Corvelo Benz & Ro-
483   driguez (2023), which conducted a systematic theoretical analysis of scenarios where well-calibrated
484   AI confidence may lead to suboptimal utility for rational decision-makers. They also introduced the
485   concept of AI confidence human-alignment, enabling rational decision-makers to achieve optimal
      utility. Previous works in AI-assisted decision making assume that human decision-makers are ho-


                                                          9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      mogeneous, overlooking heterogeneity (Rambachan, 2024; Rambachan et al., 2024; De-Arteaga
487   et al., 2024) in their expertise. Since heterogeneity in expertise may stem from historical inequities
488   in education and access to resources, mitigating the resulting utility disparity is critical for promoting
489   societal welfare. When AI assistance is used as a new information resource, failing to account for
490   expertise heterogeneity can exacerbate utility disparities among decision-makers, exacerbating soci-
491   etal inequities. Distinguished from Corvelo Benz & Rodriguez (2023), our work addresses a novel
492   and previously unexplored dimension of AI-assisted decision-making: ensuring equitable utility for
493   human decision-makers with varying levels of expertise. Furthermore, we contribute to a solid theo-
494   retical framework for analyzing and mitigating fairness issues in AI-assisted decision-making. Dif-
495
      ferent from the commonly used algorithmic fairness in the fair machine learning area, which mainly
      aims to ensure unbiased outcomes for individuals being decided upon (e.g., patients), our focus is on
496
      ensuring fairness for the decision-makers (e.g., doctors). Pleiss et al. (2017) investigated the compat-
497
      ibility between calibration and Equalized Odds. Beyond differences in application contexts—where
498   their focus is on predictor scenarios rather than AI-assistance—our work achieve human-alignment
499   and inter-group alignment in a compatible manner, also targeting a distinct fairness concept. This
500   fairness concept shares some similarities with accuracy disparity in centralized model training (Chi
501   et al., 2021) and egalitarian fairness in decentralized learning (Donahue & Kleinberg, 2023), but
502   it requires fundamentally different solving methods tailored to the context of AI-assisted decision-
503   making.
504
505   6.2   S COPE AND FUTURE WORK
506
507   Multi-class and multi-groups. Our theoretical results regarding the existence and mitigation for the
508   unfair utility are currently limited to binary classification tasks and binary human decision-maker
509   groups. The proofs presented in our work are modular, and it is possible that illuminating proper-
510   ties exist in the broader context of AI-assisted decision-making processes. However, extending the
511   theoretical analysis directly to multi-class classification and multiple human decision-maker groups
512
      presents several challenges. The first challenge is identifying more natural properties that utility
      functions may satisfy in multi-class classification. For example, the utility of diagnosing a patient
513
      with Type 1 diabetes (Y = 1) as Type 2 diabetes (T = 2) may yield higher utility than diagnosing
514
      them as disease-free (T = 0); that is, u(T = 2, Y = 1) > u(T = 0, Y = 1), which may be more
515   complex than binary classification. Second, with multiple human groups, there may be alternative
516   forms of fair utility, such as focusing on the max-min gap or the standard deviation of the utilities
517   across all human decision-maker groups, reflecting different social welfare objectives. This raises
518   questions about human decision-makers behavior analysis and their preferences regarding various
519   notions of fair utility, deserving further exploration in subsequent research.
520
      Fairness metrics. In this work, we use utility disparity for measuring fair utility, aligning with the
521   decision-makers’ primary goal of making more accurate decisions with AI assistance (Steyvers &
522   Kumar, 2024). The concept of utility disparity is also evident in centralized learning (Chi et al.,
523   2021) and decentralized learning (Donahue & Kleinberg, 2023). While other fairness metrics, such
524   as demographic parity and equalized odds (Mehrabi et al., 2021), emphasize the equality of positive
525   or true positive outcomes, these metrics primarily relate to individuals being judged (e.g., patients)
526   and diverge from the fair utility objective for decision-makers (e.g., doctors). Nonetheless, exploring
527   diverse fairness concepts remains an interesting avenue for future work.

      7     C ONCLUSIONS
530
531
      In this work, we have systematically analyzed the issue of unfair utility that arises when human
532
      decision-makers with heterogeneous expertise engage in AI-assisted decision-making. We have
533
      identified that rational decision-makers incorporating AI confidence may not achieve equal util-
534   ity under existing AI confidence calibration criteria. To address this issue, we have introduced a
535   novel confidence calibration criterion, inter-group-alignment, which, when combined with human-
536   alignment, establishes an upper bound on utility disparities. Building on this foundation, we have
537   proposed group-level multicalibration to enable AI confidence to achieve both human-alignment
538   and inter-group-alignment simultaneously. Experiments conducted on real datasets have thoroughly
539   evaluated the effectiveness of our new AI confidence calibration criterion and approach in providing
      optimal and fair utility across heterogeneous human decision-maker groups.

1220   Following the data processing (Corvelo Benz & Rodriguez, 2023), we transform the original

1222   interactive model (Section 2). In the original dataset, predictions by participants are from different
1223   but overlapping sets of countries across tasks, who are told the AI advice has different accuracy.
1224
       Thus, to control for these confounding factors, we focus exclusively on participants from the United

1225
       a sensitive attribute, a recommended factor that may influence expertise of human decision-makers
1226
       across different tasks but should be treated with equal utility in AI assistance for social good (Zap-
