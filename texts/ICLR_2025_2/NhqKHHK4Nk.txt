009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            The traditional objective in regression is generalization. That is, learning a func-
012            tion from training data that performs well beyond the training data. Symbolic
013            regression adds another objective, namely, interpretability of the regressor. In the
014            context of regression, interpretability means that the representation of the regres-
015            sor facilitates insights into mechanisms that underlie the functional dependence.
016            State-of-the-art symbolic regressors provide such insights. However, the state of
017
               the art predominantly incurs high costs at inference time. The recently proposed
               transformer-based end-to-end approach is orders of magnitude faster at inference
018
               time. It does not, however, achieve state-of-the-art performance in terms of in-
019
               terpretability, which is typically measured by the ability to recover ground truth
020            formulas from samples. Here, we show that the recovery performance of the end-
021            to-end approach can be boosted by carefully selecting the training data. We con-
022            struct a synthetic dataset from first principles and demonstrate that the capacity to
023            recover ground truth formulas scales with the available computational resources.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Given labeled training data, regression is the task to estimate a functional dependency between
029   independent variables, typically denoted as x, and dependent variables, typically denoted as y. The
030
      key objective in regression is, as in most of machine learning, generalization, that is, achieving small
      prediction errors beyond the training data.
031
032   Symbolic regression adds a second objective, namely interpretability of the underlying model. In-
033   specting and interpreting the symbolic expression of a functional dependence can facilitate funda-
034   mental insights into its underlying mechanisms. Therefore, symbolic regression has found applica-
035   tions in almost all areas of the natural sciences (Udrescu & Tegmark, 2021; Cornelio et al., 2023;
036
      Camps-Valls et al., 2023), in engineering (Wu & Zhang, 2023; Abdusalamov et al., 2023; Tsoi et al.,
      2024), and in medicine (Christensen et al., 2022; La Cava et al., 2023b; Zhang et al., 2024).
037
038   We discuss both objectives on the bar magnets example by Strogatz (2000) from the field of ordi-
039   nary differential equations (ODEs). It is given by two bar magnets on a common pin joint in the
040   plane, attracted by spatially opposing north and south
041   poles. From four different starting orientations of the
042
      magnets, we observe rotation angles x0 and x1 of the
      north poles of the two bar magnets and the change y of
043
      the first magnet’s angle over time. Solving the regression
044
      problem, means using the observations to find a function

080   Given a problem instance, state-of-the-art symbolic regression methods either implicitly (Landa-
081   juela et al., 2021) or explicitly (Kahlmeyer et al., 2024) search a space of small expressions that
082   are built from a predefined set of operations, usually represented by expression trees. Notably, the
083   search is done at inference time and starts anew for each problem instance. Although symbolic re-
084   gression aims for small expressions, because large expressions lose interpretability, search costs can
085   be substantial, mostly because the space of expression trees grows exponentially with the number of
086   variable and operator nodes in the expression trees (Virgolin & Pissis, 2022).
087   Biggio et al. (2021) proposed scalable neural symbolic regression with transformers to address the
088   problem of compute-intensive inference by shifting the heavy lifting into a training phase. Indeed,
089   inference with the resulting symbolic regressor can be orders of magnitude faster than state-of-the-
090   art symbolic regressors. Kamienny et al. (2022) extends this work from three to up to ten input
091   variables. Their approach performs well in terms of the standard regression measures model fit
092   and model complexity. It does, however, perform poorly in terms of the standard measure of inter-
093
      pretability, namely, the ability to recover formulas, up to symbolic equivalence, from sampled data.
      On the established symbolic regression benchmark SRBench (La Cava et al., 2021), the transformer
094
      approach as presented by Kamienny et al. (2022) recovers only a maximum of 1.59% of the ground
095
      truth formulas, whereas state-of-the-art symbolic regressors can recover up to 55% percent. Shojaee
096   et al. (2023) trade off learning against search-based approaches by using a transformer to guide the
097   search for symbolic expressions, which improves recovery at the cost of increased inference time.
098   However, while being much slower at inference time, it still does not achieve state-of-the-art recov-
099   ery. Follow-up work on pure transformer approaches by Lalande et al. (2023) and Vastl et al. (2024)
100   again performs well in terms of standard regression performance measures, but poorly in terms of
101   recovery. Given that even better accuracy can be achieved by conventional methods such as poly-
102   nomial regression or neural networks, which are not designed to be interpretable, the question of
103   whether transformers can also recover interpretable expressions is still open.
104   Here, we show that the subpar recovery rate achieved by the end-to-end symbolic regressor of Kami-
105   enny et al. (2022) is not a problem of the overall architecture, but of the training data. By systemati-
106   cally designing a synthetic training dataset that covers a diverse set of functions, we demonstrate that
107   the recovery performance of the end-to-end approach is limited only by the available computational
      budget, while preserving the advantage of fast inference.

      2     T RAINING DATA G ENERATION
109
110
      Data in a standard regression task is a set of labeled data points that is split into training and test data,
111   or sometimes into training, validation, and test data. Standard regressors are typically evaluated by
112   assessing their generalization ability, which is measured in terms of their prediction accuracy on the
113   test data. Generalization is also important for symbolic regressors, but another important objective
114   is interpretability. Interpretability can be measured in terms of model complexity, for instance, by
115   the number of nodes in an expression tree. A more direct measure of interpretability is the ability
116   of a symbolic regressor to recover a ground truth formula. However, just having labeled data is not
117   enough to measure recovery. The function from which the labeled data have been sampled must
118   also be explicitly known. Therefore, data for symbolic regression tasks are explicitly given functions
119
      together with labeled data that have been sampled from the functions. Moreover, the transformer-
      based end-to-end approach to symbolic regression does not only need the functions in the evaluation
120
      phase but also in the training phase.
121
122   Generating data for training and evaluating symbolic regressors thus comprises two tasks, namely,
123   selecting a set of explicitly given functions and computing representative samplings from these
124   functions. In the following, we describe how we address the two tasks.
125
126   2.1   R EGISTER M ACHINE P ROGRAMS
127
      The most commonly used representation of formulas in symbolic regression are expression
128
      trees (Lample & Charton, 2019; Kommenda et al., 2020; Petersen et al., 2021; Virgolin et al., 2021).
129   In their end-to-end approach, Kamienny et al. (2022) also randomly sample expression trees. Ex-
130   pression trees, however, are not a particularly compact representations, mostly because common
131   sub-expressions are not factored out. A more succinct representation that effectively handles com-
132   mon sub-expressions are expression DAGs (Kahlmeyer et al., 2024). Expression DAGs thus have the
133   advantage of being expressive while remaining short. Here, we represent symbolic expressions by
134   register machine programs (RMPs) that are structurally equivalent to expression DAGs. In compar-
135   ison to expression DAGs, RMPs have the advantage that they are naturally represented in sequential
136   form, which facilitates their straightforward integration into the transformer architecture.
137   Register Machine Programs (RMPs). Given a set of unary and binary instructions I (see the sup-
138   plement for details), input variables x0 , . . . , xD−1 and registers a0 , . . . , aL−1 . A register machine
139   program with input x ∈ RD , output y ∈ R, and L lines is a sequence of the form
140                                                                         L−1
141
                                    y = RMP(x) = ai := inst(Ai ) i=0 ,
142   where inst ∈ I and Ai ⊆ {x0 , . . . , xD−1 } ∪ {a0 , . . . , aL−1 }. At each line of the RMP, an
143   intermediate result, designated ai , is generated, where i is the line number. The final intermediate
144   result is then treated as the output of the RMP. As an example, consider the symbolic expression
145   x20 + x0 x1 , which can be written as the following RMP with dimension D = 2 and L = 3 lines,
146
                      a0 := sq({x0 }),      a1 := mult({x0 , x1 }),       a2 := add({a0 , a1 }).
147
148   In the example, we have A0 = {x0 }, A1 = {x0 , x1 }, and A2 = {a0 , a1 }.
149   Since many formulas in the application domains of symbolic regression include constants, we also
150   allow constants in our RMPs. In principle, it is enough to include only one constant from which
151   additional constants can be computed.
152
      For training a transformer-based symbolic regressor, we should not use all RMPs, because there are
153
      many redundant RMPs that compute the same function f : RD → R. RMPs that compute the same
154
      function f are called equivalent.
155
156   Equivalence classes of RMPs. Let f : RD → R be a function. The equivalence class of f is the
157   following set of RMPs,
                                  RMP | ∀x ∈ RD , RMP(x) = f (x)

159   For instance, the RMPs
160       [a0 := add(x0 , x0 ), a1 := add(x0 , a0 )]      and    [a0 := mult(x0 , 2), a1 := add(x0 , a0 )]

      Of course, it is enough to consider only one RMP from an equivalence class. Conceptually and
163   practically, it makes sense to choose an element of minimal length from each equivalence class.
164
165   Minimal RMPs. An RMP is called minimal for its equivalence class if it has minimal length L
166
      among all the RMPs in the class. Still, minimal RMPs need not be unique. If, for a given equivalence
      class, several minimal RMPs exist, then we call these programs minimal alternatives.
167
168   Our basic approach thus becomes to generate minimal RMP alternatives up to a given length, such
169   that every equivalence class is covered at most once and thus, for a given computational budget, the
170   number of covered functions is maximized.

172   2.2   S AMPLING AND S TANDARDIZATION
173
174
      For most RMPs, the corresponding functions f : RD → R do not have a “representative” data
      sample. Therefore, we are facing the problem to sample the data samples themselves. Here, we adapt
175
      the sampling approach by Kamienny et al. (2022), who already recognized the need to maximize the
176
      diversity of data samples. A sample with N data points in D dimensions is generated as follows:
177

            1. Sample a number of clusters k P∼ U {1, . . . , kmax } and k weights wi ∼ U [0, 1] , and
178
179             normalize the weights so that i wi = 1.
180

181                    D

               U [0, 1] , and a distribution shape Di ∈ {N , U} (Gaussian or uniform).
182
183         3. For each cluster i ∈ {1, . . . , k}, sample ⌊wi · N ⌋ input points from Di (µi , σi ), apply a
184
               random rotation sampled from a Haar distribution, and scale the input points such that their
               axis-aligned bounding box becomes [−a, a]D , for a given scaling parameter a > 0.
185
186         4. Use the RMP to compute the function values yi = f (xi ) at all sample points xi , i ∈ N .
187         5. Standardize the set of all sample points, that is, the inputs xi and outputs yi , by subtracting
188            their mean and dividing by the standard deviation along each dimension.

      only standardize the input points {xi }N                                   N

      an intermediate function f , which is subsequently mapped back to the target domain via the inverse
192
      of the standardization process. However, as demonstrated in previous work, end-to-end symbolic
193
      regression approaches are prone to domain overfitting (d’Ascoli et al., 2022; 2023). That is, the
194
      performance of these models often drops significantly when evaluated on data outside the training
195   domain. To mitigate this issue, we standardize both the inputs and the outputs by subtracting their

198
      As a consequence of standardization, we cannot distinguish samples from RMPs that differ by input
      and output translations and scalings. Therefore, we call two RMPs affinely equivalent when they
199
      differ only by such translations and scaling.
200
201   Affinely equivalent RMPs. Two RMPs are called affinely equivalent if their outputs are identical
202   up to scaling and translation transformations. Specifically, two register machine programs RMP1
203   and RMP2 are called equivalent if there exist constants c1 , c2 , c3 ∈ R and c4 ∈ RD such that, for
204
      any input x ∈ RD , the output of RMP1 can be transformed into the output of RMP2 as follows
205                                 RMP2 (x) = c1 · RMP1 (c3 · x + c4 ) + c2 .
206
      That is, c1 represents an output scaling factor, c2 represents an output translation, c3 represents an
207
      input scaling factor, and c4 represents an input translation vector.

      2.4   S AMPLING OF T RAINING DATA
286
287   For training a transformer-based symbolic regressor with stochastic gradient descent, we need to
288   sample batches of RMPs and then input-output examples from the sampled RMPs. Since the number
289   of RMPs grows exponentially in L, sampling from all RMPs uniform at random would be biased
290   towards large RMPs. For an unbiased sampling, generated RMPs are placed in (D, L) buckets,
291   where D ranges from 1 to 10 and L from 1 to 12. Remember, that buckets with L < D − 1 cannot
292
      hold an RMP. Feasible (D, L) buckets contain minimal alternative RMPs from different equivalence
      classes. Now, we can sample batches of RMPs by first sampling a feasible (D, L) bucket uniformly
293
      at random and then an RMP from the bucket. In the sampled RMPs, constant placeholders are
294
      replaced with constants drawn uniformly at random from a finite interval (−b, b). For each sampled
295   RMP, input-output examples are then sampled as described in Section 2.2.

      The goal of the experiments is examining the impact of our training dataset on the recovery perfor-
      mance of transformer-based symbolic regression. First, in Section 3.1 we explain our experimental

      setup. Then, in Section 3.2, we benchmark the recovery of a model trained on our training dataset

304
305   3.1   E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
306
307   All experiments were conducted on a single NVIDIA RTX A6000 GPU.
308
      Tokenization, embedding, and architecture. The encoder-decoder transformer architecture em-
309
      ployed in our experiments follows the one described in Kamienny et al. (2022). For the encoding
310   of the data points (x, y) ∈ RD × R, numbers are represented in base 10 floating-point notation,
311   rounded to four significant digits, and encoded as sequences of three tokens Charton (2021). These
312   tokens are the sign, the mantissa (between 0 and 9999), and the exponent (from E-100 to E100).
313   For example, the number 0.3 is encoded as [+, 3, E-1]. Therefore, each data point (xi , yi ) is
314   represented by 3(D + 1) tokens.
315
      RMPs are written sequentially line by line from the first instruction to the last instruction. Instruc-
316   tions from the instruction set and intermediate results ai are each represented by a single token.
317   Constants are represented by placeholders without any indication of their numerical value. For ex-
318   ample, the RMP for the function f (x0 , x1 ) = − sin(x0 ) + 0.3 sin(x0 − x1 ) is encoded as:
319          [ neg, x 1, =, a 0, add, x 0, a 0, =, a 1, sin, a 1, =, a 2,
320
               mult, c 0, a 2, =, a 3, sin, x 0, =, a 4, neg, a 4, =, a 5,
321
322
               add, a 3, a 5, =, a 6 ]
323   Since each RMP line has at most five tokens, and we only consider RMPs of length at most twelve,
      we need 60 tokens for the RMP, or 62 tokens when we also include start and end tokens.

324
      As in Kamienny et al. (2022), the tokens for each data point (xi , yi ) are first concatenated and then
325   fed into a two-layer perceptron, which projects each data point down into the embedding dimension
326   demb , with demb = 512 in our experiments. The resulting N embeddings of dimension demb are
327   then fed into a standard transformer encoder stack with four layers. Given that input points for a
328   multivariate regression problem do not naturally adhere to sequential order, we do not use positional
329   embeddings within the encoder.
330
      The RMP tokens are embedded into demb -dimensional space using a standard embedding layer.
331   The embeddings are then fed into a standard transformer decoder stack. During the experiments,
332   the decoders are varied from one to 16 layers. Moreover, experiments were conducted with input
333   lengths of 192, 448, and 960 data points, respectively. The largest model has 86M parameters.
334
      Training. The overall training strategy follows Kamienny et al. (2022). Here, we only highlight
335
      the differences, but a summary of all important training parameters can be found in the supplement.
336
      To avoid bias in the validation dataset, we withhold four equivalence classes for each dimension-
337   length combination (D, L) with L > 1. This provides us with a validation and a test dataset with
338   82 equivalence classes and 164 RMPs each. Models are trained until the cross-entropy loss on the
339   validation set is saturated.
340
      Inference. In contrast to Kamienny et al. (2023), we have to standardize the input and output
341
      dimensions of the data points, before they are tokenized and fed into the transformer. Then, k
342
      candidate RMPs are generated by a beam search with beam size k. If a generated RMP contains a
343   constant placeholder, then the placeholder is replaced by a constant that is fitted with the adapted
344   Broyden–Fletcher–Goldfarb–Shanno (BFGS) algorithm by Nawi et al. (2006). To correct for the
345   standardization, scaling and translation constants are also fitted by the adapted BFGS algorithm.
346   After, fitting the constants, the k candidate RMPs are ranked based on the R2 -score (Pearson, 1909).
347   The highest scoring RMP is returned as the result of the inference.

      3.2   SRB ENCH R ESULTS
351
352
353
      We benchmark the transformer-based symbolic regressor that is trained on our dataset against the
      state-of-the-art symbolic regression methods from the SRBench test suite (La Cava et al., 2021).
354
      SRBench supports the evaluation of symbolic regressors on a set of 252 regression problems and
355
      provides results for 14 state-of-the-art regressors. The regression problems themselves are divided
356   into two groups: 130 ground truth problems, where the true underlying formula is known, and 122
357   black box problems, where only the samples are given.
358
359
      Here, we focus on the recovery of ground truth formulas, because we regard recovery as the most di-
      rect measure of interpretability for symbolic regressors. We take the 130 ground truth formulas from
360
      the Feynman Symbolic Regression Database (Udrescu & Tegmark, 2020b) and the ODE-Strogatz
361
      Repository (La Cava et al., 2023a). Following the practice of La Cava et al. (2021), a ground truth
362
      formula f is considered recovered by a regressor fˆ if either f − fˆ can be resolved symbolically to
363
      a constant, or fˆ is non-zero and f /fˆ can be resolved symbolically to a constant. We also report the
364
      complexity of expressions, which is measured by the number of nodes in corresponding expression
365
      trees. All symbolic checks are delegated to the Python library SymPy (Meurer et al., 2017).
366
367   Recovery performance. Figure 4 shows the recovery performance and complexity for the regres-
368   sors included in the SRBench and the transformer approach trained with and without our training
369
      dataset. By using our training dataset while keeping the architecture fixed, the recovery perfor-
      mance of the transformer approach improves from 1.59% (E2E) to 34.02% (E2E-RMP). It now
370
      ranks third only behind the state-of-the-art search-based approaches UDFS (Kahlmeyer et al., 2024)
371
      and AIFeynman (Udrescu & Tegmark, 2020a), which require inference times that are orders of mag-
372   nitude larger. More specifically, as can be seen in Figure 5, the average inference time per formula of
373   the Feynman dataset is 0.32 seconds, which is three orders of magnitude faster than state-of-the-art
374   search-based approaches. The training data also impact the complexity of the formulas generated
375   by the transformer approach. When not using our training data, the transformer approach gener-
376   ates complex formulas with multiple constants, whereas it generates formulas of lowest complexity
377   among all regressors when using our dataset. Moreover, our training data makes the transformer
      approach more robust with respect to noise.

398   3.3     A BLATION S TUDIES
399
400   We perform ablation studies to assess the influence of the parameter size, the size of the training
401   dataset, the beam size, and the input length on recovery performance. We compare the results on
402   three datasets: 164 test RMPs sampled uniformly at random from all feasible (D, L) buckets not
403   used for training, 164 training RMPs sampled uniformly at random from the feasible buckets, and
404   the 119 formulas from the Feynman Symbolic Regression Database. Results are shown in Figure 5.
405   Number of parameters. The recovery performance on the Feynman dataset shows a linear in-
406   crease from the low 40% to 50% as the number of parameters increases. For attributing the recovery
407   performinance to the memorization and the generalization capabilities of the model, we observe
408   that 51% of the expressions of the Feynman dataset are in the training dataset. That is, successful
409   recovery becomes a mixture of memorization and generalization. Furthermore, 82.6% of the re-
410   covered formulas are in the training dataset and could thus be considered successfully memorized.
411
      The remaining 17.4% of the recovered formulas are not among the training data and are therefore
      the result of generalization. Memorization in our context is significantly more complex than mere
412
      memorization of a fixed sample of inputs and corresponding outputs, because, for a given function,
413
      input-output pairs are sampled with a highly diverse sampling strategy. This makes it improba-
414   ble that the model has encountered the test instance during training. Moreover, linearly increasing
415   recovery is also observed on both the test and training sets. The recovery scores, however, are sig-
416   nificantly lower than the score on the Feynman dataset. This likely is because the test and training
417   RMPs have been sampled uniformly from all feasible (D, L) buckets. We show in the supplement
418   that the difficulty of test instances increases with D and L. Therefore, there are more difficult test
419   instances among the test and training RMPs than among the Feynman formulas, which are typically
420   smaller.
421   Size of training dataset. As can be seen in Figure 5, recovery increases linearly with the number
422   of RMPs that ranges from one million to 16 million. That is, the number of RMPs and thus equiv-
423   alence classes of functions seen during training has a significant impact on recovery. For a better
424   understanding, we compare our data generation method to a generation method that samples RMPs
425   as random instruction sequences, and to the expression tree generation method by Kamienny et al.
426   (2022). For a comparison, we count the number of equivalence classes covered by the respective data
427   generation methods out of 10 000 sampled RMPs. The random instruction sequence method covers
428
      only 760 different equivalence classes, and the expression tree sampling method by Kamienny et al.
      (2022) covers about 6 400 equivalence classes.
429
430   Beam size and input length. As can be seen also in Figure 5, recovery improves significantly
431   when more than one beam is used. It saturates around 16 beams. Moreover, recovery degrades with
      increasing input length.

      3.4   F INDINGS
451
      From our experimental results, we derive the following three findings that facilitate the training of
452
      transformer-based symbolic regressors that perform well in terms of recovery.
453
454   Training data matter. Our experimental results show that the careful selection of synthetic data
455   is key to successful end-to-end learning. This supports similar findings in works on large language
456   models (LLMs), such as, for instance, in (Abdin et al., 2024). Moreover, in transformer-based
457
      symbolic regression, data memorization is desirable, because it directly improves recovery.
458   Standardization is necessary. In application areas of symbolic regression, for instance, physics,
459   data are measured at vastly different scales and under different sampling conditions (Keren et al.,
460   2023). Deep learning methods, however, are known to be highly susceptible to overfitting to
461   the training domain and the sampling conditions. Therefore, standardization is necessary for
462   transformer-based symbolic regression to avoid overfitting to the training domain, that is, to the
463
      domain from which the training data points are sampled.
464   Scaling works. While we were able to significantly boost recovery in transformer-based symbolic
465   regression, we still do not achieve state-of-the-art performance. However, our experimental setup
466   was limited to a single NVIDIA RTX A6000 GPU. Still, our experimental results provide evidence
467   that the recovery performance of transformer-based symbolic regression scales with the size of the
468   training dataset and with the number of model parameters. Since both the synthetic data generation
469
      method and the underlying model architecture are also scalable, we are confident that better results
      can be achieved with more resources.

      4     C ONCLUSION
473
474
475   The transformer-based approach to symbolic regression shifts computational effort from inference
476   to training. Inference with transformer-based symbolic regressors is up to three orders of magni-
477
      tude more efficient than competing search-based state-of-the-art approaches. Therefore, invested
      computational resources for training a transformer-based symbolic regressor more than amortize
478
      when they are reused for inference, for instance, by distributing the trained regressor to users or by
479
      providing inference as a service. However, hitherto the transformer-based approach was by far not
480   able to compete with state-of-the-art regressors in terms of recovery, an important measure of inter-
481   pretability. In this work, we have shown that recovery in transformer-based symbolic regression can
482   be boosted significantly by using a carefully designed training dataset. In our experiments, using
483   fairly limited computational resources, we have not yet reached the recovery performance of state-
484   of-the-art regressors. We have shown, however, that recovery scales favorably with the available
485   computational resources. Thus, it seems likely that state-of-the-art performance can be achieved
      with larger computational budgets.
