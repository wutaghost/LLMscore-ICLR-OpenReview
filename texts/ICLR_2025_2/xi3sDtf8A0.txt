009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011
               With the emergence of large-scale models, the machine learning community has wit-
012            nessed remarkable advancements. However, the substantial memory consumption
013            associated with these models has emerged as a significant obstacle to large-scale
014            training. To mitigate this challenge, an increasing emphasis has been placed on
015            parameter-efficient fine-tuning methodologies, which adapt pre-trained models
016            by fine-tuning only a subset of parameters. We observe that in various scenarios,
017            fine-tuning different layers could lead to varying performance outcomes, and selec-
018            tively fine-tuning certain layers has the potential to yield favorable performance
019            results. Drawing upon this insight, we propose L-MSA, a novel layer-wise fine-
020
               tuning approach that integrates two key components: a metric for layer selection
               and an algorithm for optimizing the fine-tuning of the selected layers. By lever-
021
               aging the principles of the Method of Successive Approximations, our method
022
               enhances model performance by targeting specific layers based on their unique
023            characteristics and fine-tuning them efficiently. We also provide a theoretical anal-
024            ysis within deep linear networks, establishing a strong foundation for our layer
025            selection criterion. Empirical evaluations across various datasets demonstrate that
026            L-MSA identifies layers that yield superior training outcomes and fine-tunes them
027            efficiently, consistently outperforming existing layer-wise fine-tuning methods.

030   1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032   With the increasing application of large-scale models across diverse task domains(Devlin et al., 2019;
033   Dosovitskiy et al., 2021), domain-specific fine-tuning has emerged as a pivotal strategy to bolster their
034   effectiveness in downstream tasks(Käding et al., 2017; Raffel et al., 2020). However, these fine-tuning
035   methods are often resource-intensive, presenting significant challenges in the development of large-
036
      scale models. Efforts to address these challenges have led to the development of Parameter-Efficient
      Fine-Tuning (PEFT) techniques, which aim to mitigate computational costs. These techniques
037
      encompass various approaches, such as prompt-based methods(Diao et al., 2022; Hambardzumyan
038
      et al., 2021; Lester et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2023), adapter methods(Diao et al., 2023; Houlsby et al.,
039   2019; Hu et al., 2021), and selective methods(Li et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2021; Zaken et al., 2021).
040
041   Among the array of Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT) techniques, layer-wise fine-tuning
042
      algorithms have emerged as a promising solution(Lee et al., 2022; Pan et al., 2024). Instead of
      updating all parameters simultaneously, layer-wise fine-tuning focuses on iteratively fine-tuning
043
      individual layers of the model. This approach not only reduces computational costs but also allows
044
      for more targeted adjustments, potentially leading to improved performance on downstream tasks.
045
046   However, the specific layer to fine-tune may vary based on the relationship between the source and
047   target datasets. To explore this, we conduct experiments with a Data-efficient Image Transformer
048
      (DeiT)-Tiny (Touvron et al., 2021) in three scenarios:
049
            1. Pre-training on ImageNet(Deng et al., 2009a) and fine-tuning on CIFAR-100(Krizhevsky,
050
               2009).
051
052         2. Pre-training on CIFAR-100 and transforming the input data by element-wise multiplication
053            with a matrix, where each entry is ex and x follows a standard normal distribution. Fine-
               tuning is then performed on the transformed data.

085   the state and co-state variables in MSA, which serves as the criterion for selecting layers. The
086   second component focuses on utilizing the MSA to optimize the fine-tuning of the selected layers.
087   This integrated approach ensures efficient optimization by systematically refining the layer-wise
088
      fine-tuning process, ultimately leading to improved performance.

      Adopting the control viewpoint for layer-wise fine-tuning offers a structured optimization process
136
      through Pontryagin’s Maximum Principle (PMP)(Pontryagin et al., 1962). This perspective treats
137
      each layer as part of a controlled dynamical system, enabling precise adjustments to specific layers by
138   assessing their impact on the overall loss via the Hamiltonian. Consequently, this method facilitates
139   efficient fine-tuning by focusing on layers that offer the most significant performance improvement,
140   thereby making the optimization process more systematic and effective.

218   As indicated in Section 1, it is often the case that fine-tuning the entire network is unnecessary. Rather,

220   L-MSA, a novel approach for layer-wise fine-tuning that consists of two key components: a metric
221
      for layer selection and an optimization algorithm for fine-tuning the selected layer.
222   We leverage the principles of the Method of Successive Approximations (MSA) (Chernousko &
223   Lyubushin, 1982; Li et al., 2018) as the foundational principle for both layer selection and layer
224   fine-tuning. Our method aims to improve model performance by focusing on the specific strengths of
225   individual layers, targeting the most impactful layers for fine-tuning.

331   4.1   BASELINE M ETHODS
332
333   To compare with other baselines, we follow the setups from prior work(Lee et al., 2022). We employ
334   full fine-tuning as a baseline and focus on the comparison with layer-wise methods such as LIFT(Zhu
335   et al., 2023), LISA(Pan et al., 2024), and surgical fine-tuning(Lee et al., 2022). Among these methods,
336   surgical fine-tuning provides a metric, RGN, for selecting layers. We include a comparison between
337
      our proposed metric and theirs to evaluate performance.
338   Full Fine-tuning is a widely used approach for adaptation. The model is initialized with pre-
339   trained weights and biases, and all parameters undergo gradient updates during fine-tuning. In our
340   experiments, we use the Adam optimizer to update all layers of the model.
341   LIFT(Zhu et al., 2023) is a layer-wise method where only one layer(or transform block) is updated in
342   each iteration. The selection policy for updating the layers can follow one of three strategies: (i) front
343   to end, (ii) end to front, or (iii) random. In our experiments, we test all three strategies and report the
344   average performance.
345
      LISA(Pan et al., 2024) applies the idea of importance sampling to different layers in LLMs and
346
      randomly freezes most middle layers during optimization. LISA consistently fine-tunes the first and
347   last layers, while updating each middle layer with a fixed probability.
348
349   Surgical Fine-tuning(Lee et al., 2022) shows that selectively fine-tuning a subset of layers matches
350
      or outperforms commonly used fine-tuning approaches. The authors propose two criteria for au-
      tomatically selecting which layers to freeze, with the Relative Gradient Norm (RGN), defined as
351
352   RGN = ∥g∥     2
                 ∥θ∥2 , showing better performance according to their findings. We compare our metric with
353   RGN and also evaluate the performance of our L-MSA method against Auto-RGN, which fine-tunes
354   the layer selected based on the highest RGN value.

      5     L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE D IRECTIONS
463
464   While our layer-wise fine-tuning algorithm shows promising results, it is important to acknowledge
465   its limitations. Firstly, we select layers based on the approximated updated loss, which provides a
466   good estimation of the training loss. However, this does not always guarantee strong generalization to
467   the test data. Additionally, while layer-wise fine-tuning reduces the computational burden compared
468   to full fine-tuning, it may still demand substantial computational resources due to performing both
469   forward and backward propagation, especially in large-scale models.
470   Future work could explore periodically reselecting layers and adjusting the training configuration
471   after a certain training period, allowing for continuous optimization and more efficient resource use,
472   potentially enhancing performance over time.

476   6.1   L ARGE -S CALE M ODELS
477
478   The emergence of large-scale models has revolutionized various domains, ranging from natural lan-
479   guage processing to computer vision. These models, characterized by their extensive parameterization
480   and sophisticated architectures, have demonstrated remarkable capabilities in capturing complex
481   patterns and representations from vast amounts of data.
482   In natural language processing, models like BERT(Devlin et al., 2018) and GPT(Radford et al.,
483   2018) have set new benchmarks in a variety of tasks, such as language understanding and generation.
484   By leveraging vast text corpora, these models learn rich semantic representations, excelling in
485   various downstream tasks. Similarly, in computer vision, models like ResNet(He et al., 2016) and
      EfficientNet(Tan & Le, 2019) have demonstrated unprecedented performance in image classification,


                                                          9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      object detection, and semantic segmentation tasks. By leveraging large datasets like ImageNet(Deng
487   et al., 2009b), these models learn hierarchical features essential for understanding visual content.
488
489   Despite their impressive performance, these models pose significant computational challenges,
490
      particularly due to high training costs. Addressing these issues is a key research focus, with ongoing
      efforts aimed at developing more efficient techniques for both training and inference.
491
492
493
      6.2   PARAMETER -E FFICIENT F INE -T UNING
494   Parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) techniques are designed to adapt pre-trained models by
495   selectively fine-tuning only a subset of parameters. In general, PEFT methods can be categorized into
496   three classes:
497
      Prompt-based methods prioritize the optimization of input tokens or input embeddings while
498   keeping the model parameters frozen(Diao et al., 2022; Hambardzumyan et al., 2021; Lester et al.,
499   2021; Liu et al., 2023). Continuous and differentiable forms of prompt engineering (soft prompt) are
500   designed to ease optimization. These approaches typically incur the lowest training cost among the
501   various types mentioned. However, they do not effectively reduce back-propagation costs.
502
      Adapter methods typically introduce an auxiliary module with much fewer parameters than the
503
      original model. During training, updates are exclusively applied to the adapter module, allowing for
504   more efficient parameter fine-tuning(Diao et al., 2023; Houlsby et al., 2019; Hu et al., 2021). These
505   approaches require manual design and many of them also do not effectively reduce back-propagation
506   costs.
507
      Selective methods focus on the optimization of a subset of the model’s parameters without the
508
      addition of extra modules. For instance, Exclusively fine-tuning bias terms can yield competitive
509
      performance comparable to fine-tuning the entire model(Zaken et al., 2021). Recently several
510   noteworthy techniques have been developed in this area, particularly through the concept of layer
511   freezing(Li et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2021). Compared with previous ones, Selective methods are more
512   closely related to our approach.
513
514   6.3   T RANSFER L EARNING
515
516   Previous research in transfer learning has extensively explored the efficacy of fine-tuning to adapt
517
      pre-trained features to a target distribution(Oquab et al., 2014; Sharif Razavian et al., 2014; Yosinski
      et al., 2014). To maintain the valuable information obtained during pre-training, numerous studies
518
      have proposed various methods to regularize the fine-tuning process(Li et al., 2020; Shen et al.,
519
      2021; Zhang et al., 2020). These methods aim to strike a balance between retaining the learned
520   features from the pre-trained model and adapting to the new target domain, thus ensuring effective
521   knowledge transfer. Notably, several works have demonstrated that freezing certain parameters within
522   the pre-trained model can significantly reduce overfitting during fine-tuning(Kirkpatrick et al., 2017;
523   Lee et al., 2019).
524
      Contrary to most of the prevailing approaches, our work presents a counterintuitive finding: perform-
525
      ing fine-tuning on the early layers can yield superior performance in specific scenarios. This intriguing
526   finding resonates with recent investigations in the field(Lee et al., 2022), further undermining the
527   prevailing notion that fine-tuning endeavors should predominantly concentrate on later layers, which
528   are assumed to be more intricately tied to task-specific features.
529

      7     C ONCLUSION
532   In conclusion, we have presented L-MSA, a novel layer-wise fine-tuning approach that integrates
533   a metric for layer selection with an optimization algorithm based on the Method of Successive
534   Approximations (MSA). This framework allows for efficient and targeted fine-tuning of individual
535   layers, significantly enhancing model performance while reducing computational costs. Our experi-
536   ments across various datasets and tasks validate the effectiveness of L-MSA, demonstrating that our
537   method consistently outperforms baseline techniques. By algorithmically determining which layers
538   to fine-tune, we provide a practical solution to the challenges posed by large-scale models. Overall,
539
      our work advances the field of layer-wise fine-tuning, offering new insights into optimizing model
      training and setting the stage for future research in this area.

      A.2     E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
811
812   In this section, we elaborate on the experimental setup used in our study, covering various aspects
813   such as datasets, models, pretraining, training details, and environment.
814
815   A.2.1    DATASETS
816
817   CIFAR-100(Krizhevsky, 2009): A widely used benchmark dataset consisting of 100 classes, with
818   600 images per class. The dataset is split into 50,000 training images and 10,000 test images, with
819   each image being a 32x32 RGB image.
820   CIFAR-10-C(Hendrycks & Dietterich, 2019): A corrupted version of CIFAR-10, containing 10
821   classes with various common corruptions applied to the original test set, making it ideal for evaluating
822   model robustness under real-world noisy conditions. The dataset has 5 levels of severity, and we
823   evaluate with the most severe level. We tune on 1000 images from CIFAR-10-C.
824   CIFAR-Flip(Lee et al., 2022): A synthetic task where the inputs are from the original CIFAR-10
825   dataset, but the target labels are flipped such that each label y is transformed to 9 − y (e.g., label
826   0 becomes label 9, label 1 becomes label 8, etc.). This task provides a controlled setting to assess
827   model performance when faced with adversarially shifted labels.
828
      Living-17(Santurkar et al., 2020): A specialized dataset for classifying living organisms, including
829
      animals and plants, with 17 distinct classes. It presents a challenging test case due to its domain-
830   specific nature, requiring the model to differentiate between fine-grained categories of organisms. For
831   Living-17, we tune on 850 images from the target distribution, evenly split between the 17 classes,
832   giving 50 images per class.
833
      ImageNet-C(Kar et al., 2022): A corrupted version of the ImageNet dataset, where common
834
      corruptions such as Gaussian noise, blur, and weather distortions have been applied to the validation
835
      set. Similar to CIFAR-10-C, the dataset has 5 levels of severity, and we evaluate with the most severe
836   level. We tune on 5000 images from ImageNet-C, evenly split between classes, giving 5 corrupted
837   images per class.

869   A.2.3    P RETRAINING
870
871
      For the CIFAR-100 dataset, we utilize a DeiT-Tiny model pre-trained on ImageNet. For the CIFAR-
      10-C and CIFAR-Flip datasets, we employ Wide ResNet-28-10 models that have been pre-trained on
872
      CIFAR-10. For the ImageNet-C dataset, we use a ResNet-50 model pre-trained on ImageNet.
873
874   For the Living-17 dataset, we load a ResNet model pre-trained on ImageNet and train it on the source
875   data for 5 epochs. Initially, we tune only the head for 3 epochs, followed by fine-tuning all layers for
876   an additional 2 epochs, following the Linear Probing then Fine-tuning(LP-FT) strategy(Kumar et al.,
877
      2022), while utilizing the Adam optimizer.
878
      A.2.4    T RAINING D ETAILS
879
880   Layer Configuration: For the DeiT model, each transformer block is treated as a layer, resulting in
881   a total of 12 layers. In the case of Wide ResNet-28-10 and ResNet-50, each block is considered a
882   layer, with the input convolutional layer (conv1) combined into the first layer and the output fully
883   connected layer treated as a separate layer. Consequently, Wide ResNet-28-10 consists of 3 blocks
884   plus the output layer, totaling 4 layers, while ResNet-50 comprises 4 blocks and the output layer,
885   resulting in 5 layers.
886   Fine-Tuning with Adam Optimizer: For all baseline methods, we fine-tune the model on the labeled
887   target data for a total of 10 epochs, with a batch size of 64. We explore 3 different learning rates:
888   1 × 10−3 , 1 × 10−4 , and 1 × 10−5 for all methods, except that for CIFAR-Flip, we adjust the learning
889   rates to 1 × 10−1 , 1 × 10−2 , and 1 × 10−3 for last-layer fine-tuning.
890   Fine-Tuning with L-MSA: We fine-tune the model on the labeled target data for 10 epochs for
891   CIFAR-100 and 5 epochs for other datasets, using a batch size of 64 and setting the hyper-parameter
892   ρ to 1. To optimize the maximization Hamiltonian, we utilize the Adam optimizer with a learning
893   rate of 1 × 10−4 . The optimization process runs for a total of 5 iterations.
894
      Validation Stategy: For all datasets and experiments, we implement early stopping based on the
895
      accuracy observed on a held-out validation subset of the labeled target data. We report the test
896   accuracy corresponding to the epoch with the highest validation accuracy.
