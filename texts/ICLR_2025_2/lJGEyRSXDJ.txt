009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Large language models (LLMs) are susceptible to hallucinations—outputs that
012            are ungrounded, factually incorrect, or inconsistent with prior generations. We
013            focus on close-book Question Answering (CBQA), where previous work has
014            not fully addressed the distinction between two possible kinds of hallucinations,
015            namely, whether the model (1) does not hold the correct answer in its parameters
016            or (2) answers incorrectly despite having the required knowledge. We argue that
017            distinguishing these cases is crucial for detecting and mitigating hallucinations.
018
               Specifically, case (2) may be mitigated by intervening in the model’s internal
               computation, as the knowledge resides within the model’s parameters. In contrast,
019
               in case (1) there is no parametric knowledge to leverage for mitigation, so it should
020
               be addressed by resorting to an external knowledge source or abstaining. To help
021            distinguish between the two cases, we introduce Wrong Answer despite having
022            Correct Knowledge (WACK), an approach for constructing model-specific datasets
023            for the second hallucination type. Our probing experiments indicate that the two
024            kinds of hallucinations are represented differently in the model’s inner states. Next,
025            we show that datasets constructed using WACK exhibit variations across models,
026            demonstrating that even when models share knowledge of certain facts, they still
027            vary in the specific examples that lead to hallucinations. Finally, we show that
028            training a classifier on our WACK datasets leads to better hallucination detection
029
               of case (2) hallucinations than using the common generic one-size-fits-all datasets.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
033
      Large Language Models (LLMs) are prone to generating outputs that lack grounded in the model’s
034
      input or real-world facts, as well as outputs that may be inconsistent with earlier generations within the
035
      same session (Ji et al., 2023; Sharma et al., 2023; Kalai & Vempala, 2023). These issues, collectively
036   known as hallucinations, are critical to address due to their impact on LLM reliability.
037
038   Numerous studies have focused on the detection and mitigation of hallucinations (e.g. Li et al. (2023);
039
      Zhang et al. (2024); Marks & Tegmark (2023); Chen et al. (2024); CH-Wang et al. (2023)). However,
      existing work often fails to distinguish between the different causes of hallucinations, conflating
040
      two distinct types: first type, denoted as HK− , refers to cases where the model lacks the required
041
      information, leading it to hallucinate. The second, denoted as HK+ , type occurs when, although
042   the model has the necessary knowledge and can generate correct answers under certain prompts, it
043   still produces an incorrect response in a different but similar prompt setting. These types represent
044   fundamentally different problems, requiring different solutions: When a model lacks knowledge one
045   should consult external sources (or abstain), but when a model has the knowledge it may be possible
046   to intervene in its computation to obtain the correct answer. Failing to differentiate between these
047   causes can weaken the effectiveness of detection and mitigation techniques, which often categorize
048   outputs simply as either ’hallucination’ or ’factually correct’ without further investigating these two
049   distinct types of hallucination (Marks & Tegmark, 2023; Azaria & Mitchell, 2023; Li et al., 2023;
050
      Rateike et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024; Zou et al., 2023; Hoscilowicz et al., 2024).
051   Our first contribution in this work is an automatic approach to obtain Wrong Answers despite
052   having Correct Knowledge (WACK). This approach constructs a model-specific hallucination dataset
053   that captures the distinction between the two types of hallucinations. It differentiates between
      hallucinations caused by a lack of knowledge (HK− ) and those caused by incorrect generation despite

096

100   2    M ODEL - SPECIFIC DATASET C ONSTRUCTION
101
102   In this section, we outline the process of creating a model-specific dataset, with a focus on generating
103   HK+ hallucination examples. Figure 1 provides a detailed overview of this setup. The process begins
104   by classifying examples based on the model’s knowledge, labeling all instances where the model
105   lacks knowledge as HK− . Next, using examples where the model knows the correct answer, we
106   create a scenario in which hallucinations can occur despite the model’s knowledge (HK+ ). The next
107   two subsections describe these steps. We focus on closed-book question answering (CBQA) tasks
      with short answers.

      2.1      C ATEGORIZATION OF K NOWLEDGE
109
110   In our CBQA setting, a model is given a question q and generates an answer ã, which may match
111   the factually correct gold answer ag or else constitute a hallucination. Knowledge in a language
112   model can be viewed as lying on a spectrum. We refer to the model’s parametric knowledge as being at the
113   ‘low-knowledge end’ when there is little to no association between ag and q, and as the ‘high-knowledge end’
114   when this association is strong. Hallucinations at the low-knowledge end of the spectrum are somewhat
115   expected, as the model is unlikely to generate ag (that is, we expect ã ̸= ag ). However, hallucinations
116   can occur anywhere along this spectrum, including at the high-knowledge end. Detecting the cause
117   of hallucinations in the middle of the spectrum is more complex, as they may arise either from
118
      insufficient knowledge or despite adequate knowledge.
119   To simplify our analysis, we focus on the two ends of the spectrum: high-knowledge and low-
120   knowledge, which still provide a compelling overview of the two types of hallucinations. To this
121   end, given model M , we follow the setup of Gekhman et al. (2024) in which M generates various
122   completions to q, and then we verify the existence of the answer ag in the output. Specifically1 , we
123   perform one greedy generation plus five generations with a temperature of 0.5. We use a 3-shots
124
      in-context learning scenario (Brown et al., 2020), generate a maximum of 5 tokens, and look for an
      exact match to ag . If the model did not generate ag in any of the generations, the example is labeled
125
      HK− . If the model generates ag in all the attempts, this example is considered a high-knowledge
126
      scenario and we next label it as either factually-correct or HK+ .

129   2.2      H ALLUCINATION D ESPITE KNOWLEDGE
130
131   To label a high-knowledge example as either factually correct or HK+ , we follow Zhang et al.
132   (2023), who demonstrated that after a model produced an incorrect answer, it was likely to generate
133
      an incorrect explanation to justify its error, which they termed the “snowballing effect”. Similar
      behaviors were also shown when using persuasion techniques that modify the prompt to include
134
      persuasions (Xu et al., 2023; Zeng et al., 2024), and weak semantics (Yao et al., 2023) that modify
135
      the prompt to reduce its semantic content.
136
137   We argue that these settings are important to focus on as they reflect mistakes in the prompt, which
138   can originate from either the user or the model’s previous generation.
139            1. User mistakes: We cannot expect users to have perfect knowledge—they can write a wrong
140               fact or make grammar or language mistakes—and if their own errors cause the model to
141               hallucinate, this represents a real-world problem that needs to be addressed.
142            2. Model’s previous mistakes: The model may create a snowballing effect on its own by
143               generating mistakes in previous turns that would be added to the prompt (e.g., Zhang et al.
144               2023). If the model produces a hallucination, we do not want this to affect subsequent
145               generations, thus this issue should also be mitigated.
146   To facilitate the creation of HK+ hallucinations in large quantities, we design two synthetic setups
147   that align with the ideas described above: (a) Bad-shots, leveraging snowballing, and (b) Alice-Bob,
148   leveraging persuasion and weak semantics. Later in the paper, we demonstrate that one setting
149   generalizes to the other one, indicating that it is valid to use these specific setups to investigate the
150   general phenomenon of HK+ .
151
152   Bad-shots setting. This setting illustrates how mistakes in the context can create a cascading
153   effect that may compromise the accuracy of the model’s subsequent generations. To imitate the
154   snowballing effect on a large scale, we propose a synthetic method which we name Bad-Shot
155   Prompting. We construct 20 false QA pairs using ChatGPT 3.5 (OpenAI, 2022), where the false
156   answer is semantically similar to the correct one. For instance, here is a good-shot example and its
157   corresponding bad-shot example:
158
            Good-shot question: Which element has the chemical symbol ’H’? answer: Hydrogen
159
            Bad-shot question: Which element has the chemical symbol ’H’? answer: Helium

228
229   Equipped with our process for separating examples of low and high knowledge (Section 2.1) and
230   and further labeling high-knowledge examples (Section 2.2), we create model-specific datasets.
231   As sources for examples to label, we use two common closed-book question answering datasets:
232   TriviaQA (Joshi et al., 2017) and NaturalQuetions (Kwiatkowski et al., 2019). We experiment
233   with three models: Mistral-7B-v0.3 (Jiang et al., 2023), Llama-3.1-8B (Dubey et al., 2024) and
234   Gemma-2-9B (Team et al., 2024).
235   Table 2 provides the number of examples in each category for the resulting model-specific datasets.

237   are labeled as factually correct rather than hallucinations. Still, we are left with sufficient cases of
238   hallucinations-despite-knowledge (HK+ ), which we use in the subsequent sections. In the Alice-Bob
239   setting, we observe similar trends, but with fewer hallucinations (Appendix C). For more details

      5.1         D IFFERENT MODELS HAVE DIFFERENT KNOWLEDGE AND DIFFERENT HALLUCINATIONS
410
411   To demonstrate the heterogeneity in knowledge and hallucinations across models, we measure the
412   Jaccard similarity (also known as intersection over union) of WACK datasets generated for different
413   models. To compare knowledge of models, we calculate the Jaccard similarity of examples deemed
414   as high-knowledge in two models, following our procedure from Section 2.1. To compare cases of
415   hallucinations despite knowledge (HK+ ), we calculate the Jaccard similarity of HK+ examples in
416   two models out of the set of examples that both models know.
417   Figure 5 displays these similarities. Jaccard values range from 0 (completely dissimilar) to 1
418   (perfect overlap). In Figure 5a, knowledge similarity for Natural Questions (below the diagonal)
419   is approximately 0.6, indicating significant knowledge divergence between models. For TriviaQA
420   (above the diagonal), models exhibit higher knowledge similarity (around 0.8).
421
      Figures 5b and 5c reveal that hallucinations in shared knowledge cases are mostly similar (0.8–
422
      0.95). However, a 0.1–0.2 difference in similarity scores suggests each model still exhibits unique
423   hallucination patterns. The bad-shot setting shows lower hallucination similarity than the Alice-
424   Bob setting, indicating greater diversity in hallucination patterns for this scenario. These findings
425   underscore the importance of model-specific approaches to hallucination detection and mitigation, as
426   both knowledge bases and hallucination patterns vary across models and datasets.

      one. We start by explaining how to construct the generic dataset and then move to the experiments.

448
449   Generic Dataset. A generic dataset is a labeled dataset that does not account for model-specific
450   hallucinations or knowledge. Using a generic dataset is a common practice in the field of hallucination
451   research for both detection and mitigation (e.g., Li et al. (2023); Chen et al. (2024); Zhang et al.
452   (2024); Marks & Tegmark (2023); Hoscilowicz et al. (2024)). Thus, for comparison, we also compare
453   to a generic dataset. The typical method for constructing a labeled QA-closed-book dataset involves
454   using a triplet q, ag , ah , where q is a question, ag is the gold answer, and ah is the hallucinated
455   answer. A hallucination example is created by concatenating ah after q, while a factually-correct
456   example is formed by appending ag after q. This labeling approach is based on the correctness
457
      of the answers relative to world knowledge. However, many datasets only include the ag answer,
      necessitating the creation of ah . Following Li et al. (2023), we generate ah by prompting an LLM to
458
      produce a plausible yet incorrect answer. See Appendix B for dataset creation details.
459
460
461   Results. As this work focuses on cases of hallucinations despite knowledge (HK+ ), we aim to
462   show that the generic dataset is not effective in catching HK+ hallucinations. Thus, we compare
463
      classifiers trained in two binary settings: (1) model-specific setting, separating HK+ from factually
      correct examples; and (2) generic setting, separating hallucinations from factually correct examples.
464
      We test on the model-specific test set of HK+ and factually correct examples. To make the generic
465
      and specific datasets more comparable, we add the bad-shots at the start of the prompt in both cases.
466
467   As Figure 6, shows classifiers trained on generic datasets (dashed lines) demonstrate varying degrees
468   of effectiveness and are always worse than classifiers trained on model-specific datasets (solid
469
      lines). Notably, the model-specific classifiers maintain relatively high accuracy, unlike their generic
      counterparts. This comparison underscores the advantages of tailoring hallucination detection
470
      methods to individual models, suggesting that this approach more effectively captures model-specific
471
      nuances and leads to more reliable identification of hallucinations across various models. These
472   results are consistent with related work (CH-Wang et al., 2023) that showed that a generic detector
473   achieves less on specific datasets than training directly on specific datasets. Unlike us, they use a
474   specific hallucination dataset that does not separate the two hallucinations.

      5.3         P REEMPTIVE H ALLUCINATION D ETECTION U SING M ODEL -S PECIFIC DATASETS
477
478
      Our previous detection results used hidden states obtained after the model generated an answer,
479
      potentially including a hallucination. A key advantage of model-specific datasets is their ability to
480   detect potential hallucinations preemptively, before they are generated, a feature not possible with
481   generic datasets. This section explores this capability using our WACK dataset (as before using HK+
482   and factually correct examples), where each example contains only the question q without an attached
483   answer. As a result, the classifier is trained on the internal states of the examples at the last token of the question,
484   rather than the last token of the answer. This approach allows us to analyze the model’s propensity for
485   hallucination based solely on the input query, a task unfeasible with generic datasets, which rely on
      concatenated answers for labeling, providing no signal of potential hallucination before generation.

487   datasets. Model-specific preemptive detection (solid lines) shows strong potential, indicating models
488   can anticipate hallucinations. In contrast, generic post-hallucination detection (dashed lines) shows
489   random (and even lower than random) performance, suggesting this approach is ineffective for identi-
490   fying HK+ hallucinations before they are generated. In comparison, model-specific hallucination
491   detection after generation (dotted lines) yields varied outcomes: for the TriviaQA dataset, some layers
492   and models achieve detection rates approaching 60%–70%, while for Natural Questions, the detection
493   rates remain low and close to random. We conclude that post-hallucination settings are not effective
494   for preemptive hallucination detection, further highlighting the benefits of model-specific datasets.

496   6    R ELATED W ORK
497
498   Our research investigates hallucination types (HK− and HK+ ) and develops a methodology for
499   constructing HK+ hallucinations. It is related to research on hallucinations and jailbreaking.
500
501    Hallucination Detection. Detecting hallucinations can involve treating the model as a black box,
502   posing questions or sampling its outputs (Gekhman et al., 2023; Pacchiardi et al., 2023; Manakul
503   et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024a). Another line of work attempts to detect hallucinations, factuality, or
504   answerability by examining the model’s hidden representations, often by training a detection classifier
505   (Burns et al., 2022; He et al., 2023; Rateike et al., 2023; Slobodkin et al., 2023; Azaria & Mitchell,
506
      2023; CH-Wang et al., 2023; Yuksekgonul et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023; Yin et al., 2024; Levinstein
      & Herrmann, 2024; Marks & Tegmark, 2023; Li et al., 2023). Most of this prior work used generic
507
      datasets. While we also employ detectors, we focus on model-specific datasets. Some prior work did
508
      explore model-specific hallucination datasets and showed their importance (Azaria & Mitchell, 2023;
509   Ji et al., 2024; Cao et al., 2023; CH-Wang et al., 2023). However, these efforts did not differentiate
510   between the causes of hallucinations (HK− and HK+ ).
511
512    Jailbreaking. Jailbreaking refers to techniques for causing LLMs to generate unexpected or
513   incorrect answers. For instance, Zhang et al. (2023) demonstrated the snowballing effect, where once
514   the model outputs an it, it is more likely to generate an incorrect explanation for that fact. Additionally,
515   research has shown that a model’s answers can change due to persuasion, long conversations, fantasy
516   settings, LLM personas, and out-of-distribution prompts (Zeng et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024b; Xu
517   et al., 2023; Yao et al., 2023; Nardo, 2023; Joshi et al., 2023; Pacchiardi et al., 2023). These studies
518
      highlight that the correctness of a model’s output depends on many characteristics of the prompt,
      allowing hallucinations to occur even when the model knows the correct answer.
519
520   While their work focuses on identifying methods that induce hallucinations, which can lead to HK+
521   hallucinations, our investigation directly explores the HK+ phenomenon and its relationship to HK− .
522   Additionally, we introduce a method to automatically construct the WACK dataset for further analysis.
523
524   7    D ISCUSSION AND CONCLUSION
525
526   In this work, we emphasize the importance of differentiating between hallucinations caused by lack
527   of knowledge (HK− ) and those occurring despite knowledge (HK+ ). We introduced WACK, a
528   method for creating model-specific datasets based on each model’s knowledge and hallucinations.
529   We proposed bad-shots and Alice-Bob settings to induce HK+ hallucinations and showed some
530   generalization between them. This indicates that these synthetic settings are effective for studying
531   HK+ . Our findings reveal that each model has distinct knowledge and unique hallucination patterns,
532   underscoring the need for model-specific datasets. Additionally, we demonstrated that generic
533
      datasets are less effective at detecting model-specific hallucinations than our tailored WACK datasets.
534
535   8    L IMITATIONS
536
537   Our work has a few limitations. While we evaluated three popular models, the patterns may differ in
538   other ones. Additionally, we used only two settings to induce hallucinations given a model’s correct
539   knowledge; there may be many other ways to achieve similar aims. Finally, we only examined the
      two extremes of the knowledge spectrum, leaving the middle unexplored.

      A    H YPER PARAMETERS SEARCH FOR K NOWLEDGE C ATEGORIZATION
703
704
705
      Knowledge detection typically relies on the model’s output, either through logits or generation. We
      focus on the generation approach, assessing whether the model consistently produces a factually
706
      correct answer among multiple samples, similar to recent work (Gekhman et al., 2024). This method is
707
      influenced by various hyperparameters including (1) number of generations, (2) sampling temperature,
708   (3) length of generation, and (4) prompt structure.
709
710   As directly accessing factually correct is challenging, we instead examined the consistency of
711
      knowledge classification across different hyperparameter settings. A high similarity in categorization
      across settings would suggest comparable proximity to ground truth, reducing the impact of specific
712
      hyperparameter choices.
713
714   We evaluated the following hyperparameters:
715
716          • Shots: two different 3-shot examples and one zero-shot example.
717
             • Temperature: {0.5, 1, 1.5}
718
719          • Number of generations: {5, 10}
720
721          • Length of generated text: {5, 10, 20} tokens.
722
723   We started with a baseline configuration based on preliminary experiments: 3-shots, temperature of
724   0.5, 5 generations, and 5 tokens generated. We then modified one parameter at a time to assess its
725   impact on classification similarity.
726   We categorized knowledge into three classes: “does not know” if the model did not generate the
727   correct answer in any of the generations; “know” if the model always generated the answer; and “else”
728   for anything in between.
729
      We tested this approach on 1000 random TriviaQA examples across our three models. The average
730   similarity among all 8 configurations (28 unique combinations) was 93.6% for Llama, 92.7% for
731   Mistral, and 92.2% for Gemma, indicating a high consistency in knowledge classifications. The
732   lowest similarity (about 80%) occurred with zero-shot configurations.
733
      Based on these results, we adopted the baseline setting as our knowledge detector, using the 3-shot
734
      prompt corresponding to the bad-shots used in subsequent hallucination classification. The high
735
      similarity between different few-shot prompts suggests that varying the few-shot examples should
736   yield comparable results. To enhance reliability, we supplemented this approach with one greedy
737   generation, ensuring we capture the most likely output even if temperature-based generations fail to
738   produce it.
739
      Note that most examples are labeled as either “know” or “does not know” and only about 5% are
740
      labeled as “else”. Thus we leave the treatment of this category for future work.
741
742   One area for improvement in future research is the method of answer detection. While we use ‘exact
743   match’ for simplicity and achieve relatively good results, employing methods that allow for more
744   flexible matching could enhance recall.
745
746
747
      B    DATASET CONSTRUCTION SPECIFICS
748
749   For TriviaQA we took 30K random examples from its training set as our initial dataset, making sure
750   to use only examples where the answer was no longer than 5 tokens using the Mistral tokenizer.
751   In addition, as we saw that some answers were written in upper case, we also used the lower-case
752
      version of these answers if they contained more than 3 letters and did not contain numbers or the ‘/’
      symbol.

      To create the generic dataset, the key addition was obtaining an incorrect answer for each example.
      We generated these using Mistral (Jiang et al., 2023) with the following prompt:
773

                                         Question: {question}
775
                                         Correct Answer: {correct answer}
776
                                         Incorrect Answer:
777
778   We accepted the model’s greedy generation of 5 tokens as the incorrect answer if it did not contain
779   the correct answer. During this process, we removed words such as ‘Questions’ or ‘incorrect’ that the
780   model occasionally generated alongside the answer. For examples of hallucinated answers generated
781   by Mistral in the generic dataset of TriviaQA, refer to Table 4.
782
      Note that this process could potentially make the generic dataset more similar to the model-specific Mistral
783   dataset. However, the main paper results show that this way of creating the wrong answers with Mistral provided
784   no advantage for the generic dataset. In fact, this outcome further highlights the benefits of using a model-specific
785   dataset. Despite the generic dataset being generated using responses from Mistral, it still performs worse than
786   the model Mistral-specific dataset.

788   B.2    S PECIFIC DATASET CONSTRUCTION
789
790   To construct a model-specific dataset, we began with all examples from the generic dataset. We then
791   filtered these examples based on the model’s knowledge, retaining only those classified as ‘known’
792   by the model (for detailed information on this classification process, refer to Appendix A).
793   Subsequently, we employed a prompt-based approach utilizing bad-shots to generate responses from
794   the model. For each example, the model produced a greedy generation of 5 tokens. We then evaluated
795   these generations to categorize the examples: if the correct answer was present within the generated
796   tokens, we labeled the example as “factually correct”; otherwise, it was labeled as “hallucinated”.
797   This process allowed us to create a dataset that specifically reflects the model’s knowledge and
798   generation tendencies, distinguishing between instances where the model correct responses and those
799   where it generates hallucinated content.
