011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) is a popular technique for efficient fine-tuning of
014            foundation models. However, applying LoRA in federated learning environments,
015
               where data is distributed across multiple clients, presents unique challenges. Exist-
               ing methods rely on traditional federated averaging of LoRA adapters, resulting in
016
               inexact updates. To address this, we propose Federated Exact LoRA, or FedEx-
017
               LoRA, which adds a residual error term to the pretrained frozen weight matrix. Our
018            approach achieves exact updates with minimal computational and communication
019            overhead, preserving LoRA’s efficiency. We evaluate the method on various models
020            across arithmetic reasoning, commonsense reasoning, natural language understand-
021            ing and natural language generation tasks, showing consistent performance gains
022            over state-of-the-art methods across multiple settings. Through extensive analysis,
023            we quantify that the deviations in updates from the ideal solution are significant,
024            highlighting the need for exact aggregation. Our method’s simplicity, efficiency,
025            and broad applicability position it as a promising solution for accurate and effective
026
               federated fine-tuning of foundation models.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030
      The introduction of large language models (LLMs) has revolutionized natural language processing,
031   enabling unprecedented performance across a wide range of tasks (Achiam et al., 2023; Touvron
032   et al., 2023; Team et al., 2023; Chang et al., 2024; Raffel et al., 2020; Zeng et al., 2022). While these
033   models excel at transfer learning, their true potential is often unlocked through fine-tuning — a critical
034   process that aligns these general-purpose models with specific tasks or domains. Moreover, the sheer
035   size of these models presents significant challenges for fine-tuning and deployment, particularly in
036   resource-constrained or distributed environments. To address these challenges, parameter-efficient
037   fine-tuning (PEFT) methods have gained prominence, with Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) emerging
038   as a particularly effective approach (Hu et al., 2021). LoRA’s success lies in its ability to adapt LLMs
039
      to new tasks by training only a small number of parameters, while freezing rest of the parameters. This
      significantly reduces computational and memory requirements without compromising performance.
040
      Although good progress in training of LLMs has been realized by entities equipped with massive
041
      computational resources, there is hoards of unreachable data in verticals such as healthcare, finance,
042   law firms, social-media and logistics. Federated learning (FL) is a popular paradigm to learn a
043   machine learning model in this setting with multiple distributed entities (Konečný et al., 2017;
044   Kairouz et al., 2021; Bonawitz et al., 2019) holding siloed data.
045
      Federated Fine-Tuning (FFT) for foundation models addresses the challenge of leveraging distributed
046
      datasets while preserving data privacy. The current state-of-the-art, Federated Instruction Tuning
047
      (FedIT, Zhang et al. (2024b)), uses conventional federated aggregation to average the low-rank
048   matrices A and B individually. The resulting update matrix which is formed post aggregation is
049   thus the product of the averaged matrices A and B. However, the ideal update should be the average
050   of the products of the low-rank adapters A and B. The discrepancy results from the fact that "the
051   average of the products is not equal to the product of the averages". A naive adhoc intervention of
052   modifying the aggregation to directly average the client updates is not a viable solution, since the
053   subsequently obtained weight matrix loses its low-rank structure. The low-rank structure provides
      the efficiency benefits of LoRA in the first place, making this approach computationally intractable.

074   The aggregation process must be carefully designed for both accuracy and simplicity. We introduce
075   FedEx-LoRA, a method that improves federated aggregation for LoRA by incorporating an error
076   residual term, ∆Wres , into the pretrained weight matrix to address inexact aggregation, as shown in
077   Figure 1. This adjustment preserves the low-rank efficiency of LoRA without adding computational
078   overhead. Since the average update is inherently higher rank and cannot fit into the low-rank adapters,
079   it is absorbed into the pretrained weight matrix, which is already high rank. This error term requires
080   no training and is added at each aggregation step, ensuring no additional training costs.
081   Our key contributions are summarized as follows:
082
083   • We identify a critical discrepancy in traditional federated averaging of LoRA adapters and address
084     it by explicitly assigning the error residual to the pretrained weight matrix, ensuring ideal updates.
085
      • The error residual term is incorporated at each aggregation step, maintaining LoRA’s efficiency
086
        without any additional training. We propose a communication protocol that minimizes both
087     communication and computational overhead.
088
089   • We demonstrate the effectiveness of our approach through extensive experiments on models ranging
090
        from RoBERTa-base (125M) to Gemma-2 (9B) across arithmetic reasoning, commonsense reason-
        ing, natural language understanding, and generation tasks. Our method consistently outperforms
091
        state-of-the-art federated fine-tuning techniques, showing clear performance gains.
092
093   • We provide a detailed analysis of the deviations introduced by federated averaging compared
094     to ideal updates, and identify notable patterns. We further show that while multiple assignment
095     strategies exist for exact aggregation, our specific assignment approach is most effective.

      2   R ELATED WORK
098
099
      Parameter-efficient Fine-tuning. PEFT methods aim to adapt foundation models while minimizing
100
      the number of trainable parameters. Input-based techniques like prefix tuning (Li & Liang, 2021)
101
      prepend trainable prompts, and prompt tuning (Lester et al., 2021) optimizes soft prompts in the
102   embedding space - both effective for task-specific adaptations. Architectural approaches, such as
103   adapter layers (Houlsby et al., 2019), add trainable components between transformer blocks (Vaswani
104   et al., 2017), facilitating multi-task learning. LoRA (Hu et al., 2021) reduces memory overhead by
105   representing weight updates with low-rank matrices, while AdaLoRA (Zhang et al., 2023b) improves
106   efficiency by dynamically adjusting the parameter budget. Optimization techniques, like QLoRA
107   (Dettmers et al., 2024), enable fine-tuning on consumer hardware via quantization, and LongLoRA
      (Chen et al., 2024) targets long-context tasks. Recent advancements include combining multiple


                                                         2
108
      PEFT methods (Lin et al., 2024) and scaling these techniques for very large models (Zhang et al.,
109   2024a), advancing the state of efficient fine-tuning.
110
111   Federated Fine-Tuning of Foundation Models. Federated learning (Konečný et al., 2017) is a
112
      decentralized approach that allows multiple clients to collaboratively train a shared model without
      sharing their private data. Instead, clients perform local training on their own datasets, and only
113
      the resulting model updates are securely aggregated to update the global model (Kairouz et al.,
114
      2021). This iterative process of local training and global aggregation continues until the model
115   converges. FedBERT (Tian et al., 2022) introduced federated pre-training for BERT, while recent
116   efforts have focused on federated fine-tuning of foundation models (Zhang et al., 2022; Kuang et al.,
117   2024; Babakniya et al., 2023). The current state-of-the-art, FedIT (Zhang et al., 2024b)), fine-tunes
118   LLMs by averaging LoRA parameters across clients using vanilla Federated Averaging (FedAvg,
119   McMahan et al. (2017)). However, averaging low-rank adapters independently introduces noise
120   and results in inexact global updates. Federated Freeze A LoRA (FFA-LoRA) (Sun et al., 2024)
121   mitigates this by keeping one set of adapters trainable, improving aggregation stability but limiting
122   the training flexibility of other adapters. This method is particularly advantageous in privacy-sensitive
123
      settings (Huang et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2021). Another challenge arises from heterogeneous rank
      settings, where clients adjust LoRA ranks based on their capacities (Zhao et al., 2018; Li et al., 2019).
124
      Some methods address this by self-pruning local LoRA modules and employing sparsity-weighted
125
      aggregation (Cho et al., 2024), though this introduces substantial computational overhead.

      Fine-tuning with LoRA. LoRA (Hu et al., 2021) leverages low-rank matrix factorization to efficiently
      represent the updates of pre-trained model weights. Specifically, the fine-tuned weights, W′ , are
131
      expressed as a sum of the original weights W0 and a low-rank update ∆W:
132
133                                    W′ = W0 + ∆W = W0 + BA                                          (1)
134   where W0 , W′ ∈ Rm×n are the pretrained and fine-tuned weight matrices, respectively, and
135   A ∈ Rr×n , B ∈ Rm×r represent the low-rank decomposition of ∆W. Here, the rank r is
136   significantly smaller than both m and n, leading to a substantial reduction in the number of trainable
137   parameters for ∆W. Instead of directly updating W0 during fine-tuning, LoRA optimizes the
138
      smaller matrices A and B, resulting in considerable savings in memory usage. For instance, in
      GPT-2, LoRA reduces the number of trainable parameters from 124.44 M to just 0.41 M when using
139
      a rank of r = 4, with no observed degradation in performance (Hu et al., 2021).
140

      Multiple Assignment Strategies can Lead to Exact Aggregation. Several methods can be used
      for achieving exact aggregation, with our choice of assignments for Ai and Bi being particularly
235
      pivotal. Each such assignment strategy allows us to adjust the corresponding error offset within the
236
      frozen weight matrix, facilitating precise aggregation. In Section 6, we investigate various methods
237   and empirically show that our proposed assignments for Ai and Bi deliver the best performance.
238
239   Communication Protocol. At first glance, it may seem necessary for the server to transmit the
240
      high-rank update matrix ∆Wres to the clients, which could introduce substantial communication
      overhead. However, the rank of this update matrix is capped at (k · r). Consequently, ∆Wres can be
241
      decomposed into two low-rank matrices using methods such as Gram-Schmidt orthogonalization.
242
      This decomposition expresses the matrix as a product of the basis of its column (or row) space and
243   the corresponding linear coefficients. The computational overhead incurred by this operation at each
244   aggregation step is negligible compared to the numerous matrix multiplications involved in training.
245   Importantly, clients are only required to transmit their low-rank adapters Ai and Bi , avoiding the
246   need to send any high-rank update matrices. In practice, the communication overhead is minimal
247   compared to FedIT, and overall, the communication cost remains significantly lower than that of full
248   federated fine-tuning. Detailed communication overhead analysis is provided in Section 6.
249   Best Inexact Approximation. For exact aggregation, the communication cost scales linearly with the
250   number of clients, becoming prohibitive in hyperclient settings. To address this, we propose relaxing
251   the exact aggregation condition through truncated SVD of the residual matrix. This reconstruction
252   yields a low-rank approximation which, by the Eckart-Young theorem (Eckart & Young, 1936), is
253   provably optimal for the high-rank update matrix. Specifically, for a target rank r′ , the best low-rank
                          r′
254   approximation ∆Wrec    is computed as:
255
256                                        U, S, V T ← SVD(∆Wres )                                       (15)
                                    r′
257                               ∆Wrec ← U [1 : r′ ]S[1 : r′ , 1 : r′ ]V T [1 : r′ ]                    (16)
258
259   While this method introduces approximation error, it provides the theoretically optimal approximation
260   to exact aggregation. A key advantage is that the server can control communication costs, a capability
261
      absent in previous methods - FedIT (Zhang et al., 2024b) and FFA-LoRA (Sun et al., 2024).

368   6     A NALYSIS
369
370   To fully understand the implications of our method, we performed several in-depth analyses, each
371   targeting a specific aspect of FedEx-LoRA’s performance and efficiency.
372   Assignment Strategies for Ai and Bi . As discussed in Section 4, we can incorporate any high-rank
373   update matrix ∆Wres within the frozen full-rank matrix W0 . However, assignment of the low-rank
374   adapters Ai and Bi post-aggregation is less straightforward. Any selection of Ai and Bi can be
375   offset by adjusting the residual update, by ensuring that W0 + Bi Ai remains consistent across clients.
376   We evaluate three strategies: (1) Reinitialize Ai and Bi reinitializes Ai and Bi after aggregation
377   and appends the full update to the frozen weights (ensuring W0 + Bi Ai is identical). (2) Ai ← Ai
      and Bi ← Bi leaves Ai and Bi unchanged across clients, maintaining their pre-aggregation values.

525   7    C ONCLUSION
526
527   In our work, we identified limitations in state-of-the-art federated fine-tuning methods that struggle
528   with inexact aggregation. We proposed a novel method, FedEx-LoRA, which appends the residual
529   error matrix to the frozen pretrained matrix, while maintaining minimal communication and computa-
530   tional overhead. The strength of our approach lies in its simplicity and broad applicability. Extensive
531   experiments demonstrate that FedEx-LoRA consistently outperforms other federated LoRA methods
532   across various datasets and settings. Our analyses reveal that deviations in updates from federated
533
      averaging compared to the ideal solution are significant and exhibit notable patterns.
534   Testing in privacy-preserving scenarios is a natural extension of our work. FFA-LoRA (Sun et al.,
535   2024) demonstrated that noise in differential privacy leads to greater deviations from ideal updates.
536   Given that our method achieves exact aggregation and outperforms FFA-LoRA in non-private settings,
537   we anticipate similar success in privacy-sensitive applications. Our approach can be readily adapted
538   for fine-tuning other models like Vision Transformers (ViTs) and Vision-Language models (VLMs).

      A    DATASET D ETAILS
757
758
      C OMMON S ENSE 170K is a dataset combining eight commonsense reasoning datasets (Hu et al.,
759
      2023), as detailed below:
760
761
            1. WinoGrande (Sakaguchi et al., 2021) involves filling in blanks with binary choices based
762
               on sentences that demand commonsense reasoning.
763
764         2. HellaSwag (Zellers et al., 2019) asks the model to predict the most plausible continuation
765            of a given context by selecting the correct ending from several options.
766         3. ARC Challenge or ARC-c (Clark et al., 2018) consists of multiple-choice science questions
767            designed to challenge models with more complex reasoning, making them harder for
768            methods that rely solely on co-occurrence patterns.
769
            4. PIQA (Bisk et al., 2020) tests physical commonsense reasoning, where the task is to choose
770
               the best action from a set of options in a hypothetical situation.
771
772         5. BoolQ (Clark et al., 2019) focuses on yes/no question answering from naturally occurring
773            queries.
774         6. ARC Easy or ARC-e (Clark et al., 2018) consists of grade-school-level multiple-choice
775            science questions, providing a simpler set of tasks for testing models’ basic reasoning
776            abilities.
777
            7. OBQA (Mihaylov et al., 2018) contains open-book, knowledge-intensive QA tasks requiring
778
               multi-hop reasoning to answer questions that involve integrating information from multiple
779
               sources.
780
781         8. SIQA (Sap et al., 2019) focuses on understanding human actions and predicting their social
782            consequences, evaluating models’ social commonsense reasoning.
783
784   MetaMathQA dataset (Yu et al., 2024) generates mathematical questions by rephrasing them from
785   various perspectives without introducing additional knowledge. We evaluate this dataset on two
786
      benchmarks: GSM8K (Cobbe et al., 2021), which includes grade-school math word problems
      that require multi-step reasoning, and MATH (Hendrycks et al., 2021), which features challenging
787
      competition-level mathematics problems.
788
789   GLUE Benchmark is a diverse suite of tasks for evaluating natural language understanding capa-
790   bilities. It includes datasets such as SST-2 for sentiment analysis (Socher et al., 2013), MRPC for
791   paraphrase detection (Dolan & Brockett, 2005), CoLA for linguistic acceptability (Warstadt et al.,
792
      2019), QNLI for inference (Rajpurkar et al., 2018), RTE for inference, and STS-B for semantic
      textual similarity (Cer et al., 2017). Due to its comprehensive coverage of NLU tasks, GLUE is
793
      widely used to assess models like RoBERTa. Each dataset is released under its own license.
794
795   The E2E NLG Challenge (Novikova et al., 2017) dataset is widely used to evaluate systems for
796   natural language generation, particularly for data-to-text tasks. It contains around 42,000 training
797   examples, with an additional 4,600 each for validation and testing, all from the restaurant domain.
798
      Each input table has multiple reference outputs, where each data point (x, y) includes a sequence
      of slot-value pairs and its corresponding reference text in natural language. The dataset is made
799
      available under the Creative Commons BY-NC-SA 4.0 license.
