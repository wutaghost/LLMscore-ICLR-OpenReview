                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011
               Large-scale language models (LLMs) have demonstrated outstanding perfor-
012            mance in language processing tasks, yet their deployment is often hindered by
013            high memory demands and computational complexity. Although low-bit quan-
014            tization techniques, such as 4-bit quantization, present a potential solution, they
015            frequently lead to significant accuracy degradation or require substantial effort
016            for such aggressive quantization approaches. To overcome these challenges, we
017            introduce QRazor, a reliable and effortless quantization scheme designed to en-
018            able 4-bit quantization for weights, activations, and KV cache in transformer-
019            based LLMs. The scheme involves two main stages: quantization and compres-
020
               sion. During the quantization stage, weights, activations, and KV cache values
               are quantized with wider 8 or 16-bit integers as a basis to achieve nearly identical
021
               accuracy to the original full-precision LLM models, using the absolute max scal-
022
               ing. Subsequently, all data are compressed to 4-bit using our proposed significant
023            data razoring (SDR) technique, which retains only the four most salient bits while
024            discarding the others. Furthermore, we present an integer-based arithmetic unit
025            dedicated to QRazor, enabling direct low-precision arithmetic operations without
026            decompressing the SDR data. Despite the reduced quantization effort, QRazor
027            achieves LLM accuracies better or comparable to state-of-the-art 4-bit methods.
028            By also validating the hardware efficiency, our decompression-free arithmetic unit
029            achieves 61.2% and 57.8% reduction in area and power consumption, respectively.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
      Large language models (LLMs) have garnered considerable attention for their exceptional perfor-
034
      mance across various domains, including natural language processing and automated text genera-
035
      tion. However, the extensive number of parameters in LLMs poses challenges for efficient process-
036   ing. In particular, the substantial memory requirements and high computational demands can hinder
037   their deployment in resource-constrained computing environments.
038
039
      Quantization (Dettmers et al., 2022),(Yao et al., 2022) is widely recognized as one of the most ef-
      fective techniques for addressing this challenge. By aggressively reducing the precision of LLM pa-
040
      rameters from 16-bit floating-point (FP16) to low-bit integers such as 4-bit, both memory usage and
041
      computational complexity can be significantly decreased during inference. Quantization methods
042   are broadly divided into Post-Training Quantization (PTQ)(Banner et al., 2019) and Quantization-
043   Aware Training (QAT)(Liu et al., 2023). This work focuses on PTQ, due to the substantial com-
044   putational cost of retraining LLMs required by QAT. For low-bit integer PTQ of LLM, the key
045   challenge is a significant accuracy drop. According to previous research (Xiao et al., 2024; Lin
046   et al., 2024a; Yuan et al., 2023; Ashkboos et al., 2024), quantizing activations to low-bit integers is
047   more challenging than quantizing weights due to the wider dynamic range and the presence of out-
048   liers in activations. As a result, these studies have proposed various methods to identify and mitigate
049   activation outliers during the calibration phase of PTQ.
050   For instance, QLLM (Liu et al., 2024a) introduces a channel reassembly technique to minimize
051   quantization errors in activations. However, these techniques still face significant accuracy degrada-
052   tion when both weights and activations are quantized to 4-bit integers. A recent study, Quarot (Ashk-
053   boos et al., 2024), successfully quantized both weights and activations to 4-bit integers while main-
      taining reasonable accuracy in LLMs. Notably, Quarot also demonstrated the feasibility of quantiz-


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      ing KV caches down to 3-bit, significantly reducing memory usage. To achieve this, Quarot utilizes
055   orthogonal Hadamard-based rotations (Steinerberger, 2024) to suppress outliers in activations and
056   KV caches, effectively mitigating the impact of outliers in activations. However, Hadamard-based
057   rotation does not always guarantee effective outlier suppression despite its computation overhead.
058   Furthermore, since the efficacy of these rotations is highly dependent on the underlying data dis-
059   tribution, applying Quarot to models with multiple data distributions, such as in mixture-of-expert
060   models, requires significant tuning efforts.
061   This paper introduces QRazor, a PTQ method to deliver reliable 4-bit LLMs. The QRazor employs
062   two key insights: 1) Utilizing 8-bit integers for weights and KV cache values, combined with 16-bit
063   integers for activations, effectively captures data distributions, including outliers, and serves as the
064   base precision scenario in this work. 2) By capturing a few salient bits from the base precision
065   scenario, we can preserve essential characteristics of both outliers and non-outliers at reduced bit
066   precision.
067   Building on these insights, QRazor operates in two stages: quantization and compression. In the
068   quantization stage, we first quantize weights, activations, and KV caches according to the base pre-
069   cision scenario described earlier. Our experiments demonstrate that in this PTQ setup, the accuracies
070   of various LLMs remain nearly identical to those of their original counterparts. Next, the parame-
071   ters are compressed to our target precision using our significant data razoring (SDR) technique. Our
072   SDR technique captures a few salient bits and discards the other bits, implemented through bitwise
073   operations, truncation, and round-to-nearest. Such a scheme does not manipulate data distributions
074   and is easily adaptable to various models.
075   This work considers the following two scenarios based on our QRazor scheme: W4A4 (4-bit
076   weights, 4-bit activations, FP16 KV cache) and W4A4KV4 (4-bit weights, 4-bit activations, 4-bit
077   KV cache). The accuracies of these configurations are compared against state-of-the-art (SOTA)
078   4-bit LLMs. For the LLaMA-1-7B and LLaMA-13B models, QRazor demonstrates superior per-
079   formance, achieving more than a 10% improvement in accuracy compared to QLLM Liu et al.
080   (2024a). When compared to Quarot, QRazor achieves significantly better results when evaluated
081
      against the rounding-to-nearest (RTN) baseline, and nearly matching results for configurations in-
      volving GPTQ for the LLaMA-2-7B and LLaMA-13B models. For other models, such as Mistral-7B
082
      and GEMMA2-2B, QRazor achieves strong accuracy results. These findings demonstrate QRazor’s
083
      ability to deliver consistent and reliable performance across diverse LLM models.
084
085   Additionally, we develop an integer-based arithmetic unit specifically optimized for QRazor, en-
086   abling decompression-free computations and eliminating the hardware overhead associated with
087
      group-level dequantization. The multiplication of 4-bit compressed data is performed using a 4-bit
      integer multiplier, significantly improving computational efficiency. Hardware simulations show
088
      that our design, implemented for scenarios such as W4A4 or W4A4KV4, achieves 57.8% power
089
      and 61.2% area savings compared to arithmetic operations performed after decompression.

      LLM quantization. Due to the wide quantization range required for activations, most previous
095
      PTQ schemes have focused on weight-only quantization, where weights are reduced to extremely
096   low bits while activations remain in FP16 format (Frantar et al., 2023; Dettmers et al., 2023; Lee
097   et al., 2024; Kim et al., 2024; Chee et al., 2024; Cheng et al., 2024). These schemes are optimized to
098   minimize memory consumption, yet computations are still performed in high precision. To reduce
099   the precision of both weights and activations, various studies have been conducted on mitigating
100   activation outliers by balancing the data range (Wei et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023b;
101   Behdin et al., 2023; Shao et al., 2024; Li et al., 2023a). Furthermore, more fine-grained quantization
102   strategies, such as channel-wise quantization (Wang et al., 2024) and group-wise quantization (Dai
103   et al., 2021; Yao et al., 2022), have been explored to achieve reliable results in LLM services.
104   Recently, several works have succeeded in quantizing both weights and activations to 4-bit inte-
105   gers (Guo et al., 2023; Ashkboos et al., 2024). Atom (Zhao et al., 2024) and QUIK (Ashkboos
106   et al., 2023) achieve this by quantizing most of the data to 4 bits while retaining a small portion
107   of data in 8-bit form. QLLM (Liu et al., 2024a) introduces a channel reassembly technique, and
      QuaRot (Ashkboos et al., 2024) utilizes Hadamard-based rotations to suppress outlier issues and en-


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      able effective 4-bit inference. Meanwhile, KV cache quantization is another crucial research topic,
109   especially for LLMs (Sheng et al., 2023; Hooper et al., 2024), as the cache size becomes a major
110   memory bottleneck when operating with large batches or generating long contexts. Notably, QuaRot
111   (Ashkboos et al., 2024) demonstrates 4-bit quantization of all the data, including the KV cache.
112
      Outlier mitigation. Recent studies have shown that smoothing activation outliers by transition-
113
      ing magnitudes between activations and weights can effectively reduce the wide quantization range
114
      (Xiao et al., 2024). Several studies have mitigated outliers using reorder-based quantization by clus-
115   tering the activation channels (Yuan et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2024). These studies have reduced
116   addressing complexity by fusing the reorder operation into the previous layer normalization opera-
117   tion. Other approaches, which apply transformations to the matrices to reduce outlier magnitudes,
118   have also succeeded in reducing the entire weight and activation data to low-bit integers (Liu et al.,
119   2024a; Ashkboos et al., 2024).
120
      There are several encoding methods equipped with dedicated hardware support that effectively man-
121   age outliers to be processed with the same precision as non-outlier data (Ho et al., 2020). OliVe (Guo
122   et al., 2023) proposes the outlier-victim pair mechanism, which provides extra representation space
123   for outliers, thereby maintaining the same precision for all data. O2A (Ho et al., 2020) encodes the
124   entire data into low precision by leveraging additional flag bits. These flag bits, produced per group,
125   indicate the magnitude of the outlier values. The uniformly compressed bits are then dequantized
126   based on the flag bits for computation.

145   4   T HE QR AZOR S CHEME
146
147   Our QRazor scheme consists of two stages: quantization and compression. In the quantization
148   stage, we first quantize FP16 parameters to high-bit integers to accurately represent LLM parameters
149   without any loss of accuracy, which we refer to as the base precision scenario. It is important to
150   note that, at this stage, only the static quantization method (Xiao et al., 2024) has been employed.
151   Following this, the integers in the base precision format are compressed to lower-bit data using our
152
      SDR technique.
153   Figure 1 illustrates the concept of our QRazor scheme, specifically detailing the process of quantiz-
154   ing activations to 4-bit. Most 4-bit PTQ schemes divide the tensor data into multiple groups. After
155   grouping, the data in each group (initially in FP16 precision) are directly quantized to 4-bit using a
156   single scale factor with FP16 precision. In contrast, our QRazor scheme quantizes the entire tensor
157   data to 16-bit integers during the quantization stage, with a single scale factor shared across the
158
      entire tensor. Afterward, the quantized tensor is divided into multiple groups, and the 16-bit integer
      data in each group are compressed to 4-bit using our SDR technique.
159

161   KV-caches utilize per-tensor and per-head scaling, respectively, while weights employ per-channel

197   4.1     Q UANTIZATION S TAGE : W8A16 OR W8A16KV8 I NTEGER Q UANTIZATION
198
199   In the quantization stage, LLM parameters are converted from FP16 to two base precision scenarios:
200   W8A16 (8-bit weights, 16-bit activations) and W8A16KV8 (8-bit weights, 16-bit activations, 8-bit
201   KV caches). These base precisions serve as primitive data types in the subsequent compression
202   stage. Specifically, W8A16 forms the basis for W4A4, while W8A16KV8 serves as the basis for
203   W4A4KV4, where all parameters, including KV caches, are quantized. We assume that the base
204   precisions utilize a 2’s complement format to represent negative numbers.
205   As mentioned in Section 2, outliers present challenges for low-bit LLM quantization. Activations
206   typically exhibit a more comprehensive dynamic range than weights or KV caches (Lin et al.,
207   2024b), resulting in more extreme outliers observed within. Hence, quantizing activations effec-
208   tively requires using a higher-precision format, such as 16-bit integers, to adequately capture the
209   full range of values without significant loss of information. In contrast, we can successfully express
210   weights and KV caches using only 8-bit integers without hardly affecting LLM accuracies.
211   We evaluated LLM accuracies for three scenarios: W8A8 (8-bit weights and 8-bit activations),
212   W8A16, and W8A16KV8. These results support the hypothesis mentioned above well. Our base
213   precision scenarios, W8A16 and W8A16KV8, achieve nearly identical accuracies to FP16-based
214   models across various LLaMA tasks. In contrast, the quantization using W8A8 leads to significant
215   accuracy drops, clearly demonstrating that 16-bit integers effectively characterize activation outliers,
      while 8-bit integers fail to do so (See Appendix A.1 for details).

244   4.2      C OMPRESSION S TAGE : S IGNIFICANT DATA R AZORING
245
246   Algorithm 1 shows the pseudo-code of our QRazor scheme, where the detailed operation of the
247   compression stage is explained. At the beginning of the compression, the base precision tensors
248   with 2’s complement format are converted to the tensor with sign and magnitude format. This can
249   be easily obtained by processing 2’s complement for negative values while preserving their sign bits.
250
      Following this, our SDR technique identifies the “razoring point” for each group, which corresponds
251
      to the bit position of the leading one. The razoring point is determined by detecting the bit position
252   of the leading one from the bitwise OR result of all data within the group (See Appendix A.3 for
253   details). Once the razoring point is identified, a defined number of adjacent bits starting from this
254   point, known as the salient bits, are captured. The bit width of these salient bits matches the target
255   precision. For instance, to achieve 4-bit activations, the width of the salient bits must be four, as
256   illustrated in Figure 1.
257
      After capturing salient bits, we retain only the sign bit and the selected salient bits, truncating all
258   the other bits. Then, by rounding the last bit of the remaining bits, we obtain the final compressed
259   values. In such a scheme, during the rounding process of the least significant bits (LSBs), there
260   is a potential risk of overflow. For example, rounding up “011112 ,” where the most significant bit
261   (MSB) represents the sign, could alter the sign bit. To prevent this, we avoid rounding the LSBs
262   of elements where all salient bits are ‘1’. Instead, we apply flooring to the LSBs of these elements
263   while continuing to round the LSBs of other elements within the group. The detailed procedure for
264   handling the above exception is outlined in Algorithm 1.
265   Ultimately, our SDR technique eliminates the ‘0’s in higher bit positions above the razoring point,
266   which can be reconstructed using flag bits that indicate the razoring point for each group — the
267   flag bits represent the number of truncated LSBs in the group and thus inform the razoring point
268   when the base precision is known. We validate the efficacy of our SDR technique across various
269   scenarios, demonstrating that it effectively compresses all data into a 4-bit format while delivering
      reliable accuracies across multiple LLM tasks, as further discussed in Section 5.1.

      The size of the compression group can vary from 16 to 128. The reason we can accommodate
271   relatively small group sizes during compression is that our SDR technique does not involve any
272   computational quantization but simply removes zeros from the MSB portion of the integer-based
273   data, along with a straightforward rounding process. As a result, unlike other group-based quantiza-
274   tion approaches, there is no need for a scaling process per group using higher precision. This enables
275   direct computation with low-bit operands, supported by our dedicated arithmetic design. The design
276   facilitates both memory- and compute-efficient low-bit matrix multiplication, with hardware details
277   provided in Section 4.3. It is important to note that the FP16 scaling process in QRazor is applied
278   only per tensor for activations, per channel for weights, and per head for the KV cache.
279   Due to the aggressive 4-bit compression, one might be concerned that the number of zeroed elements
280   significantly increases, degrading the reliability of our QRazor. Figure 2(a), (b) demonstrates that the
281   first concern is effectively addressed in our QRazor. It illustrates the portion of leading ‘1’ positions
282   before 4-bit compression (immediately after converting to the sign-and-magnitude format). The
283   leading ‘1’ positions for activations are predominantly located between the 8th and 12th bit orders.
284   For instance, if the leading ‘1’ position in a group is the 13th bit, parameters with an MSB below
285
      the 9th bit will be rounded and zeroed after compression, as only 4 bits are retained. Fortunately, the
      number of groups where the leading ‘1’ position exceeds the 12th-bit order is minimal (only 9%),
286
      indicating that outliers are infrequent and mitigating concerns about significant parameters being
287
      truncated after 4-bit compression.
288
289   In Figure2(c) we also analyzed the proportion of zeroed elements before and after 4-bit compression.
290   For Query, Key, and Value, the increase in zeroed elements is not so considerable. However, for
291
      activations and weights, there is a notable increment. This is expected, as significant activations and
      weights often have small absolute values close to zero. Such truncation of small values does not
292
      lead to substantial errors. Combined with the low occurrence of outliers, as mentioned above, this
293
      explains why QRazor consistently delivers reliable performance across various LLMs, as discussed
294   in Section 5.1.
295
296   It is important to note that in our compression, the truncation level dynamically varies for each group
297
      during runtime. Due to this characteristic, one might draw a comparison between our compression
      and dynamic max-scaled quantization (DMQ). However, our compression technique fundamentally
298
      differs from DMQ in the following aspects:
299
300   1. Eliminating Absolute Max Computation: Instead of determining the absolute maximum value
301   within a group, QRazor detects only the leading ’1’. While the absolute maximum inherently con-
302   tains the leading ’1’, multiple parameters may share the same leading ’1’ position. In DMQ, iden-
303
      tifying the absolute maximum value which is presented in floating point data format manner, is
      essential for computing the scaling factor. In contrast, our compression bypasses this step entirely,
304
      significantly reducing computational complexity.
305
306   2. Lightweight Compression and Decompression: Per-group DMQ typically involves group-
307   level quantization and dequantization operations, which rely on arithmetic computations such as
308   multiplication and, in some cases, division. In contrast, our compression and decompression rely on
309
      bit-level truncation and shifting, which are inherently simpler and far less resource-intensive.
310   To conclude, while DMQ and our compression share the goal of dynamically reducing precision,
311   their underlying methodologies are fundamentally different. As outlined above, QRazor’s compres-
312   sion incurs substantially lower computational overhead than DMQ. Moreover, QRazor’s simpler
313   operations require fewer hardware resources, enabling more efficient implementation in dedicated

572   6     C ONCLUSION
573
574   In this paper, we introduced QRazor, a post-training quantization (PTQ) method that enables re-
575   liable 4-bit quantization for large language models (LLMs) through two key stages: quantization
576   and compression. Our approach effectively balances accuracy and efficiency by first quantizing
577   weights, activations, and KV caches to 8-bit and 16-bit integer formats, followed by compressing
578   them using the significant data razoring (SDR) technique, which preserves salient bits for low-bit
579
      computations. Experimental results on LLaMA and LLaMA2 models demonstrate QRazor’s re-
      liable performance compared to state-of-the-art methods, particularly in scenarios such as W4A4

      A.1   P RECISION A NALYSIS OF THE Q UANTIZATION S TAGE
759
760   As discussed in Section 4.1, outliers pose a significant challenge in low-bit quantization of LLMs,
761   particularly for activations, which exhibit a much broader dynamic range compared to weights or
762   KV caches. Our observations, consistent with previous research (van Baalen et al., 2023), indicate
763   that activations require quantization to higher precision, such as 16-bit integers, to capture this wide
764   range without sacrificing accuracy. On the other hand, weights and KV caches can be effectively
765   represented using 8-bit integers without significantly affecting LLM accuracy. This finding under-
766
      scores the necessity of selecting appropriate bit-widths during the quantization stage to effectively
      capture the full range of activation outliers, ensuring accurate representation without compromising
767
      model performance.
768
769   To validate these requirements, we evaluated LLM accuracy across three quantization scenarios:
770   W8A8 (8-bit weights and 8-bit activations), W8A16, and W8A16KV8. Our results confirm that the
771   W8A16 and W8A16KV8 scenarios achieve nearly identical accuracies to their FP16 counterparts
772
      across various LLaMA tasks, while W8A8 results in significant accuracy drops. These findings
      demonstrate that 16-bit integers are crucial for capturing the outlier-prone dynamic range of acti-
773
      vations, whereas 8-bit integers suffice for weights and KV caches. Note that our razoring scheme
774
      involves a delicate process of detecting salient bits at the bit level, but it must be applied to the
775   base integer data with minimal information loss. Selecting the appropriate base precision during the
776   quantization stage is, therefore, crucial to maintaining data accuracy.
