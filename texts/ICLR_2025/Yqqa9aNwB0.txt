011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            The sheer size of modern neural networks necessitates pruning techniques to over-
014            come the significant computational challenges posed by model serving. However,
015            existing pruning techniques fail to capture the nonlinear correlation between pa-
016
               rameters and gradient, which is crucial in the pruning process, thus leading to
               low accuracy under high sparsity. In this work, we propose CoPruning, a
017
               new pruning framework, which uses a copula function based joint distribution
018
               model that precisely captures the intricate nonlinear correlation between param-
019            eters and gradient, enabling more insightful pruning decisions. Additionally, we
020            integrate a local optimization approach within CoPruning to better capture rel-
021            ative change in parameters within their local context, providing new metrics for
022            achieving finer-grained optimization. Extensive experiments on various networks
023            reveal CoPruning’s comparable performance to state-of-the-art (SoTA) pruning
024            algorithms. CoPruning outperforms the SoTA with 3.09%, 1.87%, and 2.19%
025            higher accuracy on MLPNet, ResNet20, and ResNet50 at 0.98 sparsity, respec-
026            tively, and 10.43% higher accuracy on MobileNetV1 at 0.9 sparsity on ImageNet.

029   1   INTRODUCTION
030
031   Neural networks have emerged as a cornerstone of modern machine learning, facilitating ground-
032   breaking advancements across diverse application domains, spanning from image recognition to
033   natural language processing (Devlin et al., 2019; OpenAI, 2023). Nevertheless, as networks become
034
      larger and more intricate, e.g., ChatGPT, they impose substantial computational burdens, presenting
      formidable challenges in deployment, especially in resource-constrained environments such as mo-
035
      bile devices and embedded systems (Chen et al., 2016). To tackle these challenges, network pruning
036
      is regarded as a key technique to reduce the size and storage requirements of neural networks while
037   maintaining or minimally impacting their performance by removing redundant parameters (such
038   as neurons, filters, or connections) from the network (Li et al., 2017). In the field of neural net-
039   work pruning, pruning methods are generally categorized into structured pruning and unstructured
040   pruning. Structured pruning typically retains the original structure of the neural network while
041   reducing unnecessary connections and weights, improving computational efficiency without signif-
042   icantly degrading performance. For instance, Huang & Lee (2022) proposed a strategy for training
043   structured neural networks through manifold identification and variance reduction, while Molchanov
044   et al. (2017c) focused on pruning convolutional neural networks (CNNs) for resource-efficient in-
045
      ference. Other relevant works include Sze et al. (2017) and Anwar et al. (2017), who further explore
      structured pruning in neural networks. Additionally, Fang et al. (2023) and Yang et al. (2024) studied
046
      structured pruning in large-scale language models, while He et al. (2017) focused on accelerating
047
      very deep neural networks through channel pruning. On the other hand, unstructured pruning
048   allows the removal of individual weights, often resulting in a more sparse network. For example,
049   He et al. (2022) investigated how sparse pruning exacerbates overfitting, while Han et al. (2016)
050   studied deep compression and its efficient inference engine. Similarly, Molchanov et al. (2017a) uti-
051   lized variational dropout to sparsify deep neural networks, and Frantar et al. (2022) ensured speedup
052   guarantees through accurate pruning. Other notable unstructured pruning methods include Guo et al.
053   (2016), who proposed dynamic network surgery, and Aghasi et al. (2017), who introduced convex
      pruning methods that provide performance guarantees for deep neural networks.

      In neural networks, the relationship between model parameters and gradient is typically nonlinear,
055   influenced by several factors. First, the use of nonlinear activation functions, such as ReLU and Sig-
056   moid, results in a nonlinear mapping between the network’s input and output, which directly impacts
057   the correlation between the parameters and gradient Zubair & Singha (2020a). Second, the back-
058   propagation algorithm propagates gradient through multiple layers, where each layer’s nonlinear
059   transformation further enforces this nonlinear relationship (Agarwal & Ramampiaro, 2024a). More-
060   over, the loss function is often nonlinear (e.g., cross-entropy or mean square error), meaning that
061   the gradient’s relationship with parameters is highly dependent on the nonlinearity of the loss itself
062   Zubair & Singha (2020b). Finally, optimization algorithms, such as Adam and RMSprop, dynam-
063
      ically adjust learning rates based on the gradient’s history, further complicating and nonlinearizing
      the parameter updates (Agarwal & Ramampiaro, 2024b). These factors collectively contribute to
064
      the nonlinear correlation between parameters and gradient in deep learning models.
065
066   Applying nonlinear relationships to predict models can effectively enhance the performance of mod-
067   els (Kulathunga et al., 2020). Many traditional pruning methods rely on relatively simple criteria,
068   such as magnitude-based thresholds (Han et al., 2015) or heuristic techniques (Molchanov et al.,
069
      2017b). While these approaches have proven effective in various scenarios, they often simplify the
      modeling of dependencies between network parameters and gradient, which may not adequately
070
      account for the complex, nonlinear correlations present in neural networks. As a result, this simpli-
071
      fication can lead to less accuracy.
072
073   We propose CoPruning, which is a novel framework that use copula function to model the nonlin-
074   ear correlation between parameters and gradient. By disentangling the marginal distributions from
075
      their dependency structure, copulas offer a more flexible and accurate means of capturing nonlin-
      ear interactions. This challenges the traditional linear assumptions and provides a more precise
076
      foundation for pruning decisions. CoPruning integrates a local optimization approach to capture
077
      relative changes in parameters within their local context. Through rigorous experiments, we show
078   that CoPruning outperforms traditional pruning methods across multiple performance metrics.
079   Our method achieves not only higher pruning efficiency but also significantly improves model ac-
080   curacy and robustness, particularly under extreme sparsity, highlighting its superiority in practical
081   applications.

240   3.2   S KLAR ’ S T HEOREM AND C OPULA F UNCTIONS
241
242   Copula function is fundamentally grounded in Sklar’s Theorem, which provides a framework for
243   understanding the correlations between multivariate distributions. By separating the marginal dis-
244   tributions from their dependence structure, Copula functions enable the analysis of complex depen-
245   dencies among random variables while preserving the individual characteristics of each marginal
246   distribution.
247   Proposition 1 (Sklar’s Theorem) Sklar’s Theorem (Sklar, 1959) states that any multivariate joint
248   distribution can be decomposed into its marginal distribution function and a Copula function. Specif-
249   ically, denote by H(x1 , x2 , . . . , xn ) an n-dimensional joint distribution function, and denote by
250   F1 (x1 ), F2 (x2 ), . . . , Fn (xn ) its marginal distribution functions. Then there exists a n-dimensional
251   Copula function C such that for all x1 , x2 , . . . , xn ,
252
                             H(x1 , x2 , . . . , xn ) = C(F1 (x1 ), F2 (x2 ), . . . , Fn (xn ))
253
254   Here, the Copula function C captures the dependency structure between the marginal distributions.
255   This allows for the separate study of marginal distributions and the correlation structure between
256   variables, thus providing a powerful tool for analyzing complex multivariate distributions. This
257   characteristic makes Copula functions exceptionally valuable in our framework, as preserving the
258   dependency structure between network parameters is crucial for maintaining performance during
259   pruning.
260   Next, I will introduce the copula function used in the pruning process. Copula functions can be
261   classified into various types, such as Gaussian, t, and Archimedean copulas. Each type is suited for
262   different dependency structures among variables. Among these, we utilize the Frank Copula, which
263   is particularly advantageous due to its ability to model both positive and negative dependencies
264   without imposing strict restrictions on the marginal distributions. This flexibility is crucial in our
265   optimization framework, where the strength of dependencies can vary across different regions of the
266
      network.
267   We use the Frank Copula, a type of Archimedean copula characterized by the parameter θ. A key
268   property of the Frank Copula is that as θ approaches zero, it degenerates into the independence
269   copula, which means the variables become independent. This characteristic is crucial in our opti-
      mization framework, where the strength of dependencies can vary across different regions of the

      3.3   CORRELATION M ODELING USING COPULA

284   The Copula function can capture the nonlinearity, asymmetry, and correlation of distribution tails
285   between variables, which plays an important role in extreme value analysis and predicting extreme
286   events (Nelsen, 2000). A simple proof of the nonlinear correlation between parameters and gradients
287   is provided in A.1.
288   Next, we will explain the process of modeling dependence using copulas. The analysis of modeling
289   nonlinear dependence using copula functions is primarily featured in Karra (2018). Moreover, The
290   correlation between copulas and tail dependence coefficients is essential for understanding how
291   extreme events are correlated between two variables. Copula functions describe the joint dependence
292   structure between two random variables, separating the marginal distributions from the dependency
293   structure. In cases where we are particularly interested in the behavior of variables in the tails of
294   their distributions, tail dependence coefficients provide a quantitative measure of how likely it is that
295   both variables take on extreme values simultaneously. These coefficients can be computed based on
296
      the copula function, which allows us to examine the upper and lower extremes of joint distributions.
297   The upper tail dependence coefficient (λU ) describes the degree of dependence between two vari-
298   ables in the upper tails of their distributions, where both variables take on large values. In terms of
299   copulas, this coefficient can be expressed as:

766   A.2    P ROOF OF S KLAR ’ S T HEOREM
767
768   Proposition 1 (Sklar’s Theorem) Sklar’s Theorem (Sklar, 1959) states that any multivari-
769   ate joint distribution can be decomposed into its marginal distributions and a Copula func-
770   tion. Specifically, let H(x1 , x2 , . . . , xn ) be an n-dimensional joint distribution function, and let
771   F1 (x1 ), F2 (x2 ), . . . , Fn (xn ) be its marginal distribution functions. Then there exists a Copula func-
772   tion C(u1 , u2 , . . . , un ) such that:
773
774                            H(x1 , x2 , . . . , xn ) = C(F1 (x1 ), F2 (x2 ), . . . , Fn (xn ))                     (17)
775
776   Here, the Copula function C captures the dependency structure between the marginal distributions.
777   This property makes Copula functions particularly valuable in our framework, where preserving the
778
      dependency structure between network parameters is essential for maintaining performance during
      pruning.
779
780   Sklar’s Theorem establishes a fundamental relationship between any multivariate joint distribution
781   function and its marginals through a Copula function. First, let’s define the pseudo-inverse Fi−1 of
782   each marginal distribution function Fi by
783
784                                      Fi−1 (ui ) = inf{xi ∈ R : Fi (xi ) ≥ ui }
785
786   for ui ∈ [0, 1]. We construct a Copula function C : [0, 1]n → [0, 1] using these inverses and the
787   joint distribution function H, such that
788
789                         C(u1 , u2 , . . . , un ) = H(F1−1 (u1 ), F2−1 (u2 ), . . . , Fn−1 (un )).
790
791   To validate that C is indeed a Copula, we need to demonstrate it is both grounded and n-increasing.
792   The function C is grounded because if any ui = 0, then C(u1 , . . . , un ) = 0. It is n-increasing as
793   the volume VC under the Copula over any hyperrectangle in [0, 1]n is non-negative, which follows
794   from the n-increasing nature of H and the non-decreasing property of Fi−1 . The uniform margins
795   condition is satisfied because the projection of C over any axis returns ui , which means
796
