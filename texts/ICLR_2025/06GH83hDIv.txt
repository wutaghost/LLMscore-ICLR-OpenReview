                                                  A BSTRACT
011
012            In an era of “moving fast and breaking things”, regulators have moved slowly
013            to pick up the safety, bias, and legal pieces left in the wake of broken Artificial
014            Intelligence (AI) deployment. Since AI models, such as large language models, are
015            able to push misinformation and stoke division within our society, it is imperative
016            for regulators to employ a framework that mitigates these dangers and ensures
017            user safety. While there is much-warranted discussion about how to address the
018
               safety, bias, and legal woes of state-of-the-art AI models, the number of rigorous
               and realistic mathematical frameworks to regulate AI safety is lacking. We take
019
               on this challenge, proposing an auction-based regulatory mechanism that provably
020
               incentivizes model-building agents (i) to deploy safer models and (ii) to participate
021            in the regulation process. We provably guarantee, via derived Nash Equilibria, that
022            each participating agent’s best strategy is to submit a model safer than a prescribed
023            minimum-safety threshold. Empirical results show that our regulatory auction
024            boosts safety and participation rates by 20% and 15% respectively, outperforming
025            simple regulatory frameworks that merely enforce minimum safety standards.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030
031   Current Artificial Intelligence (AI) models are powerful and have revolutionized a wide swath of
032
      industries. The recent large-scale deployment of Large Language Models (LLMs) has simultaneously
      boosted human productivity while sparking concern over safety (e.g., hallucinations, bias, and
033
      privacy). We have seen industry leaders, such as Google, Meta, and OpenAI, embroiled in controversy
034
      surrounding bias and misinformation (Brewster, 2024; Robertson, 2024; White, 2024), safety (Jacob,
035   2024; Seetharaman, 2024; White, 2023), as well as legality and ethics (Bruell, 2023; Metz et al.,
036   2024; Moreno, 2023) in their development and deployment of LLMs. Furthermore, irresponsible
037   deployment of LLMs runs the risk of allowing adversaries the ability to spread misinformation or
038   propaganda (Barman et al., 2024; Neumann et al., 2024; Sun et al., 2024). Unfortunately, a consistent
039   and industry-wide solution to oversee safe AI deployment remains elusive.
040
041   Naturally, one such solution to mitigate these dangers is for increased governmental regulation over
042   AI deployment. In the United States, there have been some strides, on federal (House, 2023) and state
043   levels (Information, 2024), to regulate the safety and security of large-scale AI systems (including
044
      LLMs). While these recent executive orders and bills highlight the necessity to develop safety
      standards and enact safety and security protocols, few details are offered. This follows a consistent
045
      trend of well-deserved scrutiny towards the lack of AI regulation without the development of rigorous
046
      and realistic mathematical frameworks to regulate. Our work sets out to solve this disconnect by
047   proposing a novel regulatory framework that a regulator can follow to not only strictly enforce the
048   safety of deployed AI models, but simultaneously incentivize the production of safer AI models.
049
050   The goal of our work is to formulate the AI regulatory process mathematically, and subsequently
051   develop a mechanism to incentivize safer development and deployment of AI models. Specifically,
052   we formulate the AI regulatory process as an all-pay auction, where agents (companies) submit their
053   models to a regulator. The regulator’s job is twofold: (a) prohibit deployment of models that fail
      to meet prescribed safety thresholds, and (b) incentivize safe model production and deployment

096   Regulation Frameworks for Artificial Intelligence. A handful of work focuses on regulation
097   frameworks for AI (de Almeida et al., 2021; Jagadeesan et al., 2024; Rodríguez et al., 2022; Yaghini
098   et al., 2024). First, de Almeida et al. (2021) details the need for AI regulation and surveys existing
099
      proposals. The proposals are ethical frameworks detailing specific ethical decisions to make and
      dilemmas to address. These proposals lack a mathematical framework to incentivize provably safer
100
      models. Rodríguez et al. (2022) utilize AI models to detect collusive auctions. This work is related
101
      to our own but in reverse: AI is applied to regulate auctions and ensure that they are not collusive.
102   In contrast, our work aims to use auctions to regulate AI deployment. Jagadeesan et al. (2024)
103   focuses on reducing barriers to entry for smaller companies who are competing against larger and
104   more established incumbent companies. A multi-objective high-dimensional regression framework is
105   proposed to capture “reputational damage” for companies who deploy unsafe AI models. This work
106   allows varying levels of safety constraints, where newer companies face less severe constraints in
107   order to spur their entry into the market, which is unrealistic in many settings and only considers
      simple linear-regression models. The closest related work to ours, Yaghini et al. (2024), proposes

108
      a regulation game for ensuring privacy and fairness that is formulated as a Stackelberg game. This
109   game is a multi-agent optimization problem that is also multi-objective (for fairness and privacy).
110   An equilibrium-search algorithm is presented to ensure that agents remain on the Pareto frontier of
111   their objectives (although this frontier is estimated algorithmically). Notably, Yaghini et al. (2024)
112   considers only one model builder (agent) and multiple regulators that provide updates to the agent’s
113   strategy. We consider a more realistic setup, where there are multiple agents and a single regulator
114   whose goal is to incentivize safer model deployment. We do not believe that it is the regulator’s
115   job to collaborate with agents to optimize their strategy. Furthermore, our mechanism is simple and
116   efficient; we do not require Pareto frontier estimation or multiple rounds of optimization.
117
118
      All-Pay Auctions. Compared to the dearth of literature in regulatory frameworks for AI, all-
119
      pay auctions are well-researched (Amann & Leininger, 1996; Baye et al., 1996; Bhaskar, 2018;
120   DiPalantino & Vojnovic, 2009; Gemp et al., 2022; Goeree & Turner, 2000; Siegel, 2009; Tardos,
121   2017). These works formulate specific all-pay auctions and determine their equilibria. Some works
122   consider settings where agents have complete information about their rivals’ bids (Baye et al.,
123   1996) while others consider incomplete information, such as only knowing the distribution of agent
124   valuations (Amann & Leininger, 1996; Bhaskar, 2018; Tardos, 2017). One major application of
125   all-pay auctions are crowd-sourcing competitions. Many agents participate to win a reward, with
126   those losing still incurring a cost for their time, effort, etc. DiPalantino & Vojnovic (2009) is one of
127   the first works to model crowd-sorucing competitions as an all-pay auction. Further research, such as
128
      Gemp et al. (2022), have leveraged AI to design all-pay auctions for crowd-sourcing competitions.
      Instead of crowd-sourcing, our work formulates the AI regulatory process as an asymmetric and
129
      incomplete all-pay auction. We leverage previous analysis in this setting (Amann & Leininger, 1996;
130
      Bhaskar, 2018; Tardos, 2017) to derive equilibria.

      3    T HE R EGULATORY AI S ETTING
135
136   There exists a regulator R with the power to set and enforce laws and regulations (e.g., U.S. govern-
137   ment regulation on lead exposure). The regulator wants to regulate AI model deployment, by ensuring
138   that all models meet a given safety threshold ϵ ∈ (0, 1), e.g., the National Institute for Occupational
139   Safety and Health regulates that N95 respirators filter out at least 95% of airborne particles. If a
140   model does not reach the safety criteria ϵ, then the model is deemed unsafe and the regulator bars
141   deployment. On the other side, there are n rational model-building agents. Agents seek to maximize
142   their own benefit, or utility.
143
144
      Bidding & Evaluation. By law, each agent i must submit, or bid in auction terminology, its
      model wi ∈ Rd for evaluation to the regulator before it can be approved for deployment. Let
145
      S(w; x) : Rd → R+ be a safety metric that outputs a safety level (the larger the better) for model
146
      w given data x. In effect, each agent, given its own data xi , bids a safety level sA
                                                                                          i := S(wi ; xi )
147   to the regulator. Subsequently, the regulator, using its own data xR , independently evaluates the
148   agent’s safety level bid as sR
                                   i := S(wi ; xR ). We assume that agent and regulator evaluation data is
149   independent and identically distributed (IID) xi , xR ∼ D.
150
       Assumption 1. Agent and regulator evaluation data comes from the same distribution xi , xR ∼ D.
151
152
153   This assumption is often realistic in regulatory settings, because both agents and regulators typically
154   rely on standardized or widely accepted datasets for model evaluation, ensuring a fair and unbiased
155   assessment of safety levels. For instance, when assessing LLMs, common datasets like benchmarks for
156   toxicity or bias are employed consistently across evaluations, reflecting real-world data distributions.
157   Therefore, it is reasonable to define agent i’s safety level bid as si := Ex∼D [S(wi ; x)]. We address
158   scenarios where evaluation data may not follow the IID assumption in Section 7.
159
160   The Price of Safety. We assume that there exists a strictly increasing function M : (0, 1) → (0, 1)
161
      that determines the “price of safety” (i.e., maps safety into cost). Simply put, safer models cost more
      to attain. As a result, we define the price of attaining ϵ safety as pϵ := M (ϵ).


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162    Assumption 2. There exists a strictly increasing function M that maps safety to cost.
163
164
165   The assumption that a strictly increasing function M maps safety to cost is realistic, because achieving
166
      higher safety levels typically requires greater resources. Safer models often demand more data,
      advanced tuning, and extensive validation, all of which increase costs. Thus, defining the price of
167
      safety as pϵ := M (ϵ), where M is strictly increasing, reflects the practical trade-off that safer models
168
      cost more to develop.
169
170   Agent Costs. Unfortunately for agents, training a safer model comes with added cost. Consequently,
171   each agent i must decide how much money to bid, or spend, bi to make its model safer. By Assumption
172   2, the resulting safety level of an agent’s model will be si = M −1 (bi ).
173
174   Agent Values. (1) Model deployment value vid . While it costs more for agents to produce safer models,
175   they gain value from having their models deployed. Intuitively, this can be viewed as the expected
176   value vid of agent i’s model. The valuation for model deployment varies across agents (e.g., Google
177   may value having its model deployed more than Apple). (2) Premium reward value vip . Beyond value
178   for model deployment, the regulator can also offer additional, or premium, compensation valued
179
      as vip by agents (e.g., tax credits for electric vehicle producers or Fast Track and Priority Review
      of important drugs by the U.S. Food & Drug Administration). The regulator provides additional
180
      compensation to agents whose models demonstrate safety levels exceeding the prescribed threshold.
181
      However, the value of this compensation varies across agents due to differing internal valuations. It
182   is unrealistic for the regulator to compensate all agents meeting the safety threshold due to budget
183   constraints. Therefore, we limit the additional rewards to a top-performing half of agents who surpass
184   the threshold, ensuring that compensation targets those contributing the most to enhanced safety
185   while maintaining feasibility for the regulator.
186
187   Value Distribution. We define the total value for each agent i as Vi := vid + vip , which represents the
188   sum of the deployment value and premium compensation. Although these values can vary widely
189   in practice, we normalize {Vi }ni=1 for all n agents to be between 0 and 1 for analytical tractability,
190   allowing us to work within a standardized range. Consequently, the price to achieve the safety
191
      threshold ϵ is also normalized to fall within the (0, 1) interval, i.e., pϵ ∈ (0, 1).
192
      The proportion of total value allocated to deployment versus compensation is determined by a scaling
193
      factor λi ∼ Dλ (0, 1/2). Therefore, the deployment value is vid := (1 − λi )Vi , and the premium
194
      compensation value is vip := λi Vi . Both Vi and λi are private to each agent, though the distributions
195   DV and Dλ are known by participants. We set the maximum allowable factor at λi = 1/2, reflecting
196   the realistic constraint that compensation should not exceed deployment value. Although our results
197   primarily consider λi ≤ 1/2, theoretical extensions can be made for scenarios where λi > 1/2.
198
199   All-Pay Auction Formulation. Overall, agents face a trade-off: producing safer models garners
200   value, via the regulator, but incurs larger costs. Furthermore, in order to attain the rewards detailed in
201   Section 3, agents must submit a model with safety level at least as large as ϵ. We can formulate this
202   problem as an asymmetric all-pay auction with incomplete information (Amann & Leininger, 1996;
203   Bhaskar, 2018; Tardos, 2017). The problem is an all-pay auction since agents incur an unrecoverable
204
      cost, safety training costs, when submitting their model to regulators. The problem is asymmetric
      with incomplete information since valuations Vi are private and differ for each agent.
205
206
      Agent Objective. The objective for each model-building agent i, is to maximize its own utility ui .
207
      Namely, each agent seeks to determine an optimal safety level to bid to the regulator bi . However,
208   depending upon the all-pay auction formulation, agents would need to take into account all other
209   agents’ bids b−i in order to determine their optimal bid b∗i ,

213   utility of each agent, such that participating agents maximize their utility when they bid more than

215   mechanism, already utilized by regulators, that does not accomplish this goal, before detailing our

458   7    C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
459
460
461   As AI models grow, the risks associated with their misuse become increasingly significant, particularly
462
      given their often opaque, black-box nature. Establishing robust algorithmic safeguards is crucial
      to protect users from unethical, unsafe, or illegally-deployed models. In this paper, we present a
463
      regulatory framework designed to ensure that only models deemed safe by a regulator can be deployed
464
      for public use. Our key contribution is the development of an auction-based regulatory mechanism
465   that simultaneously (i) enforces safety standards and (ii) provably incentivizes agents to exceed
466   minimum safety thresholds. This approach encourages broader participation and the development of
467   safer models compared to baseline regulatory methods. Empirical results confirm that our mechanism
468   increases agent participation by 15% and raises agent spending on safety by 20%, demonstrating its
469   effectiveness to promote safer AI deployment.
470   Future Work. While this work addresses key challenges in regulating AI safety, several directions
471   remain open for future exploration:
472
      (1) Model Evaluation: Creating a realistic protocol for the regulator to evaluate submitted model
473
      safety levels is important to ensure agents do not skirt around safety requirements. While we leave
474
      this problem for future work, one possible solution is that agents can either provide the regulator API
475   access to test its model or provide the model weights directly to the regulator. Truthfulness can be
476   enforced via audits and the threat of legal action.
477
      (2) Extension to Heterogeneous Settings: Extending our mechanism to heterogeneous scenarios,
478
      where evaluation data for agents and regulators differs, is a critical next step. Real-world data
479
      distributions often vary across contexts, and understanding how these variations affect both model
480   safety and agent strategies will create a more robust regulatory mechanism. While explicit protocols or
481   mathematical formulations are left as future work, we have a few ideas. One idea could be establishing
482   a data-sharing framework between agents and the regulator, where each participating agent must
483   contribute part of (or all of) its data to the regulator for evaluation. If data can be anonymized, then
484   this would be a suitable solution. Another idea could be that the regulator collects data on its own, and
485   can compare its distribution of data versus each participating agents’ data distribution. If distributions
      greatly differ, then the regulator could collect more data or resort to the previous data-sharing method.

1029   The current auction structure (Algorithm 1) expects agents to submit a single model trained solely for
1030   the upcoming auction. There is no expectation that the model will be reused for a future auction, or
1031
       indication that the model has been submitted to a previous auction. Looking towards the future, we
       would like to design S IRA to fit a repeatable auction structure, in which approved or rejected models
1032

       Repeated Agent Utility. Previously, in Algorithm 1, agents start the regulatory process with zero cost
1035   and value (i.e., they are building their models from scratch). In repeating S IRA auctions, agent cost
1036   and value are accumulated across all previous auction submissions. For example, if an agent trains
1037   its already-accepted model further to attain a higher safety level si , its total accumulated training
1038   cost is M (si ). This agent’s total value becomes the value its model gained from previous auction
1039   submissions plus any value gained from the current auction.
1040
1041   By allowing repeated S IRA auctions, an agent is able to repeatedly submit its model for regulatory
1042   review. We note that repeated submissions decrease the value of model deployment; once an
1043   agent earns the reward for deploying their model, subsequent deployments of the same model with
1044
       improved safety levels can be realistically expected to earn less value than the initial deployment.
       We characterize this loss in value for repeated submissions with an indicator function in the utility
1045
       function that only allows deployment value to be obtained once, on initial acceptance of a model.
