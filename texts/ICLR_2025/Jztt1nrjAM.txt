                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
               Misinformation is a complex societal issue, and mitigating solutions are difficult to
010
               create due to data deficiencies. To address this problem, we have curated the largest
011            collection of (mis)information datasets in the literature, totaling 75. From these, we
012            evaluated the quality of all of the 35 datasets that consist of statements or claims.
013            We assess these datasets to identify those with solid foundations for empirical
014            work and those with flaws that could result in misleading and non-generalizable
015            results, such as insufficient label quality, spurious correlations, or political bias.
016            We further provide state-of-the-art baselines on all these datasets, but show that
017            regardless of label quality, categorical labels may no longer give an accurate
018            evaluation of detection model performance. We discuss alternatives to mitigate this
019
               problem. Overall, this guide aims to provide a roadmap for obtaining higher quality
               data and conducting more effective evaluations, ultimately improving research in
020
               misinformation detection.

023   1   I NTRODUCTION
024
025   Misinformation is a pressing concern for society, already causing significant negative impacts and
026   posing even greater risks with the advent of generative AI (Torkington, 2024). Extensive research has
027   been devoted to this problem, yet it remains unresolved. There has been considerable recent progress
028   in methods, especially leveraging LLMs (Chen & Shu, 2023). However, to fuel further progress, we
029   also need strong and reliable data.
030   Multiple studies have identified data availability, and especially data quality, as a barrier in this
031   domain. To begin with, obtaining high quality veracity labels is challenging and time-consuming,
032   even for experts (Zubiaga et al., 2016). Shortcuts, though, can cause severe spurious correlations
033   (Pelrine et al., 2021; Wu & Hooi, 2022), and even with high quality labels there can be issues with
034   ambiguity of input texts (Pelrine et al., 2023). There are many surveys of the method landscape
035   (Shu et al., 2017; Oshikawa et al., 2018; Zhou & Zafarani, 2020; Chen & Shu, 2023), but analysis of
036   datasets has often been limited in scale (Pelrine et al., 2021; Wu & Hooi, 2022; Pelrine et al., 2023)
037   or depth.
038   In this study, we present the largest scale survey of datasets in the literature to date, curating 75
039   datasets along with corresponding descriptive analyses and categorizations. This is nearly 3 times as
040   many as other dataset-focused surveys like Hamed et al. (2023), and many times more than general
041   surveys like Ali et al. (2022); Shu et al. (2017); Oshikawa et al. (2018); Zhou & Zafarani (2020). We
042   provide a summary of each dataset, along with key information like topic, size, modality, languages,
043   geographic region, and time period.
044   Then, we focus on 35 datasets that include claims, which serve as key sources of atomic data for
045   misinformation detection, and we evaluate their quality in depth. First, we assess label quality,
046   examining the labeling process and comparing the advantages and limitations of expert fact-checking,
047   crowdsourced labels, source-based evaluations and algorithmic labeling. Then, we examine three
048   types of potential spurious correlations and bias, that could lead to predictions based on invalid,
049   non-generalizable signals. In particular, we start by looking at keyword based correlations—which
050
      can also serve as a proxy for topic or event. Then we look at temporal correlations, and finally the
      political leaning of the statements. These quality evaluations, combined with our descriptive analysis,
051
      offer practical insights for selecting datasets in future misinformation research.
052
053   Once datasets are chosen, implementation and evaluation questions remain. To help address these, we
      first present a unified formatting and labeling schema for all 35 claims datasets. Next, we establish


                                                         1

054
      state-of-the-art baselines using GPT-4 with and without web search to collect evidence. Following
055   this analysis, we find that standard evaluation metrics like accuracy and F1, when computed simply
056   in relation to ground truth labels, are no longer sufficient to evaluate leading generative methods
057   for misinformation detection and could lead to invalid conclusions. We provide initial work on an
058   alternative evaluation as a starting point for future research, and suggest such evaluation research is
059   urgently needed in the misinformation detection domain.
060
      In summary, we present a guide to misinformation datasets, including:
061
062   • The largest scale survey of such datasets, with over 75 identified and analyzed.
063   • An in depth evaluation of the quality of 35 datasets focused on claims, identifying limitations to
064     take into account when selecting and using them.
065   • Practical tools for research using those datasets: a unified file and label schema, and state-of-the-art
066
        baselines.
      • Analysis of evaluation suggesting simple metrics like accuracy and F1 may be obsolete in this
067
        domain, and initial work on alternatives.

072   In recent years, the scientific community has shown a growing interest in fake news detection to
073   mitigate the spread of false information. Within this evolving field, several surveys have emerged to
074   offer comprehensive reviews and standardized evaluations. A pioneering effort by Shu et al. (2017)
075   provided an early framework, defining fake news, detailing its characteristics, and summarizing
076   detection techniques from a data mining perspective. Subsequent surveys, such as Oshikawa et al.
077   (2018) and Zhou & Zafarani (2020), have explored alternative methodologies, focusing respectively
078   on natural language processing (NLP) methods and interdisciplinary perspectives. However, while
079
      these surveys and others (Bondielli & Marcelloni, 2019; Gravanis et al., 2019) are valuable for
      providing a comprehensive overview of the state-of-art in fake news detection, they pay limited
080
      attention to existing datasets. Indeed, even if some emphasize the challenges of data collection or
081
      stress the importance of dataset quality, these surveys usually provide only superficial coverage of
082   existing datasets, overlooking their specific content, details, and characteristics. Assessing the quality
083   of these misinformation datasets is critical because they are often used to train and test models for
084   misinformation detection and related tasks. A lack of quality data in this context implies that biases
085   and erroneous conclusions could be introduced both in the development and in the validation process
086   of these systems.
087
      This gap has thus spurred the emergence of additional surveys dedicated to addressing these dataset-
088   centric nuances, which can be categorized into two types. The first one focuses on categorizing
089   existing datasets to guide the research community in their selection. For example, D’Ulizia et al.
090   (2021) surveyed 27 datasets based on eleven characteristics (e.g., application purpose, type of
091   disinformation, language, size, news content type, etc.) and compared these quantitatively. Another
092   example, Sharma et al. (2019) summarized the characteristic features of 23 existing datasets, providing
093   a clearer picture of those available to the public. However, these surveys have an important drawback;
094   they often lack in-depth analysis. In fact, only descriptive characteristics are listed, thus neglecting
095   key characteristics of their quality and effectiveness for future research. This is also the case for Ali
096
      et al. (2022) and Patra et al. (2022), which describe 26 and 7 datasets, respectively.
097   The second type of survey focuses on analyzing the quality, performance, and limitations of datasets.
098   For instance, Abdali (2024) examines 10 datasets to identify some of these weaknesses and strengths.
099   However, a broad approach is used to outline biases, which fails to detail the specifics of each dataset,
100   leaving researchers uncertain about their individual quality. Another example, Hamed et al. (2023)
101   highlight the limitations of 20 articles using publicly available datasets. While this approach provides
102
      a good overview of literature trends, a grey area remains regarding whether the errors in these 20
      articles stem primarily from methodology or dataset issues. We also find the work of Pelrine et al.
103
      (2021), who evaluate the quality of six datasets, focusing on their potential spurious correlations with
104
      temporal information. Wu & Hooi (2022) expands the analysis of the spurious correlations issue
105   to those induced by event-based collection, dataset merges, and labeling bias, using the Twitter15,

168   3.1   C OLLECTION P ROCESS
169
170   Our data collection process involved an exhaustive search of journal and conference articles to identify
171   relevant datasets. To achieve this, we used the Google Scholar search engine with keywords such
172   as “fake news”, “disinformation”, “misinformation”, “dataset”, “detection”, “survey”, and others
173
      highlighted in Appendix A.1. We focused on articles published between 2016 and 2024. This initial
      phase allowed us to collect 28 datasets.
174
175   We then expanded our selection by rigorously examining the citations in scientific articles related
176   to these initial datasets. Some articles listed available datasets in their literature reviews or surveys,
177   enabling us to incorporate additional data. Through these combined approaches—which are further
178   detailed in the Appendix—we identified 75 publicly accessible datasets, presented in Table 1.
179   For our analyses, we refined our selection to focus exclusively on datasets containing textual claims,
180   defined here as short statements ranging from one to two sentences. Tweets are included in this
181   definition, while lengthier online and social media posts are excluded. We chose to focus on this type
182   of data because statements and claims are more concise than other forms of information, such as
183   OP-ED and news articles, which often include opinions, commentary, and contextual details. This
184   extraneous information can obscure the core claim or statement and introduce noise in the labeling
185
      process, as information can be partly true or false. Therefore, to test label quality, we initially focus
      on claims and statements to increase the reliability of our findings. This approach paves the way for
186
      future research to investigate the labeling of other types of content. Thus, with this filtering criteria,
187
      we reduced our selection to 35 datasets.
188
189
      3.2   C LAIMS DATASETS
190
191   A summary description of each of our datasets can be found in Appendix A.2. Of these datasets, 12
192   consist of claims scraped from fact-checking or reliable websites, another 12 consist of tweets, and
193   the remaining 11 comprise claims drawn from Twitter, the internet, social media, or news websites.
194   There is variation in the topics of these datasets, but most focus on areas with significant societal
195   impact where misinformation is prevalent and potentially harmful. For example, 16 of the datasets
196   focus on health, vaccination, and COVID-19; 3 focus on political issues; 1 on environmental issues,
197
      and the rest covers various subjects, from culture, sport, the economy and so on. Unfortunately, a
      significant limitation of much of this data is the absence of information regarding the date the claim
198
      was made or fact-checked. This can potentially impact the accuracy of labeling, given that certain
199
      claims may have been true or false at the time they were made. In addition, this limitation affects the
200   scope of our temporal leakage analysis. Consequently, scholars, and practitioners alike should be
201   cautious when using these data.

252   Expert-based approach Experts and fact-checkers are a small group of non-partisan professionals
253   from various disciplines who manually verify the veracity of information. The result of these
254   verifications are often published in fact-checking websites such as Politifact or Snopes. The strength
255   of this approach lies in its rigorous review process, ensuring each piece of information is thoroughly
256   evaluated, which leads to consistent reviews across fact-checkers. However, this method is not
257   scalable and is costly (Zhou & Zafarani, 2020). As a result, experts must selectively choose the
258   information they evaluate, which leads to many pieces of information going unchecked and potential
259   biases in the selection of news and information that is evaluated (Lee et al., 2023; Markowitz et al.,
260
      2023; Walker & Gottfried, 2019).
261
262
      Crowd-sourced approach Crowdsourced fact-checking involves enlisting non-expert laypeople to
263
      assess the accuracy of online information. These evaluations are then aggregated to determine the
264   veracity of the content. This approach is advantageous because it is more scalable, and laypeople
265   can respond to misinformation much more quickly than professional fact-checkers (Zhao & Naaman,
266   2023). Additionally, this method has been shown to be effective in reducing the spread of misinfor-
267   mation and to produce veracity ratings similar to those of professional fact-checkers (Allen et al.,
268   2021; Martel et al., 2024). However, crowdsourcing also has its limitations. It can be challenging to
269   filter out evaluations from non-credible users and to ensure a balanced representation of users from
      different partisan backgrounds (Zhou & Zafarani, 2020; Martel et al., 2024).

270
      Source-based approach Source-based approaches to verifying information involve evaluating
271   the domain or author of the content. Information is then rated as accurate if it comes from reliable
272   sources and inaccurate otherwise. This method is more scalable than manual fact-checking, as it
273   consists of evaluating the credibility of the source rather than each individual story. Additionally, this
274   method is proven to be reliable, as experts generally rate news domains similarly (Lin et al., 2023).
275   However, there are notable drawbacks. For instance, individual stories can vary in accuracy even
276   within the same source, and not all content from low-quality outlets is necessarily false or misleading.
277   Additionally, source familiarity significantly influences the perceived trustworthiness of content.
278   Sources that are unfamiliar are often less trusted, which can lead to unfair negative evaluations of
279
      high-quality but lesser-known sources (Pennycook & Rand, 2019; Williams-Ceci et al., 2023)
280
281
      Algorithmic methods Finally, algorithmic methods can also be used to evaluate the veracity of
      content using NLP or other ML techniques (Zhou & Zafarani, 2020). For example, Covid-fact
282
      uses a BERT-based classifier, FaVIQ uses T5-3B, and Rumors uses an approach based on a social
283
      graph. These methods offer significant advantages in scalability, as they can process vast amounts of
284   data quickly and efficiently, making them suitable for large-scale verification tasks. However, their
285   accuracy can be questionable in many cases, ranging from struggles with nuanced or context-specific
286   content (Boukouvalas & Shafer, 2024), issues with transfer and generalization (Huang et al., 2020;
287   Pelrine et al., 2021; 2023), or just generically poor performance (e.g., even state-of-the-art methods
288   often have below 70% accuracy compared to human labels (Zhang & Gao, 2023; Pelrine et al., 2023)).
289   Thus, the quality of algorithmic labels is often dubious.
290
291   Our label unification approach In this survey, we use the original labels from the studies to create
292   a more consistent categorical variable across datasets. This allows us to test the accuracy of the
293   veracity of the labels in each dataset. Specifically, we classify content as true, false, or unknown.
294
      Although this categorical variable is less precise than the detailed scales used to classify some of the
      claims in the data, we adopted this approach to ensure consistency across all datasets. Information that
295
      is mostly true is classified as “true”, while content that is mostly false or hyperpartisan is classified as
296
      “false”. This method aligns with existing research indicating that people respond similarly to content
297   that is false or hyperpartisan (Ross et al., 2021; Pennycook et al., 2020). Finally, claims that could
298   not be verified or were ambiguous, such as those partially true or false, were classified as “unknown”.
299   The percent of true and false claims in each dataset using this coding scheme is shown in Table 2.

305   correlations. Specifically, we trained a random classifier with the 40 most frequent words in each
306   dataset, after removing stop-words. Utilizing scikit-learn, we set a maximum tree depth of 20 and
307
      retained the other default settings. The macro F1 results are shown in Table 3.
308   We particularly flag five datasets for spurious correlations between certain words and labels: IFND,
309   MM-COVID, TruthSeeker2023, CoAID, and Twitter16. For example, consider Truthseeker2023.
310   Nearly all tweets here mentioning politicians are labeled as “false”, with only those containing “Trump”
311   showing more variation (see also Appendix A.3). Obviously, in the real world, tweets mentioning
312   politicians are obviously not exclusively false. Thus, models trained on data like Truthseeker2023
313
      risk generalizing inaccurate results, and doing so on topics extremely sensitive to bias like discussion
      of politicians. Moreover, these findings extend beyond political names. Terms like “michigan”,
314
      “vaccines”, “immunity”, “pfizer”, and so on are consistently labeled as false, while words like
315
      “marijuana”, “wealth”, “terrorism”, “radical” and others are always associated with the true label (see
316   also Figure 2 in the Appendix). Similar patterns are also observed in the other four datasets with
317   highest keyword predictivity. Therefore, we urge caution about training and testing models on these
318   datasets, especially text-focused ones.

      We conducted analyses using large language models (LLMs) to assess the partisan and ideological
411   lean of claims within various datasets. By providing distributions of these leans, we offer an approach
412   for selecting datasets that better align with specific research goals. We also aim to determine if the
413   veracity of a statement is spuriously correlated with its partisan lean, because such an association
414   could in turn lead to models inaccurately assessing truthfulness based on that lean alone. Our findings
415   indicate that a slightly higher proportion of true statements across the datasets are predicted to lean
416   Democratic (12.12%) compared to those leaning Republican (9.5%). In contrast, a larger proportion
417   of false statements are predicted to lean Republican (19.44%) than Democratic (9.36%). However,
418   these proportions vary significantly across datasets, highlighting the importance of careful dataset

      selection. For a full table of results and further analysis, please see Appendix A.4.

422
423   5.1   BASELINE P ERFORMANCE
424
425   We next discuss baselines when using these datasets. Recent works have shown LLMs represent the
426   state-of-the-art for misinformation detection on generic claims Chen & Shu (2023); Pelrine et al.
427   (2023). But despite its importance Pelrine et al. (2021), both human and compute time constraints
428
      can be a barrier to comparing with such strong baselines.
429   Thus, we provide two baselines for future use. We follow the recent method of Tian et al. (2024) and
430   use GPT-4-0125 in two ways: directly prompting the LLM for a veracity evaluation, and providing
431   the LLM a web search tool to first collect evidence before forming a final verdict. We note that
      although these are state-of-the-art systems for zero-shot misinformation detection, they should not be

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      regarded as sole or permanent points of comparison. Stronger LLMs and methods could replace them
433   eventually. Nonetheless, they can provide a useful point of comparison for the near future.
434
435   We note that 8 datasets are excluded from this baseline: 7 tweet datasets that we were unable to
436
      retrieve due to X API limits, and the ESOC Covid-19 dataset because it only has a “refutes” label.
      Results on all others are provided in Table 5. Notably, because these are zero-shot approaches, they
437
      are much less vulnerable to spurious correlations than models trained on each of these datasets,
438
      sometimes leading to dramatically lower but more realistic performance compared to alternatives in
439   the literature (e.g., Twitter15 and Twitter16, where temporal classification achieves over 80% F1).

441   5.2   T HE F LAW IN C URRENT M ETRICS
442
443   When looking at the outputs of the prediction system, we observe cases where the predicted label did
444   not match the ground truth, yet the evidence and reasoning of the system was valid. For instance, in
445   one example on the FEVER dataset, the input claim is “Vietnam is a place” and the prediction said
446   roughly “Vietnam is not just a place, it’s a country!” In another example from LIAR-New, a statement
447   was marked false by PolitiFact because it was in the context of a fake video, but the statement itself
448
      did not mention the video and in isolation would be true. In cases like these (and further examples in
      Appendix A.6), a simple binary or categorical label cannot provide an informative evaluation.
449
450   To determine the prevalence of this phenomenon, two annotators manually evaluated (Appendix A.7)
451   chain-of-thought rationales from the web-search enabled baseline prediction system Tian et al. (2024).

464   detection systems using categorical labels, the predictive accuracy and similar metrics reflect a
465   reasonable lower bound on the performance—but a terrible upper one. We also observe that there is a
466
      large amount of ambiguity and room for interpretation in the examples that are being marked wrong
      by categorical label in these three datasets. Hence:
467
468   1. Categorical metrics cannot be used alone to compare generative and non-generative systems.
469      Although multiple recent works (e.g., Pelrine et al. (2023); Chen & Shu (2023); Wei et al. (2024);
470      Yu et al. (2023)) have highlighted the effectiveness of recent LLMs for misinformation detection,
471      their comparisons with prior approaches may even still be underestimating the dominance of LLMs
472      in this domain.
473   2. Generative systems need many, repeated, and large-margin measurements if the categorical lower
474
         bound alone is to form meaningful comparisons between them.
      3. There is an urgent need for better datasets and better evaluation procedures in this domain that are
475
         suitable for the generative AI era.
476
477   Although to our knowledge not addressed in the context of misinformation detection on claims,
478   challenges in evaluation of generative AI have been broadly documented in other fields (McIntosh
479   et al., 2024; Ahuja et al., 2023; Michel-Villarreal et al., 2023; Basole & Major, 2024). A common
480   approach aimed at solving this is using an LLM for evaluation (Bai et al., 2022; Sun et al., 2023b),
481   with Sun et al. (2023a) noting that LLM-powered evaluation can produce more consistent preference
482
      signals than human annotators. In general, using LLMs for evaluation enables one to leverage
      much richer signals than simple categorical predictions and labels, while avoiding reliance on often
483
      inaccessible human evaluators.
484
485   As an initial step towards higher-fidelity evaluation, we constructed an evaluator based on contra-
      dictions between the explanation generated by a predictive system, and a fact-checking article. In


                                                         9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      particular, we provide GPT-4-0409 both the prediction and the article, and ask it to score contra-
487   dictions from 0 (none) to 10. The exact prompt and other implementation details are provided in
488   Appendix A.8. We chose a score-based approach to avoid forcing a potentially misleading binary in
489   cases where there is a partial contradiction. With this approach, good predictions should have low
490   contradiction against a high quality, professional fact-checking article. We also tested binary and
491   trinary versions of this prompt, described in the Appendix, which yielded nearly identical results.
492
      Specifically, with the oracle-optimal threshold of 3 or less indicating a prediction that is not wrong,
493   and 4 or more indicating one that is wrong, this evaluation agrees 68% of the time with the human
494   labels of wrong and not wrong predictions on the LIAR-New dataset. This is higher than the 60%
495   original human agreement on this dataset (before disagreement resolution described in Appendix A.7),
496   and suggests the method extracts a meaningful but not definitive evaluation signal. We also note,
497   though, that there is more to high quality misinformation detection than just a lack of contradiction.
498   Therefore, we do not suggest using this tool as a primary evaluator. But we provide these results,
499   along with all the data—inputs, predictions, manual labels, fact-checking articles, and evaluator
500   outputs—on our GitHub, as a potential springboard for stronger evaluation methods in future research.

503   6    L IMITATIONS
504
505   First, we note that while to our knowledge this represents the largest and most comprehensive survey
506   of datasets in this domain, there are certainly many other datasets in existence and it is probable
507   that some were not included. There is also a steady stream of new datasets being created. In the
508   near future, we plan to collect external feedback and update our survey to maintain and expand the
509   comprehensiveness of our study.
510   We also note that our unified label schema simplifies some labels that might have meaningful
511   information, for example, gradations of veracity instead of binary true/false. Some granularity has
512   been traded for the ability to establish a unified schema across all the claims datasets. When using
513   these datasets, we advise careful consideration of the optimal labels to apply.
514
      As discussed previously, additional work is needed in evaluation, both to confirm that the observed
515
      validity issues with metrics like accuracy are widespread (as we hypothesize) and to create strong,
516   thoroughly tested alternatives. We also note that the baselines we have provided use old evaluation
517   procedures on LLM-based predictors. This can be flawed both for the reasons discussed in Section 5.2,
518   and potentially also because a substantial proportion of the data could be within the LLM training
519   data. Pelrine et al. (2023) indicates LLM-based methods offer the strongest performance even beyond
520   their knowledge cutoffs, and using web search to actually provide evidence can mitigate this to some
521   degree. But nonetheless, these baselines should be viewed carefully and with due attention to both
522   their strengths and limitations, and future work to establish more universal baselines—as well as
523   datasets and evaluation methods that enable them—would be very valuable.
524   Lastly, although we discussed multiple key aspects of misinformation detection datasets, there are still
525   more that are worth considering. For example, much of our analysis focuses on claims datasets, which
526   are unquestionably important but by no means the entirety of valuable data in the field. Similarly,
527   although dataset quality in terms of labels and spurious correlations is critical, there can be other
528   important considerations like ambiguous statements (Pelrine et al., 2023). We aim to address some of
529   these in future work.
530
531
532   7    C ONCLUSION
533
534   High quality data is essential for realistic results and rapid progress in the field. In this work, we have
535   provided a guide to misinformation detection datasets aiming at both quantity and quality. It also
536   highlights limitations of existing datasets and evaluation approaches, which may have uncertain labels,
537   spurious correlations, and misleading results. We hope that on the one hand this work can provide a
538   roadmap for future methods research that needs to select datasets and evaluation approaches, and on
539   the other, provide the foundational understanding and call to action to improve the misinformation
      detection dataset landscape.

813
814
      For the dataset search, all of the following set of words, as well as the ones presented in Section 3.1,
815   were used in Google Scholar: “fake news”, “false news”, “fake news dataset”, “false news dataset”,
816   “fake news database”, “false news database”, “misinformation dataset”, “misinformation database”,
817   “misinformation detection”, “misinformation survey”, “disinformation dataset”, “disinformation
818   database”, “disinformation detection”, “disinformation survey”, “fact check dataset”, “fact check
819   database”, “benchmark for fake news detection”, “benchmark dataset for fake news”, “misinformation
820   data”, “dataset for evidence-based fact-checking”, “fact-checking corpus”, “fact verification corpus”,
821   and “misinformation detection review”. In addition to these terms, and as mentioned previously, a
822   year filter was used using the advanced search. Only articles dated between 2016 and 2024 were
823
      included.
824   Once the initial datasets were identified, we then expanded our selection by (1) identifying the most
825   frequently cited papers related to these datasets (based on the number of citations in Google Scholar)
826   and (2) carefully reviewing these papers to uncover additional dataset. This review process primarily
827   focused on analyzing the articles’ literature reviews and reference lists to identify datasets that were
828   mentioned and could be pertinent to our research. For instance, according to Google Scholar, the
829
      article by Shu et al. (2020), which introduces the FakeNewsNet dataset, has been cited 1,190 times,
      ranking it among the top four most frequently cited articles that we collected. Based on this, we
830
      proceeded to review Section 2 of the article, titled Background and Related Work. In this section,
831
      the authors mention six existing datasets for misinformation detection: BuzzFeedNews, LIAR, BS
832   Detector, Credbank, BuzzFace, and FacebookHoax. If we had not already gathered these datasets
833   during our initial keyword search on Google Scholar, we collected them at this stage. We also
834   maintained the same publication year criterion, considering only datasets published between 2016
835   and 2024. Consequently, Credbank, which was published in 2015 in ICWSM’15, was excluded. BS
836   Detector was no longer publicly available.

       1, 2017, and November 1, 2017 from the fact-checking website Snopes. The rumors cover various
       topics, including politics, fraud, fauxtography, crime, and science. Each claim is also associated with
977
       tweets, and the veracity labels are as follows: true, mostly true, mixture, mostly false, false, unproven.
978
979    Snopes Fact-news (Shekhar, 2020): This dataset is scraped from the fact-checking website Snopes.
980    It contains 4,550 claims, all associated with veracity labels, the origin of the claim, a summary of this
981    origin, and short descriptions of what is true and what is false. The labels are the same as RUMORS,
982
       namely true, mostly true, mixture, mostly false, false, unproven.
983    TruthSeeker2023 (Dadkhah et al., 2023): TruthSeeker2023 is a dataset of 180,000 coded claims
984    from 2009 to 2022. To collect them, the authors initially crawled 1,400 claims and their ground-truth
985    labels from Politifact. Then, keywords from these claims were used to collect associated tweets,
986    which crowdworkers verified for accuracy. These tweets were labeled based on their corresponding
987    claims from Politifact. TruthSeeker2023 includes two label types: a five-way label (Unknown, Mostly
988
       True, True, False, Mostly False) and a three-way label (Unknown, True, False).
989    Twitter15 (Ma et al., 2017) : Twitter15 contains 1,490 tweets. To identify fake news, two rumor
990    tracking websites, Snopes and Emergent, were used. Tweets related to these fake news stories were
991    then scraped from Twitter using keywords, and their matches were cross-checked by three researchers.
992    Real news tweets was also collected from Twitter via Twitter’s free data stream. It’s important to note
993    that this is not the original dataset. The original (Liu et al., 2015) has been re-used by the authors of
994
       this new database, who have kept the same name while modifying only the labels. The veracity labels
       are “true”, “false” and “non-rumor”. To classify them, Ma et al. (2017) has labeled them according to
995
       whether or not the author denies the rumor.
996
997    Twitter16 (Ma et al., 2017): Twitter16 is a dataset containing 818 tweets. Like Twitter15, Twitter16
998    was reproduced by Ma et al. (2017). For the original dataset (Ma et al., 2016), the authors followed
999    the same data collection procedure as for the original Twitter15, but focused solely on the collection
1000
       of fake news using Snopes. Ma et al. (2017) have modified the labels, which are true, false, unverified,
       and non-rumor.
1001
1002   Verite (Papadopoulos et al., 2024): VERITE is a dataset containing 1,001 claims and associated
1003   images. The data were collected from Snopes and Reuters from January 2001 to January 2023. The
1004   topics covered are diverse, including politics, culture, entertainment, business, sports, environment,
1005   religion, and more. The labels, derived from fact-checking agencies, are coded into three categories:
1006
       true, out-of-context, and miscaptioned.
1007   WICO (Pogorelov et al., 2021): WICO is a dataset dedicated to COVID-19. It includes 364,325
1008   claims. These claims were collected via the Twitter API from January 17, 2020, to June 30, 2021.
1009   Approximately 10,000 tweets are manually annotated with the following labels: 5G conspiracy, other
1010   conspiracy, non-conspiracy, and undecidable.
1011   X-Fact (Gupta & Srikumar, 2021): X-FACT is a dataset of 31,189 short statements scraped from 85
1012   fact-checking websites. Covering various topics, the data are available in 25 languages, including
1013   Arabic, Bengali, French, Hindi, Indonesian, Italian, Spanish, Polish, and Portuguese. The veracity
1014   labels indicate a decreasing level of truthfulness: true, mostly true, partly true, mostly false, false,
1015   unverifiable, and other.

       A.4    S UPPLEMENT ON P OLITICAL L EANING
1081
1082   The meaning of ideology and its dimensions varies across countries and regions Caughey et al. (2019);
1083   Bauer et al. (2017); Lo et al. (2014). However, most datasets, including the most popular ones on
1084   claims, focus on statements made in the U.S. or pertaining to the American context (see Table 1).
1085   Given this and the polarization of the American political landscape, we use a partisanship scale that
1086   classifies statements as either leaning Republican, leaning Democratic, leaning neither Republican
1087   nor Democratic or not political. This allows us to identify the proportion of true and false claims in
1088
       each dataset that fall within these four categories. More specifically, we use the following prompt on
       gpt-4-turbo-0409:
1089
1090   Statement: {{Statement to analyze}}
1091
1092   Your task is to analyze the partisan lean of different statements. Think step-by-step.
           First, indicate whether the statement is more favorable to Republicans, more
1093
           favorable to Democrats, neither more favorable to Republicans or Democrats or not
1094       political. For instance, if a statement supports a position commonly associated
1095       with the Republican Party, it might be more favorable to Republicans. Similarly, if
1096        a statement highlights a negative characteristic of a Democratic candidate/
1097       political figure or a positive characteristic of a Republican candidate/political
           figure it might also be more favorable to Republicans.
1098
1099   After providing all of your analysis steps, summarize your analysis and state: "
1100       Republican Lean; Partisan Lean: (A)" if you think the statement is more favorable
1101       to Republicans, or "Democratic Lean; Partisan Lean: (B)" if you think the statement
1102        is more favorable to Democrats, or "Neither Republican or Democratic Lean;
           Partisan Lean: (C)" if you think the political statement is not more favorable to
1103
           Republicans or Democrats, or "Non Political; Partisan Lean: (D)" if you think the
1104       statement is not political. You should begin your summary with the phrase "Summary
1105       :"
1106
1107   Analyzing the partisan lean of statements is crucial for several reasons. First, providing distributions
1108   of the partisan lean enables researchers to select datasets that better align with their specific research
1109   goals. For instance, researchers interested in analyzing a balance of Democrat—and Republican—
1110   leaning misinformation can choose datasets accordingly. This targeted selection improves the
1111   relevance and precision of their studies. Second, if the veracity of a statement correlates with its
1112   partisan lean, models may inaccurately assess the statement’s veracity based solely on that lean.
1113   Table 8 summarizes our findings. Overall, we observe that a slightly higher proportion of true
1114   statements across the datasets are predicted to lean Democratic (12.12%) compared to those leaning
1115   Republican (9.5%). In contrast, a greater proportion of false statements are predicted to lean
1116   Republican (19.44%) than Democratic (9.36%). However, these proportions vary significantly across
1117   datasets. For instance, this partisan bias appears to be more pronounced in some of the most commonly
1118   used political datasets, such as LIAR and Twitter 15/16. However, in some datasets—particularly
1119
       those related to vaccines and COVID-19 (e.g., AntiVax, Check-COVID, MM-COVID)—a larger
       proportion of statements are classified as neither Democratic nor Republican leaning or as non-
1120
       political. This is understandable, as anti-vaccine attitudes often emerge from both sides of the
1121
       political spectrum, especially in the earlier phase of the pandemic (Roberts et al., 2022). Finally,
1122   we note that statements that are not explicitly related to politics tend to be more true on average
1123   (57.51% vs. 48.28%). This suggests a potential bias on models trained with these data, where political
1124   statements may be assumed to be more likely false.
1125
       Finally, we note our measurement approach does not account for the context surrounding a claim, it
1126
       offers a prediction of the partisan lean based solely on the claim itself. Without context, subtleties
1127
       such as veracity, sarcasm, or nuance might be missed, potentially affecting the predictions. Future
1128   research should explore how incorporating additional context might enhance the accuracy and clarity
1129   of these predictions. One approach could be to use retrieval-augmented generation by integrating
1130   external information from reliable sources for context (Kortukov et al., 2024). Without this added
1131   information, models could make incorrect predictions because of the limited context of certain claims.
1132   It is thus crucial not only to consider the distribution of the partisan lean when using datasets, but
1133   also to recognize that predictive models may lack information on the broader political context when
       evaluating the veracity of claims.

1150   A.5          I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS OF GPT-4 WITH W EB S EARCH P REDICTIVE S YSTEM
1151
1152   We implement our web-search predictive system by combining a state-of-the-art “main agent” LLM
1153   (OpenAI gpt-4-turbo-0409) with a less powerful but more efficient and cost-effective “search agent”
1154   LLM (Cohere command-r). We provide the search agent access to the internet through a Retrieval-
1155
       Augmented Generation pipeline (RAG, implemented using the Cohere search connector5 .) Specif-
       ically, the Cohere search connector applies multiple layers of filtering and reranking to efficiently
1156
       condense a large number of sources from the web into a succinct response to the query from the main
1157
       agent. Before any filtering was applied, the total number of tokens retrieved is usually in the range of
1158   hundred of thousands of tokens for every single example in the dataset. It would be prohibitively
1159   expensive and inefficient if all these sources need to be parsed using the gpt-4-turbo main agent.
1160   The summary that the search agent produces, which usually consists of fewer than 200 tokens, is
1161   substantially more efficient for the main agent to process while retaining most of the relevant details
1162   about the statement.
1163
1164                • Main agent analyzes statement (chain of thought) and proposes queries, if any, to the search
1165                  agent.
1166                • Search agent:
1167                    – Find relevant documents via open web search. (≥ 100K tokens)
1168                    – Apply re-ranking and filtering. (∼ 50K tokens)
1169
                        – Generate condensed response to query. (∼ 200 tokens)
1170
1171
                    • Main agent analyzes evidences from the search agent. Invoke search agent multiple times as
                      needed.
1172
1173                • Main agent summarizes evidences and draw conclusion.
1174
       For further discussion of how this works, please refer to Tian et al. (2024).

1186   gone up every month of the Biden presidency and just hit another 40-year high” was rated as mostly
