011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Single-cell foundation models such as scGPT represent a significant advancement
014            in single-cell omics, with an ability to achieve state-of-the-art performance on var-
015            ious downstream biological tasks. However, these models are inherently limited
016
               in that a vast amount of information in biology exists as text, which they are un-
               able to leverage. There have therefore been several recent works that propose the
017
               use of LLMs as an alternative to single-cell foundation models, achieving com-
018
               petitive results. However, there is little understanding of what factors drive this
019            performance, along with a strong focus on using LLMs as an alternative, rather
020            than complementary approach to single-cell foundation models. In this study, we
021            therefore investigate what biological insights contribute toward the performance
022            of LLMs when applied to single-cell data, and introduce scMPT; a model which
023            leverages synergies between scGPT, and single-cell representations from LLMs
024            that capture these insights. scMPT demonstrates stronger, more consistent perfor-
025            mance than either of its component models, which frequently have large perfor-
026            mance gaps between each other across datasets.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Single-cell foundation models, such as scGPT (Cui et al., 2024), have seen a surge in recent interest
032   due to their ability to be adapted to achieve state-of-the-art performance on a variety of biological
033
      tasks. However, these models have inherent limitations. A vast amount of knowledge in the field
      of biology is represented as text, but these single-cell foundation models are trained only on gene
034
      expression data, and have no way to use this information. There has therefore been significant
035
      interest in applying large language models (LLMs) to single-cell transcriptomics, as many of these
036   models have a large amount of pretrained knowledge which encompasses this knowledge of biology.
037   LLMs could potentially leverage this knowledge to drive improvements in important tasks in single-
038   cell analysis. Either as an alternative approach, circumventing the need to curate massive amounts
039   of data to train new single-cell foundation models, or as a complementary approach, merging the
040   knowledge and capabilities of LLMs and single-cell foundation models to improve performance
041   over either.
042   A popular approach for enabling LLMs to work with single-cell data is converting this data to simple
043   text sequences (i.e. cell sentences), which are encoded to generate representations that encapsulate
044   the pre-trained knowledge of these models. This method has yielded promising results that are
045   competitive with dedicated foundation models on certain single-cell analysis tasks such as cell type
046   classification (Chen & Zou, 2023) (Choi et al., 2024) (Levine et al., 2023) (Fang et al., 2024). How-
047   ever, what pre-trained knowledge is being captured, as well as how to leverage synergies between
048   this knowledge, and representations generated by single-cell foundation models remains largely
049   unexplored. These questions are necessary to understand how LLMs can meaningfully improve
050
      single-cell analysis and address the shortcomings of single-cell foundation models.
051   In this work, we therefore use interpretability methods along with an ablation study to elucidate
052   what pre-trained knowledge of biology LLMs leverage when applied to single-cell analysis. We then
053   explore how these models can be used to complement single-cell foundation models in a manner that
      leverages synergies between representations to improve performance. Our key contributions are:

      1. Despite scGPT being a state of the art foundation model, we find that fusion with LLMs can
055   improve upon its performance, indicating that cell representations derived from text and single-cell

      2. We find LLMs interpret and represent cell sentences in a way that leverages biological insight,

      2.1   L ARGE L ANGUAGE M ODELS
065
066   LLMs have received much attention due to their versatility and strong performance across a variety
067   of domains and tasks. They have been shown to perform well on classification, question-answering,
068   fact retrieval, and more, even without fine-tuning (Gallegos et al., 2024). These models, typically
069   based on the Transformer architecture (Vaswani, 2017), consist of millions or even billions of train-
070   able parameters, and are pre-trained with vast amounts of language data which often encompasses
071   many domains, facilitating this versatility (Zhang et al., 2024a).
072   LLMs are often used to generate text in an autoregressive fashion, or to generate representations of
073   text in the form of embeddings that can be used for a variety of downstream tasks. However, models
074   used for each of these tasks are generally quite different, with LLMs designed for text generation typ-
075   ically employing a different type of architecture than text embedding models (Zhang et al., 2024a).
076   We will therefore study each type of model separately.
077
078   2.2   S INGLE -C ELL F OUNDATION M ODELS
079
080   Inspired by the success of LLMs, single-cell foundation models such as scGPT have been developed
081   that display broad capabilities across many biological tasks such as cell type annotation and multi-
082   batch integration (Chen & Zou, 2023) (Cui et al., 2024). scGPT is, like most LLMs, based on the
083   transformer architecture. Key to its development was curating and pre-training on a massive amount
084   of single-cell data, specifically from over 33 million cells from CELLxGENE (Cui et al., 2024)
085   (Megill et al., 2021).
086
087   2.3   A PPLYING L ARGE L ANGUAGE M ODELS TO S INGLE -C ELL A NALYSIS
088
089   To enable large language models to work with single-cell data, existing studies generally represent
090   this data as text. Perhaps the most common representation used, which we will focus on in this study,
091   is the “cell sentence”; a textual sequence which lists gene names in descending order of expression
092   level for a given cell. For example, ”A cell with genes ranked by expression: RAB3B MT-CO1
093
      CHN1 HNRNPA1P40 SYT1.....”. The Cell2Sentence paper demonstrated that conversion to this
      representation incurs minimal information loss. This was accomplished through training a linear
094
      model to accurately predict gene expression from gene rank, and motivates our focus on this method
095
      (Levine et al., 2023). Studies that use LLMs with cell sentences for single-cell analysis broadly fit
096   into two categories; those that use generative models, and those that use embedding models.
097
098   Studies that explore the use of generative models include Cell2Sentence (Levine et al., 2023), and
099
      “How do Large Language Models understand Genes and Cells” by Fang et al. (2024). The results
      presented in the latter fell short of scGPT on tasks such as cell type annotation, despite using cell
100
      sentences to fine tune large LLMs with up to 13 billion parameters; a process which is quite compu-
101
      tationally expensive. While Cell2Sentence performance is much more competitive with single-cell
102   foundation models such as scGPT, this method requires curating large single-cell datasets for a fine-
103   tuning process which involves multiple stages and is computationally expensive. However, ideally
104   this would not be necessary when working with LLMs due to the knowledge of biology they obtain
105   during pre-training, enabling ”off-the-shelf” usage. Although not the primary focus of this work,
106   the scELMO paper by Liu et al. (2023) evaluates the zero-shot cell type annotation performance of
107   GPT-4 when using cell sentences, and reports that the method is completely ineffective, yielding an
      accuracy of 0 % on all datasets tested.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      Studies that use embedding models include GenePT (specifically, the GenePT-s approach) (Chen &
109   Zou, 2023), and CELLama (Choi et al., 2024). Both papers reported performance that was competi-
110   tive with scGPT on tasks such as cell type classification in a zero-shot setting. However, a limitation
111   of these works is that they provide little justification for their selection of embedding models, despite
112   these models often varying widely in performance on different tasks (Muennighoff et al., 2022). The
113   focus of these works is also primarily on how LLMs can be used as an alternative to single-cell foun-
114   dation models, rather than in a complementary fashion. The GenePT paper does notably experiment
115   with an ensemble approach that aggregates the nearest neighbours from GenePT-s, scGPT, as well
116   as their GenePT-w method to make a final cell type prediction (Chen & Zou, 2023). However, fusion
117
      at such a late stage ignores possible synergies between different modalities (Steyaert et al., 2023).

121   3.1   DATA C OLLECTION AND T RANSFORMATION
122
123   We focus our experiments on the datasets that were used to evaluate the cell type classification and
124   clustering performance of GenePT (Chen & Zou, 2023), as well as the subsample of the Tabula
125
      Sapiens dataset (Consortium* et al., 2022) used to evaluate CELLama (Choi et al., 2024). For each
      dataset, we use the same train/test split as each of these respective works to evaluate performance
126
      on downstream tasks. Cells were represented using cell sentences following the same approach
127
      as GenePT-s (Chen & Zou, 2023), where gene names are listed in descending order of expression
128   level, omitting genes with zero counts. These cell sentences are then passed to text encoders to
129   generate cell embeddings, or to generative LLMs for cell type classification through autoregressive
130   generation.
131
132   3.2   C ELL E MBEDDING A PPROACHES
133
134   In general, text encoders can vary greatly in performance on tasks such as classification and cluster-
135   ing. To determine what LLM of this type to use for our experiments, we therefore test a variety of
136   pre-trained models selected using MTEB (Muennighoff et al., 2022). We use the same experimen-
137   tal design and metrics as the GenePT paper to evaluate classification and clustering performance
138
      (Chen & Zou, 2023). For example, using a k-nearest neighbours method for zero-shot cell type
      classification. Details can be found in Appendix A.1.
139
140   As another experiment, we train a small multi layer perceptron (MLP) on top of text encoders used
141   to generate cell embeddings as an alternative to the 10-nearest neighbour classifier for cell type
142   classification. This setup has the potential to improve performance with minimal training, but more
143   importantly, the differentiability of the MLP facilitates a wider range of interpretability methods
144
      than k-nearest neighbours. We simply use the default architecture for the scikit-learn library’s MLP
      implementation (Pedregosa et al., 2011), and leave the text encoder frozen during training to reduce
145
      computational cost. We report accuracy along with macro-weighted precision, recall, and F1 score
146
      for all datasets.
147
148   Building on this idea of training an MLP on top of the frozen text encoder, we train a multimodal
149   network for cell type classification on top of our top performing text encoder and scGPT, which we
150
      coin scMPT. scMPT combines extracted features from each encoder, and aims to leverage potential
      synergies between representations. Both encoders are notably left frozen during training to reduce
151
      computational cost, and maintain the domain specific knowledge encoded in each model. We report
152
      accuracy along with macro-weighted precision, recall, and F1 score for all datasets. The simple
153   architecture of scMPT takes inspiration from works such as Kwak et al. (2023) and Miller et al.
154   (2020), and is presented in Figure 1 below.

196   first API call, we pass in the cell sentence for the cell we want to classify, along with the list of three

198   then feed this reasoning to GPT-4o in our second API call to generate the final cell type. We adopt a
199   two-stage setup based on previous results that suggest it can improve LLM reasoning when working
200   with multimodal information (Toma et al., 2024) (Zhang et al., 2023). We instruct GPT-4o to pick

204   3.4   I NVESTIGATING WHAT FACTORS C ONTRIBUTE T OWARD LLM S ’ P ERFORMANCE
205
206   To investigate the factors contributing to the competitive performance of LLMs, specifically focus-
207
      ing on text encoders, we first investigate what features from cell sentences are focused on by the
      model when predicting cell type. We employ two interpretability techniques. The first, integrated
208
      gradients, is a gradient based method which has seen significant recent adoption in the biomedi-
209
      cal domain, including for interpreting language models (Sundararajan et al., 2017) (Talebi et al.,
210   2024). The second, Local Interpretable Model Agnostic Explanations (LIME) is a model agnos-
211   tic interpretability method that has also seen significant usage in this domain (Ribeiro et al., 2016)
212   (Wu et al., 2023) (Laatifi et al., 2023). We apply both interpretability techniques on the model that
213   consists of an MLP trained on top of our highest performing frozen text encoder. We focus on this
214   model rather than the setup that uses k-nearest neighbours since the MLP is differentiable, facil-
215   itating the usage of integrated gradients. We limit our analysis to cell types that are specific and
      have clearly defined names. For each dataset and each interpretability method, we calculate feature

      attributions for ten cells of each type (or all cells if there are less than ten in the test set). We then
217   sum attributions across the ten cells of each type, and examine the top ten genes with the highest
218   positive attribution scores to determine what genes influence the model to predict a cell is of a given
219   type.
220
      We also conduct a series of ablation tests to determine whether there are factors contributing to the
221
      performance of text encoders that are not related to their knowledge of biology. We accomplish this
222
      through ablating each major element of biological information in the cell sentences that was derived
223   from the raw single-cell data, namely the gene names, and the ordering of gene names which was
224   based on expression level. We then observe how this impacts clustering and classification perfor-
225   mance. Specifically, for our first ablation test, we replace gene names in cell sentences with unique
226   hashes generated using the SHA-256 algorithm, truncated to ten characters. We then investigate the
227   effect of shuffling the order of gene names within cell sentences. Finally, we investigate the effect
228   of applying both of these ablations.

231   4     R ESULTS
232
233
234   Previous work on using LLMs with cell sentences has not considered the large variety of text en-
235   coders available which vary substantially in performance, and has not been able to successfully use
236
      generative LLMs with cell sentences in a zero-shot setting. We therefore address each of these major
      gaps before proceeding with our main experiments. We then investigate what biological insights,
237
      and other factors, contribute to the performance of LLMs in single-cell analysis through the use of
238
      interpretability and ablation tests. Finally, we introduce scMPT, which leverages synergies between
239   embeddings generated by LLMs and scGPT. We also introduce an alternative fusion method which
240   uses GPT-4o to complement scGPT.

243   4.1   E NCODERS USED IN P REVIOUS W ORK ARE O UTPERFORMED BY E MBER -V1

      To select an encoder for our main experiments, we investigate the performance of different pre-
246   trained LLMs for generating cell embeddings from cell sentences.
247
248   We first compare the cell type classification and clustering performance of all encoders of potential
249
      interest on the Aorta dataset (Li et al., 2020). Specifically, we compare the performance of six en-
      coders selected for their performance on the MTEB benchmark against all-MiniLM-L12-v2 (hug,
250
      b) and OpenAI ada-002 (OpenAI), text encoders which previous work has focused on for generating
251
      cell embeddings. We also compare against OpenAI text-embedding-3-large (OpenAI), and scGPT
252   (Cui et al., 2024). Results are presented in Table 1. We observe that ada-002 and all-MiniLM-
253   L12-v2 were both outperformed by several of the encoders selected from MTEB on both cell type
254   classification and clustering. Ember-V1 performed particularly well, outperforming both of these
255   encoders by a wide margin and closing much of the gap between text encoder performance and
256   scGPT performance on this dataset. We also observe that text-embedding-3-large performed signif-
257   icantly worse than ada-002, despite it being a newer text embedding model from OpenAI designed
258   to improve performance (OpenAI).
259   We then compare Ember-V1 against ada-002 and allMiniLM-L12-V2 on all other datasets col-
260   lected. This includes the MS (Schirmer et al., 2019), Artery (Alsaigh et al., 2022), Bones (Chou
261   et al., 2020), Myeloid (Cheng et al., 2021), Pancreas(Luecken et al., 2022), and subsampled Tabula
262   Sapiens (Consortium* et al., 2022) datasets. Results for classification and clustering performance
263   are reported in Tables 9,10,11,12,13, and 14 (see Appendix A.1). Overall, Ember-V1 outperforms
264   allMiniLM-L12-V2 on every metric on every dataset. The improvement in performance over ada-
265   002 is more modest, with improved clustering and classification performance on 5/7 datasets tested
266
      overall. However, ada-002 is not an open source model, which makes many interpretability methods
      challenging or impossible to use.
267
268   Ultimately, we find that switching pre-trained text encoders to Ember-V1 can lead to improved
269   performance over previous methods to generate cell embeddings from cell sentences, motivating us
      to select this encoder for our main experiments.

328   To determine what factors contribute toward the strong performance of language models, and in
329   particular text encoders, we conduct a series of interpretability and ablation tests. We focus on text
330   encoders as this form of LLM has proven to be truly competitive with scGPT on certain single-cell
331   analysis tasks, and models in this class tend to be small enough that a wider range of interpretability
332   methods are computationally feasible.
333   The interpretability methods we use are integrated gradients, and LIME. For this analysis we focus
334   on the Pancreas and Aorta datasets, given the strong performance of Ember-V1 on them. We apply
335   both interpretability methods to a model consisting of an MLP trained on top of a frozen Ember-
336   V1 encoder to determine what parts of cell sentences contribute toward the prediction of certain
337   cell types. Specifically, through calculating attribution scores for different gene names, which show
338   how much those gene names contributed toward predicting a cell was of a given type. Early in this
339
      analysis, we noticed that several of the genes corresponding to top attribution scores were marker
      genes. We therefore used PanglaoDB (Franzén et al., 2019) to investigate what marker genes were
340
      represented in the lists of top attribution scores for each cell type, calculated through aggregating
341
      results from multiple cells of a given type for a more global level of insight. Results are presented in
342   table 3 and 4. For each cell type in the Pancreas dataset, both methods had multiple markers within
343   the top ten genes with the highest attribution scores. There was also at least one and often multiple
344   markers highlighted by both interpretability methods for each cell type, with this agreement more
345   strongly indicating these marker genes were focused on by the model. For the Aorta dataset, LIME
346   once again had multiple markers within the top ten most important genes for each cell type. While
347   integrated gradients had less, it is notable that the markers highlighted by integrated gradients were
348   almost an exact subset of the markers highlighted by LIME, suggesting these markers were focused
349   on by the model. Ultimately, the representation of marker genes among the genes with the high-
350
      est attribution scores for each cell type, along with the level of agreement between interpretability
      methods indicates that the model focuses on marker genes when predicting cell type, contributing to
351
      its performance being competitive with scGPT.

473   5    D ISCUSSION
474
475   LLMs have shown great potential in single-cell analysis, often performing competitively with ded-
476   icated state of the art foundation models. In this study, after finding simple methods of improving
477   this performance, we obtain an understanding of the biological insight and other factors contributing
478   to it, and finally explore how LLMs can better be used to complement rather than compete with
479   single-cell foundation models, culminating in our introduction of scMPT.
480   We find that simply switching the pre-trained encoder used to Ember-V1 can yield significant im-
481   provements over the previously proposed GenePT-s and CELLama methods, illustrating the impor-
482   tance of testing different encoders. For generative methods, it was interesting to see that restricting
483   the output space of GPT-4o by providing a list of possible cell types improved performance so
484   dramatically compared to results from prior work. This shows that large generative LLMs can be
485   grounded effectively with cell sentences, potentially opening up new possibilities for how these
      LLMs like GPT-4o can be used in single-cell analysis.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      Ember-V1’s focus on marker genes when predicting cell type was intriguing, as focusing on markers
487   is a common approach for both automatic and manual cell type annotation (Clarke et al., 2021). It
488   is therefore highly encouraging that this is a factor contributing towards the model’s predictions,
489   and ultimately its performance. One important limitation of our analysis, however, is that integrated
490   gradients and LIME are local interpretability methods. While aggregating attribution scores across
491   many examples can help get a more global understanding of the model’s behaviour, and has been
492   used in previous work (Talebi et al., 2024), this method is still limited. We therefore take inspiration
493   from the analysis of Liétard et al. (2021) to get a more global understanding of Ember-V1 and its
494   knowledge of marker genes. For each dataset, we compute the cosine similarity between the top
495
      marker gene name for each cell type from PanglaoDB (Franzén et al., 2019) and other marker genes
      from the same cell, and compare this to the cosine similarity between these top markers and marker
496
      genes of different cells, averaging results over all cell types. As shown in table 8, we find that the
497
      cosine similarities to marker genes of the same cell type (Intra-Similarity) are higher, providing
498   further evidence that the model is able to leverage knowledge of marker genes.

      With that said, based on ablation studies, there may be other factors contributing to the performance
509
      of text embedding models that are unrelated to biology. As previously mentioned, ada-002 likely val-
510
      ues syntactic similarity between cell sentences highly. This is based on the large drop in performance
511   when shuffling gene names, but moderately small drop in performance when ablating gene names
512   (where these ablated gene names would notably still be in the same order). Ember-V1 interestingly
513   had a moderate drop in performance both when ablating gene names or shuffling gene order. A po-
514   tential explanation for this is that cells of the same type likely have higher overlap between their top
515   genes expressed (which are the only ones present in the truncated cell sentence) compared to cells of
516   different types; lexical similarity which Ember-V1 may be leveraging. This is also a potential expla-
517   nation for why encoders still maintained some performance even when combining both ablations.
518   We therefore caution that high classification and clustering performance alone does not demonstrate
519
      an encoder has a strong understanding of the underlying biology of cell sentences. With that said,
      we also note that an encoder placing some value on syntactic or lexical similarity is not strictly a
520
      flaw in this context. The highest expressed genes and their rank for different cells of a given type
521
      may indeed be quite similar, which would lead to a high degree of lexical and syntactic similarity.
522   If a text encoder exploited this simple structure to outperform models such as scGPT, this would not
523   necessarily be undesirable.
524
525
      The fusion methods evaluated, which were designed to more effectively leverage synergies between
      modalities than prior work, performed quite well, especially scMPT. On datasets where both modal-
526
      ities yielded similar performance individually, the performance of scMPT was in general modestly
527
      stronger than either individual modality. However, potentially the more valuable result was that on
528   datasets where one modality yielded poor performance, scMPT still performed well. This consis-
529   tency in performance is valuable because performance between the two modalities varied widely on
530   certain datasets. For example, Ember-V1 outperformed scGPT on the Pancreas and Bones datasets,
531   whereas scGPT outperformed Ember-V1 by a wide margin on the Multiple Sclerosis dataset.
532
      One important direction for future research would therefore be to test the performance of text em-
533
      bedding models and scGPT on many more datasets to gain a more comprehensive understanding
534   of when each type of model may perform better. Another potential direction for future research,
535   which could benefit from this, is the development of more advanced fusion methods. For example,
536   large multimodal models have shown strong multimodal classification performance, and would be
537   an interesting direction to explore (Alayrac et al., 2022). Finally, a limitation of this study is that
538   only cell sentences were focused on as textual representations of single-cell data. In future work, it
539   may be valuable to investigate whether there are other textual representations of single-cell data that
      can drive stronger performance.

707   We test a variety of pre-trained text encoders for generating cell embeddings from cell sen-
708   tences, selected using the Massive Text Embedding Benchmark (MTEB) (Muennighoff et al., 2022)
709   (https://huggingface.co/spaces/mteb/leaderboard). Encoders were selected through looking at clas-
710   sification performance on this benchmark, filtering out encoders with over one billion parameters, or
711
      that were proprietary. We then select from the top ten encoders after applying these criteria, taking
      only the top encoder from a given family if several encoders from this family appear, and omit-
712
      ting encoders that were not compatible with the sentence-transformers library. The final encoders
713
      selected from MTEB were stella-en-400M-v5 (hug, a), gte-large-en-v1.5 (Zhang et al., 2024b) (Li
714   et al., 2023), GIST-small-Embedding-v0 (Solatorio, 2024), ember-v1 (Nur & Aliyev, 2023), bge-
715   large-en-v1.5 (Xiao et al., 2023), and mxbai-embed-large-v1 (Li & Li, 2023) (Lee et al., 2024).
716   These encoders were tested against all-MiniLM-L12-v2 (the encoder used for CELLama), Ope-
717   nAI ada-002 (the encoder used for GenePT), and OpenAI’s newest text embedding model text-
718   embedding-3-large.
719
      To assess the cell type classification and clustering performance of various text encoders, we base
720   our experimental design on the GenePT paper. For classification, we apply a 10-nearest neighbour
721   classifier, classifying each cell in the test set of a given dataset based on the labels of its 10-nearest
722   neighbours from the corresponding train set, measured using cosine similarity between cell embed-
723   dings. Accuracy, along with macro-weighted precision, recall and F1 score are then reported. To
724   evaluate cell type clustering, for each encoder on each dataset, we apply k-means clustering on the
725   cell embeddings, setting the number of clusters to match the number of cell types for the given
726   dataset. We then compute the Adjusted Rand Index (ARI) and Adjusted Mutual Information (AMI)
727   to evaluate the concordance between the resultant clusters and the true cell type labels. Additional
