                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
               The two most prominent approaches for building adversary-resilient image clas-
010
               sification models are adversarial training and input transformations. Despite sig-
011            nificant advancements, adversarial training approaches struggle to generalize to
012            unseen attacks, and the effectiveness of input transformations diminishes fast in
013            the face of large perturbations. In general, there is a large space for improving
014            the inherent trade-off between the accuracy and robustness of adversary-resilient
015            models. Painting algorithms, which have not been used in adversarial training
016            pipelines so far, capture core visual elements of images and offer a potential solu-
017            tion to the challenges faced by current defenses. This paper reveals a correlation
018            between the magnitude of perturbations and the granularity of the painting process
019
               required to maximize the classification accuracy. We leverage this correlation in
               the proposed Painter-CLassifier-Decisioner (PCLD) framework, which employs
020
               adversarial training to build an ensemble of classifiers applied to a sequence of
021
               paintings with varying detalization. Benchmarks using provable adaptive attack
022            techniques demonstrate the favorable performance of PCLD compared to state-of-
023            the-art defenses, balancing accuracy and robustness while generalizing to unseen
024            attacks. It extends robustness against substantial perturbations in high-resolution
025            settings across various white-box attack methods under ℓ∞ -norm constraints.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Deep learning models excel in image classification, yet they are still vulnerable to adversarial ma-
031   nipulation (Nguyen et al., 2015; Szegedy et al., 2014; Biggio et al., 2013). Through carefully crafted
032   perturbations, attackers can manipulate the representations learned by models, leading to incorrect
033
      predictions (Goodfellow Ian J., 2014). These vulnerabilities present significant security concerns,
      especially given the integration of these models into critical domains such as autonomous driving,
034
      healthcare, and finance (Dong et al., 2020), emphasizing the disparity between current machine
035
      learning algorithms and human-level capabilities (Geirhos et al., 2018).
036
037   Two core defense approaches have been developed to address these challenges: (1) Adversarial
038   training – incorporation of adversarial examples into the training data (Madry et al., 2018; Papernot
039
      et al., 2018; Zhang et al., 2019; Singh et al., 2024) – is the most successful defense approach to date.
      However, it faces challenges in generalizing to unseen attacks (Bai et al., 2021) and maintaining per-
040
      formance on benign images (Zhang et al., 2019). (2) Defensive transformations – transformation of
041
      the input from adversarial space to benign space by filtering out adversarial perturbations. Some de-
042   fensive transformations provide theoretical guarantees (Cohen et al., 2019; Salman et al., 2020; Nie
043   et al., 2022). Certain defensive transformation techniques have been shown to “obfuscate gradients”,
044   leading to a false sense of adversarial robustness (Buckman et al., 2018; Ma et al., 2018; Guo et al.,
045   2018) while being vulnerable to adaptive attack strategies (Athalye et al., 2018). In addition to these
046   approaches, detection-based defenses (Carlini & Wagner, 2017) offer complementary strategies by
047   identifying adversarial inputs. Overall, there is an inherent trade-off between the robustness and ac-
048   curacy of adversary resilient classifiers (Zhang et al., 2019), with input transformations particularly
049   vulnerable to large perturbations.
050   In this paper, we combine adversarial training with a new defensive transformation technique utiliz-
051   ing stroke-based painting. Our key insight is that painting strokes filter out adversarial perturbations
052   while progressively reconstructing the most important image features. As depicted in Figure 1, early
053   coarse strokes filter out larger perturbations (better robustness) but display only the major elements
      of an image (lower accuracy). Later fine strokes display more image elements (better accuracy)

090
091         1. We introduce Painter-CLassifier-Decisioner (PCLD), a novel framework for adversarial

125   2.1    H IGH - LEVEL OVERVIEW OF THE P ROPOSED A PPROACH
126
127
      In his book, Nicolaı̈des (1941) emphasizes the concept of “Correct Observation” as crucial for artists
      to truly connect with and deeply understand the visual elements they paint. Painting algorithms
128
      (Collomosse & Hall, 2005; Li et al., 2020; Zou et al., 2021; Huang et al., 2019) attempt to capture
129
      the essential visual elements of an image. Intuitively, focusing on the essential visual elements helps
130   to filter out malicious perturbations while maximizing classification accuracy.
131
132   To support this intuition, we utilize a Painter (P) (Huang et al., 2019) as a defense against adver-
133
      sarial attacks. Figure 1 shows a sequence of painting steps t, generated by the painter for a given
      input image, denoted as t = ∞ (left column). Among the inputs, the top is the benign image, fol-
134
      lowed downward by its adversarial variants. While the object is recognizable after a few (30-80)
135
      strokes, the perturbations are visually apparent towards the end of the drawing process, allowing
136   early recognition of key elements for classification and avoiding malicious artifacts.
137
138   Intuitively, increasing the number of strokes (t) to create a more detailed painting not only clarifies
139
      the image but also reconstructs the perturbations. When the attack magnitude (ϵ) is greater, the
      perturbations are reconstructed earlier. This demonstrates a dependence between the Classifier’s
140
      (CL) confidence in the correct class, the painting’s granularity, and the magnitude of the attack. We
141
      empirically validate this phenomenon in Section 4.3.
142
143   In real-world scenarios, however, the magnitude of an attack is unknown to the defender, making
144   it challenging to determine the optimal step at which the painting should be provided to the classi-
145
      fier for decision-making. To navigate this uncertainty, a sequence of CL derived from intermediate
      painting steps is processed by the Decisioner (D). This last component is designed to discover pat-
146
      terns in confidence levels, effectively addressing the challenges posed by varying attack magnitudes
147
      and the need for image clarity.
148
149
      2.2    PAINTER DYNAMICS AND P ROCEDURES
150
151   As the Painter (P) we use a pre-trained model, provided by Huang et al. (2019), which does not
152   require retraining for new datasets. As illustrated in Figure 2 (left), the painting process begins with
153   a target image xi ∼ = Ci,∞ and an empty canvas Ci,0 . The painter decomposes the image into a
154   sequence of strokes ai,0 , ai,1 , ..., ai,n−1 . The next stroke ai,t+1 is derived from xi and the preceding
155   canvas Ci,t . Rendering ai,t on Ci,t generates Ci,t+1 . The painter’s goal is to generate the final
156   canvas Ci,n that is visually close to xi .
157   For the sake of completeness, we briefly overview the employed painting process. Huang et al.
158   (2019) model painting as a Markov Decision Process using a state space S, an action space A, a
159   transition function T (si,t , ai,t ) and a reward function r(si,t , ai,t ). The state space contains three el-
160   ements such that the current state includes: si,t = (Ci,t , xi , t). si,t+1 = T (si,t , ai,t ) is the transition
161   between states that results in a new stroke on the canvas. The action ai,t , determined under a deter-
      ministic action policy, controls the position, shape, color, and transparency of the resulting stroke at

163   target image xi : r(si,t , ai,t ) = Li,t − Li,t+1 , where Li,t is the predicted loss by the discriminator

168   By following this approach, the painter prioritizes regenerating the essential elements of an image

171   2.3   C LASSIFIER
172
173
      The classifier can be any model that produces an inference vector for a given image. The most
      common models for this task are convolutional neural networks (CNNs). As shown in Figure 2
174
      (middle), given a selected resulting canvas Ci,t produced by the painter, the classifier outputs an
175
      inference vector CL(Ci,t ) that contains a likelihood value for each class c. The matrix Vi [t, c]
176   contains the classification confidence for all classes (c) and the selected painting steps (t). The
177   painting process contains many steps corresponding to the strokes generated by the painter. It does
178   not make sense to apply a classifier after each stroke due to high computational costs (inference time
179   reported in Section 4.5, Table 1).
180
181   2.4   D ECISIONER
182
183   We designed PCLD as an ensemble of classifiers that can be applied to different stages of painting
184   the same image. Provided the classification likelihoods (Vi ), the Decisioner (D) is responsible for
185   making the final decision for the predicted class. Consider, for example, the likelihood in Figure 2
186
      (right). During the initial steps, we expect the confidence in the correct class (dog) to increase to
      some point. Later, the adversarial perturbations are reconstructed by the painter, and we expect the
187
      confidence of the correct class to drop in favor of another class (cat in this example). A decisioner
188
      trained on adversarial examples of confidence matrices V should learn to identify such patterns and
189   select the right class. We consider two decisioner architectures: a convolutional network and a fully
190   connected network.

      3.1   S TROKE -BASED D EFENSIVE T RANSFORMATION
196
      Kabilan et al. (2021) were the first to use sketching strokes as a defense in their framework, named
197   VectorDefense. Given an input bitmap image x, VectorDefence uses the Potrace algorithm (Selinger,
198   2003) to transform it into a Scalable Vector Graphics (SVG) image (Ferraiolo et al., 2000) using
199   strokes shaped from simple geometric primitives. The resulting SVG is then rasterized back into
200   bitmap format before it feeds to the classifier. Potrace algorithm consists of 4 steps: (1) trace a given
201   bitmap to paths by generating boundaries that divide black and white regions, (2) approximate each
202   path by an optimal polygon, (3) smooth out each polygon, and (4) optimize the generated curve by
203   connecting successive segments of the Bézier curve if possible. Although VectorDefense showed
204   promise as an effective input transformation defense, it was initially evaluated solely on the MNIST
205
      dataset. In this paper, we extend the VectorDefense testing to a subset of the ImageNet dataset,
      which comprises more complex and high-dimensional images.

      3.2   A DVERSARIAL T RAINING
209
      There have been significant advances in adversarial training over the past decade, beginning with
210   Szegedy et al.(Szegedy et al., 2013) method of training on both adversarial and clean samples.
211   Goodfellow et al.(Goodfellow et al., 2014) introduced an approach to generate adversarial examples
212   by tweaking the input based on the gradient of the loss function. This was extended by Madry et
213   al. (Madry et al., 2018) showing robustness improvements through min-max optimization. Fur-
214   ther advancements included exploration of adversarial training on large datasets like ImageNet and
215   strategies to counter overfitting and label leaking, such as avoiding the use of ground-truth labels
      (Kurakin et al., 2016).

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      Additional methods like Ensemble Adversarial Training (EAT) (Tramèr et al., 2017) and Unsu-
217   pervised Adversarial Training (UAT) (Alayrac et al., 2019) were developed to enhance robustness
218   against diverse adversarial attacks. Diffusion models have also been explored for adversarial training
219   by iteratively removing adversarial noise and training on the resulting images (Wang et al., 2023).
220   Furthermore, Vision Transformers (ViT) have been utilized in adversarial training, benefiting from
221   their global self-attention mechanisms to enhance robustness (Singh et al., 2024).
222
      Recent techniques have addressed the accuracy-robustness trade-off. Randomized Adversarial
223   Training via Taylor Expansion (RATE) (Jin et al., 2023) integrates randomness during training to
224   improve generalization, leveraging insights from both TRADES and AWP. A notable advancement
225   named TRADES (Zhang et al., 2019) defined a theoretically principled approach to balance the be-
226   nign accuracy and robustness of the model to adversarial attacks, setting a benchmark for subsequent
227   research in this domain. TRADES won the NIPS 2018 Adversarial Vision Challenge out of 1995
228   defenses, marking a significant milestone in the field. It is widely used as a competitor for adversar-
229   ial training methods to this day due to its robust theoretical foundations and empirical performance.
230   We empirically compare PCLD with TRADES and with RATE combined with TRADES (denoted
231
      as Rand TRADES).

245   We use a balanced subset of ImageNet, comprising 7000 images of seven animals: elephant, squir-
246
      rel, chicken, spider, dog, butterfly, and cat. The dataset is divided into 70% for training, 10% for
      validation, and the remaining 20% for testing. The original image size of 375x375 pixels was re-
247
      sized to 300x300 pixels and scaled in a range of 0-1. During the training phase, we incorporate
248
      random image rotations of 45◦ and apply horizontal flipping with a probability of 50%. We use
249   the CleverHans (Papernot et al., 2018) and ART (Nicolae et al., 2018) libraries to attack the models
250   under the ℓ∞ norm. The hyperparameters of the PGD attack include a step size of ϵ/Niter , while
251   default values were used for step sizes, random restarts, and confidence (for CW) in all other at-
252   tacks. Computations required GPU cores; we run it on Amazon EC2 (Services, 2023) g5.24xlarge
253   instances, including four 24GB NVIDIA-A10G GPU cores with 384GB memory. For the classifier
254   (CL), we employ a ResNet-18 architecture, pre-trained on ImageNet, and adapted for seven classes.
255   We select 15 distinct painting steps for all experiments: 50, 100, 200, 300, 400, 500, 600, 700, 950,
256   1200, 1700, 2200, 3200, 4200, 5200.

258   4.2   A DAPTIVE ATTACK S TRATEGY
259
260   The painting process is convoluted with multiple iterative steps that cause ”Exploding & Vanishing
261   Gradients” by incorporating ”multiple iterations of neural network evaluation, feeding the output
262   of one computation as the input of the next” (Athalye et al., 2018). Consequently, we extend the
263   Backward Pass Differentiable Approximation (BPDA) + Expectation Over Transformation (EOT)
264   (Athalye et al., 2018) and substitute the painter during the backward pass while keeping the forward
265   pass unchanged.
266   Each of the above paint steps approximated with an auto-encoder (e.g., for step 50, we fine-tune an
267   encoder-decoder to mimic the painter’s output at step 50 given the input image). Specifically, we use
268   pre-trained ResNet-18 classifier, trained on ImageNet, as the encoder. We modify the ResNet18 by
269   keeping the layers up to the third residual block. This results in a feature map of size 256 channels,
      which is used as the encoded representation.

      5      C ONCLUSIONS
522
523
      In this paper, we introduce the Painter-Classifier-Decisioner (PCLD) framework, a novel approach
      designed to reinforce adversarial robustness by leveraging stroke-based painting and adversarial
524
      training. Our empirical results on a subset of the ImageNet dataset show that PCLD achieves su-
525
      perior performance in both accuracy and robustness compared to state-of-the-art adversarial train-
526   ing against adaptive white-box attacks under the ℓ∞ norm. PCLD addresses the generalization
527   challenge. Initially trained on FGSM, PCLD demonstrates enhanced performance against PGD
528   (Madry et al., 2018), C&W (Carlini, 2017) and AutoAttack (Croce & Hein, 2020). Moreover,
529   PCLD maintains relatively high accuracy even as the attack strength increases, demonstrating its
530   robustness across different perturbation levels. PCLD’s ability to perform effectively in complex,
531   high-resolution settings makes it a strong candidate for robust model deployment in real-world ap-
532   plications. However, a notable limitation of our approach is the computational complexity of em-
533   ploying an iterative painting model.
534   Future research can focus on optimizing the painter’s efficiency and extending the evaluation to
535   additional datasets, such as the entire ImageNet dataset, to further validate the framework’s appli-
536   cability. These advancements would pave the way for more resilient models capable of withstand
537   sophisticated threats.
