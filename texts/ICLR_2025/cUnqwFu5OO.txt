009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Mean Field Game (MFG) is a framework utilized to model and approximate the
012            behavior of a large number of agents, and the computation of equilibria in MFG
013            has been a subject of interest. Despite the proposal of methods to approximate the
014            equilibria, algorithms where the sequence of updated policy converges to equilib-
015            rium, specifically those exhibiting last-iterate convergence, have been limited. We
016            propose the use of a simple, proximal-point-type algorithm to compute equilibiria
017            for MFGs. Subsequently, we provide the first last-iterate convergence guaran-
018
               tee under the Lasry–Lions-type monotonicity condition. We further employ the
               Mirror Descent algorithm for the regularized MFG to efficiently approximate the
019
               update rules of the proximal point method for MFGs. We demonstrate that the al-
020
               gorithm can approximate with an accuracy of ε after O(log(1/ε)) iterations. This
021            research offers a tractable approach for large-scale and large-population games.
022

024   1   I NTRODUCTION
025
026   Mean Field Games (MFGs) provide a simple and powerful framework for approximating the be-
027   havior of large populations of interacting agents. Originally formulated by Lasry & Lions (2007);
028   Huang et al. (2006), MFGs model the collective behavior of homogeneous agents in continuous time
029   and state settings using Partial Differential Equations (PDEs) (Cardaliaguet & Hadikhanloo, 2017;
030   Lavigne & Pfeiffer, 2023; Inoue et al., 2023). Subsequently, the formulation of MFGs using Markov
031   Decision Processes (Bertsekas & Shreve, 1978; Puterman, 1994) has enabled the study of discrete-
032   time and discrete-state models (Gomes et al., 2010), broadening the applicability of MFGs to Multi-
033
      Agent Reinforcement Learning (MARL) (Yang et al., 2018). Moreover, it has become possible to
      capture interactions among heterogeneous agents (Gao & Caines, 2017; Caines & Huang, 2019).
034
035
036   The applicability of MFGs to MARL drives research into their
037   computational aspects. Under fairly general assumptions, the
038   problem of finding an equilibrium in MFGs is known to be
039
      PPAD-complete (Yardim et al., 2024). Consequently, it would
      be essential to impose assumptions that allow for the exis-
040
      tence of algorithms capable of efficiently computing an equi-
041
      librium. One of the assumptions is contractivity (Xie et al.,
042   2021; Anahtarci et al., 2023; Yardim et al., 2023). However,
043   it is known that many problems are not contractive in prac-
044   tice (Cui & Koeppl, 2021). One of the more realistic assump-
045   tions is monotonicity (Pérolat et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2023; Figure 1: Overview of Algorithms
046   Yardim & He, 2024), which intuitively implies that as more
047   agents converge to a single state, the reward monotonically decreases. Under the monotonicity as-
048   sumption, Online Mirror Descent (OMD) has been proposed and widely adopted (Pérolat et al.,
049   2022; Cui & Koeppl, 2022; Lauriere et al., 2022; Fabian et al., 2023). OMD, especially when com-
050
      bined with function approximation via deep learning, has enabled the application of MFGs to MARL
      (Yang & Wang, 2021; Zhang et al., 2021; Cui et al., 2022).
051
052   Theoretically, last-iterate convergence (LIC), which ensures that the policy obtained in the final iter-
053   ation converges, is particularly important in deep learning settings due to the constraints imposed by
      neural networks (NN). In NNs, calculating the time-averaged policy like in the celebrated Fictitious


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      Play method (Brown, 1951; Perrin et al., 2020) may be less meaningful due to nonlinearity in the
055   parameter space. These considerations have spurred significant research into developing algorithms
056   that achieve LIC in finite N -player games, as seen in, e.g., Mertikopoulos et al. (2018); Piliouras
057   et al. (2022); Abe et al. (2023; 2024).
058
      Despite its importance, the literature on LIC results in MFG is quite limited. The only exception is
059
      Pérolat et al. (2022), who proved the LIC result for the continuous-time version of OMD without the
060
      quantified rates under the strict monotonicity condition. The aim of this research is to establish an
061   online learning algorithm that can achieve LIC in MFGs under non-strict monotonicity conditions.
062
063   In this paper, we propose a novel proximal-point (PP) type algorithm and prove that it achieves LIC
064
      under the non-strict monotonicity assumption. Furthermore, we demonstrate that the update rule of
      the PP can be approximated efficiently by sequentially using the Regularized Mirror Descent (RMD).
065
      We further show that RMD achieves the approximation with the accuracy of ε within O(log (1/ε))
066
      iterations. Figure 1 summarizes the overview of the algorithms in this paper.
067
068   In summary, the contributions of this paper are as follows:
069
          Contribution

072               LIC for general monotone MFGs (Theorem 4.3).
073           (ii) We prove for the first time that regularized Mirror Descent achieves exponential
074                convergence for monotone MFGs (Theorem 4.4).
075
             (iii) We combine these two algorithms as shown in Figure 1 to develop a tractable
076
                   algorithm that approximates the PP-based method (Algorithm 2).

424
425
      Last-iterate convergence (LIC) results for MFG. Pérolat et al. (2022) showed that Mirror De-
426   scent achieves LIC only under strictly monotone conditions, i.e., if the equality in the Lemma E.2
427   is satisfied only if π = πe. In contrast, our work establishes LIC even in non-strictly monotone
428   scenarios. While the distinction regarding strictness might seem subtle, it is profoundly signifi-
429   cant. Indeed, non-strictly monotone MFGs encompass the fundamental examples of finite-horizon
430   Markov Decision Processes. Moreover, in strictly monotone cases, mean-field equilibria become
431   unique. Consequently, as Zeng et al. (2024) also noted, strictly monotone rewards fail to represent
      MFGs with diverse equilibria.

479
      This paper proposes noble algorithms that can achieve last-iterate convergence under the mono-
480   tonicity condition. The main idea behind the derivation of the main algorithm (Algorithm 2) is to
481   approximate the proximal-point type algorithm (Algorithm 1) using RMD. Theorem 3.1 guarantees
