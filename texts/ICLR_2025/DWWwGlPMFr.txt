009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               Large repositories of image-caption pairs are essential for the development of
               vision-language models. However, these datasets are often extracted from noisy
012
               data scraped from the web, and contain many mislabeled instances. In order
013            to improve the reliability of downstream models, it is important to identify and
014            filter images with incorrect captions. However, beyond filtering based on image-
015            caption embedding similarity, no prior works have proposed other methods to
016            filter noisy multimodal data, or concretely assessed the impact of noisy captioning
017            data on downstream training. In this work, we propose, theoretically justify, and
018
               empirically validate LEM O N, a method to automatically identify label errors in
               image-caption datasets. Our method leverages the multimodal neighborhood of
019
               image-caption pairs in the latent space of contrastively pretrained multimodal
020            models to automatically identify label errors. Through empirical evaluations across
021            eight datasets and ten baselines, we find that LEM O N outperforms the baselines
022            by over 3% in label error detection, and that training on datasets filtered using our
023            method improves downstream captioning performance by 2 BLEU points.

076       In this work, we propose LEM O N– Label Error detection using Multimodal Neighbors – a method
          for multimodal label error detection, which can be applied to image-text pairs in datasets such as
077
          MSCOCO (Lin et al., 2014). While prior techniques utilize unimodal neighbors for label error
078       detection, LEM O N leverages multi-modal neighborhoods derived using contrastively pretrained
079       vision-language models such as Contrastive Language-Image Pretraining (CLIP) (Radford et al.,
080       2021). Specifically, in addition to considering pairwise image-text distances, we also retrieve nearest
081       neighbors in the image and text space as illustrated in Figure 2. This is motivated about rich
082       neighborhood geometry in the joint embedding space of multimodal models (Liang et al., 2022;
083       Schrodi et al., 2024). We then compute distance scores with neighbors in each modality and combine
084
          these into a single score measuring the likelihood of a label error, with the intuition that higher
          discordance (or higher distance) with neighbors indicates a higher chance of label error. We validate
085
          the utility of these scores across eight datasets, including one in a healthcare setting, and compare to
086       over ten baselines.
087
          Our key contributions and findings are as follows:
088
089                • We propose LEM O N, a novel, theoretically justified multimodal method capable of detecting
090                  label errors in large image-caption datasets (Section 3).
091                • We show that LEM O N outperforms all downstream task-unaware baselines for label error
092                  detection in the classification setting, by up to 3.4% AUROC (Section 6.1).
093                • We empirically show that LEM O N outperforms baselines in three out of four captioning
094
                     datasets, by up to 3.9% AUROC (Section 6.1).
095
                   • We demonstrate that LEM O N improves performance on downstream classification and
                     captioning models by filtering out data predicted to be label errors. (Section 6.2).
096
                   • Finally, we verify that the predictions generated by LEM O N are meaningful through a real
097
                     world analysis of LEM O N on existing datasets without known label errors (Section 6.5).

          Label Noise Detection Noisy and incorrect labels (Beyer et al., 2020) in training data may lead
          to decreased or “destabilized” (Northcutt et al., 2021a; Luccioni & Rolnick, 2023) performance on
103
          downstream tasks (Chen et al., 2023; Northcutt et al., 2021b). Two orthogonal approaches can be
104       taken to reduce the adverse effects of such labels: developing methods to learn in the presence of
105       label errors (Cui et al., 2020; Natarajan et al., 2013; Huang et al., 2023), and/or detecting and filtering
106       out instances with label errors (Zhu et al., 2024). In this work, we focus on the latter approach. Prior
107       approaches (Swayamdipta et al., 2020; Bahri et al., 2020; Pleiss et al., 2020; Northcutt et al., 2021a;
          Liang et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2020; Kim et al., 2021) for automatic label error detection include


                                                                                  2

108
      relying on the training dynamics of task-specific downstream models (Swayamdipta et al., 2020) and
109   neighborhood-based strategies (Bahri et al., 2020; Grivas et al., 2020). Some of these techniques
110   are fully supervised (Northcutt et al., 2021a; Chen et al., 2023) or unsupervised (Pleiss et al., 2020;
111   Swayamdipta et al., 2020; Grivas et al., 2020; Bahri et al., 2020), use pre-trained generative models
112   (Gertz et al., 2024) or are fully training-free approaches (Zhu et al., 2022; Liang et al., 2023). Previous
113   approaches for label error detection closest to this work includes deep k-nearest neighbor (deep k-NN)
114   methods using k-NN entropy on vector space embeddings (Bahri et al., 2020; Grivas et al., 2020) and
      SimiFeat (Zhu et al., 2022) which employs a local neighborhood-based voting or ranking for noise
115
      identification. In contrast to these methods, our work enhances label noise detection by harnessing
116   information across multiple data modalities, such as image and text. Finally, though prior works may
117   have utilized the idea of semantic neighborhoods in multimodal data (e.g. for cross-modal retrieval)
118   (Thomas & Kovashka, 2020; 2022), we believe we are the first to extend concepts to the task of label
119   error detection by proposing a novel, theoretically justified score for identifying label errors.
120
121   Contrastive Learning Contrastive learning is a representation learning strategy that contrasts
      positive and negative pairs of data instances (Chen et al., 2020; Misra & Maaten, 2020; Balestriero
122
      et al., 2023) to learn an embedding space. The core idea is to embed similar data points (positive
123   pairs) closer together than dissimilar data points (negative pairs) (Schroff et al., 2015; Sohn, 2016;
124   Oord et al., 2018). In this work, we primarily utilize pre-trained models that use the CLIP loss (where
125   the pre-training objective is predicting which text caption goes is paired with which image) for jointly
126   embedding image and text data (Radford et al., 2021).
127
128   Image Captioning The goal of image captioning is to describe a given image (Fu et al., 2024)
129
      in natural language. Prior approaches for caption generation have included supervised training of
      end-to-end models from scratch (Wang et al., 2022b; Lin et al., 2022; Hu et al., 2023; Xu et al.,
130
      2015; Fu et al., 2024). More recently, vision-language models pretrained on large datasets of noisy
131   image-caption pairs extracted from the web (Li et al., 2022; 2023; Wang et al., 2022a) – such as
132   CC12M (Changpinyo et al., 2021) – have been utilized for captioning. Some of the pretraining tasks
133   include image-text contrastive learning, image-text matching, and/or retrieval (Li et al., 2022), as well
134   as general purpose text generation conditioned on an input image (Wang et al., 2022a). Given that
135   datasets for training such large models are noisy (Kang et al., 2023), several steps have been utilized in
136
      prior work to filter out noisy captions during training. The most common strategy involves computing
      the similarity between representations of the image and caption text using another pretrained model
137
      (e.g., CLIP) prior to training (Kang et al., 2023). Another approach in training the BLIP (Li et al.,
138   2022) model is to synthetically generate noisy captions and train a classifier to distinguish between
139   high quality captions and noisy captions with a cross-entropy loss (Li et al., 2022). To the best of our
140   knowledge, no previous work has conducted a comprehensive comparison of various strategies for
141   label error detection in captioning datasets.
142
143
      Multimodal Neighborhood Methods Previous studies (Li et al., 2021; Thomas & Kovashka,
      2020; 2022; Huang et al., 2024; Liang et al., 2022; Cai et al., 2023) have examined the geometry
144
      of neighborhood spaces in multimodal models, often with the goal of improving representation
145   learning (Huang et al., 2024; Li et al., 2021) or retrieval (Thomas & Kovashka, 2020; 2022). The
146   closest related work is Thomas & Kovashka (2022), where the authors use the semantic neighborhood
147   of multimodal models to identify samples with high semantic diversity using text-based neighbors of
148   neighbors. However, as the objective of their work is different from ours, their proposed discrepancy
149   and diversity scores would not provide a signal for label error in our setting. We further clarify this in
150   Appendix B, and will empirically compare against their discrepancy score as a baseline. Although
      prior works have utilized the idea of multimodal neighbors in other settings, we believe we are the
151
      first to apply it to the setting of label error detection.

      In cifar10 and cifar100, we utilize a dataset collected in prior work (Wei et al., 2021) with

303
      For the four captioning datasets, we devise several ways to inject synthetic noise of prevalence p. The
304   simplest way is to randomly select p fraction (random) of the samples and assign their text modality
305   to be that of another random caption. In datasets where additional metadata is available (mscoco:
306   object category, mmimdb: genre of movie, mimiccxr: disease label), we can randomly swap the
307   caption with that of another sample from the same category (cat). Finally, in all captioning datasets
308   except mimiccxr, we tag each token of each caption with its part-of-speech using SpaCy (Honnibal
309
      & Montani, 2017), and then randomly assign a selected sample’s text modality to be from another
      sample with at least one noun in common (noun). Dataset processing details are also in Appendix D.
310
311   Our motivation for these noise types is to simulate an array of realistic label corruptions that one
312
      might face in the real world. We recognize that the resulting synthetic dataset may not have exact
      noise level p, as e.g. a randomly selected caption may actually be correct for the image, as well as
313
      noise in the base datasets, which we explore in Section 6.5. Unless otherwise stated, results shown in
314   the main paper are for the bolded noise type in Table 1, with 40% synthetic noise. Additional results
315   for other noise types can be found in the appendices.

534   of this uncertainty, in the case of a non-binary target, is an area of future work.
535   Regardless, we believe that our approach is a promising step to automatically detecting and filtering
536   data mislabels at scale. Through experiments on multiple datasets with synthetic and real-world
537   noise, we demonstrated LEM O N’s effectiveness in detecting label errors and its ability to improve
      downstream model performance. Extending such methods to allow for correcting label errors is
538
      another promising area of future work.

900
901    The goal of Thomas & Kovashka (2022) to identify samples with semantic diversity, which is different
902    from our goal of identifying mislabeled examples. As such, their proposed scores (i.e. ΥDIS and
903    ΥDIV ) may not be effective in identifying mislabeled samples. As an example, consider the score
904
       ΥDIS
         Y    , which computes the similarity between the original caption, and the captions of its second-
       degree neighbors in text-space. Given a particular caption, e.g. “This is a plane from the front view”
905
       in Figure 2, it could have second-degree neighbors in text-space that are semantically very similar
906    to this caption (e.g. “A plane facing the viewer”). However, only computing the distance of these
907    captions in text space does not provide any signal for whether the image is correctly paired to the
908    caption. Similarly, the ΥDIV scores also would not necessarily work, as the closeness of neighbors
909    to each other in either modality do not provide a signal for whether the original sample is mislabeled.
910    However, the score from Thomas & Kovashka (2022) that would intuitively provide a signal for
911    mislabeling is ΥDIS
                         X , which computes second-degree neighbors in text space, then examines simi-
912    larity between images. This is essentially the sum over dX (x, xmj ) terms in our Equation (3), but
913    using second-degree neighbors instead of nearest neighbors. In addition, our Equation (3) contains
914    two additional weighting terms (which we show improve label error performance in our ablation
915    experiments). Finally, our proposed score contains the sum of two additional terms, which are not
       explored in Thomas & Kovashka (2022).

1017

1018   mean and standard deviation of the probability associated with the assigned class into a single score7 .
