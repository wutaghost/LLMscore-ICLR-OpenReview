009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            SHAP values – a.k.a. SHapley Additive exPlanations – are a popular local feature-
012            attribution method widely used in interpretable and explainable AI. We tackle the
013            problem of efficiently computing these values. We cover both the model-agnostic
014            (black-box) setting, where one only has query access to the model and also the
015            case of (ensembles of) trees where one has access to the structure of the tree.
016            For both the black-box and the tree setting we propose a two-stage approach for
017            estimating SHAP values.
018            Our algorithm’s first step harnesses recent results showing that many real-world
019            predictors have a spectral bias that allows us to either exactly represent (in the
020            case of ensembles of decision trees), or efficiently approximate them (in the case
021
               of neural networks) using a compact Fourier representation. For the case of trees,
               given access to the tree structure, one can extract the Fourier representation using
022
               a simple recursive algorithm. For the black-box setting, given query access to
023
               the black-box function, we utilize a sparse Fourier approximation algorithm to
024            efficiently extract its compact Fourier approximation.
025
               In the second step of the algorithm, we use the Fourier representation to exactly
026            compute SHAP values. The second step is computationally very cheap because
027            firstly, the representation is compact and secondly, we prove that there exists a
028            closed-form expression for SHAP values for the Fourier basis functions. Fur-
029            thermore, the expression we derive effectively “linearizes” the computation into a
030            simple summation and is amenable to parallelization on multiple cores on a GPU.
031            Since the function approximation (first step) is only done once, it allows us to
032            produce Shapley values in an amortized way. We show speedups compared to
033            relevant baseline methods equal levels of accuracy for both the tree and black-
034
               box settings. Moreover, this approach introduces a reliable and fine-grained con-
               tinuous trade-off between computation and accuracy through the sparsity of the
035
               Fourier approximation, a feature previously unavailable in all black-box methods.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
039
040
      Interpretability of machine learning models is paramount, especially in high-stakes applications
041
      in areas such as medicine, fraud detection, or credit scoring. This is crucial to the extent that in
042   Europe, the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) mandates the legal right to an explanation
043   of algorithmic decisions (Voigt & Von dem Bussche, 2017). Say we are given a predictor/model
044   h : X n → R which maps an input (data) instance x∗ ∈ X n to a prediction h(x∗ ). Instance-wise
045   a.k.a. local feature attribution methods assign “importances” to each of the features x∗i ∈ X of the
046   instance x∗ which quantify how influential that feature was in the model predicting the value h(x∗ ).
047
      A widely used method for deriving attributions (importances) is the notion of SHapley Additive ex-
048   Planations, commonly referred to simply as SHAP values. Originally, the notion of Shapley values
049   was introduced in the seminal work of Shapley (1952) in the context of cooperative game theory.
050   The Shapley value is a mathematically well-founded and “fair” way of distributing a reward among
051   all the members of a group playing a cooperative game and it is computed based on the rewards that
052   would be received for all possible coalitions. The Shapley value is the unique way of distributing
053   the reward that satisfies several reasonable mathematical properties that capture a notion of fairness
      (Shapley, 1952). In the context of machine learning and statistics, the players become features, the


                                                        1

054
      reward is the prediction of the predictor h and the SHAP value is the “contribution” or “influence”
055   of that feature on the prediction. Shapley values are widely used due to their mathematical sound-
056   ness and desirable properties Gromping (2007); Štrumbelj et al. (2009); Owen (2014); Datta et al.
057   (2016); Owen & Prieur (2017); Lundberg & Lee (2017); Lundberg et al. (2020) Aas et al. (2021).
058
059
      Despite their prevalence, computing SHAP values is challenging, as it involves an exponential
      summation, i.e. a summation over exponentially many terms , see Equation 1. This is because
060
      the formula accounts for a feature’s importance in the context of all possible “coalitions” of
061
      other features, and therefore the formula covers all possible subsets of other features. Therefore,
062   approximating them and speeding up the computation has received attention in a variety of settings.
063   SHAP value computation can easily dominate the computation time of industry-level machine
064   learning solutions on datasets with millions or more entries (Yang, 2021). Yang (2021) point out
065   that industrial applications sometimes require hundreds of millions of samples to be explained.
066   Examples include feed ranking, ads targeting, and subscription propensity models. In these
067   modeling pipelines, spending tens of hours in model interpretation becomes a significant bottleneck
068   (Yang, 2021) and one usually needs to resort to multiple cores and parallel computing.
069   Significant work has gone into speeding up the computation of SHAP values for a variety of settings.
070   In the (ensemble of) trees setting, full access to the tree structure is assumed. Yang (2021); Bifet
071   et al. (2022) provide theoretical and practical computational speedups to the classic T REE S HAP
072   (Lundberg et al., 2020). Similar to these results, in this work, we provide significant speedups for
073   the tree setting over previous methods.
074   As opposed to the tree setting, which is a “white box” setting, in the model-agnostic a.k.a black-box
075   setting, we only have query access to the model. Here, our only means of access to the predictor is
076   that we can pick an arbitrary x ∈ X n and query the predictor for its value h(x). The usual approach
077   here is to approximate the exponential sum of the SHAP value computations using stochastic sam-
078   pling (Covert & Lee, 2020; Mitchell et al., 2022a; Lundberg & Lee, 2017). In this setting, Covert
079   & Lee (2020); Mitchell et al. (2022a) provide sampling methods that require fewer queries to the
080   black-box compared to vanilla K ERNEL S HAP (Lundberg & Lee, 2017) for equal approximation ac-
081   curacy. FAST S HAP Jethani et al. (2021), which introduces a method for estimating Shapley values
082
      in a single forward pass using an end-to-end learned explainer neural network model. Our algorithm
      F OURIER S HAP falls into the query-access black-box setting as well.
083
084   However, we take a different approach. We are guided by the key insight that many models used
085   in practice have a “spectral bias”. Yang & Salman (2019); Valle-Perez et al. (2018) provably and
086   experimentally show that fully connected neural networks with binary (zero-one) inputs learn low-
087   degree – and therefore sparse – functions in a basis called the Walsh-Hadamard a.k.a Fourier basis.
088
      It is well known that the Walsh-Hadamard transform (WHT) of an ensemble of T trees of depth
      d is also of degree at most d and moreover, k = O(T 4d )-sparse (Kushilevitz & Mansour, 1993;
089
      Mansour, 1994).
090
091   Our contributions: Guided by the aforementioned insights, we provide an algorithm to compute
092   SHAP values, using the Fourier representation of the tree or black-box model. We first approxi-
093   mate the black-box function by taking its sparse Fourier transform. We theoretically justify, and
094
      show through extensive experiments, that for many real-world models such as fully connected neu-
      ral networks and (ensembles of) trees this representation is accurate. Subsequently, we prove that
095
      SHAP values for a single Fourier basis function admit a closed-form expression not involving an
096
      exponential summation. Therefore, using the Fourier representation we overcome the exponential
097   sum and can utilize compute power effectively to compute SHAP values. Furthermore, the closed-
098   form expression we derive effectively “linearizes” the computation into a simple summation and
099   is amenable to parallelization on multiple cores or a GPU. The Fourier approximation step is only
100   done once, therefore F OURIER S HAP amortizes the cost of computing explanations for many inputs.
101   Subsequently, SHAP computations using the Fourier approximation are orders of magnitude faster
102   compared to other black-box SHAP approximation methods such as K ERNEL S HAP and other vari-
103   ations of it which all involve a computationally expensive optimization. We show speedups over
104   other methods such as D EEP L IFT (Shrikumar et al., 2017) and FAST S HAP (Jethani et al., 2021)
105
      as well. In addition to the speedup, our algorithm enables a reliable continuous trade-off between
      computation and accuracy, controlled in a fine manner by the sparsity of the Fourier approximation.
106
      This was not formerly possible with these two black box methods.

117   this reward among themselves. Shapley (1952) resolved this problem by a deriving a unique value
118   based on “fairness axioms” proposed in his seminal work (Shapley, 1952). The Shapley value of
119   player i ∈ [n] is:

      3.2   S PARSITY OF ENSEMBLES OF DECISION TREES
271
272   In our context, a decision tree is a rooted binary tree, where each non-leaf node corresponds to one
273   of n binary (zero-one) features, and each leaf node has a real number assigned to it. We denote the
274   function a decision tree represents by t : {0, 1}n → R. Let i ∈ [n] denote the feature corresponding
275   to the root, and let tleft : {0, 1}n−1 → R and tright : {0, 1}n−1 → R be the left and right sub-trees,
276   respectively. Then the tree can be represented as:
277                                 1 + (−1)⟨ei ,x⟩             1 − (−1)⟨ei ,x⟩
278
                            t(x) =                  tleft (x) +                 tright (x)                  (2)
                                           2                          2
279   where, ei ∈ {0, 1}n is i’th indicator vector.
280
281
      Thus, the Fourier transform of a decision tree can be computed recursively (Kushilevitz & Mansour,
      1993; Mansour, 1994). The degree of a decision tree function of depth d is d, and if |supp(tleft )| =
282
      kleft and |supp(tright )| = kright , then |supp(t)| ≤ 2(kleft + kright ). As a result, a decision tree
283
      function is k-sparse, where k = O(4d ), although in some cases, when the decision tree is not
284
      balanced or cancellations occur, the Fourier transform can be sparser, i.e., admit a lower k, than the
285
      above upper bound on the sparsity suggests.
286
287   Due to the linearity of the Fourier transform, the Fourier transform of an ensemble of trees, such
288   as those produced by the random forest, cat-boost (Dorogush et al., 2018), and XGBoost (Chen &
289
      Guestrin, 2016) algorithms/libraries, can be computed by taking the average of the Fourier transform
      of each tree. If the random forest model has T trees, then its Fourier transform is k = O(T 4d )-sparse
290
      and of degree d equal to its maximum depth of the constituent trees.

293   4     C OMPUTING SHAP VALUES WITH F OURIER REPRESENTATION OF
294         FUNCTIONS
295
296   In the previous section, we saw that many real-world models trained on tabular/discrete data can be
297   exactly represented (in the case of ensembles of decision trees), or efficiently approximated (in the
298   case of neural networks) using a compact (sparse) Fourier representation. We saw neural networks
299
      have a tendency to learn approximately low-degree, and hence by Proposition 2 sparse, functions.
      This has been attributed in numerous works to the reason why they generalize well and do not
300
      overfit despite their over-parameterized nature (Valle-Perez et al., 2018; Yang & Salman, 2019; Huh
301
      et al., 2022; Durvasula & Liter, 2020; Kalimeris et al., 2019; Neyshabur et al., 2017; Arpit et al.,
302   2017). We also saw that (ensembles) of decision trees, by nature, have sparse Fourier representations
303   (Kushilevitz & Mansour, 1993; Mansour, 1994). More generally, as made formal in Proposition 1
304   and the remarks after, any “simple” function that can be written as a summation of a “few” functions
305   each depending on a “few” of the input variables is sparse and low-degree in the Fourier domain.
306

307

310   more detailed explanation. Next, in our second step presented here, we use the Fourier representation
311   to exactly compute SHAP values.

358

359

360   convergence to make sure the approximation is accurate. The approximation in our method is con-
361   strained to the first step: computing the (approximate) sparse Fourier representation of the black-box.

367   Equation 5. Through this expression, we are able to “linearize” the computation of SHAP values
368   to a summation over the Fourier coefficients and background dataset. This allows us to maximally
369   utilize the parallelization on multiple cores and/or GPUs to speed up the computation significantly.
370   Therefore, in the presence of multiple cores or a GPU, we can get a speedup equal to the level of
371   parallelization, as each core or worker can compute one part of this summation.

533   C ONCLUSIONS
534
535   We illustrated in theory and practice how many black-box functions can be represented or efficiently
536   approximated by a compact Fourier representation. We proved that SHAP values of Fourier basis
537   functions admit a closed form expression, and therefore, we can compute SHAP values efficiently
538   using the compact Fourier representation. Moreover, this closed form expression is amenable to
539   parallelization. These two factors helped us in gaining speedups of 10-10000x over baseline methods
      for the computation of SHAP values.

      B.1   N EURAL NETWORK THEORY AND SIMPLICITY BIASES
762
      With regards to simplicity biases in fully connected neural networks, a substantial amount of re-
763   search has been dedicated to analyzing neural networks in function space. This line of research is
764   dedicated to firstly showing that “infinite-width”, randomly initialized (with Gaussian distribution)
765   neural networks are distributed as Gaussian Processes (GPs) and secondly computing the kernel as-
766   sociated to the GP (Neal, 1996; Williams, 1996; Cho & Saul, 2009; Hazan & Jaakkola, 2015; Lee
767   et al., 2017; Ancona et al., 2018; Daniely, 2017). The kernel associated with the GP is commonly
768   known as the “conjugate kernel” (Daniely, 2017) or the “NN-GP kernel”(Lee et al., 2017). Other
769   works show that in infinite-width neural networks weights after training via SGD do not end up
770   too far from the original (Chizat et al., 2019; Jacot et al., 2018a; Du et al., 2018; Allen-Zhu et al.,
771
      2019a;b), referred to as “lazy training” by Chizat et al. (2019). This allowed Jacot et al. (2018a)
      to prove that the evolution of an infinite-width neural network during training can be described by
772
      a kernel called the “Neural Tangent Kernel”. Lee et al. (2019) showed, through extensive experi-
773
      ments that the same behavior holds even for the more realistic case of neural nets of finite width.
774   Empirically speaking, it was shown by Rahaman et al. (2019) that a neural net with one input tends
775   to learn sinusoids of lower frequencies in earlier epochs than those with higher frequencies. By
776   analyzing the spectrum of the NTK’s Gram matrix, Ronen et al. (2019); Basri et al. (2020) were
777   able to formally prove this empirical finding. Yang & Salman (2019); Fan & Wang (2020) analyze
778   the spectra of the NTK gram matrix for higher dimensional inputs. Specifically, Yang & Salman
779   (2019); Valle-Perez et al. (2018) provide simplicity bias results for the case where the inputs to the
780   neural net are Boolean (zero-one) vectors.

785

805   B.3   S HAPLEY VALUES IN THE CONTEXT OF M ACHINE LEARNING
806
807   In the context of ML, many works have derived a different notion of Shapley value depending on
808   what they mean by data distribution, deleted features, etc. We refer the reader to the survey by
809   Sundararajan & Najmi (2020); Janzing et al. (2020) for a comprehensive overview. In this work we
      focus on the notion of SHAP introduced by Lundberg & Lee (2017); Lundberg et al. (2020) also


                                                        15
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
      known as “Interventional” (Janzing et al., 2020; Van den Broeck et al., 2021) or “Baseline” SHAP
811   (Sundararajan & Najmi, 2020) where the missing features are integrated out from the marginal
812   distribution as opposed to the conditional distribution (see Section 2). As pointed out by Janzing
813   et al. (2020) there are two main ways to define the SHAP value “interventional” and “observational”
814   SHAP. These are referred to by Sundararajan & Najmi (2020) as “baseline” and “conditional” SHAP
815   respectively.
816
      As pointed out by Janzing et al. (2020) the difference between these definitions can be better viewed
817   with the lens of causality (Pearl, 2009). Roughly speaking “observational” SHAP tells us about how
818   influential a feature is to the prediction of the predictor if it goes from the state of being unobserved
819   to observed. “Interventional” SHAP is causal and tells us how influential a feature is if we were
820   to reach in (through a process called an intervention) and change that feature in order to change
821   the prediction. Put into the context of credit scores and loan approvals, “observational” SHAP will
822   provide us with important features which are “observed” by the predictor and hence are influential
823   in predicting if a particular loan request will be rejected or approved. Interventional SHAP would
824   tell us which feature we could change or “intervene” in order to change the outcome of the loan
825
      request.
826   The original (ML) SHAP paper (Lundberg & Lee, 2017) proposes “observational” SHAP as the cor-
827   rect notion of SHAP. Van den Broeck et al. (2021); Arenas et al. (2021) provide intractability results
828   for observational SHAP in a variety of simple distributional assumptions on the data and simple
829   predictors f . This has lead to many attempts to approximate observational SHAP values (Lundberg
830   et al., 2020; Covert & Lee, 2020; Aas et al., 2021; Kwon et al., 2021; Sundararajan & Najmi, 2020).
831
      It is interesting to note that the version of “Kernel”-SHAP in Lundberg & Lee (2017) is also an
      approximation for observational SHAP values that ends up coinciding precisely with interventional
832
      SHAP values, which explains a lot of the confusion in the community. Janzing et al. (2020) boldly
833
      claims that researchers should stop the pursuit of approximations to “observation” SHAP values as
834   it lacks certain properties, for example, sensitivity i.e. the SHAP value of a feature can be non-zero
835   while the predictor f has no dependence on that feature. This phenomenon happens because when
836   features are correlated, the presence of a feature can provide information about other features that
837   the predictor does depend on. This does not happen in interventional SHAP. Finally, Chen et al.
838   (2020) argues that both SHAP definitions are worthy of pursuit. They emphasize that the interven-
839   tional framework provides explanations that are more “true to the model”, and the observational
840   approach’s explanations are more “true to the data”.

1024
1025   We implemented four versions of our algorithm, i.e., Equation 5, using Google JAX library (Brad-
       bury et al., 2018). JAX provides a flexible framework for developing high-performace functions

       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1026
       for vectorized computations. JAX enables automatic performance optimisation of algebraic com-
1027   putation as well as just-in-time (JIT) compilation of the vectorized functions for faster iterations at
1028   runtime.
1029
1030
       The full code base is provided as supplementary material to the paper. We refer the reader there
       for more details. Here we give a high level overview of the four versions we implemented and
1031
       experimented with:
1032
1033           • Base: In this version, frequencies are represented as n-dimensonal binary vectors. A sim-
1034             ple implenetation is provided for computing the SHAP values given a single frequency, a
1035             single background instance, and a single query instance. This is next extended to multiple
1036             frequencies, multiple background instances, and multiple query instances using multiple
1037             JAX vmaps.
1038           • Precompute: This version is a modified version of the Base version. We take a closer look
1039             at Equation 5 and pre-compute terms dependent only on frequencies f and background
1040             dataset points x (not including terms dependent on the query point x∗ ). These are then
1041             loaded from memory at run-time. This version was faster than Base in all of our experi-
1042             ments, but inherently requires more memory; in our case, more GPU memory.
1043           • Sparse: Here we utilize the sparsity of frequencies f ∈ {0, 1}n i.e. the fact that frequencies
1044             have mostly zero entries in practice. In this version, for each frequency f , we focus on
1045             positions i where fi = 1. Inspecting Equation 5, these are the only positions where f can
1046             affect the final SHAP value vector.
1047           • Positional: This version brings again builds on the previous and computes SHAP values
1048             separately for each coordinate i ∈ [n]. This adds one extra precomputation step to map
1049             frequencies to coordinates they affect, but enables mathematical simplifications as well as
1050             potential for extra vectorization. We observed that although this extra precomputation step
1051             could become time-consuming for large set of frequencies, it can result in a significantly
1052             faster SHAP value computation at run-time in the case of low-degree Fourier spectrums,
1053
                 compared to the Sparse version.

1215   F.3     U SING K ERNEL S HAP TO PRODUCE GROUND TRUTH SHAP VALUES
1216
1217   As mentioned before, in order to measure the accuracy of the SHAP values produced by our and
1218   other algorithms we need ground truth SHAP values. We use K ERNEL S HAP to this extent. K ER -
1219   NEL S HAP is an approximation method for computing interventional SHAP values. This approxima-
1220   tion becomes more precise by sampling more coalition subsets. Therefore, to generate ground truth
1221   values, for each dataset, we sample more and more and check for convergence in these values.
1222   In order to check if K ERNEL S HAP values have converged to ground truth interventional SHAP
1223   values, we update the number of subset samples K ERNEL S HAP uses to generate SHAP values for
1224   each instance. Looking into K ERNEL S HAP repo 8 , this is the default number of samples in the code:
1225
1226   self.nsamples = 2 * self.M + 2**11
1227
1228   Here, self.M is the number of indices where the instance is different from the background dataset.
1229
       To understand the convergence dynamics of K ERNEL S HAP, we multiply this number by multiple
1230
       “sample factor”s and run the algorithm. This allows us to experiment with the number of subsets
1231   sampled, and find a sample factor that ensures convergence while avoiding unnecessary extra com-
1232   putation. We test sample factors in the set {0.02, 0.03, 0.04, 0.1, 1.0, 2.0, 10.0}.
1233
1234
       For instance, for GB1 dataset, we compute the R2 score of the K ERNEL S HAP SHAP values with
       different sample factors to sample factor 10 (as the ground truth). For this dataset, the default setting,
1235
       i.e., sample factor = 1, seems good enough to make sure we are producing ground truth values and
1236
       that the algorithm has converged. This is the general procedure we use for all datasets.

1275   G.1           E XTENDING TO CONTINUOUS FEATURES
1276
       A limitation of F OURIER S HAP is that it currently does not natively support continuous features
1277
       and they have to be handled by quantization into categorical features. However, there is value
1278   in computing efficient SHAP values for models with continuous features and this is an important
1279   potential future work. In the following, we discuss how we think such an extension would work for
1280   the two classes of models we extensively covered in this work, namely trees and MLPs.
1281
       Trees in the white-box setting. Trees can be seen as inherently discrete structures even though
1282
       they perfectly work for continuous features. By setting a threshold, i.e., a continuous number to
