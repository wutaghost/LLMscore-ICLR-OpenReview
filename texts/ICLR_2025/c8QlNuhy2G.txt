009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated significant capabilities in math-
012            ematical reasoning, particularly with text-based mathematical problems. However,
013            current multi-modal large language models (MLLMs), especially those special-
014            ized in mathematics, tend to focus predominantly on solving geometric problems
015            but ignore the diversity of visual information available in other areas of mathe-
016            matics. Moreover, the geometric information for these specialized mathematical
017            MLLMs is derived from several public datasets, which are typically limited in
018
               diversity and complexity. To address these limitations, we aim to construct a
               fine-tuning dataset named MathVL, and develop a series of specialized mathemat-
019
               ical MLLMs termed MathGLM-Vision by conducting Supervised Fine-Tuning
020
               (SFT) on MathVL with various parameter-scale backbones. To extensively evaluate
021            the effectiveness of MathGLM-Vision, we conduct experiments on several public
022            benchmarks and our curated MathVL-test benchmark consisting of 2,000 problems.
023            Experimental results demonstrate that MathGLM-Vision achieves significant im-
024            provements compared with some existing models, including backbone models and
025            open-source mathematical MLLMs. These findings indicate the importance of di-
026            versity dataset in enhancing the mathematical reasoning abilities of MLLMs. Both
027            MathGLM-Vision model (based on CogVLM2, GLM-4V-9B) and MathVL-test
028            will be open-sourced.

      Recent advancements in computational linguistics have led to substantial progress in solving mathe-
      matical problems using Large Language Models (LLMs) with multi-step reasoning processes (Light-
034
      man et al., 2023). For example, models like GPT-4 (Achiam et al., 2023), Qwen (Bai et al., 2023a),
035
      GLM-4 (Team et al., 2024), LLaMA (Touvron et al., 2023a;b) have demonstrated impressive per-
036   formance on mathematical datasets such as GSM8K (Cobbe et al., 2021) and MATH (Hendrycks
037   et al., 2021). Furthermore, the development of specialized mathematical models is expanding the
038   potential of LLMs in this domain. These models, specifically designed for mathematical problem
039   solving, include notable contributions such as WizardMath (Luo et al., 2023), MAmmoTH (Yue et al.,
040   2023), MathCoder (Wang et al., 2023a), MetaMath (Yu et al., 2023), DeepSeekMath (Shao et al.,
041   2024), and others (Yang et al., 2023; Yuan et al., 2023; Gou et al., 2023; Yue et al., 2024b; Mitra
042   et al., 2024; Ying et al., 2024). These advancements highlight the growing proficiency of LLMs in
043   handling intricate mathematical reasoning and problem-solving tasks.
044   Despite significant advancements, the majority of models designed for mathematical problem solving
045   still rely predominately on textual representations. This limits their effectiveness in scenarios that
046   require visual information. Notably, approximately 63% of mathematics questions in Chinese K12 ed-
047   ucation include visual elements, highlighting the critical role of visual information in comprehending
048   and solving mathematical problems.

052   and Gemini-1.5-Pro on MathVL-test dataset, both with and without visual inputs. As shown in

077   reasoning challenges. Additionally, specialized geometric MLLMs like G-LLaVA Gao et al. (2023a),

081
082   • Current MLLMs, particularly those specialized in mathematics, predominantly focus on solving
083     geometric problems and tend to overlook the diversity of visual information in mathematics. This
084
        visual information encompasses a broad spectrum of elements, including arithmetic, statistics,
        algebra and word problems, each integral to different mathematical domains beyond geometry.
085
086   • Current fine-tuning dataset for specialized mathematical MLLMs, typically sourced from public
087     datasets like GeoQA and Geometry3K, often lack diversity and complexity. This limitation restricts
088     the models’ ability to effectively solve a broader range of mathematical problems.
089   • Current specialized mathematical MLLMs are predominantly designed to process single-image
090     inputs and lack the capability to handle multiple images simultaneously. This limitation hampers
091     their ability to tackle complex problems that necessitate the integration of information from multiple
092     visual sources.
093

094

095

096   mathematical problems, consisting of textual and visual inputs. For textual information, the MathVL
097   dataset covers a variety of mathematical subjects such as arithmetic, algebra, geometry, statistics, and
098   word problems. It includes various types of questions, including fill-in-the-blank, multiple-choice,
099   and free-form. For visual information, the MathVL dataset involves elements like functions, statistical
100   data, graphs, charts, LaTeX expressions, and geometric figures, providing a comprehensive resource
101   for complex mathematical problem solving.

      To enhance the capabilities of MLLMs in solving mathematical problems, previous efforts (Chen
      et al., 2021; 2022; Cao & Xiao, 2022; Gao et al., 2023a) focus on constructing high-quality datasets.
148
      Nevertheless, the majority of these datasets fall into the category of Visual Question Answering
149
      (VQA), which generally involves descriptive or identification tasks rather than conventional math-
150   ematical problems. Furthermore, the answers in some public datasets like Geometry3K (Lu et al.,
151   2021), GeoGPT4V (Cai et al., 2024), MathV360K (Shi et al., 2024) for standard mathematical
152   questions are often too simplistic, usually providing only the final answer without the intermediate
153   steps necessary for a thorough understanding. It is well-established that including step-by-step
154   solutions can significantly enhance the reasoning capabilities of large language models (Wei et al.,
155   2022; Lightman et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023b). Figure 3 demonstrates the
156   distribution of answer lengths in current open-source mathematical datasets.
157   To address these issues, we construct a fine-tuning dataset MathVL, including both several public
158   datasets and our curated Chinese dataset collected from K12 education levels. This dataset is
159   meticulously crafted to encompass a diverse array of mathematical problems that incorporate visual
160   information. Each problem is presented with detailed step-by-step solutions, aiming to enhance
161   the problem-solving skills of MLLMs by providing them with both the context and the procedural
      knowledge necessary for effective reasoning and comprehension.

      Model Architecture. We employ CogVLM2 (Hong et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2023c) and GLM-4V-
253   9B (GLM et al., 2024) architectures as our backbone models, and conduct Supervised Fine-Tuning
254   (SFT) on our constructed MathVL dataset. Specifically, we utilize three pre-trained multi-modal large
255   language models (MLLMs) for the fine-tuning process: GLM-4V-9B, CogVLM2-19B, and CogVLM-
256   32B. This results in the development of three distinct variants of MathGLM-Vision, designated as
257   MathGLM-Vision-9B, MathGLM-Vision-19B, and MathGLM-Vision-32B, respectively. Further
258   details about the abovementioned three pre-trained MLLMs are available in Appendix B.
259
260   Model Training. To maintain the general vision-language understanding skills of MathGLM-
261
      Vision, we incorporate 19 open-source visual question-answering datasets (VQA datasets) into the
      MathVL dataset. More details about the task type and visual context of VQA datasets are provided in
262
      Appendix C. These datasets are meticulously selected to challenge and enhance the model’s ability to
263
      interpret and integrate visual and textual information, ensuring it retains a broad understanding across
264   various contexts. By merging these varied sources, we enhance MathGLM-Vision’s specialized
265   capabilities for mathematical problem-solving and simultaneously preserve its robustness in general
266   vision-language tasks. In the end, we conduct supervised fine-tuning (SFT) across the combined
267   VQA and MathVL datasets. The training process undergoes 35,000 iterations with a learning rate
268   of 1e-5 and a batch size of 128. To ensure the stability of the training, we activate the visual
269   encoder’s parameters and adjust its learning rate to be one-tenth of that used for the remaining
      training parameters. The details of the SFT procedures are described in Appendix D.

455   We meticulously analyze the causes of errors in MathGLM-Vision-32B on the MathVL-test bench-
456   mark and illustrate the distribution of these errors in Figure 5. We summarize these errors in
457   MathGLM-Vision-32B into five types: reasoning error, knowledge error, vision recognition error,
458   calculation error, and question misunderstood error. The most prevalent type of errors, accounting
459   for 69.1% of the total, is identified as Reasoning Error. This indicates a significant challenge in
460   the MathGLM-Vision-32B’s logical deductions and inferential reasoning. Improving these capa-
461   bilities can dramatically enhance the MathGLM-Vision-32B’s overall performance. Knowledge
462   Error, which made up 12.7% of the errors, relates to the model’s misapplicaion or lack of specific
463
      factual information. Vision Recognition Error accounts for 11.4% of the total errors and involves
      inaccuracies in interpreting visual data. This type of error can be reduced through the implementation
464
      of more advanced vision encoders. Furthermore, the fact that Calculation Error constitutes only
465
      4.3% of the errors suggests that MathGLM-Vision-32B demonstrates considerable robustness in
466   numerical and computational tasks. Lastly, Question Misunderstood Error, which constitutes 2.5% of
467   the total, occurs when the model fails to correctly interpret question. Enhancing natural language
468   processing capabilities and refining context understanding can significantly reduce these types of
469   errors. Addressing these identified error types through targeted enhancements can significantly
470   boost the overall effectiveness of MathGLM-Vision-32B. Figure 6 demonstrate some cases of the

477
      have emerged as a significant area of research, which are designed to integrate information from

479

481   task layers, significantly improving performance on tasks like image captioning and visual question
482   answering. The continues evolution of MLLMs has lead to innovations in data fusion techniques.
483   Notable models such as CLIP Radford et al. (2021), ALIGN Jia et al. (2021), and BLIP Li et al. (2022)
484   have adopted contrastive learning paradigms to align visual and language information from billions
485   of image-text pairs. Concurrently, the success of LLMs Brown et al. (2020); Du et al. (2021); Zeng

      6     C ONCLUSION
527
      In this paper, we attempt to address the issues in current mathematical MLLMs. We construct a fine-
528
      tuning dataset named MathVL, upon which we conduct a Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) process. This
529
      initiative results in the development of a series of enhanced MLLMs, designated as MathGLM-Vision.
530   Specially, MathGLM-Vision contains three variations: MathGLM-Vision-9B, MathGLM-Vision-19B,
531   and MathGLM-Vision-32B, each fine-tuned on different backbone models: GLM-4-V, CogVLM2,
532   and CogVLM-32B, respectively. These developed MathGLM-Vision significantly improve the
533   capabilities of mathematical reasoning, achieving substantial performance improvements. Relative to
534   their respective backbone models, MathGLM-Vision-9B, MathGLM-Vision-19B, and MathGLM-
535   Vision-32B show improvements of 39%, 65%, and 53.7% on the Geometry Problem Solving (GPS)
536   minitest split of MathVista, demonstrating the effectiveness of MathVL in enhancing the mathematical
537   problem-solving abilities of MLLMs. Additionally, we evaluate the effectiveness of MathGLM-Vision
538
      on our curated MathVL-test benchmark. Experimental results reveal that MathGLM-Vision not only
      surpass their backbone models in specialized mathematical tests but also preserve the generalizability
539
      capabilities in general vision-language understanding domains.

829            architecture of GLM-4-9B, enhancing its capabilities to handle complex multi-modal inter-
830            actions. GLM-4V-9B takes a high resolution of 1120 * 1120 images as visual inputs. In
831
               comprehensive evaluations that test various capabilities including combined language skills,
               perceptual reasoning, text recognition, and chart understanding, GLM-4V-9B consistently
832
               outperforms competitors such as GPT-4-turbo-2024-04-09, Gemini 1.0 Pro, Qwen-VL-Max,

               and Claude 3 Opus, demonstrating its superior performance across multiple modalities.
834
835          • CogVLM2 is a series of open-source multi-modal large language models derived from Meta-
836            Llama-3-8B-Instruct, developed by Zhipu.AI and Tsinghua University. This series contains
837            two models: cogvlm-llama3-chat-19B and cogvlm2-llama3-chinese-chat-19B. The former
838
               is a monolingual language model focused on English and the latter is a bilingual model

839
               lengths up to 8K and accepts high-resolution images up to 1344 * 1344. Here, we choose

               cogvlm2-llama3-chinese-chat-19B as our backbone to pre-train our MathGLM-Vision-19B.

       Evaluation on MathVL-test We evaluate MathGLM-Vision and several close-source MLLMs using
1621   our specially constructed MathVL-test. The evaluation process of our MathVL-test is conducted
1622   through 3 key-step: generation, extraction, and scoring.
1623
1624
       For the generation step, the model responses are generated by providing the model with queries
       which incorporate the Chain of Thought (CoT) template, questions, and diagram information. The
1625
       reponses of close-source MLLMs is generated through API access. For the extraction step, we use
1626
       GPT-3.5-turbo to extract the model’s answer based on the reponses of first step. Finally, in the scoring
1627   step, the score for each question is determined by GLM-4 based on the comparison between the
1628   extracted answer and the standard answer.
