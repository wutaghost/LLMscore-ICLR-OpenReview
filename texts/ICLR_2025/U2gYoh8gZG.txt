009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            In real-world exposure correction, achieving high-quality images requires ad-
012            dressing multi-exposure conditions and managing images containing locally vary-
013            ing brightness. While recent deep learning models have improved image cor-
014            rection across various exposure levels, they often struggle in complex scenarios
015            where both under- and over-exposure coexist within an image or in over-saturated
016            areas with sparse pixel information. In this paper, we tackle these challenges
017            by proposing a color-aware guidance that employs a global prompt for tone ad-
018
               justment and a local prompt for maintaining color consistency of the output. To
               achieve this, we present a novel Prompt Interaction Module (PIM) that seamlessly
019
               integrates the global and local prompts with the input image features. Extensive
020
               experiments on multi-exposure benchmark datasets demonstrate that our method
021            achieves state-of-the-art performance, outperforming existing exposure correction
022            methods. Our approach sets a new standard in exposure correction, leveraging
023            prompt-based learning for improved color and exposure adjustments.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   In real-world photography, images are captured under various exposure conditions, and incorrect
029   exposure can obscure important details in images. To address this issue, modern cameras offer
030   exposure compensation features, and furthermore, many software-based solutions have been devel-
031   oped to automatically resolve this problem. Despite significant advancements in both hardware and
032   software that have greatly improved image quality and alleviated problems associated with severe
033
      under-exposure and over-exposure, challenges remain that often necessitate expert intervention to
      adjust settings such as aperture, shutter speed, or lighting to adapt to complex environments. While
034
      manual adjustments can improve image quality, they are impractical for CCTV systems installed in
035
      hard-to-reach areas or for vision cameras in automated factories, which require automated solutions
036   for reliable performance without manual input.
037
038   To address this issue, numerous exposure correction methods have been developed. Early research
039
      primarily concentrated on addressing either under-exposure or over-exposure, which limited the ef-
      fectiveness of these methods in managing complex exposure conditions. Considering the limitations
040
      of conventional approaches in addressing various exposure issues, recent methods have focused
041
      on developing models that can perform multi-exposure correction using a single deep neural net-
042   work by jointly training on both under- and over-exposure datasets. Among them, MSEC (Afifi
043   et al., 2021) presents a multi-exposure dataset for exposure correction and highlights that addressing
044   multi-exposure issues requires resolving the complex interaction between brightness and structural
045   information in images. Consequently, exposure correction methods have evolved to address the
046   complex degradation of lightness and structure, with approaches such as the use of Fourier trans-
047   formation (Huang et al., 2022b) and local color distributions (Wang et al., 2022; Li et al., 2024)
048   proposed for modeling this combined degradation. From another perspective, the emphasis on
049   feature-level enhancement is increasing in recent studies. For instance, DA (Wang et al., 2023b)
050
      and ERL (Huang et al., 2023) introduce pluggable modules that enhance diverse exposure inputs in
      the high-level feature space, thereby strengthening existing correction methods.
051
052   Although existing studies show significant performance improvements, sparse pixel information
053   from under- and over-exposure can result in inadequate enhancement outcomes. For instance, as
      shown in Fig. 1, conventional approaches struggle in challenging scenarios where the captured im-

068   ularly, conventional methods tend to cause color distortion when the captured images include areas
069   that are over-exposed due to a light source. Furthermore, even for the same object, color can be

071   the local color distribution of the input image to achieve accurate enhancement.
072
      Therefore, we propose a novel color-aware network utilizing Local and Global Prompts to capture
073   input-specific local color distribution and achieve natural exposure correction. In this approach, the
074   Local Prompt focuses on correcting spatial and localized features, while the Global Prompt manages
075   overall tone and exposure adjustment. These two learnable prompts interact dynamically within the
076   exposure correction network, offering essential guidance for accurate color and exposure correction.
077   Our color-aware approach for exposure correction is inspired by existing techniques that consider
078   local color distribution for other image enhancement tasks such as LCDPNet (Wang et al., 2022) and
079   CSEC (Li et al., 2024). However, unlike previous approaches, this work introduces Local Prompt
080   that is guided toward linguistically defined color categories in the low-dimensional space, based on
081
      the Color Naming model (Van De Weijer et al., 2009). This allows Local Prompt to facilitate robust
      spatial and color-specific enhancements across varying exposure conditions. As a result, even if
082
      the color of the same object varies due to different exposure levels, the correct color can still be
083
      accurately restored after applying exposure correction, even in challenging scenarios.
084
085   We call this approach CAGGLE (Color-Aware Guidance with Global and Local Prompts for
086   Exposure Correction). To the best of our knowledge, CAGGLE is the first approach to employ
087
      prompt-based learning for exposure correction, establishing a new benchmark in the field. Our main
      contributions can be summarized as follows:
088
089

090
               tures, demonstrating its effectiveness in correcting the overall image tone and addressing
091            challenges arising from exposure variations as depicted in Fig. 1.
092
093
             • By integrating the Color Naming model to guide prompt learning, we leverage local color
               statistics of input to enable color-aware exposure correction, ensuring color consistency.
094
095          • CAGGLE outperforms conventional exposure correction methods on multi-exposure
096            datasets, including MSEC (Afifi et al., 2021), SICE (Cai et al., 2018), and LCDP (Wang
097            et al., 2022), achieving state-of-the-art (SOTA) results across all datasets.

      2     R ELATED WORKS
100
101
      2.1   E XPOSURE C ORRECTION
102
103   Before the advent of deep learning methods in computer vision, exposure correction primarily relied
104   on conventional techniques aimed at improving image contrast. Methods such as Histogram Equal-
105   ization (Gonzales & Wintz, 1987) and Gamma Correction were widely used to enhance contrast.
106   Additionally, Retinex theory (Land, 1977; Jobson et al., 1997; Rahman et al., 2004) was employed
107   to address not only image contrast but also color constancy problems. Since the introduction of deep
      neural networks (DNNs), significant progress has been made in various computer vision tasks. In


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      particular, in the field of exposure correction, DNN-based approaches employing a variety of con-
109   cepts have emerged. Methods such as (Wang et al., 2019; Wei et al., 2018; Wu et al., 2022; Zhang
110   et al., 2021; 2019), which are based on Retinex theory that decomposes images into reflectance and
111   luminance, have been proposed for under-exposed image enhancement. Additionally, Retinex-based
112   methods like CMEC (Nsampi et al., 2021) have addressed multi-exposure correction through atten-
113   tion mechanisms, while LCDPNet (Wang et al., 2022) introduced an approach that considers local
114   color distribution. Moreover, to address the multi-exposure correction problem, the MSEC (Afifi
115   et al., 2021) and SICE (Cai et al., 2018) datasets were developed for both training and evaluation,
116   with MSEC also proposing a Laplacian pyramid architecture capable of managing multiple exposure
117
      conditions. Similarly, ECLNet (Huang et al., 2022c) employed a bilateral activation mechanism to
      differentiate the treatment of multi-exposure images, while FECNet (Huang et al., 2022b) introduces
118
      a lightweight spatial-frequency interaction model based on a Fourier-based approach.
119
120   Recent works have increasingly focused on regularizing and enhancing feature maps to improve
121   performance. ENC (Huang et al., 2022a) advanced this effort by incorporating an exposure nor-
122   malization module that maps varying exposure features to an exposure-invariant feature space.
123
      CSNorm (Yao et al., 2023) enhanced the generalization capability of existing methods by selec-
      tively normalizing lightness-relevant channels. ERL (Huang et al., 2023) applied regularization for
124
      multi-exposure correction, while DA (Wang et al., 2023b) introduced a contrast and detail-aware unit
125
      that can be integrated into existing architectures. The latest work, CSEC (Li et al., 2024), modeled
126   color distribution shifts at the feature level.
127
128
      2.2   P ROMPT L EARNING
129
130   Recently, prompt-based learning methods have gained widespread use in natural language pro-
131   cessing for fine-tuning inputs tailored to specific tasks. The process of finding appropriate in-
132   put prompts was introduced in (Brown et al., 2020). Unlike approaches that focus on finding
133   fixed-format prompts, CoOp (Zhou et al., 2022) introduced a method that treats prompt context
134   as learnable parameters, outperforming handcrafted prompts and demonstrating the superiority of
135   learnable prompts. Following CoOp, several methods have been proposed to generate appropriate
136   prompts (Smith et al., 2023; Derakhshani et al., 2023; He et al., 2022). CODA (Smith et al., 2023)
137   creates input-specific prompts through input-conditioned weights, HyperPrompt (He et al., 2022)
138
      addresses multi-task learning with a prompt generator, and bayesian prompt-learning (Derakhshani
      et al., 2023) models input prompts from a Bayesian perspective, adopting a probabilistic approach.
139
      In computer vision, visual prompts refer to methods that modify inputs by applying additional train-
140
      able parameters to effectively tune the model. VPT (Jia et al., 2022) shows significant performance
141   improvements over other fine-tuning methods by keeping the large transformer model backbone
142   fixed and applying a small number of trainable visual prompts. In addition, Bahng et al. (2022)
143   proposed visual prompts that can be combined with input images which are effective for CLIP. For
144   low-level vision tasks, PromptIR (Potlapalli et al., 2023) and PromptRestorer (Wang et al., 2023a)
145   use prompts to encode degradation-specific information and guide the restoration network to gen-
146   eralize to different degradation types and levels. In contrast, we propose input-specific global and
147   local prompts for multi-exposure correction, exploring both local and global information.
148
149   2.3   C OLOR NAMING MODEL
150
151   Precise color naming ensures consistency across various tasks, enabling reliable image analysis,
152   object recognition, and visual understanding, which are critical for tasks like image annotation,
153
      vision research, and photography. Basic Color Terms (Berlin & Kay, 1991) identifies semantic
      universals in the color vocabulary across linguistic boundaries. They show that, despite variations
154
      in the number of basic color terms across languages, there is a universal inventory of exactly 11
155
      basic color categories: black, blue, brown, gray, green, orange, pink, purple, red, white, and yellow.
156   Building on this foundation, a color decomposition model was proposed (Van De Weijer et al.,
157   2009), and this model outputs probability values for each pixel, indicating the likelihood that the
158   pixel belongs to one of the 11 predefined color names based on its sRGB values (Appendix. Fig. 8
159   (a)). Recent study, Serrano-Lozano et al. (2024) cluster colors with similar hues into 6 broader
160   names (red, green, blue, orange-brown-yellow, pink-purple, and white-gray-black) to facilitate easy
161   computations (Appendix. Fig. 8 (b)). Our CAGGLE employs these 6 color names to guide the
      color-aware prompts.

      3.1           OVERALL P IPELINE
189   Fig. 2 presents the overall architecture of CAGGLE, which consists of three main components:
190   the Encoder, the Decoder, and the Prompt Interaction Module (PIM). CAGGLE is built upon a
191   U-shaped residual structure, where the Encoder processes the poorly exposed input image Iin and
192   progressively transforms it into a deep feature map F . The PIM serves as a crucial bridge between
193   the Encoder and Decoder, further refining the feature map by leveraging input-specific prompts that
194   dynamically adjust the overall tone and enhance finer details such as color accuracy, contrast, and
195   sharpness of edges. In the final stage, the Decoder takes the enhanced feature representation from the
196   PIM and reconstructs it into a well-exposed output image Iout , effectively addressing challenging
197
      exposure issues.
198
199   3.2           E NCODER AND D ECODER
200
      The Encoder and Decoder are organized into conventional stages, with each stage consisting of
201
      multiple convolutional layers that are responsible for progressively transforming the input and out-
202   put features. In the Encoder, pixel-shuffled downsampling is used to gradually reduce the spatial
203   resolution and batch normalization is used to normalize features, enabling the model to capture ab-
204   stract and compressed representations from images with varying exposure levels. Conversely, in the
205   Decoder, pixel-shuffled upsampling is applied to restore the spatial resolution of the feature maps,
206   progressively reconstructing the well-exposed output image. Additionally, at each corresponding
207   stage, concatenation operations are applied from the Encoder to the Decoder, facilitating the flow of
208   essential low- and mid-level features from the encoding process into the decoding process, thereby
209   preserving important details and aiding in the final image reconstruction.
210   For more detailed information, we provide detailed specifications of the Encoder and Decoder ar-
211   chitectures in Appendix. A.1.
212
213   3.3           P ROMPT I NTERACTION M ODULE (PIM)
214
215   The purpose of the PIM is to effectively enhance the feature map F extracted from the Encoder
      through interaction with two learnable prompts: the Local Prompt and Global Prompt. In particular,

217   network h which captures spatially varying color information of the input image. The input-specific
218   Local Prompt is further processed through a specially designed cross-attention mechanism, and the
219   combined result with the Global Prompt produces an enhanced feature map, which is then passed to
220   the Decoder.

222   3.3.1   L OCAL P ROMPT
223

226   Notably, unlike previous methods (Potlapalli et al., 2023; Jia et al., 2022) that apply prompts glob-

      F to be handled in a distinct, localized manner.

230   from a shallow color estimation network h with our Local Prompt. Specifically, h takes the feature

244   Moreover, to address the color distortion issues commonly seen in conventional approaches, as in
245   Fig. 1, and to ensure consistent colors across spatially and exposure-varying objects, we design our

247
      propose a dedicated cross-attention mechanism to effectively leverage the color information.

249   Color-Aware Guidance with the Color Naming Model To address the existing issue of color
250   distortion, it is crucial that our color estimation network h accurately captures locally varying color
251
      information in poorly exposed input images. While h can be trained in an unsupervised manner,
      we leverage a color naming approach (Serrano-Lozano et al., 2024) and predefined color names.
252
      Since color names represent colors in a low-dimensional space, they are well-suited for representing
253
      distorted colors in over-exposed or under-exposed input images. This supervision ensures that Local
254   Prompts are weighted to align with the color names, which are more robust to color distortion at
255   each spatial location. Thus, h effectively captures the spatial and local color distributions of the
256   input image, allowing CAGGLE to produce color-consistent outputs regardless of input exposure
257   level. Moreover, this enables h to perform a comprehensive analysis of local structural features,
258   such as edges, in addition to color distribution, ultimately enhancing the quality of PLocal .
259
      In this work, we utilize the Color Naming model from Serrano-Lozano et al., which further clusters
260   11 predefined color names based on sRGB values (Van De Weijer et al., 2009) into 6 categories of
261   similar hues. These 6 categories, grouped by colors that differ only in intensity and share similar
262   hues, can guide the Local Prompt and provide solid guidance for exposure correction. We present
263   a detailed explanation of the loss function used to train h in Sec. 3.4. Notably, as demonstrated in
264   our ablation study in Sec. 4.5, even without incorporating the Color Naming model into the prompt
265   design, CAGGLE exhibits outstanding effectiveness compared to existing methods. However, us-
266   ing the low-dimensional color names as supervision for prompt learning significantly boosts the
267   performance of CAGGLE.

      3.3.2    G LOBAL P ROMPT
295   Inspired by previous prompt-based approaches (Zhou et al., 2022; Jia et al., 2022; He et al., 2022),
296   we introduce a Global Prompt PGlobal , a learnable one-dimensional vector, to provide guidance for
297   improving the overall and global exposure of the image. Specifically, in PIM, the Global Prompt is
298   reshaped through repeating copies to match the size of the feature map F from the Encoder, resulting
                                                  ′   ′
299   in the Global Prompt Map (MGlobal ∈ RH ×W ×C ). This design allows it to handle input images
300   of arbitrary size. Notably, unlike the Local Prompt, which helps enhance local details, MGlobal
301   can adjust the overall tone of the image and improve exposure correction quality. Therefore, in this
302   work, we employ both the Local Prompt and Global Prompt together to leverage their synergy, and
303   the process within PIM can be expressed as follows:
304                              PIM(F ) = LP-CA(F, MLocal ) + MGlobal .                               (7)

344   4.1      I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
345
346   For training, we adopt the Adam optimizer with a patch size of 256×256 and a batch size of 16. The
347   total number of epochs is set to 500, and the learning rate is 2 × 10−4 . Additionally, we set N = 6
348   and C = 128 for Local Prompt. The implementation is based on the PyTorch framework, utilizing a
349
      single NVIDIA RTX 4090 GPU, and our code will be released upon acceptance.
350   Datasets. The training and benchmark settings follow the existing exposure correction
351   tasks (Huang et al., 2022c;b; Li et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2022). We train the network on three
352   multi-exposure datasets, including the Multiple Exposure (ME) (Afifi et al., 2021), Single Image
353   Contrast Enhancement (SICE) (Cai et al., 2018), and LCDP (Wang et al., 2022). The ME dataset
354
      contains 17,675 training images, 750 validation images, and 5,905 test images across five expo-
      sure levels. The SICE dataset consists of sequences of 4–7 images of the same content at different
355
      exposure levels, and the LCDP dataset contains 1,700 different scenes with both over- and under-
356
      exposure to facilitate training and evaluation. In addition, to confirm the performance under different
357   low-light conditions, we use RELLISUR (Aakerberg et al., 2021) dataset, which has multiple low-
358   light exposure settings. This contains 850 distinct sequences with three different scales, and 2,550
359   pairs are provided, and we only use the ×1 scale for low-light image enhancement. Each image has
360   exposure values ranging from -4.5 to -2.5 or -5.0 to -3.0 in 0.5 intervals, resulting in 12,750 paired
361   images and RELLISUR dataset is split into 85% for training, 5% for validation, and 10% for testing.
362   Comparative methods. We compare CAGGLE to several state-of-the-art exposure correction
363   methods, including CSEC (Li et al., 2024), LCPDNet (Wang et al., 2022), ECLNet (Huang
364   et al., 2022c), FECNet (Huang et al., 2022b) and pluggable approaches DA (Wang et al., 2023b),
365   ERL (Huang et al., 2023), and ENC (Huang et al., 2022a). Additionally, for low-light image en-
366   hancement, we benchmark CAGGLE against various previous methods, including Zero-DCE (Guo
367   et al., 2020), Retinex-Net (Wei et al., 2018), RUAS (Liu et al., 2021), KinD (Zhang et al., 2019),
368   GLADNet (Wang et al., 2018), MIRNet (Zamir et al., 2020), and MBLLEN (Lv et al., 2018). Each
369
      comparison model is evaluated using its official network parameter weights and reproduced with its
      official code for the RELLISUR (Aakerberg et al., 2021) dataset. For PromptIR (Potlapalli et al.,
370
      2023), we set the number of prompts equal to the number of exposure values in each dataset: 5 for
371
      MSEC, 2 for SICE, 2 for LCDP, and 5 for RELLISUR. The evaluations are measured in terms of
372   Peak-Signal-to-Noise-Ratio (PSNR) and Structural Similarity (SSIM).

524   5         L IMITATIONS
525
526   While the prompts learn and provide useful information in the feature space for image enhancement,
527   improvement remains challenging in extreme cases where the input image lacks sufficient informa-
528   tion, such as over-saturated regions where pixel values are close to 255 (as shown in the red box of
529   Fig. 7). To address this issue, we are exploring the application of generative models in areas that
530   contain missing information. We will prioritize and continue to solve this problem in future work.

      6         C ONCLUSIONS
533
534   In this work, we tackle exposure correction through color-aware prompt learning with both Global
535   and Local Prompts. We emphasize spatially-aware adjustment and introduce a novel color-aware
536   prompt design incorporating color names. Our method, CAGGLE, enhances local details like color
537   and structure through the Local Prompt while managing overall tone via the Global Prompt. Ad-
538   ditionally, we propose LP-CA, which enhances Local Prompt performance. By leveraging these
539   prompt techniques, CAGGLE achieves state-of-the-art results on multi-exposure benchmarks, effec-
      tively balancing global tone adjustment with local detail enhancement.

793       A.3            C OLOR NAME
794
795
         A color term or color name refers to a word or phrase that represents a specific color, and the
         terms we use are based on the theory introduced by Berlin and Kay in Basic Color Terms (Berlin &
796
         Kay, 1991). Berlin and Kay argued that color perception is more influenced by physiological and
797
         perceptual factors than by cultural ones. They analyzed data collected from speakers of 20 languages
798      across various language families and identified 11 basic color categories: white, black, red, green,
799      yellow, blue, brown, purple, pink, orange, and gray. Since these color categories are based on human
800      physiological processes, models that classify colors using these names are perceptually grounded.
801
         Based on this theory, Van De Weijer et al. introduce a Color Naming model that categorizes the color
802
         of each part of real-world images. The Color Naming model does not aim to improve the naming
803
         of color patches, but instead focuses on accurately naming colors in real-world applications. In
804      real-world scenarios, images are captured under various conditions such as different illuminations,
805      reflections, unknown cameras, colored shadows, compression artifacts, acquisition aberrations, and
806      unknown camera settings. Therefore, robust color naming is crucial for applied research. The Color
807      Naming model uses pLSA (probabilistic Latent Semantic Analysis) (Hofmann et al., 1999) to model
808      the probability of each pixel in an image belonging to a specific color name. As the objective of our
809      method is to robustly recognize color names even under varying exposure levels and use this in the
         Local Prompt learning process, this approach aligns well with our research.
