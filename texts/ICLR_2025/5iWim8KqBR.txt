009                                                     A BSTRACT
010
011                It’s recently reported that by employing the superior In-context Learning (ICL)
012                ability of autoregressive Transformer, a method named Algorithm Distillation (AD)
013                could distill the whole Reinforcement Learning process into neural network then
014                generalize to unseen scenarios with performance comparable to the distilled al-
015                gorithm. However, to enable ICL, it’s vital for self-attention module to have a
016
                   context that spans cross-episodes histories and contains thousands of tokens. Such
                   a long-range context and the quadratic memory complexity of self-attention pose
017
                   difficulty on applying AD into many common RL tasks. On the other hand, design-
018
                   ing memory efficient Transformers for long-range document modeling is itself a
019                fast-developing and fruitful field, which leads to a natural question: Could Efficient
020                Transformers exhibit similar in-context learning ability and be used for Memory-
021                Efficient Algorithm Distillation? In this paper, we firstly build a benchmark suite
022                that is thorough, efficient and flexible. Thanks to it, we perform extensive ex-
023                periments and verify an existing method named ERNIE-Docs (ED) could offer
024                competitive performance with significantly reduced memory footprint. With sys-
025                tematic ablation studies, we further investigate various facets influencing the ICL
026                ability of ED and provide our own insights into its hyperparameter tuning.

      1       I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Building decision-making agents that could generalize to various environments and tasks has been
031   a long-standing goal of Reinforcement Learning (RL). Recently, following the trends in Computer
032   Vision (CV) (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021) and Natural Language Processing (NLP) (Brown et al., 2020;
033   Devlin et al., 2019), multiple works have explored on pretraining deep autoregressive Transformers
034   on large-size behavior dataset to obtain agents with strong generalization and adaptation ability (Reed
035   et al., 2022; Lee et al., 2022; Octo Model Team et al., 2024). Among them, Algorithm Distillation
036   (AD) takes an unique approach and proposes that by employing the superior In-Context Learning
037   (ICL) ability of autoregressive Transformer, the whole RL process could be distilled into a neural
038
      network then be ‘replayed’ on new, possibly unseen scenarios.(Laskin et al., 2023)1
039   Analogous to causal Language Modeling (LM), AD requires an autoregressive Transformer with
040   context spanning cross-episodes interaction histories to perform credit assignment, exploration-
041   exploitation balance and policy improvement that are vital to a RL algorithm. Differently and
042   crucially, all of these abilities are implicitly modeled in the weights of Transformer network instead
043   of being designed by human experts and implemented as hard-coded programming languages as in
044
      Haarnoja et al. (2018); Fujimoto et al. (2018); Schulman et al. (2017).
045   However, this cross-episodes context encompasses thousands of tokens, resembling long-range
046   document modeling in NLP: In typical RL settings, an episode contains hundreds of transitions each
047   comprising 4 components (state, action, reward and terminal):
048              2 ∼ 4 (cross-episode context length is needed) × 500 (transitions in an episode)
049                           ×4 (components in a transition) = 4k ∼ 8k (tokens)
050
      Combined with the quadratic memory cost of self attention, this requires excessively large memory
051
      footprint (around 40-160GB under batch size 10, embedding size 128 and half-precision) and prevents
052   RL researchers from readily reproducing and improving algorithms in AD’s setting.

054
      On the other hand, designing memory & computation Efficient Transformer (ET) for long-range
055   document modeling is itself an important and developing research field in NLP where many well-
056   established works have emerged (Dai et al., 2019; Ding et al., 2021; Tay et al., 2022). Therefore, a
057   natural question arises: Could Efficient Transformers exhibit similar in-context learning ability and
058   be used for Memory-Efficient Algorithm Distillation? If so, it could combine the best of both worlds
059   and significantly boost future researches in this line.
060
      However, to the best of our knowledge, there is still no works investigating this question. The reasons
061   are multi-fold: 1) It still lacks a consensus on how to design benchmarks to thoroughly test the ICL
062   ability of a given algorithm. 2) As of a meta-RL setting, the implementation of above benchmark
063   shall be computation-efficient for parallel data collection and evaluation.
064
      Therefore, in this work, we firstly design an efficient and flexible benchmark suite to thoroughly test
065
      the ICL performance of a given method. After this, with comprehensive experiments, we discover
066
      ERNIE-Docs (ED) (Ding et al., 2021), an improved variant of Transformer-XL (Dai et al., 2019),
067   could obtain competitive performance with a significantly reduced memory requirement. Finally,
068   with systematic ablation studies, we examine various factors influencing the ICL performance of ED
069   and provide our own insights in its hyperparameter tuning. In summary, our contributions are:
070
      • We provide a benchmark suite covering the whole AD process from data collection to meta training
071
        & evaluation. With 8 representative settings, we could thoroughly test a method’s ICL ability in
072
        cases of sparse reward, credit assignment, dense information and exploration-exploitation balance.
073   • We employ JAX (Bradbury et al., 2018) for computation efficiency and achieve parallel execution
074     on 10k different environments. Our code is also compatible with Pytorch (Paszke et al., 2019) for
075     its broader Transformer-related ecosystem.
076   • Based on our benchmark, we find ERNIE-Docs obtains competitive performance with a signifi-
077     cantly reduced memory requirement. We also perform systematic ablation studies to demonstrate
078     how various factors affect its performance and provide our own insights.

081   2    R ELATED W ORK
082
083   Sequence Modeling in Reinforcement Learning. Interpreting RL as a problem of sequence
084   modeling and employing Transformer for it has been a popular research direction since the recent
085   success of Transformer in NLP field (Radford et al.; Devlin et al., 2019). Chen et al. (2021) and Janner
086   et al. (2021) firstly introduced Transformer into model-free and model-based RL respectively and
087
      achieved promising results in many tasks. As a pioneering work, Laskin et al. (2023) firstly reported
      that by using autoregressive Transformer, in-context RL could be achieved with generalization to
088
      unseen scenarios. Following their work, Dai et al. (2023); Zisman et al. (2024); Sinii et al. (2024)
089
      investigated various facets to improve AD, such as training on noisy dataset and generalizing to
090   unseen action space. Differently, our work targets on the memory cost of vanilla AD.
091
092   Meta Reinforcement Learning. Meta RL aims at empowering RL algorithms the ability to quickly
093
      adapt to new environments and tasks with limited amount of on-site samples. Generally, it could be
      classified into 2 branches (Beck et al., 2023): 1) in-weight meta RL where methods like MAML (Finn
094
      et al., 2017), ProMP (Rothfuss et al., 2022) perform gradient descend on parameters of neural network
095
      for fast adaptation. 2) in-context meta RL where adaptation emerges as context of environment
096   interactions gets populated or updated as in methods like RMA (Kumar et al., 2021) and Prompt-DT
097   (Xu et al., 2022). Different from these works whose goal is fast adaptation of episode return, AD
098   aims to distill existing RL algorithms then generalize to new scenarios.
099
      Transformer for Long-range Tasks. Designing Efficient Transformers to increase the performance
100
      on long-range tasks has been a very important field (Burtsev et al., 2021; Tay et al., 2022). Dai
101
      et al. (2019) proposed integrating recurrence with Transformer’s attention mechanism and achieved
102   superior performance on long-range tasks. Later work like Rae et al. (2019) also studied to ‘compress’
103   past information into recurrence module and further improved Transformer’s performance over
104   tasks on long-range document modeling while not drastically increasing context length. Besides,
105   methods like (Gu & Dao, 2023; Wang et al., 2020) tried to design new attention mechanisms with
106   only linear complexity to context length. Since this field is both fruitful and fast-developing, in this
107   work we choose to explore how these promising methods could be used in AD’s setting such that RL
      researchers could be freed to explore on ideas more related to RL’s problems.

      3.2   M EMORY C OST A NALYSIS
163
164   We present a formal analysis of memory cost for vanilla Transformer and Recurrence Transformer
165   (abbreviated as RT and denoting XL and ED). For the sake of simplicity, only attention module is
166   focused as other modules like tokenizer, feedforward and layernorm layer are the same in our settings.
167
      Vanilla Transformer. Let’s consider an input x of shape (b, s, d) where b is batch size, s is sequence
168   length and d is hidden dimension. For a mutli-head attention layer with n heads. This requires the
169   following amount of memories:
170
171              3(d2 + d) (weights & biases) + 3(d2 + d) (gradients of weights & biases)
172              + 3bsd (Q, K, V) + nbs2 (QK T ) + nbs2 (attention score) + bsd (final output)                                                      (5)
173                     2                                     2               2
                 = 6d + 6d + 4bsd + 2nbs ∝ s
174
175   Recurrence Transformer. Let’s consider a chunk length of c and a recurrence length of m. Then for
176   each step of RT processing, the chunk input xc is in shape (b, c, d) and recurrence xm is in shape of
177   (b, m, d). The amount of memories for RT is:
178
       bmd (recurrence) + 3(d2 + d) (weights & biases) + 3(d2 + d) (gradients of weights & biases)
179
180                           + bcd (Q) + 2b(c + m)d (K, V) + nbc(c + m) (QK T )
                                                                                                                                                    (6)
181                           + nbc(c + m) (attention score) + bcd (final output)
182
                              = 3bmd + 6(d2 + d) + 4bcd + 2nbc(c + m) ∝ c(c + m)
183
184   From above analysis, the memory cost is proportional to s2 for vanilla Transformer and is proportional
185   to c(c + m) for RT methods. However, in the setting of AD, cross-episode context is needed for
186   vanilla self-attention based Transformer and encompasses thousands of tokens. On the other hand, the
187   recurrence module in RT could serve as an additional memory for important information and reduce
188   the need of contiguous context which is unnecessarily long. That is to say, with c + m ≪ h < s
189
      where h is episode length, the memory cost of RT could be greatly reduced than vanilla Transformer.

192
193   It’s still an open question over how to design a benchmark suite to test the ICL ability of AD methods.
194   Firstly, the benchmark needs to be thorough enough and include representative RL settings. Besides,
195   as an in-context meta RL setting, the benchmark needs to be computation-efficient and support
196   parallel environment interactions for both data collection and meta evaluation. Finally, the benchmark
197   shall also be flexible and compatible towards the most ecosystems.
198   In this work, taking all above considerations into account, we design a benchmark suite that is
199   computation-efficient thanks to environment parallelism in JAX and flexible since it also supports
200   building algorithm in pure Pytorch. We also carefully design environments & tasks to thoroughly test
201   given methods’ ICL performance. An illustrative framework of our benchmark is shown in Figure 2.
202   The further details could be found in Appendix A.3.

385
386   Positional Encodings (PE) like sinusoidal encoding (Vaswani et al., 2017), learnable embedding and
387
      RoPE (Su et al., 2023) are critical components for attention-based Transformer models and have been
      widely studied in NLP.
388
389   Interestingly, in our experiments, we find the us-
390   age of global positional encoding has unique ad-
391   vantages over local-context-based ones common
392   in NLP. Without change in embedding mecha-
393
      nism, the ‘global’ PE assigns to all the tokens in
      the whole learning process a positional embed-
394
      ding while context-based PE only considers to-
395
      kens in the context window. We argue global PE
396   is more suitable for in-context RL as it’s intuitive
397   for RL algorithms to take different strategies in           Figure 6: Performance when using different PEs
398   different learning phase, such as exploration in
399   the beginning and exploitation in the ending.
400
      Besides, we also design 2 experiments to verify
401
      this: 1) Global PE helps training. As shown in
402   Figure 6, we test 4 PEs on standard ADF method.
403   The 4 abbreviations represent global-learnable
404   (gl), global-sinusoidal (gs), local-learnable (ll)
405   and local-sinusoidal (ls), respectively. From the
406   results, global positional encoding significantly
407   helps stabilize and ease the training of Algo-
408   rithm Distillation. 2) Additionally, global PE

529   6   C ONCLUSION
530
531   In this work, we study the problem of reducing the excessive memory requirement of Algorithm
532   Distillation (AD) via existing Efficient Transformers (ET). With our efficient, flexible and versatile
533   benchmark, we discover the ERNIE-Docs (ED) could serve as a simple yet effective method for
534   Memory-Efficient Algorithm Distillation and illustrate how various factors like positional encoding,
535   model size, attention module and context length & memory capacity influence its performance.
536   Besides, there is still room to further improve ED’s performance and better interpret/visualize its
537   internal attention mechanism which we choose to leave as future works. Also, because ET is an
538   fast-developing field and limited by computation resources, we cannot test all promising ET methods
539   in this work. Nevertheless, we will release our benchmark code for further researches in the line of
      Memory-Efficient AD.
