009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Diffusion models (DMs) require large annotated datasets for training, limiting
012            their applicability in medical imaging where datasets are typically smaller and
013            sparsely annotated. We introduce DiNO-Diffusion, a self-supervised method for
014            training DMs that conditions the generation process on image embeddings ex-
015            tracted from DiNO, a pretrained vision transformer. By not relying on anno-
016
               tations, our training leverages over 868k unlabelled images from public chest
               X-Ray (CXR) datasets. DiNO-Diffusion shows comprehensive manifold cover-
017
               age, with FID scores as low as 4.7, and emerging properties when evaluated in
018
               downstream tasks, allowing to generate semantically-diverse synthetic datasets
019            even from small data pools, demonstrating up to 20% AUC increase in clas-
020            sification performance when used for data augmentation. Results suggest that
021            DiNO-Diffusion could facilitate the creation of large datasets for flexible training
022            of downstream AI models from limited amount of real data, while also holding
023            potential for privacy preservation. Additionally, DiNO-Diffusion demonstrates
024            zero-shot segmentation performance of up to 84.4% Dice score when evaluating
025            lung lobe segmentation, evidencing good CXR image-anatomy alignment akin to
026            textual descriptors on vanilla DMs. Finally, DiNO-Diffusion can be easily adapted
027
               to other medical imaging modalities or state-of-the-art diffusion models, allowing
               large-scale, multi-domain image generation pipelines for medical imaging.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Diffusion models (DMs) have recently emerged as robust and proficient foundational models in
033   medical imaging, exhibiting substantial capabilities in image generation, image enhancement, re-
034   construction, and segmentation (Kazerouni et al., 2023). The field of synthetic image generation
035   in particular has greatly shifted to text-to-image DMs, generating images that are nearly indistin-
036   guishable from real ones (Osorio et al., 2024; Chambon et al., 2022a; Ye et al., 2023; Aversa et al.,
037   2023; Pinaya et al., 2022) and facilitating remarkable zero-shot performance in segmentation and
038   classification tasks (Tian et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023a). However, DMs depend on the availabil-
039
      ity of large datasets containing images paired with corresponding descriptors (usually text) to guide
      the generation process, a requirement that presents a considerable obstacle in the medical domain
040
      (Beddiar et al., 2023). Medical imaging datasets are typically small, contain free-form and inconsis-
041
      tent annotations including captions, binary labels or segmentations, and are generally prohibitively
042   costly to compile and curate (Beddiar et al., 2023). To address these challenges, some works have
043   proposed pseudo-labeling with vision-language models (VLMs; Betker et al. (2023)) or have trained
044   lean mapping networks over frozen pretrained backbones to reduce the number of required annotated
045   samples (Li et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023b). However, despite their promise, pseudo-labelling ap-
046   proaches find limited applicability in the medical field given a lack of high-quality medical imaging
047   captioners (Beddiar et al., 2023). In addition, while some authors have successfully trained mapping
048   networks to bridge the gap between unimodal foundation models, they still require relatively large
049   annotated datasets to be trained (Beddiar et al., 2023).
050   These limitations represent important roadblocks for medical DMs. While the natural imaging lit-
051   erature focuses on saturating generation quality by improving the base architecture, optimization
052   process or condition alignment (Esser et al., 2024; Betker et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024), the medi-
053   cal imaging community navigates these hurdles by leveraging smaller or custom-annotated datasets
      (Chambon et al., 2022a; Ye et al., 2023; Osorio et al., 2024; Aversa et al., 2023; Pinaya et al., 2022).

085   Moreover, although mapping networks have found their footing in the diffusion literature with ap-
086   proaches such as ControlNet (Zhang et al., 2023b), these would still rely on large-scale medical
087
      DMs trained with prohibitively extensive amounts of annotated images. In this context, applying
      a self-supervised approach to DM training would be highly beneficial for medical image synthe-
088
      sis. Self-supervision enables models to learn from unlabelled data, providing exceptional results in
089
      multiple downstream tasks when used as image embedders (Caron et al., 2021; Oquab et al., 2023;
090   Pérez-Garcı́a et al., 2024; Dippel et al., 2024; Moutakanni et al., 2024).
091
092   With that in mind, we introduce DiNO-Diffusion, a novel self-supervised methodology for train-
093
      ing medical DMs at scale which conditions the image generation process on image-derived tokens
      extracted from a frozen DiNO model (Caron et al., 2021; Oquab et al., 2023), as opposed to tex-
094
      tual descriptors. DiNO-Diffusion allows independence from existing annotations, circumventing the
095
      limitations imposed by the scarcity and inconsistency of medical image labels. Moreover, it is ag-
096   nostic to the choice of DM architecture, medical imaging modality or optimization strategy. To test
097   this, a model was trained on a large corpus of open-source CXR data found in the literature which
098   do not share any common labeling or descriptor required to train regular DMs (e.g., text captions),
099   achieving low FID scores and high image quality. DiNO-Diffusion can generate medical images
100   despite using DiNO embeddings, which are derived from natural images. To test the alignment
101   between DiNO embeddings and generated images, several downstream evaluation tasks were per-
102   formed, comprising classification and segmentation, which addressed the model’s ability to improve
103   classification performance when adding synthetic data to a pool of real data or when fully replacing
104
      real with synthetic data; and assessing whether a self-supervised DM can be used to create zero-shot
      segmentation masks for distinct anatomical structures.
105

107   its independence from specific architectures, imaging modalities, available annotations, dataset sizes

      2.2   G ENERATIVE A RCHITECTURE - S TABLE D IFFUSION
181
182
      Latent Diffusion Models (LDMs) approach image generation as an iterative denoising process, trans-
183   forming pure noise xT into a defined image x0 over T steps with a parameterized DM ϵθ (zt , t, c),
184   where c represents an optional condition. LDMs address the prohibitive computational demands
185   of traditional DMs by reducing the dimensionality of the input. LDMs currently find active de-
186   velopment with ongoing research in different parameterised models, optimization strategies and
187   dimensionality reduction pipelines.
188
      This study adopts the Stable Diffusion (SD) framework (version 1, Rombach et al. (2022)) as its
189   baseline. Despite being outperformed by more recent models and its output size limitation of
190   512x512 pixels, SD’s lightweight architecture, open-source nature, and community adoption makes
191   it ideal for our proof of concept. SD comprises a frozen variational autoencoder (VAE) and a
192   trainable conditional denoising UNet.
193
      The VAE consists of an encoder (E) and a decoder (D). The encoder compresses fixed-size images
194
      x ∈ RH×W ×3 into a latent z = E(x) ∈ R(H/d)×(W/d)×k , where k = 4 is number of channels
195
      extracted by the VAE and d = 8 is the downsampling factor. The decoder maps latents back to the
196   original image space x̂ = D(z). Stable Diffusion’s VAE has been shown to generalize to medical
197   data (Chambon et al., 2022a;b). The UNet serves as the diffusion component and uses a ResNet
198   architecture as its convolutional backbone, where the condition c is incorporated through attention
199   mechanisms (see Section 2.3).
200
      With this model, training with conditional information involves two phases: the forward and reverse
201
      diffusion processes. During the forward diffusion, an image x0 (or its latent representation z0 ) and
202   condition c are chosen. A timestep t is randomly selected (t ∼ U(1, ..., T )) so a noisy latent zt is
203   generated by mixing z0 with noise ϵ ∼ N (0, 1), resulting in a partially noised latent. The reverse
204   process uses the UNet to estimate the original noise ϵ from zt , t and c.
205
      The network is optimized using the Mean Squared Error (MSE) loss between the predicted and
206
      actual noise to adjust the weights of the UNet:

      2.3                                           S ELF -S UPERVISED C ONDITIONING
248
249
      LDMs condition image generation using a semantic tensor c to guide the diffusion process. This
250
      tensor is usually obtained from a frozen transformer model fΦ that maps the label information into
251
      a tensor c = fΦ (x) ∈ RS×N , where S is the token length (of variable size), N is the embedding di-
252   mension and x represents whichever input the embedder model requires (text, image, etc.). Although
253   the current diffusion literature has mainly focused on using textual descriptors as their main condi-
254   tioning strategy, other conditioning mechanisms have been employed (Aversa et al., 2023; Pinaya
255   et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2023b).
256
      In this work we explore conditioning using image-derived semantic descriptors. Specifically, a
257
      vision transformer trained with the DiNO method (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021; Caron et al., 2021)
258   was used to produce a semantic description of the image to be generated. Vision transformers split
259   an image into small patches (usually P = 14px2 or P = 16px2 ) representing “visual words”
260   and operate over them using a standard transformer architecture. The model outputs a tensor of
261   tokens c = fΦ (x) ∈ RS×N comprising a class token cCLS ∈ RN , sometimes a pooler token
262   cP LR ∈ RN , sometimes a predefined amount R of register tokens cREG ∈ RR×N (Darcet et al.,
263   2024), and finally a series of L patch tokens cLCL ∈ RL×N , where L = H/Py ∗ W/Px . Finally,
264   the conditioning tensor outputted by the embedder was reduced to the available global information
265   cGLB = [cCLS , cP LR , cREG ] before feeding it to the UNet, as upon initial exploration the patch
266
      tokens contained too much local information of the original image x and led to trivial models that
      learnt to reconstruct images from redundant information (see Section A.1). Figure 1-(a) visually
267
      describes the training pipeline.
268

      conditioning embedding exists. In this work, two simple generation strategies were explored, to

      2.4   E VALUATION
301
      This section details four different evaluation protocols used for benchmarking DiNO-Diffusion:
302
303   Image Quality & Checkpoint Selection: the Fréchet Inception Distance (FID; Heusel et al. (2017))
304   was used to quantify generation quality at multiple checkpoints for both variants of DiNO-Diffusion.
305   The FID scores computed for the data generated via the “reconstruction” strategy (see Section 2.3)
306
      were used as a proxy for overall model performance. Similarly to Chambon et al. (2022a), FID
      scores were computed over a 5k subset of MIMIC-CXR’s p19 dataset (Johnson et al., 2019), and are
307
      reported every 2500 steps in Figure 3. Also following the same work, the FID score was computed
308
      on the feature space of a pretrained domain-specific image encoder from TorchXrayVision (Cohen
309   et al., 2022) as opposed to the default Inception-V3 model, as the latter might not provide an accurate
310   measure of image quality when dealing with medical image data. Finally, the optimal checkpoint
311   for each DiNO-Diffusion model was the checkpoint with the lowest FID score.
312
      Data Augmentation: this experiment explored DiNO-Diffusion’s ability to enhance the sample
313
      size of a dataset by training a classification model on real and synthetic data using five-fold cross-
314
      validation and testing on a held-out test set (MIMIC’s p19). For this purpose, MIMIC’s training
315   dataset (p10-p18) was subset into different data regimes with decreasing sample size Xn , with
316   n ∈ {10k, 5k, 1k, 500, 100, 50} samples in the subset. Given that MIMIC has multi-label anno-
317   tations, label balancing was performed by randomly selecting n/card(L) elements of each label
318   in the labelset L from X without replacement, ensuring sufficient representativity of all labels
319   within the training set. Smaller subsets were also enforced to be contained into bigger ones, so
320   that XNi+1 ∈ XNi . With Xn defined, synthetic data was created to increase sample size by gener-
321   ating partially-synthetic datasets X̂n with real-to-synthetic ratios of 1:1, 1:5, 1:10 and 1:50 for the
322   reconstruction- and interpolation-based synthesis (see Section 2.3). For the reconstruction experi-
323   ments, ratios larger than 1:1 represent several semantic variations of a single source image (x, y),
      which aim at introducing realistic variance into the synthetic data while retaining the label-specific


                                                            6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      image features. The interpolation experiment addressed whether intermediate embeddings could
325   still be decoded into an image that retains label-specific features from both elements in the pair. For
326   this purpose, the sample pairs were enforced to have at least one label in common (see Section 2.3)
327   without repetition. When not all the labels are in common between the pair, the labels of the in-
328   terpolated example are set to the ones of the sample it is closest to, as defined by the interpolation
329   fraction r. Finally, in the case of not having enough unique pairs for a given split, some pairings
330   were repeated with different r.
331   Full Synthetic Training: this experiment explores whether test-set AUC drops when training a clas-
332   sifier solely on synthetic data, to address whether DiNO-Diffusion can serve as a privacy-preserving
333   synthetic replacement for real data. The generation strategies, data regimes, real-to-synthetic ratios
334   and 5-fold cross-validation settings from Section 2.4 were followed as evaluation strategy.
335
      Zero-Shot Segmentation: this experiment investigates the model’s ability to learn semantic co-
336
      herence by generating segmentation masks from the internal representations generated during the
337   DiNO-Diffusion’s UNet forward pass. For this purpose, the zero-shot segmentation approach from
338   DiffSeg (Tian et al., 2023) was followed, consisting of leveraging the self-attention weights from
339   each transformer block of the UNet and iteratively merging them based on their Kullback-Leibler
340   divergence. This methodology was applied both to DiNO-Diffusion and a vanilla SD model to gen-
341   erate lung lobe segmentation masks without further training. Using a combined dataset of 1,048
342   cases with ground truth annotations (See Section 2.1), candidate masks were evaluated by their Dice
343   score and selected via non-maximum suppression. The relevant hyperparameters (merging thresh-
344   old, timestep) as well as the best performing checkpoint were selected per model (see Section B.3).
345
      Refer to Figure 1 (b-iii) for a visual depiction of the segmentation pipeline.

349   The models were trained by adapting HuggingFace Diffusers’ script for training DMs (von Platen
350
      et al., 2022). The DMs were trained for 100 epochs (∼ 140000 steps) using 4 H100 GPUs
      per model, an aggregated batch size of 512 (bs = 64, gradient accumulation of 2 steps), 8-
351
      bit Adam optimizer with constant lr = 10−4 and 1000-step warmup and xformers’ memory-
352
      efficient attention (Lefaudeux et al., 2022). The specific versions of the DiNOv1 and DiNOv2
353   image encoder architectures used were “facebook/dino-vitb16” (Caron et al., 2021) and
354   “timm/vit-base-patch14-reg4-dinov2” (Darcet et al., 2024), respectively. The web-
355   dataset library (WebDataset Contributors (2021)) was used for storing and streaming data directly
356   from the bucket during all model trainings. The classification experiments were based on training
357   HuggingFace’s implementation of a “densenet121” for 150 max epochs using T4 GPUs with
358   batch size 64, AdamW optimizer with lr = 10−4 and weight decay of 10−5 , a LR reduction-on-
359   plateau scheduler with patience 10 and early stopping after 25 epochs with no validation AUC im-
360   provement. For the checkpoint evaluation, a pretrained “densenet121-res224-all” (Cohen
361
      et al. (2022)) was employed as feature extractor. All images followed the same minimal prepro-
      cessing strategy before training or evaluation, similar to other works in the literature (Cohen et al.,
362
      2022; Chambon et al., 2022a). Dynamic intensity values (uint8, uint16) were rescaled to uint8. Im-
363
      ages were center-cropped with a 1:1 aspect ratio, resized to 512x512 pixels and padded areas were
364   removed. Minimal data augmentations were applied during all model trainings, including random
365   sharpening and affine transformations (5% shearing, 5% translation, 90%-140% scaling).

460   4   D ISCUSSION
461
462
      DMs are a cornerstone in modern foundation models, revolutionizing many tasks in Computer Vi-
463   sion. Their ability to generate high-quality images has caused a large scientific, economic and soci-
464   etal disruption, whose long-term repercussions are difficult to foresee (Liu et al., 2024). However,
465   despite their scientific and industrial utility, applying this technology in medical imaging is severely
466   limited by key challenges such as a lack of large-scale labeled datasets including high-quality textual
467   or non-textual descriptions (Kazerouni et al., 2023). Although this limitation might be temporary
468   due to current trends in AI data acquisition and improved dataset interoperability (Akhtar et al.,
469   2024), it is not clear whether the prevalent text-to-image generative recipe (Rombach et al., 2022) is
470   optimal for medical applications.
471   Some approaches employing DMs in medical data exist. Chambon et al. (Chambon et al., 2022a)
472   trained an SD architecture on the MIMIC-CXR dataset (Johnson et al., 2019) with good synthesis
473   fidelity, reporting low FID scores and high accuracy scores on several downstream tasks including
474   classification, report generation and image retrieval. However, their approach is severely limited on
475   the size of the development dataset (300k images) and the low quality of accompanying captions.
476   In histopathology, multiple authors have proposed applying DMs for image generation (Ye et al.,
477
      2023; Osorio et al., 2024). For instance, Aversa et al. relied on a custom-annotated dataset of
      large histopathology slides with segmentation masks representing different tissue subtypes within
478
      the slide and employed timestep unravelling to generate images larger than the typical 512px2 .
479
      However, their approach heavily relied on a closed-source, custom-annotated dataset, and timestep
480   unraveling might be impractical in other medical imaging modalities. In contrast, Xu et al. (Xu
481   et al., 2024) take a similar approach as the one proposed here, and train a DM conditioned only on
482   an image encoder’s cCLS for histopathology image synthesis. However, their method was partially
483   supervised, as it relied on training additional label-specific DMs for cCLS generation. Besides being
484   compute intensive, their method fails to leverage the emerging data augmentation and segmentation
485   capabilities that a self-supervision DM training conveys. Finally, Pinaya et al. (Pinaya et al., 2022)
      trained an LDM on a large dataset of 31740 3D Brain MRI images from UK BioBank. However,

501   the DM with simplified clinical variables such as age, sex, ventricular volume, and brain volume.
502   DiNO-Diffusion addresses the data limitations in medical imaging by conditioning the image gen-
503   eration process on the images themselves. This allows training DMs on unlabelled data, which
504   is more abundant in the medical field. The resulting DiNO-Diffusion models demonstrated good
505   manifold coverage, as indicated by low FID scores, and exhibited notable properties in several
506   downstream tasks. Firstly, adding synthetic data using the “reconstruction” strategy improved per-
507   formance across most configurations. However, performance gains diminished as more real data
508
      became available, which is to be expected. Secondly, the “interpolation” strategy degraded perfor-
      mance in higher data regimes. We hypothesize that, although the generated images qualitatively
509
      resemble plausible images (see Figure 2-b), naı̈vely interpolating embeddings did not ensure that
510
      the interpolated labels corresponded to the decoded image’s features, thereby hurting classification
511   performance. We leave to future work the exploration of more sophisticated interpolation strate-
512   gies. Thirdly, full synthetic training demonstrated that synthetic data can replace real data while
513   preserving privacy, and even improve performance in small-data regimes, when used in abundance.
514   Finally, DiNO-Diffusion’s zero-shot segmentation outperformed a vanilla SD architecture. This is
515   remarkable given that the dataset used to train the vanilla SD model was several orders of magni-
516   tude larger. Despite DiNO-Diffusion’s performance, conditioning the synthesis process on image
517   embeddings has theoretical advantages and disadvantages. This type of conditioning relaxes the
518   need for annotations, enabling the collection of larger datasets for model training, and has proven
519
      effective across various tasks. However, usage of an image-conditioned model is fundamentally
      different from text-based approaches, as image generation requires conditioning on an image. Still,
520
      this circular dependency between input and output could be advantageous in some use cases, such
521
      as data augmentation or privacy-preserving data sharing.
522
523   These advantages and disadvantages evidence room for improvement. Firstly, DiNOv1-Diffusion
524   outperformed DiNOv2-Diffusion both quantitatively and qualitatively, despite the larger data pool
525
      used to train the DiNOv2 image encoder (Oquab et al., 2023). This suggests that using domain-
      specific encoders (Cohen et al., 2022; Pérez-Garcı́a et al., 2024; Moutakanni et al., 2024), or even a
526
      combination of different image encoders (Esser et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024) could further improve
527
      these results. Secondly, DiNO-Diffusion would benefit from more recent diffusion architectures
528   found in the literature (Esser et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024; Betker et al., 2023). Thirdly, generation
529   based on other descriptors, such as text, could be enabled by using external networks to map the im-
530   age embedding space to the text embedding (Zhang et al., 2023b; Li et al., 2023). Finally, the failure
531   cases found in the zero-shot segmentation workstream require adapting the DiffSeg methodology to
532   datasets with different characteristics, including image-level hyperparameter optimization, further
533   attention-merging strategies, or using DiNO’s attention maps to better locate anatomic structures.
534   In conclusion, while diffusion models have significantly impacted the Computer Vision community
535   with broad scientific, economic, and societal implications, their application to medical imaging is
536   constrained by data and annotation scarcity. Our DiNO-Diffusion approach addresses this problem
537   by conditioning the image generation on the images themselves, eliminating the need for extensive
538   annotations. The approach shows promising results in manifold coverage, data augmentation, pri-
539   vacy preservation and zero-shot segmentation. Finally, this work underscores the need for innovative
      solutions in medical imaging to fully leverage the potential of DMs in this space.

      A.1     R ECONSTRUCTIONS FROM ENTIRE D I NO EMBEDDINGS
815
816   Initial explorations showed that the DiNO encodings (c) need to be compressed before feeding them
817   as conditioning during DiNO-Diffusion training. As depicted in Figure 6, when using the entire
818   DiNO image embedding (c) for conditioning, the model can learn to utilize the information richness
819   of the DiNO encoding to reconstruct the initial CXR image with exceptional detail. This richness
820   can likely be attributed to the various patch tokens (cLCL ) that the whole DiNO encoding includes,
821   since they retain local information about the corresponding image patch, but also how it relates to the
822   remaining patches in the image. In addition, Figure 6 further shows how the information gathered in
823   the DiNO encoding seems to be sufficient to allow our model to reconstruct images from modalities
824
      that have never been seen during the DiNO-Diffusion training (only CXR).
825   Further supporting this, 7 shows the reconstructions yielded by the model trained with patch tokens,
826   when the condition has half the tokens from an image and half the tokens from another image. In this
827   scenario, the regions corresponding to the patch tokens from Image A are reconstructed according
828   to image A, while the equivalent occurs for the regions corresponding to patch tokens of Image B.
829   The abrupt transition in the final image, clearly depicts how the model trained with patch tokens
830
      learns a one-to-one correspondence between each patch token and its corresponding image patch, as
      opposed to any global understanding of the underlying image domain.
831
832   Considering these two experiments would not be applicable for image data augmentation purposes.
833   In this work, we show that the latter is only achievable if the conditional information is limited to
834   the tokens that gather global information from the image (cGLB ).
