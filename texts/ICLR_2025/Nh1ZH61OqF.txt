009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            In stochastic minimax optimization, variance-reduction techniques have been
012            widely developed to mitigate the inherent variances introduced by stochastic gra-
013            dients. Most of these techniques employ carefully designed estimators and learn-
014            ing rates, successfully reducing variance. Although these approaches achieve
015
               optimal theoretical convergence rates, they require the careful selection of nu-
               merous hyperparameters, which heavily depend on problem-dependent param-
016
               eters. This complexity makes them difficult to implement in practical model
017
               training. To address this, our paper introduces Adaptive Filtered Momentum
018            (AdaFM), an adaptive variance-reduced algorithm for stochastic minimax opti-
019            mization. AdaFM adaptively adjusts hyperparameters based solely on historical
020            estimator information, eliminating the need for manual parameter tuning. The-
021            oretical results show that AdaFM can achieve a near-optimal sample complex-
022            ity of O(ϵ−3 ) to find an ϵ-stationary point in non-convex-strongly-concave and
023            non-convex-Polyak-Łojasiewicz objectives, matching the performance of the best
024            existing non-parameter-free algorithms. Extensive experiments across various
025            applications validate the effectiveness and robustness of AdaFM.

028
029   Typically, the stochastic minimax optimization problem Nouiehed et al. (2019); Lin et al. (2020); Lu
030   et al. (2020); Huang et al. (2022; 2023) can be formulated as follows:
031                                   min max f (x, y) = Eξ∈D [f (x, y, ξ)],                            (1)
032                                  x∈Rd1 y∈Y
033   where data sample ξ is a random variable following an unknown distribution D. Y ⊂ Rd2 is closed
034   and convex, and f : Rd1 × Rd2 → R is non-convex in x. We call x the primal variable and y the
035   dual variable. Problem in equation 1 is widely used in many machine learning applications, e.g.,
036   adversarial training Goodfellow et al. (2014b); Miller et al. (2020), Generative Adversarial Network
037   (GAN) Arjovsky et al. (2017); Goodfellow et al. (2014a), deep Area Under the Curve (AUC) Yuan
038
      et al. (2021; 2022), and sharpness-aware minimization Foret et al. (2021); Qu et al. (2022).
039   Since stochastic gradients on both the primal and dual parameters inherently exhibit variance Johnson
040   & Zhang (2013); Dubey et al. (2016), which slows down the convergence rate, recent studies have
041   focused on Variance-Reduction (VR) techniques Reddi et al. (2016); Xu et al. (2017); Cutkosky &
042   Orabona (2019); Ward et al. (2020); Huang et al. (2022); Xu et al. (2023); Huang et al. (2023); Liu
043   et al. (2023) to mitigate this variance, demonstrating the ability to achieve optimal sample complexity
044
      of O(ϵ−3 ) for finding an ϵ-stationary point.
045   While the aforementioned VR-based algorithms have proven highly successful at the theoretical level,
046   they often perform poorly in actual model training Defazio & Bottou (2019); Arjevani (2017). One
047   significant reason is that VR techniques introduce numerous hyperparameters that must be carefully
048   selected in minimax optimization to ensure the effectiveness of the VR techniques. For instance, in
049   stochastic minimax optimization, the VR-based algorithms mentioned above do not always guarantee
050
      convergence if the ratio of the learning rates for x and y is not selected appropriately Yang et al.
      (2022a). Moreover, the large number of hyperparameters makes the algorithm highly sensitive, such
051
      that even small hyperparameter changes can prevent the algorithm from converging.
052
053   To verify the above issues, we conducted real model training, specifically using WGAN-GP Gulrajani
      et al. (2017), on CIFAR10 and CIFAR100 Krizhevsky et al. (2009). From Figure 1, we observe that

      RSGDA faces several challenges. First, when we select the parameters from a large space, i.e., [0, 0.1]

      of the two learning rates in Figures 1a and 1c, we can see that most results are not desired enough. As
069   such, we need to compress the searching space. Consequently, the parameters are highly sensitive; for

073   cause the space of effective parameters to shift dramatically, making it difficult to provide a default
074   combination of parameters for different datasets and tasks. This results in a highly computationally
075   laborious hyperparameter search for various tasks. Therefore, to enhance the practicality of VR-based
076   algorithms, it is necessary to address the issue of excessive hyperparameters.
077   In minimization problems, the parameter-free approach Kingma & Ba (2014); Li & Orabona (2019);
078   Ward et al. (2020); Levy et al. (2021) offers an intuitive solution to enhance VR-based algorithms by
079   automatically adapting hyperparameters, thus avoiding manual tuning. However, implementing VR
080   techniques in a parameter-free manner for minimax problems remains highly challenging because
081   minimax problems require the simultaneous consideration of updates to both variables. As a result,
082   traditional VR-based algorithms for minimax problems involve nearly twice as many hyperparameters
083
      compared to those used in minimization problems. specifically, VR techniques maintain gradient
      estimators vt and wt for x and y, respectively, with the corresponding learning rates ηxt and ηyt
084
085
      carefully designed based on vt and wt . Many hyperparameters in vt , wt , ηxt , and ηyt require knowledge
      of problem-dependent parameters to be chosen properly, ensuring the effectiveness of VR-based
086
      algorithms. These problem-dependent parameters, such as the smoothness constant L and the gradient
087
      bound G, are difficult to determine during actual model training. This raises a natural question:
088
089     Can we design an adaptive VR-based algorithm to achieve the optimal convergence rate in
090     the minimax optimization problem?
091
092   In this paper, we introduce an adaptive VR-based algorithm named Adaptive Filtered Momentum
093   (AdaFM) for stochastic minimax optimization problems. Inspired by STORM Cutkosky & Orabona
094
      (2019), AdaFM incorporates variance reduction with momentum correction and features a novel
      update method for both momentum parameters and learning rates, making them adaptive and simpli-
095
      fying their computation, thus enhancing ease of use. Specifically, The momentum parameter only
096
      decreases with the number of iterations, thus avoiding parameter tuning and improving the stability
097   of the algorithm. The learning rate takes multiple factors into full account. On one hand, the learning
098   rate decreases as the cumulative value of the estimator increases. On the other hand, the learning
099   rates of x and y interact with each other, ensuring that the step sizes of x and y adapt to the desired
100   ratio. The main contributions of this paper are summarized as follows:
101
102           • We introduce AdaFM, the first adaptive VR-based algorithm for stochastic minimax opti-
103             mizations. AdaFM is an adaptive method that achieves the near optimal convergence rate in
104             the minimax optimization problem. AdaFM dynamically adjusts the momentum parameters
105
                according to the number of iterations and automatically adjusts the learning rate based on
                the current momentum parameters and historical estimator information.
106
107           • We provide detailed analyses of AdaFM in both Non-Convex-Strongly-Concave (NC-SC)
                and Non-Convex-Polyak-Łojasiewicz minimax (NC-PL) settings. Although the theoretical

      2    R ELATED W ORK
118
119
      Stochastic Minimax Optimization. Stochastic minimax optimization has gained significant traction
120
      in various machine learning applications. The prevailing approach for solving minimax optimization
121   problems typically involves alternating between optimizing the minimization and maximization sub-
122   problems, which are typically addressed by stochastic gradient descent ascent (SGDA) Nouiehed et al.
123   (2019); Lin et al. (2020); Lu et al. (2020). Notably, they can achieve a sample complexity of O(ϵ−4 )
124   in stochastic settings Nouiehed et al. (2019); Lin et al. (2020); Yang et al. (2020). Subsequently, some
125   accelerated algorithms utilizing adaptive learning rates have been extended to minimax optimization,
126   both theoretically and practically. These include approaches for strongly-convex strongly-concave
127   problems Antonakopoulos et al. (2021), nonconvex-convex problems Yang et al. (2022a); Huang
128   et al. (2023), and nonconvex-PL problems Huang (2023); Guo et al. (2023). For example, Guo et al.
129
      (2023) proposes PES to address the primal objective and duality gaps under the NC-PL setting.
130   VR Techniques. VR techniques have gained prominence in stochastic optimization, addressing the
131   inherent variance issue associated with stochastic gradients. Notable approaches include stochastic
132   variance reduced gradient Johnson & Zhang (2013); Reddi et al. (2016), SPIDER Fang et al. (2018);
133   Li et al. (2023b), and STORM Cutkosky & Orabona (2019); Levy et al. (2021), which have accelerated
134   the convergence. SPIDER has led to the development of fast HAPG Shen et al. (2019) and SVRPG Xu
135
      et al. (2020b). Momentum-based techniques such as ProxHSPGA Pham et al. (2020), SVMR Jiang
      et al. (2022), and NSTORM Liu et al. (2023) have emerged from STORM’s principles, addressing
136
      various optimization scenarios. While VR-based algorithms have demonstrated efficient convergence
137
      results, the challenge of reducing the search space for hyper-parameters remains under-explored.
138
139   Parameter-Free Algorithms. Parameter-free algorithms have significantly enhanced their utility
140   by adapting to various parameters without the need for extensive manual tuning. Some adaptive
141
      optimizers that achieve this property include AdaGrad Duchi et al. (2011), Adam Reddi et al. (2016),
      and STORM+ Levy et al. (2021). TiAda Li et al. (2023a) extends this adaptivity to minimax
142
      optimizations by separating the two timescales. Additionally, parameter-free algorithms have been
143
      extensively developed in online learning. For instance, Beygelzimer et al. (2015) focuses on online
144   boosting, Xu et al. (2020a) addresses online reinforcement learning, and Hanneke et al. (2023)
145   explores multi-class online learning. In these contexts, the primary goal is for the learner to compete
146   with the performance of the best possible function f , thereby achieving minimal regret. Note that
147   online learning primarily addresses the cold data streaming problem, which is parallel to this paper.

      6   C ONCLUSION
487
488
      In this paper, we present AdaFM, an adaptive variance-reduced algorithm that eliminates the need for
489   manual hyper-parameter tuning, improving the practical application of variance-reduction techniques
490   in stochastic minimax optimizations. AdaFM uniquely adjusts momentum parameters based on
491   iteration count and adaptively modifies learning rates using historical estimator information combined
492   with momentum parameters. Although the theoretical result in the NC-PL setting is O(κ5 T −1/3 ),
493   which is worse than the NC-SC setting’s O(κ4.5 T −1/3 ) due to the more complex properties, both
494   achieve an ϵ-stationary point with an optimal complexity of O(ϵ−3 ), which align the best results
495   among existing parametric algorithms. Extensive experimental evidence validates the effectiveness
496   and robustness of AdaFM across various scenarios. In the future, we aim to develop parameter-
497   free algorithms for more complex scenarios, e.g., minimax optimization without projection and
498   compositional minimax optimizations, and relax conditions, e.g., non-convex non-concave settings.

783   In this section, we demonstrate the effect of δ on the algorithm. From the settings of ηtx and ηty , it can
784   be observed that an increase in the value of δ further reduces the learning rate of x while increasing
785   the learning rate of y. This adjustment causes the learning rates of x and y to reach the desired ratio
786   more quickly in some scenarios. However, due to the rapid decrease in the learning rate of x, it may
787   also slow down the overall convergence rate.

789   be observed that AdaFM fails to converge at δ = 0, as shown in Figure 9a, and loses the ability
790   to adaptively control the stepsize ratio, as shown in Figure 9b. As δ increases, AdaFM adjusts
791   more effectively, and the trajectory curve approaches the stationary points with greater curvature.
792   Meanwhile, the stepsize ratio reaches the desired value more quickly. However, this also causes the
793   learning rate of x to decrease more rapidly, as illustrated in Figure 9d.
