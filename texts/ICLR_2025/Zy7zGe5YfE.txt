011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Generative and adversarial machine learning methods have been used for param-
014            eter inference of physical models from observed data in various works. However,
015            many real-world problems of interest involve non-differentiable models, a context
016            in which many approaches cease to be sufficient. An example of this can be found
017            in quantum chromodynamics, where inferring quantum correlation functions from
018            observed data is hindered by the problem’s intrinsic non-differentiability and
019            stochasticity. To overcome this, we present a framework based fundamentally on
020
               generative adversarial networks in which parameters are iteratively optimized to
               generate realistic samples. This framework is novel compared to related works in
021
               that it simultaneously circumvents non-differentiability, enables uncertainty quan-
022
               tification, and is free of assumptions on parameters. We demonstrate the utility of
023            this framework in learning synthetic distributions and simulated quantum correla-
024            tion functions.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      Parameter inference and its associated uncertainty quantification in scientific modeling is a cor-
030   nerstone of science, with many standardized techniques and tools available that enable domain
031   researchers to stress-test postulated models and theoretical frameworks against physical systems
032   (Rastogi (2021); Gábor & Banga (2015); MacLeod (2020)). In recent years, generative modeling
033   in machine learning (ML) has found applications, in this context, with potential capabilities that
034   could surpass existing methods in their performance on high-dimensional, many-parameter models
035   (Kutz (2023)). In particular, generative modeling has been applied in simulation-based inference in
036   high-energy physics Chan et al. (2023); Andreassen & Nachman (2020); Cranmer et al. (2020).
037   One of the general challenges in using ML techniques is the inherent need to construct computa-
038   tional frameworks using differentiable programming to perform standard back-propagation for ML
039   models. This requirement is particularly challenging for simulation-based inference, which involves
040   multiple components that have undergone dedicated R&D over the years and are difficult—if not
041   impossible—to rewrite with autodifferentiation capabilities.
042   An example of this occurs in nuclear particle physics, where researchers aim to reconstruct from ob-
043   servational data, using high-energy scattering experiments, the internal quark and gluon structures
044   inside nucleons and nuclei. There are dedicated and exciting programs in this field, such as those
045   at Jefferson Lab 12 GeV (McKeown (2011)), COMPASS at CERN (Abbon et al. (2007)), RHIC at
046   BNL (Aschenauer et al. (2014)), and the planned Electron-Ion Collider (Khalek et al. (2022)), where
047   the development of generative modeling for inference on end-to-end simulations could become crit-
048   ical.
049   In this work, we present a case study of using generative ML in simulation-based inference with
050   uncertainty quantification in the context of hadron structure studies that bypasses the autodifferen-
051   tiation requirements. We briefly discuss a simplified simulation pipeline that will serve as a test
052   bed for our studies. Then, we formulate the inference problem in the context of generative mod-
053   eling using Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) and discuss our strategy to avoid issues with
      back-propagation. Our main contributions are as follows:

      Non-Differentiable Model Parameter Learning: Our approach is able to accomplish parameter
055   inference on underlying physical models for which analytic differentiation is either unavailable or
056   prohibitively challenging. We demonstrate the ability to circumvent such non-differentiability and
057   seamlessly integrate with arbitrary application code with stochastic sampling.
058
      Parameter Uncertainty Quantification: The nature of our generative setup gives straightforward
059
      uncertainty distributions over the inferred parameters, effectively learning prior distributions over
060
      parameters from data. We emphasize the utility of this approach in cases where we do not have
061   assumptions we can make on the priors (or wish to not impose any such biases), so being able to
062   empirically form distributions over feasible parameters is crucial in interpretability when no other
063   knowledge is available. We focus our attention on epistemic (or model-centric) uncertainty in this
064   paper and leave aleatoric (or data-centric) uncertainty as an application-specific concern.
065
      Reduced Training Dynamics Complexity: Similar approaches to this problem tend to require addi-
066
      tional neural networks to address the inner non-differentiability. This is accomplished either through
067   training of probabilistic surrogate event generators (which can add a second inner adversarial loop)
068   or offline fitting of a differentiable surrogate physical model approximation. Both of these are high-
069   dimensional and complex mappings with non-trivial training cost. In contrast, our approach only
070   requires training of a single additional model, which is lightweight and learns a mapping simply
071   from the parameter space to the discriminator output.
072
      Assumption-Free Inference: We impose no assumptions on the parameter range or prior distribu-
073   tions. In phenomenological contexts where we may be simultaneously developing theory to fit to
074   observed data, this is a desirable paradigm.

140   This work sits at the intersection of many similar proposed solutions for related problems in adver-
141
      sarial machine learning, non-differentiable parameter inference, and PDF fitting. The naive classical
      approach of directly searching the parameter space is infeasible in this application due to the as-
142

143
      advanced parameter inference enabled by machine learning. We summarize the most closely related

147   setup with both inner and outer GANs all learning simultaneously. While there is strong success in
148   the generation of high-quality synthetic data from parameters using this surrogate event generator,
149   training requires large amounts of data, which may be costly to obtain. Moreover, many similar
150   works in stochastic parameter inference constrain possible models entirely to differentiable ones:
151   in such cases, non-differentiability is accommodated through offline fitting of a differentiable sur-
152
      rogate model to effectively replace the non-differentiable physical model (Rumbell et al. (2023)).

153
      imposed approximation. Advanced methods such as Adversarial Variational Optimization (Louppe
154
      et al. (2019)) which directly tackle non-differentiable models do so through forced priors, which we
155   wish to avoid in this case where parameter knowledge may be entirely unknown.

344   All experiments are done on GPU nodes on the Perlmutter supercomputer: 4x NVIDIA A100 GPUs
345   (40GB) per node. We do not provide scaling studies here with respect to numbers of GPUs involved.
346   In future work, to more rapidly accelerate training in an online setting, it may be useful to investigate
347   high-performance variants of GANs to ensure efficient and rapid parameter estimation. The SAGIPS
348
      paper (Lersch et al. (2024)) provides some insight into how this scaling could be accomplished.
349   This section presents results on GAN+SPN for two distributions: first, learning the rate parameter
350   of a Poisson distribution, and second, learning the defining parameters of simple PDFs from events
351   sampled from phase space densities. For visual examples, we present the generated Poisson events
352   in histogram form in Figure 2 and the reconstructed PDFs in Figures 5 and 4. For ablation studies
353   investigating the sensitivity of our results on the PDF problem to training-related hyperparameters,

      we refer the reader to the appendix.
355   For the Poisson distribution results, we present results compared with AVO. We clarify that we
356   present this comparison to AVO purely to contextualize this work among other Bayesian inference
357   approaches towards non-differentiable parameter estimation. However, we emphasize that the al-
358   lure of this particular method is that, unlike AVO, at no point do we enforce any assumptions on
359   the priors of the parameters, while known or estimated priors on the parameters are assumed and
360
      harnessed in their variational optimization approach. This difference is crucial when we consider
      the specific physics application problem, where being able to learn possible parameters without
361
      additional inductive bias of parameter priors is a crucial draw of this framework.

      6    C ONCLUSIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
504
505
      We present an adversarial ML framework which is able to infer parameters with respective uncer-
506
      tainty distributions from observed data. Our implementation only requires only one adversarial loop
507   as opposed to other similar approaches and achieves differentiable training of the generator through
508   a cheap, learnable surrogate mapping from the generated parameters to discriminator classification,
509   eliminating the need for any offline preparatory work such as the potentially costly computation of
510   a differentiable surrogate approximation of a physics model.
511
      Other GAN variants, such as Wasserstein GAN (Arjovsky et al. (2017)) and Hinge (Lim & Ye
512
      (2017)) methods, have been developed to avoid some of the instability pitfalls often encountered with
513   GANs. A brief investigation into replacing our standard GAN implementation with these approaches
514   were done with as-of-yet inconclusive results. Future work may involve a more rigorous study into
515   GAN modifications to accelerate convergence and improve stability of learned parameters.
516
      We make the brief note that in the distributions that we examine, there is a so-called unique solution
517
      to the training objective (in an extremely idealized setting with infinite data availability); that is, that
518
      there is only a single set of parameters which uniquely define the underlying probability distribution
519   functions. This may not be the case in more complex PDF parametrizations, where multiple distinct
520   parameters could define identical observable event distributions. To combat this possible issue, our
521   GAN+SPN framework can be easily extended to an ensemble setting in which multiple differently-
522   initialized networks are trained on the data, enabling independent exploration of the parameter space.
523   This would give us not only “intra”-model uncertainty estimates as we have in this work, but further
524   uncertainty estimates of parameters through aggregation of multiple generator outputs. We briefly
525   experimented with this approach but omit this in our quantitative analysis since the uniqueness of
526   the solutions in the examples we present reduces the need for such ensemble learning.
527   The ultimate goal is to integrate this work into more complex state-of-the-art PDF fitting pipelines.
528   This integration can give rise to further challenges, such as increased event dimensionality and more
529   complex simulators with detector and background effects. While we anticipate networks with greater
530   complexity and improvements to ensure adversarial training stability will be needed to accommodate
531   these challenges, the uncertainty distributions constructed through this framework will enable an
532   interpretable search of the possible parameter space in these large-scale problems.

      A    M ORE D ETAILED L ITERATURE R EVIEW
868
869
      Machine Learning Techniques in QCF/PDF analysis. A variety of works have investigated inte-
      grating machine learning into the QCF analysis pipeline in other ways. In Liu et al. (2022), neural
870
      networks are used to accelerate parameter space searching in PDF uncertainty calculations. Some
871
      works have proposed not developing analytical formulae to describe PDF behavior altogether and
872   instead parametrizing PDFs by neural networks. Some examples include Bacchetta et al. (2022),
873   Rojo (2006), and Dutrieux et al. (2022). A major open-source collection of work in using neural
874   networks to directly and flexibly extract PDFs can be found in the NNPDF Collaboration, which
875   encompasses some of the following highly useful papers in this area spanning over two decades:
876   Forte et al. (2002); Del Debbio et al. (2007); Ball et al. (2009; 2021); Del Debbio et al. (2022).
877
      Machine Learning-Based Surrogate Models for PDFs: why fit to a parametrized model? In
878   Hunt-Smith et al. (2022), the authors show that on a small example, entirely neural network-based
879   PDF extraction can aggravate uncertainty issues. Mitigating this uncertainty is an active area of
880   research among NNPDF works (Ball et al. (2022; 2024)). Moreover, there is already an expan-
881   sive amount of literature and well-developed mathematics for determining functions forms of PDFs
882   (Novikov et al. (2020); Courtoy (2024)), as well as existing collaborations such as the Jefferson Lab
883   Angular Momentum (JAM) Collaboration, from which we derive the code used for sampling
884   Mode collapse in GANs. Mode collapse is a frequently-observed and well-studied phenomenon in
885   empirical GAN training dynamics (Zhang et al. (2018); Bhagyashree et al. (2020); Salimans et al.
886   (2016)). We frequently encountered this in our own experiments, but were able to successfully
887   employ a wide variety of known GAN training techniques to avoid this pitfall. In particular, we train
888   the discriminator multiple times for each generator update, as is done in various prominent works
889   such as Gulrajani et al. (2017). A training schedule of discriminator parameter updates at a rate of
890   around 5 times more than generator parameter updates is considered common, and is thus the choice
891   that we employ by default in this work. A study of the sensitivity of resulting metrics with respect
892
      to this rate can be found later in the appendix.
893   Statistical divergence loss functions. We use the standard GAN implementation in this work,
894   which empirically accomplishes our needs for this application. However, other objective functions
895   derived from minimizing statistical divergences between output and target distributions have be-
896   come highly relied-upon, particularly in combating some of the instability issues mentioned earlier
897   and commonly observed in other works. One of the most popular alternatives is Wasserstein GAN
898
      (WGAN) (Arjovsky et al. (2017)), which minimizes the Wasserstein metric as a proxy for the Earth
      Mover Distance (Rubner et al. (1998)). The f -GAN paper (Nowozin et al. (2016)) provides a de-
899
      tailed investigation of GANs using various statistical f -divergences. For additional details on GAN
900
      training, we recommend Mescheder et al. (2018). In future work, changing the objectives for the
901   generator and discriminator networks in this paper can be handled by simple drop-in replacement
902   and may be worth further investigation for improved stability. We briefly investigated eliminating a
903   discriminator (and thus also surrogate) model altogether and instead training to minimize statistical
904   divergences (such as Kullback-Liebler (Kullback & Leibler (1951)) and Sinkhorn (Genevay et al.
905   (2018)), among others) between generated distributions and target data, but ultimately moved away
906   from this due to its lack of differentiability. However, it may be useful to return to this in future work
907   and again use a surrogate model to train a mapping from generated data distributions to a statistical
908   divergence loss or score.
