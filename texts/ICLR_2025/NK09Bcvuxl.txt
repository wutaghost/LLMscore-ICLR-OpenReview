009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Active Learning (AL) has gained prominence in integrating data-intensive ma-
012            chine learning (ML) models into domains with limited labeled data. However, its
013            effectiveness diminishes significantly when the labeling budget is especially low.
014            In this paper, we empirically verify the performance degradation of existing AL
015            algorithms in the extremely low-budget settings, and then introduce Direct Acqui-
016            sition Optimization (DAO), a novel AL algorithm that optimizes sample selec-
017
               tions based on expected true loss reduction. Specifically, DAO utilizes influence
               functions to update model parameters and incorporates an additional acquisition
018
               strategy to mitigate bias in loss estimation. This approach facilitates a more accu-
019
               rate estimation of the overall error reduction, without extensive computations or
020            reliance on labeled data. Experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of DAO in
021            both low and higher budget settings, outperforming state-of-the-arts approaches
022            across seven benchmarks.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
026   Active learning (AL) explores how adaptive data collection can reduce the amount of data needed
027   by machine learning (ML) models (Settles, 2009; Schröder & Niekler, 2020; Ren et al., 2021; Zhan
028   et al., 2022). It is particularly useful when labeled data is scarce or expensive to obtain, which
029   significantly limits the adaptability of modern deep learning (DL) models due to their data-hungry
030   nature (van der Ploeg et al., 2014). In these cases, AL algorithms selectively choose the most benefi-
031
      cial data points for labeling, thereby maximizing the effectiveness of the training process even if the
      data is limited in number. In fact, AL has been broadly applied in many fields (Adadi, 2021), such as
032
033
      medical image analysis (Budd et al., 2021), astronomy (Škoda et al., 2020), and physics (Ding et al.,
      2023), where unlabeled samples are plentiful but the process of labeling through human expert anno-
034
      tations or experiments is highly cost-intensive. In these contexts, judiciously selecting samples for
035
      labeling can significantly lower the expenses involved in compiling the datasets (Ren et al., 2021).
036
037   Many active learning algorithms have emerged over the past decades, with early seminal contribu-
038   tions from Lewis (1995); Tong & Koller (2001); Roy & McCallum (2001), and a shift that focuses
039
      more on deep active learning – a branch of AL that targets more towards DL models in more recent
      years (Huang, 2021). Depending on the optimization objective, AL algorithms can be classified into
040
      two categories. The first category includes heuristic objectives that are not exactly the same as the
041
      evaluation metric, i.e. error reduction. Examples in this category are diversity (Sener & Savarese,
042   2017), uncertainty (Gal et al., 2017), and hybrids of both (Ash et al., 2019). Second category in-
043   cludes criteria that is exactly the same as the evaluation metric, where notable approaches include
044   expected error reduction (EER) (Roy & McCallum, 2001) and its more recent follow-up works (Kil-
045   lamsetty et al., 2021; Mussmann et al., 2022).
046
      Despite the popularity of the first type of AL algorithms, existing works (Mittal et al., 2019; Ha-
047
      cohen et al., 2022) as well as our empirical analysis in Appendix A show that these methods often
048   suffer heavily in low-budget settings, where the total (accumulative) sampling quota is less than
049   1% of the number of unlabeled data points, making them less suitable for the extreme data scarcity
050   scenarios. In terms of the methods from the second category, their higher running time and reliance
051   on the availability of a validation or hold-out set remain significant limitations, constraining their
052   applicability in many data-scarcity scenarios as well. For example, EER (Roy & McCallum, 2001)
053   re-trains the classifier for each candidate with all its possible labels, where in each time also evalu-
      ates the updated model on all the unlabeled data, making its runtime intractable especially for deep

054
      neural networks. And GLISTER (Killamsetty et al., 2021), despite being much more computation-
055   ally efficient, requires a labeled, hold-out set for its sample selection process, formulated as a mixed
056   discrete-continuous bi-level optimization problem, to be optimized properly. While these constraints
057   might not be a huge limitation a few years ago, it poses a more important challenge currently as we
058   are adopting deep learning models to more areas, where labeled data may be extremely expensive
059   to acquire. More importantly, it is also worth noticing that under these scenarios, the highly limited
060   labeled data should have been better utilized for training than being reserved for AL algorithms.
061   Above limitations highlight a critical gap between the capabilities of current AL methodologies
062   and the urgent demands from real-world applications, underscoring the need for developing novel
063   AL strategies that can operate both relatively efficient while presenting little to none reliance on
064   the labeled set. To this end, we introduce Direct Acquisition Optimization (DAO), a novel AL
065   algorithm that selects new samples for labeling by efficiently estimating the expected loss reduc-
066   tion. Compared to EER and GLISTER, DAO solves the pain points of prohibitive running time
067   and the reliance on a separate labeled set through utilizing influence function (Ling, 1984) in model
068   parameters updates, and a more accurate, efficient unbiased estimator of loss reduction through
069
      importance-weighted sampling. In summary, DAO optimizes sample selections based on expected
      error reduction while operating efficiently through influence function-based model parameters ap-
070
      proximation and true overall reduced error estimation. Thorough experiments demonstrating DAO’s
071
      superior performance in the low-budget settings, out-performing current popular AL methods across
072   seven benchmarks.

074   2   R ELATED W ORK
075
      Active learning. AL has gained a lot of attraction in recent years, with its goal to achieve bet-
076
      ter model performance with fewer training data (Settles, 2009; Schröder & Niekler, 2020; Ren
077   et al., 2021). There have been different selection criteria including uncertainty, diversity, query-by-
078   committee, version space and information-theoretic heuristics (Liu et al., 2022; Zhan et al., 2022).
079   The uncertainty-based approaches are arguably the most popular and easiest to implement, which
080   includes selection criteria such as least confidence (Lewis, 1995), minimum margin (Scheffer et al.,
081   2001; Roth & Small, 2006; Citovsky et al., 2021), maximum entropy (Joshi et al., 2009; Settles,
082   2009) and others (Gal et al., 2017). At their core, these methods select points where the classi-
083   fier is least certain. However, uncertainty-based methods can be biased towards the current learner.
084   Diversity-based methods (Settles, 2009; Bilgic & Getoor, 2009; Guo, 2010; Luo et al., 2013; Elham-
085
      ifar et al., 2013; Mac Aodha et al., 2014; Yang et al., 2015; Sener & Savarese, 2017; Sinha et al.,
      2019; Agarwal et al., 2020; Wu et al., 2021), on the other hand, aim to select the most representa-
086
      tive samples of the dataset. In addition, query-by-committee (Seung et al., 1992; Abe, 1998) and
087
      version space-based (Mitchell, 1982) methods, keep a pool of models, and then select samples that
088   maximize the disagreements between them. Information-theoretic methods (Hoi et al., 2006; Barz
089   et al., 2018) typically utilize mutual information as the criterion. Hybrid method that combines both
090   uncertainty and diversity criteria, such as BADGE (Ash et al., 2019), has also been developed to
091   take advantage of both worlds. As shown later in the paper, we visually observe that the selections
092   of our proposed DAO, although not explicitly optimized towards any of these heuristics, display
093   characteristics of an hybrid approach.
094   EER-based acquisition criterion. Alternatively, EER was proposed to select new training exam-
095   ples that result in the lowest expected error on future test examples, which directly optimizes the
096   metric by which the model will be evaluated (Roy & McCallum, 2001). In essence, EER employs
097   sample selection based on the estimated impact of adding a new data point to the training set, rather
098   than evaluating performance against a separate validation set, meaning that it does not inherently
099   require a validation hold-out set. However, its necessity to retrain the model for every possible
100   candidate sample and every possible label renders its cost intractable in the context of deep neural
101   networks (Budd et al., 2021; Škoda et al., 2020; Ding et al., 2023). More recent look-ahead EER-
102   based AL algorithms (Mussmann et al., 2022) focus on addressing this efficiency concern. However,
103
      these methods either rely on a small set of validation data to be used for the evaluation of the ex-
      pected loss reduction (Killamsetty et al., 2021), or can still be quite slow when the size of labeled
104
      and unlabeled sets are large (Mohamadi et al., 2022). In this paper, we present DAO, a novel AL
105
      algorithm that improves upon EER through optimizations on both model updates as well as loss
106   estimation, efficiently and effectively broadening the applicability of EER-based algorithm.

114
      tical learning, to formulate the new candidate sample as a small perturbation to the ex-
115
      isting labeled set, so that the model parameters can be estimated without re-training;

238   3.4      E FFICIENT U NBIASED L OSS E STIMATION
239   Referring back to Eq. (1), the last challenge that we need to address is to gain access to the unbiased
240   true loss estimator Ltrue . In other words, we want to predict the true performance of ft|xi ,yi on the
241   unlabeled set Ut,i without exhaustive testing. Strictly, such evaluation cannot be drawn until ft|xi ,yi
242   is evaluated on the entire unlabeled set Ut,i . However, this is infeasible in practice.
243   Such approximation is typically carried out in other approaches (Killamsetty et al., 2021; Mussmann
244   et al., 2022) by randomly sampling a labeled validation set V at the beginning of the entire acqui-
245   sition process, which will later be used for evaluations in all the subsequent acquisition episodes.
246   Despite the simplicity as well as being i.i.d., which makes the estimated loss unbiased by nature,
247   this approximation method suffers from large variance as the size of V is usually much smaller than
248   U, which unavoidably hurts the acquisition performance. It is also contradictory to the goal of AL
249   in general, especially under the low-budget settings, as discussed in §1.
250   Different from the existing works, we propose to sample a subset C from current Lt−1 in each
251   acquisition round based on an alternative acquisition function, and then correct the bias in the loss
252   induced from this acquisition function. In the meantime, we also want to keep the variance low, so
253   that the final corrected loss enjoys both low bias and low variance, which is more preferable than the
254   zero bias but high variance that the random i.i.d. sampling has.
255   Specifically, continuing with the notations from §3.1, let C = {xt,1 , . . . , xt,m , . . . , xt,nC }, where
256   C ⊂ Ut−1 , be the subset containing nC samples selected for this true loss estimation at each round
257   t. Farquhar et al. (2021) shows that if xt,m is sampled in proportion to the true loss of each data

525   5.5    I NTERPRETING DAO WITH OTHER AL C RITERIA
526   In this section, we analyze the criterion optimized by DAO and compare it to common criteria such
527   as diversity and uncertainty, using visual representations of the data samples collected by DAO. The
528   detailed plots are available in Appendix C. Throughout multiple acquisition rounds, the data selected
529   by DAO demonstrate notable diversity with uniform distribution across the sample space. However,
530   in contrast to traditional uncertainty-based methods, selections within a single round by DAO also
531   incorporate elements of uncertainty. This hybrid approach explains the performance improvements
532   observed in §4 over algorithms that solely focus on diversity or uncertainty.
533
      6     C ONCLUSIONS
534
535   In this paper, we introduced Direct Acquisition Optimization, a novel algorithm designed to opti-
536   mize sample selections in low-budget settings. DAO hinges on the utilization of influence functions
537   for model parameter updates and a separate acquisition strategy to mitigate bias in loss estimation,
538   represents a significant optimization of the EER method and its modern follow-ups. Through em-
539
      pirical studies, DAO has demonstrated superior performance in both low and higher budget settings,
      outperforming existing methods by a significant margin across seven datasets.

758   A    L OW-B UDGET ACTIVE L EARNING : A M OTIVATING C ASE S TUDY
759
      In this section, we provide an empirical analysis to demonstrate that commonly used heuristic-
760
      based AL algorithms do not work well under very low-budget settings. Specifically, we analyze (1)
761
      uncertainty sampling methods including least confidence (Lewis, 1995), minimum margin (Scheffer
762   et al., 2001), maximum entropy (Settles, 2009), and Bayesian Active Learning by Disagreement
763   (BALD) (Gal et al., 2017); (2) diversity sampling methods such as Core-Set (Sener & Savarese,
764   2017) and Variational Adversarial Active Learning (VAAL) (Sinha et al., 2019); and (3) hybrid
765   method such as Batch Active learning by Diverse Gradient Embeddings (BADGE) (Ash et al., 2019).
766
      We test the above methods on the CIFAR10 (Krizhevsky et al., 2009) dataset starting with an initial
767
      labeled set with size |Linit | = 10, and conducted 50 acquisition rounds where after each round
768   B = 10 new samples are selected and labeled. We use ResNet-18 (He et al., 2016) as our training
769   model across all methods. And we repeated the acquisitions five times with different random seeds.
770   The results are visualized in Fig. 6, where we plot the relative performance between each method
771   and random sampling acquisition through a diverging color map.
