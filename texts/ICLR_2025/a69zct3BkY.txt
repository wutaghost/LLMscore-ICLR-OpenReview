009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Large language models (LLMs) have revolutionized knowledge storage and re-
012            trieval, but face challenges with conflicting and outdated information. Knowledge
013            editing techniques have been proposed to address these issues, yet they struggle
014            with robustness tests involving long contexts, paraphrased subjects, and continuous
015            edits. This work investigates the cause of these failures in locate-and-edit methods,
016            offering theoretical insights into their key-value modeling and deriving mathemat-
017            ical bounds for robust and specific edits, leading to a novel ’group discussion’
018
               conceptual model for locate-and-edit methods. Empirical analysis reveals that keys
               used by current methods fail to meet robustness and specificity requirements. To
019
               address this, we propose a Robust Edit Pathway (REP) that disentangles editing
020
               keys from LLMs’ inner representations. Evaluations on LLaMA2-7B and Mistral-
021            7B using the CounterFact dataset show that REP significantly improves robustness
022            across various metrics, both in-domain and out-of-domain, with minimal trade-offs
023            in success rate and locality. Our findings advance the development of reliable and
024            flexible knowledge updating in LLMs.

027   1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Large language models (LLMs, OpenAI 2023; Touvron et al. 2023a;b) have revolutionized the storage
030   and retrieval of vast amounts of human knowledge. However, their training on extensive internet-
031   based datasets inevitably leads to the incorporation of conflicting and rapidly outdated information,
032   potentially contributing to the phenomenon of model hallucination (Zhang et al., 2023). To mitigate
033
      such issues, researchers have proposed knowledge editing techniques (Sinitsin et al., 2020; De Cao
      et al., 2021), which employ carefully designed, constrained updates to specific parameters. While
034
      giving promising results (Mitchell et al., 2022a; Meng et al., 2023a), there still exist challenges in
035
      its implementation, particularly their susceptibility to failure with robustness tests, e.g., when faced
036   with long contexts, paraphrased subjects, and continuous edits (Ma et al., 2024c; Yang et al., 2024).
037   For example, in Figure 1, although one can edit the knowledge ‘Slovenia belongs to the continent of’
038   from ‘Europe’ to ‘Antarctica’, the model still falls into failure when the subject is rephrased to ‘The
039   Republic of Slovenia’.
040
      We aim to investigate the cause of such editing failures and further resolve them, with a specific focus
041
      on the widely recognized locate-and-edit methods (Meng et al., 2023a; Li et al., 2024a; Hu et al.,
042   2024b; Meng et al., 2023b), which use causal interventions to pinpoint factual knowledge in the early-
043   middle MLP layers of LLMs, treating these modules as associative key-value memories (Kohonen,
044   1972) and constructing new memories for editing. We give a new theoretical perspective into key-
045   value modeling, demonstrating that any value retrieved from the module can be viewed as a linear
046   combination of existing memories, where the weights are determined by similarities of the keys.
047   As a result, knowledge insertion can be viewed as a localized patch rather than a comprehensive
048   rewrite. We further provide a novel conceptual model, ‘group discussion’. In this model, we view
049   knowledge editing as inserting influence of a new voice among a mixture of opinion holders, and
050
      effective knowledge insertion requires a strong voice (i.e., large value) at the correct position (i.e.,
      similar keys) while balancing the risk of affecting nearby group (i.e., related knowledge).
051
052   In light of the group discussion view, we empirically analyze the keys used by locate-and-edit methods
053   and find they cannot follow the ideal scenario. In particular, we find that keys of robustness queries
      such as the same subject (e.g., Slovenia) with different surface forms (e.g., The Republic of Slovenia),

090
091   Knowledge Editing. As large language models have grown in complexity and size, post-
092   modification has become increasingly challenging due to their opaque mechanisms and vast parameter
093   spaces (Mitchell et al., 2022c; Zhong et al., 2023). This has led to heightened interest in knowledge
094   editing, a technique for precise model modification. Knowledge editing are applied to various sce-
095   narios, such as editing for safety (Wang et al., 2024c), debias (Yan et al., 2024) and concepts (Wang
096   et al., 2024d).
097   Knowledge editing approaches can be broadly categorized into two groups: those that preserve model
098   parameters and those that modify them (Yao et al., 2023). Parameter-preserving methods include
099   memory-based strategies (Mitchell et al., 2022d; Zhong et al., 2023; Hartvigsen et al., 2023) and
100   alternative approaches (Dong et al., 2022; Huang et al., 2023) incorporate additional parameters into
101   the model. On a different line of research, some work focuses on directly updating model parameters
102   to avoid cost of an external memory, which can be further divided into meta-learning methods (Cao
103   et al., 2021; Mitchell et al., 2022b; Tan et al., 2024) and locate-and-edit methods.
104   Our work is in line with the locate-then-edit methods, which draw much attention as they potentially
105   unveil how the knowledge are stored in an LLM. These approaches first identify relevant parameters
106   before updating them to modify specific knowledge, including KnowledgeNeuron’s attribution-based
107   neuron updating (Dai et al., 2022), ROME’s causal mediation analysis for MLP editing (Meng et al.,
      2023a), MEMIT’s multi-layer residual distribution (Meng et al., 2023b), PMET’s refined allocation

      Issues of Knowledge Editing. Despite the promise of knowledge editing for refining large language
135
      models, various challenges persist in practical applications. Edits often degrade general language
136
      abilities (Gu et al., 2024; Ma et al., 2024b), damage the hidden space (Wang et al., 2024b), struggle
137   to propagate to related facts (Hua et al., 2024), and are easily forgotten during sequential updates
138   (Gupta et al., 2024). Moreover, multi-hop reasoning can elicit old knowledge (Zhang et al., 2024),
139   and models may collapse after few edits (Yang et al., 2024; Brown et al., 2023).
140
      Further complications include cross-lingual inconsistencies (Wang et al., 2024a), knowledge conflicts
141
      (Li et al., 2024b), and inadequate evaluation in realistic settings such as long-form generation (Rosati
142
      et al., 2024) and neighborhood knowledge (Ma et al., 2024a). These issues underscore the need for
143   more sophisticated and comprehensive editing techniques. However, previous research largely remain
144   focused on the outcomes of knowledge editing in various scenarios, lacking a deeper understanding
145   of the underlying mechanisms of these algorithms and the true reasons behind their frequent failures.
146   Our work presents both theoretical and empirical understanding regarding the reason of robustness
147   failures of locate-and-edit methods, and proposes REP to enhance them.

      Group Discussion. The aforementioned lemma can be understood through a simple conceptual
293
      model. Imagine each piece of knowledge as a group of individuals engaged in a discussion. Each
294
      person has an existing key-value pair in the model, where the key represents their position and the
295   value represents the volume of their voice. When attempting to add new knowledge, it is necessary
296   to introduce an individual at the appropriate position (key) to influence the discussion. To impact
297   the conversation, this new individual must speak loudly —- both to counterbalance the voices of the
298   others and to ensure that those positioned further away can hear them. This scenario can be further
299   complicated by the presence of other nearby groups (representing distinct knowledge) that may be
300   in close proximity to the original group. Consequently, the heightened vocal projection of the new
301   participant has the potential to inadvertently influence the discussions occurring within these adjacent
302   groups.

      7    C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE D IRECTIONS
505
506
      In this work, we have presented a comprehensive analysis of the challenges faced by knowledge
      editing techniques in large language models, specifically focusing on the robustness issues in locate-
507
      and-edit methods. Our theoretical framework, centered on the ’group discussion’ model, provides
508
      valuable insights into the mechanisms of knowledge insertion and the requirements for robust and
509   specific edits.
510
511   The proposed Robust Edit Pathway (REP) addresses the key limitations identified in our empirical
512
      analysis, effectively disentangling editing keys from the LLM’s internal representations. Our extensive
      evaluations on LLaMA2-7B and Mistral-7B demonstrate significant improvements in robustness
513
      across various metrics, including rephrased subjects, shuffled token ordering, and long context
514
      additions, while maintaining high success rates and locality.
515
516   These results not only validate our theoretical foundations but also offer a promising direction for
517   enhancing the reliability and flexibility of knowledge editing in LLMs. The REP’s ability to generalize
518
      across different types of perturbations represents a substantial step forward in addressing the critical
      robustness issues prevalent in existing techniques.
519
520   Future work could explore the scalability of our approach to larger models and more diverse datasets,
521   as well as investigate potential applications in continual edits and dynamic knowledge updating for
522   LLMs. Overall, this research contributes to the ongoing efforts to create more adaptable and trustwor-
523   thy language models, paving the way for their responsible deployment in real-world applications.
