011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            In large vision-language models (LVLMs), images serve as inputs that carry a
014            wealth of information. As the idiom “A picture is worth a thousand words” im-
015
               plies, representing a single image in current LVLMs can require hundreds or even
               thousands of tokens. This results in significant computational costs, which grow
016
               quadratically as input image resolution increases, thereby severely impacting the
017
               efficiency of both training and inference. Previous approaches have attempted
018            to reduce the number of image tokens either before or within the early layers of
019            LVLMs. However, these strategies inevitably result in the loss of crucial image in-
020            formation, ultimately diminishing model performance. To address this challenge,
021            we conduct an empirical study revealing that all visual tokens are necessary for
022            LVLMs in the shallow layers, and token redundancy progressively increases in
023            the deeper layers of the model. To this end, we propose PyramidDrop, a visual
024            redundancy reduction strategy for LVLMs to boost their efficiency in both train-
025            ing and inference with neglectable performance loss. Specifically, we partition
026
               the LVLM into several stages and drop part of the image tokens at the end of each
               stage with a pre-defined ratio, creating pyramid-like visual tokens across model
027
               layers. The dropping is based on a lightweight similarity calculation with a neg-
028
               ligible time overhead. Extensive experiments demonstrate that PyramidDrop can
029            achieve a 40% training time and 55% inference FLOPs acceleration of LLaVA-
030            NeXT with comparable performance. Besides, the PyramidDrop could also serve
031            as a plug-and-play strategy for inference acceleration without training, with better
032            performance and lower inference cost than counterparts. We hope that the insights
033            and approach introduced by PyramidDrop will inspire future research to further
034            investigate the role of image tokens in LVLMs and explore additional methods to
035            enhance their efficiency.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
039
      In recent years, Large Vision-Language Models (LVLMs) have emerged as a central focus in deep
040
      learning research(Liu et al., 2024c; Dai et al., 2023; Bai et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024a; Chen
041
      et al., 2023a). We have witnessed remarkable progress across various application domains, including
042   image and video understanding(OpenAI, 2024; Gemini Team, 2023). The rapid development of
043   MLLMs is gradually paving the way for artificial intelligence to integrate into daily life(Li et al.,
044   2023c; Zhu et al., 2023a; Zhang et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024e).
045
      However, despite the advancements in large vision-language models (LVLMs), a significant chal-
046
      lenge lies in the escalating computational costs. Images, as continuous and information-rich signals,
047
      exhibit substantial spatial redundancy but are difficult to compress losslessly. It results in excessive
048   image tokens and a steep increase in training and inference costs, which becomes particularly pro-
049   nounced with higher image resolutions (Zhang et al., 2024a; Wang et al., 2024; Hu et al., 2024).
050   The number of image tokens increases quadratically with the resolution, driving the sequence length
051   into the tens of thousands(Li et al., 2023a). Given that the computational complexity of transform-
052   ers scales with sequence length, the associated computational costs become prohibitively high(Liu
053   et al., 2024a; Xu et al., 2024). Consequently, there is a pressing need to reduce the redundancy in
      visual information for more efficient LVLMs.

      Previous exploration of image token compression could be roughly categorized into two ideas: com-
055   pressing the token number before fed into the LVLM(Shang et al., 2024; Arif et al., 2024; Li et al.,
056   2023d; Yao et al., 2024) or dropping part of the tokens at the very shallow layer of the LVLM(Chen
057   et al., 2024a). However, both ideas inevitably hurt the performance of LVLMs: the former suffers
058   from the information loss introduced by their compression, and the latter drops part of the informa-
059   tion before the LVLMs fully understand them.
060
      To break through the limitations of the aforementioned ideas, we explore the nature of LVLMs in
061   understanding images from an intuitive question: are all image tokens necessary for all LVLM lay-
062   ers? We conduct an empirical study by removing different ratios of image tokens at different layers
063   of the LVLM at inference time and observing the benchmark performance change. As shown in Fig-
064   ure 1, the LVLMs are sensitive toward token dropping on shallow layers, regardless of the dropping
065   ratio. However, in deeper layers, image tokens gradually become less critical to the final results.
066   The results indicate that the LVLMs understand the image layer-by-layer and the redundancy within
067   image tokens increases correspondingly. We further visualize the attention between the instructions
068   and the image tokens, and we observed a consistent phenomenon that in shallow layers, the LVLMs
069
      pay attention to most image tokens to understand the image globally. With the layer increasing, it
      tends to focus on the few tokens that are related to the instruction and the rest are unnecessary.
070
071   Based on the observation, we introduce PyramidDrop, a simple yet effective image token reduction
072   strategy for LVLMs to accelerate both training and inference without performance loss. Pyramid-
073   Drop divides the LVLM into several stages, dropping a portion of the image tokens at the end of
074   each stage according to a predefined ratio. We employ a lightweight attention module to rank the
075
      image tokens, which incurs negligible overhead. With this design, we retain all image tokens in the
      shallow layers to avoid information loss, while progressively reducing the number of tokens as the
076
      layers deepen to maximize training and inference efficiency.
077
078   Extensive experiments verify the effectiveness and efficiency of our PyramidDrop. For example,
079   LLaVA-NeXT-7B (Liu et al., 2024b) trained with PyramidDrop could reduce training time by 40%
080   without sacrificing performance across 15 Vision-Language tasks. Moreover, PyramidDrop enables
081
      the LLaVA-NeXT model to be trained with doubled input resolution with only 269 GPU hours,
      which is 70% of the vanilla LLaVA-NeXT, and reaches a better performance on high-resolution
082
      benchmarks like DocVQA (Mathew et al., 2021) and InfoVQA (Mathew et al., 2022). Furthermore,
083
      PyramidDrop can function as a plug-and-play strategy for inference acceleration, offering enhanced
084   model performance and fewer FLOPs than FastV (Chen et al., 2024a).

089   Token Reduction The large language model (LLM) realm has made several efforts in applying
090   token reduction for inference acceleration and KV cache compression(Han et al., 2023). Stream-
091   LLM(Xiao et al., 2023) only keeps attention sinks and the most recent tokens to reduce the size of
092   the KV cache. FastGen(Ge et al., 2023) introduces an adaptive KV cache management approach
093   that optimizes memory usage by adjusting retention strategies according to the specific properties
094
      of attention heads. Heavy-Hitter Oracle (H2O)(Zhang et al., 2024b) employs a strategy that se-
      lectively prunes key-value pairs (KVs) during generation, utilizing a scoring mechanism driven by
095
      cumulative attention to inform the removal process. ScissorHands(Liu et al., 2024d) concentrates on
096
      identifying and retaining important tokens that show a consistent pattern of attention weight across
097   previous token windows during generation. These works attempt to address the redundancy of text
098   tokens during the inference process in LLMs. As for visual tokens, existing works (Liang et al.,
099   2022; Kong et al., 2022; Cao et al., 2023; Shi et al., 2024; Xiong et al., 2024) make explorations
100   on Vision Language Models (VLMs) before the era of large vision-language models, focusing on
101   token reduction for vision transformers (ViTs). A recent work, FastV (Chen et al., 2024a), makes
102   an early attempt at visual token reduction in LVLMs, which drops visual tokens at the second layer
103   of LVLMs during inference. In contrast, our work makes a more comprehensive study of the visual
104   redundancy in LVLMs and proposes a pyramid visual token reduction solution for both training and
105
      inference of LVLMs.

                 203
                       Previous research on image token compression typically drops image tokens before passing them to
                 204   the language model or uses a fixed compression ratio across all language model layers. However,
                 205   as we analyzed in Sec 3.1, redundancy is not consistent across different layers. Redundancy of
                 206   image tokens is relatively minimal in the shallow layers and becomes progressively larger in deeper
                 207   layers. Thus, uniformly compressing image tokens across layers may lead to the loss of valuable
                 208   information in the shallow layers while retaining unnecessary redundancy in the deeper layers.
                 209
                       Inspired by this observation, we propose PyramidDrop, which fully leverages layer-wise redundancy
                 210   to compress image tokens. The pipeline of the proposed PyramidDrop is illustrated in Figure 2. To
                 211   maximize training efficiency while preserving the essential information of the image tokens, we
                 212   choose to divide the forward pass of the LLM into multiple stages. In the shallow layers, we retain
                 213   a higher proportion of image tokens to preserve the entire vision information. At the end of each
                 214   stage, we partially drop the image tokens, until nearly all the image tokens being eliminated in the
                 215   deeper layers. This approach allows us to optimize training efficiency while maintaining critical
                       information.

      Pyramid Visual Redundancy Reduction. We partition the language into S = {sn }Sn=0 stages,
      and remove the image tokens v with a pre-defined ratio λ at the end of each stage. Formally, with
228
      the image tokens vsn as the input of stage sn , we remove ⌈(1 − λ)|vsn |⌉ tokens from the vsn and
229
      treat the rest image tokens as the next stage input vsn+1 .
230
231   Following our observation in Sec 3.1, the attention value between image and text tokens could
232   reflect the image token importance properly, so we based on it to realize the drop operation. With
233
      the concern of calculation efficiency and training-inference consistency, we calculate the attention
      between all the image tokens and the last token of the instruction (we denote it as tIj , the last-
234
      instruction token in the following).
235
236   Formally, we denote the last layer of stage sn as Fj , we obtain key states of the image tokens as kjv
237   and the query state of last instruction token qjtI with the following operation:
238
239                                          kjv = Kj (vj ),       qjtI = Qj (tIj ).                     (2)

      where Qj , Kj are the query matrix and the key matrix reused from the self-attention block of Fj .

      Efficiency Analysis of PyramidDrop Here we analyze the efficiency from two parts: the compu-
250
      tation overhead introduced by PyramidDrop, and the input sequence computation cost economized
251   by PyramidDrop.
252
253   The extra computation cost introduced by PyramidDrop mainly lay in the similarity computing for
254
      image token ranking. Benefiting from our design, the calculation is only between a query toke and
      Vs image tokens, so its computation complexity is O(n) and only S −1 times in the forward process.
255
      Further, we notice the importance of FalshAttention in practice, so we keep using it during training
256
      and extract the query and key token from the original forward to calculate our lightweight similarity
257   matrix.
258
259   When it comes to the computation cost economized by PyramidDrop. With the consideration of
260
      FlashAttn (Dao et al., 2022), we roughly define the forward inference cost of a layer with N image
      tokens as a linear function with a constant factor c that c · L, so the overall computation cost of an
261
      LVLM with L layers is c · N · L. When using PyramidDrop with S stages and the ratio λ, the overall
262
      computation cost is:
263
                                               1 − λS

455   4.3     E FFICIENT OF P YRAMID D ROP IN I NFERENCE
456
457   PyramidDrop outperforms SOTA methods as a inference-only strategy . As illustrated in Ta-
458
      ble 5, we directly apply the multi-stage compression strategy during the inference phase of the
      vanilla model, comparing it with the inference acceleration approach, FastV. The results on LLaVA-
459
      Next demonstrate that our method significantly outperforms FastV across various critical bench-
460
      marks. Specifically, we achieve an impressive score of 1533.0 on MME, surpassing Fastv by 2.5%,
461   while also exceeding it by 0.9% on both POPE and GQA. Notably, the advantages of our method
462   become even more pronounced in high-resolution benchmarks. For instance, on the relatively chal-
463   lenging DocVQA, our approach outperforms FastV by 3.5%, and on ChartQA and TextVQA, we
464   achieve improvements of 3.2% and 1% respectively.
465
      Results from LLaVA-1.5 reveal similar trends across multiple benchmarks, including MME, Sci-
466
      enceQA, and MMBenchCN, where our method not only demonstrates superior performance but
467   also achieves a greater reduction in FLOPs. When compared to the baseline, our approach consis-
468   tently reaches comparable performance levels across most benchmarks, while effectively mitigat-
469   ing information loss in high-resolution benchmarks. These findings indicate that FastV’s premature
470   compression of image tokens leads to inevitably image information loss and significant performance
471   declines in many benchmarks, whereas our multi-stage compression strategy preserves critical in-
472   formation from image tokens while maximizing the elimination of redundancy. The observation is
473   also consistent with our finding in Sec 3.1 that in shallow layers, most image tokens are critical for
474   LVLMs to understand the image properly, while in the deep layers, most of them are redundant for
475
      the LVLMs.
476

      PyramidDrop enjoys a better trade-off between performance and inference cost. We further
      compare PyramidDrop and FastV under a precise FLOPs-constrained setting with LLaVA-NeXT-
478
      7B. In practice, we adjust the drop rate of FastV and the λ of our PyramidDrop to control the model
479
      inference FLOPs and evaluate the model benchmark performance. As the FLOPs-performance curve
480   shown in Figure 4, our PyramidDrop consistently outperforms FastV under different settings and
481   across diverse benchmarks. For example, under a constraint of 12 TFLOPs, PyramidDrop outper-
482   forms FastV with 3.0% on DocVQA and 2.6% on ChartQA. When we reduce the inference cost
483   to only 8 TFLOPs, the performance gap increases, with PyramidDrop surpassing FastV by 6% on
484   DocVQA, and 5.9% on ChartQA. The results further prove that our multi-stage redundant reduction
485   strategy matches the nature of LVLMs and enables the model to understand the image better under
      constrained inference cost.
