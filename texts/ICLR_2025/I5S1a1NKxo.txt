009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Vision-language models (VLMs) have emerged as extremely strong zero-shot and
012            few-shot image classifiers, performing on par with task-specific models. How-
013            ever, they can be unnecessarily heavy-weight for task-specific downstream appli-
014            cations. While existing lines of work have successfully compressed VLMs and
015            other foundation models to varying degrees, most focus on preserving the gener-
016            ality of these models, rather than leveraging their power for a particular task. In
017
               this work, we focus on the setting in which we have a limited amount of data on
               a downstream image classification task and a limited inference budget. To satisfy
018
               these constraints, we focus on distilling the strong few-shot performance of CLIP
019
               on image classification tasks into a more efficient model. We introduce the SID-
020            CLIP (Synthesize-Initialize-Distill CLIP) method and highlight its three compo-
021            nents that are critical to obtaining strong performance: 1) augmenting the classifier
022            with synthetic data generated by leveraging CLIP itself; 2) initializing the model-
023            ing process using a smaller CLIP model pretrained on the target architecture; and
024            3) incorporating knowledge distillation to maximally mimic the performance of
025            the larger model. Our set of proposed strategies produces a compact model that
026            performs within 16% and 10% of CLIP’s linear probe performance on 1 and 8 shot
027            datasets respectively, while using a model with less than 2% of the parameters of
028
               CLIP’s image encoder. We hope our work can be useful as a practical guide for
               leveraging the power of foundation models in downstream data-scarce and budget
029
               constrained settings.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Foundation models such as CLIP-based models have been shown to perform extremely well on zero-
035   shot and few-shot image classification: via simple prompting and/or a few examples, these models
036   can achieve classification performance on-par with models trained with much more task-specific
037   data (Radford et al., 2021). However, this performance comes at a cost: the models are extremely
038   general and large-scale, and thus incur a high inference cost relative to smaller, more task-specific
039   models, making them unsuitable for many edge applications. This challenge has led to a number of
040
      methods for compressing or distilling knowledge from large foundation models into smaller models.
      Although these techniques can preserve strong performance relative to the large foundation model,
041
      they are often not task-specific, and when they are, they often focus on preserving the model’s zero-
042
      shot performance, rather than being able to take advantage of limited task-specific downstream data
043   (Popp et al., 2024; Li et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2023; Vasu et al., 2024; Sun et al., 2023).
044
045   In this work, our goal is to produce a small model that performs as close as possible to a powerful
046
      large-scale vision-language model (VLM) on a particular downstream task. We address the specific
      challenge of attempting to distill the strong performance of zero- and few-shot CLIP image classi-
047
      fication models into vastly more efficient (but task-specific) architectures. In other words, given a
048
      very limited amount of data on a desired downstream image classification task, and a very limited
049   inference-time compute budget, we obtain the best performance on a downstream compact model
050   by leveraging the capabilities of larger models. In practice, we find that three separate components
051   are central to obtaining strong performance:
052
053        1. We augment the classifier with synthetic data generated by leveraging CLIP itself. Specif-
              ically, we use a text-to-image generative model seeded with embeddings produced from

      2                    R ELATED W ORKS
091
      Synthetic data. There has been lots of evidence to indicate that synthetic data is helpful in general
092
      when training models and particularly in distillation. Azizi et al. (2023) find that augmentation of
093
      a dataset with synthetic data improves image classification performance on CNN and ViT architec-
094   tures. He et al. (2023) focuses on the zero- and few-shot domains and reaches a similar conclusion:
095   that synthetic data can be used in conjunction with real data to improve performance on image clas-
096   sification tasks. Similarly to our work, Popp et al. (2024) generate synthetic data in order to perform
097   distillation. This work differs from ours in two notable ways: they assume no access to the down-
098   stream data, rather than few samples; and the aim to transfer the general zero-shot capabilities of
099   CLIP rather than focusing on a particular downstream task. In general, the field of data-free distilla-
100   tion explores the usage of only synthetic data (no real data) during the distillation process (Chawla
101   et al., 2021; Fang et al., 2022).
102   While these directions all incorporate synthetic data, none utilize the particular image- and text-
103   conditioning generation method that we use in SIDCLIP. The image generation pipeline we use was
104   introduced in Razzhigaev et al. (2023) and achieved SOTA FID scores (a metric to assess the quality
105   of generated images) on generated images relative to other open source models.
106
107   Small CLIP models. Several previous works attempt to compress the information in CLIP by
      reducing the size of the image encoder via distillation to a smaller CLIP model or to a novel CLIP-


                                                                                                     2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      like architecture (Wu et al., 2023; Vasu et al., 2024; Popp et al., 2024). In our work, we replace
109   CLIP’s image encoder with a new vision model, namely an EfficientNet B0 (Tan & Le, 2020).
110
111
      Compression. VLMs have remarkable few- and zero-shot performance on downstream tasks and
112
      are strong image classifiers (Radford et al., 2021; Jia et al., 2021; Li et al., 2022; Yuan et al., 2021;
113
      Zhai et al., 2023). It is a natural next step to attempt to compress these high powered models
114   into smaller versions that require less memory and have lower inference times. There has been a
115   range of work in compressing foundation models, some (pruning, quantization, distillation) mirrors
116   compression in non-foundation models, while others (parameter-efficient fine-tuning such as adapter
117   layers or prompt tuning) are unique to the VLM or LLM setting (Hinton et al., 2015; Dettmers et al.,
118   2022; Frantar & Alistarh, 2023; Sun et al., 2024; Houlsby et al., 2019; Liu et al., 2022; Lester et al.,
119   2021; Jia et al., 2022).
120   In many of the existing efforts to compress foundation models, the goal has been to preserve the
121   general capabilities of the models. Rather than honing in on a model’s performance on a particular
122   task, these methods aim to broadly preserve CLIP’s generalizaiton abilities for image classification
123   (Li et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2023; Vasu et al., 2024; Sun et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2022).
124
      Li et al. (2023) distills from a CLIP ViT-L/14 teacher to a convolutional network student such as
125
      ResNet18. They measure task-specific performance as out-of-distribution performance: they per-
126   form distillation without any of the task-specific samples and then evaluate the zero- or few-shot
127   performance of their model on downstream tasks. While similar to our setting, this setting does not
128   take advantage of task-specific data during distillation and thus yields lower performance than our
129   method. Although this allows for flexibility with downstream tasks, it is not the most advantageous
130   when the downstream task is known ahead of time.
131
      TinyCLIP and MobileCLIP both preserve CLIP’s general purpose knowledge through distillation
132   (Wu et al., 2023; Vasu et al., 2024). TinyViT is another method which produces a small downstream
133   model via distillation (Wu et al., 2022). Task-specificity is not part of the distillation process for any
134   of these methods.
135
      Sun et al. (2023), like us, distill from CLIP ViT-L/14 to a smaller foundation model, and find that
136
      this distilled model outperforms a similar model trained from scratch. However, their smallest model
137
      (Swin-T) is over 5x larger than our model and they only report zero-shot numbers.
138
139
      Few-shot learning. While preserving the entirety of CLIP’s performance is a worthwhile goal,
140
      it is not the correct focus for all settings. The few-shot setting, when there is limited downstream
141
      training data available, arises in situations where data collection is expensive or challenging (Wang
142   et al., 2020). Training large-scale models from scratch is an extremely data-intensive process, so
143   usage of few-shot data to finetune an existing model can increase accessibility and customization of
144   the power of models like VLMs. While there is some work that addresses a few-shot downstream
145   setting, it is often done independently of the goal of compression, thus making these approaches not
146   as feasible of solutions for resource-constrained users (Ma et al., 2024; Wortsman et al., 2022; Islam
147   et al., 2021). Some lines of work, such as Popp et al. (2024), focus on the zero-shot setting, but leave
148   out few-shot results. If some downstream task-specific training data is available, these methods are
149   not equipped to best utilize it.

      3    T HE SIDCLIP M ETHOD
152
153
      To use CLIP as an image classifier, first an image is passed into the image encoder, and text of the
154
      possible classnames is passed into the text encoder. Then, the embedding similarity between the
155
      image and possible classnames is measured. Although this process yields high accuracy on a variety
156   of downstream tasks, the CLIP model is unnecessarily large and cumbersome for many downstream
157   applications, such as use on edge devices. The best-performing and largest CLIP model, CLIP ViT-
158   L/14, has 307M parameters in its image encoder Radford et al. (2021). But what if a user does not
159   need the full “general-purpose” processing power of CLIP? They may want to take advantage of the
160   off-the-shelf zero and few shot performance, but only need to classify images corresponding to a
161   specific task and cannot afford to run such a large model. In this case, it is desirable to transfer a
      specific portion of CLIP’s image classification capabilities to a smaller model.

184   With existing methods, a user would be able to produce a general-purpose small model, and poten-
185   tially finetune it on the task of interest, but is left without being able to optimally take advantage of
186   the limited training data they have. They would end up with a smaller version of CLIP, not a model
187
      tailored to their specific use case.
188
189   Problem setting. Suppose we are in the setting where we have access to a large-scale teacher
190
      VLM T , such as CLIP. We have a small model architecture S that fits certain budget constraints.
      Our goal is to maximize image classification performance using S on some downstream task T.
191
      However, we only have k labeled samples per class c ∈ C, for n = |C| classes, on task T.
192
193   Our method, SIDCLIP, consists of three essential components for leveraging CLIP’s power in train-
194   ing a small model in a data-constrained setting. These three components are 1) synthetic data, 2)
195   initializing the model as a small CLIP variant, and 3) distilling from CLIP to the small model.
196
197   3.1   C OMPONENT #1: S YNTHETIC DATA
198
199
      We use synthetic data to augment the limited samples per class in a few shot setting. As described in
      our problem setting, we have k labeled samples per class. We use these k × n samples Dr and their
200
      classname labels L to generate additional synthetic samples that can be used for training the model.
201
      When operating in the k-shot setting, we only use those k samples and their classnames as input to
202   generate additional synthetic data.
203

      img enc(xi ) and text enc(lc ) and combine them via a weighted combination:

      Concretely, we use the Kandinsky framework, which takes as input real images and captions (Raz-
217   zhigaev et al., 2023). This pipeline obtains CLIP embeddings for each image and caption, combines
218   them according to specified weights, and passes the joint embedding into the diffusion model to
219   produce a synthetic sample. We utilize this pipeline due to its high performance and flexibility: it
220   achieved strong FID scores relative to competitors and was the first text-to-image generative model
221   that used both image priors and latent diffusion.
222
223   3.2   C OMPONENT #2: I NITIALIZE AS S MALL CLIP
224
225   We find that initializing a student model in a CLIP-style architecture allows for performance gains
226   relative to a standalone student vision model. In this paper, we distill to the EfficientNet B0 model,
227   a small convolutional network with around 5.3M parameters (Tan & Le, 2020). For our primary
228   set of experiments, we initialize this model as a small CLIP variant, that is preserving the CLIP
229   text encoder and replacing the CLIP image encoder with an EfficientNet B0 model. This setup is
230
      pretrained on a subset of DataComp corresponding to 896M samples (Gadre et al., 2023).
231
232   3.3   C OMPONENT #3: K NOWLEDGE D ISTILLATION
233
234
      Knowledge distillation is a common model compression technique that uses a large, powerful
      teacher model to train a smaller student model by aligning the student’s output probabilities to those
235
      of the teacher. There are many variants of loss functions used to align these sets of probabilities, but
236
      the most common is based on the KL divergence as proposed in Hinton et al. (2015):
237
238                       LKL = α · T 2 · DKL (SM (ỹ), SM (ŷ)) + (1 − α) · CE(ŷ, y)
239
240   where DKL refers to KL divergence, CE refers to cross entropy, SM refers to softmax, ỹ is the
241
      teacher output probabilities, ŷ is the student output probabilities, y is the true labels, α is a hyperpa-
      rameter that trades off influence from teacher labels vs true labels, and T is a temperature parameter.
242
243   We use this standard KL setting in our experiments. We have a teacher image encoder which outputs
244   image embeddings of size dTimg and a student image encoder which outputs image embeddings of
245   size dSimg . We also have a common text encoder which produces text embeddings of size dtext .
246
247
      For each task, we append a linear layer of shape dTimg × c to the teacher image encoder and a similar
248   layer of shape dSimg × c to the student model. Before distillation, we finetune the teacher linear layer
249
      on the task of interest. We initialize the student layer with the text embeddings of each class: we
      obtain the embeddings for each caption "A photo of {classname}." or "A photo of
250
      {classname}, a photo of {category}." and concatenate them into a tensor of shape
251
      dtext × c where dSimg = dtext . Then, during distillation, the teacher and its appended layer are
252   frozen, and both the student and its appended layer are updated.
253
254   When performing distillation, we use a distillation set consisting of the real samples Dr and the
255
      synthetic samples Ds(r) generated using only those corresponding real images: D = Dr ∪ Ds(r) .

257   4     R ESULTS
258
259   We demonstrate that SIDCLIP allows us to approach the performance of CLIP ViT-L/14 while using
260   an image encoder with less than 2% of the parameters. Each of the three components (synthesize,
261   initialize, distill) is critical in achieving this strong performance. Through a series of ablations and
262   comparisons to SOTA distillation methods, we show that SIDCLIP is the dominant method when
263   operating in a low inference budget, low data regime.

      4.2   M AIN R ESULTS
325
326   Table 1 shows a comparison of SIDCLIP to several relevant baselines. For each dataset, we include
327   an “upper-bound” result: CLIP ViT-L/14, which is the teacher used in all experiments. We then
328   include the baseline of a standalone EfficientNetB0 (ENB0) finetuned on the k-shot dataset. Each
329   subsequent row adds one of the SIDCLIP elements: “+D” adds distillation, “+ I” adds initialization,
330   and “+S” adds 300 synthetic samples in addition to the real samples.
331   We additionally include comparisons to two baseline methods: TinyCLIP and TinyViT (Wu et al.,
332   2022; 2023). These methods use distillation to train a downstream image classification model. Un-
333   like our method, which allows for specialization on a specific task, these methods focus on maintain-
334   ing CLIP’s overall performance. Additionally, few-shot results are not reported in these papers. For
335   TinyCLIP, we ran few-shot linear probe experiments on the smallest available model (8M parame-
336   ter image encoder). For TinyViT, we ran few-shot finetuning experiments on the smallest available
337   model (5.4M parameters). Additional comparisons are included in section 4.3.
338   Our goal was to leverage the power of CLIP to produce a strong small-scale model, using only lim-
339   ited training data. These results indicate that, using each of our 3 components (synthesize, intialize,
340   distill), we are able to obtain a notable performance increase of around +50% higher than the starting
341   model, with performance that approaches that of the teacher CLIP model. Our method consistently
342   outperforms variations which do not include all three components as well as few-shot finetuning of
343   existing SOTA distillation methods.
344   On the Cars and Flowers datasets, SIDCLIP consistently achieves within 10% of CLIP’s perfor-
345   mance in the few shot setting. On Food, SIDCLIP remains 20-30% below the teacher model. We
346   hypothesize that this may be due to more instances of food in the pretraining datasets for both teacher
347   and student. In this case, additional food examples do not add much information to the model.

      5   C ONCLUSION
476
477
      We present the SIDCLIP (Synthesize-Initialize-Distill CLIP) method, which achieves SOTA per-
      formance in a budget-constrained, data-scarce, task-specific setting. Our method achieves the best
478
      few-shot performance on StanfordCars, OxfordFlowers, and Food101 by leveraging the power of a
479
      large-scale VLM, in this instance, CLIP. The three components of the SIDCLIP method are 1) aug-
480   menting the limited training data with task-specific synthetic data generated by using linear combi-
481   nations of the CLIP image and text embeddings of existing real data; 2) initializing the small model
482   as a CLIP-style model; and 3) using knowledge distillation to transfer more fine-grained classifica-
483   tion information from a powerful teacher. In settings with limited data and inference-time compute,
484   SIDCLIP outperforms baselines such as TinyCLIP and TinyViT.
