                                                   A BSTRACT
010
011
               Designing control policies whose performance level is guaranteed to remain above
012            a given threshold in a span of environments is a critical feature for the adoption
013            of reinforcement learning (RL) in real-world applications. The search for such
014            robust policies is a notoriously difficult problem, related to the so-called dynamic
015            model of transition function uncertainty, where the environment dynamics are
016            allowed to change at each time step. But in practical cases, one is rather interested
017            in robustness to a span of static transition models throughout interaction episodes.
018            The static model is known to be harder to solve than the dynamic one, and seminal
019            algorithms, such as robust value iteration, as well as most recent works on deep
020
               robust RL, build upon the dynamic model. In this work, we propose to revisit
               the static model. We suggest an analysis of why solving the static model under
021
               some mild hypotheses is a reasonable endeavor, based on an equivalence with the
022
               dynamic model, and formalize the general intuition that robust MDPs can be solved
023            by tackling a series of static problems. We introduce a generic meta-algorithm
024            called IWOCS, which incrementally identifies worst-case transition models so as to
025            guide the search for a robust policy. Discussion on IWOCS sheds light on new ways
026            to decouple policy optimization and adversarial transition functions and opens new
027            perspectives for analysis. We derive a deep RL version of IWOCS and demonstrate
028            it is competitive with state-of-the-art algorithms on classical benchmarks.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      One major obstacle in the way of real-life deployment of reinforcement learning (RL) algorithms is
      their inability to produce policies that retain, without further training, a guaranteed level of efficiency
034
      when controlling a system that somehow differs from the one they were trained upon. This property
035
      is referred to as robustness, by opposition to resilience, which is the ability to recover, through
036   continued learning, from environmental changes. For example, when learning control policies for
037   aircraft stabilization using a simulator, it is crucial that the learned controller be able to control
038   a span of aircraft configurations with different geometries, or masses, or in various atmospheric
039   conditions. Depending on the criticality of the considered application, one will prefer to optimize the
040   expected performance over a set of environments (thus weighting in the probability of occurrence of
041   a given configuration) or, at the extreme, optimize for the worst case configuration. Here, we consider
042   such worst case guarantees and revisit the framework of robust Markov Decision Processes (MDPs)
043   (Iyengar, 2005).
044   Departing from the common perspective which views robust MDPs as two-player games, we investi-
045   gate whether it is possible to solve them through a series of non-robust problems. The two-player
046   game formulation is called the dynamic model of transition function uncertainty, as an adversarial
047   environment is allowed to change the transition dynamics at each time step. The solution to this game
048   can be shown to be equivalent, for stationary policies and rectangular uncertainty sets, to that of the
049   static model, where the environment retains the same transition function throughout the time steps.
050   Our first contribution is a series of arguments which cast the search for a robust policy as a resolution
051   of the static model (Section 2). We put this formulation in perspective of recent related works in
052   robust RL (Section 3). Then, we introduce a generic meta-algorithm which we call IWOCS for
053   Incremental Worst-Case Search (Section 4). IWOCS builds upon the idea of incrementally identifying
      worst case transition functions and expanding a discrete uncertainty set, for which a robust policy

057   state-of-the art robust deep RL algorithms (Section 5).

      6       C ONCLUSION
471
472
473   The search for robust policies in uncertain MDPs is a long-standing challenge. In this work, we
474   proposed to revisit the static model of transition function uncertainty, which is equivalent to the
475   dynamic model in the case of sa-rectangular uncertainty sets and stationary policies. We proposed
476   to exploit this equivalence and the no-duality-gap property to design an algorithm that trades the
477
      resolution of a two-player game, for a sequence of one-player MDP resolutions. This led to the
      IWOCS (Incremental Worst-Case Search) meta-algorithm, which incrementally builds a discrete, non-
478
      sa-rectangular uncertainty set and a sequence of candidate robust policies. An instance of IWOCS,
479
      using SAC for policy optimization, and CMA-ES or grid-search for worst-case search, appeared as a
480   relevant method on popular robust RL benchmarks, and outperformed the state-of-the-art algorithms
481   on a number of environments. IWOCS proposes a new perspective on the resolution of robust MDPs
482   and robust RL problems, which appears as a competitive formulation with respect to traditional
483   methods. It also poses new questions, like the tradeoffs between policy optimization precision and
484   overall robustness, gradient-based methods for worst-case search, bounds due to approximate value
485   functions, or validity of using Qi as a surrogate of the robust value function for the Ti uncertainty set.
      All these open interesting avenues for future research.

      E    W INDY- WALK GRIDWORLD
710
      The windy-walk environment used in Section 4 is a discrete grid-world environment illustrated in
711
      Figure 2. It features 36 discrete states corresponding to positions on the grid, and 4 discrete actions
712   corresponding to cardinal moves. Six states are unreachable and correspond to walls, defining three
713   corridors. The transition model is deterministic by default, except in the corridors where the wind
714   blows. This transition model is parameterized by a scalar parameters α. In the Northern corridor:
715
716          • the W action moves West with probability 1,
717          • the N and S actions leave the position unchanged with probability 1 ´ α and the agent is
718            pushed West with probability α,
719          • the E action moves East with probability 1 ´ α and West with probability α.
720
721   The middle corridor works the same way, but with probability α3 instead of α. In the Southern
722
      corridor:
723          • the W action moves West with probability 1,
724
             • the N (resp. S) action move the agent respectively North (resp. South) with probability
725
               1 ´ α6 (unless it runs into a wall in which case the position is unchanged), and West with
726
               probability α6 ,
727
             • the E action moves East with probability 1 ´ α6 and West with probability α6 .
728
729   Rewards are -1 for all transitions and the G state is an absorbing goal states yielding zero reward.
730   The agent always starts in state S. Consequently, windy-walk is a stochastic shortest path. For small
731   values of α, the optimal policy is to go straight from S to G, but as α increases, the wind blows
732   harder, and it becomes more interesting to make a detour through the middle then Southern corridors.
733   The corresponding robust MDP problem features an uncertainty set spanned by 25 discrete values of
734   α, uniformly distributed in r0, 0.5s.
735   The instance of IWOCS evaluated in Section 4 uses value iteration as a policy optimization algorithm
736   and a brute-force grid search as a search method for worst-case transition functions, as summarized
737   in Algorithm 1. In Algorithm 1 we abusively write Tα the transition model parameterized by α.
738

739
      0.95. Monte-Carlo estimates of VTπi ps0 q use 300 independent rollouts (of length at most 104 ) from
