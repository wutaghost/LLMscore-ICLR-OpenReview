009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011              The in-context learning paradigm with LLMs has been instrumental in advancing
012              applications that require complex reasoning over natural language. An optimal
013              selection of few-shot examples (exemplars) is essential for constructing effective
014              prompts under a limited budget. In this paper, we frame the problem of exemplar
015              selection for In-Context Reasoning (ICR) as a top-m best arms identification prob-
016              lem. A key challenge in this context is the exponentially large number of arms that
017
                 need to be evaluated to identify the m-best arms. We propose CASE (Challenger
                 Arm Sampling for Exemplar selection), a novel selective exploration strategy that
018
                 maintains a shortlist of “challenger” arms, which are current candidates for the
019
                 top-m arms. In each iteration, only the arms from this shortlist and the current
020              top-m set are pulled, thereby reducing sample complexity and, consequently, the
021              number of LLM evaluations. Furthermore, we model the scores of exemplar subsets
022              (arms) using a parameterized linear scoring function, leading to a stochastic linear
023              bandits setting. In this setting, CASE identifies the top-m arms with significantly
024              fewer evaluations than existing state-of-the-art methods. CASE effectively works
025              with black box LLMs and selects a static set of few-shot examples, resulting in
026              an extremely efficient scheme for in-context reasoning. The exemplars selected
027              with CASE show surprising performance gains of up to 15.19% compared to
028
                 state-of-the-art exemplar selection methods. We release our code and data 1 .

      In-context learning (ICL) and Chain-of-Thought (COT) have emerged as important techniques for
033
      enhancing the capabilities of large language models (LLMs) across a range of tasks like question
034   answering and complex reasoning. ICL allows LLMs to perform tasks by conditioning on a context
035   that includes examples or instructions, without the need for additional fine-tuning, making it flexible
036   and adaptable. COT facilitates complex reasoning by decomposing tasks into intermediate steps, and
037   employing rationales explaining the reasoning process to the LLM. However, one key challenge in
038   maximizing the effectiveness of ICL is the careful selection of few-shot examples along with their
039   corresponding rationales (Lu et al., 2022; Zhao et al., 2021).
040   The inclusion of rationales alongside standard training examples has been found to be especially
041   important for complex reasoning tasks, such as multistep reasoning-based QA (Geva et al., 2021b;
042   Chen et al., 2022b; Lu et al., 2023b). We refer to the triplet of (input, rationale, output) as an exemplar
043   and define the paradigm of using rationale-augmented examples in ICL as In-Context Reasoning
044   (ICR). The majority of existing approaches for exemplar selection rely on heuristics or trial-and-error
045   methods Fu et al. (2023); Brown et al. (2020a), with only a few attempting to address the problem in
046   a more principled way Xiong et al. (2024). Exemplars selected for ICR can be classified into two
047   categories: task-level, where a static set of exemplars representative of the task is chosen for inference,
048
      and instance-level, where exemplars are dynamically selected for each test instance during inference.
      Instance-level selection approaches typically use similarity (Rubin et al., 2022; Xiong et al., 2024)
049
      and diversity-based measures (Ye et al., 2023b) to identify the most suitable exemplars from the
050
      training pool for each test instance, which introduces overhead during inference. Additionally, these
051   approaches do not consider the positive and negative interactions between the exemplars, as they
052   select each exemplar independently. In contrast, selecting a static set of exemplars at the task level not

080   One of the challenges in proposing a principled solution is the limited understanding of the underlying
081   mechanisms of in-context learning. Recent studies aimed at developing theoretically grounded

089   We formulate the selection of top-m exemplar subsets as the problem of identification of top-m arms
090   (Réda et al., 2021) in the stochastic linear bandit setting (Réda et al., 2021). The GIFA framework
091   (Réda et al., 2021) proposed an efficient gap-index based top-m arms identification algorithm with
092   reduced sample complexity. However, the computation of challenger arm (current candidates for
093
      top-m arms), requires computation of gap-indices between all currently estimated top-m arms and
      the remaining arms. This is impractical in our setting since each arm corresponds to a k-sized subset
094
      of the training exemplar set, leading to an exponential number of candidate arms. Hence we need an
095
      algorithm that can sample arms from the candidate sets. While some uniform sampling algorithms
096   for top-m arms identification exist, Chen et al. (2017); Kaufmann & Kalyanakrishnan (2013) for
097   the general multi-arm bandit setting, to the best of our knowledge, there are no sampling-based
098   algorithms for identification of top-m arms in the stochastic linear bandits setting.
099
      In this work, we propose CASE (Challenger Arm Sampling for Exemplar Selection) where we
100
      propose a principled sampling of challenger arms to form a shortlist challenger set, pruning the space
101
      of possible candidate arms (see fig. 1). Our key idea is to iteratively create a low-regret set of selected
102   challenger arms, in addition to the current top-m arms, from uniformly sampled arms. This leads to a
103   selective exploration-based algorithm. We concurrently apply the state-of-the-art gap-index-based
104   algorithm rule for selecting top-m arms out of the total exploration set. We also provide theoretical
105   arguments to justify our novel approach of combined selective exploration and gap-index based
106   identification of top-m arms. When applied to exemplar selection, we observe improvements in task
107   performance of upto 15.19% and reduces number of LLM calls (about 10.5x) when compared to
      state-of-the-art exemplar selection approaches. To summarize, our key contributions are:

121   Exemplar Selection for ICL. The rise of LLMs has transformed them into general-purpose answer-
122   ing engines through emergent capabilities like ICL (Brown et al., 2020b; Wei et al., 2022; 2023; Wang
123   et al., 2023a; Kojima et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2022a) where a few examples are provided to LLMs
124
      to demonstrate the task. To eliminate manual selection, several automated methods have emerged,
      such as reinforcement learning (Zhang et al., 2022; Lu et al., 2023a), trained retrievers Xiong et al.
125
      (2024), Determinantal Point Processes (Ye et al., 2023a) and constrained optimization (Tonglet
126
      et al., 2023), which are effective for reasoning tasks. Additionally, dynamic selection methods that
127   are learning-free, such as similarity-based (Rubin et al., 2022), complexity-based (Fu et al., 2023),
128   and MMR (Ye et al., 2023b), have been explored. However, dynamic selection methods increase
129   inference-time computational costs. To address this, a pre-selected, representative set of exemplars
130   can be chosen for ICL, akin to coreset selection methods (Guo et al., 2022), though the key difference
131   is that ICL does not involve parameter updates, unlike traditional deep learning training. To the best
132   of our knowledge, there has been very little research in a principled, efficient approach for task-level
133   exemplar selection, with the closest work being LENS (Li & Qiu, 2023), which depends on LLM
134   output probabilities, is expensive in terms of number of LLM calls and is unsuitable for black-box
135
      models. We propose a principled novel static exemplar selection method grounded in gap-index based
      stochastic linear bandits that works for both black-box and open LLMs.
136
137   Identification of Top-m Arms in Stochastic Linear Bandits. The top-m arms identification
138   problem aims to estimate a subset of m arms with the highest means. Various methods have
139   been proposed for top-m arm identification in both fixed-confidence (Kalyanakrishnan et al., 2012)
140   and fixed-budget settings (Bubeck et al., 2013). In this paper, we focus on the fixed-confidence
141
      setting, where the error probability in estimating the top-m arms should be smaller than a predefined
      parameter δ ∈ (0, 1). Adaptive sampling algorithms like UGapE (Gabillon et al., 2012) and LUCB
142
      (Kalyanakrishnan et al., 2012), along with uniform sampling methods (Kaufmann & Kalyanakrishnan,
143
      2013; Chen et al., 2017), have been introduced for the fixed confidence setup, but they lack efficiency
144   in terms of sample complexity. While efficient adaptive sampling methods for linear bandits, such
145   as Fiez et al. (2019), RAGE Zhang et al. (2023a), LTS Jedra & Proutiere (2020), PEPS Li et al.
146   (2023), LinGapE (Xu et al., 2017) and LinGame Degenne et al. (2020), have been proposed, they
147   primarily address best-arm identification (m = 1). To the best of our knowledge, GIFA (Réda
148   et al., 2021) was the first unified framework for efficient top-m arm identification with low sample
149   complexity. However, algorithms implemented in GIFA framework require large number of gap-index
150   computations and comparisons, leading to high sample complexity. This is due to its challenger
151   arm sampling mechanism, which considers the complement of the current top-m estimate as the
152
      challenger set, resulting in a large search space. In this work, we propose a novel and efficient
      algorithm with applications to exemplar selection for complex reasoning in LLMs.

158   In-context learning (ICL) leverages LLMs to acquire task-specific knowledge from just a few exam-
159   ples, typically structured as input-rationale-output triplets, without updating the model’s parameter.
160   The rationales serve to elucidate the reasoning process behind the input-output pair, fostering reason-
161   ing capabilities within LLMs. However, due to the financial and computational costs associated with
      processing large contexts, providing all training examples is impractical. Therefore, a key challenge


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      lies in the efficient and optimal selection of exemplars, particularly in a black-box setting where
163   access to the model’s parameters or confidence estimates is unavailable.
164
165   To address this challenge, we propose a novel algorithm for exemplar subset selection. First, within
166
      the in-context learning paradigm, the task of constructing an optimal prompt is framed as a multiple
      exemplar subset selection problem (section 3.1). In section 3.2, we model this as a top-m arm
167
      selection problem in stochastic linear bandits (Kalyanakrishnan & Stone, 2010; Réda et al., 2021),
168
      where a linear function is employed to approximate the loss of each arm (prompt). Section 3.3,
169   introduces a new algorithm based on selective exploration—conceptually related to SETC (Lattimore
170   & Szepesvári, 2020)), which samples and stores the “challenger arms” (arms that have the potential
171   to belong to the top-m set (Réda et al., 2021)). This approach significantly reduces the computational
172   overhead by avoiding the need to evaluate all possible arms in every iteration.

174   3.1   M ULTIPLE E XEMPLAR - SUBSET S ELECTION FOR I N -C ONTEXT R EASONING
175
176   In-context learning (ICL) allows LLMs to be used for supervised learning tasks by leveraging a few
177   demonstration examples (called exemplars) of the task without training or parameter fine-tuning.
178   Unlike traditional supervised learning, where training examples include the input features u and output
179
      labels v, exemplars sometime include chain of thought demonstrations, which are not available for test
      examples. For simplicity, we include them in u for the training exemplars. Let X = {ui , vi }ni=1 be
180
      the set of all n potential training exemplars and (utest , vtest ) denote a test input features and desired
181
      output. A prompt P is constructed from a subset S ⊆ X of k exemplars, which can be used for
182   prediction of utest . Hence, P = [S, utest ] = [(ui1 , vi1 ), ..., (uik , vik ), utest ]. The prompt P is then
183   passed to a response generator function f which uses a decoding mechanism G to sample responses
184   from an LLM. Hence, f (P ) = G (PLLM (r|P )). The final step is a post-processing δ applied to the
185   LLM-generated response f (P ), in order to extract the task-specific output v̂test . Commonly used
186   post-processing strategies include regular-expression matching (δregex ) and self-consistency (δSC )
187   Wang et al. (2023b) based strategies.
188   In-context reasoning (ICR) uses ICL to learn prompts for tasks involving reasoning with natural
189   language, e.g. numerical reasoning based Question Answering (QA) (Chen et al., 2022b; Lu et al.,

191   ICR, prompt generator π which uses multiple high-scoring subsets S1 , ..., Sm and a test input utest to

257

258   each iteration. We propose to mitigate this problem using: (a) identify a low-regret subset Nt of

          tasks. Steps 18 and 19 in algorithm 1 compute the revised most ambiguous arms bt ∈ Ut and
306       st ∈ Nt . st is sampled challenger arm selected from the selected set of next best arms Nt using the

308       using the revised design matrix V̂t+1 and the least squares estimation formulae described in lines
309
          23 and 24 of algorithm 1. Note that, V̂t+1 can be computed from V̂t using the incremental update

461   To address RQ II, we compare CASE and its variants against state-of-the-art task-level and instance-
462   level exemplar selection methods. Table 1 shows that CASE consistently outperforms the random,
463   as well as zero-shot and manual Few-Shot COT Wei et al. (2023) methods, which rely on random
464   or hand-picked samples without accounting for the interactions between exemplars and task-level
465   performance. Furthermore, classical coreset selection methods like Graph Cut and Facility Location,
466   which evaluate a subset’s informativeness and diversity, perform worse or on par with random
467   selection. This is largely because these coreset methods were not designed for the ICL paradigm. We
468   also observe that CASE outperforms dynamic selection methods like KNN, PromptPG, and MMR.
469
      Beyond the efficiency gained during inference, task-level exemplars demonstrate greater robustness
      (see Appendix E) compared to dynamic methods, which selects exemplars for each test instance,
470
      introducing more variance. The independent selection of exemplars in dynamic methods may result
471
      in negative interactions or skill redundancy, where lexically different exemplars may represent similar
472   skills, limiting their utility in reasoning tasks. To further support the claim that CASE selects more
473   representative task-level exemplars, we conduct a qualitative analysis comparing exemplars chosen
474   by LENS and CASE (see Appendix G). We find that CASE consistently selects exemplars with
475   diverse skills required for solving tasks, whereas LENS selects exemplars with redundant skills.

      In this work, we propose an efficient task-level exemplar subset selection method that identifies
534   highly informative exemplar subsets. Our approach significantly reduces resource consumption
535   by minimizing the number of LLM calls, offering a clear advantage over existing state-of-the-art
536   exemplar selection methods. Additionally, we find that exemplars selected using smaller LLMs
