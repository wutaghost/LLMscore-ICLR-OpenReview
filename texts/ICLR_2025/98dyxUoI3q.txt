009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            We investigate the generation of minority samples using pretrained text-to-image
012            (T2I) latent diffusion models. Minority instances, in the context of T2I generation,
013            can be defined as ones living on low-density regions of text-conditional data distri-
014            butions. They are valuable for various applications of modern T2I generators, such
015            as data augmentation and creative AI. Unfortunately, existing pretrained T2I diffu-
016            sion models primarily focus on high-density regions, largely due to the influence
017
               of guided samplers (like CFG) that are essential for producing high-quality gener-
               ations. To address this, we present a novel framework to counter the high-density-
018
               focus of T2I diffusion models. Specifically, we first develop an online prompt
019
               optimization framework that can encourage the emergence of desired properties
020            during inference while preserving semantic contents of user-provided prompts.
021            We subsequently tailor this generic prompt optimizer into a specialized solver that
022            promotes the generation of minority features by incorporating a carefully-crafted
023            likelihood objective. Our comprehensive experiments, conducted across various
024            types of T2I models, demonstrate that our approach significantly enhances the ca-
025            pability to produce high-quality minority instances compared to existing samplers.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
055
056
      Text-to-image (T2I) generative models (Xu et al., 2018; Ramesh et al., 2021; Nichol et al., 2021)
057
      have recently attracted substantial interest for their capability to convert textual descriptions into
058
      visually striking images. At the forefront of the surge are diffusion models (Song & Ermon, 2019;
059   Ho et al., 2020), augmented by guidance techniques (Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021; Ho & Salimans,
060   2022) such as classifier-free guidance (CFG) (Ho & Salimans, 2022). The guided T2I samplers
061   encourage generations from high-density regions of a data manifold (Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021),
062   producing realistic images that faithfully respect the provided prompts.
063
      A key challenge is that the inherent high density focus of modern T2I samplers makes it difficult to
064
      generate minority samples – instances that reside in low-density regions of the manifold. This lim-
065   itation is particularly significant as T2I-generated data is increasingly incorporated in downstream
066   applications (Tian et al., 2024a;b; Afkanpour et al., 2024) where the majority-focused bias within
067   the data may be perpetuated. Furthermore, the unique attributes found in minority instances are
068   crucial for applications like creative AI (Rombach et al., 2022; Han et al., 2022), where generating
069   novel and highly creative outputs is essential.
070
      In this work, we present a novel approach dubbed as MinorityPrompt that counteracts the high-
071   density bias of T2I samplers to improve their capability of minority generation. Our framework is
072   built upon the concept of prompt optimization, an intuitive technique that exhibits strong perfor-
073   mance in enhancing T2I diffusion models for various tasks (Gal et al., 2022; Chung et al., 2023b;
074   Park et al., 2024). Unlike existing T2I-based online prompt-tuning methods that modify the entire
075   input prompts (e.g., by optimizing their text-embeddings during inference), our approach updates
076   the prompts in a selective fashion to preserve the intended semantics while encouraging generations
077   of unique low-density features. Specifically during inference, we incorporate learnable tokens into
078   the input prompts, e.g., by appending them to the end of the text. We then adjust the embeddings
079
      of these tokens across sampling timesteps, targetting the minimization of a likelihood metric de-
      signed to capture the uniqueness of noisy intermediate samples. See Figure 2 for an overview. An
080
      additional benefit of our token-based approach is that it offers enhanced semantic controllability,
081
      enabling users to express specific desired semantics in generated samples by selecting appropriate
082   initialization words for the learnable token embeddings. To further improve the performance of our
083   sampler, we provide new design choices that can be synergistically employed with our approach
084   for T2I minority generation. Comprehensive experiments validate that our method can significantly
085   improve the ability of creating minority instances of modern widely-adopted T2I models (including
086   Stable Diffusion (SD) (Rombach et al., 2022)) with minimal compromise in sample quality and text-
087   image alignment. In addition, we emphasize that our framework can work on distilled backbones
088   like SDXL-Lightning (Lin et al., 2024), which demonstrates its robustness and practical relevance.
089   As an additional application, we explore the potential of our prompt optimization framework to
090
      improve the diversity of T2I models, further exhibiting its versatility as a general-purpose solver
      applicable across various tasks.
091
092   Given that our prompt optimization is performed in an online manner, does not require expensive
093   fine-tuning of T2I models, and is entirely self-contained, i.e., implementable solely with a pretrained
094   T2I model, we believe our approach open a new avenue for creative AI, emphasizing the practical
095   relevance of our framework.

100

106   sifier into the reverse process for low-density guidance. A limitation is that their methods rely upon
107   external classifiers that are often difficult to obtain, especially for large-scale datasets such as T2I
      benchmarks (Schuhmann et al., 2022). The challenge was recently addressed by Um & Ye (2024)

151   Prompt optimization has been widely explored in the context of T2I diffusion models due to their
152   strong dependence on language models. This approach has exhibited significant performance across
153
      various tasks, including inverse problems (Chung et al., 2023b) and image editing (Park et al., 2024;

154
      tire prompts to find the ones that best perform the focused tasks (e.g., minimizing data consistency
155
      loss (Chung et al., 2023b)). In contrast, our framework updates only the attached learnable tokens,
156   thereby preserving the original prompt’s semantics while encouraging the emergence of low-density
157   features. Additional use cases of prompt tuning include personalization (Gal et al., 2022; Han et al.,
158   2023) and object counting (Zafar et al., 2024). Similar to ours, their frameworks introduce vari-
159   able tokens and tune their embeddings. However, their optimizations aim to learn visual concepts
160   captured in user-provided images, whereas our focus is to invoke low-density features through opti-
161   mized prompts. Also, their methods are not online, requiring separate training procedure which can
      be potentially expensive.

      Our focus is to generate high-quality minority instances using text-to-image (T2I) diffusion models,
165   which faithfully reflect user-provided prompts while featuring unique visual attributes rarely pro-
166   duced via standard generation techniques1 . To this end, we start with providing a brief overview
167   on T2I diffusion frameworks and the essential background necessary to understand the core of our
168   work. We subsequently present our proposed framework for minority generation based on the idea
169   of prompt optimization.

236   3.2   S EMANTIC - PRESERVING PROMPT OPTIMIZATION
237
238
      The key idea of our optimization approach is to incorporate learnable tokens into a given prompt P
      and update its embedding on-the-fly during inference. Specifically, we append a placeholder string2
239
      S to the prompt P, which acts as a mark for the learnable tokens. For instance, the augmented
240
      prompt could be PS := “A portrait of a dog S”. This additional string is treated as a new vocabulary
241   item for the text-encoder T . We assign a token embedding v to S, and denote the text encoder
242   incorporating it as T ( · ; v).
243
244
      We propose optimizing this embedding v rather than C. The proposed online prompt optimization
      at sampling step t can then be formalized as follows:
245
246                                            vt∗ := arg max J (zt , Cv ),                                         (3)
                                                             v
247
248   where Cv := T (PS ; v). Afterward, the optimized text-embedding Cvt∗ is obtained by text-processing
249   PS with the updated token-embedding of S, therefore Cvt∗ := T (PS ; vt∗ ).
250   Note that our optimization does not affect the embeddings of the tokens w.r.t. the original prompt
251   P. This is inherently more advantageous for preserving semantics compared to existing methods,
252   which alter the entire text-embedding C and thereby effectively impact all token embeddings. We
253   also highlight that unlike existing learnable-token-based approaches that share the same embedding
254   throughout inference (Gal et al., 2022; Han et al., 2023; Zafar et al., 2024), our framework allows
255   the token embedding v to change over timesteps t. This adaptive feature offers potential advan-
256   tages, since the role of v in maximizing J can vary with the changing nature of zt across different
257
      timesteps. This point is also implied in previous works that employ adaptive text-embeddings over
      time (Chung et al., 2023b; Park et al., 2024).
258
259   Intuitively, our optimization can be understood as capturing a specific concept relevant to noisy
260   latent zt within the token vt∗ , guided by the objective function J . Thanks to its general design that
261   accommodates any arbitrary objective function J , this framework is versatile and can be employed
262   in various contexts beyond minority generation. For instance, it can be used to diversify the outputs
263
      of T2I models. See details in Table 3b.

367   (contrary to the case of Eq. (5)), thereby sidestepping the gradient blocking issue that we mentioned
368   earlier. Another significant improvement comes from the use of an annealed timestep s, which was
369   originally adhered to a fixed value in Um & Ye (2024). We empirically found that employing an an-
370   nealing schedule based on the inverse of the sampling step (e.g., s = T − t) outperforms other fixed
371
      choices of s. Similar to Um & Ye (2024), we conduct our prompt optimization intermittently (i.e.,
      once every N sampling steps) to reduce computational costs. We found that during non-optimizing
372
      steps, employing the base prompt C instead of Cv (with the most recently updated token embed-
373
      ding) yields improvements in text-alignment and sample quality. See Algorithms 1 and 2 for the
374   pseudocode of our approach.

      5     C ONCLUSION
532
      We developed a novel framework for generating minority samples in the context of T2I generation.
533
      Built upon our prompt optimization framework that updates the embeddings of additional learnable
534   tokens, our minority sampler offers significant performance improvements both in text-alignment
535   and low-likelihood generation compared to existing approaches. To accomplish this, we meticu-
536   lously tailor the objective function with theoretical justifications and implement several techniques
537   for further enhancements. Beyond our main interest of minority generation, we further demon-
538   strated the potential of our framework in promoting fairness and diversity. During this process, we
539   also showed that the proposed optimization framework can serve as a general solution, with potential
      applicability to various optimization tasks associated with T2I generation.

541
542
      One potential concern associated with our approach is the possibility of its malicious use to inhibit
543   the generation of minority-featured samples. For instance, this could occur by flipping the sign of
544   the objective function Eq. (6), yielding a focus on high-density generations. It is crucial to recognize
545   this risk and to ensure that our proposed framework is employed responsibly to foster fairness and
546   inclusivity in generative modeling.
