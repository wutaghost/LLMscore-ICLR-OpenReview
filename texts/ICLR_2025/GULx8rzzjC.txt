009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Machine learning (ML) models often require large amounts of data to perform
012            well. When the data available to the model trainer is insufficient to obtain good
013            performance on their desired task, they may need to acquire more data from external
014            sources. Often, useful data is held by private entities who are unwilling to share
015            their data due to monetary and privacy concerns. This makes it challenging and
016
               expensive for model trainers to acquire the data they need to improve their model’s
               performance. To tackle this problem, we propose Mycroft, a data-efficient
017
               method that enables model trainers to evaluate the relative utility of different data
018
               sources while working with a constrained data-sharing budget. Leveraging both
019            functional and feature similarity, Mycroft identifies small but informative data
020            subsets from each data owner. This allows model trainers to identify useful data
021            owners and improve model performance with minimal data exposure. Experiments
022            across multiple tasks in two domains show that Mycroft converges rapidly to
023            the performance of the full-information baseline, where all data is shared.
024            Moreover, Mycroft is robust to label and data noise, and can effectively recover
025            a utility-based ranking of data owners. We believe Mycroft paves the way for
026            democratized training of high performance ML models.
027
028   1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Machine learning models’ performance relies heavily on their training datasets, but the data available
031   for training is often insufficient, outdated, or unrepresentative of the task (Stacke et al., 2021; Elsahar
032   & Gallé, 2019; Liu et al., 2023). As models expand into new domains and existing ones exhaust public
033   data, data scarcity becomes a key challenge. According to economic and manufacturing experts, one
034   of the primary reasons that AI technology is not being widely adopted in manufacturing is the lack of
035   relevant public data available for production tasks (Alam et al., 2024). While large corporations and
036   governments can afford large-scale data collection or use methods like crowdsourcing (Sigurdsson
037   et al., 2016) and federated learning (Kairouz et al., 2021) to increase their access to diverse datasets,
038
      entities without such resources, such as individuals and small businesses, find it extremely challenging
      to collect their own data.
039
040   Such entities may need to acquire data from private entities (referred to as data owners), who are
041   often unwilling to share it publicly, especially when it involves proprietary or sensitive data, such
042   as educational or health records (Spector-Bagdady et al., 2019). Acquiring data from these private
043   data owners can involve high overhead, such as the creation of complex data sharing agreements,
044
      monetary compensation, and compliance with regulations such as (GDPR, 2021). Therefore, data
      sharing protocols which help model trainers assess which data owners are most likely to provide
045
      useful data before acquiring data at scale are needed.
046
047   Challenges of external data augmentation: The model trainer needs to efficiently select one or
048   more data owners whose datasets match their requirements. In the case with a profusion of data
049   owners, while a large number can be filtered out just on the basis of metadata (i.e. domain, collection
050
      methodology, licensing requirements etc.), it is unclear which of the remaining data owners the model
      trainer should acquire data from. Even in the case of a single data owner with a large pool of data,
051
      both the model trainer and data owners will benefit from a method to assess the dataset’s potential
052
      effectiveness. In summary, the key question we aim to answer in this paper is:
053
      How can a model trainer assess the usefulness of data owners without acquiring their entire dataset?

054
      Mycroft for external data augmentation: We present Mycroft, a framework that helps the
055   model trainer and data owners identify relevant data (from the data owners) for improving the
056   model trainer’s performance. The framework follows three main steps: (1) the model trainer sends
057   information about the task on which their model is under-performing, typically through data samples,
058   to data owners; (2) the data owners use an efficient algorithm to identify a small, relevant subset of
059   their data to demonstrate their dataset’s usefulness and send it to the model trainer; and (3) the model
060   trainer evaluates this subset to decide whether to acquire additional data from the data owners.
061   The key technical challenge we solve in this paper lies in Step (2), where each data owner must find
062   the most “relevant” training data with respect to the model trainer’s transmitted data samples, which
063   may not follow the same distribution as their training data. Our proposed algorithms select data based
064   on either functional similarity, which compares sample-wise gradients from task-specific models,
065   or feature similarity, which measures the distance between samples in a relevant feature space, or a
066   combination of both. In summary, our contributions are as follows:
067   1. Modelling the problem of external data augmentation (§2:) We formally model the practical
068   problem of acquiring data from private data owners by stating key assumptions and adding constraints
069   inspired by the likely real-world operating conditions for any external data augmentation algorithm.
070
      2. Mycroft: a protocol for identifying and sharing useful data (§3:) We outline a data sharing
071
      protocol, which crucially depends on methods for the data owner to identify relevant data. We propose
072
      two data selection approaches, based on functional (loss gradient) similarity and feature similarity,
073   and highlight their applicability. We adapt existing tools to make them suitable for our goal and in
074   cases where these techniques were ineffective (such as for tabular data), we develop a new metric
075   for data selection. Finally, we unify both methods via a joint optimization objective to leverage the
076   notions of similarity arising from each approach.
077
      3. Extensive methodological assessment (§4:) We compare Mycroft against two baselines: (i)
078
      full-information, where each data owner shares all their data with the model trainer, serving
079   as the optimal baseline, and (ii) random uniform sampling. Across multiple classification tasks from
080   2 domains (computer vision and tabular data) and a range of data budgets, our experiments show that
081   Mycroft significantly outperforms random sampling and quickly approaches the performance of
082   full-information under a much smaller budget. In the vision domain, Mycroft outperforms
083   random-sampling by an average of 21% for five data budgets on four datasets. In the tabular
084   domain, Mycroft with just 5 samples outperforms random-sampling with 100 samples and
085   matches full-information performance for 65% of sharing scenarios.
086   4. Case studies of using Mycroft in practical settings (§5): We conduct four additional case
087   studies to evaluate Mycroft’s effectiveness across different scenarios that may be encountered in
088   practice. We show that Mycroft is robust to instance and label noise in the data provided by the
089   data owners. When this data lacks labels entirely, feature similarity is still effective at finding relevant
090   data samples. When multiple data providers are involved, Mycroft successfully reconstructs a
091   utility-based preference order that closely aligns with the full-information setting. These
092   findings confirm that Mycroft offers a highly data-efficient solution for data owners to reliably
093
      demonstrate the value of their data to a model trainer, even in noisy or complex environments.
094   We hope Mycroft and its associated open-source code 1 paves the way for more efficient and private
095   data-sharing frameworks, in turn helping democratize the training of performant ML models.

      2.1       C HALLENGES OF EXTERNAL DATA AUGMENTATION
104
105   The most direct approach to external data augmentation is to acquire all data from all available data
106   providers. However, this approach presents the following challenges:

      1. Data owners are unwilling to share all their data: Due to privacy and proprietary concerns,
109      data owners are often unwilling to share all their data. They also typically expect compensation
110      which necessitates the use of data budgets to establish the utility of the dataset before sharing.
111
      2. Sharing poorly curated data can negatively impact performance: A large portion of the data
112
         owned by data owners may be poorly curated or irrelevant for model training. Including such data
113
         in the training process can degrade model performance, so it is crucial for model trainers to select
114      only high-quality and relevant subsets from the data owners’ contributions.
115
      3. Data budgets can ease computational concerns: Sharing large volumes of data from multiple
116
         owners makes it difficult and costly for model trainers to update their models frequently.

153   data to convince MT of their data utility due to privacy and economic concerns. After utility is
154   established, MT may enter into an agreement with the best DO (s).

      3.1   F UNCTIONAL S IMILARITY VIA L OSS G RADIENT M ATCHING
177
178
      For models trained using a differentiable loss function, the gradient of the loss with respect to each
179   model parameter shows how each sample affected the model during training. Samples that produce
180   similar gradients are considered functionally similar. This concept has been used in several studies
181   to sub-select training data to improve the efficiency of model training (Mirzasoleiman et al., 2020;
182   Killamsetty et al., 2021a). Ideally, to find the most relevant samples to Dhard , the model used would
183   be MMT . However, due to constraint C.1 that no DO has access to MT’s model, we assume that each
184   DO has a model Mi with parameters θi trained using a differentiable loss function L that can function
185   as a reasonable proxy (our empirical findings show that this method works well in practice).
186   We formulate the problem of finding Diuseful as that of obtaining a k-sparse weight vector w over Di ,
187   with the weight assigned to each sample corresponding to its utility. To find this k-sparse w, we: (1)
188   find the averaged gradient of the loss L computed on Dhard with respect to the parameters θi of Mi
189   (denoted ∇θi L(Dhard )); (2) compute the gradient of the loss L computed on each sample zj ∈ Di
190   with respect to the parameters θi of Mi ; (3) solve the following regularized optimization problem:

201   We detail OMP in Algorithm 2. The choice of which state of the model to use (typically stored as
202   checkpoints) is explored in §4.3.
203   Other potential gradient based techniques: Before we settled on utilizing OMP on the model
204   gradients for the DataSelect process, we also explored various other coreset techniques (Zhou
205   et al., 2022; Coleman et al., 2019; Toneva et al., 2018; Ducoffe & Precioso, 2018; Ren et al., 2018).
206   Since these techniques are designed to create subsets of data that can approximate the loss of an entire
207   dataset, most of them are not directly applicable to our setting which requires finding a small subset
208   which will be useful for a specific task to the MT. Additionally, these techniques are designed to be
209   used jointly when models are trained from scratch whereas we are only interested in efficient solutions
210   that can find useful samples by leveraging an already trained model. Among coreset techniques
211
      that can be adapted to our problem setting, we decide to use OMP because other techniques either
      underperform our chosen approach (Mirzasoleiman et al., 2020) or are too computationally intensive
212

217
218   We also find that data samples which are similar to samples from Dhard in an appropriate feature space
219   are useful for MT to augment their training set. This is especially useful for models which have been
220   trained without differentiable loss functions. Given any good feature extractor ϕ(·) that maps a sample
221   xj to its feature representation ϕ(xj ), we compute the distances of each sample in Dhard to each
                                                            hard
222   sample in Di and store them in a matrix Ψ ∈ R|D |×|Di | . We construct Diuseful by using a greedy
223   heuristic which first sorts this matrix in the row dimension followed by the column dimension. We se-
224   lect the top-k samples by iterating over the columns which selects |Diuseful | samples with the minimum
225   distance to Dhard samples while ensuring coverage of Dhard (see Algorithm 3 for more details). The
226   choice of feature representations ϕ(·) and distance function d(·, ·) is contingent on the domain of the
227   data and the classification task. For the image datasets, we use the feature space of a image retrieval
228
      model called Unicom (An et al., 2023) and L2 distance as our distance function. For the tabular
      dataset where existing techniques were ineffective, we propose a ExtractBinningFeatures
229
      algorithm using Hamming distances over adaptive grids (Appendix 4).

367   4.3   A BLATION STUDIES
368
      We conduct ablation studies on the image datasets to analyze key design choices of Mycroft. For
369
      tabular dataset ablations, see Appendix E.2.
370
371   Fine-tuning vs training from scratch: We compare two approaches for the MT to incorporate Diuseful
372   into their model: finetuning and retraining. We find that retraining from scratch and fine-tuning both

374
      training MT’s models with externally augmented data from scratch takes 8 hours on average on a
      single Nvidia A40 GPU whereas finetuning only requires approximately 50 minutes. Hence, we
375
      choose to use finetuning for all our experiments.

      useful gradients for gradient matching (Figure 6). Please refer to Appendix E for more details.

      6     R ELATED W ORK
475
476
477
      Data augmentation and synthetic data generation: Numerous studies have investigated ways
      to enhance training data quality by leveraging existing datasets through methods such as image
478
      overlay (Inoue, 2018), random erasure (Zhong et al., 2020), and common data augmentation tech-
479
      niques like rotation and cropping (Inoue, 2018; Zhong et al., 2020; Chlap et al., 2021; Shorten &
480   Khoshgoftaar, 2019; Hussain et al., 2017; Feng et al., 2021). These strategies are designed to generate
481   new samples, reduce overfitting, and improve model generalization. However, they are less effective
482   if the test data deviates significantly from or is underrepresented in the training set. Similarly, using
483   generative models to create additional training samples (Tripathi et al., 2019; Such et al., 2020; Jiang
484   et al., 2024) can enhance performance, but this approach often requires substantial data to train the
485   generative model itself. Moreover, synthetic data may fail to capture the full complexity of real-world
      data distributions, limiting its impact on hard sample performance.


                                                         9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      Data augmentation using publicly available data: Researchers have also explored sourcing data
487   from publicly available data lakes (Nargesian et al., 2023; Castelo et al., 2021; Yakout et al., 2012;
488   Esmailoghli et al., 2021; Santos et al., 2021; Zhu et al., 2016; Castro Fernandez et al., 2019; Fernandez,
489   2018; Galhotra et al., 2023). For example, METAM (Galhotra et al., 2023) profiles various datasets
490   to identify and select the most relevant ones, thereby improving the quality of training data and
491   enhancing performance on downstream tasks. Another approach, Internet Explorer (Li et al., 2023),
492   retrieves task-specific images from the internet to support self-supervised learning. However, these
493   solutions rely on open access to data and are ineffective in scenarios where data access is restricted.
494   Consequently, they do not address the challenge of sourcing data from private entities.
495   Selecting data without full information: Recent work called Projektor (Kang et al., 2024) addresses
496   the problem of selecting and weighting useful data owners without full information about the
497   underlying data. Projektor assumes that some of the DO ’s data is publicly available as pilot data and
498   can predict the usefulness of the entire dataset based on this subset. This assumption may not be true
499   in our context as the publicly available pilot data is not selected based on any knowledge about the
500   MT ’s task, and may be irrelevant for MT. Techniques like Projektor can complement Mycroft by
501
      finding the best combination of relevant samples from different DOs.
502   Deriving coresets for large datasets: There is also extensive research on identifying useful subsets
503   of datasets, such as work on “coresets" (Killamsetty et al., 2021c; Kim & Shin, 2022; Guo et al.,
504   2022), which focus on selecting a representative subset that approximates the cost function of the
505   entire dataset. However, most of these methods are better suited for dataset compression rather than
506   creating task-specific subsets. In our context, approximating the entire cost function is irrelevant to
507
      the model trainer’s needs. Instead, we prioritize selecting a subset most relevant to the specific task
      and have adapted some of these techniques accordingly.

      7    D ISCUSSION
511
512
513
      Why does Mycroft help? The fact that Mycroft outperforms random-sampling in almost
      all scenarios and quickly approaches full-information, even when operating under a limited
514
      data budget has scenario-dependent explanations. First, for many DOs, the majority of their data is
515
      irrelevant to the MT’s task, as such, random-sampling is inefficient and sharing all their data is
516   unnecessary. Secondly, in many cases, MT may only need a small subset of the data to drastically
517   improve their model’s performance. For example, in the Dogs & Wolves dataset, only a small subset
518   of the data is needed break the spurious correlations and improve generalization. Similarly, for the IoT
519   attack data, the significant distinction between attack distributions implies a small subset is sufficient
520   to differentiate between benign and malicious traffic, and different kinds of malicious traffic.
521
      Limitations and Future Work: One limitation of our work is that it assumes that the model trainer
522   already has access to a set Dhard that they want to improve on. This may often be a reasonable
523   assumption because model trainers are looking to improve on the predictions of Dhard that they
524   perform poorly on or have low confidence about. However, the case where Dhard is too limited and
525   therefore, does not give the data owners enough of a signal to determine useful data, is an important
526   for future work. In addition, our method also assumes that the most useful data to share are likely
527   those that are similar to Dhard . In reality, data that are not close to Dhard might also be useful to share.
528   In this paper, we do leverage functional similarity via gradient matching to include some diverse data
529   points. However, explicitly promoting diverse data selection is a key direction for future work.
530   Finally, there remains the issue that model trainers may not be willing to share even small subsets
531   of their data with the data owners, in which case more privacy-preserving methods can be explored
532   on top of Mycroft. Future directions to address the privacy limitations include developing a
533   version that avoids directly sharing data samples but still provides useful information to the model
534   trainer, such as using techniques like noise addition, feature (instead of data) sharing, synthetic data
535   generation, and other privacy-preserving methods on top of Mycroft. For a detailed discussion of
536
      extensions towards privacy-preserving data sharing, see Appendix G. Additionally, exploring versions
      of Mycroft that are resistant to strategic, and even malicious, data owners is a promising area for
537
      future work.
538
539   We hope our approach in this paper lays the foundation for more efficient and privacy-focused
      data-sharing frameworks, ultimately democratizing the training of highly performant ML models.

       D.1.1     D OGS & W OLVES DATASET
1007
1008
       Neural networks are known to learn spurious correlations in supervised settings. While test data
1009   containing the correlations learnt during training often gets classified correctly, data which does
1010   not contain such correlations is prone to misclassification. We exploit this phenomenon to create a
1011   dataset which helps us simulate a controlled MT-DO interaction. In our case, the MT has a training
1012   and validation dataset which contains spurious correlations but a test dataset which does not contain
1013   them and thus their model suffers on the test dataset.
1014   Concretely, we curate a dataset which consists of two classes: Dogs and Wolves. Spurious correlations
1015   are introduced in it by controlling the background of each image which can either be snow or grass.
1016   The MT has data from both animals being on one type of background. In particular, the dogs are on
1017
       grass and the wolves are on snow. In the absense of negative examples, the model takes a shortcut
       by associating the true label with the background and not the animal. We refer to MT’s training and
1018
       validation subset as Dogs & Wolves - Spurious. However, the model performs poorly when the test
1019
       samples do not contain the spurious correlations i.e., dogs on snow and wolves on grass. We refer to
1020   this subset as Dogs & Wolves - Natural. We simulate a DO which has a dataset containing both Dogs
1021   & Wolves - Spurious and Dogs & Wolves - Natural and thus their model does not learn background
1022   related spurious correlations. An illustration of this dataset is provided in Figure 5.
1023   Now, if the MT wants to perform well on data from Dogs & Wolves - Natural, they must acquire
1024   data from that distribution and retrain their model in order to break the spurious correlations. This
1025   motivates the MT to acquire new data form the DO. It should be noted that the MT is oblivious as to
       why their model performs poorly on Dogs & Wolves - Natural.

1366   This section demonstrates the feasibility of external data augmentation for ML problems of practical
1367   interest. Since data sharing raises several concerns with regards to the privacy of the data, we discuss
1368   some strategies to mitigate privacy concerns as well as some alternative techniques to Mycroft and
1369   why they are not feasible for solving our problem.
1370   Why not use federation?: The main issue with methods such as federated learning, whether privacy-
1371   preserving or not, is the need to federate. There needs to be a centralized server which collects
1372   the model updates from different agents, aggegates them, and then sends them back for the next
1373   round. In many scenarios where MT needs to improve its model performance, it may not have the
1374   resources or technical ability to set-up this centralized server. It would also need to convince the DOs
1375   to participate in this federated learning protocol for a number of rounds. In contrast, external data
1376
       augmentation does not need a centralized coordinating server, just a method to send and receive data
       asynchronously. Completely decentralized peer-to-peer learning is also an option, however this needs
1377
       the model that MT wants to train to be passed around in some fashion to its peers, which violates
1378
       constraint C1.
1379
1380   What about secure multi-party computation?: The main issue with using MPC to solve the
1381   problem tackled in this paper is that if DO computes the function (model) jointly with MT, it will likely
1382
       use all of its data when training. Such a protocol will be expensive and can leak information Salem
       et al. (2018) about all of DO’s’ training data. This motivates this paper’s focus on DO only sharing a
1383
       part of its data.
1384
1385   Given these barriers to the adoption of direct decentralized model training, we believe that a good
1386   solution to the problem posed in this paper is using external data augmentation. Mycroft provides an
1387   initial protocol that has been demonstrated to be useful while sharing data within a specified budget.
1388
       However, even this may be too much information to share in certain settings. There are several ways
       in which the privacy of Mycroft can be improved in follow-up work, which we discuss here:
1389
1390   1. Sharing features instead of data: MT can, instead of directly sharing data with DO, just share
1391      an appropriate compressive feature representation of the data which reduces the amount of
1392      information being shared. This will make it harder to determine the exact data held by MT.
1393      However, this can leak information about MT’s model, as well as cause issues with data selection
1394      if there is mismatch between the architecture of the models used by MT and DO, since comparisons
1395
          will have to be done in feature space directly to determine utility.
1396   2. Adding noise to the data: Differentially private noise addition can be used to prevent the inference
1397      of sensitive attributes from Dhard , which is particularly important for domains such as electronic
1398      health records. As is normal, this will lead to a privacy-utility trade-off for the identification of
1399      Duseful , which is an interesting direction for future work. Noise-resilient protocol design for data
1400
          selection may be challenging depending on the magnitude of noise added.
1401   3. Operating on encrypted data: The most computationally expensive method for ensuring privacy
1402      would be for one or both of MT and DO to only share encrypted data. Past work Bost et al. (2014)
1403      has shown that classification can be performed over encrypted data using primitives such as
          partially homomorphic encryption. The proposed data selection and subsequent fine-tuning could


                                                          26
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1404
          be extended to operations only over encrypted data, although both efficiency and effectiveness are
1405      likely to suffer.
1406
       4. Using ZKPs: In case the DO does not wish to share data at all, the following thought experiment
1407
          implies that ZKPs may be a fruitful approach for a DO to prove to MT that it does have relevant
1408
          data, once it has received Dhard and used an appropriate data selection mechanism. A possible
1409      protocol would work as follows: 1) MT would share some information I about Dhard (such as an
1410      encrypted sample of the data or a sample with noise added); ii) DO would use I to find Duseful
1411      from within its own dataset, using Step 3 in Algorithm 1 (DataSelect); iii) DO would then
1412      commence a ZKP protocol with MT to prove the statement that ’DO has data that is useful for
1413      MT, without ever sharing its data directly. While this prevents MT from training a model on an
1414      augmented dataset, it does allow for the determination of which DOs are useful and which not.
1415      Note that MT does have to share some information about Dhard in order to have a useful protocol.
1416      Designing such a zero-knowledge proof protocol is beyond the scope of this paper, although the
1417
          fundamental abstraction that motivates Mycroft would remain the same for the overall algorithm,
          as would the use of some DataSelect. Hardware solutions such as trusted enclaves at the DOs’
1418
          end could be used to secure Dhard as well.
