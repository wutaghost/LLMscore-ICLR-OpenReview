009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Recent advancements in Large Language Models (LLMs) have been significant,
012            largely due to improvements in network architecture, particularly the transformer
013            model. With access to large training datasets, LLMs can train in an unsupervised
014            manner and still achieve impressive results in generating coherent output. This
015            study introduces a transformer-based generative model, EEGTrans, designed for
016
               sequentially generating synthetic electroencephalogram (EEG) signals. Given the
               inherent noise in EEG data, we employ a quantized autoencoder that compresses
017
               these signals into discrete codes, effectively capturing their temporal features and
018
               enabling generalization across diverse datasets. The encoder of EEGTrans pro-
019            cesses EEG signals as input, while its decoder autoregressively generates discrete
020            codes. We evaluate our method in a motor imagery Brain-Computer Interface
021            (BCI) application, where merging data across datasets is particularly challeng-
022            ing due to experimental differences. Our results demonstrate that the synthetic
023            EEG data effectively captures temporal patterns while maintaining the complex-
024            ity and power spectrum of the original signals. Moreover, classification results
025            show that incorporating synthetic data improves performance and even surpasses
026            that of models based on Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs). These findings
027
               highlight the potential of transformer-based generative models to generalize effec-
               tively across multiple datasets and produce high-quality synthetic EEG signals.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Large language models (LLMs) have been extensively utilized across various scenarios due to their
035   powerful model characteristic: the generative models. These models are not restricted to producing
036   specific forms of output; instead, they can generate output in any form. The progress of LLMs is
037   largely attributed to the implementation of the attention mechanism (Vaswani et al., 2017). This
038   mechanism enables the processing of long-range dependency inputs and can be adapted to numer-
039
      ous domains, such as text-to-image generation (Ramesh et al., 2021) and speech-to-text generation
      (Radford et al., 2023). Recent studies demonstrate that transformer models can generate activations
040
      resembling those observed in the human brain (Schrimpf et al., 2021; Caucheteux & King, 2022).
041
      Additionally, models that perform well in predicting the next word in a sequence also show profi-
042   ciency in predicting brain measurements. This computational evidence highlights the crucial role
043   of predictive processing in shaping the brain’s comprehension of language. This leads to a new
044   question: Can transformer architecture effectively generate brain signals?
045
      The most common generative models used in Electroencephalography (EEG) research are Gener-
046
      ative Adversarial Networks (GANs) (Goodfellow et al., 2014). Researchers have extensively ex-
047
      plored the application of GANs to various EEG fields, including motor imagery (Hartmann et al.,
048   2018; Xu et al., 2021; Fahimi et al., 2020), emotion recognition (Luo & Lu, 2018; Luo et al., 2020),
049   epileptic seizure detection (Wei et al., 2019; Rasheed et al., 2021), etc. Many of these applications
050   aimed to address the data scarcity problem by generating synthetic EEG data, often incorporating
051   both Conditional Generative Adversarial Networks (CGAN) (Mirza & Osindero, 2014) and Wasser-
052   stein Generative Adversarial Networks (WGAN) (Arjovsky et al., 2017). However, GAN models
053   encounter a limitation when applied to EEG, as they lack inherent temporal generation processes
      (Bird et al., 2021). The generated output adheres to a fixed format and cannot extend indefinitely


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      to accommodate varying sequence lengths. This limitation might pose challenges for EEG applica-
055   tions where the duration of signal generation is ambiguous. Moreover, in the generation process, the
056   signal output in each timestep lacks influence on the subsequent one, whereas EEG possesses a high
057   temporal resolution characteristic.
058
      Brain-computer interfaces (BCIs) enable direct communication between humans and machines.
059
      EEG is a common method in BCI due to its mobility and millisecond-range temporal resolution.
060
      With the decreasing data collection costs over recent decades, the effort to collect more data has be-
061   come more accessible (Wolpaw et al., 2002). While more public datasets are being made available,
062   recent research has primarily focused on advancing sophisticated models to enhance BCI perfor-
063   mance. The emergence of the transformer architecture (Vaswani et al., 2017), successfully applied
064   in both computer vision (CV) and natural language processing (NLP) with models like the Vision
065   Transformer (ViT) (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020) and Generative Pre-trained Transformer (GPT) (Brown
066   et al., 2020), has led to its adoption in EEG as well (Song et al., 2022).
067   The scalability of these transformers is widely recognized, as they tend to perform better with larger
068   datasets (Kaplan et al., 2020; Henighan et al., 2020; Zhai et al., 2022). However, unlike in CV and
069   NLP, where data from various sources can be combined to create a larger dataset, this approach is
070   not feasible in EEG research. This is mainly due to significant variations among data from different
071   subjects within the same dataset (Morioka et al., 2015; Jayaram et al., 2016), as human brains differ
072   from each other (Gu & Kanai, 2014), resulting in differences in recorded brain activity. Moreover,
073   the inter-variation between datasets is much greater than the intra-variation within datasets. Each
074   BCI field employs distinct experimental designs, and even within the same field, each dataset has
075
      unique experimental settings. Challenges arise when attempting to merge datasets due to differences
      in experimental tasks, setups, and even the equipment used. The key strength of the transformer ar-
076
      chitecture lies in its attention mechanism, which can effectively learn representations across various
077
      domains. An interesting idea arises from this concept: Can a generative model be developed that
078   can be applied across multiple EEG datasets exploiting the transformer’s capabilities?
079
080   In this paper, we introduce EEGTrans, a novel framework designed to train generative models using
081
      data from multiple sources, capable of generating high-quality synthetic EEG data from unseen
      datasets. When we refer to ”unseen”, we are exploring the potential of applying knowledge acquired
082
      from a previously seen dataset (source dataset) to generate synthetic data for a new dataset (target
083
      dataset). Our EEGTrans employs a quantized autoencoder to compress EEG data into discrete codes.
084   This process imposes a more stringent penalty on the generative models, discouraging the learning
085   of trivial solutions. We conducted experiments using two publicly available motor imagery datasets.
086   The quality of synthetic data can be evaluated through visualization and measuring the differences
087   between real and synthetic data. At the same time, the model’s performance will be assessed based
088   on the downstream classification task. The contributions of this work can be summarized as:
089
090         1. This paper introduces a novel framework that employs a transformer-based generative
091
               model trained on multiple datasets in motor imagery EEG. To the best of our knowledge,
               this is the first research effort to take such an approach.
092
093         2. We demonstrate that, without explicit training on the new dataset, EEGTrans can generate
094            synthetic signals that closely resemble real data, suggesting that transformers can capture
095            the EEG characteristics present in motor imagery datasets.
096         3. We introduce a new loss design that utilizes the synthetic data generated by EEGTrans to
097            enhance downstream BCI classification performance.

      2.1   G ENERATIVE M ODELS FOR B IOLOGICAL S IGNALS
102
103   GAN Numerous studies have explored the utilization of GANs for generating synthetic EEG data
104   related to motor imagery. Hartmann et al. (2018) explored the feasibility of using Convolutional
105   Neural Network (CNN) to train a GAN progressively for generating synthetic EEG data. By modi-
106   fying the improved WGAN training, they could train a GAN in a stable manner to generate synthetic
107   signals closely resembling real EEG signals from a single channel and a single subject, both in the
      time and frequency domains. Roy et al. (2020) utilized Bidirectional Long Short-Term Memory


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      (Bi-LSTM) to build GANs for generating synthetic EEG data from a single channel. However,
109   the methods mentioned above did not provide information on the classification performance after
110   generating synthetic data.
111
112
      Given the limited amount of data available from stroke patients compared to that from normal pa-
      tients, Xu et al. (2021) utilized Cycle-consistent Adversarial Networks (CycleGAN) (Zhu et al.,
113
      2017) to generate motor-imagery EEG data of stroke patients from normal patients, thereby enhanc-
114
      ing the classification performance. Xie et al. (2021) combined Long Short-Term Memory Genera-
115   tive Adversarial Networks (LGANs), Multi-output Convolutional Neural Network (MoCNN), and
116   an attention network to enhance motor imagery classification performance. Classifiers and GANs
117   for EEG signals need subject-specific training due to inter-subject variation, though the referenced
118   studies above did not explicitly mention this. Fahimi et al. (2020) proposed a novel approach based
119   on the Conditional Deep Convolutional Generative Adversarial Networks (DCGANs) to generate
120   subject-specific artificial EEG by training on subject-independent data. This is accomplished by ap-
121   pending a subject-specific feature vector to both the generator and discriminator during the training
122   process.
123
124   Transformer GPT-2 models were utilized to produce synthetic biological signals (electromyog-
125   raphy and EEG) (Bird et al., 2021). However, the process of preparing these biological signals for
126   interpretation by GPT was not elaborated upon. They utilized pre-trained weights on GPT, yet GPT
127   is pre-trained on NLP, where inputs consist of tokens, which is notably different from processing
128   continuous EEG signals. Moreover, for n classes of data, n GPT-2 models are trained, making the
129
      process time-consuming, especially when integrating new classes or datasets, as it requires addi-
      tional time. Niu et al. (2021) built upon the previous research by utilizing GPT-2 models to generate
130
      EEG signals, aiming to improve the prediction of epileptic seizures.
131
132   A similar work that uses transformers to learn generic representations across multiple EEG datasets
133   is LaBraM (Jiang et al., 2024). However, LaBraM primarily focuses on learning representations and
134   improving downstream classification tasks. In contrast, our study mainly investigates the generative
135
      properties of the transformer architecture, exploring whether motor imagery datasets share under-
      lying features, and whether transformers have the capability to generate synthetic EEG signals that
136
      capture these features.
137
138   Currently, there are no methods that attempt to train a generative model using multiple source EEG
139   datasets.
140
141   2.2   V ECTOR Q UANTIZATION
142
143   Vector quantization serves as a method to compress inputs into discrete codes while simultaneously
144   ensuring the essential fidelity of the data is preserved (Gray, 1984). Recently, vector quantization
145   has gained widespread usage in deep learning following the introduction of Vector Quantized Varia-
146   tional AutoEncoder (VQ-VAE) (Van Den Oord et al., 2017). For example, SoundStream (Zeghidour
147   et al., 2021) and EnCodec (Défossez et al., 2022) both use a residual vector quantizer (RVQ) (Juang
148
      & Gray, 1982) to quantize the output of the encoder. Following previous works, DeWave (Duan
      et al., 2023) utilized VQ-VAE to encode EEG signals into discrete codes. They suggested that en-
149
      coding EEG signals according to their proximity to the nearest neighbor in the codex book could
150
      decrease variations among different subjects, thus improving generalization across subjects. Draw-
151   ing inspiration from DeWave, we integrate the residual vector quantizer into our research and design
152   a novel architecture to leverage its capabilities, aiming to attain generalization across datasets.

175   3.2      RVQ
176
177   With EEG sequences X ∈ RC×T , where C represents the channels and T signifies the times-
178   tamps, we utilize an RVQ to transform the continuous signals into discrete codes. An autoencoder
179   is constructed for this process, comprising three modules: encoder, RVQ, and decoder, illustrated in
180
      Figure 1. Since the number of channels can differ among datasets, this procedure handles channels
      individually, one at a time. Initially, the encoder transforms a single-channel EEG sequence x ∈ RT
181
      into embeddings ze (x). Subsequently, these embeddings are substituted with the latent variables e
182
      corresponding to one of the codebooks in the RVQ. This is accomplished by calculating the distance
183   between the embeddings and the latent variables and then substituting them with the latent vari-
184   ables that have the closest distance. Finally, the quantized embeddings are fed through the decoder
185   to obtain the reconstructed sequence xr . The autoencoder is trained using the reconstruction loss
186   to minimize the distance between the input sequence and its reconstructed sequence, as well as to
187   align their mean and standard deviation. Additionally, the codebooks are trained by minimizing the
188   distance between the ℓ2 -normalized latent variables and the embeddings as shown in Equation 1:

      3.3      EEGT RANS
199
200
201
      We adopted the original dense transformer architecture (Vaswani et al., 2017) as our transformer-
      based generative model. Using this encoder-decoder architecture, we train an autoregressive model
202
      that takes EEG sequences as input and generates corresponding discrete codes. A similar setup
203
      can be found in Whisper (Radford et al., 2023), which was originally designed for speech-to-text
204   translation. The input (continuous) and output (discrete) formats in Whisper align with ours. How-
205   ever, while spectrograms are commonly used as input in speech processing, this approach is less
206   common in the EEG domain. For example, models like EEG Conformer (Song et al., 2022) and
207   LaBram (Jiang et al., 2024) directly employ EEG signals. Therefore, we followed this approach and
208   excluded spectrogram components from EEGTrans.
209
      The architecture of the proposed EEGTrans utilized in this study is depicted in Figure 2. During
210   the training process, EEGTrans is trained using the next code prediction task. Here, the decoder is
211   tasked with predicting the code Yt+1 corresponding to the next timestamp, based on the EEG inputs
212   x and the codes Y≤t received up to the current timestamp. Once discrete codes are generated, we
213   employ the decoder from the pre-trained RVQ autoencoder to get the signals x̂ back. To obtain
214   synthetic data that more closely represents the original data, we further fine-tune the pre-trained
215   RVQ decoder. This fine-tuning occurs during EEGTrans training, where we continue to train the
      decoder alongside EEGTrans, all while keeping the encoder and codebooks frozen. The training

      4.2   DATA P REPROCESSING
271
272   As multiple datasets are utilized, it is essential to standardize them into a common format to facilitate
273   model interpretation. For instance, various datasets may have different sampling rates, meaning
274   that a fixed number of timestamps may represent varying durations across datasets. Consequently,
275   preprocessing the data is crucial to enable model training across datasets. The first step is epoching:
276   segmenting the complete EEG sequence of each dataset into epochs using event markers, preserving
277   only the data occurring from the onset of the event to 2 seconds after the event onset. Subsequently,
278
      the data is resampled to 128 Hz, the lowest sampling rate among the five datasets used, using a fast
      Fourier transform. As the motor imagery field is chosen for validating the proposed method, only
279
      channels relevant to motor imagery are selected (refer to Appendix B.2 for more details). Finally,
280
      the signals within each epoch are normalized to zero mean and one standard deviation along the
281   timestamp dimension. These processed data are then ready for training the generative models.
282
283   4.3   I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
284
285   The RVQ autoencoder is built exclusively with a 1D convolutional layer (Conv1D) for the encoder,
286   while both Conv1D and transpose Conv1D are employed for the decoder. The RVQ autoencoder
287   is trained using the AdamW optimizer, with a learning rate of 1e-3 and a weight decay of 1e-4 for
288   1000 epochs. Please refer to Appendix C for further information on the configuration of the RVQ
289   autoencoder.
290   The EEGTrans model architecture comprises an encoder and a decoder. The input embedding layer
291   of the EEGTrans model consists of 6 Conv2D layers. The encoder block consists of 4 layers with an
292   embedding size of 256 and 4 attention heads. The decoder block mirrors the settings of the encoder
293   block, except for the input embedding layer, which is a simple lookup table storing embeddings of
294   a fixed dictionary size. The output then passes through a multilayer perceptron (MLP) that maps
295   the embeddings to discrete tokens. EEGTrans is trained using the AdamW optimizer with a cosine
296
      learning rate scheduler and a weight decay of 1e-3. The initial learning rate is set at 1e-6, with a
      warmup epoch of 20 and a maximum learning rate of 1e-3. Following 1000 epochs, the learning rate
297
      gradually decays to 1e-5. The RVQ decoder is trained with the AdamW optimizer with a constant
298
      learning rate 1e-3 and a weight decay of 1e-3.
299
300   The training of all models takes place on a single RTX 4090 GPU. Training for each epoch occurs
301   sequentially, following the order: BCI Competition II Dataset III, BCI Competition IV Dataset 2b,
302
      and BCI Competition IV Dataset 1. Please refer to Appendix C for a more detailed description of
      the configurations for cycleGAN.

305
306   We evaluate performance by visually inspecting the synthetic data and measuring differences be-
307   tween the real and synthetic datasets, including variations in the frequency domain and sample
308   entropy. Furthermore, we utilize the synthetic data to train a classifier and assess whether it pro-
309   vides any advantages for downstream classification tasks. For EEGTrans, synthetic data is created
310   by feeding real data into the encoder, which then prompts the decoder to iteratively generate the next
311   timestamp code starting from the SOC token. Then, the fine-tuned RVQ decoder is utilized to con-
312   vert the discrete codes back into signals. CycleGAN employs generator G to convert EEG signals
313   directly into discrete codes, followed by the use of the RVQ decoder to reconstruct the signal.

      4.6   T IME S ERIES C OMPLEXITY A NALYSIS
408
      Besides visual inspection, we also calculate spectral entropy and sample entropy to verify synthetic
409   data quality. Spectral entropy measures signal complexity or randomness in the frequency domain,
410   derived from Shannon entropy applied to the power spectral density. Lower spectral entropy indi-
411   cates power concentration at specific frequencies. Sample entropy quantifies the complexity and
412   irregularity of time-series data, assessing the likelihood that similar patterns persist over time. Low
413   sample entropy suggests the time series is more regular and predictable.
414
      Spectral entropy and sample entropy are calculated for each time series. We report the values for
415   each subject by averaging across all samples and all channels. As shown in Table 2, only EEGTrans
416   closely matches the ground truth (real data) with minor differences, retaining high sample entropy
417   and thus indicating high complexity and variation. However, it does not retain high-frequency com-
418   ponents, which is evident in the spectral entropy. The synthetic data from CycleGAN loses power
419   in important frequency bands for motor imagery and shows huge amplitude differences from the
420   ground truth in the time domain. This might explain its low sample entropy, suggesting the syn-
421   thetic data is not sufficiently representative.

509   4.8   A BLATION S TUDY
510
511   We conducted an ablation study on EEGTrans’s architecture to assess the encoder-decoder design’s
512   impact. Firstly, we removed the encoder, leaving the decoder to generate tokens with the aid of initial
513   ground truth tokens (i.e., 25%) during inference. Secondly, we excluded the RVQ autoencoder,
514   resulting in direct EEG sequence generation by the decoder, trained using mean squared error loss.
515   This adjustment required introducing a zero vector as the substitute for the SOC token during both
516
      training and inference phases. Please refer to Appendix E for more details.
517   The classification results of the ablation study using only synthetic data for training are shown in
518   Table 4. Here, we only present the average classification accuracy across nine subjects. Further
519   details, including individual subject accuracy and data visualization, are available in Appendix E. As
520   shown in Table 4, removing either the encoder or the discrete codes from the proposed framework
521   significantly hinders the model’s training effectiveness. In fact, data visualization indicates that
522
      without these components, the generated data shows no variation across different channels within the
      same data or even among different data. If inference starts from the SOC token instead of using 25%
523
      ground truth tokens, classification performance is similar to the version without tokens, suggesting
524
      that accuracy beyond random guessing is due to the 25% ground truth tokens. During training,
525   EEGTrans without tokens converges with a mean squared error loss of around 1e-6. However, during
526   inference, providing only a zero vector for autoregressive data generation leads to poor performance,
527   indicating that the model ends up learning a trivial solution without tokens.

807   C.1   RVQ AUTOENCODER M ODEL A RCHITECTURE
808
809   Here, we provide the details of the RVQ Autoencoder, including the hyperparameters used for each
      layer.


                                                       15
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
      RVQ autoencoder consists of an encoder, RVQ, and a decoder. RVQ comprises multiple stages that
811   cascade Nq layers of VQ, with each layer containing k codebooks initialized uniformly with dimen-
812   sion d. To make it easier to train generative models, the latent variables that represent the codebooks
813   for each layer are shared, which helps in reducing complexity. Additionally, to enhance code uti-
814   lization, ℓ2 -normalized codes are used (Yu et al., 2022), and the method employed in SoundStream,
815   which replaces codes with hits below a certain threshold (we set the threshold to 2) with randomly
816   selected vectors from the current batch, is applied. Throughout this study, we fix Nq at 4, k at 2048,
817   and d at 128 for the RVQ autoencoder. We choose these values as they provide high code utilization
818   while maintaining a low reconstruction loss, and set β to 10 in the training loss. The RVQ encoder’s
819
      configuration will ultimately determine the timestamps within the discrete token space (τ ). We set
      the compression factor to be 4 (τ = T4 ).
820
821   The encoder features several 1D convolutional layers and GELU activations. It starts with a Conv1d
822   layer (1 input channel, 16 output channels, kernel size 3, stride 1, padding 1, no bias), followed by
823   GELU. Next is another Conv1d (16 to 32 channels, kernel size 3, stride 1, padding 1, no bias), a
824   grouped downsampling Conv1d (32 to 32 channels, kernel size 2, stride 2, groups 32, no bias), and
825
      GELU. This is followed by a Conv1d (32 to 64 channels, kernel size 3, stride 1, padding 1, no bias)
      and GELU. The final layers include a Conv1d (64 to 128 channels, kernel size 3, stride 1, padding
826
      1, no bias) and another grouped downsampling Conv1d (128 to 128 channels, kernel size 2, stride 2,
827
      groups 128, no bias).
828
829   The decoder features a combination of 1D convolutional and transposed convolutional layers along
830   with GELU activations. It starts with a transposed convolution (128 input and output channels,
831
      kernel size 2, stride 2, groups 128, no bias), followed by a Conv1d (128 to 64 channels, kernel size
      3, stride 1, padding 1, no bias), and GELU. Next, it includes a Conv1d (64 to 32 channels, kernel
832
      size 3, stride 1, padding 1, no bias) with GELU, followed by a transposed convolution (32 input and
833
      output channels, kernel size 2, stride 2, groups 32, no bias). This is followed by a Conv1d (32 to 16
834   channels, kernel size 3, stride 1, padding 1, no bias) with GELU, and the final layer is a Conv1d (16
835   to 1 channel, kernel size 3, stride 1, padding 1, no bias).

      C.2   RVQ C ODEBOOK U TILIZATION
839
840
841   We ran two versions of the RVQ autoencoder by varying the number of codebooks, while keeping
842   the encoder and decoder architecture unchanged, to examine the effect of codebook number on
843
      the quantization process. We trained the autoencoder with 2048 codebooks (as in previous works
      like DeWave (Duan et al., 2023)) and a larger set of 16384 codebooks. With 2048 codebooks, the
844
      mean squared error (MSE) loss between the EEG sequence and its reconstructed sequence in the
845
      BCI Competition IV Dataset 2a was 0.152, while with 16384 codebooks, the MSE loss was 0.131.
846   Although more codebooks improve quantization performance, they significantly increase memory
847   consumption because each latent variable requires GPU memory allocation, with 16384 codebooks
848   requiring more than 24GB of GPU memory during training. Another metric, active code (code
849   utilization), showed that roughly 50% of the codes were used with 16384 codebooks, compared to
850   almost 100% utilization with 2048 codebooks. Therefore, we decided to use the 2048 codebook
851   version.

854   C.3   EEGT RANS M ODEL A RCHITECTURE
855
856
      The architecture of the proposed EEGTrans utilized in this study is depicted in Figure 2. EEGTrans
857
      includes both an encoder and a decoder. In the encoder, the input embedding layer is made up of 6
858   Conv2D layers, which closely resemble the encoder of the RVQ autoencoder, except for the fact that
859   the last two convolutional layers have output channels of 256. The encoder block consists of 4 layers
860   of attention blocks with an embedding size of 256 and 4 attention heads. Similarly, the decoder block
861   follows the same configuration as the encoder block, except for the input embedding layer, which is
862   a simple lookup table storing embeddings of a fixed dictionary size of 256. Subsequently, the output
863   undergoes processing through a MLP with two linear layers of output dimensions 256 and 2048,
      incorporating a GELU activation function in between, to map the embeddings to discrete tokens.

      C.4   C YCLE GAN M ODEL A RCHITECTURE
865
866   As shown in Figure 5, CycleGAN consists of two generators G and F and two discriminators Dx
867   and Dy .
868
      Generator G is composed of convolutional layers and a MLP. These convolutional layers fall into
869
      two categories: those with padding and those without. The ones with padding start with a Re-
870   flectionPad1d layer (padding 1), followed by a Conv1d layer (kernel size 3, stride 1, padding 0),
871   InstanceNorm1d layers, and a GELU activation function. Conversely, the ones without padding
872   begin with a Conv1d layer (kernel size 2, stride 2, padding 0), followed by InstanceNorm1d lay-
873   ers, and a GELU activation function. The arrangement of this generator follows a pattern of two
874   padding convolutional blocks, one downsampling convolutional block, two padding convolutional
875   blocks, one downsampling convolutional block, and finally, a MLP containing two linear layers
876   with output dimensions of 128 and 2048, with a GELU activation function between them, to map
877   the embeddings to discrete tokens.
878   Generator F is composed of transpose convolutional layers and convolutional layers, basically a
879   reverse process of Generator G. The transpose convolutional block begins with a ConvTranspose1d
880   layer (with a kernel size of 2 and a stride of 2), succeeded by a ReflectionPad1d layer (with a padding
881   of 1), a Conv1d layer (with a kernel size of 3, a stride of 1, and no padding), InstanceNorm1d layers,
882   and finally, a GELU activation function. The convolutional block comprises a ReflectionPad1d layer
883   (with a padding of 1) and a Conv1d layer (with a kernel size of 3, a stride of 1, and no padding).
884
      To reconstruct a continuous signal from discrete codes, this generator is built with three transpose
      convolutional blocks followed by one convolutional block. A token embedding is also required to
885
      convert the inputs from discrete codes into vectors.
886
887   Discriminator Dx is composed of a sequence of Conv1d layers. Initially, there is a Conv1d layer
888   with a kernel size of 4, a stride of 2, padding of 1, and no bias, followed by a LeakyReLU activation
889   function. Subsequently, three consecutive convolutional blocks consist of a Conv1d layer (kernel
890
      size 4, stride 2, padding 1, no bias), an InstanceNorm1d layer, and a LeakyReLU activation function.
      The last Conv1d layer in these blocks has a stride of 1. Finally, the model concludes with a Conv1d
891
      layer (kernel size 4, stride 1, padding 1, no bias). The model’s output undergoes average pooling
892
      in the timestamp dimension to distinguish whether the input data is real or synthetic. Discriminator
893   Dy shares a similar model architecture with Discriminator Dy , with the distinction that it operates
894   with discrete input. Therefore, a token embedding is necessary to convert the code into vectors.

      C.5   T RAINING AND I NFERENCE OF G ENERATIVE M ODELS
897
898   The training procedure for EEGTrans is outlined in Section 4.3, but here are some additional details.
899   We train EEGTrans for 1000 epochs, although overfitting to the source datasets typically starts after
900   about 100 epochs. To address this, we employ an early stopping technique. Additionally, we select
901   the model checkpoint that performs best on the target datasets by monitoring the cross-entropy loss
902   of these datasets, which is then used for inference. With early stopping, the training typically takes
903   less than a day on a single RTX 4090 GPU.
904   CycleGAN is trained using the Adam optimizer and the AdamW optimizer, respectively, with the
905   same learning rate scheduler as EEGTrans. We apply the same early stopping strategy for Cycle-
906   GAN. However, for CycleGAN, the checkpoints selected for inference are based on the smallest
907   generator loss on the target datasets. Utilizing the early stopping strategy enables us to maintain the
908   training duration under a day.

       E    A BLATION S TUDY
1002
1003   We performed an ablation study on the architecture of EEGTrans to determine the impact of the
1004   encoder-decoder design on the final results. Additionally, we tested the model without the RVQ
1005
       autoencoder to see if it could still deliver satisfactory performance. First, we remove the encoder
       architecture from EEGTrans while keeping everything else unchanged. This means the decoder can
1006
       no longer use cross-attention on the EEG sequences when generating discrete tokens. Since the
1007
       generated data would be completely random without an encoder if we start the inference from the
1008   SOC token, we address this by providing the first 25% of the ground truth discrete tokens as inputs
1009   to the decoder during inference. Second, we exclude the RVQ autoencoder from the framework,
1010   so the decoder directly generates the continuous EEG sequence. Thus, the decoder is now trained
1011   using mean squared error loss, minimizing the distance between the generated synthetic data and the
1012   corresponding real data, instead of using cross-entropy loss. After removing the RVQ autoencoder,
1013   there is no SOC token anymore. Therefore, the model is trained with a vector consisting of all zeros
1014   prepended at the front, which serves as the SOC token in continuous form. During inference, only
1015   this zero vector is provided initially for the decoder.
1016   The classification results of the ablation study using only synthetic data for training are shown in
1017   Table 7. The synthetic data generated by EEGTrans, without the encoder and RVQ autoencoder, is
1018   shown in Figures 6 and 7. In Figure 6, it is evident that only the segments where ground truth tokens
1019   are provided closely resemble real data. When EEGTrans starts generating tokens autoregressively,
1020   the synthetic data lacks meaningful EEG features. This is reflected in the training loss, indicating
1021   that EEGTrans does not train well without the encoder. Therefore, the encoder currently plays a
1022
       crucial role in EEGTrans. However, one limitation that needs to be addressed in the future is the
       requirement for entire EEG sequences as input for the encoder. As shown in Figure 7, the amplitude
1023
       of the synthetic data is nearly zero during inference.
