009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Conversational Tabular Data Analysis, a collaboration between humans and ma-
012            chines, enables real-time data exploration for informed decision-making. The
013            challenges and costs of collecting realistic conversational logs for tabular data
014            analysis hinder comprehensive quantitative evaluation of Large Language Models
015            (LLMs) in this task. To mitigate this issue, we introduce TAPILOT-C ROSSING,
016            a new benchmark to evaluate LLMs on conversational data analysis. TAPILOT-
017            C ROSSING contains 1024 conversations, covering 4 practical scenarios: N ORMAL,
018
               ACTION, P RIVATE, and P RIVATE ACTION. Notably, TAPILOT-C ROSSING is con-
               structed by an economical multi-agent environment, D ECISION C OMPANY, with
019
               few human efforts. This environment ensures efficiency and scalability of generat-
020
               ing new conversational data. Our comprehensive study, conducted by data analysis
021            experts, demonstrates that D ECISION C OMPANY is capable of producing diverse
022            and high-quality data, laying the groundwork for efficient data annotation. We
023            evaluate popular and advanced LLMs in TAPILOT-C ROSSING, which highlights
024            the challenges of conversational tabular data analysis. Furthermore, we propose
025            Adaptive Conversation Reflection (ACR), a self-generated reflection strategy that
026            guides LLMs to learn from successful histories. Experiments demonstrate that
027            ACR can evolve LLMs into effective conversational data analysis agents, achieving
028            a relative performance improvement of up to 44.5%.

033
      The exponential growth of big data calls for accessible data analysis techniques that cater to a wide
      range of applications, such as healthcare, games, and entertainment (Khanbabaei et al., 2018; Han
034
      et al., 2011; Fayyad et al., 1996). Recently, the development of LLM agents (Liu et al., 2023c; Xu
035
      et al., 2023b; Zeng et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2023a; Deng et al., 2024; Si et al., 2023) has attracted a
036   lot of attention. They are capable of understanding natural language queries, as well as generating
037   codes for data manipulation and visualization, through reasoning (Huang & Chang, 2023; Wei et al.,
038   2022; Yao et al., 2023) and tool calls (Li et al., 2023b; Huang et al., 2023c; Qin et al., 2023). Among
039   the vast types of data available, tabular data stands out as one of the most prevalent and interpretable
040   formats organized by rows and columns.
041
      Tabular data analysis agents, such as SheetCopilot (Li et al., 2024c), TableGPT (Zha et al., 2023),
042   and Data-Copilot (Zhang et al., 2023a), provide automatic workflow based on user queries. However,
043   the dynamic and uncertain nature of real-world analysis hinders effective human-agent conversation,
044   since user intents can often be ambiguous (De Vries et al., 2020; Yan et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024),
045   and users may need to adjust their analysis strategies based on intermediate results (Yan et al., 2023;
046   Yao et al., 2020). For example, in Figure 1, the notable opponents could refer to a variety of
047   interpretations, such as the opponents with the highest wins, or the most frequent opponents. Towards
048   this end, a comprehensive benchmark is indispensable for gauging their capability in conversational
049   user engagement within data analysis scenarios.
050   In this paper, we introduce TAble coPILOT C ROSSING (TAPILOT-C ROSSING), a new benchmark
051   for evaluating LLM agents in conversational data analysis tasks. TAPILOT-C ROSSING is designed
052   to simulate real-world data analysis scenarios, where users converse with LLM agents to generate
053   codes for data exploration and decision making. It includes 1024 user-machine conversations with
      1176 user intents, spanning four practical scenarios, as shown in Figure 1: 1) Normal mode refers to

098   conversational data analysis agents, we propose Adaptive Conversation Reflection (ACR), which
099

100

101

148   3.1      DATASET C ONSTRUCTION OVERVIEW
149
150   The construction of TAPILOT-C ROSSING is mainly based on the AI Agent Sandbox, D ECISION
151   C OMPANY, as depicted in Figure 2. D ECISION C OMPANY is a multi-agent environment where four
152   GPT-4 agents (Administrator, Client, Data Scientist and AI Chatbot) converse with each other to
153   perform data analysis tasks. The construction process involves the following steps: Data Acquisition
154   & Preprocessing, Client Persona Generation, Analysis Scenario Generation, Plan Discussion and
155   conversation Log Simulation. During these stages, human intervention may be required to correct
156
      errors or eliminate harmful or biased content.
157   Data Acquisition & Preprocessing. The first step in the construction of TAPILOT-C ROSSING is
158   the acquisition and preprocessing of data. We collect open-source tables from Kaggle 1 , a popular
159   data science platform. These tables cover 18 analysis topics under 5 common domains, namely
160   ATP Tennis, Credit Card, Fast Food, Laptop Price, and Melbourne Housing,
161

163   meanings and value illustrations.

165   Client Persona Generation. The construction of TAPILOT-C ROSSING proceeds to the generation
166
      of client personas. These personas with specific tasks and topics related to the data are created by
      the Administrator Agent. Each persona is defined by a Name, Location, Job, and Background
167
      with a diverse range of interests and backgrounds.
168
169   Simulation of Analysis Scenarios. Then, Administrator Agent conducts interviews with each Client
170   Agent to ask about their Scenario description, Scenario Name, and the Goal of using the
171   dataset for the scenario. In the TAPILOT-C ROSSING dataset, human annotators will intervene here
172
      and select the most reasonable or interesting scenarios. This ensures that the scenarios included in the
      TAPILOT-C ROSSING dataset are meaningful, and not too general across different clients. For instance,
173
      in B.3 of Figure 2, we select Court Condition Impact because Player Performance
174
      Analysis is too general and Sponsor Attraction requires too much additional information
175   out of the table contents, which leads to too many unanswerable questions.
176
177   Plan Discussion. In this process, the Data Scientist Agent engages with the Client Agent to convert
178
      the requirements of client into a series of specific data analysis questions with well-defined conditions.
      Each question is provided by an expected result type, such as dataframes, lists, or various plot
179
      types, which helps reduce question ambiguity and ease the pressure on evaluation metrics (Yin et al.,
180
      2023; He et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023c). The dialogue between the agents further refines the
181   questions with specific conditions. For example, as depicted in Figure 2 B.4, the client Garcia’s
182   question could be further elaborated on the basis of his following responses, making all questions
183   more answerable. In particular, Agent Garcia, fully cognizant of his persona created in B.2, adds
184   the condition grass, reflecting his London location. This implies that the role-playing aspect of
185   the agent can be instrumental in generating a wider range of questions that are both diverse and
186   reasonable (Li et al., 2024a; Park et al., 2023).
187   Conversation Log Annotation. Following the plan discussion, the conversation simulation phase
188   begins. Here, the AI Chatbot Agent takes the lead, executing the data analysis plan agreed on during
189   the previous stage. The AI Chatbot Agent converses with the Data Scientist Agent to answer a series
190   of questions defined in the plan by generating codes and analyzing returned results.

193   3.2   H UMAN C ALIBRATION A ND A NNOTATION
194
195   While the D ECISION C OMPANY can generate a wealth of data analysis conversations in a zero-shot
196   prompting manner, human intervention is indispensable to ensure the quality of the data set annotation
197   (Lu et al., 2023). Our observations indicate that only 23.5% of the original responses produced by the
198   AI Chatbot Agent can be directly used as reference codes. Therefore, we engage two PhD students in
199   each stage of the generation process to calibrate the errors and meaningless conversations. While
200   human intervention is required, it is worth notice that modifying existing answers or codes is more
201
      efficient than creating them from scratch. We preserve all natural and meaningful conversations, both
      agent-to-agent and human-to-machine, throughout the action setting collection.
202
203   The involvement of two PhD students follows a careful workflow to ensure the quality and accuracy of
204   the benchmark data. Each student, with over 10 years of experience in data analysis, is assigned with
205   distinct sets of initial data analysis scripts consisting of topics, tabular data, and first-turn questions.
206   During each conversational turn, the AI Chatbot generates code in response to specific analysis
207
      questions. The students then execute this code, verify the outputs for correctness, fix any bugs, and
      ensure that the results align with the expected output types, which can answer the question accurately.
208
      This refined code is referenced as ground-truth code for the benchmark. Additionally, the students
209
      generate evaluation scripts (eval.py) for testing model-generated code. Following this, the students
210   execute the code and feed the results back to the AI Chatbot, which generates free-form text analysis
211   statements (e.g., "The credit card application rate of people with 4-year
212   employment is 73.5% higher than those with no employment"). The stu-
213   dents review and correct these statements, converting them into multiple-choice question formats for
214   more reliable and objective evaluation. All corrected code and free-form text analysis are stored as
215   static user-AI conversation history, enabling further reference during annotation. After completing
      this conversational process, the students exchange their annotated data for cross-validation, achieving

      3.3       P RIVATE L IBRARY M ODE E VOLUTION
235
236   Data analysts frequently rely on their private libraries (Zan et al., 2022). These libraries, often tailored
237   for specific needs, allow more efficient and customized data processing and analysis. Furthermore,
238   generating codes by user-defined packages can test the true semantic parsing abilities of agents rather
239   than merely testing their memorization of standard syntax from libraries such as Pandas or Numpy. It
240   also evaluates their ability to understand and implement custom functions, which is a crucial aspect
241   of real-world data analysis. In this work, we prompt GPT-4 to autonomously convert prototype codes
242   with pre-trained functions like Pandas and Numpy into private codes. It contains three steps: 1)
243   summarizing packages from original code; 2) refactoring and converting them to user customized
244
      functions with light human supervision; 3) regenerating codes via customized functions with human
      calibration. Please refer to Appendix T for more details.

      3.4       E VALUATION S CRIPT C ACHING
248   Each example will be provided by a specific evaluation script to ensure the precision of our assess-
249   ments (Lai et al., 2023). To manage the extensive effort required to design scripts for each example,
250   we introduce a cache-based evaluation binding approach. Initially, we classify mainstream result
251   types, which are collected through steps introduced in Section 3.1, and develop highly specialized
252   scripts for each type, such as dataframes and dictionaries. When new data is generated, an evaluation
253   script is automatically assigned based on the result type. Two PhD students then review the assigned
254   script to ensure its accuracy; if necessary, they adapt and generate a new script tailored to the specific
255   case. This method allows us to streamline the evaluation process, making it more efficient with
256
      minimal human intervention. The details of the evaluation script in each result type can be found in
      Section L.

      Accuracy (Acc). Acc is a metric that evaluates the ability of agents in generating codes that execute
301
      correctly or answer multiple-choice questions accurately. It is defined as the proportion of instances
302
      whose predicted outputs match the expected reference output, across all evaluated tasks.
303
304
      Accuracy with Private Library Recall (AccR). Recognizing the importance of accurately lever-
305   aging specific user-defined libraries in code generation, we extend Acc to include a recall-based
306   adjustment for instances involving private libraries. This ensures that AccR not only evaluates the
307   direct accuracy of code execution and question answering but also the inclusion and correct usage of
308   private library functions. We conduct an in-depth analysis of the impact of AccR on Private mode
309   evaluation in Section N.

347   6     E VOLVING LLM S T OWARDS C ONVERSATIONAL DATA A NALYSIS AGENTS
348
349   In this section, we discuss our approach of equipping LLMs as data analysis agents with tools
350   and reasoning. We then introduce our self-generated reflection strategy, ACR, to enhance their
351   performance in conversational settings.
352
353   6.1   T OOLKIT
354
355   Our tool sets include an executor, a user simulator, and a chart-to-table converter. The executor
356   provides an environment for models to observe real-time feedback on their intermediate code results
357   (Xie et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024). The user simulator (Wang et al., 2024; Yan et al., 2023),
358   powered by GPT-4-Turbo, tests the agents’ ability to generate codes after clarifying details when
359
      facing under-specific questions. The chart-to-table (Liu et al., 2023a) converter mitigates the prevalent
      issue of LLMs’ inability to comprehend plots by converting them into tables. Detailed descriptions
360
      of these tool sets can be found in Appendix I.2.
361
362
363   6.2   R EASONING
364
      Reasoning is a critical process for transitioning LLMs into data analysis agents (Huang & Chang,
365
      2023). In TAPILOT-C ROSSING, we incorporate two primary reasoning methods for code generation
366
      and multiple-choice answers. The first is the Chain-of-Thought (COT) prompting technique (Wei
367   et al., 2022), which enhances the complex reasoning abilities of LLMs by dividing the reasoning
368   path into multiple steps. The second method is Reasoning & Action (ReAct), which enables models
369   to make decisions by generating reasoning traces and actions in an interleaved manner, inducing
370   writing, executing them, understanding results, and analyzing them to make the final choices (Yao
371   et al., 2023).
372
373   6.3   A DAPTIVE C ONVERSATION R EFLECTION (ACR)
374
375   Successful conversations are important since they encapsulate the logic necessary to meet user
376   requirements and ensure correct steps of analysis or code generation. Motivated by this, we propose
377   the Adaptive Conversation Reflection (ACR) approach to enable data analysis agents to learn from
      successful user-code histories through a two-step process.

506    8                          R ELATED W ORK
507
508   The use of LLMs for data analysis has garnered significant interest, with In-Context Learning applied
509   to tasks like SQL query generation (Pourreza & Rafiei, 2024a; Gao et al., 2023; Lei et al., 2023;
510   Zhang et al., 2023b; Gu et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2023a; Pourreza & Rafiei, 2024b; Li et al., 2024b),
511   pandas or Python code generation (Jain et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024; 2023a; Li et al., 2024c; Zha
512   et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023a; Zheng et al., 2024b), and data visualization (Chen et al., 2023b;
513
      Huang et al., 2023a). While most studies focus on single-turn conversations with explicit queries,
      recent work highlights the need for conversational data analysis to refine user intents (De Vries et al.,
514
      2020; Yan et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024). Benchmarks like HumanEval (Chen et al., 2021) and
515
      Spider (Yu et al., 2018) for single-turn, and CoSQL (Yu et al., 2019a) and ARCADE (Yin et al.,
516   2023) for multi-turn conversations, primarily target code generation, while often neglecting data
517   visualization and intermediate result understanding. Our benchmark, TAPILOT-C ROSSING, addresses
518   this gap by evaluating LLM agents in conversational data analysis using the multi-agent environment
519   D ECISION C OMPANY. Inspired by multi-agent environments for data generation (Lu et al., 2023;
520   Ding et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023b; Park et al., 2023), we generate realistic conversation logs for data
521   analysis, thus pioneering an conversational benchmark for evaluating data analysis agents.
522
523    9                          C ONCLUSION
524
525   We introduce TAPILOT-C ROSSING, a new benchmark for evaluating LLM agents in conversational
526   data analysis tasks. TAPILOT-C ROSSING is constructed via a cost-effective and expert-recognized
527   high-quality multi-agent environment, D ECISION C OMPANY, and covers a wide range of practical
528   scenarios. We evaluate data analysis agents based on popular LLMs on TAPILOT-C ROSSING, high-
529   lighting the challenges of conversational data analysis and the need for more advanced conversational
530   data analysis agents. We also propose ACR, an effective reflection strategy for conversational data
531   analysis agent evolution. Our experiments demonstrate that ACR can significantly enhance the
532   performance of LLM agents.

954

956   (7) Claude-3-Opus: Claude-3-Opus is the latest in the Claude series, bringing substantial improve-

959   tasks.
960   (8) Llama-3-70B-instruct: Llama-3-70B is a LLM with 70 billion parameters. This model is
961   designed to tackle the most challenging NLP tasks, providing unparalleled performance in terms of
962   accuracy and coherence in text generation.
963
      (9) GPT-4-32k: GPT-4-32k is a variant of the GPT-4 model with an extended context window of
964   32,000 tokens. This extended context window allows it to handle long-form content more effectively,

1284   L.6   C ODE S IMILARITY E QUIVALANCE (CSE)
1285
1286   In the context of TAPILOT-C ROSSING, the complexity of code generation tasks—many of which yield
1287
       a score of zero—presents huge challenges in evaluating performance through Acc or AccR only. This
       is particularly evident when distinguishing between codes that differ by merely a single line of error or
1288
       output, both of which would result in an Acc or AccR of zero, despite their obvious differences in code
1289
       generation capabilities. To overcome this limitation, we propose the introduction of Code Similarity
1290   Equivalence (CSE), a nuanced evaluation metric designed to assess the similarity between generated
1291   codes and reference codes. Given that these codes originate based on identical user instructions,
1292   a high degree of similarity is expected. Our approach leverages a hybrid combination of models
1293   to reduce the bias, incorporating CodeT5++ and OpenAI Ada (text-embedding-ada-002)
1294   models, which are affordable and available for most institutes. This combination has demonstrated a
1295   strong correlation with human evaluative preferences, offering a more refined and accurate measure
       of code generation performance.

1455   instead of the correct attribute ‘[‘employment’]’. This error type suggests that the model may overly
1456   rely on its trained patterns rather than accurately assessing the real structure of the data, leading
1457   to ‘hallucinated’ column references. (2) Lazy Assumption (37%) refers to instances where the

1713   Dataset Limitations. 1) The TAPILOT-C ROSSING dataset assumes that all human-machine con-
1714   versation history is clean and correct. However, in real-world scenarios, the conversation history
1715   is often not clean, and may contain noise or require multi-turn clarifications for a single question.
1716   Therefore, future work should consider a more realistic, noisy conversational benchmark in data
1717   analysis. It is also worth noting that even with a clean history, the most capable model, GPT-4-32k,
1718   only achieves a score of 30.2 in the Inter-Agent mode. 2) The creation of our dataset is both
1719
       cost-effective and efficient; however, the evaluation phase demands considerable efforts due to the
       inherent complexity and unpredictability of data analysis questions. Given it is challenging to discern
1720
       subtle differences in performance among data analysis agents, especially in the context of long-form
1721
       code generation and execution accuracy. This difficulty is exacerbated by the fact that the execution
1722   results of code with a single error (i.e., one-line error) and a completely incorrect code (just one-line
1723   output) are also determined as 0. Therefore, a soft-metric evaluation system should be introduced in
1724   the future as Appendix L.6. It would improve our ability to accurately gauge how close an answer is
1725   to the expected output, even when the executed output is zero, thereby providing a more fine-grained
1726   observation of code generation capabilities. 3) Finally, our work only concentrate on tabular data
1727   based analysis, while in the future, we would like to involve relational database (RDB)-based analysis
       with the programming language of SQLs.

       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025

       Method Limitations. Our proposed reflection strategy can evolve LLMs into more effective
1729   conversational data analysis agents, but relies heavily on the accuracy of previous conversations.
1730   This reliance becomes less reliable in instances where the historical dialogue is cluttered with errors,
1731   suggesting the need for retrieval-augmented tools or methods to identify successful past conversations.
1732   Additionally, this strategy does not enhance agent performance in initial conversations due to the
1733   absence of historical data. Finally, while effective in many ACTION settings, this focus on conversation
1734   history may limit the inferential capabilities of LLMs by prioritizing past conversations over present
1735   context. Future efforts will be directed towards refining this approach to better balance the benefits of
1736   leveraging historical conversations against the need to maintain or enhance the inferential capabilities
1737
       of LLMs.
