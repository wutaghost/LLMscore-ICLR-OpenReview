011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Recently, synthesizing video from the text, i.e, Text-to-Video (T2V), has demon-
014            strated remarkable progress by transferring the pre-trained Text-to-Image (T2I)
015            diffusion models to the video domain, whose core is to add new temporal layers
016
               for capturing temporal information. However, these additional layers inevitably
               incur extra computational overhead, as they need to be trained from scratch on
017
               large-scale video datasets. Instead of retraining these costly layers, we conjecture
018
               whether temporal information can be learned from the original T2I model with
019            only Spatial Attention. To this end, our theoretical and experimental explorations
020            reveal that Spatial Attention has a strong potential for temporal modeling and
021            greatly promotes training efficiency. Inspired by it, we propose ETC, a new T2V
022            framework that achieves high fidelity and high efficiency in terms of training and
023            inference. Specifically, to adapt the video to the spatial attention of T2I, we first
024            design a novel temporal-to-spatial transfer strategy to organize entire video frames
025            into a spatial grid. Then, we devise a simple yet effective Spatial-Temporal Mixed
026            Embedding, to distinguish the inter-frame and intra-frame features. Benefiting
027
               from the above strategy that actually reduces the model’s dependence on the text-
               video pairing dataset, we present a data-efficient strategy, Triple-Data (caption-
028
               image, label-image, and caption-video pairs) fusion that can achieve better per-
029
               formance with a small amount of video data for training. Extensive experiments
030            show the superiority of our method over the four strong SOTA methods in terms
031            of quality and efficiency, particularly improving FVD by 49% on average with
032            only 1% training dataset.

055   structure of the T2I diffusion model, including pre-trained parameters. However, these additional
056   attention layers must be learned from scratch on large-scale video datasets to perform well, which
057   inevitably brings a huge training overhead. We note that zero-shot video generation (Hong et al.,

100   2.1   T EXT- TO -V IDEO V IDEO G ENERATION
101
102   In computer storage, a video is composed of multiple frames of images, and in the field of genera-
103   tion, video information is considered to consist of spatial information within images and temporal
104
      information between images. A common approach among video generation researchers is to build
      upon previously pre-trained image generation models, extending them with temporal models for
105
      video generation.
106
107   For instance, CogVideo (Hong et al., 2023b) enhances the large-scale T2I transformer
      CogView2 (Ding et al., 2022) by incorporating temporal information through inter-frame attention

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      mechanisms. In contrast, Make-A-Video (Singer et al., 2023) diverges from the typical reliance on
109   text-video pairs for T2V generation by leveraging a pretrained T2I model, thereby eliminating the
110   need for text-video paired training. Imagen Video (Ho et al., 2022) builds upon Imagen (Saharia
111   et al., 2022) by employing a cascaded diffusion model that utilizes both attention and convolution
112   across multiple resolutions. Furthermore, as the quality of video generation improves, recent re-
113   search has begun to explore various settings for generation. For example, Tune-A-Video (Wu et al.,
114   2022) introduces a one-shot video tuning method for T2V generation, incorporating temporal atten-
115   tion into the Stable Diffusion framework (Rombach et al., 2022b). And Text2Video-Zero (Khacha-
116   tryan et al., 2023a) enables zero-shot T2V generation without training video. These works all under-
117
      score the utilization of pre-trained image generation models to supply spatial information for video
      generation, which is undeniably effective. However, they each introduce a new trainable module to
118
      the original model for processing temporal information. This module requires training from scratch,
119
      making the process significantly resource-intensive. And using additional modules may result in a
120   potential waste of resources, see Appendix A and B.

      2.2   Z ERO -S HOT V IDEO G ENERATION
123
124   While video generation researchers generally agree that spatial and temporal information should be
125   processed separately to reduce computational load and preserve the quality of the original generated
126   image, some have begun to explore merging spatial and temporal information in zero-shot settings.
127   For instance, VidToMe (Li et al., 2023b) extends ToMe (Bolya et al., 2022) to video generation by
128   merging video tokens into image tokens for attention processing. Similarly, another work Li et al.
129   (2023a) employs an expectation-maximization iteration to update a basis set for temporal modeling
130   within spatial attention. Latent-shift (An et al., 2023) propose a parameter-free temporal shift mod-
131
      ule that can leverage the spatial U-Net as is for video generation. Text2Video-Zero Khachatryan
      et al. (2023b) encoding motion dynamics in the latent codes, and reprogramming each frame’s self-
132
      attention using new cross-frame attention. Some works use LLM as directors to process temporal
133
      information. DirecT2V (Hong et al., 2023a) utilizes LLM directors to divide user inputs into sepa-
134   rate prompts for each frame to generate videos. Free-Bloom (Huang et al., 2024) uses LLM directors
135   to generate high high-fidelity frames with an annotative modification LDM. These studies focus on
136   zero-shot video generation, which tends to produce lower-quality outputs compared to models that
137   undergo temporal tuning. In contrast, Lee et al. (2024) takes a different approach by modeling tem-
138   poral information within spatial attention. It concatenates four images into a single large image and
139   uses an original image diffusion model to handle video through autoregressive interpolation. These
140   models do not use additional timing modules when generating videos, and complete video genera-
141   tion tasks such as frame insertion and video editing. Inspired by those approaches, we have designed
142
      and implemented a model that eliminates the need for the temporal module of typical text-to-video
      generation. We hope this work will provide valuable insights for future advancements in this scope.

145   3     O BSERVATION
146
147   The additional temporal parameters in text-to-video models bring huge training costs and require
148   large-scale text-video datasets. We note that recent zero-shot video generation is no need for ad-
149   ditional temporal parameters. By introducing subtle adjustments to the noise level, the layout of
150   the generated images can be influenced to reflect temporal changes without compromising quality.
151   This demonstrates that a well-trained image generation model is capable of generating temporal
152
      information, which can be unlocked with appropriate methods, thereby eliminating the need for sep-
      arate temporal attention. So, which structure can handle the temporal information for pure spatial
153
      image diffusion? Image diffusion has three compositions: 2D convolution, Spatial Attention, and
154
      Cross Attention. Cross Attention is responsible for integrating textual information into the gener-
155   ation process, while the inter-frame information handled by 2D convolution remains independent.
156   Consequently, image diffusion, with only spatial attention layers, possesses the potential to process
157   temporal information. Therefore, we speculate that spatial attention is the module for the original
158   T2I model to learn temporal information.
159
      With the above conjecture, we verify it in both theoretical and experimental aspects:

      5.1   S ETTINGS
354
355   Datasets. Follow the settings of previous text-to-video generation models, we select two close-
356   domain public video datasets and create an open-domain dataset for testing, including a) MSR-VTT
357   (Xu et al., 2016), a caption-video pair dataset, b) UCF-101, an action recognition dataset, which
358   contain label-to-video pairs, and c) VC Video Caption dataset with 500 prompts, which consists of
359   full sentences generated by ChatGPT (OpenAI, 2021).
360
      Metrics. To comprehensively evaluate the effectiveness and efficacy of different text-to-video gen-
361   eration models, we adopt four commonly used metrics as follows: a) FVD (Unterthiner et al.,
362   2018), which is pertained by Kinetics (Kay et al., 2017) dataset to evaluate the quality of spatial
363   and temporal features in video generation, b) Clip-Score Hessel et al. (2021), which is to measure
364   the alignment of text and image denoted as CLIP, c) User Study to measure the human-like, and d)
365   Speed, which aimsto provide an assessment of the practical running speed by frame per second.
366
      Training Details. The spatial modules are initialized with weights of SD2.1 (Rombach et al.,
367   2022a). The base training resolution is set to 256 × 256 at 16 frames. We utilize the selected

      6   C ONCLUSION
487
488
      We aim to improve video generation models by removing temporal attention and transferring its
489   function to spatial attention. To support this approach, we propose the Spatial-Temporal Mixed
490   Embedding, allowing the same attention mechanism to distinguish between intra-frame and inter-
491   frame information. Additionally, we introduce the FPS-based Triple-Data Driven Training. As a
492   result, we develop a high-quality, high-speed video generation model with minimal data dependency.
493   We believe that our work corrects mistakes in the design of previous video generation models and
494   will inspire future advancements in video generation.
495   However, unlike autoregressive models, we do not support changes in resolution and frame rate
496   without additional training. We expect that with training on mixed resolutions, the Spatial-Temporal
497   Mixed Embedding can enable the model to generate videos with different resolutions and frame
498   rates. This issue will be explored in more detail in future research.

828
829   To demonstrate that spatial attention is capable of modeling temporal information, we begin by
830   providing a mathematical proof that the traditional spatial and temporal processing approach is a
831   linear combination of the input video, rather than a quadratic or derivative relationship, which allows
832   it to be replaced by a single spatial mechanism in this section.
833   Attention (Vaswani, 2017) transforms input data into query, key, and value (QKV) representations
834   and processes them using the Scaled Dot-Product Attention, as described by Eq. 8. This mecha-
835   nism captures relationships within the same data source (Self-Attention) or across different sources
836   (Cross-Attention). While it involves a nonlinear softmax operation, the core of the attention mecha-
837   nism is largely driven by linear operations. Building on this, Schlag et al. (2021) proposed a linear
838   Transformer model that achieves efficiency gains by linearizing the Attention mechanism. Although
839   their primary focus was on improving computational efficiency, their work also demonstrated that
840
      Attention can, in some cases, be approximated or replaced by linear operations. Furthermore, Zheng
      et al. (2022) explored the linearization of self-attention mechanisms, proposing a novel method that
841
      maintains performance while reducing computational complexity to linear. Therefore, for simplifi-
842
      cation, we consider attention as a linear relationship.

1152   E.2      T RAINING
1153
1154   To fully utilize the high-performance tensor cores available in NVIDIA Ampere GPUs, we use
1155   mixed-precision training (precision=16) in all our training runs. Specifically, we store all trainable
1156   parameters as 32-bit floating point (FP32) but temporarily cast them to 16-bit floating point (FP16)
1157   before evaluating the model. We store and process all activation tensors as FP16, except for the
1158   embedding network and the associated per-block linear layers, where we opt for FP32 due to their
1159   low computational cost.
1160   During the model training process, we employ the DeepSpeed strategy (stage=2) and enable op-
1161   timizer offloading to the CPU (offload optimizer=True) to reduce memory usage effectively. This
1162   strategy allows us to train larger models on limited hardware resources. Additionally, we use the
1163   CPUAdam optimizer, provided by DeepSpeed, which performs optimization calculations on the
1164   CPU, further reducing the computational burden on the GPU. Specifically, we configure the opti-
1165   mizer with a base learning rate, betas set to (0.9, 0.9), and a weight decay of 0.03.
1166   Furthermore, we utilize the DeepSpeed internal checkpointing feature to store partial gradients and
1167   other intermediate states during training. This helps manage memory efficiently and ensures smooth
1168   training progress.
