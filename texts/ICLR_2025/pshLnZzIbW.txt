011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            In this work, we propose two efficient parallel algorithms, LinAst and LinAtg, that
014            improve both the approximation ratio and query complexity of existing practi-
015            cal parallel algorithms for the non-monotone submodular maximization over the
016            ground set of sized n under a cardinality constraint k. Specifically, our algorithms
017            keep the best adaptive complexity of O(log n) while significantly improving the
018
               approximation ratio from 1/6 − ϵ to 0.193 − ϵ and reducing the query complexity
               from O(n log(k)) to O(n). The key building block of our algorithms is LinAdapt,
019
               a constant approximation ratio within O(log n) sequence rounds and linear queries.
020
               LinAdapt can reduce the query complexity by providing O(1) guesses of the opti-
021            mal value. We further introduce the BoostAdapt algorithm returning a better ratio
022            of 1/4 − ϵ within O(log(n) log(k)) adaptive complexity and O(n log(k)) query
023            complexity. Our BoostAdapt works in a novel staggered greedy threshold frame-
024            work that alternately constructs two disjoint sets in O(log k) sequential rounds.
025            Besides theoretical analysis, the experiment results on validated benchmarks con-
026            firm the superiority of our algorithms in terms of solution quality, the number of
027            required queries, and running time over cutting-edge algorithms.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032
      Maximizing a non-negative (not necessarily monotone) submodular set function under cardinality
033   constraint is a fundamental and important problem that has a wide-range of applications in the fields
034   of artificial intelligence and machine learning, such as data summarization (Kuhnle, 2021b; Lin
035   & Bilmes, 2011; Chen & Kuhnle, 2023; Fahrbach et al., 2019a; Han et al., 2021; Mirzasoleiman
036   et al., 2016), revenue maximization in social networks (Kuhnle, 2021b; Chen & Kuhnle, 2023),
037   recommendation systems (Mirzasoleiman et al., 2016) and weight cut (Chen & Kuhnle, 2023; Kuhnle,
038   2021b). Given a finite ground set V sized n, a submodular set function f : 2V 7→ R+ and a positive
039   number k (cardinality constraint), the submodular maximization under cardinality constraint (SMC)
040   problem asks to find a set S ⊆ V with |S| ≤ k so that f (S) is maximized. The problem has received
041
      much attention in finding approximation solutions with theoretical bounds (Buchbinder et al., 2014;
      2015; Kuhnle, 2019; Chen & Kuhnle, 2023; Gupta et al., 2010).
042
043   However, the problem must face exponentially increasing search space due to the explosion of input
044   data. This motivates much effort to design efficient parallel algorithms that can power the parallel
045   architectures of computer systems to obtain a good solution promptly (See Table 2 for an overview of
046   parallelizable algorithms). In this context, the concept of adaptive complexity or adaptivity becomes
047
      an essential measure of the feasibility of any parallel algorithm. This concept is to evaluate the
      number of sequential rounds of an algorithm that can execute many independent polynomial oracle
048
      queries in parallel (Balkanski & Singer, 2018).
049
050   It is noted that improving adaptive complexity from O(log2 (n)) to O(log n) dramatically reduces
051   the number of sequential iterations, thereby significantly reducing the running time of the algorithms
052   in practice (Fahrbach et al., 2019a; Ene & Nguyen, 2020; Amanatidis et al., 2021; Fahrbach et al.,
053   2023; Kuhnle, 2021b; Chen & Kuhnle, 2024). Although the recent studies significantly reduce the
      adaptive complexity from O(log2 (n)) to O(log n), they still face with many challenges, including:

083   Our contributions and techniques. In this work, we tackle the above questions with the following
084   contributions:
085
086         • First, we introduce LinAdapt, the first constant approximation ratio (1/(12 + O(ϵ))) within
087           near-optimal adaptivity O(log n) and linear query complexity O(n), where ϵ > 0 is a
088           constant parameter. LinAdapt is used as a building block to reduce the query complexity of
089
              our latter algorithms. The key idea to obtain a such improvement lies in: (1) constructing
              two sets S, S ′ in O(log n) sequential rounds with an interesting property: f (X ∪ S) ≤
090
              O(1) · f (S ′ ) with high probability for any set X ⊆ V, |X| ≤ k, and S ′ with |S ′ | ≤ k is the
091
              set of last elements added into S; (2) combining (1) appropriately with the unconstrained
092           submodular maximization algorithm to get the desired ratio.
093
            • Second, we introduce two algorithms LinAst and LinAtg which run O(log n) adaptivity
094
              and O(n) query complexity and return the approximation ratios to 1/6 − ϵ and 0.193 − ϵ,
095
              respectively. Therefore, our algorithm LinAtg improves the approximation ratio from
096           1/6 − ϵ to 0.193 − ϵ and significantly reduces the query complexity of the current best
097           practical algorithm of (Chen & Kuhnle, 2024). Both LinAst and LinAtg use a common
098           framework: first adapting LinAdapt as a subroutine to get the O(1) number of guesses of the
099           optimal, then using the adaptive simple threshold (Chen & Kuhnle, 2024) and iterated greedy
100           framework (Gupta et al., 2010; Chen & Kuhnle, 2024) to get better theoretical bounds.
101         • Third, we further introduce BoostAdapt that provides a considerable approximation ratio of
102           1/4 − ϵ in O(log(n) log(k)) adaptive complexity and O(n log(k)) query complexity. Thus,
103           BoostAdapt significantly improves the ratio of the ATG (Chen & Kuhnle, 2024)(the algo-
104           rithm with same adaptive and query complexity) from 0.193 − ϵ to 1/4 − ϵ and also reduces
105           the query complexity of ParSKP2 (Cui et al., 2023) (the algorithm with same approximation
106           ratio and adaptive complexity) by a factor of Ω(k log2 (n)/ log(k)). BoostAdapt follows a
107           novel staggered threshold framework: updating alternately two disjoint sets X and Y only
              in O(log k) iterations by adapting a threshold sampling. It must be noted that the staggered

122   2   R ELATED W ORKS
123
124   Non-parallel Approximation Algorithms with Low Query Complexity. The first popular approach
125
      to solving the SMC problem in practice is to design approximation algorithms with low query
      complexity. The greedy algorithm was an effective approach for submodular optimization problems.
126
      It sequentially selects elements with the largest marginal gain and explores the diminishing return
127
      property to get performance bounds. (Gupta et al., 2010) first developed an iterated greedy algorithm
128   with 1/6 ratio in O(nk) queries for SMC. The work of (Lee et al., 2010) latter improve the ratio
129   to 1/4 − ϵ by developing a local search method but this wasted an expensive query complexity of
130   O(n4 log n). Significantly, the elegant random greedy in (Buchbinder et al., 2014) could provide the
131   ratio of 1/e with O(nk) query complexity. Instead of selecting an element with the best marginal gain
132   as the naive greedy, it chose a uniformly random element from the set of k elements with the largest
133   marginal gain. (Buchbinder et al., 2015) then improved the query complexity to O(n log(1/ϵ)/ϵ2 )
134   but still kept the same 1/e ratio. To the best of our knowledge, the best ratio for SMC was 0.385 by
135   (Buchbinder & Feldman, 2019). However, this work used the multi-linear extensions method and
136
      used a considerably high query complexity of O(n5 ) (Chen & Kuhnle, 2023). Besides, some efforts
      focused on devising deterministic approximations but did not improve the performance ratio or reduce
137
      the query complexity (Chen & Kuhnle, 2023; Kuhnle, 2019; Buchbinder & Feldman, 2018). Finally,
138
      it must be emphasized that the above works weren’t well parallelized due to their high adaptive
139   complexities of Ω(n).
140
141   Parallel Algorithms with Low adaptive complexity. Research on parallel algorithms was initiated
142
      by (Balkanski & Singer, 2018). In this seminal work, they introduced the concept of adaptive
      complexity or adaptivity that measuring the parallelizable of algorithm, showed a lower bound adaptive
143
      complexity of O(log(n)/ log(log n)) to achieve a constant ratio and devised an 1/3-approximation
144
      algorithm in O(log n) adaptive rounds for the monotone SMC problem. Since then, many works
145   focused on devising algorithms with low adaptivity with tighter ratio for SMC (Balkanski et al., 2019;
146   Fahrbach et al., 2019b; Ene et al., 2019; Chen et al., 2021). The best parallel algorithm for monotone
147   SMC had an optimal ratio of 1 − 1/e − ϵ in O(log n) adaptive complexity and O(n) queries was due
148   to (Chen et al., 2021). However, their performance bounds heavily relied on the monotone property
149   and did not hold for non-monotone.
150
      For the non-monotone SMC, (Chekuri     √ & Quanrud, 2019; Balkanski et al., 2018) first developed
151
      parallelizable algorithms with 3 − 2 2 − ϵ and 1/(2e) − ϵ ratios, respectively; both took O(log2 n)
152
      adaptivity. (Ene & Nguyen, 2020) improved the ratio to (1/e − ϵ) in nearly optimal adaptive
153
      complexity of O(log n). However, due to the high query complexity of Ω(nk 2 log2 n) to access the
154   multilinear extension of a submodular function and its gradient. Therefore, the (Ene & Nguyen, 2020)’
155   algorithm and algorithms based on multilinear extension methods in general are still impractical in
156   some real applications (Kuhnle, 2021b; Fahrbach et al., 2019a; Chen & Kuhnle, 2024; Cui et al.,
157   2023). To attack the above issue, (Fahrbach et al., 2019a) first aimed to reduce the query complexity
158   to near-linear of O(n log k) and still kept the O(log n) adaptive complexity. However, their algorithm
159   resulted in a small ratio, 0.039 − ϵ. In subsequent work, (Kuhnle, 2021b) tried to boost the ratio to
160   1/6 − ϵ in O(log n) adaptive rounds with O(n log k) queries. Their algorithm further improved the
161   ratio to 0.193 − ϵ in O(log2 n) adaptive rounds by exploring iterated greedy framework in (Gupta
      et al., 2010). It must be noted that the ratios of both (Fahrbach et al., 2019a) and (Kuhnle, 2021b)

      may not hold because they adapt the threshold sampling routine in (Fahrbach et al., 2019b), which
163   was pointed out disable with non-monotone functions (Chen & Kuhnle, 2024). Recently, (Chen &

171
      Given a ground set V of sized n, and the set function f : 2V 7→ R+ is submodularity iff it satisfies the
172
      diminishing return property, i.e., f (A ∪ {e}) − f (A) ≥ f (B ∪ {e}) − f (B) where A ⊆ B and e ∈     / B.
173
      The marginal gain (or contribution gain) of a set T to a set S is defined as f (T |S) = f (T ∪ S) − f (S).
174   For simplicity, we denote f ({e}|A) by f (e|A) and assume f normalized, i.e., f (∅) = 0. Given a
175   positive number k (cardinality constraint), the SMC problem is to determine arg maxS⊆V,|S|≤k f (S).
176
177   We define [k] = {1, 2, . . . , k} for any integer k. We denote an instance of SMC by a tuple (f, V, k)
178
      and O is an optimal solution with the optimal value opt = f (O). In this work, we assume that
      there exists an oracle query that returns f (S) when queried for the set S with any S ⊆ V . The
179
      parallelization capacity of an algorithm is evaluated through the following definition.
180
181
      Definition 3.1 ((Balkanski & Singer, 2018)). Given a value of oracle of f , the adaptivity or adaptive
      complexity of an algorithm is the minimum sequential number of rounds needed such that in each
182
      round the algorithm makes O(poly(n)) independent queries to the evaluation oracle.
183
184   We recap two sub-problems for solving SMC in parallel setting, Batch Selection with Threshold
185   (BST) and unconstrained submodular maximization (USM). Given an instance (f, V, B), a fixed
186   threshold τ and ϵ > 0 as inputs, BST asks to find a subset S ⊆ V in O(log n) adaptive complexity
187   satisfying two conditions: (1) f (S) ≥ (1 − ϵ)τ |S|; (2) if |S| < k, f (e|S) < τ for any e ∈
                                                                                                 / S.
188   Although several attempts exist to solve the BST, most only work with a monotone submodular
189   function (Fahrbach et al., 2019b; Kazemi et al., 2019). In this work, we use the ThreshSeq algorithm
190   in (Chen & Kuhnle, 2024) and its theoretical satisfaction to analyze our algorithm’s performance in a
191   nonmontone setting.
192   Theorem 3.2 (Theorem 3 in (Chen & Kuhnle, 2024)). Let (f, V, k) be an instance of SMC. For any
193   constants ϵ, δ > 0, the algorithm ThreshSeq outputs A′ ⊆ A ⊆ V such that the following properties
194   hold: 1) The algorithm succeeds with probability at least 1 − δ/n. 2) There are O(n/ϵ) oracle
195   queries in expectation and O(log(n/δ)/ϵ) adaptive complexity. 3) It holds f (A′ ) ≥ (1 − ϵ)τ |A|. If
196   |A| < k, then f (e|A) < τ for all e ∈ V . 4) It also holds f (A′ ) ≥ f (A) and |A′ | ≥ (1 − ϵ)|A|.
197
      Regarding USM, which aims to find arg maxS⊆V f (S), there exist several solving methods giving
198
      the constant approximation ratios such as the (Fahrbach et al., 2019a)’s algorithm (USM1) provides a
199
      ratio of 1/4−ϵ with probability at least 1−δ for the problem in O(1) adaptive round and O( 1ϵ log( 1δ ))
200   queries and an essentially optimal algorithm of (Chen et al., 2019) (USM2) slightly improves the
201
      approximation ratio to 1/2 − ϵ using O(log(1/ϵ)/ϵ) adaptive rounds and O(n log3 (1/ϵ)/ϵ4 ) query
202   complexity. Due to the space limitations, Pseudocodes for ThreshSeq and USM algorithms are given

514
      Motivated by the big data challenge for non-monotone submodular maximization under cardinality
515   constraint, in this work, we focus on parallel approximation algorithms based on the concept of
516   adaptive complexity. In particular, we proposed efficient parallel algorithms that significantly improve
517   both approximation ratio and query complexity but keep the near-optimal adaptive complexity
518   of O(log n). Our algorithm also expresses superior solution quality and computation complexity
519   compared to state-of-the-art algorithms. However, there is still a weakness in our contribution, which
520   is about the approximation factor. It leads to an opening question: how to reduce the gap between
521   ours and the best ratio for O(log n) adaptive complexity in (Ene & Nguyen, 2020)?

724

725   solution X over ground set V then finding another solution Y over new ground set V \ X; (2) it then

727   ground set X and returns the best one among X, Y , and X ′ . Our algorithm also differs from the twin
728   greedy strategy Han et al. (2020) and Interlaced Greedy Kuhnle (2019), allowing simultaneously
729   updating two disjoint sets simultaneously in each iteration. In contrast to these algorithms, our
730   algorithm allows the integration of ThreshSeq in each iteration; thus, it can be effectively parallelized.

1898   I   A DDITIONAL LITERATURE REVIEW MONOTONE SMC
1899
1900   People studied non-adaptive methods for SMC first. Nemhauser et al. (1978) showed the best
1901   approximation algorithm with a factor of 1 − 1/e based on the sequential greedy approach. However,
1902   the sequential searching of greedy made time running too slow if the input increased. It led other
1903
       works to try to reduce the time consumption problem. In Badanidiyuru & Vondrák (2014), they
       proposed a deterministic approximation algorithm to reduce time consumption down to O(n log n)
1904
       by streaming fashion model. Significantly, Kuhnle (2021a) and Li et al. (2022) have simultaneously
1905
       proposed a linear-time approximation algorithm with a tight factor of nearly 1−1/e recently. However,
1906   these algorithms cannot work for non-monotone functions.
1907
1908   To work with the approach of parallel, the first major contribution belonged to Balkanski & Singer
1909
       (2018) when first applied the adaptive sampling method for the monotone SMC problem with constant
       3-approximation ratio and O(log n) adaptivity. Their algorithm filtered a fixed fraction of the elements
1910
       out of the ground set to get a quadratic query complexity. In contrast, Breuer et al. (2020) sped up
1911
       the sampling method to enhance the approximation factor to 1 − 1/e within O(log n log k) adaptive
1912   rounds and O(n log(log k)) queries.
