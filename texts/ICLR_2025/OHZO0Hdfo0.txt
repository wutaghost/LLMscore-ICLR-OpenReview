011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Knowledge Graph Question Answering (KGQA) involves answering natural lan-
014            guage questions based on information provided by knowledge graphs. Large lan-
015            guage models (LLMs), utilizing their exceptional natural language understand-
016
               ing capabilities and factual knowledge from knowledge graphs, have made some
               progress in KGQA reasoning. However, existing methods overlook the amplifica-
017
               tion of hallucinations in large language models caused by irrelevant information
018
               within vast knowledge graphs. This oversight leads to answers containing seem-
019            ingly correct but unrelated responses, decreasing reliability. In this paper, we
020            propose Generation-Evaluation-Reflection (Ger), an LLM-enhanced reflective
021            reasoning framework for KGQA. The Ger mechanism introduces evaluation and
022            reflection steps during the reasoning process, enabling LLMs to better utilize fac-
023            tual information in knowledge graphs for assessing and correcting their answers.
024            This process systematically reduces errors and hallucinations while improving the
025            reasoning accuracy of LLMs. Extensive experiments on multiple KGQA bench-
026            mark datasets demonstrate that Ger enhances reasoning performance, providing
027
               more reliable and interpretable results, and achieves new state-of-the-art levels.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Knowledge Graph Question Answering (KGQA) is a task to answer questions expressed in natural
033
      language through reasoning over the entities and relationships in a knowledge graph (KG). Knowl-
      edge graphs store vast amounts of information in triples (e.g., Freebase, Wikidata (Vrandečić &
034
      Krötzsch, 2014)), providing factual information as a basis, which plays a crucial role in applications
035
      requiring reliability. Due to this importance, KGQA is widely researched and applied in customer
036   service, information retrieval systems, and more, such as Google, Apple Siri, and Microsoft Cortana
037   (Lan et al., 2022). KGQA faces two main challenges: comprehensively understanding the user intent
038   embedded in the natural language questions and having the capability to perform correct multi-hop
039   reasoning over large-scale knowledge graphs without being misled to derive the correct answer from
040   thousands of candidates (Lan et al., 2022).
041
      Classical works (Sun et al., 2018; Shi et al., 2021; He et al., 2021; Jiang et al., 2022), such as
042   UniKGQA (Jiang et al., 2022), typically use GNN-based methods to prune and retrieve a question-
043   specific graph that provides information related to the question and predict the correct answer from
044   a large pool of candidates based on the question. While GNN-based methods effectively adapt to
045   the network structure of KGs, they often lack natural language understanding capabilities, leading to
046   potential misinterpretation of the question intent. Recently, large language models (LLMs) (Brown
047   et al., 2020; Chowdhery et al., 2023; Touvron et al., 2023; Achiam et al., 2023) have demonstrated
048   powerful capabilities in tasks related to natural language understanding.
049   By combining LLMs with KGs and utilizing the instruction-following capabilities of LLMs, some
050   existing works can achieve more relevant logical reasoning related to the questions and have made
051   significant progress. However, LLMs are often criticized for having a “hallucination” problem (Bang
052   et al., 2023; Pan et al., 2024), where they output seemingly correct but factually incorrect responses.
053   Research has shown that this phenomenon could be influenced by the input content of LLMs: the
      more irrelevant and disordered the input, the more severe the hallucination. Existing methods that

076   For instance, RoG (Luo et al., 2024) leverages LLMs to generate inference paths and answers to final

081
082   This paper introduces a framework named Generation-Evaluation-Reflection (Ger). This frame-
083   work is composed of three modules: generation, evaluation, and reflection. It systematically reduces
084   hallucinated answers and reflects on errors to explore correct answers. The generation module uti-
085   lizes large language models to explore the knowledge graph and produce preliminary answers, which
086
      may contain hallucinations and partial errors. The evaluation module then assesses the completeness
      and correctness of the answers through a dual-granularity evaluation strategy. Based on the evalu-
087
      ation results, the reflection module guides the LLM to filter potential hallucinations and explore
088
      missing answers, yielding the final answer. These three modules work collaboratively to enhance
089   the reliability and precision of the LLM in knowledge graph question answering tasks. Our contri-
090   butions are summarized as follows:
091
092   • We introduce a novel evaluation and reflection framework (Ger) for KGQA that combines knowl-
093     edge graphs and LLMs to systematically reduce the numerous hallucinations, improving overall
094     answer accuracy.
095   • We design a dual-granularity evaluation mechanism that not only removes individual hallucinated
096     answers but also assesses answer completeness in the context of the questions, thereby enhancing
097     the reliability and integrity of LLMs in knowledge graph question answering.
098
      • Extensive experiments demonstrate that our method significantly improves the hit rate of correct
099
        answers, surpassing existing state-of-the-art methods and achieving efficient and interpretable rea-
100
        soning over the knowledge graph.

157   3     A PPROACH
158
159   In this section, we introduce our method: Generation-Evaluation-Reflection (Ger), which consists
160   of three components: 1) a Generation module that generates preliminary answers to questions based
161   on knowledge graphs and LLMs; 2) an Evaluation module that assesses the correctness of answers
      based on reasoning paths in the KG; and 3) a Reflection module that guides the LLM to eliminate

      hallucinations and generate new responses based on the evaluation results. The overall framework
163   of Ger is illustrated in Figure 2.

166   3.1     G ENERATION -E VALUATION -R EFLECTION
167
168   Recently, numerous techniques have been developed to address Knowledge Graph Question An-
169   swering (KGQA) by integrating knowledge graphs with LLMs. However, the large amount of irrel-
170   evant information often present in KGs can exacerbate the issue of hallucinations in LLMs, resulting
171   in inaccurate plans and incorrect answers (Ji et al., 2023).
172   To tackle this challenge, we propose a novel Generation-Evaluation-Reflection (Ger) framework.
173   This framework leverages knowledge graphs to validate and optimize the responses generated by
174   LLMs, systematically minimizing the impact of irrelevant information in KGs on LLM hallucina-
175   tions.
176   By treating relation paths as plans, we ensure that these plans are grounded in knowledge graphs,
177   allowing the LLMs to perform accurate and interpretable reasoning. In essence, we frame Ger as
178   an optimization problem, aiming to maximize the likelihood of delivering accurate answers from a
179   knowledge graph G in response to a question q. This is achieved by generating an initial answer
180   a′ , evaluating it to obtain an evaluation result e′ , and reflecting on these insights to produce a final
181   answer a                    XX

185   e′ denotes the evaluation result of the initial answer. The first term Pgen (a′ |q, G; θ) is the probability

297   Datasets. We assess the performance of Ger using two benchmark KGQA datasets: WebQues-
298   tionSP (WebQSP) (Yih et al., 2016) and Complex WebQuestions (CWQ) (Talmor & Berant, 2018).
299   These datasets involve extracting subgraphs from Freebase (Bollacker et al., 2008) based on entities
300   mentioned in up to 4-hop questions. The WebQSP dataset includes 2,826 questions for training
301
      and 1,628 for testing, while the CWQ dataset comprises 27,639 questions for training and 3,531 for
      testing.
302
303   Evaluation Metrics. Consistent with previous research, we employ Hits@1 and F1 as evaluation
304   metrics. Hits@1 evaluates the percentage of questions for which the top-1 predicted answer is
305   correct. Since a question may have multiple correct answers, F1 accounts for the coverage of all
306   possible answers, balancing the precision and recall of the predicted answers. For large language
307
      models (LLMs), due to the autoregressive nature of answer generation, which may not preserve the
      order of predicted answers, we align with Luo et al. (2024) in calculating the Hits@1 metric.
308
309   Implementation Details. For the Ger evaluation, we use LLaMA2-Chat-7B (Touvron et al., 2023)
310   as the foundational LLM and apply instruction fine-tuning over 3 epochs using the WebQSP and
311   CWQ training datasets. Given that Rog (Luo et al., 2024) represents the current state-of-the-art
312   in this domain, we reference the results from their study along with those of other baselines for
313
      comparative purposes.

      We conducted ablation studies to analyze the effectiveness of the evaluation module in our approach

      5   C ONCLUSION
433
434
      This paper presents the Generation-Evaluation-Reflection (Ger) framework, specifically designed to
435   improve large language models (LLMs) in knowledge graph question answering. By systematically
436   integrating generation, evaluation, and reflection components, Ger enhances the logical consistency
437   and factual accuracy of outputs, effectively minimizing hallucinations that are amplified by irrelevant
438   facts from the knowledge graph in LLM reasoning processes.
439
      Despite these advancements, Ger faces certain limitations. Its dependency on the completeness
440
      and accuracy of the underlying knowledge graph can limit its performance in scenarios where the
441   graph data is sparse or erroneous. Moreover, including additional evaluation and reflection stages
442   introduces a layer of computational complexity, potentially affecting scalability and response time
443   for complex queries. Addressing these areas in future work could involve developing more efficient
444   algorithms and exploring strategies to handle incomplete graph data more effectively.
445
      Experimental evaluations demonstrate that Ger achieves higher accuracy and reliability in
446
      KGQA tasks than existing methodologies, providing a robust framework for leveraging LLMs in
447   knowledge-driven applications. Future research directions include optimizing the framework’s com-
448   putational efficiency and extending its applicability to more diverse and challenging datasets.
