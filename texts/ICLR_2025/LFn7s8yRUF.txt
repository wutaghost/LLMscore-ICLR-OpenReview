013            A BSTRACT
014            With the development of personalized AI models, particularly those emulating
015            characters from novels, games, anime, and films, a significant challenge is the
016            scarcity of suitable dialogue data. These works often feature distinctive styles and
017            character dialogues that may not generalize well to everyday conversations. Data
018
               augmentation is crucial for enriching these limited datasets, ensuring sufficient
               data for learning the target character’s tone and linguistic habits. This paper in-
019
               vestigates the impact of various data augmentation techniques on personalized AI
020
               models in NLP, specifically focusing on models trained using LLaMA3 through
021            Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA). We employ different data augmentation strate-
022            gies, including random deletion, synonym replacement, swapping, random inser-
023            tion, back translation, and paraphrasing. To provide a comprehensive analysis,
024            we apply these techniques across three distinct datasets, each representing dif-
025            ferent dialogue styles and contexts. By systematically comparing these methods,
026            we demonstrate their influence on model performance and robustness. This study
027            provides valuable insights into the effectiveness of different data augmentation
028            strategies for enhancing the versatility and robustness of personalized AI systems
029
               trained with LLaMA3 using LoRA.

      1   INTRODUCTION
033
034
      Data augmentation techniques are widely used in the field of computer vision (CV). When datasets
      are limited, methods such as rotation, flipping, cropping, color adjustment, noise addition, and
035
      brightness alteration can effectively enrich the dataset and enhance the model’s generalization capa-
036
      bilities. However, in natural language processing (NLP) tasks, text data augmentation is relatively
037   rare. This is because, unlike CV, where augmentation does not alter the inherent nature of images
038   (analogous to semantic preservation in NLP), modifying a sentence or replacing a word in NLP can
039   drastically change the meaning of the text. This can lead to models learning incorrect information.
040
      Although data augmentation (DA) has not been deeply explored in the field of natural language
041
      processing (NLP) and lacks diverse methods, the rise of ChatGPT in recent years has brought sig-
042
      nificant attention to NLP in AI. Especially with the emergence of powerful models like ChatGPT-
043   4OpenAI (2024) and LLaMA3Huang et al. (2024) this year, NLP has once again become a focal
044   point. ChatGPT-4, compared to its predecessors, boasts stronger natural language understanding
045   and generation capabilities, handling more complex dialogues and producing more coherent and
046   accurate responses. LLaMA3 further enables the personalization of local AI. By using LoRAHu
047   et al. (2021) technology, we can fine-tune the LLaMA3 model and train it using datasets containing
048   dialogues of specific characters. However, these datasets often present two major issues:
049
050        1. Most dialogues of characters in films or games are within a specific framework, such as
051
              war-themed films where characters’ dialogues mainly revolve around war.
052        2. Some novels or film scripts contain extensive monologues and descriptions, with actual
053           dialogues concentrated in specific scenes. This makes it challenging to scale the dataset
              and results in a more single-directional training focus.

054
      In such cases, data augmentation becomes particularly necessary. By applying DA techniques, we
055   can expand and enrich existing datasets, making them more diverse and enhancing the model’s
056   generalization capabilities and adaptability. This not only addresses the issue of data scarcity but
057   also provides the model with a broader range of learning material, enabling it to generate reasonable
058   dialogues in various contexts.
059
      In this paper, we will explore the impact of various data augmentation (DA) methods on the training
060
      of personalized AI models, focusing on both positive and negative effects. For any negative impacts
061   identified, we will investigate effective mitigation strategies and determine the best DA combinations
062   for smaller datasets. In the subsequent sections, we will first describe our dataset preparation and

064   DA to the original models to illustrate the effects of DA on training. Following this, we will discuss
065   methods to eliminate negative impacts and present the optimal DA combinations. Finally, we will
066   address future challenges and potential improvements.

070   2.1     DATA AUGMENTATION M ETHOD
071
072   In the area of natural language processing (NLP), data augmentation has become an essential tech-
073   nique for improving the robustness and generalization of machine learning models. This section
074   discusses several widely used data augmentation techniques, each of which contributes uniquely to
075   the enhancement of NLP models.
076
077   2.1.1    S YNONYM R EPLACEMENT, R ANDOM I NSERTION , R ANDOM D ELETION , AND
078
               R ANDOM S WAP
079   Wei and Zou (2019) Wei & Zou (2019) introduced these methods to create variations in text data by
080   replacing words with synonyms, inserting new words, or swapping word positions. These simple
081   techniques help models generalize better by exposing them to diverse sentence structures. Despite
082   their simplicity, these methods can significantly improve model performance in text classification
083   tasks by introducing lexical diversity.
084
      Synonym Replacement substitutes words in a sentence with their synonyms, maintaining the orig-
085   inal meaning while introducing variety. This method broadens the model’s exposure to different
086   word choices in similar contexts, helping it generalize better across different linguistic expressions.
087
088
      Random Insertion adds randomly selected words into new positions within the sentence, introduc-
      ing syntactic variation. This challenges models to handle non-standard word orders, improving their
089
      robustness and flexibility in understanding diverse sentence structures.
090
091   Random Deletion randomly removes words from a sentence, training models to interpret incom-
092   plete or abbreviated text. This technique is particularly effective in preparing models to deal with
093   real-world scenarios where text may be noisy or partially missing, enhancing their resilience.
094   Random Swap exchanges the positions of words within a sentence, altering its syntactic structure.
095   This method forces models to learn the importance of word order and helps them generalize across
096   different syntactic patterns, which is especially crucial for tasks like machine translation or syntactic
097   parsing.
098
099   2.1.2    BACKTRANSLATION
100
      This method, described by Sennrich et al. (2016) Sennrich et al. (2016), involves translating text
101
      from one language to another and then back to the original language to generate paraphrases. It
102   is particularly effective in low-resource settings and helps in generating diverse training examples
103   without altering the original meaning. Understanding Back-Translation at Scale Edunov et al. (2018)
104   has further expanded the application of backtranslation, showing that leveraging large-scale mono-
105   lingual data through backtranslation can significantly improve machine translation models’ perfor-
106   mance. By using noise-injected sampling during the backtranslation process, as opposed to tradi-
107   tional beam search, the quality of the generated synthetic parallel data can be improved, which in
      turn enhances the training process. Additionally, backtranslation has been recognized as a powerful

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      tool for domain adaptation, as demonstrated by Sriram et al. (2017) Sriram et al. (2017), where they
109   employed a similar approach to adapt sequence-to-sequence models to new domains with minimal
110   labeled data, achieving remarkable generalization and performance gains.

      2.2   PARAPHRASING
114
      As a critical data augmentation technique, paraphrasing in Natural Language Processing (NLP)
115
      serves to generate diverse training data while maintaining the original meaning of the text. This
116   approach enhances model robustness by introducing varied sentence structures and word choices,
117   which is essential for improving model generalization.
118
119
      Paraphrasing encompasses two primary tasks: Paraphrase Generation (PG) and Paraphrase Identi-
      fication (PI). In Paraphrase Generation, models like T5 and GPT-3 are employed to produce new
120
      sentences that convey the same meaning as the original. Particularly, T5 is fine-tuned to transform
121
      various NLP tasks into a text-to-text format, making it a versatile tool for generating fluent and se-
122   mantically consistent paraphrases Palivela et al. (2021). GPT-3’s Davinci variant, especially when
123   optimized with specific prompts, excels in generating high-quality paraphrases that effectively pre-
124   serve crucial entities, which is vital for tasks such as Named Entity Recognition (NER) Author et al.
125   (2021).
126
      On the other hand, Paraphrase Identification focuses on determining whether two sentences are
127
      paraphrases of each other. This task leverages the same models used in paraphrase generation, com-
128   paring their output vectors to assess semantic similarity Palivela et al. (2021). Integrating both tasks
129   within a unified model architecture not only conserves computational resources but also enhances
130   the model’s capability to perform multiple tasks simultaneously, achieving superior results in both
131   paraphrase generation and identification Palivela et al. (2021).

      GPU kernels and optimized computation steps. These optimizations significantly reduce training
153
      time and memory usage, making it possible to train large language models more efficiently without
154
      the need for hardware upgrades.

156   One of the key advantages of Unsloth is its ability to achieve these performance improvements purely

158
      erations, Unsloth allows users to maximize the capabilities of their existing hardware, enabling the
      training of complex models like LLaMA3 with fewer resources.
159
160   Additionally, Unsloth supports distributed training across multiple GPUs, allowing for scalability in
161   handling large datasets and complex tasks. This makes it an ideal solution for researchers and prac-

      What is LoRA?
181
      Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) Hu et al. (2021) is a technique designed for efficiently fine-tuning
182   large language models, particularly in scenarios where computational resources are limited. Instead
183   of fine-tuning all the parameters of a pre-trained model, which can be computationally expensive
184   and memory-intensive, LoRA focuses on modifying a smaller subset of parameters. Specifically, it
185   treats the pre-trained model’s weights as fixed and injects trainable low-rank decomposition matrices
186   into each layer of the Transformer architecture. This method significantly reduces the number of
187   trainable parameters while maintaining model quality that is often comparable to full fine-tuning,
188   making it an attractive approach for optimizing large-scale models.
189   LoRA is particularly effective because it leverages the intrinsic low-rank structure present in the
190   weight matrices of deep neural networks, especially within the self-attention mechanism of Trans-
191   formers. By applying low-rank decomposition to the query (Wq ) and value (Wv ) matrices within
192   the self-attention modules of each Transformer layer, LoRA introduces minimal additional compu-
193   tational overhead while still allowing the model to adapt to new tasks. The low-rank matrices Aq ,
194   Bq , Av , and Bv are much smaller than the full-rank matrices they augment, leading to a substantial
195   reduction in memory usage and computational costs.
196   Mathematically, the adaptation is expressed as shown in Equation 1, where the changes to the query
197   and value matrices are represented as the product of two smaller matrices. These modifications are
198   then added to the original matrices, as shown in Equations 2 and 3, resulting in the updated query
199   and value matrices (Wq′ and Wv′ ) that incorporate the learned task-specific information.
200
201                                 ∆Wq = Aq Bq       and   ∆Wv = Av Bv ,                               (1)
202
203
204                                   Wq′ = Wq + ∆Wq = Wq + Aq Bq ,                                     (2)
205
206
207                                   Wv′ = Wv + ∆Wv = Wv + Av Bv .                                     (3)

213   In the following section, we will utilize the Data Augmentation methods outlined below to enrich our
214   dataset, compare the results of models trained with each method, and evaluate their effectiveness.
215   Our goal is to identify the most suitable approach for training specific personalized AI models,
      particularly under resource-constrained conditions:


                                                       4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
            • Synonym Replacement works by replacing selected words in the text with their synonyms.
217           In the implementation, words are first segmented using the Jieba library. For each word that
218           is either a verb or adjective, and not a common particle or auxiliary word, a synonym is
219           selected using the Synonyms library. If a synonym is found, it replaces the original word
220           in the text.
221         • Random Insertion involves randomly selecting words from the text and inserting them
222           back into random positions within the sentence. The number of words to be inserted is
223           determined by a proportion of the total words in the sentence, controlled by the parameter
224           p. This helps in adding redundancy and variation to the text.
225         • Random Swap randomly selects pairs of words in the sentence and swaps their positions.
226           Similar to Random Insertion, the number of swaps is determined by a proportion p of the
227           total words. This method disrupts the original word order, adding syntactic variety while
228           preserving the overall meaning.
229         • Random Deletion randomly removes words from the sentence. Again, a proportion p of
230           words is chosen, and those words are removed from the text. This method tests the model’s
231           ability to handle incomplete or abbreviated text, which is common in real-world data.
232
            • Back Translation is a technique used to generate paraphrases by translating text from the
233
              source language to a target language and then back to the source language. This method
234           introduces variability in the phrasing while maintaining the original meaning of the text.
235           By leveraging the inherent differences in how languages structure information, Back Trans-
236           lation creates diverse linguistic expressions that can help improve the robustness of models
237           trained on augmented data. This technique is particularly useful in enhancing a model’s
238           ability to generalize across different textual representations of the same underlying con-
239           tent.
240         • Paraphrasing using Large Language Models (LLMs) involves generating alternate ver-
241           sions of a given text that convey the same meaning but with different wording and sentence
242           structure. Utilizing a model like SparkDeskV4, which has been trained on vast amounts of
243           data, allows for the creation of high-quality, contextually appropriate paraphrases. This
244           method is valuable for introducing more sophisticated and nuanced variations into the
245           dataset, which can improve the model’s ability to handle a wide range of inputs. By con-
246
              trolling the paraphrasing intensity, it’s possible to create both subtle and more pronounced
              changes to the original text, offering flexibility in how the data is augmented.
247

      By applying these methods, we aim to enhance data diversity and ultimately improve the accuracy
249   of the models trained for our specific NLP tasks.

      4.1.2    I MPACT OF DATA AUGMENTATION T ECHNIQUES
387
388
      The impact of data augmentation techniques on loss trends varied between the two datasets. For
389   the Zhenhuan dataset, which contains classical language with intricate sentence structures, certain
390   techniques like Synonym Replacement and Back Translation led to more stable validation loss,
391   implying better generalization. In contrast, techniques like Paraphrasing, which were expected to
392   increase diversity, sometimes led to overfitting, as indicated by the rising validation loss.
393
      This overfitting is particularly pronounced with the Paraphrasing with LLMs technique due to the
394
      unique characteristics of the two datasets:
395
396   In the Zhenhuan dataset, the presence of many classical Chinese phrases or idiomatic expressions,
397   commonly used by ancient people, poses a significant challenge. Without a model trained specif-
398
      ically on classical Chinese, it is difficult to achieve high-quality paraphrasing that maintains the
      original meaning and tone. This inadequacy in paraphrasing quality likely leads to the model over-
399
      fitting to the original phrases, as it struggles to generate sufficiently diverse outputs.
400
401   For the Paimon dataset, which is based on a character from an open-world exploration game, the
402   content includes many original elements, such as the game’s unique world view and place names.
403   These elements are often creative and specific to the game’s universe. Without proper training on
404
      such domain-specific content, the model used for paraphrasing—SparkDesk—often fails to generate
      meaningful or varied paraphrases, leading to outputs that are identical or nearly identical to the orig-
405
      inal sentences. This lack of diversity in the paraphrased outputs likely contributes to the observed
406
      overfitting during training.
407
408   In contrast, the performance differences among other data augmentation techniques such as Random
409   Deletion (RD), Random Insertion (RI), Random Swap (RW), Back Translation (BT), and Synonym
410
      Replacement (SR) can be explained by their respective impacts on the linguistic structure and mean-
      ing of the sentences:
411
412   Random Deletion (RD) can inadvertently remove key information or important context from a
413   sentence, which might lead the model to learn incomplete or skewed representations of the data. For
414   example, in the Zhenhuan dataset, where sentences are often laden with classical expressions and
415   complex syntax, removing even a single word can significantly alter the meaning or disrupt the flow
416
      of the sentence. This loss of crucial information can cause the model to struggle during validation,
      leading to poorer performance.
417
418   Random Insertion (RI), on the other hand, introduces new words into a sentence, but these in-
419   sertions might occur at inappropriate positions, leading to semantic distortions. This method could
420   result in the insertion of words that clash with the original context, thereby confusing the model and
421   reducing its ability to accurately interpret the sentence. In the Paimon dataset, where many sentences
422
      are tied to specific game-related contexts and terminologies, inappropriate insertions could mislead
      the model, affecting the training outcome.
423
424   Random Swap (RS), while it does alter the order of words and might affect the syntactical structure,
425   does not typically disrupt the core semantic relationships within the sentence. This method is less
426   likely to interfere with the model’s ability to extract and learn from key features. By rearranging
427   words, RS introduces variability that challenges the model to recognize patterns without fundamen-
428
      tally changing the sentence’s meaning. This makes it a more reliable method compared to RD and
      RI, though it still might not be as effective as more sophisticated techniques.
429
430   Back Translation (BT) and Synonym Replacement (SR) were observed to perform the best across
431   both datasets. These methods maintain the original sentence’s meaning while introducing enough
      variation to enhance the model’s generalization capabilities.

466   across all metrics (BLEU, ROUGE-1, ROUGE-2, and ROUGE-L). This reinforces the earlier ob-
467   servation that these methods maintain the original sentence meaning while introducing sufficient
468   diversity, resulting in high-quality text generation.
469   Random Swap (RS) performed relatively well, achieving moderate scores across all metrics. How-
470   ever, its scores were slightly lower than BT and SR, likely due to its limited ability to introduce
471   substantial variation while maintaining coherence.
472
      Random Deletion (RD) and Random Insertion (RI), as expected, yielded lower scores across all
473
      metrics. This aligns with the earlier loss analysis where these methods were found to disrupt the
474
      original sentence meaning, leading to poorer quality outputs.
475
476   Interestingly, despite showing signs of overfitting in the loss analysis, Paraphrasing with LLMs
477   (PG) achieved BLEU and ROUGE scores comparable to or even higher than those of Random
478
      Deletion (RD). This can be attributed to PG’s ability to maintain the core semantics of the original
      sentence, even when it fails to introduce sufficient diversity. In contrast, RD often disrupts the
479
      sentence’s integrity by removing crucial information, leading to lower scores in semantic similarity-
480
      based metrics like BLEU and ROUGE. Consequently, while PG may overfit the training data, its
481   outputs are often more aligned with the reference sentences, resulting in relatively higher scores.
482
483   4.2.2        C OMPARISON AND S UMMARY
484
485   Overall, the BLEU and ROUGE score analysis supports the conclusions drawn from the loss trends
      but offers additional insights. Notably, while the loss analysis highlighted issues like overfitting, the

      BLEU and ROUGE scores directly quantify the quality of the text generated by each DA method.

492   4.3   F UTURE C ONSIDERATION
493
494   Based on our experiments, using large models for paraphrasing as a data augmentation (DA) tech-
495   nique may not be the most effective approach for training personalized AI models, especially with
496   datasets like Zhenhuan and Paimon. The unique terminology and world-specific references in the
497
      Paimon dataset, for example, are challenging to paraphrase meaningfully. Without specialized train-
      ing data for the paraphrasing model, the results may not differ significantly from simpler tech-
498
      niques like synonym replacement or backtranslation. Moreover, training a paraphrasing model re-
499
      quires a substantial amount of data, which contradicts the very reason for employing DA in the first
500   place—data scarcity. Therefore, in resource-constrained environments, it might be more practical
501   to use straightforward techniques like synonym replacement or backtranslation for DA, rather than
502   investing resources in training a paraphrasing model specifically for AI.

      5     L IMITATIONS
505
506
      5.1   DATA S ET L IMITATIONS
507
508   One of the primary limitations of this study is the restricted scope of the datasets used for data
509   augmentation. With only two datasets available—Paimon from Genshin Impact and Zhen Huan
510   from Empresses in the Palace—the sample size may not be sufficient to generalize the conclusions
511   drawn about the effectiveness of each data augmentation method. The specific characteristics of
512   these datasets may lead to insights that are not fully applicable to more general cases. Therefore,
513   caution should be taken when extrapolating these findings to other domains or broader applications.
514
515   5.2   C OMPUTATIONAL R ESOURCES
516
      Another significant limitation is the constraint posed by computational resources. Due to limited
517
      resources, the fine-tuning of the LLaMA3-8B model was capped at 2000 steps. Although the loss
518
      appeared to converge within this range, it’s possible that extending the training steps could alter the
519   observed trends, particularly for methods like paraphrasing, where overfitting might diminish with
520   additional training, leading to better performance compared to other data augmentation techniques.
521
522
      Moreover, the use of the LLaMA3-8B model, while efficient, may not fully capture the potential
      improvements that could be achieved with larger models like LLaMA3-70B. Previous studies have
523
      suggested that the performance difference between these models is minimal, but there is still a slight
524
      edge in favor of the larger model. With sufficient computational resources, it would be possible to
525   explore the performance of LLaMA3-70B and compare it against other models. This could poten-
526   tially reveal more general trends and provide a deeper understanding of the effectiveness of different
527   data augmentation strategies across a broader range of model parameters.

      6     CONCLUSION
531
      This study has explored the impact of various data augmentation (DA) techniques on the training
532
      of personalized AI models using LLaMA3 and Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA). Our results indi-
533
      cate that while advanced techniques like paraphrasing using large models offer potential, they may
534   not be suitable for specialized datasets with unique terminology, such as the Paimon dataset. In
535   resource-constrained environments, simpler methods like synonym replacement or backtranslation
536   can be more effective and practical. These findings highlight the importance of selecting DA tech-
537   niques that align with the specific characteristics of the dataset and the computational resources
538   available. Ultimately, this research contributes to optimizing the training of localized and personal-
539   ized AI models, ensuring that they can effectively capture and replicate character-specific tones and
      linguistic habits.
