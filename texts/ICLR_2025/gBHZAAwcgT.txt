009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            In this paper, we study an under-explored but important factor of diffusion gen-
012            erative models, i.e., the combinatorial complexity. Data samples are generally
013            high-dimensional, and for various structured generation tasks, additional attributes
014            are combined to associate with data samples. We show that the space spanned by
015            the combination of dimensions and attributes is insufficiently sampled by exist-
016            ing training scheme of diffusion generative models, causing degraded test time
017
               performance. We present a simple fix to this problem by constructing stochastic
               processes that fully exploit the combinatorial structures, hence the name Com-
018
               boStoc. Using this simple strategy, we show that network training is significantly
019
               accelerated across diverse data modalities, including images and 3D structured
020            shapes. Moreover, ComboStoc enables a new way of test time generation which
021            uses asynchronous time steps for different dimensions and attributes, thus allowing
022            for varying degrees of control over them.

044   1   I NTRODUCTION
045
046   Diffusion generative models rely heavily on modeling the desired behavior over the whole space
047   of possibilities, so that the generative models cover all data distributions systematically. However,
048   the current training schemes generally focus on a single transport path from the source pure noise
049   distribution to the target data distribution (Albergo & Vanden-Eijnden, 2023; Albergo et al., 2023; Liu
050
      et al., 2023; Lipman et al., 2023). The training therefore gives insufficient sampling of large regions
      of the entire space of possibilities, which nevertheless can be encountered because of stochastic
051
      sampling during evaluation and produce inaccurate behavior, leading to poor generation results.
052
053   To solve this mismatch between training scheme and test time evaluation, we propose to fully sample
      the space of combinatorial complexity. To see why the space of possibilities has a combinatorial

055   combinatorial structures. For example, the most powerful generative models so far use transformers as
056   the network model (Peebles & Xie, 2023; Ma et al., 2024), and treat an image sample as a collection
057   of patch tokens to be generated in parallel; moreover, each of the patch token is encoded as a vector

062   even more pronounced: each part has numerous attributes encoding different properties like its

      existence, bounding box and part shape, in addition to the part/patch decomposition and multiple
      feature channels analogous to images.

      2    BACKGROUND ON DIFFUSION GENERATIVE MODELS
091
092
      The problem of generative modeling aims at capturing the complete distribution of a set of data
      samples. Its state-of-the-art solutions include denoising diffusion probabilistic models (Ho et al.,
093
      2020), score-based models (Song et al., 2021) and flow matching (Lipman et al., 2023; Liu et al.,
094
      2023), all of which transform a simple source distribution (e.g. the unit normal distribution) into the
095   target distribution following the dynamics specified by variations of stochastic differential equations.
096   Remarkably, the different formulations can be unified through the framework of stochastic interpolants
097   (Albergo & Vanden-Eijnden, 2023; Albergo et al., 2023). In particular, the stochastic interpolants
098   framework defines the process of turning data samples into source distributions and vice versa as
099   a simple interpolation between the two distributions, augmented with random perturbations during
100   the processes. Without loss of generality, we reproduce the formulation of a simple linear one-sided
101   interpolant process below (illustrated in Fig. 2(a)):
102
                                          xt = (1 − t)z + tx1 , t ∈ [0, 1]                                            (1)
103
104   where z ∼ N (0, 1) samples the source distribution, x1 ∼ D samples the target data distribution,
105   t ∈ [0, 1] is the interpolation schedule. A network model fθ (xt ) can be trained to recover the
106   interpolation velocity ∂x
                              ∂t = x1 − z, the target data sample x1 , or the noise z (Albergo et al., 2023).

      integrates them numerically into the final samples.

127   3      C OMBINATORIAL STOCHASTIC PROCESS
128
129   Most interesting data samples are high dimensional. For example, state-of-the-art generative models
130   encode images as latent patches with both spatial and feature dimensions (Ma et al., 2024; Peebles &
131   Xie, 2023). 3D shapes structured as part ensembles include even more attributes in addition to spatial
132   and feature dimensions, for example, the varying numbers of parts and their bounding boxes and
133   positions (Mo et al., 2019b); to generate such data requires the handling of more flexible dimensions.
134   No matter how many dimensions and attributes a data sample has, standard diffusion generative
135   models treat them homogeneously and in synchronization. For example, for the stochastic interpolants,
136   the generative model is trained on samples distributed on densities with shrinking coverage along
137   the transport paths connecting the source distribution and each target data sample, as illustrated in
138   Fig. 2(a). In Appendix A.1, we provide a formal proof and visualization of the shrinking coverage.
139   This design leaves the low density regions insufficiently trained, and once they are sampled in test
140
      stage via solving stochastic differential equations, the network tends to produce poor results.
141   To address the above problem, we emphasize the combinatorial complexity of individual dimensions
142   and attributes of data samples. In particular, we purposefully sample points with asynchronous diffu-
143   sion schedules of dimensions and attributes, as shown in Fig. 2(b). To implement the asynchronous
144   schedules is rather simple. We turn the interpolation schedule t of Eq. (1) into a tensor t of the
145   same shape as x, and use different values independently and uniformly sampled within [0, 1] for the
146
      dimensions and attributes, to obtain the sample points:
147                                             xt = (1 − t) ⊙ z + t ⊙ x1                                    (2)
148   where ⊙ is elementwise product. By construction, the sampling density is uniform within the
149   subregions spanned by each pair of source and target data points. Correspondingly, the network
150   fθ (xt ) is trained to predict velocity, or the target data sample, etc.
151
      The benefits of using these augmented samples from combinatorial stochasticity lie in three folds:
152
153              • We make sure the network coverage is broader than the synchronized schedule, so that
154                during test stage the network performs more robustly and with higher quality.
155              • We encourage the network to learn the correlations of different dimensions and attributes, as
156                the network is trained to synchronize them to reach the final data points.
157
                 • The trained network enables more flexible control over the generation process, where
158                different dimensions and attributes can be given varying degrees of finalization to be
159                synthesized in the final result.
160
161   Next, we discuss the detailed adaptations and achieved effects through generative tasks from two
      different domains, i.e. images and structured 3D shapes.

      Images. For image generation, we take on the baseline of SiT (Ma et al., 2024) which applies
      highly scalable transformer networks and achieves state-of-the-art performance on ImageNet scale
173
      generation. In particular, a given image is encoded via the VAE encoder from Rombach et al. (2022)
174
      as a latent image x1 of shape C×H×W , and the network is trained to predict velocity given the
175   diffused latent image xt (Eq. (1)) and optionally conditioned on the image class c and interpolation
176   schedule t, i.e., fθ (xt ; c, t) = x1 − z.
177
178
      Correspondingly, we make several simple adaptations to implement the ComboStoc scheme. First,
      we construct t with the same shape of C×H×W , and update the timestep embedding module of
179
      SiT to accommodate this change (see Appendix A.3). Note that the conditioning on class labels and
180
      timesteps are mixed and implemented as modulation operations in Ma et al. (2024), and therefore are
181   not symmetric to the data samples in importance1 . Second, importantly, we note that for velocity
182   prediction, the samples with asynchronous t should not predict the original velocity x1 − z only;
183   otherwise there will be drift off the target data points during test stage integration (illustrated as
184   Fig. 2(c) dotted line; see Appendix A.2 for a formal analysis). To mitigate this issue, we propose
185   two possible approaches to compensate the velocity, as detailed in Appendix A.2. Due to limited
186   computational resources, in our experiments, we have applied only the first approach of minimizing
187   off-diagonal drift by gradient descent, and leave the test of the second approach for future work.
188
189   Structured 3D shapes. We use the generative modeling of structured 3D shapes as a new task to
190   further demonstrate the importance of exploiting combinatorial complexity. Indeed, structure 3D
191   shapes have even stronger combinatorial complexity than images, as shown in its varying numbers
192   of parts, their positions and bounding boxes, as well as the detailed shape variations for each part.
193   Precisely, we denote a structured 3D object as a collection of object parts, i.e., x = {pi }, i ∈ [L],
194   where we set L = 256 to cover the maximum number of parts in a dataset. An object part is further
195
      encoded as p = (s, b, e), where s ∈ [0, 1] indicates the existence of this part, b = (x, y, z, l, w, h)
      denotes the bounding box center (x, y, z) and length l, width w and height h, and e ∈ R512 is a latent
196
      shape code encoding the part shape in normalized coordinates. Note that under this representation, a
197
      permutation of the part indices does not change the 3D shape, which is quite different from images
198   represented as a feature grid of fixed order and size.
199
200   To generate structured 3D shapes with semantic parts, we train a stochastic interpolant model. In
201
      particular, given a structured 3D shape x1 = {pi } and its diffused sample xt (Eq. (2)), we make the
      network predict the target data sample directly for simplicity, i.e., fθ (xt ; c, t) = x1 , where c is the
202
      optional class label of the 3D shape. Note that here t assigns different time steps for all the different
203
      attributes and dimensions of each object part. We validate the generative model for structured 3D
204   shapes by training on the PartNet dataset (Mo et al., 2019b), as discussed in Sec. 4.

      While 3D diffusion generative models are increasing (Zheng et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023), few
      works have been done for structured shape generation. Mo et al. (2019a) studies the representation

469
      Compared to Mo et al. (2019a), Wang et al. (2023) proposes a rewriting model to enable generalizable
470   cross-category generation. In comparison, we focus on generating flatly structured 3D shapes with
471   leaf level semantic parts. Moreover, by specifying parts and attributes independently, our model
472   enables diverse tasks like shape completion and assembly. Previously, such diverse applications have
473   been studied individually via specialized solutions (Huang et al., 2020; Sung et al., 2015), but in
474   this paper we have shown that they can be potentially unified by a single model generating highly
475   structured data.

      6    C ONCLUSION
478
479
      We have proposed to focus on the problem of combinatorial complexity of high-dimensional and
480   multi-attribute data samples for diffusion generative models. In particular, we note that for one-sided
481   stochastic interpolants that model many variants of diffusion and flow based models, there exists the
482   problem of under-sampling regions of the path space where the dimensions/attributes are off-diagonal
483   or asynchronous. We propose to fix this issue by sampling the whole space spanned by combinatorial
484   complexity uniformly. Experiments across two data modalities show that indeed by utilizing the
485   combinatorial complexity, performances can be enhanced, and new generation paradigms can be
      enabled where different attributes of a data sample are generated in asynchronous schedules to achieve


                                                        9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      varying degrees of control simultaneously. We hope that our work can inspire future works that look
487   through the combinatorial perspective of generative models.
488
489   Limitation and future work Our ComboStoc scheme will only have significant effects when
490   the data has combinatorial and structural information, such as different patches for images and
491   different parts for 3D shapes. When the data resides in a vector space whose dimensions are nearly
492   independent, it is hard to exploit the correlation of dimensions and train a model that works well under
493   the combinatorial schedule of different dimensions. Indeed, in such a case the individual dimensions
494   may ideally be generated separately by different models. However, we note that many data types in
495   real life contain strong structural and combinatorial information; particularly eminent are tasks within
496   scientific domains, including molecule docking and protein folding (Corso et al., 2022; Wu et al.,
497
      2024; Yim et al., 2024), where diffusion models that better handle their combinatorial structures can
      be desirable.

      i.e., StructRe (Wang et al., 2023) and StructureNet (Mo et al., 2019a), in terms of FPD, COV and

904
      can be decomposed into fine parts by following the tree. Exploiting this hierarchical data, the two
905
      baselines expand coarse parts into fine parts progressively, which helps constrain the generated shapes
906   toward better regularity. In comparison, our network does not use this hierarchical information and
907   directly generates the leaf level parts. Nevertheless, the results by unsync_all show performances
908   within the baseline results. Visually, we find our results generally show stronger diversity than the
909   shapes by Wang et al. (2023) and Mo et al. (2019a). Finally, it is an interesting topic to study how
910   to combine the approaches of hierarchical generation and diffusion generative models, which have
911   differing advantages in aspects of structure regularity and diversity.

915   align the time step embedding module with the baseline SiT by setting CC = CH (Fig. 12). The two

917   speeds up the convergence of SiT by ≈ 1.75×. We plan to investigate these modifications thoroughly

      A.9   B ROADER IMPACT
922
923   In this paper we have presented ComboStoc which improves and extends baseline diffusion generative
924   models, across tasks of image generation and structured 3D shape generation. Image generation
925   can be misused potentially, although our model as well as the baseline model uses very coarse level
926   class name conditioning that prevents highly targeted applications. Structured 3D shapes are mostly
927   furniture like daily objects, so their generation is unlikely to be misused. In terms of methodology, we
928   have advocated the importance of sampling the combinatorial flexibility for both model performance
929
      and new applications. The combinatorial complexity of high-dimensional and multiple-attribute data
      samples can be further explored theoretically based on our work, for example from the perspective of
930
      ergodicity (Walters, 2000) as emphasized by our dense sampling of all possible interpolation points.
