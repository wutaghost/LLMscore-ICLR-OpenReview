009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Video face re-aging deals with altering the apparent age of a person to the target
012            age in videos. This problem is challenging due to the lack of paired video datasets
013            maintaining temporal consistency in identity and age. Most re-aging methods
014            process each image individually without considering the temporal consistency
015            of videos. While some existing works address the issue of temporal coherence
016            through video facial attribute manipulation in latent space, they often fail to deliver
017            satisfactory performance in age transformation. To tackle the issues, we propose
018            (1) a novel synthetic video dataset that features subjects across a diverse range of
019
               age groups; (2) a baseline architecture designed to validate the effectiveness of our
               proposed dataset, and (3) the development of novel metrics tailored explicitly for
020
               evaluating the temporal consistency of video re-aging techniques. Our compre-
021
               hensive experiments on public datasets, including VFHQ and CelebV-HQ, show
022            that our method outperforms existing approaches in age transformation accuracy
023            and temporal consistency. Notably, in user studies, our method was preferred
024            for temporal consistency by 48.1% of participants for the older direction and by
025            39.3% for the younger direction.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Video face re-aging or video-based face re-aging aims to transform the apparent age in facial videos
031   while ensuring the temporal consistency in both age and identity. This field holds significance
032   relevance across diverse domains, including computer graphics, forensics, entertainment, and ad-
033   vertising. Despite the extensive research conducted in this domain, the challenge remains largely
034   unexplored when it comes to videos. One of the remaining challenges is that existing image-based
035   methods yield inconsistent identities when applied to videos or consecutive frames featuring varying
036   expressions, viewpoints, and lighting conditions.
037   Seminal studies Alaluf et al. (2022); Tzaban et al. (2022) have leveraged StyleGAN-based Karras
038   et al. (2019) frameworks to develop techniques for manipulating facial attributes in videos, aim-
039   ing for greater attribute consistency. Zoss et al. (2022) have utilized the synthetic images labeled
040   with existing re-aging techniques Alaluf et al. (2021) to curate a paired dataset for re-aging. Their
041   study shows that supervised training on synthetic dataset yields favorable outcomes for still images.
042   Preechakul et al. (2022); Chen & Lathuilière (2023); Li et al. (2023); Wahid et al. (2024) introduce
043   diffusion models to tackle this problem. Lin et al. (2024) employs a reward-based approach by es-
044
      tablishing a consensus between the aging process and the aging personalization agents to generate
      robust faces. However, these approaches are trained on static images and significantly suffer from
045
      temporal inconsistencies.
046
047   Thus, training face re-aging methods on videos is beneficial for addressing the temporal consistency
048   problem in video re-aging tasks. Therefore, we propose a pipeline to generate a synthetic video
049   dataset. This dataset comprises paired data for supervised training, consisting of various ages, poses,
050
      and expressions. The creation of this dataset involves three major steps.
051   Firstly, we utilize StyleGAN to synthesize a face image. Then we apply existing re-aging method
052   SAM Alaluf et al. (2021) to generate images of the same person with varying ages (Sec. 3.1.1).
053   Next, we build key frames consisting of various poses and expressions of that individual (Sec. 3.1.2).
      Lastly, we introduce natural motion with frame interpolation (Sec. 3.1.3).

072
      In addition to the dataset, we introduce a baseline architecture designed to utilize the temporal coher-
073
      ence inherent in our proposed video dataset. This architecture primarily comprises recurrent blocks,
074   employing a fusion-based approach that leverages concatenated inputs to exploit temporal consis-
075   tency. Drawing inspiration from seminal works in video generation Clark et al. (2019); Saito et al.
076   (2020); Tulyakov et al. (2018), we incorporate a video discriminator equipped with 3D convolutional
077   layers to ensure both realism and natural motion in the generated videos.
078
      Recognizing that existing aging metrics are not well-suited for video-based methods, we address this
079
      gap by developing novel metrics to assess temporal continuity in video-based re-aging. Through ex-
080   tensive experiments, we demonstrate that our video-based architecture produces remarkable results
081   and outperforms existing state-of-the-art methods across various public datasets.
082
083
      Given the challenges and limitations of current methods, our work introduces several contributions
      in video re-aging as follows.
084
085
              1. We introduce a pipeline designed to generate a synthetic video dataset specifically for video
086              re-aging. This dataset features age-paired videos of individuals across various ages, poses,
087              and expressions, allowing model to be trained with supervised learning.
088
              2. We present a baseline network architecture custom-designed for our synthesized video
089
                 dataset. Our generator is built upon a combination of recurrent blocks consisting of U-Net
090              architecture, utilizing both 2D image-based and 3D video-based conditional discriminators.
091
              3. We propose novel metrics to evaluate the temporal consistency of video re-aging meth-
092
                 ods. These include Temporal Regional Wrinkle Consistency (TRWC) and Temporally Age
093
                 Preservation. These metrics provide a robust framework for evaluating the quality of age
094              transformations over time.

099   2.1     I MAGE - BASED FACE R E -AGING
100
      The study in Antipov et al. (2017b) pioneered the use of a conditional GAN for face aging. Sub-
101
      sequently, several influential works such as Antipov et al. (2017a); Wang et al. (2018); Or-El et al.
102   (2020); Li et al. (2021); Yao et al. (2021b); Kim et al. (2024); Guo et al. (2024) emerged, expanding
103   on this concept. Similar to Li et al. (2021), SAM Alaluf et al. (2021) emphasizes continuous age
104   progression. SAM is a StyleGAN-based model Karras et al. (2019) capable of generating high-
105   resolution images. In contrast to other methods, it does not use an age classifier to estimate the input
106   age. FRAN Zoss et al. (2022) trains a simple encoder-decoder network in a supervised manner with
107   a generated synthetic dataset. Rather than adopting age classifiers or embedding, it extends the input
      to a 5-channel image, including two binary masks for input and target ages. CUSP Gomez-Trenado

      et al. (2022) introduces style and content encoders to disentangle the style and content of the in-
109   put. This approach also incorporates the GB algorithm Springenberg et al. (2014) within the CUSP
110   module, ensuring that only age-relevant features are processed. AgeTransGAN Hsu et al. (2022)
111   disentangles the encoded image into identity and age components with two independent modules.
112   PADA Li et al. (2023) and FADING Chen & Lathuilière (2023) adopt a text-driven approach and
113   integrates the pre-trained CLIP Radford et al. (2021) and diffusion models. However, these works
114   only focus on images, and directly applying these methods on individual frames does not consider
115   temporal consistency, affecting the quality of re-aging quality over time.

117   2.2     V IDEO - BASED FACE R E -AGING
118
119
      Most video face re-aging methods transform the face by manipulating the age in latent space, except
      Duong et al. (2019) that proposes a reinforcement learning method for the sequence of video frames.
120
      Yao et al. (2021a) proposes editing the vectors in the StyleGAN latent space with various pre-
121
      processing steps that are independently applied to every single frame of an input video. Tzaban
122   et al. (2022) identify the inconsistencies in PTI Roich et al. (2022) and suggest e4e Tov et al. (2021)
123   encoder with it for finding the pivots. Video editing methods often crop faces as a prepossessing step.
124   To overcome this problem, Yang et al. (2023) addresses this issue by proposing changes in the initial
125   StyleGAN layers to overcome the cropping problem. Kim et al. (2023) proposes a diffusion-based
126   editing approach by using Preechakul et al. (2022) that disentangles the video into time-dependent
127   features (such as motion) that are applied to each frame and time-independent features (such as
128   identity) which are shared across all the frames. While improving temporal consistency over image-
129   based methods, they still struggle to accurately transform faces to the target age.

132   2.3     V IDEO FACE R E -AGING DATASET
133
134   Video face re-aging presents significant challenges, primarily due to the lack of dedicated video
135   datasets. Existing image-based face re-aging datasets are typically labeled either automatically using
136   an age classifier or manually via crowdsourcing Zhang et al. (2022); Liu et al. (2021); Karras et al.
137   (2019); Rothe et al. (2018). However, these datasets do not provide paired ages for supervised
138
      training. Zheng et al. (2017) proposed a technique to generate a synthetic dataset that is labelled
      through SAM Alaluf et al. (2021). Duong et al. (2019) also labelled a video dataset, which remains
139
      private.

      3.1.1    I MAGE - BASED FACE R E -AGING DATASET
150
151   Creating a high-quality image-based re-aging dataset is a crucial in our pipeline because it directly
152   influences the quality of our subsequent video re-aging dataset. However, obtaining these paired
153   image datasets is a challenging task. To overcome this limitation, we turn to insights from Zoss et al.
154   (2022), which show that training on synthetic datasets can yield realistic results on real images. For
155   instance, the neural network can learn how wrinkles change from the synthetic images and apply
156   this knowledge to real images. We refer to these learned changes as delta images Dt , as illustrated
157   in Fig. 2.
158   Firstly, we leverage StyleGAN Karras et al. (2020) which takes a random noise as input and generate
159   high-resolution synthetic image. Then we utilize SAM Alaluf et al. (2021) that manipulates the
160   latent vector with StyleGAN for age transformation. The intuition behind choosing SAM over other
161   existing methods is its re-aging performance in terms of age error, that is also evident through our
      experiments. Using this approach, we construct a synthetic facial image dataset:

171
      The subsequent step in the outlined pipeline involves key frames generation. This key frames cap-
172
      ture specific snapshots or moments in videos to enable seamless transitions between sequences.
173
      The challenge lies in acquiring diverse facial images to adequately encapsulate the video’s dynam-
174   ics, including various poses and expressions. To address this, we utilize a recent face reenactment
175   technique Wang et al. (2021a) that modifies the pose and expression of a source image based on a
176   driving image. Incorporating this method into our pipeline allows us to generate multiple images of
177   an individual with varying poses and expressions. These resulting images serve as key frames.
178
      In this study, we employ the off-the-shelf model OSFV1 Wang et al. (2021a) to produce synthetic
179
      key frames using our image dataset. Our approach diverges from the original methodology in that
180   we rely solely on source images from our dataset. Instead of utilizing driving images, which can be
181   challenging to gather, we employ random values for the rotation matrix R, translation matrix Q, and
182   expression keypoints δ to generate various poses and expressions as shown in Fig. 1 (b). We can
183   write this process as follows:

188   different key frames through this method. While it is also possible to repeat this procedure to create
189   a motion video, but we have observed a degradation of the quality in the resultant videos. Therefore,
190   we come up with an alternative approach to address this problem in next section.

      Evaluating age transformation performance in a video context is both essential and challenging. Ac-
      cording to the best of our knowledge, there is no specific metric to assess the temporal consistency
301
      of re-aging methods. Most existing evaluation metrics are designed for image-based re-aging meth-
302
      ods as they measure the age transformation performance on individual images Zoss et al. (2022) or
303   use metrics designed to evaluate the continuity of identity drift Alaluf et al. (2022). Regarding this,
304   we propose two metrics, TRWC and T-Age, capable of effectively assessing temporal continuity in
305   terms of age in video-based re-aging task.

532
533
      In this paper, we introduced a paired video dataset for the video re-aging. This dataset encompasses
534   subjects from a wide range of age groups. Shifting from the conventional model-centric focus, we
535   adopted a data-centric approach. Inspired by recent advancements in face reenactment and frame
536   interpolation, we encompasses various facial poses and expressions in the proposed data. Conse-
537   quently, we proposed a baseline network architecture to evaluate the proposed dataset, emphasizing
538   both temporal consistency and the quality of age transformations. Furthermore, we formulated two
539   novel metrics to evaluate temporal consistency in video re-aging, which consider age-relevant fea-
      tures such as facial wrinkles over time. We validated our method with comprehensive experiments.
