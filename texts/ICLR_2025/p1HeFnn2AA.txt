                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
010
               We initiate the study of deep learning for the automated design of two-sided
               matching mechanisms. What is of most interest is to use machine learning to
011
               understand the possibility of new tradeoffs between strategy-proofness and stability.
012
               These properties cannot be achieved simultaneously, but the efficient frontier is not
013            understood. We introduce novel differentiable surrogates for quantifying ordinal
014            strategy-proofness and stability and use them to train differentiable matching
015            mechanisms represented by neural networks that map discrete preferences to valid
016            randomized matchings. We demonstrate that the efficient frontier characterized
017            by these learned mechanisms is substantially better than that achievable through a
018            convex combination of baselines of deferred acceptance (stable and strategy-proof
019            for only one side of the market), top trading cycles (strategy-proof for one side,
020            but not stable), and randomized serial dictatorship (strategy-proof for both sides,
021
               but not stable). This gives a new target for economic theory and opens up new
               possibilities for machine learning pipelines in matching market design.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027
      Two-sided matching markets, classically used for settings such as high-school matching, medical
      residents matching, and law clerk matching, and more recently used in online platforms such as
028
      Uber, Lyft, Airbnb, and dating apps, play a significant role in today’s world. As a result, there is a
029
      significant interest in designing better mechanisms for two-sided matching.
030
031   The seminal work of Gale & Shapley (1962) introduces a simple mechanism for stable, one-to-
032   one matching in two-sided markets—deferred-acceptance (DA)—which has been applied in many
033
      settings, including doctor-hospital matching (Roth & Peranson, 1999), school choice (Abdulkadiroğlu
      & Sönmez, 2003; Pathak & Sönmez, 2008; Abdulkadiroğlu et al., 2009), and cadet-matching (Sönmez
034
      & Switzer, 2013; Sönmez, 2013). The DA mechanism is stable, i.e., no pair of participants prefer each
035
      other to their match (or to being unmatched, if they are unmatched in the outcome). However, the DA
036   mechanism is not strategy-proof (SP), and a participant can sometimes misreport their preferences
037   to obtain a better outcome (although it is SP for participants on one side of the market). Although
038   widely used, this failure of SP for the DA mechanism presents a challenge for two main reasons. First,
039   it can lead to unfairness, where better-informed participants can gain an advantage in knowing which
040   misreport strategies can be helpful. Second, strategic behavior can lead to lower quality, unintended
041   outcomes, and outcomes that are unstable with respect to true preferences.
042   In general, it is well-known that there must necessarily be a tradeoff between stability and strategy-
043   proofness: it is provably impossible for a mechanism to achieve both stability and strategy-
044   proofness (Dubins & Freedman, 1981; Roth, 1982). A second example of a matching mechanism is
045   random serial dictatorship (RSD) (Abdulkadiroglu & Sönmez, 1998), which is typically adopted for
046   one-sided assignment problems rather than two-sided matching. When adapted to two-sided matching,
047   RSD is SP but not stable. In fact, a participant may even prefer to remain unmatched than participate
048   in the outcome of the matching. A third example of a matching mechanism is the top trading cycles
049   (TTC) mechanism (Shapley & Scarf, 1974), also typically adopted for one-sided assignment problems
050
      rather than problems of two-sided matching. In application to two-sided matching, TTC is neither SP
      nor stable (although it is SP for participants on one side of the market).
051
052   There have been various research efforts to circumvent this impossibility result. Some relax the
053   definition of strategyproofness (Mennle & Seuken, 2021) while others characterize the constraints
      under which stability and strategyproofness are achieved simultaneously (Kamada & Kojima, 2018;


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      Hatfield et al., 2021; Hatfield & Milgrom, 2005). The tradeoff between these desiderata remains
055   poorly understood beyond the existing point solutions of DA, RSD, and TTC. However, we argue that
056   real-world scenarios demand a more nuanced approach that considers both properties. The case of the
057   Boston school choice mechanism highlights the negative consequences of lacking strategy-proofness,
058   resulting in unfair manipulations by specific parents (Abdulkadiroglu et al., 2006). At the same time,
059   the importance of stability in matching markets is well understood (Roth, 1991).
060
      Recognizing this, and inspired by the success of deep learning in the study of revenue-optimal
061   auction design (Duetting et al., 2019), we initiate the study of deep learning for the design of two-
062   sided matching mechanisms. We ask whether deep learning frameworks can enable a systematic
063   study of this tradeoff. By answering this question affirmatively, we open up the possibility of using
064   machine learning pipelines to open up new opportunities for economic theory—seeking theoretical
065   characterizations of mechanisms that can strike a new balance between strategyproofness and stability.
066
      We use a neural network to represent the rules of a matching mechanism, mapping preference reports
067   to a distribution over feasible matchings, and show how we can use an unsupervised learning pipeline
068   to characterize the efficient frontier for the design tradeoff between stability and SP. The main
069   methodological challenge in applying neural networks to two-sided matching comes from handling
070   the ordinal preference inputs (the corresponding inputs are cardinal in auction design) and identifying
071   suitable, differentiable surrogates for approximate strategy-proofness and approximate stability.
072
      We work with randomized matching mechanisms, for which the strongest SP concept is ordinal
073   strategy-proofness. This aligns incentives with truthful reporting, whatever an agent’s utility function
074   (i.e., for any cardinal preferences consistent with an agent’s ordinal preferences). Ordinal SP is
075   equivalent to the property of first-order stochastic dominance (FOSD) (Erdil, 2014), which suitably
076   defines the property that an agent has a better chance of getting their top, top-two, top-three, and
077   so forth choices when they report truthfully. As a surrogate for SP, we quantify during training the
078   degree to which FOSD is violated. For this, we adopt an adversarial learning approach, augmenting
079   the training data with defeating misreports that reveal the violation of FOSD. We also define a suitable
080   surrogate to quantify the degree to which stability is violated. This surrogate aligns with the notion of
081
      ex ante stability—the strongest stability concept for randomized matching.
082   We propose two different neural network architectures to represent matching mechanisms — a
083   simple fully connected neural network (MLP) and a convolutional neural network (CNN). Both
084   architectures are trained with stochastic gradient descent (SGD) on loss functions that is based on
085   various convex combinations of the two surrogate quantities. This allows us to construct the efficient
086   frontier for stability and strategy-proofness for different market settings. Our main experimental
087
      results demonstrate that this novel use of deep learning can strike a much better trade-off between
      stability and SP than that achieved by a convex combination of the DA, TTC, and RSD mechanisms.
088
089   The CNN architecture, specifically designed for matching, uses 1 × 1 convolutions that treat inputs
090   as two channels—each representing one side of the market’s preferences. Since the number of sides
091   is fixed, this architecture avoids the need to increase the number of hidden units (or filters, in the
092   case of CNNs) as the input size grows. Moreover, the use of 1 × 1 convolutions ensures permutation
093
      equivariance, which narrows the search space, enhances generalization, and reduces training time
      by eliminating the need to calculate strategy-proofness violations for each agent individually. This
094
      architecture allows our approach to scale efficiently to markets with up to 50 agents on each side.
095
096   Taken as a whole, these results suggest that deep learning pipelines can be used to identify new
097   opportunities for matching theory. For example, we identify mechanisms that are provably almost
098   as stable as DA and yet considerably more strategy-proof. We also identify mechanisms that are
099
      provably almost as strategy-proof as RSD and yet considerably more stable. These discoveries raise
      opportunities for future work in economic theory, in regard to understanding the structure of these
100
      two-sided matching mechanisms as well as characterizing the preference distributions for which this
101
      is possible.

106   Dubins & Freedman (1981) and Roth (1982) show the impossibility of achieving both stability and
107   SP in two-sided matching. Alcalde & Barberà (1994) also show the impossibility of individually
      rational, Pareto efficient, and SP allocation rules, and this work has been extended to randomized

108
      matching (Alva & Manjunath, 2020). RSD is SP but may not be stable or even individually rational
109   (IR) (Abdulkadiroglu & Sönmez, 1998). We will see that the top trading cycles (TTC) mecha-
110   nism (Shapley & Scarf, 1974), when applied in a two-sided context, is only SP for one side, and is
111   neither stable nor IR. The DA mechanism (Gale & Shapley, 1962) is stable but not SP; see also (Roth
112   et al., 1993), who study the polytope of stable matchings. The stable improvement cycles mecha-
113   nism (Erdil & Ergin, 2008) achieves as much efficiency as possible on top of stability but fails to be
114   SP even for one side of the market. Finally, a series of results show that DA becomes SP for both
115   sides of the market in large-market limit contexts (Immorlica & Mahdian, 2015; Kojima & Pathak,
116   2009; Lee, 2016).
117   Aziz & Klaus (2019) discuss different stability and no envy concepts. We focus on ex ante stabil-
118   ity (Kesten & Ünver, 2015), also discussed by (Roth et al., 1993) as strong stability. Mennle &
119   Seuken (2021) discuss different notions of approximate strategy-proofness in the context of matching
120   and allocation problems. In this work, we focus on ordinal SP and its analog of FOSD (Erdil, 2014).
121   This is a strong and widely used SP concept in the presence of ordinal preferences. There are a lot
122   of other desiderata, such as efficiency, that are also incompatible with strategyproofness. Mennle &
123
      Seuken (2017) study this trade-off through hybrid mechanisms which are convex combinations of a
      mechanism with good incentive properties with another which is efficient. In the context of social
124
      choice, other work studies the trade-off between approximate SP and desiderata, such as plurality and
125
      veto voting (Mennle & Seuken, 2016).
126
127   Conitzer & Sandholm (2002; 2004) introduced the automated mechanism design (AMD) approach
128   that framed problems as a linear program. However, this approach faces severe scalability issues
129
      as the formulation scales exponentially in the number of agents and items (Guo & Conitzer, 2010).
      Overcoming this limitation, more recent work seeks to use deep neural networks to address problems
130
      of economic design (Duetting et al., 2019; Feng et al., 2018; Golowich et al., 2018; Curry et al.,
131
      2020; Shen et al., 2019; Rahme et al., 2020; Duan et al., 2022; Ivanov et al., 2022), but not until
132   now to matching problems. As discussed in the introduction, two-sided matching brings about new
133   challenges, most notably in regard to working with discrete, ordinal preferences and adopting the
134   right surrogate loss functions for approximate SP and approximate stability. Other work has made
135   use of support vector machines to search for stable mechanisms, but without considering strategy-
136   proofness (Narasimhan et al., 2016). A different line of research is also considering stable matching
137   together with bandits problems, where agent preferences are unknown a priori (Das & Kamenica,
138   2005; Liu et al., 2020; Dai & Jordan, 2021; Liu et al., 2022; Basu et al., 2021; Sankararaman et al.,
139   2021; Jagadeesan et al., 2021; Cen & Shah, 2022; Min et al., 2022).
140   There have also been other recent efforts that leverage deep learning for matching (in the context
141   of online bipartite matching (Alomrani et al., 2022)) and other related combinatorial optimization
142   problems (Bengio et al., 2021). Most of these papers adopt a reinforcement learning based approach
143   to compute their solutions. Our approach, on the other hand, is not sequential but rather end-to-end
144   differentiable, and our parameter weights are updated through a single backward pass. Additionally,
145   the focus of our work is on matching markets and mechanism design, and is concerned with capturing
146
      core economic concepts within a machine learning framework and balancing the trade-offs between
      stability and strategy-proofness.

      A significant advantage of using CNNs is that the number of input channels to the network consistently
299   remains at two, which means that the number of filters required does not vary significantly. Conversely,
300   in a fully connected neural network architecture, the size of the input layer expands linearly with the
301   number of workers and firms. Consequently, to achieve meaningful data representation, there is also
302   a corresponding need to increase the number of hidden layers.
303
      We also note that this implementation is an adaptation of the exchangeable matrix layer in Hartford
304
      et al. (2018) used to design permutation equivariant auctions (Rahme et al., 2020). Rather than
305   employing the typical row-wise and column-wise mean calculations to compute the additional
306   channels, our model utilizes the max operation. Since the max operation is still a commutative
307   pooling operation, the permutation equivariance remains preserved (Ravanbakhsh et al., 2017).
308
      Additionally, our benchmark mechanisms are permutation equivariant and we found that incorporating
309
      this property into the learned mechanisms offers several advantages. It enhances model complexity
310
      by reducing the search space and improves generalization as it inherently augments the training data
311   by considering all permutations of the input data, effectively increasing the diversity of data the
312   model is exposed to during training without actually expanding the minibatch size. Additionally, this
313   symmetry ensures that the expected SP violation across all agents is the same, thereby reducing the
314   need to individually compute the SP violations of all agents.

      with a fixed priority order as our baseline.

505   6     D ISCUSSION
506   The methodology and results in this paper give a first but crucial step towards using machine learning
507   to understanding the structure of mechanisms that achieve nearly the same stability as DA while
508   surpassing DA in terms of strategy-proofness. This is an interesting observation, given the practical
509   and theoretical importance of the DA mechanism. There are other interesting questions waiting to be
510   addressed. For instance, can we use this kind of framework to understand other tradeoffs, such as
511   tradeoffs between strategy-proofness and efficiency?
512   As discussed previously, a challenge in scaling to larger problems is the need to find defeating
513   misreports, as exhaustively enumerating all misreports for an agent becomes intractable as the number
514   of agents on the other side of the market increases. A simple remedy that we adopted here is to
515   work in domains where there exists some structure on the preference domain, so that not all possible
516   preference orders exist; e.g., single-peaked preferences are an especially stark example (Black, 1948).
517   Another remedy is to restrict the language available to agents in making preference reports; e.g., it
518   is commonplace to only allow for “top-k preferences” to be reported. It will also be interesting to
519
      study complementary approaches that relax the discrete set of preference orderings to a continuous
      convex hull such as the Birkhoff polytope and using gradient ascent to identify misreports. Despite
520
      this limitation, our current approach scales much further than other, existing methods for automated
521
      design, which are not well suited for this problem. For instance, methods that use linear programs or
522   integer programs do not scale well because of the number of variables required to make explicit the
523   input and output structure of the functional that must be optimized over.
524
525
      A second challenge is that we have not been able to find a suitable, publicly available dataset to test
      our approach. As a fallback, we have endeavored to capture some real-world structures by varying
526
      the correlation between agent preferences and the truncation probabilities of preferences. Using
527
      such stylized, probabilistic models and simulations for validating approaches is a well-established
528   and prevalent practice, consistently utilized when investigating two-sided matching markets (Chen
529   & Sönmez, 2006; Echenique & Yariv, 2013; Das & Kamenica, 2005; Liu et al., 2020; Dai &
530   Jordan, 2021). For instance, Chen and Sönmez (Chen & Sönmez, 2006) design an environment for
531   school choice where they consider six different schools with six seats each and where the students’
532   preferences are simulated to depend on proximity, quality, and a random factor. Echenique an
533   Yariv (Echenique & Yariv, 2013) use a simulation study with eight participants on each side of
534   the market, with the payoff matrix designed such that there are one, two, or three stable matches.
535   Further, recent papers on bandit models for stable matching model agent preferences through synthetic
536
      datasets (Das & Kamenica, 2005; Liu et al., 2020; Dai & Jordan, 2021).
537   In closing, we see exciting work ahead in advancing the design of matching mechanisms that strike
538   the right balance between stability, strategyproofness, and other considerations that are critical to
539   real-world applications. As an example, it will be interesting to extend the learning framework to
      encompass desiderata such as capacity limitations or fairness considerations.
