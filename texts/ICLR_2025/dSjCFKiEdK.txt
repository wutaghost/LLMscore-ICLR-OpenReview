038                                              A BSTRACT
039
040            Diffusion-based image editing methods have garnered significant attention in
041            image editing. However, despite encompassing a wide range of editing priors,
042            these methods are helpless when handling editing tasks that are challenging for
043            users to accurately describe. We propose InstructBrush, an inversion method for
044            instruction-based image editing methods to bridge this gap. It extracts editing
045            effects from example image pairs as editing instructions to guide the editing of new
046
               images. Two key techniques are introduced into InstructBrush, Attention-based
               Instruction Optimization and Transformation-oriented Instruction Initialization,
047
               to address the limitations of the previous method in terms of inversion effects and
048
               instruction generalization. To explore the ability of visual prompt editing methods
049            to guide image editing in open scenarios, we establish a Transformation-Oriented
050            Paired Benchmark (TOP-Bench). Quantitatively and qualitatively, our approach
051            achieves superior performance in editing and is more semantically consistent
052            with the target editing effects. The code and benchmark will be released upon
053            acceptance.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
055
056
057   Recently developed diffusion-based image editing methods Hertz et al. (2022); Tumanyan et al.
058   (2023); Brooks et al. (2023); Xu et al. (2024) enable users to effortlessly achieve their editing
059   goals using natural language prompts. While they have garnered significant attention owing to their
060   flexibility and versatility in image editing, they still face challenges when dealing with editing tasks
061   that are difficult for users to describe. Specifically, while guiding image editing with language is
062   natural and straightforward, it becomes particularly challenging when users wish to apply analogous
063
      manipulation on others’ finished edits or image transformations implemented by other tools, as shown
      in Figure 1. In such cases, using text or a single image as a condition to guide diffusion models for
064
      editing is quite difficult. It makes sense to provide a pair of example images to demonstrate this
065
      editing effect.
066
067   This motivates the demand for the problem of visual prompt editing. Similar to image analogies
068   Jacobs et al. (2001), this problem learns an edit concept from image pairs, and subsequently applies
069
      it to edit new images. These image pairs that provide information about image transformations are
      also called visual prompts, which serves as a valuable replacement when language is imprecise in
070
      describing specific editing concepts.
071
072   One way to implement visual prompt editing is visual in-context learning Yang et al. (2024); Gu
073   et al. (2024). It constructs the visual prompt as well as the input image and prediction noise as a
074   grid-like input, and then uses the inpainting diffusion model to model the task as an inpainting task
075
      to predict the output. Although this paradigm can learn general image transformations by analogy
      with visual prompts, its performance is slightly inferior for the specific task of image editing. In
076
      addition, due to the limitation of grid input, it cannot be applied to the editing of high-resolution
077
      images. To address these issues, visual instruction inversion Nguyen et al. (2023) replaces the inpaint
078   diffusion model with the instruction-based editing model Brooks et al. (2023); Geng et al. (2023)
079   to improve the performance on image editing tasks while supporting high-resolution image editing.
080   It uses the text inversion method Gal et al. (2022a) to invert the editing concepts revealed by visual
081   prompts into the feature space of text instructions to guide the editing of new input images, but it
082   struggles with the editing effects for two reasons: 1) Inverting instructions in textual space limits their
083   representational ability. Since the text encoder is aligned on text-image pairs with rough descriptions,
084   it is challenging to provide specific representations of the image editing details Chen et al. (2023d). 2)
085   Its semantic-level instruction initialization introduces editing-irrelevant content from visual prompts,
086
      hence limiting the generalization of the instruction in generalized scenarios.
087   To bridge these gaps, we introduce InstructBrush, an instruction inversion-based method for visual
088   prompt editing by leveraging the instruction-based image editing model. In contrast to the previous
089   methods, we propose the Attention-based Instruction Optimization. It improve the representation
090   ability of instruction guidance by localizing and learning the editing concepts represented by visual
091   prompts in the cross-attention layer of the diffusion model. To introduce semantic-level guidance
092
      related to editing, we introduce the Transformation-oriented Instruction Initialization. It ingeniously
      separates editing-related information from the content of visual prompts and incorporates it into the
093
      learned instructions. This effectively mitigates the risk of previous method Nguyen et al. (2023)
094
      compromising instruction generalization by introducing irrelevant content information, and promotes
095   semantic alignment of the instruction with the objectives.
096
097   To investigate the ability of the visual prompt editing methods in guiding image editing in diverse
098
      scenarios, we establish Transformation-Oriented Paired Benchmark (TOP-Bench). This benchmark
      comprises a total of 750 images, encompassing 25 distinct editing effects, with each effect having 10
099
      pairs of training data and 5 pairs of testing data. The creation of this benchmark not only helps to
100
      evaluate the potential of existing methods in guiding image editing, but also paves the way for further
101   research in visual prompt editing. Qualitatively and quantitatively, our method surpasses the existing
102   methods in terms of performance and demonstrates greater semantically consistency with the target
103   editing effects.
104
      In summary, our contributions are threefold:
105
106
107          • We introduce InstructBrush, a novel solution to visual prompt editing, which extracts the
               editing concepts from exemplar image pairs for the subsequent image editing task.

      Instruction-based Image Editing. Text-guided diffusion models Nichol et al. (2021); Ramesh et al.
121
      (2022); Saharia et al. (2022); Rombach et al. (2022); Podell et al. (2023); Betker et al. (2023); Dai
122   et al. (2023) have taken the world by storm. By leveraging the robust generative priors of these
123   models, InstructPix2Pix (IP2P) Brooks et al. (2023) makes the initial attempt to use a triplet dataset
124   for training a model that edits images based on instructions, achieving intuitive and user-friendly
125   instruction-based image editing. HIVE Zhang et al. (2023b) incorporates reward learning from
126   human feedback to fine-tune IP2P for instruction editing that is more aligned with user preferences.
127   MagicBrush Zhang et al. (2023a) constructs a large-scale manually annotated dataset to fine-tune IP2P,
128   greatly improving the effect in real image editing. Several existing methods, such as InstructDiffusion
129   Geng et al. (2023) and Emu Edit Sheynin et al. (2023) extend instruction-based editing methods to
130
      new visual tasks, demonstrating its potential as a universal framework for visual tasks. Recently,
      some efforts Fu et al. (2023); Huang et al. (2023a) leverage Multimodal Large Language Models
131
      (MLLMs) to enhance the performance of instructions, facilitating more accurate editing. Other efforts
132
      Simsar et al. (2023); Guo & Lin (2023); Li et al. (2023a) concentration flexible and high-fidelity local
133   editing, addressing the limitations of instruction-based editing in processing local details of images.
134   Additionally, instruction-based image editing has been extended to 3D Chen et al. (2023b) and video
135   Xing et al. (2023) editing tasks, showcasing its tremendous application value.
136
      Visual In-context Learning. In-context learning Brown et al. (2020), which originated from the field
137
      of natural language processing (NLP), has been promoted as a learning paradigm. This paradigm
138
      enables the execution of a given task on a sample query after learning the task from a set of examples.
139   VisualPrompting Bar et al. (2022) first introduced the concept of visual contextual learning. It
140   uses an inpainting-based approach with grid-like inputs and has shown remarkable results in many
141   tasks. Subsequent works Wang et al. (2023a;b); Fang et al. (2024) broaden the application areas
142   of the framework, such as keypoint detection Wang et al. (2023a), image denoising Wang et al.
143   (2023a), image segmentation Wang et al. (2023b) and 3D point cloud Fang et al. (2024). Recent
144   works Wang et al. (2024); Chen et al. (2023c) introduce in-context learning on diffusion models
145   to accomplish various visual tasks, but they require guidance from textual instructions. Yang et al.
146   (2024); Gu et al. (2024) models visual transformations as a diffusion-based inpainting problem.
147
      However, it still requires grid images as input, which poses a significant burden when processing
      high-resolution images. Unlike these methods, Visii Nguyen et al. (2023) focuses on editing tasks. It
148
      inverses exemplar image pairs into a text instruction within an instruction-based image editing model,
149
      replacing textual instructions to guide the editing of new images. Our approach similarly focuses
150   on image editing based on instruction inversion and achieves more robust editing and generalization
151   ability to new scenarios.

4   M ETHOD
The pipeline of InstructBrush is demonstrated in Figure 2. Based on the instruction-based image edit-
ing methods Brooks et al. (2023), InstructBrush inverts exemplar image pairs as editing instructions
and applies them to editing new images. It proposes novel Attention-based Instruction Optimization
and Transformation-oriented Instruction Initialization modules. The former introduces the editing
instruction into the cross-attention layers of the instruction-based image editing model and directly
optimizes the Keys and Values corresponding to the instruction within these layers, facilitating more
effective instruction inversion (Section 4.1). The latter introduces semantic-level guidance related
to editing, ingeniously separates editing-related information from the content of visual prompts and
incorporates it into the learned instructions. This effectively promotes semantic alignment of the
instruction with the objectives. (Section 4.2).

      4.1   ATTENTION - BASED I NSTRUCTION O PTIMIZATION
217
218   Inspired by Textual Inversion Gal et al. (2022a), The current instruction inversion method Nguyen
219   et al. (2023) optimizes the embeddings of the text encoder using image pairs, aiming to represent
220   the transformation effects between image pairs in textual space. However, the text encoder is trained
221   on text-image pairs with rough descriptions, and its feature space is prone to losing the detailed
222   representation of the image Chen et al. (2023d). Therefore, it is difficult to achieve the requirement
223   of only optimizing the instruction that represents the target transformation in this space. Instead, we
224
      focus on optimizing the features in cross-attention layer of the diffusion model. These features are
      projected from textual embeddings to representations consistent with image features, enabling a more
225
      precise representation of image transformation details Hertz et al. (2022); Simsar et al. (2023). As a
226
      result, we introduce an attention-based instruction optimization that optimizes editing instructions in
227   the image feature space of the cross-attention layers in the diffusion model, fostering more effective
228   instruction inversion.
229
230
      Attention-based Instruction. Considering a single-head cross-attention, let Q be the query, K, V be
      the keys and values from the instruction, respectively, the cross-attention is given by:
231
232
                                                           \begin {aligned} \text {Attention}\left (Q, K, V\right )= \text {Softmax}\Big (\frac {QK^T}{\sqrt {d'}} \Big )V. \end {aligned}\label {eq:cross_attention}                                                   (4)
233
234
      Here, K, V ∈ Rl×d , where l represents the token length of the instruction, and d represents the
235
      feature dimension, the value of which depends on the position of the cross-attention layer in the
236   U-Net framework. We optimize the features γK , γV ∈ Rm×d with a length of m ∈ l in the key and
237   value corresponding to the first m tokens of the text instruction. Because after linear projection,
238   instruction embeddings transform from text embedding to image features, exhibiting stronger image
239   representation capabilities. To optimize the feature embeddings of the editing instruction, our
240   optimization objective is derived from the simplified least squares error in Eq. 2:
241
242
                            \begin {aligned} \gamma = \argmin \Eb {\mathcal {E}(x), \mathcal {E}\left (c_I\right ), c_T, \epsilon , t}{ { \left \| \epsilon - \epsilon _\theta (z_t, t, \mathcal {E}(c_I), c_T) \right \|^2_2}}. \end {aligned}\label {eq:total_loss}   (5)
243   Here, γ = {γK , γV }1...n represents the features of keys and values from the first m tokens of the text
244   instruction in all n cross-attention layers. The value of m corresponds to the number of text tokens
245   used for instruction initialization, as described in Section 4.2.
246
      Time-aware Instruction (Optional). In the text-guided diffusion models, the denoising process
247
      focuses on image generation from low-frequency structure to high-frequency details Daras & Dimakis
248
      (2022); Zhang et al. (2023c). We believe that a similar property also exists in instruction-based editing
249   models, where different denoising processes primarily focus on distinct transformations. We confirm
250   this view in Figure 15. Therefore, we divide the instruction optimization equally into j parts based on
251   denoising time steps, emphasizing instruction learning within the editing-related denoising time steps.

      Now, we have γ = {γK , γV }1...n , where j is 5 by default. In this way, the learned instructions can
253   capture more details of transformations, which can guide the editing of new images more robustly.

255   4.2   T RANSFORMATION - ORIENTED I NSTRUCTION I NITIALIZATION
256
257   Concept inversion Gal et al. (2022a); Voynov et al. (2023); Zhang et al. (2023c) uses the semantic
258   class word (e.g., dog, cat) for initialization, providing prior information for the target concept learning.
259   However, instruction inversion requires learning a sentence as an instruction that describes the image
260   transformation. Manually initializing a sentence based on the transformation of reference image pairs
261
      is not only laborious but also subjective. The existing work Nguyen et al. (2023) utilizes the caption
      method Wen et al. (2023) to obtain the caption of after-editing images in the training set as the start
262
      point of the optimization. Despite the introduction of transformation-related prior knowledge, it
263
      simultaneously introduce editing-irrelevant content information about the training scenario, hindering
264   the generalization of instruction to new scenarios. In addition, although existing multimodal large
265   language models (MLLM) can directly compare two images to obtain a description of the differences,
266   the daunting model size and lack of prior knowledge in professional vocabulary have caused certain
267   obstacles in its practical application. In contrast, our approach extracts transformation-related
268   information in a simpler and more effective way. Specifically, we first extract unique phrases
269   that differentiate the images before and after editing as editing-related priors. Subsequently, we
      incorporate them into the instruction template for instruction initialization.

      Unique Phrase Extraction. Given a set of image pairs {{x}, {y}}, where {x} and {y} represent
271   the image sets before and after editing, for a single set {x}, we search for the fixed-length phrase set
272   Px = {< p1 >, . . . , < pr >} with the highest cosine similarity between image and text features. Here,
273   < pi > represents a text phrase from a vocabulary set, which can be customized according to the task
274   domain or use a public vocabulary set pha (2022). And r represents the adjustable number of phrases
275   to form the caption, which is set to 5 by default. Subsequently, we compare the feature similarity
276   between Px and the image sets {x}, {y} respectively, and then measure the difference in feature
277   similarity of the same phrase with the two sets as the sensitivity. This process can be represented as
278   follows:
279                          \begin {aligned} sens_i\left (<\!p_i\!> \right )=sim\left (<\!p_i\!>, \{x\}\right )-sim\left (<\!p_i\!>, \{y\}\right ) \end {aligned}\label {eq:unique_phrase}                                                             (6)
280
281   Here, sensi denotes the sensitivity of the ith phrase in Px and sim denotes the CLIP feature similarity.
282
      We identify the phrase with maximum sensitivity as the unique phrase < px > of the set {x}. However,
      there exist certain edits whose editing- related information cannot be recognized. To avoid the unique
283
      phrase containing editing-irrelevant information, we define the truncation conditions:
284
285
286
                                                \begin {aligned} <\!p_x\!> = \begin {cases}<\!p_x\!> & \text { if }sens\left (<\!p_x\!>\right ) \geq \eta \\ \varnothing & \text { otherwise},\end {cases} \end {aligned}\label {eq:phrase_condition}   (7)
287
288   where η represents a constant that controls the truncation of unique phrases, set to 0.15 by default.
289   Instruction Template. With the above method, we can get the unique phrases < px > and < py > for
290   sets {x} and {y}. Then we incorporate them into the instruction template. The form of the instruction
291   template is strictly aligned with the base model’s editing instructions to maximize the use of the
292   textual prior. For example, we use ′′ turn < px > into < py >′′ as a starting point for instruction
293   optimization. Note that when < py >= ∅, we use None instruction for initialization and optimize
294   fixed-length features for Keys and Values in cross-attention. Although the initialized instruction is
295   not sufficient to express the target editing effect, it can introduce prior knowledge of transformation,
296
      aiding the semantics of learned instruction to be close to the target.

298   5    T RANSFORMATION - ORIENTED PAIRED B ENCHMARK
299
300   To investigate the editing capabilities of various instruction inversion methods in open scenarios
301   and facilitate a fair comparison of these methods, We establish a benchmark named TOP-Bench
302   (Transformation-Oriented Paired Benchmark), which can be utilized for both qualitative and quantita-
303   tive evaluations. Our benchmark contains few-shot rather than one-shot datasets because of the effect
304   of image transformation that is difficult to fully visualize with a single image pair. It spans 25 datasets
305
      corresponding to different editing effects. It covers a wide range of editing categories and scenarios,
      allowing for division from multiple dimensions. Each dataset consists of 10 pairs of training images
306
      and 5 pairs of testing images, totaling 750 images. Additionally, we provide text instructions aligned
307
      with the transformation effects for each dataset. Please refer to the Supplementary for data acquisition
308   and detailed introduction.
309
310   To further analyze the advantages of our method, we categorize the benchmark into two different
311
      categories: TOP-Global and TOP-Local, corresponding to datasets of 14 global editing effects and 11
      local editing effects, respectively. We compare the quantitative results of different methods in these
312
      two categories to validate the effectiveness of our method.

      the optimization process. As depicted in Figure 5, the use of the content-oriented initialization

      method results in the leakage of content information from the training image into the edited image.

      By enabling instruction initialization to prioritize image changes over image content, it not only
474   enhances the editing capabilities of learned instructions, but also aligns the edited image with the

511   7    L IMITATIONS AND C ONCLUSION
512
513   Although optimization-based methods represented by our framework are easier to learn editing
514   concepts from multiple pairs of reference images, they increase the time cost in training. In addition,
515
      our initialization method is limited by the vocabulary used to search for unique phrases. If the phrase
      is not present in the vocabulary, our initialization method will initialize using None instruction, which
516
      will not introduce any editing prior.
517
518   Our method extracts editing effects from image pairs for editing tasks that are difficult for users to
519   describe. It introduces a new instruction optimization and initialization method, achieving better
520   instruction optimization and generalization. Numerous experiments have demonstrated the advantages
521
      of our method. In the future, we will apply our method to more powerful instruction-based image
      editing models for more robust editing performance. We hope that this work will stimulate more
522
      research and serve as a prior extraction method to aid in the training of downstream tasks.

729
730   Diffusion-based Prompt Inversion. The diffusion-based prompt inversion methods aim to learn the
731   text prompt from a handful of images describing concepts, thereby guiding the generation of diffusion
732   models. Textual Inversion Gal et al. (2022a) learns text embeddings corresponding to pseudo-words
733   to represent the target concepts. The pseudo-words can be combined with free text to guide the
734   generation of images containing target concepts. Based on their research, some works Daras &
735   Dimakis (2022); Voynov et al. (2023); Zhang et al. (2023c); Alaluf et al. (2023); Zhao et al. (2023)
736   explore the effects of different inversion spaces on prompt inversion. Other works Gal et al. (2023);
737
      Wei et al. (2023); Arar et al. (2023); Chen et al. (2023a); Ye et al. (2023); Li et al. (2023b) train
      an image encoder based on text inversion to achieve generation guided by a given reference image.
738
      Additionally, ReVersion Huang et al. (2023b) focuses on learning the relation between objects through
739
      contrastive learning. PEZ Wen et al. (2023) inverts hard prompts by projecting learned embeddings
740   onto adjacent interpretable word embeddings, providing a new solution for image captioning. Vinker
741   et al. (2023) decomposes a visual concept, allowing users to explore hidden sub-concepts of the
742   object of interest. Lego Motamed et al. (2023) uses carefully designed prompt learning methods to
743   learn abstract concepts that are entangled with the subject from few samples. These methods focus
744   on learning concepts to guide image generation, while our study aims to learn the transformations
745   between image pairs to guide image editing.

      Implementation Details. The implementation is based on one NVIDIA Tesla V100 GPU. We use
750   public vocabulary set pha (2022) to search unique phrases for instruction initialization. Afterward,
751   based on pre-trained IP2P Brooks et al. (2023), we optimize the features of the keys and values
752   corresponding to approximately 10 initialization instruction tokens. Note that our method is not
753   limited to IP2P and can also be applied to other instruction-based editing models Geng et al. (2023);
754   Zhang et al. (2023a); Sheynin et al. (2023). We divide the learned instructions into 5 parts according
755   to the denoising time step, and optimize each part with 1000 steps using a learning rate of 0.001 and
      a batch size of 1, respectively, for a total of 5000 steps. The whole training process takes about 20


                                                       14
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


756
      minutes. During both training and inference, we adopt a text guidance scale sT = 7.5 and an image
757   guidance scale sI = 1.5. And we use the Euler ancestral sampler with denoising variance schedule
758   Karras et al. (2022) with a sampling step of T = 20 during the inference process.

      Evaluaion Metrics. We use six objective evaluation metrics on the benchmark. Specifically, we
      employ full-reference quality metrics PSNR, SSIM Wang et al. (2004), and LPIPS Zhang et al. (2018)
761
      to assess the consistency between the generated images and the ground truth, quantifying the image
762
      editing capabilities of each method. Among them, higher PSNR indicates more similarity between
763   the results and the ground truth; higher SSIM indicates that the results are structurally more similar to
764   the ground truth; we implement the evaluation of LPIPS based on AlexNet Krizhevsky et al. (2012),
765   and smaller LPIPS indicates that the results has a better features similarity between the results and
766   the ground truth. We also use CLIP image similarity score and DINO score to assess the consistency
767   between the generated images and the ground truth. In addition, we measure the CLIP directional
768   similarity Gal et al. (2022b) between image pairs to evaluate the semantic alignment between the
769   editing direction of each method and the target. Specifically, we measure the consistency between the
770   average editing direction from the input images to the generated images and the average direction of
771
      the training image pairs. The CLIP image directional similarity Parmar et al. (2023); Nguyen et al.
      (2023); Gal et al. (2022b) is defined as follows:
772
773                                           1-\operatorname {cos}\left (\Delta _{x \rightarrow y}, \Delta _{x^{\prime } \rightarrow y^{\prime }}\right ),                   (8)
774
775
      where ∆x→y is the CLIP direction from the input image to the result image, and ∆x′ →y′ is the CLIP
      direction between the reference images.

      E     A PPLICATIONS
928
929
      Image Retouching. Image retouching is the process of changing or improving the quality of an
      image. This involves enhancing colors, removing imperfections, adjusting lighting, or making other
930
      edits to improve the overall appearance of an image. Implementing image retouching using the
931
      instruction-based image editing models is challenging because the vast majority of image retouching
932   transformations are difficult to describe using textual instructions. Our method helps in this task.
933   Given paired data before and after image retouching, our InstructBrush can extract editing instructions
934   representing this image transformation from the prior of generative model. The aligned instructions
935   obtained through this process facilitate training for downstream tasks. As shown in Figure 7, our
936   InstructBrush can extract image tones based on three image pairs that represent tonal transformations
937   from PPR10K Liang et al. (2021) and apply these transformations to new images.
938   Hybrid Instruction. Hybrid instruction allows the use of textual and visual instructions together
939   to guide the editing of an image. Adopting the approach mentioned in Nguyen et al. (2023), we
940   concatenate the embeddings representing visual and textual instructions for guided editing. For visual
941   instruction optimization, we disabled time-aware instruction optimization to achieve better hybrid
942   instruction results. As shown in Figure, our method can combine the visual instruction that represent
943   a particular style with different textual instructions in order to jointly guide the image editing. In
944   contrast, the visual instruction of Visii Nguyen et al. (2023) which represents style is forgotten in the
945   process of combining with textual instructions.

957   shows in local editing tasks, training images contain more editing-irrelevant scene information. The
958   content-oriented initialization of Visii introduces them to the initialized instructions, posing a greater
959   obstacle to optimization. Our method can accurately capture the transformations between image pairs
960   and use them for initialization, thus improving instruction generalization.

964   initialization design of our method. Additionally, our method demonstrates more consistent editing
