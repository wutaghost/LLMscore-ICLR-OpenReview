009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Continual Test-time Adaptation (TTA) addresses sequential out-of-distribution
012            scenarios with unlabeled data but overlooks long-term and recurring in-
013            distribution aspects of the real world. Therefore, we introduce Lifelong Con-
014            tinual Adaptation, which enables models to efficiently retrieve domain-specific
015            knowledge when encountering in-distribution data streams with sequential and
016            recurring domains. We found that optimization-based Continual TTA methods
017            underperform on the proposed problem due to two major pitfalls: updating the
018
               model’s parameters is expensive and impractical for resource-constrained devices,
               and these methods exhibit instability when adapting to long-term recurring do-
019
               mains. To address these challenges, we propose a diffusion-based prompt gen-
020
               eration method (DiffPrompt). Specifically, instead of continually optimizing the
021            foundation model, we generate domain-specific prompts for it to adapt. We use
022            a conditional diffusion model to learn a prompt-space distribution for various do-
023            mains. During testing, the diffusion model generates prompts for the current do-
024            main based on the incoming batch of data, facilitating the continual adaptation
025            of the foundation model. Our experiments demonstrate that DiffPrompt enables
026            stable and efficient deployment in practical scenarios involving sequential and re-
027            curring domains.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Domain shifts significantly degrade the performance of deep learning models due to the misalign-
033
      ment between the domains of the training and deployment phases (Quionero-Candela et al., 2009;
      Koh et al., 2021). This challenge is further compounded when models encounter sequential domain
034
      changes during deployment (Wang et al., 2022). For example, the driving conditions for autonomous
035
      vehicles are always evolving such as lighting conditions at various times of the day and varying
036   weather conditions. It causes different visual data, demanding the model to adapt wisely.
037
038   To address these challenges, a line of research known as continual Test-Time Adaptation (TTA)
039
      focuses on continually adapting machine learning models. They typically aim to address sequen-
      tial novel domains with unlabeled data examples encountered during deployment. The assumption
040
      is that both data instances and domains are previously unseen during training (Wang et al., 2022;
041
      Döbler et al., 2023; Yuan et al., 2023). However, the extant framework of continual TTA fails to
042   account for three critical real-world scenarios. First, they emphasize sequential out-of-distribution
043   (OOD) scenarios, where training and deployment domains differ, while overlooking the sequential
044   in-distribution (ID) data, resulting in an incomprehensive evaluation. Second, the scarcity of the
045   number of sequential domains limits the assessment of a model’s long-term adaptation capabilities.
046   Finally, the recurring nature of certain domains encountered repetitively during deployment is dis-
047   regarded (Hoang et al., 2023). Therefore, we introduce a novel problem setting, known as Lifelong
048   Continual Adaptation (LCA), that aims to foster model continual adaptation in more realistic scenar-
049   ios characterized by long-term sequential, recurring, and in-distribution data, described in Table 1
050
      and Figure 1. LCA explores how to efficiently retrieve and utilize knowledge of seen domains during
      deployment.
051
052   We conducted an analysis of multiple baseline methods on the proposed LCA setting, including the
053   non-adapted domain generalization approach, Empirical Risk Minimization (ERM) (Vapnik et al.,
      1998), and various continual TTA methods (Wang et al., 2022; Döbler et al., 2023; Yuan et al., 2023).

      In our findings, ERM achieves only moderate performance due to its lack of domain adaptation when
078
      encountering sequential domain shifts. Conversely, continual TTA approaches endeavor to adapt to
079   different sequential domains by optimizing model parameters using manual-crafted self-supervised
080   training objectives. However, these methods present two primary drawbacks. First, the optimization
081   of model parameters demands substantial computational and memory resources, rendering deploy-
082   ment on resource-constrained devices impractical (Bommasani et al., 2021). Even worse, the recent
083   trend of scaling laws in foundation models exacerbates the issue (Kaplan et al., 2020). Second, our
084   empirical results, as presented in Table 4, reveal that optimization-based continual TTA methods
085   exhibit significant instability, impeding their capability to adapt to long-term recurring domains.
086   Alternatively, as a paradigm of parameter-efficient fine-tuning, prompt tunning (Zhou et al., 2022b;
087   Jia et al., 2022) can guide foundation models to adapt to downstream tasks by optimizing only the
088   learnable prompt vectors prepended to the input space, while maintaining the rest of the model
089   frozen. Inspired by this paradigm, we propose a prompt generation framework that enables vision
090   foundation models, such as CLIP (Radford et al., 2021), to adapt to long-term sequential domains
091   under the resource-constrained scenario. Specifically, we introduce a diffusion model as a domain
092   prompt generator, directly sampling a domain prompt through an iterative denoising process con-
093
      ditioned on incoming batches of data examples from each specific domain. The training of the
      diffusion-based prompt generator is decomposed into two stages: prompt collection and diffusion
094
      training. The primary objective of the prompt collection stage is to encapsulate the prompt distri-
095
      bution of each training domain through a set of learnable prompt samples, wherein domain-specific
096   knowledge is presumed to be encoded within each domain’s prompt distribution. During the diffu-
097   sion training stage, we train a conditional latent diffusion model using the collected prompt samples
098   from each specific domain. This model aims to generate domain-specific prompts from Gaussian
099   noise, conditioned on mini-batch data from that domain. The principal advantage of employing a
100   diffusion-based generative approach to represent domain-specific knowledge lies in its robustness
101   compared to discriminative counterparts, as it learns the distribution of domain prompts rather than
102   relying on a single domain prompt. In addition, directly generating a domain prompt without prompt
103   tuning alleviates gradient backpropagation, leading to significant resource efficiency during adapta-
104
      tion.
105   Our work makes three contributions. First, we introduce a novel problem setting for continual do-
106   main adaptation, known as Lifelong Continual Adaptation, which emphasizes the evaluation of long-
107   term sequential, recurring, and in-distribution domains in real-world scenarios. Second, we propose
      a framework that formulates the continual adaptation process as generating a sequence of domain-

      specific prompts to guide the foundation model for domain adaptation, utilizing a diffusion-based
109   generative approach. Diffusion models have shown success in generating inputs (e.g. images (He

115   2     R ELATED WORK
116
117   Prompt tuning and adaptation Prompting (Petroni et al., 2019; Brown et al., 2020; Lester et al.,
118   2021; Li & Liang, 2021; Liu et al., 2021; Yao et al., 2023; Zhu et al., 2023) has emerged as a
119   crucial technique for enhancing the performance of pre-trained models in various downstream tasks.
120   Radford et al. (2021) introduces CLIP, a powerful vision-language model which uses textual prompts
121   to guide image classification. Following this, CoOp (Zhou et al., 2022b) proposes to adapt CLIP
122   by learning textual prompts on the text encoder. CoCoOp (Zhou et al., 2022a) extends CoOp by
123   conditionally tuning to improve performance. VPT (Jia et al., 2022) introduces fine-tuning prompts
124
      on Vision Transformers (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021) to adapt to downstream tasks.
125   Continual adaptation Some work (Hoffman et al., 2014; Wulfmeier et al., 2018; Volpi et al.,
126   2021; Liu et al., 2020; Kumar et al., 2020) considers an evolving domain adaptation where the target
127   domain evolves over time. A line of recent research known as continual Test-Time Adaptation
128
      (TTA) focuses on continually adapting a source model to target unseen sequential domains (Wang
      et al., 2022). These methods are mainly based on a teacher-student self-training framework and
129
      utilize source prototype pulling (Döbler et al., 2023) and resampling (Yuan et al., 2023) strategies to
130
      improve stability. The LCA setting in our work differs from continual TTA in its focus. Continual
131   TTA aims to prevent performance degradation across sequential domain shifts by learning from
132   unseen domains during test time. In contrast, LCA focuses on achieving stable and high performance
133   by retrieving knowledge for each encountered seen domain during deployment.
134   Continual learning Continual learning addresses catastrophic forgetting, the performance degra-
135   dation on old tasks when learning new ones (Wang et al., 2024b). Domain incremental learning, a
136   subset of continual learning, involves sequential domains and aims to balance performance across
137   old and new domains (Mirza et al., 2022; Shi & Wang, 2023). In contrast, LCA focuses on retrieving
138   and utilizing knowledge of an old domain during test time, without learning new domains.
139   Diffusion-based generation Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Models (DDPM) have garnered
140   significant attention for their ability to produce high-quality data through a process of iterative de-
141   noising (Ho et al., 2020; Luo, 2022; Rombach et al., 2022; Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021; Peebles & Xie,
142   2023; Croitoru et al., 2023). Several studies employ diffusion models for data augmentation (He
143   et al., 2023; Trabucco et al., 2024), and these models are also explored for classification tasks (Li
144   et al., 2023; Du et al., 2023; Prabhudesai et al., 2023). Furthermore, recent research investigates
145   the application of diffusion models for generating neural network weights (Erkoç et al., 2023; Nava
146
      et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024a). Some work also utilizes diffusion models to generate bounding
      boxes for object detection (Chen et al., 2023) and to enhance the quality of semantic segmenta-
147
      tion (Tan et al., 2022).

      3.3   F EATURE DISTRIBUTION AS CONDITION
271
272   The condition module is designed to perceive domains in order to provide conditions that are sensi-
273   tive to different domains. Inspired by discussions on the relationship between domains and feature
274   distribution in the field of domain adaptation, we recognize that domain shifts result in varied fea-
275   ture distributions extracted by discriminative models. A line of research focuses on overcoming this
276   issue by forcing models to extract similar feature distributions from images of different domains to
277   achieve domain adaptation (Ganin et al., 2016; Ganin & Lempitsky, 2015; Sun & Saenko, 2016). In
278
      this work, however, we exploit this characteristic within the condition module; in other words, we
      use feature distributions as conditions.
279
280   Concretely, we employ a pre-trained image encoder from CLIP in the condition module. It produces
281   features F from the incoming batch of images from streams. Afterward, we compute the distribution
282   statistics for the batch of features, adopting the mean and standard deviation as follows:

308   4.1   DATASETS
309
      We include two datasets in the experiments, DomainNet (Peng et al., 2019) and ImageNet-C (Croce
310
      et al., 2021), which are widely used in domain adaptation and test-time adaptation tasks.
311
312   DomainNet The dataset contains 6 domains: Clipart (clip), Infograph (info), Painting (paint),
313   Quickdraw (quick), Real (real), and Sketch (sketch). It has 345 classes and is naturally class-
314   imbalanced in each domain. We follow the official split to organize the training and test sets for
315   each domain.
316
      ImageNet-C This dataset is derived by applying various corruptions to the images in the valida-
317
      tion set of ImageNet. There are 4 categories of corruptions (weather, noise, blur, digital) aimed at
318   mimicking a range of natural environmental conditions that may be encountered during deployment.
319   Following the RobustBench benchmark (Croce et al., 2021), we adopt 15 corruption domains, in-
320   cluding brightness (bri), frosted glass (gla), JPEG compression (jpe), contrast (con), defocus blur
321   (def), impulse noise (imp), motion blur (mot), snow (sno), zoom blur (zoo), frost (fro), pixelation
322   (pix), gaussian noise (gau), elastic transformation (ela), shot noise (sho), and fog (fog). Similar
323   to the split of DomainNet, we adopt a 70%/30% split to obtain the training and test sets for each
      domain.

      4.2   E XPERIMENTAL SETUP
325
326   The experimental setting mimics a practical scenario where a model is deployed in data streams
327   featuring sequential and recurring domains. It has two primary conditions. Firstly, a sequence of
328   domains is presented in streams, which is the same setup as recent continual TTA works (Song
329   et al., 2023; Döbler et al., 2023), with associated experimental results detailed in Tables 2 and 3.
330   Secondly, the sequence of domains in the first condition recurs in streams. We set the number of
331   recurring times to 15, and the associated results are shown in Tables 4 and 5. For all methods, we
332
      use the same base model, which is the ‘ViT-B/16’ version of the CLIP model. All methods follow
      the paradigm of prompt tuning, where only the prompt is updated while CLIP’s model parameters
333
      remain frozen. The batch size is uniformly set to 64.

      4.3   BASELINES
336
337   We include 5 baseline methods in the comparative experiments: Zero-shot, Empirical Risk Mini-
338   mization (ERM), two fully test-time adaptation methods: TENT (Wang et al., 2021) and SAR (Niu
339   et al., 2023), and three recent continual test-time adaptation methods: RMT (Döbler et al., 2023),
340   CoTTA (Wang et al., 2022), and RoTTA (Yuan et al., 2023).
341
      Zero-shot Zero-shot means that we use the base model, a pre-trained CLIP model, to be directly
342
      evaluated on the test data streams. The prompt applied to the text encoder is the commonly used
343
      template ”a photo of a [CLASS]”.
344
      ERM In this baseline, the CLIP model is trained with the training sets of all domains. The textual
345   prompt is trainable while the CLIP model itself is frozen, as in CoOp (Zhou et al., 2022b). The
346   initialization of the prompt is the template ”a photo of a [CLASS]”. The training recipe is consistent
347   with that in the prompt collection stage of our method. After training, the model with the trained
348   prompt is evaluated on the test streams.
349   TENT TENT employs an entropy minimization loss to increase prediction confidence on test data,
350   enabling adaptation during test time.
351   SAR SAR applies entropy minimization loss to filtered, reliable samples and further reduces the
352   sharpness of the entropy.
353   CoTTA This method uses a teacher-student self-training framework, where the student model is
354   continually trained with the data in streams and pseudo labels from the teacher model. The teacher
355   model is updated by an exponential moving average of the student weights, and it produces the
356   pseudo labels with test-time augmented input data. In the continual test-time adaptation setting, the
357   method continually optimizes a source model in the streams of sequential target domains. In our
358
      experiments, for consistency with other baselines and our method, this method is evaluated starting
      from the ERM-trained model in streams.
359
      RMT The method adopts a similar teacher-student self-training framework as CoTTA while fur-
360
      ther introducing a contrastive learning method with computed source prototypes and a source replay
361
      strategy. In our experiments, we also use the ERM-trained model as the initialization for this method.
362   We use the training sets to compute the prototypes and conduct the source replay. Due to mem-
363   ory constraints, we do not include the evaluation of this method on the ImageNet-C dataset (1000
364   classes) because it needs to simultaneously keep two computational graphs for backpropagation for
365   two losses associated with two different inputs.
366   RoTTA RoTTA is also based on a teacher-student self-training framework while it introduces a
367   resampling approach to enhance stability and performance in online streams. We also evaluate the
368   method using the ERM-trained base model.

417
      Adaptation (TTA) methods, CoTTA and RoTTA retain stable performance, aligning with the ERM-

      Does the condition module perceive different domains? The condition module is designed to
      perceive different domains so that it can produce conditions from images to guide the diffusion

      tion module fed with test images from different domains. As shown in the t-SNE visualization in

464
      ditioned on the incoming batch of images are expected to be domain-specific and tailored to the
465
      current domain. To verify this, we conduct an inter-domain-condition experiment. In this experi-
466   ment, we test the generative prompts conditioned on images from one domain across the sequential
467   domain streams. As shown in Figure 3b, each colored line represents a different domain of images
468   that the prompt generation is conditioned on. The names surrounding the circles indicate the test do-
469   mains. It can be observed that, for each test domain, the generative prompts conditioned on images
470   from the same domain exhibit the best performance compared to those conditioned on images from
471   other domains. This demonstrates that the generative prompts are domain-specific and well-suited
472   for the encountered domain.

      5   L IMITATION AND DISCUSSION
522   In this work, the setting does not include the domain generalization problem, where a model learns to
523   generalize to an unseen domain from seen domains. The proposed prompt generation method learns
524   a prompt-space distribution from the training sets of seen domains, without the aim of generalizing
525   to the distribution of an unseen domain. Therefore, experiments show that generative prompts condi-
526   tioned on images of an unseen domain only result in moderate performance similar to the zero-shot
527   method. On the other hand, in Stable Diffusion (Rombach et al., 2022) for image generation, the
528
      diffusion model is trained with a wide range of conditions, while in our case, the model is trained
      with image conditions from only limited domains. This is a reason why this work does not primarily
529
      consider the generalization problem.
530
531
532   6   C ONCLUSION
533
      In this paper, we introduce Lifelong Continual Adaptation, which enables models to efficiently re-
534   trieve domain-specific knowledge when encountering sequential and recurring in-distribution data
535   streams. For this realistic setting, we propose a novel prompt generation method that leverages a
536   diffusion model to learn a prompt-space distribution for domains. During deployment, it generates
537   domain-specific prompts conditioned on incoming images to adapt foundation models. We demon-
538   strate that our generative prompts enhance model performance in practical data streams compared
539   to baselines. Future work could explore integrating more diverse conditions into the prompt-space
      diffusion model training to improve generalization across unseen domains.
