011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Recently, fine-tuning of language models (LMs) via zeroth-order (ZO) optimization
014            has gained significant traction due to their ability of memory-efficient deployment,
015            significantly reducing memory cost over first-order methods. However, the existing
016            studies on ZO optimization for LM fine-tuning often exhibit slow convergence and
017            reliance on the hand-crafted prompts. In this paper, we first investigate the impor-
018            tance of the adaptive gradient-based ZO optimization method in mitigating these
019            limitations. Toward this, we revisit memory-efficient zeroth-order Adam (MeZO-
020
               Adam) and make important findings that merely considering adaptivity can enable
               faster convergence while improving the generalization ability compared to previous
021
               studies. Interestingly, we further observe that decreasing the level of adaptivity
022
               might be recommended in ZO optimization potentially due to the high variance
023            of ZO gradient estimate, hypothesized as weak adaptivity hypothesis. Based upon
024            our hypothesis, we propose MeZO-A3 dam, MeZO-Adam with Adaptivity Ad-
025            justments according to the parameter dimension. We provide the dimension-free
026            theoretical guarantee on both the convergence and the generalization of MeZO-
027            A3 dam, providing strong evidence for our hypothesis. Extensive experiments show
028            that MeZO-A3 dam can achieve faster convergence and better generalization over
029            several baselines across LMs of various sizes on diverse datasets. By adaptivity
030            adjustments, MeZO-A3 dam outperforms MeZO, MeZO-SVRG, and MeZO-Adam,
031
               with up to an average of 36.6%, 16.9%, 6.8% improvements in performance and up
               to an average of ×12.6 and ×1.8 faster convergence, respectively. Furthermore, by
032
               leveraging an off-the-shelf low-bit optimizer, MeZO-A3 dam achieves an average
033
               of 40.3% and 43.6% memory reduction from MeZO-SVRG and MeZO-Adam.

      1    I NTRODUCTION

      Since the rise of large language models (LLMs), research has focused on leveraging their capabilities
      (Radford et al., 2019; Zhang et al., 2022; Touvron et al., 2023). Building on their success, fine-
040
      tuning pre-trained LLMs becomes a key strategy for adapting them to downstream tasks. However,
041
      it requires significant memory, making it impractical for practitioners. This challenge has led to
042   alternatives like in-context learning (ICL, Min et al. (2022)) and parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT,
043   Li & Liang (2021); Lester et al. (2021); Hu et al. (2022)). However, ICL often yields suboptimal
044   performance (Malladi et al., 2023) and requires time-consuming prompt engineering, introducing
045   memory overhead. While PEFT is more efficient than full fine-tuning (Full FT), it still requires more
046   memory than inference alone due to activation memory (Azizi et al., 2024) and one may increase the
047   number of trainable parameters to achieve satisfactory performance, potentially driving up resources.
048   As a consequence, a separate line of research, the zeroth-order (ZO) optimization recently have
049   been explored to fine-tune LLMs (Malladi et al., 2023; Guo et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024b; Zhang
050   et al., 2024). ZO optimization requires neither the memory nor the computation costs associated
051   with gradient calculations. Additionally, unlike ICL, it does not require longer context length. The
052   pioneering study, MeZO (Malladi et al., 2023) demonstrated that zeroth-order stochastic gradient
053   descent (SGD) (Robbins & Monro, 1951) can effectively fine-tune language models with only
      inference-time memory and computation budget.

066   However, while MeZO reduces memory consumption, it suffers from slower convergence and
067
      longer training time. Also, MeZO requires hand-crafted, task-specific prompts to achieve acceptable
      performance, which limits its broader applicability. While prompts are common in LLM fine-tuning
068
      (Wei et al., 2022; Sanh et al., 2022; Ouyang et al., 2022; Chung et al., 2024), their complexity
069
      diminishes the practicality of ZO optimization. To speed up convergence, sparse gradient methods
070   (Liu et al., 2024b; Guo et al., 2024) have been proposed, selectively updating a small subset of
071   parameters, though prompts are still required. More recently, MeZO-SVRG (Gautam et al., 2024)
072   integrates the stochastic variance-reduced gradient (SVRG) estimator for faster convergence without
073   prompts, however, it incurs high computational costs due to full-batch gradient calculations. In
074   addition, the variance-reduced method may not be effective for deep learning tasks (Defazio & Bottou,
075   2019). Furthermore, their accuracy gap compared to full FT still remains to be often significant.
076   All the aforementioned studies have focused on vanilla SGD while adaptive gradient methods such as
077   Adam (Kingma & Ba, 2015; Loshchilov & Hutter, 2019) or Lion (Chen et al., 2023) have become
078   standard in traditional fine-tuning of LLMs. In MeZO, only a brief introduction to MeZO-Adam
079   and simple experimental results in prompt-dependent settings have been presented, showing little
080   difference from MeZO. However, the full potential of adaptive gradients in more generalized ZO
081   fine-tuning scenarios is yet to be fully unveiled.
082   Toward mitigating the above limitations of MeZO, in this paper, we revisit the MeZO-Adam in LLM
083   fine-tuning. We first empirically discover that the adaptive gradients are advantageous in a prompt-
084   free fine-tuning scenario. More importantly, we observe that controlling the amount of adaptivity
085   of MeZO-Adam indeed matters, upon which we introduce weak adaptivity hypothesis and propose
086   MeZO-A3 dam that allows to adjust the adaptivity according to the parameter dimension.
087
      Our main contributions are summarized below:
088
089     • We thoroughly investigate on the adaptive gradient ZO optimization for fine-tuning and first
090       reveal its importance in a prompt-free fine-tuning scenario. Specifically, we make an important
091       observation that decreasing the adaptivity level can potentially be beneficial in ZO optimization.
092       This may be attributed to the high variance of ZO gradient estimate. We then articulate weak
093       adaptivity hypothesis and present MeZO-A3 dam. This allows the degree of adaptivity according
094       to the parameter dimension enabling faster convergence compared to non-adaptive alternatives.
095     • We theoretically analyze the convergence and the generalization of MeZO-A3 dam. While the
096       convergence complexity and the generalization error bound heavily depend on the parameter
097       dimension d in most ZO optimization analysis, we make an important theoretical observation
098       that decreasing the adaptivity level can allow us to obtain a dimension-free guarantee. This
099       provides strong evidence for the weak adaptivity hypothesis. Additionally, we provide theoretical
100       insights into how much the degree of adaptivity should be adjusted. Table 1 summarizes the
101       comparisons in theory among state-of-the-art memory-efficient zeroth-order methods.
102     • Our extensive experiments demonstrate that MeZO-A3 dam consistently surpasses several existing
103       ZO baselines across various sizes of language models on different benchmarks, in all three
104       aspects: generalization, convergence, and memory consumption. More precisely, MeZO-A3 dam
105       outperforms (1) {MeZO, MeZO-SVRG, MeZO-Adam} with up to an average of {36.6%, 16.9%,
106       6.8%} better generalization, (2) {MeZO-SVRG, MeZO-Adam} with an average of {×12.6,
107       ×1.8} faster convergence speed, and (3) {MeZO-SVRG, MeZO-Adam} with an average of
          {40.3%, 43.6%} lower memory consumption, respectively.

      3.2   M E ZO-A 3 DAM : M E ZO-A DAM WITH A DAPTIVITY A DJUSTMENTS UNDER W EAK
192
            A DAPTIVITY H YPOTHESIS
193
194   In this study, the adaptivity parameter δ plays a crucial role, which will be discussed in this section.
195   Note that it is known that stochastic ZO gradient exhibits high variance which comes from both (i)
196   high dimensionality of the parameter and (ii) minibatch sampling. Notably, sampling noise is also
197   inherent in first-order stochastic gradients; thus, the primary factor for the high variance of stochastic
198   ZO gradient results from high dimensionality. Based on this observation, in zeroth-order optimization,
199   we hypothesize that the base adaptivity parameter δ0 = 10−8 of Adam might encourage excessive
200
      adaptivity where the ZO gradient estimate already has a high variance, which can eventually hinder
      the optimization process. Under this intuition, we articulate the weak adaptivity hypothesis.
201
202   Weak Adaptivity Hypothesis. The adaptivity parameter of zeroth-order Adam should be scaled
203   according to the parameter dimension relative to the base adaptivity parameter of first-order Adam.
204   In other words, this hypothesis means that the small amount of adaptivity is enough in ZO optimization.
205   The hypothesis directly motivates our proposed algorithm, MeZO-A3 dam, which is MeZO-Adam
206   with Adaptivity Adjustments. In details, the line 9 with a non-trivial scaling function h in Alg. 1
207   characterizes the key feature of MeZO-A3 dam.
208
      As a proof-of-concept, we explore the optimization of training RoBERTa-Large (Liu, 2019) on
209
      SST-2, focusing on two main points: (i) whether using adaptive gradients in zeroth-order optimization
210   can outperform existing ZO methods such as MeZO or MeZO-SVRG; (ii) whether scaling up δ
211   in MeZO-Adam actually provides any benefits in terms of optimization. Figure 1 illustrates our
212   proof-of-concept results on the learning curves of both fine-tuning loss and validation accuracy. As
213   MeZO-Adam itself surpasses ZO baselines in terms of both convergence and generalization, the
214   advantages of using adaptive gradients can be clearly observed. More surprisingly, MeZO-A3 dam
215   which adjusts the adaptivity with δ = 10−2 shows faster convergence and achieves lower fine-tuning
      loss as well as better generalization than MeZO-Adam, which partially supports our hypothesis.

      6                                      C ONCLUSION
529
530   In this paper, we revisited MeZO-Adam and highlighted the advantage of using adaptive gradients in
531
      zeroth-order fine-tuning in a prompt-free scenario. Further, we provided an important observation
      that reducing the level of adaptivity of MeZO-Adam is highly recommended in the zeroth-order
532
      regime, which is hypothesized as weak adaptivity hypothesis. Given our hypothesis, we proposed
533
      a MeZO-A3 dam, which adjusts the adaptivity according to the parameter dimension. We analyzed
534   the convergence and generalization of MeZO-A3 dam, providing dimension-free guarantees and
535   presenting strong theoretical evidence for our weak adaptivity hypothesis. We also validated that
536   MeZO-A3 dam outperforms several existing ZO baselines in practice for fine-tuning various sizes
537   of language models on benchmark tasks across all three aspects: generalization, convergence, and
538   memory consumption, which empirically corroborated the weak adaptivity hypothesis. In future
539   work, we plan to investigate the zeroth-order adaptive gradient methods for more challenging loss
      landscape.

      A     R ELATED W ORK
706
      Fine-tuning Language Models.            One popular approach is parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT), where
707   only a small subset of parameters are optimized. Examples include Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) (Hu et al.,
708   2022), prefix-tuning (Li & Liang, 2021), and prompt-tuning (Lester et al., 2021). While PEFT reduces the
709   number of parameters involved in optimization, it still requires significant memory and computation due to the
710   need for backpropagation and the storage of intermediate activations, optimizer states, and gradients. Moreover,
      obtaining significant performance gains often necessitates expanding the number of parameters being fine-tuned.
711
712   Another method for adapting LLMs is in-context learning (ICL) (Min et al., 2022), which provides task-specific
713   instructions and examples to leverage LLMs’ inherent language understanding. While gradient-free, ICL faces
      challenges such as selecting proper instructions and examples, increased inference times due to long context
714
      lengths, and lower performance compared to fine-tuning.
715
716   Fine-tuning LMs with Zeroth-Order (ZO) Optimization.                 Zeroth-order optimization, long explored
717   in conventional machine learning (Spall, 1992; Ghadimi & Lan, 2013), was first applied to LLM fine-tuning by
718   MeZO (Malladi et al., 2023). This method reduces memory consumption by generating perturbations on-the-fly
719   through random seeds, eliminating the need to store large perturbation vectors. As a result, ZO optimization
      requires only inference-level resources. MeZO also provides theoretical guarantees, suggesting that scaling the
720
      learning rate by the problem dimension ensures dimension-free convergence. However, this leads to slower
721   convergence rates. Additionally, MeZO’s use of hand-crafted task-specific prompts introduces extra overhead,
722   limiting its general applicability.
723
      MeZO-SVRG (Gautam et al., 2024) improves zeroth-order optimization by incorporating the SVRG algorithm,
724   addressing the challenges of non-prompted fine-tuning through reduced gradient variance, which leads to
725   faster convergence and better performance. Similarly, SparseMeZO (Liu et al., 2024b) leverages sparsity by
726   updating only a subset of parameters for quicker convergence, while Fisher-informed sparsity (Guo et al., 2024)
727   selects parameters based on Fisher information for greater efficiency. Additionally, Zhang et al. (2024) offers a
      comprehensive benchmark of ZO optimization methods like SGD, SignSGD, and Adam across various models
728
      and tasks, with results suggesting that while Adam may not always outperform SGD, its success in LLM
729   fine-tuning merits further exploration.

744   and Word-in-Context (WiC) (Pilehvar & Camacho-Collados, 2019).
745   The datasets are sourced from the Huggingface datasets library. For each dataset, we randomly select 512
746   samples for training and 256 for validation, reporting validation accuracy since the test labels for GLUE and
747   SuperGLUE benchmarks are unavailable. This setup is identical to that used in MeZO-SVRG (Gautam et al.,
