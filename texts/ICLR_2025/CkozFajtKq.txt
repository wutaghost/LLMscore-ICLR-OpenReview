009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            We introduce L I F LOW, a generative framework to accelerate molecular dynamics
012            (MD) simulations for crystalline materials that formulates the task as conditional
013            generation of atomic displacements. The model uses flow matching, with a Prop-
014            agator submodel to generate atomic displacements and a Corrector to locally
015            correct unphysical geometries, and incorporates an adaptive prior based on the
016
               Maxwell–Boltzmann distribution to account for chemical and thermal conditions.
               We benchmark L I F LOW on a dataset comprising 25-ps trajectories of lithium dif-
017
               fusion across 4,186 solid-state electrolyte (SSE) candidates at four temperatures.
018
               The model obtains a consistent Spearman rank correlation of 0.7–0.8 for lithium
019            mean squared displacement (MSD) predictions on unseen compositions. Further-
020            more, L I F LOW generalizes from short training trajectories to larger supercells
021            and longer simulations while maintaining high accuracy. With speed-ups of up to
022            600,000× compared to first-principles methods, L I F LOW enables scalable simu-
023            lations at significantly larger length and time scales.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
      Atomic transport is a fundamental process that governs the performance of materials in various tech-
028
      nologies, including energy storage, catalysis, and electronic devices (Balluffi et al., 2005; Yip, 2023).
029
      Solid-state electrolytes (SSEs) are a prime example, emerging as a safer and more stable alternative
030   to liquid electrolytes commonly used in lithium-ion batteries (Bachman et al., 2016). The study and
031   design of SSEs rely on fast and accurate atomistic simulation techniques to model the intricate ionic
032   diffusion behaviors that dictate the atomic transport in these materials. The standard method, ab
033   initio molecular dynamics (AIMD), involves costly density functional theory (DFT) calculations for
034   each propagation step in the scale of femtoseconds. Hence, their application is limited to small spa-
035   tiotemporal scales and a few simulations, often insufficient for characterizing diffusive dynamics or
036   screening candidate materials. Recently, universal machine learning interatomic potentials (MLIPs),
037   trained on large-scale DFT calculations, have emerged as a promising alternative (Friederich et al.,
038
      2021; Ko & Ong, 2023). However, even with MLIPs, dynamics must be discretized in sufficiently
      small time steps to ensure stable and accurate propagation (Fu et al., 2023a) and are still too slow to
039
      enable scalable simulation to perform high-throughput screening from large material databases.
040
041   To accelerate MD simulations for small bio/organic molecules, methods such as Timewarp (Klein
042   et al., 2023a), Implicit Transfer Operator Learning (ITO, Schreiner et al. (2023)), Score Dynamics
043   (SD, Hsu et al. (2024)), and Force-Guided Bridge Matching (FBM, Yu et al. (2024)) have been
044
      proposed. These methods leverage a generative model to propagate the conformational distribution
      from time τ to time τ + ∆τ , where ∆τ is much larger than the typical MD time steps. A similar
045
      approach has been applied to coarse-grained polymer electrolyte simulations in Fu et al. (2023b).
046
047   In the context of all-atom simulations of crystalline materials across different temperatures, these
048   methods do not account for symmetries or handle various atom types under various simulation
049   conditions. This work aims to address this by developing a tailored, flow-matching-based, gen-
050
      erative acceleration framework designed for scalable and cost-effective simulations of crystalline
      materials and diffusive dynamics in SSEs. The key objective is to construct a model capable of
051
      accurately reproducing relevant kinetic observables, such as mean squared displacement (MSD) and
052
      self-diffusivity of mobile ions, in comparison to long MD simulations using MLIPs or AIMD.
053
      Our contributions are as follows:

065   2.1    P RELIMINARIES
066
067   Crystalline materials and representation The crystal structure, assuming perfect order with
068
      translational symmetry, can be idealized as an infinite repetition of atoms, each assigned an atom
      type from the periodic table A, within a unit cell with periodic boundary conditions (Ashcroft &
069
      Mermin, 1976). In this work, the structure of a material with n atoms in the unit cell is represented by
070
      the tuple M = (X, L, a), where X = (x1 , x2 , · · · , xn )⊤ ∈ Rn×3 denotes the Cartesian coordi-
071   nates of the atoms, L = (l1 , l2 , l3 )⊤ ∈ R3×3 with rows defining the basis vectors of a 3-D repeating
072   unit cell, and a ∈ An is the atom types. We impose a graph structure on the material by connecting
073   pairs of nearby atoms with edges (Schütt et al., 2017), possibly across unit cell boundaries. An edge
074   ((i, j), k) ∈ J1, nK2 × Z3 is formed between atoms i and j if the distance between atom i and atom
075   j, displaced by k unit cells from i, is smaller than the cutoff, i.e., ∥xj + kL − xi ∥2 < rcutoff . An
076   a × b × c supercell of M is defined as
077
                         (X ′ , L′ , a′ ) = (⊕abc                                   abc
                                              κ=1 (X + 1n ⊗ kκ ), L diag(a, b, c), ⊕κ=1 a),                     (1)
078
079   where ⊕ denotes concatenation, ⊗ is the outer product, and kκ ∈ Za × Zb × Zc represents the
080   index of unit cell repetitions. Although the method is designed for general crystalline materials, the
081   primary application of this work is on lithium SSEs, which are further discussed in Appendix A.1.
082
083   Molecular dynamics for materials MD is a simulation methodology used to sample ensembles
084   of configurations or trajectories of atomistic systems by solving the equation of motion, M Ẍτ =
085   −∇U (Xτ ), over time τ ,1 where M = diag(m) is the diagonal matrix of atomic masses, m =
086
      m(a), and U (X) is the potential energy (Frenkel & Smit, 2023). Given the initial position X0
087   and the velocity Ẋ0 , typically sampled from the Maxwell–Boltzmann distribution, the equation of
088
      motion is usually discretized and propagated with a time step of 0.5–2 fs, depending on the fastest
      motion of the system. For organic and biomolecules with a limited number of atom types, classical
089
      force fields with specified functional forms are routinely used to approximate the system energy
090
      with reasonable accuracy. However, for inorganic solid systems containing various elements across
091   the periodic table, the parametrization of classical force fields is challenging, and U (·) is often
092   derived from quantum mechanical calculations (ab initio MD), which are computationally much
093   more expensive and scale poorly (O(n3 ) in theory). For more details, refer to Appendix A.2.
094
095   Machine learning interatomic potentials Due to the high computational cost of ab initio cal-
096   culations, machine learning interatomic potentials (MLIPs) based on graph neural networks have
097   been developed to approximate the results of the quantum calculations (Friederich et al., 2021; Ko
098   & Ong, 2023). Recent advances in universal MLIPs, such as MACE-MP-0 (Batatia et al., 2024)
099   and CHGNet (Deng et al., 2023), enable faster simulations and linear scaling with respect to num-
100
      ber of atoms, but pre-trained models often tend to overestimate the kinetic properties (Deng et al.,
      2024). We employed MLIPs to generate our trajectory dataset of lithium-containing materials to
101
      demonstrate chemical transferability across different materials.

115   2.2    R ELATED W ORKS
116
117   ML surrogates for dynamics simulation Several works have explored ML surrogates for time-
118
      coarsened dynamics by learning transition probability densities. Timewarp (Klein et al., 2023a)
      employs a conditional normalizing flow (CNF) with Markov chain Monte Carlo sampling, while ITO
119
      (Schreiner et al., 2023) is a conditional diffusion model designed as an arbitrary time-lag propagator.
120
      Arts et al. (2023) models coarse-grained (CG) dynamics with diffusion models, SD (Hsu et al.,
121   2024) learns the score function of the transition density, F3 low (Li et al., 2024) models protein CG
122   frame transitions with flow matching, and FBM (Yu et al., 2024) uses a conditional bridge process
123   with a correction mechanism based on intermediate force fields. These methods are applied to
124   biomolecular simulations, with less chemical diversity and different symmetry requirements and
125   task formulations from our work. Notably, Fu et al. (2023b) targets non-Markovian dynamics in CG
126   polymer materials by learning the acceleration and using a score-based corrector. While CG allows
127   for the explicit modeling of dynamics over longer timesteps using the equations of motion, our
128   task requires all-atom modeling, necessitating a generative surrogate for the dynamics. Moreover,
129
      none of these approaches considered a task-specific, physically motivated adaptive prior, which was
      crucial to the improved performance in this work.
130
131
      Generative models for materials As a time-hopping conditional generative model for material
132
      structures, our approach shares design principles with crystal generation models (Xie et al., 2022;
133
      Jiao et al., 2023; AI4Science et al., 2023; Zeni et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2024; Miller et al., 2024),
134   which use diffusion or flow matching to generate atomic identities and positions within a unit cell.
135   While these methods often handle position generation as fractional coordinates with periodic bound-
136   aries, our task requires modeling displacements in Cartesian coordinates directly without wrapping
137   positions back into the unit cell (see Appendix A.3).
138
139   Prior design While the normal distribution is commonly used in diffusion and flow-based genera-
140   tive models, incorporating task-specific inductive biases into the prior can improve the performance.
141   Lee et al. (2022) introduced data-dependent priors in diffusion models, Guan et al. (2023) used de-
142   composed priors for ligand generation, Jing et al. (2023) applied harmonic priors for protein struc-
143   ture, and Irwin et al. (2024) employed scale-based priors for molecular conformation. The common
144
      goal in these methods is to reduce the transport cost by initializing the prior closer to the data distri-
      bution. We use a physically motivated prior based on the Maxwell–Boltzmann distribution, which
145
      additionally accounts for differences between atom types and reflects thermal and phase conditions.

167   3.1     P ROBLEM S ETTING
168
169   Similar to the ML-based MD acceleration methods in Section 2.2, our goal is to model the transition
170   probability density of a material structure over a time interval ∆τ , conditioned on the temperature T :
171   p(Mτ +∆τ |Mτ , T ). For this task, we fix the lattice L (constant volume) and atom types a, and set
172
      ∆τ as 1 ps, which is 1,000 times larger than the usual MD time step of 1 fs (Marx & Hutter, 2009).
      In MD simulations used to model the kinetics of materials, unwrapped coordinates are utilized,
173
      meaning atomic coordinates are not confined to the unit cell, in order to keep track of the atomic
174
      displacements (von Bülow et al., 2020). As a result, unlike previous ML surrogates for dynamics
175   of bio/organic molecules (Section 2.2) with a single connected component with the fixed center of
176   mass, the distribution of positions does not have a finite support. Therefore, we opt to model the
177   distribution of displacements over time interval ∆τ , D∆τ := Xτ +∆τ − Xτ . In summary,
178
179     Task: learn the conditional distribution of atomic displacements p(D∆τ |Xτ , L, a, T ) from a
180     dataset of time-separated pairs of structures D = {((Xτ , Xτ +∆τ ), L, a, T ))}, extracted from
181     MD trajectories across various material compositions and temperatures.
182
183   More details and rationale on the task design choices can be found in Appendix A.3.

239   between the prior sample and the data sample as a conditional flow:

      This satisfies the symmetry constraints in Eqs. (8) and (9). The marginal flow approximator
244   vt (Dt , Xτ , L, a, T ; θ) should also respect these symmetry constraints. We adopt the PAI NN model
245   (Schütt et al., 2021) to balance expressiveness with inference speed. PAI NN is an equivariant graph
246   neural network that outputs scalar and vector quantities based on the atomistic graph, incorporating
247   scalar and vector node features. The structure is encoded using a radial basis function expansion of
248   atomic distances and the unit vector directions along edges (Eqs. (26a) and (27a)). We observed that
249   encoding the intermediate structure Xτ + Dt significantly improves prediction performance (Ta-
250   ble A1). Thus, we modify the message-passing layers of PAI NN to accept two structural inputs: Xτ
251   and Xτ +Dt . Additionally, the intermediate displacements Dt are used to construct the vector node

      features. Further details on the model architecture and modifications are given in Appendix D.2.

254   Propagator and Corrector models While, in theory, a single generative model should suffice to
255   learn the density, prediction errors would arise from two sources: inaccuracies in the marginal flow
256
      prediction and discretization errors in the flow integration. Moreover, since the trajectory generation
      is performed autoregressively, applying the generative model iteratively compounds these errors
257
      over time. To address this, in addition to the flow matching model described earlier (Propagator),
258
      we introduce an auxiliary flow matching model named Corrector, inspired by Fu et al. (2023b), to
259   rectify potential errors in the predicted displacements.
260
261   Although the Corrector model is intended to correct errors in the final displacement resulting from
262
      the integration of Propagator, directly mapping the generated output to an actual data sample to
      compute the target correction value can be complex, as it may require differentiating through the flow
263
      integration. Therefore, we decouple the Propagator and Corrector models, training the Corrector to
264
      denoise positional noise of arbitrary small scale. Given a perturbed configuration X̃τ = Xτ + D,
265
      where the noise displacement is sampled from D|σ ′ ∼ N (D; 0, diag(σ ′ )2 ⊗ I3 ) with the noise
266   scale σ ′ ∼ U(σ ′ ; 0, σmax 1n ), the flow is trained to generate the possible denoising displacements
267
      −D conditioned on X̃τ .

      4.1   DATASETS AND M ETRICS
303
304   Universal MLIP dataset (Section 4.2) To train a compositionally transferable generative model
305   for time-shifting conformational distributions, long-time simulation trajectories that span a diverse
306   range of compositional spaces in solid-state materials are required. A total of 4,186 stable lithium-
307   containing structures were retrieved from the Materials Project database (Jain et al., 2013) to capture
308   various modes of lithium-ion dynamics across different compositions. For each structure, 25 ps MD
309   simulations were performed using the MACE-MP-0 small universal MLIP model (Batatia et al.,
310   2024) at temperatures of 600, 800, 1000, and 1200 K, with a time step of 1 fs (25k steps per struc-
311   ture). The distribution of elements in these structures, shown in Fig. A1a, spans 77 elements across
312
      the periodic table. The mean squared displacement (MSD) of lithium atoms for each structure over
      the 25 ps trajectories is shown in Fig. A1b, indicating that the dataset captures a broad range of
313
      atomic environments and dynamic behaviors. The dataset is divided into training (90%) and test
314
      (10%) sets based on material composition, with the validation set sampled from the training portion.
315   Details of the simulations and dataset statistics are provided in Appendix C.1.
316
317
      AIMD datasets (Section 4.3) To evaluate the ability to extend accurate atomistic dynamics from
318   short AIMD simulations, we employed two sets of AIMD trajectories that exhibit diffusive lithium
319   dynamics. The first set includes LPS (Li3 PS4 ) simulations from Jun et al. (2024b), with ∼250 ps
320   trajectories for 128-atom structures of α-, β-, and γ-LPS, conducted at 600–800 K. The second set
321   comprises LGPS (Li10 GeP2 S12 ) simulations from López et al. (2024), which includes ∼150 ps MD
322   trajectories for a 2 × 2 × 1 supercell (200 atoms) of LGPS at temperatures of 650, 900, 1150, and
323   1400 K. We used the first 25 ps of each trajectory as the training set. Refer to Appendix C.2 for the
      rationale behind system selection and further details.

508   5     D ISCUSSION
509
510   Conclusion and outlook We proposed the L I F LOW model, a generative acceleration framework
511   designed to accelerate MD simulations for crystalline materials, with a focus on lithium SSEs. The
512   model consists of two key components: a Propagator, which generates atomic displacements for
513   time propagation, and a Corrector, which applies denoising. Both components utilize a conditional
514   flow matching scheme, and we introduced a thermally and chemically adaptive prior based on the
515
      Maxwell–Boltzmann distribution and modified the PAI NN model as a marginal flow approximator,
      both of which were critical for the accurate reproduction of dynamics. In our analysis of lithium-
516
      containing material trajectories, we consistently observed a Spearman rank correlation of 0.7–0.8
517
      for lithium MSD in unseen compositions. This indicates the potential of the L I F LOW model for
518   computational screening to identify materials with high lithium diffusivity. Furthermore, we demon-
519   strated the ability to extend short-length accurate AIMD trajectories by training the L I F LOW model.
520   This allowed us to infill insufficient observations, reproduce accurate temperature dependencies, and
521   maintain high accuracy when scaling up to much larger supercells. Compared to simulations using
522   MLIPs and AIMD, L I F LOW offers significant speedups of 400× and 600,000×, respectively. This
523   provides a practical means of scaling MD simulations to larger spatiotemporal domains.
524
525   Limitations and future directions First, although we have demonstrated the importance of de-
526   signing the prior for the flow matching process, determining the appropriate prior scale remains
527   a hyperparameter. A theoretical analysis of the optimal prior distribution would provide a more
528   principled approach to designing priors tailored to specific acceleration tasks and material systems.
529   This also applies to the choice of time step ∆t: we used a fixed time step based on observation
530   (Appendix A.3), but given the site-to-site hopping nature of atomistic transport, our method may
531
      benefit from adaptive or controllable time stepping (e.g., Schreiner et al. (2023)). Additionally,
      while L I F LOW performs well within the trained temperature range, it struggles to extrapolate be-
532
      yond the training regime, where system dynamics may differ significantly from the training data. As
533
      a result, the current approach lacks the broad generalizability seen in universal MLIP models, which
534   preserve the physical dynamics of systems while approximating the potential energy landscape. To
535   improve reliability and develop a model capable of capturing emergent system behaviors, genera-
536   tive approaches would benefit from incorporating thermodynamic principles more explicitly (Tiwary
537   et al., 2024; Dibak et al., 2022; Herron et al., 2023). Lastly, the accuracy of L I F LOW is inherently
538   limited by the accuracy of the reference dynamics. Given the variety of MD simulation methods and
539   their trade-offs between accuracy and speed, transfer learning or multi-fidelity frameworks could be
      considered for efficient training in practical applications.

      This work raises ethical considerations related to the general use of machine learning in scientific
548

549   is intended to accelerate dynamics simulations for materials science, there is a potential for misuse
550   in harmful applications, such as the development of dangerous materials or chemicals. Although un-
551   likely in the current form of our methodology, we acknowledge the following potential scenarios for
552   misuse, as ML-driven simulations could be misused to design materials with undesirable properties,
553   such as highly reactive compounds that may be hazardous to health or the environment:
554
555     • Environmentally harmful materials: Simulations could lead to the creation of materials that,
556
          when manufactured or disposed of, could pose long-term environmental risks, such as non-
          biodegradable or highly polluting compounds.

558     • Unstable materials: Inaccurate predictions or malicious use of this framework could result
559       in the generation of materials with undesirable or unstable properties, such as those prone to
560
          explosive reactions or dangerous degradation.
561     • Chemical weapons: Simulations may be applied to develop advanced nanomaterials with toxi-
562       cological risks or harmful capabilities, including those used in biological or chemical warfare.

      A     A DDITIONAL BACKGROUND
865
866
      A.1   S OLID -S TATE E LECTROLYTES
867
868   While lithium-ion diffusion in most solid-state materials is generally slow, solid-state electrolytes
869   are a special class of materials in which lithium ions can undergo fast diffusion, often referred to as
870   superionic conductors (Manthiram et al., 2017; Jun et al., 2024a). They serve as a key component for
871   all-solid-state batteries, where enhanced safety is anticipated by replacing flammable organic liquid
872   electrolytes with solid-state alternatives. A major class of solid electrolytes discussed in this work
873
      consists of inorganic crystalline materials, which have long-range atomic ordering. In most of these
      inorganic crystalline solid electrolytes, the anions (Wang et al., 2015) and the non-lithium cations
874
      (Jun et al., 2022) remain immobile, selectively permitting the translational motion of the lithium
875
      ions. This allows lithium ions to percolate through the crystal structure with a flat energy landscape
876   (or low migration barrier), resulting in a high diffusion coefficient and ionic conductivity.
877
878
      A.2   AIMD S IMULATION
879
880   Ab initio MD AIMD simulations are typically carried out using the Born–Oppenheimer molecular
881   dynamics (BOMD) approach (Marx & Hutter, 2009), as defined with the following equation of
882   motion (Eq. (15)) and the time-independent Schrödinger equation (Eq. (16)):
883
                                      M Ẍ = −∇X min ⟨Ψ0 |H(X)|Ψ0 ⟩ ,                                   (15)
884                                                    Ψ0
885                                  E0 Ψ0 = H(X)Ψ0 ,                                                   (16)
886
887   where H(X) represents the electronic Hamiltonian, Ψ0 is the ground state wavefunction, and
888
      ⟨Ψ0 |H(X)|Ψ0 ⟩ denotes the quantum mechanical expectation value. At each time step, the ground
      state wavefunction is obtained by solving Eq. (16), after which the forces are computed as a deriva-
889
      tive of the ground state energy with respect to atomic coordinates (Eq. (15)).
890
891   For crystalline materials, Eq. (16) is typically solved using Kohn–Sham density functional theory
892   (DFT, Kohn & Sham (1965)), where the ground state energy is expressed as
893
                                   min ⟨Ψ0 |H(X)|Ψ0 ⟩ = min E KS [{ϕi }; X]                             (17)
894                                 Ψ0                       {ϕi }
895
      with E KS being the Kohn–Sham energy functional and {ϕi } denoting a set of auxiliary functions
896
      called Kohn–Sham orbitals, which depend on the electron positions. The functional optimization in
897   Eq. (17) is equivalent to solving the set of coupled Kohn–Sham equations H KS ϕi = ϵi ϕi , where H KS
898   is the one-particle Hamiltonian and {ϵi } are the Kohn–Sham eigenvalues. Solving these eigenvalue
899   equations, which involves diagonalizing the Hamiltonian, scales as O(n3 ) with respect to the system
900   size n, defined by the number of atoms (as in the main text) or electrons.
901
902   Thermostat choice To maintain a constant temperature T in the system, the dynamics is cou-
903   pled with an external control mechanism known as a thermostat. We used Nosé–Hoover thermostat
904   (Nosé, 1984; Hoover, 1985) for dataset generation. To avoid thermostat-dependent dynamical arti-
905   facts, velocity scaling thermostats (e.g., Berendsen, Nosé–Hoover, and stochastic velocity rescaling)
906   should be used instead of velocity randomization thermostats (e.g., Langevin and Andersen). The
907
      latter may lead to reduced diffusivity values due to rapid decorrelation of velocities (Basconi &
      Shirts, 2013).

      A.3   TASK D ESIGN
911
      Fixing the volume In AIMD simulations for solid electrolytes, or in general when modeling the
912   transport properties of atomistic systems, simulations are typically conducted under the NVT (con-
913   stant volume) ensemble. Although real materials are often under constant pressure conditions, em-
914   ploying a barostat in simulations to control pressure modifies cell volume, potentially leading to
915   significant changes in particle positions and dynamics (Maginn et al., 2018). In practice, AIMD
916   simulations are initiated after energy minimization of the material structure (with respect to both
917   atomic coordinates and cell dimensions) under the assumption that thermal expansion of the cell
      does not significantly affect the transport properties.


                                                        17
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


918
      Unwrapped coordinates In atomistic systems with periodic boundary conditions (PBCs), parti-
919   cles that exit one side of the simulation box effectively reenter from the opposite side. A straightfor-
920   ward way to handle this is to use wrapped coordinates, where the positions are continuously confined
921   within the simulation box. However, this introduces jumps in atomic positions during long-range
922   motions, which can distort the calculation of kinetic properties such as MSD and diffusivity. To
923   avoid this, the coordinates must be unwrapped before computing such properties. Alternatively,
924   particle positions can be propagated using unwrapped coordinates from the start, without wrapping
925   them back when crossing the cell boundaries.
926   It is possible to unwrap trajectories during the post-processing of AIMD simulations, assuming that
927   no particles move more than half the cell dimensions between time steps. This condition generally
928   holds for typical AIMD simulations, which use small time steps. However, in the case of L I F LOW
929   modeling in this work, particle displacements can exceed half the box size because (1) we simu-
930   late with a much larger time step ∆τ , and (2) AIMD simulation cells are typically small due to
931   high computational costs (see Appendix A.2). Hence, we use unwrapped coordinates directly when
932   formulating the displacement modeling task for L I F LOW.
933
934   Choice of ∆τ Since the goal of generative displacement modeling in this work is to efficiently
935   accelerate MD simulations, the propagation time step ∆τ must be significantly larger than the MD
936   time step δτ . However, due to the high cost of generating data, ∆τ should not be so large that the
937   modes of atomic displacements are not adequately covered by the training set trajectories.
938
      To determine ∆τ , we consider the time evolution of lithium MSD for typical lithium-ion solid-state
939
      electrolytes. For small ∆τ values (< 0.1 ps), the MSD grows approximately as MSD ∝ ∆τ 1.42 ,
940   reflecting the ballistic and vibrational motion of lithium ions (He et al., 2018). In this regime, the
941   benefit of generative modeling is limited, as the evolution of atomic positions is closely related to
942   the initial velocities. For larger ∆τ (≳ 1 ps), the MSD grows linearly as MSD ∝ ∆τ , indicating the
943   onset of diffusive motion, as described by Eq. (13). Given that our training trajectories span 25 ps,
944   we select ∆τ = 1 ps to ensure that the generative model captures a diverse range of displacement
945   modes present in the training data.

1109   D.2    F LOW M ODEL A RCHITECTURE
1110
       We adapt the PAI NN model (Schütt et al., 2021) to parametrize the marginal flow approxima-
1111   tor vθ (Dt |Xτ , L, a, T ) for both the Propagator and Corrector. Schreiner et al. (2023) em-
1112   ployed a modified version of the PAI NN model, named C HIRO PAI NN, for a similar task for small
1113   biomolecules, introducing cross products during message passing in order to break reflection sym-
1114   metry. Their modification was necessary due to their use of coarse-grained protein representation
1115   (Cα coordinates), where the mirror image of a Cα trace does not correspond to the mirror image of
1116   the full-atom structure. In contrast, we represent the material structure using all atomic coordinates
1117   without coarse-graining, preserving the reflection symmetry of the atomistic system. As a result, we
1118   chose to modify the original PAI NN architecture instead of C HIRO PAI NN.
1119
1120   Node input features The model employs a learnable atomic embedding function, fatom : A →
1121   Rdf , to map atomic species to feature vectors, where df is the feature dimension. For continuous
1122   values, the embedding function fcont : R → Rdf /2 is defined using a sinusoidal encoding:

1127   and fixed during training. The invariant node embedding for atom j is computed as fatom (aj ) +
1128   (fcont (T /1000) ⊕ fcont (t)), for temperature T and flow matching time t. Rather than initializing
1129   equivariant node features to zeros as in the original model, they are initialized from the current step
