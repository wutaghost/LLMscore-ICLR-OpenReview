009                                                      A BSTRACT
010
011                In deep learning for data release scenarios, it is crucial to focus on data privacy
012                protection. Dataset distillation has demonstrated potential in defending against
013                membership inference attacks while maintaining training efficiency. However, this
014                study first identifies that data generated by state-of-the-art dataset distillation meth-
015                ods strongly resembles to initial private data, indicating severe privacy leakage.
016                We define this phenomenon as explicit privacy leakage. We theoretically ana-
017                lyze that distilled datasets with a high IPC1 can weaken both the defense against
018                membership inference attacks and explicit privacy. To address this, we propose a
019
                   plug-and-play module, Kaleidoscopic Transformation (KT), designed to introduce
                   enhanced strong perturbations to the selected real data during the initialization
020
                   phase while preserving semantic information. Extensive experiments demonstrate
021
                   that our method ensures both defense against membership inference attacks and
022                explicit privacy, while preserving the generalization performance of the distilled
023                data. Our code will be publicly available.

      The primary purpose of dataset distillation is to condense large datasets into small generated datasets,
073
      where models trained on the distilled data perform comparably to those trained on the original dataset,
074
      thereby enhancing training efficiency (Wang et al., 2018; Zhao et al., 2020). Consequently, releasing
075   distilled datasets can enable more people to efficiently train models. Although the distilled data
076   has been verified to adhere to differential privacy properties (Dong et al., 2022), the privacy risks
077   associated with directly releasing data differ from those encountered in the model-release scenario,
078   as illustrated in Figure 1 . In the model-release scenario, attackers can only access the model to
079   carry out membership inference attacks (Shokri et al., 2017). In contrast, in the data-release scenario,
080   attackers have direct access to the data itself, allowing them to perform visualizations or train models.
081   If the data is not adequately protected, this could lead to privacy leakage.
082   As the field of dataset distillation advances, the distilled data generated by the state-of-the-art dataset
083   distillation method e.g. DATM (Guo et al., 2024) strongly resemble to the private data, particularly
084   with high IPC (e.g., IPC = 50), as visualized in Figure 2 (a), suggesting severely privacy leakage.
085   We define the phenomenon as explicit privacy leakage, characterized by a strong visual similarity
086   between distilled and private images. Furthermore, with a higher IPC, the distilled dataset imposes
087   less stringent privacy restrictions on individual data points, making it more vulnerable to membership
088   inference attacks, as depicted by the solid green line in Figure 2 (a). Consequently, reducing the
089   IPC is necessary to enhance explicit and the defense against membership inference attacks, which
090   inevitably decreases the model performance, as shown by the purple bar in Figure 2 (a).
091   In this study, we aim to ensure both explicit privacy and resistance to membership inference attacks
092   while maintaining the performance of the distilled dataset. We begin to analyze the sources of privacy
093   leakage in dataset distillation by focusing on two phases: initialization and matching optimization.
094   As demonstrated in Section 3.2 , this leakage arises from the common practice of initializing distilled
095   images as real data, a method known for its potential to enhance effectiveness (Dong et al., 2022; Yu
096   et al., 2024). Consequently, we propose a plug-and-play method—Kaleidoscopic Transformation
097
      (KT)—aiming at protecting the privacy of selected real data at the initialization phase. KT implements
      enhanced perturbations on these samples without engaging with the distillation process, thereby being
098
      integrated with existing state-of-the-art dataset distillation methods, as illustrated in Figure 2 (b). As
099
      a plug-and-play module, with IPC increases, KT ensures both resistance to membership inference
100   attacks (dashed green line) and explicit privacy (dashed red line), as depicted in Figure 2 (a).
101
102   In summary, our contribution is threefold:
103   (a) To the best of our knowledge, this work is the first to explore the explicit privacy of the distilled
104       data. We reveal that when IPC is high, the distilled images strongly resemble to the initial private
105       images, indicating a significant explicit privacy leakage.
106   (b) Through theoretical analysis of multiple phases in dataset distillation, we identify that random
107       training sample initialization is the root cause of explicit privacy leakage, as subsequent matching
          perturbations provide insufficient protection.

109       (KT), to implement enhanced perturbations to the selected real data at the initialization phase.
110       Extensive experiments show that KT effectively protects explicit privacy and defends against
111       membership inference attacks while maintaining generalization performance.

      2     R ELATED W ORK
115
116
      The scenario of data-release introduces new privacy risks compared to model-release scenarios. In
      traditional machine learning, published models are often targeted by privacy attacks. Common
117
      defenses include differential privacy (Dwork et al., 2006). However, in data-release scenarios, we
118
      must ensure the published data itself is privacy-protected. Attackers can directly access the data for
119   visualization or model training. Thus, we need to integrate protection into data generation, using
120   methods like Data Generators (Goodfellow et al., 2014) and Dataset Distillation (Wang et al., 2018).
121
122   2.1   M ODEL - CENTRIC M ETHODS FOR P RIVACY P RESERVATION
123
124   Differential Privacy. Differential Privacy (Dwork et al., 2006) is a privacy-preserving technique
125   that introduces perturbation into the outputs to obfuscate the accurate return value, thereby preventing
126   the adversary from learning the exact private information (Dwork et al., 2006; Farayola et al., 2024).
127   Shokri et al. (2017) first indicate that the learning task based on differential privacy can reduce the
128   success probability of the membership inference attack against this task. Jayaraman & Evans (2019)
129
      evaluate the effectiveness of (ϵ, σ)-DP and its variants in neural network models by using membership
      inference attack. The application of differential privacy spans various domains, including health
130
      (Torfi et al., 2022; Adnan et al., 2022), as well as finance (Wang et al., 2022b).
131
132
      2.2   DATA - CENTRIC M ETHODS FOR P RIVACY P RESERVATION
133
134   Data Generator. Generative models can serve as an alternative for data sharing (Goodfellow et al.,
135   2014). However, Chen et al. (2020) demonstrate that privacy risks exist not only when training with
136   raw data but also when using synthetic data produced by these generative models. To address this
137   issue, researchers have applied differential privacy (DP) (Dwork et al., 2006) to develop differentially
138   private data generators (referred to as DP-generators) (Xie et al., 2018; Cao et al., 2021; Harder
139   et al., 2021; Ghalebikesabi et al., 2023). However, the noise introduced by differential privacy often
140   results in low-quality generated data, which impedes its effectiveness. Additionally, the training of
141   DP-generators can incur significant computational costs.
142
143   Dataset Distillation. Dataset distillation (Wang et al., 2018) aims to improve training efficiency
144
      by extracting knowledge from a large-scale dataset and construct a significantly smaller distilled
      dataset, enabling models trained on it achieve comparable performance to those trained on original
145
      dataset. Current solutions can be categorized based on their optimization mechanisms (Lei & Tao,
146
      2023), including Gradient Matching (GM) (Zhao et al., 2020; Zhao & Bilen, 2021; Kim et al., 2022),
147   Distribution Matching (DM) (Zhao & Bilen, 2023; Yin et al., 2023), Trajectory Matching (TM)
148   (Cazenavette et al., 2022; Guo et al., 2024). Remarkably, RDED (Sun et al., 2024) introduces an
149   optimization-free paradigm, which directly crop and select realistic patches from the original data,
150   and then stitch the selected patches into the new images as the distilled dataset. It achieves promising
151   performance, particularly for large-scale and high-resolution datasets.
152
      As the field progresses, state-of-the-art dataset distillation methods (Yin et al., 2023; Guo et al.,
153   2024; Sun et al., 2024) are able to produce distilled data that achieve performance comparable to the
154   original data. However, these distilled data closely resemble to real private data, especially at high
155   IPC (e.g., IPC = 50). This strong resemblance raises significant privacy concerns, necessitating
156   urgent measures to safeguard the privacy of the distilled datasets.
157
158   Privacy of Distilled dataset. Dong et al. (2022) first build the connection between dataset distilla-
159   tion and differential privacy, proving that distilled data—generated via DM (Zhao & Bilen, 2023),
160   DSA (Zhao & Bilen, 2021), and KIP (Nguyen et al., 2020)—can satisfy the definition of differential
161   privacy. However, Carlini et al. (2022b) point out that Dong et al. (2022) incorrectly used Assumption
      4.8, thus failing to provide privacy guarantees. Furthermore, recent state-of-the-art dataset distillation


                                                          3

162
      methods, including TM-based methods, such as MTT (Cazenavette et al., 2022), DATM (Guo et al.,
163   2024) and non-optimization-based methods like RDED (Sun et al., 2024), have not been considered.
164   Therefore, we focuses on examining the privacy of distilled datasets generated by these state-of-the-art
165   distillation methods, from both theoretical and empirical perspectives in Section 3.2 and Section 4 .

      3     P RIVACY A NALYSIS AND P ROTECTION IN DATASET D ISTILLATION
169
      This section begins by introducing preliminary definitions. Subsequently, we theoretically demon-
170
      strate that the distilled dataset with high IPC weakens differential privacy preservation and also
171   causes severely explicit privacy leverage. Our analysis reveals that the issues predominantly arises
172   from the common practice of initializing distilled imaegs as real data. To address these challenges, we
173   propose a plug-and-play module, named KT, which applies expanded transformations to the selected
174   real samples during initialization. KT ensures both differential privacy and explicit privacy while
175   maintaining the generalization performance of the distilled data.

230   Following Dong et al. (2022), we begin by studying the privacy bound of models trained on distilled
231   data in a differential privacy (DP) manner: how does removing one sample in the original dataset
232   impact models trained on distilled dataset. It is important to highlight that our demonstration diverges
233   from that of Dong et al. (2022) because we avoid the non-rigorous assumption in Dong et al. (2022).
234   Our analysis focuses on the two phases of dataset distillation: the initialization of the distilled data
235   and the subsequent matching optimization. We individually assess the differential privacy property of
236   each phase, as elaborated in Proposition 1 and Theorem 1 .
237
238   Phase 1: Differential privacy brought by random sampling initialization is unreliable. To
239
      enhance the performance of distilled datasets, most dataset distillation methods use random sampling
      from real data as the initialization for distilled data (Sun et al., 2024; Guo et al., 2024; Yin et al.,
240
      2023). Therefore, we analyze the differential privacy guarantees of this initialization method using
241
      the following proposition.
242
243    Proposition 1 . Given a training dataset of size |T |, random sampling without replacement
244    achieves (ln |T |T |+1    |S|
                       |+1−|S| , |T | )-differential privacy, where |S| is the subsample size.
245
246   This proposition suggests that random sampling initialization achieves differential privacy through
247   randomized response (Dwork et al., 2014). (See Appendix B for proof details.)
248
249
      However, δ = |S|/|T | reflects the leakage of private data used for initialization and is proportional
      to IPC. If subsequent distillation phases fail to introduce sufficient randomness to the initialized
250
      distilled dataset, this could directly expose the training data used for initialization, motivating us to
251
      introduce the concept of explicit privacy leakage.

371   Datasets and Neural Networks: We conduct experiments on both small-scale and large-scale

409   MIA Settings and Attack Metrics. We consider a typical scenario where the adversary possesses
410   access to the distilled dataset S and employs it to train a target model fS . The objective of adversary
411   is to infer membership information of the original dataset T .
412   For our membership inference attack framework on distilled datasets, we address a critical oversight
413   in previous works (Dong et al., 2022; Carlini et al., 2022b) that incorrectly treated training data not
414   used for initialization as non-members. We consider the entire original training set as members of the
415   distilled dataset, as all samples contribute to the distillation process. To ensure fairness, we employ
416   identical test samples and shadow models across various distilled and original datasets (see Figure 7
417   in Appendix E.3 for a detailed illustration of our framework). Following Carlini et al. (2022a), we
418   use TPR @ 0.1% FPR as the success criterion for membership inference attacks.
419   Further comprehensive experimental configurations, including detailed settings aligned with the
420   original distillation methods and specific hyperparameter choices, are provided in Appendix E .

      A.1    DATASET D ISTILLATION
759
760   Current solutions can be categorized based on their optimization mechanisms (Lei & Tao, 2023): (1)
761   Meta-Learning Framework: Distilled data are considered as hyperparameters, which are optimized in
762   a nested loop according to the distilled-data-trained model’s risk with respect to (w.r.t.) the original
763   data, including DD (Wang et al., 2018), KIP (Nguyen et al., 2021) and FRePo (Zhou et al., 2022). (2)
764   Gradient Matching: Aims to match the network gradients computed by the original dataset and the
765   distilled dataset, including DC (Zhao et al., 2020), DSA (Zhao & Bilen, 2021), and IDC (Kim et al.,
766
      2022). (3) Distribution Matching: Directly matches the distribution of original dataset and distilled
      data. Methods in this category includ DM (Zhao & Bilen, 2023), CAFE (Wang et al., 2022a), SRe2 L
767
      (Yin et al., 2023). (4) Trajectory Matching: Matches the training trajectories of models trained on
768
      original and distilled data over multiple steps. This category includes MTT (Cazenavette et al., 2022)
769   and , DATM (Guo et al., 2024). The above methods are based on optimization. Notably, RDED (Sun
770   et al., 2024) introduces an optimization-free paradigm, which directly crop and select realistic patches
771   from the original data, and then stitch the selected patches into the new images as the distilled dataset.
772   It achieves remarkable performance, particularly with large-scale and high-resolution datasets.
773
774   A.2    L I RA
775
776   Specifically, the privacy attack LiRA encompasses three stages. Firstly, the adversary randomly
777   samples N datasets from natural distribution to train shadow models. Therefore, for each data sample,
778
      there are N/2 shadow models trained on it (the IN models) and another N/2 that are not trained on it
      (the OUT models). Secondly, the adversary estimates the means µin , µout , and the variances σ 2in , σ 2out
779
      of model confidence for the IN and OUT models, respectively. Finally, to attack, the adversary
780
      queries the victim model f with a target example (x, y) to estimate the likelihood Λ, defined as:
781
782                                                  p(conf    | N (µ ,σ 2 ))

785   Here, f (x)y represents the probability assigned by the target model f to the true membership label y
786   when evaluating the attack test example x.
787   Note that LiRA determines if a data point was part of the training set by comparing a calculated

949   E.3      A N EW MIA F RAMEWORK FOR D ISTILLED DATASETS
950
951   Our membership inference attack framework for distilled datasets addresses the limitations of previous
952   approaches by treating the entire original dataset as potential members. Figure 7 illustrates our unified
953   evaluation method using LiRA, which employs common test samples for training shadow models.
954   This framework ensures a fair comparison across different distillation methods by using identical test
955   samples and shadow models.
956
      Our framework consists of three main steps:
957
958             • Target Model Training: We train the target model using the distilled dataset, following
959               the same training procedure across all methods. We utilize the original dataset’s training
960               samples, designated as members, while the test set comprises non-members.
961
962             • Shadow Model Training: We train multiple shadow models, ensuring that each sample
963               is treated as a member for half of the shadow models and as a non-member for the other
964               half. To mitigate the potential impact of data augmentation on privacy, we apply DSA with
965               multiple queries during this phase.
966
967             • Attack Evaluation: We input test cases into both the target and shadow models, computing
968               scores to determine the attack results.
