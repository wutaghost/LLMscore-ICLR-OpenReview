009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Dynamic scene reconstruction using Gaussians has recently attracted increased
012            interest. Mainstream approaches typically employ a global deformation field
013            to warp a 3D scene in canonical space. However, the inherent low-frequency
014            nature of implicit neural fields often leads to ineffective representations of complex
015            motions. Moreover, their structural rigidity can hinder adaptation to scenes with
016
               varying resolutions and durations. To address these challenges, we introduce a
               novel approach for streaming 4D real-world reconstruction utilizing discrete 3D
017
               control points. This method physically models local rays and establishes a motion-
018
               decoupling coordinate system. By effectively merging traditional graphics with
019            learnable pipelines, it provides a robust and efficient local 6-degrees-of-freedom
020            (6-DoF) motion representation. Additionally, we have developed a generalized
021            framework that integrates our control points with Gaussians. Starting from an initial
022            3D reconstruction, our workflow decomposes the streaming 4D reconstruction into
023            four independent submodules: 3D segmentation, 3D control point generation,
024            object-wise motion manipulation, and residual compensation. Experimental results
025            demonstrate that our method outperforms existing state-of-the-art 4D Gaussian
026            splatting techniques on both the Neu3DV and CMU-Panoptic datasets. Notably,
027
               the optimization of our 3D control points is achievable in 100 iterations and within
               just 2 seconds per frame on a single NVIDIA 4070 GPU.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Reconstructing real-world scenes is a longstanding challenge in computer graphics. Recently, 3D
033   Gaussian Splatting (3D-GS) Kerbl et al. (2023) have demonstrated remarkable success in producing
034   high-quality reconstructions for static scenes. This technique utilizes discrete Gaussians to model the
035   scene, attributing them with physically meaningful properties, and achieves rendering by "splatting"
036   these Gaussians onto the image plane. For dynamic scene reconstruction, current Gaussian Splatting
037   methods Yang et al. (2023b); Huang et al. (2023); Wu et al. (2023); Lin et al. (2023b) build upon
038
      the concept introduced by dynamic NeRFs Park et al. (2021a;b); Pumarola et al. (2021). These
      methods typically decompose scene motion into a canonical space and employ an implicit neural
039
      field to capture global motion. However, the low-frequency characteristics and rigid structure of
040
      implicit neural networks limit their ability to accurately handle complex motions or scenes with
041   diverse resolutions and varying lengths.
042
043   Compared to NeRF, one significant advantage of 3D-GS lies in its discrete structure. This structure
044
      ensures that scene representation—via Gaussians—is concentrated at influential positions within the
      scene, avoiding the inefficiencies of a global field that waste representational capacity on empty space.
045
      Similarly, the global implicit neural field used for deformation faces a similar issue: only a small part
046
      of the scene is dynamic, while the majority remains static. Therefore, a discrete, localized motion
047   representation holds promise, as it offers precise and flexible modeling of 3D motions at a local level.
048
049   Previous methods, both traditional Vedula et al. (1999) and learning-based Huang et al. (2023), have
050
      attempted to represent local 3D motion in a discrete fashion. However, traditional methods often
      struggle with complex 3D alignment challenges, while learning-based approaches face difficulties in
051
      convergence due to their high degrees of freedom (DoF).
052
053   Our approach differs by integrating graphics and learnable pipelines. Recognizing that optical flow is
      the 2D projection of scene flow, and that optical flow estimation Ranjan & Black (2017); Sun et al.


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      (2018); Teed & Deng (2020); Kroeger et al. (2016) is a well-established technique, we simplify the
055   problem by introducing a novel local decoupling system. In this system, local 3D motion is decoupled
056   into the observable and hidden components. The observable component is tied to optical flow, while
057   the hidden component remains learnable. We refer to this new form of motion representation as the
058   "3D control point" approach.
059
      Building on the introduced 3D motion representation, we present a novel generalized streaming
060
      framework for 4D real-world reconstruction. Beginning with an initial 3D reconstruction, our
061   workflow breaks the streaming 4D reconstruction process into distinct submodules: 3D segmentation,
062   3D control point generation, object-wise motion manipulation, and residual compensation. This
063   structured approach helps to minimize topological errors and ensures a compact, robust representation.
064
      Our key contributions are as follows:
065
066          • We model the 3D motions of dynamic objects discretely using an innovative method that
067            combines graphics techniques with learnable pipelines. This approach decouples the compo-
068            nents of 3D motion: observable part is tied to optical flow, while the hidden part is learned.
069            This motion representation, referred to as "3D control points," improves both convergence
070            speed and reconstruction accuracy.
071          • We introduce a generalized streaming pipeline that employs Gaussians and 3D control points
072            to reconstruct 4D real-world scenes. Our method establishes a new benchmark, achieving
073            state-of-the-art performance on the Neu3DV and CMU-Panoptic datasets.

      2.1   4D R ECONSTRUCTION
078
079   The recent Neural Radiance Field (NeRF) Mildenhall et al. (2021) approach has proven efficient for
080   scene reconstruction by utilizing a global continuous implicit representation of the scene. Subsequent
081   works have enhanced reconstruction quality by evolving from MLPs to grid-based structures. For
082   instance, Müller et al. (2022) introduced the Instant Neural Graphics Primitives method, while
083   Fridovich-Keil et al. (2022) proposed Plenoxels. Other methods, like Mip-NeRF Barron et al. (2021;
084   2022), modeled rays as cones to achieve anti-aliasing.
085   To accelerate rendering, various strategies have been proposed, such as pre-computation Wang et al.
086   (2023c; 2022); Fridovich-Keil et al. (2022); Yu et al. (2021) and hash coding Müller et al. (2022);
087   Takikawa et al. (2022).
088
      Efforts to extend NeRF’s representation to dynamic scenes have also been explored. For instance,
089
      Xian et al. (2021); Wang et al. (2021) challenged the static scene hypothesis by providing separate
090   representations over time. Mainstream methods Liu et al. (2022); Park et al. (2021a;b); Pumarola
091   et al. (2021); Song et al. (2023); Du et al. (2021); Li et al. (2021); Liu et al. (2023) decoupled motion
092   from the scene using a deformation field to warp the 3D scene at a canonical timestep.
093
      Further advancements included expanding the grid Fang et al. (2022); Shao et al. (2023) and pla-
094
      nar Cao & Johnson (2023); Fridovich-Keil et al. (2023) structures with a time dimension to boost
095
      rendering speed and reconstruction quality. Other research Wang et al. (2023b); Li et al. (2023);
096   Lin et al. (2022; 2023a) focused on refining reconstruction quality by leveraging prior knowledge
097   of camera positions. Additional supervision information such as depth Attal et al. (2021) and op-
098   tical flow Wang et al. (2023a) was incorporated during training. NeRFPlayer Song et al. (2023),
099   notably, utilized self-supervised learning to segment dynamic scenes into static, deforming, and newly
100   appearing regions, applying tailored strategies to each.
101
      Recently, 3D-GS Kerbl et al. (2023) introduced an elegant point-based rendering approach with
102   efficient CUDA implementations. The 4D reconstruction methods using Gaussians closely resemble
103   those in NeRF. For instance, Yang et al. (2023a) incorporated time-variant attributes into Gaussians,
104   while Dynamic-GS Luiten et al. (2023) directly learned dense Gaussian movements. 3DGStream
105   introduced Neural Transformation Cache to represent Gaussian motion on a frame-by-frame basis,
106   and Gaussian-Flow Lin et al. (2024) employed Dual-Domain Deformation Model to represent point-
107   wise movement. HiFi4G Jiang et al. (2023) combined an implicit method Wang et al. (2023d) for
      surface reconstruction with a traditional graphics approach Sumner et al. (2007) for surface warping.

      Other studies Wu et al. (2023); Yang et al. (2023b); Huang et al. (2023); Lin et al. (2023b) represented
109   4D scenes by warping Gaussians in a canonical space using global implicit deformation fields.

      2.2     3D M OTION R EPRESENTATION
112
113   Decoupling 3D motion from the canonical scene offers significant advantages by reducing redundant
114   information in the time dimension. While implicit neural representations are compact, they often lack
115   the flexibility needed to adapt to varying scenes and require redesigns. Traditional graphics meth-
116   ods Sorkine (2005); Yu et al. (2004) have provided flexible deformation solutions while preserving
117   geometric details. Among these, Sumner et al. (2007) introduced sparse control points (ED-graph) to
118
      represent the motion of dense surfaces, balancing compactness and flexibility.
119   HiFi4G Jiang et al. (2023) utilized the ED-graph approach directly, but its surface reconstruction
120   demands dense camera distribution and is time-consuming. SC-GS Huang et al. (2023) also adopted
121   the concept of sparse control points, yet direct optimization is difficult due to the high degrees of
122   freedom. Moreover, unlike meshes, the volumetric radiance representation of 3D Gaussians is not
123   well-suited for the thin nature of surfaces Huang et al. (2024).
124   To address these challenges, we propose a novel 3D control points method tailored for volumetric
125   radiance representations. By decoupling 3D motion into observable and hidden components, we
126   apply distinct strategies for motion acquisition. This approach leverages the strengths of traditional
127   graphics techniques and learnable pipelines, enabling precise and flexible motion representation
128   while maintaining the efficiency and compactness necessary for further applications.

      3.1     OVERVIEW OF E ACH M ODULE
156
157   3D Segmentation. The goal of 3D segmentation is to label each Gaussian as either a moving object
158   or part of the static background. As outlined in Sec. 1, representing local motion discretely by an
159   object-wise approach is often more efficient than global methods. Consequently, defining the regions
160   where each local representation is applicable is crucial. To achieve this, we use multiview masks
161   and adopt a Gaussian category voting algorithm to divide the scene into dynamic objects and a static
      background. Only Gaussians assigned to specific objects are influenced by the 3D control points.


                                                                          3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      3D Control Points Generation. The goal of 3D Control Points Generation is to generate 3D control
163   points for all moving objects, which govern the motion of their associated Gaussians on an object-wise
164   basis. This module plays a pivotal role in our workflow. Direct optimization of 3D control points
165   is challenging due to their high degrees of freedom. To address this issue, we introduce a local
166   decoupling coordinate system that links partial parameters of the 3D control points with 2D optical
167   flow. This innovative approach effectively reduces the control points’ degrees of freedom from nine
168   to three, enabling faster convergence during optimization.
169   Object-wise Motion Manipulation. The goal of Object-wise Motion Manipulation is to control the
170   movement of Gaussians for each object using the 3D control points. Motion manipulation involves
171   transforming the position and rotation attributes of Gaussians from one timestep t − 1 to the next
172   t. By manipulating these attributes on an object-wise basis, each Gaussian is influenced only by
173   the control points associated with its category. This selective control method allows for accurate
174   handling of topology changes of spatially adjacent objects and improves the precision of discrete
175   motion representation.
176   Residual Compensation. The goal of Residual Compensation is to mitigate error accumulation and
177   ensure stable long-term reconstruction. This module activates only at keyframes. Specifically, during
178   non-keyframe intervals, Gaussian attributes are frozen, and updates are confined to the control points.
179   At keyframes, both Gaussian attributes and control points are optimized, enabling comprehensive
180   adjustments that preserve the continuity and accuracy of the scene reconstruction. It is important
181   to note that the term "residual" in this context refers to adjustments in the attributes of the existing
182   Gaussians, without the introduction of new Gaussians.
183
184   3.2   3D S EGMENTATION
185
      A straightforward and efficient method for 3D segmentation is to leverage objects’ 2D masks
186
      combined with a statistical method to categorize Gaussians based on their projections from multiple
187
      viewpoints. We utilize the SAM-track method Cheng et al. (2023) to obtain continuous 2D object
188   masks and implement a Gaussian category voting strategy similar to the approach used in SA-GS Hu
189   et al. (2024). A notable improvement in our method is its ability to distinguish between multiple
190   objects in the foreground.
191
      Specifically, each Gaussian is assigned a counter for every object category, with the total number of
192
      counters corresponding to the number of 2D object masks. For each Gaussian g, we project it onto
193
      the j th image plane. If the projection of the Gaussian falls within the boundary of the k th object’s
194   mask, the counter for that object’s category is incremented by one. This process is repeated across
195   all training views, denoted as Ck . After processing all views, we label each Gaussian as L based on
196   the category with the highest counter value, indicating its assigned object category. The complete
197   algorithm is summarized as follows:

      3.3   3D C ONTROL P OINTS G ENERATION AND L OCAL 6-D O F M OTION D ECOUPLING
203
204   To effectively represent local 6-degrees-of-freedom (6-DoF) motion, the attributes of a 3D control
205   point must include its position, local spatial translation, and rotation. However, only a portion of this
206   motion is visible from a single viewpoint, which is captured as optical flow. To address this limitation,
207   we develop an advanced coordinate system termed the "ray coordinate system." This system correlates
208   observable 3D motion components with optical flow from a single viewpoint. Motion components
209   hidden from this viewpoint are optimized using global image loss, as their information is implicitly
210   captured across multiple views.
211   To streamline the representation of motion, we model a small cluster of localized rays from the
212   scene to the camera as parallel lights. This approach enables the rays to share a unified coordinate
213   system, focusing on local motion rather than individual point-wise motion. The reasoning behind
214   approximating these rays as nearly parallel is explained in Appendix .1. In this model, the z-axis of
215   the ray coordinate system aligns with the center ray of the cluster, which extends from the camera
      towards the scene. The motion decoupling is depicted in Fig. 2(a) and Fig. 2(b).

      3.5   R ESIDUAL C OMPENSATION
319
320   Our approach utilizes control points to predict 3D motion but suffers from error accumulation
321   over time due to two main factors. First, scene reconstruction relies on information from earlier
322   frames, which may fail to capture previously occluded areas, such as the sides or backs of objects.
323   Second, there are discrepancies between the predicted motion of the Gaussians and the ground truth,
      particularly for Gaussians distant from neighboring control points.

      To mitigate error accumulation, we employ a strategy inspired by video coding techniques, specifically
325   residual coding Wiegand et al. (2003); Sze et al. (2014). Residual coding in video technology
326   compensates for differences between original and motion-compensated frames. Our model uses a
327   keyframe updating method to optimize Gaussians after object-wise motion manipulation, thereby
328   improving long-term streaming reconstruction.

      5                 C ONCLUSION AND D ISCUSSION
530
531
      We introduce a novel discrete 6-DoF motion decoupling model that combines traditional graphics
      with learnable pipelines. This approach employs partially learnable control points for local 6-DoF
532
      motion representation, enabling fast convergence and robust reconstruction for real-world datasets.
533
      Additionally, we have developed an innovative workflow for streaming 4D real-world reconstruction
534   using Gaussians and 3D control points. Starting with an initial 3D scene reconstruction, our approach
535   progresses through several independent submodules, allowing each to be optimized individually
536   for future improvements. Our method outperforms existing state-of-the-art 4D Gaussian splatting
537   methods on real-world datasets. The workflow also has limitations: the quality of 4D reconstruction
538   highly depends on the initial static reconstruction, a factor that remains underexplored and presents
539   potential for further research. The current method does not support monocular videos due to its
      reliance on multi-view initial frames. We plan to address these limitations in future work.

      .2     2D M OTION P RIOR ACQUISITION
802
      We provide an intuitive workflow for the prior acquisition of 3D motion. The inputs consist of two
803   consecutive frames and an object-wise mask.
804
805   We first input two consecutive frames into the optical flow network, outputting a whole frame of
806
      optical flow. Then, a corresponding sampling grid, based on the objects’ masks, is generated for
      each object in the view. The local motion calculator is the abstract representation of the method we
807
      introduced in Sec. 3.3. The final processed 2D motion prior binds with 3D control points.
808
809   Note that 3D control points are obtained from all training viewpoints independently. Thus, the 3D
      control points collect all the training viewpoint control points. Hence, their distribution is much

811   points will be smaller. When sampling control points, a larger control point sampling interval should
812   be selected, and for each control point, a smaller range around it should be selected to compute the
813   motion prior.
