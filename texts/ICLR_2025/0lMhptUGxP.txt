009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Aligning Large Language Models (LLMs) is crucial for enhancing their safety and
012            utility. However, existing methods, primarily based on preference datasets, face
013            challenges such as noisy labels, high annotation costs, and privacy concerns. In
014            this work, we introduce Alignment from Demonstrations (AfD), a novel approach
015            leveraging high-quality demonstration data to overcome these challenges. We
016            formalize AfD within a sequential decision-making framework, highlighting its
017            unique challenge of missing reward signals. Drawing insights from forward and
018
               inverse reinforcement learning, we introduce divergence minimization objectives
               for AfD. Analytically, we elucidate the mass-covering and mode-seeking behaviors
019
               of various approaches, explaining when and why certain methods are superior.
020
               Practically, we propose a computationally efficient algorithm that extrapolates over
021            a tailored reward model for AfD. We validate our key insights through experi-
022            ments on the Harmless and Helpful tasks, demonstrating their strong empirical
023            performance while maintaining simplicity.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   The alignment of Large Language Models (LLMs) is essential for their safe and effective deployment
029   in various applications. Current research has focused extensively on reinforcement learning from
030   human feedback (RLHF) (Christiano et al., 2017; Ouyang et al., 2022). However, the majority of
031   advancements in RLHF (Rafailov et al., 2024b; Zhao et al., 2023; Yuan et al., 2023; Dong et al., 2023;
032   Azar et al., 2023; Munos et al., 2023) rely on preference-based datasets annotated by humans or
033
      general-purpose LLMs (Bai et al., 2022; Lee et al., 2023; Guo et al., 2024), facing several significant
      challenges that can impede their performance or limit their applications:
034
035   1. Noisy Labels Harm Alignment Performance: Research indicates that noisier data leads to less
036      accurate reward modeling and poorer alignment performance (Zheng et al., 2023). Since the
037      same language model generates the response pairs in preference-based learning, the preferences
038      provided by annotators can be highly uncertain and noisy (Azar et al., 2023).
039
      2. High Cost in Preference Annotation: Although it is theoretically and empirically justified that
         the ideal approach to learning from preference data involves continuous querying of annotators
040
         during the learning process (Guo et al., 2024; Xiong et al., 2023; Touvron et al., 2023; Tang et al.,
041
         2024), this approach can be prohibitively expensive.
042   3. Requirement of Inductive Biases in Reward Modeling: Utilizing preference-based data often
043      requires assumptions like the Bradley-Terry model (Bradley & Terry, 1952) or the Kahneman
044      Tversky model (Ethayarajh et al., 2024). These assumptions may not always hold true, as discussed
045      in (Azar et al., 2023; Munos et al., 2023; Swamy et al., 2024).
046   4. Privacy Concerns in Preference Generation: Collecting preference over data with the help of
047      annotators or commercial general-purpose LLMs is not always feasible, particularly when dealing
048      with private data that cannot be shared externally (Li et al., 2023; Pouplin et al., 2024).
049   To address these challenges, we propose aligning LLMs using a demonstration dataset, referred to as
050   Alignment from Demonstrations (AfD), as an alternative to preference-based alignment. Specifically,
051   AfD offers the following advantages: (1) demonstration data always enjoys higher quality and less
052   noise; (2) AfD does not require continuous querying and comparison; (3) AfD does not rely on
053   assumptions inherent in preference-based methods; (4) AfD enables LLM alignment without the
      need for external annotators, hence can be applied to private dataset locally.

113      Markov Decision Processes (MDP) In Markov Decision Processes, decisions are made in discrete
         time steps and affect the state of the environment in the subsequent step. Formally, an MDP is denoted as
114
         M = {S, A, T , R, ρ0 , γ}, where S ⊂ Rd denotes the d-dim state space, A is the action space. Broadly,
115      the environment includes T and R, the former denotes the transition dynamics T : S × A 7→ ∆(S)
116      that controls transitions between states, and the reward function R : S × A 7→ R provides feedback.
117      ρ0 = p(s0 ) ∈ ∆(S) denotes the initial state distribution. γ is the discount factor that trades off between
118      short-term and long-term returns.
119

120

      AfD Beyond Supervised Fine Tuning. While BC is conceptually simple and easy to implement,
206
      it faces a fundamental challenge known as the distributional shift — during evaluation, the state
207   distribution is generated by rolling out the learned policy π, rather than the data-generation behavior

209   where the dynamics model is available to generate roll-out samples during learning (Pomerleau, 1991;
210   Finn et al., 2016; Abbeel & Ng, 2004). For a more detailed discussion on the benefits of accessing
211   dynamics models, refer to Appendix C.1.
212

214   is the accessibility of online interactions with the dynamics model, rather than the reward model, that

360     samples as positive examples in reward model training is straightforward. However, as π0 and πβ
361     are heterogeneous models, so nuanced differences, such as specific verb usage or response formats
362     in πβ can dominate reward model learning rather than the desired alignment properties.
363
      • SFT-Demo RM: Using samples generated by πSFT examples and demonstration dataset samples
        as positive examples faces the same challenge. Moreover, since πSFT and πβ are closer in terms of
364
        the desired properties to align (scores), reward hacking is even more likely.
365
      • Init-SFT RM: To avoid potential reward hacking caused by using heterogeneous data in reward
366     model training, we can use samples generated by π0 as negative examples and samples generated by
367     πSFT as positive examples. Unlike the previous approaches, where positive and negative examples
368     are generated by heterogeneous models, these two models are homogeneous since the SFT policy
369     is fine-tuned from the initial policy.
370   • Preference-based RM (BT-RM): In preference-based reward modeling, both preferred and dis-
371     preferred responses are samples from the same LLM (Ouyang et al., 2022). Therefore, there is no
372     issue of heterogeneity between the positive and negative samples.
373   When applying the learned reward models at inference time to determine which responses are superior,
374   these responses are generated by πSFT , therefore, the Init-SFT RM should outperform other choices.
375   We provide pseudocode in Appendix E.1. As has been insightfully pointed out by our reviewer,
376   we discuss an alternative closed-form reward model in Appendix G for interested readers. Before
377   using empirical studies to verify our findings, it is worth further discussing the related work on the
      superiority of having explicit reward models at the end of this section.

390   It is worth noting the links and differences between our explicit reward modeling approach and the
391   direct alignment methods such as DPO (Rafailov et al., 2023) that are designed for alignment using
392   demonstration datasets (Chen et al., 2024).
393   Regardless of the data format, DPO-type algorithms have a crucial distinction: they explicitly
394   assume the existence of a score-based scalar reward derived from the Bradley-Terry model, which
395   requires pair-wise data for effective application. In contrast, adversarial learning approaches utilizing
396   discriminative models do not rely on such explicit assumptions about the Bradley-Terry model or the
397   preference-based data format. While constraining the reward function to a specific form may mitigate
398   the reward ambiguity issue in inverse RL (Fu et al., 2017; Ng et al., 2000; 1999; Chan et al., 2024),
399   it also limits the expressivity of the reward space. Adversarial imitation approaches, as introduced
400   in our work, do not presuppose any specific reward model form. This allows for a broader range of
401
      alternatives to the Bradley-Terry model, including direct preference objectives (Azar et al., 2023;
      Munos et al., 2023) and prospect theory objectives (Ethayarajh et al., 2024).
402
403   Moreover, DPO does not extrapolate over its implicit reward model. When applying DPO iteratively
404   to the demonstration dataset, as proposed in Chen et al. (2024), the underlying assumption is that
405   the current policy (starting with the SFT policy) is always weaker than the demonstrations. Hence,
406   the Bradley-Terry model can be repeatedly applied to these pairwise data. At the convergence of
407
      iterative training (Chen et al., 2024), the performance of the aligned LLM is upper-bounded by
      the performance of the demonstrations, as the demonstration dataset is consistently regarded as the
408
      positive examples in implicit reward modeling. The first panel of Figure 3 illustrates the learning
409
      objectives and how policies evolve during learning.
410
411   Our method, as an Inverse RL approach, explicitly learns the reward model and extrapolates over
412   it. As illustrated in the right panel of Figure 3, our reward modeling mechanism extrapolates the
413
      reward model based on task scores. Conversely, if we naively follow the SPIN setup — using the
      SFT checkpoint’s generation as negative examples and the demonstrations as positive examples —
414
      the generated reward model can negatively impact heterogeneity, leading to poor performance. We
415
      will empirically demonstrate this point in our experimental section.
416
417   This distinction highlights the flexibility and potential of our approach in achieving super-
418   demonstration performance in LLM alignment, as will be empirically verified in the next section.

      5     C ONCLUSION
531
      In this paper, we addressed the limitations of preference-based alignment for Large Language Models
532
      (LLMs) by proposing an alternative approach: Alignment from Demonstrations (AfD). Our study
533
      highlights the benefits of using high-quality demonstration data, which avoids the noise, cost, and
534   assumptions inherent in preference-based methods, and privacy concerns. By framing the AfD prob-
535   lem within a sequential decision-making framework and introducing trajectory distribution matching
536   objectives, we provide a solid foundation for AfD. Our empirical results, validated on the Harmless
537   and Helpful tasks of the Anthropic HH-RLHF dataset, demonstrate the effectiveness of AfD
538   in achieving superior alignment performance. This work establishes AfD as a viable and efficient
539   alternative to Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF), paving the way for safer and
      more reliable deployment of LLMs in various applications.

921   A.1   I MITATION L EARNING AND I NVERSE R EINFORCEMENT L EARNING
922
923   In contrast to the prevailing approaches in LLM alignment research, which rely on preference datasets,
924   this work focuses on offline expert demonstration datasets. These datasets are more accessible in real-
925   world applications and serve as the basis for developing algorithms that can surpass the performance
926   of Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT), the common practice for such datasets. The use of demonstration
927
      datasets, combined with the accessibility of the dynamics model, naturally frames the problem as an
      Imitation Learning (IL) or Inverse Reinforcement Learning (Inverse RL) task.
928
929   The simplest approach to IL in the literature is Behavior Cloning (BC) (Pomerleau, 1991), which
930   leverages supervised learning to predict the actions in the demonstration dataset given the states.
931   However, this method is often unreliable due to compounding errors (Ross et al., 2011). Adversarial
932   Imitation Learning algorithms (Ho & Ermon, 2016; Fu et al., 2017; Ghasemipour et al., 2020;
933
      Kostrikov et al., 2018; Orsini et al., 2021), inspired by both Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs)
      (Goodfellow et al., 2014) and Inverse RL (Ng et al., 2000; Ziebart et al., 2008), aim to solve this
934
      problem by matching distributional characteristics. Specifically, GAIL seeks to learn a policy whose
935
      state-action space occupancy measure is indistinguishable from that of the expert demonstrations. A
936   key difference between Inverse RL and IL is whether or not the reward model is explicitly modeled
937   (Fu et al., 2017). With a learned reward model, the objective can go beyond matching demonstration
938   behavior to extrapolating the reward model for super-demonstration performance (Brown et al., 2019).
939
      There are two unique properties in the LLM alignment Markov Decision Process (MDP) that
940
      differentiate it from conventional IL and Inverse RL literature:
941
942
943   1. Known and Deterministic Transition Dynamics: In LLM alignment, the transition dynamics
944      are known and deterministic, allowing us to explicitly define the trajectory distribution and use
945      trajectory distribution matching as the learning objective.
946
947   2. Sparse Reward Signals: The reward signal is provided and is mostly meaningful only at the
948      trajectory level, making the alignment problem a sparse-reward IL task. This sparsity means
949
         that learning a step-wise reward function, as done in existing work (Fu et al., 2017), may not be
         feasible.
950
951
952
      A.2   R EINFORCEMENT L EARNING FROM H UMAN F EEDBACK
953
954
      Introduced in the seminal paper by (Christiano et al., 2017), Reinforcement Learning from Human
955
      Feedback (RLHF) provides an alternative to traditional scalar reward signals in policy learning. In the
956
      context of LLMs, (Ouyang et al., 2022) proposed a three-step alignment framework consisting of SFT,
957   reward modeling (RM), and policy learning with proximal policy optimization (PPO). This framework
958   relies on two distinct types of datasets: 1. the SFT dataset contains queries and expert-generated
959   responses to those queries, under the form of Ddemo = {xi , yi∗ }i∈[Ne ] ; and 2. the preference dataset
960   Dpref = {xi , yi+ , yi− }i∈[Np ] that contains queries, multiple language model responses, and human
961   preferences over those response labeled by human annotators.
962
963
      Current RLHF practices adhere to this two-stage, two-dataset framework, with several enhancements
      introduced in recent literature. For instance: the DPO circumvents explicit reward modeling and
964
      stabilizes the learning process on preference dataset using supervised signals (Rafailov et al., 2024b);
965
      SLiC-HF (Zhao et al., 2023) gains insight from contrastive learning and learns from closed-form
966   losses that maximize the margin between the preferred and dispreferred generations; other alternatives
967   include iterative supervised learning (Yuan et al., 2023; Dong et al., 2023), regularizing the genera-
968   tion (Azar et al., 2023) or game-theory-based methods (Munos et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024; Cheng
969   et al., 2023). These advancements collectively contribute to refining the RLHF framework, address-
970   ing various challenges associated with preference-based alignment in LLMs. Different from those
971   approaches, our work focuses on Alignment from Demonstrations, where only a single demonstration
      dataset is used.

       A.3    G ENERATIVE A DVERSARIAL N ETWORKS ON T EXT G ENERATION M ODELS
973
974    The use of GANs in text generation is also relevant to our research. Specifically, TextGAIL (Wu et al.,
975    2021) explores GAN training for text generation that surpasses supervised learning performance.
976    Other notable works using GANs for sequence generation include (Yu et al., 2017; Ke et al., 2019;
977    Che et al., 2017; Guo et al., 2018; Adiwardana et al., 2020; Zhou et al., 2020; Caccia et al., 2018), all
978    of which focus on text domain sequence generation.
979    Our work diverges from this line of literature in several key ways:
980
981    1. Focus on Alignment: Unlike GAN-based text generation, which often aims to generate context
982       under specific formats (e.g., story generation), our work focuses on aligning LLMs to human
983       intentions rather than merely generating text.
984    2. Objective Comparison: GAN-based methods are more akin to adversarial imitation techniques,
985       aiming to reproduce the training dataset’s distribution (Ho & Ermon, 2016). In contrast, our
986       objective is to improve language model alignment by learning a reward model inspired by Inverse
987       Reinforcement Learning (IRL) (Fu et al., 2017).
988    3. Evaluation Metrics: In many GAN-based text generation tasks (Wu et al., 2021; Yu et al., 2017;
989       Ke et al., 2019; Che et al., 2017; Guo et al., 2018; Adiwardana et al., 2020; Zhou et al., 2020;
990       Caccia et al., 2018), oracle evaluation metrics are available, eliminating the need to infer the
991       underlying intention of demonstrations. In alignment tasks, however, human intention is not
992       directly accessible as a function, necessitating a different approach.
993    4. Motivation, Formulation, and Explanation: Our work is motivated by the challenge of lacking
994       reward signals in LLM alignment, formulated as an RL problem. We derive objectives from IRL
995       literature to explain when and why SFT and IRL techniques are effective.
996
       5. Practical Implementation: Unlike GAN-based methods, which rely on iterative training, our
997
          implementation does not. Instead, we extrapolate the learned IRL reward model (Brown et al.,
998       2019) to further enhance the performance of SFT-ed LLMs.
999
1000   This differentiation highlights our unique approach to LLM alignment, focusing on the nuances
1001   of reward modeling and alignment objectives, distinct from traditional GAN-based text generation
1002   methods.

1004   A.4    C OMPARISON OF D IFFERENT S ET- UPS OF RL
1005
1006   In this section, we contextualize the differences and connections among various RL problem setups.
1007   Specifically, we discuss (online) RL, Offline-RL, Imitation Learning, Inverse-RL, Learning from
1008
       Demonstrations, and Preference-based RL.
1009   To elaborate on Table 3, we outline the following distinctions:
1010
1011   • Online RL: In this setup, both the external dynamics model and the reward model are accessible.
1012     An agent learns through trial and error by interacting with these live models.
1013   • Offline RL: Neither the dynamics model nor the reward model is available. The agent learns solely
1014     from an offline dataset that includes information on states, actions, rewards, and transitions.
1015
       • Imitation Learning (IL): The reward model is unknown, but the dynamics model is accessible. The
1016     agent learns from demonstrations to optimize its policy, without explicitly modeling the reward.
1017
1018
       • Inverse RL (IRL): Similar to IL, the reward model is unknown, but the dynamics model is accessible.
         The agent learns from demonstrations with the objective of building an explicit reward model to
1019
         guide policy optimization.
1020
1021   • Offline IRL: Both the dynamics model and the reward model are unknown. The agent must learn
1022     from an offline dataset that contains demonstrations, but without direct access to the dynamics or
1023
         reward models.
1024   • Learning from Demonstrations: An agent initially uses an offline demonstration dataset to warm-
1025     start, followed by learning through online interactions with the environment, which includes the
         dynamics and reward models.

1070   A.6    C OMPARING D IRECT AND E XPLICIT R EWARD M ODELING M ETHODS IN A LIGNMENT
1071
1072   In the following, we compare the direct alignment methods with explicit reward modeling methods
1073   from the perspective of generalization.
1074   Explicit reward modeling has been shown to generalize to OOD samples better than direct alignment
1075   methods, both theoretically and empirically (Lin et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2024). The central insight
1076   behind this advantage lies in the observation that learning a discriminative model, such as a reward
1077   model that evaluates the quality of responses, can often be easier than learning a generative model
1078   that produces complete outputs (Ouyang et al., 2022). Such an efficiency arises from the fact
1079   discriminative models only need to capture the decision boundaries in identifying response qualities,
       rather than modeling the full response distribution.

1198   Imitation Learning (IL) In order to alleviate the challenge of compounding error we discussed
1199   above, IL considers the setting where a dynamics model is available during learning. The objective
1200   of IL is to learn from a (decision) demonstration dataset, with access to a dynamics model — such
1201   that the current policy can be rolled out in the real environment. Intuitively, with such a dynamics
1202   model, the optimization objective will no longer be st ∼ pπβ (τ ) but could be st ∼ pπ (τ ) — the
1203   distributional shift problem can be alleviated. It has been shown in the literature that having access
1204
       to a dynamics model is essential in controlling the error bound. (Ross et al., 2011)
1205   There are many practical methods for implementing such a learning process, and the most famous
1206   work in the Deep-RL era is the GAIL (Ho & Ermon, 2016), which conducts IL through adversarial
1207   learning: the policy is a generator of behaviors, while a discriminator then tries to identify whether a
1208   trajectory is generated by the behavior policy πβ or by the generator (the policy learned).

1210   tion on st ∼ pπ (τ ) and optimal labels a∗t , and attains generalization error ϵ on st ∼ pπ (τ ), then
1211   ℓ(π) ≤ C + T ϵ is the best possible bound on the expected error of the learned policy.
