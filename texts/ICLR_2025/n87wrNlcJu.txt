                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
               Many Knowledge Graphs (KGs), despite their size, remain highly incomplete.
010
               This problem has motivated many approaches to complete the KGs by embedding
011            their constituents in a latent space to find missing links. While these methods per-
012            form well on the commonly used metrics, they rank the missing links with scores
013            that are uncalibrated and context-dependent. The fact that the scores are “local”,
014            in the sense that they relate to a specific context, makes it difficult to determine
015            the final link truth value and to answer complex queries. Another limitation is that
016            their learning depends on negative sampling, which is challenging due to the Open
017            World Assumption (OWA).
018            To solve these issues, we propose a novel auto-regressive generative model that
019            learns a joint distribution of the entities and relations of the KG without resort-
020            ing to negative sampling. This distribution allows us to compute “global” scores
021
               for the missing links which are calibrated and interpretable in different contexts.
               Moreover, our method has the advantage that it offers probabilistic semantics for
022
               complex reasoning and knowledge base completion. Finally, our empirical eval-
023
               uation shows that although our method is not suitable for KGs with a regular
024            topology that can be easily learned by prior local methods, it excels with KGs that
025            have a more complex structure with many inter-contextual dependencies, achiev-
026            ing or even surpassing state-of-the-art performance on KB completion and with
027            consistent scores across the entire KG.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Motivation Knowledge Graphs (KGs) are structured representations of knowledge organized as
033
      graphs which model relationships between entities. KGs are highly popular and widely used in
      various domains, from search engines and recommendation systems to data integration Hogan et al.
034
      (2021). Currently, KGs are widely studied in academia and extensively utilized in industry, driving
035
      advancements in both research and practical applications Zhang et al. (2023); Wang et al. (2017b).
036
037
038   Problem An important problem that limits the usefulness of KGs in real-world scenarios is that
039
      they remain highly incomplete, i.e., many links (and/or nodes) are missing. An important step
      to solve this problem consists of predicting, given a KG as input, which of the unknown links is
040
      likely to be true (Lü & Zhou, 2011). To this end, the mainstream approach consists of learning
041
      latent representations of the KG to rank effectively potential missing links. These methods, known
042   as Knowledge Graph Embeddings (KGEs) (Wang et al., 2017a), transform the set of entities and
043   relationships into continuous vector spaces, and then rank the missing links by evaluating their
044   likelihood with a given scoring function. Conceptually, several prominent KGE approaches can
045   be seen as binary classifiers since their primary objective is to construct models where each link
046   corresponds to a distinct Bernoulli random variable (Trouillon et al., 2016; Nickel et al., 2011;
047   Lacroix et al., 2018; Nguyen et al., 2018; Bordes et al., 2013c). For example, given a KG with a set
048   of entities E and relations R, a link from a source s ∈ E to destination d ∈ E with a specific relation
049   type r ∈ R, denoted as ⟨s, r, d⟩, is assigned to the variable Y⟨s,r,d⟩ .
050   A major strength of KGE methods lies in their ability to use the likelihoods calculated by these
051   models as reliable scoring mechanisms for ranking potential missing links. However, an important
052   limitation is that they rely on the generation of false links for training the model, which is challeng-
053   ing to do under the Open World Assumption (OWA). Typically, these models resort to employing
      randomly generated links as false evidence, which is a technique known as negative sampling (Bor-


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      des et al., 2013b). An additional limitation is that these models lack a consistent global probabilistic
055   interpretation of the scores (Friedman & den Broeck, 2020; Zhu et al., 2023), hence we say that
056   their scores have a local interpretation. The fact that scores have only a local interpretation becomes
057   particularly problematic when, for instance, we need to use the scores to determine the final truth
058   value of the links by selecting an optimal threshold value or for answering complex queries since
059   the scores in different domains cannot be compared(Arakelyan et al., 2023).
060
      Both limitations have received considerable attention in the community. Some techniques have
061   investigated the value of calibrating the scores in a post-processing phase to reduce the differences
062   between domains Zhu et al. (2023); Arakelyan et al. (2023). Others have proposed to exploit the
063   inherent structure of certain tensor factorization models (Lacroix et al., 2018; Nickel et al., 2011;
064   Balažević et al., 2019; Trouillon et al., 2016) to normalize the scoring functions. By normalizing the
065   scoring function, the edges are no longer modelled with separate binary variables but one random
066   variable Y (Loconte et al., 2023). In this last case, the idea is to learn a distribution p(Y, S, R, D)
067   (for modeling the truth value, source, relation type and destination of the link, respectively) and
068   to consider p(Y = true, S = s, R = r, D = d) as the scoring function for the link ⟨s, r, d⟩.
069
      Note that any joint distribution p(Y, X) can be rewritten into p(Y |X) · p(X). Since the former is
      a classification component (p(Y |X)) while the latter is a generative one (p(X)) we refer to such
070
      an approach as an hybrid model Lasserre et al. (2006). While this approach yields global scores
071
      (since we have a single random variable Y ), it still depends on negative sampling for training the
072   classification component. Hybrid models learn the data distribution to improve the decision making
073   process, however, learning the distribution of false edges is problematic if we only know which
074   edges are true.
075
076
      Contributions To overcome the limitations stated above, we propose a new generative method
077
      that learns directly the distribution p(S, R, D|Y = 1) or more simply p(S, R, D). We argue that
078   this method has a benefit over the current ones since it produces global scores without resorting
079   to negative samplings during learning. To learn the joint distribution, we first decompose it as
080   p(S, R, D) = p(S)·p(R | S)·p(D | R, S). Then, we employ (deep) AutoRegressive Models Gregor
081   et al. (2014); Tomczak (2022) to learn the conditional distributions p(R | S) and p(D | R, S), while
082   for the marginal distribution p(S) we employ different strategies with the source entities in the
083   training data set to learn a function that resembles the true marginal distribution.
084   We empirically demonstrate that our method yields significantly more consistent scores across dif-
085   ferent domains by assigning high probabilities only to links deemed highly likely to be true, as
086   indicated by their top positions in local rankings. Additionally, our results achieve the best global
087   performance in terms of Mean Average Precision. We consolidate unseen facts into a single global
088   ranking and evaluate the truth value of the top-k positions in this ranking. Our method is competi-
089   tive in link prediction (local rankings) using standard metrics; it reaches state-of-the-art performance
090   when considering the consistency of the probabilities, making it particularly suitable for more com-
091   plex processing tasks such as Knowledge Base Completion or Complex Query Answering.

      2   P RELIMINARIES
095
096
097   Knowledge Graph Embedding Models A common way to represent information in a KG is
098   through triples. A triple (s, r, d) consists of a source (subject), relation (predicate) and destina-
099   tion (object), where the sources and destinations come from the same set of entities, s, d ∈ E and
100   r ∈ R, the set of all relations. Every edge can be precisely characterized by a triple of the form
101   (si , ri , di ), and a KG is a subset of E × R × E.
102   KGE models consist of two components. One component represents entities and relations as contin-
103   uous vectors (embeddings) in a low-dimensional space. These embeddings are designed to capture
104   the semantic meanings and structural information of the entities and relations while preserving the
105   inherent structure of a KG. The second component is a scoring function that assigns a score to the
106   vector representation of every triple, ϕ(es , rr , ed ). That score is usually a real number and repre-
107   sents the model’s belief in the truthfulness of the triple, typically in contrast to the score of other
      triples with the same source and relation.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      Probabilistic interpretation The embeddings are in a continuous space. However, we can interpret
109   them as categorical variables because they are direct mappings from the entities and relations.
110
111   Let S be a random variable with values in E, R a random variable with values in R, and
112
      D a random variable with values in E. The collection of all triples can be described by the
      joint distribution p(S, R, D). According to the general product rule, we can factorize this
113
      distribution, for example, in p(S) · p(R|S) · p(D|R, S) or p(R) · p(D|R) · p(S|R, D), etc.
114
115
116
117   Deep AutoRegressive Models AutoRegressive models are sequence models that originate from
118
      time-series models Ho & Xie (1998), where observations from previous steps are used to predict
      the value at the current time. However, as elaborated in the notes by Grover (2018), they have also
119
      proven useful in modeling joint distributions when the data is not inherently sequential (van den
120
      Oord et al., 2016; Salimans et al., 2017). To do this, the model assumes that the conditional distri-
121   butions correspond to a specific random variable, for instance, a Bernoulli random variable, which
122   means that we limit the expressiveness of the conditional distribution. Then, we can approximate the
123   distribution using parameterized functions e.g., neural networks, to represent the conditionals. To
124   enforce that assumption, we can use a Softmax for categorical variables or a Sigmoid for Bernoulli
125   random variables. The benefit of this assumption is that the entire joint distribution does not re-
126   quire global normalization. By ensuring that each conditional distribution is a valid and restricted
127   distribution, the global normalization is enforced locally.
128   Deep AutoRegressive Models for Knowledge Graphs Although a KG only has three variables,
129   specifying a probability for all possible triples of E × R × R quickly becomes infeasible, even for
130   small-size KGs Loconte et al. (2023). Fortunately, the KG data is inherently categorical. Therefore,
131   the assumption that random variables have to be Bernoulli is not an issue, and an autoregressive
132   model is well suited to approximate the joint distribution p(S, R, O) in one of the factorized forms.
133   Optimization Generative models optimize the distance between the data and the model distribution.
134   By assuming that points in the dataset are sampled i.i.d. from the true data distribution, we obtain
135   an unbiased Monte Carlo Maximum Likelihood Objective.
136
      The goal of MLE objective is to find the parameters, θ, that maximize the log-probability of the
137
      observed data points. Generative models are designed to generate new data points from the learned
138
      distribution. Generating new data points in AutoRegressive models is an iterative and, thus, slow
139   process. Besides that we use an AutoRegressive model to learn only two variables i.e., two iterations,
140   we do not require generating new triples. We are only interested in evaluating the likelihood of a
141   triple, which can be done in parallel.
142

144   3   R ELATED W ORK
145
146   One of the motivations for implementing an AutoRegressive model using Convolutions, as discussed
147   in Section 4, is to highlight its similarity to ConvE (Dettmers et al., 2018). Both approaches utilize
148   Convolutions on embedding vectors. ConvE estimates the distribution p(Ysr |S, R), treating each
149   edge as a Bernoulli random variable (Loconte et al., 2023). Our model replaces the Sigmoid func-
150   tion with a Softmax function to learn p(D|S, R). Additionally, we introduce a conditional distribu-
151   tion p(R|S) and incorporate an exogenous prior p(S). Furthermore, we do not rely on generating
152
      artificial negative triples, our approach focuses on modeling the distribution of the triples directly,
      moving away from classification.
153
154   Other methods have applied AutoRegressive models to assign a likelihood to triples (Chen et al.,
155   2021; Yao et al., 2019; Tresp et al., 2021; You et al., 2018). Those methods either use Large Lan-
156   guage Models, model local subgraphs, or do not interpret the data describing a KG as a collection of
157   three categorical variables. In this study, we focus explicitly on methods that learn a joint distribu-
158
      tion of three categorical random variables and are trained using MLE. To the best of our knowledge,
      this has only been accomplished by Loconte et al. (2023).
159
160   They use a different class of generative models to model the data distribution: Energy-Based Mod-
161   els (EBMs). Contrary to AutoRegressive models, there is no restriction on the expressiveness of the
      function representing the distribution. Naturally, the function must still be a valid probability distri-


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      bution where the total outcome equals one and all probabilities are larger or equal to zero. EBMs,
163   thus, are more expressive but require global normalization. Calculating the normalization constant
164   for KGs, i.e. evaluating all possible triples, requires the summation of E × R × E triples at every
165   training step and is infeasible even for small KGs. Exploiting the inherent structure of certain KGE
166   models, namely viewing them as parameterized structured computational graphs circuits, they are
167   able to calculate the normalization constant in O((|E|+|R|)·cost(ϕ)) time. With this normalization,
168   they converted the binary classification models p(Y |X) into generative models p(Y, X).
169   The tensor factorization methods that they convert typically have simple scoring functions, e.g, the
170   scoring function of Complex (Trouillon et al., 2016) is simply the (complex) dot product of the
171   embedding vectors. This can be considered a feature when the goal is to learn a relatively simple
172   ranking function. When learning a more intricate function, such as a joint distribution, this can
173   become a limitation; by using neural networks to model the joint distribution, we can have more
174   parameters in the scoring function to potentially better capture this function.
175   TractOR (Friedman & den Broeck, 2020) decomposes the binary classification models by assuming
176   conditional independence, such that p(Ysro = 1|S, R, D) = p(Es = 1|S) · p(Tr = 1|R) · p(Eo =
177   1|D). Although conditional independence is a strong assumption and TractOR is not a probabilistic
178   model but a decomposition, they do meet the assumption of Probabilistic Databases that require
179   every triple to be an independent Bernoulli random variable. Also, the decomposition into unary
180   statements ensures fast computation of the probability of a complex query in Probabilistic Databases.

184   In this section, we describe the challenges of designing an AutoRegressive KGE model called ART
185   and motivate our design decisions.
186
187   4.1   A DECOMPOSITION TRADE - OFF
188
189   A joint distribution p(S, R, D) can be factorised into multiple equivalent decompositions. Two such
190   decompositions are p(R) · p(S|R) · p(D|R, S) and p(S) · p(R|S) · p(D|R, S). Although equivalent,
191   they have different implications for modelling the data that describes a KG. The conditional distri-
192   butions can be learned with neural networks, while the marginal distribution can not. The marginal
193   distribution can be modelled with a prior. A KG usually has significantly fewer relations than entities
194
      e.g., WikiData5M (Wang et al., 2021) has 5 Million entities and just 822 relations. Therefore, the
      number of facts for every r is, on average, higher than the number of facts for every source. Ideally,
195
      we want to place the prior on r such that the prior distribution reflects the true marginal; this because
196
      there is relatively more information on this variable, resulting in a more informative prior. Unfortu-
197   nately, this comes at a great cost. Namely, this means that the complexity of modelling the second
198   conditional distribution increases drastically. Because when modelling the second conditional as
199   p(S|R), we now have to produce an output for |E| ∗ |E| values instead of the |R| ∗ |E| outputs when
200   modelling the second conditional as p(R|S). Because typical real-world KGs are large, we put the
201   prior on s. However, if the graph is small-scale, we advise placing the prior on r for the best results.
202
203   4.2   P RIOR SELECTION
204
205   Due to the fact that the marginal distribution, p(S), by definition, is not conditioned on another
206   random variable, this component does not have embeddings as input. This does not preclude ex-
207
      ploring several strategies to find a prior that accurately describes the true marginal distribution. The
      prior function can be any function based on background knowledge as long as it is informative of
208
      the true marginal p(S). In this work, we opt for a frequentist approach and use the proportion of
209
      sources (subjects) in the train set as our prior for modelling p(S). When working with infinite data
210   and assuming that the distribution in the data is similar to that of the train set, this will result in a
211   prior that resembles the true distribution. However, when we know or observe that the proportion
212   is not an informative prior, we want to reduce the impact of the prior, p(s), on the overall pre-
213   diction p(s) · p(r|s) · p(d|r, s). In its extreme, this is a uniform prior; every source is considered
214   equally likely. A less extreme approach is to smoothen the function with a Softmax; the degree of
215   smoothness can be controlled via a hyper-parameter T , often referred to as the Temperature (Renze
      & Guven, 2024). A more sophisticated approach is to model every entity with a logit (learnable

240   4.3   D IRECTIONALITY AND FAST I NFERENCE
241
242   By inferring p(s) · p(r|s) · p(d|r, s) ∼ p(S, R, D), our model can assign a probability to any given
243
      triple. However, KGE methods typically require scoring all candidate entities given a specific entity
      and relation, as in the cases of head and tail prediction in link prediction tasks.
244
245   For tail prediction, our model allows straightforward computation by selecting the relevant variables
246   and evaluating p(S = s) · p(R = r|S = s) · p(D =?|R = r, S = s). Since the first two components
247   are constant, and the third component is a Softmax over all entities, we can efficiently compute the
248   joint probability of all candidates simultaneously.
249   In contrast, for head prediction rank(d|r, s), the values for all three components differ, requiring
250   the triples to be collected iteratively. While this approach is feasible and valid, it is computationally
251   slow. To align with the standard KGE framework, we introduce an inverse triple for every KG,
252   a common practice introduced in Dettmers et al. (2018). Notably, rank(d|r, s) is equivalent to
253   rank(s|r−1 , d), allowing us to score all candidates simultaneously for head prediction, avoiding the
254   need for iterative processing. This approach also adds directionality to our model, enabling different
255
      confidence levels for the same triple, which is sometimes useful Trouillon et al. (2016).

      5.2   R ESULTS ON GLOBAL LINK PREDICTION
306
307   Task definition. Prior art has been mostly evaluated on query-based local link prediction. Essen-
308   tially, this task consists of ranking all possible completions for a given query and then assess the
309   position in the ranking of known true triples. While this method is useful to evaluate the predictive
310   power, it does not evaluate the ability of producing consistent scores across queries, which may be
311   needed in a downstream task, e.g., for complex query answering or global KB completion.
312   To fix this problem, we evaluate our method differently. Instead of evaluating using many rankings,
313   we construct one global ranking of facts by executing all queries from the test set and merging the
314   obtained scores together. Like in traditional link prediction, we filter out the triples from the train
315   and validation graph and duplicates (filtered settings). Moreover, we assume that unknown facts are
316   false, such that we can borrow standard metrics from Information Retrieval to evaluate the ability
317   of the models to rank the facts globally. This is an assumption adopted in the related literature as
318   well. We use the mean-average-precision (MAP), and find the optimal threshold for which every
319   fact is considered true or false. We optimise the precision and recall curve by considering as many
320
      thresholds as there are unique scores. We can then calculate the optimal F 1∗ score that balances
      precision and recall.

358   (with an equivalent MAP), indicating that it is more effective at ranking previously unknown true
359
      facts in a more plausible context. One may ask why we did not manually verify the truth of the
      unknown triples. The challenge lies in the nature of the dataset, which involves biomedical research
360
      data, where determining the truth value of triples is often a subject of ongoing research itself.
361
362   Case 3: Local non-generative methods are more suitable. This case can be seen on WN18RR,
363   where the generative models (ART and Complex2 ) are clearly outperformed by the discriminative
364   models (NBF and Complex). There are several reasons for that. Firstly, WN18RR is a small KG. The
365
      train subset only has 80K triples and 11 relations, which is problematic because generative models
      need sufficient training data to learn a complex function as the joint distribution while discriminative
366
      models need only to determine a truth value based on the local embeddings. Also, it is known
367
      that WN18RR is a rather regular dataset where there is not much interplay between the entities and
368   relation types. Consequently, methods that limit to learn local scores become competitive also when
369   there is one global ranking. Finally, it has been observed in WN18RR that there is a distribution shift
370   between the train and validations sets Loconte et al. (2023), which means that the prior distribution
371   may be misleading. Indeed, we observed only a 1% drop in performance when we artificially cancel
372   out our first two components i.e., p(s) = 1 · p(r|s) = 1 · p(o|r, s).

457   6    D ISCUSSION A ND F UTURE W ORK
458
459   Prior We have shown that it is important to have a prior that resembles the true marginal distirbution
460   p(S), especially when the frequency of the train facts does not resemble the true underlying distri-
461   bution. We have overcome this by internalizing the prior in the model. However, future work can
462   improve performance by learning more complex priors.
463   Compatibility with PDBs A natural next step in formalizing Complex Query Answering is to use
464   the extensive theory on PDBs Suciu et al. (2011). PDBs require independent Bernoulli variables for
465   every triple, that is, every triple is true or false with probability p. When modelling the joint distri-
466   bution over three categorical random variables, the total probability of all outcomes is one and thus
467   does not meet the requirement of PDBs. However, when the probabilities are learned independently,
468   the scores are not calibrated. Applying min-max normalization to unnormalized scores from a joint
469   distribution, such that every value is between zero and one, and using them as if they represent the
470
      score for a triple that is learned independently, led to better-calibrated scores Loconte et al. (2023).
      Learning independent yet calibrated probabilities to meet the requirements of PDBs remains an open
471
      challenge Friedman & den Broeck (2020).
472
473   Increasing the model complexity We have shown good performance with a stripped down trans-
474   former and simple 1D convolutional model on embeddings of rank 150 to emphasise the importance
475   of learning the joint distribution. Future work can increase the rank to 2000 as Complex in this study
476
      or increase the model complexity towards the level of NBF to future improve performance.
477   One Joint Distribution A distribution can not be learned if there is insufficient training data. How-
478   ever, we observe that some relations with sufficient training evidence are not picked up at all by both
479   probabilistic methods, Complex2 and ARM, please see the Appendix C.4. Why some relations are
480   considered out-of-distribution, while there is significant training data, is a question that we leave for
481   future work. Perhaps not all links can be easily captured in a single joint distribution. Next, another
482
      limitation of probabilistic methods is that if a link is missing exactly because it is out-of-distribution,
      then it can not be recovered.
483
484   To conclude, in this paper we have shown that by sampling the links from a joint distribution
485   p(S, R, D) we can learn consistent probabilities across the KG, with the additional benefit that
      we no longer need negative sampling for learning. Our results show that, unlike other models,


                                                          9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      ours only gives high scores to links for which there is a strong belief they are true. Therefore, the
487   scores can be reliably used for establishing the final truth value of the links, and consequently for
488   Knowledge Base Completion. Moreover, our method returns probabilities that can be used as-is
489   to perform Complex Query Answering systems and thus plugged in existing systems like Complex
490   Query Decomposition (Arakelyan et al., 2021), which require global consistency to simple queries.
