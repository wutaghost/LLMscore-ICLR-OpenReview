009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Recent studies in interpretability have explored the inner workings of transformer
012            models trained on tasks across various domains, often discovering that these net-
013            works naturally develop surprisingly structured representations. When such repre-
014            sentations comprehensively reflect the task domain’s structure, they are commonly
015            referred to as “World Models” (WMs). In this work, we discover such WMs
016            in transformers trained on maze tasks. In particular, by employing Sparse Au-
017            toencoders (SAEs) and analysing attention patterns, we examine the construction
018
               of WMs and demonstrate consistency between the circuit analysis and the SAE
               feature-based analysis. We intervene upon the isolated features to confirm their
019
               causal role and, in doing so, find asymmetries between certain types of interven-
020
               tions. Surprisingly, we find that models are able to reason with respect to a greater
021            number of active features than they see during training, even if attempting to spec-
022            ify these in the input token sequence would lead the model to fail. Futhermore,
023            we observe that varying positional encodings can alter how WMs are encoded in
024            a model’s residual stream. By analyzing the causal role of these WMs in a toy
025            domain we hope to make progress toward an understanding of emergent structure
026            in the representations acquired by Transformers, leading to the development of
027            more interpretable and controllable AI systems.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
031
      The study of world models (WMs) in AI systems has gained significant traction of late, yet much
032
      interpretability research focuses on large language models trained on diverse, complex datasets (Bel-
033
      rose et al., 2023; Lieberum et al., 2023; Olsson et al., 2022). In an attempt to seek a more compre-
034   hensive understanding, our work examines WMs acquired by Transformers (Vaswani, 2017) in a
035   controlled, synthetic environment. In particular, we use Maze-solving tasks (Subsection 2.1) as
036   an ideal testbed for understanding learned WMs due to their human-understandable structure, con-
037   trollable complexity, and relevance to spatial reasoning. Using this constrained domain, we can
038   rigorously analyze how transformers trained (Subsection 2.2) to solve mazes construct and utilize
039   internal representations of their environment.
040   Our methodology leverages Sparse Autoencoders (SAEs) (Bricken et al., 2023) to overcome the
041   limitations of linear probes in detecting WM features. While linear probes can and have been used
042   to identify latent directions associated with features defined in an imposed ontology, SAEs are found
043   to discover features actually used by our models to make decisions (Section 3). By manipulating
044   specific features identified by the SAEs and observing the impact on our models’ maze-solving be-
045   havior, we provide strong evidence that these features are causally involved in the model’s decision-
046   making process (Section 4). This stands in contrast to prior work analyzing the formation of WMs
047   in maze settings where no causal features were able to be isolated (Ivanitskiy et al., 2024).
048   Our findings provide important considerations for AI interpretability and alignment. By investigat-
049   ing how transformers form causal WMs even in relatively simple tasks, we hope to provide new
050   avenues for understanding representations and potentially steering behavior in more complex AI
051   systems. This work lays the groundwork for future research into how we might intervene on WMs
052   to better align transformer-based AI systems to desired constraints.

074   heads that appear to construct world model features by examining their attention patterns across
075   maze coordinate tokens (A). We validate these findings in Subsection 3.3 by training sparse autoen-

080   Contributions
081
082               • Empirical Findings: We show that transformers form WMs when solving mazes and that
083                 these WMs are causal: they can be intervened upon in the latent space of SAEs. Sur-
084                 prisingly, we find that interventions that activate features are more effective than those
085
                    that remove them, suggesting an asymmetry in how transformers utilize WM features. In
                    performing these interventions we also uncovered our models’ abilities to reason in the
086
                    presence of a out-of-distribution number of activated features than would naturally arise
087
                    for a given token sequence length.
088
089               • Methodological Insights: By effectively utilizing decision trees to isolate WMs features
090
                    in SAEs, we demonstrate that transformers utilizing different encodings schemes may use
                    varyingly compositional codes to represent their WMs. More generally, our analyses sug-
091
                    gest that SAEs are generally better suited than linear probes to isolate WMs, even in the
092
                    absence of feature splitting.

097   2.1     E NVIRONMENT
098
099
      Though it remains a matter of debate whether Large Language Models (LLMs) construct structured
      internal models of the real world, we can begin to understand the representations acquired by such
100
      models by focusing on “toy” tasks with clear spatial or temporal structure (Brinkmann et al., 2024;
101
      Momennejad et al., 2024; Jenner et al., 2024; McGrath et al., 2022). Previous works along these lines
102   (Li et al., 2022; Ivanitskiy et al., 2024; Nanda, 2023; Karvonen, 2024; He et al., 2024) have found
103   a variety of both correlational and causal evidence for internal models of the environment within
104   trained transformers. In this work, we utilize maze-dataset (Ivanitskiy et al., 2023), a package
105   providing maze-solving tasks as well as ways of turning these tasks into text representations. In
106   particular, we use a dataset of mazes consisting of up to 7 × 7 grids, generated via constrained
107   randomized depth first search (see Subsection 2.2) (which produces mazes that are acyclic and thus
      have a unique solution).

      2.2      M AZE S OLVING T RANSFORMERS
133
134   Utilizing the tokenized representations of mazes provide by the maze-dataset library, a suite of
135   transformer models implemented using TransformerLens ((Nanda & Bloom, 2022)) were trained to
136   predict solution paths in acylic mazes. We performed extensive hyperparameter sweeps (Figure 10)
137   over several variants of the transformer architecture, yielding models with stronger generalization
138   performance than those found by prior work Ivanitskiy et al. (2024).
139
      To allow the testing of generalization to large maze size, the models were trained on 5 × 5 fully-
140   connected and 6 × 6 sparsely connected mazes, embedded in a 7 × 7 lattice. This ensured that all
141   coordinate tokens in the 7×7 vocabulary had been seen during training time, such that generalization
142   to 7 × 7 mazes was conceivable but out-of-distribution during inference. For our experiments, we
143   investigated the two best performing models for each positional embedding (Su et al., 2024) scheme,
144   as shown in Table 1. Note that whilst these models had different numbers of heads, their parameter
145   counts varied only slightly - on account of Stan’s use of learned positional embeddings.

      3       D ISCOVERING W ORLD M ODELS
148
149
      Broadly speaking there are two ways to go about trying to identify internal world models: 1) Assum-
150   ing the form of the world model and inspecting the transformer with e.g. supervised probes to see if
151   this world model is present ((Nanda, 2023) SELF CITE Workshop proceeding), or 2) Exploring the
152   model internals and investigating any structure which may be present in the representations to see if
153   something akin to a world model exists. In our work we take both approaches.

      First, in Subsection 3.1 we investigate attention heads in the earliest layer of our models and find
163   heads specialising in the construction of representations akin to a world model. On the basis of this,
164   we use SAEs (an unsupervised method) alongside supervised classifiers to identify latent features
165   corresponding to the world model. Finally, we use patching experiments and interventions to show
166   that both investigations yield consistent features, and that these form a causal world model with
167   some interesting properties.

170   3.1                     W ORLD M ODEL C ONSTRUCTION : C ONNECTIVITY ATTENTION H EADS
171
172   We began by examining the attention patterns of the maze-solving transformer models and uncov-
173   ered a notable pattern: in both models, some or all of the attention heads at the first layer (“layer
174   0”) appear to consolidate information about maze connections into the ; context positions. In
175   particular, for all 4ith context-positions tokens (the semicolon separation tokens ; ), these heads
176   attend back 1 or 3 tokens - that is, to one of the two coordinate tokens corresponding to the given
177   connection preceeding the ; token. This pattern is observable for 3/8 L0 heads in Stan (Figure 3)
178   and 4/4 L0 heads in Terry (Figure 14). This observation suggests the hypothesis that these heads
179
      are in essence constructing a world model for the maze task, for use by later layers.
180   If this were the case, then we should expect that the output of these heads, mediated by the “OV-
181   Circuit” (Elhage et al., 2021), should consist of combinations of the coordinates captured in a given
182   connection. This can be measured by taking the WOV matrix for each head and measuring the cosine
183   distance between its elements and the model’s token embeddings (where coordinate directions are
184   directly given)1 . With this in mind, we investigated the structure of these vectors more closely. We
185
      find an intriguing pattern in the magnitudes of these vectors in the Stan model (Figure 4), while the
      patterns in Terry were less clear cut (Figure 5).

      3.2     W ORLD M ODEL R EPRESENTATION : S PARSE AUTOENCODERS
245
246   As previous work (Ivanitskiy et al., 2024) struggled to intervene on WM features identified via lin-
247   ear probing (Alain & Bengio, 2016), we trained Sparse Autoencoders to attempt to find disentangled
248   features in our models (Cunningham et al., 2023; Bricken et al., 2023). Sparse Autoencoders are
249   motivated by the notion of superposition (Elhage et al., 2022) which posits that artificial neural
250   networks store more features than an orthogonal representation would allow. By training an autoen-
251   coder with a higher-dimensional latent space than that of the transformer, tasked with reconstructing
252   a residual stream vector under a sparsity penalty, the hope is that the SAE will recover interpretable
253   features which the transformer was forced to superimpose. Similar approaches have previously seen
254
      success on other toy tasks (He et al., 2024; Karvonen et al., 2024).
255   To prevent “neuron death” in the SAE latent space, resulting from high sparsity penalties, we apply
256   the method of “Ghost Gradients” proposed by Jermyn & Templeton (2024). The resulting trained
257   SAEs faithfully reconstructed the activations (in our case, the residual stream after L0), and re-
258   placing these activations with their SAE reconstructed counterparts did not affect model behaviour
259   (Figure 8), giving confidence in the completeness of their representation.
260   Initial attempts to isolate SAE features corresponding to connections in the maze attempted to use
261   differences in the features present in mazes with or without certain connections. This approach
262   worked well in some cases, but not in others, as not all relevant features varied in magnitude by the
263   same amount, and many features were co-active to a given connection (i.e. those implicated in the
264   path representation, which itself might change when connections are added/removed). To address
265   this, we instead trained decision trees to isolate the relevant features in our transformers (akin to
266
      Spies et al. (2022)), as shown in Figure 6.
267   This analysis yielded our first unexpected finding: Stan’s WM consisted of two features for each
268   connection - a somewhat generic “semicolon” feature, as well as a connection specific feature. We
269   visualize highly activating examples of these features in Figure 17, and show that Stan’s representa-
      tion was stable for an additionally trained SAE in Figure 18.


                                                                                                           5
270
      We speculate that this “compositional code” arises in Stan as a result of the transformer imperfectly
271   separating positional information from its WM. This representation also explains why previous ef-
272   forts to intervene on models with learned positional encodings by using linear probes were unsuc-
273   cessful - as intervening with a single direction yielded from supervised decoding would also affect
274   the semicolon feature.
275
      It is also interesting to note that Terry encoded connection information very cleanly into single
276
      features for each connection - i.e., a single direction in the residual stream. This is in-spite of the
277   fact that Terry’s attention heads appeared to operate in a more entangled fashion than those of Stan.

303   In Subsection 3.1 we advanced the claim that certain L0 heads construct features representing maze
304   edges at the ; context positions, specifically by attending to earlier positions containing maze-cell
305   token embeddings, and rewriting those embeddings by application of their WOV matrices. Subsec-
306   tion 3.2 identified features representing maze edges via an independent line of reasoning, by training
307   SAEs, and identifying which of their features were indicative of the presence of a maze edge.
308   To verify whether these approaches yielded consistent features for the WM, we first calculated the
309   cosine similarity between the features written by isolated attention heads, and those encoded in the
310   SAE (Figure 7a). These showed excellent agreement for Stan, where the attention patterns were
311   clear, but only once the compositional code was taken into account (see Appendix G for details).
312   Though these results were promising, we carried out a further comparison (Figure 7b) to minimize
313   the assumptions required, and to account for two potential effects: 1) There may be “wiggle room”
314   between feature directions in the model’s residual stream, and the circuits that construct them (which
315   would lead to low cosine similarities, even for the same features), 2) As our SAEs are trained after
316   an entire block of computation, it is possible that the MLPs, applied after attention, also played a
317   role in forming the representations. In this second experiment we patched attention head values in
318   the presence of a connection to the mean of a maze set without that connection.By looking at the
319   effect of patching the attention heads on the resulting SAE Latent vectors, we were able to observe
320
      that the features considered relevant for any given connection were indeed sensitive to the heads
      implicated in constructing those features.

322   In particular, we consider the effect on the SAE features identified in Subsection 3.2 when each
323   attention head is patched at the semicolon position for with its average non-connection value across
      500 examples (i.e. removing the contribution a given head toward encoding that connection).

357        4                              I NTERVENING ON W ORLD M ODELS
358
359      Though a universally agreed upon definition does not exist, we shall consider world Models to be
360      “structure preserving [...] causally efficacious representations” (Millière & Buckner, 2024) of an
361      environment; i.e. representations which preserve the causal structure of the environment as far as
362      is necessitated by the tasks an agent needs to perform. As such, we are interested in understanding
363      how the WMs we have discovered are leveraged by our models to facilitate generation of valid
364
         solution sequences. In Figure 8, we give an example of perturbing a feature to “fool” the model
         into behaving as though it is in a different maze. When patching in the SAE-reconstructed residual
365
         stream without perturbations we still see the same behavior as in the original model; when patching
366
         in with a modified feature, we see a change in the path. We perform such interventions across 200
367      examples for each connection feature, and show the resulting intervention efficacies in Figure 9.
368
369      The intervention process involves toggling a feature on (to the maximal value observed for that
370
         feature in a small dataset) or turning it off (setting it to 0) at all semicolon positions2 . We measure
         the impact of these interventions on the model’s maze-solving accuracy, with a particular focus
371
         on how activating versus removing features affects performance. Our results reveal an intriguing
372
         asymmetry that constitutes our second finding: interventions that activate features tend to be more
373      effective in altering the model’s behavior compared to those that remove features.
374
375         2
              For the case of adding a connection, this is necessary as there is no semicolon in the sequence which
376      “belongs” to the connection that doesn’t exist. We also experimented with toggling to a fixed maximal value in
377      Figure 19, but this was generally less effective. In the case of removal, it made little difference if the feature
         was disabled everywhere, as it is almost always exactly 0 for a non-matching connection semicolon


                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                 7
378
       This suggests that the transformer may rely more heavily on the presence of certain connectivity
379    cues rather than their absence when constructing its internal world model.
380
381    Our final finding relates to the toggling of features in Stan. Though Stan utilized a compositional
382
       code, activating the connection-specific features at unrelated semicolons worked in 35% of cases.
       Conversely, we saw that all removal interventions failed for Stan, for the simple reason that Stan
383
       was unable to generalize to sequences containing more connections than it had seen during training
384
       - thus failing when shown examples containing the additional connection to be removed (this failure
385    was a result of Stan using learned positional embeddings (Table 1), as shown in Figure 10). The fact
386    that activating connections in the space of the SAE worked at all means that Stan’s maze-solving
387    behaviour was at least partially able to generalize in the latent space, where it was decoupled from
388    the positional embeddings.

427    5                 R ELATED W ORK
428
429    Our work builds on existing literature in interpretability (Räuker et al., 2023), particularly how trans-
430    formers develop structured internal representations, often called world models. World Models, as
431    defined by Millière & Buckner (2024), are “structure-preserving, causally efficacious representa-
       tions of properties of [a model’s] input domain.”


                                                                                                                                                        8
432
      Here, structure-preserving means that the representations reflect the causal structure of the observa-
433   tion space and causally efficacious means that the model leverages these representations to enable
434   relevant interactions with its environment.
435
436
      Research into world models has gained traction across various domains, with transformers trained
      to play complex games like chess being prime examples. For instance, McGrath et al. (2022) trained
437
      linear probes to extract various features in AlphaZero’s chess model, showing how different aspects
438
      of the game, such as piece positioning and potential future moves, are captured within the model’s
439   layers. Similarly, Karvonen (2024) investigates the internal representations of a chess model us-
440   ing linear probes and contrastive activations, revealing structured representations of the game state.
441   Jenner et al. (2024) explores the emergence of learned look-ahead capabilities in Leela Chess Zero,
442   where the model encodes an internal representation of future optimal moves.
443
      Another task used to study internal representations in transformers is Othello. Several works have
444
      explored the emergence of causal linear world models in this domain Li et al. (2022); Nanda (2023),
445   with recent advancements leveraging SAEs (see Subsection 3.2 to uncover these world models He
446   et al. (2024).
447
448
      Beyond game-playing tasks, the study of learned world models in transformers extends to other
      domains, such as natural language processing, where Hewitt & Manning (2019) used probing tech-
449
      niques to uncover the syntactic structure encoded by BERT. This line of research demonstrates that
450
      transformer models can implicitly learn hierarchical structures in their residual streams, as explored
451   by Manning et al. (2020). Further supporting this, Pal et al. (2023) demonstrated that the residual
452   stream corresponding to individual input tokens encodes information to predict the correct token
453   several positions ahead, highlighting the model’s capacity for structured, anticipatory reasoning.
454
      Additionally, graph traversal as multi-step reasoning has been investigated both from a model capa-
455
      bilities perspective Momennejad et al. (2024) and through mechanistic interpretability Brinkmann
456
      et al. (2024); Ivanitskiy et al. (2024), providing further evidence of transformers’ ability to encode
457   and utilize structured representations in complex tasks.

460   6   C ONCLUSIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
461
462   In this work, we demonstrated that transformers trained to solve maze navigation tasks form highly
463
      structured internal representations that capture the connectivity of the maze and thus act as world
      models. Through exploratory analysis of attention patterns, we found that connection information
464
      was consolidated into semicolon tokens by a subset of attention heads. By using Decision Trees
465
      to analyze the latent space of Sparse Autoencoders on these semicolons, we were able to identify
466   sparse features that encoded the position in the maze. We showed that these world models were con-
467   structed differently in transformers leveraging learned vs. rotary positional encodings, suggesting
468   that simpler methods such as activation steering or probing would have been insufficient to extract
469   causal world models in at least some cases. More interesting still, we showed that interventions to
470   add connections by toggling features were consistently more effective than interventions that sought
471   to remove connections by zeroing the corresponding features. Furthermore, we found that models
472   with learned position encodings, which were unable to generalize to longer input sequences (i.e.,
473   mazes with more connections), were able to behave consistently if additional connection features
474
      were enabled via SAE interventions, even if the corresponding token sequence would have caused
      the model to fail.
475
476   These findings shed light on the inner workings of transformers trained on sequential planning tasks
477   and suggest that maze-solving tasks are a rich testbed for understanding the formation of world
478   models in transformers. Future work should aim to uncover whether our findings on intervention
479   asymmetries and steerability are universal - and if not, which conditions give rise to each. An
480
      empirical understanding of the reliability of SAE feature discovery and steerability is crucial for AI
      Safety efforts that attempt to constrain or coerce model behavior through interventions or monitoring
481
      based on such methods.

      In an effort to better understand the notable “1- and 3-back” attention patterns appearing in heads

      vectors for token and positional embeddings, and their overlaps. The scalar products between queries
857   and keys of token embeddings for L0H3 are shown in figure 20. The most striking feature of this
858   plot is the row corresponding to the query vector of the ; token, and in particular its overlap
859   with the maze cell tokens. Plotting these scalar products on the maze cell grid (figure 21) a clear
860   pattern emerges, analogous to that shown in figure 4, accounting for LH03’s tendency to attend to
861   even-parity cells, and LH05’s and LH07’s tendencies to attend to odd-parity cells. Examining the

       In Figure 7a we compute the cosine similarity between SAE edge features and OV circuit edge
1137   features.
1138
1139   SAE edge features are formed from a linear combination of the specific edge feature and a “generic
1140
       edge” feature, with the generic feature coefficient of −0.6 being chosen to maximise cosine similar-
       ity.
1141
1142   OV edge features are formed from a weighted sum:
1143                                           X
1144                                               ahc WOV
                                                        h
                                                           tc
                                                   h,c
1145
1146   Here, h indexes heads L0H3, L0H5 and L0H7, with WOV       L0H3
                                                                      , for example, giving the OV matrix of
1147   L0H3. c indexes the two cells present in the edge of interest, and tc is the token embedding of a cell
1148   c. The coefficients ahc are given by the attention directed by head h to cell c from the ; context
1149   position following the specification of the edge of interest. Data was averaged averaged over 100
1150   examples (see Figure 3 for one such example).
