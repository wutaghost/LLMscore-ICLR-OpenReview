009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Current time series analysis methods predominantly rely on quantitative ap-
012            proaches, providing accurate yet often superficial statistical indicators. However,
013            these methods struggle to capture the underlying evolution patterns and lack in-
014            tuitive, qualitative insights. This paper addresses these gaps by seeking explicit
015            mathematical expressions for the time-varying nature of time series, offering a
016            more intuitive understanding. We frame this task as a combinatorial optimization
017            problem and propose a reinforcement learning-inspired approach. Using Monte-
018
               Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) as the basis, we incorporate symbolic regression to
               derive expressions for the non-linear dynamics in time series. To overcome the
019
               inefficiencies and excessive randomness in MCTS, we enhance it with neural
020
               networks, forming the Neural-Enhanced Monte-Carlo Tree Search (NEMoTS)
021            method. This integration leverages neural networks’ superior fitting capabilities
022            to introduce priors and replace the simulation phase, significantly improving gen-
023            eralizability and computational efficiency. Experiments on six real-world datasets
024            demonstrate NEMoTS’s clear advantages in performance, efficiency, reliability,
025            and interpretability.

      of time series data. They focus primarily on how data evolves over time, but largely overlook
034
      what drives these changes and why specific patterns emerge. By deriving explicit mathematical
035
      expressions for time series, we can uncover global evolution patterns across different timeframes
036   Angelis et al. (2023); Makke & Chawla (2022b;a).

072   However, current symbolic regression techniques, mainly designed for fitting specific sample, and
073   based on combinatorial optimization methods, often depend on complex heuristic designs to fit a par-
074   ticular case. They use simulation or search algorithms to generate expressions matching that case
075   Angelis et al. (2023); Makke & Chawla (2022b). These methods face challenges such as computa-
076
      tional inefficiency, high complexity, and restricted generalization abilities, especially when handling
      larger datasets Udrescu & Tegmark (2020); Nicolau & Agapitos (2021); Chen et al. (2016). The in-
077
      creased computational requirements and extended model search durations, particularly in extensive
078
      iterative processes, diminish their effectiveness in big data scenarios. Additionally, because these
079   techniques concentrate on fitting specific samples, they struggle to identify common patterns across
080   different samples and lack broader learning abilities. This not only hinders performance improve-
081   ment but also underutilizes the rich knowledge embedded in extensive datasets.
082
      To overcome these limitations, we propose Neural-Enhanced Monte-Carlo Tree Tearch (NEMoTS)
083
      for time series analysis. NEMoTS uses the Monte-Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) framework, where
084
      expressions are represented as tree structures. This approach aligns with parse tree structures and
085   follows context-free grammar rules, ensuring the validity of generated expressions Hopcroft et al.
086   (2001); Kusner et al. (2017). By balancing exploration and exploitation, NEMoTS narrows the
087   search space, improving search efficiency and expression quality compared to other methods Sun
088   et al. (2022); Kamienny et al. (2023). Considering that several challenges still affect the performance
089   of MCTS in symbolic regression, for example: during the selection phase, MCTS relies on random
090   selection without the guidance of a prior distribution, leading to exponential growth of the search
091   space and limited generalization capability. In the simulation phase, the complex rollout operations
092   are computationally demanding and time-consuming. To address these issues, NEMoTS integrates
093
      neural networks into the MCTS framework. Neural networks guide the selection of promising nodes,
      focusing the search, and replace complex simulations with advanced fitting capabilities, streamlining
094
      MCTS operations and improving efficiency. This integration also allows NEMoTS to learn from
095
      larger datasets, enhancing both fitting accuracy and generalization capacity.
096
097   NEMoTS consists of four main components: a pre-defined basic function library, Monte-Carlo Tree
098   Search (MCTS), a policy-value network, and a coefficient optimizer. At its core, MCTS guides the
099
      process. In MCTS, the selection and simulation phases are influenced by the policy-value network’s
      assessment and output regarding the overall state of the expression. Each operation within MCTS
100
      and the resulting expression originate from a pre-defined basic function library. MCTS produces
101
      an initial expression ’backbone’, which lacks numerical coefficients. These are then refined by the
102   coefficient optimizer to create a full expression. Expanding on ideas from Sun et al. (2022), we
103   integrate a Symbolic Augmentation Strategy (SAS) during training. SAS improves the simulation
104   phase of the Monte-Carlo tree search by accumulating high-rewarded composite functions. This
105   approach is akin to frequent pattern mining Agrawal et al. (1993), involving the random amalga-
106   mation of various basic functions to identify frequent, high-rewarded composite function patterns.
107   These frequently occurring composite functions are then added to the function library based on their
      average rewards, significantly enhancing the model’s fitting abilities.

190
191   In this section, we will discuss how to generate the ”backbone” of an expression using NEMoTS. It
192   is important to note that each node in the tree maintains two variables: the total reward Q and the
193   visited count N .

195   3.2.1              S ELECTION
196
      Initially, a node designated as ”Root” serves as a starting point for subsequent operations but does
197
      not contribute to expression generation.
198
199   The selection phase commences at the ”Root” node and involves iterative selection of child nodes
200   (potential mathematical operations from the pre-defined function library) until a partially expanded
201   or unexpanded node is encountered. This process is a tree traversal employing a specific recursive
202
      strategy known as the Polynomial Upper Confidence Tree (PUCT). Formally, under tree state S, the
      PUCT score for a child node a is given by:

221
222   3.2.2       E XPANSION
223
      The expansion phase begins after the selection phase, focusing on a node that is either partially ex-
224
      panded or unexpanded, referred to as the ”target node.” This node is critical for adding new elements
225   to the expression tree, central to the entire expansion process.
226
227   In this phase, a function from the library is randomly selected for expansion, following a uniform
228
      distribution. The selected operation, denoted as a , is then integrated into the tree as a new node. This
      node’s visit count N (S, a) and total reward Q(S, a) are initially set to zero. This method ensures
229
      equal opportunity for each function to be chosen, promoting fairness and variety in the exploration.
230
231   After integrating the new node, the process moves to the simulation phase. This stage is vital for
232   the overall search strategy, involving simulations to foresee possible actions and outcomes. These
233   simulations are key to shaping future decisions. The effectiveness of the simulation phase greatly
234
      affects the selection of future nodes and the development of the expression tree.
235
      3.2.3       S IMULATION
236
237   In MCTS, particularly within our NEMoTS framework, the simulation phase is key for assessing
238   the potential rewards of newly expanded nodes. This phase typically follows the expansion phase
239   and starts from the most recently added node in the expression tree. It involves a rapid simulation
240   method, often random, and continues until the expression path surpasses a pre-defined length,
241   the terminal condition. The focus here is on quick evaluation rather than deep exploration.
242   NEMoTS diverges from traditional random simulations, which are time-intensive. Instead, we uti-
243   lize the policy-value network’s reward estimator for immediate reward estimations. This approach
244   effectively evaluates the potential rewards of the current state and enhances the efficiency of the
245   simulation process.
246
      Crucially, during training, numerous random simulations are essential to provide supervised signals
247   to the reward estimator. This ensures the policy-value network’s scores are accurate and reflect real-
248   world outcomes. This accuracy is vital for the effectiveness and precision of NEMoTS’s simulation
249   phase. The simulation concludes when the expression path reaches a predetermined length. Follow-
250   ing the simulation phase, the generated expression path is transformed into an expression and further
251   refined by the coefficient optimizer. The optimized expression is then assessed with the input signal
252   as per Eqn. 1, leading to the back-propagation phase.
253
254   3.2.4       BACK -P ROPAGATION
255
      The back-propagation is a critical component in the MCTS, particularly in updating the decision-
256
      making mechanism. It follows the simulation phase and initiates at the node where simulation began,
257   often the newly expanded node, and proceeds back to the root node, referred to as the ”Root.”
258
259   In this phase, for each node along the path from the start node of the simulation to the root, we
260
      update both the visit count N (S, a) and the total reward Q(S, a). These updates are influenced by
      the simulation outcomes and serve to adjust Q(S, a), reflecting the new average or expected reward
261
      for an action a in state S. Concurrently, N (S, a) is incremented, indicating an additional visit to that
262
      child node. The reward data obtained at the end of the simulation is vital, as it helps in evaluating
263   the long-term strategic benefits of the node.
264
265   Back-propagation is integral to refining the overall decision-making process. It enables the algo-
266
      rithm to better understand and adapt to the decision space through continuous learning. This phase
      ensures more efficient navigation of the expression tree and improves decision-making by reinforc-
267
      ing successful paths and reassessing less effective ones.
268
269   The process iterates through these four phases until the expression path reaches a pre-determined
      threshold. At this point, a preliminary ”backbone” expression is formed, which still lacks specific

      3.3      M ODEL T RAINING
295
296   The NEMoTS training process involves two primary components: refining the policy-value network
297   and augmenting the function library through symbolic augmentation. The objective of optimizing
298   the policy-value network is to improve its accuracy and efficiency in aiding the MCTS process. On
299   the other hand, expanding the function library focuses on enriching the initial set of basic func-
300   tions with more complex ones. This expansion serves a dual purpose: it provides encapsulation for
301   expressions and customizes the library to more effectively match the specific dataset.
302
303   3.3.1        P OLICY-VALUE N ETWORK
304
      In NEMoTS, the policy-value network serves as a black-box model, handling two types of input: the
305   expression path sequence and the input signals, namely the time series being modeled. Its outputs
306   are twofold: the chosen operation and the estimated reward value, both based on the expression path
307   sequence and input signals.
308
      For our implementation, we employ Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks Hochreiter &
309
      Schmidhuber (1997) to encode the expression path sequence. To process the input signal sequence,
310
      we use Temporal Convolutional Networks (TCN) Oord et al. (2016). We then concatenate the en-
311   coded representations from these two sources and use a Multilayer Perceptron (MLP) for further
312   processing. This results in outputs for the three branches, as depicted in Fig. 3 (A).
313
314
      A key aspect of our approach is optimizing the neural network. This optimization primarily focuses
      on designing an effective loss function that aligns with the MCTS process requirements.

      3.3.2    S YMBOLIC AUGMENTATION S TRATEGY
343
344   In our study, we observed that relying solely on elementary functions in the function library often
345   proved inadequate for accurately representing complex nonlinear dynamical systems in symbolic
346   regression tasks. Consequently, the incorporation of more sophisticated composite functions became
347   essential for precise time series representation. Contrary to previous approaches that depended on
348   random selection in the MCTS simulation phase to identify high-reward expressions from a plethora
349
      of randomly generated composite functions Sun et al. (2022), our method takes a different path.
      We substituted the traditional random simulation with neural networks, which initially lacked the
350
      specialized ability to create specific composite functions for certain samples.
351
352   During the training phase, we implemented a strategy similar to frequent pattern mining Agrawal
353   et al. (1993). This involved tracking expression paths and their corresponding high-reward compos-
354   ite functions. Typically, these expressions with high rewards emerged from random combination
355
      selections. According to the law of large numbers Durrett (2019), among these paths, some consis-
      tently received high rewards. After training, we analyzed the frequency of these high-reward paths.
356
      The most recurrent paths were deemed as optimal composite functions for our dataset and subse-
357
      quently incorporated into our function library. This addition significantly enhanced our model’s
358   ability to represent time series and boosted the accuracy of modeling complex systems.
359
360   For practical applications, a novel element, termed ’augmented symbols’, is introduced into the
361
      function library. During the expansion phase in MCTS, if the model selects this ’augmented sym-
      bol’, it triggers a secondary sampling process. This process is based on a probability distribution
362
      reflecting the occurrence frequency of the top k high-reward composite functions. Under this mech-
363
      anism, a higher frequency of a specific ’augmented symbol’ in the dataset indicates its more preva-
364   lent usage during training, suggesting a better fit for the current dataset, as illustrated in Fig. 3 (B).
365   This approach markedly improves the model’s adaptability to specific dataset characteristics, thus
366   enhancing both the accuracy and efficiency of the symbolic regression process.

      A.1   S YMBOLIC R EGRESSION
674
675   Symbolic Regression (SR) emerges as a sophisticated technique in regression analysis, uniquely
676   characterized by its ability to simultaneously identify both the parameters and the functional forms
677   of equations that best describe given datasets. This method stands apart from traditional regres-
678   sion approaches, such as linear or quadratic regression, by offering a more holistic and adaptable
679   framework for data analysis Angelis et al. (2023); Tohme et al. (2022); McConaghy (2011).
680   Central to SR’s methodology is its data-driven nature, which operates independently of preconceived
681   models or theories about the system under investigation Kabliman et al. (2019). This characteris-
682   tic is particularly advantageous when dealing with datasets that encompass ambiguous or complex
683   relationships. SR’s capacity to unearth these intricate associations not only provides innovative so-
684   lutions to challenging problems but also fosters a deeper comprehension of systems that are only
685   partially understood. Furthermore, SR’s prowess in generating closed-form mathematical expres-
686   sions renders it an invaluable asset in the realm of generalizable AI. Its compatibility with various
687
      modeling tools, such as finite element solvers, underscores its versatility and broad applicability
      Gilpin (2021).
688
689   One of the most notable achievements of SR is its ability to rediscover and validate fundamental
690   physical laws, exemplified by its replication of Newton’s law of gravitation through purely data-
691   driven means Lemos et al. (2023); Liu & Tegmark (2021). This capacity underscores SR’s potential
692   in empirically grounding theoretical constructs. However, it’s important to acknowledge the chal-
693
      lenges inherent in this method. The risk of deriving spurious results due to oversimplified datasets or
      the absence of robust evaluation metrics is a notable concern Matsubara et al. (2022). SR shows par-
694
      ticular efficacy in analyzing complex, nonlinear dynamic systems, distilling governing expressions
695
      directly from observational data. Despite these strengths, SR’s effectiveness can be compromised
696   by factors such as data scarcity, low fidelity, and noise. Nevertheless, the application of Bayesian
697   methodologies has shown promise in mitigating these limitations Wilstrup & Kasak (2021). Com-
698   pared to several machine learning models, SR has demonstrated superior performance, especially
699   in scenarios involving small datasets. However, a significant drawback of SR is its computational
700   intensity, as the evaluation of numerous potential equations can be time-consuming. This aspect
701   makes SR more suitable for scenarios with a limited number of input parameters Cao & Zhang
      (2022).

      A.2   M ONTE -C ARLO T REE S EARCH
703
704   Monte-Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) is a prominent method in artificial intelligence for making op-
705   timal decisions by sampling randomly in the decision space and constructing a search tree based
706   on the outcomes Browne et al. (2012). This approach has significantly impacted AI, particularly in
707   fields that can be modeled as trees of sequential decisions, like games and planning problems Silver
708   et al. (2016; 2017).
709   A typical MCTS consists of four main stages:
710
711         1. Selection: The process begins at the root node and involves selecting successive child nodes
712            until a leaf node is reached. This selection is based on a tree policy, typically the Upper
713            Confidence Bound (UCB) applied to trees, which balances exploration and exploitation.
714
               The UCB formula takes into account both the average reward of the node and the number
               of times it has been visited;
715
716         2. Expansion: Once a leaf node is reached, one or more child nodes are added to expand the
717
               tree, depending on the available actions. This step is crucial for exploring new parts of the
               search space;
718
719         3. Simulation: This involves simulating a play from the newly added nodes to the end of the
720            game using a default or random policy. The simulation phase is where MCTS diverges
721
               from traditional tree search methods, as it involves playing out random scenarios to get an
               estimate of the potential outcome from the current state.
722
723         4. Back-propagation: In the final phase, the results of the simulation are propagated back up
724
               the tree. The nodes visited during the selection phase are updated with the new information,
               typically involving updating their average reward based on the outcome of the simulation
725
               and incrementing the visit count.
726
727   MCTS is fundamentally a decision-making algorithm grounded in search and probability rather than

729   rithm encounters several challenges: (1) Its intricate simulation process requires numerous simula-
730   tions, resulting in high complexity and low efficiency; (2) As a decision-making algorithm, MCTS
731   operates only at the instance level and lacks the capacity to learn from large-scale data, thus missing
732   the opportunity for inductive learning from extensive datasets to enhance performance. This paper
733
      addresses these two critical issues. We adapt MCTS to the specific needs of symbolic regression
      tasks, focusing on discovering expressions while also aiming to enhance MCTS’s efficiency in this
734
      domain. Additionally, we enable MCTS to acquire learning capabilities, allowing it to optimize
735
      itself with vast amounts of data.
