010                                                A BSTRACT
011
012            Time-series Generation (TSG) is an impactful research direction, as generating
013            realistic sequences can be used to create educational materials, in simulations
014
               and for counterfactual analysis in decision making. It has further the potential
               to alleviate the resource bottleneck that arises from a lack of diverse time-series
015
               data required to train large time-series foundational models. However, most exist-
016
               ing TSG models are typically designed to generate data from a specified domain,
017            which is due to the large divergence in patterns between different real-world TS
018            domains. In this paper, we argue that text can provide semantic information (in-
019            cluding cross-domain background knowledge and instance temporal patterns) to
020            improve the generalisation of TSG. To do so, we introduce “Text Guided Time
021            Series Generation” (TG2 )—the task of generating realistic time series from hand-
022            ful of example time series paired with their textual description. We further present
023            a Self-Refine-based Multi-Agent LLM framework to synthesise a realistic bench-
024            mark for TG2 and show that the collected text descriptions are both realistic and
025
               useful for time-series generation. We develop a first strong baseline for the TG2 ,
               B RIDGE, which utilises LLMs and diffusion models to generate time series which
026
               encode semantic information as cross-domain condition. Our experimental results
027
               demonstrate that B RIDGE significantly outperforms existing time-series genera-
028            tion baselines on 10 out of 12 datasets, resulting in data distributions that are more
029            closely aligned to target domains. Using the generated data for training positively
030            impacts the performance of time series forecasting models, effectively address-
031            ing training data limitations. This work bridges the gap between LLMs and time
032            series analysis, introducing natural language to help the TSG and its applications.
033
034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036
      The generation of time series (TS) data is an important task in various domains, including finance
037   (Sezer et al., 2020), healthcare (Hong & Chun, 2023), meteorology and environmental science (Has-
038   nain et al., 2022). For example, realistic synthetic medical electrocardiogram (ECG) patterns can be
039   used to train medical residents (Hong & Chun, 2023), while simulating regional electricity usage can
040   be used for stress testing the power grid (Westgaard et al., 2021). Previous methods like TimeGAN
041   and TCGAN (Huang & Deng, 2023) utilise GANs to produce realistic TS, showing remarkable per-
042   formance even with limited labeled data. Similarly, VAE-based approaches enable decoupling the
043   mapping process from standard VAE training, allowing for precise control over generated outputs
044   (Bao et al., 2024). However, such models are confined to generating single-domain data. In contrast,
045
      generating TS representations from unseen domains during training introduces additional complexi-
      ties, as real data resources are often scarce, private, and highly valuable, while TS patterns and scales
046
      vary significantly across different domains. This stands in stark contrast to the domains of NLP and
047
      CV, where the availability of large-scale datasets has led to foundational Large Language Mod-
048   els (LLMs), which have demonstrated strong generalization and reasoning abilities (Brown et al.,
049   2020; Mirchandani et al., 2023), and have shown efficient utilization of data (Wang et al., 2024),
050   even in few-shot or zero-shot scenarios. In particular, their demonstrated ability to generate images
051   (Zheng et al., 2023) and videos (Liu et al., 2024e) from text prompts, creating a timely opportunity
052   to extend these capabilities to other modalities, such as time series. Leveraging text as a source of
053   cross-domain information for TSG via LLMs could facilitate the capture of complex patterns and
      semantic relationships, akin to their application in other domains.

054
      Recent research has explored two approaches to leveraging LLMs for time series analysis: adapting
055   existing LLMs to handle time series data (TS-for-LLM) and developing specialized LLMs for time
056   series from scratch (LLM-for-TS). The TS-for-LLM approach aims to utilize LLMs’ semantic capa-
057   bilities by representing time series as word embeddings, requiring minimal training and data (Wang
058   et al., 2022; Ye et al., 2024). Methods include aligning word and time-series embeddings through
059   clustering (Pan et al., 2024) and contrastive learning (Sun et al., 2023). However, this approach
060   faces challenges in accurately representing continuous time series data with discrete vocabularies
061   and may not necessarily require LLMs (Tan et al., 2024). The LLM-for-TS approach seeks a more
062   fundamental solution by pre-training models on time series data, as exemplified by TimesFM (Das
063
      et al., 2023) and Chronos (Ansari et al., 2024). While these methods have shown promising re-
      sults, they primarily focus on building foundational models for time series forecasting. In contrast,
064
      the challenge of cross-domain time series generation, particularly leveraging textual information to
065
      guide and enhance the generation process, remains underexplored. In addition, the limited availabil-
066   ity of time series data compared to NLP and CV domains poses a significant challenge in developing
067   models with emergent abilities similar to traditional LLMs, making it difficult to consistently meet
068   the data requirements for such approaches.
069
      We argue that using text to assist in cross-domain TS generation can help overcome the data
070
      scarcity issues inherent to the TS domain, as the knowledge provided can be transferred to other
071
      domains (Shang et al., 2021). Side-stepping the issues associated with TS-for-LLM and LLM-for-
072   TS, we do not directly input TS data into the LLMs or pre-train LLMs on TS. Instead, we take an
073   intermediate step by learning Text-to-TS prototypes (also known as bases (Harpham & Dawson,
074   2006)) that serve as basic elements to construct soft prompts. These prototypes capture underlying
075   temporal patterns, such as trends, seasonalities, and semantic information for domains, which are
076   used to generate TS data with a diffusion model. During training, the proposed model uses both
077   text descriptions and TS samples as input, employing a prototype assignment module to create tai-
078   lored “prompts” for each sample. During sampling, texts and few-shot samples serve as context to
079   generate “prompts”, which condition TS generation in a process akin to instruction tuning (Zhang
080
      et al., 2023). Here, LLMs act as assistants rather than generators, leveraging the accessibility of text
      while avoiding the limitations of conventional LLM-based TS methods. In this setup, the proposed
081
      model achieved state-of-the-art performance on the majority of datasets and demonstrated strong
082
      robustness in few-shot learning scenarios, particularly on unseen datasets.
083
084   The lack of resources for TS is particularly pronounced for TG2 tasks, which poses a significant
085   challenge in validating our proposed approach. This likely stems from the difficulty in precisely
086
      describing TS with words (Yang & Lee, 2009; Liu et al., 2024a). The nature of automatically find-
      ing textual descriptions for TS is akin to prompt optimisation for LLMs, where prompt variations
087
      greatly impact performance (T et al., 2024). Although automated prompt generation methods like
088
      random search (Zhou et al., 2023b), genetic algorithms (Liu et al., 2024c), and reinforcement learn-
089   ing (Guo et al., 2024a) have been developed, they were not applied for TG2 . To address this gap,
090   we leverage on the recent advancements in LLM-based multi-agent systems for complex problem-
091   solving (Guo et al., 2024b) and propose a role-based LLM collaborative multi-agent framework to
092   generate a high-quality benchmark for TG2 . The experimental results highlight the significance of
093   the proposed framework. Compared to the original text, the revised text achieves at least a 15%
094   performance boost. Additionally, multi-agent collaboration systems provide more comprehensive
095   outputs compared to the straightforward generated text.
096   To summarise, this paper makes the following novel contributions: First, We propose a multi-agent
097   framework to create a text guided time series generation TG2 benchmark. Our numeric experiments
098   show that the descriptions provide helpful information for time-series models. Second with this
099   benchmark, we analyse the impact of different types of time-series descriptions, which advances the
100   understanding of how LLM can be used to assist time series prediction and generation in a zero-shot
101   setting. Third, we propose B RIDGE, a novel text-based time series generation framework via LLMs
102   and diffusion. The proposed method outperforms all baselines on 10 out of 12 datasets and achieves
103
      the best performance on data from unseen domains in a few-shot setting, demonstrating strong cross-
      domain generalization. Finally, we show that the B RIDGE effectively addresses the lack of time-
104
      series resources as forecasting models trained on synthetic data perform similarly compared to when
105
      trained on real data.

      2     R ELATED WORK
109
110   Large Language Models for Time Series: Recent studies explore LLMs for time series (TS)
111   analysis. Some, like Das et al. (2023), pre-train models from scratch, while others, such as Chronos
112
      (Ansari et al., 2024), tokenize TS data to leverage NLP techniques. These methods achieve strong
      performance but require significant computational resources, limiting scalability. Alternative ap-
113
      proaches align LLMs with TS embeddings, as seen in EEG-to-Text (Wang & Ji, 2022) and GPT4TS
114
      (Zhou et al., 2023a). Enhancements include trend decomposition (TEMPO (Cao et al., 2024)), two-
115   stage fine-tuning (LLM4TS (Chang et al., 2023)), and specialized embeddings or architectures (e.g.,
116   UniTime (Liu et al., 2024d), GATGPT (Chen et al., 2023), ST-LLM (Liu et al., 2024b)). Time-LLM
117   and Lag-Llama apply LLaMA for TS tasks (Jin et al., 2023; Rasul et al., 2023). Despite progress,
118   challenges remain in bridging the gap between discrete text and continuous TS data.
119
120   Time Series Generation: Traditional TS generation has used various generative models to cap-
121   ture temporal structure. GANs were among the first, using supervised and adversarial objectives to
122   encourage temporal coherence, as in TimeGAN (Yoon et al., 2019). VAEs adapt to TS by adding
123   decoder structures for trend and seasonal components (Desai et al., 2021). Advances include vector
124   quantization with bidirectional transformers, enhancing temporal consistency (Lee et al., 2023), and
125   mixed models combining GANs, normalizing flows, and ODEs for complex patterns (Jeon et al.,
126
      2022). Denoising diffusion models (DDPMs) generate TS by reversing a noise-added Markov pro-
      cess and support conditional generation, although current models lack domain-specific conditional
127
      details (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015; Ho et al., 2020).

141
      We first explored whether it is feasible to directly use human-readable text descriptions to prompt
142   LLMs to improve performance. During the experiment, the LLMs still struggles to grasp the over-

      assigns them with the corresponding semantic prototypes. Then, a diffusion model is conditioned
      on the semantic prototypes and generate TS samples using few Text-TS pairs as demonstrations.

242   4.1                  P ROBLEM F ORMULATION
243
244   Consider training time series datasets Ds gathered from a specific data source s ∈ S. Given the
                       i
245   dataset D = {X1:τ  }N
                          i=1 of N samples of time-series, covering a period of τ time steps, the goal of
246   TSG is to learn a model distribution pθ (X) that approximates the data distribution qD (X). In this
247
      work, we aim to enable few-shot learning in cross-domain TSG, analogous to NLP where new data
      is generated based on task descriptions and examples, even in an unseen domain.
248
249   Definition 1 (Text-Guided Time Series Generation (TG2 )). Let Xs represent an observation from
250   domain s, which may originate from the training sets S or from an unseen domain. Given its corre-
251   sponding text description Us , our objective is to learn the conditional distribution pθ (X|Xs , Us ) to
252   approximate the true data distribution qDs (X), without providing a domain label s.
253
254   4.2                  S EMANTIC P ROTOTYPING
255
256   During model training, the data utilized covers multiple domains, necessitating the model to pos-
257   sess robust generalization capabilities to learn the distinct distribution characteristics of different
258   domains. However, during inference, domain labels are not provided; instead, only sample data and
259   its descriptions are available. Consequently, the model must infer the potential domain to which
260   the sample data belongs—if it aligns with any domains encountered during training—or deduce the
261
      characteristics of the domain if it is novel. This highlights the need for models capable of effectively
      analyzing and generalizing across diverse data distributions. To tackle this issue, we propose using
262
      semantic prototypes to encode knowledge from different perspectives and employ adaptive proto-
263
      type allocation to associate features with time series, referred to as Bases (Ni et al., 2023). Bases
264   represent a small set of fundamental features extracted from time series data. Each basis encap-
265   sulates certain core attributes of time series. While each observed sequence may exhibit different
266   realisations of these attributes, the bases should come from the same pool, meaning that every time
267   series in the dataset can be reconstructed by weighting these basis. Thus, we can utilize these bases
268   as common knowledge to bridge across domains. We define a set of latent arrays as prototype
269   P ∈ Rnp ×d to represent domain-agnostic time-series commonsense. The prototypes P are initially
      set with random orthogonal vectors and then fixed.

      4.3   S EMANTIC P ROTOTYPE A SSIGNMENT
271
272   Although the same set of prototypes is used across different instances, the degree to which each
273   prototype explains different instances varies. To address this, we assign prototypes to each time
274   series and text description pair, which serves as a condition for the generation model. For each input
275   sequence x (comprising both the time series and text embeddings), a weight vector is generated,
276   the dimension of which corresponds to the number of prototypes. This is achieved via a feature
277   extractor ϕ. Each element of the vector ϕ(x)i reflects the contribution of each prototype unit pi in
278
      the prototype set P , and these weights modify the attention mechanism used during generation. As
      a result, the model is conditioned on the assigned weighted prototypes. The weights are applied
279
      through an attention mask m, which operates on the attention weights for prototypes. To ensure
280
      sparsity, we discard prototype units that are assigned with negative weights by setting their attention
281   weights to zero. Formally, the prototype assignments are transformed into attention mask m as
282   follows:

307   4.5   D IFFUSION BASED T G2 M ODEL
308
309   As shown in Figure 2, instead of training separate models for each dataset, we propose a unified
310
      training approach that leverages data from multiple domains simultaneously. While each dataset
      may represent only a small fraction of the overall data distribution, this strategy allows the model
311
      to capture a broader range of patterns by sharing information across domains. During generation,
312
      both the time series and text data are fed into the model as joint embeddings, where we use an MLP
313   to project the text embeddings from the LLM into the same dimensional space as the time series
314   embeddings. The projected text is then prepended to the input embeddings, functioning as soft
315   prompts that condition the diffusion model based on the contextual information extracted from the
316   text. By constructing the conditioning input in this manner, the model generates samples that adhere
317   to the selected domain while avoiding being constrained by the general temporal patterns exhibited
318   in the selected samples. When the number of expected generated samples exceeds larger than the
319   number of input sample, we employ a strategy of repeatedly generating with each assignment in
320   selected samples until the number of expected samples is satisfied.

      7    C ONCLUSION
534   In this work, we explored the potential of using text to guide time series generation (TSG). We pro-
535   posed a multi-agent system for optimizing time series textual descriptions, as well as a TSG model
536   that incorporates text. Experiments demonstrate that concise text enhances TSG performance, with
537   our model outperforming baselines, particularly in few-shot learning, thereby demonstrating strong
538   generalization capabilities. Additionally, the results show that the designed semantic prototypes ef-
539   fectively utilize domain information. Our findings lay the groundwork for further advancing fully
      human-preferred text-based generation while also highlighting the challenges of this task.

840   A.5.1    F RAMEWORK W ORKFLOW
841
842   We propose a structured, multi-agent collaboration framework designed to iteratively optimize text
843
      generation through systematic refinement. While the system is capable of operating with a single
      team employing distinct strategies, our experimental results demonstrate that employing two inde-
844
      pendent teams yields superior outcomes in terms of both quality and diversity of generated outputs.
845
      As can be seen from Figure 8, the framework comprises three primary stages:
846
847   In Stage 1: Task Planning, a manager agent assumes responsibility for overseeing the workflow.
848   This agent coordinates all subsequent activities by distributing tasks and results from prior iterations
849
      to ensure seamless progress and alignment among team members. The manager also defines the ob-
      jectives for the teams, thereby establishing a structured foundation for collaboration. Stage 2: Intra-
850
      group Collaboration constitutes the core of the system, wherein two independent teams of agents
851
      work concurrently to refine the given text. Each team is composed of four roles: a planner, a scien-
852   tist, an engineer, and an observer. The planner serves as the team leader, formulating strategies and
853   supervising operations. The scientist analyzes the input data and formulates detailed optimization
854   plans. The engineer executes these plans, generating improved text outputs. The observer critically
855   evaluates the plans and outputs, raising questions to identify shortcomings and potential improve-
856   ments. Teams operate in iterative cycles, guided by the observer’s critiques. This self-refining loop
857   continues until the observer ceases to raise objections or a predefined maximum number of iterations
858   is reached. Through this iterative process, each team independently produces a refined output. In
859   Stage 3: Inter-group Discussion, the leaders of the two teams engage in a structured dialogue moder-
860
      ated by the manager. This stage facilitates the integration of insights from both teams, encouraging
      comparative evaluation and collaborative refinement of their outputs. The discussion continues until
861
      a consensus is reached, resulting in a unified solution that incorporates the strengths of both teams.
862
863   The finalized output is then subjected to Post-Processing. This phase includes a validation step,
      where the text is evaluated against a predefined model to ensure its quality and adherence to target

1220   C.1   T IME S ERIES G ENERATION M ODEL
1221
       TimeVQVAE(Lee et al., 2023) is a generative model designed for sequential data. It combines
1222
       the strengths of a variational autoencoder (VAE) with vector quantisation to discretise latent space
1223
       representations, making it effective for time series data. The model consists of an encoder that
1224   compresses the input data into a discrete latent space and a decoder that reconstructs the time series.
1225   TimeVQVAE is particularly useful for generating realistic time series samples while maintaining
1226   key temporal dependencies. The quantisation step helps in learning discrete representations that can
1227   be reused for efficient time series modelling and generation.
1228
       TimegGAN (Yoon et al., 2019) is a variant of the GAN framework specifically tailored for time
1229
       series data. It combines both supervised and unsupervised learning approaches, using a generator
1230   to create synthetic time series and a discriminator to differentiate between real and generated data.
1231   Additionally, it integrates an embedding network to capture temporal dependencies and preserve
1232   temporal correlations between generated samples. The model ensures that the generated time series
1233   not only closely mimic the statistical properties of the original data but also maintain the correct
1234   temporal ordering and dynamics. TimegGAN is particularly useful in applications requiring realistic
1235   synthetic data generation, such as forecasting and anomaly detection.
1236   GT-GAN (Jeon et al., 2022) introduces a novel architecture for time series generation by incorporat-
1237   ing both global and local perspectives. The model features two generators: one focuses on capturing
1238   the global trends across the entire time series, while the other focuses on local variations. The two
1239   components work together to ensure that the generated time series exhibit realistic patterns on both
1240   macro and micro levels. GT-GAN uses a two-stream discriminator that evaluates both the global
1241   and local outputs, ensuring high fidelity in the generated data. This model is effective for generating
       complex time series where both long-term trends and short-term fluctuations are important.


                                                         23
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1242
       TimeVAE (Desai et al., 2021) extends the traditional VAE architecture to model time series data. It
1243   uses an encoder to map time series data into a continuous latent space, from which the decoder re-
1244   constructs the original time series. The model captures uncertainty and variation in the data through
1245   the latent space’s probabilistic structure, making it well-suited for applications where capturing la-
1246   tent factors and generating multiple plausible future scenarios is important. TimeVAE can be applied
1247   to various tasks, such as anomaly detection, forecasting, and data augmentation, by learning com-
1248   plex temporal dependencies and generating realistic time series that adhere to the original data’s
1249   statistical properties.
1250   Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Models (DDPM) (Ho et al., 2020) extend the traditional genera-
1251   tive model framework by utilizing a diffusion process to model data generation. DDPMs begin with
1252   a Gaussian noise and iteratively refine it through a reverse diffusion process, gradually transforming
1253   the noise into a realistic data sample. This process involves a series of denoising steps where the
1254   model learns to remove noise from the data at each step, allowing for high-quality data generation.
1255
1256
1257   C.2   T IME S ERIES F ORECASTING M ODEL
1258
1259   Time-LLM (Jin et al., 2023) is a powerful TS LLM that outperforms specialized forecasting models,
1260   which repurposes LLMs for time series forecasting by reprogramming input data and employing the
1261   Prompt-as-Prefix (PaP) technique for enhanced context alignment.
1262   GPT4TS (Zhou et al., 2023a) takes advantage of pre-trained language and vision models for general
1263   time series analysis. By demonstrating that supervised fine-tuning (SFT) can successfully extend
1264   LLM capabilities to time series tasks, GPT4TS bridges the gap between natural language processing
1265   models and temporal data analysis. The model’s architecture shows the feasibility of applying large
1266   pre-trained models to time series, leading to significant performance improvements in various time
1267   series applications.
1268   LLM4TS (Chang et al., 2023) is an innovative framework that repurposes pre-trained LLMs for
1269   time-series forecasting, employing a two-stage fine-tuning strategy and a two-level aggregation
1270   method to align with and enhance the model’s ability to process multi-scale temporal data, out-
1271   performing state-of-the-art models in both fune-tuing and few-shot scenarios.
1272
       TEMPO (Cao et al., 2024) proposed using prompts to adapt to different time series distributions. It
1273
       demonstrates superior performance in zero-shot settings across diverse benchmark datasets, show-
1274   casing its potential as a foundational model-building framework for capturing dynamic temporal
1275   phenomena.
