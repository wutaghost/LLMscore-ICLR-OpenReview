011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            There are two primary approaches to learning from data: discriminative models,
014            which make predictions based on provided data, and generative models, which
015            learn data distributions to create new instances. This paper introduces a novel
016
               framework, Discrimination for Generation (DFG), as the first attempt to bridge
               the gap between discriminative and generative models. Through DFG, discrim-
017
               inative models can function as generative models. We leverage Neural Tangent
018
               Kernel (NTK) to map discriminative models into a connected functional space,
019            enabling the calculation of the distance between the data manifold and a sampled
020            data point. Our experimental results demonstrate that the proposed algorithm can
021            generate high-fidelity images and can be applied to various tasks such as Targeted
022            Editing and Inpainting, in addition to both unconditional and conditional image
023            generation. This connection provides a novel perspective for interpreting models.
024            Moreover, our method is algorithm-, architecture-, and dataset-agnostic, offering
025            flexibility and proving to be a robust technique across a wide range of scenarios.

050   1   I NTRODUCTION
051
052   There are mainly two approaches to learning from data. Discriminative approaches use sampled train
053   set to predict properties of test set from unknown distribution, and generative approaches generate
      new instances based on the distribution learned from sampled data. The two approaches are related


                                                       1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      in the sense that they are trained to understand the true distribution of data. Discriminative models
055   aim to make accurate predictions even for unseen data by learning patterns from the sample data,
056   which it generalizes under the assumption that it represents the entire distribution. Generative models
057   strive to replicate or create new data points that reflect the broader characteristics of the actual data
058   distribution, going beyond the limited set of observed samples. In other words, these methodologies
059   are equivalent because their ideal goal is to understand the ‘true’ data distribution. By empirically
060   understanding the connection between the two, we can obtain an important insight regarding how
061   deep learning handles data. Nevertheless, current line of researches draws light to only one direction
062   of the equivalence. There have been works that directly utilize generative models as discriminative
063
      models, (Li et al., 2023; Kingma et al., 2014a), while the other way around – using discriminative
      models as generative ones – is rarely discussed (Haim et al., 2022; Lee et al., 2024).
064
065   There have been successful works that demonstrated how generative models such as VAE (Kingma
066   & Welling, 2022) and diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020a) can be used as discriminators (Kingma
067   et al., 2014b; Li et al., 2023). Despite the successful conversion from a generative model to a dis-
068   criminative model, attempts to conduct generation with discriminative models are scarce. Similar
069
      works focus solely on training data reconstruction through model inversion (Wang & Kurz, 2022),
      which cannot generate unseen images within the data manifold. The work on Deep Support Vectors
070
      (DSVs) (Lee et al., 2024) is an attempt to find support vectors in any deep neural network. DSVs
071
      effectively generates visually plausible images even from high-dimensional data, and since the gen-
072   erated images visualize hidden decision criteria rather than being mere reconstructions, DSVs can
073   be considered a generative approach. However, DSVs can only generate images conditioned on
074   class labels, which limits its classification models, making it difficult to classify it as a true genera-
075   tive model. Additionally, it lacks a mathematically plausible explanation. These limitations are also
076   present in related works. On the other hand, our algorithm overcomes this issue.
077
      Our work ambitiously pioneers a way to transform discriminative models into generative models.
078   We leverage the Loss Tangent Kernel (LTK) (Chen et al., 2023), a variant of Neural Tangent Kernel
079   (NTK) (Jacot et al., 2018) to map the tuple of a dataset, training algorithm, and neural network ar-
080   chitecture to a trained network. This enables us to generalize the score function of diffusion models,
081   allowing it to compute loss directly from the trained model. The entire process is grounded in firm
082   mathematical justification. To the best of our knowledge, our work is the first attempt to integrate
083   NTK and the score function for utilizing discriminative models in generative tasks. Our method goes
084   a step further in achieving practical efficiency. While generative models are notoriously challenging
085   to train (Salimans et al., 2016) our approach offers the advantage of directly applying already-trained
086
      discriminative models for generation without additional training. Although the performance may not
      yet surpass state-of-the-art generative models, our method demonstrates that nearly all types of dis-
087
      criminative models can function as generative models, as shown through extensive experiments. In
088
      addition, we provide an eXplainable AI (XAI) method by visualizing specific features using only
089   model parameters. This innovative approach represents a shift from traditional feature visualization
090   techniques in model explainability.
091
092
      This paper presents Discrimination for Generation(DFG): a method for utilizing discriminative mod-
      els in generation tasks and explains the approach in a mathematical framework. The mathematical
093
      explainability is key advantage of our method over visualization of DSV (Lee et al., 2024). We
094
      show that any discriminative model can be transformed into a generative model using the universal
095   methodology. The generalization of DFG is validated thoroughly through extensive experiments.
096
097   We generalize the score-matching problem from probability space to the one involving arbitrary
098
      measures. The problem of calculating the distance of a data point to a manifold in the input space
      is transformed into the problem of calculating the distance in the functional space. We then show
099
      that this process can be written in the same form as the objective function of a conditional diffusion
100
      model. This is supported by experiments using various architectures, including ViT (Dosovitskiy
101   et al., 2021), ResNet (He et al., 2015), and DETR (Carion et al., 2020). Different training method-
102   ologies – such as self-supervised learning, classification, and object detection – were applied to
103   demonstrate that the proposed approach can be applied universally. In the end, we show that we can
104   generate images from discriminative models similarly to diffusion models.
105
      Our contributions are as follows:

114   2   R ELATED WORKS
115
116   Score-Based Generative models generates by approximating the actual probability of the data
117   it wants to generate (Hyvärinen & Dayan, 2005; Song et al., 2020b). The approximation cannot
118   be made directly, so diffusion process is employed. The process adds noise to target distribution
119   to create a distribution we know of – for example, Gaussian distribution (Ho et al., 2020b; Song
120   et al., 2020a) – and reverse the process to obtain a solution. In a nutshell, score-based generative
121
      models generate images by iteratively moving from Gaussian distribution towards image manifold.
      Training models to solve this problem requires delicate methodologies; learning a score function
122
      instead of function distribution (Ho et al., 2020a), handling data differently at each time-step. Noisy
123
      classification is also necessary to perform different classifications at different time steps.
124
125   Our method Discrimination for Generation(DFG) fundamentally solves the probability approxima-
126   tion task the score-based model wanted to solve but failed due to complexity. We directly approxi-
127
      mate the distance between data manifold and a sampled image by leveraging Neural Tangent Kernel
      (Jacot et al., 2018). Based on empirical and theoretical evidence that the discriminative model in-
128
      herently understands the data manifold, we leverage this to compute a generalized distance. This
129
      way our methodology allows the use of a pretrained discriminative model as a plug-and-play gener-
130   ative model without the need for additional training or new architectures. Similarly, the conditioning
131   process does not require any noisy guidance.
132
133   Model Visualization diverges into two lines of research. First, gradient-based method is used in
134   Feature Visualization, one of the approaches in eXplainable AI (XAI) (Zeiler & Fergus, 2014),
135   where images are generated to maximize a model’s specific output, such as neurons and layers (Er-
136   han et al., 2009) to understand how the model makes decisions (Mahendran & Vedaldi, 2015; Fel
137   et al., 2023). A fundamental problem with this method is that it relies on gradient ascent. We can-
138   not claim that such visualizations accurately reflect the model’s decision criteria. One example is
139   adversarial attack, which also exploits gradient ascent. The visualizations here manipulate inputs to
140   maximize specific objectives, however, the results do not provide any insights on decision criteria.
141
      XAI methods may generate plausible images, yet it is still uncertain whether these images truly
      reflect the model’s decision boundary.
142
143   Second is maximal-margin based technique. It employs stationarity condition along with the equiv-
144   alence between Support Vector Machine(SVM) and deep-learning models to reconstruct training
145   dataset (Lyu & Li, 2020). Since it is an application of SVM, most works are conducted in SVM-like
146   settings. Their work is usually limited to binary classification (Haim et al., 2022) and uses only small
147
      fraction of dataset (Buzaglo et al., 2023). The setting is also extremely overfitted and restricted to
      small dimensional data such as CIFAR10 and MNIST with simple architecture like ConvNet and
148
      MLP (Goodfellow et al., 2016). Although Deep Support Vector (Lee et al., 2024) is relatively free
149
      from such limitation discussed above, it is still bounded to classification tasks since it is based on
150   SVM. Naturally, it can generate images only when class labels are given as condition, requiring a
151   label pair for image generation. This consequence implies DSV lacks ‘true’ generation ability as it
152   cannot estimate unconditional image distribution.
153
      Discrimination for Generation overcomes the limitations the two model visualization methods have.
154
      Unlike conventional gradient-based methods that focus on maximization of specific neurons or lay-
155
      ers, DFG leverages the entire model and replaces gradient ascent with functional approximation.
156   This ensures that the generated data optimally approximates the existing manifold, making it the ob-
157   jective to provide the model’s decision criteria based on realistic images aligned with the underlying
158   manifold. These characteristics provide justification to apply our visualization on XAI area. Also,
159   DFG is free from all restrictions maximal-margin based technique possesses. We now insist we have
160   successfully transformed various discriminative architectures such as those for classification, SSL
161   (Self-Supervised Learning) and object detection to generative models in an architecture-, algorithm-,
      dataset-agnostic way.

      3     P RELIMINARIES

      Kernel Methods are conventional machine learning techniques where a bivariate kernel k(·, ·)
165   maps two data points in a high-dimensional feature space to inner product k(x, y) = →ω(x), ω(y)↑.
166   Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) guarantees the existence of the space ω where the close-
167   ness of ω(x) and ω(y) implies the closeness of x and y. Fixing one of the inputs, we get a univariate
168   kernel k(·, x). It is worth noting that this kernel exists in a functional space, i.e., it is a function.
169
170   Neural Tangent Kernel tries to explain the generalization ability of deep learning models us-
171   ing conventional kernel methods. It suggests that for every deep learning network being trained,
172   a corresponding kernel exists. The kernel is defined by the gradients of the given deep learn-
173   ing model, !(x, x→ ) = ↓ω f (x)↑ ↓ω f (x→ ), reflecting how the model trains in the input space.
174
      However, as NTK does not account for loss dynamics, Loss Tangent Kernel Chen et al. (2023)
      extends NTK by incorporating the derivative of the loss function. The LTK is expressed as:
175
      K(x, x→ ) = ↓f ε(f (x; ϑ))↑ !↓f ε(f (x→ ; ϑ)).

180   In this section, we introduce our algorithm Discrimination for Generation (DFG). The introduction
181   is done step by step. First, we generalize the computation method of the score model in Sec.4.1 and
182
      argue that the goal of this framework is to minimize the distance between the generated image and
      the data manifold. We revisit the idea that a discriminative model actually perceives the manifold
183
      when it achieves generalization ability in Sec.4.2. Then in Sec.4.3 we introduce the concept of
184
      Functor to which we can apply distance measure. We demonstrate that the Neural Tangent Kernel
185   (NTK) is a suitable Functor for the purpose. Finally, we explain DFG in Sec.4.4, the metric that
186   calculates distance between images and the manifold, using what have been discussed throughout
187   the three previous steps.

189   4.1   S CORE - BASED G ENERATIVE MODEL
190
191   Designing a generative model includes estimating data distribution p(x) using a model f . There are
192   two important points to consider. One is that p(x) is unknown. What we do know is the sampled
193   dataset Ds ↔ p(x). Another is, the image space Rhw is so high-dimensional that the manifold
194   M ↗ Rhw where actual data resides in is relatively small and hard to approximate.
195   In a sampling process of score-based generative model, we first sample x0 from a known distribution
196   and iteratively update it for T steps to generate xT . The resulting output is expected to be placed
197   within the manifold xT ↘ M. The update from x0 to xT is restricted so that the sample approaches
198   the manifold M every step. In other words, d(xt+1 , M) < d(xt , M) must hold for some distance
199   metric d. Finding the proper d is critical to successfully lure the sample towards the data manifold.
200   Sampling process can be understood as an optimization process that reduces the distance in this
201
      sense.
202   Since we have no access to M, the above process should be done using an approximation using
203   Ds ↗ M. This can be written as the following formula.

453   6     A PPLICATIONS
454
455   6.1   I MAGE E DITING
456
457   What a diffusion model learns is the reverse process of converting an image to pure noise. Based on
458   this property, editing tasks in diffusion include sending the object image to a noise-like image and
459   retrieving it back. Condition is injected into this process in the form of masking (Couairon et al.,
460   2022). For the case of DFG, what it conducts is not the denoising process but the direct estimation
461   of manifold distribution p(x). Editing is therefore possible at image level without adding noise to
462
      the target image. Masking is also unnecessary. The result of directly applying conditional generation
      to real image without adding noise nor masking is shown in Fig. 6. The outstanding point is that the
463
      semantic has changed without largely deforming the input images. For example, in the photo of a
464
      chimpanzee, a spot of light fallen at the background is naturally transformed to a head of iguana.
465   The body of the animal is trivially deformed to consist shadow and body of the iguana. This was
466   possible by consistently injecting our score function as restraint, preventing the result from straying
467   away from the manifold.
468
469   6.2   I MAGE I NPAINTING
470
471   Image inpainting is a technique that visually restore the damaged or omitted part of the target image.
472   To enable this, the ability to generate new contents according to the whole context it understood
473   is mainly required. Diffusion model is suitable for inpainting tasks in this sense. Our methodology
474   bears the same ability of solving inpainting tasks as diffusion models since it is, in the end, an
475
      algorithm that samples from noise. We generated an empty patch to mask the ground-truth and
      applied our DFG loss to it. Then the masked areas were effectively restored under the naive setting
476
      that only requires to meet the boundary condition. The results in Fig. 7 experimentally show that our
477
      algorithm shares the properties of diffusion models.

529   7    C ONCLUSION
530
531   In this paper, we demonstrate for the first time that discriminative models can be used as general
532   generative models, and introduce a schema called Discrimination for Generation (DFG) to achieve
533
      this. Using the discrimination model and the Neural Tangent Kernel, DFG directly calculates the
      distance from a data point to the data manifold and generates images based on that measure. We val-
534
      idated DFG through extensive experiments, showing that it can generate images in an architecture-
535
      and dataset-agnostic manner, with or without conditions. Furthermore, DFG can be applied to other
536   downstream tasks. It can be used not only for conventional vision challenges like image editing and
537   inpainting, but also for global explanations leveraging the concept of DFG.
