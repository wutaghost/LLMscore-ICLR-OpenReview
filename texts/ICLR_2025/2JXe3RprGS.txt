011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Turn-by-turn (TBT) navigation systems are integral to modern driving experi-
014            ences, providing real-time audio instructions to guide drivers safely to destina-
015            tions. However, existing audio instruction policy often rely on rule-based ap-
016
               proaches that struggle to balance informational content with cognitive load, po-
               tentially leading to driver confusion or missed turns in complex environments. To
017
               overcome these difficulties, we first model the generation of audio instructions as
018
               a multi-task learning problem by decomposing the audio content into combina-
019            tions of modular elements. Then, we propose a novel deep learning framework
020            that leverages the powerful spatiotemporal information processing capabilities of
021            Transformers and the strong multi-task learning abilities of Mixture of Experts
022            (MoE) to generate real-time, context-aware audio instructions for TBT driving
023            navigation. A cloud-edge collaborative architecture is implemented to handle the
024            computational demands of the model, ensuring scalability and real-time perfor-
025            mance for practical applications. Experimental results in the real world demon-
026            strate that the proposed method significantly reduces the yaw rate compared to
027
               traditional methods, delivering clearer and more effective audio instructions. This
               is the first large-scale application of deep learning in driving audio navigation,
028
               marking a substantial advancement in intelligent transportation and driving assis-
029
               tance technologies.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      Navigation system in the era of mobile internet have improved the driving experience by providing
035
      drivers with route information and directions in real-time via visual and audio instruction on the
036   navigation terminal Yang et al. (2024). Compared with visual information, drivers tend to rely more
037   on audio instructions for the sake of driving safety Zhong et al. (2022). However, current turn-by-
038   turn (TBT) driving navigation Fabrikant (2023) often find it challenging to strike a balance between
039   yaw rate, play timing, and play density for audio instruction. Overly complex audios can increase the
040   driver’s cognitive load, making it difficult to understand information quickly and safely. Conversely,
041   a simplistic audio may fail to provide sufficient guidance for navigating complex intersections and
042   intricate road networks, leading to yaw and compromised safety Large & Burnett (2014).
043   The principal difficulty in generating real-time audio instructions lies in how to play the accurate
044   and complete audio at the correct time. Current methods typically use rule-based policies or pre-
045   defined configuration tables Jensen et al. (2010); Large & Burnett (2014); Yang et al. (2021) that
046   lack the flexibility, makes it easy to fall into the seesaw effect between yaw rate, play timing, and
047   audio density. These approaches may result in audio instructions that are either too general, failing
048   to convey critical information, or too verbose, overloading the driver with unnecessary details.
049   To address these challenges, we propose a novel pipeline for audio instruction generation: firstly,
050   decomposing the audio context into modular elements, then the audio instruction model in charge
051   of element recall, play timing, and order selection, and finally generating coherent speech via text-
052   to-speech (TTS) module Kaur & Singh (2023). This pipeline transforms the complex task of audio
053   instruction generation into manageable components, allowing for precise and adaptable instructions
      that effectively balance informational content with cognitive load.

      Building upon this formalization, we introduce the first deep learning framework utilizing sequence
055   models for real-time, context-aware audio instruction generation in practical TBT driving naviga-
056   tion. Our method captures the complex dependencies and variations inherent in driving scenarios.
057   By leveraging sequence modeling, our approach effectively handles complex intersections and ef-
058   fectively reduces the yaw rate.
059
      Our main contributions are as follows:
060
061          • The First Deep Learning Based TBT Driving Navigation: To the best of our knowledge,
062            we are the first to implement the deep learning based audio instruction for practical appli-

      The basic flow of audio instructions is presented in Figure 1, illustrating key concepts within the
101
      TBT driving navigation we have defined. The navigation point represents the location where the
102   navigation system expects the driver to make a steering to avoid yawing, typically at a fork in the
103   road. The directed connecting line between two neighboring navigation points is called as segment.
104   All segments form the navigation path. The primary goal of the audio instruction policy during
105   navigation is to select the appropriate content at the proper time to prevent the driver from yawing or
106   violating traffic rules, where yawing refers to the driver deviating from the planned route provided
107   by the navigation system, which usually means that the instruction content is wrong or poorly timed,
      causing the driver to go the wrong way.

108
      The audio content must follow a standardized approach and be brief to ensure quick comprehension
109   by the driver. Consequently, when generating instruction audio, sentence diversity is not a consider-
110   ation, unlike other language generation tasks. This allows us to organize instructional audio content
111   using key elements and consistent connectors, where the key information entities related to driving
112   are called elements. We define a set of elements Eelem = {Eaction , Einfo } that encapsulate the key
113   information to be conveyed through audio instructions. These elements are categorized into:
114
115          • Action Elements Eaction : Elements that require the driver needs to turn the wheel following
116            the audio instruction, such as “turn left” or “merge right.”
117
118
             • Info Elements Einfo : Elements that provide information without requiring to turn the wheel,
119            such as “speed camera ahead” or “exceeding speed limit, reduce your speed”.
120
121
      Each audio instruction contains multiple action elements, at most one info element, play timing,
122
      and play order in the segment. We have listed all the element types in the appendix A.1. Based on
123
      this, we structure an instruction audio as: the elements that need to be revealed, the order within the
124   segment, and the play timing of the audio. The play timing is indicated by the relative distance from
125   the audio playing position to the navigation point. The term ”order” refers to the position of the
126   current audio in the segment after all audios are sorted by play position. It is related to the selection
127   of the connectors. Figure 1 shows the connections of play order in the segment and the organization
128   of audio content through an example: 3 green boxes represent 3 different instruction audios, each
129   with the same element ”turn left”. However, the connectors for the element vary depending on the
130   play order, resulting in corresponding content changes.
131   Many studies have been devoted to optimizing the content and timing of audio navigation messages
132   to improve driving safety and experience. Some researchers Yang et al. (2021); Bian et al. (2021)
133   investigated the effects of different cue timing patterns and cue message types through a driving
134   simulation experiment. They found that the interaction between cue timing and cue messages sig-
135   nificantly affected drivers’ psychological state and vehicle operation. And some studies investigated
136   the initiation function of in-vehicle audio commands and found that audio commands can effectively
137   facilitate drivers’ quick and safe responses to the road environment Keyes et al. (2019). Wunderlich
138
      et al. propose to use landmark augmented audio navigation to enhance the spatial awareness for
      drivers Wunderlich et al. (2023).
139
140   While these studies have advanced the understanding of how navigation prompt messages impact
141   driver behavior, they primarily rely on handcraft or rule-based audio instructions. While these meth-
142   ods have yielded commendable results in simulation or specific scenes, the ever-changing and intri-
143   cate nature of real-world roadways renders it manifestly inadequate to depend solely on handcrafted
144
      or rule-based audio instructions to guarantee effective instructions under all conditions. Enhancing
      the audio density might reduce yaw rates at straightforward intersections; however, the consequent
145
      increase in instructional content can infringe upon the timing of subsequent audio at complex inter-
146
      sections. This encroachment can lead to the compression or omission of critical elements, thereby
147   inducing driver confusion and route deviation. This dilemma is referred to as the seesaw effect
148   among yaw rate, audio density, and play timing in audio instruction. Nonetheless, by integrating
149   deep learning into TBT driving navigation and training neural network-based audio instruction pol-
150   icy on carefully curated high-quality data that exemplifies effective guidance, it becomes feasible to
151   leverage the robust generalization capabilities of neural networks. Such an approach holds the po-
152   tential to transcend the seesaw effect mentioned above, thereby further enhancing the performance
153   and reliability of dio instructionsaui systems.

163
164   To address the challenges in generating real-time, context-aware instructions, we model the audio in-
165   struction in TBT driving navigation as a multi-task learning problem. Enables the model to optimize
166   the necessary components for generating the audio simultaneously.
167
      As presented in Section 2, the audios within each segment have a strong spatiotemporal correlation,
168   so segments are selected as the granularity for modeling driving scenarios oriented towards audio
169   instruction. We sample features for the audio instruction model in segments at 1-second intervals:
170   xt1 , xt2 , . . . , xtT , where T is the total number of time steps the car passes the segment. The feature
171   components are listed in Appendix Table 9.
172
      Considering the importance of feature sequences for the audio instruction task, we aim to learn a
173
      function that maps the input sequence data to several outputs to compose audio instructions:

      real-time computation, and resource optimization. The responsibility of the cloud side is to embed
217   features that are relatively static in the segment. The edge side is responsible for embedding other
218   features that have high real-time requirements and then performing model inference on the edge side
219   along with the feature embedding sent down from the cloud. Detailed description and analysis of
220   the advantages of the cloud-edge architecture is provided in the appendix A.2.
221
      Since the current audio content generation is strongly correlated with the historical play within the
222
      segment we combine the features of the current timestep with the features of the previous n − 1
223   moments in chronological order as sequence data Xt = {xt−n , ..., xt−1 , xt } for model input. If
224   there is less than n audio data in the segment, zero padding is applied to the missing portion of the
225   sequence. Unlike conventional sequence labeling tasks, the TBT audio instruction task encounters
226   challenges in calculating sequence loss Huang et al. (2015) due to the inability to predetermine the
227   input sequence. Furthermore, the TBT audio instruction task does not conform to the autoregres-
228   sive training paradigm employed by large language models Zhao et al. (2023), as the current audio
229   instruction cannot be inferred solely from historical audios but is also strongly correlated with the
230   current driving context. Therefore, we have tailored a sequence model for TBT audio instruction by
231
      integrating the spatiotemporal information processing capabilities of the Transformer.
232   Upon completion of model inference, the predicted timing by the trigger head is used to assemble
233   a complete sentence by combining the inferred action via the action head and information elements
234   via the info head with connecting word templates based on the voice order head. The sentence is
235   then converted into speech via a Text-to-Speech (TTS) module and ultimately plays in the driver’s
236   navigation terminal. This process is iteratively executed throughout the entire navigation path, con-
237
      stituting a comprehensive TBT audio instruction system.
238   It should be noted that the action head and the info head are responsible for recalling elements
239   required in the current play content, while all candidate elements are given to the model as input
240   features. The candidate elements in the input features are generated per segment by the scheduling
241   unit based on the current road graph and path planning information.
242   The model architecture illustrated within the dashed box in Figure 2 can be broadly divided into 4
243   levels: the Feature Encoder, the Deep CrossNet Zheng et al. (2018), the GPT Decoder Brown et al.
244   (2020), and the MoE Prediction Layer:
245
      Initially, the sequence data undergoes Feature Encoder. All element-related features are embedded
246
      and concatenated, which are indicated by the orange arrows in Figure 2. The element embeddings
247   are then fully encoded and mixed through the Multi-Head Attention (MHA). After that, the mixed
248   element embedding is encoded via a Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP) along with other input fea-
249   tures. The Feature Encoder transforms the high-dimensional sparse feature representations into
250   low-dimensional dense vectors while capturing and preserving the intrinsic structure and semantic
251   information of the data, facilitating subsequent model processing.
252
      Subsequently, the encoded element features are combined with position embeddings. Different from
253   the existing position embedding method Vaswani et al. (2017); Devlin et al. (2019); Su et al. (2024),
254   we integrate domain-specific prior knowledge to transform the conventional absolute position em-
255   bedding into a combination of temporal sequence encoding and spatial semantic encoding. Tempo-
256   ral sequence encoding targets each effective time slice in the sequence, performing reverse indexing
257   and learning through a position embedding matrix. As for spatial semantic encoding, considering
258   that the density of audio instruction increases as the car approaches the next navigation point dnp , a
259   distance-based weight discount γ is applied. The greater dnp , the smaller γ. A detailed description
260   of the position embedding is given in Appendix A.3.
261   The data is then processed through the Deep CrossNet, which constructs and learns high-order cross-
262   feature combinations. The data is then merged through a residual connection and further encoded
263   by an MLP before being input into the next part.
264
      The GPT Decoder is designed to exploit the spatiotemporal coupling information inherent in se-
265   quential data. Each time slice in the data can establish associations with other time slices in the
266   sequence, rather than relying solely on adjacent time slice data. By computing the attention map,
267   the model adaptively captures the rich semantic information within the sequence. We choose a
268   GPT-like architecture for the spatiotemporal data processing module because its self-supervised
269   training paradigm naturally aligns with predicting current audio based on sequence features. This


                                                        5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      autoregressive framework allows the model to effectively learn temporal dependencies within the
271   sequence data, enhancing its capacity to generate accurate and context-aware audio instructions.
272
273   Finally, the sequence data processed by the GPT Decoder is fed into the MoE Prediction Layer for
274
      multi-task learning. This layer simultaneously learns to predict 4 sub-tasks necessary for generating
      an instruction audio: the audio trigger time, the action-type elements included in the audio, the
275
      information-type elements included in the audio, and the audio play order within the segment. The
276
      underlying shared features are learned using the MoE. Through different combinations of these
277   expert networks, each subsequent sub-task head can efficiently focus on the features most pertinent
278   to its specific requirements.

342   4.1   DATASET AND M ODEL CONFIGURATIONS
343
344   To train and evaluate our TBT audio instruction model, we construct a large-scale dataset derived
345   from real-world driving navigation logs. We collect navigation trajectory data from actual drivers
346   over 8 days, from June 11 to June 18, 2023. The audio instruction policy for online data collec-
347   tion is a language generation policy modeled using Hidden Markov Models, which will be denoted
348   as “HMM” in subsequent experiments. HMM is described in more detail in Appendix A.4. To
349   maintain data quality and relevance, we have strict selection criteria. Only navigation paths initi-
350   ated by cars are included, and we exclude trajectories with muted driver terminals, abnormal driving
351
      speeds, yawing, and GPS drifting during navigation. We then apply a secondary filter to the naviga-
      tion trajectories based on our domain knowledge. This helps us to identify high-quality navigation
352
      trajectories with normal element transmission and audio play timing that meets the expectations
353
      within each segment. Finally, these filtered high-quality real online trajectories are used to construct
354   the dataset for the audio instruction model training. A more detailed dataset generation process is
355   presented in Appendix A.4.
356
357
      The datasets are partitioned into training, validation, and test sets. Feature standardization is per-
      formed using the mean and standard deviation calculated over the dataset. Sequential sample data
358
      are constructed by concatenating individual positioning point samples. The final dataset comprises
359
      approximately 1.56 billion sequence samples, with 1.1 billion samples in the training set (including
360   10 million supreme quality samples for supervised fine-tuning), 140 million samples in the valida-
361   tion set, and 320 million samples in the test set. This extensive dataset provides a robust foundation
362   for training model and assessing its performance in generating effective TBT audio instructions.
363
      The model input features have 2139 dimensions, as detailed in Table 9 in the appendix. The length
364
      of sequence data is set to 3. The MoE part contain 3 experts. The model comprises 4 output heads:
365
      the trigger head outputs a scalar activated by the sigmoid function, the action head outputs a 28-
366   dimensional vector also activated by the sigmoid function, the info head outputs a 25-dimensional
367   vector activated by the softmax function, and the voice order head outputs a 5-dimensional vector
368   activated by the softmax function. The model parameters are 1,147,943. During training, the learn-
369   ing rate is set to 0.001, the batch size is 800, and the model is trained for 400,000 steps, which takes
370   approximately 38 hours on 8 NVIDIA T4 GPUs. Additional training parameters are provided in
371   Table 10 in the appendix.
372   For online deployment, the model trained with float32 is converted to the float16 model. The model
373   is split into two parts: one for the edge device and one for the cloud server, which is deployed on
374   the user’s navigation terminal and cloud server, respectively. The cloud part primarily consists of
375   cloud feature embeddings. After converting the model to ONNX using TensorRT, the model size
376   is approximately 807 KB, and it is inferred on the cloud server. The end part of the model is first
377   converted to ONNX using TensorRT and then to MNN Jiang et al. (2020), resulting in a model size
      of approximately 2.3 MB. This part of the model is inferred on the user’s navigation terminal.

      The A/B test experiment period collected vehicle navigation data via our navigation system from
      August 28, 2024, to September 3, 2024, spanning a week and encompassing data from about 600
406
      million segments. The primary results of the online experiment are illustrated in Figure 3. The
407
      comparison mainly focuses on the yaw rate, the average words played per segment and the average
408   play density of elements. The yaw rate is the ratio of yaw segments to all segments. Lower yaw rate
409   means more accurate audio instruction. The play density of an element is define as the element play
410   counts divided by the number of segments which can play this element. The average word count
411   is positively related to the driver’s difficulty in comprehending the content of the instruction audio,
412   and the element play rate is positively related to the amount of information in the output content of
413   the audio instruction model. In general, the more elements that are played with fewer average words
414   represents a better content organization ability of the audio instruction model.
415   Compared to existing HMM-based approaches, our sequence model achieves a significant reduction
416   in yaw rate. As shown in Figure 3 (a), except for the intelligent mode which has a slightly higher
417   yaw, our model achieves a significant yaw rate reduction on all other modes, especially concise and
418   minimalist. Note that since our daily online user volume is billions, 0.01% reduction in the yaw rate
419   represents success in helping hundreds of thousands people drive to their destinations correctly. So
420   the yaw improvement on the order of 0.01% is also significant.
421   Moreover, the increase in the average number of words played per segment, as illustrated in Figures
422   3(b) and 3(c), indicates that our model incorporates more main action elements with only 2-4 words
423   increase. More element play rates are revealed in Appendix A.5, and for most of them, the play
424   rate increases. This indicates that our sequence model breaks through the seesaw effect of yaw rate,
425   play density, and timing: with almost no increase in audio play words, the audio information density
426   increase is achieved by significantly increasing the element play rates, and the impact on the play
427   timing is few because the audio text length is almost unchanged. This in turn reduces the yaw rate.
428   In summary, the results of the real-world A/B test validate the effectiveness of our method in pro-
429   viding real-time, context-aware audio instructions that significantly reduce the yaw rate. In addition,
430   our model can adapt to drivers’ diverse navigation detail preferences, as evidenced by its superior
431   performance in different modes, highlighting its robustness and generalizability under real-world
      driving conditions.

529   In this paper, we introduce a novel deep-learning framework leveraging sequence models for real-

532   approach effectively balances informational content with cognitive load. Extensive experiments,
533   including real-world A/B tests and blind evaluations, demonstrated that our model significantly re-
534   duces yaw rates compared to HMM-based policies, successfully incorporates more informative ele-
535   ments into audio instructions without overwhelming the driver. And the ablation studies confirmed

      the critical contributions of each component in our model.
537   Our method represents the first large-scale application of deep learning in practical driving audio

539   work will focus on further optimizing model performance in complex scenarios and exploring per-
      sonalized navigation experiences by integrating individual driver preferences and behaviors.

785   As shown in Figure 4, we implement a cloud-edge collaborative architecture that cloud-edge collab-
786   oration framework for practical applications. This design leverages the strengths of both platforms
787   to enhance real-time performance, system scalability, and driving experience.
788   By deploying the model inference on edge devices, we capitalize on the computational capabilities
789   of user hardware. Processing data locally allows for real-time responsiveness, which is crucial for
790   delivering timely audio instructions in navigation. It minimizes latency and ensures that drivers
791   receive immediate feedback, enabling them to make quick and safe decisions on the road.
792   Offloading inference tasks to the edge also reduces the computational burden on the cloud servers.
793   This not only decreases operational costs but also enhances the system’s scalability by allowing
794   it to support a larger user base without proportionally increasing cloud resources. It prevents the
795   wastage of cloud server resources that would occur if all computations were centralized, especially
796   considering that edge devices often have underutilized processing power.
797
      While the edge devices handle real-time inference, the cloud server performs pre-processing tasks
798   and generates embeddings for static and complex features such as road graph data, a part of element
799   features, and personalized driver features. These computations benefit from the cloud’s superior pro-
800   cessing power and centralized data storage, which allows for up-to-date and comprehensive feature
801   embeddings that can be periodically updated without impacting the edge devices. The scheduling
802   unit on the edge is responsible for requesting the cloud-side model embedding for this segment from
803   the cloud at the beginning of each segment and for orchestrating the edge-side play model inference.
804   An important advantage of this cloud-edge collaboration is the flexibility it provides in updating the
805   model. With the inference model on the edge and feature embeddings on the cloud, we can update
806   components independently. This modularity accelerates the iteration cycle of the model, reducing it
807   from a monthly to a weekly timeframe. As a result, we can deploy updates and improvements more
808   rapidly, responding promptly to user feedback and evolving requirements. This agility opens up
809   greater possibilities for supporting additional features in the future, enhancing the system’s adapt-
      ability and longevity.

      Moreover, differentiating the tasks based on their timing requirements optimizes system perfor-
811   mance. Real-time processing is handled by the edge, meeting the immediate demands of navigation
812   instructions. In contrast, the cloud handles tasks that can be pre-computed, like embedding updates,
813   which do not require instant processing. This separation ensures efficiency by aligning computa-
814   tional tasks with the most suitable platform.
815
      In conclusion, our cloud-edge collaborative approach effectively balances efficiency, effectiveness,
816
      and cost. By leveraging the computational strengths of edge devices for real-time inference and the
817   cloud for intensive pre-processing tasks, we optimize resource utilization. The flexibility in updating
818   the model enhances iterative efficiency, allowing for faster deployment of improvements and new
819   features. This architecture not only improves the scalability and performance of the TBT navigation
820   system but also significantly enhances the driver’s experience by providing timely, accurate, and

875   It is worth noting that the online TBT audio instruction mechanism used in data collection is not
876   implemented through a neural network model but rather employs a Hidden Markov Model (HMM)
877   for mapping and Viterbi inference to select the elements and timing for audio instruction Zhou
878   (2012). We refer to this as the HMM model. The HMM model determines the number of elements
879   to be played based on the remaining distance dnp to the next navigation point and infers the layers
880
      accordingly. Starting from the last audio at the navigation point, the inference is conducted towards
      the current driver position. It then combines segment history audios, driver information, and other
881
      contextual features, scoring each candidate node in each layer based on a ranking score system to
882
      ultimately decide the audio content and timing. The observation probability of a node is calculated
883   as the average score of the element combination at the node:
884

                                          P (Ot | St ) =  (Savg + Scum )                                (8)
886                                                     2
887   where P (Ot | St ) is the observation probability of the node at time t. Ot is the observation of the
888   current node, which is the audio instruction consisting of the selected element and timing, and St
889   is the state of the current node, which judges the value of the audio instruction represented by the
890   current node. Savg is the average element combination score, calculated as the geometric mean of
891
      the scores of individual elements in the node’s element combination. The individual element score
      is setted based on prior knowledge. Scum is the cumulative element combination score, evaluating
892
      the attachment strength of the element combined with the main action element.
893
894   The transition probability primarily evaluates the accuracy and reasonableness of transitions between
895   nodes in different layers:
896                                       P (St | St−1 ) = Sdiff · Srep · Strans                           (9)
897   where P (St | St−1 ) is the state transition probability from the node at time t − 1 to the node at
898   time t. Sdiff is the information difference score, evaluating the information gain during the transition
899   between two nodes, including information increase, decrease, and repetition. Srep is the segment
900   repetition score, considering the global historical features within the segment and down-weighting
901
      elements that have already been played. Strans is the transition reasonableness score. Evaluating the
      rationality of state transfers based on priori rule constraints Finally, the optimal path and nodes are
902
      determined by computing the Viterbi algorithm over all possible paths.
