011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Nowadays large language models (LLMs) have achieved remarkable success in
014            various NLP tasks. However, they often misinterpret human instructions and gen-
015
               erate incorrect or outdated responses, highlighting the need for more effective con-
               tinual learning techniques. While recent efforts have introduced unlearning meth-
016
               ods to remove erroneous knowledge, existing approaches still struggle in multi-
017
               task learning scenarios. To overcome these limitations, we propose Locate-then-
018            unlearn, a new framework that identifies and selectively unlearns task-specific
019            neurons to enable efficient multi-task learning. We hypothesize that LLM neu-
020            rons can be broadly categorized into task-specific neurons for handling individual
021            tasks, and general neurons to maintain the model‚Äôs foundational capabilities. To
022            accurately identify task-specific neurons, the locating process includes: (1) rank-
023            ing task-related neurons based on their importance to each task, and (2) identifying
024            task-specific neurons by applying intervention to assess how neuron activity im-
025            pacts task performance, isolating those most critical to each task. We conduct
026
               comprehensive evaluations in two experimental setups: single-task specialization
               and multi-task generalization. The results show that our method significantly im-
027
               proves performance across both settings. This indicates that our method effec-
028
               tively balances model efficiency and accuracy in multi-task continual learning.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Large language models (LLMs) have recently demonstrated outstanding performance in diverse
034   areas such as natural language understanding (DusŐĆek et al., 2020), mathematical reasoning (Imani
035   et al., 2023), and knowledge-intensive question answering (Sun et al., 2024). However, despite their
036   impressive capabilities, LLMs remain prone to misinterpreting human instructions and generating
037   incorrect or outdated responses (Bai et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024a). This leads to the exploration of
038   various continual learning and lifelong model editing techniques aimed at refining LLMs‚Äô behavior
039
      over time (Ji et al., 2024; Wang & Li, 2024).
040   In addition to directly fine-tuning LLMs on specific tasks, recent studies have introduced unlearning
041   techniques to enable LLMs to discard specific erroneous knowledge while preserving their overall
042   functionality. Building upon this concept, Ni et al. (2023) propose the ‚Äúforgetting before learning‚ÄĚ
043   paradigm, where LLMs are first trained to forget incorrect answers before learning new information,
044   leading to improved performance over direct fine-tuning. This approach mirrors human cognitive
045
      processes, where learning is often more effective when mistakes are first identified and avoided.
      However, current unlearning methods face limitations in maintaining performance across multiple
046
      tasks simultaneously. One major issue is the lack of task-specific differentiation, which causes
047
      interference between the knowledge acquired for different tasks (Dong et al., 2023). This may
048   lead to catastrophic forgetting of previously learned tasks. And the difference in the order of fine-
049   tuning between tasks will also have a significant impact on the performance of the model (Bell &
050   Lawrence, 2022; Wang & Li, 2024). Additionally, fine-tuning all model parameters across multiple
051   tasks consumes considerable computational resources and significantly reduces learning efficiency.
052   While parameter-efficient fine-tuning solutions have been proposed, their effectiveness diminishes
053   in multi-task settings, making it challenging to strike a balance between efficiency and overall per-
      formance (Leng & Xiong, 2024).

054
      To address these limitations, we aim to identify regions within LLMs that are responsible for
055   handling different tasks. Inspired by the neuron definition from Chen et al. (2024b); Tang et al.
056   (2024), we hypothesize that neurons in LLMs can be mainly categorized into two types: (i) task-
057   specific neurons, which focus on processing particular tasks, and (ii) general neurons, which aim
058   to maintain the model‚Äôs core capabilities in text understanding and generation. From a perspec-
059   tive of parameter-efficient multi-task learning, we selectively update the task-specific neurons while
060   leveraging general neurons to preserve the model‚Äôs foundational abilities, thereby improving both
061   efficiency and effectiveness in multi-task scenarios.
062   The primary challenge in achieving this lies in accurately formulating the task-specific neurons for
063   different tasks. To this end, we propose Locate-then-unlearn, a new framework that identifies
064   task-specific neurons and selectively unlearns them to enable efficient and continual learning across
065   multiple tasks for LLMs. Based on previous works (Geva et al., 2022; Meng et al., 2022a), we extract
066   logit scores from the activation layer of each neuron, using these scores to determine the importance
067   of each neuron for the given task. Tang et al. (2024) utilizes the activation score of each neuron
068   to identify language-specific regions. Inspired by this, in our multi-task setting, we adaptively rank
069
      neurons based on their logit scores across different tasks. Then we can determine which neurons
      contribute the most to a specific task and filter out less relevant ones. Since some task-related
070
      neurons may exhibit high logit scores across multiple tasks, we use a neuron intervention method
071
      to further assess their task specificity. By comparing the difference in correct answer probability
072   before and after neuron intervention, we can identify neurons whose performance shifts significantly,
073   categorizing them as task-specific neurons.
074
075
      Once task-specific neurons are identified, we apply parameter-efficient fine-tuning on these neurons
      within the unlearning set. In terms of downstream task learning, we design two evaluation settings
076
      for comprehensive comparison: (i) Single-task specialization: Fine-tuning a separate unlearned
077
      model for each downstream task and evaluating each model independently. (ii) Multi-task gen-
078   eralization: Fine-tuning a single unlearned model across multiple task datasets and evaluating its
079   performance on all tasks collectively.
080
081
      Experimental results show that our method significantly outperforms all baselines in multi-task gen-
      eralization, demonstrating its superiority in enhancing the model‚Äôs ability to generalize across tasks.
082
      Additionally, in single-task specialization, our method achieves optimal results while reducing train-
083
      ing complexity, indicating that our approach effectively identifies task-specific neurons and balances
084   both performance and efficiency in multi-task learning.
085
086   To sum up, our main contributions can be described as follows:
087   ‚ÄĘ We propose Locate-then-unlearn, a new framework that facilitates efficient and continual learn-
088   ing for LLMs across multiple tasks.
089
      ‚ÄĘ We develop a new locating method to accurately identify task-specific neurons by assessing their
090
      importance and isolating those critical to each task.
091
092   ‚ÄĘ We design two experimental settings for comprehensive evaluation: single-task specialization and
093   multi-task generalization. Experimental results show that our method achieves significant improve-
094
      ments in both settings, demonstrating an effective balance between performance and efficiency.

      2.1   T HE S TRUCTURE AND K NOWLEDGE M ECHANISM OF L ARGE L ANGUAGE M ODELS
099
100
      The development of LLMs has revealed great potential in solving various NLP tasks. However, the
101
      occurrence of hallucinations in LLMs may hinder their broader adoption in real-world applications.
102   Consequently, neuronal interpretability has gained much attention in recent years. Several studies
103   have investigated the mechanisms underlying knowledge storage in LLMs. For instance, Geva et al.
104   (2022) and Meng et al. (2022a) have found that the multilayer perceptron (MLP) layers in Trans-
105   former models function as key-value memory, storing vast amounts of knowledge. Other works,
106   such as Geva et al. (2023a), Lv et al. (2024), and Yu & Ananiadou (2024b), have shown that knowl-
107   edge accumulates progressively throughout the layers. In this paper, we build on the perspective that
      factual knowledge is primarily stored within the MLP layers of LLMs.

      2.2   M ODEL EDITING
109
110   As the knowledge stored in LLMs may become outdated due to the rapid growth of our society, it is
111   essential to edit and update this information accordingly. Some recent studies focus on identifying
112   where knowledge is stored before editing. For example, ROME (Meng et al., 2022a) uses the method
113   of attributing logits to find the location of knowledge and then edits it by updating specific factual
114   associations. Meng et al. (2022b) is an effective method to locate knowledge and directly update
115   large scale memories. Our work follows the workflow of locating and editing by identifying multiple
116
      task-specific neurons and then updating them accordingly.
117
118
      2.3   L ARGE LANGUAGE MODEL U NLEARNING
119
      Machine unlearning (Cao & Yang, 2015) serves as an important technique to remove the knowledge
120   about the restricted data while keeping other knowledge and system abilities. Yao et al. (2023a) and
121   Maini et al. (2024) use the method of gradient ascent to unlearn harmful or private knowledge. And
122   Ni et al. (2023) employs parametric arithmetic to facilitate the forgetting of old knowledge and learn-
123   ing of new knowledge. They first finetune LLM on the old knowledge and use the original model
124   to subtract the old knowledge parameters to finish the knowledge update. However, directly em-
125   ploying parametric arithmetic without considering the utility of each neuron can also be harmful to
126   model performance on other tasks. Chen et al. (2024c) proposes allow-redundant alignment method
127   named ALLO, focusing on optimizing the most related neurons with the most useful supervised sig-
128
      nals. They use the signal to detect unaligned knowledge and unlearn it. Our work further explores
      unlearning by not only identifying task-specific neurons across different tasks but also selectively
129
      unlearning these neurons to prevent knowledge conflicts and preserve model performance.

      5.1      DATASETS
281
282   In this work, we utilize five datasets, each representing a distinct task. For the knowledge QA task,
283   we employ ZsRE (Levy et al., 2017), a widely recognized Question Answering dataset that lever-
284   ages question rephrasings generated through back-translation. ZsRE contains over 160,000 samples.
285   For the logical reasoning task, we use SingleEQ (Koncel-Kedziorski et al., 2015), which comprises
286   508 questions, 1,117 sentences, and 15,292 words; this dataset helps train LLMs to enhance logi-
287
      cal reasoning skills. For the human safety alignment task, we apply PKURLHF (Dai et al., 2023),
      the first publicly available multi-round RLHF dataset in China, which includes constraints across
288
      more than ten dimensions, such as insults, discrimination, crime, psychological harm, and privacy,
289
      aligning LLMs with human values. Lastly, for natural language understanding, we use SST-21 and
290   QQP2 . While both datasets focus on semantic classification, SST-2 is aimed at sentiment classifi-
291   cation, whereas QQP focuses on similarity and paraphrase tasks. Thus, they can be considered as
292   representing different tasks.
293
294   5.2      E VALUATION M ETRICS
295
296   In the knowledge QA task, our goal is to modify answers for original and paraphrased questions
297   while preserving the responses for related questions. Following Ni et al. (2023)‚Äôs evaluation set-
298   ting, we measure our model‚Äôs performance using four metrics: Reliability, Generalization, and
299   Locality. The first two assess accuracy in editing original and paraphrased questions, while the
300   third ensures that answers to unrelated questions remain unchanged. For SingleEQ, QQP, and SST-2
301
      tasks, we use the Accuracy index to evaluate performance. In the PKURLHF task, which focuses
      on aligning outputs with human values and avoiding harmful content, we assess performance using
302
      the Harmful Rate indicator. This requires the GPT-4 model to identify and count harmful content
303
      in its outputs.

396   6.2                    A BLATION STUDY
397
398   We conduct ablation experiments to identify the most effective components of our method in multi-
399   task generalization settings, designing four verification methods. First, we consider the removal or
400
      preservation of overlapped neurons, these two constitute ablation methods called Remove all over-
      lapped neurons and Preserve all overlapped neurons. The third ablation method called Random
401
      selection involves randomly selecting one related task as specific while keeping overlapped neurons
402
      frozen during training on other tasks. Lastly, instead of using the designed algorithm with Skm , we
403   utilize Ckm as positioning indicators, which means that we only consider the impact of a neuron on
404   one task and ignore its impact on other tasks. This method constitutes the fourth ablation method,
405   which is called Locate-then-unlearn with Ckm .
406
      We observe from Table 1 that when we compare four ablation methods and our Locate-then-unlearn
407
      method, either preserving or removing all overlapped neurons is less effective than employing meth-
408
      ods that identify more specific tasks. Furthermore, Locate-then-unlearn using Skm demonstrates bet-
409   ter performance than the ablation method which uses Ckm , confirming that when we locate one-task
410   specific neuron, we should also guarantee that it has little impact on other tasks, otherwise, fine-
411   tuning this neuron in one task will also greatly affect its performance in other tasks, which will
412   greatly affect model performance.

      To verify our method‚Äôs adaptability on different parameter selections, we choose to first change the

424   gradually increases, but as őĽ increases from 0.3 to 0.9 the overall performance noticeably declines.

443   6.3.2    VALIDITY OF NEURONS ARE TASK - SPECIFIC AND OUR IDENTIFICATION METHOD
444
445   We conduct further experiments to verify neurons are really task-specific and the accuracy of our
446   identification method. We consider implementing experiments to verify by letting each task-specific
447   neuron learn on other task datasets instead of their respective task datasets in the single-task spe-
448   cialization setting, and we compare these results to those of unlearning and updating within their
449
      respective task datasets as well as full parameter fine-tuning. We have found that performance
      is better when we let task-specific neurons learn on their corresponding tasks compared with the
450
      other two settings, especially in ZsRE, SingleEQ and PKURLHF. For instance, when we use ZsRE-
451
      specific neurons to fine-tune the ZsRE dataset, the accuracy reaches 89%. In contrast, using QQP or
452   SST-2-specific neurons for the same task results in an accuracy of less than 40%. This demonstrates
453   that task-specific neurons only perform best when fine-tuned on their corresponding datasets, which
454   could perform even better than fine-tuning on all parameters, further validating the effectiveness of
455   our localization method.
456
      However, we observe slightly improved performance when using SST-2 task-specific neurons to
457
      fine-tune the QQP dataset and vice versa. This can be attributed to the fact that are less dependent
458   on task-specific neurons than the other three tasks, as using ZsRE, SingleEQ and PKURLHF-specific
459   neurons as well as full parameter fine-tuning can still yield good or even better results. This finding
460   aligns with the observation in Table 2, and we believe that SST-2 and QQP neurons have less reliance
461   on task-specific neurons compared to the other three tasks. Overall, the experimental results confirm
462   that our task-specific neuron localization is sufficiently accurate.

      7     C ONCLUSION
527
528
      In this paper, we propose a new method called Locate-then-unlearn, which consists of three main
529   steps: First, we identify all task-related neurons using activation probabilities. Next, we propose
530   a new algorithm to deduplicate overlapped neurons by comparing the correct answer probability
531   before and after intervening individual neurons, retaining only the task-specific ones. Finally, un-
532   learning and learning occur exclusively on these identified neurons. Experiments across five datasets
533   demonstrate that our method not only achieves significantly better results compared to recent ap-
534   proaches in multi-task generalization settings but also performs well in single-task specialization
535   scenarios. Additionally, we conduct further studies to validate the efficiency of our method, with
536   plans to explore more sophisticated techniques for task-neuronal localization to enhance knowledge
537
      updating effectively.

      A.1   C ONSTRUCTION OF FALSE KNOWLEDGE AND TRUE KNOWLEDGE
651
652   On dataset construction side, Since our work is based on forgetting the false knowledge first, in
653   the ZsRE and PKURLHF datasets false knowledge is presented as wrong answers, while in the
654   SingleEQ, QQP and SST-2 datasets false knowledge needs to be constructed through model gener-
655   alization. To say it more specifically, in SingleEQ dataset to one specific question we use the LLMs
656   to generate an incorrect answer that is inconsistent with the correct answer, and use the chain-of-
657   thought method to let the model generate a chain of thoughts that infers the incorrect answer. We
658   use the GPT-4 model to test the integrity and diversity of the generated chain of thoughts, and finally
659   the filtered chain of thoughts and answers together construct false knowledge. For QQP and SST-2
660
      tasks, as they all belong to classification tasks, true knowledge is given in the form of categories, so
      the construction of false knowledge adopts the method of randomly generating categories that are
661
      different from true knowledge.

790   A.4       M ULTIPLE FALSE ANSWERS COMPARING WITH ONLY ONE FALSE ANSWER
791
792   Since our main experiment is based on false answers within false knowledge, we conducted fur-
793   ther experiments to determine the optimal number of false answers. We conduct a comparative
794   experiment to evaluate the impact of using multiple false answers. In the SingleEQ dataset, which
795
      originally contained no false answers, we use GPT-4 to generate four plausible incorrect answers
      along with their intermediate reasoning processes, all differing from the correct answer. For the
796
      ZsRE and PKURLHF datasets, which each had one false answer per question, we added three ad-
797
      ditional incorrect answers based on the original false answer, resulting in four false answers per
798   question. For the classification tasks SST-2 and QQP, which have a fixed number of categories, we
799   are unable to experiment with multiple false answers.
800
801
      Results Table 6 shows that the method with multiple false answers performs slightly better than the
      single false answer method in SingleEQ but worse in ZsRE and PKURLHF. We believe this perfor-
802
      mance difference stems from the lower quality of the false answers generated by the LLM compared
803
      to those in the original datasets. The primary goal of incorporating false knowledge is to create a
804   gradient opposite to the correct knowledge, helping the model forget incorrect knowledge and learn
805   new information. However, since the LLM-generated false answers were not as representative as
806   original false answers, the model‚Äôs ability to forget was hindered, leading to reduced performance.
807   In the case of SingleEQ, where no false answers were originally present, using multiple false an-
808   swers showed a slight improvement in performance, but the effect was minimal. Given the time
809   costs associated with generating multiple false answers, we ultimately choose to use a single false
      answer per question in our main experiment.

837   A.7    E XPLANATION OF THE DEFINITION OF ‚ÄĚ TASK ‚ÄĚ
838
839   In this work, the five datasets represent the model‚Äôs knowledge question answering, logical reason-
840   ing, harmful replies, and the model‚Äôs ability to judge paraphrases and sentiment classification. They
841
      are quite different from each other, so we can define different datasets to represent one task. In
      this way, the five datasets represent five tasks respectively. To further verify that the five datasets
842
      are quite different, we draw inspiration from Leng & Xiong (2024) which defines task similarity
843
      to measure the relationship among tasks. In detail, We transform each task into a feature f that
844   can represent the task and measure the similarity distance of the features. We use the LLAMA2-7B
845   model to get the embedding of each task‚Äôs corresponding dataset. We perform padding operations
846   for different task embedding dimensions, and average the embeddings of multiple pieces to get the
847   feature represented by the task. The similarity between any two tasks a and b as follows:
848
849                                                           fa ¬∑ fb
850                                         sim(a, b) =
                                                          ||fa || √ó ||fb ||
851
852
       By defining features in this way, we can obtain the similarities between the five datasets, and the
      results are shown in the Fig 5. From this figure, we can see that the similarity values between
853
      different tasks do not exceed 0.5. In particular, only SST-2 and QQP have a similarity of 0.42
854
      (because they both belong to classification and have slightly higher similarity), while the similarity
855   values between other datasets are all lower than 0.3. This proves that the similarity between tasks
856   is very small, and the five datasets can be defined as five different tasks. This also verifies the
857   rationality of our task definition.
