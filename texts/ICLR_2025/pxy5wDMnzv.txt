009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Studying investor decision-making processes under herd behavior is of great sig-
012            nificance in microeconomics and behavioral finance. Large Language Models
013            (LLMs) can be leveraged to assist in solving specific classes of complex invest-
014            ment problems. However, the investment decisions generated by existing LLMs
015            often deviate from real-user data. One method to align LLMs with investor
016
               decision-making processes is Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT), which requires a
               substantial amount of real-user data that is costly to collect and raises concerns
017
               about privacy and security. To overcome data scarcity, in this work, we pro-
018
               pose InvestAlign, a low-cost and high-quality method that constructs large-scale
019            SFT training datasets based on the theoretical solution to a specific simpler opti-
020            mal investment problem, rather than the original complex one. We theoretically
021            demonstrate that fine-tuning LLMs with these datasets leads to faster parameter
022            convergence compared to using real-user data. By fine-tuning LLMs, we obtain
023            InvestAgents, which align more closely with real-user data than pre-SFT LLMs
024            in both the simple and original complex problems examined in our study. This
025            highlights InvestAlign as a promising approach with the potential to address com-
026            plex optimal investment problems and align LLMs with investor decision-making
027
               processes in economics and finance.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   In financial markets, investors typically make decisions based on their risk preferences to achieve
032   higher returns, lower volatility, and maximize their utility. (Merton (1969)). Investment decisions
033   are crucial as they not only impact individual financial outcomes but also shape market dynamics
034   and overall economic stability, making them a key driver of both personal wealth and broader market
035   efficiency (Ahmad & Wu (2022)). During this process, investment assistants such as financial ana-
036   lysts and fund managers, play a significant role by sharing their own investment decisions through
037   platforms (Brown et al. (2008)). These investment assistants often have rich investment experience
038   and extensive influence, leading investors to mimic their behaviors. This is commonly referred to
039
      as herd behavior in microeconomics and behavioral finance (Bikhchandani & Sharma (2000)). The
      prior works in (Wang & Zhao (2024a;b)) have investigated the optimal investment problem consider-
040
      ing herd behaviors between one investment assistant and one investor, and theoretically analyzed the
041
      impact of herd behavior on investment decisions. However, there are more complex problems where
042   the above models fall short or only provide qualitative insights, failing to offer optimal investment
043   advice (Zhou & Liu (2022)), which prompts us to explore alternative approaches.
044
045
      Large Language Models (LLMs) have been widely adopted in various domains as generative agents
      to assist with specific tasks (Kovač et al. (2023); Bran et al. (2023)). There is an emerging trend that
046
      LLM agents are equipped with human-like intelligence to simulate human decision-making pro-
047
      cesses (Gao et al. (2023)). In economics and finance, substantial works have been done on aligning
048   LLMs with human values and decisions, particularly in models for market behavior prediction and
049   the analysis of complex economic data for policy-making (Zhao et al. (2023); Lee et al. (2024)).
050   These efforts often focus on macroeconomic issues, such as information dissemination and collec-
051   tive decision-making in global markets (Li et al. (2024b)). To our best knowledge, little attention has
052   been paid to LLMs’ performance in microeconomics and behavioral finance, especially concerning
053   investor decision-making under herd behavior, and current LLMs are shown to not fully align with
      investors’ behavior in micro-level financial decision-making, as demonstrated in Section 3.

      Achieving the alignment of LLMs to investors’ decision-making processes often relies on large-
      scale real-user data in Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) (Zhang et al. (2023)). Fine-tuned with specific
065
      training datasets, LLMs can better reflect investor behavior in complex problems. However, it faces
066
      the following obstacles. Collecting real-user data can be costly due to the wide variation in investors’
067   attributes, such as risk preference and herd behavior degree (Abbot (2017)). Additionally, many
068   investors are reluctant to share their investment decisions due to privacy and security concerns.
069
070
      To address the data scarcity problem, note that for some simple problems such as the one in Wang
      & Zhao (2024a), we have already found its theoretical solution, using which we can generate a large
071
      amount of data. Therefore, one possible solution is, given a complex problem, we first identify a
072
      similar and simpler problem with a theoretical solution, construct the SFT training dataset using
073   this theoretical solution, and then fine-tune LLMs to solve the original complex problem. There are
074   several issues to be addressed when following this approach:
075
076
      Question A: Given the complex problem, how to identify a similar and simpler problem? What are
      the requirements and constraints?
077
      Question B: Do the theoretical solution of the simpler problem align with real users’ investment
078
      decisions, and can they be used to construct a training dataset that mirrors investor decision-making
079   processes?
080   Question C: How can we generate the training dataset based on the theoretical solution of the sim-
081   pler problem? How does it perform in aligning with investors’ decision-making processes compared
082   with real-user data?
083   Question D: How to adapt the fine-tuned LLMs to solve the original complex problem, and what is
084   its performance?
085   To verify the feasibility of the proposed approach and to address the above four issues, in this work,
086   we consider the following simple scenario of optimal investment as an example. Assume that there
087   are two agents, one is an investment assistant and the other is an investor whose investment deci-
088   sions are unilaterally influenced by the assistant under the herd behavior. For the original complex
089   problem to solve, we consider the relative herd behavior in Wang & Zhao (2024b) where the investor
090   adjusts his/her investments in response to changes made by the investment assistant within the same
091   time interval and imitates the changing rate of the investment assistant’s decision. Note that for this
092   problem, even though we find its theoretical solution, its computation complexity is very high. To
093
      answer Question A, we use the absolute herd behavior in Wang & Zhao (2024a) as the simpler
      problem where the investor replicates the entire portfolio of the investment assistant, and its estab-
094
      lished theoretical solution can be derived more easily. Note that the two problems are similar in their
095
      mathematical forms, while they differ in their methods of measuring herd behavior. Then, to answer
096   Question B, we collect real-user data on the simpler problem using interviews and questionnaires,
097   and apply statistical methods to validate the consistency between real-user data and the theoretical
098   solution. Next, to answer Question C, we construct SFT training datasets based on the theoretical
099   solution, and theoretically prove that fine-tuning LLMs on the above training datasets leads to faster
100   parameter convergence than using real-user data. Then, to answer Question D, given the training
101   dataset, we fine-tune the LLMs and develop the InvestAgents, which can make decisions similar
102   to the theoretical solution, thus aligning with real-user data. Finally, we conduct another real-user
103   test to verify the performance of InvestAgents on solving the original complex problem, and exper-
104
      imental results show that InvestAgents exhibit better alignment performance than pre-SFT LLMs.
105   In conclusion, our contributions include: (1) we explore and utilize LLMs in finance and economics,
106   particularly in the domain of optimal investment under herd behavior; (2) we propose the LLM align-
107   ment techniques, which construct a large amount of high-quality dataset effectively using the theo-
      retical solution of the corresponding mathematical model, and then apply SFT to fine-tune LLMs.

      2   R ELATED W ORKS
109
110
      LLMs in Finance and Optimal Investment. For finance-related tasks, several specialized LLMs
111   have been developed, e.g., BloombergGPT (Wu et al. (2023)), FinGPT (Yang et al. (2023a)), and
112   XuanYuan 2.0/3.0 (Zhang & Yang (2023)). The success of these models depends on large amounts
113   of training data, and the challenge is how to effectively collect and generate high-quality data, which
114   is a key goal of our proposed method. Focusing on the optimal investment problem, prior studies
115   have explored the use of LLMs in different scenarios such as investment idea generation and quan-
116   titative investment (Li et al. (2023); Wang et al. (2023a)). However, within agent-based modeling,
117   there are only a few works that use LLMs as generative investors to simulate or complement human
118   investor behavior, e.g., InvestLM in Yang et al. (2023b) and EconAgent in Li et al. (2024b). Sim-
119
      ilar agent-based ideas using LLMs have been widely used in many areas such as problems in the
      economic system (Horton (2023); Chen et al. (2023); Geerling et al. (2023)), social science (Ghaf-
120
      farzadegan et al. (2023); Liu et al. (2024); Wang et al. (2024b)), and natural science (Bran et al.
121
      (2023); Boiko et al. (2023)). While several studies in other domains have explored the LLM agents’
122   irrational behaviors to mirror human cognitive biases (Liu et al. (2024); Wang et al. (2024a); Xiao
123   et al. (2024)), existing agent-based LLM models for investment have not yet accounted for the herd
124   behavior (Bikhchandani & Sharma (2000)), which is significant in microeconomics and behavioral
125   finance. Understanding its influence on the optimal investment problem while incorporating LLMs
126   is crucial for analyzing investor behavior (Ahmad & Wu (2022)).
127
      LLM Alignment. LLM alignment with human values has emerged as a critical area of research
128   (Wang et al. (2024d)). AI alignment aims to make AI systems behave in line with human intentions
129   and values (Ji et al. (2023)). Although LLMs excel in various tasks, issues like untruthful answers
130   (Bang et al. (2023)), sycophancy (Perez et al. (2022)) and deception (Steinhardt (2023)), along
131   with the rise of LLM-based agents (Xi et al. (2023); Wang et al. (2024c)), raise concerns about
132   controllability and risks in advanced AI systems. To achieve forward alignment, which ensures that
133   trained systems meet alignment requirements, numerous methods for policy learning and scalable
134   oversight are proposed (Ji et al. (2023); Wang et al. (2024d)). For LLMs, a typical approach is
135   reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) (Christiano et al. (2017)), with extensions like
136
      reinforcement learning from AI feedback (RLAIF) (Bai et al. (2022)) and reinforcement learning
      from human and AI feedback (RLHAIF) (Bowman et al. (2022)). Their pipeline includes supervised
137
      fine-tuning (SFT) (Ouyang et al. (2022); Rafailov et al. (2024)). In economics and finance, only a
138
      limited number of studies involve LLMs (Li et al. (2024b); Horton (2023)), focusing on macro-level
139   alignment while ignoring specific and microcosmic behaviors of human decision-making.
140
141   SFT Methods in Optimal Investment. Supervised fine-tuning (SFT), also known as instruction
142
      tuning, is a widely adopted technique in the field of LLMs for improving model performance on
      specific tasks by refining pre-trained models with a dataset tailored to the target task (Zhang et al.
143
      (2023)). Many tricks and methods of SFT have been proposed to achieve better preference alignment
144
      of LLM to humans, e.g., Ding et al. (2023); Wang et al. (2023b); Xie et al. (2024); Li et al. (2024a).
145   In the domain of finance, SFT has been applied to various investment-related tasks such as stock
146   prediction, financial reports summarization, sentiment analysis, portfolio optimization, etc. (Zhao
147   et al. (2024); Guo & Hauptmann (2024); An et al. (2024)). These advancements highlight the power
148   of SFT in tailoring LLMs to meet the specific needs of investment strategies, enabling models to
149   simulate or complement human-like behaviors. However, collecting large, high-quality datasets for
150   fine-tuning in optimal investment remains a challenging problem (Abbot (2017)).

168   the deposit, v and σ as the excess return rate and volatility of the stock, and T as the terminal time.

      Herd behavior can be categorized into two types: (1) absolute herd behavior, where investors repli-
177   cate the entire portfolio of the investment assistant; (2) relative herd behavior, where they adjust their
178   investments in response to changes made by the investment assistant within the same time interval
179   and imitate the changing rate of the investment assistant’s decision (Lakonishok et al. (1992); Wang
180   & Zhao (2024b)). Considering the herd behavior, the investor jointly maximizes his/her expected
181   utility of the terminal fund Eϕ[X(T )] and minimizes the distance between his/her own and the in-
182   vestment assistant’s decisions D(P, Q). Following the prior work in Rogers (2013), we assume that
183   the investor’s utility of the terminal fund is ϕ[X(T )] = − α1 exp[−αX(T )], where α is his/her risk
184   aversion coefficient. In summary, the optimal investment problem under herd behavior is
185                                   sup{P (t)}t∈T Eϕ[X(T )] − θD(P, Q),                                      (2)

187   call the risk aversion coefficient α and the influence coefficient θ the investor’s investment attribute.

522   6      C ONCLUSION
523
524
525
      Studying investor decision-making processes under the herd behavior is of great significance in
      microeconomics and behavioral finance. LLMs can be leveraged to assist in solving complex in-
526
      vestment problems. To fine-tune LLMs for alignment with human decision-making processes, a
527
      substantial amount of real-user data is required. However, the cost of collecting the real-user data
528   is high, and there are concerns regarding privacy and security. To address these challenges, we
529   propose InvestAlign, a novel method that constructs training datasets using the theoretical solution
530   of a similar and simple problem to align LLMs with investor behavior under herd behavior. We
531   demonstrate that fine-tuning LLMs on these training datasets leads to faster parameter convergence
532   compared to using real-user data. The experimental results indicate that InvestAgents, fine-tuned
533   with InvestAlign, achieves superior alignment performance in the original complex problem.
534   As an initial exploration in this field, InvestAlign does not claim universal applicability to all com-
535   plex optimal investment problems, and its theoretical solutions may not fully encapsulate the in-
536   tricacies of real-world investor behavior. Our primary focus is on addressing a specific challenge:
537   data scarcity in training LLMs for investor decision-making. In future work, we plan to explore its
538   applicability across diverse investor profiles and complex behavioral biases. Additionally, we aim to
539   investigate the impact of RLHF on InvestAgents and compare it with SFT to assess the effectiveness
      of different alignment strategies in investment decision-making tasks.
