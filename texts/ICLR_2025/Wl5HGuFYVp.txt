009                                                    A BSTRACT
010
011                Label enhancement is a novel label shift strategy that aims to integrate the feature
012                space with the logical label space to obtain a high-quality label distribution. This
013                label distribution can serve as a soft target for algorithmic learning, akin to label
014                smoothing, thereby enhancing the performance of various learning paradigms
015                including multi-label learning, single positive label learning, and partial-label
016                learning. However, limited by dataset type and annotation inaccuracy, the same
017
                   label enhancement algorithm on different datasets struggles to achieve consistent
                   performance, for reasons derived from the following two insights: 1) Differential
018
                   Contribution of Feature Space and Logical Label Space: The feature space and log-
019
                   ical label space of different datasets contribute differently to generating an accurate
020                label distribution; 2) Presence of Noise and Incorrect Labels: Some datasets contain
021                noise and inaccurately labeled samples, leading to divergent outputs for similar
022                inputs. To address these challenges, we propose leveraging CLIP (Contrastive
023                Language-Image Pre-training) as a foundational strategy, treating the feature space
024                and the logical label space as two distinct modalities. By recoding these modalities
025                before applying the label enhancement algorithm, we aim to achieve a fair and
026                robust representation. In addition, we further explained the reasonableness of our
027                motives in the discussion session. Extensive experimental results demonstrate
028
                   the effectiveness of our approach to help existing label enhancement algorithms
                   improve their performance on several benchmarks.

      1       I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Currently, improving the performance of multi-label learning algorithms [30], single positive label
034   learning [26] and partial-label learning [29] by using the label shift become a common means, which
035   is represented by the label enhancement method [21, 25]. Label enhancement (LE) employs both the
036   feature space of the samples and the logical label space to generate a high-quality label distribution [7],
037   which is then enforced as a regularization term on a variety of learning paradigms (see Figure 1(a)).
038   Despite impressive achievements, current LE algorithms have yet to deliver consistent performance
039   across various benchmarks. Regarding this issue, we introduce two insights: 1) Many LE algorithms
040   for label distribution generation assume equal importance between the feature space and the logical
041   label space, suggesting they make an equivalent impact. Indeed, the contribution of their features is
042
      not fair. 2) Some LE datasets include noisy and inaccurate labels, potentially resulting in significantly
      varied outputs for analogous inputs.
043
044   For the first insight, we give statistical evidence (see Figure 1(b)) that the difficulty of projecting the
045   feature space to the label distribution and the logical label space to the label distribution is different
046   on multiple benchmarks. Note that not in all datasets, logical label space moves to label distribution
047   space at the least cost. Here, the evaluated method considers the magnitude of the energy [11] of
048
      the feature space X and the logical label space L projected to the label distribution space D with the
      help of the matrix W, i.e., the matrix energy of W (the sum of the product of the matrix eigenvalues
049
      and eigenvectors1 ). W can be regarded as a cost matrix, and its higher energy indicates that the
050
      projection of the matrix is more difficult, that is, the original matrix is further away from the target
051   matrix. Fig. 1(b) illustrates that the challenge of transforming logical labels → label distributionis
052   significantly lower than that of feature space → label distribution across the majority of datasets.

072   Current LE algorithms [6, 12, 13, 15, 22, 24, 27, 32] overlook this distinction. Indeed, this oversight
073   often results in these algorithms generating label distributions that are biased towards either the
074   feature space or the logical label space, which hinders their ability to perform universally well across
075   various benchmark datasets (particularly when the feature space transformation is less complex). For
076   the second insight, there is already a large body of literature [10, 14, 33, 34] supporting that these
077   datasets contain noise and incorrect labels. These works primarily address the issue by identifying
078
      and tolerating noisy data in the label distribution space, without considering the constraint of similar
      feature space inputs on the similarity of outputs in the label distribution space.
079
080   To address the challenges posed by the above two insights, we propose in this paper an LE framework
081   based on CLIP (Contrastive Language-Image Pre-training) [20] alignment. First, we develop a
082   pre-processing algorithm that harmonizes the transformation difficulty between the feature space and
083   the logical label space, with the assistance of CLIP. In this context, we consider feature space vectors
084
      and logical label vectors as distinct modalities that convey identical semantics, namely, the label
      distribution to be derived. Significantly, this paper ensures that the dimensionality of the transformed
085
      feature vectors aligns with that of the transformed logical label vectors. Second, to mitigate the
086
      impact of noisy data, we use the similarity matrix generated during the pre-training process of CLIP
087   for constraints i.e. by optimal transmission2 [23] to move its distribution closer to the distribution of
088   the label distribution.
089
090
      Based on the proposed novel LE framework, our contribution includes:
      i) We propose a pre-processing algorithm that ensures fairness in the representation of the recoded
091
      feature space and the logical labeling space to help downstream LE algorithms achieve better
092
      performance.
093   ii) We propose a novel regularization method to constrain the modeling process of CLIP to ensure
094   that the semantic information of new features is not corrupted. Extensive experimental results and
095   discussions demonstrate the importance of fairness representation, and there is also a significant
096   performance improvement for downstream LE algorithms.

131   Assumptions. We have three critical assumptions for a CLIP2LE framework (see Figure 2).
132   a) We recognize the feature space and the logical label space as inherently misaligned modalities,
133   which can be reconciled using the assistance of CLIP. Moreover, fortunately, a large part of these
134
      benchmarks are tabular data, and the model can gobble up a large number of samples in a single
      batch, which ensures the performance of CLIP.
135
      b) We identify a portion of the dataset that has noise and incorrect labels in the logical label space,
136
      which may lead to instability in the output of the LE model, and CLIP ensures that similar inputs
137   generate similar outputs.
138   (c) Since both the feature space and the logical label space point to the semantics of the label
139   distribution, their similarity should also be consistent with the distributional morphology of the label
140   distribution. Here, we use optimal transmission to ensure the robustness of CLIP modeling.
141

152
      First, given a pairwise feature space vector xi and a logical labeling vector Gi , we input xi and Gi into
153
      the feature encoder FE and the logical label encoder LLE, respectively, to obtain the re-encoded
154   features fi and li . Here, both FE and LLE use MLP as an encoder where the activation function
155   uses ReLU. Next, we conduct joint embedding and regularization for features fi and li . This step can
156   be written as follows:
157
                                        Ie = Normalize(fi Wi ), Te = Normalize(li Wt ),                                              (1)
158
159
      where Wi and Wt denote affine matrices (prototype matrix) and Normalize denotes normalization.
      Finally, we compute the cosine similarity between features (Ie , Te ) and perform a contrast loss
160
      function. This step can be written as follows:
161
                                                              logits = Ie TTe × Et ,                                                 (2)


                                                                         3
162
                                loss = (CEL(logits, dim=0) + CEL(logits, dim=1))/2,                          (3)
163
164
      where CEL denotes the cross-entropy function, t denotes temperature parameters for index E, and
      dim=0 and dim=1 denote computations done in a given dimension, respectively.
165
166   Feature distribution homogenization. So far, we expect features fi and li to be smooth, which
167   facilitates fast convergence of the downstream LE model during training. In general, we can
168   use distribution measure formulas such as KL dispersion, J dispersion, etc., however, they are
169   computationally expensive. Here, we use optimal transmission (OT) to make the distributions of fi
170
      and li close to the uniform distribution at a small cost. Specifically, first, we need to compute the
      migration cost of these two distributions; in this paper, we use earth mover’s distance (EMD)3 . Next,
171
      we use Sinkhorn [5] to optimize this transmission cost. This step can be written as follows:
172
173                               Sinkhorn → EMD(fi , U), Sinkhorn → EMD(li , U),                            (4)
174
      where Sinkhorn denotes the optimization method, U denotes uniform distribution, and here the
175
      hyperparameter we set to the 10−3 .
176
177   High-quality feature generation. After N iterations we obtain high quality fi and li . For the range
178   of values of fi and li , we use Tanh for constraints to avoid outliers. These two new features are used
179   in downstream LE algorithms for inference to obtain accurate label distributions Li .
180   Logical label vectors with the help of TableGPT. Predictably, we struggle with treating the logical
181   label vector as a modality, due to the fact that the logical label vector contains less information and
182   is sparser compared to the feature space X . Simply increasing the neurons and depth of the LLE
183   can lead to pre-training that is difficult to converge and an imbalance between LLE and FE exists.
184   To address this problem, we attempt to introduce a prior on the large model (TableGPT) to precode
185   the logistic label vectors. We input it as tabular data into TableGPT’s Table Encode to obtain a
186
      high-quality representation. It is worth noting that we are frozen the parameters of TableGPT during
      the training process. We also try to input the logic label vectors into BERT as text, but it doesn’t work
187
      well. Here, we extracted the 256-dimensional vectors of the middle layer in Table Encoder as new
188
      feature inputs to the MLP with only 3 layers.
189
190   Construction of the prototype matrix Wi and Wt . To make the output space of FE and LLE
191   more compact, we construct a prototype matrix as the projection space. The prototype space consists
192
      of a set of representative vectors. Specifically, we begin by splicing the input space X and the logical
193
      label space G into a matrix Y ∈ RM ×(N +L) . Next, we use the nearest neighbor algorithm to obtain
      L clusters, the center of which is the representation vector. Finally, we concatenate these L cluster
194
      centers into a matrix Ŵ and use PCA to compress it to the required dimensions. The whole step can
195
      be written as:
196
                                   Wi = PCA(CONCAT(KNN(SPL(X , G)))),                                      (5)
197
198   where Wi and Wt are constructed in the same way.

284   Experimental setup. In the experiments, we adopt twelve widely-used multi-label learning datasets
285   [9], which cover a broad range of cases with diversified multi-label properties. To evaluate the
286
      performance of our method + SPMLL [26] methods, we generate the single positive training data by
      randomly selecting one positive label to keep for each training example in the multi-label learning
287
      datasets. For each dataset, we run the comparing methods with 80%/10%/10% train/validation/test
288
      split. The validation and test sets are always fully labeled. Five popular multi-label metrics Ranking

534   In this paper, we propose a generalized label enhancement pre-processing method that can be applied
535   to tasks such as image, text, and speech. Here, we consider label enhancement as a multimodal fusion

537   CLIP algorithm. In the discussion session, we further illustrate that extant label enhancement
538   algorithms that do not take into account the fairness of the representations result in not all-round
539   performance on multiple benchmarks. Extensive experimental results demonstrate that our proposed
      CLIP2LE is effective and robust.
