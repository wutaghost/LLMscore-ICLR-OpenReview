009                                                   A BSTRACT
010
011             Generative Flow Networks (GFlowNets) are probabilistic samplers that learn
012             stochastic policies to generate diverse sets of high-reward objects, which is essen-
013             tial in scientific discovery tasks. However, most existing GFlowNets necessitate
014             training, becoming costly as the diversity of GFlowNets expands and trajectory
015             lengths increase. To alleviate this problem, we propose a method to Generate
016             high-performing GFlowNet parameters based on a given model structure, called
017             GenFlowNet. Specifically, we first prepare an autoencoder to extract latent rep-
018
                resentations of GeFlowNet parameters and reconstruct them. Then, a structure
                encoder is trained alongside a conditional latent diffusion model to generate the
019
                target GFlowNet parameters based on the given structure information. To the best
020
                of our knowledge, it is the first exploration to generate parameters of a probabilis-
021             tic sampler using the diffusion process. It enables us to obtain a new GFlowNet
022             without training, effectively reducing the trial-and-error cost during GFlowNet
023             development. Extensive experiments on diverse structures and tasks validate the
024             superiority and generalizability of our method.

070
      synthesize the desired GFlowNet parameters.

      2.1   P RELIMINARIES
109
110   Generative Flow Networks (GFlowNets) are designed to learn generative models over com-
111   plex distributions that are defined by unnormalized density functions within structured spaces.
112   GFlowNets model the generation of objects x within a sample space X as a sequential decision-
113   making process structured as an acyclic deterministic Markov Decision Process (MDP). This MDP
114   is defined by a set of states S ⊃ X and a set of actions A ⊆ S × S. The process initiates from a
115   designated initial state s0 , which has no incoming actions and concludes in a set of terminal states
116
      that coincide with the objects in X . Any object x ∈ X can be generated from s0 through a sequence
      of actions s0 → s1 → · · · → sn = x, with each action (si , si+1 ) ∈ A. These sequences, known as
117
      complete trajectories, are collectively denoted as T .
118
119   A (forward) policy PF is defined as a collection of distributions PF (s′ |s) over the subsequent states
120   for each non-terminal state s ∈ S \ X . This policy induces a distribution over the complete tra-
                                                             Qn−1
121   jectories T : PF (τ = (s0 → s1 → · · · → sn )) = i=0 PF (si+1 | si ). The policy enables the
122   sampling of objects in X by sampling a complete trajectory τ ∼ PF and returning its terminal state.
123   This induces a marginal distribution PFT over X , where PFT (x) represents the sum of PF (τ ) over
124
      all complete trajectories τ ending in x (an often intractable sum). Typically, the policy model can
      be implemented using simple multi-layer perceptrons (MLPs) with a few layers, though other archi-
125
      tectures could also be applicable. The objective of GFlowNet training is to estimate a parametric
126
      policy PF (·|·; θ) such that the induced distribution PFT is proportional to a non-negative  given re-
127
      ward function R : X → R≥0 , i.e., PF⊤ (x) = Z1 R(x) for ∀x ∈ X , where Z = x∈X R(x) is the
                                                                                          P
128   unknown normalization constant (partition function).
129
130   Conditional diffusion models extend the standard diffusion model by incorporating conditions into
131
      both the forward and reverse processes. The conditional information, defined by c, allows the model
      to generate data tailored to specific attributes or requirements.
132
133   The forward process in conditional models involves adding noise to an initial sample while con-
134   ditioning on c. The probability of transitioning from  √ xt−1 to xt under condition c is modeled
135   as a Gaussian distribution q(xt |xt−1 , c) = N (xt ; 1 − βt xt−1 , βt I) where βt are the timestep-
136   dependent noise levels, and I represents the identity matrix. The complete forward process, condi-
                                                     QT
137   tioned on c, is expressed as q(x1:T |x0 , c) = t=1 q(xt |xt−1 , c). The conditional reverse process
138   is designed to reconstruct the original sample from its noisiest state xT conditioned on c. And it
139
      is formulated by pθ (xt−1 |xt , c) = N (xt−1 ; µθ (xt , t, c), Σθ (xt , t, c)). In this process, µθ and Σθ
      are functions estimated by a neural network, which also processes the condition c, ensuring that the
140
      recovery of data respects the conditional constraints.
141
142   The training procedure involves minimizing the Kullback-Leibler(KL) divergence between the for-
143   ward and reverse conditional distributions, specifically:
144
145                         Ldm = Eq(x0 ,c) [DKL (q(xt−1 |xt , x0 , c)||pθ (xt−1 |xt , c))].                (1)
146
147   During inference, the model generates new samples by conditioning on c and sequentially applying
148   the learned reverse transitions from a noise distribution. This approach enables the generation of
149   data that closely adheres to the specified conditions.

151   2.2   PARAMETER AUTOENCODER
152
      The autoencoder (AE) in GenFlowNet comprises an encoder and a decoder, both of which are based
153
      on 1D Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs). The encoder’s role is to map GFlowNet parameters
154
      into a latent space, which serves to reduce the computational and memory costs of the diffusion
155   model (Sec. 2.3). The decoder is responsible for reconstructing these latent representations back
156   into the original parameters.
157
158
      Dataset preparation. To collect the training data for AE, we train GFlowNet models from scratch
      with stochastic gradient descent (SGD) optimizer and save dense checkpoints during the final epoch,
159
      generating a series of parameter training samples. We adopt P to indicate a single training sample.
160
161   Training Procedure. Given a parameter training sample P , we flatten it into a one-dimensional
      vector Θ ∈ RD , where D is the dimension of the subset of parameters. The encoder is then trained

      to obtain its robust latent representation, which can be used to reconstruct these flattened parameters
163   via the decoder. The encoding and decoding processes are formalized as follows:

181   2.3    C ONDITIONAL PARAMETER GENERATION
182
183   Diffusion models can inherently model conditional distributions through inputs like text (Reed et al.,
184   2016), semantic maps (Isola et al., 2017; Liu et al., 2019), or images (Isola et al., 2017). Inspired by
185   these, we design a diffusion model, taking various GFlowNet structures as conditions, to facilitate
186
      GFlowNet parameter generation.
187   Generating GFlowNet via generating MLP. GFlowNet algorithms learn a policy where the prob-
188   ability of sampling a terminating state s is proportional to R(s).
189
      From GFlowNet to policy: As illustrated in Fig. 3 (a), the policy generates trajectories starting in
190   state s0 by sampling actions at ∈ A(s) according to π(at |st ) = PF (st+1 |st ), where PF repre-
191   sents the “forward” transition probability within the constructive GFlowNet process. The learnable
192   forward policy governs the transition and specifies the GFlowNet, which also determines the struc-
193   tured internal constructions as illustrated in Fig. 3 (a) to (b). Consequently, the overall GFlowNet is
194   predominantly determined by the forward policy.
195
      From policy to MLP: In terms of neural architecture, the most straightforward GFlowNet employs
196   a neural network that is scalable depending on the available computational resources. This network
197   outputs a stochastic policy π(at |st ), where st represents a partially constructed object and at denotes
198   one of the possible actions from st . As illustrated in preliminaries and Fig. 3 from (b) to (c), a
199   GFlowNet forward policy can be effectively learned by a simple architecture, like a multi-layer
200   perceptron (MLP) (Bengio et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2023). At each step, the same MLP is employed
201   to generate a stochastic output at , which subsequently results in the next state st+1 = T (st , at ),
202   with T being dependent on the specific application.
203   Hence, the GFlowNet can be determined by an MLP. This allows us to utilize the structure of the
204   MLP as a condition for our GFlowNet parameter generation process.

      Conditional embedding. To effectively manage the diverse structural configurations of GFlowNets,
217   we introduce an encoder τstructure , (ρ; ·), where ρ represents the encoder’s parameters, designed to
218   encode a GFlowNet structure into a conditional embedding. Specifically, as illustrated in Fig. 3 (c) to
219   (d), the structure condition embedding, contains four critical structural parameters: state dimension
220   (s), horizon (h), number of dimensions (n), and size (d). These parameters collectively define
221   the architecture of the MLP within the GFlowNet. We concatenate these parameters to form the
222   condition vector c = [s, h, n, d] ∈ R4 . Then, this vector is fed into the structure encoder and
223   yielding τstructure (ρ; c), which will be used to control the diffusion model to generate parameters
224   tailored to specific structure requirements in the following.
225   Parameter Generation: A straightforward strategy for generating novel parameters is by directly
226   synthesizing them via a diffusion model. However, the computational and memory demands for this
227   approach can become excessively high, particularly when dealing with high-dimensional parameter
228   spaces. To mitigate this, we design a conditional diffusion model to generate latent representations
229   of parameters tailored to the specific structural conditions c of GFlowNets.
230   Unlike image data, which has an inherent spatial structure, GFlowNet parameters lack such spatial
231   correlations. Therefore, instead of using 2D convolutions typically employed in image synthesis
232   tasks, we adopt 1D convolutions to construct the diffusion model ϵθ , based on U-Net arachitecu-
233   ture (Ronneberger et al., 2015). During the generation phase, the latent representation of parameters
234   is progressively refined from an initial Gaussian noise distribution through successive denoising
235   steps. Specifically, at each step t, the current denoised parameter latent zt and the conditioning in-
236   formation c are passed through the diffusion model to predict the residual noise. To handle different
237
      GFlowNets and tasks, the conditional embedding τstructure (ρ; c) is integrated into the intermediate
      features of the diffusion model ϵθ via element-wise addition. The diffusion model is trained using
238
      the following objective function:
239

      where t is uniformly sampled from the interval [1, T ], ϵθ represents the denoising network parame-
      terized by θ. This formulation ensures that the model effectively learns to generate parameter latent
244
      that adhere to specific structural constraints.

      Inference procedure. During generation, we can feed random Gaussian noise, along with the spe-
247   cific condition c corresponding to a given structure into the diffusion model. It reverses the diffusion
248   process and progressively refines the initial noise based on the provided condition, ultimately syn-
249   thesizing a new set of parameter latent representations. Then, the pre-trained AE decoder projects it
250   from the latent space into new parameters, which are tailored for the GFlowNet as you wish.

428   variance across structures and models varies a little, which means that the neural field is compact
429   enough to be demonstrated by generative modeling of MLP representation with our diffusion model
430   and each layer parameters matter similarly for the final output representation. This is also observed
431   in (Erkoç et al., 2023). The appearance of the generated parameters is similar to the baseline, which
      verifies the robustness of our method.

      4   R ELATED WORK
487
488
489   Diffusion Models: Diffusion models have achieved outstanding results in visual generation tasks.
490
      These methods (Ho et al., 2020; Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021; Ho et al., 2022; Peebles & Xie, 2023;
      Hertz et al., 2022; Li et al., 2023) are grounded in non-equilibrium thermodynamics (Jarzynski,
491
      1997; Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015), following a generative process similar to GANs (Zhu et al.,
492
      2017; Isola et al., 2017; Brock et al., 2018), VAEs (Kingma & Welling, 2013; Razavi et al., 2019),
493   and flow-based models (Dinh et al., 2014; Rezende & Mohamed, 2015). Broadly, diffusion models
494   can be classified into three main branches. The first focuses on improving synthesis quality, as
495   demonstrated by models such as DALL·E 2 (Ramesh et al., 2022), Imagen (Saharia et al., 2022),
496   and Stable Diffusion (Rombach et al., 2022). The second branch aims to accelerate sampling speeds,
497   featuring models like DDIM (Song et al., 2020), Analytic-DPM (Bao et al., 2022), and DPM-Solver
498   (Lu et al., 2022). The final branch reinterprets diffusion models from a continuous perspective,
499   exemplified by score-based models (Song & Ermon, 2019; Feng et al., 2023).
500   Conditional Generation: Conditional generation has garnered significant attention in both com-
501   puter vision and natural language processing. Three key frameworks dominate this area: conditional
502   GANs (Mirza & Osindero, 2014; Isola et al., 2017; Zhu et al., 2017), conditional VAEs (Sohn et al.,
503   2015; Yan et al., 2016), and conditional diffusion models (Rombach et al., 2022), all of which in-
504   tegrate conditions to guide the generative process. This approach facilitates the creation of visually
505   coherent and semantically meaningful data samples. Conditional GANs incorporate attribute or la-
506   bel information to condition the generation process, while conditional diffusion models advance this
507
      further by generating high-quality images from textual descriptions. These diffusion-based models
      have outperformed GANs in terms of both visual coherence and semantic accuracy. Building on the
508
      success of conditional diffusion models, we extend this concept to the generation of neural network
509
      parameters, conditioned on specific attributes, for efficient model tuning.
510
511   Parameter Generation: Parameter generation has seen rapid advancements, with approaches like
512   HyperNetworks (Ha et al., 2016) and generative models of neural network checkpoints (Peebles
513
      et al., 2022) emerging as notable contributions. Ha et al. (2016) introduced HyperNetworks, which
      generates neural network parameters by learning from an auxiliary network. Finn et al. (2017) pro-
514
      posed Model-Agnostic Meta-Learning (MAML), a technique that learns an initialization that allows
515
      efficient fine-tuning across tasks. More recently, Peebles et al. (2022) developed G.pt, a model de-
516   signed to predict parameter updates given an initial parameter vector and a prompted loss function.
517   Schürholt et al. (2022) trained an autoencoder on a model zoo to learn hyper-representations for
518   generating new model weights, while Knyazev et al. (2021) applied a GNN-based model to sam-
519   ple network parameters. In a similar vein, Erkoç et al. (2023) generated neural implicit fields by
520   leveraging synthesized MLP weights. Wang et al. (2024) extended diffusion models to generate
521   high-performing neural network parameters across various architectures and datasets. Unlike these
522   prior works, our approach focuses on conditional and controllable parameter generation, aiming to
523   produce high-performance parameters tailored to specific GFlowNet. Our method not only achieves
524
      high-performance parameter generation but also demonstrates strong generalizability across differ-
      ent tasks and architectures.
525

      5   D ISCUSSION AND CONCLUSION
529
530   In this work, we propose GenFlowNet, a framework for generating GFlowNet parameters based on a
531   given structure. Our approach combines an autoencoder with a conditional latent diffusion model to
532   capture the distribution of GFlowNet parameters, allowing us to obtain high-performing parameters
533
      without further training. Our experiments demonstrate that GenFlowNet effectively generates novel,
      high-quality, and diverse parameters and exhibits remarkable generalizability across diverse tasks
534
      and structures.
535
536
537   Limitations and future works. However, there are still unresolved challenges, including better
538   GFlowNet structure design in various tasks, and ensuring the performance stability of the generated
539   parameters across more applications. Furthermore, integrating scientific knowledge into conditions
      offers promising directions for GenFlowNet.
