011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Inductive link prediction is a significant challenge in knowledge graphs, focusing on
014            predicting potential relations between unseen entities during training. A promising
015            approach is to utilize Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) to extract entity-independent
016
               features from surrounding subgraphs. However, existing mainstream subgraph
               extraction methods may lead to the loss of key entities and relations, resulting in
017
               many disconnected reasoning paths that seriously hinder effective message passing.
018
               To address this challenge, we propose a novel framework called Common Neighbor
019            Induced Message Passing (CNMP), designed to enhance message passing even
020            when reasoning paths are disconnected. We observe that the common neighbors of
021            two entities must share a reasoning path. Based on this insight, CNMP enhances
022            message passing by updating the distance labels of isolated common neighbors,
023            even if they are unreachable. This allows CNMP to incorporate new connected
024            equivalent relations, facilitating effective message passing. Furthermore, we in-
025            troduce a CNMP+ strategy that further improves the preservation of entities and
026            relations during the message-passing process. CNMP+ involves maintaining a list
027
               of common neighbors at various distances and using a probing strategy to recon-
               struct complete reasoning paths. Experiments across multiple datasets demonstrate
028
               that our method significantly outperforms existing state-of-the-art methods.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Knowledge graphs organize human knowledge in the form of factual triples (head entity, relation, tail
034
      entity), and these graphs represent entities as nodes and relations as edges. Examples of knowledge
035
      graphs include WordNet (Miller, 1995), Freebase (Bollacker et al., 2008), and DBPedia (Auer et al.,
036   2007). Recently, knowledge graphs have been widely used in natural language processing (Zhang
037   et al., 2019), question answering (Huang et al., 2019), and recommendation systems (Wang et al.,
038   2018). However, real-world knowledge graphs confront the challenge of continuously emerging
039   new entities, such as new users and products in e-commerce knowledge graphs or new molecules in
040   biomedical knowledge graphs(Breit et al., 2020; Bonner et al., 2022). Moreover, knowledge graphs
041   often suffer from incompleteness, i.e., some links are missing.
042   To address these challenges, extensive research efforts have been devoted to the inductive link
043   prediction task (Tran et al., 2016; Hamaguchi et al., 2017; Chamberlain et al., 2022; Lin et al., 2022).
044   Inductive link prediction intends to predict missing links between entities in knowledge graphs, where
045   entities during the training and inference stages can be different. Despite the importance of inductive
046   link prediction in real-world applications, many existing knowledge graph completion methods focus
047   on the transductive link prediction task (Bordes et al., 2013; Lin et al., 2015b; Trouillon et al., 2016;
048   Yang et al., 2015), which can only handle the entities seen during the training stage(Sadeghian et al.,
049   2019). Inductive link prediction is challenging because it requires models to predict missing relations
050
      between unseen entities during training. This means that models need to generalize what they have
      learned from training entities to unseen entities.
051
052   Existing inductive link prediction methods mainly focus on extracting surrounding subgraphs around
053   the relations to be predicted and learning entity-independent features within these subgraphs, such as
      relational representations and relational correlations. Graph Neural Network (GNN)-based approaches

054
      are well-known for their effective message-passing mechanism (e.g., graph convolution) that leverages
055   the structure of graphs to extract features within subgraphs. For example, GraIL (Teru et al., 2020)
056   is the first to introduce a GNN-based framework for relation prediction, which models relational
057   representations and reasons over subgraphs. CoMPILE (Mai et al., 2021) updates both relation

059   et al., 2021) focuses on modeling semantic correlations of relations within subgraphs, which uses
060   topology-aware correlations to boost edge-level interactions.

      The above methods are mainly based on enclosing subgraphs (Teru et al., 2020; Mai et al., 2021;
082
      Geng et al., 2022; Lin et al., 2022; Xu et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2021), which contain relations in the
083
      n-hop neighborhood intersection of the target entities(the head and tail of the relation to be predicted).
084   However, key entities and relations may be lost during the process of extracting enclosing subgraphs,
085   causing a significant amount of disconnected reasoning paths. This severely hinders message passing
086   for GNN-based methods, and consequently impacts overall reasoning performance. As shown in
087   Figure 1, the extraction of the 2-hop enclosing subgraph induced by entities u and v results in the
088   drop of key entities e1 and e3 , leading to the loss of relations r2 and r3 , leading to the disconnected
089   reasoning paths of the isolated node e2 . The 3-hop enclosing subgraph can preserve the reasoning
090   path, while it introduces irrelevant relations r4 and nodes e4 as well. On the WN18RR dataset
091   (Toutanova & Chen, 2015), over 45% of 2-hop enclosing subgraphs extract only the target relations,
092
      and over 75% of 3-hop enclosing subgraphs contain irrelevant relations (see Section 2.2).
093   To tackle this problem, we observe that the common neighbors of two entities must share a reasoning
094   path (please refer to Appendix A for detailed proof). Based on this insight, we propose a novel
095   Common Neighbor Induced Message Passing (CNMP) framework. Specifically, without introducing
096   any irrelevant information, we propose CNMP strategy to restore the connectivity of the disconnected
097   reasoning paths, and further propose CNMP+ strategy to iteratively reconstruct the disconnected
098
      reasoning paths.
099         (i) CNMP strategy. Given that the message passing mechanism relies on the distance infor-
100             mation between nodes to select the next passing node. To ensure that isolated common
101             neighbors on disconnected reasoning can perform message passing, CNMP strategy update
102             their distance to the target node from infinity to their shortest distance on the original graph.
103             Moreover, to preserve as much information from the original reasoning path as possible, we
104             introduce a new equivalence relation (the original relation with added distance information)
105             as the content of the message passing for the isolated common neighbor.
106        (ii) CNMP+ strategy. First, CNMP+ strategy maintains a list of common neighbors at varying
107             distances from the target node as the data foundation for probing. Then, through a target-
                driven probing strategy, CNMP+ starts with isolated common neighbors and systematically

      2.1   M OTIVATION
123
124
      To further elaborate on our motivation, we introduce a rule-based learning perspective to analyze the
125   reasoning process within the subgraph. Rule-based approaches seek to extract first-order logical rules
126   by analyzing reasoning paths within knowledge graphs. Each rule comprises a head and a body, with
127   the head representing a single atom, i.e., a fact expressed in the form of Relation(head entity, tail
128   entity), while the body is a set of atoms. Given a head R(y, x) and body B1 (y, z1 ) ∧ B2 (z1 , z2 ) ∧
129   · · · ∧ BT (zT −1 , x), there is a rule R(y, x) ← B1 (y, z1 ) ∧ B2 (z1 , z2 ) ∧ · · · ∧ BT (zT −1 , x) . A rule
130   is connected when each atom within it shares a common variable with at least one other atom, and a
131   rule is closed if each variable in the rule appears in at least two atoms (Yang et al., 2017).
132   In particular, we pay more attention to the closed and connected rules, that is, the reasoning paths
133   between target nodes, as closeness and connectedness prevent finding rules with irrelevant relations
134   (Sadeghian et al., 2019). These closed and connected paths are also crucial for ensuring the
135   effectiveness of message passing and feature extraction. We define the length of a rule as the count
136   of nodes forming the rule, and define the relevant rules as those that are both closed, connected,
137   and with a length not exceeding 2h + 1. The irrelevant rules are those that are either not closed,
138   not connected, or possess a length exceeding 2h + 1, where h denotes the number of hops for the
139
      neighboring nodes of the target nodes. To align with prior work (Teru et al., 2020), we set the hop
      value as 2.
140
141   Existing methods mainly reason on enclosing subgraphs (Teru et al., 2020; Mai et al., 2021; Geng
142   et al., 2022; Lin et al., 2022; Xu et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2021). The original intention of the enclosing
143   subgraph is twofold: to effectively eliminate the irrelevant rules and preserve the relevant rules
144   around the target link. However, the enclosing subgraph cannot achieve both criteria. Specifically,
145
      the 2-hop enclosing subgraph produces disconnected reasoning paths, hindering message passing and
      failing to preserve relevant rules. Conversely, the 3-hop enclosing subgraph allows effective message
146
      passing but introduces irrelevant rules, leading to lower-quality features extracted through message
147
      passing.
148
149   We propose two toy examples as illustrations and further conduct statistical analyses to reveal the
150   prevalence of the phenomenon in Section 2.2.

152   2.2   A NALYSIS OF THE ENCLOSING SUBGRAPH
153
154   The basic steps for subgraph extraction are as follows: For a triple (u, rt , v), the initial step involves
155   the extraction of the enclosing subgraph around the target nodes u and v (Teru et al., 2020). The
156   subsequent steps outline the procedure for generating the enclosing subgraph between nodes u and v.
157   First, we compute the neighborhoods Nk (u) and Nk (v) for u and v, respectively. Here, k denotes
158
      the maximum distance used to define the neighborhoods around nodes u and v. Second, we take an
      intersection of Nk (u) and Nk (v) to get Nk (u) ∩ Nk (v). Third, we calculate the enclosing subgraph
159
      G(u, rt , v) by removing isolated nodes from the intersection of sets Nk (u) and Nk (v), where isolated
160
      nodes are defined as nodes lacking any edges connecting them to other nodes within Nk (u) ∩ Nk (v).

      2.3   T HE CNMP STRATEGY
246
247   The enclosing subgraph method may result in the problem of disconnected reasoning paths, as in
248   Figure 2 the reasoning paths between node e2 and target nodes u and v are disconnected. Therefore,
249   we propose CNMP strategy to restore disconnected reasoning paths in enclosing subgraphs.
250
      Specifically, for isolated nodes, we use the shortest path distances in the original knowledge graph to
251
      update their unreachable distance labels, so the isolated nodes can still pass messages. Moreover,
252
      to explicitly distinguish the actual connection situation between the nodes in the common neigh-
253   borhood and the target nodes u and v, we directly incorporate the distance information into the
254   relation representation of the node for message passing. Specifically, this is reflected in the vector
255   concatenation operation. For example, in the 2-hop enclosing subgraph of Figure 2, for the node
256   e2 that is an isolated common neighborhood, the CNMP strategy replaces the relations r2 and r3
                                                                            ′
257   with new equivalent relations to the target nodes. Specifically, r2 = r2 ⊕ (d(e2 , u), d(e2 , v)) and
       ′
258   r3 = r3 ⊕(d(e2 , u), d(e2 , v)), where ⊕ denotes vector concatenation, and d(i, u) denotes the shortest
259   distance between nodes i and u in the original knowledge graph without considering any path through
260   node v.
261

262   between node e2 and targets nodes u and v, and we also effectively eliminate nodes and corresponding

      We evaluate the models on both classification and ranking metrics. For both metrics, we compare
354   our method to existing state-of-the-art methods, including Neural LP (Yang et al., 2017), DRUM

357   (Xu et al., 2022), and TACT(Chen et al., 2021). Nearly all the existing state-of-the-art methods are
358   based on rule learning or enclosing graphs. They have advantages such as rich logical information
359   and good scalability, but may lose key entities and relations during the extraction of enclosing
360   subgraphs, leading to many disconnected reasoning paths, which severely hinders message passing
361   and consequently impacts overall reasoning performance. Comparing our approach with these
362
      methods can demonstrate the superiority of our approach.

364   3.3.1    C LASSIFICATION METRIC
365
      We use the area under the precision-recall curve (AUC-PR) as the classification metric following
366   GraIL (Teru et al., 2020). We substitute either the head or the tail entity of each test triple with a
367   randomly selected entity to generate the corresponding negative triples through sampling. Then we
368   score the positive triples with an equal number of negative triples to calculate AUC-PR following
369   GraIL. To make the results more reliable, we run each experiment five times with different random
370   seeds and report the mean results.
371
      From the AUC-PR results in Table 3, we observe that on the twelve versions of the three
372   datasets,models based on CNMP strategies have reached the optimum in all AUC-PR values. Specifi-
373   cally, CNMP models outperform rule-based baselines, including Neural LP, DRUM, and RuleN by a
374   significant margin. Compared with the subgraph-based models GraIL, CoMPILE, TACT, RMPI, Con-
375   GLR, and SNRI, the best performance CNMP models can achieve average AUC-PR improvements

      4.1    T RANSDUCTIVE L INK P REDICTION
500
501   Link prediction is the problem of predicting the existence of a link between two nodes within a
502   network(Liben-Nowell & Kleinberg, 2003). Following the success of word embeddings in language
503   modeling Mikolov et al. (2013); Pennington et al. (2014), various transductive link prediction models
504   have been developed based on entity and relation embeddings, including TransE(Bordes et al., 2013),
505   TransR(Lin et al., 2015a), ComplEx(Trouillon et al., 2016) and Distmult(Yang et al., 2015). However,
506   these methods can only handle the entities seen during the training stage(Sadeghian et al., 2019).
507
508
      4.2    I NDUCTIVE L INK P REDICTION
509
510   Inductive link prediction can predict missing relations between unseen entities during training.
511   Rule-based methods and GNN-based methods are two mainstream approaches for inductive link
512   prediction. Rule-based approaches (Cohen, 2016; Yang et al., 2017; Sadeghian et al., 2019) aim to
513   discover logical rules from frequent co-occurrence patterns of relations, and these rules are entity-
514   independent, making them inherently inductive. While traditional rule-based methods face scalability
515   and expressive power issues with large knowledge graphs, recent methods like Neural LP (Yang
516   et al., 2017) and DRUM (Sadeghian et al., 2019) improve this by using differentiable approaches
517   and low-rank matrix approximation, respectively. GNN-based methods, such as GraIL (Teru et al.,
518
      2020) and CoMPILE (Mai et al., 2021) , enhance link prediction by reasoning over subgraphs, but
      they overlook semantic correlations between relations. To address this, approaches like SNRI (Xu
519
      et al., 2022) and ConGLR (Lin et al., 2022) improve the representation of neighboring relations,
520
      while NBFNet (Zhu et al., 2021) introduces a neural Bellman-Ford network for link prediction. More
521   details about inductive link prediction can be found in Appendix D.
522
523
524   5     C ONCLUSION
525
526
      In this paper, we propose a novel Common Neighbor Induced Message Passing framework (CNMP),
527
      which effectively ensures message passing even when reasoning paths in subgraphs are disconnected,
528   and to avoid introducing irrelevant information. Specifically, for disconnected reasoning paths,
529   we propose CNMP strategy to efficiently restore path connectivity, and further introduce CNMP+
530   strategy to iteratively reconstruct the accurate and complete paths. To demonstrate the effectiveness
531   of our method, we combine the CNMP strategies with classical GNN-based methods in inductive link
532   prediction as the CNMP models. Experiments demonstrate that CNMP models accurately extract key
533   information from subgraphs based on a new messaging mechanism, leading to superior performance
534   over existing state-of-the-art methods on inductive link prediction. Moreover, experiments under
535   large KGs and fully inductive settings demonstrate a superior scalability and generalization ability of
536
      CNMP models.
537   Regarding the limitations of CNMP method, although it achieves remarkable performance in the
538   inductive link prediction task with classical RCN and RGCN, more new GNN-based methods can
539   be explored in future works. Moreover, CNMP focuses on inductive link prediction tasks, and its
      effectiveness can be verified on various forms of tasks in the future.

      A     P ROOF OF C OMMON N EIGHBORS IN A R EASONING PATH
757
758
      A.1    PART 1: S INGLE - HOP N EIGHBORS
759
760   Let G = (V, E) be a graph with vertex set V and edge set E. For any vertex x, its single-hop
761   neighborhood, denoted N (x), is defined as the set of vertices directly connected to x, i.e., N (x) =
762   {y ∈ V |(x, y) ∈ E or (y, x) ∈ E}. A reasoning path is a sequence of vertices (x1 , x2 , . . . , xn )
763   where x1 = u, xn = v, and (xi , xi+1 ) ∈ E for all 1 ≤ i < n, denoted Puv .
764
      Theorem: If two vertices u, v ∈ V have a common single-hop neighbor c, then there exists a
765
      reasoning path Puv from u to v that includes c.
766
767   Proof of Theorem: Given u, v ∈ V and a common single-hop neighbor c ∈ N (u) ∩ N (v), by
768   definition, (u, c) ∈ E and (c, v) ∈ E. The sequence of vertices (u, c, v) forms a valid reasoning path
769
      Puv , since it starts with u, ends with v, and each consecutive pair of vertices is connected by an edge
      in E.
770
771
772   A.2    PART 2: M ULTI - HOP N EIGHBORS
773
774
      For any vertex x ∈ V and a positive integer k, the k-hop neighborhood of x, denoted by Nk (x), is
      defined as the set of vertices that can be reached from x by traversing exactly k edges. Formally,
775
      Nk (x) includes vertex y if there exists a sequence of vertices (x = x0 , x1 , . . . , xk = y) such that
776
      (xi−1 , xi ) ∈ E for all 1 ≤ i ≤ k.
777
778   Theorem: If two vertices u, v ∈ V have a common multi-hop neighbor c, then there exists a
779   reasoning path Puv from u to v that includes c.
780   Proof of Theorem: Let u, v ∈ V be two vertices with a common multi-hop neighbor c ∈ V . This
781   implies that for some positive integer k, c ∈ Nk (u) ∩ Nk (v). By the definition of Nk , there exist
782   paths Puc from u to c and Pcv from c to v, each consisting of k edges.
783
      To construct a reasoning path from u to v that includes the common multi-hop neighbor c, we
784   concatenate the paths Puc and Pcv where c is the common vertex between the two paths. Formally,
785   we can write:
786
787
788                                              Puv = Puc ⊕ Pcv                                           (1)
789
790   where ⊕ denotes the concatenation of the paths such that the common vertex c appears only once in
791   the concatenated path Puv . Explicitly, if
792
793
794                                              Puc = (u, . . . , c)
795                                              Pcv = (c, . . . , v)
796
797   then the concatenated path Puv is given by:
798
799
800                                          Puv = (u, . . . , c, . . . , v)
801
802
      This results in a reasoning path that starts at u, passes through c, and ends at v, demonstrating that
803   there exists such a path Puv that includes c.

      prediction relation link and other negative candidate sample links for both classification and rank

      tasks. Note that the CNMP-base model is only based on RCN.

       Incorporating human knowledge is one of the key task for artificial intelligence (AI) (Ji et al., 2022).
1060
       Knowledge representation and reasoning entails the structuring of knowledge in a manner that
1061   empowers intelligent systems to address intricate tasks (Newell et al., 1959). Knowledge graphs,
1062   as a structured representation of human knowledge, have attracted extensive research interest from
1063   both academia and industry (Dong et al., 2014; Nickel et al., 2015; Pan et al., 2023). A knowledge
1064   graph comprises entities, relations, and semantic descriptions. Entities contain both tangible real-
1065   world objects and abstract concepts, while relations denote the connections between entities. The
1066   semantic descriptions of entities and their relations encompass well-defined types and properties.
1067   The prevalent use of property graphs or attributed graphs is noteworthy, where nodes and relations
1068   possess corresponding properties or attributes (Bordes et al., 2011).

1070   D.2    L INK P REDICTION
1071
1072   Link prediction is the problem of predicting the existence of a link between two nodes within a
1073   network (Liben-Nowell & Kleinberg, 2003). Given the prevalence of networks, link prediction is
1074   widely used in numerous applications, including friend recommendation within social networks
1075   (Adamic & Adar, 2003), co-authorship prediction in citation networks (Shibata et al., 2012), movie
1076
       recommendation on platforms such as Netflix (Bennett, 2007), forecasting protein interactions in
       biological networks (Qi et al., 2006), drug response prediction (Yang et al., 2019), metabolic network
1077
       reconstruction (Lü & Zhou, 2011a), and identification of covert terrorist groups (Lü & Zhou, 2011b).
1078
1079   Link prediction pertains to the prediction of connections in homogeneous graphs, where nodes and
       links possess singular types. This constitutes the most elementary scenario, and a significant portion

       of link prediction research concentrates on this configuration. In bipartite user-item networks, it
1081   is recognized as matrix completion or recommendation systems, where nodes have two different
1082   types (user and item), and links can exhibit multiple types, correlating with diverse ratings that users
1083   may assign to items. For knowledge graphs, link prediction is typically denoted as knowledge graph
1084   completion, where individual nodes represent different entities, and links represent multiple relations.
1085   Notably, it is often the case that a link prediction algorithm initially formulated for homogeneous
1086   graphs can be readily adapted to heterogeneous graphs, such as and knowledge graphs, through the
1087   incorporation of heterogeneous node type and relation type information.

1150   As mentioned in Section 2.3, after the extraction of the enclosing subgraph, the next step involves
1151
       node labeling. For example, a technique referred to as Double Radius Node Labeling (DRNL) is
       used to assign integer labels to each node within the subgraph to give them with additional distance
1152
       features. The node labeling process makes nodes more differentiable within the enclosing subgraph.
1153
1154   DRNL operates through the following steps. Initially, it assigns label 1 to both node x and node y.
1155   Then, any node i characterized by a radius denoted as (d(i, x), d(i, y)) = (1, 1) receives label 2 and
1156   (1, 2) or (2, 1) are assigned with label 3. Nodes with a radius of (1, 3) or (3, 1) are assigned label 4.
1157
       Nodes with a radius of (2, 2) are assigned label 5, and so forth.
1158   In essence, DRNL incrementally assigns ascending labels to nodes based on their expanding radial
1159   distance from the two central nodes, x and y.

1165   A1: We acknowledge the observation regarding the “1 vs. 50" setting adopted in CNMP’s inductive
1166   link prediction, where 50 negative samples are randomly generated for each positive triple. It is
1167   important to note that this setting is not unique to CNMP but has its roots in prior works, such as
1168   GraIL (Teru et al., 2020), and is widely used in (Mai et al., 2021; Lin et al., 2022; Zhu et al., 2021;
1169   Xu et al., 2022).
1170   Moreover, we would like to emphasize that the use of ranking strategy, i.e., the "1 vs. 50" setting,
1171   serves as a robust method for evaluating the model performance. This approach provides a repre-
1172   sentative subset of data that effectively captures the comprehensive ranking behavior of the model
1173   (Lebanon & Mao, 2007).
1174   While we appreciate the concerns raised regarding the applicability of CNMP to the “1 vs. all"
1175   setting, we contend that the chosen experimental framework is well-suited for practical applications.
1176   Specifically, in scenarios such as product recommendation based on knowledge graphs and user
1177   recommendation relying on user graphs, inductive link prediction models, including CNMP, play an
1178   important role in accurately ranking a subset of nodes. It can be seen as a refining stage after ranking
1179   all entities to get more precise ranking results (Covington et al., 2016).
1180   In conclusion, we maintain “1 vs. 50" experimental setting, which is both valid and practical. We
1181   believe that models assessed under this setting are practical for real-world applications and important
1182   for inductive link prediction.

1273
